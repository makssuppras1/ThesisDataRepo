Summary 
Growing pressure on Denmark’s limited sand and gravel reserves, coupled with am-
bitious infrastructure plans, is intensifying the search for lower‑impact road‑building 
materials. This thesis evaluates nine alternative pavement structures in which natural 
aggregates in the unbound base and/or sub‑base layers are replaced by recycled 
crushed asphalt, recycled crushed concrete, recycled crushed brick and foam‑glass 
lightweight aggregate (FG-LWA). 
A comparative attributional cradle-to-laid life‑cycle assessment (LCA) was car-
ried out for a functional unit of 1 m² of low‑traffic pavement over 20 years in Denmark. 
Impact scores were calculated in SimaPro using the EN 15804 +A2 (adapted) / EF 3.1 
method and the natural‑aggregate pavement (Scenario 1), cross‑checked against In-
fraLCA for global‑warming‑potential equivalence. Up‑front costs were derived from 
Ramboll unit prices and correlated with environmental indicators. Local one‑at‑a‑time 
sensitivity analysis explored transport‑distance break‑even points. 
Across 9 of 16 midpoint categories, incorporating FG‑LWA in the subbase layer 
of a pavement structure was preferable to using sand or crushed brick. For the re-
maining 7 impact categories, crushed‑brick substitution was preferable. Scenario 1 ex-
hibited the highest impacts in 10 categories. Transportation represented the dominant 
contribution for most Scenarios, except those containing FG-LWA, for which the FG-
LWA production itself was the major contributor in most impact categories.  
The study concludes that targeted substitution of virgin aggregates can halve 
several midpoint impacts without cost penalties, but careful attention to shipping dis-
tances and energy sources for FG‑LWA production is critical. Policy recommendations 
include updating Danish pavement specifications to recognise FG‑LWA and 
crushed‑brick blends, and integrating transport‑distance thresholds into construction 
machinery and material‑selection guidance.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
1.1.1 Motivation 
A more resource-efficient approach to infrastructure development is urgently needed 
to balance economic growth with environmental sustainability. As global demand for 
construction-grade sand and gravel materials rises and scarcity worsens due to eco-
nomic development and increased building activities, it becomes crucial to evaluate 
the environmental impacts associated with the extraction and use of raw materials in 
construction. The construction sector was the principal contributor to Denmark's ma-
terial carbon footprint in 2023, accounting for 31% of the total (Keys et al., 2023). A 
study conducted by NIRAS (2020), as cited by Danske Regioner, highlights that the 
primary source of climate impact within the construction and civil engineering industry 
arises from the extraction of raw materials in construction (Danske Regioner, 2020). 
The Danish government has updated building regulations, limited buildings’ climate 
footprints, and implemented requirements to improve the traceability of construction 
and demolition (C&D) to reduce the environmental impact of the construction and dem-
olition sector. Still, more waste reduction efforts and innovative resource use should 
be made (Keys et al., 2023). 
Among non-metallic mineral materials, sand, gravel, and crushed rock —com-
monly called construction aggregates—accounted for the largest share of materials 
used and extracted worldwide in 2017 (OECD, 2019). Denmark faces a projected in-
creasing lack of sand, gravel, and stone supply, with an assessment indicating the 
country could exhaust its resources in about 44 years. Consumption was projected to 
rise from 29 million m³ in 2016 to around 45 million m³ by 2040, although long-term 
uncertainty remains, as socio-economic fluctuations were not accounted for (Larsen 
et al., 2019). According to data from Statistik Denmark (2024), there has been a 15% 
increase in the extraction of stone, gravel, and sand in Denmark between 2009 and 
2023. This upward trend follows a notable decline in extraction rates in the 2007-2009 
global economic recession. The subsequent recovery of the Danish economy post-
2009 (Danmarks Nationalbank, 2010) led to a resurgence in extraction activities, given 
that economic upturns increase raw material consumption (Larsen et al., 2019). Sand 
extraction impacts all 17 sustainable development goals (SDGs). Naturally occurring 
==PAGE:12==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
12 
in coastal, river, and underground environments, sand plays a vital role in controlling 
erosion, delivering nutrients, supporting food security, filtering water, and maintaining 
aquifer quality (Agnusdei et al., 2024). In Denmark, gravel extraction is constrained by 
gravel pits occupying less than 1 % of the national territory, reflecting intense land-use 
competition. Additionally, mineral resources belong to the landowner, requiring their 
consent for extraction. Marine extraction faces increasing restrictions from various 
competing interests and is further hindered by high initial costs for transportation lo-
gistics. To address this potential shortage, material substitution is suggested (Larsen 
et al., 2019). 
In the past three decades, the demand for construction-grade sand and gravel 
materials has increased exponentially worldwide due to the economic development in 
many countries and the consequential growth in building activities (Agnusdei et al., 
2024). In Denmark, proposals such as the "Denmark Forward – Infrastructure Plan 
2035," developed in 2021, aim to develop infrastructure by improving national road 
and railway systems (Transportministeriet, 2021). This proposal contradicts Den-
mark’s efforts to reduce the environmental impact associated with C&D, given the ma-
terial-intensive nature of the sector and the high carbon intensity of traditional building 
materials (Keys et al., 2023).  
A potentially more resource-efficient and circular approach involves utilizing re-
cycled aggregates to replace virgin aggregates in pavement base and subbase layers, 
addressing the growing demand for virgin aggregates in road construction (Khan et 
al., 2024). The "Denmark Forward" proposal does not discuss using alternative mate-
rials in the layers (courses) within the pavement structure. Crushed asphalt, crushed 
brick/tile, and crushed concrete have been accepted as pavement layer aggregate 
substitute materials by the Danish Road Directorate (Danish Road Directorate, 2024). 
From an availability perspective, asphalt constituted Denmark's largest share of con-
struction and demolition waste in 2022, followed by concrete (Danish Environmental 
Protection Agency, 2024). Given that building systems have historically been designed 
using asphalt and concrete due to their favorable technical properties, recycling these 
materials would seem to be the most practical, low-effort way to cut landfilling and 
conserve virgin resources. However, the use of construction and demolition (C&D) 
waste—primarily fractions of concrete, masonry, and asphalt—for pavement con-
struction has been associated with a risk of leaching, potentially affecting subsoil and 
==PAGE:13==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
13 
groundwater resources (Butera et al., 2015). There is an increasing demand to inves-
tigate alternative materials with a lower life cycle impact that perform similarly to tradi-
tional construction aggregates (Perera et al., 2021). 
According to Sing (2004–2005, as cited in Koirala & Joshi, 2017), the term con-
struction quality sand is used for rock particles predominantly composed of silicon di-
oxide (SiO2). The same applies to commercial container (soda-lime) glass (Moretti & 
Hreglich, 2013). Various forms of glass can be considered supplementary materials in 
road pavement layers, enhancing material composites while offering environmental 
benefits (Perera et al., 2021).  
Glass has a low heating value, making it unsuitable for energy recovery in ther-
mal treatments or incineration (Demetrious & Crossin, 2019). Yet, 0.93% of the total 
glass waste in Denmark was incinerated in 2022 (Statistik Denmark, 2024). Although 
the exact rationale for this disposal route is not specified, the fraction is thought to 
arise from two streams: (a) glass that is mistakenly discarded in mixed municipal waste 
and therefore follows the residual-waste pathway (Statistics Denmark, 2022), and (b) 
glass deemed hazardous when contaminated (Danish Environmental Protection 
Agency, 2024). Denmark has a well-established and reasonably documented waste 
management system, but off-spec waste, which does not meet quality standards in 
separation facilities, is not adequately documented. Redirecting this off-spec material 
presents an opportunity: the company Dansk Celleglas uses it to produce a foam glass 
lightweight fill aggregate (FG-LWA) made out of 96-98% waste glass cullet for insula-
tion and drainage in construction (Green Gravels, 2024). Thus, glass that is unsuitable 
for re-melting or closed-loop recycling could be valorised in FG-LWA applications, re-
ducing the share that currently ends up in incineration.

The company Ramboll has shown an interest in researching the impacts asso-
ciated with FG-LWA utilization in pavement structures in Copenhagen. FG-LWA can 
act as an anti-capillary barrier beneath pavements by blocking upward moisture mi-
gration (Dansk Celleglas, 2023). This prevents freeze–thaw damage in soils with high 
organic content found in Copenhagen (Andersen, 2018), which are especially prone 
to water retention (O’Kelly & Sivakumar, 2014) and frost heave.  
Due to concerns with technical performance, alternatives to sand struggle to be 
considered viable alternatives, even when meeting the technical specifications. To 
overcome this barrier, it is essential to update technical standards and government 
guidelines, such as infrastructure specifications, while evaluating the economic bene-
fits of using alternative materials in roadworks and addressing associated environmen-
tal concerns (Tefa et al., 2022).

1.1.2 Technical Aspects of Road Design Specifications in Denmark 
Roads, streets, and paths interact with their surroundings; therefore, the road's design 
must take this into account (Vejbefaestelser, 2010).  
A traffic load class refers to the grouping of traffic loads into classes T0 to T7, 
from lower to higher traffic roads, based on the number of lorries or E10 loads. E10 
refers to the equivalent of the load transferred to the road from the wheels on a 10-ton 
weighing axle (Vejdirektoratet, 2023). 
Flexible pavements are layered systems. The layers are organized in ascend-
ing order to receive and transmit loads (Balaguera et al., 2018). The following de-
scribes the constituent layers of a flexible pavement from the lowest to the highest 
layer. 
 
Figure 2. Principles of the constituent layers and structure for the construction of an asphalt 
road in Denmark. Taken from Vejdirektoratet (2004) and translated. 
 
The soil, the road's subgrade or foundation, is delimited by the raw soil surface 
or formation layer. The subgrade is the result of processing when excess topsoil is 
removed where the road is being excavated, or mineral soil is added and compacted 
where the soil is being filled. The top layer of soil below the formation layer may be 
==PAGE:16==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
16 
treated with a stabilizing agent like lime or cement to increase the bearing capacity of 
the subsoil (Vejbefaestelser, 2010). 
Pavement is the term for all the layers above the subgrade. The pavement is 
often named after the top layer, e.g., asphalt pavement, concrete pavement, etc., even 
if the pavement consists of several layers of different types of material (Vejbe-
faestelser, 2010). 
The subbase layer is between the subgrade and the actual bearing layers in 
the road pavement. This layer is implemented in Denmark for any traffic load class 
(high or low traffic), given that it acts as a surface drain, draining away any water that 
has penetrated the pavement and therefore providing frost protection. It also helps to 
increase the overall bearing capacity of the road pavement (Vejbefaestelser, 2010). 
This layer is traditionally made of sand, gravel, or bottom ash (Vejdirektoratet, 2023). 
The road surface refers to the upper part of the pavement, and it traditionally 
consists of an unbound base layer, a bound layer, a binder course (only for T5 to T7) 
(Danish Road Directorate, 2013, Table 15), and a wearing course. 
The unbound base function is to support the bound base course of the road at 
the bottom (Christensen, 2010), often composed of gravel or macadam (Vejbe-
faestelser, 2010).  
Bound layers are those bound by either bitumen or cement (Vejdirektoratet, 
2015). The bound base layer provides the necessary load-bearing capacity by distrib-
uting the loads from traffic from the road surface down to the subgrade so the individ-
ual layers of the pavement and the subgrade remain intact without experiencing forces 
that cause damage or sinking (Vejbefaestelser, 2010). This layer is typically made out 
of gravel (Vejdirektoratet, 2023). 
The binder layer, between the bound base layers and the wearing course in 
flexible pavements, is added to ensure proper adhesion between the two layers 
(Vejbefaestelser, 2010). This layer is typically made from asphalt concrete binder 
course (ABB) or asphalt concrete (AB) (Vejdirektoratet, 2023). 
The top layer, a typical asphalt concrete surface wearing course 
(Vejdirektoratet, 2023), provides a smooth surface of high quality (Christensen, 2010) 
as well as friction, driving comfort, drainage, tightness, wear resistance, durability, and 
light reflection properties (Vejbefaestelser, 2010). 
==PAGE:17==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
17 
The subbase can consist predominantly of recycled aggregates (RA) since it is 
subjected to relatively lower stress levels from vehicular traffic. However, the quality 
and composition of the subbase layer still significantly influence the structural re-
sponse of the upper asphalt layers, affecting both their design life and thickness (Tefa 
et al., 2022). 
During construction, and depending on the type of road, 8–15 tons of materials 
are typically used per meter of lane (Christensen, 2010). 
 
1.1.3 Road construction key material parameters 
Extensive field and laboratory investigations have been done to study the performance 
of pavements made with different types of coarse aggregates. The properties that af-
fect performance have been incorporated into the aggregate classification system 
(Peapully et al., 1994). Some key properties that characterize aggregate performance 
as road materials are elastic modulus, crushing resistance, Los Angeles Abrasion 
(LA), grading, and fines content.  
The elastic modulus (E) represents a constant ratio of stress and strain (a stiff-
ness). A material is elastic if it can return to its original shape or size immediately after 
being stretched or squeezed, as long as the applied load does not cause it to deform 
permanently (Pavement interactive, n.d.).  
Compressive resistance (or crushing resistance or hardness) is the maximum 
crushing load an aggregate can withstand before cracking or breaking (Kaliyan & Mo-
rey, 2009).  
The L.A. abrasion test evaluates the degradation of a coarse aggregate sample 
in a rotating drum with steel spheres. Aggregates that lack adequate resistance to 
abrasion and polishing can lead to premature structural failure and reduced skid re-
sistance. Poor abrasion resistance may also produce excessive dust, potentially caus-
ing environmental and mixture control issues. Lower L.A. abrasion loss percentages 
indicate tougher, more abrasion-resistant aggregates (Pavement interactive, n.d.).  
Aggregate gradation, the distribution of particle sizes, affects properties such 
as stiffness, durability, workability, stability, and moisture susceptibility. Allowable gra-
dations ensure that mixtures meet required standards for void filling, strength, and 
==PAGE:18==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
18 
durability, influencing factors like cement and water requirements and shrinkage. In-
stability may result from excessively small maximum sizes, and poor workability and/or 
segregation may result from substantial maximum sizes (Pavement interactive, n.d.).  
The fines content is quantified because a mix with a high fines content will be 
more challenging to compact than a mix with low fines content (Pavement interactive, 
n.d.).  
 
1.1.4 Mathematical Model of Pavement Performance (MMOPP) Software 
MMOPP was introduced by Ramboll as the software used to design pavement struc-
tures in accordance with regular Danish practice. It is a design program linked to the 
Road Standard for the Design of Pavements and Reinforcement Surfacings. The soft-
ware can design flexible pavements (asphalt layer laid on unbound base course of 
gravel layers), calculating the required layer thicknesses to achieve a theoretically ex-
pected lifespan that meets the desired design period (Danish Road Directorate, 2017). 
When using the analytical design tool, its possible to select the traffic load class, 
the type and thickness of the wearing course, whether bitumen will be incorporated, 
the type and quality of the unbound base layers, and the type of subgrade (“frost-
proof”, “frost-susceptible”, or “splitting frost susceptible”). After defining these param-
eters, the user can select the desired design period as well as adjust the E values for 
the pavement materials. The default rigidity of a “splitting frost susceptible” subgrade 
is 20 MPa, indicating a soft soil with low stiffness. The default material for the subbase 
is a ”BL (bundsikring) II, U ≤ 3” (Danish Road Directorate, 2017), where BL is used as 
an abbreviation for sand and gravel (Vejdirektoratet, 2023). 
 
1.1.5 Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) 
1.1.5.1 Definition 
Life cycle assessment is a methodology that was developed to understand and ad-
dress the potential environmental impacts (as the use of resources and the release of 
pollutants) associated with products and services throughout their life cycle, from raw 
material acquisition through production, use, end-of-life treatment, recycling, and final 
disposal. LCA allows for direct comparisons among products or systems and alternate 
forms of the same product or system (Dettling & Margni, 2009). 
An LCA is comprised of four phases: 
==PAGE:19==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
19 
A. Goal and scope definition:  
a. Defining the functional unit, a quantitative description of the function or ser-
vice for which the assessment is performed, which is the basis of determin-
ing the reference flow of product that scales the data collection in the inven-
tory analysis (Hauschild et al., 2018).  
b. Determining the purposes of the study.  
c. Determining the boundaries of the system life cycle and identifying important 
assumptions that will be made. 
B. Inventory analysis: compiling a record of the important material and energy

1.1.5.2 SimaPro software 
SimaPro is one of the world’s most widely used software programs for LCA. It in-
cludes many life cycle inventory (LCI) datasets, including the ecoinvent v3 data-
base, which covers over 10,000 processes (PRé Sustainability, 2016). There are 
different dataset versions in SimaPro. The allocation database follows the principles 
of attributional modelling for splitting impacts and handling co-products. Allocation 
is the term used to refer to the partitioning of the inputs or outputs of a process or 
product system between the product system and one or more other product systems. 
The type of allocation is mostly economic allocation (based on market values). An 
attributional modelling framework aims to represent a product system in isolation from 
the rest of the technosphere or economy, targeting the environmental impact attributed 
to products (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
Processes within ecoinvent are either unit or system-based. They differ in the 
level of detail visualized. Unit processes refer to all emissions and resource inputs 
specific to one stage. In contrast, a system process gives a complete inventory 
throughout all stages (as in a complete LCA for that process) for one product, in an 
aggregated result, for which you cannot identify individual contributions within the 
process. Choosing a unit or system process should not differentiate the results sig-
nificantly.  
Within SimaPro, a product or scenario analysis can be done, as well as the 
comparison of different scenarios. The software uses libraries to store standard back-
ground data (like ecoinvent) and projects for specific study data (foreground pro-
cesses). It contains various impact assessment methods, including a ”EN15804+A2 
(adapted) V1.02 / EF 3.1 normalization and weighting set”. This allows for obtaining 
a contribution and inventory analysis (list of elementary flows), identifying differ-
ences within each environmental impact category.

1.1.5.4 InfraLCA 
InfraLCA is an LCA tool that was generated to quantify the environmental impacts 
associated with the construction of roads, railways, and buildings in Denmark. Alt-
hough the tool is not third-party validated, actors in Ramboll utilize it, and it was ad-
vised to use it for this assessment, as it is commonly used in the construction sector.  
The tool calculates environmental impacts for the production phase (A1-A3), 
the construction process phase (A4-A5), replacements (B4), end-of-life (C1-C4), and 
the next product system (D), as defined in EN15804:2012+A2:2019.

stated. InfraLCA assumes that GWP (from EN15804+A1) and GWP total (from 
EN15804+A2) are comparable and are therefore summed together (Sweco, 2023). 
The tool calculates the environmental impacts based on the quantities of mate-
rials, the processes involved in infrastructure projects, and the period analysis. It uti-
lizes data from EPDs and the Ökobaudat database (containing EPD datasets for build-
ing materials and processes as well as transport processes). InfraLCA representatives 
indicated that Ökobaudat alone provides only the emission factor for material 
transport. An average of the impacts from 2 or more EPDs is utilized for the emission 
factors of material-related impacts. For the A5 module, parameters for machinery and 
default energy consumption come from a mix of project data, literature, and assump-
tions, in that order of priority. The same construction process is assumed for each 
pavement material layer. No waste generation is considered. In B4, material lifetimes 
are defined based on the average of material performance, which comes from mainte-
nance projects data. The waste generation in the C modules is defined as stated in 
the utilized EPDs.

1.2 Problem Statement  
This thesis aims to evaluate the environmental impacts associated with the incorpora-
tion of different aggregate materials for unbound base course and subbase course 
layers in a new low-traffic load road in Denmark. Three specific gaps motivate this 
study: 
I. 
Resource scarcity – increasing scarcity of natural sand and gravel aggre-
gates. 
II. 
Knowledge gap – the environmental impacts of incorporating foam glass light-
weight aggregate as a subbase layer material are not yet fully understood.  
III. 
Methodological inconsistency – existing LCAs omit impact categories and 
processes, limiting comparability. 
 
1.3 Research Goals and Methods 
This research pursues three main goals: 
I. 
Generate Pavement Design Scenarios 
On the premise that all candidate materials meet the required performance standards, 
develop traffic class 2 pavement design scenarios with varying proportions of natural 
and recycled aggregates in the unbound base and subbase layers. Scenarios include 
complete (100 %) substitution of either layer with recycled aggregates. The recycled 
aggregates that are considered are crushed asphalt, crushed concrete, foam glass 
lightweight fill aggregate, and crushed brick. The pavement designs vary as follows: 
i. 
Natural aggregates are used for the unbound base layer and subbase layer  
ii. 
Recycled aggregates are used in the unbound base layer, and natural ag-
gregates are used in the subbase. 
iii. 
Natural aggregates are used in the unbound base layer, and recycled ag-
gregates are used in the subbase. 
iv. 
Recycled aggregates are used in both the unbound base and subbase lay-
ers. 
II. 
Environmental Impact Analysis 
i. 
Assess and compare the environmental impacts of each scenario to deter-
mine when and why incorporating recycled aggregates offers environmental 
advantages.

1.4 Deliverables  
Impacts will be compared with a contribution analysis, quantifying contributions for 
each process or stage to the total results for an impact category (Hauschild et al., 
2018). 
Key deliverables include the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI), a list of quantified phy-
sical elementary flows for the product system associated with providing the function 
described by the functional unit (Hauschild et al., 2018). Another deliverable is the Life 
Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA), reported as numerical values of the elementary 
flows contribution to each environmental impact category. Consequently, the LCIA 
allows for determining potential environmental impacts, comparing different flows, and 
providing valuable insights for subsequent interpretation.  
Sensitivity analysis is one tool within the interpretation phase for understanding 
the reliability of LCA results by examining how variations in key inputs and assum-
ptions influence the study's conclusions. Life cycle interpretation is the final phase that 
uses the results of the previous phases to generate conclusions and recommenda-
tions. Additionally, an upfront cost assessment per functional unit (FU) will be presen-
ted for each scenario. 
 
2. State of the Art and Research Gaps 
Balaguera et al. (2018) generated a review of LCA studies on the use of alternative 
materials in road construction. Mentioned alternative materials include rubber, steel 
slag, polymers, recycled asphalt pavement (RAP), recycled concrete pavement 
(RCP), recycled asphalt shingles (RAS), and fly ash. Most studies looked into the en-
ergy consumption only, rather than a full overview of different impact categories. Table

formulation of asphalt and concrete mixtures.  
A review by Khan et al. (2024) comments on the findings of studies on the use 
of C&D waste aggregates as a base and subbase layer aggregate. Most of the re-
viewed studies concluded that C&D waste could be utilized as the base/subbase ma-
terial in highway pavements after material testing to satisfy the pavement layer speci-
fications, despite having lower quality than virgin aggregates. The study mentions how 
the current scope of LCA in road pavement evaluates environmental impacts across 
materials, construction, maintenance, and end-of-life (EoL) stages, focusing on energy 
use, emissions, and resource depletion. A suggestion for future studies was to include 
broader sustainability metrics, such as social and economic factors of employing the 
recycled waste material in pavement base/subbase layers. 
Butera et al. (2015) conducted a study based on an LCA for C&D waste utiliza-
tion in road construction in Denmark. They remarked that relative environmental effi-
ciency depends on several factors, such as transport distances, the source of the al-
ternative aggregates being replaced, and the importance given to different impact cat-
egories, including toxicity, given that end-of-life leaching can be significant and non-
extensively addressed in other studies.  
A review by Perera et al. (2021) touches upon the incorporation of glass as fine 
recycled glass (FRG), medium recycled glass (MRG), coarse recycled glass (CRG), 
glass powder, glass fibres, foamed glass and glass geopolymers directly into layers 
as an aggregate replacement and in the formulation of geopolymers, cement, and as-
phalt. The reviewed studies primarily evaluate glass mixtures' mechanical and physi-
cochemical properties with conventional aggregates (recycled concrete aggregate 
(RCA), crushed rock/waste rock, clay/soil, sand, gravel, and limestone). Foam glass 
was described as a material that can offer significant benefits in weight reduction, in-
sulation, freeze-thaw resistance, and potential for use in soft soil. Although some ben-
eficial properties have been studied, foamed waste glass was recommended only for 
use in pavement layers as a supplementary component in stabilized mixes or with 
higher-quality aggregates, owing to its high water absorption in coarse particles and 
poor durability.

Yousefi (2019), as part of his University of Waterloo’s master’s thesis, evalu-
ated FG-LWA as a replacement for Expanded Polystyrene (EPS) in the subgrade layer 
of low-traffic Canadian pavements. His study concluded that FG-LWA conforms to 
Ontario's standard quality and performance criteria for all granular aggregate catego-
ries. However, arsenic and lead exceedances were found using Toxicity Characteristic 
Leaching Procedure (TCLP) tests, categorizing FG-LWA needing specific distance 
constraints from groundwater and wells. Mechanistic (using Kenpave) and LCA (using 
SimaPro and OpenLCA) approaches indicated enhanced pavement performance and 
reduced environmental impacts compared to EPS. The LCA in SimaPro was done 
using the TRACI 2.1 LCIA method, defining the functional unit as one lane‑km of driv-
ing pavement. Two scenarios were assessed: a baseline pavement using EPS as the 
lightweight fill, and an alternative pavement where FG-LWA replaced EPS. Both de-
signs used the same base and subbase thicknesses, varying only the hot‑mix asphalt 
layer thickness, sized according to the AASHTO 93 pavement design method, and 
adjusting the subgrade thickness to accommodate the EPS or FG‑LWA materials. The 
assessment focused only on the material production and construction stages. The use, 
maintenance, and repair life were excluded, assuming the same function, design life, 
serviceability, and maintenance for both scenarios. The EoL stage was also excluded 
due to the materials’ long service life and uncertainties with future disposal practices. 
Quantified environmental impact categories were: ozone depletion, global warming, 
acidification, eutrophication, carcinogenic and non-carcinogenic effects, smog poten-
tial, respiratory effects, ecotoxicity, and fossil fuel depletion. Overall, FG-LWA demon-
strated lower environmental burdens than EPS in nearly all evaluated categories, ex-
cept for ozone depletion in the SimaPro model. Although these findings highlighted 
FG-LWA as a viable and more sustainable option for Canadian roads, its applicability 
in other regions (e.g., Denmark) should be assessed under respective standards and 
conditions. Dansk Celleglas has performed an environmental product declaration 
(EPD) for their “Durapor” foam glass production (Green Gravels OÜ, 2024). However, 
there remains a lack of LCA studies specifically examining the incorporation of FG-
LWA aggregates in the subbase layer of Danish pavements.

3.2 Pavement Design  
As mentioned in section 1.3, all materials are assumed to meet the required perfor-
mance in accordance with the Danish road standards and specifications. 
MMOPP software was utilized to calculate the required layer thicknesses of each of 
the nine pavement design scenarios. MMOPP adjusts pavement thickness so that 
pressure on the top of the unbound layers (unbound base course, subbase, and sub-
grade) stays below its maximum load, and the maximum stretching (tensile strength) 
on the bottom surface of the asphalt layers stays below its fatigue limit. While the 
subbase layer has a minimum thickness due to frost protection, its thickness is also 
adjusted based on traffic load and subgrade stiffness, and it can increase when 
needed. When the underlying support (subgrade rigidity or subbase quality), quantified 
directly by its E-modulus, is low, the unbound base layer thickness is increased to 
distribute loads and protect the layers beneath it. If the unbound base layer is thin or 
less supportive (low E-value), the program compensates by increasing the thickness 
of the overlying bound layers (Danish Road Directorate, 2017). 
A traffic class 2 was chosen after consultations with pavement specialist advi-
sors at Ramboll. This traffic class was selected due to its suitability for using recycled 
aggregates. Figure 5 contains data provided by RGS Nordic, the supplier of recycled 
aggregates for construction, showing the traffic classes where the use of recycled ag-
gregates is permitted according to the Danish Road Directorate. 
 
Figure 5. Applicable traffic classes (trafikklasse), trucks on the road per day (køretøjer), and applica-
ble recycled aggregates for the respective traffic class (RGS Nordic, n.d.). 
 
The choice of traffic class and type of subgrade (splitting frost-susceptible) 
makes a pavement with a minimum thickness of approximately 700 mm (Danish Road 
Directorate, 2013, Table 11).  
==PAGE:36==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
36 
Both wearing course and bound base course materials were selected following 
guidance provided by Ramboll. All scenarios, or pavement designs with different sub-
stitutions, were designed with a wearing course made of asphalt concrete (AB 
160/220). The 160/220 type is the default in MMOPP, referring to the softness of the 
bitumen used in asphalt material, since it is suited for colder climates. A default thick-
ness of 25 mm for the wearing course was chosen based on guidance from advisors 
at Ramboll. All bound base courses were designed with gravel asphalt concrete (GAB 
I) with a default bitumen penetration grade of 70/100.  
For those scenarios with gravel in their subbase layer, a gravel of type “SG II” 
(short for stabil grus, meaning stable gravel in Danish) was selected, as it is more 
commonly used than SG I, which has a higher E-modulus value.  
Scenarios with FG-LWA were designed using the default BL II (U≤3), given that 
both materials have the same e-modulus value. 
Scenarios with crushed brick, referred to as CB throughout this report, were 
designed by modifying the default BL e-modulus value, which adjusts all other layer 
thicknesses. The e-modulus value for crushed brick alone was set as 150 MPa (see 
Table 10; Danish Road Directorate, 2013), like the value for the crushed concrete/brick 
(KBT in Danish) type III. This type is chosen under the assumption that it is the one 
with the largest quantity of crushed brick. Relevant documentation on KBT types does 
not specify the composition of KBT III. KBT II is noted to be composed of ≥ 90 % of a 
mix of concrete, mortar, brick, and many other materials.  
Given that Danish soils have been linked with a risk of heave (as mentioned in 
section 1.1.1) and it rains almost half of the year (Statistics Denmark, 2012), a splitting 
frost-susceptible (”frostfarlig” in Danish) type of subsoil was selected for all designs.

3.4 Goal & Scope Definition of the LCA  
3.4.1 Method Assumptions and Impact Limitations 
The categories considered in this study are those included in the “EN15804+A2 
(adapted) V1.02 / EF 3.1 normalization and weighting set” LCIA method. This study is 
therefore limited to characterisation factors from 2024 (latest version). A slightly limited 
representation might be attributed to assumed values for calculation purposes and 
data variability from using the Ecoinvent databases. Further discussion on the repre-
sentativeness of the data choices for this assessment is covered in section 3.5. 
 
3.4.2 Decision Context and Reason for Carrying Out the LCA Study 
The reason for carrying out this study is to support the decision for environmentally 
preferred future handling of the fates of waste materials in Denmark. The outcome 
should contribute to research efforts on the benefits and drawbacks of utilizing alter-
native materials for pavement construction based solely on an environmental perspec-
tive. 
The decision context of this LCA is micro-level decision support, a study in-
tended to support a decision without causing significant structural changes in the sys-
tems interacting with the studied scenarios. The study might lead to limited changes, 
such as a reduced/increased demand for raw materials, electricity, etc.

is deemed appropriate as it aligns with the study’s objectives and the nature of the 
analysis, which focuses on the environmental performance without explicitly modeling 
broader downstream effects or system-wide changes. ILCD recommends that attribu-
tional LCA should be combined with the use of average processes, or those processes

3.4.5 System Boundaries and Completeness Requirements  
All unit processes necessary to deliver the reference flow defined by the functional unit 
must be included within the system boundaries. Ideally, all flows crossing the system 
boundaries to the ecosphere or “environment” should be elementary flows, or those 
substances or energy entering or leaving the system without human transformation. 
No material, energy, product, or waste flow for treatment should cross the system 
boundaries. All unit processes of an LCI are represented within the technosphere, or 
everything “man-made”. These boundaries are categorized into two main compo-
nents: foreground processes and background processes. The foreground processes 
encompass all direct and explicitly modelled processes specific to the system being 
studied. The background processes represent the broader system context, used in 
various product systems and modelled with generic data (Hauschild et al., 2018). Fore-
ground processes in this assessment are those outlined by the green line, as shown 
in Figure 16. All processes are categorized as foreground processes since they are 
needed to fulfill the functional unit, except for the energy and diesel for the machinery 
in the construction phase. Most processes are common for all scenarios, although 
materials vary for each scenario. For scenarios incorporating FG-LWA in the subbase 
layer, a different compactor is used, and the material is transported via a combination 
of truck and ship. This makes them the only scenarios that include maritime transport, 
whereas all others rely solely on trucks. Crushed brick scenarios also differ by includ-
ing an additional rock-crushing process. 
This assessment is of a Cradle to Laid nature, which includes processes from 
raw material extraction to the end of construction (Salehi et al., 2021), specifically en-
compassing raw material extraction, material production, transportation to the con-
struction site, and the construction processes associated with the placement of pave-
ment layers. The choice of assessment type delimitates the system boundaries of this 
study.

3.5 Representativeness of LCI Data  
The data collected for the foreground system is a mix of data from suppliers’ EPDs, 
data provided by the company Dansk Celleglas for FG-LWA, assumptions from data 
utilized in InfraLCA, and general assumptions. Thus, it represents the details of the 
included scenarios to an extent.  
 
3.5.1 Geographical representativeness  
In the absence of spatial-specific data specific for Denmark, the data includes average 
processes for Europe and global data. Global data (GLO) represents a global average 
from regional datasets or extrapolated data from site-specific data (ecoinvent, n.d.).  
Rest of Europe (RER) averages values for European countries and GLO data for some 
processes, giving a representative average for Europe. Utilized EPDs are from mate-
rial supplier companies based in Denmark. Only the FG-LWA is produced outside 
Denmark and includes data for Estonia, Europe, and global mixes. Vehicle emission 
limits are assumed to be the same as in Europe. Machinery data is assumed from 
suppliers located across Europe. 
 
3.5.2 Time-related representativeness 
Data is linked to the time of data registration as well as the latest updates on the pro-
cesses and models in SimaPro. The EPD data were registered for manufacturing pro-
cesses done between 2019 and 2023. Table A 10 in Appendix A.2 presents the EPDs 
with their reference year. As presented in Table 1, EF 3.1 LCIA methods have a ref-
erence year. It is important to mention that the reference year is the start year for which 
the dataset is valid (European Commission, Joint Research Centre, n.d.-b). The pro-
cedure and materials included in this assessment are for reference data validated by 
Ramboll for pavement construction in 2025. Normalization factors from the EF 3.1 
method use 2010 as their world population reference year.  
 
3.5.3 Technological representativeness 
Suppliers provide material EPDs directly, so they reflect the current manufacturing 
technology. Even though EPDs can be related to data uncertainty, they cover the main 
processes associated with the production. The type of transportation, machinery type,

3.8.3 Construction installation process (A5) 
All scenarios include the process of excavation and compaction; however, for 
those scenarios incorporating FG-LWA (scenarios 4, 5, and 6), a plate compactor is 
used instead of a roller compactor for the subbase layer compaction. The roller com-
paction process was modelled based on a generic model of a soil compactor from 
Volvo Construction Equipment (2024). A simplified productivity method based on Strip-
ple (2001) was applied to estimate the hours required per functional unit. Productivity 
was calculated using the equipment’s average speed, effective coverage width, num-
ber of passes, and an assumed working efficiency. The roller’s effective width was set 
to 85% of its total width, the speed to 4 km/h, and the effective working time to 50 
minutes per hour. Although the reference compactor model is capable of traveling at 
speeds higher than 4 km/h, the actual average speed during compaction is typically 
lower due to operational conditions. Therefore, the 4 km/h value used by Stripple 
(2001) was adopted to reflect realistic working speeds. The number of passes was 
consulted with Ramboll and set as 10.  
According to Foamit’s guidelines for infrastructure design and construction (Uu-
sioaines Oy, 2019), compaction of FG-LWA on soft soil should be done with a vibrating 
plate compactor. A plate of 150-200 kg is mentioned, for which a 199 kg operating 
weight Ammann plate compactor model was used as the reference to calculate the 
capacity. The number of passes is delimited as ”more than two” for which 4 passes 
were assumed for proper compaction.  
A machine operation using diesel and with a high load factor was assumed as 
the process to model the roller compactor. The load factor represents the average 
proportion of rated power used by an engine during regular operation. Processes in

3.9 Basis for Sensitivity and Uncertainty Analyses 
The sensitivity analyses in this assessment are conducted on the assumed processes 
that present large contributions to the total impact. It aims to answer: 
i. 
At what transport distance do roller compactor scenarios equal or outperform 
Scenario 4? 
ii. 
Up to what distance do recycled-aggregate, roller-compactor scenarios remain 
better than the natural-aggregate pavement baseline scenario (1)? 
iii. 
What’s the contribution of distance to the climate change impact per km?

To address the abovementioned questions, two reference pavements were fixed at 
their originally modelled 30 km transport distance: Scenario 1 (roller compactor, natu-
ral aggregates) at 80 kg CO₂-eq m⁻² and Scenario 4 (plate compactor, FG-LWA) at 54 
kg CO₂-eq m⁻². A range of distances (km) from 0 to 100 was tested for all scenarios 
utilizing recycled aggregates and a roller compactor for the subbase layer – scenarios 
2, 3, 7, 8, and 9. Impacts were calculated for the scenarios at 0, 30 (as modelled), 50, 
and 100 km, to create a linear representation.  
 
3.10 Basis for Cost Assessment 
Up-front costs for each pavement scenario were derived by pairing material prices with 
Ramboll’s historical unit rates for transport, equipment, and labour. Multiplying each 
quantity by its corresponding unit price yielded the input’s contribution to the total sce-
nario cost. 
Transport is costed per hour, so transport distances were divided by an as-
sumed average speed of 60 km h⁻¹ to convert kilometres to hours. 
The required operating hours of the compactor machinery were taken directly 
from the respective input needed for either a roller or plate compactor type. 
Excavator hire is also priced per hour. A 46 m³ h⁻¹ productivity rate was used, 
obtained by linear interpolation within the 40–100 m³ h⁻¹ range reported by Ismail 
(2013) for 11–50 t excavators, to represent a 15 t machine. 
Transportation from the brick's building site to the production/waste processing 
site wasn’t accounted for, and a crushed brick price from RGS Nordics was assumed, 
as was the other crushed aggregates.

5. Conclusions, Limitations, and Recommendations  
Conclusions 
In this study, even though foam glass lightweight aggregate (FG-LWA) is the only ma-
terial produced outside of Denmark, all 3 FG-LWA integrating scenarios (4, 5, and 6) 
presented lower impact scores in 9 of the 16 impact categories, primarily because they 
use a plate compactor for the subbase layer instead of a heavy roller. This substitution 
means that, for the remaining scenarios, the transportation of the heavier roller be-
comes the principal contributor to most impact categories. Opting for lighter equipment 
reduces diesel consumption and transport-related emissions. 
Material quality, especially stiffness, and construction design choices (layer thi-
ckness, aggregate type) emerged as drivers of both environmental and cost outcomes. 
Higher stiffness materials in the unbound base enable a thinner bound base gravel 
==PAGE:63==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
63 
asphalt concrete (GAB) layer, reducing GAB quantity and associated impacts; for ins-
tance, scenarios using the highest E-modulus unbound base material in this assess-
ment (crushed concrete) showed measurable reductions in both environmental bur-
dens and costs.  
Beyond machinery weight, the sensitivity analysis further classified the travelled 
distance contribution to the climate change impact. Climate change impacts of roller-
compacted alternatives become equivalent to plate-compacted scenarios (2, 3, 7, 8, 
and 9) when machinery transport distances lie between 12.6 km and 20.6 km, depend-
ing on the scenario. This highlights that the logistics of machinery transport are a key 
driver of climate change performance. 
In scenarios integrating FG-LWA, the FG-LWA production is the governing pro-
cess in most impact categories. Notably, for 7 out of 16 impact categories, the pave-
ment with crushed concrete + crushed brick (scenario 9) had the lowest impact score, 
demonstrating another recycled aggregate mix that could be promising when mecha-
nical requirements are met. However, the scalability capacity of alternative materials 
to virgin aggregates varies. Recycled asphalt (KAS) and concrete (KB) can feasibly 
replace large shares of virgin aggregates nationwide, being the largest share within 
Denmark’s construction & demolition (C&D) waste and having established perfor-
mance data. Brick’s share within the C&D waste stream is also large enough, but brick 
is constrained by mechanical limits on its own. FG-LWA shows promise for specialised 
applications such as low bearing capacity soils or frost barriers. Additionally, using 
lightweight aggregate lowers the quantity needed in the subbase layers, but FG-LWA 
remains supply-limited. 
Lastly, the correlation between unit cost and environmental impact suggests a 
significant positive linear association in the same 9 of the 16 impact categories in which 
the FG-LWA integrating scenarios have a lower environmental impact. However, with 
a sample size of 9 scenarios, these findings should be considered exploratory and 
interpreted with caution rather than definitive proof of a consistent relationship. 
 
Limitations 
This assessment offers a first approximation of potential environmental impacts and 
comparison across scenarios—including FG-LWA integration—based on the chosen 
functional unit, system boundaries, and average process data.  
==PAGE:64==
      
Sustainable Alternatives for Aggregate Substitution in Denmark’s Construction Sector 
64 
Several modelling assumptions may affect accuracy. Fuel use for compactors 
was taken from generic system processes in SimaPro, which could overestimate con-
sumption if real equipment runs more efficiently or under different duty cycles. Further-
more, EPD data reported per 1 tonne were scaled linearly to match specific material 
quantities. In reality, fixed energy for plant startup or minimum batch-size requirements 
do not shrink in exact proportion for small orders, so linear scaling can underestimate 
per-unit impacts at low volumes. The compactor size itself was assumed (with guid-
ance) and not measured on an actual project, influencing both fuel estimates and 
transport logistics. The location of the new road construction is also assumed, and 
distances vary depending on that assumption, to which impacts are related. A 102 km 
distance for the natural aggregates transportation could be overestimating the actual 
transportation distance, given that there are manufacturing locations in the capital re-
gion of Copenhagen.  
Finally, while emissions have been quantified, the future environmental dam-
ages caused by those emissions remain uncertain.  
 
Recommendations 
Transport distances should be minimized, especially for heavy machinery: each addi-
tional kilometer of transportation raises environmental burdens and costs. The scena-
rio-specific “break-even” distances identified in this study can be used as guidance. 
When distances approach those limits, reassessing equipment weight and type can 
yield net savings. Likewise, revisiting compactor sizing assumptions with real equip-
ment data and duty cycles or field trials will refine fuel-use estimates and transport 
frequency. 
Material-specific recommendations include further performance testing of crus-
hed brick (CB) aggregate mixtures and FG-LWA to establish the maximum feasible 
replacement ratio without compromising mechanical performance.  
Before implementing the proposed pavement designs, the key assumptions 
should be validated at scale and adjusted where necessary. To extend the analysis 
beyond the baseline unit length by segmenting a longer route (e.g., 100 km) into sec-

A.3. Life Cycle Impact Assessment Results 
Normalisation results in Figure A 3, are presented in a logarithmic scale for a more appropriate visualization that emphasizes 
larger differences between impact scores while representing data that spans a vast range. However, normalised LCA results 
cannot be interpreted as reflecting the importance of one impact category relative to others. A "large" normalised score means it's 
large relative to the chosen reference system, not necessarily that it is the most critical environmental problem (Hauschild et al., 
2018).
