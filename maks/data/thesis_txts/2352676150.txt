Abstract 
 
 
I 
 
Abstract 
 
During the last decade, a number of fire exposed concrete buildings have collapsed. A look at the 
current codes for structural fire safety design concerning concrete could, therefore, be interesting 
to examine.  
The scope of this thesis is to investigate the concrete structures under compression and high 
temperature.  
The investigation is carried out by conducting a variety of experiments. Both transient tests, where 
the stress applied is constant, and the temperature is varying, and non-transient tests, where the 
temperature is stabilized, and the stress is applied until failure is occurring are performed. The 
strain behavior from the test results are analyzed and compared to a number of theoretical 
models. This includes models from the EN/1992-1-2 and various authors such as Anderberg and 
Thelandersson, Hertz, Lie, and Terro. 
The experiments are performed at the fire lab at DTU with a special circular oven, where pressure 
can be applied simultaneously. Both the heating and cooling phase of transient tests at several 
preload levels are examined as well as the strain due to chemical changes in a transient test at 
300◦C with a preload level of 60%. Additionally, non-transient tests are carried out at a range of 
temperatures.   
    
The results of the investigation regarding transient test show that the strain behavior is very 
dependent on the preload level and the failure of concrete occurs sooner than what is expected in 
the current design codes. Furthermore, the cooling phase has also revealed that the strain 
behavior during the initial cooling exhibits more or less the same behavior, however, as the 
temperature decreases further, especially the concrete with high preload level contracts more 
than measured at the highest temperature. The results of the transient test at 300◦C with a 
preload level of 60% also indicate that the strain development is expanding due to the chemical 
changes in the concrete. 
The non-transient tests prove compliance between some of the reviewed models regarding the 
degradation of the ultimate strength and the test results. The strain at peak stress, the results 
indicate that the strain development is more in correspondence with the theoretical models 
suggested by Hertz and Terro. Furthermore, an and an over-estimation of the strain in the EN, 
which also leads to a higher prediction of the ductility.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation of study 
A common misconception is that fire cannot damage concrete structures. However, as several of 
the more severe cases in recent years, for instance, the fire-induced collapse of the Architectural 
building of the University of Delft 2008, and the partial collapse of the Windsor Tower, Madrid, 
2005,  have showcased, that fire events can have severe consequences. Although the probability 
of a fire event is very low, it is important to consider the damages posed by elevated 
temperatures. The current design approach concerning structural fire safety for concrete is 
described in the EN 1992-1-2.[1] (EN) 
In the EN[1], the degradation data is provided as a function of the temperature. The peak 
compressive strength and strain and the ultimate strain are given in the form of tables based on 
the type of aggregate. Interpolation is needed in order to obtain intermediate values. The tables 
are based non-transient test, where the temperature is constant, and the strain is varying. The 
strain at peak stress does not differ according to the type of aggregate, in contrast to what was 
described by Lie and Lin[2], that the kind of aggregate plays a significant role in the temperature 
development in the concrete, which can have a part in the degradation.  
Additionally, Dušková[3] reported that there is a discrepancy between various theoretical models 
of strain at peak stress compared to the Eurocode. The prediction of higher strain can lead to a 
higher prediction of value regarding ductility, which can be unsafe as the concrete is more prone 
to deformation, before failure, as stated by Li and Purkiss[4].  
The understanding of preloaded concrete structures during a fire exposure is limited, as there is 
only a small number of studies, where Anderberg and Thelandersson conduct the most 
comprehensive during the 1970’s. Additionally, the current Eurocode does not take the level of 
preloading into account, with so-called transient tests, where the temperature is varying, and the 
stress is constant. As noted by Hertz[5], the compressive strength of concrete in the transient test 
can be up to 25% stronger compared to the compressive strength of the non-transient test.  
Furthermore, in the EN[1] it is stated, that possible strength gain during the cooling phase should 
be neglected. Although noted by Hertz[5], the strength of concrete is even more deteriorated in 
the cooling phase. However, it is not clear how the effects of the cooling phase are taken into 
account in the EN.

3. Investigate the cooling effects of preloaded concrete structures.  
As the design procedure regarding fire exposed concrete structures is prescribed in the Eurocode,

4. Due to the previously mentioned discrepancy, to study the behavior of strain at peak stress of

1.2 Object and method 
The aim of this present thesis is, therefore, to investigate and find a reliable model for concrete in 
compression exposed to elevated temperatures of the previously mentioned discrepancy. This is 
done by reviewing and comparing various models of strain at peak stress including ductility. 
An investigation of the non-transient test will be made by conducting new experiments at 
different temperatures and compared to results from the literature.   
A comparison of transient tests at various preloading levels is made by performing new 
experiments and pairing the results with data from the literature. With preloading levels, up to 
60% of the ultimate strength at ambient temperatures based on the accident limit state as 
prescribed in the EN. 
Furthermore, an investigation of strain development during the cooling phase in connection with 
the heating phase of the transient tests will be performed. 
Additionally, transient tests at 300◦C will be carried out, in order to study the effects of the 
chemical reactions when a load is applied to the concrete specimen.  
Regarding both the transient and non-transient tests, a concrete specimen will be heated up in an 
oven where pressure can be applied simultaneously, carried out at the fire lab at DTU. For this 
purpose, the concrete samples used in the experiments are concrete cylinder with a diameter of 
103 mm and a height of 1000 mm.

2 Theoretical aspects 
In order to investigate the topics mentioned in section 1.2, this section introduces the theoretical 
aspects of concrete behavior during exposure to high temperatures and cooling.  
The thermal properties of concrete depend on several factors as described by Purkiss[4], namely 
the type of aggregates, the initial moisture content, the concrete mix and the age of the concrete. 
In the following, some of the thermal, material and mechanical properties will be presented.  
Therefore, a look at the material properties is first conducted, in order to understand the behavior 
of concrete exposed to fire. 
 
2.1 Material behavior of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures 
Concrete is a combination of constituents, cement gel and aggregates, which makes concrete a 
complex inhomogeneous material. As each of the constituents have different reactions to fire, 
therefore, in the following a close look at processes in the microstructures in concrete is 
performed.  
  
2.1.1 Heating 
Hertz[5] and Fletcher[6] describe both the chemical and the physical transformation of concrete 
caused by fire exposure.  When the concrete is exposed to high temperatures, the free water in 
the concrete and chemically bound water from calcium silica hydrates (C-S-H) in the concrete 
starts to boil at 100◦C and evaporates at around 140◦C. If the concrete is unloaded (so-called non-
transient test, introduced in section 2.2.1.2), the expansion is irreversible at 300◦C. Furthermore, 
the aggregates tend to expand as the cement paste shrinks above 150◦C. As previously mentioned 
water in the concrete starts to boil, it builds up pressure in the concrete. When the water 
evaporates, calcium hydrate is formed, which peaks at around 270◦C. At around 300◦C microcracks 
propagate through the concrete. The microcracks result in a reduction of the compressive 
strength, the E-modulus, the thermal conductivity and the tensile strength. Crystals of calcium 
hydroxide (Ca(OH)2) change into calcium oxide(CaO) and water between 400-600◦C, which 
exacerbates the properties of the concrete even more.   
When temperatures in the concrete reach above 600◦C, a further deterioration of calcium silica 
hydrate is progressing. This process is plateauing at 710◦C. At 800◦C the concrete can be crumbled 
to gravel with fingers, feldspar is melting at 1150◦C, and consequently, the residual minerals in the 
cement paste turn into a glass phase. 
 
2.1.2 Cooling  
After being exposed to elevated temperatures, the concrete is cooling. In the first period after the 
heat exposure, water is absorbed from moisture in the ambient air. The calcium oxide (CaO) 
absorbs water and forms calcium hydroxide Ca(OH)2. This process propagates the cracks already

2.1.3 Effects of aggregates 
The type of aggregates in the concrete mix has an influence on the crack formation, as some 
aggregates are more expansive than others. Indicated by Khoury [8], the natural aggregate 
concretes, such as siliceous and to a lesser extend basalt aggregates, are very expansive, resulting 
in larger thermal strains. On the other hand, lightweight aggregates were not as prone to heating, 
as a result of that, a smaller variation of the thermal strain, when tested up to 600◦C. Fletcher[6] 
explicates that siliceous-based aggregates will expand at 575◦C due to mineral transformation, 
whereas calcareous aggregates will decompose at 800◦C.   
 
2.1.4 Heating rate 
When a concrete specimen is subjected to heating, the heating rate has a large influence. If the 
heating rate is low, the temperature inside the concrete will be more homogeneous, in contrast to 
a high heating rate, where the surface of the concrete has a higher temperature compared to the 
core of the concrete specimen. Also, an effect of lower heating rate is a lower risk of explosive 
spalling. Because the internal temperature gradient is lower, the possibility of moisture expanding 
in the concrete is lower[6].  
Furthermore, when the heating rate is low, there is a larger moisture loss [8] and therefore more 
shrinkage of the concrete, which leads to a smaller total thermal expansion during the heating 
phase.

2.1.5 Influence of design parameters 
As Hertz[7] describes, for concretes with a water/cement-ratio down to 0,4, the w/c-ratio has no 
influence on the compressive strength of the concrete subjected to fire. Which is due to the fact 
that excess water in the cement paste will evaporate.  When the ratio is lower than 0,4, the un-
hydrated cement could act as a fire-resistant filler, and as a result thereof, the strength loss is 
reduced. Normally, the curing age of the concrete has no specific influence on the performance of 
concrete when subjected to fire. Apart from the normal strength development during the curing 
phase at ambient temperatures. However, freshly made concrete, a forced hydration will occur up 
to 150◦C when heated, before damages transpire, and as a consequence, the compressive strength 
is increased to some extent.  
 
2.1.6 Shrinkage  
Drying shrinkage due to temperature increase is typically included in the thermal creep and 
depends on the initial moisture content. Another aspect of moisture loss is the sealed versus 
unsealed concrete specimens.  When specimens are unsealed, the free water, the absorbed water, 
and the chemically bound water can evaporate. Whereas, with the sealed concrete specimens, 
according to Khoury[9] a hydrothermal chemical reaction is the dominant process, which could 
result in a stronger or weaker CSH-gel, based on the C/S (CaO/SiO2) ratio, which is dependent on 
cement replacement as fly ash or silica fume.  However, in practice, it can be somewhat difficult to 
make sealed concrete specimens, especially with temperatures above 100◦C, because a high steam 
pressure could build up inside the concrete when heated.  Shrinkage can, however, be avoided in 
another way, namely by drying out the concrete before the test[10].

2.2 Concrete in Fire 
Based on the understanding of material behavior of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures 
presented in the previous section, the comprehension of thermal properties and temperature 
distribution are now addressed. The testing methods are firstly described, in order distinguish 
between the two procedures. 
 
2.2.1 Testing of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures 
Normally there are two main methods of testing concrete exposed to elevated temperatures, 
namely a transient and a non-transient test. In the following, the two test methods will be 
presented. 
 
2.2.1.1 Transient test  
The transient test is a testing method where the concrete is loaded with a constant external stress 
during the heating phase, and a strain behavior is obtained as the temperature increases. Contrary 
to the non-transient test,  with the transient test the concrete can be up to 25 % stronger when 
loaded during the heating, according to Hertz[7] with the largest difference between the transient 
and non-transient tests around 500-700◦C. This may stem from the fact that the crack formation in 
the concrete is hindered during the heating phase. It is obtained when the stress applied is 25-30% 
of the ultimate strength at 20◦C [7].  
 
2.2.1.2 Non-transient test 
Non-transient tests are also called steady state test, where the concrete is heated up to a 
temperature, and then stress is applied. The idea with this test method is to get a stress-strain 
relationship at a stabilized temperature level. Additionally, Hertz[5] points out that it could be 
safer to use values from non-transient tests as the deformations are large when no load is applied 
to the concrete.

2.2.3 Temperature calculations of concrete elements

manners. As Purkiss[11] describes, either a graphical solution or an empirical solution. The first

Fourier heat transfer equations. Due to the fact that normal concrete has a high density and a low 
thermal conductivity, the penetration time for concrete is high. In other words, the time it takes to 
increase the temperature inside the concrete is considerable. Estimation of the temperature 
distribution through a cylindrical concrete cross section is represented by a parabola. The 
temperature profiles can either be calculated using empirical formulas or finite element programs. 
However, in the following, a number of empirical models are introduced. In the following, the 
equations for the one-dimensional case are presented, as the cylindrical cross section experiences

2.2.4 Mechanical properties of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures 
The next step is to understand the mechanical properties, which will be presented in the following 
section. A look at the various strain components will be conducted.   
 
2.2.4.1 Strain components in concrete  
In this section, the components in various strain models are presented, namely the creep strain, 
stress related strain, transient strain and thermal strain.   
 
2.2.4.1.1 Creep strain   
The creep strain is a function of time, temperature and the stresses applied.  According to 
Anderberg and Thelandersson[10] the moisture content, moisture migration and moisture loss are 
very important parts of creep strain. As water in the concrete evaporates at temperatures up to 
150◦C.  
However, according to Lie[16], the creep strain only increases a small amount at temperatures up 
to 320◦C. Additionally, when the concrete is subjected to external stresses, the creep is more 
predominant when temperatures are higher than 400◦C. However, Anderberg and 
Thelandersson[10] mention that normally the creep strain is such a small part of the total strain, 
that it can be ignored, due to the period in which a structural fire normally last is such a short 
amount of time.  Also in the EN the creep strain is neglected for concrete structures when the 
heating rate is between 2 and 50◦C/min, as stated in section 3.2.1 in the DS/EN 1992-1-2[1]. 
   
2.2.4.1.2 Thermal strain  
As previously mentioned in sections 2.1.1 and 2.1.3, the thermal strain is dependent on 
temperature changes in the concrete as the concrete is heated,  which is due to thermal expansion 
in the aggregates. Which is why the thermal strain is vastly dependent on the type of aggregate, as 
stated by Anderberg and Thelandersson[10], the larger amount of quartz, the larger thermal 
expansion. Furthermore, Anderberg and Thelandersson mention that the ratio between the 
amount of aggregates and cement paste also influences the thermal expansion of the concrete.

2.2.4.1.3 Stress related strain 
Stress related strain is the strain associated with the application of an external load. This strain is 
dependent on temperature and stress.  Schneider[18] found out that introducing a load on the 
concrete during the heating phase would have a larger compressive strength and higher E-
modulus compared to the tests, where the concrete was not loaded. Furthermore, the ultimate 
strain was lower for the test specimens which were loaded during the test as opposed to the test 
where the specimens were not loaded. As explained by Anderberg and Thelandersson[10] and 
Purkiss [19], the stress-strain relation is changed due to the increasing temperatures up to 450◦C, 
which could be as a result of a load applied to the concrete. In other words, when a concrete 
specimen is loaded, the expansion is hindered to some extent, however, the size of the load 
applied have little influence on the strain. Further, Anderberg and Thelandersson mention that the 
E-modulus decreases as the temperature increases. According to Purkiss, the stress related strain 
is so-called stress history dependent.

2.2.4.1.4 Transient strain 
The transient strain can be described as a change of chemical composition of the concrete. It is not 
clear whether the transient strain is included in the EN models for the strain. Hertz[5] explains that 
transient strain could be seen as a hindered part of the thermal expansion for loaded concrete 
specimens when exposed to elevated temperatures. Moreover, the transient strain is proportional 
to the ratio between the compressive strength and the compressive strength at 20◦C and also the 
thermal expansion. According to Anderberg and Thelandersson[12], the transient strain is 
developed at increasing temperatures when stress is applied.  Furthermore, the transient strain is 
irreversible and occurs only at the first heating cycle of the concrete. It is not possible to design a 
test, under which the transient strain can be directly measured. Therefore, it can be defined as a 
part of the total strain in stressed concrete under heating, which can otherwise be accounted for.

2.3.2.2 Modulus of elasticity  
As Hertz[5] describes, the modulus of elasticity is reduced when the temperature is elevated. 
Especially when the temperature reaches 500◦C. Furthermore, Hertz[22] indicates that the 
reduction in E-modulus is larger than the reduction of the compressive strength, which points 
towards a large ultimate strain. 
 
2.3.2.3 In-elastic deformation 
In-elastic deformations of concrete happen when stress-strength ratios are large. When concrete 
is heated to a certain level the strength of the concrete decreases and therefore the stress-
strength ratio increases. As a result, the in-elastic part of the stress-strain curve will eventually be 
reached when the temperature is high enough[10].

In the succeeding section, the experiments previously conducted by other researchers will be 
described, in order to establish a basis on which the experiments made by the author can be 
conducted.  
3.3.1.1 Abrams  
Abrams made experiments on 76x152 mm concrete cylinders from 93-871◦C on different kinds of 
aggregates and at different loading and heating sessions. 
The samples were first cured in a fog chamber and after that, in a climate room with 30-40% 
relative humidity at temperatures between 21-24 ◦C until a monitored sample reached 75% R.H in 
the center. Some were kept in an air-controlled room until they were tested and others were 
predried at 100 ◦C. The heating procedure was restrained, by controlling the output of the oven, 
which the temperature in the concrete was uniform through the concrete sample, limited to a 
difference of 83 deg.  
Furthermore, Abrams defined two different tests as: 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
Experimental setup

Unstressed- where the unloaded concrete is heated to a specific temperature and the loaded until 
failure, which is the same as a non-transient test.  
Stressed- which is essentially a transient test. The samples are stressed at 25%, 40% and 55% of 
the ultimate strength.  
Abrams concluded that the strength of the stressed concrete samples at elevated temperatures 
was 5-25% higher compared to the unstressed samples, which, as previously mentioned, also was 
pointed out by Hertz.  
3.3.1.2 Anderberg and Thelandersson 
Anderberg and Thelandersson[12] conducted several tests of both transient test and non-transient 
tests.  Moreover, they have investigated the influence both heating rate and pretreatment of the 
concrete cylinders. The concrete samples were made of aggregates containing quartz. The 
different treatments are standard, where the samples are cured in 65% R.H and 20 ◦C, predried, 
where the sample is heated in an oven up to 105 ◦C for, and the last where samples are water 
saturated using a vacuum pressurization.  The results are illustrated in Figure 14 and Figure 15. 
However, only one sample for each test. Additionally, the cylindrical concrete samples with a 
diameter of 75 mm, had a hole in the center with a diameter of 10 mm, so that the rod could be 
placed, that was connected to a piston to measure the deformation.    
At the transient tests, the concrete was heated up to 800 ◦C or to failure at a constant load. The 
respective stress percentages of the ultimate strength at 20 ◦C are 0; 22,5; 35; 45; 67,5; 90. The 
heating rates are 1◦C /min, 5◦C /min and the maximum capability of the oven, which is 25 ◦C during 
the first 7 minutes and the varying up to 500 ◦C, where the heating rate is 5 ◦C /min. Furthermore, 
non-transient tests have been conducted. The stabilized temperatures range between 20 and 
795◦C. The heating rates are the same as at the transient tests.  The stress rate applied are 0,057 
and 0,23 MPa/s. Finally, a few of the samples were subjected to a constant stress level during 
heating, and when the desired temperature was reached, additional stress was applied at the rate 
specified.  
As presented in above, there is a good agreement between the two test-series conducted by the 
authors, concerning pretreatment of the concrete samples. Although there is a difference in the 
test setup, as Anderberg and Thelandersson had a hole in the center of their concrete sample.

3.4 Test-series 
The main experiment is divided into three-test series presented in the following. 
 
3.4.1.1 Test-series A – transient test 
The test program is to load the specimen to a given stress level and then heated to failure occur or 
to 600◦C with a heating rate of 120◦C/hour.  For this project, a compressive stress of both 40% and 
60% is chosen. The lower is chosen, since that measuring transient strain, 20-30% of the ultimate 
stress is needed.  
The loading cell on the compression is adjusted, so that it reaches a compressive load of the given 
percentage of the ultimate stress, as previously mentioned. The ultimate stress value is in 
accordance with the average compressive force given in Table 8. 
The program is loaded with the 60% of the ultimate strength over 30 seconds. Then the load 
duration is set to 11 hours, in order to make sure that it will last the whole experiment. The end 
load is set to 10 kN, at a rate of 400kN/min, so if the concrete fails, there is just enough load to 
keep it in place. Via the Instron 8500, a load protection of 5 kN is set.  
The first test series is loaded with 111kN, which corresponds to 60% of the ultimate strength.The 
second test series is loaded with 86 kN, which is equivalent to 40% of the ultimate strength. 
The third test series is loaded with 43kN =20% ultimate strength.  Additionally, a test with 0% 
preload level is conducted, this is data from the heating phase during the non-transient test at 600 
deg. 
For T60_0.7,1,3 and all the tests at 40% and 20% preload level, the strain at the cooling phase is

3.4.1.2 Test-series B – non-transient test 
The test program is to heat up the concrete to a specific stabilized temperature and load the 
concrete specimen until failure occurs.  The different chosen temperatures are 600◦C and 400◦C, 
the temperature inside the oven was set to a higher temperature, so that the temperatures could 
be reached, as the controller measures a higher temperature, than the thermocouples.   
The loading program on the Instron is programmed, so that during the heating up to the stabilized 
temperature, a load of 5kN is applied. This is done so that the concrete specimen is held in place, 
but still allowing the specimen to expand. When the desired temperature is reached a loading rate 
is chosen to be 14 MPa/min, so that the effects of creep can be neglected. 
When the concrete has reached the desired temperature, a position limit of 3 mm is set on the 
load cell. This is to prevent that the specimen is crushed completely.  During the heating the 
temperature, load and displacement are logged every minute. When the desired temperature is 
reached, the logging is changed to every millisecond, in order to get a good representation of the 
working curve. The test was stopped after failure in the concrete had occurred. 
The reason for only conducting the test at both 600◦C and 400◦C is that during the tests at 400◦C, 
the failure in the concrete cylinder occurred outside the heated area, although the strain in the 
concrete cylinder is the same. 
3.4.1.3 Test-series C – transient test, 300◦C 
The reason for choosing the 300◦C is that the calcium silica hydrates have started to decompose, 
with the maximum reaction at 280◦C. The compressive load is chosen to be 60% of the ultimate 
compressive strength.  
The test program is analogue to what is described in section 3.4.1.1. However, the temperature 
was set to 370◦C, so that the temperature measured by the thermocouples was 300◦C. The 
selected temperature was held for 120 min so that a more even temperature profile could be 
obtained through the concrete cross section.  The chosen 120 minutes are in accordance with 
Anderberg and Thelandersson[12], as they state, based on experiments, that 120 minutes is 
sufficient in order to obtain an even temperature distribution through the concrete cross section, 
although minor discrepancies in temperature distribution cannot be avoided.  
The load applied for these tests is 121 kN.

5  Conclusion 
 
5.1 Summary 
The basis for this thesis was an aspiration to investigate the behavior of concrete exposed to 
heating and cooling phase. The investigation is made partly by comparisons of previous research h 
results and theoretical models and partly by creating new tests in the fire lab at DTU.   
In the report a number experimental data and theoretical models by other researchers have been 
presented. Especially the result and models by Anderberg and Thelandersson, and by Hertz, Terro, 
and the EN have been relevant in the light of this study.  
In the section of the experimental setup, it was described how the test series were produced, so 
that it is evident how the results have come to light. It has been important not only to describe the 
type of concrete, the tools but also the casting approach on which basis the results can be 
compared to other models and results.  
Additionally, the temperature distribution through a cylindrical cross-section was described. A 
formula for estimating the temperature profile through a circular cross-section was evaluated so 
that the temperature profiles for the test samples could be estimated.  
In the section of the test outcomes, it is shown how concrete behaves both during heating and 
cooling phase with and without the application of external load. By both performing transient 
tests, where the stress applied is constant at several preloading levels and the temperature is 
changing, in order to study the strain effects of the external load and the strain effects of chemical 
changes in the concrete due to heating. As well as non-transient tests, where the temperature is 
constant, and the stress is increasing, in order to study how the stress-strain relationship develops 
due to increasing temperatures. 
In this thesis, it was found that the level of preloading plays a substantial role in the strain 
behavior of concrete exposed to elevated temperatures. It can be said that, as the preloading 
stress increases, a lower temperature is needed before failure occurs. The results from Anderberg 
and Thelandersson were characterized by this tendency.  The test results at 0 and 20% preload, 
were different at higher temperatures compared to Anderberg and Thelandersson.  
During the study of the effects of the formation of calcium hydrate at 300◦C, a transient test at 
60% preload level, it was found that the stresses induced by the chemical changes were higher 
than the stresses applied at 60%, as the concrete expanded. After that it contracted as the 
concrete was degrading further.  
The cooling phase of the transient tests were investigated. It was found that the strain behavior is 
affected by the preloading level. For all the different preloading levels tested, during the initial 
cooling the concrete expanded and after that it level off. When the strain reached a plateau, the 
concrete contracted. This was found to be potentially unsafe, especially at 60% preload, as the 
concrete contracted even further, than the initial contraction during the heating phase.  
==PAGE:70==
 
 
Conclusion 
 
 
63 
 
A number of non-transient tests were conducted, which indicated that the degradation factor 
corresponds to the model presented by Anderberg and Thelandersson, which has very little 
degradation at 400◦C. This could be due to an uneven temperature distribution through the 
concrete cross-section.  At 600◦C, the results correspond both with the models presented in the 
EN, by Hertz, and by Li and Purkiss.  
 
5.2 Highlights  
 Based on the transient tests it was seen that the level of preloading has a significant 
influence on the strain behavior during the heating phase. Although a failure in the 
concrete was not obtained, the results point towards a lower critical temperature with the 
increase of preloading level. Additionally, it was seen that the strain follows the same trend 
as indicated by Anderberg and Thelandersson, although significant variations are seen, 
especially at 0 and 20 % preload level. 
 
 It was seen that the chemical formation of calcium hydrate at around 300◦C at 60 % 
preload level, gives a small expansion of the concrete, and after that a further contraction. 
Which is also up for consideration.   
 
 During the cooling phase, it was proven that the strain behavior follows the same trend of 
an initial expansion and after that a contraction of the concrete. During the cooling phase, 
a larger contraction of the concrete at 60% preload level was seen compared to the strain 
at peak temperature, which could point towards a failure in the concrete.  
 
 Regarding the degradation factor, it was seen that test results correspond to the models 
presented by Hertz, by Anderberg and Thelandersson, by Li and Purkiss and the model 
predicted in the EN. 
 Based on the non-transient tests, it was found that the strain at peak stress at 400◦C and 
600◦C is corresponding more to the models suggested by Hertz and Terro. These results are 
indicating that the EN is overestimating the strain-value, which can lead to an over-
estimation of the ductility-value, this could be considered critical, as a failure in concrete 
structures might occur sooner than expected.  
In the light of the fire-induced collapse of concrete structures, described in the introduction and 
the results obtained in this present thesis, an indication of a possible reassessment of the EN1992-
1-2 would be suggested. Caused by the indications of strain at peak stress is overestimated. 
Moreover, based on the results a more explicit inclusion of preloading is recommended, as the 
preloading influences greatly on the strain behavior. Furthermore, the cooling phase should be 
considered more explicitly; the mechanical properties were deteriorated further in the cooling 
phase as indicated both in this thesis and by Hertz.  
 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
Conclusion 
 
 
64 
 
 
5.3 Limitations of the study and indication of further studies. 
The findings in this thesis and the discrepancies mentioned, points towards a reassessment, 
however further studies and tests would be essential. Therefore, it is suggested to make additional 
temperature tests, so that an even more accurate temperature distribution formula for a circular 
cross-section can be found for different kinds of aggregates. Furthermore, to make more transient 
and non-transient tests in order to corroborate the results presented in this thesis. A closer look at 
the prediction of ductility in relation to the strain at peak stress could be conducted, as it was not 
possible during this thesis.  A more consistent casting process should be considered and 
developed, especially the vibration of the large concrete cylinders. Even though an effort was 
made to ensure that the vibration of the large cylinders was consistent, not all the air was 
removed from the concrete.
