Abstract
This thesis explores how reinforcement learning can be applied to improve the energy
efficiency of district heating systems, focusing on the Vatnsendi water distribution system
in Iceland. The thesis develops a dueling deep Q-network agent capable of dynamically
adjusting pump pressure setpoints to reduce electricity consumption while maintaining
service reliability.
A validated hydraulic model of the Vatnsendi system was built using EPANET and inte-
grated with a custom Python-based simulation interface. A reward function was designed
to promote high pump efficiency, low energy use, and pressure compliance. The rein-
forcement learning agent was trained in a discrete action space tailored to reflect the
operational flexibility of real-world pumps.
The trained agent was evaluated on realistic demand scenarios derived from actual flow
data. On a low-demand summer day, it achieved energy savings of 14.59%, and on a
high-demand winter day, 11.10%, compared to the static control baseline. These im-
provements correspond to estimated annual monetary savings of nearly 680 000 ISK
(34 000 DKK) in pure electricity. The agent demonstrated intelligent behavior by shifting
workload to more efficient pumps during periods of high demand.
Despite a limitation in the reward system, identified late in the project which occasionally
resulted in sub-optimal decisions, the agent still consistently outperformed the existing
control strategy. The system’s modular design enables easy retraining with new objectives
or constraints, highlighting its adaptability to evolving operational needs.
Overall, the results validate that reinforcement learning offers a viable, energy-efficient
alternative to static pump control in district heating systems, and represents a meaningful
step toward more intelligent and sustainable infrastructure.

Introduction
Iceland is a country rich in natural energy resources. With its abundant waterfalls, geother-
mal activity, and strong environmental policies [1] . This renewable energy is harnessed to
support distributed heating systems, industrial processes, and electricity generation, mak-
ing the country’s energy infrastructure both sustainable and resilient. In 2021 the electricity
produced totaled 19.614 GW h and was 99.99% from renewable resources, such as such
as hydropower and geothermal energy, the remaining 0.01% was from fossil fuels.[2]
Thanks to the abundant geothermal resources, approximately 90% of the energy required
for space heating is supplied by geothermal plants, with the remainder provided by elec-
tricity and lastly oil [2]. The distribution of energy sources for space heating over the years
is illustrated in Figure 1.1. These systems distribute hot water from the plants via insu-
lated underground pipes to homes, businesses, and public buildings. To ensure reliable
service and indoor comfort, especially during the cold Icelandic winters, the water must be
maintained at appropriate temperature and pressure throughout the distribution network.

Even though Iceland’s energy is renewable, using it efficiently still matters. Electricity,
even when renewable, has value, especially during periods of high demand or when sup-
plying energy-intensive industries. In district heating systems and other water distribution
systems (WDSs), centrifugal pumps draw electrical power to maintain the pressure head
needed to circulate water throughout the network. If these pumps run harder than neces-
sary, they consume more electricity than needed, leading to unnecessary waste and in
the end higher operational costs.
Veitur is Iceland’s largest utility company, providing district heating, electricity, water sup-
ply, and wastewater services to much of the population. The company emphasizes sus-
tainable resource management, supplying hot water from geothermal sources and manag-
ing electricity distribution in the Reykjavik area and beyond. Veitur also operates extensive
water and wastewater infrastructure, ensuring clean water, environmental protection and
this thesis is done in collaboration with them. [3]
After discussions with the stakeholders at Veitur, it was agreed that investigating the po-
tential benefits of modernizing the pump control strategies at older pump stations would be
valuable for both parties. The system selected for this project is Vatnsendi WDS, located
in the southeast of the Reykjavik Capital Area, see Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Vatnsendi Water Distribution System
The Vatnsendi WDS contains two distinct pressure zones. The purple zone operates at
a higher pressure than the light yellow zone, primarily due to differences in elevation, the
yellow zone lies closer to sea level. The green circles in Figure 1.2 indicate the Rjúp-
nasalir and Vatnsendi pump stations which provide the system with water pressure. The
red circles represent downstream internet of things (IoT) pressure sensors, which are a
relatively recent addition to the system. Veitur’s goal in the future is to leverage these
downstream sensors to better control the pressure. [4]
Currently, the pump stations operate with fixed pressure setpoints (see Table 1.1), mean-

1.1
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to develop an AI-based control strategy, specifically a RL
agent that dynamically adjusts pump station pressure setpoints in response to real-time
demand in the Vatnsendi WDS, aiming to improve energy efficiency while maintaining
reliable service.
To realize this overarching goal, the project is structured around the following key objec-
tives. Each one contributes a critical step toward implementing a robust, intelligent control
solution and achieving the main aim of the thesis.
1. Model the pump stations Vatnsendi and Rjúpnasalir, and validate the hydraulic model
of the Vatnsendi area with these stations included.
2. Design and implement an simulation interface, a type of application programming
interface (API), to enable interaction between the RL agent and the hydraulic simu-
lation software.
3. Develop a reward function that promotes high pump efficiency and low energy con-
sumption while maintaining quality service.
4. Implement a RL agent that learns optimal pump control strategies based on the
defined reward system.
5. Demonstrate that the agent outperforms the current control strategy in terms of en-
ergy efficiency.
1.2
Limitations
Limitation or gentle guidelines set by the stakeholders at Veitur [4].
1. The pressure delivered to consumers must not fall below 3 bar at any time.
2. Pumps cannot be individually switched on or off; they must operate continuously.

Analysis

2.1
State of the Art Review
Traditional rule-based and PID controllers often struggle to keep pump systems running
efficiently under changing conditions in water and heating networks. Aziz et al. [5] ad-
dressed this challenge using metamodeling techniques such as support vector machines
(SVMs) and neural networks (NNs) to approximate hydraulic behavior. Their surrogate
models produced near-optimal pump schedules much faster than full simulations, show-
ing that NNs can enable fast and accurate control in real time. These results motivated
the exploration of RL techniques that leverage NNs.
Gyires-Tóth et al. [6] demonstrated the potential of RL for real-time pump control in water
distribution systems by applying a duelling deep Q-network (DQN) agent that relied solely
on pressure measurements. Their results showed that the agent was able to closely
match the performance of traditional optimization methods while operating significantly
faster, highlighting the practical advantages of NN based RL approaches.
Further supporting this, a study by Róbertsson [7], conducted for the stakeholders Veitur,
applied a duelling DQN to a different pump network supplying low-temperature geothermal
wells in Iceland. The agent reduced power consumption by approximately 6.5% in near
real time while still meeting demand, reinforcing the effectiveness of the duelling DQN
architecture in real-world industrial systems.
Taken together, these findings make a strong case for selecting duelling DQN as the
control strategy in this thesis. It brings together the efficiency benefits demonstrated by
Aziz et al., the adaptive learning capabilities shown by Gyires-Tóth et al, and proven
industrial applicability from Róbertson’s work.

2.2.1
Vatnsendi Hydraulic Model
To simulate and analyze the behavior of the WDS under varying operational conditions,
a suitable hydraulic simulation tool was required. After evaluating several alternatives,
two main candidates were identified: Simscape Fluids (part of MATLAB Simulink) and
Environmental Protection Agency Network (EPANET), see Figure 2.1.
(a) Simscape Fluids a Matlab Simulink
toolkit
(b) An example of an hydraulic model in
EPANET [8]
Figure 2.1: The two candidates for the hydraulic simulation software
Simscape Fluids offers a block-based simulation environment within MATLAB Simulink
for modeling hydraulic components such as pumps, valves, and pipelines. It supports
multi-domain physical modeling and integration with mechanical, electrical, and thermal
systems, enabling comprehensive system-level simulations. [9]
EPANET, developed by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), is an
open-source software specifically designed for modeling water distribution networks. It
provides detailed analysis of flow rates, pressures, tank levels, and pump operations over
extended simulation periods [8]. A major advantage of EPANET in this project is that the
geographic information system (GIS) data provided by Veitur, information on pipe sizes,
lengths, and other network characteristics (does not include the pump stations), is built
using the same engine that EPANET uses [4]. This would enable a seamless import of the
GIS data into the simulation environment. Furthermore, EPANET has a Python wrapper
called EPANET Python Toolkit (EPyT), which allows for easy programmatic access to the
network model [10]. This makes it straightforward to modify simulation parameters and
automate simulations an important feature for integrating the hydraulic model with RL
agents developed in Python.
Given these considerations, EPANET is the chosen tool for hydraulic simulation software
in this project. The pump stations Vatnsendi and Rjúpnasalir will then be modeled with
EPANET and combined with the GIS data from Veitur to create the hydraulic model of the
Vatnsendi WDS.

The stakeholders at Veitur have provided time series data for the pump stations, including
flow rates, pressure, and power consumption for each individual pump. Using this data,

key requirement for the model is that, when simulated with real demand patterns from the
time series, the simulated flow, pressure, and power consumption closely align with the
historical measurements within a reasonable margin of error.

Simulation Interface (API)
To train and evaluate the RL agent effectively, a simulation interface is needed to connect
the agent with the hydraulic model, some kind of API. Based on the earlier decision to use
EPANET with its Python wrapper EPyT, the simulation environment can be programmati-
cally controlled through Python ensuring compatibility with modern RL frameworks.
While DQN-based RL libraries are readily available [11], no existing tool or middleware
was found that supports RL directly over an EPANET based system. Specifically, there
is no public framework that retrieves simulation states (e.g., pressures and flows), sends
actions to the hydraulic model, and steps the simulation forward. To meet the specific
integration needs of this project, a custom solution is required. After reviewing similar
work in the state of the art review, the following functionalities were identified as necessary
for such an interface:
1. Retrieve system states (e.g., pressures, flows, pump efficiency, power consumption)
at each timestep.
2. Calculate the reward of the current system state
3. Provide the state and reward to the RL agent.
4. Receive control actions from the agent (e.g., adjusting pump speeds).
5. Apply actions and optionally update the demand scale, then solve the simulation for
the next timestep.
To implement this, a custom Python class will be developed to wrap the EPANET engine
via EPyT. This class will serve as the core of a modular simulation environment, acting
as the bridge between the hydraulic model and the learning agent, and enabling training
and testing of control strategies.

2.2.3
Reward System
A well designed reward system plays a central role in RL, as it defines the objective that
the agent learns to optimize. It is essentially the only signal the agent receives to evaluate
its actions and outcomes, and must numerically express whether a state and the action
leading to it are favorable under current operational conditions. [12]
In this project, a hydraulic simulation environment acts as an omniscient system, it has
complete knowledge of all physical variables: pressure, elevation, demand, flow, valve
positions, and so on. However, in a real world deployment, the agent will not have access
to all of these states. To develop a practically usable control policy, the agent must learn
from a state representation that reflects what can actually be observed or measured in
the physical system.
Therefore, only a subset of the full state space is used, variables that are both measurable
and relevant to the control objectives. These include:
1. Pressure at junctions
2. Flow at pump stations
3. Power consumption at pump stations
4. Pump speed settings.
These variables represent a balance between observability and control relevance. They
are sufficient to capture the system’s behavior from the perspective of the agent and allow
it to learn meaningful policies without relying on inaccessible simulation only information.
With the state space defined, the reward system is constructed to guide the agent toward
three overarching goals: maintaining adequate service (pressure), minimizing electricity
consumption, and maximizing pump efficiency. These goals are reflected in the reward
function design:
• Pressure compliance: The agent receives a positive reward proportional to the
ratio of junctions where pressure remains above a defined threshold (e.g., 3 bar),
promoting reliable service delivery.
• Pump efficiency: Using manufacturer supplied efficiency curves, the reward sys-
tem encourages operation near the optimal efficiency point of each pump.
• Energy consumption: A penalty is applied based on the total power consumption
of the pump stations, incentivizing energy savings.
This multi-objective reward formulation imposes several technical requirements on the
environment, shaping one of the project’s core work packages: the development of the
reward function which must be differentiable in terms of performance yet stable enough
to guide learning over many episodes, even as the agent explores suboptimal control
strategies early in training.

RL Agent
As discussed in the state of the art review, RL has shown strong potential for controlling
water distribution and heating systems. Its learning-by-interacting is particularly suited to
complex, dynamic environments where feedback is delayed and conditions evolve over
time. This made RL a natural candidate for this thesis early on.
As stated in the introduction the goal was to develop a control agent capable of adjusting
pump pressure setpoints intelligently and autonomously in response to changing system

demands. Several control strategies were considered rule-based logic and machine learn-
ing approaches such as regression and heuristic optimization. While useful in simpler
settings, these methods lacked the flexibility and sequential decision-making capabilities
required for the multi-variable, time-dependent nature of the system. [13]
RL, by contrast, can learn adaptive policies that optimize long-term performance. Cov-

Many existing implementations use small, discrete action spaces with uniform pump ad-

by 10% and decreasing another by 3%, depending on current system conditions. This
flexibility better leverages the pattern recognition capabilities of NNs and enhances both
efficiency and adaptability under varying or unforeseen demand scenarios.
Ultimately, the dueling DQN architecture was chosen for its robustness, efficiency, and
demonstrated success in similar control tasks. Its structure supports faster convergence,
better generalization, and improved performance in real-time, making it well-suited for
optimizing pump operations in distributed heating systems.
The agent must be capable of:
• Interpreting the current state of the hydraulic network, represented as a structured
vector of pressures, flows, and current pump speeds.
• Mapping this state to an ”optimal” action from a discrete action space (operational
range of the pumps).
• Learning over time which actions tend to lead to higher cumulative rewards, partic-
ularly under varying demand profiles.
To support this, a dedicated work package was defined for the agent implementation and
training pipeline, which includes:
• Configuring an appropriate NN architecture in the DQN for this task.
• Implement a training pipeline
This work package serves as the core of the project, bringing together all other compo-

the physical system, the API to interface with it, the reward function to guide learning, and
the training pipeline to iteratively improve its performance. Each of these elements is
essential to enabling the agent to learn a robust and efficient control policy.

3.1
EPANET
In the analysis it was concluded that EPANET would be chosen to model the Vatnsendi
WDS. The EPANET simulation engine supports two types of hydraulic analysis: demand-
driven and pressure-driven. This project uses the demand-driven approach, which is also
EPANETs default. It assumes that all node demands are fully met regardless of system
pressure, which may result in non-physical conditions such as negative pressures.
EPANET models WDS as networks of interconnected nodes and links. Links can repre-
sent pipes, pumps, or valves, while nodes can represent junctions, tanks, or reservoirs.
A schematic example of these components in a network is shown in Figure 3.3
Figure 3.3: Example network with components [8]
Junctions are points in the network where links meet and water is either introduced or
withdrawn. The primary input parameters for junctions in this model include:
• Elevation
• Water demand
Simulation outputs from junctions include:
• Pressure
Reservoirs are used to represent an infinite external water source or sink. These may cor-
respond to natural water bodies like rivers or lakes, or in this case, a geothermal reservoir.
The main input parameter for reservoirs is the hydraulic head, which defines the elevation
of the water surface. As boundary conditions, their head values remain constant and are
not influenced by changes in the rest of the network.
Pipes are used to convey water between nodes. EPANET assumes that all pipes are
pressurized at all time, a reasonable assumption for most closed-loop systems. Flow
direction is determined by the difference in hydraulic head between nodes. The key input
parameters for pipes in this study are:
• Start and end nodes (typically junctions)
• Diameter
• Length
• Roughness coefficient (used to calculate head losses)
The corresponding simulation outputs for pipes are:
Central Heating Optimization in Iceland using AI
15
==PAGE:28==
• Flow rate
• Velocity
• Head loss
Pumps are mechanical devices used to increase the pressure and flow of water in the
network, enabling it to overcome elevation differences and friction losses. The main input
parameters for pumps include:
• Start and end junctions
• Pump curve or performance curve (relating flow rate to head gain)
• Speed setting
Simulation outputs for pumps include:
• Flow rate through the pump
• Pressure added by the pump
• Power consumption
Providing accurate inputs for junctions, pipes, pumps, and reservoirs was essential to
ensure the reliability and accuracy of the model and simulation results. Steps were taken
to include as much detail as possible from the available data.

Simulation Interface (API)
A core component in RL systems is the environment interface, which defines how an agent
interacts with the system it seeks to control. Typically, such interfaces expose standard
methods, most commonly reset() to initialize a new episode and step(action) to apply
actions and receive feedback from the environment. These methods are accompanied
by clearly defined state and action spaces, enabling consistent agent-environment inter-
action. To ensure modularity, robustness, and compatibility with existing RL tooling, the
interface should adhere to established frameworks such as OpenAI Gym [17]. This de-
sign abstraction facilitates development and debugging, promotes code reusability across
experiments, and enables rapid testing of different control strategies or system configura-
tions.
4.1
Design of the Interface
The interface is designed to have one Python class which handles most of the required
functionality and one extension of that class which handles demand patterns so the agent
can see demand that varies.
4.1.1

The environment was designed to be modular and object-oriented. It is implemented as
a Python class called wds() that can be easily instantiated with different configurations,
allowing flexibility in simulation parameters. Key elements that can be customized include:
• Hydraulic model file (.inp).
• which pumps are controlled together
• Number of simulation timestep.
• Scale rewards weights

A core functionality of the environment is its ability to simulate the effect of control actions
of the agent on the hydraulic system. At each timestep, the agent provides an action
consisting of pump speed adjustments. The environment applies the speeds, solves the
updated network hydraulics using EPANETs Python wrapper EPyT, then returns the sys-

from the base WDS class, WdsWithDemand maintains consistency while introducing realistic

Step Function
The step(action_idx) function is the most crucial component of the environment class,
as it’s where the agent’s chosen action is translated into changes in the simulation and
where the resulting reward is computed. This function maps the given action index to
pump speeds using the action_map, then applies these speeds to pumps in their respec-
tive groups. It does this in the following sequence:
1. Decode the discrete action index to pump speeds per group.
2. Apply pump speeds to the respective pumps.
3. Scale junction demands if training or using demand pattern
4. Solve the hydraulic network using the EPANET solver via EPyT.
5. Compute the reward based on efficiency ratios, power usage, and pressure con-
straints.
6. Return the concatenated state vector, reward and additional information.
Reset Function
The reset() function initializes a new episode by resetting:
• The timestep counter.
• Pump speeds to nominal values (1.0).
• Junction demands to their base demands without scaling.
• Hydraulic solver state by running an initial solve.
State Representation
The agent observes a continuous state vector composed of:
• Current pump speeds for all pumps.
• Pressures at all network junctions.
• Flow rates through each pump.
This state is constructed and returned by the get_state() method as a concatenated
NumPy array suitable for input into NNs.
Reward Calculation
compute_reward() returns a value for the the current state which is based the pressure
delivered, total power used and pump efficiency. See Chapter 5 for more details.

variability, helping the agent learn a more robust and generalizable control policy
suitable for dynamic real-world conditions.
This dynamic demand handling in WdsWithDemand allows for comprehensive evaluation
and training of RL agents, enabling them to generalize effectively across a spectrum of
operational conditions, from predictable daily cycles to unpredictable demand surges or
drops.

Reward System
The reward system plays a key role in shaping the behavior of the agent. It acts as the
primary source of feedback, telling the agent whether its actions are moving it closer to or
further from the desired goal. Without a meaningful reward signal, the agent has no basis
for learning or improving its performance over time.
As Sutton and Barto put it, “the reward signal is your way of communicating to the agent
what you want it to achieve” [18]. The design of this signal has a direct influence on
the strategies the agent learns. A well-designed reward helps guide the agent toward
solutions that align with the system’s objectives, while poorly designed rewards can result
in unintended behavior or failure to learn useful policies.
In this project, the reward function was carefully constructed to reflect the key operational
goals of WDSs: maintaining sufficient pressure, minimizing energy consumption, and en-
couraging efficient pump operation. The following sections explain how each component
of the reward signal was formulated to support these objectives and guide the agent to-
ward balanced, reliable control strategies.

components: pump efficiency, customer satisfaction (in terms of pressure compliance),
and total energy consumption. This section details how each component is computed

6.2
Dueling DQN Architecture
As the agent selected in this work is of the dueling DQN architecture, it is helpful to first
understand the foundational concepts leading up to it.
In traditional tabular RL, an agent learns an action-value function Q(s, a) also known as
a Q-table, which stores the expected cumulative reward for taking action a in state s and
following the learned policy thereafter. Through repeated interaction with the environment,
the agent updates the Q-values in the table using the Bellman equation, defined as:
Q(s, a) = E
[

This recursive relationship expresses the expected return of an action in a given state
as the immediate reward plus the discounted value of the best future action. Over time,
the Q-table converges to encode the optimal policy by indicating which action yields the
highest expected return in each state. [18]
While this method works well for small and discrete state-action spaces, it quickly be-
comes infeasible in high-dimensional or continuous environments due to the explosion of
possible state-action pairs. To address this, the DQN was introduced [20], which replaces
the Q-table (along with the Bellman equation) with a neural network that approximates the
Q-function. Instead of storing values explicitly, the DQN generalizes from experience by
learning to predict Q-values for any given state-action pair based on a compact, learned
representation of the state.
Although DQN significantly improves scalability, it still faces limitations in environments
where many actions have similar effects. In such cases, learning accurate Q-values for
each action can be slow and unstable, since the network must estimate many nearly
identical outputs. This is where the dueling DQN architecture offers a key advantage.
The dueling DQN modifies the network structure by decomposing the Q-function into two
separate estimators: the state-value function V (s), which evaluates how good it is to be

the relative importance of each action in that state. These components are combined to

has zero mean across actions, enabling the network to better differentiate between the
value of the state and the choice of action.
To summarise, a standard Q-table builds a policy by explicitly storing estimated returns for
all state-action combinations. A DQN replaces this table with a neural network, allowing
the agent to generalize across large state spaces. Dueling DQN further improves upon
this by separating the estimation of state value and action advantage, which leads to
faster learning and better performance in environments where many actions yield similar
outcomes.

6.3.1
Agent Architecture
The agent is implemented in PyTorch using two core components: a neural network class
DQN, and a control class Agent; see Listing A.2 in Appendix A for the full code. The Agent
class is responsible for the full decision-making and learning process. It maintains both the
policy network (used for selecting actions) and a target network (used to stabilize training
by providing fixed targets for short periods). A replay buffer stores past experiences in the
form of transitions, which are sampled randomly to break correlation between successive
updates.
The agent uses an ϵ-greedy strategy for action selection, which balances exploration and
exploitation. With probability ϵ, the agent selects a random action to explore the envi-
ronment and discover potentially better strategies, while with probability 1 −ϵ, it exploits
its current knowledge by choosing the action with the highest predicted Q-value. This
approach prevents the agent from prematurely converging to suboptimal policies by en-
suring ongoing exploration.
To train the policy network, the agent minimizes the difference between predicted Q-
values and target Q-values using stochastic gradient descent. The Adam optimizer is
employed for this purpose, which adaptively adjusts learning rates for each network pa-
rameter based on estimates of the first and second moments of the gradients. This adap-
tive approach typically results in faster convergence and more stable training compared to

47
==PAGE:60==
traditional gradient descent methods. This modular structure ensures separation of con-
cerns: the neural network focuses on function approximation, while the agent manages
learning dynamics, exploration strategies, and interaction with the environment. It is worth
noting that this section provides a high-level summary of the dueling DQN architecture.
For a more thorough and formal treatment, the reader is referred to the original work by
Wang et al.[21]. [18] [23]

Demand Scaling and Training Setup
To encourage the agent to learn a robust policy that generalizes across varying system
loads, each training episode uses a randomly scaled demand profile. At the beginning
of every episode, the environment samples a new scaling factor from a uniform distribu-
tion. This scaling factor is applied to the system, effectively simulating different real-world
operating conditions such as varying weather, occupancy, or consumption.
The range of this uniform distribution was chosen based on analysis of historical flow data
from the pump stations. As shown in Figures 6.3, 6.4 and 6.5, ∼85% of the flow range
from 0.6x to 1.4x, with 1.0x representing the average flow rate. Since flow directly corre-
sponds to demand, this range was used to define the potential variation. However, during
preliminary testing, the simulation became unstable for demand scales below 0.7. There-
fore, the final sampling range was restricted to [0.7, 1.4] to ensure feasible and physically
valid episodes throughout training.

Training Process
The training environment is implemented using the WdsWithDemand environment. Each
episode represents a single time step in the hydraulic system. At the start of every episode,
a new demand scale is sampled from a uniform distribution in the range [0.7, 1.4], simu-
lating a wide range of realistic operating conditions. The use of a uniform distribution en-
Central Heating Optimization in Iceland using AI
49
==PAGE:62==
courages the agent to generalize well and avoids learning biased behavior. Pump speeds
from the previous episode are preserved, introducing temporal continuity and allowing the
agent to experience how previous control actions influence new conditions.
The agent–environment interaction and learning process follow this looped sequence:
1. The agent observes the current system state as a state vector.
2. It selects a discrete action from a predefined set of pump speed configurations using
an ϵ-greedy strategy.
3. The environment executes the selected configuration and returns the resulting new
system state and scalar reward.
4. The transition (s, a, r, s′) is stored in the replay buffer for future learning.
5. A minibatch of transitions is sampled from the replay buffer and used to update the
policy network using gradient descent.
6. The Adam optimizer is used to minimize the Q-value loss, and the exploration rate
ϵ is gradually decayed over time to shift from exploration to exploitation.
7. Periodically, the target network is updated by copying the weights from the policy
network (e.g., every 200 steps).
8. A new demand scale is sampled, and the process repeats.

7.3
Discussion on Agent Behavior and Modularity
As previously discussed, the reward function was designed to ensure that the legally re-
quired minimum pressure of 3 bar is delivered to all consumers. The agent was trained
with this constraint in mind, and thus, the results presented represent the best-case en-
ergy savings achievable under this condition. Nevertheless, the savings are significant
and demonstrate the potential of this control method.
A major advantage of this RL-based approach is its modularity and flexibility. The control
strategy can easily be reconfigured by modifying the minimum pressure constraint and
retraining the agent. This means that the trade-off between energy consumption and
pressure delivery (i.e., customer satisfaction) can be finely tuned to meet the needs of
different operational policies or regulatory frameworks.
To provide insight into the agent’s decision-making process, Figure 7.11 illustrates the
estimated Q-values for different actions during the high-demand (winter) day. At each
time step, the agent receives the current state of the system, and the NN estimates the

associated with the highest Q-value.

7.4
Future Work
There are several directions for future work aimed at improving the robustness and prac-
ticality of the control system:
• Refinement of the Reward Function: The current reward structure may not ad-
equately distinguish between optimal and suboptimal states, which could lead to
instability. A more nuanced (non linear) reward system could help the agent better
differentiate between small variations in energy efficiency and service level.
• Increased Control Resolution: The agent currently operates on hourly data, which
limits its responsiveness. In contrast, the existing control methods (e.g., PID con-
trollers) react in real time. Increasing the temporal resolution of the agent’s control
(e.g., sub-hourly decisions) would be required.
• Edge Case Robustness: The current setup has not been extensively tested under
edge-case scenarios, such as sudden drops in demand or sensor failures. Ensuring
the agent behaves reliably under such conditions is essential for real-world deploy-
ment.
• Training Domain: Investigate and resolve issues to enable the hydraulic model to
incorporate the full range of demand, rather than being limited to only 85%.

This thesis set out to explore how RL can be used to optimize pump operations in a district

service quality, guiding the agent’s learning process effectively.
• A dueling DQN agent was implemented and trained using the PyTorch framework,
learning control strategies from interaction with the environment.
• The trained agent was tested under realistic seasonal demand scenarios and was
shown to consistently reduce energy usage while maintaining the required pressure
levels.
The evaluation demonstrated that the agent achieved energy savings of up to 14.59 %
on low-demand days and 11.10 % on high-demand days compared to the existing static
control strategy.

the developed system. The agent can be retrained with updated constraints, new objec-

In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates that RL is not only a viable but also a powerful
tool for optimizing control in distributed heating systems. Despite the limitations in the
