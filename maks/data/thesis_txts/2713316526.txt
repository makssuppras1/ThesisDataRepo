Abstract
Squeezed states of light present a noise level below the standard quantum limit, and
for this reason are essential tools in quantum sensing and quantum information protocols.
However, squeezing generation is a delicate task in which losses highly influence the final
achievable level of squeezing. In this work we report on-chip generation of squeezed vacuum
states, where a straight waveguide is used as a guided optical parametric oscillator, repre-
senting a valid alternative to bulk optical cavities. For instance, periodically poled lithium
niobate (PPLN) crystals with Titanium indiffused waveguides have shown as low losses as
0.02dB/cm and the high non-linearity of the crystal can be exploited to obtain significant
squeezing. The chip is pumped with a 775 nm wavelength pump laser, generating single-
mode squeezed vacuum states at 1550 nm through the parametric down-conversion process.
Telecom-wavelength squeezed vacuum states are then measured in a homodyne detection
scheme. This proves the feasibility of the generation and detection of squeezed states. All
the setup is fiber-coupled, which enables easy implementation of the device in the standard
telecommunication network for quantum applications.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Quantum Internet is the future of communications: it will open new applications, from
remote quantum computation to ultra-secure data transmission [41].
The vision behind
quantum internet is an infrastructure made of many quantum nodes - quantum computers
and quantum devices - connected by quantum communication channels. This conceptually
new way of connecting and sharing information is unconditionally cryptographically secure:
that is, the laws of Nature ensure the security of the links [41]. The way toward the re-
alization of this infrastructure is now clearer than ever, thanks to advances in science and
technology. For instance, it has been proven feasible to connect two users, on earth [44]
and on space [22], using quantum information protocols such as quantum key distribution.
In addition, a fundamental breakthrough has recently been published by Xanadu - a Cana-
dian startup - being the first to claim quantum computational advantage with an integrated
photonic platform: Borealis [24]. This new hardware can solve computational tasks that
would require millions of years for a classical computer to solve, in just a few seconds. This
opens many possibilities in various fields such as computational chemistry, finance, logistics
and energy storage. Current applications of quantum information includes cryptography,
metrology, machine learning, biopharma and many more [24] [40] [41] [17].
Integrated photonics is a promising platform to develop scalable quantum computer hard-
ware because of its intrinsic stability, low power consumption, weight, and size features
(millimetres scale). In addition, on-chip manipulated photons can maintain the correct en-
coding of the information even when guided through long-distance links, have shown high
phase stability and have a high quality of gates processing and read-out techniques [2][7].
Gaussian Boson Sampling (GBS) is a promising candidate for the realization of a large-scale,
fault-tolerant quantum computer - yet to be realized - [31], as it can solve the challenges of
scaling to larger numbers of photons [16] [20]. This computational task consists of sampling
the photon number statistic of a Gaussian state [8]. Squeezed states of light have been used

2.2. Squeezed states
2.2
Squeezed states
Squeezed states are particular states of light, also described as a state of minimum un-
certainty, where the uncertainty in one of the quadratures is reduced beyond that minimum.
In order to satisfy Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle, it comes at the expense of increasing
the uncertainty in the other quadrature, which is not being squeezed. Squeezed states can
be described by the squeezing operator:
ˆS(ξ) = e(ξ∗ˆa2−ξˆa†2)/2 .
(2.33)
The terms ˆa and ˆa† are scaling quadratically, and this highlights the second-order nonlin-
earities involved in the physical process of squeezed states generation. Physically, one of the
ways in which these states can be generated is the spontaneous parametric down-conversion
process, which will be later explained in 3.3. This happens, for example, in an optical para-
metric oscillator composed of a bulky crystal placed inside a cavity. Here, the crystal has
some χ(2) nonlinearity and is shone with a strong pump laser. If the laws of conservation
of energy and momentum are satisfied, two photons are generated inside the crystal: the
signal and the idler. In the definition of the operator, ξ, the squeezing parameter, depends
on the pump field strength, the material properties of the crystal, the interaction time of the
photons inside the crystal and the overlap of the spatial modes of the fields. By changing
these parameters, it is possible to tailor the squeezing.
Squeezed vacuum
The first example of squeezed states is the case of squeezed vacuum. As discussed, the
vacuum is a minimum uncertainty state, meaning that the noise in both the amplitude and
phase quadrature is the same. In a phase diagram like the one in Figure 2.2, it is possible
to visualize such state. Here ∆ˆX1 and ∆ˆX2 represent the square root of the variance.

the description is the one of a coherent state: |α⟩= ˆD(α) |0⟩. It is possible to represent a

Guided optical parametric oscillator
The platform experimentally used to generate squeezing is a straight waveguide res-

3.4
Photorefractive effect
When indiffusing the Titanium atoms in the crystal, defects are introduced in the various
steps of the fabrication process.
These defects consist of ions free to move around the
crystal, the most common being Fe2+ and Fe3+, and they are the cause of small absorption
of the propagating light. When generated by the incoming pump field, these charges get
trapped at the edges of the waveguide [19]; as a result, the ions’ movement leads to a
modification of the refractive index, which also changes the spatial profile of the modes
propagating in the waveguide. We call this the photorefractive effect. As the pump power is
increased, photorefraction changes the quasi phase-matching condition [25]. This is because
it induces a refractive index variation in the waveguide and this can result in a detuning from
perfect resonance, degrading the squeezing [11]. In general, the photorefractive effect can
be compensated for by increasing the operating temperature, as this increases the charges’
mobility [33]. However, working at too high temperatures (∼190 °C) can be impractical, as
we experienced first-hand the damage of some fiber components after a few months’ use. An

The physical platform used to generate squeezed states is a guided optical parametric

This consists of a straight waveguide resonator in a Z-cut periodically poled

numerically compute the interaction strength and the threshold power of the resonator.
4.1
Lithium Niobate crystal
Lithium niobate (LiNbO3) is a transparent birefringent crystal consisting of two distin-
guishable axes: depending on its polarization, when propagating inside the crystal, the light
field will feel two different refractive indices. These are called ordinary (electric component
of the light field is perpendicular to the z-axis of the crystal) and extraordinary (electric
component of the light field is parallel to the z-axis of the crystal) axis.
The strongest
non-linear interaction between light and matter is achieved when the electric component of
all interaction light fields (signal. idler and pump) are parallel to the z-axis of the crystal:
the extraordinary axis. This enables the exploitation of the d33 non-linear coefficient of the
crystal. Knowing this, it is possible to design the chip in the most efficient way. An impor-
tant parameter to consider is the losses, as they can limit the generation of squeezing. In
particular, due to the fabrication process, the air-chip interface is an irregular, rough surface

5.1
Laser
The laser used in this experiment is a laser at 1550 nm, with thermal control over the
wavelength of 1 nm. The pump at 1550 nm is amplified up to 5 W before being sent through
a lithium niobate crystal. Here, second harmonic generation enables the production of a
second beam at 775 nm wavelength. As discussed, the phase-matching condition has to be
satisfied for this process to happen. In particular, it is possible to fine-tune the temperature
of the laser so as to optimize the second harmonic generation for a specific wavelength of the
incoming pump. This is done by maximizing the power (measured with a power meter) at
the output of the 775 nm wavelength light beam. This is true when the wavelength of the
laser at 1550 nm is fixed. However, it is sometimes necessary to scan the wavelength of the
laser to observe the reflection dip and the transmission peak. When this mode of operation
is used, the optimal condition for the phase matching is not met anymore. This means that
there will be less production of photons at 775 nm, hence a higher power at 1550 nm. As
a result, the data acquired will have a more or less pronounced linear increase or decrease
in power depending on the direction of the detuning corresponding to the central frequency
of the laser. The change in power will not alter the results but its presence needs to be
considered, for example, when performing data analysis.

scan would not lead to a linear behaviour in the refractive index change. Therefore, we chose

5.3
Cavity on resonance
For many of the measurements that will be carried out in this experiment, it is required
to put the cavity on resonance. Once the fibers are aligned to the chip, we scan the laser
in order to see the transmission peak and fine-tune the alignment to have the maximum
intensity. When this is done, we stop the ramp of the laser and we observe just a straight
line with an offset. This is because all of the factors contributing to the peak observation
are off: the wavelength of the laser is fixed and both the physical and the optical length of
the cavity are fixed as well. At this point, we perform a fine-tuning of the laser frequency in
order to maximize the offset of the straight line: the cavity is now in resonance.

temperature. For these reasons, a Peltier is also used. The main idea is for the Peltier to
transfer heat from the outer oven to what we refer to as the inner oven. This is a smaller
cage made of copper, inside of which we place the chip. We also fix a temperature sensor

Squeezing
The goal of this Master’s thesis is to measure squeezed vacuum produced by a sub-
threshold guided optical parametric oscillator. In this chapter, I will present the squeezing
measurement and the results obtained. First, I will introduce the Homodyne detector, then I
will describe the characterization of the parametric gain and finally present the measurement
of squeezing.
6.1
Homodyne detection
With a homodyne detection measurement, we are able to access the quadratures of
a signal beam by interfering it with an auxiliary field. The signal beam is mixed with a
strong, coherent field - the local oscillator (L.O.) - in a 50/50 beam splitter, as displayed in
Figure 6.1. The phase of the L.O. in respect to that of the signal determines the measured
quadrature of the signal beam. If the local oscillator interferes with vacuum, we measure
the vacuum fluctuations: this is the shot-noise calibration. Practically this is performed by
not injecting any field in one of the two input ports of the beam splitter. At the output of
the beam splitter, the signals are collected using two identical, independent photodiodes.

a time-scan. First, the dark noise has been measured. After that, the power of the local
oscillator has been scanned from a minimum power of 0.6 mW to a maximum of 2.5 mW

Chapter 7
Conclusions
This Master thesis aimed to produce squeezed vacuum from an integrated photonic platform.
The chip used is made of lithium niobate crystal where straight-waveguides are formed by
the indiffusion of Titanium atoms. To carry out the experiment, it has been necessary to
develop a theoretical background both in quantum optics and integrated photonics. After
that, the system’s geometry and mode field propagation has been simulated. This allowed for
a better understanding of the platform and linked the theoretical study to the experimental
realization. The setup has been built from scratch and it has been fully characterized. First,
the intracavity losses characterization has been performed. This enabled the selection of the
best-performing waveguides and the estimation of the escape efficiency. Subsequently, the
coupling efficiency has been measured. This allowed making a prediction on the detectable
squeezing. The crystal has then been heated. This procedure has been particularly tedious
as the alignment has been lost many times, forcing us to cool down the system and heat
it again. Once the desired temperature was reached, the final characterization consisted in
performing a temperature optimization of the second harmonic generation phase matching
and measuring the parametric gain. Finally, the squeezing measurement has been performed.
The highest recorded squeezing is -2.8 dB. The coupling from chip to fiber is one of the main
challenges and gives rise to the highest losses in the detection scheme. Accounting for these
losses, we can comfortably claim that the squeezer can produce -4.7 dB of squeezing.
This work can be further developed by implementing locking schemes in the system. For
instance, it is possible to seed the parametric down-conversion process with a weak light
field at 1550 nm. By doing so, the phase of the seed can be locked in respect to the pump
in amplification or deamplification, effectively selecting the squeezed quadrature. Similar
locking schemes can also be implemented in the detection, where by locking the phase of the
seed to that of the local oscillator it is possible to select which quadrature to measure.
