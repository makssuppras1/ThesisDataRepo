Abstract 
Cysbio ApS have specialized in optimizing the sulfation pathway in E. coli for their cell factory that 
produce large titers of the valuable sulfated compound, zosteric acid (ZA). The sulfotransferase 
rSULT1A1 used for carrying out the biological sulfation reaction show broad substrate specificity, 
which give opportunities for producing a wide range of sulfated biochemicals other than ZA, using 
same optimized sulfation reactions. The first new sulfated biochemical to be investigated is 4-
vinylphenol sulfate (4-VPS), and it is the topic of this study. I have worked on developing a cell factory 
for overproducing 4-VPS from L-tyrosine that would be based on a single-plasmid expression design. 
Furthermore, recombineering-based strategies for genome manipulation was investigated to try out 
design choices that included heterologous expression from the genome. Later, I investigated different 
cell factory designs for the production 4-VPS in 1-L fed-batch bioreactors. Results reveal that 
production of 4-VPS is highly problematized by the intermediate 4-vinylphenol (4-VP). Accumulation 
of this intermediate at concentrations 2 result in bioreactor foaming and a decline in respiratory 
activity at concentrations 2 mM. In another finding, excessive overexpression the sulfate membrane 
transporter CysPBsu was found to trigger lethal physiological responses in the expression host. In shake-
flask settings, CysPBsu expression increases the production of ZA, but in fed-batch fermentations, 
product titers are significantly higher when CysPBsu is not expressed. Lastly, a library of rSULT1A1 
mutant was investigated but did not reveal improved sulfation phenotypes in fed-batch fermentation 
settings.

1 Introduction 
The objective of this study is to develop a microbial cell factory that produces 4-vinylphenol sulfate 
(4-VPS). Much inspiration will be taken from the CysBio cell factory that produces zosteric acid (ZA), 
as these are structurally related compounds. 4-VPS can be formed instead of ZA by introducing one 
enzymatic reaction to the existing design, catalyzed by phenolic acid decarboxylase (PAD). This study 
showcases a typical cell factory engineering campaign: from designing and assembling genetic parts 
over enzyme characterization and engineering, screening, analytics, to fed-batch fermentations. The 
process also contains elements of iteration of biological engineering as shown in Figure 1.  
 
Figure 1: Design-build-test-learn cycle.  
Figures made with BioRender. 
Specifically, I have worked on developing a new single-plasmid expression platform that would be 
entirely based on the BglBrick assembly standard. This work included troubleshooting leaky expression 
from the inducible expression system and toxic effects of expressing sulfate membrane transporter 
CysPBsu. Following this work, an existing library of mutants of the sulfotransferase enzyme (rSULT1A1) 
was tested for altered activity and substrate specificity. As an alternative approach, we investigated 
genome integration designs which was obtained by recombineering. Final cell factory designs 
obtained were evaluated in 1-L fed-batch fermentations.  
In the following introduction chapter, the background of this cell factory design is presented. First, the 
market potential of the product, 4-VPS, and other related sulfated compounds are presented (Section 
1.1) Second, the metabolic pathway for producing 4-VPS is presented, including the sulfation reaction 
pathway (1.2). Third, the sulfotransferase rSULT1A1 is described and a set of mutations that will be 
screened in this study (1.3). Finally, the cell factory expression host and expression system used is 
explained (1.4) and how this will be built through synthetic biology tools (1.5) and assessed through 
fed-batch fermentations (0).

1.1 Cysbio sulfation technology 
The Cysbio sulfation technology enables the biological production of sulfated biochemicals. The 
technology is based on engineering E. coli cell factories optimized to produce high titers of sulfated 
products through fermentation. Chemical synthesis of sulfated compounds requires harsh chemistry 
and generates waste biproducts. In one chemical synthesis, chlorosulfonic acid would be used to drive 
the sulfation (O-sulfonation) reaction, and would require multiple extractions [1]. In a biological 
production process, these conditions can be avoided by harnessing the power of enzymatic catalysis. 
Furthermore, cheap fermentable carbohydrates can be used as starting material. Currently, efforts 
have been directed towards producing zosteric acid (ZA), which can be formed by sulfating the 
precursor p-coumaric acid (pHCA) [2].  
As part of engineering the ZA cell factory, a general sulfation pathway has been developed that can 
be extended to many sulfation precursors other than pHCA. This has created the opportunity to 
develop a general platform, defined as the Cysbio sulfation technology. This technology can be used 
to perform full biosynthesis of sulfated product starting from a cheap carbohydrate source. It may also 
be used as a biotransformation to produce sulfated conjugates from precursors. In this study, we show 
that the technology can be extended to produce 4-vinylphenol sulfate (4-VPS). Here, we hypothesize 
that 4-VPS can be produced instead of ZA by including a single intermediate reaction step catalyzed 
by phenolic acid decarboxylase (PAD). 
Sulfated biochemicals have distinct chemical properties that can be used for different applications in 
society. The attachment of a sulfate group to a given molecule adds a divalent negatively charged 
group which drastically increases its water solubility and gives it new chemical properties. In nature, 
sulfated compounds are relatively rare, especially compared to phosphorylated compounds. Kinases 
are widely found in all living organisms and they play a critical role in many cellular pathways. On the 
other hand, the equivalent enzymes in sulfation reactions, the sulfotransferases, are scarce and are 
almost exclusively found in higher eukaryotes, particularly mammals [3]. Thus, many of the sulfated 
products that can be made by the Cysbio sulfation technology are in fact xenobiotics, and this also 
means that less inspiration can be taken from nature to include in our cell factory design.

Zosteric acid (Figure 2) 
Zosteric acid [p-(sulfooxy) cinnamic acid], figure x, is naturally found in 
eelgrass, Zostera marina, where it was first isolated [4]. This secondary 
metabolite is believed to function as a chemical defense for preventing 
settlement of marine biofilm. Therefore, it has received much 
attention due to its applications as an antifouling agent. Biofouling is 
especially a phenomenon in marine environments: In the shipping industry, biofouling adds serious 
drag resulting in increased fuel consumptions [5]. In marine aquaculture, biofouling result in 
consequences for the health of farmed organisms and farming equipment [6]. Marine biofouling 
organisms includes bacteria, fungi and diatoms that colonizes early on, followed by settlement of 
larger eukaryotic organisms algal spores and invertebrate larvae [7]. Antifouling coatings was 
developed in 1860’s and have been used since to prevent fouling of these organisms [8]. These 
coatings function by releasing biocides from the surfaces to inactivate biofouling organisms. The 
biocides have a history of being toxic for the environment, and therefore biocides used today are 
under close scrutiny [9]. Therefore, there is an urgent demand for developing effective and sustainable 
antifouling agents. ZA is one of the most studied natural antifouling compounds. It stands out as 
environmentally friendly, as it does not bioaccumulate in fatty fish tissue, does not have a lethal dose 
on non-target organisms and has a fairly quick half-life in seawater of 12 days [9]. Furthermore, it has 
shown to be a broad-spectrum antifouling agent effective against bacterial and fungal biofoulers and 
macrofoulers including algae, ascidians, barnacles, bryozoans, tubeworms and mussels [9]. 
Zosteric acid also have applications in agriculture as a crop protectant against fungi. The intended 
mechanism of action is that ZA provides a very hydrophilic surface on plants that prevent adhesion of 
fungal spores. ZA has shown to prevent adhesion of M. grisea on rice plants, C. lindemuthanum on 
bean plants and C. fragariae on strawberry plants [10] [11].

group attached. The de-sulfated form of 4-VPS, 4-vinylphenol (4-VP), is used to produce

1.2 Sulfation metabolism 
In this section, the metabolic pathway design for producing 4-VPS is presented. This design requires 
rewiring two key metabolic pathways that are native to E. coli. One of those is the L-tyrosine 
biosynthesis pathway. L-tyrosine will be the key intermediate to generate the sulfate acceptor 
substrate 4-vinylphenol (4-VP). The other pathway is the sulfate assimilation reduction pathway. From 
this pathway, the sulfate donor substrate PAPS [3’-phosphoadenosine 5’-phosphosulfate] will be 
generated. These pathways will then be converged through the expression of a sulfotransferase that 
will catalyze the transfer of sulfate from PAPS to 4-VP, forming 4-vinylphenol sulfate (4-VPS). 
  
1.2.1 Sulfation metabolism in E. coli 
The sulfate donor PAPS is generated through the sulfate assimilation reduction pathway. This pathway 
is native to E. coli and other prokaryotes, fungi, and photosynthetic organisms, and serves to assimilate 
inorganic sulfate into organic molecules, such as cysteine methionine [15]. 
Inorganic sulfate is not easily assimilated due to its low redox potential. Therefore it must be activated 
by ATP hydrolysis through release of pyrophosphate and formation of a SO42--AMP bond [16]. As 
shown in Figure 4, this reaction is catalyzed by ATP sulfurylase (EC 2.7.7.4) and forms adenosine 5’-
phosphosulfate (APS). In E. coli, this enzyme constitutes of two subunits encoded by cysD and cysN. 
APS is then phosphorylated by APS kinase (EC 2.7.1.25), encoded by cysC in E. coli, to form PAPS. ATP 
sulfurylase and APS kinase are separate enzymes in most plants and microbes, including E. coli. 
However, animals and some bacteria have a single bifunctional protein, the PAPS synthetase, that 
catalyzes both reactions [17]. PAPS is reduced to sulfite, SO32-, then further reduced to sulfide, S22-, 
which is then incorporated into cysteine, completing the pathway [15].

Once the sulfate group has been transferred from PAPS, PAP [3’-phosphoadenosine 5’-phosphate] is 
formed. PAP is then hydrolyzed to AMP by a nucleotidase (EC 3.1.3.7) encoded by cysQ in E. coli. AMP 
then undergoes phosphorylation twice to form ATP, which then can be used for assimilating a new 
sulfate anion. As such, Regeneration of PAPS can be considered as a cyclic pathway shown in Figure 4.  
 
Figure 4: The sulfation pathway. 
In our cell factory design, modifications will be made to this pathway to facilitate the production of 4-
VPS. First, a sulfotransferase reaction will be included that catalyzes the transfer of the sulfuryl group 
from PAPS to a sulfate acceptor substrate. In this work, the sulfotransferase from Rattus norvegicus 
(rSULT1A1) has been selected. This enzyme is active towards both p-coumaric acid (pHCA) and also 4-
vinylphenol (4-VP) [2]. The basis of this sulfotransferase is further elaborated in 1.3.1. Second, 4-VP 
will be synthesized from L-tyrosine by introducing two reactions, catalyzed by a tyrosine ammonia 
lyase (TAL), and phenolic acid decarboxylase (PAD). TAL catalyzes the deamination of L-tyrosine, 
forming p-coumaric acid (pHCA). PAD catalyzes the decarboxylation of pHCA to form 4-vinylphenol (4-
VP). For our cell factory, we will express the highly active and specific TAL enzyme from Flavobacterium 
johnsoniae-derived enzyme (TALFjo) [18]. For PAD, the enzyme from gram-positive Bacillus subtilis 
(PADBsu) will be used. Ultimately, these modifications provide a completed metabolic pathway for 
biosynthesis of 4-VPS.

1.2.2  Sulfate membrane transporters 
Sulfate uptake is an important topic of this study, and therefore an overview of sulfate uptake 
pathways in bacteria is presented here. Sulfate should not become a limiting source intracellularly in 
the final cell factory, and this is governed by the flux of sulfate going into the cells. Sulfate uptake can 
be optimized by increasing the number of sulfate transporters on the plasma membrane or optimizing 
them to better take up sulfate, but it requires intricate engineering. Perturbance of the membrane 
protein composition influences membrane fluidity and maintenance of the membrane potential 
maintenance. Therefore, increasing sulfate uptake can quickly become a trade-off of cell fitness.  
Sulfate may enter bacterial cells through four known types of sulfate transporters: SulT, SulP, CysP 
and CysZ. These are listed in Table 1. This transportation is an energetically unfavorable process. Under 
physiological conditions in bacteria, the periplasm is maintained at a lower pH than the cytosol. 
Therefore, transportation of divalent sulfate anions requires either primary active transport or 
secondary active transport [19].

1.3 Sulfotransferases and enzyme optimization through 
rational protein engineering 
In this section, the sulfotransferase rSULT1A1 is reviewed. This enzyme is the driving force for the 
sulfation activity. In this study, I have investigated a small library of previously designed rSULT1A1 
sulfotransferase mutants, and these mutations will here be characterized. 
1.3.1 SULT sulfotransferases 
Sulfotransferases comprise a large family of enzymes that are primarily native to higher eukaryotes, 
especially mammals. Sulfotransferases are divided into two families; the cytosolic sulfotransferases, 
nomenclated as SULT enzymes [23], and the Golgi-residing sulfotransferases. All these enzymes have 
in common that they use PAPS as sulfate donor substrate. 
In mammals, SULT enzymes play two roles of regulating endogenous hormones, neurotransmitters 
and bile acids and detoxifying exogenous xenobiotics. Often it is the same enzymes that are active on 
endogenous and exogenous compounds [3]. Sulfation converts hydrophobic compounds into water-
soluble sulfate conjugates that are more readily excreted [3]. Furthermore, in many cases, sulfation 
disables the bioactive effect of a given compound [24]. Human SULTs have been under tremendous 
research because mutants are associated with the risk of cancer [25].  While SULTs are active towards 
relatively small molecular weight molecules, the golgi-residing sulfotransferases are responsible for

modifying proteins and glycosaminoglycans, such as heparan, with sulfate groups. These enzymes are 
membrane bound proteins and can therefore easily be distinguished from SULTs, which are globular. 
As such, these families share very little amino acid sequence similarity with one another, however 
they do share structural similarity in the PAPS binding pocket [3].  
The sulfotransferase chosen for this cell factory is the rSULT1a1 from Rattus norvegicus, which shows 
excellent sulfation activity towards pHCA and 4-VP [2]. It belongs to SULT1A subfamily, which 
comprises a list of SULTs that have an extremely broad substrate specificity [26], [27]. These enzymes 
can act on various phenols, alcohols and N-hydroxy arylamines [3]. The broad specificity is probably 
related to giving ability of detoxifying a wide list of foreign molecules instead of specific ones. For our 
purpose, this broad substrate specificity is a double-edged sword. It allows us to use the same enzyme 
on various substrates. However, specificity is very much appreciated in a final cell factory because we 
do not want to contaminate the product with a mixture of various sulfated compounds. 
 
1.3.2 Rational protein engineering of rSULT1a1 
An X-ray crystal structure of rSULT1a1 has not yet been published. As an alternative, a homology 
model had been made with SWISS-MODEL (https://swissmodel.expasy.org). The amino acid sequence 
of rSULT1a1 was used as target and the PDB file 1LS6 of hSUL1TA1 had been used as the template 
protein [28]. This template was selected because (1) it also belongs to the SULT1A subfamily, (2) it 
shares high amino acid sequence similarity with rSULT1a1 (89.9%), and (3) the specific crystal structure 
have been obtained with high resolution (1.90 Å). The homology model is shown in figure X. 
The rSULT1A1 enzyme features two binding pockets, one for PAPS, and one for sulfate acceptor 
substrates. Once bound, the sulfate group on PAPS is oriented in line for transfer to the substrate [29]. 
This reaction is catalyzed by residues Lys-102 and His-104 in the active site [3].  Targeting these critical 
residues for mutagenesis will destroy the catalytic activity. The residues responsible for shaping the 
PAPS binding pocket are well-established, and are visualized in [28]. PAPS will still be used as the 
sulfate donor in our cell factory design, so these residues will not be ideal targets for mutagenesis. 
The sulfate acceptor binding pocket is large and hydrophobic and can accommodate for many 
different hydrophobic compounds [30]. As visualized in Figure 5, this pocket accommodates for two 
p-nitrophenol molecules simultaneously. Furthermore, the substrate binding pocket dynamics is 
governed by a loop region of residues 236-250. To alter substrate specificity, targeting residues that 
shape the binding pocket is a great strategy.

rSULT1a1, position 219 is usually a leucine. hSULT1A1 is a rare case where this position contains 
isoleucine. One study identified this leucine and 5 other mutations to give hSULT1A1 increased 
thermostability temperature [30]. Because the position is located distantly from substrate binding 
sites, it is not expected to alter substrate specificity.

1.4 Cell factory host, expression system and genetic 
parts  
In this section, we describe the cell factory host that we will build our design into, the inducible 
expression system we are using and lastly the plasmid that will be used for expression.  
The current cell factory design used for producing ZA uses three plasmids for expression. These 
plasmids are built from pETDuet-1, pRSFDuet-1 and pCDFDuet-1 vector backbones from Novagen [34]. 
In this study, cell factories with this plasmid genotype setup are referred to as triple-plasmid strains.

1.4.1 BL21(DE3) and the T7-expression system 
The E. coli BL21(DE3) strain will be used as the host for this cell factory (genotype: see Table 6) BL21 
strains are excellent for expressing heterologous proteins because they are Lon and OmpT protease 
deficient. Without these proteases, degradation of proteins is significantly reduced [35]. Furthermore, 
the BL21 strain is recA positive. RecA is a recombinase that plays a pivotal role in DNA repair [36]. RecA 
expression is advantageous in the final cell factory design because it reduces spontaneous 
mutagenesis and increases strain stability. However, RecA expression is a nuisance during strain 
development because it is counteractive for genome editing and may significantly reduce efficiency, 
(see 1.5.3).  
One of the genes we have investigated for knock-out is fhuA. This gene encodes an outer membrane 
protein. It functions as a receptor for the siderophore complex ferrichrome-iron, and, together with 
TonB, FhuA mediates transport of chelated iron [37]. However, it also serves as a receptor for various 
bacteriophages (T1, T5, UC-1, and φ80) and therefore it promotes bacteriophage infection. We believe 
that this membrane protein can be removed to make the strain more resistant towards phage 
contamination. 
The (DE3) suffix indicates that the strain is a derivative lysogen of bacteriophage DE3, and it contains 
the genes required to drive T7-expression. The bacteriophage DNA fragment encodes for the lacI gene, 
the lacUV5 promoter, the beginning of the lacZ gene and the T7 RNA polymerase gene [38].  
The inducible T7 expression system will be used in this cell factory design. Heterologous expression 
from the T7 system is advantageous because it can be decoupled from native expression system. The 
mechanism of transcription is shown in Figure 6. Without induction, transcription from T7 promoters 
is repressed by the repressor LacI. Repression can then be relieved by induction of allolactose or its 
non-hydrolyzable analog IPTG [Isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside]. Upon induction, T7 RNA 
polymerase is expressed which will then transcribe heterologous genes from T7 promoters. The T7 
RNA polymerase is extremely promoter-specific and has very low transcription error rate [38]. It 
should be mentioned that there will always be some expression of the T7 RNA polymerase in 
BL21(DE3) strains [39]. Therefore, the T7-expression system is naturally leaky, and a basal level of 
heterologous proteins will always be expressed.

1.5 Synthetic biology tools and methods 
In this section, the molecular cloning methods and strategies used for building our cell factory design 
is described. The BglBricks assembly standard was followed for constructing the single-plasmid 
expression system for producing 4-VPS. Thus, most cloning would be based on restriction enzyme 
and ligation (RE/L). RE/L cloning is advantageous because it is a PCR-free strategy with a lower error 
rate. However, unique restriction sites are not always available for all design choices, and RE/L 
cloning also forms a scar sequence that can be unwanted. In such cases, Gibson assembly was used. 
 
1.5.1 BglBricks assembly standard 
The BglBricks assembly standard was followed for constructing the single-plasmid expression system 
for producing 4-VPS. Following an assembly standard brings standardization to both the design and 
assembly method of the DNA sequence. Thus, the design and cloning can be significantly simplified, 
and constructs can easily be shared in repositories. 
The original BioBrick assembly standard was introduced by Thomas Knight [41]. The aim is to bring 
standardization into synthetic biology by defining DNA sequences as parts. A part is a non-reducible 
element of DNA sequence that serves a specific function. These parts include promoters, ribosome 
binding sites (RBS), coding sequences (CDS), terminators and so on. Furthermore, Biobrick parts are 
flanked with unique restriction sites that are used for standardizing the RE/L cloning process by using 
the same sets of restriction enzymes. Functional parts can then collectively stored within databases 
and can be assembled to design complex systems [42]. 
In the BglBricks assembly standard, introduced by Jay D. Keasling and coworkers, the flanking regions 
consist of restriction sites for Ecori+BglII upstream and BamHI+XhoI downstream [42]. To successfully 
use the assembly standard, Bglbrick parts must not contain any of these restriction sites, because such 
sites would be digested during assembly. BglII and BamHII-digested ends are complementary and 
creates a scar that differs from the original restriction sites upon ligation. When the scar is formed, 
the new BglII and BamHI restriction sites are kept unique. When these rules are conformed, the 
assembly procedure explained in Figure 7 can be followed.

1.5.3 Genome integration by recombineering 
In this study, I also investigated integrating genes and gene cassettes, which functions had been 
verified, into the genome. This would be done by swapping out the native fhuA-gene with my genes 
of interest through homologous recombination. Currently, two prevalent methods exist for 
performing genome editing in E. coli: CRISPR-Cas9-mediated genome editing and λ-Red phage-
mediated recombination engineering (recombineering). Due to patent right issues for using Cas9-
mediated genome editing, we have opted to go for the recombineering strategy.  
Recombineering describes the use of bacteriophage recombination proteins to integrate a DNA 
fragment into the genome through homologous recombination. Recombineering uses a double 
stranded or a single stranded DNA fragment for genetic insertion. The DNA fragment is designed with 
flanking regions that are homologous to the target site. After introducing the DNA fragment to the 
cell, the bacteriophage recombination proteins recombine the DNA fragment at the designated target 
site through homologous recombination [36]. 
The bacteriophage λ Red system is probably the best characterized used for recombineering and 
allows for high-efficiency recombination. The system is comprised of the three Exo, Bet and Gam [36]. 
The Gam protein inhibits the native RecBCD complex. This prevents degradation of the inserted DNA 
fragment. Gam is not strictly necessary for recombination, but can increase frequency by up to 20-
fold [44]. The Exo protein is an exonuclease that degrades linear double stranded DNA from its 5’-end, 
exposing a single-stranded 3’-overhang. The Bet protein, a recombinase with similar role as E. coli-
native RecA, then binds to the single-stranded 3’- overhang and promotes homologous recombination 
with a complementary DNA strand. If ss-DNA fragments are used for integration, only the Bet is 
required for successful recombineering. As complementary DNA strands on the genome are revealed 
at replication forks during replication, the highest efficiency is obtained during rapid cell replication, 
i.e., at exponential growth. Furthermore, efficiency has shown to be up to 30-fold higher for ssDNA 
that complements the lagging strand during replication since the mechanism proposed is integration 
in the lagging strand during replication [45].  
For this purpose of replacing genes by recombineering, a two-step selection/counterselection 
procedure was followed, explained in Figure 9. First, a plasmid containing the λ Red protein genes is

1.6 Fed-batch fermentation 
Final cell factory designs were used for fermentations in 1-L DASGIP Fed-batch bioreactors. The 
objective of these fermentations was to compare their performance to the current strains and find 
out if they perform as expected in shake flask growth experiments, but at higher cell densities and 
volumes. Fermentations were carried out in 1-L DASGIP bioreactors equipped with instruments that 
measure and adjust for temperature, pH and dissolved oxygen concentration. This allow cultures to 
be more closely monitored during fermentation and to evaluate the metabolic profile at defined 
physiological conditions. In this section, we describe the fundamental setup of the fermentation, 
including a description of the medium formulation, fermentation process and feed profile. 
1.6.1 Mode of operation 
We operate the fermentations as a fed-batch setup. Initially, fed-batch operations are operated 
similarly as batch fermentations because they do not have any feed flow. At some point, feed(s) 
containing nutrients and/or inducer are introduced. Fed-batches are typically run with no outlet feed, 
where fermentation broth is left in the reactor while new medium is being fed, resulting in an 
increasing volume. The advantage of this setup is that high cell density can be obtained at a desired 
growth rate. Most importantly, the concentration of carbon source can be maintained at a desired 
low concentration. E. coli tend to produce high amounts of acetate when the amount of carbon source 
exceeds the oxidative capacity of the cells, resulting in overflow metabolism [47]. Acetate production 
is a symptom of non-complete oxidation of carbon substrate and can inhibit protein expression. Fed-
batch operations can thus be maintained to keep the carbon source concentrations below the acetate 
production threshold. In this study, a quite unusual feed profile is used, shown in Figure 10. Here, the 
glucose feed increases linearly, but then drops to a plateau, equal to 75% of the maximum feed. 
Previous fermentations without this plateau have shown that the oxidative capacity would be 
exceeded, and cells would begin to produce acetate [personal talk with Joakim]. Figure 10 also 
highlights the induction feed profile (IPTG). In the current setup, induction is fed at a fixed time point 
(15 hours after inoculation). Once induction starts, cells begin overexpressing L-tyrosine and the 
enzymes leading to the production of sulfated product.

1.6.2 Medium formulation 
The fermentation medium must be defined correctly to satisfy growth and product formation during 
fermentation. Each component and its relevance for the medium are presented in Table 5.  
Other additions are not shown in Table 5. One of those is antibiotics, which serve to put selective 
pressure on marker genes. Without the addition of antibiotics, cells can survive without the plasmids 
that have been introduced to them. Usually, cells will tend to grow better without plasmids, which 
would result in those cells outgrowing the producing strains. If working with an inducible expression 
system, obviously, the inducer must be supplied at some point during fermentation. This study relies 
on IPTG to induce the T7-expression. Antifoam is another addition that serves to reduce foaming. 
Foaming result in different challenges for the fermentation setup that ultimately reduces cell fitness 
in many ways if left uncontrolled. Foaming is often caused by protein denaturing on the medium 
surface. Antifoams can then be added to reduce surface tension.  
A final component in the aerobic bioreactor is oxygen. Oxygen is essential for obligate aerobic 
organisms, and seriously increases the growth rate for the facultative anaerobic E. coli. As previously 
hinted, dissolving oxygen into the medium is a cumbersome task, and it is often a rate-limiting factor 
in industrial fermentations. Bioreactors are optimized with spargers, impellers, and baffles to 
maximize oxygen dissolving.

2.1.2 Molecular biology methods 
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) 
PCR was used to amplify target DNA sequences on the PCR Thermal Cycler XT96 from VWR. 
Transformants were analyzed by colony PCR by taking a small scrape of the colonies and loading it 
with OneTaq® DNA Polymerase from New England Biolabs according to protocol [51]. PCR was also 
used to amplify DNA fragments with specified homology for Gibson assembly. Here, the Phusion® 
High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase from New England Biolabs was used. Annealing temperatures and 
extension times used was modified depending on primer sequence and the length of amplicon. 
Gel electrophoresis 
Linear DNA fragments was separated by gel electrophoresis. Agarose gel consisting of 1x Tris-acetate-
EDTA (TAE) buffer with MIDORI Green Advance DNA/RNA stain and 1% or 1.5% agarose, depending 
on the length of the DNA product, was casted. Hardened gels were submerged in TAE buffer on an 
Advance Mupid-One Submarine Electrophoresis System from Takara Lab, loaded with DNA products 
and run for 30 minutes at 70 V.  
Purification of DNA 
Plasmid was purified from overnight liquid cultures using the NucleoSpin® Plasmid DNA purification 
kit and protocol from Macherey Nagel for plasmid purification from E. coli [52]. DNA products was 
purified from agarose gel slices using the NucleoSpin® Gel and PCR clean up kit from Macherey Nagel 
[53]. 
Sequencing 
DNA products were verified by Sanger sequencing by Eurofins Genomics using the Mix2Seq kit [54]. 
DNA digestion and ligation 
Plasmid DNA was digested according to the Fast Digestion of DNA protocol from Thermo Fisher 
Scientific [55]. Linearized DNA fragments were ligated using the Sticky-end Ligation protocol from 
Thermo Fisher Scientific [56]. 3:1 molar ratio of insert to vector was used for the reactions. 
Transformation into heat-competent E. coli NEB5a cells 
Plasmids, Gibson assembly products and ligation products were transformed into competent E. coli 
NEB5α from New England Biolabs following protocol C2987H [57]. 
Preparation and transformation of electrocompetent E. coli BL21(DE3) cells 
E. coli BL21(DE3) strains (mainly SUL361) were made electrocompetent for transformations. Colonies 
were inoculated in shake flasks containing 50 mL LB medium and incubated at 37°C and shaking at 250 
RPM until reaching an optical density (600 nm) of ~0.60. Exponentially growing cells were then chilled 
on ice bath and washed twice in iced MilliQ water to remove electrolytes. This was done by 
centrifugating culture broths at 4°C, discarding supernatant and resuspending in MilliQ water. Final 
pellet was resuspended in 1 mL iced MilliQ water with 10% glycerol for cryo-stocking. 50 µL of 
electrocompetent cells was mixed with up to 2 µL plasmid DNA in 0.2 cm ice-cold electroporation 
cuvettes and electroporated using the MicroPulser Electroporator from Bio-Rad. Cell mixture was then 
added to 950 µL SOC medium and recovered by incubation for 1 hour at 37°C, shaking at 250 RPM. 
Cells were then covered on LB agar plates containing appropriate antibiotic marker(s).

2.1.3 Growth experiments 
Deep well growth experiments 
Growth experiments were carried out to evaluate activity mainly sulfotransferase activity. These 
experiments were done by inoculating a colony from a desired strain in LB-Lennox medium 
(Appendix), and then incubated in an Eppendorf incubator for 24 hours O/N at 37°C, shaking at 250 
RPM. Inoculate was then divided in triplicates and transferred to 1 mL M9 minimal medium (Appendix) 
in 96x deep-well plates at a ratio 1:100 and left to incubate again for 24 hours O/N at 37°C. Other 
chemicals would be added to the M9 medium depending on the experiment, for example IPTG and 
0.5 mM of L-tyr or pHCA or 4-VP.  
Shake flask and microtiter growth experiments 
Cell cultures were grown in microtiter plates to follow growth Colonies were inoculated in 5 mL 2xYT 
media for 24 hours at 37°C, shaking at 250 RPM. Cell cultures were then centrifuged, resuspended in 
MilliQ water, and then inoculated in shake flasks with M9 minimal medium with 5 mM K2SO4. Cell 
cultures from shake flasks were then transferred to a microtiter plate to follow growth rates. This was 
done in 200 µL triplicates. The microtiter plate was loaded in a SpectraMax i3 and incubated at 37°C, 
shaking. Time-lapsed measurements of optical density (OD600) were taken every 10 minutes. After 24 
hours, end-point samples were taken from the shake flasks. 
Fed-batch fermentation 
Fermentations were carried out in DASGIP 1 L fermenters (configuration specified in Table 17). An 
initial volume of 395 mL bioreactor medium plus batch medium (see composition in appendix) was

Temperature was kept constant at 37°C. pH was adjusted to 7.0 controlled with 6 M ammonia. 1:100 
Overnight culture was transferred to 2X YT medium and incubated for at least 2 hours. 10 mL of this 
seed culture was used for inoculating the batch media. Meanwhile, OD600 was determined from seed 
culture. Samples were taken manually during fermentation. OD600 was determined from samples, and 
supernatant was isolated for HPLC analysis.

3.1 4-VPS expression plasmid assembly and testing 
My first objective was to assemble the 4-VPS expression plasmid and test each component one by 
one. Here, I followed a general workflow. First, BglBricks were assembled by RE/L cloning using the 
BglBricks assembly standard. Plasmid were then verified (colony PCR) and transformed into the 
expression host E. coli BL21(DE3). This expression host were then used in minimal medium (M9) 
growth experiment. This was carried out at 1 mL volume in deep-well plates, and 0.5 mM precursor 
for the given reaction would be added to the medium to determine activity. Cell density would be 
measured from broth, and the supernatant would be used for HPLC analysis.  
I managed to assemble four BglBricks into one single plasmid that would encode for TALFjo, PADBsu, 
CysQ and CysPBsu. The TAL-containing plasmid on the pMK-RQ vector backbone from GeneArt, pCR_6, 
was used as the backbone for the successive RE/L cloning. pCR_7 containing PAD was inserted, forming 
the TAL-PAD plasmid pCR_11. CysQ from pCR_8 was inserted next to produce pCR_13: TAL-PAD-CysQ. 
Finally, CysPBsu was inserted, forming pCR_17: TAL-PAD-CysQ-CysPBsu.  
Tyrosine ammonia lyase (TAL) and phenolic acid decarboxylase (PAD) show high activity from 4-VPS 
expression plasmid, but induction is not controlled. Minimal medium growth experiments showed 
that the TAL and PAD bricks worked optimally. Initially, plasmids containing TAL, PAD or both TAL and 
PAD were transformed into E. coli BL21 and these plasmids were used in a growth experiment with 
0.5 mM addition of either L-tyrosine or p-coumaric acid. Concentrations were quantified by HPLC 
analysis on supernatant, and provided the results presented in Figure 12. Strains containing a plasmid 
with TAL form p-coumaric acid (pHCA) from L-tyrosine (L-tyr), and strains containing a plasmid with 
PAD form 4-vinylphenol (4-VP) from pHCA. Little to no substrate is left in cultures in this experiment, 
suggesting that these enzymes show high activities. Figure 12.a reveal that L-tyrosine can be 
metabolized by the central metabolism in cells, as the control samples contained lower L-tyr 
concentrations than pure medium samples. On the other hand, Figure 12.b shows that pHCA cannot 
be metabolized. Unexpectedly, a 1:1 molar conversion of pHCA to 4-VP is not observed in strains 
expressing the PAD enzyme (see Figure 12.b . 
Importantly, results from Figure 12 show that induction have little to no effect on expression. This is 
apparent in that comparable enzyme activities are observed in samples with and without IPTG. This is 
presumably caused by the combination of having a low abundance of the lac repressor LacI available, 
and that genes are expressed from high-copy number plasmid.

3.6.2 Cell viability and plasmid stability experiments 
To investigate cell viability, CFU counts was carried out. Plasmid stability was investigated from CFU 
by streaking colonies on antibiotic containing LB medium. This was carried out for fermentation 
samples from SUL432 duplicates, SUL380 and sulCR103. The objective for these experiments was to 
determine if the double-plasmid strain showed higher plasmid stability than the triple plasmid 
reference strains. Furthermore, this experiment can show if there is variation on stability of the three 
expression plasmids.  
The number of viable cells without antibiotic resistance increase equally over time in fed-batch 
fermentations. Count-able colonies were obtained by making a dilution series of culture broth (orders 
106 to 109) and streaking 100 µL on LB plates. Loss of antibiotic resistance was determined by streaking 
those single colonies onto plates of LB ampicillin, LB kanamycin, LB spectinomycin and finally LB 
(control). Results are shown in Figure 25 and show that the number of viable cells drop for triple 
plasmid strains, SUL380 and SUL432 over time in the fermentation. For SUL432#2, only a few colonies 
were obtained for last timepoints, and it therefore appear to have high plasmid stability.  Interestingly, 
the number of viable cells without antibiotic resistance increased over time for all cultures. The double 
plasmid strain did not appear to have higher plasmid stability than the triple plasmid strains. Almost 
all colonies retained all plasmids at the first timepoint t = 21 hours. At the last timepoint t = 45 hours, 
about 25% of viable cells have lost one antibiotic resistance plasmid.  
Specific antibiotic marker plasmids lost correlate with the copy number of the given plasmid. A slight 
variance in specific antibiotic marker loss was found for triple plasmid strains SUL432 and SUL380. For 
these strains, the percentage distribution of antibiotic resistances lost was calculated. This calculation 
was based on samples from all timepoints (sample size = 342 colonies). pCDFDuet-1 plasmid was lost 
at the highest frequency, 4.10%, followed by the pETDuet-1 plasmid at 2.92%, and finally the 
pRSFDuet-1 plasmid at 0.58%. This correlates with their proposed copy-number [34]. Thus, low-copy 
number plasmids were observed to be lost at a higher frequency. For sulCR103, the sample size was 
small and no significant variance between the pRSFDuet-1 and pCDFDuet-1 plasmid loss was found.

 Discussion 
Many results were obtained in this study. To keep track, I present my discussion topics in two sections. 
In the first section, I describe the key findings that was learned from engineering the 4-VPS expression 
plasmid. In the second section, I go through each enzyme and intermediate in the 4-VPS biosynthetic 
pathway and discuss what have been found from screening experiments and fed-batch fermentations. 
3.7 Discussion topic #1: Assembly and testing of 4-VPS 
expression plasmid 
The foundation of this study was to engineer an expression system based on a single plasmid design 
to fully drive the synthesis of 4-VPS form L-tyr. Combined with standardized BglBrick parts, the single 
plasmid provides a simplified expression system. The future 4-VPS cell factory would have to be 
plasmid stabilized through other means than using antibiotic resistance markers. This is most easily 
designed with a single-plasmid expression system. In comparison, the triple-plasmid strains 
investigated in this study will require a more complex plasmid stabilization method. While the final 
expression plasmid was never fully assembled, it revealed key discoveries that would shape the future 
design choices. 
Inducible expression cannot be controlled on the GeneArt pMA vector.  
IPTG induction had little to no effect on the expression of TAL and PAD from the pMA vector. This can 
be explained by the fact that LacI repressors are not abundant enough to inhibit expression from all 
lac operators. Without a lacI gene on the expression plasmid, repression in the designed system is 
exclusively maintained by the single lacI gene from the DE3 prophage. This could perhaps be sufficient 
if a low-copy number expression plasmid were used. But pMA is a high copy plasmid: It shares the 
same origin of replication as pETDuet™-1, ColE1, but does not have the Rop protein that serves to 
keep the plasmid at a low copy number. 
The leaky expression was mostly solved by changing the vector backbone out with either pCOLA-Duet1 
or pRSF-Duet1 from Novagen. For future work, these vectors are preferred for expression. 
Furthermore, observing the relative TAL activity from pRSFDuet-1 and pCOLADuet-1 expression, 
higher plasmid copy number lead to increased leaky expression. Because these plasmids contain a lacI 
gene, the amount of gene copies of lacI is proportional with the plasmid copy number. This solution 
was found to solve leaky expression to a moderate extent; some TAL activity was still observed. The 
reason for this is that the T7 expression system is known to be somewhat leaky in most conditions.

To improve control on induction, another strategy would be to express T7 lysozyme which inhibits the

Overexpression of CysPBsu from the pMA vector is lethal to E. coli BL21(DE3). 
Overexpression of the sulfate membrane transporter protein CysPBsu was found to trigger a lethal 
physiological response in E. coli BL21(DE3). This was observed on two occasions: First, when 
attempting to transform the cysPBsu-containing 4-VPS expression plasmid, pCR17. Second, when 
attempting to transform the PAPSS screening library (pSUL71-pSUL77). Transformations were 
unsuccessful because of leaky expression from pMA GeneArt plasmid. 
The PAPSS library can conveniently be screened from Duet vectors from Novagen.  
As I learned that the of leaky expression of CysPBsu from GeneArt vectors is lethal for expression hosts, 
I devised that the PAPSS library might be able to still be screened if it was moved into a less leaky 
system; the pRSFDuet-1 or pCOLADuet-1 vector backbones. This proved to work for one of the 
plasmids from the library, pSUL73.  
For future work, the gene cassettes of the remaining plasmid library, pSUL71-77, can be digested and 
ligated into one of those vectors for successful screening of PAPSS activity. Annoyingly, cysPBsu would 
remain in the resulting plasmid library, but results have shown that toxic effects can be overcome in 
lower-copy number Duet vector pCOLADuet-1. As another option, Gibson assembly can be used to 
just transfer the PAPSS-cysQ gene cassette. Then, the resulting plasmid library can be co-expressed 
with pCBJ329 encoding for rSULT1A1, and the PAPSS library can then be evaluated by growth 
experiments in minimal medium containing pHCA.  
Expression of the sulfate transporter protein CysPBsu lowers ZA production titers in fed-batch 
fermentations, but not in shake-flask experiments. 
From the previously mentioned findings on CysPBsu, it became clear that its expression quickly can 
become a large physiological fitness trade-off. These findings raised more attention to investigating it 
in the currently best-performing cell factory, SUL432. SUL432 is a triple plasmid strain genetically 
identical with SUL380 but with CysPBsu removed. 
In fed-batch fermentations, Removing CysPBsu expression drastically increased ZA production titers and 
the ability to perform high levels of respiration during fermentation. ZA production titers increased 
from ~5mM to 57mM with pCBJ272 (strain SUL432) instead of pCBJ368 (strain SUL380). Cell densities

were found to be slightly improved, and the respiratory activity could now keep up with the glucose 
feed profile after IPTG induction.  
Interestingly, when same strains were compared in shake-flask experiments, expression of CysPBsu in 
SUL380 resulted in increased production of ZA and led to less accumulation of pHCA. This confirmed 
with previous results [2]. The explanation for this is that shake-flask experiments and fed-batch 
fermentations offer two very significantly different growth conditions. In the fed-batch fermentation, 
dissolved oxygen concentrations are higher, pH is stably controlled at optimal settings and nutrients 
are readily supplied. Thus, cells can perform respiration at a much higher level. Destabilization of cell 
membrane in E. coli by perturbing with additional numbers of membrane proteins is known to impair 
the function of membrane-bound proteins involved with aerobic respiration [62]. Thus, it is very 
possible that CysPBsu only becomes physiologically lethal when the expression host engage in high 
respiratory activities. This was also found in results, as SUL380 would prematurely stop consuming 
oxygen. CysPBsu and its function as a sulfate transporter is indeed an element that can be investigated 
to increase sulfation activity. However, its expression must be tuned to fit with the given conditions.  
 
Replacing rSULT1A1 from plasmid to genome expression resulted in dramatically reduced growth 
rates and ZA production in fed-batch fermentations. 
Strains with the Δfhua::rSULT1A1 mutation (sulCR103 and sulCR104) had much slower growth in fed-
batch fermentations. These strains were exceptionally slow at consuming the batch glucose and their 
respiratory activity declined upon induction. This deleterious behavior was unexpected, and in fact 
sulCR103 and sulCR104 was expected to grow better than reference strains because they only contain 
two selective plasmids instead of three. CFU experiments did not show that double plasmid strains 
had higher plasmid stability. Deletion of fhuA is likely not the cause for this observation, as other 
strains have shown to grow optimally with this knock-out. An explanation to this may be that, as 
pETDuet-1 removed, the plasmid copy number of the two other plasmids, pRSFDuet-1 and pCDFDuet-
1 increases, which then result in worsened growth. However, these plasmids contain different origin 
of replications that should not affect one another.  
Genome integration strain development by recombineering offer a strong tool for investigating more 
complex strain designs. However, from my experimentation with tetA-sacB-based counterselection, 
some shortcomings with the method were found. The selective pressure from counterselection plates 
without fusaric acid provide a lot of non-successful colonies. Many colonies had to be tested and 
verified by growth experiments. Therefore, it would be beneficial to find a solution to including fusaric

3.8 Discussion topic #1 Optimizing the 4-VPS biosynthetic 
pathway 
Throughout growth experiments and fed-batch fermentations, I managed to assess each intermediate 
and enzyme playing a role in the 4-VPS biosynthetic pathway starting from L-tyrosine. In the final 4-
VPS cell factory, there will always be one bottleneck that determines the productivity. Therefore, each 
component in the pathway is equally important to optimize for the formation of 4-VPS. Here, I discuss 
the results for each of those components starting from L-tyrosine. 
Tyrosine ammonia lyase show moderately high activity towards L-tyrosine but may become a 
bottleneck in a future optimized cell factory. 
TAL was found to show high activity towards L-tyr in all growth experiments. Still, concentrations of L-
tyr were measured in where TAL was present, indicating that there is room for optimization. In fed-
batch fermentations with SUL432, L-tyr levels appeared to steadily increase over time.  
High concentrations of p-coumaric acid appear to be toxic for E. coli. 
Fermentations with sulCR96, a strain with very low sulfation activity, lead to pHCA accumulation. Once 
pHCA concentrations passed 15 mM, the respiratory activity began declining in the bioreactor. This 
indicates that pHCA is toxic at high concentrations. For future experiments, pHCA toxicity may be used 
to provide selection for sulfotransferase libraries. In theory this would be carried out by stressing cells 
with up to 20 mM pHCA. Cells expressing sulfotransferases with high specificity and activity towards 
pHCA would form ZA and survive the selective pressure. 
Phenolic acid decarboxylase is highly active towards p-coumaric acid.  
In all growth experiments and fermentations where PAD was expressed, no pHCA could be detected 
from samples as all of it was decarboxylated to 4-VP. These results indicate that PAD is highly active 
towards pHCA. For a future optimized cell factory, sufficient decarboxylation may be achieved even 
with lowered PAD expression levels.  
Importantly, no ZA was found from fermentations when PAD was expressed. This can be explained by 
two reasons. One, the activity of PAD is so great on pHCA that it outcompetes sulfotransferase activity 
completely. Two, PAD may show specificity towards ZA and may form 4-VPS after the sulfation 
reaction. This theory can relatively simply be examined in a growth experiment with a PAD-expressing 
==PAGE:73==
Christoffer Rode 
 
DTU 
73 of 95 
 
strain (sulCR13) in a minimal medium containing a small amount of ZA. A hypothetical decarboxylase 
with substrate preference towards ZA could draw a new biosynthetic pathway that avoids the 
formation of toxic 4-VP. 
The formation of 4-vinylphenol caused foaming and severely reduced respiratory activity in fed-
batch fermentations. 
In all strains expressing PAD, 4-VP could be detected and simultaneously caused foaming and drop in 
4-VP. These damaging effects was observed with 4-VP concentrations at 2 mM. The toxic behavior of 
4-VP is not fully understood. One explanation may be that 4-VP is toxic to cells at the found 
concentrations, leading to cell lysis, which then causes foaming. In another explanation, 4-VP may be 
a foaming agent, and the foaming then results in the reduced oxygen transfer rates. To further 
investigate this toxicity, growth experiments could be carried out in minimal medium with 2 mM 4-VP 
added.  
These findings reveal challenges for producing 4-VPS through the described pathway, but it can 
perhaps be resolvable through adjusting fluxes in the pathway. One solution could be significantly 
decrease PAD expression. This way, 4-VP may be sulfated to form 4-VPS before toxic concentrations 
are obtained. Furthermore, a highly active sulfotransferase with specificity to 4-VP can be engineered.  
PAPS regeneration pathway may be improved by replacing CysD, CysN, CysC with PAPS synthetase. 
The PAPS regeneration pathway has not been dissected in this study, but it has previously been found 
that the pathway must be overexpressed to observe sulfotransferase activity. A PAPSS library have 
been worked on that may lead to increased sulfation activity.  
Another strategy for increasing PAPS availability may be to reduce the flux of PAPS going into sulfite. 
This reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme cysH. It has been shown that transcriptional repression of 
cysH could increase PAPS availability by over 3-fold while not affecting cell growth. In this study, this 
was achieved by inhibiting transcription with CRISPRi strategies [63]. 
 
Wildtype rSULT1A1 remains to be the most active sulfotransferase towards p-coumaric acid, and 
probably also for 4-vinylphenol. 
In deep-well growth experiments, mutation I219M was found to be excellent at forming ZA from 
pHCA. Similarly, Δ236-250 was excellent at forming 4-VPS from 4-VP and lost specificity towards pHCA. 
Despite these promising results, the same level of activities was not found in neither shake-flask 
==PAGE:74==
Christoffer Rode 
 
DTU 
74 of 95 
 
experiments nor fed-batch fermentations. This indicates that low-volume growth experiments involve 
uncertainties. 
A key parameter when engineering rational mutations is thermostability. Mutations can either be 
stabilizing or destabilizing. If the protein is given many stabilizing mutations, it becomes too rigid and 
loses its dynamic properties and activity. On the other hand, if proteins are given many destabilizing 
mutations, it becomes too dynamic resulting in unfolding and aggregation. Engineering new functions 
in enzymes are often destabilizing, and therefore it is a common practice to first to engineer 
compensatory mutations that increase thermostability [64]. For rSULT1A1, target residues most suited 
for compensatory mutations are those that are distant from the active site to not interfere with 
enzyme activity. Once these mutations have been engineered and verified, substrate binding pocket 
residues can be targeted.

4 Conclusion 
This study highlights the advantage of sequentially assembling and testing each new BglBrick plasmid 
construct. Because each addition of BglBrick was investigated in growth experiments, it was possible 
to pinpoint the exact relationship between an observed phenotype and assembled plasmid genotype. 
While the complete BglBrick plasmid was not fully developed, two key changes to the original design 
are found. One, the pMA vector backbone from GeneArt is not optimal because it results in high 
amounts of leaky expression. Second, expression of the sulfate transporter protein CysPBsu must be 
kept very low to avoid physiological constraints on the expression host.  
For future designs, it may be valuable to begin redesigning the current triple-plasmid design into a 
single-plasmid design, like the 4-VPS expression plasmid worked on in this study. With one plasmid, 
only a single marker gene is required. Furthermore, plasmid stability should be obtained through 
antibiotic-free design methods in final cell factories, which is much more easily done with a single-
plasmid setup. Currently however, the triple plasmid simply appears to be the optimal setup for 
expressing the sulfation pathway. 
Results from shake-flask experiments highlight that expression of CysPBsu is an important factor for 
improving ZA productivity. The issue is that transporters are very difficult to properly control without 
perturbating cell physiology, especially during high aerobic respiration activity. For now, the removal 
of CysPBsu in the triple-plasmid cell factory design is a serious improvement in terms of ZA productivity. 
Formation of 4-VPS is feasible in the investigated cell factory designs even at 1-L fed-batch 
fermentations. However, the productivity is severely limited by the toxic behavior of 4-VP 
accumulation. Undoubtedly, finding a solution to this problem is the most important factor for the cell 
factory design.
