Problem description 
In order to preserve the protective performance of the ship hull coatings the regular removal of 
biofouling buildup is necessary. Extensive cleaning procedures such dry-docking as are very time- 
and resource-demanding, therefore the frequency must be minimised to the best possible extent. 
Underwater grooming has emerged as a technological solution to address this challenge. Although, 
the currently available biocide free FRCs currently suffer from a lack of durability, leading to a 
shorter service life. Hence, there is a need to develop more robust and long-lasting biocide-free 
FRCs to extend their effectiveness and reduce the frequency of cleaning operations.

Abstract 
Ship hulls are constantly subjected to two degrading processes: corrosive environments and 
biofouling accumulation. Surface coatings have been used as a protection strategy against these 
challenges for thousands of years. To maintain the high performance of the protective coating, 
regular dry-docking is necessary to remove the attached biofouling from the vessel. However, dry-
docking is a time-consuming and resource-demanding task. Therefore, there is a demand for 
coating formulations that can decrease the frequency of required dry-dockings, thereby reducing 
costs and saving resources. 
In the past decades there has been a shift in focus not only towards coating formulations but also 
towards cleaning technologies to minimise the need for dry-docking procedures. In water cleaning 
(IWC) has gained popularity over the years, and advancements in robotic grooming technology 
have made it possible to remove accumulated biofouling at an early stage. 
However, the commercially available biocide free anti-fouling (A/F) coatings are not mechanically 
resistant enough for long-term rotary brush cleaning. Therefore, the objective of this study is to 
explore potential reinforcement methods for the fragile foul-release coatings (FRCs). 
Various fillers, including lignin, nano aluminium-oxide (n-Al2O3), nano zirconium-oxide (n-
ZrO2), and hollow sphere glass bubbles were added to neat epoxy, and hybrid epoxy-silicone based 
coatings. The goal was to assess the effects of these fillers on the mechanical performance and 
durability of the coatings under abrasive conditions and during marine exposure. 
TABER Rotary Platform Abraser and the same grooming technology that was cleaning the 
exposed specimens were the chosen abrasive tests. To monitor the response to these abrasive 
impacts, the tests were periodically halted at predetermined stages to measure the surface 
properties such as the advancing water contact angle (WCA), the surface roughness, and the 
thickness. Additionally qualitative data was collected by visually examining the panels after each 
stage using both the naked eye and a digital microscope. 
Upon evaluating the results, it became evident that the hybrid resin formulations with incorporated 
nanoparticles or hollow spheres are promising candidates for durable biocide-free antifouling 
coatings.

1. Introduction 
Paints and coatings are applied on surfaces to prevent the degradation of the substrate mainly caused 
by corrosion and wear. Corrosion is an irreversible damage of metal surfaces, leading to unwanted 
material failure, which has economic and environmental consequences. Biofouling is undesirable 
accumulation of microorganisms on surfaces and have several indirect adverse effects on the 
environment. 
Ship hulls are continuously immersed in marine water, hence exposed to corrosive species, and 
fouling microorganisms. The maintenance with dry-docking is crucial, yet not enough as it is time 
and resource demanding. Increasing the time between the dry-dockings is possible with a high 
performance coating system, which has anti-corrosive and anti-fouling properties as well. These 
surfaces still need mechanical cleaning to remove the weakly attached biofouling. In the past decade 
the development of underwater grooming technology seems to solve the problem of the biofouling. 
Although the state-of–art in anti-fouling coatings are not strong enough mechanically to bear the 
impact of the underwater cleaning without damaging. 
In this study the focus will be on formulating antifouling coatings, that are mechanically resistant 
against the abrasion of the grooming technologies. Developing a high performance anti-fouling 
coating for grooming technologies is not only important for protecting the vessel, but to reduce the 
amount of microplastics released in the marines. 
Promising solution is to make a hybrid coating with antifouling properties and mechanical 
resistance, anti-corrosive barrier coatings is proposed to be a possibility for increasing the durability 
of these systems.  
The performance and efficiency of the produced coatings will be compared to each other alongside 
different hypotheses. Some samples will be exposed at outdoor port, others will be tested under 
controlled conditions in the laboratory. 
2. Constituents of coatings 
Commercial film coatings usually have four main components: the binder, the carrier fluid, 
pigments, and additives. In the following sections these constituents in details and their role in 
the coating system will be explained. 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
 
Figure 1 Schematic representation of film coating compositions 
2.1. 
Binder 
This is the basis of continuous film, sealing and protecting the surface with adhesive forces 
between the painting and the substrate [1].  Made from polymers or resins, the choice of 
components is dependent on the application and cure, the quality of the protectable surface and the 
conditions the coating will be exposed to. Besides the sealing property binders also create the 
continuous phase in the coatings and hold all the other components in the mixture. 
The classification of film formers based on their molecular weight; the difference that the low 
molecular weight polymers cannot form solid films without further chemical reaction on the 
contrary of the high molecular weight binders. 
2.1.1. Water-based 
In one-component binders the hard and durable film formed by physical drying, when the water 
evaporates from the system. In these systems some of the components are dissolved in water, but 
the main film former is not, therefore the ‘aqueous phase’ is a more precise description. The 
emulsion is made from water and latex polymer, the chemical composition of the latex consists of 
vinyl acetate, vinyl chloride, acrylics, acrylonitrile, ethylene, styrene, butadiene, and isoprene [1]. 
The advantages of using water are its availability, non-toxicity, however most coating ingredients 
has organic origin, which may not be dissolved in water. The slow drying rate and high melting 
point, makes it unfavourable [2]. Among the usual applications paints, primers can be mentioned.  
2.1.2. Solvent-based 
In two-component binder systems the solid film forms through chemical reaction between the resin 
and the curing agent, creating a cross-linked polymer network. Usually low molecular weight 
organic, specifically either hydrocarbons or oxygenated compounds used as coating solvents [3]. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Huge advantage compared to water their volatility, which makes preferable for quick drying. 
Concerning factors about organic solvents the possible harm they may cause on the environment 
and the human health [4]. Epoxy binder is one of the most commonly used example, as its chemical 
and mechanical resistance makes it favourable for industrial settings. 
 
Figure 2 Comparison of water-borne and solvent based coatings film formation through time, the water-borne coating has a 
coalescing step [5] 
In summary both types of binder have their own advantages for different applications, the choice 
is influenced by factors such as the substrate the coating will be applied on, the method it will be 
applied with and the conditions the film will be exposed to. 
2.2. 
Pigments 
Pigments are solid, insoluble particulates dispersed in the binder, the function is to improve the 
film aesthetically (colour, opacity) and mechanically (strength, adhesion), control the rheology of 
the paint [1]. Although pigment particle size refers to the average diameter of the particles, it is 
important to consider that this measurement involves several assumptions and simplifications since 
the particles' shape is often non-spherical. Organic pigments are smaller between 0.01 – 1.00 µm 
than inorganic pigments, which have particle size from 0,1 µm up to 5,00 µm.  
The literature distinguishes pigments to primary pigments, which the main contributors of the 
appearance and anticorrosive properties. The rest is supplementary pigments, extenders, fillers 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
added to reduce the overall cost of the paint, these are also contributing to the mentioned functions, 
but at a less extent [6]. 
Titanium dioxide is the most used primary pigment, due to the consumer preferences towards white 
and pastel shades. A pigment with a high refractive index and superior development is produced 
with a narrow particle size distribution. It is chemically inert and can be used as a pigment; 
however, its photoactivity is reduced by applying a surface coating on the crystal. The 
photoactivity can be reduced by the addition of silica and alumina, which also improve the pigment 
dispersibility in the binder.

The coloured pigments have either inorganic or organic origin. Many of the inorganic ones are 
replaced by less toxic ones. Most of the lead-type of pigments have been replaced, although the 
function of these pigments was not only to colour the surface, but their usage was also for anti-
corrosive properties. 
Aluminium flake is used as a metallic finish in the automotive industry, the flakes dimension and 
orientation give the shiny effect.  
Among the extenders calcium-carbonate can be mentioned, mixed with titanium dioxide as a 
matting agent, in white paints. Usually, the extenders do not contribute to the colour, and only little 
effect on the opacity due to their particle size is close to the continuous phase (the binder).  Ground 
mica a platelet type extender can increase the corrosion resistance of the coating, by changing the 
water permeability.  Synthetic extenders are manufactured for special needs, like changing the 
refractive index by introducing voids in the paint. Submicrometric air bubbles are blended in the 
Australian ‘Spindrift’ polymer beads, in latex paints the emulsification small droplets of volatile

fluid which evaporates after coalescence, or the combining coalescing and non-coalescing latex 
particles [6]. 
2.3. 
Solvents 
The purpose of the solvents is to dissolve the binder, disperse insoluble ingredients creating a 
stable solution. Thinners make the coating easily appliable to the surfaces by reducing the 
viscosity, while diluents control the properties, and an affordable way to increase the volume of 
the mixture. In the early stage it affects the mechanical properties of the film such as hardness and 
flexibility. Requirement is to have a boiling point between 120-175 °C and avoid highly volatile 
solvents [1]. Other determining parameters in choosing the solvent are evaporation rate, odour, 
toxicity due to environmental and human health protection, viscosity, solvency considering 
applicability and cost [3]. 
Solvent free coating systems are made from thermoplastic powders, which are 100% 
polymerizable systems such as styrene. The application on the preheated substrate can be dipping 
into a heated fluidized bed of the powder, electrostatic spraying, flame spraying with in situ 
application is possible. 
2.4. 
Additives 
The amount is less than 1% of the total weight of volume, however its importance is more 
significant as it decreases the arising defects in the liquid paint and in the dry film. The main 
defects are settlement of pigment and skinning in the can; aeration and bubble retention on 
application; cissing, sagging, and shrivelling of the paint film; pigment flotation; and flooding. 
Rheological-control additives, usually known as thickeners change the viscosity of the paint and 
prevent ‘sagging’ the uneven coating as the result of excessive flow of a paint on a vertical surface. 
Catalyst and driers, usually metallic salts (e.g., cobalt) of naphthenic acids which fasten up the 
drying process. Wetting agents are reducing the surface tension of the coating fluid to make the 
application easier on the substrate surface. Dispersing agents stabilize colloidal system of the 
pigment particles in the liquid coating. Defoamers are used against bubble caused defects, often 
based on silicone oils. Fungicides are used for mildew prevention in the building and bactericides 
used for elongating the shelf-life of latex paintings [8]. 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 ‘Shrivelling’ is the development of a wrinkled surface in films that dry by oxidation; the uneven 
surface caused by imbalanced cross-linking within the film and the film in contact with the surface. 
In some cases, the reformulation of the drier components is enough to solve the issue.  
‘Cissing’ is the appearance of small, saucer-like depressions in the surface of the film.  ‘Sagging’ 
is the development of an uneven coating as the result of excessive flow of a paint on a vertical 
surface. The main difference is the extent of the irregularity and the responsible physical property, 
that cause the problem. When there is a localized change in the surface tension of the coating 
‘cissing’ might appear, while ‘sagging’ is associated with rheology, which is a bulk property of 
the film. Changing the paint viscosity to non-Newtonian behaviour might mitigate the ‘sagging’ 
problem. 
‘Floating’ is the term used for the colour differences that can occur in a paint film because of the 
spontaneous separation of component pigments after application. ‘Flooding’ (also known as ‘brush 
disturbance’) is the permanent colour change of a paint subject to shear after application. Both 
floating and flooding are consequences of colloidal stability problems. The separation of the 
dispersed pigments is according to particle size differences, which can be reduced by co-
flocculation. 
One group of the additives are driers, usually metallic salts (e.g., cobalt) of naphthenic acids which 
fasten up the drying process. The two-step acceleration mechanism is catalysed uptake of oxygen 
and polymerization caused by the sensitized decomposition of peroxides. These peroxides are 
forming free radicals, that is added to the double bonds.  
Additives used in water-based paints are plasticizers, which smooth out the drying surface and 
lower the polymer glass transition temperature below room temperature, emulsifiers, surface active 
agents, thickening agents, preservatives, and coalescing aids. Freeze-thaw stabilizers such as high 
boiling glycols, with slow evaporation allows the merging of the latex particles during the film 
formation.

3.1. 
Anti-corrosion (A/C) coatings 
3.1.1. What is corrosion? Types and consequences 
The physiochemical interaction between metals and their environment, usually an electrochemical 
reaction, which transforms the properties of the metal is called corrosion. The damage on the 
material leads to high economic cost, technological delays even structural failures that might end 
as a catastrophe. 
Science differentiates three main types of corrosion: general attack, localized and galvanic 
corrosion. In case of general corrosion, the oxidation is even on the entire surface, while the 
localized corrosion attacks a specific area. Galvanic corrosion has specific conditions, requires two 
metals and their electrical connection by an electrolyte. The created battery cell results the 
corrosion of the less noble metal [9]. 
3.1.2. Corrosive environments 
Before the application of anticorrosive coating, it is essential to clarify the environment the 
protective film will be exposed to. The literature group the outdoor exposure in three categories: 
immersion, atmospheric and splash zone, which means the material periodically immersed and 
exposed to the atmosphere, due to waves and tides. The three outdoor environments can be 
specified into subgroups based on the nature of the exposure, but in further discussion the focus 
will be only on the relevant ones for ship hull applications. Materials exposed to atmospheric 
marine environment are in highly corrosive conditions due to the high level of chloride ions. Sea 
water is also aggressive towards metal, more than fresh water, because of the salinity mainly from 
sodium chloride attack the material and the protective coating.

3.1.3. Classification of A/C coatings 
Anti-corrosive coatings are required to be durable, resistant against the environment, but a certain 
level of flexibility is needed as well. Too rigid coatings, that cannot respond to the emerging 
internal stress will have defects like cracking, which is visually unpleasant and decrease the level 
of protection. For the best performance anti-corrosion coatings are systems built up from multiple 
layers, which have different functionalities. The individual coats based on their origin can be either 
metallic, inorganic, or organic.  
First comes the primer in direct connection with the protectable material, therefore good adhesion 
to the substrate is necessary. On the primer several layers of intermediate coat applied, to create a 
thick barrier that slows down the diffusion of the corrosive species. The system is finalised with a 
top coat, exposed to environment, hence it must be resistant for the changing conditions, such as 
weather, ultraviolent radiation and in the same time provide an aesthetic appearance [10]. 
The general classification of anti-corrosive coatings is their protective mechanism, the three types 
are: barrier, sacrificial and inhibitive coating. 
Sacrificial coatings 
The working mechanism is based on the galvanic effect, the coating is more reactive in the 
environment than the substrate. As the name refers to that, the coating is continuously corroding 
and depleting. Sacrificial coatings are only effective if they are applied as primers, direct 
connection between the coating and the protectable material is required. 
Zinc is a widely used material as anodically active coating, which can preserve the substrate by 
galvanic effect until there is enough zinc in the system for conduction. Over time with zinc 
corrosion the coating becomes nonconductive, the oxidated zinc will seal the material and act as a 
barrier coating [11], [12]. For the highest transfer of the galvanic current sacrificial coatings are 
highly pigmented, therefore other components added for mechanical resistance have lower content 
in the coating. 
Inhibitive coating 
The coating is passivating the substrate by creating a protective layer of insoluble metallic 
complexes, which forms a barrier against the corrosive species. Applied as a primer coating, for 
the highest protection the inhibitive pigments must react with the protectable metal. The inhibitive 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
pigments in the presence of moisture are partially dissolve and react with the substrate and results 
a passive protective layer on the surface. 
An effective inhibitive coating must release enough inhibitor for the protective layer by dissolving, 
but the formed film needs to be a barrier against water and other aggressive species. Therefore, the 
coating mechanism is either dominated by the barrier behaviour of the pigment in case of low 
permeability, or the inhibitive pigment effect, when the mechanism driven by the dissolved 
pigments [10]. 
Barrier coatings 
Immersed structures are protected with barrier coatings, the working mechanism is based on the 
low ionic permeability, that blocks the diffusion of aggressive species. The moisture between the 
substrate and the coating conductivity minimized, hence the corrosion slowed down. The thickness 
of the barrier coating is the key to an effective protection, thicker systems, which is preferably 
built up from several thin layers. Thicker barrier coatings have lower possibility for layer 
delamination [13]. The arising question about the multiple thin layers is the economic value against 
the cost, the barrier system therefore needs optimization. High cross-link density is another 
important dimensional parameter in terms of protective performance, to reduce corrosion under 
the film [14]. 
The nature of binder system has also an impact on the efficiency, the choice limited by the exposure 
conditions. Polar groups in the binder of the primer coat can enhance the adhesion to the metal 
substrate. 
Barrier coatings can be reinforced with cathodic protection, which protect the substrate by creating 
an external current. This is executed either by attaching a less noble material, which act as a 
sacrificial anode or by applying an external current source, a rectifier is an often-used example for 
that [10]. 
This study will examine the performance of barrier coatings, as it is the best option for recurring 
grooming activities. The mechanical resistance of this coatings is key factor to conserve of the 
structural integrity against repetitive abrasion impact.

3.1.4. Epoxy coatings 
Epoxy resisns are amorphous thermosets, excellent properties can be designed for the different 
applications with choosing the proper resin, the curing agent and setting the appropiate conditions. 
Widespread application as coatings, adhesives, composites and in electrical devices is due to their 
mechanical properties, heat- and chemical resistance and low shrinkage, which is exeptional 
among the thermosetting polymers. High performance industrial applications  The resin general 
structure consists at least one epoxy group, the non-epoxy part can be either aliphatic, 
cycloaliphatic or aromatic. This polar functional groups result high performance anti-corrosive 
coatings on metal surfaces such as carbon steel, even when it is immersed in sodium chloride 
solution [15]. 
The epoxy systems consist of two components, the base which is the resin and the curing agent 
which initiate the cross-linking [16]. The cross-linking reaction can be achieved by catalytic self-
homo-polymerization or by curing with co-reactants. The mechanism is a condensation, however 
without any by product. The most often used curing agents are secondary amines and acid 
anhydrides [15] [17]. After the hardening process the resin achieve a high degree of cross-linked 
network, therefore the coating becomes more brittle.  
Bisphenol-A (BPA) 
The most popular epoxy resin is the product of bisphenol-A reacted with epichlorohydrin. Low 
molecular weight products with a small number of repeating units are viscous liquids, with higher 
molecular weight the products are more viscous liquid or solid.  
Figure 5 Epoxy functional group [16]

Figure 6 Bisphenol-A resin synthesis [18] 
The stoichiometric ratio of the two raw material is 2:1, but in practice higher quantities, two to 
three times of the epichlorohydrin is applied.  
Bisphenol-F (BPF) 
Substituting Bisphenol-A with bisphenol-F lowers the viscosity of the product, which makes it 
easier to handle. The resin has a higher epoxy content, and better mechanical and chemical 
properties, therefore BPA is often mixed with BPF in epoxy resins to avoid crystallization  [15], 
[19]. 
Novolac 
This resin has structural similarity with Bakelite since novolac is a formaldehyde and phenol 
derivative. The reaction conditions of the resin production are similar to BPA and BPF, although 
the product has higher degree of cross-linking, hence more vicious, handling the product therefore 
is more difficult. The high cross-linking density enhances the resin thermal- and chemical-
resistance, on the other hand it has an adverse effect on the mechanical properties, the coating is 
more brittle. [15], [20].

3.2. 
Anti-fouling (A/F) coatings  
According to [21] the definition of marine biofouling is the undesired accumulation of 
microorganisms, plants, and animals on artificial surfaces immersed in sea water. The rate of this 
continuous growth depends both on the condition of the environment (salinity, temperature) and 
the vessel [22]. Several negative consequences arise when this biological settlement spread on ship 
hulls. Among the effects the increased fuel consumption of the ship to sustain its speed and 
manoeuvrability as well as the heightened frequency of dry-docking, both have high impact on the 
environment, such as air-pollution. Another problem is that the deteriorated coating is not 
protecting the substrate surface, which leads to the degradation of the material. Lastly the 
appearance of these invasive species, which has negative effect on biodiversity, human health, and 
economy. 
Ships with low duty cycle, spend most of their time in static place in the port are excessively 
exposed to biofouling. Representative example for these conditions is the Navy vessels, these ship 
hulls are usually coated with biocidal, ablative copper bearing products [23]. 
3.2.1. Fouling Release Coatings (FRC) – ultrasmooth, low surface energy coatings 
This type of coating provides ultra-smooth surface with low friction, hence prevent any kind of 
organism to stick to the surface. To achieve this adhesion resistance the chemical structure needs 
a flexible linear backbone to avoid unwanted interactions, with enough surface-active groups, 
which can move and transmit surface energy in a certain range. The whole chain (backbone and 
the side chains) must have high molecular mobility, but stable physically and chemically at the 
same time for immersion in sea water [24]. 
As the biocide in the antifouling coatings most of the time contain heavy metals, like tin, lead, 
copper, arsenic, and mercury, their bioaccumulation has potential risk on the environment [25]. 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
Therefore, lately the focus shifted on the development of environmental friendlier possibilities, 
one of them are FRCs, beneficial features for the environment comes from the copper-free 
composition. 
Chemical durability and extended fouling control elongate the service time of the film. The smooth 
surface influences the ship performance, maximise the speed, therefore minimise the fuel 
consumption [26]. 
Nowadays these surfaces are made from fluoropolymers and silicones. Fluoropolymers have low 
surface energy and create a non-porous coating layer. The structural reason is the surface oriented 
perfluoroalkyl groups, which are permanently cross-linked to minimise the surface molecular 
diffusion or rearrangement after immersion. However, the closely packed film results low mobility 
and block the rotation around the backbone, and the higher bulk modulus will increase the required 
stress for the fouling release.  
Silicone based FRCs are improved compared to fluoropolymers, the fouling-release is slower, but 
enhanced as it requires less energy. The most used ones are the Poly(dimethylsiloxane)-based due 
to their low surface energy, low glass transition temperature, low microroughness and high elastic 
modulus. The fouling release of the poly(dimethylsiloxane)-based coatings can be improved with 
additives, which create a weak layer by migrating to the surface. Although after the depletion of 
these oil additives the coating will be more brittle which results cracking in the protective layer, 
their lifetime is approximately 2 years. 
Promising solution to combine the advantages of the two types is fluorine substituted polysiloxanes 
[26]. 
The limitations of the currently available FRCs are their service time, which is maximum 3 years, 
the cost, and the poor mechanical resistance. In comparison of the biocide containing antifouling 
coatings the fouling-release is only effective above a certain speed of the vessel, as the foul release 
initiated by hydrodynamic action [27]. The reparation of the damaged layer is circumstantial as it 
needs a tie coat before recoating, with the present abrasive cleaning technology it is more likely to 
happen [28].  
3.2.2. Self-Polishing Coatings (SPC) 
Tributyltin (TBT) copolymer paints were banned from the market in 2003, due to its high toxicity 
on the environment. As TBT coatings had an excellent performance against biofouling, to find an 
alternative is became an urgent need to the industry. The desired characteristics for these paintings 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
include smooth paint surfaces, thin and stable leached layer to ensure the constant biocide release, 
and a polishing rate which means the coating exhibits an A/F activity in stationary periods and 
increases linearly with sailing speed. 
The available alternatives on the market today can be classified in two groups [29]: 
First the tin-free SPCs as the name shows, it is working in the same way as the tin-based versions 
were. Under slightly alkaline conditions, like in sea water the leached layer, where the biocide 
concertation is depleted hydrolysis takes place in the coating system. The loss of biocide will make 
the coating brittle as the glass transition temperature increase, and the surface will become 
hydrophilic. Over time the residual brittle backbone will be eroded, and the less reacted layer will 
be exposed to the surface. The performance of the acrylic matrix is highly influenced by the 
chemical structure of the pedant group. After several studies, none of these tin-free options are as 
effective anti-fouling as TBTs were. 
 
 
Figure 9 Self-polishing antifouling coating working mechanism [30] 
The other group is the controlled depletion systems (CPDs), the surface protecting mechanism is 
the same as it would be with soluble matrix paints, where the rosin-based coatings have water 
soluble binder. Although these systems have several issues like poor self-smoothening, and with 
their longevity [26]. 
3.2.3. Hydrogels – nature inspired coatings 
Recent tendency in natural sciences to mimic nature for a certain property, the natural antifouling 
material is the mucus on marine animals on their bodies. Mucin is the chemical compound of this 
natural hydrogel on the marine species [30]. 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
Hydrogels are hydrophilic materials, as they can absorb large amounts of water to their three-
dimensional cross-linked polymer network. The anti-fouling mechanism is based on the formed 
hydration layer, a physical barrier against the fouling organisms [31]. 
Natural hydrogels like alginate gel, agar gel and collagen gel are biodegradable and biocompatible. 
Synthetic hydrogels are fabricated by physical and chemical crosslinking methods from 
polyethylene glycol (PEG), polyvinyl alcohol (PVAL), polyacrylamide (PAM), polyurethane (PU) 
and acrylate hydrogels [32]. The physical method is less toxic, no need for initiators for the 
reaction, but the product is less stable than the chemically cross-linked ones [33]. 
Despite the excellent antifouling capabilities of hydrogels their poor mechanical resistance is 
limiting the practical applications. To overcome this fragility, hydrogels are strengthened by robust 
mechanical stabilization. Several methods have been tested, among the promising one double 
network (DN), self-generating, nanocomposite polymer hydrogels can be mentioned. However, 
these laboratory successes have still long way to marine environment applications [30]. 
Hydrogel modification with polymer brushes seem solution for sea water exposure, based only on 
the results of laboratory tests [34]. The working mechanism of the polymer brushes is that they act 
as a steric barrier against bacteria and large molecules. Hence the research is narrowed down to 
medical A/F applications [30]. 
Composite materials’ popularity increased over the past decade, due to their high performance, 
cheap and easy mass production. Against diatom adhesion synthetic orthosilic acid analog 
(SOSA)-incorporated hydrogels were synthetised [35]. For antibacterial properties nanocomposite 
hydrogels reported effective protection, SOSA-hydrogel has not been studied in this aspect. The 
mentioned nanofillers in studies were nano copper oxide, silver nanoparticles, nano silicon dioxide 
[36]–[38]. Nanoparticles has a high tendency for aggregation, which decrease the composite 
durability, therefore adequate dispersion and dosage Is important in the fabrication [30].  
In chapter 5 the chosen materials for this study will be discussed in detailes.  
4. Underwater cleaning with grooming technology 
4.1. 
Present technology 
In terms of antifouling, cleaning is necessary in many cases, however the reduction of the dry-
docking frequency is essential as it has a huge impact on the environment and high-cost, time- and 
money-wise. In-water cleaning (IWC) is an effective way to maintain the ship hulls free from 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
biofouling, therefore increasing the time between the necessary dry-dockings. However, the 
uncaptured debris has an adverse effect on the surrounding marine environment, the direct release 
of Non-Indigenous Species (NIS) and harmful Anti-Fouling System (AFS) biocides from the 
coating [39]. 
IWC can be categorised in two groups: reactive and proactive cleaning. The former schedule the 
cleaning only when the decreased performance of the vessel is detected. The latter is also called 
grooming, which is defined as “the gentle, habitual and frequent mechanical maintenance of 
submerged ships’ hulls in order that they remain free from extraneous matter such as fouling 
organisms and particulate debris, with minimal impact to the coating” [23]. In this study the 
formulated coating will be tested with proactive IWC. 
Advantages of consistent grooming: enhance the performance of the vessel while reduce the 
energy consumption, ensure the longevity of the coating, and reduce the total cost [40]. The 
grooming technology must balance out an effective fouling removal without damaging the coating 
on the ship hull, but the coating itself also must be resistant enough to stand the impact of the 
mechanical cleaning. 
The cleaning duration and frequency is dependent on the ship duty cycle, coating and biofouling 
type and area. The nature of biofouling, such as composition and thickness are influenced by the 
ship class, the coating system, the coating condition, and the location of the harbour. Naval ships 
are especially exposed to biofouling, due to the they spend long stationary periods in ports [41]. 
The frequency of the grooming influences the abrasion of the coating, with weekly grooming the 
depletion of the film was less over 35 months than with monthly grooming. Although the 
difference was not significant, but by the end of the study after 72 months the monthly groomed 
surface had remarkably less loss of the coating than on the weekly groomed [40].  
Other results showed that the damage on flourosilicone FRC caused by the weekly grooming was 
less, than the abrasion caused by the applied force to clean a heavily fouled surface [42]. 
Small vessels usually cleaned manually, with brushes, cloth and scraping tools by a snorkeler, 
although the efficiency is about 60% of this method. For higher cleaning performance 
mechatronics equipment has been developed, both handheld and Remotely Operated Vehicles 
(ROV) cleaners. The ROV systems have higher performance, due to enhanced efficiency and no 
limitation of the working hours. 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
Powered rotary brush cleaning systems are rotating brushes or brushes powered by hydraulic 
motors, working best on large flat or slightly curved surfaces. However, with specific brush design 
the niche areas, such as the propellers, rudders of the ship hull   can be cleaned from fouling. The 
built-up fouling is removed with the applied mechanical and frictional forces of the rotating brush 
[22]. 
There are different materials for the cleaning brush, the choice is dependent on what kind of 
biofouling must be removed and what kind of material the surface is. Non-abrasive brushes like 
nylon ones are used on fiberglass, wood, aluminium, and steel ship hulls, for removing slime, 
algae, and other soft biofoulings. Metallic, abrasive brushes only can be used on steel and 
aluminium surfaces to remove hard biofoulings, such as calcareous barnacles and lower valve 
oysters [22]. 
Bottleneck of the powered rotary brushes is the resulting abrasion on the surface, which is reducing 
the coating lifetime, and might damage the welds, rivets, and protrusions. The cleaning methods 
for vessels are too aggressive for most antifouling coating systems, and usually increase the 
amount of the released biocide in SPCs. One of the reasons is that the grooming is not performed 
until the formation of a well-established attachment on the ship hull [43]. 
Novel technology to reduce the possible damages due to the abrasion is the contactless underwater 
cleaning methods, for example high-pressure water cleaning jets, cavitation jet method and 
ultrasonic cleaning method [44]. Although in this study the coating systems will be designed for 
powered rotary brush cleaning devices. 
A recent study [23] has tested five different antifouling coating system with weekly grooming. The 
five coatings were: one ablative copper, two self-polishing copper formulations and two copper-
free formulations. All the coatings have reported free from macrofouling at least for 11 months,

up to 24 months. Cleaning with grooming proved to be successful against macrofouling 
accumulation. However, a thin layer of tenacious biofilm remained after grooming, which 
obviously had lower level of negative consequence than the ungroomed films [23]. In another 
study the impact of the low-profile tenacious biofilm on the skin friction drag could not be even 
quantified precisely [42].  
4.2. 
The limitation of A/F coatings application 
Anti-fouling coatings has three strategies against microorganisms, coatings with biocide release, 
coatings with super smooth surface to prevent the attachment and hard-inert surfaces with regular 
cleaning. The concerns about copper-based coatings on the possible bioaccumulation and 
environmental impact are increased after the use of TBT was banned [45]. The other problem with 
the self-polishing system is the leached layer, the biocide depleted layer has more pores, and thus 
the diffusion of the aggressive species is accelerated [46]. 
 The environmental friendlier alternatives for antifouling coatings had issues with their mechanical 
resistance, and it is clearly still under development. Anticorrosive coatings have the appropriate 
durability for underwater cleaning, together with anti-fouling coating the coating system can be 
adequate for underwater cleanings as well.  
5. Designing coatings suitable for grooming 
5.1. 
Selection of epoxy resin 
Novolac resin has superior chemical and thermal resistance, nonetheless the mechanical properties 
are weaker than for the other bisphenol resins. Due to its brittleness and the higher viscosity, 
handling and application as a coating is difficult.  BPF based epoxy resins has enhanced protective 
performance than BPA, although BPA is satisfactory for marine exposure. No need for extreme 
thermal resistance, the exposure is on ambient temperature. The only required chemical protection 
against the seawater salinity, hence the corrosive environment. BPA epoxy resin is a commercially 
popular anticorrosive coating material, widely available economic choice. 
5.2. 
Epoxy silicone hybrid coatings 
Non sticky polymers such as poly(dimethyl siloxane) (PDMS) is a non-toxic alternative for anti-
fouling coatings. The created hydrophobic surface has low surface energy with prevents the 
adhesion of biofouling, furthermore, is an environmentally friendly flame retardant [20]. Although, 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
these excellent properties pair with poor mechanical resistance and weak adhesion to the substrate 
[25]. 
Epoxy resins modified with silicone are promising solution to overcome the mechanical limitations 
of the silicone polymers. Despite of the high performance thermal-, chemical-resistance and 
durability of epoxy resins start to degrade in the long-term outdoor exposure [47]. Furthermore, 
the highly cross-linked network is brittle, and hydrophilic therefore has limitations when it comes 
to applications as an antifouling coating [25].  
Commercially available Intergard® 264 epoxy coating antifouling performance were tested [43], 
they were rapidly covered with biofouling without grooming, however even with frequent 
grooming the speed of the biofouling accumulation on panels was not reduced significantly. On 
the other hand, the groomed epoxy coatings seemed undamaged after the test, but the observation 
was difficult as there were always a certain level of residual slime on the panels. On the contrary 
biocide free FR silicone coatings remained foul-free up to 120 days, with 3-day grooming 
frequency [27]. 
Therefore, adding PDMS to the epoxy resin can improve the toughness and giving better 
antifouling properties for the coatings, without compromising the thermos-oxidative properties 
and flame retardancy. Incorporating the epoxy coating with silicone also improves the resin UV 
resistance, which makes them ideal for use in outdoor applications.  With other modifiers, like 
thermoplastic polymers, liquid rubbers or nanofillers the epoxy resin would loss its excellent 
thermal resistance. Pure PDMS requires a certain level of modification to have better compatibility 
with the epoxy resin. The flexible siloxane bind has free rotation; hence the tensile strength of the 
resin is declined [48]. 
 
In summary epoxy silicone hybrid coatings are a type of coating that combines the benefits of 
both epoxy and silicone resins make them ideal for use in a wide range of industrial and

commercial applications. Among the favourable advantages easy application, quick curing and 
the formed film high mechanical and chemical resistance can be mentioned. 
5.3. 
Biobased materials for improved mechanical property – lignin, natural epoxy structure 
A lot of emphasis is put on research today to find renewable alternative for fossil-based polymer 
materials. A promising material substituting BPA in epoxy resins lignin, found in abundant 
quantities in the plant cell walls and so far, treated as a by-product of biomass extraction [49]. 
Lignin is a fibrous and though component of the plant cell wall, structurally highly branched three-
dimensional polymer network. The three monomers lignin built from are the following 
phenylpropanes: p-coumaryl alcohol, also known as H-units, coniferyl alcohol, the G-units and 
sinapyl alcohol, the S-units [50].  
In the plant cell lignin has a mechanical support role, increasing the strength of the structure, while 
protecting it from the harsh environment [51]. The attention to this natural polyphenol has been 
increased in the past years, as it is probably the largest renewable resource for aromatic compounds 
[52]. The building blocks and other phenolic compound derived from lignin can be used for 
numerous polymer production, for example in epoxy resins. The phenolic structure has antioxidant 
activity, and able to block the UV radiation, in contrast with synthetic epoxies, which performance 
is weakened when it is exposed in outdoor environments. [53]– [55]. The rigid network with high 
cross-linking density results high Tg, providing a stiff mechanically resistant polymer. The glass 
transition temperature is also affected by the strong secondary forces between the molecules, the 
π-π stacking and the hydrogen bonding [56]. 
Biobased epoxy resin can be synthetised with different lignin forms, from the macromolecular 
form, from depolymerized lignin and from lignin model compounds. 
Macromolecular lignin structurally stubborn and blending in the epoxy matrix is difficult, due to 
its low reactivity. With functionalization lignin compatibility can be increased, amination, 
esterification and glycidylation are reported successful methods for [57], [58]. The higher content 
of lignin will enhance the cross-linking density in the structure, which is lowering the molecule-
chain mobility. Means the network after a certain content of added lignin becomes stiff, hard to 
shape. With lower molecular weight lignin better mechanical properties could be achieved even 
with highly cross-linked network, although the thermally was less stable [59], [60]. 
With depolymerization the three aromatic alcohol building blocks can be produced, which are 
more reactive in the resin production [61]. From the monolignols several lignin-derivates can be 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
synthetised as well, like vanillin. The resin from depolymerized lignin had better mechanical 
properties, but the thermal stability was lower than the resin from raw lignin, similarly as the resin 
from low molecular weight lignin [62]. Furthermore, the difficulties of the depolymerization, the 
required harsh conditions and the product complexity, which makes the separation expensive 
compared to the achievable yield [63]. 
The third option for epoxy resin production is lignin model compounds, as the raw lignin is 
structurally a complex material, and the depolymerization routes are unstable [49]. The main type 
of pre-polymers derived from lignin are eugenol, which is a phenylpropanoid, aromatic acids such 
as vanillin, triphenols and furan-polyphenols.  
Overall, lignin-based epoxy coatings have the potential to be a sustainable and environmentally 
friendly alternative to traditional epoxy coatings. However, further research is needed to optimize 
the properties of these coatings and to develop cost-effective methods for their modification and 
application as a coating resin. 
In the present study the functionality of the added unmodified lignin is a mechanical reinforcement 
filler in the formulated coating. Lignin lately is very popular filler in biopolymers, it has good 
mechanical properties, while the raw material is widely available and propose several possibilities 
to modifications. Incorporating lignin as a reinforcement will replace all the conventional fillers 
and extenders in the formulation, without compromising the durability. Due to the polar hydroxyl 
groups, lignin easily blended in polar polymer matrixes. [64]. 
5.4. 
Ceramics for durability and improved mechanical property 
Ceramics are defined as natural or synthetic inorganic, non-metallic, polycrystalline materials, 
prepared from powder form with heat [65]. The chemical bonds the ceramic structures are built 
from are covalent and ionic bonds, stronger than metallic bonds. The chemical structure of 
ceramics results excellent properties, such as high hardness, high melting point and chemical 
inertness. The lack of free electrons makes most ceramics thermal and electrical insulator [66]. 
Although the properties for ceramics cannot be generalised, as it is dependent on the crystal 
structure, one example for an exception that the high temperature superconductive materials based 
on ceramic oxide, or ceramics with piezoelectricity. 
Ceramic coatings are improving the resistance against corrosion, wear, heat, and chemical 
environment on metal surfaces. On the other hand, this high protection against external impacts 
pairs with brittleness, the formed film has low ductility and low tensile strength, it might debond 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
from the substrate, furthermore the ceramic coatings are difficult to repair [67]. The stiffness can 
be eliminated with controlled microstructure. 
For industrial applications aluminium oxide (Al2O3) is very favoured ceramics, due to its inertness, 
thermal stability, mechanical-, corrosion-resistance, excellent electrical insulation, and cheap 
production [68]. Although the practical application as a reinforcement filler has difficulties with 
its dispersion due the particle properties. The alumina particles are very small with high specific 
area; therefore, it tends to agglomerate [69]. 
The α-alumina allotropic used for engineering applications. Alumina nanoparticles used in 
clearcoats, as it is improving the scratch resistance without any aesthetic modification of the 
coating.  
Epoxy matrix composite coatings with inorganic nano fillers has a growing research interest in the 
past 20 years. Nano-silica is a cheap filler that significantly boost the corrosion and abrasion 
resistance, the UV absorbance, without compromising the gloss or the transparency of the coating 
and the adhesion is good for most substrates [70]. Nanoparticles are significantly improving the 
coating properties and have better performance compared to micro sized particles. Silicon-
modified epoxy nanocomposites are reported to have anti-fouling properties as well [71]. 
Silicone-based coatings mechanical fragility can be improved with 
ceramics, stereoscopic polysiloxanes (structure with hyperbranched 
network) with added inorganic core creates a rigid, but tough coating [72]. 
However chemical hybridization required for the combination of the 
organic polymer with the inorganic ceramic materials. 
Overall, ceramic coatings can provide a long-lasting durable coating on 
substrates, however, it is important to note that ceramic coatings are not 
invincible, and they may still be susceptible to damage from heavy 
impacts or chemical exposure.

6.  Summary of the literature study and the hypothesis 
Biofouling is the other major problem besides corrosion for static ship hulls in the port. To 
reduce the necessary frequency of dry-dockings underwater grooming tools has been developed 
for biofouling removal in the past decades. The commercially available coatings are mostly used 
on the ship hulls, which spend long static periods in harbours are the copper ablative ones. The 
environmental concern is serious on these biocides containing antifouling coatings, due to the 
heavy-metal release and its possible accumulation in the water. Therefore, the focus of the 
antifouling coating research is on to develop a durable, yet effective antifouling system, 
preferably without any biocide.  
The objective of this research is to develop and improve antifouling coatings suitable for 
grooming technologies. The coating must be able to withstand the corrosive species and 
abrasions caused by underwater grooming, while also providing some level of flexibility. 
Promising solution is to incorporate silicone with epoxy resin, the coating will create a super-
smooth surface that is resistant to biofouling attachment. The performance of lignin in the 
prepared antifouling coatings as a reinforcement filler will be studied. Other additional fillers 
will be tested in the system like nano zirconium-oxide and nano-aluminium, to see if they 
provide better mechanical resistance against the abrasion of the grooming. 
 
 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
Hypothesis: 
1. The ceramic nanoparticles and the hollow glass bubble spheres will have a reinforcement 
effect on the epoxy-silicone based coatings.  
These compounds act as inert high-performance fillers, enhancing the mechanical resistance 
of the coating system. By incorporating these fillers, the overall durability and strength of the 
coatings are improved, making them more capable of withstanding various mechanical 
stresses and environmental conditions. 
2. The hybrid epoxy-silicone based coating will reduce the required grooming duration to 
remove the biofouling from the surface. 
This is primarily attributed to the low surface energy of the silicone-based foul-release 
coatings (FRCs). Due to their low surface energy, these coatings exhibit reduced adhesion of 
biofouling organisms, making it easier to remove them by the grooming processes. 
3. The number of TABER abrasion cycles will have a correlation with the long-term 
abrasive impact of the grooming technology. 
TABER Rotary Platform Abraser is a widely used testing method for all kind of materials by 
mimicking the real abrasive environment. The results are usually used for predicting the 
lifespan of the product. Therefore, the number of TABER abrasion cycles could be considered 
as an indicator of the long-term abrasive impact that the grooming technology may have on 
the tested materials.

7.2.3. TABER abrasion test 
TABER test is an industrial method for measuring the abrasion resistance of materials, which is 
important to ensure the quality of the product. Furthermore, it is a fast and handy way to provide 
reliable information about the life expectancy of the material. The first appearance of the test was 
in the 1930’s, and quickly became popular. 
The key step of the TABER abrasion test is to set up the instrument according to the life-like 
abrasive environment. Therefore, the results can be correlated to the impact the coating will be 
exposed to, and the long-term performance of the coating can be expected. 
In the coating industry specific standards, such as the ASTM or ISO are used to test the product. 
The standards with their specific set of parameters ensure the reproducibility of the measurement. 
The TABER Rotary Platform Abraser is built up with a vertically rotating turntable, where the 
sample is placed. Two abrasive wheels create the rub-wear action on the specimen, while it is 
rotating. The parameters that the operator can set up on the instrument are the applied normal force, 
the material of the abrasive wheels, the rotation speed, and the number of cycles. 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
The results are usually quantified by weight loss, volume loss, the change in thickness of the 
material or by the TABER Abarsion Index (TAI). Qualitative results are from the visual inspection 
of the surfaces, looking for appearance changes and wear patterns [75].  
The speed was 60 cycles/minute, the CS-17 abrasive wheel was used, the weight load of the 
abrasing arms were the minimal 250 grams on each. 4 measuring points were planned for the total 
1000 cycles after 100; 200; 500 and 1000 cycles. The recorded surface properties were the same 
as for the unexposed groomed panels. The data later was used to examine the performance of the 
formulations and determine the possibility of correlation between the TABER test and the 
grooming process. The vacuum cleaner connected to the TABER were operated at 70%. To remove 
the rest of the abraded debris pressurised air was blown on the samples before the analysis. 
7.3. 
Measuring the change in surface properties 
In this study quantitative and qualitative data were collected through the mechanical tests. 
Roughness, thickness, and the advancing water contact angle (WCA) were measured on the surface 
at the planned stop points (mentioned 7.3.2.; 7.3.3.). Visual inspection was conducted with the 
naked eye as well as with digital microscope to see whether the coating lost its structural integrity 
or not. 
7.3.1. Water contact angle (WCA) 
WCA is the characterization technique, where the wettability of the surface can be quantified. With 
the measured data the result tells the quality of the surface whether it is hydrophobic or hydrophilic.

The relation above is true for the real WCA, when the value is measured among idealistic

completely spherical, no external forces acting on the droplet and the equilibrium between the 
liquid and the solid is perfect.  
The epoxy-silicone hybrid coatings were expected to have lower surface energy, hence higher 
water repellence than the neat epoxy resins. The reason for that is the flexible, linear siloxane 
backbone, which is stable and relatively resistant to intermolecular interactions. 
For the coatings with the incorporated nanoparticle the effect on the WCA was not straightforward, 
as the change in roughness can either increase or decrease the WCA the result, depends on several 
factors, like the nanoparticle size, shape, and concentration as well as the conditions and method 
of the coating preparation. 
Drop Shape Analyzer – DSA30 (manufactured by KRŰSS) was used for determining the WCAs 
of the specimens. On each sample three individual points were inspected by measuring the 
advancing contact angles three times on each point. The initial drop was 8 µl, then the drops were 
manually increased, through the dosing syringe. 
Measuring the advancing angle is a dynamic method, which is more preferred than quantifying the 
static angle on solid surfaces. The measured contact angles are freshly wetted points solid surface,

which are close to each other. Therefore, the errors that usually caused by droplet evaporation and 
local inhomogeneities are minimised [77].

7.4. 
Dry Film Thickness (DFT) 
The thickness of the coatings was measured by Elcometer 355, the instrument has ±1 µm accuracy. 
The magnetic gauge can identify the film thickness of a nonmagnetic coating on a magnetic steel 
substrate, by measuring the change in the magnetic field. The Type 2 (the categorization is defined 
by the Society for Protective Coatings - SSPC) magnetic gauge is a non-destructive method for 
dry film thickness (DFT) determination [79].  
7.5. 
Roughness 
The roughness of the specimens was measured by the KEYENCE VR-3000 profilometer. The non-
contact optical surface profilometer has triple telecentric lenses which allows to take accurate 3D 
shots. The measuring principle is based on the Vertical Scanning Interferometry (VSI), the 
transmitter lens scatter light across the surface. From the surface the reflected light is observed by 
the receiver lens. Different heights have distinct interference patterns and a CMOS 
(Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor) sensor calculate the height and the position of each 
point [80]. 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
 
The surface roughness was measured of the panels before the abrasive test and during, at the 
previously determined stop-points. Two types of unevenness data were collected through the tests. 
One was the arithmetical mean height (Sa) is a parameter which represents the average difference 
in height from the mean plane [81]. The other was the maximum height (Sz), which is equivalent 
sum of the tallest peak and the deepest valley of the investigated area. The accuracy of the latter 
parameter might be altered by scratches, contamination, and measurement noise [82]. Therefore, 
for the evaluation the Sa values were compared.

8.4. 
Correlation between the grooming and the TABER test 
The trend in the change of wettability was different for the two testing method, probably the 
reason was the different mechanism of the abrasion. The wear of the ‘dry-grooming’ were 
predominantly characterised by plowing marks, whereas the TABER wear resulted in scratches 
and cavities on the tested surfaces. 
Wear and abrasion are usually interchangeable terms, nevertheless the ASTM distinct the two by 
definition. Wear defined as "damage to a solid surface (generally involving progressive loss of 
material), caused by the relative motion between that surface and a contacting substance or 
substances". Abrasion on the other hand by the ASTM definition is “the wearing away of any 
part of a material by rubbing against another surface". 
However, the observable material loss most of the time cannot be characterised by only one type 
of wear, the response of the material to the external impact is complex. The parameters of the 
mechanical impact that have a high influence on the level of degradation on the material are the 
following: contact geometry, length of exposure, environmental conditions, material composition 
and hardness, interacting material surfaces, normal force, sliding speed [83]. 
Extrapolating the TABER results is not recommended due to the complexity of the abrasion 
process. The measured values may not exhibit a linear relationship with the number of cycles. 
Therefore, it is essential for result correlation to replicate all the influencing factors as closely as 
possible to the actual abrasion conditions. Numerous factors can be changed in the used TABER 
set up to mimic more precisely the grooming.

The primary difference between the two abrasive conditions was the nature of the abrading 
material. The CS-17 abrading wheels used in the TABER test are made of a harsher material 
resulted a higher mechanical impact, than the nylon bristle brush. For testing organic coatings, 
the CS-10 rubber wheel is recommended to generate a milder abrasive action. 
The speed of the rotating grooming brush was 4 times faster than the speed of the TABER cycles. 
Additionally, the applied force on the specimen was also unequal between the two tests. 
To sum up, the third hypothesis, which said the TABER abrasive test can be correlated to the 
used grooming technology has not been proved. Although, the test still serves a rapid and 
convenient method for conducting preliminary measurements, especially when comparing 
material with wide variety of formulations. In the context of this study the test can be viewed as 
a representation of worst-case scenario that the coating may face. 
9. Conclusion 
Overall, with this study the preliminary measurements on the mechanical resistance of different 
anti-fouling coating formulations can be used for further investigation.  
It was shown by the ‘dry-grooming’ that the abrasion of the nylon bristles has an impact on the 
surface wettability. The change in the surface hydrophobicity might induce the variation in 
fouling rate. Therefore, the grooming duration might change overtime to remove the buildup 
from the surface, even if the frequency of the process remains the same. As the wear is influenced 
by the exposure length of the mechanical impact, the lifespan of the coating might be less than 
the expected. 
From the 4-week of marine exposure and the measured grooming durations the minimum 
frequency for the fouling removal should be bi-weekly. This might be more frequent for different 
places, where year-round climate supports continuous fouling growth. 
The roles of the different compounds on the coating mechanical resistance and surface properties 
were explored. The following conclusions were made based on the measurement results and 
observations: 
• Silicone created a hydrophobic surface, that, in most of the cases retained its low 
wettability.  Contrary to expectations, there was no radical decrease in the required 
grooming duration compared to the same formulations with an epoxy base. Therefore, the 
second hypothesis has not been proven. 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
• Lignin was a dominant filler compared to the nanomaterial in terms of influencing the 
surface roughness and hydrophobicity. However, the reinforcement effect on the structure 
was not significant. 
• Nanoparticles: the mechanical resistance was improved both for the neat epoxy and the 
epoxy-silicone resin. Lower grooming durations were achieved with the nanoceramics. 
• Hollow spheres: the application at higher concentrations, glass bubbles exhibited excellent 
properties for the anti-fouling coating. The high-performance coating demonstrated the 
lowest grooming duration and the highest resilience against abrasion. 
For future studies it would be worthwhile to investigate the long-term effects of underwater 
grooming and marine exposure on the epoxy-silicone formulations that incorporate nanoparticles 
or glass bubbles. To provide additional insights on the mechanical resistance against the 
underwater grooming, periodically measuring the change in surface properties to monitor the 
performance during the exposure time.
