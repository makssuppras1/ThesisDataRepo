Summary
In this thesis, a lumped elemental model is presented and partially mathematically
derived. In particular, two models are presented, the reduced Klippel version and
the full Klippel version.
Both are based on the same equivalent magnetic circuit
presented in Klippels patent[1]. The ﬁrst is the model shown in the patent with fully
detailed derivation of the electrical side, using the assumptions which the model is
based on. The second is the same model without any further assumptions after the
equivalent magnetic circuit.
After the mathematical derivation, measurements were planned and performed
with the intent of analysing the performance of the model. The measurement were
performed in three parts. A laser measurement, meant to be used as a comparative
baseline down the line; A ﬁxed displacement voltage and current measurement, used
for ﬁnding the values needed to estimate all the nonlinearities in the model; A fre-
quency sweep, used as a follow up measurement to conﬁrm the resonance frequency
and a suspicion on the signal generator’s output impedance.
The data collected from the ﬁxed displacement was collected and processed. Re-
sulting in usable data points of measured voltage drop across the inductor and the
derivative of the current. Through these variables a gradient descent optimization
was performed to estimate the values of sx and sk, where sx and sk are components
in a series expansion used to represent the magnetic permeance of the armature. The
optimization was performed with 4 diﬀerent models, diﬀering in whether they used
all frequencies measured, what order the series expansion was of and whether they
used the reduced or full Klippel model.
The results showed a inductor with values that did not match the expected values
and had negative values for some displacements and currents. This was due to the
model and the data processing not taking lossy inductance into account and the result
was seen in the optimization giving inaccurate results.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The balanced-armature receiver (often referred to as BA receiver) is widely known
for its high eﬃciency, high output and miniature size. Due to these properties, this
particular type of loudspeaker is commonly used in hearing aids. There the utilization
of the miniature size and eﬃciency is ideal since the users of hearing aids do not want
to have large uncomfortable ear pieces in all day. Moreover, the device needs to be
capable of producing sound pressure levels over 100dB with minimal distortion. It
is therefore paramount that the devices are designed to perform the best they can
within these parameters. Due to the nonlinearities in the balanced-armature receiver
it is a major challenge to design a device that is small enough while maintaining high
eﬃciency and high output. Often, one element is sacriﬁced in favor of the other. This
is due to the fact that all these properties share correlations with one another; if the
size is reduced then the maximum output is reduced. If the maximum output level is
maintained, then that is usually done at the cost of reduced sound quality at higher
output levels.
The reduction of the sound quality is caused by the nonlinearities in the balanced-
armature receiver. They can be traced to two main components; the geometrical
change in the armature caused by the movement of the armature, and the eﬀect of
magnetic saturation in the armature. Fully understanding the cause and interaction
of these nonlinearities with other functions of the receiver could result in improved
designs and, more importantly, could allow for nonlinear compensation. Nonlinear
compensation is the method of converting the input signal of the receiver in such a
manner that the output can be removed of distortion content. That is to say, the
preprocessing of the input will counteract the nonlinearities of the receiver. This has
already been done successfully for the common moving coil loudspeaker [2], [3]. This
would prove beneﬁcial for the makers of hearing aids who rely on the linearity of the
miniature loudspeaker to provide high quality devices.
Performing nonlinear compensation for the balanced-armature receiver is a chal-
lenging problem. It is further exacerbated by the lack of literature on the nonlin-
earities found in the miniature loudspeaker. This is contrasted with the extensive
documentation that exists on the moving coil loudspeaker [4]–[6]. Perhaps one of
the best available documents on the BA receiver is J. Jensen’s thesis [7], in which J.
Jensen goes through many of the properties of the BA receiver and derives a model to
express the expansive nonlinearities of the device. By the end of the thesis, he shows
that the geometrical nonlinearities are, in fact, dominated by the magnetic saturation,

resulting in compressive nonlinearities. In Klippel’s patent [1], the model derived by
J. Jensen is revised to include the magnetic saturation. Additional work using lumped
element modeling are described in [8]–[11]. Some models use ﬁnite element modeling
[12] and combinations of it with lumped element modeling [13], [14]. In [15] a lumped
multimode modeling through modal analysis is presented. While these methods of
modeling are eﬀective, they do not explore the relationships and causes between the
multiple nonlinearities found in the BA receiver.
In order to get a deeper and more accurate understanding of the nonlinearities
found within the balanced-armature receiver, this thesis follows the model presented
in Klippel’s invention [1]. However, before any nonlinear compensation algorithms
can be derived, the model must ﬁrst be derived and evaluated. In the patent, some
assumptions are made that simplify the math and the full mathematical derivation is
not presented. This thesis therefore starts by deriving the detailed mathematical ex-
pression for every nonlinear component expressed and does so in two parts. The ﬁrst
part is according to the assumptions made in the patent while the second is with-
out them. Following that, measurements are performed that are used to optimize
the model and evaluate it afterwards. The optimization was successfully completed,
however the data used for it did not take lossy inductance of the coil int account.
In turn, the solutions delivered by the optimizations turned inaccurate and no sim-
ulation could be performed. It was, however, shown that through this measurement
methodology, the model needs to include the lossy inductance of the coil to be ac-
curate. Any further analysis into the lossy inductance could not be performed due
to time constraints. If the inductor in the electrical domain had been approximated
with the common L2R2 model[16] and the measured data adjusted, then perhaps the
simulation could have been performed. Ideally, simulation is the last step required for
validating the models before moving on to develop the nonlinear compensation. If,
however, the simulation were to prove the model inaccurate then further correction
is needed before an evaluation should be carried out once more.

2.1
Distortion Measurements
Since one of the most important parts of loudspeakers is how nonlinear they are,
it is pertinent that it can be quantiﬁed in some manner. Distortion measurements
are ways and methods for evaluating how nonlinear the loudspeaker is. A nonlinear
system is any system that is not linear, i.e. does not follow the superposition principle.
The principle states that the system must fulﬁll the two properties of additivity and
homogeneity, shown as:
F(x1 + x2) = F(x1) + F(x2),
(2.1)
aF(x1) = F(ax1),
(2.2)
respectively. Where F is the transfer function of the system, x1 and x2 are two
separate inputs to the system and a is a scalar value. The superposition principle can
then be expressed as
F(ax1 + x2) = aF(x1) + F(x2).
(2.3)
In the case of loudspeakers, it can be noted that if any change in saturation is
apparent in the response, given a change in the level, the rule of additivity is broken.
Similarly, if any cross-terms are created when the system is driven with two inputs,
the rule of homogeneity is broken. The ideal loudspeaker would follow this principle.
Moreover, the function F should only serve as a scalar multiplier; perfectly recreating
the input signal. A loudspeaker, or receiver, will usually not be ideal, although it can
be considered linear in some regions, maintaining minimal nonlinearities. Headroom
is a term used to describe how input level the system can handle while still being
considered linear.
When examining a nonlinear system it is important to be able to evaluate the
nonlinearities with robust methods. In acoustics there are two such methods that
are used to evaluate the amount of nonlinearities generated in an acoustic system.
Those are Harmonic Distortion and Intermodulation Distortion. Given a signal that
a system is driven with, they compare the output powers of the driving frequencies
versus harmonic frequencies and intermodulated frequencies, respectively.

evaluating the amount of harmonic contents exists in a waveform. It is a useful tool
for evaluating the output powers of the fundamental frequency of the driving signal
versus its harmonics found in the output. Figure 2.1 shows two waveforms; the blue
waveform shows a clean sinusoid with no harmonics while the red shows the same

than one source in the red waveform.

Figure 2.2 shows two identical signals in the frequency domain. The blue plot,
again, showing the pure sinusoid while the red plot shows the Fourier transform
of the waveform with added harmonics.
In the frequency domain, the energy in
each frequency bin can be calculated and compared to the bin corresponding to the
fundamental frequency. The method of Total Harmonic Distortion does exactly that.
The calculated THD value in the red curve will therefore be higher than that of the

The THD level of the red curve will be higher, but its exact value will depend on
what deﬁnition is used for the THD.

2.2
State Space Analysis
The concept of a state space analysis is that if a system of any kind is understood,
then given an input and a starting state, all subsequent states can be calculated.
In this context, a state is a collection of variables at a given time t, where those
variables represent diﬀerent properties. In a state space analysis for BA receivers,
those variables can be the current passing through, displacement of and velocity of
the armature reed. If the relationships between input voltage, current, displacement
and velocity are known, then the resulting velocity at any sample can be calculated
through a simulation using the state space analysis. In short, a state space analysis
is the mathematical method for simulating or calculating the response of a system
given some input.
2.2.1
State Space Model
The most essential part of the state space analysis is the state space model. The state
space model is the mathematical model which describes the behavior of a system
through diﬀerential equations for every variable in a state.
When the diﬀerential
equations needed to calculate the derivative of every state variable are known, the
next state can be found. The standard mathematical expression for the state space
model is the following:
dx(t)
dt
= A(t)x(t) + B(t)u(t),
(2.6)
where x(t) is the state vector at a given point t, u(t) is the input into the system
and A and B represent the state space model. Viewing Equation 2.6 it can be seen
that if the present state x(t), input u(t) and the state model variables A and B
are known, then ﬁnding the change in the state can be achieved. This is, of course,
because the derivative of the state vector represents the change in the states.

Optimization - Gradient Descent
One of the most commonly used optimization algorithms is the gradient descent [19],
[20].
It is designed around the ideology that if the gradient of a function being
optimized is known, then for any given data point x(n) a direction and step size can
be calculated to get closer to a local minimum. Thus, the local minimum can be found
through iteratively taking that step in the right direction until the local minimum is
realized.
The gradient descent algorithm is composed of the cost function, the gradient
function, step size calculation and the exit criteria. The cost function is the function
being optimized, it describes the problem in a way that can be solved through min-
imization. One of the most common ways of doing this is the sum of squared error.
Error in a case such as this would be the output of the model versus the expected
output and squaring that will ensure that all cumulative errors are combined without
cancelling one another out. Mathematically, it would be expressed as

One possible drawback of the gradient descent is that it only ﬁnds the local min-
imum. While it is impossible to say if a global minimum is reached, attempts can
be made to increase the chance of ﬁnding it or a similarly good solution. This can
be done in multiple ways, with the most popular ones being grid search and random
search. Grid search employs n nested loops to search for best n-dimensional parame-
ters through ﬁxed values. Although this method will always deliver decent results, it
is unlikely to deliver the best results, given that the search space is detailed enough.
It can also be very time consuming as well as computationally ineﬃcient. Random
search picks a random data point within the search space and evaluates it. While
it risks never ﬁnding a good data point due to being randomly generated, it has a
better possibility of ﬁnding the best data point. It is still, however, more eﬃcient in
achieving approximately the same performance as grid search [21].

2.5
Electromagnetic Effects
In the balanced-armature receiver there are two main eﬀects that need to be under-
stood in this thesis. Those are the hysteresis curve and saturation, which are very
related. While the Eddy current losses are also important for the balanced-armature
receiver, they won’t be taken into account in the model used later on. As such only
the eﬀects of the hysteresis curve and its saturation are explained here. Since the
eﬀects of magnetic hysteresis and saturation has been largely described in [22], [27]
and [23] this section will mainly be used for simple description of their eﬀect and
behavior.
When an electrically generated magnetic ﬁeld passes through a mater which has
internal magnetic ﬁeld it can be attributed to both the generated ﬁeld and the internal
ﬁeld.
Moreover, it is diﬃcult to separate the terms from one another, as all are
measured in Tesla and it has become common practice to refer to the diﬀerent ﬁelds
with diﬀerent variables.
The B-ﬁeld is the total magnetic ﬁeld generated by the
system and the H-ﬁeld, or magnetic ﬁeld strength, is the ﬁeld generated through
electricity. The magnetic ﬁeld due to the internal magnetic ﬁeld of the material is
referred to as magnetization. The relationship between the three ﬁeld properties is
given as
B = µ0(H + M),
(2.23)
where µ0 is the vacuum permeability, H is the H-ﬁeld value and M is the magne-
tization of the material. In ferromagnetic materials such as iron, the magnetization,
M, of the material can be inﬂuenced by the H-ﬁeld and thereby a direct relationship
between the H-ﬁeld and B-ﬁeld can be found. Figure 2.4 shows the relationship be-
tween the H- and B-ﬁelds in a ferromagnetic material. The blue line represents the
anhysteretic curve, it is the external ﬁeld as a function of the magnetic ﬁeld strength
through the material if the system is in a thermodynamic equilibrium. This means
that no change on a macroscopic level and the curve solely being due to the change
magnetic moments, with no energy leaving the system. On either end of the line a
saturation is reached. This happens when the internal magnetic domains in the ma-
terial have a uniform polarity and no further change can be made. Since the H-ﬁeld
can still increase there is a small slope still visible in the saturated region although no
additional change will be caused by the magnetization of the material. The steepness
of the slope in the saturated region shows that the change in material magnetization
has a far larger eﬀect on the magnetic ﬁeld than just the magnetic ﬁeld strength on
its own for ferromagnetic materials.

3.1
The Balanced-Armature Receiver
The balanced-armature receiver is a type of acoustical transducer designed to trans-
late electrical signals to sound pressure. It has a ferromagnetic pin, generally referred
to as the armature reed, which is placed between two permanent magnets. The two
magnets have the same polarization; thus, the ferromagnetic material is pulled in both
directions. Since the reed has stiﬀness it will not easily get pulled to either magnets;
however, the resulting stiﬀness is not nearly as large as the mechanical stiﬀness when
the magnets are in place. There is also a coil wound around the back of the armature
reed and when electricity is sent through, an H-ﬁeld is produced. The H-ﬁeld will
then magnetize the ferromagnetic reed. The change in external B-ﬁeld created in
the pin will cause it to move towards one of the permanent magnets. Which magnet
depends on the polarity of the magnetization of the reed and the permanent magnets.
The movement can therefore be controlled by the electrical signal sent to the receiver.
The reed is connected to a diaphragm, enlarging the surface area of moved air which
causes the pressure waves to generate. Figure 3.1 shows a sliced balanced armature
receiver where the moving iron is the armature reed and the two darker grey blocks

3.2
The Models
In J. Jensens thesis the magnetic reluctance of the armature reed was originally
considered to be very small compared to the eﬀects created by the magnetic pull
on the armature [7].
The nonlinearities in the balanced-armature receiver would
therefore be expansive rather than compressive as is the norm in most receiver and
loudspeaker designs. This would be due to the fact that as the reed gets closer to
the two permanent magnets, the magnetic pull would be stronger; resulting in the
armature reed moving closer to the magnet than intended. A simple sinusoidal input
would therefore result in something closer resembling a saw wave as the peaks of the
waveform are pulled out further. By the end of the thesis, however, Jensen found
that the magnetic saturation of the reed is quite important and seems to dominate
expansive nonlinearities due to the pull.
In an attempt to improve the design, a
resistor is added to the equivalent circuit to represent the magnetic reluctance of the
armature.

3.4
The Full Klippel version
A large portion of the model presented in the reduced Klippel version is based on the
assumption that the nonlinearities of most balanced armature receivers are dominated
by the magnetic saturation. This may not always be the case and could prove the
model ineﬀective in those cases.
The claim is further strengthened by Figure 3.5
which shows multiple THD curves as a function SPL. Therein, some of the curves
show a bump in harmonic levels, followed by a reduction before the slope starts
ascending once more. It is theorized that the ﬁrst bump is due to the expansive non-
linearities of the balanced armature receivers and the reduction happening is due to
the compressive and expansive non-linearities are cancelling one another out before
the saturation becomes the dominant one.
Figure 3.5: Multiple THD curves showing interesting dips in high level THD values.
(Courtesy of Oticon)
In any case, a more detailed study into Klippel’s equivalent circuit without the
assumption in Equation 3.18 is warranted as well as a more detailed examination
of receivers that might be showing behavior as theorized above. Since, the model
without the assumption should be mathematically sound there is no reason not to

CHAPTER 4
Measurements
The measurements performed were the following: a laser measurement performed
with a vibrometer for multiple voltage inputs, a ﬁxed displacement measurement of
current for multiple displacements and multiple driving voltages at diﬀerent frequen-
cies and a frequency sweep. The sweep is used to conﬁrm the resonant frequency
as well as showing the error behind using the chosen signal generator. By ﬁxing the
displacement, the velocity becomes zero and the electrical circuit will be expressed
entirely with a voltage source, resistor and a nonlinear inductor. By collecting diﬀer-
ent data points for multiple displacement and current levels, the nonlinear inductance
can be captured. Moreover, through ﬁnding the nonlinear inductance, the nonlinear
ﬂux function is also found. The whole model can then be simulated from the results.
The laser measurements serve as a comparative measurement. Ideally, a mechanical
measurement where the force and its direction could be measured over all possible
displacements and DC-voltage input would be preferred. The data collected there
could serve to optimize for the mechanical domain in the same way as the electrical
domain and would allow the comparing of results. If the results diﬀer by a large
degree, then it is plausible that there is a signiﬁcant element not encaptured by the
model. However, due to time constraints, this measurement could not be performed.
4.1
Scale Model
The scale model of a balanced-armature receiver built by J. Jensen was used for
validating the model. The scale model can be seen in Figure 4.1. The exact physical
properties and material descriptions can be found in J. Jensen’s thesis [7]. Overall,
the receiver is quite large for a BA receiver, having the dimensions given as (W x H
x D) 75 mm x 80 mm x 90 mm.

One of the major beneﬁts of using the scale model is the size of it and that it
has no diaphragm. This, combined with its size, makes the receiver easier both to

That combined with the fact that no

is warranted to repeat some measurements.
4.2
Laser Measurement
By measuring the displacement and current of the receiver when driven with diﬀer-
ent voltages and frequencies, a baseline can be made. That baseline can be used to
compare simulated models with the receiver, through THD and spectral information
comparison. The baseline can even be used to simulate one variable where the other

In this chapter a detailed overview is written on preparing, processing and optimiza-

the collected data to a format where it can be optimized on. Using properties of

5.1
Data Cleaning and Extraction
In order to make the data collected in the ﬁxeddisplacement usable, two things need
to happen. The noise needs to be ﬁltered out of the signald and the data needs to be
preprocessed into a better format, with the valid data points extracted. A manually
annotated list of frequencies and DC voltage level was recorded for every displacement
in the measurement. That list also contained information on where errors were made,
that way the correct data can be used. The data from each measurement could then
be extracted by using a script that reads the list and all the data exported from the
measurements.
Slicing and Normalizing
For every measurement track, the recording needs to be sliced for ever pulse. This
means that the data was collected from 10 second intervals, every 15 seconds. Since
the Pulse software did not allow for an exact 15 second total pulse length (only allowed
14.9997s) and to reduce the eﬀect of onset transients, the data collection was started
at a slight oﬀset. Starting at 0.35s in every pulse and ending at 9.85s, resultin in the
total usable track length is 9.5 seconds. For 10Hz that translates to 95 cycles that
can be used for evaluation. For the 100Hz and 1kHz, the result is 950 and 9500 cycles,
respectively. The resulting data is then labelled with the corresponding displacement

and measured driving voltage. The measured driving voltage is diﬀerent from set
driving voltage due to high output impedance of the signal generator. Segments that
are labelled with error during the measurement are then discarded.

that the resting position around them would equal zero. The same is also applied to
the voltage measurement for insurance, although no change is expected.
Harmonic Cleaning and Derivatives
The measured data has a lot of noise due to the AC part of the signal being quite small.
This can be combated to some extend by removing all frequencies that are neither
the harmonics of the fundamental driving frequency nor 0Hz, DC voltage. This can
easily be performed using Matlab’s ﬀt function, and removing every frequency bin
that doesn’t correspond to harmonics or DC. In this case, the ﬁrst 10 harmonics are
kept and the frequency bins on either side of it, in case of spectral leakage.
Assuming that the inductance ,as seen from Equation 4.3, is ideal, then the deriva-
tive of current with respect to time is needed for approximating L(x, i). This can also
be done while in the frequency domain by multiplying the values of all frequency
bins with jω where ω is the frequency of corresponding bin in radians and j is the
complex imaginary number. The real part of the rate of current in the time domain
was then recovered though taking the real values of the inverse fast fourier transform
in Matlab.
Single cycle Average
Due to noise in the measurement, there can be some diﬀerence between every cycle in
each measured pulse. In order to mitigate that, a single loop averaging is performed.
The signal is resampled such that one cycle equals the number of samples per second
according to the sampling frequency fs. In this case that means to resample the cycle
length to equal 8192 samples. This is done to ensure that every cycle can be ﬁt in
exactly n many samples, where n is an integer. Otherwise, the sinusoids will not
match when averaged later on. Since for 10Hz, one cycle takes exactly 819.2 samples
this is necessary. Equivalent applies for 100Hz and 1kHz.

In this section an evaluation of the model is performed. Since the simulation could

Preliminary analysis will, however, show that both measurements for the Reduced

constraint optimization or some sort of penalization, it might also imply an inaccuracy
in the model or measurements.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis two models are examined and evaluated. Those models are the reduced
Klippel and full Klippel, both are based on the same equivalent magnetic circuit
presented in Klippels patent[1]. The goal was to measure the performance of the
models through simulation and comparative analysis of the estimated nonlinearities
with measurements. Since the lossy inductance of the coil was not taken into account
the optimization proved inaccurate and a simulation turned impossible. If the lossy
coil had been taken into account when preprocessing the data, the optimization would
have been more accurate. Resulting in a measurement that would have been accurate
for the mechanical domain at least. It would still need to be evaluated, but it would
have been possible to do so.
7.1
Future Work
The models evaluated in this report and the measurement of them provide information
on the BA receiver. Many interesting topics were realized and the further work was
found to be needed in related topics. Those topics are listed in this section to give
the reader an idea of what can be done to further the research presented here.
During the sweep measurement, an interesting relationship between the voltage
and current was observed as a function of driving voltage in both the absolute
impedance level and the angle of it. Whether this was caused by some artifact in
the signal generator or not can easily be conﬁrmed through a secondary sweep mea-
surement. If the relationship persists than a more detailed research into their causes
might result in further understanding of the miniature loudspeaker.
The lossy inductance that the measurement indicated needs further exploration.
Moreover, the models need to be revised to include its eﬀect. After revising the models,
they need to be re-evaluated in some way. If the revised models prove accurate then
the development of a nonlinear compensation algorithm can be started.
The models presented here do not take into account the magnetic hysteresis and
Eddy currents. By using the Gyrator-Capacitor approach, a model could be derived
that is more accurate in its power dissipation and could be adapted to include the
magnetic hysteresis and Eddy currents. If the described model were to be derived it
should result in a more accurate representation of the balanced armature receiver.
The phase diﬀerence between derivative of the current and the measured voltage
drop over the inductor behaved unexpectedly with respect to both magnetization

increased magnetization and decreased frequency. Compared to A. King’s ﬁndings[33]
in which it is stated that the inductor approaches and ideal state when moving further
away from the magnets whereas in the balanced-armature receiver the phase diﬀerence
decreases with increased magnetization. Reduced phase diﬀerence implying a more
ideal state. Further work is needed to analyze the exact behavior observed here.
During data processing it was found that the eﬀective DC resistance of the receiver
