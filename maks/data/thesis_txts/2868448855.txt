Abstract 
Cyclobutanes have seen a massive surge in academic interest over the recent decades, re-
sulting in a multitude of novel synthetic methods and applications. Especially in medicinal 
chemistry, this structural motif has gained a prominent position, with utilization in a multitude 
of development campaigns and (pre)clinical candidates. Even with this increasing interest, 
cyclobutanes are still poorly represented in approved pharmaceuticals, and their applications 
do not fully utilize their unique capabilities. In this project, conditions for an intermolecular 
photochemical [2+2]-cycloaddition between an alkaloid-derived starting material and malei-
mides were developed. The product of this reaction contains both the tropane and succinimide 
scaffolds, both of which are known to be privileged in medicinal chemistry. The development 
included screening of a variety of photocatalysts, solvents, and loadings, ultimately culminat-
ing in optimized conditions using isopropylthioxanthone and 375 nm UV irradiation. The scope 
of this reaction was then explored, affording cyclobutane-containing products with a variety of 
differently substituted maleimides. Lastly, an intramolecular variant of the reaction was per-
formed, resulting in medium-ring-containing products without a cyclobutane. These reactions 
afforded a total of 15 novel photochemically derived products.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Why cyclobutanes? 
The academic interest in both the synthesis and application of cyclobutanes has seen a sig-
nificant increase in recent decades.1–4 In particular research into bioactive cyclobutane-con-
taining compounds, both naturally occurring and synthetic, has come to the forefront. The 
structure has also been touted as an important motif in drug discovery and development, cor-
roborated by there being at least 39 (pre)clinical cyclobutane-containing drug candidates as 
of January 2021.5 This clinical interest is not surprising, as cyclobutane, like other saturated 
cyclic compounds, is a source of sp3 hybridized carbons. This is of great importance as the 
fraction of sp3 hybridized carbons (Fsp3) has been linked with increased clinical success.6,7 
With the correlation being attributed to the 3-dimensionality afforded by sp3 carbons, allowing 
for stronger and more selective interactions with target proteins. 
 
Even with this increase in clinical and academic interest, cyclobutane remains rare in approved 
pharmaceuticals. A database search with ChEMBL8 on the number of approved pharmaceu-
ticals containing a variety of saturated cyclic compounds shows a clear discrepancy between 
cyclobutane and both larger and smaller rings (Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1: Overview of a variety of saturated cyclic compounds and the number of approved pharmaceuticals 
containing them based on numbers from ChEMBL.8 
This is even though medicinal chemists are always on the search for novel structures with 
unique spatial arrangements and functionalization vectors. Here, cyclobutanes of course offer 
a unique set of vectors for utilization as exemplified by comparison with cyclopentane and 
cyclohexane (Figure 2). 
 
Figure 2: 3D structure and measurements of cyclobutane, cyclopentane and cyclohexane.5,9,10 
==PAGE:12==
2 
 
The significant distortion from the optimal tetrahedral bond angle of 109.5° as well as the 
smaller intramolecular C-C cross distance shows that cyclobutane offers a unique functional-
ization vector that can be exploited for novel 3-dimensional connectivity. 
 
This potential, particularly in regard to spirocyclic fusions of 4-membered rings, was also high-
lighted in 2014 by Carreria and Fessard in their review “Four-Membered Ring-Containing Spi-
rocycles: Synthetic Strategies and Opportunities”.4 Herein, they highlight the large number of 
isomers spirofused small ring-containing structures have in comparison with similar larger ring 
structures. This allows for significantly higher structural diversity in compounds with similar 
atom counts and comparable sizes. In recent years an even greater emphasis has been put 
on the benefits of spirocycles in medicinal chemistry due to these benefits.7,11 Spiro-fusion and 
geminal di-substitution are specifically very attractive for cyclobutanes as they result in a re-
duction of ring strain. With the strain energies for unsubstituted cyclobutane and gem-dimethyl 
cyclobutane being 26.3 kcal/mol and 18.2 kcal/mol, respectively, moving it closer to cyclopen-
tane 7.2 kcal/mol, which is generally considered stable.5  
 
Even with the relatively low number of approved pharmaceuticals, the number of (pre)clinical 
tests shows that cyclobutanes have become an increasingly important structure in the medic-
inal chemistry toolbox. With common methods of utilization being well represented in the al-
ready approved pharmaceuticals, such as its employment as the terminal part of a substituent 
group to fill out a binding pocket as in Butorphanol (Figure 3), which is an analgesic most 
commonly used to relieve migraines.12 Beyond this it is also utilized based on its unique prop-
erties relative to other motifs more commonly employed in small-molecule drug design. An 
example of this is Abrocitinib (Figure 3), which is used in the treatment of atopic dermatitis, 
where a cyclobutane is used to rigidify a linker. This is a common utilization of cyclobutanes, 
as the substitution of a flexible alkyl chain can force the molecule into a biologically relevant 
conformation while also reducing the entropy loss upon binding. With both factors contributing 
to increasing the potency of the molecule.5,13 Lastly, in Mirogabalin, used in the treatment of 
diabetic peripheral neuropathic pain, and Carboplatin, a chemotherapeutic alkylating agent, 
where the unique functionalization vectors are used more prominently.14,15 
 
Figure 3: Examples of approved pharmaceuticals containing cyclobutane.5,12–15 
Beyond cyclobutanes uses in approved pharmaceuticals, a variety of other applications have 
been reported in the literature relating to the development of small-molecule drugs. Cyclobu-
tanes have been utilized by Laporte et al.16 in their development of inhibitors for p97 ATPase, 
which is implicated in cancers. Here, they employ a geminal cyclobutyl to introduce a kink into 
an otherwise flexible linker, directing the piperazine into the desirable conformation (Figure 4). 
This, in combination with better utilization of a hydrophobic pocket by the cyclobutyl, resulted 
in a 10-fold increase in potency. 
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
 
Figure 4: p97 inhibitor optimization by Laporte et al.16 
Another utilization of cyclobutanes was demonstrated by researchers from Pfizer.17 They em-
ployed a 1,3-substituted cyclobutane as an isostere for a para-substituted benzene ring in their 
development of a histamine H3 receptor antagonist. The goal of introducing the cyclobutane 
ring was to avoid utilizing a biphenyl motif, seen in the initial HTS hit compound, which has 
known genetic safety issues. The cyclobutane was able to conserve the distance between two 
essential nitrogen atoms while replacing one of the benzene rings in the biphenyl. The general 
scaffold structure was retained throughout development and was also present in the final clin-
ical candidates developed (Figure 5). 
 
Figure 5: Development of histamine H3 receptor antagonist.17 
Beyond the examples outlined above, cyclobutane has been utilized to prevent cis/trans isom-
erization of alkenes and preserve the stereochemistry of cyclic alcohols under physiological 
conditions. Enhanced metabolic stability and increased aqueous solubility have also been 
achieved in some cases when cyclobutane is employed as a geminal-dimethyl isostere or 
when used in linkers.5 
 
Cyclobutanes are also well represented in natural products (NPs), with examples from all ma-
jor classes. These compounds encompass both hetero- and homo-dimers of known NPs, such 
as Grahamine E and Sceptrin, or as structural motifs in unique scaffolds, such as in Phai-
nanoid A and Bielschowskysin (Figure 6).18–20 
 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
 
Figure 6: Examples of cyclobutane containing NPs.18–20 
Grahamine E, as well as four other close analogs, were isolated from Schizanthus grahamii, 
a plant in the nightshade family, and is of special interest as it contains the privileged alkaloid 
scaffold tropane.18 The exact mechanism for its formation is unknown, but it has been hypoth-
esized that the formation of the cyclobutane takes place through photocatalyzed [2+2]-cy-
cloaddition. This is based on the compound only being found in the aerial parts of the plant 
while being absent in the roots. Similar observations have also been made for other cyclobu-
tane-containing alkaloids.18 So far, a naturally occurring enzyme that performs photocatalyzed 
[2+2]-cycloadditions has not yet been identified, with only engineered proteins having been 
reported to do so.18,21  
 
Phainanoid A has shown potent immunosuppressive activity after being isolated from a plant 
used in traditional Chinese medicine.22 Sceptrin, originally isolated from marine sponges, has 
shown potent antibacterial activity, including implementation in developments against drug-
resistant bacteria.23,24 While Bielschowskysin shows anti-tumor activity, exhibiting potent and 
selective cytotoxicity against EKVX non-small cell lung cancer and CAKI-1 renal cancer cells.20 
 
Beyond the relatively sparse representation of cyclobutanes in approved pharmaceuticals, a 
point of note is the stark difference in utilization of the cyclobutane motif when comparing 
medicinal chemistry applications and many cyclobutane-containing NPs. As exemplified by 
the four NPs mentioned previously, cyclobutanes commonly feature as core, structurally de-
fining scaffold elements in larger, complex structures. In Grahamine E and Sceptrin the dis-
tinctive vectors of the cyclobutane define the spatial arrangement of the substituents, while in 
Phainanoid A and Bielschowskysin, the cyclobutane allows for unique connectivity in highly 
==PAGE:15==
5 
 
congested multiring systems. Contrasted with the approved pharmaceuticals, where cyclobu-
tanes rarely feature in the core scaffold. This indicates that there is a latent part of chemical 
space that is utilized by NPs but is still lacking representation in pharmaceuticals.  
1.2 Cyclobutane synthesis 
The large uptick in the scientific focus on cyclobutanes has resulted in the development of a 
wide variety of synthetic methods for their formation. These range from heavily substrate-
specific as in the many reported total syntheses of cyclobutane NPs, to more generally appli-
cable reactions for use in medicinal chemistry, polymer chemistry, and so on. One general 
method is through intramolecular direct ring closure, as exemplified by the alkylative cycliza-
tion by lactam enolates such as reported by Cuvigny et al.25 (Scheme 3). 
 
Scheme 3: Cyclobutane formation by intramolecular ring closing alkylation as reported by Cuvigny et al.25 
Another general method is rearrangement as exemplified by the tandem ring-contractive 1,2-
Wolff rearrangement/ketene addition as reported by Ghosh et al.26 (Scheme 4). 
 
Scheme 4: Cyclobutane formation by Wolff rearrangement as reported by Ghosh et al.26 
Transition metal catalysis is also commonly utilized in cyclobutane formation, with a wide va-
riety of different catalyst systems and substrates reported. One example of this is the enanti-
oselective copper-catalyzed hydroalkylation reported by Wang et al.27 (Scheme 5). 
 
Scheme 5: Copper catalyzed cyclobutane formation as reported by Wang et al.27 
Lastly, the most common and broadly applicable method for the formation of four-membered 
rings is by [2+2]-cycloaddition reactions of two unsaturated species. Cyclobutanes are formed 
if these two unsaturated species are alkenes (Scheme 6).  
 
Scheme 6: Generalized reaction for [2+2]-cycloadditions in formation of cyclobutanes. 
However, as dictated by the Woodward-Hoffmann rules for pericyclic reactions, thermal [2+2] 
cycloadditions of ordinary alkenes are not allowed.28 Instead, photochemical conditions are 
required to perform the reactions, as under these conditions the rules for which reactions are 
allowed are switched. This is best illustrated by comparing the orbital energy diagram when 
mixing the π orbitals of the alkenes (Figure 7). 
 
==PAGE:16==
6 
 
 
Figure 7: Orbital mixing of π bonds in [2+2]-cycloaddition under thermal and photochemical conditions. Produced 
with inspiration from Clayden et al.28 
As seen in the thermal scenario, orbital mixing results in two electrons going down in energy 
while two electrons go up in energy. This provides no net energy change in reaction and hence 
no driving force for bond formation. Instead, when an electron is in an excited state in one of 
the double bonds, three electrons move down in energy while one moves up in energy when 
mixing the orbitals, allowing for bonding to occur.28  
 
As indicated by the name, photochemical reactions utilize light in the excitation of electrons in 
the reagents. By interacting with a photon of the correct energy, an electron can be excited 
from its ground state (S0) into an excited singlet state (Sn). There are multiple mechanisms by 
which the excited electrons can return to the ground state without having participated in a 
reaction. These different mechanisms are best shown in a Jablonski diagram (Figure 8). 
 
Figure 8:  Jablonski diagram. From Edinburgh Instruments29. 
The energy can be released through internal conversion and vibrational relaxation down to 
the lowest excited singlet state (S1). After which vibrational relaxation can continue, or the 
electron can return directly to the ground state by release of a photon through fluorescence. 
Alternatively, the electron can undergo intersystem crossing (ISC), wherein the spin of the 
excited electron is changed to reach the excited triplet state (T1), which is also able to perform 
[2+2]-cycloaddition. The triplet state is longer-lived than S1 due to the requirement of changing 
spin to return to the ground state but can still return through vibrational relaxation or release 
of a photon through phosphorescence. 
==PAGE:17==
7 
 
Even though alkenes can absorb light themselves to facilitate the [2+2] cycloadditions, direct 
excitation of many substrates requires harsh UV irradiation, limiting practicality, safety, and 
functional group tolerance of the substrates. While conjugated alkenes are more easily ex-
cited, the population of excited reactive species is often low, resulting in slow reaction rates.30 
Instead, molecules called photosensitizers or photocatalysts (PCs) are often utilized to obtain 
the longer-lived triplet states. PCs are chosen based on having strong absorption at a specific 
wavelength, a high probability of ISC to their T1, long T1 lifetimes, and a T1 higher than the 
intended substrate.31 The mechanism by which PCs produce substrates in T1 is known as 
Dexter energy transfer (Figure 9). 
 
 
Figure 9: Dexter energy transfer. Produced with inspiration from Strieth-Kalthoff et al.31 
The initial step is an excitation of the PC from S0 into an excited singlet state, after which T1 is 
obtained through ISC. The triplet state energy can then be transferred to the substrate, forming 
the substrate T1 and returning the PC to S0, allowing it to repeat the catalytic cycle. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:18==
8 
 
A multitude of different PCs are known and utilized for different substrates, with organic and 
organometallic variants both being commonplace (Figure 10). 
 
 
Figure 10: Examples of organic and organometallic photocatalysts. 
All of the PCs shown above have triplet state energies in the range of 60-70 kcal/mol.30,32,33 
This is a very useful range, allowing for Dexter energy transfer to many common [2+2]-cy-
cloadditions substrates such as pyrones, styrenes, and maleimides.30 Benzophenone, xan-
thone, and isopropylthioxanthone are all excited utilizing long-wave UV light (315-400 nm), 
while the remaining three are excited with blue light (400-490 nm). Organometallic PCs are 
not limited to iridium complexes, other transition metals are also used, with the most common 
ones being ruthenium and rhodium. 
 
As photochemical reactions have grown in prevalence, research into the design and optimi-
zation of PCs has also been reported. Such as in the work by Elliot et al.34 wherein they report 
the synthesis of nine new thioxanthone derivatives, utilizing different substituents and substi-
tution patterns to alter the triplet state energy (Figure 11). These include multiple analogs 
excited by light in the visible range, as well as PCs with triplet state energies similar to the 
commonly utilized high triplet state energy PC benzophenone. The latter ones being of great 
interest as benzophenone is well known to perform side reactions and often requires high 
catalyst loading.33,34 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
 
Figure 11: Overview of the thioxanthone derivatives synthesized by Elliot et al.34 as well as their absorbance 
spectra. Figure from reference.34 
Enantioselective PCs have also been developed, with multiple examples of both organic and 
organometallic PCs having been reported. These are, however, often substrate specific, rely-
ing on specific intermolecular interactions between substrate and PC. Development has 
mostly been focused on quinolones and pyrones due to their propensity for hydrogen bonding, 
allowing strong complexing with the PC. An example of this is the chiral thioxanthone analog 
reported by Tröster et al.35(Scheme 7). 
 
 
Scheme 7: Enantioselective [2+2]-cycloaddition utilizing thioxanthone derivative. Reproduced from Tröster et 
al.35 
As an alternative to PCs, [2+2]-cycloadditions can also be catalyzed by Lewis acids such as 
the enantioselective boron-based system reported by Brimioulle and Bach36 (Scheme 8). 
 
Scheme 8: Enantioselective Lewis acid catalysed [2+2]-cycloaddition as reported by Brimioulle and Bach.36 
==PAGE:20==
10 
 
Generally, coordination of Lewis acids decreases the energies of the excited states, allowing 
for direct excitation at longer wavelengths and expanding substrate scope. Additionally, Lewis 
acids extend the lifetime of the excited states, increasing reaction probability.31,36 One limita-
tion of Lewis acid catalysis is the large loading generally required, with upwards of 50 mol% 
commonly being required.2,36 
 
Dual catalyst systems utilizing a combination of chiral Lewis acid with a PC have also been 
reported, such as the reaction by Blum et al.37 (Scheme 9). Here coordination of a scandium-
based enantioselective Lewis acid shifts the triplet state energy of the chalcone starting ma-
terial, allowing for excitation by Dexter energy transfer from the low triplet state energy PC 
Ru(bpy)32+. No reaction was seen if either Lewis acid or PC was absent, and the system gave 
good yields and selectivity with significantly lower loading than seen for purely Lewis acid-
based systems. 
 
Scheme 9: Lewis acid/PC dual catalyst system reported by Blum et al.37 
1.3 Tropane and succinimide as scaffold 
Tropane alkaloids are a group of NPs most commonly found in the Solanaceae (nightshade) 
and Erythroxylaceae (coca) families of plants.38 The defining feature of the group is the pres-
ence of an 8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octane as the core scaffold. Compounds carrying substituent 
groups in all positions have been identified, but they are most commonly seen on the nitrogen 
or in the 7-position (Figure 12).38 
 
Figure 12: General tropane scaffold structure. 
Tropane alkaloids are of interest as the scaffold appears in a large number of approved phar-
maceuticals, with the scaffold being privileged for ligand-gated ion channels and G-protein-
coupled receptors (GPCRs). Naturally occurring tropane alkaloids such as atropine and sco-
polamine act as anticholinergic drugs against muscarinic acetylcholine GPCRs (Figure 13), 
while synthetic tropane analogs such as tiotropium bromide and granisetron act on ligand-
gated ion channels.39,40 The receptors and ion channels that interact with tropane-based phar-
maceuticals are dispersed all over the body, including in the central nervous system, and are 
associated with many different biological functions. This has resulted in a wide variety of indi-
cations for tropane-based pharmaceuticals, such as motion sickness, asthma, and Parkin-
son’s disease.41  
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
 
Figure 13: Examples of drugs containing the tropane scaffold.39,40 
Another privileged scaffold found in NPs and approved pharmaceuticals is pyrrolidine-2,5-
dione, more commonly known as succinimide. Some examples of succinimide-containing 
pharmaceuticals are ethosuximide, which is used as an antiepileptic drug, lurasidone, which 
is used as an antipsychotic in the treatment of schizophrenia, and pomalidomide, which is 
applied in the treatment of the cancers multiple myeloma and Kaposi sarcoma (Figure 14).42–
44 Additionally, a large number of succinimide-containing NPs and synthetic analogs are under 
clinical investigation for a wide range of indications, which shows its importance as a scaffold 
in pharmaceutical development.42 
 
Figure 14: Structure of succinimide and approved pharmaceuticals containing the succinimide motif. 42–44 
Cyclobutane-fused succinimides can be formed by [2+2]-cycloadditions of alkenes with ma-
leimides. Examples of these reactions under both catalyst-free conditions and with PCs are 
reported. Most commonly, iridium-based catalysts or thioxanthone and its derivatives are 
used.45,46 The reaction is proposed to go through a 1,4-diradical mechanism (Scheme 10).45,47 
==PAGE:22==
12 
 
 
Scheme 10: Proposed mechanism for [2+2]-cycloaddition of alkenes and maleimides through 1,4-diradical.45,47 
The triplet state of the maleimide is obtained either through ISC from the directly excited singlet 
state or, as shown by Dexter energy transfer from a PC, reaction with alkene then results in 
the formation of the triplet 1,4-diradical, which then, after ISC, can perform ring-closing bond 
formation, affording the bicyclic succinimide/cyclobutane product. 
1.4 Prior work in the group 
Previous PhD student, Ruwei Yao, focused on the synthesis and biological evaluation of a 
pseudo-natural product library that included tropane as one of the core scaffolds. Here tropane 
was utilized as a privileged primary fragment, which was coupled to a variety of secondary 
fragments to produce a library of compounds. The secondary fragments were predominantly 
ones also found in NPs and were combined with the tropane scaffold either by edge fusion or 
as spirocycles to produce a diverse, highly 3-dimensional alkaloid-inspired compound collec-
tion (Figure 15).48 These compounds were then screened against a variety of aminergic 
GPCRs and ion channels, with some of the tropane-based compounds coming up as hits. 
 
Figure 15: Overview of design strategy utilized by Ruwei Yao in synthesis of alkaloid-inspired compound collec-

2 Aims and objectives 
The aim of this project is to design an optimized reaction for cyclobutane formation by photo-
catalyzed [2+2]-cycloaddition of a tropane-derived scaffold and maleimides. In the optimiza-
tion, a variety of PCs, light sources, solvents, and additives will be tested. The starting material 
will be synthesized from commercially available nortropinones utilizing the corresponding Wit-
tig reagents. When the optimal conditions have been identified, the scope of the reaction will 
be explored with differently substituted scaffolds and maleimides. The goal is also to assess 
the viability of intramolecular variants of the reaction. Here the maleimide will be attached to 
the scaffold with an amide linker of varying lengths, resulting in medium-ring-containing, highly 
3-dimensional molecules (Scheme 11).

3.1 Photochemical setups 
Three different setups were utilized for the [2+2] cycloaddition reactions. One of these was a 
blue light reactor with a wavelength of 465 nm, consisting of four 10 W LEDs placed in a 3D-
printed PETG enclosure. The enclosure was equipped with a cooling fan for both the reaction 
vessels and LEDs. The reaction vessels were held in the reactor with special 3D-printed lids, 
which hold the vials above the LEDs. The 2 mL vials used for screening were held approxi-
mately 4 cm above the LEDs, while the 20 mL vials used in scale-ups were held approximately 
2 cm above the LEDs. The light source was placed on top of a stirring plate and was shielded 
utilizing a clinical waste bucket partially lined with aluminum foil. An external cooling fan was 
placed at an opening in the side of the bucket to maintain a constant internal temperature 
(Figure 17). 
 
 
Figure 17: Blue light reactor setup. 
The second reactor was a UV reactor with a wavelength of 375 nm, consisting of eight 10 W 
UV LEDs equipped with a heatsink and a cooling fan. The light source was placed inside a 
repurposed TLC chamber, which had also been equipped with a cooling fan. The TLC cham-
ber was placed on top of a magnetic stir plate to allow for stirring of reactions. Reaction vessels 
were placed at approximately 2.5 cm from the LEDs (Figure 18). This reactor was utilized as 
the UV light source in the entirety of the screening and scope exploration unless otherwise 
specified. 
 
 
Figure 18: UV reactor setup.

3.2 Condition screening 
This section covers the screening of a variety of different conditions for the [2+2]-cycloaddition 
leading up to the finalized conditions utilized in scope exploration. 
3.2.1 Reaction choice and initial reaction conditions 
The outline for this project was loosely inspired by unpublished work performed by a previous 
PhD student of the Laraia group, Mikkel Krell-Jørgensen, who performed some condition 
screening on a similar substrate utilizing a photochemical high-throughput screening platform 
he developed as part of his project. Specifically, the work was focused on a [2+2]-cycloaddition 
of a tropane-based scaffold containing an exocyclic double bond bearing a phenyl group and 
N-methylmaleimide (Scheme 12).50

A variety of PCs, solvents, concentrations, loadings, additives, and reaction times were 
screened, ultimately resulting in the final conditions (Scheme 13). 
 
Scheme 13: Finalized reaction conditions by Mikkel Krell-Jørgensen.50 
Even with the optimized conditions, product was obtained in poor yield and largely as a mixture 
of stereoisomers owing to the presence of four stereocenters in the formed cyclobutane ring. 
Additionally, the part of the product isolated as a single diastereomer contained a significant 
amount of maleimide dimerization product.50 Due to these complications, the scope of the 
reaction was also never investigated. 
 
The idea was to design and optimize a reaction for the same tropane-based scaffold that 
avoided some of these issues. To limit the number of stereoisomers produced, a starting ma-
terial with no terminal substituent was used instead. For the screening, utilization of maleimide 
carrying an aromatic substituent would allow for easy tracking in TLC and LC-MS with UV 
detection. Additionally, it would allow for detection of the dimer side product, which had been 
an issue with the N-methylmaleimide. 
 
The tropane-based starting material that will be the primary substrate of the [2+2]-cycloaddi-

When looking at the combined yields, the majority of the reactions show good to excellent 
yields. For the successful reactions, short reaction times were generally seen (1 to 6 hours), 
with the only exception being (5m) (22 hours). These short reaction times also resulted in 
significant ITX degradation only being observed for (5m). Some degradation was seen in all 
reactions, but this never led to ITX being limiting or complicating purification. All these factors 
combined indicate that the conditions are well optimized for not just the screening substrates 
but also a wide scope of maleimides.    
 
NMR analysis of the major diastereoisomer products showed a trace impurity in 0-10% abun-
dance. The impurity was also seen in all crude NMRs, even the ones where it was not present 
in the final product. These compounds have never been identified in TLC and LC-MS, likely 
due to their low abundance and co-elution with desired products. Furthermore, purification by 
both DCVC and preparative HPLC were unable to separate the impurities from the desired 
product. A diagnostic proton for the presence is seen in 1H-NMR as a signal in the 5.8-5.6 
ppm range, and in higher abundance examples also as peaks in the range corresponding to 
the tropane protons. Analysis of the mixed product of (5d/5d’) where the side product had 
highest abundance showed a trisubstituted alkene carrying a proton which showed H2BC 
coupling to what corresponded to the protons on the bridging carbon in the tropane and a 
COSY signal to the same proton and one additional proton. The alkene proton also had nOe’s 
to multiple other protons present in the 7-membered ring. Lastly, multiple HMBC signals were 
seen for both the alkene carbons from different protons in the tropane. Due to overlapping in 
the NMR and relatively low abundance, full identification was not possible. However, similari-
ties in the NMR to products later obtained in attempts at intramolecular [2+2]-cycloadditions 
(see section 3.5) hinted at their identities. These compounds also contain a tri-substituted 
alkene with similar NMR chemical shifts and coupling patterns to the impurities and were fully 
characterized, for overlaid NMR spectra of (5d/5d’) and (12c), see appendix 7.3. These simi-
larities indicate that the unknown impurities are compounds (5a-n’’) shown in Figure 23.

The formation of (5a-n’’) is hypothesized to be the result of an intramolecular 1,5-hydrogen 
abstraction that takes place instead of cyclobutane ring-closure (for a proposed mechanism 
see section 3.5). This is likely a minor competing pathway in all 1,4-diradical [2+2]-cycloaddi-
tions but is only observed in systems with significant steric hinderance in the ring-closure. This 
mechanism is further supported by the stability testing of (5a) where no change in product 
ratios was seen over time, showing that the side products are not a result of ring-opening, 
which as an example could be the result of a Norrish type II reaction. 
 
Compound (5e) was the only product where dimer could be fully removed. The initial purifica-
tion of (5e) yielded compound in excess of 100% yield, clearly indicating the presence of dimer 
in significant amounts, which was also confirmed by NMR. In general, the dimers were either 
difficult or impossible to track at all in reaction monitoring by TLC and LC-MS, as an example 
the methyl was not observed in either method. Varying levels of precipitate formation of the 
dimer were seen for the reactions, with moderate precipitation observed for N-methyl dimer. 
This, as well as solubility problems in preparing the NMR sample, indicated a large solubility 
difference between product and dimer. A solid-liquid extraction of the isolated compound fol-
lowed by another DCVC purification reduced the dimer impurity in (5e) to 3%. However, be-
cause of the multi-step purification (5e) was only obtained in a moderate yield of 56%. The 
dimer problem could potentially be improved by filtering the reaction mixture prior to column 
purification, however, due to time constraints the reaction was not repeated to evaluate this.

tions was the same. The selectivity seems to at least partially be a result of steric effects, as 
larger substituents resulted in better selectivity for the major product. Due to heavy overlap-
ping in the 1.5 to 2.2 ppm range in all major products, determining the identity of the diastere-
oisomers by contrasting 1H NMR coupling constants utilizing 3D modeling and the Karplus 
equation could not be done. One nOe signal between C13-H and C1-H/C2-H in NOESY spec-

pointing up (exo), as this correlation should not be possible if the maleimide is pointing down

Four of the maleimides tested were unsuccessful. Two of these contained carbonyl substitu-
ents, while the other two were phenyls with electron-donating groups in para-positions. In both 
reactions with the carbonyl-substituted maleimides, full degradation of ITX was seen after two 
to four hours, while little to no conversion of (3) was observed. In both reactions, an additional 
10 mol% ITX was added after full degradation had been observed, but this failed to further 
conversion in both cases. Strong darkening and precipitation were also seen in both reactions, 
potentially hindering penetration of light into the reaction mixture. No product formation was 
observed on TLC in either case, however, for both reactions, LC-MS indicated the presence 
of the desired product. Even though full conversion of (3) was observed in neither reaction 
after >20 hours, purifications were attempted. In both cases, this, however, failed to yield any 
desired product. The rapid degradation of ITX, darkening of solution, and precipitation indicate 
that unwanted side reactions were occurring with only slight formation of the desired product 
at the start of the reaction. This could potentially be remedied by doing the reaction in flow, as 
this would stop the side products from interfering with the desired reaction. 
 
For the para-EDG containing phenyls, neither showed any conversion after >20 hours of re-
action time with almost full degradation of ITX seen in both reactions at this point. An expla-
nation for this could be that the triplet state energies of the maleimides are shifted up by the 
increased electron density in the imide, thereby disallowing Dexter energy transfer from ITX 
to the maleimide. However, as the triplet state energies for these compounds are yet to be 
reported, this would have to be verified utilizing higher triplet state energy PCs such as ben-
zophenone or the thioxanthone derivatives covered in section 1.2. 
 
The three maleimides containing methoxyphenyl substituents were synthesized after the un-
successful reaction with the phenol-substituted maleimide to analyze the electronic effects of 
electron-donating substituents in different positions. This was done according to conditions 
reported by Nikitas et al.52 (Scheme 18).

As shown in Table 4, [2+2]-cycloadditions with both ortho- and meta-methoxy-substituted 
phenyls were successful. These results were expected for the meta-substituted product, as 
the oxygen lone pair cannot be delocalized into the maleimide resonance system, and the 
group instead is inductively withdrawing. Regarding the ortho-substituted product, electroni-
cally no reaction would be expected as seen for the para-substituted. The reactivity seen is 
likely to be explained as a result of steric clashing between the methoxy group and imide 
carbonyls disallowing the correct conformation required for delocalization of the oxygen lone 
pair, which requires coplanarity of the phenyl and maleimide π-systems. 
 
When analyzing the NMR of (5k) two peaks corresponding to the tBu and methoxy were seen 
in the 1H-NMR and the majority of the peaks in the 13C-NMR also showed as two peaks with 
a slight difference in chemical shift. In the 2D experiments all these split peaks had the same 
connectivity indicating that the product was seen in two distinct conformations. This initial 
analysis had been performed in CDCl3 and when changing solvent to DMSO-d6 this splitting 
was still seen albeit less prominent, with the methoxy still clearly split while the tBu showed as 
a broad peak with shouldering. Variable temperature (VT) 1H-NMR was performed to see it 
equilibration of the two compounds could be seen (Figure 26).

In the aromatic region, the proton in the ortho position relative to the methoxy group showed 
peak splitting at lower temperature, which then collapses to a single peak upon heating (Figure

and broadened (Figure 26b). Lastly, the tBu initially showed increased resolution splitting into

These results do conclusively show two equilibrating conformations as the methoxy peaks did 
not converge. The methoxy peaks instead indicate the presence of two atropisomers, which 
would arise from hindered rotation of the aromatic ring. Atropisomers of ortho-substituted aryl 
maleimides have been reported, strengthening the argument that this is what is observed for 
(5k).46,53

As covered in section 1.3, [2+2]-cycloadditions with maleimides are proposed to go through a 
di-radical mechanism. One method by which the observed product could be formed is through 
an intramolecular hydrogen abstraction by the formed radical intermediate that would normally 
perform the ring-closing reaction to give the cyclobutane (Figure 30).

After initial excitation of the maleimide double bond to its excited triplet state by Dexter electron 
transfer from ITX, the excited electron allows for bond formation between the two alkenes, 
resulting in a 1,4-diradical. Then, because of high strain, the normal ring closing is either not 
able to proceed or the rate is significantly lowered, resulting in an intramolecular 1,5-hydrogen 
abstraction taking place, forming the alkene seen in the isolated product. Similar proposed 
mechanisms have been reported by Meyer et al.59 and Graßl et al.60 in attempted intramolec-

were also reported by Yu et al.61 in photodimerization of 2,3-Dimethylmaleimides, which cor-

4 Conclusions and future work 
In conclusion, 15 novel compounds containing both the tropane and succinimide scaffolds 
were synthesized through photochemical reactions. Optimized reaction conditions for the in-
termolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition reaction between a tropane-based scaffold with an unsubsti-
tuted exocyclic alkene and maleimides were developed by screening a variety of photocata-
lysts, solvents, and loadings. These conditions were then used to synthesize 10 cyclobutane-
fused compounds. The major diastereoisomer was isolated in all cases, while the minor dia-
stereoisomer was also isolated for 2 of the 10 reactions. In 8 of the 10 major diastereoisomer 
products, an impurity identified as the non-ring-closed side product was seen. One additional 
set of cyclobutane-fused products was synthesized through initial [2+2]-cycloaddition with ma-
leic anhydride, followed by succinimide formation. This yielded a 4-methoxyphenyl-substituted 
product, the synthesis of which had been unsuccessful by [2+2]-cycloaddition with the corre-
sponding maleimide. Lastly, the optimized conditions were utilized in the [2+2]-cycloaddition 
with the phenyl-substituted alkene variant of the scaffold, affording one diastereoisomer in a 
20:1 ratio. 
 
Three compounds were also synthesized through an intramolecular variant of the reaction. 
Here the conditions were altered to include slow addition of maleimide-coupled starting mate-
rial as a means of hindering oligomerization. The final products obtained from these reactions 
were, however, not the expected cyclobutane-containing compounds, instead being identified 
as the non-ring-closed products, akin to the side product seen for the intermolecular variant. 
These are hypothesized to be formed through an intramolecular 1,5-hydrogen abstraction. 
 
For future work, methods for hindering the formation of non-ring-closed products could be 
explored, like changing solvents or performing reactions at different temperatures. Alterna-
tively, different methods of purification could be attempted, potentially utilizing derivatization 
of the alkene, such as by oxidation, for better separation.  
 
The intermolecular [2+2]-cycloaddition on the maleimides with electron-donating substituents 
could be attempted with higher triplet state energy photocatalysts. This would be interesting 
in further exploring the effects of electron-donating substituents on maleimide triplet state en-
ergies. Here, utilizing high-energy thioxanthone derivatives would likely be optimal, as benzo-
phenone was shown to perform side reactions with (3). 
 
For the intramolecular variants, optimization to further limit oligomerization could lead to in-
creased yields. Addition rate and catalyst loading are two factors that could be explored. The 
initially proposed cyclobutane-containing products could be synthesized by initial [2+2]-cy-
cloaddition followed by intramolecular amide coupling. Expanding the scope to longer linker 
lengths would be interesting, both to see if these would result in higher yields and to see if 
cyclobutane ring closure becomes favorable. Lastly, as the intramolecular reaction yields 
products with the maleimide position corresponding to the minor diastereoisomer, designing 
a reaction with a cleavable linker could be interesting, as it would allow for the selective syn-
thesis of these.
