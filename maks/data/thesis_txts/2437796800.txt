Quantification of Leveling in Conventional Antifouling Coatings 
II 
 
Abstract 
Conventional AF coatings are widely used as the outermost coating layer to prevent biofouling. 
However, it is easy to generate an unsmooth surface texture due to its high solid content. The 
coating surface texture is crucial for coating performance since its influence on frictional resistance, 
anti-fouling properties and aesthetic appearance. A smoother coating surface could be potentially 
achieved by improving leveling performance, which is affected by coating properties and 
application conditions. In this project, the effects of each individual parameter on final leveling 
performance was studied, including average wet film thickness, ripple wavelength, initial viscosity 
and solvent evaporation. Semi-empirical models were developed to quantify the effects of those 
parameters on leveling rate.

Background

Background 
Multi-layers of coatings are applied on ship hulls to control biofouling. Biofouling is defined as 
the undesired settlement and the subsequent growth of flora and fauna on the structure surface 
immersed in seawater. Biofouling on marine vessels has long been a problem to the global shipping 
industry since it increases surface roughness, frictional resistance and reduces the fuel efficiency 
[1] [2]. To control biofouling on ship hulls, two types of fouling control coatings (FCCs) are widely 
used; one is biocides-based antifouling (AF) coatings and the other is fouling release (FR) coatings. 
AF coatings are based on the idea of releasing the toxicant material at ship hull surface to prevent 
biofouling, which is harmful to organism attached. While FR coatings aims to form a low-friction, 
ultra-smooth surface on ship hulls to avoid the attachment of biofouling. 
Most current FCCs coatings are applied mechanically by airless spraying or by hand with a roller 
or brush. After application, it is a common experience that brush marks, striations, or unpleasantly 
orange peel (coating surface looks like orange skin) will be left on the coating surfaces, no matter 
how smooth the substrate surface is, as shown in Figure 1.  
 
Figure 1 Examples of uneven coating surface texture. Left: brush marks; Right: Orange peel [3]. 
 
Comparing with FR coatings, AF coatings owing to the high solid content usually form an uneven 
coating surface, which has negative effects on coating performance. First of all, the surface 
roughness will contribute to extra frictional resistance and thereby increase fuel consumption to 
maintain same sailing speed and more harmful compounds will be released into environment [4]. 
==PAGE:14==
Background 
2 
 
Besides that, when coating surface is rough, the ship hull becomes vulnerable to biofouling and 
attract more microorganisms to attach, which will deteriorate the coating performance and 
accelerate corrosion [1]. Furthermore, the surface structure also affects the aesthetic appearance of 
coatings and determined the sales. Because waviness of coating surface will disturb light reflection 
and give rise to bad optical appearance. Customers unconsciously connect the coating quality with 
visual appearance and draw the conclusion that smooth coating surface equals to high quality [5]. 
Thus, coating surface condition plays an important role in the visual and technical performance of 
AF coatings. 
The formation of the final topography by spray application consists of two stages [6]. The first one 
is the formation of the initial surface structure after application. Generally, the droplets first flow 
together and coalesce after application, which leads to the formation of a very uneven surface 
(Figure 2a). The surface structure at this stage is usually performed as non-uniform thickness with 
irregularities which is like a series of parallel peaks and valleys or orange peels. In the second 
stage, the wavy surface tends to level out driving by surface tension to form a smoother surface 
during drying (Figure 2b). This physical process is called leveling. However, the final surface will 
not be completely smooth. Therefore, smoother coating surface could be potentially achieved by 
optimizing the leveling performance.  
 
Figure 2 Schematic illustration of the formation of the final film topography by spray application [6]. 
 
Owing to the high solid content and spray application, the ultimate appearance of the finish is 
inevitably rough for AF coatings. To optimize the leveling performance of conventional AF 
coatings, it is crucial to investigate the effects of different affecting parameters on leveling 
==PAGE:15==
Background

performance. In this project, an experimental investigation of parameters influencing leveling was 
performed and the effects were quantified based on developed semi-empirical models. A 
commercial AF coating and model coatings based on commercial AF coatings were used in 
experiments. Firstly, the effects of individual parameters (wet film thickness, wavelength, 
viscosity and solvent evaporation rate) on leveling performance were investigated by parameter 
study. Subsequently, transient values of each parameter during leveling were obtained 
experimentally. Finally, the effects of those parameters on leveling performance were quantified 
by two semi-empirical models according to different time span. 
The report consists of three parts. Part I is literature review introducing the development of leveling 
theory. Part II is about the experimental methodology and the modeling strategies. The 
experimental and simulation results are discussed in Part III.

1.  Conventional AF coating systems 
AF coatings are mainly used as the outermost layer of protective coatings on ship hulls to prevent 
the settlement of marine organisms. The protecting strategy of conventional AF coating is to form 
a coating surface, which continually releases biocides into seawater. After immerging, seawater 
will ingress into coatings and dissolve biocides which are embedded in a coating matrix. 
Subsequently, the dissolved biocides are carried out by the back-diffusion of seawater (leaching). 
The lifetime and effectiveness of AF coatings depend on the remaining level of biocides and the 
rate of biocides release (leaching rate), as well as the types of binders used [1] [7] [8]. The thickness 
of the biocide-depleted layer (leaching layer) determines the rate of seawater diffusion and the rate 
of biocides release, which will be retarded by the building-up of leach layer. To control the 
thickness of leach layer, the binder will be released moderately (polishing). This process is 
illustrated in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3 Schematic illustration of the AF coatings working mechanism [1]. 
 
 
==PAGE:20==
Part I: Conventional AF coating systems

Conventional AF coatings are formulated with various compositions serving different functions, 
which includes the following five basic ingredients [9] [10]: 
- 
Binders hold all components together to form a continuous film, control the release rate of 
biocides and guarantee the adhesion on the substrate. Because of the importance of binders, 
coating systems are often named by the corresponding type of binders. 
- 
Pigments give mainly color effects for coatings.  
- 
Solvents as the major part of coating system dissolve the binder, disperse other compounds 
and gives coatings a degree of flowability. 
- 
Biocides are active components to prevent fouling. One of the most commonly used types is 
copper-based compound. 
- 
Additives are an auxiliary part with small amounts added into coating formulation and aim to 
modify coating properties, such as rheology modifiers, dispersing agent, etc. 
The conventional AF coating system studied in this work is a thermoplastic solvent-borne acrylic 
coating, along with cuprous oxide (Cu2O) as active biocides. Acrylic binder was widely used in 
the coating industries owing to its non-wetability, chemical inertness and environmental stability. 
They are mainly prepared through the polymerization of acrylic and methacrylic acid or their 
corresponding esters. The solvent based acrylic coating plays an important role in AF coatings due 
to its enhanced adhesion, high durability and fast drying process. The film formation of 
thermoplastic solvent-borne acrylic coatings relies on solvent evaporation to physically transfer 
the wet film into solid coating without cross-linking reactions [10].  
Another classical binder of conventional AF coatings is rosin. Including rosin into coating 
formulation will improve the coating resistance to cracking and obtain better polishing control. 
Generally, rosin is mainly obtained from the natural exudation of pine and fir trees. Since its 
composition is diverse, it contributes to a relatively unpredictable property of natural rosin-based 
coatings. About 85-90% composition in rosin are acidic materials which contain two double bonds 
and a carboxyl group [1].

2.  The leveling process 
Due to the importance of leveling properties on coating performance, the first investigation of the 
mechanism of leveling can be dated back to the 1920s [11] and various investigations have been 
conducted afterwards. However, owing to the complexity of this problem, the conclusions from 
the previous studies were drawn based on some simplified assumptions, for instance, the 
Newtonian fluid behavior and non-solvent evaporation involvement. The theories about leveling 
mechanism and its influencing factors are still incomplete. The previous works about leveling are 
presented in the following part. 
2.1  Driving forces for leveling 
The occurrence of leveling phenomenon is because that the uneven coating film tends to lower its 
surface energy by reducing its surface area, which results in the migration of wet film from thicker 
part to thinner part. This tendency is also in agreement with the second law of thermodynamics 
[12]. Two driving force mainly discussed in the literature are: 
- 
Surface tension  
 
Figure 4 Illustration of leveling mechanism based on uniform surface tension. 
 
Initially, the widely-understood driving force of leveling is surface tension stated by Smith, 
Orchard and Rhind-Tutt [13]. The mechanism is illustrated in Figure 4 for a sinusoidally 
structured film surface with constant surface tension. The liquid surface curvature leads to the 
formation of capillary force and the appearance of varying capillary pressure (𝑃) at the peaks 
==PAGE:22==
Part I: The leveling process 
10 
 
and valleys according to the Young-Laplace equation. Thus, the leveling is mainly driven by 
the capillary pressure gradients (∆𝑃) caused by surface curvature [14]. 
- 
Transient surface tension gradient 
Afterwards, Overdiep [15] firstly proposed the formation of transient surface tension gradient 
due to non-uniform solvent evaporation would give effect on the formation of final surface 
texture. Since the solvent evaporation rate is related to the diffusion rate of solvent in the 
coating film. For a coating film, a slight thickness difference between thinner and thicker part 
leads to different solvent evaporation rate. The peak of ripples (thicker part) will obtain lower 
solvent evaporation rate than the valley. Subsequently, the solid content at the peak will be 
lower than the one at valley, which leads to lower surface tension near peaks. Notice that the 
surface tension rises with the increasing solid content due to solvent evaporation. Thus, the 
surface tension gradient was formed. Thus, the development of transient surface tension 
gradient due to non-uniform solvent evaporation will force coating flow from lower surface 
tension region towards higher surface tension region, which gives effect on the formation of 
final structure and promotes leveling [12] [16]. This procedure is illustrated in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5 Schematic of temporary surface tension gradient formation on the coating surface. 
 
2.2  Leveling theory for Newtonian flow 
In 1931, Waring [11], who is known as the first to study the leveling process, raised an idea that 
the yield stress was the major factor to determine the leveling performance. Additionally, owing 
to the influence of yield stress, the leveling process could be ceased when the surface tension is

2.3  Leveling theory about non-Newtonian flow 
The analytical solution of leveling procedure mentioned above is only applicable for idealized 
cases with Newtonian materials. Numerical simulation based on lubrication theory and a time-
dependent solution of Navier-Stokes equations were widely used. When the leveling investigation 
consider the influence of non-Newtonian behavior, solvent evaporation and irregular surface 
topography.  The simplified assumption is that the thin fluid film has both longer wavelength and 
==PAGE:26==
Part I: The leveling process 
14 
 
smaller amplitude compared to the average film thickness. Based on these assumptions, various 
numerical models have been developed and included different effects of affecting parameters on 
leveling performance, such as, gravity, surface tension, surface tension gradients and rheological 
properties of non-Newtonian coatings [5]. Each numerical model discussed below takes one or a 
few effects into consideration.  
Overdiep [15] developed a simple yet effective model for a coating drying process including the 
effect of surface tension gradient. Later, Wilson [21] continued working on the analysis and 
generalization about this model by using Overdiep experimental results on a planar substrate. It 
was found that the experimental results were in good agreement with the numerical calculation. A 
mathematical model including surface tension, viscosity, solvent diffusivity and solvent 
evaporation was generated by Howison [16] and his coworkers based on Overdiep equation and 
lubrication model for the coating system with non-volatile resin and volatile solvent. Since coating 
has non-Newtonian property, Cohu et al. [22] used five rheological parameters to study leveling 
viscosity and developed a numerical model for residual amplitude calculation of thixotropic 
coatings, which was in a good agreement with their experimental data. However, the lack of a 
solvent loss term made it still not complete. Schwartz [23] and his coworkers derived a 
mathematical model for actual non-Newtonian architecture coatings, which included not only 
shear-thinning rheology but also the thixotropic property.  
2.4  Conclusions about leveling theory development 
During the development of leveling theory, various inevitable effects have been taken into 
consideration in the previous investigation, such as, surface tension, transient surface tension 
gradient caused by non-uniform solvent evaporation, gravity, thixotropic property. Since leveling 
behavior is the results of their interplay effects and thereby this topic is very complex, it is not 
surprising that only a few effects have been considered in each theory and the investigation of 
leveling is still not complete. Nowadays, the Orchard equation is still widely used as a fundamental 
equation in the investigation of leveling performance.

3.  Affecting factors of leveling and the corresponding analytic methods 
Along the development of leveling theory, the importance of coating flow and coating properties 
has been recognized. Leveling performance is influenced by several factors, for instance, coating 
rheological properties, the initial wet film geometry and various applying conditions [5]. The 
effects of different factors and their corresponding analytic methods are discussed in the following 
part. 
3.1  Coating rheology 
Rheology is an essential physical property of coatings. It is the science of how materials flow and 
deform when subjected to external forces [24] [25]. For a commercial coating, its rheological 
behavior can be used effectively to predict coating performance, for instance, coating viscosity 
determines coating performance after spray application. The following parameters are commonly 
involved in rheological studies [24]. 
- 
Shear stress, an external force, leads to the deformation of material. Various shear stresses 
can be applied to the material in different processes, for instance, application, leveling and 
sagging process. 
- 
Shear rate is defined as the rate of change of strain against time. The materials will be 
subjected to significantly different shear rate during application and drying. Figure 8 shows 
the corresponding shear rate of diverse processes. 
- 
Viscosity is a widely used and well-understood rheological term. It describes the flow 
resistance of materials. Generally, viscosity is a function of shear rate, temperature, time and 
pressure. 
Furthermore, several specific terms are defined to characterize a few complex coating rheological 
behaviors [24]. For instance, ‘Shear thinning or shear thickening’ is defined for the condition 
when the coating is subjected to an increasing shear rate, coating viscosity will decrease or increase, 
correspondingly. ‘Thixotropic’ effect of coatings describes a time-dependent viscosity behavior. 
It is a common experience that the coating becomes thinner with stir and the coating rebuilds to its 
original viscosity after standing. Technically, coating viscosity decreases under steady shearing 
==PAGE:28==
Part I: Affecting factors of leveling and the corresponding analytic methods 
16 
 
and then recover to the initial value when the applied shear force is removed. This property 
guarantees an easy applicability and prevents the coating from sagging. 
 
Figure 8 Typical shear rate ranges encountered during common process in coating application and drying 
[26]. 
 
In the article [28], various rheological parameters have been correlated to leveling behavior of 
coatings. Due to the complexity of this topic, coating viscosity behavior will be discussed in the 
following part to elucidate the rheology influence on leveling performance. 
3.2  Effects of coating viscosity 
Coating viscosity is known as the flow-retarding parameter and is one of crucial coating properties 
in leveling investigation. A high viscosity will significantly retard the flowability of wet films and 
result in poor leveling performance, which is in an agreement with Orchard’s theory [12]. In 
another words, low viscosity ideally leads to a better coating performance. However, in practice, 
the low viscosity unfortunately results in undesired sagging problem, which also gives negative 
effect on the smoothness of coating surface. Thus, the focus should be on optimizing viscosity to 
improve leveling behavior without causing sagging problem. 
==PAGE:29==
Part I: Affecting factors of leveling and the corresponding analytic methods 
17 
 
Furthermore, most coatings are non-Newtonian material and its viscosity will be affected by the 
appearance of following phenomenon during drying, 
- 
Solvent evaporation: The solid concentration will be increased during solvent evaporation, 
which will give rise to the increase of viscosity. 
- 
Molecular entanglement: Due to the crosslinking reaction, molecular starts to entangle with 
each other along coating curing procedure, which will also build up coating viscosity. 
- 
Thixotropic recovery: Most coating materials are thixotropic materials and viscosity will 
recover after removing the high shear rate during application process. 
The increasing rate of viscosity also plays an important role in leveling performance. If the wet 
film becomes too viscous in a short time, the film fails to level out completely and the leveling 
process will be ceased by higher viscosity. The effect of viscosity increasing rate was proved by 
the study of Clifford [29], he found out that the coating sample with high viscosity increasing rate 
exhibited worse leveling performance than the counterpart based on experimental results. 
Due to the importance of the effect of viscosity on leveling performance, various attempts have 
been performed to study the evolution of viscosity against time and transient viscosity during 
leveling by means of rheometer. 
- 
Step-shear method 
In 1972, Dodge [30] firstly emphasized the best way to predict the viscosity behavior during 
leveling was closely simulating the application experience of coatings [14]. The dynamic viscosity 
profile (evaluation of viscosity) is generated by using different shear rate to simulate the shear rate 
applied to film during leveling and application process. This method consists of two stages with 
different shear rates, a higher shear rate (around 104 s-1) stage and a lower shear rate (around 10-1 
to 10-3 s-1) stage, representing the application process and the leveling procedure, respectively. 
Afterwards, in order to consider the effect of shear rate of loading sample, the three interval 
thixotropy test (3ITT) was developed [28], which consists of three stages as the name suggested. 
The low shear, high shear and low shear intervals of this method correspond to the sample loading, 
application and leveling process. An example of how coating viscosity build-up measured by using 
this method is exhibited in Figure 9. Coating structure is destroyed and viscosity forms a lower 
plateau under the high shear rate. After removing the high shear rate, the coating structure and 
==PAGE:30==

18 
 
viscosity will recover under a low shear rate. Although this method simulates very closely to the 
actual condition, it still fails to take the effect of solvent evaporation into account, which has 
significant effect on the evolution of viscosity.  
 
Figure 9 Evolution of viscosity of a waterborne coating sample in 3ITT [28]. 
 
- 
Measurement based on the solid content 
The viscosity build-up during leveling can also be attributed to solvent evaporation for physically 
curing coating. Thus, it is possible to measure the viscosity of coating samples with different 
solvent contents to study the viscosity evolution. The mass loss of coated panel during drying 
process is carefully measured after different flash-off time, which reflects the amount of solvent 
evaporation and residual solid content. Based on this information, subsequently, coating samples 
with different solid contents are generated corresponding to transient coating conditions during 
leveling are generated. The transient viscosities at different time are obtained by measuring the 
viscosity of each corresponding sample. The dynamic viscosity profile is indirectly acquired by 
plotting the viscosity of different samples against the time. 
The major shortage of two methods mentioned above is the difficulty in providing the precise shear 
rate applied to coating sample simulating leveling process during measurements and only an 
approximate range has been stated in literature.

3.3  Effects of surface tension 
As the main driving force of leveling process, surface tension is reasonable to earn more attention 
in the investigation history. According to Orchard equation, a rise of surface tension will mildly 
increase the leveling rate. That is to say, higher surface tension results in faster leveling process. 
While raising surface tension will unfortunately bring problems to the wetting of substrate. Thus, 
an appropriate value of surface tension for leveling should also be a pursuit in future investigation.  
In the previous investigation, the optical tensiometer has been widely used for liquid surface 
tension measurement. A drop of objective liquid is generated by a probe, as shown in Figure 10 
(Left). Surface tension of objective sample can be calculated by fitting the droplet profile with the 
theoretical Young-Laplace equation through analysis software. The most distinctive feature of this 
method is only a small amount of sample needed for the measurement and this technology is 
suitable for the coating sample with better flowability. Seeler et al. [5] used this technology to 
study the evolution of surface tension by measuring coating sample scraping off from primed wet 
film after different flash-off time. Another surface tension measurement used in literature is 
directly measuring the tangent angle at the three-phase contact point on a sessile droplet, as shown 
in Figure 10 (Right). Measuring the contact angle of distilled water on targeted film surface and 
the surface tension of film will be calculated by analysis software [31]. This method is able to 
measure the surface tension of coating film with higher initial viscosity which has a problem to 
generate a pendant droplet.

Although surface tension is the main driving force for leveling phenomenon, surface tension will 
only increase by a few percent at most, which has been proved by experimental work about the 
relationship between surface tension and the binder concentration [15]. A recent work about 
coating surface tension during leveling also showed a good agreement with it [5]. The surface 
tension of UV curable coating studied in their work showed a slight increase (from 32 to 36 mN/m) 
in first 100 s and then held to a stable value during solvent evaporation. Thus, it is reasonable to 
assume the surface tension as a constant in the previous investigation. 
3.4  Effects of coating surface geometry 
According to the previous investigation, the leveling performance is highly influenced by initial 
coating surface geometry, for instance, average film thickness and initial wavelength, which are 
with the third order and the fourth order respectively in Orchard equation (Eq. 6). Based on 
Orchard’s conclusion, increasing film thickness and decreasing wavelength accelerate the leveling 
rate.  
The evolution of film thickness can be indirectly estimated based on recording the mass loss during 
solvent evaporation [33]. Assuming that the density of solvent blend was approximately same with 
the density of binder (film density is a constant value during solvent evaporation) and the film 
thickness loss was exclusively due to the mass loss of solvent. Thus, the shrink of film thickness 
is able to be calculated based on the total volume change of wet film. 
Furthermore, advances in instrumentation allow instantaneous observation of coating surface 
texture and subsequently, the film morphology has been studied in a quantified aspect during 
drying. Nowadays, the non-contact optical profilometer is widely used to scan the surface 
geometry and observe the shrink of film thickness without longer time consumption as the 
conventional contact equipment [5]. Figure 11 shows the working mechanism of an optical 
profilometer. Besides that, the atomic force microscope AFM can also be used to characterize the 
coating surface geometry [31]. Figure 12 is an example of AFM measurement of coating surface.

3.5  Other factors 
Except for the widely studied parameters mentioned above, other essential and complex affecting 
parameters are going to be discussed, for instance, the solvent evaporation, the droplet size, the 
gravity and applying conditions. 
- 
Solvent evaporation 
Solvent evaporation is always accompanied by a simultaneous change of solid content, average 
film thickness, surface tension and viscosity [5]. An inappropriate solvent evaporation rate might 
cause a rapid increase of viscosity, which will highly retard leveling performance. Besides, the 
appearance of non-uniform solvent evaporation rate on coating surface results in the formation of 
temporary surface tension gradient. It may leave negative effect on leveling performance as the 
unusual behavior of alkyd coatings by Overdiep [15]. 
- 
Droplet size and distribution 
It was reported that smaller droplet size will lead to smoother film surface and the more even 
droplet size distribution, the flatter surface structure will be generated [5]. 
- 
Gravity 
In the previous investigation, the effect of gravity on leveling performance was negligible when 
the coating had a thinner film thickness. Since Smith and his coworkers [13] found there was no 
evident difference of brush marks among the testing panels left on the wall, on the floor or on the 
ceiling. However, the gravity does have effect on the instability of liquid film and affect sagging 
performance. Thus, when studying the leveling performance of high film thickness, the effect of 
gravity should be considered. 
- 
Application conditions 
In addition to coating properties, the application condition also leaves the effect on leveling 
performance. For instance, the temperature, the higher temperature will accelerate the rate of 
solvent evaporation and curing reaction, which leads to a rapid viscosity increasing rate and 
compromises the leveling effect. Furthermore, the initial surface smoothness is also determined by 
the application method, such as airless spraying always leaves unsmooth coating surface (orange 
peel). What’s more, the weather condition, i.e. humidity, UV radiation, should also be taken into 
==PAGE:35==
Part I: Affecting factors of leveling and the corresponding analytic methods 
23 
 
account for the further investigation about leveling performance. Besides that, the surface 
condition of substrate also influences the leveling performance by affecting the initial surface 
topography of coating film. 
According to the discussions above, leveling is a complex process and the final leveling 
performance is affected by the complex interplay of coating rheology, surface tension, applying 
conditions and substrate geometry as well. Besides, owing to the non-Newtonian property of 
coatings, it is difficult to predict the precise value of shear rates for leveling. Thus, owing to the 
complication of this problem, it is reasonable that the past investigations failed to take all affecting 
factors into account and draw a complete conclusion about correlation between coating properties 
and leveling performance. In this report, effects of average film thickness, viscosity, wavelength 
and solvent evaporation on leveling performance are studied.

4.  Experimental approaches for previous leveling investigations 
The traditional leveling measurement is based on visual evaluation and the leveling performance 
is susceptible to be operator-dependent. In the previous investigations, various special blades and 
corresponding evaluation standards have been designed for laboratory practical studies of leveling 
by means of evaluation the fluidity of coating to level out [35]. For instance, NYPC leveling test 
blades (ASTM D 2801) shown in Figure 13a, which is a U-shaped film applicator with pairs of 
narrow notches with different clearances. The leveling performance was evaluated based on the 
merged degree of adjacent coating stripes according to the corresponding standard. While this 
method has a problem that coating sample was subject to different shear rate when passing through 
the notches with different clearances. Afterwards, the improved Leneta leveling test method base 
on a grooved drawdown rod (see Figure 13b) was developed and widely used for investigation 
[28]. Although the measuring methods mentioned above are convenient and provide fast analysis 
about leveling performance of a freshly applied coating, the leveling performance can only be 
studied in a qualitative aspect and they fail to explain the effect of different affecting parameters.

aspect. In 1995, Cohu [22] used a laser displacement transducer to study experimentally leveling 
performance experimentally and verify his leveling model including the thixotropic property. After 
application by rolling cylinder applicator on a horizontal panel, ripples appeared on the coating 
surface. Subsequently, a displacement transducer was placed on the top of the film to image

In Peter and his coworkers’ work [33], leveling behavior and evolution of surface texture of 
automotive coatings was studied by a non-contact profilometer (Veeko Wyko NT3300) and 
dynamic viscosity measurement. In order to generate uneven coating surface and magnify leveling 
performance, after applying clearcoat on the primed substrate, a 2.5cm linear notch (as shown in

(caused by notch generation) resulted in lower viscosity during this process. Furthermore, only 
one objective location was studied for leveling performance in their work, the limited sample 
amounts failed to avoid the influence of uncertainty. 
Additionally, the experiment material was clearcoats of automotive coating system, which surface 
structure was mainly influenced by curing temperature. The drying process was conducted in a 
high temperature with different temperature increasing rates. Thus, this method might not be 
representative for leveling study of conventional AF coatings.

4.3  Strategy 3: Combination of numerical simulation and experiments 
The method used by Fabian [5] to investigate leveling was a combination of numerical simulation 
and experimental measurement. The main superiority of numerical simulation comparing with 
analytical calculation based on theoretical is that leveling performance of coating during solvent 
evaporation with irregular surface topography can be predicted. While, for the analytical 
calculation, only the leveling performance with sinusoidal structure is able to be analyzed. 
The leveling simulation was performed by using the numerical simulation program provided by 
Fraunhofer IPA. The main program is able to predict the leveling performance and calculate the 
3D or 2D coating surface geometry. The simulation consists of three main steps. Firstly, the initial 
topography is uploaded by the characteristic waviness parameter, which is estimated by the 
superposition of droplets with a defined droplet size distribution. Secondly, the changing tendency 
of coating physical properties with time is uploaded to obtain the information that initial geometry 
levels with the time-dependent physical properties. During leveling process, the film thickness is 
slightly decreased due to the solvent evaporation which was also included in the simulation. The 
corresponding experimental measurement will be conducted to provide the changing profiles of 
coating physical properties against time. Lastly, using a virtual stripe projection obtains the final 
topography. Figure 17 exhibits the simulation sequence.

Part I: Conclusions 
To prevent biofouling, AF coatings are applied on ship hulls, which are easy to form a relative 
rough coating surface. The rough coating surface will not only influence the visual appearance but 
also increase frictional resistance, which will affect coating performance and decrease fuel 
efficiency. Thus, it is meaningful to generate a smooth coating surface after application. Leveling 
is a crucial phenomenon for coating surface structure formation, which is influenced by the 
interplay of coating properties and various application conditions.  
Based on the literature study, the experimental methodology of this work is initially generated. It 
will mainly focus on the influence of average film thickness, ripple wavelength and leveling 
viscosity on leveling performance along solvent evaporation. Although surface tension is the 
driving force of leveling, it was found the changing tendency was not notable comparing with 
other parameters. Thus, surface tension during solvent evaporation is assumed as a constant. The 
investigation of leveling in this work consists of two parts. One is qualitative parameter study to 
investigate the effects of various parameters on leveling with the assumption that all parameters 
will not change with solvent evaporation. Another one is the quantitative analysis about the 
evolution of average film thickness, wavelength and leveling viscosity during leveling. Combining 
with the evolution of surface texture recorded by a 3D profilometer, the influence of each 
parameter along time on leveling will be quantified.

Based on the literature study, the leveling performance of conventional AF coating is going to be 
investigated by conducting experiments. In this work, first of all, the effects of individual 
parameters on leveling performance were investigated, including wet film thickness, wavelength, 
initial viscosity and solvent evaporation rate. The leveling performance was evaluated by the 
change of waviness parameter with time recorded by a 3D profilometer. Since a fast leveling period 
was found to be few minutes after application, the dynamic changes of coating physical properties 
were studied by means of a 3D profilometer and an advanced rheometer. Based on the Orchard 
equation, two modeling strategies were developed using experimental data to describe the effects 
of different parameters on final leveling performance. The experimental methodology, equipment 
and modeling strategies are presented in this part.

6.3  Solvent evaporation experiment 
The coating sample was directly applied on a 15cm×10cm pre-weighted acrylic panel using the 
automatic coating application system. Subsequently, the panel was placed on a precision balance 
(ENTRIS623I-1S from Sartorius with an accuracy of 0.001 g) and the weight loss was carefully 
recorded regularly in the next four hours. Based on the solvent evaporation curve, the residual 
solvent content (wt%) in the coating after different flash-off times was calculated.  
6.4  Viscosity measurement 
Using conventional step-test measurement alone failed to account for the influence of solvent 
evaporation on the increase of viscosity. The solvent evaporation effect is reflected by the 
remaining solvent content, which is correlated to the flash-off time. 
Therefore, an indirect method was used in this work to track the dynamic viscosity during leveling 
procedure. Through solvent evaporation experiment, residual solvent content (wt%) after different 
flash-off time could be obtained. Based on this information, coating samples with different solvent 
contents were prepared to represent conditions corresponding after different flash-off time. 
Subsequently, the viscosities of those coating samples were measured by means of a DHR-2 
rheometer as shown in Figure 23. A flat plate with a diameter of 40 mm was used. The unexpected 
solvent evaporation during measurement was avoided by using the solvent trap cover. By means 
of the rheometer, the viscosity of coatings in can was measured by single flow peak hold test (shear 
rate of 117.4 s-1) and the transient leveling viscosity was measured by two steps flow peak hold 
test as following statement.  
In the first step, a constant high shear rate (1000 s-1) was applied to coating sample, which 
simulated the coating application process. Following, a constant low shear rate representing the 
leveling process was applied. The used high shear rate of 1000 s-1 is a technical value of shear rate 
by brush application [5]. However, no precise value of shear rate representing leveling process has 
been stated in the literature, only an approximate range between 10-1 s-1 and 10-2 s-1. Therefore, for 
the low shear rate step, shear rates of 10-1 s-1 and 10-2 s-1 were both used. The leveling viscosity 
values were taken at the corresponding times from the viscosity curve.

8.  Discussion of uncertainties and assumptions 
Since the real system is too complex to study thoroughly, the analysis in this project was based on 
some assumptions and simplifications. Besides, some measurements are relied on the available 
technologies and equipment, which may lead to some uncertainties on the experimental results.  
• 
Uncertainties analysis 
The 3D profilometer and automatic coating application system are two separated systems, which 
should be switched on manually. It is difficult to promise that the time interval between coating 
application and scan of coating surface are same in every experiment. Even though there is only 
the difference of a few seconds, waviness will be largely different owing to the fast leveling rate. 
Besides, since the magnitude of waviness is quite small (micrometer), a slight deviation on coating 
surface will be reflected by a huge difference on waviness value, especially when the coating 
samples with lower viscosity were used. Thus, several repeating experiments were conducted to 
reduce the uncertainties caused by experimental measurement and the average value of a few 
replicates were used to generate conclusions, which is aimed to reduce the influence of 
uncertainties. The standard deviation of waviness was calculated as shown in Appendix B.  
Furthermore, when studying the dynamic change of film thickness, it is difficult to obtain the initial 
film thickness just after application. Because the panel after application should be placed into a 
specific place and the profilometer recording needs a few seconds to launch. Thus, the plot of film 
thickness change against time started around 30 seconds after application. In addition, the value of 
film thickness was obtained by the manually measurement. Thus, an average value of film 
thickness was used to diminish the uncertainty. 
• 
Simplified assumptions 
Since leveling is a complex physical process and influenced by various factors, a few assumptions 
were made to simplify the experiments and data treatment. 
- 
Changing ratio of waviness was used to express leveling rate instead of the changing ratio of 
amplitude. 
==PAGE:64==
Part II: Discussion of uncertainties and assumptions 
52 
 
- 
The uneven coating surface caused by airless spray application was simulated by using spiral 
coating applicators with different dimensions. 
- 
In the parameter study experiment, the different final leveling performance in a series of 
experiments was only caused by the influence of an aimed individual parameter. For instance, 
in the experiment about the effect of film thickness, the difference in final surface waviness 
was elucidated by the different initial wet film thicknesses and the influences of other 
parameters were negligible. While, in practice, the final leveling performance is affected by 
the interplay of all affecting parameters. 
- 
All affecting parameters were assumed as constant after 11 mins flash-off time in numerical 
model development.

reasons. It may be due to the measurement delay of first point caused by equipment. The first data 
represented recording start time rather than beginning time of leveling (application finishing time).

and first data recording. For 500 μm film thickness, which has better leveling effect, it might obtain 
a smoother film surface than rest two groups in a same time interval. Thus, 500 μm experiment 
had the lowest initial Wa value (see first recording point). Besides, another possible reason might 
be attributed to different starting times for automatic coating application system caused, which 
might be a few seconds difference. However, the leveling performance at initial several seconds 
after application is able to leave a significant effect on surface waviness, owing to the high leveling 
rate.

9.3 Effects of initial viscosities 
The viscosity influence on leveling performance is presented in Figure 33. The final waviness 
increases with viscosity, which proves that leveling performance is retarded by increasing viscosity. 
Since the higher value of coating viscosity results in worse fluidity of wet film, which plays an 
important role in leveling performance. In addition, the final Wa value of different experiments 
did not drop linearly with the decrease of viscosity. Surface waviness was more sensitive to the 
change of viscosity when the value of viscosity was lower than 2.3 Pas, and the influence of 
viscosity on Wa was not evident when viscosity values were in the range of 4.0 Pas to 2.2 Pas.  
Comparing results from experiments with different initial viscosities, all of them have a similar 
changing tendency of waviness except the experiment with viscosity of 8.56 Pas. For the coating 
sample with the highest viscosity (8.56 Pas), its surface waviness kept decreasing during drying 
process, because the fluidity of this coating sample was largely limited by the relatively lower 
initial solvent content (high viscosity).

Conclusions

12.  Conclusions 
The effects of affecting parameters on leveling performance of conventional AF coatings have 
been studied in this project. A novel combination of optical 3D profilometer with automatic 
coating application system was used to record the change of coating surface geometry immediately 
after application. This method largely diminishes the uncertainty resulted from human operation 
during application. The leveling performance was evaluated by surface waviness change, which 
was obtained by surface data analyzing software.  
First of all, the effects of wet film thickness, wavelength and initial viscosity on leveling 
performance were proved to be in good agreement with literature statement. The higher film 
thickness has positive effect on leveling performance, while a rise of wavelength and initial 
viscosity will retard leveling performance. The coating sample with higher viscosity (4.09 Pas) 
evidently showed these conclusions, while for the one with lower viscosity (2.17 Pas), the effects 
of different parameters on leveling performance were not distinct. That is because, in these cases, 
the lower viscosity mainly dominates the leveling performance, which could cover the influence 
of other parameters.  
Secondly, from solvent evaporation experiments, abrupt dropping of remaining solvent content 
with constant evaporation rate was found in the first few minutes and the duration of constant rate 
period corresponds to the fast leveling period. A moderate decreasing of waviness was observed 
along the solvent evaporation with a falling evaporation rate. Thus, the different solvent 
evaporation rate is able to elucidate the decreasing tendency of surface waviness. Furthermore, the 
duration of constant rate period is relative to the initial solvent content. The coating sample with 
higher initial solvent content (low initial viscosity) will obtain a longer constant evaporation rate 
period (longer fast leveling period) and better leveling performance than the counterpart. Thus, 
solvent content plays an important role in leveling performance as well.  
Furthermore, since the fasted leveling occurs in the first few minutes after coating application, the 
leveling performance and the behavior of coating physical properties of a specific leveling 
procedure were focused in the next step. The dynamic change of waviness, wavelength and average 
film thickness were studied by 3D profilometer. By means of rheometer measurement, the transient 
viscosity value was obtained through measuring coating samples with different solvent contents,

Future work

13.  Future Work 
In this project, the leveling performance was studied by means of 3D profilometer and two models 
describing leveling performance were developed. However, some improvements of experimental 
design and data treatment strategy were found, which are meaningful for the further investigation.  
In marine industry, AF coating are mainly applied by airless spray application, which was simply 
simulated by using spiral applicators in this work. Thus, for the further investigation, it is 
recommended to design some complex coating applicators with changing wavelength (or film 
thickness) on a rod giving same film thickness (or wavelength), which is able to perform better 
simulation of coating surface applied by spray. In addition, with the development of lab, it might 
be possible to perform small scale of spray application in the lab. Then, the leveling performance 
on coating film generated by spray application is able to be studied and the conclusions will be 
meaningful for the coating industry. 
The quantification of experimental results is also possible to be improved in the future. The 
development of empirical model by MATLAB was only based on a specific leveling procedure 
with the same initial film thickness and wavelength. Since the transient wavelength during leveling 
barely changed comparing with the change of average film thickness and viscosity, it might lead 
to underestimate the effect of wavelength on leveling performance. Thus, it is meaningful to repeat 
same experimental procedure by using the applicators with different wavelengths and take these 
experimental data into consideration during the model development. 
Furthermore, in this work, the model verification was performed by using a commercial coating 
produced by industrial factory. The empirical model underestimated the leveling performance of 
a commercial coating sample since different building up manner of viscosity. It is necessary to 
reconduct the MATLAB simulation by using the experimental results from the commercial coating 
to illustrate the influence of viscosity based on different coating samples. 
Besides of the experimental optimization of the empirical model, the estimation strategy can be 
improved as well. The problem that the uncertainty of constant in model generated from MATLAB 
is a higher value than the parameter indicates this model still not complete. In the further 
optimization of this model, some suggestions are recommended to perform. Firstly, since the  
variable representing wavelength barely changed during the whole process comparing with others,
