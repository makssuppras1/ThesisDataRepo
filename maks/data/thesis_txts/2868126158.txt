Abstract
This study evaluates several existing sand transport models for the swash zone through
both qualitative and quantitative analyses. The quantitative assessment involves compar-
ing measured and predicted sand transport rates, as well as the gradients of sand trans-
port rates, to evaluate model performance. Qualitative analysis focuses on identifying the
strengths and weaknesses of each model. The evaluation is based on ten experimental
datasets covering a diverse range of wave conditions, sediment characteristics, and bed
profiles. An utmost quality assurance analysis is conducted on the datasets to identify
and exclude time instants that could potentially yield inaccurate results. Following the
evaluation, a new sand transport model is proposed based on the equilibrium beach state
assumption for the swash zone. Proof-of-concept testing demonstrates that the model can
produce highly accurate sand transport rate predictions when provided with appropriate
input parameters. Among these parameters, the equilibrium beach slope is parameterized
using the experimental datasets, and a novel expression for its calculation is introduced.
This new expression addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a clear defini-
tion of the beach slope. The proposed model is tested with the newly defined equilibrium
beach slope using experimental data and is compared against existing models. Results
show that the new model delivers promising predictions and, in some cases, outperforms
the existing models. However, in overall performance, the new model demonstrates slightly
weaker results compared to the Larson model.

Abstract in Danish
Denne undersøgelse evaluerer flere eksisterende sandtransportmodeller for opskylszonen
gennem både kvalitative og kvantitative analyser. Den kvantitative vurdering omfatter
sammenligning af målte og forudsagte sandtransportrater, samt gradienter af disse rater
for at evaluere modellernes præstation. Den kvalitative analyse fokuserer på at identifi-
cere styrker og svagheder ved hver model. Evalueringen er baseret på ti eksperimentelle
datasæt, der dækker et bredt udvalg af bølgeforhold, sedimentegenskaber og bundpro-
filer. En omfattende kvalitetssikringsanalyse er udført på datasættene for at identificere
og udelukke tidspunkter, der potentielt kan give unøjagtige resultater. Efter evaluerin-
gen foreslås en ny sandtransportmodel baseret på antagelsen om en ligevægtsstrandtil-
stand for opskylszonen.
Proof-of-concept-test viser, at modellen kan levere meget nø-
jagtige forudsigelser af raterne for sandtransport, når de rette inputparametre anvendes.
Blandt disse parametre er en ligevægt for strandskråningen parameteriseret ved hjælp af
de eksperimentelle datasæt. Fra dette udledes et nyt udtryk til beregning heraf. Dette
nye udtryk adresserer en væsentlig mangel i litteraturen ved at give en klar definition af
strandskråningen. Den foreslåede model testes med den nye definition for ligevægten af
strandskråningen, ved brug af eksperimentelle data, og sammenlignes med eksisterende
modeller. Resultaterne viser, at den nye model leverer lovende forudsigelser og i nogle til-
fælde overgår de eksisterende modeller. Dog viser modellen samlet set svagere resultater
sammenlignet med Larson-modellen.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Understanding the sediment transport mechanisms in the swash zone is crucial for the
effective planning and management of sandy beaches. Coastal regions provide numerous
societal benefits, such as habitation, economic opportunities, and recreation, making it
crucial to address their potential future challenges. As the effects of climate change con-
tinue to escalate on a global scale, it is well-recognized that coastal regions will be among
the most significantly impacted areas. Accordingly, the necessity of implementing timely
and appropriate measures has become more critical than ever. With the development and
application of robust methodologies and predictive models, it becomes possible to antici-
pate both unforeseen and unpredictable changes with reasonable accuracy. This objective
serves as the primary motivation for this study, which seeks to enhance the understanding
and management of coastal dynamics in an era of increasing environmental uncertainty.
1.2
Background
A typical beach profile consists of distinct zones, each characterized by unique mechanisms
and processes specific to their respective locations within the profile. As waves propagate
from the deeper sections of a beach toward shallower waters, they undergo several distinct
physical processes. In deeper waters (kh > π, where k is the wave number and h is the
water depth), waves have minimal interaction with the seabed. However, as they move
into shallower areas, they begin to ”feel” the bottom, resulting in interactions that initiate
sediment entrainment at the seabed. A schematic illustration of the zones in a typical
beach profile is shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Schematic illustration of different zones in a typical beach profile
As seen in the figure, during propagation from deep waters towards the shore, waves
first meet a zone where they experience a phenomenon called shoaling. In the shoaling

zone, waves start to interact with the bed, which results in losing some of its energy and,
therefore, the wave speed reduces. The reduction in the wave speed causes the waves to get
shorter as the wave period stays the same while the wave speed reduces. Furthermore, since
the wave energy is conserved during shoaling, the wave height increases to compensate for
the loss in wavelength and speed. The resulting increase in wave height and decrease in
wavelength causes waves to steepen. After a threshold, the waves cannot steepen more
and thus break in a zone named surf zone as shown in Figure 1.1. This threshold, known
as the breaking criterion, is typically H/h = 0.77, where H is the wave height and h is
the water depth. The breaking process leads to significant energy dissipation, primarily
caused by the intense turbulence generated during wave breaking. After waves break, they
transform into turbulent bores and propagate towards a new zone where the bores meet
the shore. This region, known as the swash zone, is the primary focus of this study.
The swash zone, a dynamic region of the beach covered intermittently by wave run-up,
as shown in Figure 1.1, is critical for sediment transport and beach morphodynamics. It
experiences intense sediment entrainment due to wave breaking at the shore and turbu-
lence. Sand transport in the swash zone is primarily driven by two mechanisms: suspended
sediment load, where sand particles are carried by turbulence and a mean flow without
bed contact, and sheet flow, a concentrated sand layer near the bed influenced by inter-
granular forces and flow interactions. The dominance of these mechanisms varies across a
swash event, which is defined as the period during which a wave advances onto the shore,
reaches its run-up limit, and recedes.

further complicate swash zone dynamics.

Swash zone sand transport processes
2.1
Swash zone
The swash zone is defined as the region where the sand bed is alternately covered and
exposed by the uprush and backwash of incoming waves. It is bound by the surf zone on
one side and the backshore on the other. As waves propagate from the surf zone after
breaking they enter the swash zone and give rise to two significant events: wave uprush
and backwash. The uprush event occurs when an incoming wave moves towards the shore,
extending up to the maximum run-up limit. The backwash event, contrarily, begins as the
uprush reaches the maximum run-up height and subsequently recedes back into the sea.
The swash zone is a very dynamic region where fast morphology changes occur due to high
turbulence, large sediment transport rates, and unsteady flows. A schematic illustration
of the swash zone with its physical processes is shown in Figure 2.1
Figure 2.1: Schematic illustration of the swash zone
It is seen from the figure that the swash zone is considered to consist of three regions:
The lower swash, the mid swash, and the upper swash region. These regions are deter-
mined based on the experiments conducted by Aagaard and Hughes, 2006. The study
differentiated the regions of the swash zone (lower, mid, and upper) based on the fraction
of time the Acoustic Doppler Velocimeter (ADV) was immersed during data collection.
Specifically, the upper zone was defined as when the ADV was immersed for 1–40% of an
instrument run, while the mid swash zone was defined as when the ADV was immersed
for 40–75% of the run. Finally, the lower swash zone was described as when the ADV
was immersed for 75–95% of the run. These regions are also identified by the experiments
conducted by van der Zanden et al., 2019a.
The swash zone is critically important because it serves as a transitional bridge between
two mediums: land and sea. This unique role makes understanding the physics of the

swash zone essential, as it directly influences shoreline development.

onshore-directed and offshore-directed conditions.
2.2
Sand transport processes
Sediment grains can either stay stationary or move depending on the forces acting upon
them.
Once the driving forces exceed the stabilizing forces, sediment grains start to
move. Shields, 1936 introduced the non-dimensional Shields parameter, which quantifies
the ratio of driving to stabilizing forces. As mentioned earlier, the extreme turbulence and
high velocities during uprush and backwash events in the swash zone can result in high
Shields parameters reaching up to O(5-10), leading to intensive sediment entertainment
and sheet flow in the swash zone.
Sediment transport in the swash zone occurs by means of two different transport modes,
namely, bed load and suspended load. van der Zanden et al., 2019b distinguish the total
sediment transport in the swash zone under energetic wave conditions as a sum of sheet
flow and suspended sediment transport. Sheet flow is a thin layer of high sediment con-
centration, moving just above and parallel to the bed, and suspended load refers to the
sediment grains transported freely without having contact with the bed. Heavy sediment
grains tend to be transported within the sheet flow whereas relatively lighter grains stirred
up by the turbulence and bore collapse show the tendency to be transported as suspended
load. Both modes are controlled by several physical processes, including various wave-
swash interactions that occur within the swash zone. The most important drivers of the
sediment transport in the swash zone are the uprush and backwash events. Therefore, be-
haviors of the events and their effects on sediment transportation must be well-understood.
Swash zone dynamics are often examined within a swash cycle. A swash cycle is defined
as the time it takes for an incoming wave to travel to the shore (uprush stage), reach its
maximum run-up height, descend to the maximum run-down depth (backwash stage), and
then interact with the next incoming wave.
Despite the fact that an uprush and the subsequent backwash are caused by the same
incoming wave, this does not necessarily imply that the mechanisms of these events are of
the same or similar magnitude and differ only in direction, with one being onshore-directed
and the other offshore-directed. In reality, the mechanisms and their resulting impacts on
the beach profile are significantly different, reflecting the complex dynamics of sediment
transport and morphological changes within the swash zone.

drodynamic processes during uprush and backwash, along with both suspended and sheet
load rates, is shown in Figure 2.2. It is important to note that the figure is illustrated
for a basic scenario where there is no interaction between the backwash and the incom-
ing waves. In the literature, this snap of time is also called a dam break scenario. The
consequences of the wave-swash interaction (also called swash-swash) are discussed in the
following subsections.

Swash uprush occurs when the wave bore, propagating from the surf zone, reaches the
shoreline and ascends to the run-up limit. During the uprush, the water depth at the
shoreline increases, gradually flattening out as the wave reaches the maximum run-up
limit. The water column carried by the wave forms a boundary layer whose thickness is
initially larger during the early stages of the uprush. This occurs because the rapid increase
in velocity, driven by high acceleration during this phase, leaves insuﬀicient time for the
boundary layer to develop fully (Figure 2.2a). As the velocity of the water decreases,
the boundary layer progressively thins and eventually vanishes when the wave reaches the
run-up limit. Additionally, the bed shear stress is proportional to the velocity gradient at
the bed (τb ∝du/dy). The velocity gradient is steepest in the early uprush as the flow
accelerates quickly, even as the boundary layer begins to thin under the influence of this
accelerating flow. Therefore, the bed shear becomes the highest during the early uprush
and diminishes as the wave reaches the maximum run-up limit (Figure 2.2c). Similarly,
during the uprush, the friction factor undergoes significant variation. It can be expressed
as f = τb

velocity in this context). It reaches a local minimum during the uprush phase due to
the dynamic interplay between bed shear stress (τb) and the rapidly changing velocity of
the uprush. Furthermore, the resulting suspended load and bed load hits the maximum
later than the bed shear stress (Figure 2.2e). This is due to the fact that the phase lag
effects occur when sediment is stirred up and does not respond directly to the orbital
wave motion as it takes time to fall back to the bed (Janssen, 1999). Another important
physical phenomenon that takes place is the infiltration of the water towards the land.
During the infiltration, the boundary layer thickness gets smaller. Hence, the velocity
gradient increases even more, and as mentioned earlier, since the velocity gradient is
proportional to the shear stresses at the bed, it is explicitly related to sediment transport
mechanisms. However, Butt et al., 2001 showed that an increased downward pressure
gradient caused by infiltration can also increase the resistance of sediment grains to move.
Additionally, the onshore flow velocities during the uprush stage are higher than offshore
flow velocities during the backwash stage, while the duration of the uprush stage is shorter
than that of the backwash stage, and the maximum flow velocities occur at the beginning
of the uprush as stated in some studies (Masselink and Hughes, 1998, Butt et al., 2001,
and Masselink et al., 2009)
During the backwash stage, the aforementioned mechanisms are all reversed but with
different magnitudes. Once the incoming waves reach their maximum run-up limit, they
begin to recede, driven by the slope of the shore and gravitational forces, eventually reach-
ing the maximum run-down depth. During this stage, the flow direction changes towards
offshore, and the associated hydrodynamic and sediment transport processes are governed
by the retreating water’s velocity and shear stresses. As the water retreats under the
influence of gravity, its velocity increases, reaching its maximum value just before it en-
counters the incoming wave bore. When the wave bore interacts with the receding water,
the velocity begins to decrease due to the opposing flows, eventually diminishing entirely
as the receding water is completely overtaken and absorbed by the incoming bore. During
the initial phases of backwash, the boundary layer development is slower as it has time to
develop due to relatively slower velocities and higher water column thickness. However,
as the receding velocity increases and the water depth decreases, the boundary layer can
no longer fully develop and results in higher velocity gradients (Figure 2.2a). Similarly,
the resulting bed shear stress increases as the water retreats toward the sea, reaching
its peak just before encountering the incoming wave bore, and then diminishes once it
meets the bore (Figure 2.2d). In contrast to the velocity and bed shear stress changes,
the friction factor, f, follows a slightly more symmetrical pattern during the backwash

the backwash stage, the dynamics of suspended and bed load sediment transport differ
from those observed during the uprush. Suspended sediment concentrations are generally
lower than during uprush due to reduced turbulence levels. However, as backwash flow
intensifies, shear stress increases, lifting sediment into suspension, with the peak concen-
tration occurring slightly later than during the uprush phase. Bed load transport typically
reaches its maximum during the late stages of backwash, when velocities are at their high-
est. Furthermore, unlike the uprush phase, where a downward pressure gradient drives
landward water flow, the backwash involves water receding from the land. This results in
increased boundary layer thicknesses, lower velocity gradients, and, consequently, lower
shear stresses at the bed. Despite these lower shear stresses, exfiltration during backwash
reduces the stabilizing forces acting on sediment grains, making them more vulnerable
to movement. This interplay between hydrodynamic and sediment transport processes
highlights the unique characteristics of the backwash stage compared to the uprush.

and backwash stages in the swash zone, sediment transport moves towards onshore and
offshore, respectively.
Accretion and erosion in the swash zone depend on the balance between sediment transport
during uprush and backwash. When offshore-directed processes outweigh onshore-directed
ones, erosion occurs; the opposite is the case for accretion. Figuring out the dominant
direction of sand transport, however, is not always straightforward. For example, it might
seem logical to assume that sediment transport in the swash zone is mostly offshore-
directed due to the negative velocity skewness (see Figure 2.2b). However, as Chen et
al., 2023 point out, if velocity-based sand transport models like the one by Bailard, 1981
were applied, beaches would never accrete — which we know is not true. This suggests
that other mechanisms must contribute to onshore-directed transport, favoring uprush
processes. To explore this further, the next subsection dives into the physics behind both
directions of transport.
2.2.1
Onshore-directed processes
One of the key mechanisms driving sediment transport in the onshore direction is tur-
bulence generated by the incoming wave bore. When waves break, the collapsing bore
produces intense turbulence, mobilizing sediment as suspended load and sheet flow, which
is then transported onshore. Additionally, the thinner boundary layer that develops dur-
ing uprush results in steep velocity gradients and consequently high bed stresses, further
aiding in the onshore transport of mobilized sediment.
Another significant factor is the suspended sediment carried into the swash zone from the
surf zone, which contributes to onshore-directed processes. Furthermore, the asymmetry
and skewness of waves play a crucial role. As waves propagate from deeper waters — where
they do not interact with the seabed — into shallower regions, they lose their symmetry
and become skewed and asymmetric. This transformation enhances their ability to trans-
port sediment onshore. Austin et al., 2009 highlight the influence of these asymmetric and
skewed waves on onshore-directed sediment transport, demonstrating their importance in
shaping coastal dynamics.
2.2.2
Offshore-directed processes
The gravitational forces acting on sediment grains are the primary drivers of offshore-
directed processes.
On a sloping seabed, sediment grains reduce the stabilizing effect
of their own weight, making it easier for the backwash flow to transport them offshore.
Additionally, the friction angle of sediment grains, often referred to as the angle of repose,
contributes to offshore sediment transport when combined with the effects of the bed slope.
As mentioned earlier, infiltration/exfiltration effects also play a significant role in swash
zone sediment transport. According to Bakhtyar et al., 2009, finer sediments with grain
diameters of d50 < 0.2 mm tend to move offshore, while coarser grains (d50 > 0.4 mm) are
more likely to experience onshore-directed transport due to infiltration/exfiltration. It is

important to note that these mechanisms occur over the short time scale of a single swash
cycle. The resulting accretion or erosion, however, develops on longer time scales after
many swash cycles.
2.2.3
Wave-swash interactions
The mechanisms that take part in the swash zone sand transport process are investigated
without taking the wave-swash interactions into account. The interaction between incom-
ing waves and backwash flows further complicates the dynamics.. Combining this with the
complexity of the other physical processes in the swash zone is beyond the scope of this
thesis. A study done by Cáceres and Alsina, 2012 classifies the wave-swash interaction into
three groups. These are namely wave capture, weak wave-swash interaction and strong
wave-swash interaction. Types of wave-swash interactions are illustrated by Cáceres and
Alsina, 2012 and are shown in Figure 2.4.
Wave capture arises when the incoming wave captures the previous one that propagates
as uprush in the swash zone. Weak wave-swash interaction occurs when the backwash
flow has less energy than the incoming wave. Similarly, the strong wave-swash interac-
tion is observed when the backwash flow has the same or more energy than the incoming
wave. Findings from Alsina et al., 2018 indicate that wave capture is less powerful than
an individual uprush event, likely due to the previous uprush creating a protective layer
(swash lens) above the bed, which reduces the impact of the subsequent uprush. However,
the location at which the wave capture occurs has effects on the sediment transport mo-
bility. van der Zanden et al., 2019b report that the location of the cross-shore maximum
in uprush transport relates to the location of wave capture during the uprush and that
landward from this location, the merged, highly turbulent bore propagates over a dry bed
and is expected to induce high bed shear stresses and sediment mobilization.
During the weak wave-swash interaction, since the backwash is dominated and dissipated
by the incoming wave, the advection of the sediment is in the onshore direction.
For the strong wave-swash interaction, the backwash pushes the incoming waves and typ-
ically leads to a hydraulic jump, hence resulting in more intense turbulence and sediment
transport. Cáceres and Alsina, 2012 show that this type of interaction typically results in
offshore sand transport.

2.3
Sand transport formulations
Estimating sediment transport rates in the swash zone has long been a challenging issue,
with numerous models based on various approaches proposed, as previously discussed.
These models have been reviewed and classified by Chen et al., 2023. The existing models
are categorized based on the required inputs and the methodologies used in their devel-
opment.
Within the scope of this study, three practical sand transport models have been used and
evaluated: The triangular sand transport distribution model proposed by van Rijn, 2009,
the Larson model introduced by Larson et al., 2004, and finally, an improved version of
the Larson model introduced by Chen et al., 2024. All three models are then evaluated in
chapter 4 based on the experimental data discussed in chapter 3.
Sand transport distribution refers to the methods used to calculate sand transport in the
swash zone of a beach profile. These methods are employed in wave-averaged morphody-
namic models to account for the evolution of the swash beach. The distribution is done by
allocating the sand transport rate from the landside boundary of the surf zone landward
up to the maximum run-up height using various distribution functions. These distribution
methods are based on simplified assumptions and do not explicitly model the effects of
hydrodynamic factors on sand transport.
2.3.1
The van Rijn model
The van Rijn model is based on a concept called sand transport distributio. Sand trans-
port distribution refers to the methods used to distribute the calculated sand transport
in the swash zone of a beach profile. These methods are employed in wave-averaged mor-
phodynamic models to account for the evolution of the swash beach. The distribution is
done by allocating the sand transport rate from the landside boundary of the surf zone
landward up to the maximum run-up height using various distribution functions. These
distribution methods are based on simplified assumptions and do not explicitly model the
effects of hydrodynamic factors on sand transport.
A series of studies - L. C. van Rijn, 2007a, L. C. van Rijn, 2007b, and L. C. van Rijn, 2007c

total bed load rate plays an important role in accretion and erosion. The above-mentioned
study was then further developed by van Rijn, 2009.

by integrating the sediment transport in one swash cycle. One of the well-known sand

2.3.3
The Chen et al. model
The Chen et al. model introduced by Chen et al., 2024 is a re-visited version of the original
Larson model introduced in the previous subsection. Two features of the original Larson
model have been improved. First of all, the applicability range of the original Larson
model has been extended. The original model was developed specifically for the region
above the still water level and was, therefore, limited to describing the morphological
evolution within that zone. The improved model, however, has been extended to include
the maximum run-down limit. The study defines the maximum run-down limit as the
water depth corresponding to thirty per cent of the maximum run-up limit. Secondly,
rather than using a constant equilibrium slope discussed in Equation 2.3, shape functions
for equilibrium slopes have been utilized for both erosive and accretive conditions. These
shape functions have been inspired by the so-called Weir shape functions introduced in
Weir, Hughes, et al., 2006. The main idea is to approximate the shape of the sand transport
rates across the beach profiles for both erosive and accretive conditions. According to the
field campaign done by Weir, Hughes, et al., 2006, behavior of the sand transport can
be categorized within three shapes: Shape-I, Shape-IIa, Shape-IIb, and Shape-III. These
shapes are shown in the error bar plots in Figure 2.6.

3.1
Data quality assessment
Data quality is crucial for ensuring the reliability and validity of research outcomes. High-
quality data serves as the foundation for a meaningful analysis, allowing for accurate
interpretations and conclusions.
Poor data quality, on the other hand, can introduce
errors and uncertainties, ultimately compromising the integrity of the results.
Several
factors can affect data quality. For instance, the purpose of the experiments may not
necessarily focus on the swash zone sand transport dynamics, or the sensitivity of data
acquisition devices may vary between experiments. Furthermore, even the type of bed
profile measurement sensor can cause differences in the results of the same experiment.
As will be seen in the next chapter, the existing sand transport models are evaluated by
comparing the sand transport rates obtained from measurements with those predicted by
the models. Therefore, the quality assurance of the bed profile datasets listed in Table 3.1
is based on the parameters that could affect the quality of the sand transport rates in the
swash zone.
One can estimate the total sediment transport rate between two time instants from the
measured bed profiles by using the following discrete form of the sediment continuity
equation (also known as the Exner equation):
qT (x) = qT (x −∆x) + ∆x(1 −n)∆zbed(x)
∆t
(3.1)
where qT is the sediment transport rate, ∆x is the horizontal discretization of the bed
profile measurements, ∆zbed(x) is the vertical difference between two bed measurements
at location x, ∆t is the time difference between the two measurements, and n is the
porosity. Note that the porosity is set to n ≈0.35 for the experiments where it is not
explicitly provided. In order to solve Equation 3.1 across the whole profile, an initial value
of the sand transport rate must be known. The initial guess for the solution should be
based on a point where the sand transport rate is known. This point is either the most
offshore or the most onshore location in the profile, where no movement in the vertical
direction (ie zero transport rate) is expected. This is also where the quality of the datasets
is determined: datasets showing no movement at one end of the profile are considered to
have higher quality compared to those where the bed profile is not stable at either end.
Consequently, the highest quality case is when there is no movement at both ends of the
profile. It must be taken into consideration that the results from both sides will not yield
the exact results but will follow the same trend, with the transport rate shifted upwards
(tendency in onshore direction) or downwards (tendency in offshore direction). Larsen et
al., 2023 argue that this shift can be due to compaction effects and non-uniformity in the
bed profile. Although these differences are small, they can cause a shift in the transport
rate curves. Following that, one can argue that the sand transport rates are more accurate
at both ends of the beach profile, provided that the sand transport rates at both ends are
known. Therefore, the total transport rate across the beach profile can be calculated as a
weighted average between each end based on the following expression:

3.2.1
BARDEX II
The BARDEX II experiments, whose experimental setup is depicted in Figure 3.2, feature
a barrier with a lagoon situated behind it, as shown in the figure. The dashed lines in
the figure represents the varying water level at two sides of the beach. A unique aspect
of BARDEX II (Masselink et al., 2016) is the inclusion of this lagoon, which allows for
experimental manipulation of the groundwater hydrology within the barrier. The primary
objective of BARDEX II is to gather a comprehensive large-scale dataset of energetic
wave interactions with a sandy beach.
This dataset aims to enhance the quantitative
understanding of sediment transport processes in the inner surf, swash, and overwash
zones (Masselink et al., 2016).
Figure 3.2: Experimental set-up of BARDEX II as shown in Masselink et al., 2016
As mentioned earlier, experimental data obtained from BARDEX II is a good indicator
of the effects of varying groundwater levels in sand transport processes in the swash zone,
which will definitely affect the infiltration/exfiltration effects in the swash zone. The closer
the groundwater table is to the surface, the lighter the sediment grains become; hence,
the easier it is to move the sand.
The BARDEX II experiments primarily involve accretive wave conditions, resulting in
berm development in the swash zone. In the A1 test series, the water depth in the lagoon
was maintained at the same level as the water depth at the paddle, ensuring that the
groundwater level remained at the still water level (SWL). This condition led to minor
accretion in the upper swash zone and the formation of a bar in the surf zone due to
sediment transport from the shore toward offshore areas, resulting in shoreline recession.
In the A2 and A3 test cases, the water depth in the lagoon was increased to 4.3 meters,
nearly reaching the highest level of the beach.
This adjustment led to a reduction in
the height of the previously formed berm and a restoration of the shoreline, i.e. causing
the beach to return to its original position. For test case A4, the lagoon water depth
was reduced to 1.7 meters, which led to a very small decrease in the berm height and a
reduction in the offshore bar height. Relatively milder wave climate was used in cases A6,
A7, and A8. In case A6, the water depth in the lagoon was restored to the SWL, resulting

In case A7, the berm height continued to increase gradually as the lagoon water level was
raised to 4.25 meters. Conversely, in case A8, berm development occurred at a faster rate,
with the lagoon water level lowered to 1.7 meters. In both A7 and A8, the offshore bar
height decreased further. In case B1, wave action intensified compared to cases A6, A7,

3.2.2
DynaRev experiments
DynaRev experiments (Blenkinsopp et al., 2021) is a larger-scale series of experiments
carried out in the Large Wave Flume (Großer Wellenkanal, GWK), Hannover, Germany.
A total of 141.6 hours of testing under wave action was completed.
The experiments
comprised two phases, with each phase being split into a series of “runs” varying from 20
minutes to 3 hours in duration. The beach profile was only reset between the two phases.
Thus, all runs had a different antecedent morphology corresponding to the beach profile
at the end of the preceding run. Phase SB is conducted to understand the response of
sand beaches under rising water level conditions. An artificial beach with a slope of 1:15
was exposed to wave action with varying water levels. After the end of each case, the
water level at the flume was increased by 0.1 meters up to a total water level of 0.4 meters
under the same wave climate. Following the completion of the water level increments,
resilience testing was completed to investigate the short-term response of the beach to a
range of different wave conditions for both erosive and accretive conditions. These cases
were undertaken at the highest water level (4.9 m). Each test was divided into 3 to 7
runs with durations ranging from 20 to 60 minutes. These runs were labelled as SBE
for erosive cases and SBA for cases expected to cause accretion. The second phase of
the experiments is not taken into consideration within this study as it focuses on the
dynamic revetment of the beaches with a rock armour layer. With a grid resolution of 2
cm, DynaRev experiments provide very finely resolved data for further assessment.
During the case SB0, sand transport was offshore directed, resulting in erosion at the SWL
and an offshore bar formation at the surf zone. It is also noticed that ripple formation
at the offshore boundary of the swash zone took place. There was almost no accretion at
the upper swash zone. The Shoreline erosion was still maintained during the case SB1, in
which the water level at the flume was increased by 0.1 meters. The ripple formation was
also maintained in the seaward boundary of the swash zone. Due to the resulting offshore-
directed sand transport, the offshore bar kept growing and moved offshore. Similar sand
transport behaviors were observed in the cases SB2, SB3, and SB4. In the cases SBE1 and
SBE2, however, the previously formed ripples were washed out. Erosion was maintained,
and the height of the offshore bar remained the same while the bar moved offshore. In the
last case, SBA1, where the beach was exposed to relatively milder wave action, the beach
morphology mainly remained the same. It was seen that ripple formation occurred again.
Unlike in the previous ripple formations, this time, ripples formed both on the onshore
and offshore sides of the offshore bar. In general, the beach receded. The morphological
evolution of the beach is shown in Figure 3.5 where the blue dashed-dotted line represents
the new water level, whereas the black thin line represents the initial water level.

3.2.3
Experiments in Krauss and Larson, 1988
Experiments shown in Krauss and Larson, 1988 differ from the rest of the experiments
listed in Table 3.1. Unlike other experiments where the cases are connected in a way that
the final profile of a case is simultaneously the initial profile of another, these experiments
are conducted in a way such that there is no connection between different cases. Also,
the time scale of the experiments is longer. This is a good example of illustrating the
equilibrium beach states as the individual cases were run for a longer time without in-
terruption at different wave climates or water level changes in the flume. Unfortunately,
due to the lack of advanced measurement devices back in the time when experiments were
carried out, the spatial resolution of the experiments is extremely coarse (≈1.2 meters).
Furthermore, the time spacing between the measurements is also quite long (in the order
of an hour). These factors lead to accuracy problems in the calculation of sand transport
rates. Therefore, these experiments are not to be used in the model evaluation chapter.
However, the experiments are helpful for understanding the mechanisms of morphological
changes in longer time scales.
Profile development of the experiments is shown in Figure 3.7. In all the experiments,
the Shoreline has experienced a recede towards onshore. Since these experiments were
conducted for longer times than the other experiments listed in Table 3.1, the equilibrium
beach state can be observed within Krauss and Larson’s experiments. As seen in the figure
below, the beach did not change significantly towards the end of the experiments (darker
lines). There are also some spikes in the data (CE100, CE500). This is another reason
that reduces the reliability of the dataset.

3.2.4
RESIST
The RESIST experiments (van der Zanden et al., 2019a) were conducted in large scale fa-
cilities at CIEM (Canal d’Investigació i Experimentació Marítima) and in three sequences,
with the bed profile being redone after each sequence. Therefore, the sequences are inde-
pendent of one another. Within each sequence, erosive and accretive wave climates were
applied in varying orders. Once the wave runs were completed, the bed elevation was
redone. It is important to note that a 30-minute test with random waves, referred to as
the Benchmark case, was conducted for each sequence prior to initiating the erosive and
accretive wave actions. These Benchmark cases served to compact the fresh beach surface,
thereby ensuring more reliable experimental results. The beach slope for all the RESIST
experiments is 1:15.
In the first sequence of the RESIST experiments, the beach was subjected to a total wave
action duration of 1560 minutes, of which 1200 minutes consisted of two accretive cases
(see Table 3.1). The beach profile evolution of the RESIST - Sequence 1 experiments
is shown in Figure 3.8a.
During case E1, the sand transport rate is offshore directed
within the lower swash region, contributing the formation of an offshore bar in the surf
zone. In the upper swash region, on the other hand, the direction of the sand transport is
onshore. Due to the onshore-directed sand transport, sand accumulation near the run-up
limit was observed. The accretive case, A1, promotes recovery of the sand lost during the
preceding case. By the end of this case, the offshore bar was nearly flattened, with the
sand redistributed to its previous positions. The next erosive case, E2, which is a milder
case relative to E1, transports sand back towards the surf zone and creates the offshore
bar again. The upper swash zone, however, still remains the same. During the last wave
action, A2, the offshore bar loses some height. At the end of the case, the beach profile
around the SWL is almost identical to the initial profile before being exposed to the waves.
The most onshore point in all cases remains the same.
In the second sequence of the RESIST experiments, the profile change in time is shown
in Figure 3.8b.
Similar to the first sequence, the dominant wave climate is accretive
(with two cases having a total duration of 1200 mins in 1560 mins). During case E2,
an offshore bar formation is observed in the surf zone due to the sand transport from the
lower swash zone. The upper swash zone is mostly stable. As case A1 kicks in, the offshore
bar migrates towards the lower swash zone while reducing its height. Additionally, the
accumulation of sand takes place in the vicinity of the Shoreline. This accumulation is,
however, transported both onshore and offshore, resulting in accretion at the run-up limit
and the formation of two offshore bars, respectively.
For the final sequence of the RESIST experiments, the profile development in time is
shown in Figure 3.8c. The beach profile is, again, dominantly exposed to the accretive
wave conditions. This time, the accretive cases used (A2 and A3) are relatively milder,
and the duration is much longer (wave action for a total duration of 2580 mins, of which
2220 mins consists of accretive conditions). During case E1, the sand transport direction
at the lower swash zone is offshore-directed, and it is onshore-directed in the upper swash
zone, resulting in the formation of the offshore bar in the surf zone and sand accumulation
at the run-up limit, respectively. Once case A2 takes the stage, the direction of the sand
transport is mainly onshore. This results in the formation of a berm in the swash zone.
The offshore bar is almost flattened. The berm is then destroyed as the case E2 kicks in,
flattening almost all the formations in the swash zone. The berm, however, appears again
due to the new and mildest wave climate in the experiment (A3). The berm keeps growing
as the time continues. The main sand source of the berm is the region at the boundary of
the surf and swash zones.

4
Evaluation of existing swash zone sand
transport models
This chapter presents a comprehensive comparison between the measured sand transport
rates obtained from experiments and the existing formulations used for the estimation.
The goal of this analysis is to evaluate the accuracy and applicability of the different
models in capturing the dynamics observed in the experimental data, particularly within
the swash zone. By examining how well the existing formulations predict the transport
rates, it is aimed to identify the strengths and limitations of each approach and highlight
areas where improvements may be needed.
There are several approaches to conducting a comparison study for sand transport rates.
First, a comparison can be made by evaluating the bed elevation changes predicted by ex-
isting models against experimental data. However, this method can be misleading because
some models calculate only sand transport rates, requiring bed elevation changes to be
derived using the Exner equation. The challenge with this approach lies in the potential
accumulation of errors when solving the Exner equation, which can result in inaccurate
bed elevation predictions. Secondly, the volume of sand transported can be considered
as part of the comparison. While this method provides valuable insights, it also relies
on bed elevation data from the models. Consequently, inaccuracies in predicting eleva-
tion changes may propagate into the volume calculations, reducing the reliability of this
approach.
In this study, two methods are considered for comparing the models. The first method
involves directly comparing the predicted sand transport rates with the measured ones.
This approach is considered more reliable than methodologies that depend solely on the
sand transport rates themselves. While comparing sand transport rates provides valuable
insights into a model’s performance, it may overlook certain important aspects. For exam-
ple, the measured and predicted sand transport rates might exhibit similar shapes (e.g.,
parabolic) but differ in spatial alignment (e.g., a phase lag between two parabolic shapes).
Such a discrepancy could suggest poor model performance when evaluating only the sand
transport rates. However, if the gradients of these rates are closely aligned, the predic-
tion might still be accurate in terms of capturing the morphological changes, as it is the
gradient of the sand transport rates that drives bed morphology changes. This limitation
highlights the importance of the second employed method, which focuses on comparing the
gradients of the sand transport rates. By evaluating the gradients, this method addresses
the phase-alignment issue and provides a more comprehensive assessment of the model’s
ability to predict morphological evolution.
As mentioned earlier in chapter 2, the Larson model (Larson et al., 2004) requires two
critical inputs: the calibration factor, Kc, and the equilibrium slope, tan(βe). To eval-
uate the performance of the Larson model, these parameters are determined through an
optimization study. This process involves tuning the calibration factor and equilibrium
slope to minimize the discrepancy between the predicted and measured sand transport
rates, ensuring optimal alignment with experimental data. Since the Chen et al. (Chen
et al., 2024) model calculates the equilibrium slope based on the Dean number, only the
calibration factor, Kc, obtained from optimization of the Larson model, is utilized for its
performance assessment. This approach allows for a consistent comparison while leverag-
ing the Dean number-based equilibrium slope calculation as implemented in Chen et al.,
40
Assessment and Improvement of Cross-Shore Sediment Transport Models for the Swash Zone
==PAGE:57==
2024. The resulting calibration factor and the equilibrium slope are presented in Table A.1
for all experiments.
Additionally, since the comparison between the models and measurements is based on
sand transport rates, and the van Rijn model (van Rijn, 2009) provides results in terms of
bed elevation change, the model results were converted back to sand transport rates using
Equation 3.1. Although this conversion may slightly affect the model’s performance, the
impact is not expected to be significant.
Finally, the maximum wave run-up, an essential input for the existing models, is deter-
mined directly from the experimental data instead of relying on empirical formulas (e.g.,

datasets. To identify the maximum run-up height, a statistical analysis of bed elevation
changes is performed for each case across all experiments. After extensive evaluation, it

profile where the standard deviation of the bed elevations remains below the threshold

4.2
Assessment of the models
In this section, the advantages and limitations of the implemented models are discussed.
Evaluating model performance is a complex task, as demonstrated in the previous section:
a model may perform well and provide accurate estimates under certain conditions while
yielding entirely different and less satisfactory results under others. Consequently, rather
than identifying the ”best” model, it is more meaningful to assess the strengths and weak-
nesses of each model, recognizing that each has distinct advantages and drawbacks. The
performance of these models varies significantly depending on their underlying theoretical
assumptions and the specific conditions for which they were developed. The highly vari-
able nature of the swash zone presents a significant challenge in developing a generally
applicable model, as each experiment may exhibit a unique behavior that is not accounted
for in existing literature or model formulations.
4.2.1
The van Rijn Model
The primary strength of the van Rijn model lies in its straightforward implementation.
Since the sediment continuity equation is already incorporated into the model, there is no
need to solve the Exner equation to compute bed elevation changes. However, a notable
limitation of the model is its assumption that the distribution of the sand transport follows
a triangular shape, which does not always reflect real-world conditions. In practice, the
distribution of sand transport can take on various forms, and the triangular assumption
may not always yield accurate results.
Furthermore, the simplified triangular assumption might lack the direct incorporation of
hydrodynamic features such as wave energy, bed friction, or turbulence. Therefore, having
all this oversimplification implemented can result in poor performance in capturing the
actual shape of beach profile changes.
Another important aspect of the van Rijn model is its reliance on the sand transport rate
at the seaward boundary of the swash zone. L. van Rijn et al., 2011 suggests that this
boundary is located between hl = 0.1 m and hl = 0.2 m, requiring the sand transport
value at a depth in between these limits to be used as an initial condition. However, the
specified range for the boundary condition can have a significant impact on the results, as
presented in Figure 4.16.

4.2.2
The Larson Model
The Larson model can be considered as a semi-empirical and semi-equilibrium model
(Chen et al., 2023). Unlike the van Rijn model, where sand is distributed by a triangular
shape, the Larson model uses the equilibrium slope approach and the resulting shape
due to the equilibrium slope, angle of repose and run-up limit. However, the underlying
ballistic theory proposed by Shen and Meyer, 1963 is only applicable above the still water
level, thereby limiting the validity of the Larson model to regions above this boundary.
Therefore, the Larson model get to use the initial condition that the bed elevation at the
still water level does not change in time (the gradient of the sand transport is zero), which
is an unrealistic approach considering the natural evolution of beach profiles. According
to this approach, the shoreline functions as a pivot point, remaining stationary while the
beach morphology adjusts around it, with no significant changes occurring directly at the
pivot itself.
In addition to this, the input parameters used in the model, namely the calibration factor
(Kc) and the equilibrium slope (tan(βe)), are major factors that affect the performance
of the Larson model. Especially, the effect of the equilibrium slope is higher than that of
the calibration factor since the calibration factor only changes the magnitude of the sand
transport rate but not the distribution (see Equation 2.3). Therefore, the main focus must
be on determining the equilibrium slope.
Another significant drawback of the Larson model is that when the model is run for
extended time periods, the solution becomes unstable and diverges due to the bed elevation
gradient at the run-up limit. To illustrate this, consider an artificial beach with an initial
slope of m0 = 1/15 and an equilibrium slope of tan(β) = 1/25, representing a beach that
continues to erode until it reaches the equilibrium slope. Assuming a fixed run-up limit,
the resulting beach evolution after 60 minutes is shown in Figure 4.17.

4.2.3
The Chen et al. Model
The Chen et al. model, introduced by Chen et al., 2024, addresses several limitations of
the Larson model and proposes a series of enhancements. One key improvement involves
extending the applicability of the original model from the still water level to the run-down
limit, offering a more comprehensive framework for modeling sand transport in the swash
zone. Additionally, a significant challenge in the Larson model—the parameterization of
the equilibrium beach slope—has been effectively addressed. The equilibrium slope in the
Chen et al. model is parameterized using Weir shapes, as suggested by Weir, Hughes,
et al., 2006 and Weir, Baldock, et al., 2006, with corresponding shape functions derived to
enhance the model’s accuracy. These advancements represent a meaningful step forward
in improving the depiction of swash zone dynamics and resolving a critical limitation of
the Larson model.
The shape functions, however, were derived from a limited dataset and still require further
development with additional data to enhance their accuracy and reliability. The deriva-
tion of the shape functions assumes a planar initial slope, represented by the term m0 in
Equation 2.6. This assumption represents a significant limitation of the equilibrium slope
expressions, as a planar beach profile is not typically observed in natural beach environ-
ments. Another assumption used in the shape functions is that they are calculated based
on the global equilibrium slope (see Equation 2.7). This assumption leads to nonphysical
results, such as predicting a zero slope for high-energy wave cases. Moreover, the expres-
sion provided for the global equilibrium slope is discontinuous, causing a sudden jump
around the threshold, as shown in Figure 2.7.
Additionally, unlike the Larson model, the Chen et al. model leverages equilibrium slopes
defined by shape functions, enabling it to predict accretion in the upper swash zone while
simultaneously capturing offshore-directed sand transport across the rest of the beach
profile.

4.3
Potential improvements
Several suggestions for the practical sand transport models discussed earlier can be made.
For example, the boundary condition for the seaward boundary used in the van Rijn
model may be parameterized. This can give a more robust prediction ability to the van
Rijn model. Another improvement can be implemented in the sand transport distribution
method. For instance, the triangular shape for distributing the sand transport rates can
be changed to more complex shape. The method to find a new distribution shape can be
obtained by a similar method introduced in Weir, Hughes, et al., 2006 and Weir, Baldock,
et al., 2006.
Furthermore, the Larson model and the Chen et al. model could also benefit from signif-
icant improvements. For instance, the calibration coeﬀicient should be parameterized to
reflect the factors influencing its behavior. Performing a dimensional analysis could help
establish a relationship between the calibration factors and relevant dimensional quanti-
ties. According to the underlying theory (Larson et al., 2004), the calibration factor is
dependent on grain size (d50) and the friction factor (f). Establishing a robust relationship
between these parameters and the calibration factor could mark a significant improvement
of the Larson model.
In addition, a detailed investigation of the equilibrium slope is necessary. The approach
suggested by Chen et al., 2024, which employs shape functions, is promising. With access
to more comprehensive datasets, it may be possible to derive a more generalized function
for the equilibrium slope estimation, further enhancing the accuracy and applicability of
the models.
Moreover, coupling the Larson model and the Chen et al. model with a larger morpho-
logical framework would enable more comprehensive predictions of beach evolution over
longer time scales, integrating nearshore and offshore processes. This advancement could
extend the applicability of the Larson and Chen et al. models beyond the swash zone,
enabling their use for modelling the entire beach profile.
Finally, instead of introducing potential improvements to the van Rijn, Larson or Chen
et al. models, a new sand transport model that incorporates both the strengths of these
models and addresses their limitations could be developed. Such a model would ideally
integrate the detailed process-based understanding of swash zone dynamics with a more
flexible framework for handling varying beach morphologies and hydrodynamic conditions.
Within the following subsections, a new sand transport model is introduced and tested
across various scenarios to evaluate its applicability under different conditions.

5.1
Equilibrium slope
As previously mentioned, the equilibrium slope concept is based on the assumption that,
over long time scales of wave action, a beach profile evolves to an equilibrium state where
no further development or changes occur, resulting in zero net sand transport across the
profile. However, the equilibrium state can change at any time if the wave action changes,
making it a complex natural phenomenon to describe or identify. Therefore, there have
been several attempts to find an expression for estimating the equilibrium beach slope
in the literature (Bruun, 1954;Swart, 1975;Vellinga, 1982;Dean, 1991). The introduced
expressions, however, are developed for the beach profile below the still water level and
do not account for the portion of the beach extending up to the maximum wave run-up
height. Additionally, Kriebel et al., 1991 and Masselink, 1993 conducted studies on the
equilibrium slope in the swash zone. However, both studies lack a clear definition of the
slope, and the expressions provided do not seem to adequately describe the observed data.
The key parameter for determining the equilibrium state is the time scale over which a
beach is exposed to a specific wave climate. In nature, however, this time scale cannot
be controlled due to the fast-changing environmental conditions. Consequently, identify-
ing the equilibrium beach state during field campaigns can be relatively challenging. In
contrast, laboratory experiments can provide more valuable insights into the equilibrium
beach state, as the wave climate and time scale of the experiments can be easily controlled
along with the other parameters that might affect the equilibrium state, such as initial
beach profile, sediment characteristics, etc.
To utilize the experimental datasets for studying the equilibrium beach state, it is essential
to ensure that the experiments exhibit a clear ”tendency” towards equilibrium. This is
particularly important because the timescale required to achieve an equilibrium beach
state may be significantly longer than the duration of the experiments. In this study,
two potential indicators of an equilibrium state are used. Namely, the evolution of the
shoreline, as discussed in chapter 3, and the temporal evolution of the beach slope, which
will be presented in the following sections. Both methods provide valuable insights into
the tendency towards an equilibrium beach state.
For instance, tracking changes in shoreline position over time can provide insight into
whether the shoreline is converging toward an equilibrium position, as no further move-
ment would indicate stabilization. However, relying solely on shoreline evolution can be
misleading, as the overall beach morphology may continue to change even when the shore-
line position appears static. In contrast, the evolution of the beach slope over time serves is
a more reliable indicator of equilibrium, as it reflects sediment redistribution and changes
in steepness.

steady-state behaviors, and providing a deeper understanding of the interplay between
forcing factors and sediment dynamics.

5.2.2
Strengths and limitations
The new sand transport model is considered to be a combination of sand transport distri-
bution models and equilibrium models, leveraging the strengths of these types of models.
The new model introduces an innovative approach to parameterizing the equilibrium beach
slope, addressing a significant gap in existing sand transport models. This enhancement
ensures a more consistent representation of swash zone dynamics. In certain scenarios, the
model demonstrates exceptional performance, outperforming existing models and show-
casing its ability to handle specific wave and sediment conditions effectively. Its flexible
design enables easy calibration and adaptation to various datasets, making it versatile for
different applications. Moreover, the model can be seamlessly integrated with other sand
transport models operating outside the swash zone. Unlike the existing models evaluated

in this study, the new model does not require a specific boundary condition (e.g., a zero

However, the model does have some limitations. It relies heavily on accurately defined
input parameters, including the equilibrium beach slope (tan(β)), sediment transport rate
at the shoreline (q0), maximum wave run-up height (R), its location in the swash zone

infiltration/exfiltration effects may not fully capture the complexity of real-world processes
as these factors are not considered. Additionally, since the model is calibrated using specific
experimental datasets, its applicability to broader scenarios may be limited.

6
Conclusions
This study focused on the assessment and improvement of sediment transport models for
the swash zone, providing a comprehensive evaluation of some of the existing cross-shore
sand transport models for this dynamic part of beach. Three models have been chosen
in this regard: the van Rijn sand transport distribution model applied in the CROSMOR
profile model (van Rijn, 2009), the semi-equilibrium Larson model (Larson et al., 2004),
and an improved version of the Larson model, referred to in this report as the Chen et
al. model (Chen et al., 2024). The evaluation has been conducted through both quan-
titative and qualitative analyses. Quantitative analysis involved testing the models with
experimental data from the literature, comparing their predicted sand transport rates
and gradients against measured values. Qualitative analysis assessed the models’ perfor-
mance in specific cases, identifying aspects that may not be evident through quantitative
evaluation alone.
The study incorporated 12 experimental datasets from the literature, each featuring vary-
ing wave conditions, sediment characteristics, and initial beach profiles. Nine of these
datasets were collected from large-scale facilities, with the inclusion of three small-scale
experiments conducted as part of a larger project with consistent Froude scaling. The in-
clusion of these small-scale experiments was considered beneficial for the consistency with
the other experiments conducted within the same project. A quality assurance process
was applied to each dataset to ensure their suitability for swash zone analysis. This step
was critical, as the primary focus of many experiments extended beyond the swash zone,
necessitating careful evaluation of their relevance. Following this process, 10 experimental
datasets were selected for further analysis, ensuring a robust foundation for evaluating the
sand transport models.
The performance of each model is assessed using two widely adopted error metrics: the
root-mean-squared error (RMSE) and the coeﬀicient of determination (r2). These error
metrics are computed for each model in every time instant across all experiments included
in the model evaluation. The analysis concluded that the Larson model demonstrated the
best overall performance, whereas the Chen et al. model was outperformed by both the
van Rijn model and the Larson model.
To use the Larson model, two key inputs, the calibration coeﬀicient (Kc) and the equi-
librium beach slope (tan(β)), were determined by tuning the model against the measured
data for each time instant. This tuning process played a crucial role in the model’s evalu-
ation, as it provided valuable insights into the model’s consistency and physical relevance.
Ideally, the calibration coeﬀicient (Kc), which is influenced by sediment and wave charac-
teristics, should remain consistent (or close) for similar wave climates and sediment types.
Similarly, the equilibrium beach slope should not vary significantly within a given wave
climate. However, the results obtained during the tuning process revealed discrepancies.
Even for identical experiments with the same wave climate and sediment characteristics,
the calibration coeﬀicient and equilibrium slope exhibited substantial variations.
This
inconsistency raised concerns about the model’s robustness and its ability to reliably re-
flect physical conditions. Unlike the initial tuning strategy, which aimed to determine the
calibration coeﬀicient and equilibrium slope that minimize the error between predictions
and measurements, an alternative approach was adopted. In this new strategy, the fo-
cus shifted to finding a general calibration coeﬀicient that minimizes the variance in the
equilibrium slope within a specific wave climate. The goal of this approach was to achieve
90
Assessment and Improvement of Cross-Shore Sediment Transport Models for the Swash Zone
==PAGE:107==
more consistent values for both the equilibrium slope and the calibration coeﬀicient across
similar wave conditions. While this alternative strategy successfully produced consistent
results — assigning a single calibration coeﬀicient for each wave climate and yielding an
equilibrium slope with minimal variation — the resulting sand transport rates calculated
using these inputs deviated significantly from the measured data. Notably, the calibra-
tion coeﬀicients obtained through the new tuning strategy were in the order of O(10−3),
whereas those derived from the initial strategy were in the order of O(10−5), closely align-
ing with the order of magnitude of the measured sand transport rates. Therefore, the
input parameters for the Larson model are obtained using the first tuning methodology,
which focuses on minimizing the error between the measured and predicted sand transport
rates.
Building on the strengths and addressing the weaknesses of the existing models, a new
sand transport model was proposed. This proposed model represents a hybrid approach,
combining aspects of sand distribution models and equilibrium models. Its formulation is
dependent on the equilibrium beach slope (tan(β)), the sand transport rate at the shoreline
(q0), the maximum run-up height (R) and its location (xR), as well as a calibration
coeﬀicient (K). The new model is conceptually tested, yielding highly promising results.
It demonstrates the ability to effectively capture both the magnitude and direction of the
sand transport, as well as accurately reflect the shape of the sand transport distribution.
The concept of equilibrium beach slope was explored in detail, as it constitutes the keel of
equilibrium models and the newly proposed sand transport model. A review of the litera-
ture revealed that the definition and understanding of the equilibrium beach slope remain
ambiguous. This gap was addressed in this study by analyzing experimental datasets,
where it was observed that a specific section of the beach profile exhibits a tendency to-
ward an equilibrium slope. Meanwhile, other sections continue to undergo morphological
changes. This region was identified as the portion of the beach profile lying between the
shoreline and the point corresponding to 20 per cent of the horizontal distance to the
maximum run-up limit (x/xR = [0; 0.2]). As a result, the slope of this specific region
was utilized as a reference for parameterization, and a new equilibrium slope expression
was introduced to represent the observed trends. The new equilibrium slope expression
combines the deep-water wave steepness and the Dean number together. It is important
to highlight that the equilibrium slope study operates under the assumption of monotonic
convergence, meaning that the beach slope evolution steadily approaches the equilibrium
slope through either consistent steepening or mildening. This assumption was necessary
because not all experiments demonstrated a clear tendency towards equilibrium, primarily
due to the limited duration of wave action in the experiments. Therefore, this constraint
made it particularly challenging to identify a definitive equilibrium state, necessitating a
simplified approach to analyze the observed trends. Furthermore, the equilibrium slope
dataset used for parameterization spans a limited range, with the mildest equilibrium slope
being 0.033 and the steepest reaching 0.22. This suggests that the new equilibrium slope
expression provides relatively high confidence in estimating equilibrium slopes below 0.22.
The new sand transport model was subsequently tested using the newly derived expression
for the equilibrium beach slope. The additional inputs required by the model — specifi-
cally, the sand transport rate at the shoreline (q0), the maximum wave run-up height (R)
and its horizontal position (xR) — are directly obtained from the experimental data. The
calibration coeﬀicient (K), however, is determined through a tuning process to achieve
the best match between the model predictions and the measurements. The results demon-
strated that the predictions aligned well with the measurements, effectively capturing both
the direction and magnitude of the sand transport overall. When compared to the exist-
Assessment and Improvement of Cross-Shore Sediment Transport Models for the Swash Zone
91
==PAGE:108==
ing models evaluated in this report, the new model was found to outperform the van Rijn
and Chen et al. models but fell slightly behind of the Larson model in terms of overall
performance. This outcome, however, is not surprising, given that the Larson model was
specifically tuned for each time instant across all experiments, whereas the new model
relied on the proposed equilibrium slope expression. Nevertheless, despite this distinction,
it can be concluded that the new model still delivers reasonable and reliable predictions
using the new equilibrium slope expression.
The new sand transport model has its strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side,
it introduces a more systematic approach to incorporating the equilibrium beach slope,
which adds physical relevance to the predictions.
The model effectively captures the
direction and magnitude of sand transport in various cases and demonstrates flexibility by
accommodating a range of input parameters, such as sediment transport rates and wave
run-up characteristics. Additionally, its performance in specific scenarios highlights its
potential to outperform existing models under certain conditions.
However, the model also has some limitations. It heavily relies on accurately defined input
parameters which can significantly affect its predictions if not well-determined. Although
the comparisons demonstrated that the new model outperformed other sand transport
models, it is important to acknowledge that the comparison may not have been entirely
fair.
The new model relies heavily on several input parameters derived directly from
the experimental data, providing it with a distinct advantage over other models that are
less dependent on such direct inputs. Furthermore, several critical physical phenomena,
such as infiltration and exfiltration effects, swash-swash interactions, and the influence of
infragravity waves, have not been incorporated into the new model. These processes play
a significant role in sediment transport dynamics within the swash zone and can introduce
considerable complexity to modeling efforts.
The newly proposed model represents a significant advancement in understanding and pre-
dicting sediment transport in the swash zone. To maximize its potential and broaden its
applicability, future research should prioritize the parameterization of its required inputs,
such as the sand transport rate at the shoreline, q0, the maximum wave run-up height, R,
its horizontal position, xR, and the calibration coeﬀicient, K. Developing robust param-
eterizations for these inputs will make the model less reliant on extensive experimental
data, increasing its practicality and usability in a variety of coastal engineering appli-
cations. This effort would bridge the gap between theoretical modeling and real-world
implementation, enabling more reliable predictions in diverse environmental conditions.
Furthermore, advanced machine learning techniques can be leveraged to parameterize
the aforementioned inputs more effectively. By employing sophisticated algorithms such
as support vector machines, K-means clustering, and other data-driven approaches, the
dependencies of the inputs on various parameters can be systematically analyzed and un-
derstood. These techniques offer the potential to uncover complex, nonlinear relationships
that traditional methods might overlook. Lastly, integrating the model into larger coastal
evolution tools and validating its performance over extended temporal and spatial scales
can significantly enhance its practical utility for coastal management.
