Abstract
In this project, the effective gain in SNR were measured technically with the phase-inversion
method for two hearing aid noise-reduction algorithms (both in full-directional mode with
maximal digital noise-reduction) in response to five categories of environmental sound
target, in addition to speech, presented from eight azimuth directions (0°, ± 45°, ± 90°,
±135° and 180° ) in a higher-order-ambisonic (HOA) reproduction of a virtual realistic
soundscape of a busy university food court at three SNRs (-15 dB, -5 dB and +5 dB). Valid
results suggest that the five categories of environmental sounds were enhanced by both
hearing aid algorithms in a similar way as speech but to different extends when the targets
were presented from the frontal hemisphere at the two negative SNRs.

Introduction
The perception of speech in complex realistic sound scenes (usually with multiple talkers),
often referred to as the “cocktail party effect”, has been extensively studied over the years.
In a noisy background with low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), the hearing impaired suffer
more from reduced speech intelligibility and increased listening effort than people with
normal hearing (Gygi and Shafiro 2013, Holube, Fredelake, and Hansen 2007). The SNR
mentioned before and hereafter refers to the unweighted, long-term averaged, broadband
signal-to-noise ratio of a sound mixture, calculated by dividing the root-mean-square (rms)
sound pressure of the desired signal by that of the unwanted noise, converted to dB.
Correspondingly, noise-reduction algorithms have been developed in modern hearing aids
to increase the SNR by attenuating noise and enhancing speech towards the front of
the user, and thus effectively enhancing speech understanding and reducing listening
effort for people with hearing loss (Ohlenforst et al. 2018, Chung 2004, Bentler 2005,
Brons, Houben, and Dreschler 2014). A typical noise-reduction algorithm is realised by
two independent processes: directional beamforming followed by digital noise reduction.
Directional beamformers are designed to improve the SNR based on spatial differences
between speech and noise, instead of spectral or temporal differences. They are effective
even when the noise and speech share similar spectral and temporal features, as long
as they are easy to separate spatially. In noisy environments with low SNR, if a listener
is able to face the target talker, directional hearing aids provide a significant increase
in SNR for the user because it is maximally sensitive to sounds originating from the front
and less sensitive to sounds incident from the sides or the back (Thompson 2003, Powers
and Hamacher 2002). Most high-performing commercial hearing aids implement a dual-
microphone adaptive beamformer consisting of two close-by omnidirectional microphones
with matching frequency response and phase. This system is capable of an omnidirec-
tional mode by using either one of the two microphones alone, a directional mode with
selectable but unvarying directivity patterns or automatically varied directivity patterns de-
pending on the location of the noise. Common directivity patterns include dipole, cardioid,
hyper-cardioid and super-cardioid (Chung 2004, Ricketts and Henry 2002).
Subsequently, digital noise reduction is applied to the output signals from the beamform-
ers in order to further reduce the remaining noise. This happens in environments where
noise is diffuse or in the same direction as the target talker. While the directional beam-
former is designed to take advantage of the spatial separation between speech and noise,
the digital noise reduction algorithm is designed to take advantage of the temporal and
spectral differences between speech and noise. The majority of commercial hearing aids
now employ the digital multichannel adaptive noise reduction algorithm. The ultimate aim
of such an algorithm is to attenuate the speech and noise mixture at a particular moment
in time and in a particular frequency channel if the noise dominates. The amount of gain
reduction in each frequency channel is decided by the algorithm based on several fac-
tors including the estimated modulation and SNR, the frequency-importance weighting
function, the sound pressure level of the incoming signal/noise, and the degree of noise
reduction selected in the fitting software. The decision rules are different and proprietary
for different hearing aid manufacturers (Chung 2004, Walden et al. 2000).
Unlike the perception of speech in noise, not many researches have been conducted
on the perception of environmental sounds in complex acoustic environments and so it

follows naturally that not much has been done in modern hearing aids to intentionally en-
hance the perception of environmental sound targets in dense and noisy environments
with low SNRs. Environmental sounds belong to a broader class of ecologically relevant
sounds and are non-linguistic in nature and represent rather inadvertent byproducts of
distal events (Shafiro et al. 2016, Gaver 1993). They play a pivotal role in everyday lis-
tening situations by transmitting critical information about their sources for one’s safety
(e.g.sirens, alarms, car horns), sense of connection to the surroundings and well-being
(Ramsdell 1978).
The purpose of this project is to objectively evaluate how different hearing aid algorithms
treat the target environmental sound in background noise. Namely, are they treated sim-
ilarly as the target speech or as part of the background noise? The phase-inversion
method by Hagerman and Olofsson 2004 was chosen to make technical measurements
of the effect of hearing aid noise reduction algorithms. This method enables the separa-
tion of speech and noise at the output of the hearing aids so that the output SNR can be
directly computed (Husstedt, Mertins, and Frenz 2018). Combinations of two hearing aid
models: a) Oticon OPN S1 miniRITE, b) an Oticon prototype and two algorithms: 1) om-
nidirectional, no noise-reduction, 2) full-directional, maximal noise-reduction, are tested.
Five categories of environmental sounds are used as target signals in a virtual sound-
scape. The target signal comes from eight azimuth directions in sequence and mixed at
three SNRs.

3
Methods
Output SNR measurements were made on two hearing models and two hearing aid noise-
reduction algorithms, with five environmental sound (non-speech) target signals s1(t),
s2(t),...,s5(t), one speech target signal s6(t), and one HOA virtual background sound
scene n(t) mixed at three different SNRs using the Hagerman and Olofsson (2004) method.
3.1
Stimuli
3.1.1
Virtual Soundscape
A 28-second excerpt of a 64-channel HOA reproduction of a busy university food court
was used to represent a virtual real-life listening environment. The food court sound scene
belongs to the Ambisonic Recordings of Typical Environments (ARTE) database and has
an estimated reverberation time T30 of 0.9 second Weisser et al. (2019). This relatively
homogeneous 28-second background sound scene n(t) was carefully chosen to avoid any
distinctive speech and disproportionate transient sound. The sound scene was sampled
at 48 kHz (16 bps) and presented at a fixed overall level of 70 dB SPL unweighted. The
overall level was defined by an equivalent mono signal which was the time-domain sum
of signals from all 64 channels.
3.1.2
Environmental Sound Target
Five categories of environmental sounds were selected from the Norms for Environmental
Sound Stimuli (NESSTI) database (Hocking et al. 2013). These sounds were mono signal
and are normalized to the same length of 1 second. From each category, six sounds were
chosen,
1. Low-frequency animal vocalization: cow, dog, lion, owl, pig and pigeon.
2. High-frequency animal vocalization: budgie, canary, cat, chicken, horse and mouse.
3. Instrument: flute, guitar, music box, piano, saxophone and trumpet.
4. Household: broom, clock, coffee machine, cutlery, drill and vacuum.
5. Transport: bicycle, car, firetruck, helicopter, plane and train.
For every category, each of the six sounds were presented four times. Each sound was
first up-sampled from 44.1 kHz to 48 kHz (16 bps) and set to the target level tested (55,
65 or 70 dB SPL) before they were concatenated in a random order to form a mono envi-
ronmental sound target signal si(t), i = 1, 2, ..., 5. Consecutive sounds were forced to be
different and a silence of 167 ms was inserted in between so that the total duration of the
target environmental sound signal was 28 seconds, the same length as the background
sound scene n(t). The spectrograms of each categories of environemntal sounds were
included in Figure A.1 in Appendix A.
3.1.3
Reference Speech Target
The mono speech target signal s6(t) was a 28-second excerpt of a chapter of Lord of the
Flies read by a Danish male speaker, also presented at target levels 55, 65 and 70 dB SPL.

Calibration
For a single speaker, the median long-term averaged broadband rms value across all 64
loudspeakers was 0.04, corresponding to 67.95 dB SPL measured at center of the room
with a testing chair placed underneath. The sound levels of the background sound scene,
targets as well as the SNRs they were mixed at, were all defined based on the rms values
of the signals in relation to the median rms value of 0.04 at the center of the room.

3.5
Estimation of SNR gains from output signals
The separated output target and output noise from 2-FD and 4-FD were first baseline
corrected by those from 1-REF and 3-REF, respectively to estimate the effective SNR
gain provided by the two noise-reduction algorithms alone.
The PSD of the separated output target and output noise were calculated using MATLAB’s
built-in function of Welch estimates with sampling frequency of 48 kHz, frame size of 1092
(40 ms) and 50% overlap. From which the equivalent transfer functions, and the effective
gain in SNR as a function of frequency could be calculated according to eq. (2.8), eq. (2.9),
and eq. (2.10).
The third-octave power spectra (from 160 Hz to 6.3 kHz) were calculated by power sum-
mation of the PSD within each third-octave bands as specified by the ANSI S1.11 standard
(ANSI 2004). It follows from eq. (2.10) that the unweighted third-octave output SNR gain
was then defined by the quotient of the third-octave power spectrum of the output target
to that of the output noise within each frequency band.

Results
For SNR of +5 dB, the error signals were not considered sufficiently small according to
the criteria specified in Chapter 2 of 10 dB less compared to the output signals. Therefore,
no valid results exist for this SNR. This could be due to the fact at +5 dB SNR, the overall
sound pressure level in the listening setup was too high since the level of the background
sound scene was fixed at 70 dB SPL. It seemed that this high sound level was distorting
the hearing aid outputs in some unexpected way. Therefore, only results from the two
negative SNRs -15 and -5 dB were presented.

only the left hearing aid gains were presented. In addition, SNR gains at the eight target

better visualization in the polar plots, the SNR gains for target types household, transport
and audiobook (male speech) at positive angles (+45, +90, +135) were mirrored to the
right hemisphere at negative angles (-45, -90, -135).

Discussion
Shown in Figure 4.1 and Figure 4.2, the effective SNR improvements of the two full-
directional, high noise-reduction hearing aid conditions 2-FD and 4-FD were corrected re-
spectively by the baseline omni-direction, no noise reduction conditions 1-REF and 3-REF.
So that any improvement in SNR was solely the result of the combined noise-reducing
effect of the directionality and the digital noise reduction algorithm. As seen, the lower
the SNR, the greater the gain provided for all types of target in the frontal hemisphere by
both 2-FD and 4-FD.
For 2-FD,
1. At the lower SNR of -15 dB, in the front (0° azimuth), the noise-reduction effect was
the greatest as a result of frontal directionality. Animal_highfreq received the most
SNR gain in this direction, followed by audiobook (speech), instrument, household,
animal_lowfreq and lastly transport. The greatest SNR gain deviation was observed
between animal_highfreq and transport at less than 2 dB.
Taking a closer look at the corresponding unweighted third-octave SNR gains in
Figure 4.3 (left) along with the third-octave equivalent transfer functions in Figure 4.5
(left), it was clear that the environmental targets were enhanced in the same manner
as speech but the gain level varied among the targets in individual frequency bands.
Contrasted with the third-octave input SNRs in Figure 4.7 (left), the gain levels were
inversely proportional to the input SNR levels, the lower the input SNR the higher
the gain, in each frequency band.
2. At the higher SNR of -5 dB, in the front (0° azimuth), the noise-reduction effect was
the greatest as a result of frontal directionality. Audiobook (speech) received the
most SNR gain in this direction, followed by animal_highfreq, instrument, house-
hold, animal_lowfreq and transport. The greatest SNR gain deviation was observed
between audiobook (speech) and transport at less than 2 dB.
Looking at the corresponding unweighted third-octave SNR gains in Figure 4.4 (left)
along with the third-octave equivalent transfer functions in Figure 4.6 (left) and the
third-octave input SNRs Figure 4.8 (left) , the same general observations and in-
ferences were made as with the lower SNR discussed before. For the specific tar-
get animal_lowfreq, less gain was received in low frequency bands below 500 Hz,
which accounted for the broadband SNR gain being lower than that of the audiobook
whereas the opposite was true for the lower SNR.
For 4-FD,
1. At the lower SNR of -15 dB, in the front (0° azimuth), the noise-reduction effect was
the greatest as a result of frontal directionality. Animal_highfreq, instrument and
audiobook (speech) received almost the same SNR gain at roughly 7.5 dB. Ani-
mal_lowfreq, transport and household received more or less the same SNR gain
at about 5.5 dB. The deviation in SNR gain was 2 dB. Compared to 2-FD, more gain
was provided by 4-FD to all targets.
As seen in the corresponding unweighted third-octave SNR gains in Figure 4.3 (right)
along with the third-octave equivalent transfer functions in Figure 4.5 (right), it was
clear that the environmental targets were enhanced in the same manner as speech

Conclusion
Both noise-reductions algorithms tested (2-FD and 4-FD) were realized via a full-directional
microphone system followed by digital noise-reduction. From all valid output SNR mea-
surements discussed, it was safe to conclude that when the targets were presented at
-15 dB and -5 dB SNRs from the frontal hemisphere, the five categories of environmental
sounds were enhanced by the two hearing aid noise-reduction algorithms tested (2-FD
and 4-FD) in a very similar way as speech, but the gain level varies across the different
categories in individual third-octave bands from 160 Hz to 6.3 kHz.

Future Works
7.1
Frequency analysis
Further spectral analysis on the SNR improvements and equivalent transfer functions can
be conducted in different frequency bands other than the third-octaves, for instance in the
same bands 2-FD and 4-FD operate in. Also for different azimuth angles other than the
direct front, at ±45°.
7.2
Behavioral test
To subjectively evaluate the effect of hearing aid algorithms on the perception of envi-
ronmental sounds, a behavioral test needs to be conducted on hearing impaired par-
ticipants with hearing aids fitted to their own audiograms and configured to the tested
noise-reduction algorithm and microphone directionality. This was the original goal of this
project before COVID-19. Due to the unfortunate situation, recruiting hearing impaired
test participants was impractical. However, the behavioral test was already developed,
implemented and ready for test participants before the pandemic.
7.2.1
Method
Virtual Sound Scene
The test is going to take place in AVIL where a test participant will sit on the chair placed
beneath the center of the 64-loudspeaker spherical array of radius 2.4 m instead of a
HATS. The head of the test participant will be positioned at the center of the sphere. The
same food court HOA sound scene from the ARTE database is reproduced by the loud-
speaker array. Instead of using the 28-second excerpt, a 45-second excerpt is selected.
This longer excerpt is also rather homogeneous and free from distinguishable speech
and loud transients. This 45-second excerpt is then divided into 45 1-second windows,
randomly shuffled and then concatenated back to the original length, where consecutive
windows are forced to be different. The random window shuffling was performed 40 times
to generate 40 excerpts of 45 seconds.
Speech corpus
The speech material consists of 40 segments (45 seconds each) of the audio book Lord
of the Flies read by one Danish male speaker. For each segment, one contextual multiple
choice question is prepared. The audiobook is presented only from the front at 0° azimuth.
Environmental Sounds
The same set of environmental sounds are used, five categories and six sounds each.
Within each segment of audiobook, six targets belonging to the same category are in-
serted at random temporal positions spaced by at least 5 seconds at one broadband
SNR level of -5 dB with respective to the level of the background sound scene at the in-
serted temporal position averaged over the length of each target sound (1 second). Each
target is presented from an azimuth angle randomly selected from ±45°and ±135°, corre-
sponding to the four azimuth quadrants. The first 20 audiobook segments are going to
be tested with hearing aid algorithm 1, from which 120 targets are inserted. Of these 120
targets, each category (six sounds each) is repeated four times at each quadrants (5 time
4 times 6). The remaining 20 audiobook segments are going to be tested with hearing
aid algorithm 2, from which 120 targets are inserted. Of these 120 targets, each category
(six sounds each) is repeated four times at each quadrants (5 time 4 times 6).

7.2.2
Response Recording
A MATLAB GUI is designed to display and record the answer to the multiple choice ques-
tion after each segment of audiobook is played. During each segment, the test subject
holds a VR handle connect to a PC outside logging the active x-y-z position of the handle
in real time. When the subject detects one target sound while focusing on the audiobook
in the front, he/she points the VR handle to any direction within the quadrant from which
the target is presented and press down on the trigger. The detection time is defined by
the time the target is played to the time when the subject moves the VR handle. A correct
hit is defined by the correct localization of the target before the next target is presented
and also when the multiple choice question about the audiobook is answered correctly.
