Abstract
This project concerns the development and implementation of a strongly coupled CFD-VPP
for a 49er skiff. The key physical behaviors of the 49er, and its interactions with the air and
water was identified.
A CFD model was implemented to resolve the hydrodynamic forces acting on the hull of the
49er, and to handle the time-discretization and motion of the CFD-VPP. Mesh convergence
studies for different parts of the hull was completed to determine the necessary meshing
parameters to sufficiently resolve the flow of the water around the hull.
A parametric model of the sail forces was implemented, to determine the driving forces of
the 49er in different wind conditions. A de-powering model was implemented, to model how
the sail forces are affected by trimming the sails, using the ease and twist parameters.
The sail forces model and CFD model of the hull-water interaction were combined, to a
strongly coupled CFD-VPP. This allows for the performance analysis: An input of wind
parameters and sail trim setting will result in a velocity of the 49er.
An optimization routine was created to find the optimal trim parameters, ease and twist, for
different wind conditions - in real world terms, this is modeling how the sailors would trim
the sails in different conditions. To achieve this, a 2D-mapping of the velocities as functions
of the sail forces, was created. Racing strategy was included in determining the optimization
target, using maximal VMG as the optimization goal. As a result, an optimized strategy for
the sail trim setting was produced.
Using the optimized strategy, the predicted performance of different wind conditions was de-
termined, using on the strongly coupled CFD-VPP routine. These results were compared to
data from real world races. The results were found to match reasonable well, considering the
simplifications made to the CFD-VPP model, to implement it within the project time frame.
The further development of the implementation of the CFD-VPP model was discussed, and
key areas for further development was highlighted.

1
Introduction
The 49er is a one-design two-person high-performance skiff used for competitive sailing. It
has a length of 4.9m - hence it’s name. It is used by both men and women - for women the
class is called 49erFX, and a slightly smaller sail plan is used compared to the men’s 49er
class. As it is a one-design sailing class, the tactics and trim of the sails are the determining
factor in outperforming the competition. Currently, the approach used to determine the
optimal way of sailing the 49er skiff at competitions is based on the sailors’ knowledge from
previous competitions and training sessions, as well as tracked data.
A different approach is simulating the performance of the 49er skiff. Being able to simulate
the performance of different strategies and sail trim settings would allow to better prepare
the sailors on the optimal way of sailing the 49er. The volume of different strategies that can
be tested would be increased substantially, as utilizing computational power is fast becoming
cheaper and more abundant, than testing using 49er sailors. Direct A/B comparison would
also be easier to do, as the exact identical weather conditions would be achievable. Hence
the aim of the project is to develop and implement a model that can predict the performance
of a 49er skiff.
Simulating how a 49er is sailing requires modeling of a complicated system, with many
variables and complex physics. Some of the key elements to include are:
• The physical shapes and construction of a 49er.
• How 49er’s are sailed in competitive Sailing.
• The physics of the interaction between wind and the sails
• The physics of the interaction between the water and the hull.
The approach of simulating or computing the performance of a sailing yacht is usually done
by creating a Velocity Prediction Program (VPP). This can be done in various degrees of
direct modeling vs. empirical modeling. This thesis report will describe the work carried
out towards creating a strongly coupled CFD-VPP, to simulate the performance of the 49er
skiff. This includes using CFD to directly resolve the forces on the hull and the motion of
the hull, dynamically in the time-space.

2
Method of analysis
To determine the velocity of the 49er, at varying wind speeds and wind directions, a strongly
coupled CFD-based velocity prediction program (CFD-VPP) is developed and implemented.
This involves a dual-faceted approach.
The first facet is creating a simulation model that predicts the velocity of the 49er based on
various inputs, such as hull shape, the wind direction, how the sail is trimmed, etc. This
prediction model will balance the forces and moments on the 49er skiff, to determine the
velocity and rotation of the skiff. The driving forces, generated by the wind acting on the
sails, will be calculated using a quasi-steady parametric sail force model, including a model
to describe the effects of de-powering the sails. These forces will be balanced by the hydro-
dynamic forces acting on the hull of the 49er, caused by the velocity of the skiff. These forces
are resolved using direct modeling in a CFD solver. When the forces are balanced in each
DOF, the theoretical velocity is determined, for that combination of inputs. The method
applied for this model is a modified version of the method proposed by Persson et. al[1].
The second facet is using the simulation model to identify which conditions that result in
the best performance for the 49er. This involves identifying the optimal trim parameters of
the sails, within the limitations of the 49er, for different wind speeds and directions. This
includes de-powering the sails in some cases. De-powering the sails is sometimes required to
limit the heeling moment caused by the sail forces. If the heeling moment becomes larger
than the maximal possible righting moment, the skiff will capsize. The best performance is
also inherently affected by the definition of the performance, whether it is maximal velocity in
the 49er’s sailing direction or the velocity, towards or away, from the wind dirrection (VMG).
To limit the scope of the simulation work, it was decided only to focus on the 49erFX class,
as the difference between the two classes primarily are the sails and the crew weights. Hence
there should be no difference in the CFD meshing of the hull.

State of the art
Multiple VPP-methods to determine the performance of sailing yachts have been proposed
in recent years.
The Offshore Racing Congress (ORC) uses a VPP to determine the theoretical performance
of a sailing yacht, in order to apply handicaps to allow different yacht designs to compete in
the same races. ORC uses a method described in the ORC VPP Documentation[2] which
uses a combination of different empirical models to determine forces and moments, and the
resulting performance. This empirical approach makes it time-efficient to compute and it is
well suited for the specific task of estimating performance in the context of handicapping.
The empirical nature of this approach, however, also limits the accuracy of the VPP model
for a specific yacht, as it uses generalized models.
Kerdraon et al.[3] has implemented a system-based approach that combines empirical mod-
eling and interpolated CFD results, to achieve dynamic simulation in the time-domain. The
sail forces were modeled using a quasi-steady empirical approach. To model the hydrody-
namic forces, a polynomial response surface was created from CFD simulations in flat water,
and combined with empirically calculated wave forces, outside of te CFD solver.
In the field of strongly coupled VPP solvers, previous work has been done by Roux et al.[4],
Azcueta et. al[5], Boehm et al.[6], Larsson et al.[7], and Persson et al.[1].
Roux et al.[4] described a coupled 5-DOF simulation model with pre-computed sail forces,
on an AC90 yacht. Azcueta et. al[5] studied the effects of including the 6th DOF, yaw.
Boehm et al.[6] and Larsson et al.[7] both describe a coupled simulation model in detail, but
with slow convergence and issues with the free surface discretization at high heel angles. An
improved method was proposed by Persson et al.[1], by implementation of an integral rudder
controller, wave damping boundary conditions, and an improved grid motion setup.
The method used in this study is based on the proposed method from Persson et al.[1] - with
some alterations made to the sail force model. Instead of using the commonly used reef and
flat model to describe the de-powering of the sails, another model, proposed by Hansen et
al.[8], is used: The Ease and Twist model, which better represents how the sails are trimmed
on a 49er.

4.2
Forces on a sailing yacht
A sailing yacht will experience aerodynamic and hydrodynamic forces. If the resulting force
in any direction is different from zero, the yacht will experience an acceleration in that
direction. When the resulting force becomes zero, the yacht have reached an equilibrium,
and will maintain its current velocity.
4.2.1
Driving forces - Sails
As the wind interacts with a sail, it will produce an aerodynamic force, acting from the cen-
tre of effort of that sail. This aerodynamic force is commonly described by its lift and drag
force components FL and FD. The individual aerodynamic forces produced by multiple sails,
can be summed to a total resulting aerodynamic force, that acts from the combined centre
of effort, CE, of all sails. Again, this total resulting aerodynamic force can be described by
its lift and drag force component. This is illustrated in figure 2, seen from a top view. It
should be noted that the sail forces also can have a vertical component.
Figure 2: Combined sail forces
4.2.2
Resistance forces - Hull
As the main hull is driven through the water by the aerodynamic forces, generated by the
sails, it will be affected by hydrodynamic forces from the hull-water interaction. This is
illustrated in figures 3 and 4 for the X1 and X2 directions (App. denotes the side forces
from the appendages; Centerboard and rudder). The X2 force component of the rudder
is determined by how much it is rotated (used for stearing). The main forces in the X3
direction are the gravitational force on the 49er and the crew, and the bouyancy force on
the hull.

5
Strongly coupled CFD-VPP
The approach chosen to simulate the performance of the 49er is a strongly coupled CFD-
VPP. The routine will be hosted inside the CFD solver, which will handle the time-discretized
movement of the 49er, as well as resolving the hydrodynamic forces from the hull-water inter-
action. The driving sail forces will be modeled using a quasi-steady parametrical approach,
including a de-powering model.
The aim is to give the CFD-VPP an input of TWA and TWS, and a strategy of how to trim
the sails. The CFD-solver should then accelerate up or down until the forces and moments
are in equilibrium, which will then produce a resulting velocity. Repeating this for different
TWA and TWS will produce the VPP.
To limit the scope of the project, the following decisions have been made:
• The heel of the 49er should remain at 0◦.
• The pitch is kept constant, at neutral pitch.
• Only flat water is considered - except for the waves generated by the 49er.
• The spinnaker was not implemented
• The rudder rotation was not implemented - The yaw rotation was kept locked.

Hydrodynamic force - CFD
STAR CCM+ is used for this project for the CFD analysis. Specifically, CFD is used to
resolve the hydrodynamic forces on the 49er hull, and thus the induced velocity, caused by
the aerodynamic forces from the sails. The sails are not modeled directly in the CFD solver,
as the sail forces are calculated parametrically. The 49er hull consists of the main hull, a
daggerboard (a specific kind of centerboard), and the rudder, as can be seen in figure 7. The
geometry of the main hull has been modeled by use of 3D scanning, to accurately capture
the shape of the hull.
Figure 7: Front, Side, and Top views of the 49’er hull
6.1
Geometric CFD setup
An overview of the CFD domain, and the inital position of the hull, can be seen in figure
8. The dimensions of the background domain are 22 × 20 × 20m. The distance from the
domain walls to the hull ensures no close-wall effects will affect the hull. The motion of the
hull, with respect to the domain, is locked off for all DOF except the heave motion, DOF:
X3. Hence to simulate the hull moving, one of two approaches is used:
The first approach is the locked off approach, where the 49er is kept stationary in all DOF
but X3, and adding velocity to the water. This method is used if the resulting hydrodynamic
forces are to be determined as a function of the flow velocity.

Both approaches are used in this project, at different stages. The free surface of the water

6.2.1
VOF - Free surface modeling and slip velocity
To model the free surface of the water, the Volume of Fluid (VOF) model and VOF waves
model are applied. The VOF model tracks the volume fraction of all cells in the domain and
tracks the interface between the two fluids. A common issue when simulating this kind of
interaction between the hull and the free surface is numerical ventilation (An example can
be seen in the appendix). This is when the simulation over-predicts the amount of trapped
air underneath the hull, in an non-physical way. This leads to the free surface not being suf-
ficiently resolved. To mitigate this issue, a slip velocity phase interaction model is included
as well. This model allows for different velocities of the air and water through algebraic
computation, and thus recovers the interface between the two fluids. On a general level, the
slip velocity phase interaction model implements the following behavior: Far from the hull,
an air bubble will have only a vertical slip velocity. Near the hull, an air bubble will have a
slip velocity tangential to the hull, allowing the air to escape. A blending function is used
to determine the slip velocity when an air bubble is approaching the hull. The inclusion of
this model was found to eliminate the numerical ventilation issue, but is does increase the
computational time however.
6.2.2
DFBI - Motion of the overset hull
The motion of the hull is included using a DFBI solver, using overset meshing. For the
locked off approach, used to obtain the resulting forces on the hull for a specified velocity,

only the X3 DOF, heave, is enabled. For this approach the DFBI body is released after 0.1
seconds and then the forces and moments on the body are ramped up over 1 second. This is
to allow the flow to initialize properly before the solver starts moving the DFBI body. For

6.2.3
K-ϵ Turbulence - Turbulence modeling
To model the turbulence of the flow, the K-ϵ turbulence model is used, with the realizable
K-ϵ Two-Layer turbulence model. The benefit of using the two-layer approach is a more
accurate near-wall region treatment, without needing an extremely fine mesh.
6.3
Meshing
Background domain
For the unrefined part of the domain, far from the free surface and the hull, an isotropic
mesh size of 0.5m was used. To capture the detail of the water close to the free surface,
some mesh refinements were done for the region close the free surface (Initially located at
z = 12.6m).
Free surface refinement 1: Coarse refinement region
For the the region of z =
12.6 ± 0.25m, an anistropic mesh size trimmer was added for the z-direction. In this region
the mesh size for the z-direction is 0.25m (50% base size).
Free surface refinement 2: Fine refinement region
For the the region of z = 12.6 ±
0.1m, an anistropic mesh size trimmer was added for the z-direction. In this region the mesh
size for the z-direction is 0.025m (5% base size).
Wake refinement:
This refinement region is defined as the intersect of the coarse refine-
ment region and a scaled up version of the hull, scaled by the factors [1.5, 4.0, 1.5]. In this
region an anistropic trimmer is applied with mesh size [0.05m, 0.05m, 0.025m].
Overset refinement:
An overset refinement region is also applied, on the surface of the
49’er hull, with an isotropic size of 0.025m.
The resulting mesh of the background domain can be seen in figures 9, 10, and 11, where
the pink mesh is the overset refinement mesh boundary (the hull is inside this region).

9
Sail force modeling
The forces generated by the sails are greatly affected by how they are trimmed. Hence, it
is important to include this behavior in the CFD-VPP model. Fundamentally the sails are
modeled parametrically as a lift-drag device.
FL = 0.5ρAirASailAWS2CL
(3)
FD = 0.5ρAirASailAWS2CD
(4)
CL, CD, and ASail are dependent on the shape and size of a sail. CL and CD are also affected
by the trim of the sail, as this alters the angle of attack between the AWA and the sails.
The optimal trim varies for different TWA. For sailing upwind the angle of attack will be
small, and it will be the lift force that propels the yacht. For downwind sailing, where the
sails will mostly be catching the wind, it will be the drag force that is most dominant in the
propulsion of the yacht. For any combination of lift and drag, and the AWA, the thrust and
side force can be calculated by:
F1 = sin(AWA)FL −cos(AWA)FD
(5)
F2 = cos(AWA)FL + sin(AWA)FD
(6)
Due to the relatively large sail plan of the 49er skiff, the skiff will often be overpowered. As
the 49er has no keel, the maximum allowable heeling moment is largely determined by the
maximum righting moment of the two-person crew. Hence, modeling of how the sails are
de-powered has to be included in the sail power modeling.
9.1
De-power modeling - Ease and twist
The most used method of modeling de-powring is with a reef and flat model. As it is not
possble to take a reef, on a small skiff such a 49er, this model is not very suitable. Instead
a different model has been implemented. The de-power modeling has been based on the
approach described by Hansen et al.[8] where the parameters Ease and Twist are utilized.
In this context, the parameters ease and twist mathematically describes the amount of ease
and twist applied in real life. Hence it is not a direct description of how the sails are actually
moved/trimmed on the 49’er, but a description of the end effect of the trimming on the yacht.
The Ease parameter, ϵ, models trim changes that de-power the sails without changing the
height of the centre of effort of the sails. The Twist parameter, t, models trim changes that
does lower the height of the centre of effort of the sails as it de-powers the sails. As the
heeling moment is reduced by lowering the vertical centre of effort, this can allow for larger
sail forces in some instances, without exceeding the limiting righting moment. Hence it can
be beneficial to apply some twist before easing the sails.

10
Trim optimization - Optimal values of ease and twist
The second facet, of identifying optimal trim input parameters for the sail, can now be
investigated. The ease and twist parameters affect the lift and drag forces produced by the
sails. The combination of these forces determines how fast the 49er will sail in any given
direction. Hence there is an optimal combination of ease and twist, that provides maximal
velocity in any given direction. For racing applications the aim is to move as quickly as
possible, either upwind or downwind, towards marker buoys. This is measured as Velocity
Made Good (VMG).
Figure 41: Illustration of VMG
It should be noted that optimizing for VMG cannot be done for TWA’s close to 90◦for
obvious reasons.
An optimization analysis was done to determine the optimal ease and
twist parameters for different values of AWA and AWS. The aim is to represent the knowl-
edge of the sailors of how to trim the sails at different wind conditions, in the CFD-VPP
implementation.
10.1
Optimization target
Two optimization targeting strategies are needed. When the TWA is close to 0◦or 180◦,
maximizing VMG is suitable. When the TWA is close to 90◦VMG is obviously not relevant,
and hence a different optimization target is needed. For the purpose of this analysis the
simple ratio fopt = V1 −10V2 is chosen, when close to TWA = 90◦. More suitable target
functions might be possible, but as sailing in this range of TWA is not often relevant for a
49’er, this was not investigated further.

==PAGE:41==
10.2
Optimization restraints
10.2
Optimization restraints
As mentioned previously, the limiting factor in terms of how much of the available wind
power can be utilized, is the righting moment. The maximal righting moment of a 49erFX
crew is estimated as from an ideal racing weight around 130 kg. The width of the 49er is
2.9m, and it is estimated that the cg of the crew can be pushed 1m further out than the edge
of the 49er. Hence:
Mrighting,max = mcrew · g · ycrew,max = 130kg · 9.81m
s2 · 2.45m = 3104Nm
(13)
10.3
Optimization method
A simple method of testing all possible combinations if AWA, AWS, Ease, and Twist, within
some boundary values, is used to find the optimal trim values. The bounderies are:
• AWA range: [0◦; 180◦]
• AWS range: [0m/s; 10m/s]
• Ease range: [0; 1]
• Twist range: [0; 1]
The AWA range is discretized by 180 regularly spaced points. The AWS range is discretized
by 10 regularly spaced points. For each combination of AWA and AWS, the optimal com-
bination of ease and twist is to be determined. These are each discretized by 100 regularly
spaced points.
Using the calculation methods described in chapter 9, the driving force
F1,Sail and the side force F2,Sail, are determined. M1,Sail is computed using F2,Sail and zCE.
To determine the resulting velocity of the 49er, caused by the sail forces, the hydrodynamic
resistance needs to be determined, as a function of the driving forces F1,Sail and F2,Sail.
10.3.1

ulations, the locked off approach was used, so that yacht was kept stationary and the velocity
and direction of the water was altered, using the same setup as for the mesh convergence
testing. Interpolation was used to create a map of the velocity in the X1 and X2 direction,
as a function of the driving forces and velocities. The result of this can be seen in figures 42
and 43.

CFD-VPP results
The identified optimal trim strategy is added to the CFD-VPP solver routine through linear
interpolation between the calculated points shown in figure 49.
With this implemented, the CFD-VPP routine does the following for each timestep:
• A TWA and TWS is specified by the user.
• The AWA and AWS is calculated based on the TWA, TWS, and the current Vs of the
49er.
• Based on the AWA and AWS, the optimal Ease and Twist is determined, from the
pre-dertermined strategy.
• From TWA, TWS, Ease, and Twist, the sail forces are calculated and projected on the
F1 and F2 forces, which are applied to the DFBI hull body.
• The CFD solver iterates, accelerating the DFBI body if the forces are not in equilib-
rium.
An example of the resulting simulation behavior can be seen in figure 50.
Figure 50: Example of the simulation behavior of the CFD-VPP model
In the example, a new TWA and TWS was set after 10 seconds. The resulting driving forces
were then smaller than the hydrodynamic resistance forces, and thus a de-acceleration began.
At approx. 27 seconds a new equilibrium was reached. A new TWA and TWS was then
again set, and now the driving forces were larger than the hydrodynamic resistance forces,
and thus an acceleration began. At approx 40 seconds a new equilibrium was reached. This
is how the CFD-VPP is used to evaluate the performance for a given set of TWA and TWS.
The following section will describe the results that was found at various TWA and TWS.

12
Current limitations and future work
12.1
Sailing mechanics to be implemented
The current implementation of the CFD-VPP for the 49er shows some promising results,
but it also has some limitations that could be improved. Some of the mechanics of how the
49er is sailed has not been implemented fully:
• The movement of the rudder has not yet been implemented. Currently the rudder is
always parallel to the X1 direction. A rotation controller needs to be implemented in
such a way, that the resulting yawing moment of the 49er is zero. This will add a small
amount of additional resistance to the 49er, and thus a small decrease of the velocity,
and additional side force. Hence it will have a small impact on the VMG of the 49er.
• The spinnaker has not yet been included in the current model. To include it, it is
necessary to create a model of in which wind conditions it is hoisted. This will influence
the sail area and possibly the optimal lift and drag coefficients of the combined sail
plan, at full power. Including the spinnaker will increase the simulated velocities in
downwind conditions.
• Pitch control of the hull has not been implemented yet. For the scope of the project,
a neutral pitch has been used. To implement this feature, first, an analysis is needed
of the pitch of the 49er as a function of variables such as crew position, velocity, sail
forces, etc. When the possible pitch positions has been established, the optimal pitch
strategy can then be implemented. As the pitch does have an effect on the velocity of
the 49er, it is relevant to include as well.
Including these mechanics in the CFD-VPP model would result in a behavior of the 49er that
closer resembles how it is actually sailed in the real world - on flat water. A further level of
getting closer to actual real world conditions, would be to include waves. Adding linear waves
can be done with current the CFD setup, using the VOF waves model. The difficult part is
modeling the dynamic behavior of how the 49er is interacting with the waves, and especially
the dynamic nature of how the crew is moving around, changing rudder angles, and changing
the sail trim. This is a major increase in complexity in terms of the necessary modeling,
and has to be evaluated against how much more the sailors will learn from the results of it.
The computational cost of a dynamic model with wave dynamics is also likely to increase,
compared to the quasi-static approach with flat water, which is a further cost-benefit trade.
12.2
Computational requirements
During this project, one of the main impediments has been the large computational time to
reach a converged result in the CFD solver. As an example, for the final simulations, where
the velocity and VMG was determined for different TWA’s and TWS’s, it took upwards of
24 hours to simulate 10 seconds. This was with a timestep of 0.01s on a high performance
Page 49 of 51
==PAGE:57==
12.3
CFD user knowledge and communication of findings
cluster with 96 nodes. The memory usage was also significant, approaching 60 GB for the
same simulations. Hence it could be an idea to look for ways to decrease the necessary
computational time for each data point.
One way of achieving this is by decreasing the amount of cells, either in the background
domain or the overset hull. This means either reducing the domain size or the mesh resolu-
tion. For both options there is the risk of not resolving the flow sufficiently. Another way
could be to apply adaptive time-stepping. The current model uses a constant timestep. This
results in the accuracy of the resolved flow is equal for both the acceleration phase between
velocities, and the end velocities. A more efficient approach could be to use a larger timestep
for most of the acceleration phase, until the velocity is almost converged, and then going to
the small time step at the end, to achieve the required accuracy. Before implementing such
a method, a study would have to be made of how the well the larger timestep resolves the
hydrodynamic forces on the 49er.
A third option is to look at different ways of eliminating numerical ventilation than the slip
velocity phase model, as this model is computationally expensive. Other approaches was
tried during this project, but the slip velocity approach gave the best results.
12.3
CFD user knowledge and communication of findings
From the perspective of producing a tool that can be used by sailors, and their coaches, it is
also important to consider the workflow of the final product, and how be findings can best
be communicated. The current implementation of the CFD-VPP model requires knowledge
of how to use the commercial CFD program STAR CCM+, which is not necessarily a skill
set that a sailor or coach has.
Furthermore, the implemented de-power model describes the total effect of ease and twist
- not exactly how the amount of ease and twist is created by trimming the sails. Hence,
the current implementation is describing what is possible, in terms of performance, but not
exactly how to reach that performance. The step of translating the ease and twist into trim
parameters could be studied further.

==PAGE:58==
13
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to develop and implement a strongly coupled CFD-VPP for the
49er skiff, allowing for a direct quasi-dynamic simulation of the performance.
In order to sufficiently resolve the flow of water around the hull, in the CFD solver, the
necessary mesh resolution was found to be quite larger. This resulted in large computational
requirements to run the solver.
The method of using the ease and twist parameters to model the de-powering of the sails,
was able to be implemented into the CFD-VPP routine. Hence, predictions of the velocity
of the 49er could be made, based on wind and sail trim parameters.
Performance results predictions were simulated using the implemented strongly coupled
CFD-VPP, and the optimized sail trim strategy, which was developed for the 49erFX. For
upwind sailing the results were found to be matching quite well with real world tracking
data from actual races. For downwind, the lack of spinnaker in the CFD-VPP model meant
that the model under-predicted the performance severely.
Finally, some key areas of the model, that should be improved upon before it can be used
as an actual tool, was discussed.
