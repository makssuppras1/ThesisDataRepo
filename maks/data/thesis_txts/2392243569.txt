Abstract
Presence of distributed energy resources is ever-increasing in modern power systems. This
includes small-scale generation in a form of small PV and Wind farms integrated in the
distribution network, opening a possibility for forming microgrids. Microgrids operating in
islanded mode pose a serious challenge to the protection design due to the low short circuit
power, as well as bidirectional power ﬂow.
Power converters are usually used as an interface between the renewable generation (such
as wind turbine or PV plant) and the grid, and mainly they provide complete control over
power injection, voltages, and current at the converter terminal. It is possible to design
several diﬀerent control strategies in each converter, allowing to provide additional support
for solving some of the protection issues.
The focus of this thesis is to present diﬀerent control strategies for power converters
and try to propose new protection philosophy for islanded microgrids.
A simple radial
network is developed and designed in Matlab Simulink software package, together with
power converter control models, including grid-forming power converter control which is
essential for microgrid operation in islanded mode.
Based on the obtained simulation results, we have analysed possible solution for microgrid
protection issues and problems.

Voltage Source Converter (VSC)
A typical VSC structure is the three-level, three-phase, neutral point clamped VSC. It
consists of three three-level half-bridge converters connected in parallel, one for each phase.
They permit a bidirectional power ﬂow. A standard diagram of the VSC is shown in ﬁgure
1. [1]

1.2
Phase Locked Loop (PLL)
Synchronization with the main grid is one of the most important stages of power con-
verter control. The role of PLL is to detect the magnitude and phase angle of the positive
sequence component of the main grid voltage under steady state operation, and especially
under unbalanced and distorted conditions. There are a number of proposed models in the
literature (f.e. [2]), such as Synchronous and Stationary Reference Frame PLL (dq and αβ
reference frames, SRF PLL), Decoupled Double Synchronous Reference Frame PLL (DDSRF
PLL), Double Second-Order Generalized Integrator FLL (DSOGI FLL, which is based on
detecting the grid frequency), etc.
The control of power converters in this study is implemented on the synchronous reference
frame, and as per [2], we will only consider the PLL models based on the synchronous
reference frame. The conventional PLL model is the SRF PLL. Under ideal conditions (no
harmonic distortion or unbalance of the voltage) it provides a fast and accurate measurement
of voltage magnitude and phase. The block diagram of the SRF PLL is given in ﬁgure 2.
Figure 2: Block Diagram of SRF PLL
The voltage vector is translated to the synchronous reference frame by using Park’s
transformation. During normal operation, the ’d’ voltage component represents the voltage
magnitude, while the phase is determined by the feedback loop on ’q’ component. The
feedback loop forces the ’q’ component to zero. In order to check the performance of the
proposed PLL, we simulated it by using a simple Matlab Simulink model, as shown in
ﬁgure 3.
Figure 3: SRF PLL Model Built in Matlab Simulink
The block ’PI Controller & Integrator’ is used for determining the phase angle θ and the

1.3
VSC Current Controller
Operation and control of power converters under abnormal grid conditions proves to be a
challenging prospect, especially with increasing demand for reactive power support and low-
voltage-ride-through (LVRT) under unbalanced faults. Control of the positive and negative
sequence currents can alleviate some of the issues arising during such conditions, and can
provide a platform for developing new concepts of microgrid protection. [1]
Most often used control models are the ’PI’ controllers in the synchronous reference frame
and the ’PR’ controllers in the stationary reference frame. In this paper, we will focus on
the synchronous reference frame control, which implements ’PI’ controllers. This solution
is extensively used and well known. The model of the standard controller in synchronous
reference frame is shown in ﬁgure 9.
Figure 9: VSC Current Control System
Currents id and iq are state variables, voltages vd and vd are disturbance inputs (feed-
forward voltage), while voltages ud and uq are control inputs. This implies that the converter
currents id and iq can be controlled by voltages ud and uq, respectively. d- and q- axis control
loops are identical, so the parameters for the ’PI’ controllers are also identical.
Under normal operating conditions, this type of controller provides accurate control
of current injection. However, under unbalanced conditions, this solution is inadequate,
because it does not provide control over both positive and negative sequence currents. This
deﬁciency can be resolved by double control loops, which is explained in the following section.

1.4
Power Converter Classiﬁcation
Power converters can be classiﬁed based on the type of operation in the microgrid. The
grid-forming converters regulate the voltage magnitude and frequency in the given microgrid,
and they operate almost exclusively in island mode. The grid-supporting converters regulate
their power output in order to keep the frequency and voltage close to the nominal values.
They are characterized by the fact that they can operate in grid-connected (as current source)
and island (as voltage source) modes. Finally, the grid-feeding converters can deliver power
to the already formed grid, which means that in island mode they can operate only alongside
grid-forming or grid-supporting (as voltage source) converters.

1.4.1
Grid-Forming Power Converters
The primary role of the grid-forming power converters is to regulate the voltage amplitude
and frequency to predeﬁned value in a microgrid operating in island mode. They are usually
supplied by stable DC source, powered by fuel cells, batteries etc. A typical control structure
of grid-forming power converter is shown in ﬁgure 13. [4]
The control consist of two cascaded loops, namely the outer voltage control loop and the
inner current control loop. The voltage control loop is fed with voltage magnitude reference
and the actual measured voltage. The error signal is then provided to the PI controller,
which generates current references for the current control loop. The reference phase angle
is obtained by integrating the predeﬁned angular frequency.
During steady state operation, only positive sequence power is regulated. However, when
a disturbance occurs (unbalanced voltage sag), both positive and negative sequence power

1.4.2
Grid-Supporting Power Converters
There are two main types of grid-supporting power converters:
1. Power Converter operating as a current source; the converter objective is to supply
the load and to contribute to voltage and frequency regulation (droop control);
2. Power Converter operating as a voltage source; the converter can operate in both grid-
connected and island mode, so there is no need to connect any grid-forming converters
in the microgrid.
Since we already have the grid-forming converter model, we will use the grid-supporting
converter as a current source. The focus of the thesis is on the voltage disturbances, so the
frequency droop will not be implemented as there will be no load changes during testing.

3.4
Microgrid Protection Issues and Solutions
As is well documented, the greatest challenge for protection of microgrids operating in
islanded mode is the problem of selectivity (false or unnecessary tripping) and sensitivity
(faults not being detected). The short circuit power of islanded microgrid is usually sig-
niﬁcantly lower, in comparison to grid-connected microgrids, making it diﬃcult to detect
a fault. Bidirectional power ﬂow in islanded microgrids makes the protection coordination
more diﬃcult.
The protection design largely depends on the rated power generation as well as on number
and location of distributed energy resources. In this thesis we have considered three diﬀerent
radial microgrid topologies in order to analyse the inﬂuence of generation distribution along
the feeder and investigate the possible protection strategies.
The simulations were performed under a big assumption that the available power of the
grid-forming (reference) converter is substantial, allowing to inject high short circuit current
into the grid.
For the ﬁrst topology when the generation is concentrated at one end of the feeder,
the current distribution is similar to that of the conventional distribution radial network. A
typical protection system for radial networks can be used, but it largely depends on the level
of short circuit power. For microgrids with low short circuit power, a protection system based
on the negative sequence current could be implemented. Simulations have shown that the
levels of negative sequence current injected indicate the presence of disturbance for all types
of faults. The problematic section of the radial network would be from buses 11 to 13, since
the current would be injected from both sides of the fault. For this section a directional
protection could be implemented based on the negative sequence current amplitude and
phase angle.
The second case that was analysed is a conﬁguration with converters located at the
middle section of the feeder. It is relatively easy to distinguish at which half of the feeder
the fault occurred, because the current in the faulty half of the feeder will be considerably
higher than the normal load current. The protection coordination can be implemented as
if the grid is comprised of two parallel feeders. Even for microgrids with low short circuit
power, the fault current would be distinguishably higher than the normal load current, since
the distance between any fault and the converters have been eﬀectively reduced, and the
fault current would be ﬂowing only through half of the feeder in the worst case.
The case that proves to be the most challenging is when the generation is distributed
evenly along the feeder. This is especially emphasized for single phase-to-ground fault. The
fault current will be injected from both ends of the feeder, and from the reference VSC
located at the middle section, making it necessary to use directional elements. Low short
circuit current will contribute to further complicate the problem. The most obvious solution
would be to design the protection based on negative sequence current amplitude and phase
angle (direction).

To make protection design in islanded microgrids even more complex, power generated
from renewable resources is unpredictable and can vary greatly due to dependence on wind,
sun, etc. Microgrids are usually designed to operate in grid-connected mode. All of this
means that the microgrid protection will have to be designed with capability to adapt to
diﬀerent grid conditions and conﬁgurations, and with the possibility to operate in both
grid-connected and islanded modes.
Power storage will certainly continue to develop in the coming years, making it easier to
design adequate protection strategies. As we have shown, power converter control can also
play a signiﬁcant role in alleviating some of the issues mentioned in this paper.

Conclusion
The main focus of this project was on the power converter control strategies in islanded
microgrid during unbalanced faults. In order to obtain adequate control, we have modelled
diﬀerent control strategies for diﬀerent scenarios in the microgrid. One of the most important
parts of converter control is the synchronization to the grid, and the model we presented in
the paper have shown capability of fast and accurate synchronization of the converter with
the microgrid. The output current control was modelled on the basis of decoupled double
synchronous reference frame (DDSRF) controller.
The grid-forming power converter is an essential part of an islanded microgrid, which
regulates the voltage and frequency in the grid, as well as performing power balancing
between load and consumption. We have shown the adequacy of the model during normal
operation, load stepping, and unbalanced faults. The model is capable of maintaining the
voltage in healthy phases at rated value without exceeding the current limit.
The grid-supporting power converter control was also modelled, proving to be fast and
reliable. The current limitation strategy implemented for this controller have maintained
the currents in each phase below the maximum value for each test that has been performed.
Two diﬀerent power control strategies have been implemented, balanced current control for
normal system operation, and constant active power control for unbalanced conditions.
Based on the simulations performed in this project, we have shown the response of power
converters for diﬀerent types of faults located along the feeder. Detecting and locating a fault
within a microgrid largely depends on the available short circuit power, power generation
distribution, and power converter control strategies. Negative sequence current amplitude
and phase angle can be key parameters for microgrid protection design. It is possible to
implement diﬀerent control strategies for power converters, and by controlling the negative
sequence current injection make it easier to detect faults and improve both sensitivity and
selectivity of protection design.
