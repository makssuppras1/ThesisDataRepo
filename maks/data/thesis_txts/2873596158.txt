Abstract 
Virus-like particles (VLPs) are a promising platform for vaccines and therapies thanks to 
their high immunogenicity, favourable safety profile and design flexibility. This study aimed 
to establish and optimise a transient gene expression (TGE) platform based on Chinese ham-
ster ovary (CHO) cells for producing Newcastle disease virus-like particles (NDV-VLPs). 
This involved integrating media selection, helper plasmid tuning and process optimisation. 
The UDP-glucose ceramide glucosyltransferase (UGCG) lipid metabolism enzyme was co-
expressed at three different promoter strengths (10, 40, and 100 RPU) to evaluate its impact 
on cell growth, viability, and VLP production. Throughout the study, the UGCG10 and 
UGCG40 promoters consistently outperformed the controls (Mock+VLP and VLP100%) in 
terms of viable cell density, viability retention, and specific productivity (qp). This demon-
strates the advantage of moderate helper expression in balancing membrane biosynthesis 
with viral protein assembly. Although HyCell TransFx-C medium sustained higher post-
transfection viability than CD-CHO, qp remained low at high cell density (HCD) due to 
limited transfection efficiency. This suggests that the cell density effect (CDE) is a bottle-
neck in the platform. This work provides a scalable framework for producing NDV-VLPs in 
CHO cells and the strategies can be applied to other enveloped VLPs and advanced biophar-
maceuticals.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Introduction to Biopharmaceuticals 
Biopharmaceuticals are a broad class of pharmaceutical products that are manufactured 
using recombinant DNA technology and biological sources such as cells and microorgan-
isms. Well-known examples include vaccines, recombinant proteins, monoclonal antibodies, 
gene therapies and cell-based therapies. These are meticulously engineered to diagnose, treat 
or prevent disease by harnessing biological mechanisms.1,2 Since recombinant human insulin 
became the first biopharmaceutical to be approved in 1982, over 300 biopharmaceutical 
products have been introduced to the market, including more than 100 between 2015 and 
2018 alone.3 This demonstrates the rapid development of the biopharmaceutical sector over 
the last 40 years, driven by advances in biotechnology, genetic modification and bioinfor-
matics.4  
The biopharmaceuticals market is set to be worth around $300 billion by 2025, reflecting 
its critical role in modern medicine.3,5 This growth is primarily due to the significant ad-
vantages over synthetic drugs, such as their high specificity, improved efficacy and reduced 
adverse effects.2,6,7 Unlike chemically synthesised pharmaceuticals, biopharmaceuticals are 
produced using biological systems, such as microorganisms or mammalian cells, and are 
typically well-tolerated due to their natural biological origin.7 The biopharmaceutical man-
ufacturing process is also more sustainable than chemical synthesis because it uses renewa-
ble biological materials and milder operating conditions, thus reducing the overall environ-
mental impact.7 This growth is also supported by the increasing demand for personalised 
therapies, particularly for cancers, autoimmune diseases and genetic disorders.2,7  
However, despite their success, these advanced therapies are still facing some challenges. 
The production of biopharmaceuticals requires complex and costly production processes, 
which require living cell systems, sterile environments, and highly controlled bioreactor con-
ditions.7 Furthermore, biopharmaceutical molecules are usually large proteins or nucleic ac-
ids that are inherently unstable and require sophisticated formulation and storage condi-
tions.1,2,4

Another key challenge in biopharmaceutical production is deciding between transient 
(TGE) and stable (SGE) gene expression systems. TGE enables rapid and flexible protein 
production without integrating DNA into the host genome, making it well-suited to early-
stage research and quick manufacturing. However, it is often associated with greater varia-
bility between batches, reduced consistency and limited long-term scalability. In contrast, 
SGE involves permanently integrating the gene into the host cell genome, enabling long-
term, stable production and greater reproducibility, albeit at the expense of longer develop-
ment times and greater initial resource investment.8,9 These issues, combined with the gen-
eral complexity of working with living systems, underscore the need for continued optimi-
sation and innovation in biopharmaceutical production.7,10 
1.2 Virus Like Particles  
Virus-like particles (VLPs) are nanoscale, self-assembled structures composed of one or 
more viral structural proteins. They mimic the morphology and antigenic properties of au-
thentic viruses but lack viral genetic material. Due to their lack of a genome, VLPs are non-
infectious, making them ideal for medical applications such as vaccination, drug delivery 
and gene therapy.11–13 
VLPs offer several advantages over traditional vaccines and viral vectors. They have high 
immunogenicity due to their repetitive, virus-mimicking surface structures, which enhance 
recognition by pattern recognition receptors and facilitate strong B and T cell responses.11,14 
They also have a superior safety profile due to the complete absence of viral genetic material, 
which eliminates the risk of replication or integration.11,15 Moreover, they are versatile in 
terms of vaccine design, as they allow multiple epitopes or chimeric antigens to be displayed 
on a single particle.11,16 This enables the development of broad-spectrum or multivalent vac-
cines. These features have resulted in the commercial success of VLP-based vaccines against 
hepatitis B (Engerix-B®, Recombivax HB®) and human papillomavirus (Gardasil®, Cer-
varix®).13–15 
Beyond vaccination, recent research has significantly expanded the therapeutic potential 
of VLPs for use in gene therapy applications. One innovative strategy involves encapsulating 
CRISPR–Cas9 ribonucleoproteins in customisable VLPs to create new tools for precise in

vivo gene editing. These modified VLPs have demonstrated promising therapeutic out-
comes, effectively treating conditions such as ocular neovascular diseases and Huntington's 
disease in preclinical models.17 
Furthermore, VLPs can be classified into different types based on their origin, protein 
composition or the presence of a lipid membrane. These structural characteristics lead to two 
major categories: enveloped and non-enveloped VLPs.12,13 Non-enveloped VLPs consist 
solely of viral capsid proteins and are derived from viruses that naturally lack a lipid enve-
lope, including noroviruses, papillomaviruses and polyomaviruses. These VLPs are gener-
ally simpler to produce and more stable during storage.11,15  In contrast, enveloped VLPs 
(eVLPs) consist of a lipid bilayer from host cell membranes, within which viral envelope 
proteins are embedded. These structures are more complex and often require the co-expres-
sion of multiple viral proteins, such as matrix, fusion and surface glycoproteins, for proper 
assembly.11,14 Despite being more fragile than non-enveloped VLPs and presenting addi-
tional challenges in terms of production and stability, eVLPs offer significant immunological 
advantages, particularly for viruses that rely on surface glycoproteins for host entry and im-
mune evasion. The correct folding and glycosylation of these proteins are essential to ensure 
immune recognition and vaccine efficacy.15,18 
Of the various types of eVLPs, those derived from the Newcastle disease virus (NDV) 
show exceptional promise. NDV is a highly contagious avian pathogen belonging to the 
Avulavirinae subfamily and is responsible for significant economic losses in the global poul-
try industry. It has an enveloped virion structure with two main surface glycoproteins, fusion 
(F) and haemagglutinin-neuraminidase (HN), which help the virus enter host cells and are 
important targets for neutralising antibodies (Figure 1).19–21

NDV-derived VLPs (NDV-VLPs) efficiently incorporate the F and HN proteins while 
preserving their native conformation and immunogenicity. This structural fidelity renders 
them highly suitable for vaccine development. Studies have shown that NDV-VLPs provide 
complete protection in vaccinated animals, significantly reducing both viral load and shed-
ding compared to conventional vaccines.20–22 
One of the key advantages of NDV-VLPs is their versatility in production. These particles 
can be generated using various expression platforms, including mammalian, insect and plant 
cells, each of which offers different benefits in terms of biosafety, scalability and cost-effec-
tiveness. In addition, the NDV-VLP platform is amenable to genetic engineering, enabling 
the presentation of heterologous antigens and broadening its utility as a multivalent vaccine 
carrier or gene delivery system.14,23 
Given these advantages and their demonstrated functional potential, further research into 
the production and optimisation of NDV-VLPs is necessary and appropriate. For this reason, 
optimising the production of NDV-VLPs was selected as the focus of this thesis. 
1.3 How to produce NDV-VLPs? 
The production of NDV-VLPs depends on the expression of three key structural proteins: 
matrix (M), F and HN. These components must then assemble to form enveloped particles 
that mimic the morphology and immunogenic surface architecture of the native virus. As 
NDV is an enveloped virus, successful VLP production requires a host expression system 
that can carry out accurate post-translational modifications (PTMs), particularly glycosyla-
tion. Incorrect or inconsistent glycosylation can lead to reduced therapeutic performance or 
adverse immune responses. Therefore, selecting the right host system is crucial for the suc-
cessful manufacture of VLPs, ensuring the correct folding, antigenicity and immunogenicity 
of the surface glycoproteins.20,22 
Mammalian cells, particularly Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells, are widely recognised 
as the gold standard for expressing complex biotherapeutics due to their ability to carry out 
human-like PTMs, including appropriate N- and O-linked glycosylation, disulfide bond for-
mation, and proteolytic processing. These modifications are essential for the functional and 
antigenic integrity of the F and HN proteins in NDV-VLPs. In contrast to prokaryotic or 
insect cell systems, which may produce improperly folded or non-glycosylated proteins,

CHO cells guarantee that the resulting VLPs closely resemble the native virus in terms of 
both structure and immunogenic potential.10,12,24,25 
In addition to their PTM capabilities, CHO cells offer several advantages for VLP pro-
duction. They are well characterised, scalable and have a long-standing track record of safety 
and regulatory acceptance in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. They can be cultured in sus-
pension in serum-free, chemically defined media, making them suitable for high-yield, 
GMP-compliant production processes. Their robust growth in high-density cultures and tol-
erance to genetic manipulation further enhance their suitability for producing recombinant 
proteins and VLPs.8,24,26 
As mentioned previously, selecting the correct transfection method is important. TGE is 
a rapid and flexible expression strategy that enables the production of proteins within days 
of transfection. Unlike stable gene expression (SGE), which involves lengthy clone selection 
and scale-up, TGE bypasses these steps, making it particularly appealing for preclinical de-
velopment and process optimisation. TGE is already widely used in the biotechnology in-
dustry for producing research-grade proteins and VLPs, particularly when time and adapta-
bility are critical factors.9,24,27  
 Given the combined advantages of accurate PTMs, industry-relevant scalability, high 
yields, rapid turnaround and regulatory compatibility, CHO cells and TGE are well-suited 
to producing NDV-VLPs. This project therefore adopts a strategy based on transiently trans-
fecting CHO cells using Freestyle transfection reagents, selected for its proven efficiency 
and compatibility with suspension-adapted, serum-free cultures, to study and optimise the 
production of NDV-VLPs. 
1.4 Cell density effect 
High cell density (HCD) cultures are a crucial component of intensified biomanufacturing 
strategies. By increasing the number of metabolically active cells per unit volume, HCD 
processes can boost volumetric productivity, shorten production timelines and reduce overall 
manufacturing costs. This is particularly important for complex biologics such as NDV-
VLPs, for which production costs are often dominated by the cost of raw materials.28,29 In-
tensification through HCD can therefore enable faster responses to outbreaks, reduce the 
required facility size, and improve economic viability.

Page 9 
 
 
Despite these benefits, HCD cultures are challenged by the Cell Density Effect (CDE), a 
phenomenon whereby increasing cell density in a cell culture significantly reduces protein 
expression and productivity during biopharmaceutical production, especially when transient 
gene expression (TGE) is employed. This phenomenon has been widely documented in var-
ious cell-based systems, including mammalian, insect and avian cell cultures used for protein 
and viral vector production.28,30,31  
At high cell densities, inhibitory molecules accumulate in cultures, including extracellular 
vesicles (EVs), secreted proteins and metabolic by-products, which can interfere with the 
uptake and trafficking of transfection complexes. EVs can bind to polyplexes or cell surface 
proteoglycans, competitively inhibiting DNA entry.29,32,33 HCD also causes intrinsic altera-
tions in cellular physiology, including reduced nucleocytoplasmic transport, changes in en-
dosomal trafficking, transcriptional downregulation and stress-related gene expression. 
These conditions have a negative impact on cellular metabolism, gene delivery, transcription 
efficiency and crucial PTMs, such as glycosylation. Ultimately, this reduces the yield and 
quality of produced recombinant proteins and VLPs.28,29,32,34 
One promising metabolic engineering approach to mitigating the CDE in TGE processes 
is to overexpress UDP-glucose ceramide glucosyltransferase (UGCG). UGCG is a key en-
zyme that catalyses the conversion of ceramide to glucosylceramide, which is the first com-
mitted step in the synthesis of complex glycosphingolipids.35,36 These lipids are integral to 
cellular membranes, contributing to processes such as vesicular trafficking, endocytosis, and 
protein sorting.37 In the context of viral and VLP production, UGCG has been shown to play 
a role in viral entry and intracellular transport, supporting the efficient processing of incom-
ing viral particles in endosomes. The importance of UGCG extends beyond viral infection: 
recent studies have demonstrated that modulating its levels can alter cellular physiology in 
a way that increases recombinant protein and VLP yields.29,38 
In high-density TGE cultures, intracellular sequestration of polyethylenimine (PEI):DNA 
polyplexes within endosomal compartments is one of the contributing factors to the CDE, 
as it prevents efficient nuclear delivery of plasmid DNA.29 Overexpression of UGCG has 
been reported to reduce this sequestration, likely by modulating endosomal membrane com-
position and trafficking dynamics. This facilitates the release of polyplexes into the cyto-
plasm. This intervention has been shown to improve transfection efficiency by up to 17% in 
==PAGE:12==

Page 10 
 
HEK293 cells, and when combined with strategies such as extracellular vesicle depletion, it 
can increase VLP production by over 45% at very high cell densities.29,38 Given its ability to 
address both extracellular and intracellular components of the CDE, UGCG overexpression 
is an attractive target for process intensification in NDV-VLP production. 
In this project, UGCG was overexpressed in CHO cells producing NDV-VLPs, with the 
additional variable of promoter strength driving UGCG expression. The strength of the pro-
moter significantly influences UGCG expression and, consequently, overall protein produc-
tivity.29,35 Promoters of varying strengths (10 relative promoter unit (RPU), 40RPU and 
100RPU) regulate UGCG transcription levels and, consequently, the glycosylation capacity 
of host cells. Insufficient UGCG expression due to weak promoters can limit its beneficial 
effects on glycosylation and transfection. Conversely, overly strong promoters can cause 
metabolic stress through excessive enzyme production, resulting in cellular dysfunction, re-
duced viability, and poor protein quality.35,36  The results will help to define an expression 
profile that maximises NDV-VLP production while maintaining cell health and productivity. 
This offers a scalable strategy for overcoming the CDE in high-cell-density TGE processes.

2. Aim of the work 
The primary aim of this project is to establish and optimise a robust and scalable platform 
for producing NDV-VLPs for use in biopharmaceuticals. Recognising the increasing im-
portance of VLPs as next-generation vaccine and therapeutic platforms, the project will seek 
to improve their productivity, quality and process efficiency by applying TGE in mammalian 
cells. TGE will be carried out using the FreeStyle transfection system.  
A structured Design of Experiments (DoE) approach will be implemented to systemati-
cally identify the optimal plasmid DNA-to-transfection reagent ratio, as well the cell density 
and culture medium composition at transfection will be studied. These factors play a critical 
role in determining transfection efficiency, protein expression levels and the final NDV-VLP 
yield. The project aims to define conditions that maximise productivity while maintaining 
product quality by integrating these variables into a controlled optimisation framework. 
In addition to process optimisation, this project will examine the function of UDP-glucose 
ceramide glucosyltransferase (UGCG) in the production of NDV VLPs. UGCG has been 
linked to improved membrane organisation, enhanced glycosylation and greater secretion of 
recombinant proteins. In order to explore its potential for overcoming productivity bottle-
necks, such as the Cell Density Effect (CDE), the expression of UGCG will be modulated 
using promoters of three defined strengths: 10 RPU, 40 RPU and 100 RPU. 
Successful outcomes from this project will facilitate large-scale, cost-effective VLP pro-
duction and expand their utility as advanced therapeutic platforms in the biopharmaceutical 
industry.

3. Materials and Methods 
3.1 Cell line and culture conditions 
The cell line used was Chinese hamster ovary (CHO-S) cells in a serum-free suspension. 
The cells were cultivated in 125 mL disposable polycarbonate vented shake flasks or 6-well 
plates. The cells were culture first in in CD CHO culture medium (Gibco, ThermoFisher 
Scientific, San Jose, USA) supplemented with 8 mM GlutaMAX (Gibco, ThermoFisher Sci-
entific, San Jose, USA). Then the cells were adapted to HyCell TransFx-C (ThermoFisher 
Scientific, San Jose, USA) supplemented with 4 mM GlutaMAX and 0.1% Pluronic F-68 
(Gibco, ThermoFisher Scientific, San Jose, USA).  Finally a line of CHO-S knockout Δtnxb  
was cultivated in CD CHO culture medium. 
All cell batches were cultivated in a Hera Cell incubator (ThermoFisher Scientific, San 
Jose, USA) adapted with a benchtop shaker (Infors HT, Bottmingen, Switzerland). The tem-
perature was set to 37 °C, the carbon dioxide concentration to 5%, the relative humidity to 
85% and the speed to 130 rpm. The cells were passaged at 2- or 3-day intervals at densities 
ranging from 0,3 to 0,5x10⁶ cells/ml. This approach was adopted to ensure maintenance of 
the exponential growth phase, with viability exceeding 95%. Cell concentration and viability 
were measured using a NucleoCounter NC-250 (Chemometec, Allerød, Denmark). 
3.2 Transient transfection and protein expression 
To produce NDV-VLPs, the plasmid construct encoded the NDV matrix protein, fol-
lowed by a green fluorescent protein (GFP) reporter sequence. This enabled the measure-
ment of transfection efficiency via GFP expression. The commercial plasmid pMAX, which 
encodes only GFP, was used as a positive control. A mock plasmid containing no relevant 
expression cassette was used as the negative control. 
Unless otherwise stated, standard transfections were carried out at a cell density of 1x10⁶ 
cells/mL with a final total plasmid DNA concentration of 1 mg/mL. FreeStyle™ Max rea-
gent (ThermoFisher Scientific, San Jose, USA) was used as the cationic transfection reagent 
and OptiPRO™ SFM (Gibco, ThermoFisher Scientific, San Jose, USA) was used as the 
serum-free diluent. 
==PAGE:15==
Chapter 3 Materials and Methods 
Page 13 
 
 
To form the complex, the required amount of plasmid DNA was diluted in OptiPRO SFM 
in a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube, then vortexed for 10 seconds. FreeStyle reagent was then 
added at a DNA-to-reagent ratio of 1:1 (w/w), followed by gentle pipette mixing. The mix-
ture was then incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes to allow the formation of the 
DNA–lipid complex. The transfection mixture was added to the cell culture at a volume 
equivalent to 5% of the total culture volume (Figure 2).  
For UGCG co-transfections, the total DNA concentration remained at 1 µg/mL, consist-
ing of 50% UGCG-encoding plasmid and 50% NDV-VLP plasmid. For the control co-trans-
fections, the same proportions were used, except that the UGCG was substituted with a mock 
plasmid. All transfections were performed in triplicate to ensure reproducibility. 
 
Figure 2. Transfection protocol using FreeStyle reagent. 
3.3 Harvesting  
For transfection efficiency measurement, samples were collected 72 hours after transfec-
tion. 700 µL of each sample was transferred to a 1,5 mL tube. The cells were then centrifuged 
at 300 g for five minutes. The pellet was subsequently fixed in 300 µL of 2% formaldehyde 
for ten minutes. The tube was then centrifuged again, this time at 500 g for five minutes. The 
supernatant was discarded afterwards, and the pellet resuspended in 700 µL of phosphate-
buffered saline (PBS). The fixed cells were stored at 4 °C until analysis. 
To measure VLP production, a 500 µL sample of the culture was transferred to a 1,5 mL 
tube and centrifuged at 1.000×g for five minutes. The resulting VLP-containing supernatant 
was carefully collected and stored at −20 °C for further analysis.

3.4 NDV-VLPs Quantification through Flow Virometry 
The NDV-VLPs were measured using a CytoFLEX LX (Beckman Coulter, Brea, USA). 
The VLPs were detected based on violet side scatter (V-SSC) and fluorescein isothiocyanate 
(FITC) fluorescence signals. The laser gains were set to 72 for forward scatter (FSC), 135 
for side scatter (SSC), 9 V-SSC and 500 for FITC. Prior to analysis, the samples were diluted 
in filtered PBS to achieve a concentration of 500–5.000 events/μL, ensuring an abort rate 
below 5%. A minimum of 20.000 VLP events were recorded at a flow rate of 10 µL/min. 
The VLPs were distinguished from background noise using V-SSC vs. B525-FITC density 
plots (Figure 3.A). The results were normalised using an internal control. Data analysis was 
conducted using CytExpert v.2.3 software (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA, USA).

3.6 Transfection efficiency analysis by Flow Cytometry 
The expression of green fluorescent protein (GFP) in CHO-cells that had been transfected 
was quantified using a CytoFLEX LX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter, Brea, California, 
USA). GFP-positive cells were identified using a combination of forward scatter (FSC-A), 
side scatter (SSC-A) and fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) fluorescence signals. The laser 
gain settings were configured at 10 for FSC and SSC, and at 20 for FITC. A minimum of 
10.000 cellular events were recorded for each sample at a flow rate of 10 µL/min. 
Gating was performed to isolate the main cell population using FSC-A vs SSC-A plots 
(Figure 4.A), followed by singlet discrimination using FSC-H vs FSC-A gating (Figure 4.B). 
GFP-positive cells were distinguished from background fluorescence using a FITC-A vs 
FSC-A dot plot (Figure 4.C) and confirmed with a FITC-A histogram (Figure 4.D). Wild-
type (non-transfected) CHO cells were used as internal negative controls to establish the 
fluorescence threshold for GFP positivity. The results were expressed as the percentage of 
GFP positive. Data analysis was conducted using CytExpert v.2.3 software (Beckman Coul-
ter, Brea, CA, USA).

3.7 Design of experiments (DOE) 
A face-centered central composite design (FCCD) was employed to analyse the impact 
of plasmid DNA and FreeStyle™ (FS) transfection reagent concentrations on GFP expres-
sion and cell viability; both measured as a percentage. The two independent variables, DNA 
and FS reagent concentrations, were treated as continuous numeric factors and explored at 
three coded levels: low (−1), centre (0) and high (+1). All concentrations were expressed in 
µg/mL. The design included factorial, axial (face-centred) and replicated centre points to 
enable estimation of curvature in the response surface (Figure 5).

statistical analysis. 
3.7.1 Response Surface Methodology (RSM) 
The FCCD was modelled using a second-order polynomial regression equation to capture 
the linear, quadratic and interaction effects of the two variables on the response. Where y is 
the response, either GFP (%) or viability (%); β0 is the offset term; βi is the linear coefficient;

3.8 Gene Edition 
The following methodology was used to delete an undesired stop codon that was present 
in the NDV-M protein plasmid: 
3.8.1 PCR Amplification 
Two pairs of primers (PR5132_CLED-PR5133_CLED, PR5134_CLED-PR135_CLED) 
were designed to amplify the plasmid in such a way that the resulting fragments excluded 
the stop codon, splitting the plasmid into two: the insert and the plasmid backbone (tem-
plate). Based on the locations of the primers, the expected sizes were 1.132 bp for the insert 
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 3 Materials and Methods 
Page 18 
 
and 4.371 bp for the template. Amplification was carried out using Phusion™ Plus PCR 
Master Mix (Thermo Fisher Scientific), following the manufacturer's protocol. 
3.8.2 Amplicon verification by gel electrophoresis 
The PCR products were analysed by agarose gel electrophoresis to confirm the correct 
fragment sizes. The reactions were run on a 1% agarose gel prepared in 1× TAE buffer 
(Thermo Scientific). SYBR Safe DNA Gel Stain (Invitrogen) was added to the agarose so-
lution at a final concentration of 5 µL per 50 mL. The gels were run at 100 V for 20 minutes, 
using a GeneRuler 1 kb DNA Ladder (Thermo Scientific) to estimate the sizes. A sample of 
the original plasmid was also loaded onto the gel for comparison. 
3.8.3 Gel Clean up 
The expected PCR bands, corresponding to the insert and template, were cut out of the 
gel and purified using a NucleoSpin® Gel and PCR Clean-up kit (Macherey-Nagel), accord-
ing to the manufacturer's instructions. DNA concentrations were then quantified using a 
NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 
3.8.4 DNA assembly 
The purified PCR fragments corresponding to the template and the insert were assembled 
using NEBuilder® HiFi DNA Assembly Master Mix (New England Biolabs). Equimolar 
amounts (0.2 pmol each) of the two DNA fragments were combined in a total reaction vol-
ume of 20 µL, including 10 µL of NEBuilder HiFi DNA Assembly Master Mix and nucle-
ase-free water to adjust the final volume. 
3.8.5 Bacteria transformation  
The NEBuilder HiFi DNA Assembly Product was transformed into chemically competent 
E. coli NEB5α cells (New England Biolabs) using a heat shock protocol. The competent 
cells were thawed on ice. Then, 50 µL of the cells were transferred into sterile 1,5 mL mi-
crocentrifuge tubes. 1 µL of the DNA assembly reaction was gently added to the cells and 
mixed by pipetting up and down four to five times to avoid vortexing and maintain cell 
integrity. The mixture was subsequently incubated on ice for 30 minutes. This was followed 
by a heat shock at 42 °C for 30 seconds. The tubes were then immediately placed back on 
ice for two minutes. 
==PAGE:21==
Chapter 3 Materials and Methods 
Page 19 
 
 
Next, 950 µL of LB broth at room temperature was added to each transformation reaction 
and the tubes were incubated at 37 °C for 60 minutes with vigorous shaking to allow recov-
ery and expression of antibiotic resistance. Meanwhile, LB agar plates containing kanamycin 
were pre-warmed at 37°C. After the recovery period, 100 µL of each transformation culture 
was spread onto the warmed selective LB agar plates. All plates were then incubated over-
night at 37 °C. 
3.8.6 Colony PCR screening  
Following the overnight incubation period, 10 colonies were randomly selected from each 
plate for preliminary screening by colony PCR. In advance, a 96-well plate was prepared, 
with each well containing 180 µL of LB broth supplemented with 1% of the appropriate 
antibiotic. 
The OneTaq® Quick-Load® 2X Master Mix with Standard Buffer (NEB) was used to 
prepare the colony PCR reaction mix according to the manufacturer's protocol. Colonies 
were picked using sterile pipette tips. Each colony was first transferred to a well of the 96-
well plate by gently stirring the tip into the liquid medium to inoculate the culture. The same 
tip was then used to add 2 µL of the mix to the PCR tube, stirring gently. The PCR primers 
(PR5134_CLED-PR1758_CLED) were specifically chosen to amplify the region of the plas-
mid containing the matrix protein of the NDV and GFP. The expected size of the PCR am-
plicon was 1.408 bp. 
Once all the colonies had been sampled, the 96-well plate was placed in a shaking incu-
bator at 37°C to allow the bacteria to grow. The PCR tubes were then placed in a thermocy-
cler, and the cycling conditions were adjusted according to the primers used and the expected 
amplicon size.  
3.8.7 Gel Electrophoresis of Colony PCR Products 
The colony PCR products were analysed by agarose gel electrophoresis to confirm the 
presence of the inserted fragments. The PCR products were loaded onto a 1% agarose gel 
and run at 100 V for 25 minutes. A GeneRuler 1 kb DNA Ladder was used as a molecular 
weight reference. 
Colonies exhibiting PCR amplicons of the expected size were considered positive candi-
dates. Five colonies showing the correct fragment sizes were selected for further analysis.

3.8.8 Culture expansion  
Selected colonies were expanded from their respective pre-seed cultures. For each colony, 
4 mL of LB medium supplemented with the antibiotic was prepared in a culture tube. Ap-
proximately 200 µL of the overnight culture from the Eppendorf tubes was transferred to the 
fresh medium. The cultures were then incubated overnight at 37 °C with shaking at 250 rpm. 
The next day, plasmid DNA extraction (miniprep) was performed on the remaining cul-
ture volume using a commercial miniprep kit (NucleoSpin® Plasmid Mini, Macherey-
Nagel) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The plasmid DNA was eluted in nucle-
ase-free water and quantified using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. 
3.8.9 Sanger sequencing  
To verify the removal of the stop codon, the plasmid DNA of the five selected clones was 
sent for Sanger sequencing. Sequencing was performed using a custom primer located near 
the original stop codon site to allow precise confirmation of its deletion. The sequencing 
results were analysed using standard alignment tools in Benchling (Benchling Inc., USA) by 
comparing the sequence to that of the original reference plasmid. Clones with a confirmed 
deletion of the stop codon and correct sequence integrity were considered successful. 
3.8.10 Full plasmid sequencing 
To ensure complete sequence integrity, two plasmid samples with confirmed stop codon 
deletions (as determined by Sanger sequencing) were selected for full plasmid sequencing. 
High-quality plasmid DNA was submitted to Plasmidsaurus, a nanopore sequencing (Plas-
midsaurus Inc.). The raw sequencing data were then aligned and analysed using Benchling 
(Benchling Inc.). The full-length sequences were then compared with the reference plasmid 
to confirm the presence of the intended edit and the absence of off-target mutations.

Figure 14.A shows how viable cell density (VCD) and cell viability changed throughout 
the culture period. Initially, all conditions had comparable VCDs (approximately 1.5 × 10⁶

transfection in all cases, though this was not significant compared to the MOCK+VLP con-

The sustained VCD observed in UGCG10+VLP and UGCG40+VLP suggests that mod-

differences in per-cell performance rather than transfection efficiency.

95%.  High viability is essential to ensure that most of the culture consists of metabolically 
active cells capable of efficiently internalising plasmid DNA. Cells that are stressed or dying 
prior to transfection are less likely to survive the additional stress associated with the process. 
This leads to lower VCD. Additionally, low initial viability decreases transfection efficiency, 
shortens the productive culture period and increases the release of cellular debris and prote-
ases into the medium. This can interfere with transfection complexes and negatively impact 
VLP assembly.

TransFx-C medium under LCD and HCD conditions. Six experimental setups were evalu-
ated: three co-transfections of the VLP plasmid with UGCG plasmids (UGCG10, UGCG40 
and UGCG100); a co-transfection with a mock plasmid (Mock+VLP); and a 100% VLP 
plasmid condition (VLP 100%), as well as a 100% mock plasmid control condition serving 
as a negative control. 
Figures 16.A (LCD) and 16.B (HCD) illustrate the evolution of viable cell density (VCD) 
and cell viability over the three-day culture period. At LCD, all conditions started with sim-
ilar initial viabilities (>95%) and VCDs (~1,5 × 10⁶ cells/mL), ensuring optimal conditions 
for plasmid uptake. By day 3, a general decline in viability was observed across all groups, 
although this reduction was more pronounced in UGCG100+VLP and VLP100% conditions, 
indicating increased cellular stress under these setups. In contrast, UGCG10+VLP and 
==PAGE:36==

UGCG40+VLP maintained higher viabilities (>80%) and achieved the largest increases in 
VCD, suggesting improved cell survival and growth after transfection.

duction in HyCell transfx-C medium in different cell densities. (A-B) Viable cell density (VCD, left Y-axis)

At HCD, all conditions began with ~9 × 10⁶ cells/mL and >95% viability. Post-transfec-

But VCD declined more strongly in the groups of UGCG100 and MOCK+VLP, whereas 
UGCG10+VLP and UGCG40+VLP sustained higher densities. This pattern mirrors the 
LCD observations, suggesting that moderate UGCG expression can partially alleviate post-
transfection stress in HyCell TransFx-C medium, even at HCD. 
The expression of GFP (Figure 16.C-D), derived from the integrated GFP in the VLP 
plasmid, varied depending on the strength of the UGCG promoter and the cell density. At 
low cell density (LCD), GFP expression increased proportionally to the strength of the 
UGCG promoter. UGCG40+VLP and UGCG100+VLP exhibited higher levels of GFP ex-
pression than UGCG10+VLP. This trend suggests that moderate to strong UGCG expression

may enhance transcription or plasmid stability. As expected, the Mock 100% control (con-
taining no GFP-expressing plasmid) exhibited a negligible GFP signal, likely due to back-
ground noise or autofluorescence. 
In the high cell density (HCD) condition, transfection efficiency decreased substantially 
across all groups compared to LCD. This is consistent with reduced uptake efficiency and 
higher stress at elevated cell concentrations. However, the UGCG40+VLP group exhibited 
a notable increase in GFP expression relative to the other HCD conditions. This suggests 
that UGCG40-driven expression provides a beneficial balance between promoter strength 
and the cellular stress response, even under suboptimal conditions. 
Interestingly, the trend in specific productivity (qp), as measured by the number of VLPs 
produced per cell per day, correlates with GFP expression. The highest qP was produced at 
LCD (Figure16.E) for UGCG10+VLP and UGCG40+VLP, but a decrease in qP was also 
observed for UGCG100+VLP. At HCD (Figure 16.F), there is no significant difference in 
qP between any of the conditions compared to MOCK+VLP, and it decreases substantially 
across all groups despite higher viable cell densities (VCDs) compared to transfection in 
CHO medium. This is likely due to a combination of productive (transfected) cells being 
diluted, competing for resources, and reduced plasmid delivery efficiency at high density, 
leading to a drop in per-cell productivity. 
It is also important to highlight the behaviour of the VLP100% condition, in which the 
amount of NDV-VLP plasmid was doubled while maintaining an overall constant DNA in-
put. In both LCD and HCD cultures, this condition resulted in relatively high viability rates 
throughout the post-transfection period, with only moderate declines compared to initial val-
ues. This suggests that doubling the VLP plasmid load did not result in excessive cytotoxi-
city under either density condition. Furthermore, robust cell growth in the VLP100% condi-
tion suggests that the increased plasmid proportion did not significantly impair proliferation. 
In terms of GFP expression, however, the impact of doubling the VLP plasmid was den-
sity dependent. At LCD, VLP100% resulted in a significant increase in the proportion of 
cells expressing the VLP-associated reporter compared to the mock control and most UGCG 
co-transfection conditions. This may be due to the higher relative copy number of the VLP-
GFP construct per transfected cell, which could drive stronger reporter expression. In con-

where transfection efficiency naturally declines.

5. Discussion 
5.1 Initial Plasmid Performance and Redesign 
Despite high transfection efficiency with the pMAX-GFP control, the original NDV-VLP 
construct showed poor reporter (GFP) expression and low qp. This suggested that the issue 
lay not in plasmid delivery, but in the design of the VLP construct itself. Sequencing revealed 
a premature stop codon between the NDV-M gene and the GFP reporter, which prevented 
reporter translation and likely disrupted VLP assembly. Correcting this defect restored the 
construct's ability to express reporters and enabled further optimisation. 
This initial assessment of CD-CHO and HyCell TransFx-C media also revealed that Hy-
Cell consistently maintained higher post-transfection viability and viable cell density 
(VCD). This suggests that the nutrient composition and buffering capacity of HyCell support 
cell health under the metabolic stress of TGE. These findings are consistent with studies 
showing that media rich in stabilising agents and with balanced nutrient formulations can 
prolong the productive phase of CHO cultures.25,28 In contrast, CD-CHO cultures exhibited 
a sharper decline in viability, which is detrimental for both transfection efficiency and sus-
tained protein production. 
5.2 DOE results 
Applying DOE to this study enables the systematic evaluation of multiple process param-
eters for producing NDV-VLPs in CHO cells. This approach has significant advantages over 
the traditional one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) method. DOE enables the simultaneous evalua-
tion of various factors and their interactions, providing a more comprehensive understanding 
of the relationships between transfection conditions. The effectiveness of this approach has 
been demonstrated in other complex viral vector systems, such as rAAV.39 
It was found that the interaction between the amount of transfection reagent and the total 
DNA concentration was significant, which is consistent with observations in MD-FCCD 
optimisation for rAAV.39 High DNA with low-to-moderate FreeStyle supported the best bal-
ance between viability and GFP expression, whereas excessive FreeStyle reduced viability, 
consistent with the known cytotoxicity of high cationic lipid/polymer doses.

5.3 Effect of UGCG on Cell Health and VLP Assembly 
Across all experimental rounds and process conditions that were tested, UGCG-contain-
ing transfections were consistently demonstrated to outperform Mock+VLP controls, thus 
confirming that productivity and cell culture performance are enhanced by UGCG expres-
sion. Improvements were observed in terms of viable cell density retention, cell viability and 
specific productivity, supporting the hypothesis that modulating lipid metabolism can pro-
mote membrane stability, trafficking efficiency and viral particle assembly.29,35 
Compared to the Mock+VLP control, co-expression of UGCG improved qP under certain 
LCD conditions and maintained significantly higher viability throughout the production pe-
riod, thereby extending the productive phase. These benefits were also evident when 
UGCG10+VLP and UGCG40+VLP were compared to VLP100% (VLP plasmid only, no 
UGCG): both moderate UGCG conditions achieved higher qP while maintaining greater vi-
ability. A critical distinction is that, although VLP100% sometimes exhibited high qP in 
surviving cells, its severe viability loss limited volumetric productivity. In contrast, moderate 
UGCG expression preserved both per-cell output and total biomass, leading to higher overall 
yields. 
These findings are consistent with those of recent studies in HEK293-based systems, in 
which tuning of the helper plasmid improved rAAV and VLP yields.29,39 In both CHO and 
HEK, optimal helper expression appears to balance metabolic support with avoiding cellular 
overload. In both CHO and HEK cells, achieving the optimal level of helper expression ap-
pears to strike a balance between providing sufficient metabolic support and avoiding cellu-
lar overload. However, the results also suggest subtle differences in behaviour between CHO 
and HEK cells: CHO cells appear to be more sensitive to excessive helper expression 
(UGCG100), exhibiting sharper qP declines, potentially due to variations in lipid metabo-
lism and glycosylation capacity between the two hosts. 
Additionally, the increased transfection efficiency and VLP production when using 
VLP100% further suggests that increasing the amount of DNA complex delivered during 
transfection could help overcome limitations in transfection efficiency and particle yield, 
particularly under HCD conditions. At HCD, GFP expression and qP were generally reduced 
across all conditions, which is consistent with the CDE.32  
==PAGE:41==
Chapter 5 Discussion 
Page 39 
 
 
This finding is supported by the results of HCD experiments conducted using the HyCell 
TransFx-C medium. These experiments demonstrated improved post-transfection viability 
and sustained growth of cultures compared to CD-CHO, confirming the suitability of HyCell 
for maintaining healthier cells under TGE conditions. This translates into relatively high 
volumetric yields of approximately 5 × 10⁷ VLPs/mL. However, when this value was nor-
malised to the IVCD, the resulting qP was unexpectedly low. This discrepancy reflects a 
scenario in which a large number of viable cells are maintained, but the per-cell output is 
limited, likely due to low transfection efficiency at HCD.  
One possible explanation is that insufficient DNA–reagent complexes are available per 
cell in denser cultures, resulting in fewer cells producing VLPs. Increasing the total amount 
of DNA complexes delivered at the time of transfection, while carefully controlling for cy-
totoxicity, could improve the proportion of productive cells and thereby increase qP under 
HCD conditions. Optimising this parameter will be critical for translating the volumetric 
advantages of HCD cultures into true per-cell productivity gains.

6. Conclusion and Outlook 
This project established an initial transient transfection platform to produce Newcastle 
Disease Virus-like particles (NDV-VLPs) in CHO cells, with the objective of supporting 
VLP-based biopharmaceutical development. Correcting the initial VLP plasmid restored its 
expression capability, enabling the systematic optimisation of transfection conditions using 
a Design of Experiments (DOE) approach. Analysis showed that DNA and FreeStyle™ re-
agent concentrations significantly influence transfection efficiency, cell viability and VLP 
yield. 
This study demonstrated that co-expressing UGCG in CHO cells can significantly en-
hance Newcastle disease virus (NDV)-virus-like particle (VLP) production. This is achieved 
by improving cell viability and maintaining higher viable cell densities, as well as increasing 
specific productivity (qP), compared to both the Mock+VLP and VLP100% controls. Mod-
erate UGCG expression levels (UGCG10 and UGCG40) consistently outperformed high ex-
pression (UGCG100) across all experimental rounds, confirming that fine-tuning helper 
plasmid strength is essential to avoid metabolic overload while supporting lipid metabolism 
for efficient VLP assembly. These findings are consistent with those reported in other TGE-
based systems, such as HEK293, and also highlight host-specific sensitivities that require 
optimisation tailored to CHO cells. 
Media selection also emerged as a critical factor, with HyCell TransFx-C sustaining 
higher cell viability and growth post-transfection than CD-CHO. However, despite achiev-
ing high volumetric yields at HCD, the normalised qP in HyCell remained low due to subop-
timal transfection efficiency, likely caused by limited availability of the DNA–reagent com-
plex per cell in dense cultures. This highlights a significant limitation of the platform: the 
CDE, which restricts both transfection efficiency and per-cell productivity under HCD con-
ditions. 
To further enhance NDV-VLP production, future work should focus on optimising trans-
fection conditions for the HyCell TransFx-C medium. Additionally, adjusting the timing of 
transfection relative to the cell growth phase could help to align cellular readiness with DNA 
delivery, potentially improving expression outcomes. 
==PAGE:43==
Chapter 6 Conclusion and Outlook 
Page 41 
 
 
Another promising strategy is to implement a media replacement step prior to transfec-
tion. This could help to remove accumulated metabolic by-products and inhibitory com-
pounds, improve nutrient availability and enhance transfection efficiency, particularly in 
dense cultures where stress and waste build-up are more pronounced. Incorporating this step 
could also help to synchronise cellular conditions and reduce variability across replicates. 
Ultimately, translating these optimised conditions into scalable bioprocesses will neces-
sitate the development of robust protocols for both the upstream and downstream processes. 
Scaling up to bioreactor systems with controlled parameters will be crucial to achieving re-
producibility and yield. In parallel, establishing VLP purification workflows will be essential 
for assessing particle quality, purity and structural integrity.
