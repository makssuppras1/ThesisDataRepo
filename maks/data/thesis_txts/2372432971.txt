Abstract 
This thesis examines the kinetics of the formation of scale from existing literature. The focus is on 
the common scales in the North Sea; BaSO4, SrSO4, CaCO3, FeCO3 and FeS.  
There has been some work conducted previously on the topic, but there is not an abundance of 
work on the subject. The diffusion reaction theory of crystal growth seems to be the best way of 
consistently describing the kinetics of the formation. This is because the expression for growth is 
simple and the experiments for determining the specific values can be conducted similarly for all the 
different scales hereby yielding consistent results across the spectrum. Other models than the 
diffusion reaction models are also presented and they give a more nuanced picture of the growth of 
the scale.  
The diffusion reaction models are used along with ScaleCERE to numerically simulate the growth of 
scale. For the case of BaSO4 and SrSO4 the simulations are done with pure binary mixtures and 
together. The results show that there is not much difference in the results of the model when the 
two scale re either modeled together or apart. Other models suggest that there should be some 
interaction between the two minerals when precipitating, therefore more research is needed in the 
area.  
There is a discrepancy between the scale precipitated in the bulk solution and that which is 
deposited on a surface. This is interesting because as long as the scale remains in the bulk solution 
and is not deposited it is not as big a problem.  
There are still more experiments that need to be conducted in order to model the kinetics of scale 
formation accurately.

Resumé 
Dette speciale undersøger kinetikken ved mineraludfældning gennem eksisterende litteratur. Fokus 
er på de mest almindelige mineraler der udfælder i Nordsøen; BaSO4, SrSO4, CaCO3, FeCO3 og FeS. 
Der er lavet en del arbejde indenfor området tidligere, men der er ikke en overflod af videnskabelige 
artikler tilgængelige. Af de gængse modeller er ”Diffusion Reaction” modellen den bedste til at 
beskrive mineraludfældningen fra en given opløsning. Udtrykket for mineraludfældningen er simpelt 
og de eksperimenter der skal til for at bestemme de specifikke værdier der hører til det enkelte 
mineral kan udføres på alle typer mineral. Således kan mineraludfældningen sammenlignes på tværs 
af mineraler. Der er findes også andre modeller som giver et mere detaljeret og nuanceret indblik i 
mineraludfældningen. Disse er også præsenteret i specialet. 
Diffusion- og reaktionsmodellen er brugt sammen med ScaleCERE til numerisk simulering for at 
undersøge hastigheden af mineralvækst. BaSO4 og SrSO4 udfældning er undersøgt bade som rene 
binære opløsninger af ioner og som en samlet blandet opløsning. Resultaterne viser at der ikke er 
den store forskel på den modellerede hastighed for mineraludfældningen ved rene binære 
blandinger og ved den samlede opløsning. Der er anden litteratur der beskriver at der er en 
hastighedsforskel på udfældningen grundet en interaktion mellem de to mineraler og derved skal 
det undersøges nærmere. 
Der er en forskel mellem den mængde mineral der udfældes i opløsning og den mænge minreal der 
sætter sig på en overflade der ikke i forvejen er et mineral. Det er interessant da mineral der ikke 
sætter sig på en overflade men bliver i opløsningen ikke nødvendigvis er et problem. 
Det er stadigt muligt at lave eksperimenter således at det er muligt at modellere kinetikken ved 
mineraludfældning mere præcist.

Investigations of the Kinetics of scale formation. 
The purpose of this thesis is investigate the kinetics of the common scaling agents in the north sea – 
BaSO4, SrSO4, CaCO3, FeCO3 and FexS – in order to prepare for the implementation of the kinetics 
into an already existing model for prediction of scale formation which is based on thermodynamic 
equilibrium. 
The work is done primarily through a study of existing literature and the comparison of the different 
results on each scaling agent.  
The results will be discussed and a model for predicting each of the scaling agents will be produced 
as the best fit from existing literature.  
Recommendations for further work based on the existing results obtained in all literature will also be 
discussed and made.

1 Introduction 
The energy consumption and demand of Denmark and indeed the world is rising and is most likely to 
continue to rise for the foreseeable future (IEA). In the past, fossil fuels such as Coal, Oil and Gas 
have driven the energy production, but this is changing as more renewables enter the energy 
system. However as seen on Figure 1 the fossil fuels are expected to still have a significant role to 
play. 
 
Figure 1 - Expected global fuel consumption (BP Energy Outlook 2035, 2015) 
The Paris agreement reached in 2016 calls for all countries in the world (now with the exception of 
the United States) to take steps towards mitigating climate change (UNFCCC). The future of energy 
production is therefore changing as cleaner technologies are utilised and old technologies such as 
coal are phased out.  
Oil and Gas does still have a significant role to play during the transition into a clean energy future, 
as seen on Figure 2, and it is therefore still important to look into these technologies. 
 
Figure 2 - Forecasted use of fuels in the Danish energy mix (Energistyrelsen, 2017a)

 In order to produce from a reservoir a significant amount of money needs to be invested and the 
wells should therefore produce for a long time until the investment is repaid and the wells become 
profitable. Another strategy of meeting demand for oil and gas is to prolong the life of the wells so 
that they produce more for longer and then a very large investment is not needed. One way of 
maximizing production from a reservoir is by injecting water through some of the wells and 
producing from others. This has been done in the north sea for a long time and today the production 
of water actually supersedes the production of oil from the reservoirs. 
 
Figure 3 - North Sea oil and water production (Energistyrelsen, 2017b) 
While the injection of sea water into the reservoir indeed boosts production, there are some issues 
that arise. Most notably mineral scale is formed, both is the reservoir, but also in the production 
equipment where the effective internal diameter is then lowered and the maximum production 
capability of a well with scale problems is lowered. Managing scaling problems will extend the 
lifetime of a well and therefor also increase the total production from an already developed field. 
Prolonging the life of existing infrastructure and production equipment will help offset large 
investments needed for new production that is then required to produce to cover costs and be 
profitable even though the world is moving away from fossil fuels. The prolonging of current 
production will help cover oil demand during the transition period. 
Scaling problems are not limited to just oil/gas wells, but also for geothermal wells where 
completely carbon free heat can be recovered by circulating water from the surface down into a 
reservoir where the temperature is higher so that the heat can be extracted at the surface. 
Geothermal project are not as profitable as Oil and Gas projects are, therefore it is important to be 
sure that the expected production is also what will be produced for these projects which is why 
understanding scale formation is important. The Danish government has looked into utilising 
geothermal energy as part of the energy mix and the geological survey of Denmark (GEUS) have 
produced a map showing the potential for geothermal energy.

The thermodynamic equilibrium and resulting scale formation is relatively well understood, however

2 Theory 
This section goes into the theory behind crystallisation and solubility of electrolytes. 
2.1 Thermodynamic equilibria 
The question of whether a chemical reaction will occur is primarily up to the equilibrium state of the 
chemical system. The equilibrium cannot tell how fast a certain reaction will occur, that will depend 
on the kinetics of the specific reaction and this will be covered more extensively in a later chapter.

The saturation index has the ability to predict whether or not a solution has an oversaturation of a

2.5 Nucleation 
When the saturation index is above one and the solution is supersaturated then the salts should 
precipitate and nucleate, however this is not always the case. Solutions that are supersaturated with 
some salt have a metastable zone where, even though there is supersaturation, the salt will not 
precipitate. There needs to be a certain amount of supersaturation in order for the spontaneous 
precipitation and nucleation of the salt. This is due to the fact that a certain amount of energy 
(driving force) is needed in order to overcome the change in Gibbs energy needed for nucleation of a 
particle. 
The above is true for a solution without impurities or other crystals present, however the 
precipitation of the salts can be promoted by a couple of factors such as salting out agents or by 
impurities. In subsurface conditions, such as oil reservoirs, it is assumed that there are plenty of 
impurities for the crystals to grow on. 
Nucleation is not homogenous, but happens in various stages. Most commonly the nucleation is 
stated as being either primary or secondary where the primary nucleation is all nucleation that does 
not contain crystalline matter, whereas the secondary nucleation does (Mullin, 2001). The primary 
nucleation can be split into two parts where one is the spontaneous nucleation from a 
supersaturated liquid known as the homogeneous nucleation. Then there is the other nucleation 
based on foreign particles that is known as the heterogeneous nucleation.

2.7 Crystal Growth 
There are several theories regarding crystal growth that are built upon different theories: 
2.7.1 Surface energy theories 
The surface energy theories are practically similar to that of a liquid drop on a surface where the 
drop will minimize the surface energy and assume a corresponding shape. The same principle can be 
used in crystal growth where the crystals will grow out from the surface that adds the lowest 
amount of energy to the system (Mullin, 2001). 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
2.7.2 Adsorption layer theories 
In the adsorption layer theories units of crystal will land on the surface of the growing crystal and 
“move” towards a centre where the crystal is growing from adding to the crystal growth. Ideally a 
layer will finish growing before the next layer starts on top, but this is not usually the case. The 
growth units of the crystal exists in the “Adsorption layer” that can be categorized as a layer that is 
neither in the solution nor a part of the crystal. This layer is usually up to 10 nm thick but can be 
smaller (Mullin, 2001). 
2.7.3 Kinematic theories 
Kinematic theories look at the evolution of steps of the growing crystals. The speed with which the 
steps grow forward are measured in these theories. In the kinematic models the steps of the 
growing crystal will be at different elevations and the steepness of the growth side of the crystal has 
some influence on the speed as well (Mullin, 2001). 
2.7.4 Diffusion reaction theories 
The diffusion reaction theories are built upon the idea that the deposition of the solid onto a surface 
is a diffusion process. The process of deposition onto the surface is governed by the concentration at 
the solid surface and the concentration of the bulk solution, more specifically the difference in 
concentration. This means that at low saturations the growth is slow and at high saturations the 
growth is high. The general expression for diffusion reaction is usually presented as below in 
equation (2.7.4.1) where 𝑘 is the rate constant, 𝑆 is the surface area, 𝐶 the bulk concentration, 𝐶𝑒𝑞

3 Bariumsulphate 
One of the more common forms of mineral scale in the oil and gas industry is Bariumsulphate. 
Bariumsulphate is also more often referred to as Barite or Baryte in both literature and daily life. 
Barite is formed when the Barium ion Ba2+ is mixed with sulphur in its oxidised state as the sulphate 
ion SO42- is sufficient concentration so that the saturation index is above 1. 
In a normal marine environment, the saturation with regards to barite is below 1 and so the 
precipitation does not occur in the water column by itself (Griffith & Paytan, 2012). In order to 
maintain pressure inside an oilfield, seawater is injected in to the hydrocarbon bearing formation 
(Ragulin et al., 2006). When the Sulphate-rich sea water is mixed with the Barium rich formation 
water the mixture becomes oversaturated with regards to Barite and then the mineral will 
precipitate (Griffith & Paytan, 2012; Merdhah & Yassin, 2009; Poonoosamy et al., 2016; Stark, 2004; 
Zhen, 2016). This precipitation can occur over a wide variety of pressures and temperatures as 
shown on Figure 8. These pressures and temperatures can be found in the deeper formations of the 
earth (Griffith & Paytan, 2012). 
 
Figure 8 - Barium precipitation in geological conditions (taken from (Zhen, 2016)) 
As water injection for pressure maintenance is occurring in oil fields around the globe, mineral scales 
have also been found in all regions of oil and gas production (Mavredaki, Neville, & Sorbie, 2008). 
The mineral scales are found in many different systems that is used in the oil and gas industry such 
as down hole pumps, tubing, casing flow lines, heat treatment equipment, tanks as well as most 
other production equipment (Moghadasi, Muller-Steinhagen, Jamaialahmadi, & Sharif, 2006).  
Traditionally the way Barite scale has been managed has been with inhibitor squeeze treatments 
(Tantayakom, Fogler, Charoensirithavorn, & Chavadej, 2005) where the well is shut in for a time as 
not producing. This is a costly manoeuvre due to the lack of production from the well in the time it 
takes for the treatment to be effective (Browning & Fogler, 1995). However developing an inhibitor 
for limiting the scale formation that is suitable is not an easy task and remains a challenge (Merdhah 
==PAGE:25==

& Yassin, 2009). Another way of removing barite scale is mechanically where the well will need to by 
shut and it is therefore also a costly way of removing the scale (Zhen, 2016). 
Modelling and predicting the growth of Barite scale can therefore be a valuable tool in order to 
mitigate the impact of scale formation. The thermodynamic equilibrium of the scale formation is 
reasonably well understood and modelled (Villafafila Garcia, 2005). However, there is a need for the 
kinetics of scale formation to be coupled with the thermodynamic equilibrium model in order to fully 
understand the phenomenon. There has been conducted research into the kinetics of barite 
formation since the late 50ies (Nielsen, 1958).

4 Strontium Sulphate 
Another major sulphate scaling mineral is Strontium Sulphate, SrSO4, also known as Celestite or 
Celestine. The mineral is naturally found in sedimentary rocks such as limestone and sandstone that 
are also common oil reservoir rocks. The crystals belong to the orthorhombic system and are in that 
way also very similar to Barite, BaSO4 (Green, 1970). Celestite forms as scale when the fossil water 
that has a high concentration of Strontium ions mixes with the incompatible injected waters used for 
pressure maintenance that is rich in oxidised sulphur, SO4 (Ragulin et al., 2006). This is exactly the 
same mechanic that occurs for Barite as described earlier. The minerals Barite and Celestite are so 
similar that the cations, Sr2+ and Ba2+, are incorporated into the same mineral creating a solid 
solution (YuHang et al., 2007).  
The crystal cell of strontium sulphate is orthorhombic with the following characteristics as shown in  
(Simonov & Troyanov, 1987):  
 
Figure 14 Crystal cell of Strontium Sulphate (a = 8.389 Å b = 5.365 Å c = 6.885 Å) 
Barite is also orthorhombic and has a size very similar to Celestite. The two crystals are also 
isomorphs (Villafafila Garcia, 2005). 
The effect of the similarity of the crystalline structures will be examined at the end of this section. 
The kinetics of the formation of Strontiumsulphate scale have been examined differently in 
literature.  
Analogue to Barite the two main approaches has been to either look at the spontaneous 
precipitation of the crystals in a reaction vessel where the concentration of strontium is measured 
over time or to use an atomic force microscope in order to examine the mechanisms of growth as 
well as the speed of it.

5.4 Precipitation and deposition. 
Many studies have been designed is such a way that the measurements of the falling concentration 
of ions in the solution is equivalent to the formation of the scaling minerals (Leung & Nancollas, 
1978; Nancollas, 1969). However a study by Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2005) has looked into the 
mechanisms of precipitation and deposition onto a given surface. 
By looking at the concentration in the bulk solution along with the mass on a surface it is possible to 
say something about the relationship or differences between the precipitation and the deposition. 
This is important to know when exploring options in scale mitigation and control. 
By using an electrochemical technique utilising rotating disc electrodes for examination of the 
surface coupled with inductively coupled plasma examination of the bulk fluid (Chen et al., 2005).  
By utilising the fact that reduction of oxygen happens at the active site of the electrode and that that 
is also where the scale will form. The scale formation, in this specific case CaCO3 inhibits the 
reduction of oxygen on the active surface of the electrode and it is therefore possible to measure 
the formation of scale on the surface. From the inhibition of the reduction of oxygen the area 
covered by scale is determined, but the actual mass of scale must be determined by physically 
removing the electrode from the solution at a set time, dissolve the scale and determine the 
concentration that subsequently gives the total mass. The bulk concentration of precipitated scale is 
measured using inductively coupled plasma (Chen et al., 2005; Neville, Hodgkiess, & Morizot, 1999) 
A more detailed description and in depth discussion of the technique can be found in the work by 
Neville et al. (Neville et al., 1999).  
In works utilising this method it has been shown that there is a difference between the amount of 
scale that is deposited upon the surface and the amount of scale that precipitates in the bulk 
solution (Chen et al., 2005; Neville et al., 1999)

6 Iron Carbonate 
 
Scale consisting of Iron Carbonate, FeCO3, is found in some formations in the north sea and other 
places around the world (Figueiredo, Junior, Flaten, Beck, & Seiersten, 2015). Usually iron carbonate 
is found in nature as the mineral Siderite (“Siderite Datasheet,” 2005).  
The FeCO3 precipitates from the solution when the iron ions are oxidised to Fe2+ and when there are 
carbonate ions in the injection fluid. However if the reservoir is particularly sour, then the iron ions 
may precipitate as FeS instead (Figueiredo et al., 2015).   
Analogue to the kinetics of CaCO3 the rate of nucleation can be described by the rate equation as

8 Discussion 
In this section a discussion of the different models of the kinetics of scale formation will follow.  
There is a general tendency for models to be based on surface reactions, single crystal growth or in 
bulk precipitation (seeded growth) experiments.  A graphic summary of the discussed kinetic models 
can be seen in Figure 27. 
8.1 Reaction Diffusion Models 
The top equations for each scaling mineral is a kinetic model describing the seeded growth of 
crystals in a bulk solution.  
The driving force in these models are the saturation index with respect to the salts in the solution. 
The higher the saturation index, the faster the precipitation will be. This is true not only for the 
seeded growth experiments and models, but also for the other types of kinetic models. The 
saturation index and the effect on the kinetics will be discussed later. 
8.1.1 Rate constants 
The rate constant, k, is what differs between the minerals as they are unique to each. The rate 
constant are reported in with different values as they may refer to concentrations [M/s], mass 
[mol/s] or a speed [m/s]. This depends on how they were measured in the first place and some are 
also reported per seeded area as well. 
A common characteristic of the rate constant in the diffusion reaction model is that they can all be 
described by the Arrhenius equation and therefore are functions of temperature.

Calculating equilibrium concentrations 
ScaleCERE gives the saturation index before and after equilibrium as well as the amount of scale 
agent that has precipitated. The inputs into the model is temperature, pressure and the amount of 
ions. The temperature is kept constant to the value that is needed whereas the amount of ions is 
varied to keep the saturation index at the desired value by using goal seek in Excel. 
The amount of ions added to the solution in order to get given saturation index is now known as well 
as the amount of precipitate. By a simple mole balance it is possible to obtain the amount of ions 
that are left in the solution and thereby the equilibrium concentration is found.  
This calculation needs to be repeated for each different step of a simulation in order to get the right 
values. All values used can be found in the appendix.

8.3 Single crystal growth 
Some other models look at the specific single crystals and how fast they grow in order to get a 
kinetic expression of the growth. 
8.3.1 Stack & Godinho 
A notable application is the work by Stack & Godinho (Godinho & Stack, 2015) on the growth of a 
single BaSO4 crystal.  
By looking at specific growths in specific mediums growths can be modelled by equations, however 
they are not a function of saturation index, so it is difficult to get any dynamic results from the 
proposed model. It is a good way to see how a crystal grows as a qualitative tool, but for applications 
in scale control and mitigation it is not necessarily a good model.

8.4.2 Calculation 
To further add to the complexity there are several different ways of calculating the activities and 
solubility product there are being used. Most notably PHREEQC by the United States Geological 
Survey (USGS), ScaleSoftPitzer by Rice University and ScaleCERE from the Techincal University of

Depending on what programme is used for the calculations, slight variations will occur between each 
one. It is based on experience from calculating the saturation index for several of the solutions in 
this thesis that there may be somewhat of a difference. Exactly why there is a discrepancy has not 
been examined as it was out of the scope of this project. 
To summarize it is important to look at which way the saturation index is defined as well as the 
programme used to calculate said index before evaluating the kinetic model. Future research would 
benefit from a standardised set of units and definition to make the results more easily comparable. 
8.4.3 Surfaces 
In the work by Neville et al. it is shown that there is a difference in the amount of precipitate in the 
fluid and the amount of scaling agent on the surface. There is not as much scale formation on the 
surface as what would be predicted by the seeded growth experiments and this should be taken into 
account when interpreting simulations and extrapolating into real systems. The assumption must be 
that the models will overshoot the actual scaling as some of the precipitated crystals are likely to 
stay in the solution and be flushed out before being deposited onto the surface. This is not shown 
directly but is likely to be a consequence of the results obtained by Neville et al..  
8.4.4 Sensitivities 
As the main driver of the kinetics is the degree of saturation it is indeed very important to take a 
look at what factors may influence the precipitation rate. Any impact onto the solubility of an 
electrolyte in solution will have an influence on the kinetics of the formation of scale. The CO2 partial 
pressure is poised to have a great influence on the kinetics of the formation of the carbonates due to 
the changes solubility products and thereby also the SI of the salts. Total pressure does also have 
somewhat of an effect, but it is not that significant compared to the chemical influences on SI and 
thereby on the kinetics.  
However it is interesting to note that the temperature is present in the Arrhenius expression and 
also has a significant role to play in the saturation of the salts the same is not the case with the

pressure, but that does not rule out the possibility of the pressure having an effect on the rate 
constant.

8.6 Modelling potential 
From all the different models that have been presented in this thesis it is only the seeded growth 
methodology that yields the same type of model for all scaling agents. In order to get consistent 
results that can be used for all scale formations, then the seeded growth equations should be 
implemented as the standard kinetic model. The other models presented in this thesis give a good 
qualitative indication as to what may impact the growth of scale as well as the specific growth 
mechanics such as the spiral growth mechanism and the kink site integration. Models also show that 
there is co-precipitation and that the growth slows when both solutions contain ions in a saturated 
amount. 
 
9 Future work 
In order to have a greater understanding of the formation of scale, it could be interesting to look 
into the co-precipitation of the different scales eg. Sulphate or Carbonates. Seeded growth 
experiments could be done for each group to get rate expressions for the formation and 
precipitation of the crystals when not only in pure solutions. 
Another piece of work that should be done is to look at the relationship between what is 
precipitated in the bulk solution and what is deposited on a surface. There may be some of the 
precipitated crystals that are not deposited and are flushed out resulting in an overestimation of 
scale on the surface by the models.  
Both the various partial pressures, such as the CO2 partial pressure and total pressure may have an 
effect not only on the saturation and thereby indirectly on the kinetics, but perhaps also on the rate 
constant and could be investigated in order to either confirm or rule it out.

10 Conclusion 
A lot of work has been done in understanding the kinetics of scale formation over the last couple of 
decades. The work is diverse and covers many different aspects of the growth of crystals from 
solutions. The seeded growth experiments can consistently be done for each of the scaling minerals 
examined in this thesis and thus makes the diffusion reaction models the best to compare between 
the scales. The seeded growth models are also relatively simple and can give a decent picture of the 
precipitation and crystallisation of the scale minerals from a solution. There are some issues that 
need to be taken into account with the differences between the precipitation from the fluid and the 
deposition onto a surface that cause the seeded growth models to overestimate the scale on the 
surface.  
There are still many unanswered questions in the kinetics of scale formation, especially when it 
comes to non-binary mixtures where there still needs to be some research done in order to get 
closer to “real” systems.
