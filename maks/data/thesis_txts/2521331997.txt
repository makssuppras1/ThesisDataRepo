Abstract
Hybrid Wind & Solar Energy - Case Study
In 2020 there is more focus on sustainable energy than ever before. With the goal of having
a carbon neutral energy strategy, the weaknesses and potential shortcomings of the proposed
energy solutions needs to be acknowledged and properly managed - speciﬁcally the invari-
ability of renewables such as wind and solar energy. Through the use of hybridised power
plants (HPP) containing both solar photovoltaic power production and wind energy power
production, the risk of going entirely without one resource is signiﬁcantly diminished. With
the introduction of hybrid power plants come several new issues that need to be taken into
account, including optimal PV/wind ratios and sizing based upon regional weather data, grid
capacity and available area.
A Python-based modelling approach was constructed and optimised, in collaboration with
DTU Risø researchers, in order to perform relevant simulations and analysis of HPP projects.
The model integrates NREL cost-modules for balance of site, as well as turbine costs, and
uses historical hourly normalised wind and solar production, and Nordpool electricity prices,
to investigate all possible wind/PV ratios in order to ﬁnd the optimal proﬁt-maximisation
solution.
Through modelling of two example cases, and a case-study regarding the estate of Venner-
slund, this thesis looks to investigate the importance of several key variables in regards to
the planning of HPP systems. Two example scenarios are modelled based upon Denmark and
France, and are used to validate the code - through comparisons to real life projects - as well
as to investigate the interactions between the model and input parameters such as varying
weather conditions. A modelling of the proposed Vennerslund Estate project - developed by
DTU Risø - is performed as a case study, with the focus on creating a relevant real-life simu-
lation. This is done in order to conﬁrm the validity of assumptions in the proposed project,
and perform sensitivity analysis on key parameters - in particular grid connection capacity,
CAPEX for PV and WT, and available area.
Analysis of several scenarios are performed for the case study, and recommendations are given.
The proposed project is deemed to be economically viable, however it can be improved with
the use of larger amounts of wind turbines. In the case of being given less than the requested
60 MW of grid capacity, scenarios on the change in revenue are presented. By viewing the
project as an overplanted project, results for 30-40 MW of allowed grid capacity are analysed,
and it is recommended to keep the proposed size, eﬀectively overplanting.
It is possible to further improve the proposed project by later overplanting, up to a proposed
115 MW, focusing primarily on the addition of wind turbines.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In 2020 there is more focus on sustainable energy than ever before. Overwhelming public
support for ’green’ energy throughout the world, as well as concrete plans to reduce rising
temperatures and limit GHG emissions - UN Sustainable Development Goals, Paris agree-
ment and EU Commission 2030/2050 goals - have created the necessity to further optimise
the way sustainable energy production is approached. With the goal of a carbon neutral
energy strategy, the weaknesses and potential shortcomings of the proposed energy solutions
need to be acknowledged and properly managed. The main challenges of solar- and wind
energy power production is the uncontrollable variability of the resources. Through the use
of forecasting, reasonably accurate production predictions can be gathered, however without
suﬃcient energy storage a wind-less week can leave an entirely wind-reliant system without
power. Through the use of hybrid power plants (HPP) containing both solar photovoltaic
power production and wind energy power production, the risk of going entirely without one
resource is signiﬁcantly diminished.
1.1
Problem Deﬁnition
The introduction of HPP systems introduces several new issues to take into account, including
correctly predicting the sizing and ratio of installed solar/wind production as well as accu-
rately forecasting energy production compared to seasonal availability of wind/solar energy.
It has been observed that for an average year, wind and solar energy are highly complemen-
tary, with higher wind availability in the winter and more sun in the summer [1]. With HPP
systems, the complementary nature of wind and solar energy can be a great beneﬁt, and lead
to overplanting being a viable possibility. Overplanting is the sizing of HPP components, to
capacity above the level of allowed grid evacuation. The idea of overplanting however raises
further questions regarding the necessity for curtailment, which is the forced use of less than
the available energy, as well as the economics of overplanting and the state of grid connection
capacity.
This thesis will focus on developing a model for determining optimal sizing of PV/wind
turbine installations, based on historical weather and energy time series, through the use of
ﬁctional example cases, and one real life case study. The case study will focus on the estate
of Vennerslund in Denmark, and will look to compare the results of an optimisation model
with the proposed project, which is currently being planned. The optimisation will aim to
investigate key variables and constraining factors, while investigating scenarios of overplanting

1.1.1
Objectives
Through analysing the case study, and optimal component sizing through modelling, this
thesis will look to answer the following questions:
• Can a model, using historical weather data and market prices, be used to accurately
optimise a real life case, such as Vennerslund?
• What is the optimal sizing of the Vennerslund project?
• Can the currently proposed sizing of the Vennerslund project be improved through the
use of a mathematically modelled optimisation code?
• Is there a potential for overplanting, and what beneﬁts/risks come with this potential?
Upon the completion of this thesis, an in-depth understanding of sustainable hybrid energy
production, and methods for calculating a ﬁtting sizing of Wind/PV installation will be
gathered.
1.2
Scope, Limitations & Assumptions
The scope of this thesis is the sizing and optimisation of a HPP, with a base in a real life
case study. Hybrid power plants will be introduced and relevant literature will be reviewed.
A mathematical model and simulation method will be used to optimise sizing, and through
comparisons with real life data, will ﬁrst be validated through two example projects - and
then used to suggest an optimised Vennerslund case. Through this, recommendations can be
given, and feasibility can be analysed.
The model constructed will look to simulate and optimise a HPP system, using historic
time series for normalised wind and solar power production, and historic electricity market
prices. This is preferable to using yearly averages, but still comes with certain uncertainties.
When extrapolating for the lifetime of a plant (20-30 years) the modelling does not take into
account yearly variations. Prices vary, wind speeds are intermittent, and even global warming
may have unknown impacts on the solar and wind factors in the next 20-30 years [2]. However,
due to the availability of historical data, and the scope of this thesis, it was chosen to use a
one-year time series of hourly values, extrapolated for plant lifetime.
The use of curtailment, implies the tools and skills to operate an energy management
strategy are available. It is understood that not all wind turbine or power plant operators in
Denmark have the technology available to perform curtailment, however this thesis assumes
that a suitable operator can be found, as well as no direct additional costs for the inclusion
of a controller.
The potential of energy storage is noted and mentioned in this thesis. In particular stor-
age has great potential for overcoming some of the challenges a HPP system with curtailment
faces - enabling greater load shifting. However, initially, storage is not included in the model.

This thesis introduces hybrid power plants, and analysis cases with focus on curtailment, grid
capacity and overplanting. The principal contributions of this thesis are:

mathematical model becomes the foundation of a code, designed to perform optimisation
analysis

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Hybrid energy systems are deﬁned as energy systems, comprised of more than one energy
source. It is common for most large-scale energy systems to be some variation of a hybrid
system, due to the complementary nature of having multiple energy sources as backup for
each other. An example of a hybrid energy system is the Danish national grid. The danish
national energy system is made up of several large power producers, consisting primarily of
a combination of on-/oﬀshore wind turbines and CHP plants utilising wood pellets, coal and
natural gas [3]. Due to the heavy involvement of renewable energy in the Danish energy sys-
tem (with wind energy comprising up to 40% of national electricity consumption in 2015 [3]),
the Danish national energy system can be deﬁned as a hybrid system with renewable energy
sources (HSRES).
V. Lazarov et. al [4] deﬁne a HSRES as "..a power system, using one renewable and one
conventional energy source or more than one renewable with or without conventional energy
sources, that works in “stand alone” or “grid connected” mode". In the previous decades hybrid
power systems have drawn much attention from researchers, and HSRES are now commonly
being used to power isolated, non-grid-connected systems such as small islands [5], remote
villages [6] and in some cases hotels and houses [4]. However, with the rising importance of
renewables in the energy system, grid-connected HSRES as power producers in the larger grid,
are becoming more common. Due to the intermittent nature of renewable energy sources, in
particular wind energy and photovoltaic energy, several studies outline the advantages in util-
ity scale hybrid power plants (HPP) consisting of wind turbines and solar photovoltaics. [7] [8]
This thesis focuses on grid-connected hybrid power plants, which look to optimise the amount
of installed wind and PV capacity, based on grid availability and area constraints, in order
to eﬃciently maximise proﬁts. Overplanting is considered as a means to maximise proﬁt,
and steps are taken to minimise curtailment. In the next sections, literature is studied and
reviewed with emphasis on diﬀerent aspects relevant to modelling a proposed HPP system
in Denmark, with the goal of performing an optimisation analysis. These aspects include
but are not limited to; design objectives, load shifting, overplanting, curtailment, modelling
methodology and grid utilisation.

Literature Review
2.1
Design objectives
When designing a HPP, there are several design objectives that need to be considered and
ranked in order of importance. The most common design objectives found in literature include;
• cost minimisation
• emission minimisation
• security of supply
Cost minimisation is often seen as the primary objective when designing a HSRES, and
proﬁt maximisation is preferred when regarding HPP systems[9].
This section will explore previous works regarding HSRES and HPP, and their interpre-
tation of design objectives, and the importance of each.
2.1.1
Cost minimisation & proﬁt maximisation
Cost minimisation is often seen as the primary goal when designing a HSRES, and is widely
discussed in reference literature [10][9]. Another common goal, which can be seen as similar
to cost minimisation, is proﬁt maximisation, as seen in reference literature [11] [12]
When investigating proﬁt maximisation and cost minimisation, the total cost of the sys-
tem needs to be included, and this includes both capital expenditure (CAPEX) and recurring
operation and maintenance expenditures (OPEX). In order to equally evaluate these costs,
occurring at several diﬀerent points of time, there are generally two preferred methods; ei-
ther the recurring costs are aggregated based on expected lifetime and factored into capital
expenditure, or the initial costs are written of as a prorated expenditure and paid oﬀduring
the lifetime of the system.
Most reference literature tends to use the former method of lumping the recurring costs as
an initial investment, typically referred to as the Net Present Cost (NPC) [10] [13]. In these
cases, the cost objective function used for modelling is deﬁned as the total NPC of the system,
including the cost of the initial investment as well as the present values of all future costs
throughout the total lifetime of the system.
In his 2007 paper, Hakimi deﬁnes the NPC as a complex function of capital cost, replace-
ment cost, O&M costs taking into account interest rates, component life-cycle as well as total
system lifetime [14]. The system lifetime is usually considered to be the expected lifetime of
the PV panels, which tend to have the longest lifetime in the system. The most commonly
used values for lifetime are either; 20 years [15] [13] [14] [16] [17] or 25 years [10] [18][19].
The NPC of a system is greatly dependant on the energy demand, system size and system
lifetime, but however fails to diﬀerentiate between the diﬀerence in size and lifetime when
comparing diﬀerent systems. Due to these shortcoming, another term, which in many places
is preferable, is introduced; the levelised cost of energy (LCOE). LCOE is a design objective
that is often used to measure the economic feasibility of a system. LCOE measures the cost
of each unit of energy provided by the system. This calculation is usually done by annualising
the capital cost, as well as operation and maintenance costs, for for the entire system. Annual
cost cost in then divided by annual energy produce in order to evaluate the LCOE.
The method of LCOE energy was used in several pieces of literature, including [20].
The LCOE approach is beneﬁcial in modelling an HPP system due to the higher accuracy
from taking into account system size and demand, as well as simplifying the cost function

and requiring less computational rates than the NPC method. The LCOE method does the
opposite of the NPC method - which lumps costs based on components lifetimes, replacement
costs, salvage values. In the LCOE method, an annualised cost for each component can be
calculated and simply summed to obtain annualised cost for the entire system.
In this report, proﬁt maximisation is the preferred metric of optimisation. Due to the nature
of the case study, where the project is viewed from the perspective of a farmer looking to
invest in a hybrid power plant, cost minimisation is less important than proﬁt maximisation.
Based on literature, it is suggested to investigate the NPC of the project as a lump cost
compared to the total sum of lifetime revenue, in order to maximise proﬁt. However, issues
with this occur when regarding economic analysis, where the value of money in the current
year is diﬀerent than in 20 years. Although the code looks to maximise proﬁt, by simply
summing O&M costs for the lifetime with capital costs, economic analysis will have O&M
costs annualised, with the capital costs occurring as a lump expenditure in year 0.
2.1.2
Emission Minimisation
The goal of emission minimisation is usually justiﬁed by regulatory and environmental consid-
erations. Several examples of design objectives including emission minimisation were found
[10] [21]. In these reports one of the objectives was to minimise CO2 operational (direct)
emissions, whereas in [22], embedded emissions were also considered through life cycle assess-
ment.
Because the primary objective of this thesis is the optimisation of proﬁt when introducing
a HPP to the grid, it is chosen to not include emission minimisation. Due to the exclusion
of fuel-fed traditional energy sources, the proposed HPP is assumed to be an environmen-
tally friendly option, however emission minimisation will not be quantiﬁed or investigated.
Emission minimisation is covered for a proposed HPP sizing in the DTU-Vennerslund report
[23].
2.1.3
Security of Supply
Maximising security of supply is particularly important when dealing with stand-alone hybrid
power systems. Diﬀerent reliability criteria can be used in the objective function of the HPS
capacity planning problem. For example, the loss of power supply probability ratio - the ratio
of total energy deﬁcit to total load demand - can be optimised, or one can look to minimise
the unmet load for a system in the objective function.
Security of supply is important when investigating stand-alone systems, but not of critical
importance to a HPP. In some cases, where the HPP looks to provide its own load or heating
demand, the security of supply can be included in the objective function.
It is however
assessed that the relative size of proposed energy generation for the case study presented in
this thesis, will far exceed the required load demand, and in a worst-case scenario the estate
is grid-connected and can purchase directly from there. Therefore security of supply will not
be considered in either the example cases or the case study.

2.2.1
Grid Connection
Wind turbines and photovoltaic panels both beneﬁt from locations in rural, open areas. For
wind turbines, this also includes oﬀshore locations. This is due to the minimised roughness
eﬀects on wind, and due to the rather large area requirements for both wind turbines and PV
panels. This means the power penetration of renewables is often highest in rural or oﬀshore
locations, where there can be a weak grid. This can lead to challenges with integrating the
renewable energy into the weak grid, and can lead to forced curtailment of power during
high wind seasons. A collaborative report between DTU Risø and The Indian Ministry of
Non-Conventional Energy Sources, released in 2000, investigates the eﬀects of weak grids
on RES integration, focusing in particularly on India. The main limiting factor, in these
weak-grid areas, is the evacuation capacity of energy into the network. As a consequence
of a weak grid, and limited evacuation capacity, power plants can be disconnected during
periods of excessive power generation, rendering the produced energy void. These periods
of excessive power generation are decided by the characteristics of the collective network,
and the connected RES power plants.
In systems with large amounts of connected wind
turbines, a high-wind period will often lead to overproduction, and either forced export or
curtailment due to limited network capacity. This issue of limited evacuation capacity is not
limited to developing networks like India. As the share of renewables in an energy system
increases, challenges occur in securing the high value of these renewables. A 2015 report from
Ea Energianalyse and Energinet [24] details the expected growth of wind power generation
in Europe, with an expected growth of up to 447% by 2040, with the largest share being
generated in Great Britain, France and Germany. It is estimated that by 2040, an average
of 2% of all produced wind energy in Europe will have to be curtailed - and an estimated
3,25% of energy produced in Denmark - unless measures are taken. One such measure is the
proposed Viking Link, which will connect the power networks of Denmark and Great Britain
with a 1400 MW HVDC submarine power cable. By providing a means of exporting power
to Great Britain, Denmark is provided a power-sink during periods of excess production, and
as such reduces the necessity to curtail energy. The Viking Link will enable Denmark and
Great Britain to trade energy, and regulate prices - reducing the average price of electricity in
Great Britain while adding value to, and increasing the price of, the electricity generated in
Denmark. With the Viking Link in place, Denmark is projected to curtail 2,25% of produced
wind energy in 2040, a 1% reduction compared to the base case.
The idea of limited evacuation capacity, requires the planning of hybrid power plants
to take into account the eﬀects of curtailment on the overall viability of the project. It is
often the case, that a maximum grid evacuation capacity is assigned to a hybrid power plant
when seeking planning permissions, in order to maintain grid code compliance. In order to
maximise revenue, the HPP should be dimensioned to generate close to the maximum allowed
evacuation capacity, while minimising curtailment. This is where the concept of controlled
overplanting becomes a viable solution [7].
2.2.2
Overplanting
Overplanting is when a system - often a HPP - is over-dimensioned in relation to the evacuation
capacity of the grid it is connected to. The value of overplanting is beneﬁcial in a HPP due to
the intermittency of solar and wind resources - with wind energy capacity factor often within
the range of 30-40% and solar capacity factor of 10-20% [7]. If a system with a 30 MW grid
evacuation capacity dimensioned its components to reach 30 MW with maximum production,

9
it is not unreasonable to predict that the system will never exceed 15-20MW of production,
leaving the electrical infrastructure unused for large amounts of time. However, if the system
components are sized for 50-60 MW - 66-100% overplanting - then the base production will
be closer to the maximum 30 MW of allowed grid evacuation. It is ﬁnancially beneﬁcial for
the HPP to have its average production as close to the maximum allowed grid evacuation as
possible. However, while overplanting the need for curtailment happens during the periods of
time where the plant produces more than the allowed capacity.
2.2.3
Curtailment
High levels of wind and solar energy can be challenging to integrate into the power system.
In some cases the increased renewable penetration levels can lead to forced transmission or
operational constraints, making it necessary for the system operator to limit the amount of
wind or solar energy accepted [25]. The use of the word curtailment in this context broadly
refers to the forced use of less wind or solar power than is available at a given time. In Denmark
there is a high penetration of wind energy, accounting for around 40% of the Danish electricity
generation. With wind integration on this scale, the system is supported by interconnection to
neighbouring systems and well-functioning international energy markets, including negative
price signals as an incentive to reduce production during periods of overproduction. In 2012
there were 405 hours where wind energy production exceeded consumption in the DK1 region
of western Denmark, and energy was sent through high-voltage direct current connections to
Norway and Germany.
2.2.4
Storage
Although it has been decided that the scope of this project does not include the modelling
of storage, it is important to note the potential of storage. It is commonly acknowledged
that the need for grid-connected storage systems will grow worldwide in the future, due to
the expansion of intermittent renewable energy sources. There are many potential solutions
to the issue of energy storage, including pumped hydro energy storage, ﬂywheel storage,
compressed air storage and thermal energy storage. Reference literature [26] examines the
diﬀerent types of grid-connected storage units, with focus on redox ﬂow batteries.
NREL reference literature [27] examines the concerns of grid reliability due to intermittent
renewables, and how much these renewables can contribute to the system before storage
methods are needed. It is hypothesised that storage methods are not inherently necessary to
complement renewables, however it becomes an economic issue in regards to the integration
cost of storage, and how they may enable larger amounts of renewables. In conclusion, storage
is not necessarily a requirement, however, it was found that updated operational practices -
such as load shifting - were necessary as the share of renewables increase.
2.2.5
Load Shifting
Load shifting is the act of managing the energy consumption to ﬁt with the - often intermittent
- output of energy production. The more freedom of load shifting a HSRES has, the more
reliably it can cover its energy demand.

2.3.1
Iterative/Analytical modelling
A common and quite simple approach to determining optimum component size for a stand-
alone micro-grid consisting of PV-Wind-Batteries is to use an analytic iterative approach.
In the 1998 paper, Habib et al. analyse a simple iterative method for optimising a hybrid
photovoltaic system to satisfy a given load demand [29]. The optimisation procedure presented
is based upon calculating average daily, monthly and yearly power provided by each 18kW
wind turbine and for each square meter of solar panel installed at the site. The optimisation
model then compares the renewable energy availability with load demand, and returns a
value for energy storage capacity required to oﬀset the diﬀerences in load and production.
By iterating in 10% increments, the value of solar/wind ratio from 0% to 100%, the simple
approach is able to estimate an optimal ratio, taking into account the total system cost. The
method presented by Habib et al.
is simple and with a number of doubts.
The method
presented iterates at 10% increments of solar/wind ratio, and in order to be more accurate
should look to iterate at smaller increments, in order to obtain a better solution. The method
also does not take into account grid connection, and the relevant operating strategies that
this inevitably brings.
Ji Xiang et al. suggest a similar approach in their 2012 paper [30]. In their approach,
a model similar to Habib et al.
is used to simulate an isolated island hybrid microgrid
in China. Xiang et al. investigate the pros and cons of modelling batteries, and tackle the
problems regarding state of charge and how to represent batteries as energy storage/outputters
but not as energy generators. To ﬁnd the optimal ratio of solar and wind, ﬁrst the total
amount of 10kW wind turbines to independently power the system is calculated and simulated.
Based on this simulation, the required battery capacity is found comparing peak loads and
demands for the year, and a total system cost is calculated. An iterative program is used
to reduce the number of wind turbines by one in each iteration, until the system is entirely
comprised of PV panels. Using this iterative approach, a total system cost is found for each
version of the proposed system.
The approach taken by Xiang et al.
is a robust albeit

Linear Programming
Another approach often seen in literature is the linear programming approach, with con-

rules attached to them than the previously mentioned analytic approaches, where the latter
look only to meet a certain demand and minimise the cost, linear programming has the added
beneﬁt of assigning priority to several other crucial values. In his 1997 paper[31], Chedid uses
linear programming to model a HSRES with grid connection and operating policies, as well
as taking into account environmental eﬀects of importing energy from the grid.

3.1
Research Design
This section will outline the proposed methods for sizing the components of installed wind
turbine and photovoltaic capacity, to optimise the proﬁt of the proposed hybrid power plant.
As discussed in section 2.3, there are a few ways to model and investigate a hybrid power

[12], is undertaken.
This choice is based upon the idea that this report is to present an
overview through the eyes of a smaller company or private farm, looking to expand with a
proﬁt maximising hybrid power plant.
With the goal of suggesting an optimal amount of installed wind turbines and solar PV,
an iterative modelling approach is taken, similar to the approach taken by Xiang et al. [30].
Key tasks the model must be able to perform are:
• Optimise the proﬁt of a simulated hybrid power plant, using time series of hourly wind
and solar normalised production factors, and hourly market prices for electricity
• Take into account key parameters, such as: grid connection, allowed overplanting, cap-
ital costs, O&M costs
• Integrate NRELs LandBOSSE and TurbineCostSE modules
The methods chosen to simulate the results have been inﬂuenced by the desire to be
relevant and available to the industry. Open source programs such as LandBOSSE and Tur-
binecostSE, both available in Python and produced open-source by NREL, were utilised. A
modiﬁed version of Kaushik Das’ code, developed for the HYBRIDize project, in cooperation
between the Indian Department of Science and Technology and the Danish Innovation Fund,
was utilised in order to accurately model scenarios for wind/PV hybrid power plants.

3.2.1
Turbine Costs
The TurbineCostsSE module is a Python-based turbine cost calculating module, produced by
NREL, as part of a larger family of modules, referred to as WISDEM, designed to asses wind
plant cost. The Wind-Plant Integrated System Design and Engineering Model (WISDEM)
family utilises OpenMDAO in order to perform multi disciplinary optimisation for several
aspects of wind plant system design. The TurbineCostSE module is used in this project,
in order to accurately calculate the pricing of the main wind turbine components. The Tur-
bineCostSE code utilises weight based analysis, in order to calculate price pr. MW of installed
wind turbine capacity.
3.2.2
LandBOSSE
NREL’s Balance-of-Site Cost Model for Land-Based Wind, referred to as LandBOSSE, is a
tool for modelling the balance-of-site (BOS) costs of land based wind plants. The BOS costs
include all major costs related to the system, excluding the turbine itself. The costs featured
in LandBOSSE include material, equipment, permitting & planning, foundation, electrical
infrastructure and tower installation, among others. LandBOSSE is used in this thesis to
estimate all costs associated with the HPP, apart from the turbine capital cost, PV capital
cost and O&M for both wind and PV.

Scaling LandBOSSE
LandBOSSE is designed to be used for screening various innovative designs and their feasibil-
ity, aiming to achieve a American Association of Cost Engineers Class 4 rating. This class 4
rating comes with an expected accuracy range of -10% to -20% on the low end of estimation,
and a +20% to +50% accuracy range on the top end [32]. Due to these fairly loose accu-
racy ranges, the model in its base version, is not suggested for real-life planning and project
management, and in order to become valuable to this project, is modiﬁed and scaled to ﬁt
reference data. The intended scale of LandBOSSE is in the order of approximately 100MW of
installed wind turbine capacity, divided into approximately 70 1.5MW turbines. This order
of magnitude is signiﬁcantly larger than what is expected in the upcoming case study, which
leads to several implications in regards to the balance of site costs. In order to use Land-
BOSSE for this thesis, some amendments need to be made. The assumed cost breakdown of
onshore wind farms, is a well documented ﬁgure, and several reports handle this matter in
detail. In order to correctly scale LandBOSSE for this project, it is deemed necessary that
the code consistently approaches the industry standards for cost breakdown, and two reports
are used for reference. The International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) [33] and The
European Wind Energy Association (EWEA) [34] published reports regarding the standards
of cost breakdown, for onshore wind farms, in 2012 and 2009 respectively. Although these
reports could be considered dated, the results were compared with NRELs 2017 Cost of Wind
Energy Review [35], and were shown to be similar. To verify LandBOSSE, all three reports
were taken as reference.
Due to the real life application of this study focusing on a prospective small business or
farm utilising their land for hybrid energy production, an order of magnitude of around 15-
25MW or approximately 4-8 3.45MW turbines was chosen as a reasonable goal. In order to
scale from the LandBOSSE standard of 70 turbines, totalling more than 100MW, certain cost
parameters were tuned, with the goal of providing realistic cost breakdowns, consistently for
ranges between 4 and 8 turbines.

Power generation for wind turbines and photovoltaic cells greatly depends on weather con-

3.3
Area usage
One of the key constraining factors when optimising the HPP is the land-area available to
build on. Wind turbines and PV panels both take up space, as well as the land occupied by
BOS factors like access roads and maintenance and storage units. This section outlines a few
key parameters to consider when taking land area into account.
3.3.1
Land cost
When placing a HPP, it is important to consider the cost of land being built on. The average
price of land in Denmark is around 155,000 DKK/Ha or 23,000 USD/Ha [38].
This is a
signiﬁcant cost, when analysing plants of 30-60 MW capacities, often occupying 100 hectares
or more. However, due to the nature of this thesis, with the case study focusing on a farming
estate, with available land, it is assumed the only land cost to be analysed is the cost of lost
farming production on this land, and not the land cost itself.
3.3.2
PV Area
When modelling for area occupied by PV panels, there are a wide range of technologies to
take into account. These include; ﬁxed, 1-axis, 2-axis ﬂat panel and variations of concentrated

solar power (CSP) setups. A 2013 NREL report, regarding the land-use requirements of solar
power plants in The United States is used as reference to estimate the required area used for
PV in this project [39]. The NREL report details both direct area, and total area, occupied
by PV installations. The area requirements range from 0.81 Ha/MW, for CSP plants, to
3.8 Ha/MW for 2-axis ﬂat panel setups. Discounting CSP, the weighted average for smaller

substations and other tertiary buildings. For PV, the direct impact area is chosen, as the
additional impact areas, from the total impact areas, will be factored into the wind turbine

3.3.3
Turbine spacing
In this study, one of the constraining factors when ﬁnding the optimal amount of wind and
PV energy, is the area available. As previously mentioned in subsection 3.3.2, PV area is
strictly deﬁned, based on capacity of hectares/MW. However, wind turbine area is slightly
more complicated. The area a wind turbine requires includes both required open area between
each wind turbine - in order to reduce wake eﬀects - as well as the direct area occupied by the
wind turbines and wind turbine components themselves. Quantifying the required area for a
wind turbine can be a challenge due to the various conﬁgurations of placements, as well as
the diﬀerent deﬁnitions of required land. A 2009 NREL report [40], investigating the land-use
metrics of wind power plants in the continental United States, identiﬁes that there is reason
to not only include land directly disturbed by the wind turbine, but also the surrounding area
that may potentially be impacted, as well as assessing both the quality and duration of the
impact. For the sake of this project, only two factors will be taken into account when deﬁning
required wind turbine area;
• Wind turbine spacing - to reduce wake eﬀects
• Direct impact areas - where farming will be unavailable
The direct impact areas are deﬁned as the areas that are occupied by;
• Wind turbine pads
• Access roads
• Substations
• Service buildings
• Other infrastructure which physically occupies land

3.3.4
Wind Farm Layout
Much work has been published on optimal layout of wind farms, focusing on reducing wake
eﬀects and optimising performance. These studies tend to focus on large wind farms, as seen in
the 2019 European Academy of Wind Energy report [41]. Here the layout of large wind farms,
in the scale of 100 turbines or more, is discussed and analysed. For farms of this magnitude,
several optimisation problems arise, and the spacing between turbines is often suggested to
be around 7-8 rotor diameters in-line with the wind, and 5 rotor diameters abreast [42].
In cases with smaller turbines, there are a few simpliﬁcations that can be assumed. Most
importantly, in the scale of 4-8 turbines, the spacing in-line with the wind can be discounted,
assuming adequate knowledge of wind-rose conditions. With this knowledge, the turbines can
be placed in a single string, facing the most common wind direction, and will therefore only
need to take into account the 5 rotor diameters abreast. In this project, it is assumed that
the number of turbines will be of a manageable size, and can therefore be placed in a single
string, discounting in-line rotor spacing.

3.4.3
IRR
The internal rate of return (IRR) is used in budgeting to estimate the proﬁtability of an
investment. The internal rate of return is the discount rate that makes the net present value
(NPV) of a project equal to zero. IRR calculations are calculated using the same formula as
NPV, setting NPV to zero and solving for the discount rate.
Generally, the higher a projects IRR is, the more proﬁtable it is.

Operation and Maintenance
Studies have shown that operation and maintenance (O&M) costs of renewable energies ac-
count for a signiﬁcant 20-25% of the lifetime project costs [33]. Data regarding O&M costs
from commissioned projects are however not widely available. Due to the changes in costs for
both onshore wind turbine technology and photovoltaics, within the last two decades, care
must be taken when extrapolating data. One thing is for certain; O&M costs, following the
same trend as installation costs, have declined from the USD 33/MWh in the 1980’s and
USD 22/MWh in the 1990’s. For the 65 major onshore wind projects in the United States
installed in the 2000’s, the O&M costs were estimated at USD 10/MWh [33]. Although the
data is widely distributed, and far from uniform, the trend is a clear reduction in overall
O&M costs for wind turbine technology. This is likely due to more recent projects using fewer
and larger, more complicated turbines, with higher capacity factors, that do not require more
maintenance than older versions.
IRENA quantiﬁes O&M as a variable cost, based upon kWh of production, however it is
not uncommon for O&M to be quoted as a total of USD/kW of installed capacity, pr year.

ﬁxed grid access fees and service contracts for scheduled maintenance. For Denmark, IRENA
[33] estimates a range of variable O&M cost of 0.014 - 0.018 USD/kWh, and NREL ATB data
suggest an annual cost of 44 USD/kW.

By taking an average of prices for the reference year, an expected yearly revenue can also
be estimated. The average selling price of electricity, in the DK2 region of Nordpool, for the
year 2016, is 32.6 USD/MWh. This method estimates a yearly revenue of 2.8 million USD.
There are however several factors that make this rough estimation method less relevant, one
being the supply and demand factor of energy and another being the price dependency on
weather eﬀects, like strong winds. In a region like Denmark, where there is a large penetration
of wind turbines, the price (demand) of wind energy falls during stormy times, when the wind
turbines are producing at rated speed. Therefore a yearly average is highly uncertain, and
the best way to model is using hourly prices - linked with hourly wind and solar data. By
looking at the yearly revenue suggested by the model and Excel validation, a more reasonable
average selling price can be estimated at 30.8 USD/MWh.

6.1
Introduction to Vennerslund
The Vennerslund estate is located on the island of Falster, in south-eastern Denmark, ap-
proximately 2 hours south of Copenhagen. The Vennerslund estate includes 2200 hectares
of land, much of which is used for agricultural farming and forestry of the surrounding ar-
eas. The estate dates back to the 12th century, with the current family, Brockenhuus-Schack,
having owned it for the better part of the last 200 years. In 2012 the current owner, Kim
Brockenhuss-Schack, began formulating a plan regarding a vision of a more sustainable future
for the estate. His goal was to ﬁnd an economically feasible solution to put his agricultural
business on a more sustainable path, while also serving as an inspiration for other farmers in
Denmark. Through the use of green energy production from wind turbines and solar photo-
voltaics, as well as a large-scale electriﬁcation of traditional diesel farming equipment, the plan
was to develop several diﬀerent scenarios - focusing on sustainability and economic feasibility.
In the case of this report, the suggested scenarios 2 & 3 are of particular interest. These
scenarios regard the implementation of wind turbines and solar photovoltaics, as a hybrid
power plant, and argue that the harvesting of wind and solar energy can be referred to as a
harvesting of an energy crop, and therefore be directly compared to the harvesting of current
agricultural crops.

lifetime proﬁt, choosing to limit focus on sustainability and emission reduction. It is however
suggested, that with the introduction of the proposed wind turbines and photovoltaic panels,
the energy produced should exceed the requirements to oﬀset the carbon footprint of the
farm itself. Other scenarios include overhauling and optimising energy demand throughout

Assumptions
Grid connection
At the time of this report, grid connection has not been guaranteed by the local municipality.
The island of Falster is a hub of wind energy, to the point where the local area is more than
self-suﬃcient with electricity.
It is common for the electricity generated on Falster to be
transported further north into the capital region of Denmark. Due to this, concerns regarding
future renewable projects, and the grid capacity they can be allocated, is growing.
The
project Vennerslund will seek the full 60MW connection to cover their production, but in case
of diﬃculties obtaining this, other values of grid connection and their eﬀect on the project
will also be investigated.
Land cost
As the land is owned by the estate, the land cost is assumed negligible. In order to evaluate
the economic usage of land, the Vennerslund Estate often measure their proﬁt in DKK/Ha of
land. This can be compared with the results from the proposed HPP.
Turbine spacing
As mentioned in section 3.3, it is possible to allow farming in the non-direct impact areas in-
between the wind turbines. A 3-year period for the land between the wind turbines to return
to farm-able condition is allowed, and it is assumed that 10% of the total area required for the
wind turbines is direct-impact areas, where no farming is possible (access roads, maintenance
and storage facilities, tower bases etc.). It is assumed no farming can be done on land occupied
by photovoltaic panels.
Taking these factors into account, the revenue available for farming between the turbines
can be deﬁned as:
RevenueFarming = (Landtotal −LandPV −(LandWT × 0.1)) × ProfitFarming
(6.1)
Where Profitfarming is the proﬁt per hectare per year of a reference crop.
The total revenue is then deﬁned as:
RevenueHPP = RevenueWT + RevenuePV + RevenueFarming
(6.2)
and the key proﬁt function to be maximised is:
ProfitHPP = RevenueHPP −(CostWT + CostPV + CostO&M)
(6.3)
A value of 1000 USD/hectare of proﬁt for a given crop, chosen to be feed wheat, is used
for reference.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and discussion
Hybrid Power Plants have great potential in the current energy-climate, and through the
complementary nature of wind and solar energy, can excel when overplanting occurs.
By the means of a Python-based model, optimisation simulations were performed for two
example cases and a case study. Through analysis of the two example cases, the model was
deemed to be consistent with real-life results, and fulﬁlled the goals set in section 3.1.
The model was tested on two example cases in order to verify the results, and provide
insight into how the optimisation changes with weather inputs, installation costs, and grid
capacity. Through simple economic analysis, the example projects were deemed to be eco-
nomically infeasible. Questions can be asked in regards to the correct dimensioning of key
parameters such as installation and O&M costs, as these were found to keep the projects
infeasible. Performing sensitivity analysis on the installation price of the project, and average
electricity price, it was found that the assumed capital costs may be too conservative.
For the case study conducted on the Vennerslund estate, the optimisation model proved
to be capable of estimating the optimal component sizing of the real life case, but did however
not agree with the proposed sizing presented in the Vennerslund report. For a HPP system
with 0% overplanting, the beneﬁts of using hybrid wind and solar PV is arguably non-existent
- unless focus is added on security of supply which is not the case in this thesis - and the
option with the lowest LCOE should be preferred and built to maximum allowed capacity.
The optimal solution for Vennerslund, in case of 0% overplanting will be to build as many
wind turbines as possible, until the grid capacity or area constraints are reached.
If granted 60 MW of grid connection, it can be argued that choosing not to overplant leads
to a signiﬁcant cost of opportunity, of nearly 20 million USD, equivalent to a 51% increase
in NPV, when comparing the model optimisation result with the currently proposed case.
With overplanting, it is found that nearly 30% of the energy produced in a year is curtailed,
however despite this, the project is still enhanced in regards to lifetime proﬁt. The amount
of energy curtailed can lead to opportunities for greater proﬁt, if methods of utilising this
energy are developed. These methods could be in the form of private purchasing agreements
with neighbours, using the excess electricity to charge vehicles or run manufacturing machines,
or by implementing storage to store energy during curtailment and discharging at a later time.
In case the proposed project faces diﬃculties obtaining the desired 60 MW of grid con-
nection, it is suggested not to downscale the project, but instead - with the incorporation of
a controller unit for curtailment - treat the project as an overplanted HPP. A comparison of
revenue for diﬀerent grid capacities, lower than the requested 60 MW, shows how 60 MW of

Discussion
Through the comparison between the example cases and case study, the importance of instal-
lation and O&M costs stands out. The same project, modelled using gathered reference values
from reports, NREL modules and real life examples was deemed to be economically infeasible,
and a net loss of value over the lifetime. However, the same project, modelled with the costs
presented in the Vennerslund report, was shown to be economically beneﬁcial. In order to
be secure in the ﬁnances of a project of this size, it is important to have complete knowl-
edge of costs beforehand, and perform sensitivity analysis on unexpected increase in costs.
The pricing inputs presented in chapter 4 were gathered using real life examples, technology
baseline catalogues, and several up-to-date reports. However, the diﬀerence in feasibility of
the projects using Vennerslund inputs and reference inputs is large. Questions can be asked
whether the reference inputs were too conservative, but it could be important to be careful
with the validation of Vennerslund inputs as they may be too optimistic. It is recommended
that before the Vennerslund case initialises, extra care is taken in validating the assumed costs.
An uncertainty in regards to the validity of the optimisation modelling is the use of 1-year
data, which is then subsequently extrapolated for 20-30 years, depending on lifetime. It is
important to perform serious sensitivity analysis on the development of weather conditions -
and on price forecasts. The reference year used in this report was shown to be a relatively
low-price year, in regards to Nordpool day-ahead prices. This may have skewed the results,
and led to ineﬃcient optimisations. An analysis with scaled prices, to a 14 year average,
yielded the same optimisation results, however it is important to conduct further research on
price developments in the region of interest.
The French example case was modelled using Nordpool 2016 prices, while using 2017 nor-
malised production data. This is inherently incorrect, but due to the limited available data
was necessary. Due to this, the link between prices and production cannot be commented on.
While it is recommended to increase the amount of built wind turbines in the Vennerslund
==PAGE:77==
67
project, there are factors the model does not take into account; in this case social factors and
land-evaluation factors. It is by no means guaranteed to be allowed to construct the required
amount of turbines, due to push-back from local oﬃcials and neighbours. Similarly, wind
turbines need steady ground, suitable for construction and foundation work. All available
area is not always suitable for this, and should be taken into account.
The LandBOSSE module was scaled for the Vennerslund project, and the example cases.
However, when investigating the large overplanting scenarios at Vennerslund, it should have
been scaled further, to match this increased installed capacity. This was not initially per-
formed in this analysis.
Further Studies
It is suggested, for further analysis, storage is added into the modelling. Storage has the
option to signiﬁcantly negate several of the problems that a HPP faces - as well as problems
a simple wind-only plant, suggested for a 0% overplanting Vennerslund case, faces. Further
studies could be conducted in regards to which is more important: grid connection or storage,
and whether or not storage will improve HPP or single-resource systems more.
The model optimisation was based on total lifetime proﬁt, taking into account lifetime
O&M costs summed up to a net present cost.
However the Python modelling does not
initially include any ﬁnancial factors such as discount rate, NPV and IRR. Further work is
recommended, to integrate these ﬁnancial models directly into the optimisation model, so the
model performs economic analysis itself.
