Abstract
The ﬁeld of transcriptomics is moving towards analysis of single-cell gene expressions, which is
particularly valuable in the research of heterogeneous tissues such as cancer tumors and bone
marrow.
This thesis initially dives into classifying transcriptomic breast cancer data using the spaceRAT
R package that visualizes labeled training data in principal component space and classiﬁes
unlabeled test data. Batch eﬀects between training and test datasets of diverse studies and
technologies are handled with rank transformation of the gene expression values. Test data
of single-cell gene expressions will undergo imputation and tumor cell identiﬁcation prior to
classiﬁcation. This analysis reveals diverse subtypes within individual samples, hence oﬀering
more insights compared to classifying bulk gene expression samples.
The second part of the thesis focuses on distinguishing between bulk gene expression of Acute
Myeloid Leukemia and Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia samples based on cell type proportions.
The proportions are assessed from a single-cell bone marrow scaﬀold, revealing high propor-
tions of neutrophil precursors in Acute Myeloid Leukemia samples and higher proportions of B
cells and progenitors in some Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia samples. However, the bone mar-
row scaﬀold is solely based on a single healthy individual and lacks certain cell types, raising
questions about the scaﬀold’s generalizability.
spaceRAT demonstrates versatility across diverse transcriptomic datasets, making any classiﬁ-
cation possible, emphasizing the importance of considering the dataset quality before drawing
any conclusions.

Introduction
Molecular subtypes stratify diseases based on bulk genetic expression proﬁles and have been
deﬁned for breast cancer[16], glioblastoma[4], colorectal cancer[24] and more [7, 5, 22, 39].
Cancer subtypes are linked to disease prognosis and treatment response. There are several
techniques to access the genetic expression proﬁles of samples and the methods have changed
over the years.
Since the mid-2010s, the number of PubMed-indexed articles of RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq)
studies has been higher than those utilizing microarrays, with an exponential rise in single-
cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) studies since 2015 as illustrated in Figure 1. The counts
are extracted from the PubMed webpage and only studies analyzing the transcriptome are
included[28].
Figure 1: Publication trends of microarray, RNA-seq, and scRNA-seq from 1998 to
2023. The counts are extracted from the PubMed webpage under Results by Year
with search words microarray transcriptome, RNA sequencing transcriptome, and
single cell RNA sequencing transcriptome.
Due to the increasing use of scRNA-seq, there is an interest in exploring its potential for cancer
subtyping, given the heterogeneity observed in cancer tumors[9]. In the case of classiﬁcation
of subtypes, the training data is a dataset containing known subtypes on which a model is
constructed, whereas the test data comprises samples for which the goal is to predict the
subtypes. Challenges related to batch eﬀects stemming from diverse gene expression proﬁling
technologies and study designs across training and test sets need to be addressed. Several batch
eﬀect correction tools model both biological and technical variances, aiming to eliminate the
technical variance[17, 42]. While these tools are highly sophisticated and eﬀective when the
biological and technical statuses of both groups are known, they present limitations in disease
Page 8 of 61
==PAGE:9==

subtyping. In clinical settings, the test data usually only consists of a single sample, making
it impossible to determine the variance distribution due to lack of replicates. Additionally, the
aim in the clinic is to predict the biological variance (subtype) of the test sample, hence the
biological variance is not known prior to the analysis. Rank transformation of gene expression
values does not require modeling of technical nor biological variance and has showed promising
results when microarray samples served as the training set and bulk RNA-seq samples as the test
set[3]. The method is robust to changes in the test set size, as there is consistency in subtype
classiﬁcation of the same sample, regardless of the test set size. With the rise of scRNA-seq, it is
interesting to test the rank transformation approach across bulk and single-cell gene expression
proﬁling technologies, and investigate if it can facilitate comparability between the datasets
and potentially aid in exploring disease subtypes.
Aim of thesis
This thesis is part of a larger project aimed at developing an R package called spaceRAT.
The package is designed to build scaﬀolds in principal component (PC) space based on any
transcriptomic data and to project new transcriptomic data onto these scaﬀolds.
Prior to
scaﬀold construction and projection, both transcriptomic datasets are rank transformed to
handle batch eﬀects. spaceRAT will also be available as a Shiny app (web service), designed to
be easy to use for non-bioinformaticians and doctors. To accommodate this audience, the tool
aims to be user-friendly, with preprocessing that can be performed on a web server.
The practical applications of spaceRAT are shown through four scenarios:
1. Projecting bulk gene expression samples onto a scaﬀold of bulk gene expression data.
2. Projecting scRNA-seq samples onto a scaﬀold of bulk gene expression data.
3. Projecting scRNA-seq samples onto a scaﬀold of scRNA-seq data.
4. Projecting bulk gene expression samples onto a scaﬀold of scRNA-seq data.
In this thesis, I will focus on scenarios 2 and 4 to investigate if rank transformation can handle
batch eﬀects across bulk gene expression data and scRNA-seq data.
For scenario 2, I will build two breast cancer scaﬀolds using samples analyzed with bulk gene
expression (microarray and bulk RNA-seq) samples from two subtyping schemes: CIT and
PAM50. ScRNA-seq samples will be projected onto this scaﬀold, and each cell will be assigned
a probability of belonging to a speciﬁc subtype. This approach aims to take a closer look

at the heterogeneity within breast cancer and assess the uncertainty of subtyping bulk gene
expression samples. Furthermore, pseudobulked scRNA-seq data and real bulk RNA-seq data
from the same samples will be compared to evaluate the information obtained from single-cell

For scenario 4, I will build a scaﬀold using scRNA-seq data of bone marrow cells from a

Subsequently, bulk gene expression data from bone marrow samples of Acute Myeloid Leukemia

By focusing on these scenarios, this thesis seeks to demonstrate the versatility and potential

it can support doctors in making treatment decisions for cancer patients by providing technical
assistance and a deeper understanding of cancer biology for informed decision-making in medical
practice.

Theory
Cancer subtypes
Cancer is a complex disease characterized by uncontrolled growth and proliferation of cells that
can create tumors. The area of the tumor is called the tumor microenvironment (TME). The
TME is a community of diverse cell types, including immune cells, stromal cells, and blood
vessels, contributing to the heterogeneity of the tumor[1]. Molecular subtypes of cancer are
stratiﬁed based on gene expression patterns of the average of all the cells in the TME, since the
samples are analyzed with bulk expression techniques. Understanding disease subtypes can be
crucial for selecting treatments and predicting patient prognosis.[14, 19, 40]
Breast Cancer Subtypes
In the clinic, breast cancer is categorized based on the expression of three receptors: estrogen
receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2
(HER2). The status of the receptors expressions deﬁnes three main subtypes: luminal, HER2-
positive, and triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC). Each subtype has distinct characteristics
and treatment implications.[25]
Two of the most known molecular subtyping schemes for breast cancer are PAM50[2] and
CIT[12]. The PAM50 subtyping scheme is deﬁned on bulk expression of 50 genes to stratify
breast cancers into ﬁve subtypes: LumA, LumB, Her2, Basal, and Normal-like.
The CIT
subtypes are deﬁned by unsupervised clustering of bulk transcriptomic data to stratify cancer
samples in to six subtypes based on 256 genes: lumA, lumB, lumC, mApo, basL, and normL.
Bone Marrow Cell Types and Leukemia
All cells in our body originate from stem cells and can diﬀerentiate into more specialized cells,
as illustrated in Figure 2. Hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) have the ability to either clone
themselves or diﬀerentiate into the slightly more mature multipotent progenitor cells (MPPs).
HSPCs (hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells) sometimes refers to a broader group of cells
that includes both HSCs and various types of progenitor cells. MPPs can diﬀerentiate into
common myeloid (CMP) or lymphoid progenitors (CLP). Myeloid progenitors can give rise to
erythrocytes (red blood cells) and cells of the innate immune system, such as macrophages
and neutrophils. Myeloid-erythroid progenitors (MEPs) is a cell-stage between the myeloid
progenitors cells and the erythrocytes, like the name implies. On the other hand, lymphoids

are cells of the adaptive immune system, including B lymphocytes and T lymphocytes.[18, 6]
Progenitors, precursors and blasts are all terms for cells that can diﬀerentiate further into other
cells[32].
CD34 is a surface protein that are often used as a biomarker for HSCs[18]. The protein has
been associated with controlling proliferation and diﬀerentiation of progenitor cells, and have
been found on cancerous stem cells of leukemia. The role of CD34 in cancer is not completely
understood but might play a role in the prognosis of the disease[29].

Leukemia is cancer of the white blood cells. There are two major subtypes of leukemia: Acute
Lymphocytic Leukemia (ALL) and Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML). In the case of ALL, it is
the CLP that have become cancerous and overrepresented[36], while for AML the same trend
is observed for the CMP[37].
The classiﬁcation of leukemia subtypes is based on the identiﬁcation of which type of progenitor
cells have become cancerous and are overrepresented in the bone marrow. One way of accessing
cell types involves analyzing surface proteins, where a cell expressing certain markers is assigned
to a particular cell type. This simple way of deﬁning cell types have been criticized for drawing
rigid boundaries between cell types, which might overlook the heterogeneity of cells within the
same cell types. It is therefore suggested to used more complex methods to determine cell
types[41].

Transcriptomics
Transcriptomics is the study of RNA transcripts produced by cells. Transcripts are produced
during protein synthesis when DNA is transcribed to RNA, hence the name. Transcripts are
also called messenger RNA (mRNA) since they message to the ribosome which nucleotides
are needed for the synthesized protein.
The collection of a cell’s transcripts is called the
transcriptome. Analysis of the transcriptome can provide insight into gene expression patterns
which can be compared across groups. Comparing the transcriptomes between healthy and
sick individuals might reveal diﬀerences in gene expressions between the two, which can aid in
understanding the disease and even point in direction of therapeutic targets.
The sequence of a transcript can be accessed from either hybridization to probes or sequencing.
Microarray is a technique that utilizes hybridization, whereas RNA-seq employs sequencing, as
the name suggests. Once the sequence is known, it can be aligned to a reference genome to
reveal the gene from which the sequence was transcribed. Although transcriptomics has been a
large contribution to research, it is important to note that the analysis only captures a snapshot
of cellular activity at the time of sampling.[20]
Microarray
A microarray consists of a glass plate with nucleotide probes, which are sequences that corre-
spond to known genes. Transcripts from a sample are ﬁrst reverse transcribed to complementary
DNA (cDNA) since it is more stable than RNA. Subsequently, the cDNA is fragmented, labeled
with ﬂuorescence, and then allowed to bind to the probes on the microarray. If the sequences
are complementary, the fragments bind, and a computer captures the light emitted by the
ﬂuorescent label. The intensity of light intensity corresponds to the amount of cDNA bound
to a particular probe, indicating the level of gene expression related to that probe.[11] The
microarray technique is restricted to evaluating gene expression solely for genes that align with
the probes on the plate. This limits the research of novel sequences.[21]
RNA-sequencing
RNA-seq is a high-throughput method, which means that it can process large amounts of
sequencing data. Similar to the microarray technology, the transcripts are reverse transcribed
to cDNA and fragmented. The fragments are labeled with unique sequence-indexes speciﬁc
to the sample. The sequences are then ampliﬁed to form clusters of identical fragments for
subsequent sequencing.
The fragments are read to determine the sequence.
This is called
sequencing.[20] RNA-sequencing can be performed either on bulk level or single cell level.

In bulk RNA-seq, RNA is extracted from all cells within a sample, providing the average
gene expression across the entire cell population. As a result, this approach may overlook the
potential heterogeneity present in the sample, masking the diﬀerences among various cell types.
ScRNA-seq is used to analyze the gene expressions of individual cells within a sample which
can reveal heterogeneity. There are several techniques for performing scRNA-seq and two on
them are: 10x Genomics Chromium system and Smart-seq2.[34]
10x Genomics Chromium system
The 10x Genomics Chromium system utilizes a droplet-
based technique to encapsulate individual cells into oil droplets labeled with unique barcodes.
Subsequently, sequencing is performed via a high-throughput method, which constructs the
gene expression proﬁle of each individual cell measured in unique molecular identiﬁers (UMIs).
A large advantage of the 10x system is that can analyze thousands of cells at a time.[35] One
challenge of the 10x system is the limited sensitivity to detecting very low gene expression
levels, leading to zeros in the data[34]. Additionally, gene expressions often occur in bursts, as
genes switch between active and inactive states based on the binding of transcription factors.
If RNA is extracted during an inactive state, the RNA of the given gene may be absent in the
cell, depending on rates of transcription and degradation. Consequently, the expression of the
gene is read as zero, despite having been expressed shortly before and after extraction. Several
biological and technical factors can contribute to zeros in scRNA-seq data. The proportion
of zeros can be up to 90%, resulting in what is commonly termed as sparse data[15]. The
proportion of zeros is calculated by how many percentages of the genes have zero values on
average across samples.
Smart-seq2
Smart-seq is a plate-based method that involves isolation of individual cells into
the wells of the plate. The cells are isolated using ﬂuorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS).
In the wells, the RNA of each cell is reversed transcribed to cDNA which is ampliﬁed and
sequenced. After the reads are mapped to genes, the gene expression of the cells is measured in
transcripts per kilobase million (TPM).[26] Smart-seq2 is a low throughput since the number
of cells that can be analyzed at once is limited. A large advantage of Smart-seq2 is that it is
more sensitive for detecting genes with low expression levels compared to 10x.[34]
In summary, single-cell RNA sequencing methods such as the 10x Genomics Chromium system
and Smart-seq have expanded our understanding of cellular heterogeneity, which is especially
relevant in cancer research. With bioinformatic tools such as Seurat (R-package), it is possible
to estimate the cell type of each cell from the gene expression.
For the sc-RNA-seq data, especially 10x, can be very sparse. When this data needs to be
compared to bulk data, it can cause problems, since most gene expressions might be zeros,

where very few are zero in bulk RNA-seq. Imputation can be used on scRNA-seq data, but
these can be very time consuming and diﬃcult to interpret. Since the goal is to run everything
on a Shiny app used by doctors, a fast-running alternative to imputation is thought out which
we call pseudobulk neigbours. First, it is important to understand pseudobulk.
Pseudobulk
Pseudobulk, as the name implies, is a technique used to mimic bulk RNA-seq expression from
scRNA-seq data by summing the gene expressions of individual cells. The method allows for
aggregating all cells of a scRNA-seq dataset into a single data point, emulating a bulk gene
expression sample.[27]
Pseudobulk Neigbours
To handle sparsity, a method called "pseudobulk neighbours" was thought out by the co-
supervior on this thesis, Søren Helweg Dam. Like the name implies, the method is a hybrid of
k-nearest-neighbours and pseudobulk. The technique employs the shared nearest neighbours
(SNN) which quantiﬁes the neighbourhood to each sample, opposite to conventional nearest
neighbours which returns a binary value describing weather the neighbourhood between two
samples are present or not.
In a PC space deﬁned by N PCs, the k-nearest-neighbours are deﬁned for each sample, collec-
tively forming a neighbourhood. For any pair of samples, the SNN’s are deﬁned by the ratio of
samples in the intersect of the samples’ neighbourhoods to k. The pair of samples are deﬁned
as neighbours "with the value" of SNN. If none of the neighbors are shared between the neigh-
borhoods, SNN is 0/k = 0, while if all neighbors are shared, SNN is k/k = 1. In the function
for this method, several parameters can be adjusted. The neighbor_cutoff parameter is set
to a number between 0 and 1 which deﬁnes a threshold for the SNN value for two samples to be
considered as neighbours. The nn_count parameter controls the number of nearest neighbors
to consider for pseudobulking. If a cell has fewer than this number of neighbors, all are used.
For each pair of samples, an SNN value is calculated. Subsequently, for each sample, N neigh-
bours with an SNN value above a certain threshold are pseudobulked. This technique ensures
that the resulting dataset maintains the same number of pseudobulk samples as the original
number of cells.

it is assumed that it is the diﬀerential expression of the tumor cells that drive the subtypes. It

Identify Tumor Cells
CopyKAT is an R package designed to distinguishing tumor cells from stromal and immune
cells in scRNA-seq datasets. The name CopyKAT is derived from "copy number karyotyping of
aneuploid tumors". The tool operates on the assumption that aneuploid copy number proﬁles
are found in the majority of human tumors and not present in stromal cell with diploid genomes.
The tool is tested on breast cancer, among other cancer types. The copy number is the number
of copies of a speciﬁc gene in the cell. Cells exhibiting aneuploid copy number proﬁles have an
abnormal chromosome count.[10]
spaceRAT
The ﬁrst part of the thesis focuses on building scaﬀolds using spaceRAT https://github.com/
shdam/spaceRAT. "RAT" in spaceRAT stands for Ranked Analysis for Transcriptomes and also
allude to the animal that eats anything; hence the tool can process any transcriptomes data. Ad-

contains prebuilt scaﬀolds which the user can project samples onto. Scaﬀolds developed in this

Diﬀerential Gene Expression Analysis
A scaﬀold can be build with all available genes
or a subset hereof. The subset is deﬁned from diﬀerentially expressed genes between samples
of each phenotype. Diﬀerentially expressed genes are deﬁned via diﬀerential gene expression
(DGE) analysis which compares the mean gene expression between two groups of samples. In
spaceRAT, samples of each phenotype are compared to the remaining samples. A DGE is
performed gene wise and returns two values: log2 fold change (log2FC) and p-value. log2FC is
the eﬀect size estimate, calculated as logarithm base 2 of the ratio between the mean expression
of samples of a given phenotype divided by the mean expression of the remaining samples.
Hence, a log2FC of zero means that the mean gene expression of two groups are identical. The
p-value is calculated by a t-test and adjusted for multiple testing.[31]
For a given gene, if the log2FC is above and the p-value is below respective thresholds, the gene
is considered diﬀerentially expressed between two groups. In spaceRAT, the user can manually
Page 17 of 61
==PAGE:18==
June 2024
DTU Master thesis
set the thresholds, which will aﬀect which genes are considered diﬀerentially expressed, and
hence inﬂuence the subset of genes used to build the scaﬀold. The default thresholds are 2 for
log2FC and 0.05 for p-value. While the default number of diﬀerentially expressed genes deﬁning
each group is unlimited, the user can manually adjust this with the n_genes parameter.
The next step is rank transformation.
Rank Transformation
Rank transformation of a list involves assigning a rank of 1 to the
smallest value, 2 to the next smallest, and so forth. In cases of ties, where multiple values are
equal, the ranks are averaged out. For example, if two values are tied for the second position,
they both are assigned a rank of 2.5. Rank transformation of gene expression provides intra-
sample relative expression, which means that the samples are analyzed independent of other
samples.[3] The parameter rank_scale determines whether the ranks are adjusted by a scaling
factor related to the maximum value in the ranks. In practice, the more instances of the highest
value in the list, the higher all ranks will be adjusted.[30] The default for this parameter is FALSE.
After rank transformation, a PCA is performed to visualize the analysis in a two-dimensional
space.
Principal Component Analysis
PCA is a method used to reduce the dimensions of a
dataset in a linear matter, while conserving the variance. The analysis deﬁnes vectors, principal
components (PCs), in the direction along which the data has the most variance. Each vector
comprises weights for each feature of the original dataset. In the case of a transcriptomics data
set, the features are typically genes. The PCs are orthogonal to each other and ordered by
the proportion of variance they explain in the data, such that PC1 explains the most. The
number of PCs matches the number of samples in the dataset, and together they explain 100%
of the variance across all the PCs.[13] The parameter rank_scale centers and scales the data.
Centering adjusts the data by removing the mean of each column, while scaling adjusts the
spread of the data by dividing each column by its standard deviation or root mean square.
This normalization process ensures that all variables are on a similar scale. The default for this
parameter is TRUE.

Projecting New Samples onto Scaﬀold
The projectSample() function takes a scaﬀold object and new transcriptomic data as input.
It handles diﬀerent gene identiﬁers and genes in the new data, translating alternative identiﬁers
to match those of the scaﬀold. The parameter subset_intersection determines whether the
scaﬀold is reduced to only include genes that intersect with the new data. If many genes are
absent in the new data while subset_intersection is set to TRUE, there is a risk that the
scaﬀold is altered signiﬁcantly. When subset_intersection is set to FALSE, the absent genes
of the new data will be introduced and get the value of 0. This will increase the sparsity in the
data, which may obscure the actual information present in the data. Subsequently, the new
data is processed similar to the scaﬀold with regards to rank transformation and is projected
onto the scaﬀold. The output of projectSample() is a PC plot displaying the scaﬀold with
the new samples projected onto it, as showed in Figure 3. The projected samples can then be
classiﬁed as one of the phenotypes of the scaﬀold.
Classiﬁcation
Classiﬁcation is performed by the simple distance-to-centroid method. Centroids are computed
by averaging the gene expression levels across scaﬀold samples within each phenotype, resulting
in one centroid per phenotype. The Euclidean distance between each sample and the centroids
are then calculated. Classiﬁcation is assigned to the phenotype corresponding to the centroid
with the shortest distance to the new sample.
To quantify the classiﬁcations with probabilities and enable comparison of distances across
samples, a scaling and normalization factor can be applied to convert the distances into prob-
abilities that sum to 1. The distances are scaled by a negative scaling factor, since a smaller
distance corresponds to a higher probability for a given subtype, as showed in Equation 1. Each
scaled distance is normalized by dividing with the sum of all the scaled distances for the given
sample, as demonstrated by Equation 2.

Materials and methods
In this thesis, spaceRAT is used to build three scaﬀolds: two for breast cancer, derived from two
distinct bulk expression datasets annotated with their respective breast cancer subtypes (CIT
and PAM50), and one bone marrow scaﬀold based on a scRNA-seq dataset from a healthy
individual featuring deﬁned cell types.
For the breast cancer scaﬀolds, new breast cancer
scRNA-seq samples will be projected onto and each cell will be classiﬁed as a subtype. For
the bone marrow scaﬀold, bulk bone marrow samples from leukemia patients will be projected
onto and the goal is to identify the over-represented cell types of the bulk samples.
Transcriptomic datasets
Several of the datasets used in this thesis are from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) Program,
which was created to support the study of cancer and precision medicine.
TCGA holds a
collection of samples from over 11,000 individuals across 33 cancer types. There are several
data types in TCGA and in this thesis the gene expression data is used.
Breast Cancer Scaﬀold Data:
PAM50
The dataset is the breast invasive carcinoma
cohort from the TCGA and comprises RNA-seq data obtained from 1,092 samples and 58,581
genes. Each sample have been assigned one of ﬁve subtypes (LumA, LumB, Her2, Basal, and
Normal-like (Normal)) based on 50 genes by Bernard PS et al. in their study [2]. The format
of the data is TPM transformed RNA-seq data.
Breast Cancer Scaﬀold Data: CIT
The dataset is from the CITBCMST R package (not
maintained) and comprises microarray data obtained from 355 samples and 54,675 probes[12].
Each sample has been assigned one of six subtypes (LumA, LumB, LumC, mApo, Basal, and
normL) based on 375 probes in the original study, which translated to 257 core genes.
Single-Cell RNA-sequencing Breast Cancer Projection Data
The dataset comprises
scRNA-seq data obtained from 26 primary breast cancer tumors, downloaded under GEO ac-
cession number GSE176078. In the original study, the PAM50 subtype of cancer cells was
determined for 20 samples [38]. Additionally, bulk RNA-seq samples were available for 25 sam-
ples and analyzed 58,387 genes. However, 2 out of the initial 20 samples were excluded due to
missing bulk RNA-seq data, leaving a total of 18 samples analyzed in this thesis. Pseudobulk-
ing will be performed for the scRNA-seq samples, so the bulk RNA-seq data will be referred to
as "real bulk RNA-seq" to avoid misunderstandings.

Bone Marrow Scaﬀold
The dataset comprises of scRNA-seq data obtained from bone mar-

Discussion
For the discussion, I will ﬁrst take a look at what information we actually have when working
with transcriptomic data, both bulk and single-cell. Further discussion will be about the breast
cancer scaﬀolds and the classiﬁcations of the bulk and single-cell samples. Finally, the bone
marrow scaﬀold and its applications will be discussed.
Transcriptomic data
The use of transcriptomic data in research is widespread and provides insights into gene expres-
sion at the time of the sampling, which can vary within short timeframes[20]. In the context of
breast cancer patients, tumor samples are typically obtained through biopsies or during surgery.
In both cases, one would think that the well-being of the patient is the top priority, which may
result in the handling of the sample becoming a secondary concern. There is a risk that samples
may not consist solely of the tumor tissue but could also include surrounding adipose tissue or
be contaminated with skin cells. Additionally, the sample might not be preserved immediately
after sampling, which could lead to RNA degradation. These factors should be considered when
drawing conclusions from the analysis of transcriptomic data.
Breast Cancer Scaﬀold
The data used to build the PAM50 scaﬀold was unbalanced, with over half of the samples being
of the LumA subtype and LumB being the second-largest subtype, with a sample count that
was less than half of LumA’s. In the space of the ﬁrst two PCs, the LumA samples overlap with
the LumB samples. Among the 18 real bulk RNA-seq samples, six were classiﬁed as LumA and
only two as LumB, which could be due to the imbalance and overlap of the two subtypes. In
contrast, the Basal subtype, with a sample count one-third of LumA’s, comprised seven of the
18 real bulk samples. In the scaﬀold, the Basal subtype appeared relatively isolated from the
other samples, suggesting it may be easily distinguishable from the other subtypes.
The pseudobulk and the real bulk RNA-seq data of the 18 breast cancer tumor samples were
compared. Though the two data points did not comprise the exact same cells, they did stem
from the same tumor tissue, essentially serving as replicates. It was therefore expected that
they would be classiﬁed as the same subtype. 15 of the 18 samples were indeed classiﬁed as the
same subtype using the PAM50 scaﬀold. This indicates that the pseudobulk of these scRNA-seq
samples emulates the real bulk RNA-seq samples.

The three samples with discrepancies in subtype classiﬁcation between the pseudobulk and
the real bulk RNA-seq data had conﬂicting subtypes located near one another: Basal/Her2,
LumA/Normal, and LumA/LumB. When projecting the samples onto the CIT scaﬀold, six
samples exhibited discrepancies in classiﬁcation between the pseudobulk and real bulk RNA-seq
data. These conﬂicting subtypes were mApo/basL, lumA/lumB, normL/lumA, and normL/lumC,
all of which are closely positioned on the CIT scaﬀold. The discrepancies might suggest that
these subtypes share similarities, or that diverse subtypes of cells are present within one tumor.
The analysis of individual cells from the scRNA-seq data, initially pseudobulked, involved trans-
forming the raw counts via pseudobulk neighbours and identifying tumor cells using CopyKAT.
The transformation of raw single-cell RNA-seq counts using the pseudobulk neighbors function
led to a decrease in the percentage of zeros by 14 to 38 points across the 18 samples. This
validates that the method can serve as an imputation tool or denoise zeros. Like other impu-
tation methods, information is borrowed across closely located cells. Clustering cells based on
their shared neighbours, establishes a more robust deﬁnition of neighbourhoods, compared to
the conventional nearest neighbours method. Despite identifying robust clusters of the cells, it
is uncertain whether the pseudobulk of a cluster represents the true expression of the cell.
CopyKAT was used to identify the tumor cells of the pseudobulk neighbours data. The tool
was developed to handle raw scRNA-seq data, so giving it pseudobulk neighbours data as input
might have caused problems. In hindsight, it might have been a better approach to identify
tumor cells before performing pseudobulk neighbors. The tumor cells identiﬁed in 15 out of
the 18 sample showed consensus with those identiﬁed in the original study, which suggests that
CopyKAT actually handles the pseudobulk neighbours data quite well. Although it is possible
that both methods could be incorrect, the consensus among the majority of identiﬁed tumor cells
increases conﬁdence in the results. It also enhances the certainty that the pseudobulk neighbor
data actually represents the true expression of the cells, though this cannot be concluded.
Subtype classiﬁcation of the tumor cells was conducted using distance-to-centroid. Comparing
the PAM50 subtypes predicted in this thesis with those from the original study, ﬁve of the
18 samples were not classiﬁed as the same subtype in pseudobulk. Three of the ﬁve samples
were also those where the pseudobulk and real bulk RNA-seq classiﬁcation did not agree. In
the original study, three samples were classiﬁed as the Her2 subtype, of which only one was
classiﬁed as such in this thesis. This might suggest that the PAM50 breast cancer scaﬀold does
not distinguish the Her2 subtype very well.
The pseudobulk data of sample CID4066 was classiﬁed as Her2 in the original study with around
50% of the tumor cells classiﬁed as the same subtype. In this thesis, the pseudobulk of the
same sample was classiﬁed as Normal and with over 75% of the cells classiﬁed as Her2. In this

thesis, seven samples (CID4513, CID4523, CID4290A, CID4461, CID4535, CID4066, CID4471)
showed the same trend, with the pseudobulk data not being predicted as the same subtype
as the majority subtype of the tumor cells. In the original article, this was only the case for
3 of the samples (CID4067, CID4463, CID4523). It would be expected that the pseudobulk
data would be predicted as same subtype as the predominant subtype of the tumor cells if the
subtypes were positioned close to each other in the scaﬀold. On the other hand, if the subtypes
of the tumor cells were positioned far from each other in the scaﬀold, the pseudobulk would be
expected to be classiﬁed in the middle of the two subtypes, which might be a third subtype.
For the classiﬁcation using the CIT scaﬀold, more discrepancies in classiﬁcation between the
pseudobulk data and real bulk RNA-seq data were observed compared to the PAM50 scaﬀold.
The CIT scaﬀold was deﬁned by almost three times as many genes as the PAM50 scaﬀold,
while the PAM50 scaﬀold explained 3 percentage points more in the ﬁrst two PCs compared
to the CIT scaﬀold. The higher number of genes in the CIT scaﬀold may have introduced
noise that could aﬀect sample classiﬁcation. Having a greater number of genes in the scaﬀold
provides more opportunities for samples to exhibit diﬀerences, which potentially could lead to
diverse subtype classiﬁcations. Additionally, there was one more subtype in the CIT scaﬀold
compared to PAM50, which made for an additional possibility for diverse classiﬁcation. On the
other hand, cross-validation of the distance-to-centroid method showed higher performance for
the CIT data compared to the PAM50. These discrepancies between performance on the bulk
data and performance of the cross-validation are odd.
Despite cross-validation of the distance-to-centroid method showing promising performance,
for further work on the breast cancer subtyping scaﬀolds, it could be beneﬁcial to test and
benchmark diverse classiﬁcation aiming for even higher performance. A potential next project
could involve deﬁning new scaﬀolds. A simple approach could be to combine the bulk gene
expression data from the PAM50 and CIT datasets and use an unsupervised clustering method,
such as k-means, to predict new subtypes and compare with the original ones. Another project
idea could involve deﬁning new subtyping schemes based on scRNA-seq data. The subtypes
could be deﬁned based on the fractions of distinct cell types or on the expression of tumor
cells. It is interesting to look at the tumor cells since those are the ones that are expected to
be diﬀerent between patients, though knowledge of the fraction of immune cells could also be
of value, especially if considering immunotherapy[8].
Bone Marrow Scaﬀold
The aim of the bone marrow scaﬀold was to investigate similarities to the hematopoietic tree.
The tree-like structure of diﬀerentiation of hematopoietic stem cells into mature cells has been

challenged, suggesting that the cut oﬀbetween a CLP’s or a CMP’s may not be too rigid[6].
Creating a scaﬀold based on gene expression of multiple genes might illustrate the continuous of
stem cells diﬀerentiation, as opposed to the categorical structure of the traditional hematopoi-
etic tree.
The bone marrow scaﬀold was build using scRNA-seq data from ﬁve individuals: one healthy
individual and four AML patients. When attempting to build a scaﬀold on all ﬁve individuals,
grouping within the patients showed, despite including cell types as the phenotype and with
subset_deg set to TRUE. This suggested that the largest diﬀerence in gene expression was across
patient and not cell type.
To address this, the n_genes parameter was adjusted to only include ﬁve diﬀerentially expressed
genes per cell type. This showed in-class similarity and between-class variation in PC space
with up to 35% of the variance explained in the ﬁrst two PC’s. While this seemed promising,
the scaﬀold was built using only 48 unique genes, making it less robust for projection of new
samples that might not include all the scaﬀold genes.
Moreover, this scaﬀold included both cancerous cell and healthy cells, which could make the
interpretation diﬃcult. Therefore, it was decided to use build the scaﬀold solely from data of
the healthy individual. While having more samples from healthy individuals would have been
ideal, more data was simply not available.
Projection of scRNA-seq data from the four patients tested whether the cancerous cells would
be projected to the same cell types as assigned in the original analysis. This would determine
if the simple single-individual scaﬀold was generalizable. This was a somewhat of a diﬃcult
question to answer, as the samples from the patients included more diverse cell types than the
healthy individual. The majority of the cells of the patients had been assigned as CD34+ or
CD34- Blasts, and while CD34+ Blasts were represented in the scaﬀold, there were few of them
and they overlapped with HSC/MPPs and MEP, making them diﬃcult to distinguish.
Additionally, for the AML patients it was expected that there would be a large amount of
CMP’s, which were not represented in the scaﬀold. Neutrophil and erythroid precursors, stem-
ming from CMP’s, could potentially serve as indicators for categorizing AML samples in this
context. In sample P2, there was a high proportion of neutrophil precursors, while second-
highest proportion was of B cells and progenitors. The B cells and progenitors stem from CLP,
which in high amounts are associated with ALL. Analyzing the more mature cell types instead
of CMP and CLP might not be meaningful, as these are not the cells expected to be cancerous
and overrepresented in AML and ALL, although they might share similarities with the more
mature cells. Nevertheless, the work was continued with the available information.

Conclusion
The two scenarios of using the spaceRAT R package were presented with two breast cancer
scaﬀold of bulk expression data and one bone marrow scaﬀold of single-cell expression data.
For the breast cancer scaﬀolds, there were fewer discrepancies between the pseudobulk data
and real bulk RNA-seq classiﬁed of the PAM50 scaﬀold compared to the CIT, which might
have to do with fewer genes and fewer subtypes of the PAM50 scaﬀold. Classiﬁcation of single-
cell data to cancer subytypes showed to be feasible, though it might not be meaningful to use
a classiﬁcation scheme that is based on bulk expression for this purpose. Hence it would be
interesting to investigate the potential of single-cell expression scaﬀolds and how new subtypes,
potentially within diverse cell types, would look like.
The bone marrow scaﬀold showed lack of cell types that might be necessary for leukemia classi-
ﬁcation. The classiﬁcation of AML and ALL samples showed diversity between the two, though
associating the observed proportions of cell types to the diseases was challenging, emphasizing
the need for a more nuanced scaﬀold with more diverse cell types from multiple individuals.
Through simulation of pseudobulk samples, distance-to-centroids probabilities, subtype clas-
siﬁcations, and exploration of cell type distributions, spaceRAT has demonstrated versatility
across diverse transcriptomic data sets which can be used in research and in the clinical setting.
