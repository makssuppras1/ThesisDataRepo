==PAGE:4==
Abstract
In this thesis, the energy management system developed by Bertin Technologies was improved
using data mining and machine learning algorithm. This energy management system (EMS)
has been developed to control hybrid PV plants with storage. The study was divided into two
subjects.
The ﬁrst one focused on how to improve the way the performance of a plant with the EMS is
estimated. The goal was to build a dataset of simulated performance and to be able to predict
the performance by using a multivariate polynomial regression trained on this dataset. This
regression was then used to predict the performances of a plant over 25 years. It will now be
reused as a standard tool of Bertin.
The second one was to improve directly the model implemented in the EMS to estimate the PV
power production of the plant. To do so, a neural network was trained and tested on past data
observed on an operating plant. The neural network showed good performances and helped to
decrease the error between the estimated power of the model and the real PV power produced.
The next step will be to implement similar neural networks on the other models implemented
in the EMS (batteries, inverters and transformers).

Chapter 1
Introduction
The exhaustion of fossil fuel resources and the increasing demand for electrical power have led
to more and more investigation and interest towards renewable energy in the last decades.
The energy mix in France relies on nuclear power, with 77% of the generated power in France
coming from nuclear sources in 2014. Yet by 2050 it would be possible to have a 90 to 100% share
of renewables in the energy mix according to the ADEME [2] and the negaWatt association.
In the French overseas departments, the renewable energy penetration can already reach up
to 30% during a sunny and windy day. This growing share of renewable energy is leading to
intermittence issues, as the human consumption is not correlated to solar irradiation or wind.
In 2009, CRE (Energy Regulation Commission) launched several calls for tenders to develop
PV and wind power plants coupled with storage systems to try to solve this intermittence
issue. Indeed for both PV and wind power the use of a storage system would allow to decrease
variability and increase predictability of the output power sent to the grid.
Bertin Technologies, within its Smart Energy department, developed an energy management
system (EMS) for PV plants coupled to storage systems. Their goal is to reply to the speciﬁ-
cations of the diﬀerent calls for tenders launched by CRE in the french overseas departments.
Bertin Technologies’ EMS aims at allowing these hybrid systems to work optimally in regards
of weather forecasts, economic matters, environmental matters and the constraints from CRE’s
call for tenders.
This EMS solution is composed by both hardware and software. Indeed, Bertin Technologies
has developed an Energy Management System (predictive controller), a web monitoring plat-
form and a simulation tool. This simulation tool is always used when trying to predict the
performances of a plant, it can help the project owner to decide how to size the battery, the
PV plant and the wind farm. But it can also be used to provide revenue estimations and
help the project owner to get ﬁnancing from banks or private investors. The ﬁrst part of the
master thesis consisted in developing a statistical analysis tool able to predict accurately the
performances of a plant over its lifetime by using as few simulations as possible.
The second part of the project was to improve the EMS, by working on the numerical model
used to represent the diﬀerent components of the plant. In order to optimize the revenues
generated by such a wind or solar plant it is essential to have accurate models. These models
will largely inﬂuence the way the EMS deﬁnes its injection proﬁle strategies, and how the
1
==PAGE:15==
system will react when following these strategies. If these strategies are not optimized, then it
can penalize the system and decrease the net incomes generated by the plant.

Chapter 2
Problem Statement
Two independent projects were carried out during the thesis. The aim of the ﬁrst part of the
thesis is to rethink the simulation tool developed by Bertin and try to implement a predictive
model able to estimate the performances of a plant by minimizing the number of simulations to
perform. The objective of the second part is to adjust the numerical model of the power plant
used by the EMS, using machine learning, to better predict the behaviour of the real plant.
2.1
Predictions of the Performances of a Plant
Predicting the performances of a PV plant without storage is easy, all that is needed is a dataset
of past irradiances and the eﬃciencies of each components. A computer can, then, calculate
very quickly the possible power production and expected revenues from the plant. But in the
case of a hybrid PV plant with storage controlled by an EMS, it is more complicated. Indeed
the way the EMS will control the plant is tough to predict, therefore it requires simulations to
know how the EMS will behave with a given input of forecasted and measured data.
Bertin already had an existing simulation tool, which enables them to simulate the behaviour
of a hybrid plant (PV and batteries) with an EMS. This tool has some drawbacks, indeed it
requires a set of expensive, historic data for each plant such as weather forecasts and measure-
ments of solar irradiances. But the main downside of the simulation tool is that it is very costly
in terms of computing time. Indeed simulating the performances of a plant over a year takes
approximately 3 to 4 days of calculations by a computer. The ﬁrst question which is raised is
how to rethink the simulation app, so it can predict quickly, accurately and with a minimum
of simulations, the performances of a hybrid PV plant with an EMS.
To overcome these two issues, it is decided to develop a statistical model able to predict the
performances of a plant over several years by using only a few sets of simulations. This model
should also be able to predict performances of a plant in new locations close to the simulated
one. In this way, Bertin can achieve accurate performance prediction faster and at a lower cost.
This statistical model is developed in collaboration with URBASOLAR which needs to predict
the revenues of several of their plants over their lifetime (20 to 25 years) in order to get ﬁnancing.

2.2
Adjustment of the Numerical Model
In order for the EMS to plan an optimal steering strategy of the plant, the model used by
the EMS to represent the diﬀerent components of the plant must be very precise and predict
accurately the output of the real system given certain inputs (solar irradiance, cell temperature,
eﬃciency of the diﬀerent inverters and transformers...).
When it comes to modeling solar cells or batteries, predicting the behaviour of these components
is tough. For example a diﬀerence in the solar production predicted by the numerical model
using a measure of the solar irradiance and the actual power produced was noticed on an
operating PV plant on the island of La Reunion. This diﬀerence can be seen on ﬁgure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: PV power predicted by the numerical model and actual measured power
The goal of the second part of the project is to implement and use a neural network to correct
this error between the PV power estimated by the numerical model and the PV power produced
by the plant.

Context
As it was said in Chapter 1, until now the energy management system has been developed for
a very speciﬁc market of PV plants coupled with storage in the French overseas department.
This market was created by the Regulatory Commission of Energy (CRE) in 2009 when they
launched several tenders to develop wind and solar power plants coupled with storage systems
in the French overseas department. The locations where the EMS has been and will be set up
can be seen on ﬁgure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Map of the operational EMS and future projects
Bertin’s EMS was developed to reply to three of these tenders, CRE ZNI tenders 2011 [3],
2015 [4] and 2016 [5]. These three tenders have some similarities :
• The capacity of the battery has to be at least of 0.5 kWh/kWpeak
• For each tender, the PV plant owner needs to notify at 4 PM the DSO with a planning
of injection for the following day
• The PV plant owner has to pay penalties in case the injection doesn’t respect the notiﬁed
power

CRE ZNI tender 2011
For the 2011, tender the Regulatory Commission of Energy decided that the notiﬁed power to

The 2011 expected injection proﬁle can be seen on ﬁgure 3.2.

3.2
CRE ZNI tender 2015
The 2015 tender is quite diﬀerent from the 2011.
First of all, there is no more imposed injection proﬁle throughout the day, the EMS can decide
freely the proﬁle of the power notiﬁed to the DSO one day before at 4PM. During the day the
EMS can redeclare to the DSO the notiﬁed power. The redeclaration has to be done at 4AM,
10AM and 2PM.
But the major diﬀerence is that over one day there are two diﬀerent electricity selling prices.
During the day the price is constant. Its value depends on the location of the site and is around
200 e/MWh. Between 7 PM and 9 PM the electricity price increases up to 400 e/MWh. The
goal of that shifting in the price is to encourage the plant owner to inject power at night when
the load on the grid is high. Therefore, the EMS, in order to maximize revenues, will aim at
charging the battery during the day and discharging all of it in two hours at night. The goal of
the EMS is to try to have a fully charged battery at 7 PM just before the price increases, but
also to try and inject as much power as possible during the day to minimize the curtailment of
the PV power.
The value of the notiﬁed power during the peak can’t be modiﬁed after the ﬁrst redeclaration
of the day at 4AM. The last two redeclarations can only aﬀect the notiﬁed power until 7PM.
One day of operation of the EMS was simulated and plotted on ﬁgure 3.4.
Figure 3.4: Typical power injection proﬁle of the 2015 tender
It can be seen that throughout the day, the battery is charging, in order to inject as much
energy as possible between 7 PM and 9 PM. Figure 3.4 also shows that the battery has to
discharge sometimes during the day, here around 9 AM, so that the power injected to the grid
remains close enough to the notiﬁed power in order to avoid penalties. A penalty is applied
if the actual power injected to the grid is too far from the power notiﬁed to the DSO, when

Description of the EMS Developped by
Bertin
The function of an EMS is to generate, in real time, steering strategies of an energy system.
For example for PV with storage applications, an EMS will deﬁne the level of power sent to
the grid and the level of power charged to or discharged from the battery.
The EMS developed by Bertin Technologies allows to deﬁne an optimal steering strategy for
diﬀerent hybrid energy systems. These systems are controlled in real time. By anticipating daily
disturbances, the EMS allows them to work optimally in regards of economic and environmental
matters.
This EMS can be used in many hybrid systems, but for now it is only used to control PV plants
with storage in the context of the CRE ZNI tenders presented in chapter 3. Such a PV plant
controlled by the EMS can be seen on Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Architecture of the power plant controlled by the EMS

The PV plants are composed by:
• PV panels and PV inverters.
• Batteries and battery inverters.
• Bertin’s EMS, controlling those inverters.
• Auxiliaries which are just a load consuming energy from the plant to provide cooling to
the batteries.
• Transformer connected to the grid to adjust the voltage of the plant to the voltage of the

• An AC bus which connects the PV inverters, the battery inverters, the auxilliaries and

Architecture of the EMS
The EMS developed by Bertin is a 2 level system, it is composed by:
• An optimization and scheduling module which uses solar forecast and the current state
of the plant to deﬁne steering strategies. This module makes a new calculation every 15
minutes.
• A control module which applies the optimal strategy and reacts to any unexpected devi-
ations with a time step of 1 second. This control of the plant is realized on the inverter
of the PV panels and the inverter of the storage system.

Operation Principle of the Scheduling and Optimiza-

The scheduling and optimization module can be divided into three modules: the numerical

Each module fulﬁlls a speciﬁc role:

• The cost function evaluates the quality of this evolution. The optimization criteria are

system then the control strategy selected by the solver might not be optimal.

5.1.1
Model Used
In order to simulate the performances of the EMS, the operation of a PV plant has to be
simulated. To begin it is necessary to develop numerical models for all the components of the
plant. Most of those numerical models are developed under Matlab/Simulink, though the PV
pannels production is calculated with Python by using the package pvlib [7].
Diﬀerent assumptions are made when developing and using the numerical models.
The ﬁrst one is that the model does not take into account any voltage or current, it only
deals with power ﬂows throughout the whole plant.
This is justiﬁed by the fact that the
tender’s constraints does not specify any control on the voltage, only the active power has to
be controlled by the EMS.
The second one is that the model uses a discrete timestep of 1 minute. It means that the
model is always supposed to be in steady state, the transient state in all the electronic parts is
neglected, as it occurs for less than a second, which is negligible compared to a minute.
Finally only some elements are considered and modeled, they are : the PV panels, the battery,
the inverters and the transformers.
Their respective models will be explained in the next
sections. The losses in the other components (cables, small electronic devices...) are neglected,
as they are very low compared to the losses induced by the four modeled components.
5.1.1.1
Batteries
The model of the storage system developed for the Bertin’s simulation tool can be broken down
in two diﬀerent parts. The ﬁrst one is the prediction of the state of charge (SOC) of the battery
depending on the power charged to or discharged from the battery and the second one concerns
the prediction of the battery aging, or battery state of health (SOH).
The SOC represents the amount of energy left in a battery compared with the energy it had
when it was full, it gives the user an indication of how long a battery will continue to perform
before it needs recharging. It is a measure of the short-term capability of the battery. The
model deﬁned to calculate the SOC of the battery is simple. As it was stated in section 5.1.1,
the model is only calculating power ﬂows throughout the system. This is why in order to
calculate the state of charge of the battery, only an eﬃciency is taken into account. Figure 5.2
represents the model used for the battery, where, η(Pcharged) and η(Pdischarged) represents the
eﬃciency of the battery when it is being charged or discharged.

Concerning the battery SOH, as one of the major drop in the performances of a hybrid PV
plant with storage is due to battery aging, it is crucial to evaluate this aging in order to simulate
correctly the battery behaviour in the model.
To model battery aging, Bertin used the work of Pierre Haessig [8].
In an electrochemical battery, there are two parameters that tend to change over the years:
the battery capacity which decreases and the internal resistance which goes up. The battery
capacity represents the maximum autonomy of the energy storage system, while the internal
resistance limits the maximum power that the battery can deliver. Usually, the end of life of a
battery is deﬁned by one of these two criteria:
• A 20% capacity fade is observed compared to the capacity observed when the battery
was new;
• The internal resistance increased by 100% since the begining of operation of the battery;
These criterion are usually used when dealing with electric vehicules. In the case of the CRE
ZNI tenders, the end of life of a battery is deﬁned by a 30% fade of the capacity.
Modelling battery degradation is complex, it is inﬂuenced by several factors, but it can be
decomposed into two parts:
• Calendar aging : deterioration of the battery when the battery is not operating (Pbat = 0),
it is the deterioration only aﬀected by time;
• Cyclic aging : deterioration of the battery when it is subjected to either charging or
discharging (Pbat ̸= 0);
16
==PAGE:30==
Calendar aging, for Lithium-ion batteries, is directly linked to the resting conditions of the
battery, in particular the temperature of the battery and its SOC. If the temperature and
the SOC are similar whenever the battery is at rest, it is possible to use a chart in order to
determine the calendar aging of the battery. For the type of plant under the CRE ZNI tenders’
constraints, the battery is placed in a closed space with a cooler nearby, therefore it is possible
to assume that the temperature of the battery is constant. Concerning the SOC, when the
battery is at rest at the end of the night peak, its SOC is then most of the time low (under
15%) as it can be seen on Figure 3.4. Under these conditions, according to Pierre Haessig
research [8], it is possible to neglect the inﬂuence of the calendar aging, compared to the cyclic
aging of the battery.
Cyclic aging refers to a series of charging and discharging cycles. Figure 5.5 shows an example
of how many cycles a given battery can handle depending on the depth of those cycles. As it

while large cycle depth will lead to a faster deterioration of the battery.

A PV system converts sunlight to electricity. Solar cells are the main components of a PV
panel, they are semiconductors with a p-n junction. When such a cell is exposed to sunlight, if
the photon energy is greater than the band gap, a photocurrent proportional to the irradiance
is generated. The simplest model of a PV cell is composed by an ideal current source in parallel
with a diode and a series resistance. The equivalent electrical circuit is shown on Figure 5.7, this
model is called single diode model [9]. The output power of each cell can then be calculated by
multiplying the output current I by the terminal voltage V. Rs represents the series resistance,
Rsh the shunt resistance, IL the generated photocurrent, ID the diode current and Ish the shunt
current.

source package developed at Sandia National Laboratories. In this package, the single diode

require :

5.1.2
Hypotheses on the Simulation
This section presents the diﬀerent hypotheses made when performing a simulation with Bertin’s
app. As it was explained in Section 4.2, the EMS is composed by a scheduling module which
tries to deﬁne an optimal injection proﬁle over the day by using diﬀerent forecasts.
This
scheduler is developed under Python. When using the simulation app, the main hypotheses
that are made concern the number of calculations made by the scheduler each day and the
forecasted and measured data used.
When the EMS is installed on a plant a new calculation is performed every 15 minutes, which
represents 96 calculations over each day. When using the simulation tool, it is not possible to
do as many calculations per day, as it would take a month to simulate a year of operation.
Moreover for each day 96 forecast ﬁles would be required. To decrease the amount of computer
time and forecast ﬁles necessary for one simulation, it was decided to decrease the number of
calculations to 4 each day. These calculations are set to be right before each power declaration
to the DSO, which are the 4 most important moments of the day. Before these declarations the
steering strategy of the plant has to be optimized. It is the strongest assumptions made when
using the simulation tool and it is of course decreasing the performances of the plant compared
to what would happen in reality, but the simulations still give a good approximation of what
would happen.
The second hypothesis concerns the measured and forecast ﬁles, these data are bought from
a forecast provider (most of the time REUNIWATT). They are composed of past data for a
year for a given location. The simulation tool requires 5 forecast ﬁles, at ﬁve diﬀerent times of
the day (one for each calculation of the EMS), and 1 measure ﬁle. Simulations are done using
historic data and may not correspond to future years of operation.

Training Dataset of the Statistical Model
In order to build a functional regression model, it needs to be trained with a proper dataset.
This dataset should cover a large scope of value for each variable. But it should not be composed
by too many simulations. Indeed if the statistical model requires almost as many simulations as
the number of simulations required to estimate the performances of the plant over its lifetime,
then it would not make sense to use a predictive model, as the main goal of the model is to
reduce the number of simulations to run.

5.3
Statistical Model
This section presents the statistical model used to predict the performances of the plant over
several years of operation.
The model chosen is a multivariate polynomial regression.
The ﬁrst step is to deﬁne the
explanatory variables of the regression, and the second one is to determine the polynomial
degree that should be used for the statistical analysis.
5.3.1
Hypotheses
The model needs to estimate the performance of the plant for a whole year, and in order for
the model to be as precise as possible, the predictions are done with the smallest possible
granularity. In this case each day is independent from one another, as the battery capacity
almost reaches the same SOC at the end of the day. And solar phenomena have a periodicity
of one day. It is then possible to divide the simulation dataset into daily performances and to
use the model to predict day by day the diﬀerent power ﬂows of the plant. In order to obtain
the total over the whole year the diﬀerent energies and revenues are summed over the year.
In order to develop a regression model as accurate as possible, it was decided that, to test the
performances of a model, the indicators will be analyzed by calculating the MAE between the
estimated and real indicators day by day.
5.3.2
Explanatory Variables
The explanatory variables are the variables that will be used as an input for the statistical
model to predict the rest of the indicators. To select them, ﬁrst, it is necessary to determine
which variables are available before doing any long simulation. The only variables that can be
used are the ones given by the forecasters and the ones that can be calculated without doing
any long simulation. Those variables are:
• The irradiance (GHI) observed over each day.
• The PV energy (DC MPP) which can be estimated very quickly by using the measured
irradiance.
23
==PAGE:37==
• The forecasted irradiance (GHI), which is used to calculate three kinds of errors (MAE,
MBE and RMSE) between the real and forecasted GHI.
• The battery capacity.
To select which available indicator should be used as an explanatory variable, the dataset was
analysed in order to detect correlations. The indicators with low correlations between them
will enable the statistical model to work optimally and can be used together as explanatory
variables of the model. The Spearman correlation matrix was calculated and plotted on ﬁgure
5.12, it shows that the PV energy (DC MPP) is highly correlated to the irradiance, that the
three forecasting errors (MAE, MBE and RMSE) are highly correlated between each other and
that the battery capacity is not correlated at all with the rest of the variables.
Figure 5.12: Spearman correlation matrix of the available indicators
With these criteria, three explanatory variables could be chosen:
• PV energy DC MPP or GHI
• MAE, MBE or RMSE
• The battery capacity
Finally the chosen variables are:
• PV energy DC MPP, which seems to be a more accurate variable to use than the GHI,
as the GHI does not take into account cell aging.
• Useful capacity of the battery (SOH ∗Cinitial), because it is independent from all the
other available variables and has a big inﬂuence on the performances of the plant.
24
==PAGE:38==
• Forecasting error of the GHI (MBE), because it can represent whether the forecast is
higher or lower than the measured irradiance, while the RMSE and MAE only gives
information on the diﬀerence between the actual irradiance and the forecasted irradiance.
5.3.3
Indicators to Predict
As it was said in the introduction of Chapter 5, the goal of the statistical model is to predict
the diﬀerent energy and ﬁnancial ﬂows of a hybrid PV plant under the constraints of the CRE
ZNI tenders. But the main indicators that should be predicted are the ones used to calculate
the diﬀerent ratios:
• The net revenues
• The PV energy curtailed and the PV energy DC MPP to calculate the curtailment ratio
• The energy injected on peak and oﬀpeak to calculate the peak injection ratio
• The energy charged to the battery and the PV energy AC to calculate the stored energy
ratio
• The penalties and the gross incomes to calculate the penalty ratio
5.3.4
Model

the maximum energy injected during those two hours is equal to the useful capacity of
the battery, as it was explained in Section 5.2. Moreover, the lower the battery capacity,
the more the EMS tends to inject power during the day, which also tends to reduce the
peak injection ratio. When the battery capacity goes down, the peak injection ratio goes
down as well.
• Penalty ratio: at ﬁrst it might seem contradictory that the penalty ratio decline while
the PV panels and the battery are deteriorated, it would seem logical to see the penalties
increasing as the plant is aging. But most of the penalties occur during the night peak,

the peak than what it would if the battery was new. This implies that if the battery
is not charged enough and cannot supply all the energy predicted during the peak, the
penalties generated would be lower. It can explain why the penalty ratio dwindles from
the 1st to the 12th year and from the 13th to the 25th year.
• Net revenues: the net revenues are linked to the PV energy produced, the deterioration

cause, as it was explained, fewer penalties during the night peak, but for most of the

Chapter 6
Adjustment of the Numerical Model
As it was explained in Section 4.2, in order for the EMS to select the optimal injection strategy,
the numerical model used in the EMS must be as close to reality as possible. The problem is
that an error made on a numerical model cannot be identiﬁed when performing simulations, it
is necessary to check on an existing plant what the model is doing. And, so far, there is only
one power plant driven by Bertin’s EMS, which was presented on Figure 3.3. It would have
been better to train and test the adjustment model on diﬀerent plants, but as it is not feasible
only the hybrid plant on the island of La Reunion was studied. This plant is operating under
the constraints of the 2011 CRE ZNI tender. It has been operational since 2014, therefore
several years of data are available. With these data it is possible to assess the error between
all the components of the plant and their numerical models developed inside the EMS. For this
project, only the PV plant model is studied and adjusted.
The machine learning model chosen to re-evaluate the numerical model is a neural network. It
was decided that a neural network should be used because it is simple to implement, fast to
optimize and it will be easy to update once the plant will be operating. Figure 6.1 shows the
implementation principle of the neural network on the numerical model.
First, the theoretical model is executed using historical input data In(t). The measured power
Powermeas(t) is then subtracted from the estimated power by the numerical model Powerest(t)
to generate an error: Biasest/meas(t).
Secondly, a learning algorithm tries to optimize the neural network so as to calculate a correction
as close as possible from the Biasest/meas(t), given a historical set of input data.
Finally, the neural network and numerical model are associated. The Correction(t) is sub-
stracted to the Powerest(t) to generate a unique output: the corrected power (Powercor(t)).
This output was calibrated given historical output, and will allow the numerical model to
behave more like the real system.
This process will be executed every 3 to 6 months depending on the magnitude of the error
measured between the numerical model and the real system.

• PV cell aging which is not included in the model.
• Deviations between the nominal values used as parameters of the numerical model and

• Design choices, so that the models can be accurate, easy to set and quick to execute.

Training and Test of the Neural Network
This section presents how the output data from the plant was ﬁltered in order to keep only
relevant information to train the model, how the neural network model was chosen in order
to correct the error between the real PV panels power production and its numerical model
developed in the EMS, and what were the performances of the neural network when testing it
on a dataset of several months.
6.2.1
Selecting the Training Data
The raw data from the plant are imported directly from Bertin’s computer installed on the
plant of Saint-Leu. The downloaded data is a series of csv ﬁles sorted by days, each one of
these ﬁles contains the information of the plant every minute. For three main reasons it is not
possible to exploit these ﬁles right after downloading them. It is necessary to select properly
what can be exploited and used to train and test a neural network.
First, some days are missing from the exported data.
Secondly, some ﬁles are miscalibrated. The two common miscalibrations detected by the algo-
rithm are:
• The number of rows of the dataset is higher or lower than the expected one. When it is
higher, it is that the last rows are just ﬁlled with null values, this can be detected and
solved easily. But when it is lower, there are missing rows in the dataset and if it is one
of the input of neural network, the day is deleted from the dataset.
• The presence of unrepresentable data (NaN) in one or several rows. As for the missing
rows, if the NaN value are in one of the variables used by the neural network, then this
day is deleted as all the used data were not saved properly.
Finally, even when the ﬁle is saved properly, some events during the day can lead to non-usable
data to train the neural network. In the case of the power plant of Saint-Leu, the data saved
39
==PAGE:53==
did not contain any curtailment information, it was impossible to know for sure whether the
plant was operating at its MPP or whether it was curtailing some of the available PV power.
And as the numerical model can only predict the PV power production at MPP, it creates an
increase of the bias between the numerical model predictions and the output power. Figure
6.6 shows an example of the diﬀerence between numerical model output and real output when
some PV is curtailed, the bias reaches 500 kW for that day.
Nevertheless by knowing how the EMS is working it is possible to predict when the system
is susceptible to curtail. The controller of the EMS decides to curtail some of the PV energy
when the battery is fully charged. Thus it was decided that the data given by the plant when

Chosen Model
As it was stated in the introduction of Chapter 6, a Neural Network was chosen to adjust the
numerical model to the real outputs of the system.
This neural network uses one hidden layer and a logistic activation function.
Concerning the inputs passed to the neural network, they must be available in the dataset
imported from the plant, the inputs chosen are:
• The PV energy DC MPP, which is an output of the numerical model and can be saved
throughout each day of operation. This variable is used because it is the one that needs
to be corrected.
• The solar position (azimuth and elevation) which is obtained by knowing only the date
and the time. The solar position is passed to the neural network so it can learn about
the masks around the plant.
• The measured quantities, by the diﬀerent sensors on the plants, which are solar irradiance
(GHI, DHI and DNI) and air temperature. These two quantities are used as inputs for the
neural network model, because they are used by the numerical model when it calculates
the PV energy DC MPP.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Throughout this MSc thesis two projects were carried out in order to improve Bertin’s EMS
existing simulation tools and models.
7.1
Predictions of the Performances of a Plant
The ﬁrst project aimed at improving the simulation tool. Even if it is able to predict precisely
the performances of a plant, this tool has a major drawback: it takes a long computing time for
each simulation. As it was explained, simulating the performance of a plant over a year takes
around 3 to 4 days. To improve the simulation tool, the goal of the ﬁrst project was to develop
a statistical model to reduce the number of simulations when predicting the performance of a
plant over several years. The statistical model chosen is a multivariate polynomial regression.
By using it, it was possible to predict accurately the performance of a plant (with an error of
less than 2% on the net revenues) and to reduce the number of simulations from 25 to 4.
But one of the limit of this statistical model is that the prediction granularity cannot be much
lower than a year, as the daily error committed by this model can be high. The polynomial
regression cannot be used the same way as the simulation tool, indeed it is less precise as it
cannot predict the daily indicators of the plant, however it is a useful tool when predicting
many years of operation.
It was used by Bertin for one project on the 2015 tenders, but the goal is now to make it a
standard tool of the EMS either for Bertin to propose performance warranty to the plant owner
or to help the plant owner to get funding. The statistical model will be used in the following
weeks to predict the performance of several plants.
7.2
Adjustment of the Numerical Model
The second project was to improve the numerical PV model used inside the EMS by imple-
menting a neural network able to learn on past data to increase the precision of the model. This
machine learning model was trained by using historical data of the hybrid plant of Saint-Leu.
It allowed to decrease the error between the estimated output power of the numerical model
by more than 35%.
44
==PAGE:58==
As for the statistical model, Bertin wants this neural network to become a standard tool of
the EMS. But one of the limit of this model is the data treatment of the outputs of the plant,
indeed it is speciﬁc from one plant to another.
Now that the neural network has been successfully tested on the PV numerical model, the
future steps will be to implement it and test it for the other components of the numerical
model, which are the battery, the inverters and the transformers.
