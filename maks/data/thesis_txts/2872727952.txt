Abstract
Drought-related structural damage has become an increasing concern in Denmark, particularly in
masonry buildings founded on clay-rich soils. This thesis examines 75 insurance cases following the
2022–2023 drought to identify key factors contributing to damage and evaluate the effectiveness of
various geotechnical, hydrological, vegetation-related, and structural parameters in explaining damage
severity. The study also assesses the applicability of satellite-based InSAR data in this context.
Damage classification followed a modified scale by Burland et. al., with damage classes ranging from
0-5, and was analysed against continuous variables such as plasticity index, soil moisture trends, and
vegetation proximity. Results show that geotechnical parameters — especially wP and wL in the deeper
layer and IP in the foundation layer — were the most consistent predictors of damage. Vegetation with
high desiccation potential and located close to buildings appeared to contribute to damage, though the
analysis was limited by data uncertainty. Early-season drought conditions, reflected in the spring SMDI
slope and mean value at the end of the slope, correlated more strongly with damage than short-term
drought before the incident. In contrast, the InSAR ground displacement data showed no consistent
correlation with observed damage and generally suggested that the model is more suitable for global-
scale applications than for the local context of this thesis.
The findings confirm that structural damage arises from a combination of interacting factors, with no
single parameter sufficient to explain outcomes on its own. While correlations supported several trends,
visual interpretation and integrated analysis were essential to understanding damage mechanisms. The
study highlights the limitations of current classification practices and suggests the potential for more
continuous or probabilistic approaches to improve future damage assessment and drought resilience
planning.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
As climate change alters Denmark’s weather, the country is expected to experience increasingly frequent
extreme weather events — prolonged droughts, intense rainfall, and irregular summer precipitation. In
the high emission scenario, the temperature is expected to rise by 3.4 ◦C by 2100, with wetter winters,
longer dry spells and heavy downpours [1]. These changes challenge buildings on clay-rich soils since
expansive clays, common in Denmark, shrink when they dry out.
This causes desiccation cracks,
differential settlement, and structural damage. When moisture returns, the soils re-expand, further
stressing foundations.
Vegetation can speed up drying by drawing water from the soil, leading to
localised shrinkage. While droughts are not new, their frequency and severity have increased, notably
in 2018 and 2022, with significant societal and economic impacts [2].
In 2018, Denmark saw its driest summer in nearly a century, causing widespread cracking in buildings
on clay soils. Although such damage is not new, it was not included in insurance coverage until 2022.
That year, the Danish Natural Damage Council expanded its scheme to cover subsidence from drought,
showing growing recognition of the risk. As extreme weather becomes more common, understanding
and managing clay soil behaviour is increasingly important [2].
While Denmark is just beginning to tackle this, the UK has already faced major subsidence issues during
droughts. In dry years like 2003 and 2018, UK subsidence claims spiked, especially where high-plasticity
London Clay is found [3]. In response, the National House Building Council (NHBC) issued guidelines
on tree distance, soil type, and foundation depth [4].
In 2012, the Association of British Insurers
gave drought advice for homeowners, recommending expert checks on vegetation, pipe inspections to
prevent leaks, and routine maintenance. They also reassured homeowners that small cracks are usually
harmless, helping to limit unnecessary worry [5].
Despite increased awareness, the interplay between soil type, plasticity index (IP ), clay mineralogy,
vegetation, and rainfall is still not fully understood. These factors directly influence a soil’s tendency
to shrink during drought, which can cause settlement and foundation damage. Clays with a high IP
undergo significant volume changes as they dry, making them especially vulnerable to cracking and
differential settlement [6]. Root systems from trees and shrubs can intensify this drying, depending on
species, height, and distance from buildings. Especially trees with deep roots and high water uptake
remove moisture from the soil, increasing desiccation depth and settlement risk, particularly when
located close to structures [4]. Okkels showed in 1995 that both the plasticity index and vegetation
characteristics can be used to estimate the minimum foundation depth required to avoid shrinkage
damage. His work highlights that in fat clays, shallow foundations are often insufficient, and design
must account for soil type and nearby vegetation to ensure stability [7].

2.1
Definition of drought
In Denmark, drought lacks a formal physical definition and is generally described as a period when
rainfall is insufficient to meet water demands. The Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) quantifies
drought through a drought index ranging from 0 (fully saturated soil) to 10 (completely dry soil).
A day is classified as a drought day if the index exceeds 9 nationwide. This index is derived from
a water balance model estimating daily plant-available soil moisture within a reservoir set at 100
mm, accounting for soil type and root depth. The model resets annually on March 1st and considers
precipitation, evaporation, and percolation to estimate soil water content (WC). In Figure 2.1, the
number of drought days is illustrated over the period 2005-2022, with the data taken from DMI [8].
Figure 2.1: Distribution of drought days from 2005 to 2022. The year 2018 is seen to be the driest
year of that period, with the number of drought days reaching 58. Notably, the four preceding years
experienced no drought days at all, whereas each of the four years that followed recorded multiple
drought days.
Globally, drought definitions vary: the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change defines drought
as “a period of abnormally dry weather long enough to cause a serious hydrological imbalance”. The
handbook Hydrological Drought describes it as a “lack of water compared to what is normal or in
balance with what natural systems and society have adapted to”. The Geological Survey of Denmark and
Greenland (GEUS) defines drought as a “temporary decrease in average water availability, for example
due to insufficient rainfall”, emphasising that drought is a natural, climate-driven phenomenon which
can occur in any region or season and whose impacts worsen under existing water scarcity or poor
resource management [2].

Drought typically begins after prolonged periods of low precipitation, initially depleting soil moisture
and subsequently affecting shallow groundwater, while deeper layers (DL) respond more slowly. High
temperatures, evaporation, wind, and radiation can intensify drought conditions, while changes in
deeper groundwater can take days to months to become apparent. Winter precipitation is essential for
recharge, as summer infiltration is limited, making winter droughts particularly impactful. Denmark’s
diverse geology causes regional differences in recharge and drought response [2].
It is important to distinguish drought as a natural climate phenomenon from water scarcity caused
by overexploitation or pollution, though such scarcity can reduce resilience to drought impacts. The
distinction matters, since the two issues require drastically different solutions.
While dealing with
drought means being prepared and planning ahead, addressing water scarcity usually involves using
water wisely, reducing waste, and maintaining its cleanliness.
2.2
Existing guidelines
Denmark has experienced several major droughts (e.g., 1899, 1947, 1976, 1992), though focus has
historically been centred on agricultural impacts. Only in the past five years has attention shifted to
building damage, with insurance for drought-related cracks introduced in 2022 [2]. In contrast, the UK
has treated drought as a structural risk on shrinkable soils for decades. Guidance on building near trees
dates back to 1949, advising a minimum distance equal to tree height following damage from the 1947
drought. The NHBC refined this in 1969 by classifying trees by water demand, and in the 1960s–70s,
deeper foundations became a common alternative to large tree setbacks. Compared to Denmark’s recent
focus, the UK has a long-established approach to mitigating drought-related structural risk [4].
NHBC is the UK’s leading independent provider of warranty and insurance for new-build homes, and
has contributed to identifying the key factors to consider when building on clayey soils, including how
vegetation near a building can affect its structural safety. In their newest standard, effective January
1, 2025, they list potential hazards and their associated risks when building new houses. Here, they
mention trees and vegetation, where the risks are shrinkage and heave of clay soils and physical damage
caused by roots [4].

2.3
Structural parameters
Structural vulnerability to drought-related damage in masonry buildings is closely linked to key design
and structural parameters. Shallow foundations, which do not extend to shrinkage-free depths, are
particularly susceptible to soil movement caused by moisture fluctuations, making them more exposed
to differential settlement and cracking than deeper foundations. Additionally, the quality of construction

2.3.1
Assessing degree of damage in masonry buildings
When a masonry building experiences damage, it is often desired to assess the degree to which the
building is damaged. However, this assessment can be highly subjective, as perspectives may vary sig-
nificantly depending on the stakeholder involved. For example, a homeowner may perceive the damage
differently from an insurance assessor. It is therefore desirable to use a more objective classification
scheme, something that was first presented by Burland et al. in 1977 [11].
Burland et. al. proposed a framework for classifying building damage, categorising it into three broad
types: damage that affects only the visual appearance or aesthetics of the structure, damage that
impairs serviceability or function, and damage that compromises structural stability. From these, six
categories of damage were defined from 0 to 5, with an increase in severity. The lower categories from
0-2 mainly affect visual appearance. Category 3-4 affects the serviceability of the building and often
requires more repair. Lastly, category 5 affects the stability of the building [11].

The response of buildings to settlements is governed by soil-structure interaction, which, according to

2.4.1
Plasticity
When clay is deposited in still water, it initially forms a water-rich, fluid-like mass. Over time, as more
clay accumulates and water is gradually expelled due to the weight of overlying layers, the clay becomes
more compact and transitions from a liquid to a plastic state at the liquid limit (wL). In the plastic
state, clay deforms under pressure and retains its shape after unloading. Further compression reduces
the water content until it reaches the plastic limit (wP ), beyond which the clay becomes semi-solid and
may crack when dried. The difference between the liquid and plastic limits is defined as the plasticity
index (IP = wL−wP ). The plasticity index is a key geotechnical parameter for clay soils: it is negligible
in sands and silts, but significantly higher in clays, exceeding 8 % in sandy clays and reaching over 100
% in fat clays. Soils with high IP values are particularly prone to settlement and stability issues due
to it’s greater volume change potential, making IP a critical factor in evaluating the risk of foundation
damage in structures built on clay [6].
Clay-rich soils — like the moraine clay and plastic clay — have significant implications when considered
alongside climatic factors. These soils possess a high water-holding capacity due to their fine-grained
structure and low permeability.
While this trait helps retain moisture under normal conditions, it
becomes a liability during extended dry periods. In times of drought, the loss of moisture from these
soils can lead to pronounced shrinkage, causing ground movement and, consequently, structural stress
on foundations. This makes areas with clay-dominated soils particularly susceptible to damage during
increasingly frequent drought events.

2.4.2
Clay mineralogy
The shrink–swell capacity of clay soils is largely governed by the mineralogical composition, particularly
the specific surface area and structure of the constituent clay minerals. Montmorillonite, a 2:1 clay
mineral (referring to its layered structure), with a surface area of up to 800 m2/g, exhibits substantial
water retention and swelling potential. In contrast, kaolinite with a 1:1 structure has a much lower
surface area, 10–20 m2/g, resulting in limited expansion and lower shrink–swell susceptibility [6].
Clay particles attract water through surface charge interactions, forming a structured water layer.
Under loading conditions (e.g., glacial pressure), water is expelled, and the void ratio decreases, leading
to consolidation. When drying occurs, particularly during droughts, volume reduction in high-activity
(activity being ratio between IP and CC) clays leads to tensile stress buildup, eventually causing
cracks once the soil’s tensile strength is exceeded [17]. These cracks compromise the soil’s load-bearing
capacity and structural integrity, heightening the risk of differential settlements and structural damage
to buildings.

2.4.4
Soil moisture evaporation dynamics
Evaporation plays a crucial role in desiccation and is influenced by soil texture, plasticity, and envi-
ronmental conditions. Clay soils which has experienced cracking, allow for faster migration of water,
leading to faster swelling [16]. The Atterberg limits, especially the liquid limit, serve as indicators of
a soil’s water retention capacity. High-liquid-limit soils lose moisture more slowly but may experience
severe shrinkage upon drying, leading to cracking.
Furthermore, environmental parameters such as temperature, humidity, and depth of water storage af-
fect evaporation rates. Cracked soils may exhibit faster evaporation due to increased surface area, while
intact, plastic-rich soils may slow down water loss. These evaporation dynamics contribute significantly
to shrinkage intensity during drought and are thus essential in understanding structural vulnerability
[20].

2.5.1
Safe distance and shrinkage-free depth
The extent of root systems influences soil moisture depletion. Different tree species and shrubs exhibit
varying root depths, affecting desiccation depth and shrinkage-induced settlement. Trees with high
transpiration rates, such as maples and catalpa, exacerbate soil desiccation and induce differential
settlement [4]. Foundation damage correlates with the relative distance between trees and buildings
(x/H), where damage risk is significantly higher when x/H < 1.0 for single trees and < 1.5 for tree
rows, where x is the distance between the house and tree, and H is the height of the tree [7].
Empirical studies have led to the development of shrinkage-free foundation depth formulas based on
soil plasticity and vegetation influence. Smaller older houses in Denmark are traditionally founded on
0.3 m wide, unreinforced strip foundations, while the depth is set to a minimum of 0.9 m for heated
buildings today. However, due to other research, a minimum of 1.2-1.5 m for buildings on fat clay is
proposed [7].
This approach is based on a model proposed by Okkels in 1995, which estimates the shrinkage-free depth
of foundations using several influencing parameters. The study highlights that tree species, height,
distance from building, and the clay’s plasticity impact this depth. As such, accurately determining
the shrinkage-free depth is essential for ensuring the structural stability of buildings [7].

2.6
Hydrological model
The DK-model is a national, integrated hydrological model developed by GEUS to simulate the com-
plete hydrological cycle across Denmark. It includes surface runoff, infiltration, evapotranspiration,
groundwater recharge, and flow in both surface waters and aquifers, all dynamically linked and driven
by climate input. The latest version, DK-model HIP (Hydrologisk informations- og prognosesystem),
enhances spatial resolution with 500 m and 100 m grids and uses topographic and seabed data to
improve accuracy, especially in simulating near-surface groundwater conditions. Simulations are run
daily and calibrated against thousands of observations using advanced optimisation algorithms within
the MIKE SHE/MIKE HYDRO system. The model supports climate adaptation, resource assessment,
and real-time hydrological forecasting [26].
The hydrological model allows for the evaluation of historical hydrological conditions before and during
potential drought damage events, rather than relying solely on geotechnical soil analyses, which often
provide only a delayed snapshot taken weeks or months after the damage occurred.
2.6.1
Land use and vegetation
Statistics Denmark collects agricultural data, including which crops are grown, the area used, and the
annual crop yield in hectokilograms per hectare. Yield data is gathered through sample-based surveys
covering approximately 8 % of all farms.
Land use classification follows BASEMAP2012 and Agricultural crop distribution per field (LAFPM)
data from the Ministry of Food, Agriculture, and Fisheries. The main categories include grassland, for-
est (deciduous/coniferous), heathland, water bodies, impervious surfaces, and agricultural land, which
is further divided into winter crops, spring crops, rotational grass, maize, and root crops. Crop distri-
bution is based on municipality-level data, with cover crops modelled through an additional 1.5-month
growth period for spring crops.
Vegetation parameters are integrated through a two-layer unsaturated zone module in MIKE SHE,
using values for leaf area index, root zone depth, and crop coefficient. Root zone depth is primarily
determined by soil type rather than crop type, based on the assumption that soil characteristics domi-
nate rooting behaviour. Agricultural land is classified into 20 combinations of crop and soil type, with
parametrisation primarily reflecting seasonal and soil-driven variability in evapotranspiration [26].
2.6.2

The model is based on national soil maps from GEUS and river data from GeoDanmark (2019). Ground-
water–surface water interactions are estimated using soil types to assess how easily water moves between
them. Different model results are calculated at different intervals: daily for surface water, every 15 days

2.7
Interferometric synthetic aperture radar measurements
Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) is a remote sensing technique used to detect ground
deformation from events like landslides, mining, and volcanic activity. Differential forms — D-InSAR
and MT-InSAR — measure displacement along the radar’s line of sight (LOS). Though limited to one
dimension, combining multiple LOS observations enables estimation of 3D ground movement. Using
ascending (south to north) and descending (north to south) SAR data from at least three viewing angles
allows for the separation of horizontal and vertical displacement [28].

Soil deformation can be analysed using Sentinel-1 InSAR data from the European Ground Motion
Service (EGMS). It focuses on identifying subsurface instability beneath structures, aiming to support
urban planning, infrastructure monitoring, and risk management through spatially broad, cost-effective
displacement mapping.
The Sentinel-1 data is part of the EU’s Copernicus Programme. Sentinel-1 satellites (currently only
Sentinel-1A) orbit at 693 km altitude, using C-band (referring to the range of frequencies) SAR instru-

InSAR data; Calibrated, referenced to a global navigation satellite system model; and Ortho, which

the radar observes terrain to the east of the satellite’s track, and during descending orbits, it observes
terrain to the west. The satellites operate primarily in the Interferometric Wide Swath mode, which
uses the Terrain Observation with Progressive Scans (TOPS) technique. This acquisition mode provides
a swath width of approximately 250 km with a ground resolution of about 5 × 20 m [28].

The database was normalised in First Normal Form (1NF), which is the simplest method of normalising
a database. To adhere to 1NF, the following points needed to be true for the database.
• Each attribute holds only a single value (no repeating or composite values).
• The attribute domain remains constant and does not change.
• Every attribute/column has a unique and distinct name.
• The order in which data is stored does not affect its structure or integrity
For the size of this database, it was not determined to be necessary to create a database of higher
normalisation, since only 75 cases were investigated. That being said, it was important to be aware
of inconsistencies in the database, such as misspellings, since this would potentially have changed the
outcome. One way to mitigate this was to primarily use numbers for descriptors, check the database
multiple times, and be critical of the data. Running periodic checks and taking out random samples to
ensure consistency across the database was also a key aspect [38].

3.1.1.1
Observational approach
To quantify the damage to the structures, aiming to carry out a statistical analysis, the degree of
damage was determined for each case. This was done with a visual analysis based on the pictures
taken on site during the insurance investigation, held up with the description of the damages in the
geotechnical reports. Using the classification scheme from Burland et al. presented in Table 2.4, the
buildings could be categorised into 6 unique categories from 0-5.
A building was classified as DC 0 if only hairline cracks were visible and no significant structural damage
was reported. DC 1 was assigned when the cracks appeared as thin as a pencil line in the images, and
again, no major damage was described. DC 2 included cases where cracks were slightly wider than
those in DC 1, often determined through direct comparison. DC 3 was used when there were either
reports of functional issues, such as sticking doors or windows, or when cracks of notable width (5–15
mm) were observed. A few buildings were classified as DC 4 or DC 5 due to severe damage indicators
such as leaning walls or a risk of structural instability.

3.3
Vegetation
The vegetation influences the damage to a building, since roots are located in the shallow soil layers,
which is also where the foundation is situated in 75 insurance cases. In combination with building on
fat clay, not extending the foundation deep enough, and having predominantly deciduous vegetation in
the area, could contribute to an increased risk of building damage during prolonged drought periods.
Initially, all vegetation was recorded for each case, including the type (such as tree or bush) and, when
specified, more detailed classifications like deciduous or coniferous, as well as species such as birch or
elm. The distance to the nearest building wall was recorded, or estimated when not explicitly noted, but
estimation was feasible. It was also noted whether the tree or shrub was located close to the damage,
meaning if they were on the side of the house where cracks had formed. Finally, the height of the tree
was included if this information had been recorded.

3.4
Hydrological model
The indices SMDI and SGDIshallow, further denoted SGDI, were looked into in this thesis since these
directly relate to the soil moisture in the foundation layer and deeper. The hydrological model simplifies
the root zone by representing it as a uniform layer whose depth shifts with the growing season. Since
this depth changes throughout the year, simulated soil moisture reflects a varying volume, making it
hard to directly compare with measured values. To better track seasonal patterns and overall moisture
trends, the indices were used in the thesis instead of the exact values.
3.4.1
Analysis of data
The indices SGDI and SMDI were derived for the 75 cases within the hydrological model from GEUS,
except case 34, since no data were available on Samsø. Each dataset corresponded to a 100 x 100 m
grid, encompassing the locations of the specific houses. It was important to note the size of this grid,
since this introduced an uncertainty when using the model for small-scale purposes, as was done in this
study.
To generate input variables for the multivariate analysis, several values were extracted for each case.
These included the slope of the graph over the 1–2 months preceding the incident, as well as the
negative slope during spring or early summer for the SMDI. In addition, the mean of the last two data
points immediately before the incident was calculated for both indices, along with the mean of the last
two data points along the spring slope for the SMDI. The mean values indicate whether conditions in
the weeks leading up to the incident were drier than usual, while the slope reflects the rate at which
moisture conditions changed — either becoming drier or wetter — before the appearance of cracks
and/or settlements. The spring values from the SMDI were recorded to tell something about what the
soil experienced during the drier months. This was done for all 74 cases with available data.
Cyclical patterns in soil moisture were analysed by plotting SMDI curves for individual cases. Thresh-
olds were set at -1 (transition from mild to moderate drought) and +1 (indicating wetter conditions).
The areas above and below these thresholds were calculated for summer (April–September) and winter
(October–March) periods. Mean areas were then computed for each DC, allowing a single curve to
represent each class. This illustrates how SMDI fluctuates cyclically under extreme conditions. The cu-
mulative function of the areas was also plotted for each class to better illustrate the differences between
the DCs.

3.5
InSAR measurements
Satellite data from EGMS from Copernicus were downloaded in both ascending and descending orbits.
A total of 343 files were downloaded for the 75 cases, including all descending and ascending orbit
measurements for each specific area. In these, time series with the dLOS measurements were recorded,
as well as 25 metadata parameters, most notably the coordinates of each point, temporal coherence,
incidence angle, azimuth angle, mean velocity, and cos (θ). The mean velocity mentioned here was the
velocity of the displacement from 2019-2024, accounting also for seasonality in the data [39].
These time series were spatially filtered to a 75-meter radius around each house. This radius was selected
to capture not only ground movement directly at the house location, but also in the surrounding
area, enabling evaluation of whether potential damage was isolated to a single structure or reflects
broader vulnerability in the local neighbourhood. Then, a building footprint polygon was drawn around
each house in QGIS, which is an open source geographic information system software, to extract data
specifically from within the area of the structure, see Figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: Sketch of InSAR measurements showing all ascending and descending signals in the area

to characterise the immediate surroundings. The red points illustrate signals farther away, which were
less relevant to the case. White areas indicate surfaces that could not reflect signals from the satellite,

When this was done, it was found that two of the cases did not contain any data within the building
parameter, namely, cases 39 and 64. Looking into this, case 39 proved to be located near a forest,
and surrounded by crop fields, meaning no surfaces were reflective enough to facilitate the satellite
measurements. Case 64 did have points within the 75 m radius; however, no points were found within
the actual building. This was the only case with a terraced house, which could have influenced the

was not precise enough for the specific locations and was therefore not used.
A weighted average was then calculated using the temporal coherence of each point, where temporal
coherence reflected the reliability of the data: values closer to 1 indicated high reliability, while values
approaching 0 suggested greater uncertainty. This led to up to two time series per. case, one based on
all points with a negative velocity, and one based on all points with a positive velocity, both referring
to the 2019-2022 mean velocity in [mm/year].
3.6
Statistical analysis
To analyse the data, cumulative frequency functions, box plots, and probability density functions were
generated for all parameters, providing insight into their characteristics and an overall view of the
database. For parameters that cannot take values less than or equal to zero, such as IP , for example,
a log-normal distribution was fitted to ensure that the probability density function appropriately ap-
proaches zero at x = 0. This was achieved by first applying a logarithmic transformation to the data,
fitting a normal distribution to the transformed values, and then converting the results back to the
original scale using an exponential function. All PDFs are plotted with corresponding histograms and
scaled using a factor based on the ratio of the histogram’s maximum count to the PDF’s peak value.
Following this, scatter plots were used to explore cross-correlations between parameters. Since it was
necessary to limit the number of parameters considered at once, the analysis began by correlating all
relevant geotechnical parameters, determined based on the main findings from the previous studies.
The parameter showing the strongest correlation was selected for further study.
Next, this key geotechnical parameter was cross-correlated with additional parameters from the hydro-
logical model. From this step, the most strongly correlated parameters were again tested against all
geotechnical parameters to verify the consistency of the correlations. Finally, a subset of six parame-
ters, identified as most correlated and critical, were cross-correlated with each other to examine their
interrelationships in more detail.

5.1.2
Comparing analytical and visual damage classifications
The following evaluates the use of levelling measurements as a complementary or alternative method
to visual damage classification. Only nine cases included levelling measurements (see Figure 4.39 to
Figure 4.47), which limits the ability to fully evaluate this method as an alternative to visual damage
assessment.
Levelling captures actual differential settlement, whereas visual classification is based
on observable cracks and repair needs.
In three cases, as shown in Table 5.1 and Table 5.2, the
analytically derived DC exceeded the visually observed DC, suggesting that although notable structural
displacement had occurred, it had not yet resulted in severe visible damage.
This discrepancy highlights a key limitation of the visual method: it captures only the current appear-
ance, not underlying stress or future deterioration. Conversely, the analytical method may indicate
significant differential settlement without corresponding visible damage. The differences between the
two approaches underscore the difficulty of accurately estimating damage class—visual assessments risk
underestimation if based on limited or distant images, while analytical assessments may overestimate
damage if visual effects are minimal.
It should be noted that the wall height for all cases and the length for some cases were estimated,
which introduces uncertainty in the analytical calculations. Consequently, the derived DCs should be
treated as approximations rather than precise values. Due to the uncertainty in these dimensions, no
correlation analysis between the L/H ratio and damage class was conducted.
In two cases — case 43 and case 8 (see Table 4.1) — both showed very small differential settlements,
resulting in analytical DCs of 0. However, their visual classifications differed: DC 1 for case 43 and DC 0
for case 8. This suggests that even minimal settlement can produce visible damage, or that the analytical
model under-represents structural response in certain scenarios. Such inconsistencies highlight the role
of uncertainties in the input parameters, particularly estimated dimensions and material behaviour.
5.1.3
Role of geotechnical and structural factors
Differences in foundation conditions and building characteristics can help explain some of the incon-
sistencies between observed and calculated damage. For example, in the comparison between cases 22
and 61 (see Figure 4.42 and Figure 4.47), both had nearly identical settlements and wall dimensions,
resulting in the same analytical DC of 2. Yet, case 22 had a visual DC of 3, while case 61 had a DC of
2. One possible explanation lies in the soil conditions: Case 22 had a foundation on fat clay till, while

Case 61 was founded on rather fat clay till. Given the similar settlements, soil type alone may not fully
explain the discrepancy.
Another notable difference between cases 22 and 61 is building age, 43 years and 95 years, respec-
tively. While it may seem intuitive that older buildings are more vulnerable due to material fatigue
or outdated methods, the data suggest otherwise. As shown in Figure 4.3, older buildings tended to
sustain less damage. This could be because long-standing structures have already withstood various
stresses, indicating durable construction and inherent resilience. In contrast, some newer buildings may
have been built with lower-cost materials or under less rigorous standards, reducing their capacity to
resist damage. This could explain why case 22 experienced more damage than case 61, despite similar
settlement magnitudes and comparable foundation depths (1.1 m vs. 0.9 m) and identical lowest x/H
ratios of 0.4. These findings highlight that, beyond foundation conditions, structural age and build
quality can influence damage outcomes.
Following this, the number of storeys can be explored as a potential factor increasing the risk of damage.
Figure 4.4 shows the distribution of damage classifications across buildings with different numbers of
storeys. While it might be expected that single-storey buildings are more prone to visible damage due
to structural simplicity or stress concentration, the data does not support a clear correlation. All storey
types (one-, one-and-a-half-, and two-storey buildings) span a range of DCs from 0 to 3. In the context
of cases 22 and 61, case 22 is a more complex single-storey structure, while case 61 is a simpler one-and-
a-half-storey building. Although structural complexity might contribute to the observed variation in
visual damage, the small sample size makes this conclusion speculative. Notably, both buildings have
the same estimated wall length, but case 22 has a larger L/H ratio, which aligns with the expectation
that higher L/H values correlate with greater damage.

Examining the position and damage severity of the cases in Figure 4.1, most notably, the majority are

observed damage.
5.2
Vegetation parameter analysis and influence on damage
5.2.1
Limitations when assessing vegetation influence on damage class
The assessment of vegetation influence on settlement damages encountered several challenges due to
inconsistencies in the available documentation across the 75 examined cases. A major limitation was the
lack of uniformity in vegetation records. In many cases, vegetation was either insufficiently documented
or categorised in broad terms, such as simply ”deciduous tree”, without further specification regarding
species. Given the considerable variability in the level of detail, it was not feasible to establish accu-
rate categories based on expected water demand or root activity. Consequently, most vegetation was
Page 84 of 235
==PAGE:95==
Building damage assessment due to drought
DTU Sustain
DISCUSSION
classified under a general ”high desiccation potential” category, irrespective of its actual characteristics.
This approach inherently introduces some uncertainty, as it does not differentiate between, for instance,
a small shrub near the building and a tall poplar tree with high water demand and an aggressive root
system far away from the building. While assigning all shrubs and bushes to a moderate category may
help reduce this uncertainty, vegetation classification is, in reality, far more complex. Some of the NHBC
guidance regarding distances to foundations could potentially inform a more structured definition of
safe planting distances in Denmark. However, this would require additional data on species-specific
characteristics, such as root spread and water demand, to enable reliable categorisation into different
volume change potentials. Nonetheless, assuming a uniformly high desiccation potential was considered
a more cautious choice than attempting to downgrade vegetation impact based on limited information,
which could risk underestimating its role in soil desiccation.
When evaluating whether the amount of vegetation influences the extent of damage, Figure 4.56 does
not reveal a clear trend between vegetation category — representing increasing vegetation amounts
with descriptions in Table 4.2 — and DC. Since both variables are discrete, drawing distinct patterns is
inherently difficult. Generally, the discrete nature of the damage classification limits the strength and
interpretability of correlation analyses. Discrete classes imply fixed thresholds, but real-world damage
often progresses more gradually and is influenced by subjective assessments, such as whether cracks
affect usability. This is further supported by Figure 4.57, where the correlation coefficients between
DC and vegetation category are both around −0.05, indicating a negligible relationship. Figure 4.15
and Figure 4.16 reinforce the absence of a strong correlation; however, there may be a slight tendency
toward higher DCs when vegetation amounts are at or below 2. For vegetation levels above 2, the
damage is generally limited to DC 0, 1, or 2, with only four instances reaching DC 3.

In Figure 4.14, which presents the bar graph of the lowest value of x/H for each case along with the
corresponding degree of damage, the three highest values of x/H show the opposite trend from what
could be expected (higher damage at lower x/H is expected), here they range DC 2 for the highest,
then DC 1, and then DC 0. Conversely, the three lowest values are two DC 2 for the very lowest,
followed by a DC 0. Although this might be somewhat more in line with expectations, it still shows
that lower DCs appear throughout the range, and the higher DCs are seen more toward the middle of
the distribution.
This trend is echoed in Figure 4.56 when considering x/H compared to the damage. At higher values
of x/H, there appears to be a decreasing tendency in DC, suggesting that increased distance, or lower
vegetation, might be associated with less severe damage. Interestingly, the opposite trend seems to
hold at lower x/H values, where an increase in distance or decrease in vegetation height correlates
with greater DCs. A larger scatter can be seen at lower DCs, resulting in these observations. The low
correlation and data scatter may stem from the uncertainty introduced by height and distance values,
which were largely estimated from satellite imagery or approximated on-site.

5.3
Hydrological indicators and their influence on structural damage
5.3.1
Complications with delayed damage reporting
There is often a reporting delay between the occurrence of damage and its registration, adding uncer-
tainty to the actual timing of the damage, particularly if it occurs in less frequently visited areas, such
as the basement of a building or in summer houses, where owners may not notice it immediately. This
issue is exemplified by six cases in which the reported damage occurred during the winter of 2023, a
generally wetter period when no drought was observed prior to the reported events. Such late reporting
complicates the interpretation of drought indices like the SMDI and SGDI curves and highlights that
the drought event linked to a claim may not be the true cause of the damage; rather, damage devel-
opment can span several months before the reported event. Consequently, it is often necessary to test
multiple time windows and include a buffer period to more accurately capture the relevant hydrological
conditions and better understand the relationship between drought and damage occurrence.
If the
reporting of damage fit the actual incident data, alternative reasons for the damage to have occurred
can be investigated, for instance, the re-wetting and subsequent heaving of soil.

5.3.2
Depth-dependent correlations between SMDI and soil parameters
The correlation between SMDI parameters and geotechnical parameters can be analysed through com-
parison of Figure 4.52 and Figure 4.53 with Figure 4.54 and Figure 4.55. The spring SMDI decreases
from a moderate to a weak correlation when moving from the foundation layer to the deeper layer for
IP , wL, and wP . For the SMDI slope in spring, the correlations with these parameters remain moderate
across both layers. Although the correlation coefficients for CC and sensitivity increase from foundation
to deeper layers, they remain within the weak range in both cases.
These patterns suggest that the SMDI signal is more strongly reflected in the geotechnical properties
of the foundation layer than in the deeper soil layers, likely because moisture-related volume changes
primarily affect the uppermost active zone where structural loads interact with shrinking soils. Param-
eters such as plasticity index and liquid limit respond more directly to seasonal drying near the surface,
whereas deeper layers are less sensitive to short-term moisture fluctuations. The weak correlation with
sensitivity is expected, as this property is more closely tied to soil structure and remoulding behaviour
than to moisture variation. Overall, the results indicate that linking hydrological indices like SMDI to
damage potential is most meaningful when focused on the foundation-relevant soil.
5.3.3
Influence of SMDI slope on damage severity
The observed relationship between SMDI slope and damage severity during spring, seen in the box plot
in Figure 4.21 and in the PDF in Figure 4.24, shows a trend, with steeper negative slopes tending to
correspond with higher DCs. This aligns with the hypothesis that rapid soil drying during the early
growing season increases desiccation stress in clay soils, making buildings more vulnerable to settlement.
A steeper decline in soil moisture may not allow the soil enough time to equilibrate, leading to greater
shrinkage and structural strain.
Interestingly, the opposite trend appears in the period immediately before the incident seen in the right
plot in Figure 4.21 and in Figure 4.26, where the slopes become more uniform and less steep for higher
DCs. Although this may seem counterintuitive, it could indicate that once the soil reaches a critically
dry state, damage can occur even if further drying slows down. In other words, the slope just before the
incident may matter less than the total cumulative drying over time. This suggests that severe damage
may result not from rapid moisture loss alone, but from sustained exposure to dry conditions.
Taking a look at Figure 4.59, the slope of the spring SMDI curve is seen against IP and colour-coded
with the corresponding DC. DC 0 appears exclusively at low IP values and gentle (less negative) slopes,
suggesting that a combination of low plasticity at the foundation level and a more gradual decline in
spring SMDI may be associated with minimal structural damage. While higher DCs tend to occur more
frequently at steeper negative slopes, the data shows considerable overlap: high DCs also appear at
lower slopes, and lower DCs can be found at steeper slopes. Overall, the figure does not reveal a clear
or consistent trend between damage severity, the spring SMDI slope, and IP .

SMDI values. Moreover, as previously noted, soil type is not considered a primary driver of drought
index variability. Thus, neither vegetation nor soil type alone can explain the outlier behaviour observed

5.3.5
SGDI and hydrological conditions in relation to building damage
When examining the shallow groundwater table, it would be reasonable to expect that a lower ground-
water table would lead to higher damage, since less available water could contribute to soil shrinkage.
This implies a negative correlation with damage. As shown in Figure 4.18, the mean and median values
do decrease slightly with increasing DC, indicating that a deeper groundwater table is associated with
more severely damaged buildings. However, since the mean and median remain very close across DCs,
it would be misleading to draw strong conclusions, especially given the variation in the number of cases
within each class, as illustrated in Figure 4.2. This is further supported by the correlation coefficients
in Table 4.4, where the Pearson and Spearman values are −0.12 and −0.13, respectively, indicating
only a weak overall correlation with DC.
The yearly SMDI shown in Figure 4.23 represents the mean SMDI for the 12 months leading up to
the reported incident. Across all DCs, the average SMDI is approximately 1, with no apparent trend
toward drier conditions as the damage severity increases. This suggests that mean surface moisture
conditions over the year do not directly correlate with the observed damage levels.
However, when compared to other hydrological indicators, the yearly SMDI displays the strongest
correlation with SGDI, as illustrated in Figure 4.50 and Figure 4.51. The Pearson correlation coefficient
of 0.447 indicates a moderate positive relationship: the deeper the groundwater table, the lower (more
negative) the SMDI tends to be.
This relationship may reflect a broader hydrological pattern, where prolonged dry periods not only
reduce surface soil moisture (captured by SMDI) but also lead to a declining groundwater table (reflected
in SGDI). In other words, areas with deeper groundwater are likely experiencing longer or more intense
moisture deficits, affecting both shallow and deep soil layers.
While this does not directly explain
damage severity, it helps contextualise the hydrological conditions that may contribute to desiccation-
related settlement in clay-rich soils. Here it is important to remember that both of these values stem
from the same model, and such connections can therefore be expected.
5.3.6
Wetting-drying cycles based on SMDI data
The literature indicates that wetting-drying cycles play a significant role in the formation of cracks and
settlement damage in masonry houses built on clay soils subjected to prolonged drought. However,
in the studies mentioned in Section 2.4.3 and Section 2.4.5, the W-D cycles were only investigated in
laboratory conditions, whereas W-D cycles affecting houses may span several years.
Looking at Figure 4.29, arguably there are four W-D cycles leading up to winter 2023, with a starting
point in winter 2016. The first cycle spans two years, beginning with wet conditions in winter 2016,
decreasing until winter 2017, then rising again to similar levels by winter 2018. Significant changes were
seen in the SMDI from the summer of 2018, with soils only returning to wetter-than-normal conditions
in the winter of 2020. The final two cycles both span 1.5 years from winter 2020 to summer 2021, and
again from summer 2021 to winter 2023.

When examining the cumulative mean area for the different DCs in Figure 4.30, and excluding DCs 4 and

Summarising Section 5.3, the hydrological indicators show some correlation with structural damage,
particularly during early-season droughts. Stronger links are found in the foundation soil layer, and
steep spring SMDI slopes appear more relevant than conditions immediately before damage, underlining
the importance of considering drought development rather than just acute events. However, delayed
reporting, weak groundwater correlations, and variable wetting–drying patterns introduce uncertainty.
Overall, drought intensity alone cannot explain damage; timing, soil properties, and cumulative effects
are equally important.

5.4.1
Comparison of levelling and satellite measurements
When satellite measurements were introduced in this project, it was considered whether they could be
used similarly to levelling measurements to detect differential settlements across buildings. To assess
this potential, the following discusses the nine levelling measurements and their corresponding satellite
data. From Figure 4.39-4.47, the levelling results for the nine cases with this information were shown,
as well as the weighted average curves for those same cases. In Table 4.3, the main observations from
those results are summed up.
Before evaluating the data, it’s important to understand the differences between the measurement
methods. Levelling shows the relative movement between different points on the building — how much
one part of the wall has shifted compared to another. However, it does not indicate whether the wall as
a whole has settled or heaved since construction. In contrast, satellite measurements track the vertical
movement of the same point over time, revealing whether that point has settled or risen.
As summarised in Table 4.3, most cases show notable discrepancies between the levelling and satellite
measurements. In general, the levelling data indicates much larger differential settlements than those
derived from the satellite data. Since the levelling measurements were taken directly on site and are

Another important aspect to consider when interpreting the InSAR measurements is the potential in-

unevenly, some horizontal wall displacement can occur. For instance, in case 5, structural instability

highlights that such effects can introduce measurement uncertainty.

alone does not determine damage severity.

data noise, point location imprecision, and limited temporal resolution. Therefore, InSAR should not
be used for projects like this thesis, which involve localised settlements. If it is used, the results must
be interpreted with caution and always in conjunction with the geotechnical and structural context.
6
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the relationship between drought-related building damage and a wide range of
geotechnical, hydrological, vegetation, and structural parameters in Danish masonry buildings founded
on clay-rich soils. Using data from 75 insurance cases collected after the 2022–2023 drought events, the
analysis aimed to identify patterns and key risk factors influencing desiccation-induced damage. The
bullet points in the following are the conclusions for the research questions stated in the introduction
of this thesis.
• Atterberg limits have the strongest correlations with damage severity, both in the foundation and
deeper layer
Among all investigated variables, geotechnical properties — particularly IP , wL, and CC — showed
the strongest internal correlations.
These parameters influence the soil’s shrink–swell capacity and
are especially relevant in the foundation layer, where volumetric changes exert the greatest structural
impact.
The highest correlation with damage severity was observed for wP and wL in the deeper
soil layer, followed by IP in the foundation layer. Generally, damage severity increased with higher
plasticity, both in magnitude and variability, indicating a positive relationship between volume change
potential and structural vulnerability. However, correlations with the DC remained only moderate,
highlighting the limitations of using soil properties alone to predict outcomes.
Importantly, only a few buildings had foundation depths exceeding the calculated shrinkage-free depth.

==PAGE:103==

CONCLUSION
These buildings exhibited lower damage severity, which supports the practical relevance of shrinkage-
free depth as a preventive design criterion. This finding highlights how geotechnical design parameters,
when properly implemented, can effectively mitigate drought-related damage. However, apart from this
finding, the data were scattered in the plot below the line, and no clear additional trend was visible.
Structural characteristics such as building age, L/H ratio, and foundation depth also influenced ob-
served damage. Older buildings tended to be more resilient, likely reflecting more robust historical
construction practices. However, more complex building types may introduce variability not fully cap-
tured in the current analysis, suggesting the need for further study on structural layout and stiffness
distribution. Discrepancies between visual and analytical damage assessments — particularly in inter-
mediate DCs (DC 1–3) — point to the subjectivity of the methods and reinforce the need for more
objective, model-based approaches to improve consistency in future evaluations. These discrepancies
also stem partly from the analytical reliance on estimated wall dimensions, which introduced additional
uncertainty.
• Vegetation may contribute to damage, but uncertainties limit its interpretability
Vegetation type, amount, and proximity to buildings were all considered when assessing their role in
desiccation-related settlement.
Cases with high desiccation potential and low x/H ratios generally
showed higher damage levels, although the overall correlation with DC was weak. This is likely due to
uncertainties in estimating tree species, height, and exact proximity to buildings. In particular, trees
labelled as “deciduous tree” were conservatively classified as having high desiccation potential. While
this assumption was made to avoid underestimating risk, it may have led to overestimations in some
cases, thereby obscuring the true influence of vegetation on building damage.
Nevertheless, damage was rarely observed above x/H = 1.5, suggesting that this threshold remains a
valid and conservative practical guideline. Although vegetation did not emerge as a strong stand-alone
predictor, its interaction with plastic soils likely contributes to increased shrinkage, especially when
root zones extend close to shallow foundations. More precise vegetation data would be necessary to
validate this interaction and clarify the role of vegetation in drought-induced structural damage.
• The hydrological model can, to some extent, be used to describe the anomalies around the local
sites
To conclude on the applicability of the hydrological model at the local site scale, the lack of a clear
correlation between modelled and observed conditions indicates limited suitability. The model’s 100 ×
100 m grid resolution does not capture the finer-scale variability of soil moisture at the building level,
limiting its effectiveness in assessing site-specific risk. However, some correlations are seen between
the indices and the damage, indicating that it can at least be used to explain certain observations, or
possible outliers in trends.
• Spring drought trends have a greater impact on structural damage than drought conditions im-
mediately preceding the incident

The hydrological indicators — particularly the spring SMDI slope and mean values — showed a stronger
connection to damage severity than conditions immediately before reported incidents. This suggests
that early-season soil drying and the development of prolonged moisture deficits are more critical than
acute drought events alone. It answers the research question on timing: drought duration and develop-
ment over the season, rather than peak intensity at the time of reported damage, appear more influential
in triggering structural impacts. However, delayed damage reporting weakens the temporal alignment
between hydrological signals and observed damage, potentially obscuring true causal relationships.
In addition to drought development, W-D cycles emerged as another important factor. Moderately
to severely damaged buildings were more frequently associated with sites experiencing W–D cycles,
rather than prolonged dry conditions alone. This supports previous findings that cyclic desiccation,
particularly where wetting and drying phases are roughly symmetric, can drive soil settlement more
effectively than continuous drying.
Notably, DC 2 and 3 exhibited more pronounced W–D cycling
compared to DC 0 and 1, possibly explaining their higher observed damage levels.
SGDI showed only weak correlation with damage, though it was moderately correlated with annual
SMDI, suggesting a shared but diffuse signal. Still, groundwater indicators proved insufficiently re-
sponsive to explain surface damage, likely due to lag effects and spatial mismatch.
• InSAR data cannot be used to predict structural damage on its own, due to the model resolutions
Although InSAR displacement data can capture settlement and heave trends, no consistent relationship
was found between ground motion and damage severity. Extreme slope values were observed even in only
slightly damaged buildings, and slope angles varied widely across all DCs. This indicates that InSAR-
derived velocity and trend changes alone are insufficient to predict structural damage. Moreover, the
method carries considerable uncertainty due to data noise, limited temporal resolution, and imprecise
point locations.
Significant discrepancies between InSAR and on-site levelling measurements further confirm that InSAR
cannot reliably detect differential settlements in buildings. This is compounded by a mismatch between
building geometry and InSAR resolution, where point placements often do not correspond precisely to
building footprints, leading to errors in the comparisons. Additionally, the current 5 × 20 m resolution,
despite being good for larger-scale ground monitoring, is too coarse for accurate small-scale assessments.
Although the resolution of InSAR measurements is relatively high, reliable interpretation requires mul-
tiple data points within an area that exhibit a consistent trend. In this study, many cases had only
a few points—often fewer than ten—with no clear pattern, making the data insufficient for assessing
local-scale settlement or heave. As such, InSAR is not well-suited for projects like this thesis that focus
on localised ground movement. If used, InSAR results must be interpreted with caution and always in
conjunction with geotechnical, hydrological, and structural data. To improve its future applicability,
higher-resolution measurements with more accurate georeferencing are essential.

7
Future work and recommendations
7.1
Alternative assessment methods of damage class
It may be beneficial to reconsider how building damage is classified. The current use of discrete damage
categories introduces subjectivity and limits the ability to analyse the relationship between settlement
and structural impact. Instead, damage could be assessed using continuous measures — such as total
or differential settlement — to allow for more detailed interpretation of settlement behaviour over time.
Still, it must be acknowledged that large settlements don’t always correlate directly with severe cracking.
When doing so, the dimensions of the affected walls must also be recorded, as these are necessary for
calculating the deflection ratio.
To reduce subjectivity in crack evaluation, standardised image-based methods could be developed, where
the distance and camera angle could be set at fixed values, for instance. Crack dimensions should be
measured using rulers in photographs to obtain accurate lengths and widths.
In this study, many
crack assessments were based on images without scale references, increasing the risk of misclassification
and inaccurate damage grades. To improve the analytical damage assessment method, L/H could be
measured for the walls instead of being estimated. This could also lead to the possibility of correlating
the L/H ratio with the DC.
Looking ahead, a semi-continuous damage scale could be developed by linking observed crack patterns to
measured settlements in a larger dataset. This would allow the establishment of probabilistic thresholds,
leading to more consistent damage classification and better integration of structural and geotechnical
data.
7.2
Soil parameters and subsurface conditions
To improve understanding of subsurface behaviour and building vulnerability on shrink-swell soils,
future studies should incorporate a broader range of soil parameters. Specifically, soil density measure-
ments — both at root level and foundation depth — can offer insight into root penetration potential
and soil structure.
Mineralogical analysis should also be adopted as a standard, as the type and composition of clay min-
erals significantly influence a soil’s swelling behaviour and deformation risk. Furthermore, conducting
particle size distribution in combination with the plasticity index will provide essential data on fine
particle content and help evaluate the soil’s shrink-swell potential more accurately. This would also
help determine the modified plasticity index for additional cases and, consequently, assess the volume
change potential, if the particles > 425 µm are noted. This would allow for investigating whether the
trend observed in this thesis holds true with a larger dataset.

7.3
Vegetation and root system assessment
Trees and vegetation significantly influence soil moisture dynamics, particularly in clay-rich soils during
droughts. While this thesis considered only vegetation near visibly damaged areas, further analysis
shows that some tree species have extensive root systems reaching far beyond their visible canopy, both
horizontally and vertically. This underscores the importance of systematically recording all vegetation
within at least a 25–30 meter radius of buildings—or more when species like poplar are present—along
with details on species and height. Since different trees extract water at varying rates (e.g., poplars
withdraw much more than birches), such data can enhance predictions of localized soil desiccation and
support the development of a more comprehensive assessment framework.
For a more accurate assessment, it would be beneficial to consider all surrounding vegetation, including
its orientation relative to cracks and settlements, as well as its size, type, and desiccation potential.
In this context, a detailed model could be a powerful tool for identifying which vegetation poses the
greatest risk during drought periods. Such a model could also be applied in the future to evaluate plots
of land where new buildings are planned.
In this context, the Tree Radar Unit developed by ArborAeration offers a promising non-invasive
method for mapping tree roots.
The ground-penetrating radar system can detect roots up to 1.2
meters deep — well within typical foundation depths — and measure both vertical and lateral spread.
This technology allows for targeted investigation of how nearby trees may contribute to moisture loss
beneath buildings [42]. Such root mapping could serve not only as a post-damage tool but also as a
preventive measure. Knowing which trees have extensive root systems close to foundations allows for
better planning, vegetation management, and risk mitigation — especially when buildings are placed
on shrink-swell clay soils.
If no root mapping tool can be used to get further insight in the root zone, investigations on void ratio
and soil density measurements could be an alternative method. These could reveal whether the soil
near roots is drier or looser than other areas, pointing to localised desiccation effects. The type of
soil beneath vegetation may also influence root growth direction — roots in sandy soils tend to grow
deeper, while those in moist clay spread laterally — information that could be critical for predicting
future impact on foundations.
7.4
Future model for statistical analysis
It should be emphasised that the analysis in this thesis was based solely on pairwise comparisons, where
DC and other factors were tested against one parameter at a time. This approach did not account for
possible interactions between variables. In reality, damage may result from complex combinations —
where, for example, moderate plasticity, prolonged drought, and vegetation cover together contribute
more significantly than any single factor alone, as the data also suggests. Thus, a multivariate statistical
analysis with more than three parameters compared at once could be a great next step in this project.

Future work could involve training a model with damage severity as the target variable and inputs such
