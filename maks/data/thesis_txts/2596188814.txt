Abstract 
Since 1950 the global production of plastic has been increasing exponentially, with a compounded 
annual growth of almost 9 percent since. This has led to the production of around 350 Mt of plastic 
polymers in 2018 of these 350 Mt only 2 Mt is derived from bio-based plastics. The production and 
consumption of plastics is assumed to continue its growth like previously. This has led to the 
European Union creating a comprehensive strategy, depicting how the plastics will be a part of the 
future society. The European Union is not alone in creating plans, strategies and laws to try and 
decrease the growing pressure plastic production is having on the environment. In Asia both China 
and Japan have created plans and regulations on for phasing out single use plastics and non-
degradable plastic bags. The United States of America has no single strategy, and only laws and 
regulations were to be found in California. Therefore, this study will utilize a newly created 
methodology from the QSA group at DTU management, to assess the performance of bio-based 
plastics against the UN SDGs, to see how much of an influence the miniscule fraction of bio-based 
plastic could have. Therefore, the general scope of the study was determined, assessing the global 
production of plastics, and scenarios were created on the basis of the strategies found. A baseline 
scenario was created first, with the assumption that every strategy, regulation and law proposed was 
fully fulfilled. From there two scenarios was created, both assuming that regulations and strategies 
will be delaying in their implementation, and therefore is only assuming that 70 percent of these are 
fulfilled. With the change to scenario 2 being an assumption of an increase in the annual growth of 
bio-based plastics. The system boundaries, and the processes accounted for were then determined, 
and from there it was possible to create an inventory of the effects the changes to each scenario had. 
The likelihood and magnitude of each effect was then established to in order to create do a 
contribution assessment of the effects of the two scenarios. The contribution assessment show very 
similar performance of the two scenarios, with a slight better performance of scenario 2, due to the 
increase in bio-based plastics. Although both scenarios were greatly impacting the performance 
negatively in relation to the baseline. This is although also to be expected due to the decrease in 
recycling, and therefore and increase in the extraction of fossils for production of virgin plastics.

Introduction 
 
Goal of the study 
The study will assess the sustainability implications of bio-based plastics/polymers with an increase 
in future production. Assessing how this increasing production will affect the trajectory to fulfilling 
the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UN SDGs) by 2050. Carrying out this assessment 
through a comprehensive analysis of the various production methods of bio- and fossil plastics as 
well as the material breakdown phases at post-use treatment. Assessing whether an increase of 
bioplastic production and market share would have any beneficial effects on the global impact of the 
general plastic production with reference to the UN SDGs. The assessment will be carried out using 
the UN SDG Assessment Methodology and Guideline created by the Quantitative Sustainability 
Assessment Group at DTU Management. The performance is then assessed using the given tool, with 
respect to the relevant UN SDGs, as not every goal defined in the UN SDGs will have a direct 
connection with the production of bio- and fossil plastic. This assessment will then act as a foundation 
of the utilization of the given assessment tool, on product specific cases, as well as a general 
assessment of the performance of bio-based plastics, as a substitute to fossil plastic. 
 
General introduction 
As of 2018 the annual production of plastic polymers reached more than 350Mt(Chinthapalli et al., 
2019; Plastics Europe, 2019), whilst only around 2Mt being bio-based polymers(EUBPd, 2019; Zheng 
& Suh, 2019). Using this data, bioplastics makes up less than a percentage of the total polymer 
production, while the other 99% are comprised of regular fossil-based polymers, produced with 
crude oil and natural gas.  
 
The global plastic production has since 1950 seen a compounded increase of about 9% per year 
(Chinthapalli et al., 2018; Geyer, Jambeck, & Law, 2017; Morten W. Ryberg, Alexis Laurent, 2018; 
Zheng & Suh, 2019), leading up to the current production level and is set to further grow at the same 
rate. This leads to an increase in the use of crude oil for polymer production, which has a vastly higher 
carbon footprint than bio-based plastics (Zheng & Suh, 2019).  
 
In general, the plastic industry is a big part of the world economy, with around than 1.5 million people 
employed and annual European turnover of more than350 billion euro (European Commission, 
2018c).(Plastics Europe, 2019)(Plastics Europe, 2019)(Plastics Europe, 2019) This clearly underlines 
the great importance of the plastic industry, with not only providing necessary products for at 
functioning society, but also contributing greatly to the general global economy. Analyzing the global 
plastics market and its distribution of plastic, noticeable is it that Asia is producing around 50 percent 
of the global plastics, with the North American region (NAFTA) far behind with 18 percent of global 
production and lastly Europe with 17 percent of the production. These three regions produce 
collectively more than 85 percent of global plastics (Plastics Europe, 2019). From the production of 
plastics it is also important to consider the demand of plastics based on regions, as this gives an 
indication on the level of both import and export of each region. These demands are made clear in 
table 1, which describes the global demand for plastics throughout different regions and throughout 
different sectors. From this data, it is clear that Asia as a region is in a surplus of plastic production, 
and therefore must be exporting large amounts of plastic products throughout the world.

After assessing the total production and demand of plastics globally, it is natural to furthermore 
examine the end of life for plastics. Waste in general is a large issue, as still a lot gets incinerated 
instead of recycled, which still has its benefits in terms of energy recovery, with combined heat and 
electricity facilities throughout Europe. It is though only possible to either recycle, incinerate, or 
landfill the waste that gets collected. In 2018 29.1 million tons of plastic post-consumer waste was 
collected(Plastics Europe, 2019). This is far below the average annual consumption within the 
European region, which is also to be expected. Not all plastic products have a short life span, as these 
products also are general white goods, electrical appliances, products used in building constructions, 
automotive tyres, etc. Therefore, it is also to be expected, that not every product will get metabolized 
in society as short as plastic packaging and will act like a societal stock. From these 29.1 million tons 
32.5 percent was recycled, 42.6 percent was incinerated for energy recovery, and the last 24.9 
percent was landfilled. This number is based on the entirety of the European region, and the 
countries attribution to this data, will therefore have greatly different waste management systems, 
with different ratios of recycling, incineration, and landfilling.

When assessing different types of plastics, it is important ensure clear definitions of what bio-plastic 
is in all its facets. Bioplastics are commonly seen in two different ways, either as bio-based or bio-
degradable, with both of these being classified as bioplastic. (EUBPd, 2016) 
 
The two types of bio-plastic are very different, and not necessarily interconnected. The term bio-
based, as indicated, states that the polymer is produced either fully or partially from a bio-based 
feedstock. These feedstocks vary from corn, sugar beet, sugarcane, to wood pulp, dependent on 
polymer but also on agricultural location. The biodegradability purely depends on the chemical 
structure of the polymer, it is not mutually exclusive with the polymer being bio-based, as both fossil 
and bio-based polymers can be biodegradable (EUBPd, 2016). Looking further the degradability of

industrial composting plant, in a garden bin, in the soil, freshwater, or seawater. The relationship

there is a big possibility with bio-based plastics, and that it can be used as a replacement in various 
instances(Chen & Patel, 2012).Furthermore, there is an interest in understanding whether bio-based 
plastic alternatives has a better performance on a sustainability basis. This was examined in (Spierling

To a certain extend it is expected that, in the case of a one-to-one substitution of fossil plastics with 
bio-based plastics, that the bio-based plastics would have less of a environmental impact. This is due 
to the production of the bio-based plastics, consisting of a carbon neutral feedstock source, in some 
cases even being able to produce from organic waste fractions(Du, Chen, & Yu, 2004; Koller, Atlić, 
Dias, Reiterer, & Braunegg, 2010). It is not to say that an increased use in land for the production of 
more bio-feedstock will not have any consequences, as it might change the land used for agricultural 
purposes, which could result in habitat loss and loss of biodiversity, it could result in high outlets of 
fertilizers and pesticides(Nessi et al., 2018).

The total annual production of bioplastic is around 2 million tons a year. Being less than a percent of 
the global production. Furthermore, the evolution of production is estimated to be around 3 to 3.5 
percent growth annually (EUBPd, 2019). This is less than half of the expected growth of plastics in 
==PAGE:10==

general. A further important aspect is to what extend bio-based plastics can substitute fossil plastics. 
This purely comes down to the physical chemical properties of the particular plastic, as a substitute 
must possess the same qualities as the original plastic polymer in use. This is the same for both 
thermoplastics and thermoset plastic. These are the two general categories plastics are divided up 
into. Thermoplastics are polymers which can be heated up and is malleable when warm and can be 
formed into various shapes, which then when cooled will set in the particular chosen shape. This 
process of molding and remolding can usually be done over and over again. Thermoset plastics on 
the opposite, will set irreversibly when cooled down after molding, and cannot revert back to its 
original form once set.  
 
A very important aspect in the introduction of bio-based plastics, is that it can fulfill the functions 
that the fossil-based plastic has today and do so without having to make large changes in the 
production processes and facilities. Therefore, it is paramount to understand whether or not there is 
a possibility for bio-based plastics to substitute large quantities of fossil plastics. According to (Shen, 
Haufe, & Patel, 2009) it is possible for bio-based plastics to do a 90% technical substitution. A market 
which particularly is in focus when assessing the use of bio-based plastics is the packaging sector, 
with around 60 percent of the bio-based plastics produced being used in that sector (European 
Commission, 2019). 
 
As plastics have become a common commodity as a product type not questioned when used, it is not 
difficult to believe the large increase seen in production and usage of plastics since it has started to 
be used publicly after world war II. And with it being ever increasingly present in the oceans of the 
world due to not being handled correctly after use, and not being put into the waste management 
systems around the world. This has resulted in the creation of a European strategy for plastics 
(European Commission, 2018b). This new strategy is although not everything the European Union is 
doing to combat fossil resource depletion and reduce the plastics released into the oceans. The 
European Union has also imposed a ban on many Single Use Plastics (SUP) (European Parliament, 
n.d.; Wahlén, 2019).  
 
What is clear from the European strategy, is that there is a very clear focus on material circularity, 
with almost every point of it being directed towards circularity. Only one point addresses bio-based 
plastic production, and states that it should be explored in cases where benefits have been 
proven(European Commission, 2018a). This indicates, that there might still be a problem with the 
economics of bio-based plastics, as it the costs of production is currently one of the larger barriers to 
entry. Furthermore, it might also be due to a necessary increase in agricultural land. These problems 
will not be present under the current production of fossil plastics. 
 
Other regions implementing environmentally focused strategies are Asia, with both China and Japan 
showing initiative. China by banning all non-degradable plastic bags in towns and cities by 2022 (BBC, 
2020; Xinhua, 2020). It is not an overly ambitious plan, as this only tackles a small percentage of the 
total plastic usage, and also only a small part of the SUP usage. Japan has also put forth a plan to 
reduce their usage of single use plastics by 25 percent in 2030(McCurry, 2019). 
 
In the US, it was only possible to find anything in the state of California, here with a ban of single use 
plastic bags, and a ban on restaurants automatically giving out plastic straws (Brueck, 2018; Times, 
2016)

As for strategies related to plastic production directly there seems to be none, only strategies related 
to the consumption and disposal of these polymers. Various countries across the African continent 
have introduced laws and regulations prohibiting the usage of certain plastic products, such as carrier 
bags. These laws and regulations do although indirectly affect the production of the items in focus, 
as they reduce the demand therefore also reducing the production. This might be because it is easier 
to impose regulations and restrictions on usage of specific polymer types directly within each sector. 
Either that, or there might be a new framework underway, trying to assess industrial packaging more 
specifically.  
 
After thoroughly examining the relevant legislative documents, concerning the future of plastic 
polymers globally, it is clear that there is a general consensus on focusing on plastic packaging. Plastic 
packaging is the largest contributor of non-fishery related plastic polymers(European Commission, 
2018d) that end up in the environment, as well as being one of the largest sectors related to the 
production of plastic polymers. This along with the lack of planned regulation change for other 
sectors, directly suggests that a focus of change from the current production should mainly be 
investigated in that sector. Therefore, when determining the future production capacity for the 
baseline scenario, the main focus is on how the change of the packaging sector is influencing the 
system. This will be shown in the how the production capacity of the packaging sector is distributed

UN Sustainable Development goals, it was chosen to utilize the newly developed assessment method 
from DTU management on assessing impacts on the US SDGs. In order to fully utilize the assessment 
methodology must be altered such that it can be applicable in assessing the impact of polymer 
production as a whole, instead of assessing research projects, and impacts related to this. Therefore, 
appropriate changes and altercations have been made throughout the assessment phase and will be 
highlighted when relevant in the assessment.  
 
When assessing the impacts related to the production of plastic polymers, and the change of which 
bio-based polymers might have, lifecycle thinking is paramount in order to outline any possible 
changes throughout the value chain that will be affected by a change in polymer production. This is

Firstly, it is important to establish what the application of the project is, in this specific instance, as 
this study is not analyzing a project or new technology, it is necessary to deviate and adapt the 
methodology. Therefore, the application is rather a description of what the analysis is analyzing and 
how those products and processes relates to the UN SDGs. As this project is analyzing and assessing 
how bio-based plastic polymers contribute to reaching the UN SDGs put forth. The assessment is 
based on how certain theoretically derived scenarios, portray potential future production capacities 
of plastic polymers. These theoretical scenarios are then each compared to how the plastic polymer 
production capacities are likely to be in 2030, classed as the baseline scenario. The assessment is 
therefore only taking into consideration how the scenarios differ from the baseline and does not 
directly quantify the total impacts of each respective scenario. This can be compared to a 
comparative LCA study although utilizing a different Impact Assessment Method.

assessment will take into consideration in regard to the lifecycle of the polymers. When the scope

is important to have these areas established in order to be able to clearly identify and outline the 
effects related to each of the relevant scenarios. 
 
When creating the inventory of effects related to the change in each different scenario, it is important 
to clearly determine what the changes in the lifecycle processes are, if it is the addition of a process, 
a removal of a process, an increase in a process, or a decrease in a process. Then systematically go 
through all changes in the lifecycle from the raw material stage to the end-of-life stage. When all 
effects of each scenario have been mapped out, it will then be possible to establish how these effects 
affect achieving the US SDGs.

are portrayed.  
 
In 2018 the EU28+NO/CH recycled 32.5% of all collected plastic polymers, although only collected 
29.1 Mt of plastic post-consumer waste. This does not take into consideration waste generated in

Baseline and future scenarios 
The general scope for the study is the entire planet, and the production plastic polymers of all 
nations. In order to simplify the gathering of data, the main producers plastics and their future 
policies and regulations are the one focused on. This means North America, EU and Asia (Mainly 
China). This does not include every nations producing plastic, although should be representative of 
the general way the future of global plastic production is going. Thereby, the proposed laws and 
regulations by these, should be sufficient in order to estimate the general pressure plastic production 
has on reaching the UN SDGS.  
The baseline scenario should depict the most likely scenario in 2030, to act as a reference to how 
other scenarios might change the impact of the plastic industry at year 2030. The baseline scenario 
will take shape by analyzing previous production capacities, and from that estimating future 
capacities, both for fossil and bio-based plastic, while taking the proposed regulations and laws 
presented into account and adjusting the respective production capacities with regard to these.  
 
The different scenarios are created with the intent of depicted somewhat plausible outcomes, as a 
result from more concrete laws and regulations implemented in the future. The proposed scenarios 
will therefore deviate from the baseline in either severity in of specific laws and regulations as a result 
of global events, or as a result of plausible consumer driven changes on the market, which in turn 
could drive production in a different direction than currently observed.

Baseline - 100% of proposed strategies implemented by 2030 
The baseline scenario is based a fully reached European strategy focusing on improving resource 
circularity of plastic polymers as described in the report “A European Strategy for Plastics” (European 
Commission, 2018a), as well as the 2021 ban on certain single-use plastic consumer goods (European 
Parliament, n.d.). This strategy as well as this recent law has targets set up until 2030, with a goals 
for plastic bottle collection (90%) by 2029 and for use of recycled material in plastic bottle production 
(30%). Furthermore, this European strategy is focusing on making sure plastic containing products 
are designed in a way that promotes the extension of its lifecycle, by making it easier to utilize in 
recycling. It wants to increase the amount of recycled plastic waste, and the capacity of recycling. 
Furthermore, improving the collection systems, and establish a successful market for the recycling of 
plastic. The European Union wants to examine the possibility of alternative feedstocks, and make 
sure the plastic waste generation is decoupled from the economic growth, as well as developing 
solutions to prevent microplastics to entering the marine environment. 
 
California has implemented a ban on the automatic use of “single-use” plastic straws in dine in 
restaurants (Brueck, 2018), and customers have to actively ask for a straw in order to get one, as well 
as a ban on plastic bags (Times, 2016). 
 
China has banned the use of non-degradable single-use plastic tableware in all counties by 2022 for 
dine-in restaurants, and want a 30 percent reduction in non-degradable single-use tableware for 
take-out restaurants by 2025. As well as hotels being required to stop the use of single-use plastic 
wares by 2025 (BBC, 2020; Xinhua, 2020). As well as Japan with their 25 percent reduction in the use 
of SUP.  
 
The baseline scenario assumes the fulfilment of all strategy goals, as well as the laws still being in 
place and enforced. Therefore, an increase in non-degradable plastics is extremely likely, as well as a 
general lower production, as the increase in circularity will aid in the substitution of virgin material 
for recycled material.  
 
Assumptions/challenges: 
The main assumption for the baseline scenario, is that all strategy proposals will be developed further 
into more concrete legislation. This legislation is based directly on the phrasing of the current strategy 
with legislative targets set directly in correspondence with the strategy. Further, it is important to 
notice that the actual outcome and design of legislation in regard to the strategy and the actual 
targets will likely not be exactly the same, although they should be somewhat similar. An assumption 
is also that every target set, will be reached 100% and will be reached at the target year, this is not 
necessarily directly correspondent with how industries and different countries might reach these 
targets, as some might reach them years in advance while others might not achieve the target.

Results and discussion 
Form the inventory of effects it was possible to do the contribution assessment, which yielded the 
results shown in figure 5 and 6, with further data in Appendix E. First assessing figure 5, this shows 
how the effects impact the SDGs compared to the baseline. Knowing that, it is also expected that 
changes made in scenario 1 will negatively impact reaching the goals by 2030, due to the decrease in 
recycling, causing an increase in extraction of fossils and minerals. It is also possible to detect that it 
is the process of raw material extraction, raw material processing and end of life is contributing the 
most. This seems fairly trustworthy, as the process of extracting fossils and minerals have far more 
possible impacts on the environment, with potential spillages cause major damages to both 
terrestrial and marine ecosystems. As well as the end of life stage, where with an increase in 
landfilling might also cause increased leaking of toxic waste into the environment, with incineration 
causing an increase in GHG emissions. The actual production of the plastics is not necessarily very 
damaging to the environment, as it most often is done under controlled circumstances as well, as not 
being located in areas close to larger natural ecosystems and habitats.

The strategies primarily focus on circularity, but not the interconnection of circularity and bio-based. 
Whereas it would have been interesting if two regions had majorly different strategies, in order to 
assess whether a circularity focusses strategy or a bio-based strategy would have had better

This particular topic would also greatly benefit from being revisited when a fully quantitative 
methodology has been developed. This could be a comprehensive methodology in three major parts, 
trying to assess the SDSs related to the natural environment with an LCA or and LCA inspired 
methodology. Assess the SDGs related to the economy with an LCC inspired analysis, and then create 
a methodology that could quantitatively assess performance of the SDGs related to social issues. This 
would generate a much clearer depiction of the actual performance the plastic sector as a whole 
would have with regard to reaching the SDGs. And in order to assess whether or not the performance 
of each individual scenario is beneficial, it could be of interest to create in a similar way as is done 
with GHG emissions accounting and the Science Based Targets (CDP, n.d.; Science-Based Targets, 
n.d.). This would also aid in assessing whether or not the scenarios would be sufficient or if the change 
wouldn’t be enough.

Conclusion 
It is clear that the plastic industry I extremely large and plastic as a commodity will still be a large part 
of our everyday lives also in the future. Therefore, the assessment made on the performance of the 
plastic industry and more specifically the performance of bio-based plastics in the plastic industry, 
against the SDGs. With the scenarios created, the estimated future production, and the assumption 
of implementation of the proposed regulations in the baseline, and the partly implementation in the 
two scenarios. This along with the found effects related to the change from the baseline in the two 
scenarios, and the contribution assessment, gives the basis of the assessment of the performance of 
bio-based plastics against the SDGs. What seems to be the case is that under these assumptions and 
scenarios the bio-based plastic seems to have positive effect and thereby contributing towards 
reaching the SDGs. This is although only under the current assumptions as the uncertainty related to 
the study is rather large. This might still be an indication that there is a potential for a larger share of 
bio-based plastics on the global market, but as previously stated this is has to be assessed again when 
there is a better understanding of how the current global situation is affecting the general economy 
long term, and if strategies will be revised due to this or if countries yet to have made strategies 
regarding plastics will publish any. It is therefore likely that bio-based plastics have a generally 
positive contribution to reaching the SDGs.
