Abstract
A number of accidents in the past have highlighted the shortcomings of the design codes for structural ﬁre
safety in tunnels. At the same time, the number of hydrogen vehicles in recent years have increased given
risk of hydrogen ﬁres in tunnels. Therefore the need for more sophisticated approaches in the design of
tunnels are more clearer than ever.
The aim of this thesis is to develop a ﬁnite element model of a reinforced concrete slab capable of predicting
the temperature proﬁle and response when exposed to a hydrogen ﬁre. It is also desired to identify and
outline the modelling issues of concrete when using the software ABAQUS. The cracking of the concrete
is modelled by a smeared cracking model and the concept of constant fracture energy are used.
The slab is simpliﬁed to a two-dimensional beam for the sake of simplicity and only the eﬀects of plane
stresses are regarded. To validate the ﬁnite element model, validations are performed through modelling
of a 1D steel beam, a transient heat transfer analysis, an uniaxial test of a 2D concrete beam and a 2D
reinforced concrete beam.
The geometry and material properties of the reinforced concrete slab is taken from a proposed tunnel,
where the slab form the ceiling. C35/45 concrete is used and the reinforcement is having a yield stress of
500 MPa with an yield point of 0.2% strain.
The global behaviour of the slab is investigated along with a thermal-structural analysis for a standard
ﬁre and the proposed hydrogen ﬁre. Hence an ultimate bearing capacity and a critical temperature for the
standard ﬁre are also found, for which diﬀerent converging problems are addressed. Finally the eﬀect of
the hydrogen ﬁre is investigated, and though the intensity of the hydrogen ﬁre in terms of heat is extreme
compared to the standard ﬁre, only loss of tensile strength in the concrete is seen. Therefore if neglecting
the tensile strength of the concrete the bearing capacity of the slab is found to be unchanged by the
hydrogen ﬁre.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Tunnels are widely used in the construction of roads to give a passageway through mountains, oceans,
cities etc. to make the shortest distance between two points. However, it is obvious that the ﬁre hazard
in a tunnel is high due to the enclosure and with increasing traﬃc, bigger fuel loads and many diﬀerent
vehicles the consequences are only getting higher. Because of the long shape and the closed feature of the
tunnel heat emission is diﬃcult and the damage caused by a ﬁre is more severe than for other structures.
With multiple ﬁre incidents in recent years, including the Mont Blanc Tunnel [1], the Channel Tunnel [2]
and Great Belt Tunnel ﬁre [2], the need of adequate structural ﬁre safety is only getting clearer. For All
three tunnel ﬁres the high temperatures caused a signiﬁcant loss of cross-sectional area in the tunnel lining
due to spalling in the concrete, and in some sections, the carrying capacity had been placed at risk. In Fig
Figure 1, the damage on the concrete lining is seen for two of the tunnel ﬁres.
The Channel Tunnel, 1996
The Mont Blanc Tunnel, 1999
Figure 1: Fire Damage to the concrete linning due to spalling
These accidents highlight the shortcomings in the design methods of ﬁre design in tunnels, both in terms
of the applied ﬁre scenarios and the structural ﬁre design.
In recent years the number of hydrogen vehicles have increased and given risk of hydrogen ﬁres. Therefore,
it is desired to look at the behaviour of a reinforced concrete slab exposed to such a ﬁre. A hydrogen
ﬁre is characterized by a big and intensive jet ﬂame for a short period of time. The simpliﬁed methods
commonly used in designing concrete tunnels exposed to ﬁre do not account for the hydrogen ﬁre scenario
or account for aspects as spalling or cracking caused by the high temperatures. Hence, it is clear that more
sustainable methods are needed to assess the performance of concrete in a hydrogen ﬁre.
Little research has been done on this topic and the literature is limited therefore either a full-scale ﬁre
test or a numerical investigation is desired. The disadvantages of a full-scale ﬁre test is that it is ex-
pensive, time consuming and the results acquired are limited to the speciﬁc test setup. In contrary to
a ﬁre test, a numerical investigation is quite inexpensive and easy adjustable with regard to the geome-
try, the ﬁre conditions, the boundary conditions etc. and is therefore often preferred as a ﬁrst investigation.
However, the modelling of concrete in ﬁre is aﬀected by diﬀerent problems due to the limited understanding
of the thermo-plastic degradation of the material.
Among other things the use of the ﬁnite element

knowledge of the structural mechanics, structural ﬁre safety and ﬁnite element modelling is required from

Validation of the numerical model therefore has to be proven, which is done by relevant benchmarks for
the behaviour of the thermal and structural model.
1.2
Object and aim
The tunnel considered is a drilled tunnel with a suspended concrete slab as ceiling holding the ventilation
system. The concrete slab is considered to be simple supported. The considered scenario is a hydrogen
vehicle catching ﬁre in one on the lanes, a bus is imagined to be the critical vehicle because of the hydrogen
load and the fact that the valve is sitting on the top of the bus.
The aim of this project is to implement a FEM model comprised of a thermal analysis, that will take input
from a hydrogen ﬁre obtained from a CFD analysis. The CFD analysis was conducted in another project.
Then a mechanical model will be implemented, which will take the temperature map obtained with the
thermal analysis as input for the mechanical analysis. In this way the eﬀect of a hydrogen ﬁre is obtained.
Initially the mechanical model is created and validated by means of analytical calculations to check the
accuracy of the model, including elastic and plastic behaviour, and the number of integration points, plastic
hinges, heat transfer at steady state etc. In order to simplify the case the model is in 2D with only planer
problems, therefore it is only a section small section of the slab that is considered.
1.3
Method
The analysis is made from a ﬁnite element model of a tunnel slab, and the program used is ABAQUS. The
slab is simpliﬁed to a 2D problem which is validated by analytical calculations.
First the uniaxial stresses are investigated with respect global elastic and plastic behaviour. Hence a ma-
terial model is made describing the elastic and plastic behaviour for both tensile and compressive stresses.
At the same a heat transfer analysis of the concrete is made and the temperature proﬁle found, meaning
that the temperatures inside the cross-section are determined. Thereafter the results from the thermal
model are implemented in the structural model, in terms of temperature, and the bearing capacity is found
with degraded material properties due to the elevated temperatures.
The inelastic behaviour of the concrete is described by a smeared cracking model.

2.1
Mehanisms of Heat transfer
Heat transport is caused by a temperature diﬀerence between two points when thermal energy movies
from a hot to a cold region. Heat transport is governed by three fundamental physical laws, conduction,
convection and thermal radiation. For both conduction and convection the heat transport require a medium
while radiation can be transferred in vacuum as described by [31].
In order to gain a better understanding of the heat transfer the three mechanisms are presented in the
following, based on [4] and [31].
Conduction
Conduction is the transfer of internal energy within a body by which energy is transmitted through colli-
sions of neighboring particles. Conduction takes place in gasses, liquids and solids. Conduction is especially
important for solids where atoms and molecules are closer together meaning that the heat transfer is bigger.
Conduction is distinguished into two processes, steady state for which the temperature diﬀerence and
heat ﬂow remain constant, and transient heat ﬂow where temperature is changing with time. If doing
a steady state analysis, it is assumed that the heat transfer has reached an equilibrium state, meaning
that the distribution of temperature does not change with time anymore. This is seen in Appendix C and
Appendix D, where the heat transfer in concrete and steel is assessed.

2.4
Material properties of steel and concrete at ambient temperature
In order to perform a numerical model the properties of the material have to be well deﬁned. Therefore
in order to understand the behaviour of the concrete at ambient and elevated temperatures the following
chapters are made.
First the behaviour of concrete at ambient temperature is introduced and discussed, followed by the be-
haviour of concrete at elevated temperatures. Diﬀerent models for describing the stress-strain relationship
at elevated temperatures are introduced and compared, including the degradation of the material strength.
All presented stress-strain diagrams are based on the material properties used for the cantilever beam in
subsection 4.3.
Mechanical properties of concrete
When looking at the mechanical strength of concrete two essential characteristics are noted, namely soft-
ening and cracking. The softening of the concrete occurs when the compressive strength is reached and
is characterized by loss of strength, meaning that the stress is decreasing with increasing strain when
the loading goes beyond the ultimate strength. The ultimate compressive strength is also called crushing
strength. In the same way cracking occurs when the ultimate tensile strength is reached and the material
starts to crack, which is from a mechanical point of view characterized by loss of tensile strength. The
ultimate tensile strength is also called the point of cracking. For both compression and tension the concrete
has a non-linear behaviour but is often simpliﬁed as explained in the following sub-chapters.
To evaluate the strength of concrete the design codes uses stress-strain diagrams to describe the mechanical
behaviour. The tensile strength is often neglected since the contribution to the overall bearing capacity
is very small compared to the compressive strength. This is of course a conservative assumption that will
provide a result on the safe side due to underestimated total strength. However when conducting a full
plastic numerical model the tensile strength can not be neglected and the plastic behaviour in terms of
cracking has to be included. The cracking behaviour is therefore implemented by the theory of fracture
energy which will be explained in subsection 3.3.
In the following the compressive behaviour of concrete is introduced according to the design code EN1992-
1-1 [7], while the tensile behavior is assumed linear with a stiﬀness equal to the initial tangent modulus of
the compressive behaviour.

Tensile strength of concrete
As mentioned in the beginning of the chapter the tensile strength of concrete is much lower than the com-
pressive strength, primarily because of cracks propagating at tensile load. In EN1992-1-1 [7] it is suggested
to neglect the additional bearing capacity found from additional tensile strength.
According to [19] the tensile strength is depending on the dimensions of the cross-section, for which it
will be lower at big cross-sections and higher at small cross-sections. It is also explained that there is a
diﬀerence between tensile strength in direct tension and at ﬂexural tension.
Furthermore it is also explained that due to shrinkage in the construction high stresses might build up and
therefore erase the tensile strength completely. Therefore it is highlighted that it is dangerous to design
constructions of concrete based on the assumption of presence of tensile strength. Though, according
to EN1992-1-1 [7] exceptions are allowed for constructions without reinforcement including columns and
arches (without reinforcement), tunnels, some foundations and some retaining walls among others.
Also, it has to be mentioned that a lot of research has been done on the subject of tensile strength in
concrete. For instance in [20] a ﬁnite element model is compared to an experimental test and the tensile
behaviour is evaluated. It is concluded that the tensile behaviour is not completely linear but has a short
transition of non-linear behaviour before ultimate tensile strength is reach. However it is also mentioned
that the tensile behaviour of concrete is usually accepted to be linear, with an stiﬀness equal to the initial
tangent stiﬀness of the compressive behaviour.
Therefore it is decided for the later numerical model to implement the hardening of tensile behaviour as
completely linear until the point of cracking, and on the basis of the theory of fracture energy a linear
decrease is assumed for the behaviour of cracking, which is explained in subsubsection 3.3.1. An example
of a tensile stress-strain curve is seen in Figure 8, which is taken for a mesh of 0.5 m which inﬂuence the
ultimate strain also explained in subsubsection 3.3.1.

Finite element modelling
3.1
Basics of ﬁnite element modelling
The ﬁnite element method (FEM) is the most widely used numerical method for solving engineering
problems. In particular the ﬁnite element method is useful for complex problems where analytical solutions
can not be obtained [22]. The principle of this method is to divide the considered object into a number of
elements linked by nodes (deﬁning the degrees of freedom) and calculate an approximated solution when
load and boundary conditions are applied. In the following the procedures and the essential tools of ﬁnite
element modelling is introduced, including meshing and element types.
The ﬁnite element method is used in a number of ﬁelds, including structural analysis and thermal analysis
relevant for this thesis.
The process of ﬁnite element modelling
The ﬁrst part of ﬁnite element modelling is to develop a representative model of the physical object. The
object is divided into a number of elements for which physical laws are applied meaning that the behaviour
is idealized into a mathematical model containing boundary value problems.
In terms of computational modelling this includes; correct modelling of the geometry, proper meshing
(discretization), speciﬁcation of material properties and speciﬁcation of boundary, initial and loading con-
ditions. This is explained in [22].
When the model is developed and the ﬁnite element analysis has been conducted the results needs to be
assessed, due to the numerical procedure which only yields an approximated solution. If the accuracy is
not suﬃcient the ﬁnite element calculation has to be replicated with new solution parameters, for instance
a ﬁner mesh. In other words the obtained solution has to be assessed with some kind of accuracy criteria.
Also the chosen mathematical model may be necessary, or of interest, to assess by comparing to another
mathematical model. For instance a beam structure may ﬁrst me analysed by using Bernoulli beam theory
and then compared to the Timoshenko beam theory.
A more detailed explanation of the procedure of ﬁnite element modelling is illustrated in ﬁgure Figure 21.

3.1.1
Meshing and element behaviour
The meshing of the object is highly important for the accuracy of the model, because the accuracy of the
calculation is depending on the number of elements used in the mesh. The more elements used the smaller
each one will be and the more accurate the result will be, but unfortunately more elements also means
more equations to be done resulting in high computational cost.
Now follows a description of the characteristics of an element in order to get a better understanding of
the mesh. Five aspects describes an elements behaviour, namely its degrees of freedom, family, number of
nodes, mathematical formulation and integration points. These are explained in the following according
to [27].
Degrees of freedom
As mentioned elements are connected by nodes containing the degrees of freedom, which is the variables
calculated during the analysis. This means that for a stress/displacement analysis the degrees of freedom
are translation and rotation and for a heat transfer analysis the degrees of freedom are the temperature.
Hence a heat transfer and stress/displacement analysis require use of diﬀerent elements since the degrees
of freedom are not the same. Also coupled thermal-stress elements exist having both temperature and dis-
placement as degrees of freedom. Hence it is important to assign the right kind of element in accordance
with the analysis type used.

In this thesis two models are made, a mechanical model where the degrees of freedom are translation
and rotation, and a thermal model where the degree of freedom is temperature. After running the anal-
ysis of the thermal model, the obtained temperatures are transferred to the mechanical model, and the
stress/displacement analysis is conducted based on temperature dependency.
Family
From the element library of ABAQUS it is realised that the elements are categorised into families of
geometry. The broadest distinctions between elements are therefore in groups of one-dimensional, two-
dimensional and three-dimensional elements. From Figure 22 it follows that beam and truss elements are
one-dimensional, that shell, and membranes elements are two-dimensional and continuum (solid elements)
is three-dimensional.
Hence it is realised that the family determines the degrees of freedom in the element, among other things.
This means for example that the membrane and truss element consist of translation as a degree of freedom,
while the shell and beam elements include both translation and rotation.

As mentioned the degrees of freedom are calculated in the nodes of the elements. Therefore the number
of nodes in an element determines how the degrees of freedom will be interpolated over the element.

element, the displacement will be interpolated linear between the boundary nodes of the element and
quadratic for a second order element. Hence, a ﬁrst order element consist of two nodes located at the
element boundaries, and a second-order element is having a third node in the center of the element. The

3.2
Nonlinear Finite Element
As explained in [3] nonlinear behaviour arises from a number of reasons, in structural mechanics nonlin-
earity occur when materials are yielding, creeping, buckling or cracks are developed. Also in heat transfer
the phenomenon of nonlinearity often occur when material properties are temperature-dependent.
Nonlinear problems pose the diﬃculty of being described accurately by both analytical and numerical
methods and when solved numerically the computational cost might be high.
In structural mechanics nonlinear problems are grouped in three principal categories as explained in [3]:
• Material nonlinearity, in which material properties are changing because of change in stress or strain.
For example yielding and creep.
• Contact nonlinearity, in which a gab between adjacent parts may open or close or a contact force
inﬂuences the contact area or a frictional force.
• Geometric nonlinearity, in which the structure experience deformation large enough that the equilib-
rium equations have to rewritten in respect to the deformed structural geometry. Large deformation
might also change the direction of loads.
These problems are nonlinear because the stiﬀness, and maybe the load, become function of displacement
or deformation.
Thus, for nonlinear calculations, the stiﬀness matrix K and perhaps the load vector
R becomes function of D, the unknown degrees of freedom.

3.3.1
Cracking of concrete in FEM
There is two methods to model cracking of concrete in ﬁnite element programs, namely the discrete crack
and the smeared crack model. The two models are illustrated in Figure 27 and a short description of the
two models is given in the following explained by [23] and [29].
In the discrete crag method crags are modelled as geometrically discontinuities, for which the crag grows
when the stress at the surface reaches the tensile strength of the given concrete. This means that for nodes
exceeding the tensile strength the cracking will propagate into the cross-section, and split the node at the
surface into two nodes.
The drawback of this method is that it is computational expensive and is therefore not used very often.
In the smeared crag method crags are modelled as a solid element, for which the crags are accounted
for by reduction of the stiﬀness and strength. This is done by means of integration points for which the
degradation is applied on nodes where the tensile strength is exceeded. Hence the constitutive calculations
are performed with reduced strength.

a yield surface is a surface in the space of stresses. If the state of stress is inside the enclosure of the

concrete) and therefore enables ABAQUS to determine failures on basis of normal and shear stresses [30].

The Smeared Cracking model
Another way to model the non-linear behaviour of the concrete is by using the Smeared Cracking Model

integration point. This is the smeared cracking approach.

Comparison of ABAQUS model to other methods
In order to evaluate the obtained temperatures of the ﬁnite element model, other calculations of the heat
distribution are made. This is done both by the suggested method of the Danish National Annex for
EN1992-1-2 [10] and by the program Conﬁre made by Hertz. In this way the diﬀerences between the
models are also investigated. The formula of the National annex and the Conﬁre program are introduced
in subsection 2.3.
In ﬁgure Figure 40 the comparison of the time-temperature curves are presented, both at the surface and
at the depth of 40 mm from the exposed side. A clear diﬀerence in the models are seen, and an explanation
has been sought. Among other things K. Hertz has been asked why Conﬁre is deviating.
First, it should be noted that ﬁnite element models as default are not taking the moisture content of the
concrete into account. The moisture content transfer additional heat into the cross-section during the
heating phase, but when the moisture evaporates heat is also taken out of the cross-section due to the
vapour produced. In other words, ﬁnite element modelling provide incorrect results due to the movement
of moisture inside the concrete, though it should be noted that results are on the safe side.
Regarding the calculation of Conﬁre, a ﬁnite-diﬀerence calculation is made and the temperature proﬁle
is determined by means of lamellas. The outer lamella is of half the thickness of the other lamellas, and
therefore the calculated surface temperature is corresponding to the temperature at the center of a full
sized lamella. This gives a bit lower surface temperature when using ConFire compared to the other models
as seen in ﬁgure Figure 40a. The ﬁnite-diﬀerence calculation is still based on the convection of the gas
temperature according to [12].
It should also be noted that the calculations in ConFire are adjusted to experimental data which implies
the eﬀects of moisture. This means that, even though, the surface temperature obtained by ConFire may
not be very accurate, the internal temperature in the cross-section is presumable more accurate than the

Master’s thesis
temperatures obtained from ABAQUS and the tables of EN1992-1-2. In Figure 40b the temperature dis-
tribution of ConFire at the depth of 40 mm is compared to the other models.
Regarding the analytical solution in Figure 40b, it is seen that this one is more similar to the result of
Conﬁre than the ﬁnite element model. However it should be emphasized that the analytical solution in this
graph is based on a constant conductivity λ = 0.75W/mK, which causes a big deviation in the temperature
calculation since the conductivity is varying with temperature. In subsection 2.2, Figure 4 the variation of
the conductivity according to EN1992-1-2 is seen which is the variation used in the ﬁnite element model.

Uniaxial compressive and tensile test
In order to validate the material model of the concrete an uni-axial compressive and tensile test is per-
formed. An uni-axial load test causes an uniaxial stress, meaning that there is only one stress component
which therefore has to be equal to the applied load. In this way the material model is validated, meaning
if the input material stress-strain curve is identical to the stress-strain response calculated from ABAQUS
the modal is validated.
In the following this uniaxial test is presented, ﬁrst at the ambient temperature and then for elevated
temperatures. The material model used is a smeared cracking model as explained in subsubsection 3.3.1,
and the elements used for mesh are the two-dimensional CPS8 elements.

Furthermore from the two plots, Figure 58 and Figure 59 it is clear that the stresses are interpolated linear

rough mesh causes loss of area below the curve.

in order to capture the real compressive and tensile stress in the concrete. This highlight the issue of
modelling concrete structures with precision, where in one hand the physical object require a small mesh
and at the same time the numerical model of the concrete require a rough mesh.

The actual conﬁguration of the slab is presented in Figure 65. It is seen that the considered slab is sur-

The discretization of the model is depending on diﬀerent factors. Besides the lower and upper limits of

an explicit way. The steel can be attached to the concrete by an implicit method and an explicit method.
The implicit method means that the reinforcement is embedded by the embedment function in ABAQUS,
meaning that the interaction between the steel and the concrete is computed by ABAQUS. The implicit
method has turned out not to be as reliable as the explicit method for the modelling of the slab.
For the explicit method the reinforcement is modelled with the same number of elements as the concrete,
then the concrete is parted in two at the desired location of the reinforcement. Afterwards the nodes of the
reinforcement is tied to the nodes of the concrete, in the separation line between the two concrete parts.
In other words the explicit method is a manual way of modelling the interaction between the concrete and
the steel, where the nodes of the steel and the concrete it tied together.
For the reinforce concrete slab the explicit method has been the only method converging. Therefore the
discretization of this method is explained.

courses the maximum mesh size to be 50 mm above the reinforcement. Regarding the limitation of the
mesh a smaller mesh size is not desired.

Based on the preceding observations a simple way of estimating the ﬁre resistance of the slab is by taking

assigning the time to collapse based on displacement limits and so on. Another way of approaching the

the ﬁre resistance of three hours is an agreement with the analytical ﬁre resistance, and therefore seems
valid.
Finally it should be mentioned that the failure criterion can be deﬁned in three ways. Above, the criterion
was based on the curvature of the temperature-displacement plot, stating that the critical temperatures
is at maximum curvature. Therefore this criterion is not related to the displacement but to the runaway
value.

limit could be deﬁned, stating that the critical temperature is reached when the displacement reaches the
predeﬁned limit. From [26] such a criteria is found, seen in (Equation 4.26). If relating this failure criteria
to Figure 84, a critical temperature is found to be approximately 1150◦C, which corresponds roughly to a

Thermal analysis of the hydrogen ﬁre
Now follows the conduction of a thermal analysis of the hydrogen ﬁre. The proposed hydrogen ﬁre has
been handed out as a sink temperature, which is varying over the length of the beam. For the case of
simplicity the ﬁre distribution is compressed into ﬁve pieces for which the average ﬁre distribution in each
piece is calculated. Thereby, the hydrogen ﬁre is not varying over the length in each piece, but is diﬀerent
between the pieces.
Afterwards, the meshing of the thermal analysis is addressed and the temperature proﬁles are determined.
The thermal properties are as for the standard ﬁre taken from 1992-1-2 and are presented in subsection 4.1.
Furthermore the heat transfer parameters, meaning the emissivity and convection coeﬃcient are also the
same and is also presented in subsection 4.1.
It should be mentioned that the hydrogen ﬁre is asymmetric distributed on the slab, and therefore a full
model of the slab is necessary. However, due to convergence problems a model in full length was never
modelled. A lot of diﬀerent attempts were made to obtain convergence, but none of them were successful.
In Appendix E a few selected results of the full size slab are presented.
Therefore the cantilever beam from subsection 4.4 is reused to represent the slab. However, in order to do
this, the hydrogen ﬁre has to be assumed symmetricly distributed, as the loading and the standard ﬁre
were assumed symmetric distributed. This assumption is not very true to reality since the hydrogen ﬁre
is not symmetric distributed. Nonetheless, a symmetric setup for the hydrogen ﬁre is conducted.

On one hand the ther-

in Figure 105. On the other hand the structural model calls for a rough mesh when modelling plastic
behaviour of concrete, because in order to model cracking of concrete the concept of constant fracture
energy is used, which is giving mesh limitations. The sensitivity test of the structural model was seen in
Figure 70.
Therefore the thermal analysis might seem unnecessary, since the accurate temperature distribution ob-
tained is not passed on to the structural model in the end, because of the conﬂicting mesh sizes. From this
perspective the thermal analysis could be neglected and an approximated linear or bi-linear temperature
distribution might be applied to the structural model, calculated on the basis of surface temperature and
penetration time.
However it is noted, in Figure 109, that until the drop of surface temperature the structural model has
higher temperatures in the cross-section than the thermal model. This means that the structural model is
more conservative than the thermal model in the perspective of design. It is also seen that the temperature
proﬁles of the thermal and structural model, after the drop of surface temperature, actually gets closer to
each other. For example at the end of the ﬁre only a signiﬁcant diﬀerent is seen at the ﬁrst 0.01 m of the
cross-section.
Further, it should be mentioned that the temperature proﬁles of the four other intervals of the beam, are
almost identical to the one presented and an analysis of these are therefore neglected.
This is concluded from Figure 104 from which the similarities of the intervals are clear. All ﬁve curves
have alternating temperatures over time, but all with four peaks and with approximately the same maxi-
mum and minimum temperatures. Hence the temperature distribution in the cross-section are almost not
changing over the length of the cantilever beam. This also means that the horizontal heat transfer in the
cross-section is insigniﬁcant since the adjacent zones have approximately the same penetration depth and
temperature.

is obvious that if the pieces were not similar this assumption could not be made.

Bearing capacity after the hydrogen ﬁre
Because of the degradation of the materials due to heating, it is assumed that all material in the cross-
section that have experienced temperatures above 300◦C is permanently damaged and will have no bearing
capacity after the ﬁre. While the rest of the cross-section is going back to the original strength. This means
that there is only a loss of tensile strength in the concrete, since only the tensile zone is heated.
Therefore, in order to determine the bearing capacity after the ﬁre in a simple way, a model with re-
duced cross-section height is made. From the thermal analysis it is derived that approximately 2 cm of
the cross-section is experiencing temperatures above 300◦C. Thus, a model with a cross-section height of
33 cm and reinforcement located at the depth of 3 cm, would approximate the bearing capacity after the ﬁre.

the fact that the reduction of the cross-section height is coursing the element size above the reinforcement

Conclusion
5.1
Summary and highlight
The aim of this project was to develop a ﬁnite element model capable of predicting the temperature proﬁle
and response when exposed to a hydrogen ﬁre. Hence the model was comprised by a thermal analysis of
a hydrogen ﬁre and a structural analysis of a reinforced tunnel slab. The thermal-mechanical integration
was then done explicitly, by inputting the temperature distribution obtained from the hydrogen ﬁre in the
mechanical model and the ﬁre resistance was found.
The slab were simpliﬁed to a two-dimensional beam and therefore only the plan stress and strain condition
were considered. Which should be regarded as a major simpliﬁcation of the slab.
The ﬁrst step was to establish and validate a material model of the concrete. This was done through
the in-elastic strain and a constant fracture energy deﬁnition which were validated through a uniaxial
compressive and tensile test. The uniaxial test were conducted at both ambient temperature and elevated
temperatures. At this point is was already proven that small mesh sizes lead to converging problems during
the tensile test, and therefore the bi-linear and tri-linear post-failure stress-strain curves were introduced
as a way to respond on this.
Secondly, a cantilever beam was modelled, exposed to a uniform load, to validate the global behaviour in
terms of a pushover analysis compared to an analytical solution. In the pushover analysis the concern of
inherent mesh sensitivity when modelling concrete was demonstrated, meaning that no unique result was
found for the convergence of diﬀerent mesh. Furthermore, the analysis was highly sensitive to the time
increment used.
Also, the local behaviour of the cantilever beam was investigated. Especially it was seen that the lim-
itations on the mesh, due to the constant fracture energy deﬁnition, lead to rough estimations of the
stress-distribution in the cross-section. This was highlighted as being one of the challenges when modelling
concrete. Where the physical object requires a very ﬁne mesh, in order to obtain accurate results, while
the numerical model requires a rough mesh in order to converge.
Finally, the tunnel slab was considered for which both the local and global behaviour were studied at the
ultimate load. The ﬁnite element model was faced with converging problems which were addressed by
raising the fracture energy to 160 N/m, causing higher ultimate strain and by applying the reinforcement
explicit to the model, instead of using the implemented embedment function in ABAQUS.
From a pushover analysis a suﬃcient mesh of 0.05 × 0.1 m above the reinforcement, and 0.075 × 0.1 m
below the reinforcement was found. The need of the two mesh sizes is due to the explicit modelling of the
steel.
Along with the structural model, a thermal model for the standard ﬁre was made from which the tem-
perature distribution in the cross-section was determined. After establishing the structural model, the
temperatures in the cross-section found in the thermal analysis, was applied explicit to the model. In this
way the ﬁre resistance to a standard ﬁre was found to be 3 hours.
After validating the behaviour of the tunnel slab by the pushover analysis at 20◦C and the thermal-
structural analysis for a standard ﬁre, the hydrogen ﬁre was addressed. First the thermal analysis was
made, and from a sensitivity test a suﬃcient mesh of 0.0005 m was found. Hence it was realised that the

meshing of the thermal model were conﬂicting with the meshing of the structural model and a graphi-
cal comparison was made, in Figure 109, showing the temperature proﬁle of the thermal and structural
model. In either way it was seen that the reinforcement was not aﬀected by the hydrogen ﬁre, and therefore
only the tensile strength of the concrete was reduced. This leads to the conclusion that the bearing capac-

Then the thermal-structural analysis was conducted, and the global behaviour in terms of stress and

caused the displacement to vary with time. At last the loss of bearing capacity after the cooling phase
was addressed, by the assumption saying that all material heated above 300◦C were permanently damaged.
5.2
Suggestions to further work
It is obvious that this project has been limited, and a logical next step would be to reﬁne the ﬁnite element
model of the simple supported beam, so the asymmetric ﬁre load could be applied over the full length.
Afterwards the model should be extended to three-dimensions.
Also, it could be argued that a hydrogen ﬁre most likely would not take place by it self. If there is a
hydrogen release, the ﬁre will spread over more cars and a ﬂash-over ﬁre will occur. Therefore, it seems
unrealistic to only consider the hydrogen ﬁre without inﬂuence of a second ﬁre, and future work could
therefore be to combine the hydrogen ﬁre with a standard ﬁre to see the eﬀect on the ﬁre resistance.
Furthermore, the eﬀect of spalling has not been taken into consideration which has to be considered before
a ﬁnal conclusion can be made on the ﬁre resistance. Simply because if spalling is occurring in the early
state of the ﬁre, removing maybe 2 cm of concrete from the surface, the temperature proﬁle will be changed
and causing higher temperatures in the reinforcement.
Therefore, from this research, it cannot be concluded that a hydrogen ﬁre in a tunnel would not have any
structural consequences, simply because the eﬀect of spalling and ﬁre spread is not taken into account.
However, it is shown that a hydrogen ﬁre in itself is not aﬀecting the bearing capacity, if assuming no
spalling and ﬁre spread.
Also regarding the thermal-mechanical integration improvements could be done. It was seen that the
explicit method of applying temperatures from the thermal model, resulted in a very rough estimation
of the temperature proﬁle in the structural model. Therefore a coupled analysis would be of interest,
including both the heat transfer and the mechanical analysis in the same model.

D.1 Heat Transfer with ﬁxed temperature
Again, a temperature of 600◦C is applied to the exposed side in order to investigate the heat transfer in

Hence, it is seen that a material with a high value of thermal conductivity, steel in this case, is transferring
heat better than a material with low thermal conductivity, concrete in this case.
