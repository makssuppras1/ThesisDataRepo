 ABSTRACT
 
Important evidence which confirms that the visual signal influences in recognition 
of the speech is the McGurk effect. This is an illusion that occurs when one audio signal 
from one sound is paired with the visual signal from another, leading to the perception of a 
third sound. The goal of this Master Thesis was to extract from video recordings the main 
visual speech features involved in audio visual recognition. For this purpose, we have 
recorded 3 native Danish and 3 native Spanish speakers. These appeared saying different 
syllables, non-words and lists of phonetically balanced sentences. After a process of 
decoding and alignment, we have analyzed the video recordings using Principal and 
Independent Component Analysis. The application of the results of the Principal 
Component Analysis brought the following results. We reconstructed all the syllables and 
non-word video recordings with only 20 speech principal components. We also achieved 
the video reconstruction of one speaker with only 4 speech principal components. To 
continued, we applied a process of Independent Component Analysis to the results. Here, 
we reconstructed the video recordings of the same speaker with 8 speech independent 
components. To prove the results obtained from both analyses, we conducted a test with 3 
native Danish and 3 native Spanish participants. The test reproduced three different 
McGurk effects recreated with the principal stages of the project. We used the original 
video recordings, the videos after alignment and the videos reconstructed with 20 speech 
principal components. The McGurk effect which delivered better results is the one formed 
by the audio /aga/ and video /aba/. With that effect, the Spanish group obtained between 
96% and 50% McGurk responses. The Danish group overcame these results obtaining a 
minimum of 62.5% of McGurk responses. The main conclusion drawn was that the 
McGurk effect can be detected with only 20 speech principal components. Some results 
showed that it might be possible to reduce the data even more. Yet, this project has shown 
that is better to apply PCA instead of ICA to perform this reduction.  KEY WORDS
 
Visual speech features, Audio visual speech recognition, McGurk effect, Principal 
Component Analysis, Independent Component Analysis

 1. INTRODUCTION
 
The knowledge about how the brain works interpreting audiovisual signals is still 
relatively unknown. Different investigations [1] about speech recognition have been done 
with successful results. However other studies have been focused in audio visual speech 
recognition without the same success. Important evidence which confirms that the visual 
signal influences in recognition of the speech is the McGurk effect [2]. This effect was first 
demonstrated by McGurk and MacDonald who found that auditory syllables such as /ba/ 
dubbed onto a video record of speakers saying different syllables such as /ga/, were 
perceived as something auditory different such as /tha/ or /da/. Actually it is found that the 
McGurk effect is complex, if the visual and the audio signal are the opposite, that is an 
auditory syllable /ga/ dubbed onto a video record of speakers saying /ba/, is recognized as 
/bga/ instead of /tha/ or /da/ [3]. On other hand, lip-reading [4] can be considered other 
evidence which confirms that some features of the face can help to interpret the talker. For 
these reason, most of the researches about McGurk effect have focused on lip-reading 
[1][2]. Not only are the lips involved in visual recognition but also some wrinkles around 
the eyes or mouth and even some emotions [5] represented in face expressions, like smile, 
can give information about what the talker is saying. The body can also help in speech 
recognition like Cued Speech [6] system.  
Most of the studies in visual speech recognition [1] used Principal Component 
Analysis (PCA) because it is useful when there is a lot of data to analyze that it could be 
reduced into a small set of components. However, some other studies analyzed the principal 
differences between Principal and Independent Component Analysis (ICA) used in face 
recognition. The main different between these two methods is that PCA assumes that the 
whole space is described by a single multidimensional Gaussian distribution while ICA 
assumes that the space is described by a sum of statistically independent non-Gaussian 
distributions which represent different sources. In these studies, it could be concluded that 
although these comparisons are complex because differences in tasks, implementing ICA 
involving a whitened PCA process by horizontally centering [7], has its advantage. ICA is 
sensitive to high-order statics in the data, not just the covariance matrix [8].  
The main purpose of this Master Thesis is to find the most important visual speech 
features (trying not to focus only on the mouth) that help the listener to understand the 
talker in visual speech recognition. After find them, we have verified these visual speech 
features through the recreation of some McGurk effects.

16 1. INTRODUCTION 
 
First of all we have recorded Spanish and Danish native speakers saying different 
syllables or non-words like /ba/ or /aba/ and a list of sentences in their own language. 
These recordings have been edited and decoded obtaining the frames that later have been 
aligned in order to delate unnecessary movements of head. To reduce the number of visual 
speech features in order to find the principal movements, PCA was executed first in the 
frames obtained from the sentences videos and later in the syllables ones. To apply PCA, 
the frames were considered as measurement vectors and every pixel as one dimension. The 
set of frames obtained from every video was reduced into a set of principal components 
(PCs) ordered according their variance. This set of PCs contained the principal visual 
speech features. Then ICA is applied over the results obtained from PCA with the purpose 
of get stronger results that allow the reconstruction of videos with less data. To apply ICA, 
we considered every PC as one observation and each of its scores as one dimension. To 
organize the independent components (ICs), we considered them as vectors that could be 
ordered by their length. This set of ICs contained visual speech features that were not 
necessary the same contained in the PCs and, perhaps, allowed to reconstruct the videos 
with smaller number of components.  
The visual speech features found in both analyses were evaluated in psychological 
experiments. In the first three experiments we have tried to find the best solutions of 
reduction of data and the evaluations have been made in an objective way only by the 
researchers involved. In the last experiment some McGurk effects have been reproduced 
with the results found in the previous experiments. These effects have been shown to 
Spanish and Danish groups of participants in order to test the results obtained in this study.  
Although there is more future work that could be implemented with the results 
obtained from this project, the conclusions of this Master Thesis are strong enough to be 
used in different research areas. The utility of the features found in this project could be 
used in different domains such as audiovisual transmission signal reducing the amount of 
data to transmit. It could be also applied in artificial intelligence improving the human 
appearance in visual speech recognition considering that the visual speech features found in 
this project are enough to create some illusions for real humans. Besides, it could be 
beneficial in cognitive science and psychology to understand better the way the brain works 
in visual speech recognition.     1.1STRUCTURE OF THE REPORT

Once the videos were recorded, it was necessary to decode the videos to get every 
frame for the analysis. Before decoding every video, the videos were edited with the editing

1920x1080 pixels to 1280x720 because otherwise the frames were too big.

normally they were not totally static as it can be seen in the Figure 3. To execute the

command vision.CascadeObjectDetector5 from the Computer Vision System Toolbox. This 
command creates a System object detector that uses the Viola-Jones algorithm [17] to 
detect faces or pairs of eyes. After this detection, we cut the frames according to the region

The frames obtained were really similar to the results we have been expected, all

significant, especially if they were reproduce as 25 frames per second. When the program

be that the data accumulated in the memory to work with Matlab was too big that the

realigned with the same transformation or with a simpler type of transformation like rigid

Appendix 3 it can be seen differences between these two transformations.  
After the process of alignment, a process of PCA and, subsequently, ICA were 
applied to the frames. The frames were the observations to be analyzed through the method 
of PCA. In that way, the frames were considered as measurement vectors and every pixel as 
a dimension, or more commonly called, as a variable. The purpose of applied PCA is to 
reduce the set of frames into a smaller set of components through an orthogonal 
transformation. The total number of principal components resulting from PCA is equal to 
𝑛−1 observations. The minimum number of frames obtained from the decoding of videos 
was 5160 frames. As consequence the minimum number of principal components was 
5159. Also, the number of pixels or dimensions that every frame had was too high (the 
frames aligned had each one 1280 · 720 = 921600 number of pixels) and implied a huge 
amount of data to process. In addition, the principal components are the eigenvectors of the 
covariance matrix resulting from PCA which means that it is only necessary same number 
of variables and observations. In other words, it was not necessary to have 921600 pixels 
and, because of that, the frames were resized to be one third of the original resolution and 
cut to have a final resolution of 127x117 (total of 14859 pixels).  
Not all the frames belonging from every speaker have been cut in the same position 
so to cut them it has been taken in account that the parts of the face more involved in 
speech are the lips and jaw, as it can be seen from the test of Öhman’s V-to-V (chapter 7 of 
[1]). In this test, the degrees of freedom of two French speaker’s vocal tracts were obtained 
through an articulatory-driven PCA and used as parameters of a model. Nine parameters 
(belonging to mouth and jaw) were considered to represent the degrees-of-freedom. To cut 
the frames belonging to every speaker, we have focused in the reference image and created 
boxes with the fixed size but in different positions according to the speaker.

 2.3.2 INDEPENDENT COMPONENT ANALYSIS
The Independent Component Analysis (ICA) assumes that the space is described by 
a sum of statically independent distributions, each representing a different unknown source 
[20]. The purpose is to find the vectors corresponding to each source deleting the noise. In 
order not to confuse with PCA, different notation is used in ICA. In the ICA model 𝑛 
random variables, called observations, 𝑥1, … , 𝑥𝑛 are modeled as linear combinations of 
𝑛 random variables 𝑠1, … , 𝑠𝑛: 
𝐱= ∑(𝐚𝒊· 𝑠𝒊)
𝑛
𝑖=1
 
[4] 
 
The 𝑠𝑖 are the statistically independent components (ICs). Normally, a matrix 
notation is used instead of sums: 
𝐗= 𝐀· 𝐒 
[5]

PCA carries out a decorrelation of the input data, but does not deal with 
dependences of higher order [18]. The decorrelation means that the variables cannot be 
predicted from each other using a simpler linear predictor and for this reason can still being 
nonlinear dependencies between them that PCA would not be able to analyze [19]. 
Independent Component Analysis is an extension to PCA that separates the high-order 
dependencies in the input in addition to the second-order dependencies.  
With PCA the dependences in data are encoded by rotating the axes to correspond 
to direction of maximum covariance. However, ICA does not constrain the axes to be 
orthogonal; the axes are going to be placed in the directions of the ICs.

In this Master Thesis, as it could be seen in PCA, the frames were viewed as 
observations and the pixel values were random variables. We have applied the FastICA10 
package for Matlab to the results obtained in PCA. Looking the information of this package 
it can be seen that the data matrix to introduce in fastica is composed by the observed 
signals as rows. In our case the observed signals were all the principal components with 
their score in all frames. For this reason, we considered our data matrix the transpose of the 
PCA score matrix in order to have a frame column data matrix: 𝐗′ = 𝐓𝑇. 
The independent components, 𝐒, and mixing matrix, 𝐀, obtained from fastica were 
ordered in a random way, given by the speed with that ICs were calculated. If we want to 
compare these results with the ones obtained from PCA we have to introduce an order in 
these ICs due to they have a unitary variance. Knowing that the ICs and the mixing matrix 
were proportional we could order the ICs by their length. In other words, the technique 
employed was to obtain the norms of every column of the mixing matrix 𝐀 which gives the 
contributions of every IC to the PCs’ variances. Ordering the ICs following these norms 
resembles the order given by the PCA method [21].

 3.4.2 PARTICIPANTS
 
Six students from the Technical University of Denmark participated in this 
experiment. Three of them were native Spanish speakers from Spain and the other three 
were native Danish speakers. Two of them were females and four were males. All of them 
were between 24 and 26 years old.  3.4.3 PROCEDURE
The experiment was conducted in the same sound proof booth where the videos 
were recorded, the Communication 1 Room of the Hearing Systems group of the Technical 
University of Denmark. Visual and audio stimuli were presented in the laptop Lenovo 
E3116 which has a Full HD IPS (In-Plane Switching) and 13.3” screen and it was adjusted 
to have the maximum brightness (235 cd/m2). The volume was fixed to the 25% of the 
maximum volume. The participants were sitting approximately 0.5 meters away from the 
screen of the computer and were instructed to watch the videos and write down what they 
were hearing.  
The program to run the experiment was written in Matlab and reproduces the videos 
with one package17 of Matlab that allows communicate with VLC18 Media Player. First of 
all, the program differentiated between Spanish and Danish participants: “Are you Danish 
(1) or Spanish (2)? Answer 1 or 2”. The videos were randomly reproduced 20 times due to 
the perception of McGurk effect can change over the repetitions [26]. The participants were 
not allowed to reproduce them again or change the volume from the laptop. Every time one 
video was reproduced, the program suggested 3 options that could be listened and change 
according to the video reproduced: “What do you think is he/she saying? ’atha' 'aba' 'ada' 
other?”. The participants had to write down what they heard, being part of the options or 
not, and click “enter” to continue with the next video. Only one option was allowed to be 
reported. 
There was not limit of time defined but the participants were instructed to respond 
quickly and to report their first percept. Approximately the test lasted between 40 and 45 
minutes in the case of the Spanish participants and around 1 hour for the Danish ones. The 
responses were saved as an excel file and sometimes was necessary to check them in the 
console window from Matlab because the participants clicked “enter” before write down 
the answer.

 4. CONCLUSION
 
This Master Thesis has been carried out achieving the purpose of extracting the 
main visual speech features involved in audio visual speech recognition from video 
recordings. These videos recordings were taken with 3 native Danish and 3 native Spanish 
speakers. As it was planned, a process of Independent Component Analysis has been done 
with a previous Principal Component Analysis.  
Initially we tried to focus on all visual speech features from the neck to forehead. 
However we realized that the principal components that most influence in visual speech 
recognition were the ones related with the movements of the mouth and the position of the 
jaw. This is consistent with other studies mentioned in this report [1][2]. Across the first 2 
experiments, we have been able to reconstruct the syllables and non-word video recordings 
with only 20 speech principal components. The greatest achievement has been obtained 
reconstructing the videos of the Danish speaker 3 with only 4 speech PCs. These 4 speech 
PCs, which achieve the 50% of the speech variance, contain principally the visual speech 
features of mouth opening and rounding.  
The independent components obtained from the video recordings after the PCA 
showed that they provided, in their Basis Images, slightly more specific visual speech 
features. However we expected to be more precise features, like identify only the upper lip 
or the eyes, as it can be seen in the study [7]. We have expected to be able to reduce more 
data with ICA than with PCA results because we expected to be able to distinguish better 
the visual speech features. However we have seen in the experiment 3, with the Danish 
speaker 3, that this was not possible. Thus, the best solution has been achieved with the 
reconstruction of the videos of the Danish speaker 3 with 8 speech independent components 
which means adding more visual speech features than in the case of 4 speech PCs.  
One test (experiment 4) with 3 native Danish and 3 native Spanish participants has 
been carried out to prove the results obtained from both PCA and ICA reproducing 3 
different McGurk effects. These effects were reproduced with the original video recordings, 
the videos after alignment and the videos reconstructed with 20 speech PCs. Without taking 
into account the Danish participant 2 who has showed not visual influence in any McGurk 
effect, the results obtained from both groups of participants were as we had expected. When 
the McGurk effect appeared in the original videos it also then appeared in the next stages 
with a gradual decrease in the number of McGurk responses. From this experiment, we can 
==PAGE:58==
58 4. CONCLUSION 
 
almost generalize that as long as the McGurk effect appears in the original videos, we can 
reduce the data until 20 speech PCs.   
The McGurk effect which has achieved better results for both groups of participants 
is the one formed by the audio /aga/ and video /aba/. In this effect, all the participants have 
perceived the McGurk effect in all the speakers. On one hand, for the group of Spanish 
participants, it has been achieved a minimum of 50% (reconstruction with 20 speech PCs of 
the speaker 1) of the responses different to the audio stimuli. Also it has been achieved 
maximum of 96% (original videos Spanish speaker 3) of McGurk responses with a 
variability of 11.67% between the original and reconstructed with 20 speech PCs videos. 
This was the best result over all responses from the Spanish group of participants. On the 
other hand, in the case of the Danish group of participants, all the speakers achieved high 
percentages. In fact we can say that the results for this effect are even better than in the 
Spanish group of participants. The minimum percentage of McGurk responses achieved 
was 62.5% (reconstructed videos with 8 speech ICs Danish speaker 3). If only these results 
were considered we would say that when the McGurk effect appears in the original videos, 
we can also reconstruct the videos with only 4 speech PCs.    
  To sum up, the main conclusion of this Master Thesis is that we can find high 
number of responses to the McGurk effect with videos reconstructed with at least 20 speech 
PCs. In addition, PCA performs better than ICA if the objective is to reduce the data in the 
reconstruction of videos.

 5. FUTURE WORK
 
Even strong results have been achieved in this project; there is still being more work 
that could be done. As future works, we propose the following ideas to continuing working 
on the video recordings. 
 
First of all we only have analyzed deeply with the method of Principal Component 
Analysis the Danish speaker 3. The reason was that we have considered this speaker to be 
the best to recognize her movements of mouth in lip-reading. However, after the 
experiment 4 we have observed that other speakers achieved more McGurk responses, at 
least in the case of the Danish speakers. For these reason, it could be interesting to analyze 
the rest of all the speakers to find the minimum of speech principal components with which 
we could reconstruct the videos.   
 
We have also only analyzed deeply with the method of Independent Component 
Analysis the Danish speaker 3. Thus, as we said in the before, it could be also interesting to 
analyze the rest of all the speakers to find the minimum of speech independent components 
with which we could reconstruct the videos.  
 
Furthermore, we have reproduced three different kinds of McGurk effects that we 
considered as the easiest to reconstruct. However, there are lots of different kinds of 
McGurk effects that could be reproduced with the videos recordings we have taken at the 
beginning of this project. As we have found that the strongest McGurk effect in this project 
is the audio /aga/ and video /aba/ it might be found other effects that might be better or that 
might achieve similar responses. We propose to reconstruct other illusions to test with the 
people like the audio /ba/ with video /ga/, audio /ava/ with visual /ada/ and so on.  
 
Finally, it could be interesting to determine the PCs or ICs that are important for 
every one of audio visual integrations. That means that every audio visual integration that 
represents each of the McGurk effects could be principally supported for few components. 
Furthermore, it could be that only one visual speech feature is enough to get the audio 
visual integration. Thus, determine the important PCs or ICs could be really relevant to 
understand better the way this audio visual integration is produced.
