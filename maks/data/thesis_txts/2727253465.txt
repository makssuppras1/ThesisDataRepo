Abstract
Estimating semantic textual similarity (STS) is essential in Natural Language Pro-
cessing (NLP). Despite progress in STS for written text, limited research exists for
transcripts, especially in emergency calls. This thesis explores the feasibility of cre-
ating a robust system capable of performing STS tasks on transcripts, considering
the challenges of transcription errors. The project is written in collaboration with
Corti, a Danish healthcare software scale-up.
A dataset of 6,000 sentence pairs
from emergency call transcripts is generated, and state-of-the-art STS systems are
evaluated using this dataset. Additionally, Automated Speech Recognition (ASR)
systems, Corti’s in-house ASR, and OpenAI’s Whisper are used to generate two
additional datasets and test the performance of the STS systems. The results re-
veal that the fine-tuned transformer models XLNet and Sentence-BERT perform
strongly on noisy data, indicating their potential to tackle semantic similarity tasks
in critical domains like emergency response. The findings could improve emergency
call handling and quality assurance through semantic search and quality manage-
ment applications, ultimately saving lives.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) is a crucial aspect of Natural Language Pro-
cessing (NLP) that involves determining the degree of semantic similarity between
two pieces of text. Although the words used in two sentences may be completely
different, such as "I am allergic to milk" and "I am lactose intolerant," the under-
lying semantic meaning can be considered the same. This foundational NLP task
has numerous applications in various fields. It is a standalone task for measuring
the difference between sentences and an upstream task that supports other down-
stream tasks, such as document retrieval, question answering, machine translation,
and plagiarism detection.
The field of semantic similarity is rapidly evolving and has its roots in the field
of linguistics. While other work under a different name has been conducted, Se-
mantic Similarity first emerged in 2006 and quickly gained traction within the NLP
community, as seen in Figure 1.2. The 2012 conference of SemEval was a turning
point for the field as it included the task "A Pilot on Semantic Textual Similarity,"
[10] which consisted of a dataset of around 1000 sentence pairs, scored on a scale
from 0 to 5 where 0 is not similar, and 5 is very similar. Since then, the task of
estimating semantic similarity has been a part of the SemEval conference in 2013
[8], 2014 [12], 2015 [13], and 2016 [14], further cementing its significance in the NLP
community.
STS systems compute a similarity score between 0 and 5, where 0 indicates no
similarity, and 5 represents complete equivalence. This score is determined through
various techniques, such as word overlap, cosine similarity, and word embeddings,
to name a few. The advancements in NLP, particularly in deep learning, have led
to the development of more sophisticated STS systems that can accurately measure
the semantic similarity between texts, even in the presence of noise and errors.
Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) has seen several different systems being in-
troduced over the years that achieve good results.
Initially, the focus was on
knowledge-based methods, which involved creating large databases such as Word-
Net [76] and using various edge-counting methods. With the advent of deep learn-
ing and NLP, methods for word embeddings were introduced, such as GloVe [82]
and Word2Vec [73]. These methods involve embedding words and sentences into a
multi-dimensional space, with co-occurrences as a proxy for similarity. This opened
up new possibilities for measuring semantic similarity, and further advancements
led to the development of deep neural network systems, such as Long-short-term-
memory (LSTM)[35]. In recent years, transformer-based models, such as BERT [38],

Sentence-BERT [89], XLNet [108], T5 [87], have emerged as state-of-the-art in STS.
These models have shown exceptional results, leading some researchers to discuss
the possibility of an "end of history" in the field [29]. Transformer-based models
have revolutionised the way semantic similarity is measured and have opened up
new avenues for research and development in this field.
However, semantic textual similarity systems have primarily been used for writ-
ten text - not transcripts of speech.
Transcripts of spontaneous speech, which
is unplanned, have rarely, if ever, been the subject of semantic similarity.
This
is mainly due to the unique challenges speech transcripts pose, particularly those
resulting from spontaneous speech. Spontaneous speech often lacks proper gram-
matical structures and can be challenging to transcribe accurately, even if it sounds
reasonable. This is particularly true if the transcripts are verbatim, as they may con-
tain non-lexical utterances, such as "uh," "umm," and "hmmm," as well as speech
disfluencies, such as filled pauses and repetitions. Although these utterances do
not have any semantic meaning, they are still a part of the transcript and must be
considered. Additionally, transcripts of spontaneous speech are rarely well-planned,
making the task of semantic similarity even more challenging. The nature of spon-
taneous speech and its transcripts present unique challenges that must be addressed
for semantic similarity systems to analyze speech transcripts effectively.
Furthermore, the challenge of applying semantic similarity to transcripts is fur-
ther compounded by the need for high-quality transcripts, to begin with. The most
common method of generating transcripts is Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)
or Speech to Text systems. However, these systems can introduce new errors that
can significantly impact the results of semantic similarity measures.
For exam-
ple, a single character difference in a transcript, such as "Please submit your work
promptly, or you will be fined." and "Please submit your work promptly or you
will be fine.", can result in entirely different semantic meanings. This highlights
the need for careful consideration of the errors introduced by ASR systems when
applying semantic similarity to speech transcripts.

It is essential to acknowledge the limitations and potential errors introduced by
ASR systems and address them to obtain accurate results when applying semantic
similarity to speech transcripts. This research aims to explore methods for mitigat-
ing the impact of ASR errors and to develop techniques for accurately measuring
semantic similarity in the presence of noisy transcripts generated by ASR systems.

9
Figure 1.2: A figure showing the yearly usages of the words "semantic similarity"
on Google Scholar using [2]
We say that a machine learning model is robust if it fares well in the face of
errors and noisy data as in [95]. A robust system would, in our case, receive good
results in both the gold data set and data with noise and errors. Note that this
differs from the definition used in adversarial attack domains [95]. Robustness is
crucial for safety-critical use cases such as emergency services: If a model does not
perform well given noise, the model can not be used reliably.
1.2
Use case: Emergency systems
The current research project focuses on applying semantic similarity to the domain
of emergency calls, particularly 911 calls. The unique nature of emergency calls
and the stress that individuals experience during these calls can result in speech
patterns distinct from everyday speech.
Studies have shown that people tend to speak with a lower cognitive complexity
when under stress, resulting in speech that is difficult to understand even for human
readers [93]. This presents a significant challenge when applying semantic similar-
ity to emergency call transcripts. The transcripts of emergency calls are typically
generated by data annotators, who transcribe the speech verbatim. However, due
to the nature of speech during emergency calls, the resulting transcripts can often
be complex for human listeners to comprehend because of incorrect grammar. If
human listeners have difficulty understanding the transcripts, it can be assumed
that teaching a semantic similarity system to understand the text will be even more
challenging.
This thesis addresses the challenges of applying semantic similarity to call tran-
scripts. By doing so, we hope to advance the semantic similarity field and provide

practical solutions for improving the analysis of emergency call transcripts.
One potential application of semantic similarity could be in the field of quality
assurance. Quality managers could leverage the technology to perform semantic
searches to identify specific scenarios or events within audio recordings. For exam-
ple, they could search for calls where a fire has occurred, or the speaker is inebriated.
Using semantic similarity in such a scenario would be beneficial due to the limita-
tions of traditional search methods. As mentioned, ASR systems are prone to errors,
and how people speak can be highly variable and unpredictable. As a result, it can
be difficult or even impossible to anticipate every possible phrasing of a given sen-
timent. In these cases, the ability to search based on semantic similarity would
significantly improve the efficiency and accuracy of the search process. This helps
QA managers search, e.g., "calls about drunk people," without having to guess all
possible phrasings verbatim.

Corti
Corti, which this thesis is written in collaboration with, is a Danish healthcare
scale-up that uses Artificial Intelligence to help medical professionals have better
conversations. One way of doing this is through their product Clear, which analyses
call audio and allows QA managers to search for text across calls.
Corti hopes
to allow for a semantic search for sentences. Given a sentence, they want to find
sentences with similar meanings to help improve QA processes. Corti has employed
a team of "AI trainers" who can aid with data acquisition.
1.3
Research Question
As mentioned above, little to no work has been done on applying semantic similarity
measures to transcripts. Therefore, this thesis seeks to test the following hypothesis:
It is possible to construct a robust system for performing Semantic Textual
Similarity tasks on emergency transcripts

The field of NLP deals with analyzing text and speech. To do this, researchers
have created countless tasks that NLP systems are benchmarked against, including
Named Entity Recognition [77], Dependency Parsing [34], Word Sense Disambigua-
tion [88], Stopword Removal [69], and many others.

2.2.1
The history of NLP
Since emerging in the 1940s, the field has gone through many evolutions from a
mostly rules-based field to deep learning methods. The history can be divided into
5 phases, as inspired by [57]. Below is a brief description of the different periods -
and what they focused on. This broad overview helped in the process of deciding
which STS systems to use for the benchmark.
2.2.1.1
1940s
The first phase of NLP, in the 1940s, was focused on machine translation. In 1954,
the first attempt was made to translate from Russian to English. However, there
were issues with semantic and syntactic processing and linguistic variety. Computers
were primitive at the time, taking around 7 minutes to analyze a sentence. Machine
translation was criticized in the 1966 ALPAC report [56], which stated that it was
useless.
Initially, NLP focused on two separate paradigms: automaton and probabilistic
models. The former used finite-state Markov processes and finite-state machines,
based on formal language theory and parsing algorithms for language, as well as
artificial intelligence. The latter utilized probabilistic algorithms, noisy channels,
and decoding based on a metaphor. In addition, phonetics research led to machine
speech recognizers in 1952.
The symbolic approach to NLP was based on formal language theory, parsing
algorithms for language, and artificial intelligence. On the other hand, the stochastic
approach utilized Bayesian methods for character recognition and likelihood for
each letter. While the symbolic system focused on parsing syntax, the stochastic
approach focused on semantics.
2.2.1.2
1970-1983
During this period, there was a strong emphasis on incorporating world knowledge
and manipulating meaning representation in natural language processing (NLP)
research. Transformational and metamorphosis grammar were popular topics, but
they were ultimately deemed unsuitable for computational purposes.
The field of linguistics was viewed as insufficient for advancing AI, with re-
searchers believing that AI could elevate language study. As a result, efforts were
made to integrate more world knowledge into NLP systems using methods such as
semantic networks and associative lexical networks. Research into semantic primi-
tives was also conducted.
Developing frontends for NLP systems proved challenging, as it was difficult to
comprehend users’ needs, goals, and plans fully.
As NLP progressed, stochastic speech recognition algorithms based on Hidden
Markov Models emerged, as did a logic-based paradigm incorporating functional
==PAGE:20==
2.2 Natural Language Processing
16
grammar and metamorphosis grammar using rule-based approaches. Natural lan-
guage understanding (NLU) evolved to accept natural language commands and
translate them into actions, moving into semantics and discourse models. Predicate
logic, such as LUNAR, unified NLU, and logic.
2.2.1.3
1983-1993
From 1983 to 1993, it became clear that using NLP was more challenging than previ-
ously anticipated. This period is known as the grammatico-logical phase, influenced
by grammatical theories among linguists.
During this phase, there was an increased focus on computational grammar
theory and knowledge representation to understand the user’s beliefs and intentions.
Predicate calculus-style logic was heavily utilized. With growing confidence, there
was an increased emphasis on language generation, particularly dialogue.
Finite-state models were used to model syntax, while probabilistic models were
employed for probabilistic speech recognition, transitioning from speech to part-of-
speech tagging, parsing, ambiguities, and semantics.
2.2.1.4
1994-1999
From 1994 to 1999, statistical language processing focused on parsing based on
statistics and probabilities. Practical tasks were finally making progress, and the
increasing use of data on the internet contributed to this advancement. Addition-
ally, the US federal government established several evaluations to benchmark the
effectiveness of different NLP systems.
Probabilistic and data-driven models became standard during this time, and
parsing, part-of-speech tagging, and other tasks began incorporating probabilities
and methodologies borrowed from speech recognition and information retrieval. The
increasing speed of computers made NLP more usable, and as the internet continued
to grow, the need for NLP also increased.
2.2.1.5
2000-2007
During this phase, the Linguistic Data Consortium was crucial in making large
amounts of data available, including annotated collections with syntactic, prag-
matic, and semantic annotations. This allowed for developing supervised machine
learning techniques and improved parsing, information extraction, word sense dis-
ambiguation, and question answering.
Standard practices included support vector machines, multinomial logistic re-
gression, and graphical Bayesian models. High-performance computers facilitated
better deployment and training of NLP systems.
There was also increased attention given to unsupervised and statistical ap-
proaches used for machine translation, topic modeling, and other applications.
2.2.1.6
2007-2018
In the years following 2007, there have been several significant advancements in the
field of NLP. One major development has been introducing neural language mod-
els, which use feed-forward neural networks and lookup tables to process language.
Another important innovation has been multi-task learning, which involves using
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Natural Language Processing
17
convolutional models to perform tasks such as part-of-speech tagging and named
entity recognition. The concept of word embeddings has also gained prominence,
where words are represented as vectors based on their co-occurrence in texts.
Neural networks have become increasingly prevalent in NLP, with convolutional
neural networks used for tasks like sentence classification, sentiment analysis, text
classification, text summarization, and machine translation. The sequence-to-sequence
model, which uses encoder and decoder networks to map between sequences and
vectors, has also been developed.
Attention mechanisms have been introduced to help networks learn what to pay
attention to, leading to the creation of transformer models. Finally, pre-trained lan-
guage models such as BERT (Bi-directional Encoder Representations from Trans-
formers) have been developed using unlabeled text from sources such as BookCorpus
and English Wikipedia.
2.2.1.7
2018-now: Transformers
Since 2018, a large focus has been on transformers. Most state-of-the-art perfor-
mance has been obtained using transformers after the emergence of BERT and
BERT-related models. Transformers have a distinct advantage that they require
little effort to finetune. This means that researchers and commercial applications
can quickly test them in practice.
As seen above, NLP systems have moved from being largely rule-based and focused
on hand-crafting rules to a more corpus-based and big data-focused workflow. One
of the most impactful findings is the transformer, which will be described in-depth
below:
2.2.2
Transformers
A novel architecture that has gained usage over the past few years is the Transformer
architecture, put forward by [104]. The transformer architecture is built around
two principles: Encoder/decoder and attention. The encoder architecture works by
encoding a given sentence to intermediary data. Afterward, the decoder reads this
data and uses it to predict the final result.
A vital characteristic of the transformer model is the use of attention. Aptly
put by [104] in the paper of the same name, "Attention is all you need".
The
transformer architecture is a simple structure built around multi-head attention
and feed-forward neural networks.
As the transformer is a sequence-based model, there is a need to perform posi-
tional encoding on the input data. This ensures that the model learns to understand
the word order’s impact on the meaning of sentences. Since a sequence is input in
its entirety to the model, it is necessary to mask the data. Otherwise, predicting
the next word would be trivial, as it would be a part of the dataset. Therefore,
masked multi-head attention is used instead, masking a sentence’s "future" words.
The masking is done by assigning a score of −∞to the masked values. After running
softmax, the −∞values will be set to 0 instead - and have no impact on the model’s
predictions.

Key, query, and value
A building block that allows the practical use of attention
is the so-called key, query, and value approach. The key, query value approach is
built upon the analogy of databases. When querying a database, one searches for
specific keys, which are then linked to specific values.
The approach is very similar to the above explanation in that several word
embeddings are used. Instead of generating a singular matrix W , we transform
each word embedding into three different vectors: a query vector q, a key vector
k, and a value vector v. These vectors have different dimensions: q and k have
dimension dk, while v has dimension dv. Each of these vectors is then multiplied by
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Natural Language Processing
21
their corresponding weights matrix Wq, Wk, and Wv. These matrices are learned
during training, allowing us to project the word embeddings into different subspaces.
Then, we use the query vector to compute a similarity score with each key vector,
and normalize these scores by a softmax function to obtain attention weights. These
weights indicate how much each value vector should contribute to the final output.
Finally, we compute a weighted sum of the value vectors to obtain the attention
output. This way, we can train the model to obtain the best weights for both the
projection matrices and the attention weights.

Positional encoding
To derive meaning from the order of words in sentences, it
is crucial to encode the position as well. Otherwise, sentences "I am not mad, I am
happy" and "I am mad, I am not happy" would generate the same output vectors
in a different order - and thus the same output classification. There are several
different ways to embed the position:
1. Position Embeddings: Often done by summing a position vector to the
input vector. This offers a simple solution but leads to a fixed maximum text
length. If we use our model with a sequence longer than the model has been
trained with, the position embedding for those positions will not be trained.
2. Position Encodings: We can define our position encodings through a series
of functions (e.g. sine waves), one per dimension, that follow a predictable
pattern. To encode a word’s position, we can then calculate the value of each
function and sum it with the input vector.
Since a sine wave is following
a predictable pattern, the expectation is that the transformer can learn the
encoding for arbitrarily long sequences.
3. Relative Positions: We can also use relative position representations that
capture how far apart two words are in a sequence.
This way, we do not
need to encode absolute positions but only relative ones. For example, we can
use learned embeddings that depend on the distance between two words (e.g.,
-1 for the previous word, 0 for the current word, 1 for the next word) and
add them to the attention scores. This allows us to handle variable-length
sequences without losing positional information

2.2.2.3
Multi-head attention
Multi-head attention [104] is an expansion of self-attention used to improve the
relations between specific words or phrases in a single self-attention calculation.
Multi-headed self-attention works by performing a linear transformation of the input
sequence into multiple vectors of lower dimensionality. In two-headed self-attention,
two vectors are created. Each vector is then fed through a self-attention with sep-
arate key, query, and value transforms. Finally, we get two sequence vectors that
we concatenate and return. A similar process can be followed to support n-headed
attention.
2.2.2.4
Downsides of transformers
While transformers have shown impressive results, they have trouble handling spe-
cific kinds of input. For instance, BERT is known to ignore negation [29]. Further-
more, some tasks require a very long context window.

While related, the field of NLU is different from Speech Recognition. As described

2.2.3.1
Phonology
Phonology is centered around the systematic arrangement of sound. It is the study
of how sound is used within language. Phonology also includes how the semantic
use of sound can convey meanings within language.
2.2.3.2
Morphology
Morphology deals with the structure of words and the pre-and suffixes of words.
Words are comprised of Morphemes - the smallest unit of meaning. For example,
precancellation is structured around three morphemes: Pre, the root cancella, and
the suffix -tion. The indivisible words that have a meaning by themselves (e.g., car)
are called lexical morphemes. The words used with a lexical morpheme are called
grammatical morphemes (e.g. not, -ed, or -ing). Inflectional morphemes are used to
change grammatical tense etc. Finally, derivational morphemes change the semantic
meaning of a word.
2.2.3.3
Lexical
Lexical is centered around the meaning of individual words (similar to the entries
of a lexicon). Lexical analysis is used e.g. part-of speech tagging, removing of stop
words, stemming, and lemmatization of words. At the lexical level, analysis of words
is performed based on their lexical meaning and part of speech - not based on their
semantic context.
==PAGE:28==
2.2 Natural Language Processing
24
2.2.3.4
Syntactic
At the Syntactic level, words are grouped into phrases - and phrases are grouped to
form clauses. Clauses are then combined into sentences. The grammatical structure
between sentences is analyzed at the syntactic level, which is often done by parsing.
Syntactic also focuses on the order of words within a sentence - as that can drastically
change the meaning.
2.2.3.5
Semantic
At the semantic level, the main objective is to determine the meaning of a sentence.
This is done through the processing of the sentence to understand the possible
meanings - and the context around them.
The semantic level also incorporates
words with multiple meanings.
2.2.3.6
Discourse
The discourse level deals with more than one sentence. Instead, it deals with the
connections between words and sentences to understand the coherence of the entire
text. This can, for instance, be through coreference resolution (figuring out what
entity words like "he" or "she" refer to), which is used in many downstream NLP
tasks.
2.2.3.7
Pragmatics
The pragmatic level focuses on information from outside the document. It deals
with implications and what the listener infers. When different readers can derive
different meanings from a text, pragmatic ambiguity [58] arises. Semantic analysis
focuses on the literal meaning, while pragmatic analysis focuses on the inferred
meaning. Pragmatic analysis helps end-users uncover the intended meaning of an
utterance.
2.2.3.8
Natural Language Inference
Natural Language Inference is a sub-area of Natural Language Understanding. NLI
is focused on making inferences about language, using knowledge about how the
world works to understand things that are not explicitly stated. An example from
2000 is: "Jack needed money, so he went and shook his piggy bank. It made no
sound, and Jack was sad". In this example, we need to know that a piggy bank
usually has money in it - and that money (specifically coins) makes a sound when
shaken inside of a piggy bank.
One well-known benchmark is the Winograd Schema Challenge [62], which in-
cludes a question, "the trophy did not fit within the suitcase as it was too big.
What was too big?" In this case, the system would have to predict whether the
trophy or the suitcase was too big an object. This can only happen if the system
has embedded ways of knowing that smaller objects fit within larger ones.
In other words, the model needs to embed knowledge about the world. According
to [98], the knowledge can be split into one of three categories:
• Linguistic knowledge: Knowledge about what words mean and how words
relate to one another
==PAGE:29==
2.2 Natural Language Processing
25
• Common knowledge: Explicit information often clearly stated, e.g., "cat is
a type of animal". Common knowledge differs from field to field, and what
might be seen as standard in one field is uncommon in another
• Commonsense knowledge: Information rarely specified explicitly but most
humans would understand intuitively. To take an example from [37]: If you
see a 7-foot-tall man holding a 1-foot-tall baby and are told they are father and
son, you do not need to ask which is which. Commonsense can be seen in two
areas: Commonsense physics and commonsense psychology.
Commonsense
physics is centered around understanding that something small can fit into
something bigger - or that things that are dropped fall down. Commonsense
psychology is about analyzing people’s emotions and understanding why they
perform specific actions.
Several datasets tackle NLI from different directions, including reference resolu-
tion, question answering, and textual entailment. Reference resolution is, similar to
the example above, determining if the trophy or the suitcase is too small. Question
answering entails answering questions about a given text or commonsense questions.
Finally, textual entailment is focused on determining, based on a text, whether a
specific hypothesis is true or not. The last category is plausible inference that is
focused on predicting the most likely following action, i.e., "I knocked on my neigh-
bor’s door. What is the most likely thing that happened?" with the option "a) he
left his house or b) he invited me in."

Automatic Speech Recognition
Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) is a part of NLP centered around converting
audio into text, colloquially known as "speech-to-text." Converting speech to text
is referred to as "transcription," and the result is a "transcript." ASR models have
been developed since the 1950s and focused on one goal: Converting sounds into a
textual representation of words.
This section will provide a high-level overview of the field of ASR as well as a
brief description of the Whisper model and an older version of Corti’s in-house ASR
model. A high-level overview will help understand the output difference between
the two ASR models during the experiments.

during the audio file. The real-time factor is usually very dependent on the available
working memory of the machine using the ASR. Accuracy is generally calculated as
Word Error Rate (WER), which is given by the following formula:

2.3.2
Datasets
Several large datasets exist to train ASR models, including LibriSpeech [80], a

A greedy decoder takes a list of potential outputs and their

Prefix Beam Search
To improve results, researchers have come up with prefix
beam search [54]. Beam search uses a language model to improve decoding. PBS is
centered around calculating the probability for the following x words at a time. For a
given width w, PBS calculates the top w most likely sentences - and whenever a new
word is identified, it uses a language model constraint. This constraint effectively
forces the model to select candidates with a high language model probability. This
has been shown in [54] to lead to transcripts with fewer errors, dropping WER from
35.8% to 14.1%.

Semantic Text Similarity
Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) measures the semantic similarity between words,
sentences, and documents. It is essential to other NLP tasks, including machine
translation, question answering, text classification, and information retrieval.
When STS emerged, techniques like Bag of Words and Term Frequency - Inverse
Document Frequency (TF-IDF) were used along with vectors. However, words have
different meanings based on the context they are in. While containing the exact
words, sentences like "James and Peter like cars" and "James likes Peter and cars"
have entirely different meanings. There are many different words describing the
same concepts, which are missed by this structure, e.g., "Mary is allergic to dairy"
and "Mary is lactose intolerant." It became clear that only using lexical features,
not semantic or syntactic properties, was not advised.
In 1998, [67] described semantic similarity formally using the following formula:

SentEval
A benchmarking tool that has seen wide adoption is the SentEval benchmark [31].
This Benchmark is based on creating increasingly more sentence and word embed-

2.4.2
Knowledge-based methods
Knowledge-based methods are usually built around a lexical understanding of lan-
guage. It uses underlying knowledge sources like lexical databases, dictionaries, etc.
Among the most well-known lexical databases is WordNet [76], BabelNet [79], and
Wiktionary [7].
Knowledge-based semantic similarity methods are easily computable if a well-
structured knowledge graph is available, as most of it is based on graph traversal.
==PAGE:38==
2.4 Semantic Text Similarity
34
However, knowledge-based methods struggle with low-resource languages, as well-
structured knowledge graphs are seldom present.
Furthermore, knowledge-based
methods often have trouble with synonyms, idioms, and phrases, as those can be
ambiguous.
2.4.2.1
Edge counting methods
One can count the edges between two words within the lexical database to compare
the semantic similarity of two words. This will estimate how similar the words are
[85].
However, the edge counting method does not always consider that not all
edges are created equally - some words with a particular meaning have many edges.
Therefore, the weighted path measure was introduced, giving words deeper within
the topology a lower weight [107].
2.4.2.2
Feature-based methods
Feature-based methods [100] use the meaning of words within a dictionary. The
semantic similarity between two words can then be calculated as the overlap between
the words’ meaning and related words. For instance, Wikipedia and WordNet have
been used to calculate semantic similarity. The main limitation of feature-based
methods is that ontologies often don’t incorporate semantic features, impairing their
usability.
2.4.2.3
Combined knowledge-based methods
Several semantic similarity measures are built upon a combination of different meth-
ods. In a paper by [45], three methods are used: 1) Adding the depth between terms
as a weight, 2) Adding the depth of the LCS as a weight, and 3) Adding the IC
value as a weight. Now, the shortest path between the two terms is calculated.
2.4.3
Corpus-based methods
Corpus-based similarity methods are built upon the principle that "similar words
occur together frequently", also known as the distributional hypothesis [50]. The
actual meanings of words (e.g., from dictionaries) are not considered. Several dis-
tance measures have arisen based on these corpora and the related embeddings,
including Euclidian distance and cosine similarity.
Corpus-based systems are independent of language and domain and built upon
various statistical methods. This makes scraping and creating a corpus a relatively
simple task that can be adapted across languages and domains. However, the ac-
tual meaning of words is not captured - only the prevalence, placement, and uses.
Furthermore, building a sufficiently large and diverse corpus can often be complex,
resource-intensive, and time-consuming - and no best practice for creating corpora
has been created so far.
2.4.3.1
Word embeddings
Word embeddings are vector representations of words. Word embeddings can be
calculated in several ways, using neural networks, word co-occurrence matrices, or
context-based representations. Below is an overview of the most widely used pre-
trained word embeddings:

Word2Vec
Word2Vec is a neural network training based on Google News data
[74]. It is trained to create distributed vectors based on a given corpus. Two model
versions exist: Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) and Skip-gram. For CBOW, the
model is trained to predict a word using the context words around it. Skip-gram,
on the other hand, is used to predict the context words based on a word. word2vec
has been extended in many different ways, including sentence2vec and doc2vec .
GloVe
Global vectors for word representation (GLoVe)[82] is an embedding cre-
ated by Stanford University. The embedding is based on a co-occurrence matrix
and trained on Wikipedia dumps. It is created based on the assumption that words
that co-occur have a similar meaning. The loss function minimizes the least-squares
difference between the co-occurrence values within the window and the global co-
occurrence values. This results in a vector representation where dissimilar words
are farther away.
FastText
FastText [23] is a word embedding based on skip-gram models. It uses
a character-level embedding and therefore accounts for the morphological structure
of the words. This model is also robust in handling new words, as they can be
assigned vectors based on their morphemes or characters.

2.4.3.2
Sentence Embeddings
Sentence embeddings are a popular method for representing the semantic meaning
of a sentence as a dense vector. Various methods exist for creating sentence embed-
dings, including unsupervised and supervised techniques. Unsupervised methods
include algorithms such as doc2vec and GloVe, which learn word embeddings from
large corpora of text and then aggregate those word embeddings to obtain sentence
embeddings. Supervised methods, on the other hand, typically use a neural network
to learn sentence embeddings from labeled training data directly.
InferSent
InferSent is a supervised sentence embedding method proposed by [32].
It is built around the concept of transfer learning - in the paper, they train a model to
generate sentence embeddings for several tasks and find that embeddings generated
by the NLI task result in good results. Therefore, the authors suggest training on
the Stanford Natural Language Inference dataset. The model uses a bi-directional
Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) network with max-pooling to learn sentence
representations. The InferSent model has been published on GitHub in two versions:
GloVe word vectors, as in the original paper, and FastText vectors.
Universal Sentence Encoder
The Universal Sentence Encoder (USE) is an un-
supervised sentence embedding method[27]. The authors show a flexible architecture
that can be used to make based on the use case: One using Deep Averaging Net-
work (DAN) with lower computational cost and lower accuracy - and one using the
Transformer models with high accuracy. The model was widely used for transfer
tasks, performing well on semantic similarity and other NLP tasks with little to no
training data needed.
GloVe
As mentioned above, GloVe [82] embeddings help transform words into a
dense vector. To obtain sentence embeddings, one common approach is to average
the GloVe embeddings of the words in a sentence. While this method is computa-
tionally efficient, it may not capture complex semantic relationships between words
in a sentence as effectively as other methods.
Sentence-BERT

Afterward, dimensionality reduction is performed using Singular

to reduce the complexity of the model.

Hyperspace Analogue to Language (HAL)
HAL consists of a word x word matrix with matrix values indicating the associa-
tion strength values [70]. The values are calculated by sliding a window over the
underlying corpus and giving a higher score to words closer to each other.
2.4.3.5
Explicit Semantic Analysis (ESA)
ESA is built upon the text within Wikipedia [44]. First, a vector is created using
all the words in a given description. Afterward, a reverse index is created from each
word to other Wikipedia entries. The association strength is then calculated using
TF-IDF.
2.4.3.6
Word Alignment
Word-Alignment models work by calculating the similarity of sentences based on
their alignment across a corpus [44]. Word-alignment methods reached state-of-the-
art results in the 2015 Semeval task [13].
2.4.3.7
Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)
LDA is a way of using vectors to represent topics rather than representing every
word within the document [99]. It is often used within topic modeling as a way of
dimensionality reduction, as the number of topics is often substantially smaller than
the size of any given text.
2.4.3.8
Normalized Google Distance (NGD)
Normalized Google Distance is built around the assumption that similar words occur
more frequently within web pages [97]. It is a way of calculating semantic relatedness
rather than semantic similarity, as words with opposite meanings might appear
together frequently.
2.4.3.9
Dependency based models
The dependency based model also uses the distributional hypothesis [9]. However,
instead of focusing on the linear context, e.g. the words on either side of a given
word, the method focuses on syntactic contexts. This means that each word is em-
bedded based on the syntactic relationship that it participates in. E.g. "pizza",
"burger" serves the same purpose in most sentences and would therefore be embed-
ded near one another. Dependency-based models have been shown to excel when
ranking similar words over related words [63].

BERT
BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) [38] is a neural
network model that adopts a transformer architecture and is pre-trained on large
text corpora. Unlike previous neural language models such as GPT, BERT is bidi-
rectional, meaning it has complete visibility into the text in both directions, allowing
it to build a rich language understanding. The models are usually initialized with
the same pre-trained parameters when using BERT. To apply it to, e.g., STS, fine-
tuning takes place to change all the model weights.
BERT is first pre-trained on unlabeled data using two unsupervised prediction
tasks: Masked Language Modeling (MLM) and Next Sentence Prediction (NSP).
In MLM, BERT hides some percentage of input tokens and learns to predict them.
In NSP, BERT receives pairs of sentences and learns to predict whether the second
sentence is the next one that follows the corpus’s first sentence. After pre-training,
BERT’s parameters are fine-tuned on a specific downstream task, such as question
answering or textual similarity. Instead of always masking the token, the token is
left unchanged 10% of the time and changed to a random token 10% of the time.
For the remaining 80%, the token is changed to a masking value.
BERT is trained based on BookCorpus (800M words) and English Wikipedia
(2500 M words) It gains good results on both tokens- and sentence-level tasks Before
BERT, many neural and non-neural word representations existed. Other models,
such as ELMO [83], use the left-to-right and right-to-left representations, which are
concatenated. However, this is not as high-performing as getting a bidirectional
representation. The model size is 110 million for BERTbase and 330 million for
BERTLARGE

BERTology: How BERT works
Much research has been conducted to under-
stand how BERT represents information - and what kind of information is embedded
within it. This study is called, humorously, BERTology [91]. Based on these studies,
it seems clear that BERT can represent sentences as a syntactic tree. It is unclear
where strictly within the model this is located. An issue within BERT is the fact
that it does not understand negation. BERT also encodes semantic information,
knowing semantics, entities, and other roles. As has been identified within GPT
models, BERT also struggles with numbers and simple calculus. BERT also has an
incomplete view of the world, not understanding features of objects that are implied
but not explicitly mentioned. While it "knows" that people can walk into houses
and houses are big, it cannot reliably reason that houses are bigger than people.
Specific heads within self-attention seem to focus on different aspects of language
understanding. Some argue that self-attention is the key to interpretability, as we
can glean some information from what words trigger attention to other words.
2.4.4.2
RoBERTa
RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT pre-training Approach)[68] was created to
investigate whether BERT training could be improved. A paper by Facebook de-
scribed how changes could be made to the pre-training and finetuning of BERT.
The changes result in a state-of-the-art implementation, RoBERTa. Changes: 1)
More training, 2) Removed next-sentence, 3) Dynamic masking, not static masking,
and 4) Longer sequences and
Word sub-pairs instead of entire words - different embedding to make it more
generalizable.
More training, for longer, over more data
First and foremost, RoBERTa
realized that BERT was severely undertrained. BERT was trained on 16 GB of
data, while RoBERTa was trained on over 160GB of data. On top of BookCorpus
and the English Wikipedia, RoBERTa was also trained on CC-News (76GB), which
is a collection of 63M English news articles, OpenWebText (38GB), a dataset used to
train OpenAI GPT and Stories (31GB). Furthermore, the authors trained for up to
5x as steps as with BERT. This additional training effort, while expensive, resulted
in SOTA results at the time. On the SQuAD benchmark, RoBERTa scored 87.3
while only being trained on BookCorpus + Wiki. When trained on the additional
data, the result was 89.4 - a remarkable change. Finally, they also used bigger batch
sizes to improve the training further.

- and not removing the NSP loss.

Dynamic Masking
BERT performed masking using a static mask during pre-
training. The training data was duplicated ten times to avoid repeating the same
mask multiple times to ensure the masks were not the same across all epochs.
Instead, RoBERTa uses dynamic masking. While this has little effect on the result,
it simplifies the training process for large data sets, as the data does not have to
be generated in advance. Instead, the masks are applied every time the data is fed
into the model.
Word embeddings
In BERT, WordPiece embeddings with 30K subword units
in the vocabulary is used. Instead, RoBERTa uses Byte-Pair encoding, which has
a vocabulary of 50K subword units.
While this requires adding 20M additional
parameters, it makes the system much more robust toward unknown words and
sequences.
2.4.4.3
Sentence BERT
Another evolution of BERT is Sentence BERT [89] (also referred to as SBERT for
brevity). Many BERT use cases include calculating a score based on two sequences.
While this is entirely possible using BERT, the process would take a very long time
for n sentences, as each sentence pair would have to be inserted into BERT as a
single sequence with a [SEP] token between. Based on this input, a target value is
predicted. This would result in approx n2 sentence pairs. For n = 6,000, the most
similar pair can be found using 50 million inference computations.
A possible way of speeding up this calculation would be if extracting vector
embeddings from BERT was possible. Unfortunately, the embeddings of the fixed-
size sentence embedding extracted from the last layer of the BERT model have been
found to have poor results - even worse than averaging GloVe embeddings.
Instead, the paper finetunes BERT on the NLI task to help improve the quality of
embeddings. This was inspired by the InferSent paper, which also used the NLI task
for finetuning. By finetuning the NLI task, the model is believed to include deeper
information about how language should be modeled. These SBERT embeddings
prove to be very useful - and to improve the inference speed drastically for more
extensive computations - from 50 hours to a few milliseconds on the Quora dataset.
Since embeddings cannot be directly extracted from BERT, researchers input a
single sentence at a time and read from a fixed-size output layer. This output layer
is then used as the sentence embedding. There are three separate ways to extract
the sentence embeddings: 1) Using the embedding of the [CLS] token, 2) Mean
of all output layers, or 3) Max over time of all output. For both NLI and STSb
benchmarks, the mean embedding strategy lead to state-of-the-art results.

Siamese Network
BERT uses a Siamese network. In practice, running a Siamese
network is equivalent to running two identical networks - or using only one network,
but sequentially inputting first s1, saving the embedding, and then inputting s2
and saving the embedding. Afterward, if using, i.e., the classification objective, the
value (s1,s2,∣s1 −s2∣) would be passed through a softmax classifier and used for
optimization. When an inference, the two embeddings would be passed through a
cosine-similarity measure - and the result would be a similarity score.
2.4.4.4
XLNet
While BERT has performed well on many benchmarks, it has some inherent issues
based on its architecture and pre-training objectives. As mentioned, BERT func-
tions use a bidirectional context, reading text on both sides of the masked tokens.
Autoregressive models have historically been used on either the forward context or
the backward context - in some cases, both contexts concatenated with one another.
XLNet sets out to solve this issue - as well as some other downsides with BERT:
One of the main issues is that the masking approach creates a pre-train-finetune
discrepancy. After all, we don’t expect the text actually to include [MASK] tokens.
Furthermore, BERT assumes that each token is independent, giving rise to issues
when two or more tokens are masked in a single sentence. In the sentence "A housing
crisis turns into a banking crisis," where "banking" and "crisis" are masked, the
model would predict those two independently. This creates issues. Let us say that
the sentence is "the dog likes to

are (catch, treats). In this case, BERT can output "the dog likes to play treats" or
"the dog likes to eat catch" instead of a sentence that makes sense: "The dog likes
to eat treats."

GloVe vectors. Each word is embedded. The vectors are averaged to a single vector
to obtain the sentence embeddings. Finally, the cosine similarity of the two vectors
is calculated.
This results in a cosine similarity where the semantic meaning of
words is also included. The formula for cosine similarity can be seen here:

Choosing libraries
Among the more essential optimizations were changing the
library used to calculate edit distance and Jaro-Winkler distance. For edit distance,
the cudf python package [101] was found to be three orders of magnitude faster.
Similarly, using the cdist command from rapidfuzz increased the speed by three
orders of magnitude.
Chunking
The second change made to optimize the process was to use chunking.
Trying to load all sentences into memory and calculate the similarities between
them took up much more memory. Instead, we used chunking, creating groups of
10,000 consecutive sentences. Distances between the group of 10,000 sentences and
another group of 10,000 sentences were calculated. We can optimize the process by
only calculating each sentence pair once. After all, the distance between sentences
s1 and s2 is the same as between s2 and s1 for Levehnstein and cosine similarity.
However, Jaro-Winkler does not have this property and we cannot guarantee that
simw(s1,s2) = simw(s1,s2) because of the way that transpositions are calculated.
However, we choose to disregard this fact for computational reasons.
This optimization allowed for parallelization, as multiple threads could work on
separate parts of the data without memory issues. An illustration can be seen in
Figure 3.3. This allowed for faster calculations - especially of cosine similarity, as a
matrix of cosine similarities could be calculated for each chunk.

Annotation quality
When determining the dataset size, there were three main concerns: Budget, time-

To avoid having a large discrepancy between how other annotators might have
scored sentences, it was chosen to have 500 sentences be double-annotated while the

there are no large discrepancies between annotators. Once the 500 sentences had
been annotated, an analysis was conducted to determine the inter-annotator agree-
ment. Once the investigation had been concluded, the remaining 5,500 sentences
were annotated.
The Krippendorff’s α was used for the inter-annotator agreement, as it includes
a distance metric. After all, some errors are more severe than others (rating the
same sentence as a 0 and a 5 is worse than rating it as a 5 and a 4). The final
Krippendorff’s α was calculated as 0.928, which is far above the cutoff of 0.80
recommended by [60]. The correlation between different annotations can be seen

3.2.1
Bootstrap
To ensure that we correctly capture the performance of the models, we utilize boot-
strap sampling during the experiments [102]. Bootstrap sampling is centered around
repeatedly sampling the dataset with replacement. After doing this several times,
a sampling distribution emerges. In our case, we performed 1,000 calculations for
the models that were not finetuned and 10 for those that were finetuned. For a
dataset size n, we sample a total of n sentence pairs with replacement. This leads
to two data sets: The data that was sampled and the out-of-bag samples that were
present within the dataset but weren’t sampled. In expectation, each bootstrap
sample samples 63% of the examples, leaving out 37% [26]. These 37% are then
used for evaluation.
For models requiring finetuning, we initialized the model and used the sampled
data to fine-tune it by splitting it 80-20 into a test-validate set.
Afterward, we
validated the data against the out-of-bag distribution. For models used out-of-the-
box, we utilized only the out-of-bag distribution to ensure that the results were
comparable to the finetuned data.

like mentioned in chapter 1. The hypothesis was that emotionally charged calls,

to ASR models not being sufficiently trained on high-stress speech.
When analyzing the source, 2,679 calls with emotional speech were identified.
In our case, we define emotional speech as speech where one of the multiple criteria
is present: 1) annotated crying/screaming/yelling by the transcribers, 2) use of

Discussion
As discussed in section 4.1 have made a few noteworthy findings based on the three
experiments and the analysis of the results. Amongst those are that XLNet and
fine-tuned Sentence-BERT drastically outperforms the remaining models and that
models that include subword tokens (FastText) seem to outperform models that
don’t (i.e., GloVe). Furthermore, the scaling method was analyzed, showing that
==PAGE:78==
4.3 Discussion
74
naive scaling leads to a better Pearson correlation.
4.3.1
Findings
To conclude, experiment 1 aimed to predict similarities using golden transcripts.
XLNet and fine-tuned Sentence BERT performed the best within the Context cat-
egory, while GloVe Mean and FastText underperformed without context. Among the
Simple models, edit distance outperformed all models, while Jaro-Winkler scored
lower. In Experiment 2 with Corti ASR transcripts, XLNet and fine-tuned Sentence
BERT achieved the highest scores within the Context category. FastText outper-
formed mean and max GloVe in the no-context category, and edit distance outper-
formed Jaro-Winkler in the Simple category. Experiment 3 focused on predicting
similarities using Whisper ASR transcripts. XLNet outperformed other models in
the Context category, while FastText marginally outperformed GloVe in the no-
context category. Similar to previous experiments, edit distance scored highly in
the Simple category, while Jaro-Winkler scored much lower.
Overall, XLNet and fine-tuned Sentence BERT consistently performed well in
all three experiments within the Context category, demonstrating their strength in
capturing semantic similarities and fitting with general SOTA. FastText performed
better than GloVe in some categories, and edit distance consistently outperformed
Jaro-Winkler as a Simple metric. These results suggest that the data and ASR
errors greatly impact the results. After seeing the strong performance of XLNet, the
code was checked to ensure no data contamination. A diligent code review revealed
no data contamination as no sentence pair was used for training, validating, and
testing. Interestingly, ranking the best Pearson correlations, as seen in Table 4.1,
stays relatively constant with a maximum change in ranks of 3. This indicates that
most STS models are fairly robust in the face of transcription errors.
The fact that the STS models function better on Corti compared to Whisper ,
even in spite of the difference in word error rate (40% vs. 50%) is surprising, as we
would expect a lower WER to lead to better results. This finding also translates
to the overlapping and emotional speech categories, where models tested on the
Corti dataset outperform the models tested on the Whisper dataset. This might
be because ASR errors are not created equal - some errors distort the underlying
meaning more than others.
A theory as to why FastText seems to outperform GloVe is the fact that it
can handle subword embeddings. This means that even if the word is incorrectly
transcribed, some of the meaning-carrying subtokens might have been correctly
transcribed. Based on these results, the research question can be confirmed: Both
the fine-tuned Sentence-BERT and XLNet models are robust models, achieving the
lowest score degradation across models and consistently outperforming others. This
thesis highlights the importance of fine-tuning models on the data.
4.3.2
Limitations
In this section, we will discuss the limitations of our study, which impact the inter-
pretation and generalizability of the findings. First, we will address the potential
==PAGE:79==
4.3 Discussion
75
improvements that can be made to the dataset, including refining the surface simi-
larity metric, increasing sentence length, and expanding the dataset. Next, we will
explore alternative evaluation methods to provide a more robust assessment of the
models. Finally, we will consider the benefits of broadening the range of models
used and diversifying the types of models to enhance the overall results.
4.3.2.1
Dataset
One limitation of the dataset is that the underlying distribution of the surface
similarity metrics is skewed and that some of the three metrics were inadequate for
the use case, decreasing the dataset’s quality.
First, we can examine how the metrics’ composition impacts the dataset. As
mentioned in subsection 3.1.2, the surface similarity metric was composed of three
separate metrics that each had a different distribution similar to [106]. Because
Ratcliff/Obershelp was too slow to be feasible given the time frame of this thesis,
Jaro-Winkler similarity was used instead. The composition of the three separate
metrics resulted in a surface similarity score with three different distributions. A
way to mitigate this issue would be to use a weighted mean to get the final surface
similarity metric which would have contributed to a score that was less dominated
by the GloVe score. Furthermore, the limitation can be addressed using the Jaro
similarity instead of the Jaro-Winkler similarity, as the extra weight on the prefix
has little significance in our context and adds noise to the metric. Gro-Winkleriven a
more extended timeframe or an optimized way of calculating the Ratcliff/Obershelp
similarity, the Jaro-Winkler similarity could be swapped for the Ratcliff/Obershelp
similarity, which could have improved the surface similarity metric. However, the
severity of this limitation is minor, as the final dataset was significantly stratified.
Secondly, the sampling based on the surface similarity score could have been
altered to improve the dataset’s stratification. The underlying distribution could
have been better understood by annotating a subset of sentence pairs using a figure
similar to Figure 3.7. Sampling to ensure an approximately uniform distribution
might have improved the dataset quality.
Another limitation of the dataset is that it is primarily single-annotated, which
carries the risk that annotator errors can skew model performance.
By double-
or triple-annotating the data, the dataset could be improved. Multiple annotators
would assist in 1) giving an average score based on multiple annotations and 2)
removing sentence pairs that were difficult to annotate well. However, as mentioned
in subsection 3.1.4, this was deemed infeasible due to time and budget constraints.
Furthermore, the paper [39] argues that given a set budget, the best use of money
is to have a larger, single-annotated dataset rather than a smaller, triple-annotated
one.
A third limitation of the dataset is the short average sentence length, which
limits its generalizability to longer sentences. In this thesis, most sentences within
the dataset were short, consisting of 5-7 words due to the way that sentences were
split. To make the similarity metric easier to approximate, the length of sentences
could have been increased by choosing another splitting method. This would help
==PAGE:80==
4.3 Discussion
76
the dataset align with the one described by [106], which has an average sentence
length of 51 words.
Finally, the method of re-transcribing the sentences using Corti’s ASR and Whis-
per could have benefitted from an easier process. The process of manually aug-
menting the outputs is very error-prone and time-intensive. This was a barrier to
transcribing it using multiple other ASR models with different capabilities. If more
time was available - or another method was found, more ASR models could have
been used, helping highlight their positives and negatives.
4.3.2.2
Evaluation method
A limitation of the evaluation method is that only the Pearson correlation was used
to benchmark model performance. Papers like [90] have advocated using additional
metrics such as the harmonic mean of F1 scores of high- and low-scoring sentence
pairs. Enriching the results with multiple metrics would have improved upon the
results, ensuring that the right model was chosen as the best. Furthermore, other
ways of sampling from the dataset, i.e., stratified bootstrap, might also impact the
results. Both of these choices were made due to limited time.
4.3.2.3
Models
Finally, the findings of this report could have been improved by utilizing even more
models. Examples of models that could have been used are T5, Mnet-Sim, ALBERT,
and ensemble models. Benchmarking the datasets against those models would have
given a deeper insight into which models perform best delivered a changing dataset.
Furthermore, a more diverse set of models could have improved the findings. In the
current thesis, most models were sentence embeddings, simple metrics, or XLNet
with a classification model. This might tip the scales in favor of XLNet, making
it seem better than it is. A way to improve generalizability would be to use more
classification models. Using other models with many parameters would make the
results even more useful.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) is a growing field centered around determining
the similarity between two texts. While many papers have built increasingly perfor-
mant models and shared numerous papers sharing datasets, no reports have been
focused on the unique challenges of transcribed text. Especially within emergency
services, the ability to use semantic textual similarity e.g. semantic search, is im-
portant, as it is a crucial way to improve quality and decrease time spent on quality
assurance. This thesis seeks to investigate the errors that arise when emergency
calls are transcribed - and to find a model to mitigate them. Therefore, the fol-
lowing research question was posed: "It is possible to construct a robust system for
performing Semantic Textual Similarity tasks on emergency transcripts".
Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Semantic Textual Similarity fields were
analysed to answer this research question. Based on these analyses, diverse mod-
els were chosen for the experiments.
The models were Sentence BERT, GloVe,
InferSent, XLNet and Universal Sentence Embedding. Afterwards, a dataset was
assembled with the help of the in-house data annotation team from Corti, which
was the thesis partner. The dataset was based on human-made Seattle Fire Depart-
ment transcripts and split into sentences. To avoid getting a dataset full of sentence
pairs with a score of 0, a surface similarity metric was introduced, consisting of edit
distance, Jaro-Winkler similarity, and GloVe cosine similarity. The surface simi-
larity metric thoughtfully assisted in sampling sentences. A team of 2 annotators
transcribed the dataset. The first 500 sentence pairs were double-transcribed, while
the remaining 5,500 were single-transcribed. The analysis of the double-transcribed
sentences indicated a high inter-annotator agreement. This led to the creation of
the Gold dataset. Afterward, the audio files used to generate the human-made tran-
scripts were re-transcribed using the two ASR models: OpenAI’s Whisper model
and Corti’s in-house ASR model. The new sentences were assembled into two new
datasets, Corti and Whisper.
Based on these three datasets, three experiments were designed. The three exper-
iments analysed the performance of a set of models on each of the three datasets to
allow for a comparison of the results. The results showed that XLNet and Sentence
BERT fine-tuned performed the best across all datasets, indicating its strength in
capturing semantics even when confronted with errors. Another interesting finding
is that the ranking between models stayed relatively constant across all datasets.
Furthermore, sentence embeddings that use subword information, such as Fast-
Text, outperformed word-based embeddings on noisy data. Simple metrics like edit
distance were surprisingly strong across all datasets, consistently achieving high
performance.

5.1 Future work
78
Based on these results, we can answer the Research Question in the affirmative
after showing that when fine-tuned properly, XLNet and Sentence BERT outperform
other models for all datasets - and has the smallest score degradation. Therefore,
they can be described as robust systems.
While this thesis provides valuable insights, one must know its limitations.
Among the most important, the dataset could be refined using longer sentences,
multiple annotators, and a weighted surface similarity metric.
Employing addi-
tional models, like T5, Mnet-Sim, ALBERT, ensemble models, and other metrics
would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
Augmenting the dataset with
more ASR systems, like Google’s Speech API and AWS Transcribe, could highlight
their relative strengths.
In summary, this work demonstrates the promise of finetuned transformer mod-
els like XLNet and Sentence Bert, as well as subword-based models like FastText
for handling noisy semantic similarity tasks in sensitive domains like emergency
response. With continued progress in model capabilities, dataset, and evaluation
refinement, these techniques show potential to help improve emergency call handling
and response and ultimately save more lives.
5.1
Future work
There are several promising directions for future work based on this thesis. First, a
larger dataset of annotated sentence pairs from emergency calls might significantly
boost model performance, as more data would give models a greater opportunity to
learn nuances. An automated or improved method to generate new annotated pairs
may enable building a dataset scaled by orders of magnitude.
Second, an exciting direction is following [105] and augmenting the pretraining
data to learn from the errors that can arise from ASR transcriptions. This would
entail employing text-to-speech followed by transcribing the speech using an ASR
model, resulting in errors similar to those in the final dataset.
Another way of
generating multiple datasets would be to add synthetic noise to the data.
Finally, as mentioned during the discussion, several limitations limit the gener-
alizability of the thesis. Future work could also seek to rectify them. For instance,
the dataset could be improved using longer sentences, multiple annotators, and a
weighted surface similarity metric. This may strengthen results by giving models
more context and ensuring higher-quality annotations. Similarly, employing addi-
tional models, like T5, Mnet-Sim, ensemble models, and metrics, would provide a
more comprehensive assessment. Also, augmenting the dataset with more ASR sys-
tems, like Google’s Speech API and AWS Transcribe, could highlight their relative
strengths. Furthermore, following the recent rise of large language models, analyz-
ing the results of more models would provide insight into their performance on data
that is not contaminated.
The field of STS is a rapidly growing field with a multitude of options for im-
proving results and generalizability.

Appendix A
Project Description
A.1
Project Description
Below is the project background, the project scope and the real-life use case that
the thesis hopes to solve.
A.1.1
Background
Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) is a foundational NLP task of determining the
degree of semantic similarity between two texts.
Most STS systems compute a
similarity score between 0 and 5 where 0 equals no similarity and 5 is complete
equivalence.
A.1.1.1
STS systems
Recently, many new STS systems are being published. Among the notable ones are
[109], using a character-level convolutional network, and [30], using Google results.
On top of these, a number of pre-training methods ([108] and [38]) have emerged.
Few projects (to my knowledge) have tackled the problem of creating STS sys-
tems for noisy data, e.g. data generated by an Automatic Speech Recognition. An
example of a project working on this is [41], which uses ASR data from podcasts.
It could be interesting to do further work on this problem.
A.1.1.2
Evaluating STS
Historically, the method for evaluating the quality of a STS system is the Pearson
Correlation, which is used to evaluate the effectiveness of NLP systems.
However, the Pearson Correlation does not necessarily correlate with the accu-
racy of the STS systems within the intended use. Therefore, authors like [90] and
[31] have proposed a different way of evaluating STS systems. [31] has created a
tool named SentEval while [90] suggests using task-oriented evaluation.
A.1.2
Project Scope
This project will focus on three areas: 1) Data Acquisitions, 2) Implementing a STS
system and 3) Testing it using a number of different evaluation tools.
• Data Acquisition I will acquire data to be used in the implementation of
the STS, through Corti’s AI trainer team
• Implementation I will seek to implement a STS for semantic similarity, using
one of the state-of-the-art systems as a starting point.
• Evaluation I will evaluate the STS on a number of parameters as given in
[90] and [31] (and perhaps other papers)

Real-life use case
Corti is a Danish healthcare scaleup that uses Artificial Intelligence to help medical

The project seeks to answer the following hypothesis:
1. It is possible to construct a robust system for performing STS tasks on noisy
transcripts
The hypothesis will be tested by:
1a) Benchmarking the accuracy of STS tasks on a transcript dataset using a num-

1b) Testing whether a transformation of the transcription dataset with e.g.
a

the performance of other STS systems
1c) Constructing a metric for quality for the STS task on transcripts through
qualitative analysis

Appendix C
Annotation Guidelines
Instructions
Two statements can mean the same thing even if they use very different words and
phrases. Conversely, two statements that are superficially very similar in their word
choice, phrasing and overall composition can have very different meanings.
Your job is to compare two statements and decide the type of relationship that
holds between their underlying meanings or messages (i.e. what they say or refer
to in the world).
To do this task successfully, picture what is being described and contrast exactly
what is conveyed by one statement versus what is being conveyed by the other.
Do the statements refer to the exact same person, action, event, idea, or thing?
Or, are they similar but differ according to either large or small details? Tips:
• Be precise in your assignments and try to avoid overusing any one of the
category labels (e.g. don’t just label most of the pairs as “mostly equivalent”
or “roughly equivalent”).
• Be careful of subtle differences between the pairs that have an important
impact on what is being said or described.
• Ignore grammatical errors and awkward wordings within the statements as
long as they do not obscure what a statement is supposed to convey.
Scores and examples
