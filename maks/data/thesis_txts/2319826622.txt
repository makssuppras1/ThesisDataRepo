Abstract 
 
The concept of the internal boundary layer (IBL) describes the process of the flow adjustment to new 
surface conditions. A step change in surface roughness, temperature, humidity or in the surface flux 
of heat or moisture leads to the development of an IBL.  The IBL’s that tend to most affect the wind 
are those due to large changes in roughness, such as across coastlines.  
The scope of this project is to investigate the development of the internal boundary layer downwind 
of the Høvsøre coastline, at the Danish National Test Station for wind turbines. The test station is 
heavily instrumented with multiple sonic anemometers in different heights and locations. In addition 
to these measurements, data from the IBL-WiSH project were available, where additional 
meteorological masts were installed. In this study, simulation results, using WAsP CFD software will 
be presented and compared with measurements.  
In the case of interest, the roughness change occurs at the coastline, 150 meters downwind, the flow 
encounters the sand dune and afterwards there is an additional roughness change from the sand 
surface to a low vegetation surface. In theoretical studies and wind tunnel simulations, only one type 
of change is examined each time, which is convenient for the understanding of the phenomenon, but 
rarely fits to reality. In this report, the term Internal Boundary Layer will be used to describe the 
whole combined effect occurring after the coastline. The study will focus on both neutral and 
diabatic IBL development. In particular, the different rate of adjustment of flow properties (velocity, 
shear stress and heat flux) to the new boundary conditions, will be investigated.  
Measurement and simulations results, proclaimed a different rate of adjustment for each property, 
thus the definition of distinctive IBLs with respect to each property seems reasonable. In the upwind 
to the sand dune region, IBL development is mainly driven by the orographic effect. Sand dune effect 
is also dominant in the near downwind region, resulting in flow deceleration and turbulence 
increase. Far downwind of the sand dune, the development pattern is similar to an IBL in response to 
a roughness change, but with increased depth. The WAsP CFD simulations results were evaluated in 
the near and far downwind region of the sand dune, but only for the latter can be considered 
adequate.

1. Introduction 
 
The wind is continually affected by the surfaces over which it flows, including the surface roughness. 
When airflow encounters a roughness change, a turbulent internal boundary layer (IBL) develops, 
where the flow adjusts to be in equilibrium with the ‘new’ surface. The development and 
characteristics of the IBL are affected by the terrain morphology and thermodynamic structure of the 
atmosphere; the IBL’s that tend to most affect the wind are those due to large changes in roughness, 
such as across coastlines.  
The definition of the IBL depth is not common among researchers. A neutral IBL, can be defined by 
𝑑𝑢/𝑑𝑧 discontinuities or wind profile kink (Elliott, 1958), (Panofsky & Townsend, 1964), (Bradley, 
1968), (Antonia & Luxton, 1971) . Some researchers define the IBL top by the depth where a property 
of the downwind flow becomes equal to the upwind value. Using this approach (Shir, 1972), defined 
a “velocity” IBL at the depth where 𝑢𝐷= 0.99𝑢𝑈 and a “stress” IBL in the same way. Rao et al. (Rao, 
Wyngaard, & Coté, 1974) extended the definition of Shir by introducing an underlying equilibrium 
layer where the local stress has reached 90 % of adjustment to the new surface. In contradiction to 
the neutral IBL, the diabatic IBL is vague. An unstable IBL top can be identified by 𝑑𝑢/𝑑𝑧 
discontinuity (Raynor, Michael, Brown, & Sethuraman, 1975). Gamo, (Gamo & Yamamoto, 1982) and 
Durand (Durand, Druilhet, & Briere, 1988),found a minimum in turbulent kinetic energy above the 
aforementioned discontinuity. In stable conditions the same discontinuities in potential temperature 
gradient can be identified (Garratt, 1987), (Garratt & Ryan, 1989), among with a minimum in eddy 
diffusivity (Arritt, 1987), (Garratt, 1987).    
The scope of this project is to investigate the development of the internal boundary layer downwind 
of the Høvsøre coastline, at the Danish National Test Station for wind turbines. The test station is 
heavily instrumented with multiple sonic anemometers in different heights and locations. In addition 
to these measurements, data from the IBL-WiSH project were available, where additional 
meteorological masts and multiple ‘WindScanner’ lidars were installed. The study will focus on both 
neutral and diabatic IBL development. In particular, the adjustment of the flow properties (velocity, 
shear stress and heat flux) to the new boundary conditions, will be investigated.  
The Høvsøre case is of interest due to the presence of a sand dune along the coast. The dune affects 
the growth of IBL, leading to results that cannot be described by classical IBL models and theory. A 
three-dimensional representation of the dune will be used as a boundary condition in CFD 
simulations using the DTU Reynolds-Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) solver Ellipsys, which is part of 
the semi-automated wind software WAsP-CFD. The numerical simulation results and corresponding 
measurements from Høvsøre will be compared and analyzed. The wind modelling deviation from 
measurements in some conditions will be investigated.  
A further understanding of the characteristics and parameters that affect the IBL can benefit Wind 
Energy in a number of ways. Better estimation of annual energy production can be made in wind 
climates affected by heterogeneous roughness and stability, e.g. in coastal zones. A better estimation 
of wind conditions can also lead to more realistic loads estimation for wind turbines. In this project, 
taking terrain inhomogeneity as a starting point (most real-world sites are affected by such 
irregularities), the models used are evaluated and some further improvements and corrections are 
proposed. This work attempts to contribute to a further understanding of IBLs and their effects on 
wind energy.

2. The Internal Boundary Layer 
 
2.1 Introduction to Atmospheric Boundary Layer 
 
The layer of the fluid that is affected from a material surface is called boundary layer. In the 
boundary layer significant exchange of momentum, heat and mass takes place between the fluid and 
the material. In the boundary layer the properties of the flow, such as the the velocity temperature 
and mass concentration vary. 
The atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) is formed due to interaction between the atmosphere and the 
underlying surface for time scales between few hours to a day. For longer periods the interactions 
between surface and atmosphere occur in the whole depth of the troposphere, even though the ABL 
plays significant role in these interactions. Surface friction and heating influence is transmitted, due 
to the turbulent structure of the ABL. The mechanism of turbulent transfer or mixing can also 
transfer downwards (from the atmosphere to the surface) momentum, heat and mass. The 
atmospheric boundary layer is often divided into a surface layer and an outer layer as illustrated in 
Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 Schematic of the atmospheric boundary layer as the lower part of the troposphere (Arya, 2001) 
The atmospheric boundary layer thickness varies over a wide range (from several tens of meters to 
several kilometers). It depends in numerous factors, as the rate of heating or cooling of the surface, 
strength of the wind, the roughness and the topographical characteristics of the surface, large scale 
vertical motion, horizontal advections of heat and moisture etc (Arya, 2001).  
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
The atmospheric boundary layer has strong diurnal variations due to the diurnal cycle of heating and 
cooling of land surfaces. The ABL height over a dry land surface can vary from less than 100 m in the 
early morning to several kilometers in the late afternoon (Arya, 2001). The diurnal variation of the 
ABL height can be described as following. On a clear day, solar radiation heats the surface and the 
resulting thermal mixing causes a steadily increase to ABL height from the sunrise until late 
afternoon.  Later in the evening and until sunrise, the radiative cooling of the surface results in the 
weakening of turbulent mixing and decreases the depth of the ABL. Diurnal variations of the ABL 
height is much smaller over water surfaces as a result of the larger heat capacity of water and 
consequently smaller diurnal variations. 
2.2 Turbulence structure of the Atmospheric Boundary Layer 
 
Motion in atmospheric boundary layer is almost always turbulent. The top of the ABL is defined as 
the level where turbulence disappears or becomes insignificant (Arya, 2001). The turbulence 
mechanism drives the mixing and exchange of momentum heat and mass in the ABL. The energy 
responsible for the large scales weather and general circulation passes through the ABL (Arya, 2001). 
This transfer creates the climate near the surface and makes it habitable.  
Two mechanisms are responsible for the generation of turbulence throughout the ABL, the wind 
shear (mechanical forcing) and convection (thermal forcing). The roughness of the surface forces the 
the wind speed to decrease to zero at surface level. The second mechanism is caused by the thermal 
effects. The heating of the surface due to the solar radiation after the sunrise leads to the heating of 
the relatively colder atmosphere and turbulence is enhanced and generated by the conversion of 
thermal energy to kinetic energy (unstable conditions). During night the picture is reversed as the 
cooling from the surface reduces turbulence (stable conditions). When the thermal effect can be 
neglected, the conditions are characterized as neutral. These conditions can be met in high wind 
speed cases or in overcast cloudy skies. 
A simple phenomenological model that can describe turbulent cascade is the Richardson’s model. 
According to Richardson, turbulence is created at large scales in the form of rotating eddies. The 
application of external forcing to these eddies, such as a large scale vertical shear in the wind speed 
or a convective overturning, adds energy into the fluid. This energy is transferred through the 
breaking up of larger eddies into smaller ones. This process continues until viscous forces become 
important (this scale is known as Kolmogorov scale, η) and the kinetic energy is converted into heat. 
The kinetic energy transferred from larger scales to small scales is equal to the kinetic energy 
dissipation, ε. Figure 2 illustrates the turbulence model.

2.3  The surface layer  
 
The surface layer is defined as the lowest part of the ABL, where the earth’s rotation and the Coriolis 
effects can be ignored (Arya S. P.). The surface can also defined as the region in the ABL where the 
vertical momentum and heat fluxes are almost constant (Berg et al., 2013). The variation of 
meteorological variables with height is significant, as a result of the significant exchanges of 
momentum, heat and mass occurring in this layer. Wind profile in this layer is dominated by the 
effect surface friction and the vertical gradient of temperature. It is also the layer where most of the 
human activity takes place. The lowest part of the surface layer where the surface roughness effect is 
dominant is called roughness layer (Figure 1). 
In cases, when thermally driven turbulence is negligible compared to the wind shear, the flow in the 
ABL can be classified as neutral.

2.5 The Internal Boundary Layer 
 
Definition of the Internal Boundary Layer 
 
The concept of the internal boundary layer (IBL) describes the process of the flow adjustment to new 
surface conditions. The IBL develops within an existing ABL. A step change in surface roughness, 
temperature, humidity or in the surface flux of heat or moisture leads to the development of an IBL. 
A very common example of IBL development, occurs when air flows from sea to land. Land-water (or 
vice versa) transition is characterized by roughness change that is usually accompanied by 
temperature and flux changes. Figure 3 visualizes a simplified picture of a 2 dimensional internal 
boundary layer. 
 
Figure 3 Sketch of a two-dimensional internal boundary layer developing within a constant- flux surface layer after a 
change in surface conditions. δ is the height of the interface at distance x from a leading edge (S. A. Savelyev & Taylor, 
2001).

example 𝑧0𝑈, denotes the roughness length of the upstream surface.

2.6 WAsP & WAsP CFD 
 
WAsP (IBZ model) 
 
WAsP is a PC software for estimating the Annual Energy Production from wind turbines and wind 
farms. Several physical models to describe wind resources over different terrains and close to 
sheltering objects are contained in WAsP. A stability model for the average heat flux conditions is 
also part of the WAsP software (DTU Wind Energy, n.d.).  
The flow model of WAsP uses the ‘BZ-model’ of Troen (Troen, 1990) to calculate the wind 
perturbations induced by orographic features. The model has wind energy siting orientation and has 
the following general features (Mortensen, 2014) 
x 
It uses a high-resolution, zooming polar grid. The potential flow perturbation at the central 
point of the model, is calculated by coupling the grid with a map analysis routine. 
x 
The roughness of the terrain is integrated into the spectral or scale decomposition. A balance 
between surface stress, advection and the pressure gradient is used to calculate the ‘inner-
layer’ structure. 
x 
The atmospheric boundary layer used in WAsP has a thickness of approximately 1km to force 
the large scale flow around high-elevation areas. 
In order to estimate climate in a specific location, WAsP uses the geostrophic wind assumption. This 
implies that the geostrophic wind does not vary spatially in the area of interest. Based on this, the 
geostrophic wind can be calculated from measurements close to the site, using the geostrophic drag 
law. By applying the geostrophic drag law again, as an implicit equation of the surface characteristic, 
the climate at the site of interest can be determined. 
The IBL development is modelled in WAsP using the mixed spectral finite-difference model for 
neutrally stratified boundary-layer flow over roughness changes and topography of Beljaars, A et al. 
(Beljaars, Walmsley, & Taylor, 1987). The stability model implementation in WAsP consists of 
separate mean and RMS values of stability assigned to land and water. The default values assign 
slightly stable conditions over land and near neutral values over sea. The model has the option for 
user defined heat flux values. It worth mentioning that the heat flux values, WAsP are not identical to 
the ones determined from measurements, but have the same qualitative meaning (Mortensen, 
2014).   
 
WAsP CFD 
 
WAsP CFD is a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) wind model. It is oriented for complex terrain 
wind energy projects, where linear wind models, like WAsP IBZ, reach their limitations due to 
exclusion of non-linear effects. WAsP CFD does not require from the user high level of CFD expertise, 
since it is an automated program. The WAsP IBZ project can be directly simulated, using an online 
calculation service that uses a high-performance cluster. 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
WAsP CFD uses the CFD model EllipsSys3D of Sørensen (1995) and Michelsen (1992, 1994) to 
calculate wind flow induced by complex terrain and roughness changes. In WAsP, EllipSys3D solves 
the RANS equations and has the following general features as described in WAsP web page (DTU 
Wind Energy, n.d.).   
 
x 
It is a multi-block finite-volume discretization of the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations 
in general curvilinear coordinates. The code uses a co-located variable arrangement, and 
Rhie/Chow interpolation of Rhie (1981) to avoid odd/even pressure decoupling. 
x 
The convective terms are discretized using a third order QUICK upwind scheme, 
implemented using the deferred correction approach first suggested by Khosla and Rubin 
(1974). Central differences are used for the remaining terms. 
x 
It is parallelized with MPI for execution on distributed memory machines, using a non-
overlapping domain decomposition technique and is further accelerated, using a multi-level 
grid sequence in steady state computations. 
WAsP CFD uses the same geostrophic wind assumption as WAsP. The Coriolis force effect is 
neglected in calculations. The stability in WAsP is considered to correspond to neutral stratification. 
Due to these assumptions, the modelled wind becomes Reynolds number independent, meaning 
that instead of wind speed, dimensionless speed-ups ratios can be considered.    
WAsP CFD simulates 36 wind directions with 10° resolution, in order to take into account the large 
changes occurring in complex terrains. A directionwise reference roughness is used to define the 
boundary conditions (far-field velocity and turbulence). Although WAsP CFD calculates the flow in all 
grid, only the results in 2 km by 2 km tiles are accessible to the user. The grid resolution is fixed to 20 
meters. This region is considered to have the highest numerical accuracy. The results are returned at 
fixed heights above the ground (5, 10, 20, 33, 48, 65, 80, 100, 120, 150, 200, 250 and 300 meters). 
According to developer, numerical uncertainties of 1-2 % must be expected in complex terrain 
simulations. As the roughness and elevation affect of the flow in large distances, WAsP CFD best 
practice indicates that height contour map should be at least 20 km by 20 km and the roughness map 
at least 30 km by 30 km (DTU Wind Energy, n.d.).

3. Methodology 
 
3.1 IBL definition 
 
In literature, many definitions of IBL can be met, as aforementioned in the previous chapter. In order 
to avoid confusion, it is crucial to determine an appropriate definition to describe the investigated 
physical phenomenon. This definition is also strongly correlated with the applied methodology. In the 
particular case study there is an additional parameter that makes the description more complex, the 
presence of the sand dune. In theoretical studies and wind tunnel simulations, only one type of 
change is examined each time, which is convenient for the understanding of the phenomenon, but 
rarely fits to reality. In this study we tried to investigate a complex system, thus common definitions 
of IBL are difficult to be applied. In reality, the flow perturbation after the coastline of Høvsøre due to 
roughness change (that is often accompanied by a change in temperature and humidity) is coupled 
with the orographic effect of the sand dune, leading to combined phenomenon. The contribution of 
each effect cannot be measured leading to a phenomenon that is not simulated satisfactory by 
existing formulas. In the case of interest, the roughness change occurs at the coastline, 150 meters 
downwind the flow encounters the sand dune and afterwards there is an additional roughness 
change from the sand surface to a low vegetation surface. In this report, the term Internal Boundary 
Layer will be used to describe the whole combined effect occurring after the coastline. This definition 
is not accurate, because in reality there is a superposition of IBL's, but is used as convention.  The 
depth of of it, will be defined as the height that the flow has its upwind/undisturbed flow properties. 
Many researchers, proposed that flow properties have different rates of adjustment to the new 
boundary conditions. Shir (Shir, 1972) for example defined a “velocity” IBL which it top occurs when 
the wind velocity becomes almost identical to the upwind value (𝑢= 0.99𝑢𝑈) and a “stress” IBL in 
the same way (𝜏= 0.99 𝜏𝑈). He also indicated that shear stress adjusts faster than velocity to the 
new boundary condition. In the thermal IBL case, a similar to velocity and stress depth definition can 
be used. The thermal IBL top can be defined as the height where the stability conditions match the 
upwind flow. In contrast with the other two definitions which quantify the IBL depth (e.g  𝑢=
0.99𝑢𝑈𝑃𝑊𝐼𝑁𝐷), this definition is more qualitative. Concluding this study will investigate the IBL 
development in Høvsøre, in respect to velocity, shear stress and stability adjustment to the new 
surface conditions, for now on denoted as “velocity”, “stress” and “stability” IBL depth respectively.

 3.2 Data processing 
 
The data were retrieved from the SQL database through Matlab. The data were organized on 10 
minutes averages blocks of 20 Hz measurements (corresponding to 12000 entries at each block). The 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
first step was to check to measurements for erroneous values. The data quality check was performed 
in the following steps. 
1. The first check was to identify entries that were marked as NAN (Not A Number) or did not 
correspond to a numerical value. Entries that correspond to a non-numerical value were not 
found, indicating that the there was preliminary post-processing on the data. 
2. The next step was to check if the data contained unusual values. The data were evaluated in 
terms of 10 minutes averages. Wind velocities that exceeded 50 m/sec were rejected, as 
records of storms during the measurement campaign, were not found. Wind velocities that 
corresponded to 0 m/sec were also neglected. For wind direction the criterion to evaluate 
the data as valid was to be in the range 0° −360°. Temperature measurements that were 
considered as as extremely unlikely for the period were also neglected (less than 0℃ or more 
than 40℃) 
3. A method was applied in order to evaluate the fluctuations, during the 10 minutes block. 
Values that were outside the range of 𝑚𝑒𝑎𝑛± 3 ∗𝑠𝑡𝑑 were replaced by NAN. If the NAN 
values of the block were more than the 2% of the total values, then the block was evaluated 
as unreliable and was neglected. 
4. The last step was to arrange the data in 30 minutes averages, formed by three consequent 
10 minutes averages. The data were visually inspected for unphysical patterns. It was found 
that for some periods the measurements had an ‘’constant value pattern’’, indicating an 
instrumentation or a data registry failure.  
The next step, after the quality check, was to find the period were valid measurements were 
available simultaneously at all measuring points, using the timestamps. The outcome was 909 
valid 30 minutes averages periods starting at 08:30 of 6th June 2013 and ending at 06:00 27th June 
(95 % data coverage).

3.3 WAsP CFD Inputs 
 
CFD simulations are very sensitive to boundary conditions. The fact that the simulations results will 
be compared with actual measurements, reveals the necessity for model inputs, that represent the 
measuring conditions accurately. The model inputs are not trivia - a high resolution map is not 
enough- as there are many parameters that have to be taken into consideration. The surrounding 
conditions at Høvsøre (or any place), vary as a function of time. The coastline or the sand dune have 
a strong dependency on time and meteorological phenomena (significant differences can be 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
identified by just comparing images taken in different periods). In order to reduce the uncertainty 
from the model inputs, much effort was put in recreating the actual conditions of the measurement 
campaign.       
 
Map Input 
 
The map that used as base, was taken from the Danish Map service/ministry (Danish map service, 
n.d.). It is a very high resolution gridded map (resolution of 1.6 meters and the height contour 
interval of 10 cm). It covers an area of 4 km by 3.3 km including all the masts of IBL-WiSH experiment 
as well as the test station for large wind turbines at Høvsøre. In Figure 10 the elevation map of the 
Danish map service is presented. 
Because the map area was relatively small, and that could result in issues when WAsP applies the 
Geostrophic Wind Assumption, it was decided to extend the area of map with a lower resolution 
SRTM map. The outcome, was a map that covers 52 km by 20 km with high resolution in the region 
of interest. Figure 11 illustrates the elevation map of the Danish map service with the SRTM 
extension.

on the experimental method and conditions. In order to use roughness lengths that correspond to 
the closest possible to the measurements conditions, it was decided to determine roughness length 
from measurements. As aforementioned, three types of surfaces that affect the flow from western 
directions can be classified, the sea, the sand beach and the agricultural flat land. 
In order to determine the roughness length of these surfaces the following assumptions were

x 
Each type of surface is assumed to have temporal and spatial homogeneity. 
x

x

output of WAsP CFD the following procedure was implemented in order to determine it: 
1. Since WAsP CFD calculations are Reynolds number a velocity input was given to the model as 
input at one grid point and height of the calculation domain. 
2. Using the speed up definition (formula 3.3.2) the reference velocity for the given height is 
calculated 
3. Since in this study, the focus is only on western directions, the reference wind profile for 
these directions correspond to an undisturbed wind profile over a sea surface. WAsP 
assumes a constant value for the friction velocity as a function of height for the reference 
profile. Using this assumption and by knowing the roughness length of the sea, log law can 
be applied to get reference wind profile. 
4. Using again the speed up definition and reference velocity as function of height, the velocity 
field in the calculation domain can be calculated for the given input 
5. Using formula (3.3.3) the friction velocity can be calculated. 
6. Assuming, that the flow in a grid point over the sea at the maximum available height (300 m) 
is undisturbed, the corresponding friction velocity is assumed to be equal to the reference 
value. In order to check this assumption, the friction velocity profile in 50 grid points near the 
chosen point was plotted. It was observed that after 100-meter height the friction velocity 
was constant as a function of height. 
7. Using the definition of 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 formula (3.3.4), the 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 can be calculated. 
8. In order to verify that the 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 calculation is independent of the velocity input, 
many velocity inputs were used, but the resulting 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 was identical.  
One may wonder why the reference friction velocity was not calculated by just applying the log-law 
in step 3 since all the inputs were known. Using this method, the calculated reference friction 
velocity deviates significantly from the value that is met on undisturbed flow regions. When 
increased roughness length input was used in log-law, this value became almost identical to the one 
found in undisturbed flow regions. This proclaims that maybe the model’s boundary condition of 
reference roughness deviates from the user defined input. In order to further investigate how the 
model treats the input roughness length boundary conditions, the following method was applied: 
1. Using exactly the same procedure as described before the undisturbed wind profile and thus 
the velocity field werr calculated. 
2. Calculation points where, the upwind and downwind roughness, can be calculated by fitting 
the wind profile are chosen. The point (Lat/Lon: 56.4414188°/8.1203673°) was used for 
upwind roughness calculation. It has approximately the same latitude as BM1 mast and it is 
located 60 meters west of the coastline. It was assumed that when the wind comes from 
western direction in heights > 80 m there is no flow perturbation from the downwind 
surface, so the roughness, extracted from fitting the 80-100 m wind profile will correspond to 
the reference of western direction. For the downwind roughness the point (Lat/Lon:

and 1.7 km from the coastline. It can be assumed that is for a height of 5-10 m is in 
equilibrium with the underlying surface and is not affected by the presence of the sand dune. 
The calculated downwind roughness was equal to the input roughness (2 cm). On the other hand, the 
upwind roughness (roughness of the sea) was calculated as 2 mm, that deviates an order of

The simulations indicate that the presence of the sand dune, increase the rate of adjustment of both 
velocity and shear stress. The upwind effect of the sand dune effect drives the IBL development after 
the coastline. By comparing Figure 28 (no sand dune case) with Figures 32-34 (sand dune cases) it 
can be seen that the sand dune presence decelerates the upwind flow and decreases the shear 
stress. The flow “climbing” on the sand dune leads to acceleration and shear stress increases. Due to 
this acceleration, the velocity and the shear stress values, cross the reference upwind values and that 
can be illustrated in Figures 29 as a sudden decrease of the IBL depth, but in reality this is not true 
and is only only a limitation of the modified “IBL-definition” used. The definition used, is not valid 
when an effect causes a change of sign in speed-up or 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝. These regions are usually 
accompanied with strong gradients in speed-up and 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 profile. This inaccurate minimum 
in IBL depth is followed by a sudden increase in IBL depth up to 200-240 meters when evaluated in 
terms of shear stress and up to 160-180 meters in terms of velocity. Downwind of the the sand dune 
decrease in wind speed is observed accompanied by a dramatic increase in turbulence intensity 
(Figures 41-43 in apendix), that can indicate flow separation. The close downwind of the sand dune 
region is the most difficult to describe in terms of flow condition. This region also is also no so 
relevant to wind energy applications (no one will put a wind turbine in a region with flow separation 
indication).  In the velocity IBL an unphysical minimum is present, that can be correlated with the 
deceleration of the flow downwind of the sand dune and the change of sign in speed-up. After a 
fetch of 400 meters IBL follows an increasing depth pattern with similar gradient to the one of the no 
sand dune case. One can assume that the effect of the sand dune as the fetch increases weakens, 
and the IBL development is mainly driven by the adjustment of the flow to the new roughness. The 
induced turbulence due to the sand dune, results in a higher IBL depth, compared to the IBL affected 
only by roughness change.  
The comparison between the “default” sand dune versus the GPS measured one, reveals a similar IBL 
development pattern. In the measured sand dune case, both velocity and stress IBL have slightly 
increased depth, that can be correlated with the higher height of the reconstructed sand dune 
compared to the default one (two meters higher). Significant differences between the high resolution 
CFD case and the standard resolution case can be observed downwind of the sand dune. The depth 
upwind of the sand dune is similar in the two cases and the sudden decrease, observed in the 
velocity IBL in case 3 for a fetch around 100 seems unphysical. The depth of the IBL over the sand is 
increased on the high resolution cases. The “velocity” IBL depth after the sand dune is significantly

5. Summary of results  
 
In this chapter, a summary of the observations from measurements and simulations, will attempt to 
describe the flow conditions downwind of the Høvsøre coastline. The flow perturbation upwind of 
the sand dune is affected by the step change in roughness and the upwind effect of the sand dune. 
The study of Qian (Qian, Dong, Luo, Zhang, & Zhao, 2012), using measurements from sonic 
anemometers, indicated that the flow decelerates and turbulence increases as the flow approaches 
the sand dune, in an inversely proportional to the height pattern. The smooth to rough roughness 
change, according to literature, is expected to lead to the development of an internal boundary layer 
where the flow adjusts with the new roughness boundary condition. The phenomenon is expected to 
result in flow deceleration, shear stress and  turbulence intensity increase, inversely correlated with 
height. WAsP CFD simulations, indicate that the flow, upwind of the sand dune, decelerates and 
shear stress decreases. The 𝑢∗−𝑠𝑝𝑒𝑒𝑑𝑢𝑝 and speed-up profiles comparison between the 
simulations, with and without the sand dune, indicate that upwind effect of the sand dune is 
dominant compared to the roughness change. This can be also backed up from measurements in 
neutral cases, where a IBL development pattern as a response in roughness change, is not captured. 
In diabatic conditions, the thermal IBL development is enhanced by the presence of the sand dune. 
The intensified turbulent mixing, induced by the upwind sand dune effect, results in a much higher 
“stability” IBL depth compared to other studies (Sergiy A Savelyev & Taylor, 2005).  
Over the sand dune, according to CFD simulations, the depth of the IBL (as defined in section 3.1) is 
increased dramatically. The flow can “feel” the sand dune in heights over 150 meters. The qualitative 
effect of the sand dune is in line with other studies (Taylor, Mason, & Bradley, 1987), but it cannot be 
verified quantitatively, due to lack of measurements. Downwind of the sand dune, again according to 
simulations, the IBL depth has a sudden decrease and a dramatic increase in turbulence intensity is 
observed. This region of the flow is quite complex and probably out of the scope of this project, as 
there is a high possibility of flow separation, so it will be neglected. Several meters (>200m) 
downwind of the sand dune, the simulated IBL development proclaims a pattern similar to the one 
met in response to roughness change. It worth mentioning at this point, that beyond the sand dune, 
the surface roughness changes resulting in a “superposition of IBLs”. The turbulence induced to the 
system by the sand dune, leads to a higher rate of adjustment of flow properties than the one 
expected from a single roughness change. 
The rate of adjustment of different flow properties, indicated a very interesting pattern. 
Measurement and simulations results, proclaimed a different rate of adjustment for each property, 
thus the definition of distinctive IBLs in respect to each property is reasonable. The higher depth of 
the the “stress” IBL compared to “stability” in the unstable IBL cases is very representative. This 
pattern, is also described in literature (e.g Shir in his study, proposed a faster development of 
“stress” IBL compared to “velocity” IBL), but is commonly neglected in studies.

6. Conclusion/Further work suggestion 
 
In this chapter, a synopsis of this study will be conducted, focusing on results that can be useful in 
the wind energy field. This part will also include some recommendations, based on the experience 
gained throughout this project, to help future research. 
The different rate of adjustment of flow properties to the underlying surface, was studied. Three 
different depths can be defined, the “velocity”, “stress” and “stability” IBL, with respect to velocity, 
stress and stability adjustment. It is worth mentioning that the “stability” IBL depth can be defined 
only in diabatic conditions. Measurements indicated that unstable conditions increase the rate of 
adjustment of flow properties.  
As thoroughly described in chapter 5, upwind of the sand dune, IBL development is driven by the 
orographic effect. The effect is also dominant in the near downwind region, resulting in flow 
deceleration and turbulence increase. Far downwind of the sand dune, the development pattern is 
similar to an IBL in response to a roughness change, but with increased depth.  
According to measurements, WAsP CFD results were adequate only far downwind (> 1 km) of the 
sand dune. Simulation and measurement comparison downwind of the sand dune (~ 400 𝑚) 
revealed that WAsP CFD was unable to simulate flow conditions. It is worth reminding, that due to 
lack of upwind/undisturbed flow measurements, the model results could not be evaluated upwind of 
the sand dune. Simulations, during which the sand dune was neglected, indicated that the “velocity” 
IBL development is in good agreement with Troen formulation.  
In the wind energy field, the development of the “velocity” IBL, is more relevant as wind velocity is 
directly related with power production, load and fatigue. The installation of wind turbines and wind 
farms near the coastline is very common and the accurate description of a micro-climate affected by 
roughness changes and terrain inhomogeneity, can make wind resource estimations and loads 
estimation more precise. The increasing diameter of wind turbines make the IBL description 
increansingly relevant, as the lower part of the rotor can be within the IBL whereas the higher part 
lies outside, affecting power production and loading.  
As previously mentioned, the comparison of the simulations and measurements includes sources of 
uncertainty, questionable assumptions and simplifications. The major issue with available data was 
the lack of upwind measurements to be used as a reference to quantify the investigated effects. 
Assumptions with limited validity were used for the reference values that could bias some of the 
results and make the comparison with simulations for the upwind region of the sand dune 
impossible. In the case of Høvsøre, an offshore mast or a mast with increased height on the beach (at 
a height that flow could be considered undisturbed by the sand dune) could improve the quantitative 
description and increase the validity of comparison with simulations. Additional measurements in 
more locations and higher altitudes, in the near downwind region of the sand dune would help to 
have a better qualitative and quantitative overview of the phenomenon, since the model seems to 
fail in this particular region. The study indicated that the rate of flow adjustment is correlated with 
thermal stratification, while WAsP CFD simulations neglect thermal effects in calculations. This study 
indicated high probability of non-neutral condition occurrence over land in the summer and 
measurements from the North Sea (Coelingh, van Wijk, & Holtslag, 1996) also indicated that neutral 
conditions occur in less than 15% of cases. It can be concluded that an implementation of thermal 
==PAGE:59==
58 
 
effects in WAsP CFD will not only benefit IBL related studies but wind resource estimations in 
general.
