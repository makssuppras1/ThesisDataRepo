Abstract
In this project we develop efficient methods for simulation and uncertainty quantification in continuous-
discrete systems, i.e. systems where the dynamics is described by stochastic differential equations, SDEs
(SODEs), and the observations are nonlinear functions with measurement noise that is described by diffusion
functions. We investigate different data driven methods for modeling the diffusion function in the SDE.
We will investigate classical methods and develop a toolbox in MATLAB for estimation of parameters.
Additionally, we assess the effectiveness of artificial neural networks (ANNs) in parameter estimation, with
a particular focus on the recurrent neural network architectures called long short-term memory network. We
consider systems in the form
dx(t) = f(t, x(t))dt + s(t, x(t))dw(t),
dw(t) ∼Niid(0, Idt),
(1a)
y(tk) = g(tk, x(tk)) + v(tk),
v(tk) ∼Niid(0, R(tk)).
(1b)
The activities in the project include:
1. Literature study and background description of differential equation models in data science, ODE
examples. Simulation algorithms and examples.
2. Stochastic extension of the models developed in 1. Simulation algorithms for the stochastic system.
3. Filtering and prediction in the models from 2 with simple diffusion functions.
4. Prediction-Error-Estimation (PEM) in continuous-discrete models using the predictors in 3.
5. Illustration of the developed toolbox for the numerical case studies.
6. Train and validate different ANNs for estimating parameters.
7. Documentation, scientific writing and reporting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Dynamic systems play a pivotal role in engineering, physics, chemistry, biology, and various other
domains. Understanding their underlying mechanisms and accurately quantifying their parameters are
essential for predictive modeling, process optimization, and control system design. The accurate estimation
of parameters for dynamic systems, however, poses a significant challenge. It requires the fusion of various
disciplines, including mathematical modeling, system identification, and statistical inference, to extract
meaningful insights from data.
This thesis embarks on a exploration of the parameter estimation technique PEM, beginning with a deep
dive into an adiabatic continuous stirred tank reactor (CSTR). This exothermic chemical reactor serves as our
primary testbed, not merely due to its chemical intricacies but as a representative case for differential systems
in general. Our journey starts with an examination of the CSTR’s dynamics, specifically investigating how
key parameters, such as the Arrhenius constant, activation energy and the temperature coefficient, influence
the system’s behavior. This initial phase of the project sets the stage for the broader endeavor: parameter
estimation for differential systems.
We expand our investigation into the stochastic realm, recognizing the ubiquity of noise and uncertainties
in real-world systems. The stochastic extension of the CSTR provides valuable insights into the complexities
introduced by randomness, which are intrinsic to many practical systems.
Next, we venture into the territory of state estimation. The Continuous-Discrete Extended Kalman Filter
(CD-EKF) and the Prediction-Error-Method (PEM) emerge as our tools of choice for state estimation. We
scrutinize the accuracy and efficacy of PEM, shedding light on its performance in various scenarios.
Building upon this foundation, we present a toolbox designed for parameter estimation. This toolbox
leverages the power of CD-EKF and PEM.
Our journey culminates with a modern twist. We explore the capabilities of Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM) networks—a class of recurrent neural networks renowned for their proficiency in modeling sequen-
tial data. LSTM networks might hold the promise of revolutionizing parameter estimation by leveraging
their ability to capture intricate dependencies within time series data.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.0.1
Parameter estimation
Parameter estimation stands at the crossroads of mathematical modeling and statistical inference. It is the
process of using observed data to estimate the unknown parameters of a mathematical model that governs
the behavior of a system. In the context of dynamic systems, accurate parameter estimation is paramount for
predictive accuracy and robust control. Traditional methods like the CD-EKF and PEM have long been the
stalwarts of parameter estimation. These techniques rely on a combination of mathematical modeling and
statistical optimization to refine parameter estimates.
1.0.2
Neural networks in parameter estimation
The integration of neural networks, particularly Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, introduces
a paradigm shift in parameter estimation. Neural networks excel at capturing complex, non-linear relation-
ships within data. LSTMs, a specialized class of neural networks, exhibit a unique proficiency in modeling
sequences, making them especially suited for dynamic systems.
In this exploration, we investigate the potential of neural networks to enhance parameter estimation.
While our primary focus is to understand their capabilities, we approach this technology with a measured
perspective. Instead of claiming to provide a highly accurate model right from the outset, our objective is to
uncover the possibilities and limitations of integrating neural networks into the parameter estimation process.
By training an LSTM network on observed data, we aim to discern intricate patterns and dependencies,
enabling it to estimate parameters. Our journey seeks to illuminate the promise of improved accuracy,
efficiency, and adaptability, rather than claiming to have reached the zenith of precision in modeling complex
systems.
Throughout the following chapters, we delve into the potential synergy between neural networks and
parameter estimation, offering insights into the transformative nature of this integration.

CHAPTER 2
Adiabatic continuous stirred tank reactor
with an exothermic reaction
Wahlgreen et al. (2020) and Jørgensen et al. (2020) provide a description and mathematical models of
the laboratory reactor system that we study in this chapter.
2.1
Description of the system
The described system is a Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor (CSTR) with multiple steady states, known
as an adiabatic system. It can be represented by a three-state model, which captures the mass and energy
balances. However, for simplicity, a two-state or one-state model can be used as a good approximation. In
this system, both deterministic ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and stochastic ordinary differential
equations (SODEs) are employed. The latter are introduced to account for diffusion and model the system’s
behavior using stochastic methods. Chapter 3 will delve into these stochastic extensions.
The system itself consists of a continuously stirred reactor vessel, featuring two inlets and one outlet.
Reactants are pumped into the vessel through the inlets, where they are assumed to mix instantaneously and
uniformly throughout the reactor upon entry. These inlets have different concentrations of reactants. Inside
the reactor, an exothermic reaction takes place, leading to the consumption of reactants and the generation of
heat. The simulation of reactor temperature, considering specified inlet flow rates, allows for the examination
of the system’s behavior due to flow and other parameters. To account for stochastic variations arising from
random input fluctuations, the diffusion term mentioned earlier is employed.

Varying β

The parameter β = ∆Hr

2.2.7
Varying k0
Another parameter of interest is the Arrhenius constant, k0. It is a key component of the Arrhenius
expression (2.3), k(T), which, in turn, influences the rate of reaction (2.2), r(CA, CB, T). Unlike β, the
Arrhenius constant, k0, affects not only the production rate of temperature but also the production rates of
reactants A and B.
Figure 2.6 depicts the effect of the Arrhenius constant, k0, on the steady state curve. Similar to β, an
increase in k0 causes the semi-stable equilibrium points to shift to the right. However, unlike changes in β,
altering k0 does not change the temperature of the semi-stable points. Instead, higher values of k0 result in a
more pronounced "S"-like shape of the curves, while lower values yield a more "L"-like shape. Notably,
even at significantly lower values of k0, the semi-stable points do not converge, and the unstable region
persists. Additionally, it is worth noting that changing k0 does not affect the temperature at low flow rates.

flow rate, Fs, approaches 0, as shown in Figure 2.7.

CHAPTER 4
The prediction-error-method (PEM)
In the realm of system identification and parameter estimation, the prediction-error-method (PEM) stands
as a powerful technique that casts light upon the inner workings of complex systems.
At its core, the prediction-error-method seeks to uncover the parameters of mathematical models that
describe dynamic systems by minimizing the discrepancies between observed measurements and model
predictions. It operates as a bridge between theoretical understanding and empirical data.
In this chapter, we delve into the ways the prediction-error-method is utilized in this project and we
look into its practical implementation, shedding light on the algorithms that drive its parameter estimation
processes.
Additionally, we study the continuous-discrete extended Kalman filter for a given system which is used to
generate the prediction errors and covariances needed by the prediction-error-method.
The prediction-error-method for parameter estimation are described in Boiroux et al. (2019) and Jor-
gensen and Jorgensen (2007), providing mathematical models and insights into the process.
4.1
State estimation
In this section, we delve into the utilization of the extended Kalman filter (EKF) for nonlinear state
estimation.
The continous-discrete extended Kalman filter for state estimation are described in Wahlgreen et al.
(2020) and Jørgensen et al. (2020), providing mathematical models and insights into the process.
State estimation, is a fundamental concept with applications spanning engineering, physics, economics,
and beyond, involves inferring the present or concealed state of a system using available measurements and
a corresponding mathematical model. The EKF, an extension of the Kalman filter tailored for nonlinear
systems, leverages linearizations of system and measurement models to iteratively refine state estimates. It
finds particular utility when a system’s nonlinear dynamics can be approximated with a linear model within
a localized range.
Our project employs the continuous-discrete EKF to calculate the innovation and its covariance. This
derived information subsequently underpins the prediction-error method (PEM), a technique employed to

4.5.1
Number of prediction steps and computation times
Analyzing the number of steps used during the prediction phases for this specific problem, it is evident
that employing just one step yields the best results, both in terms of computational efficiency and precision.
In terms of computational time, it is logical that the duration increases when employing more steps for each
prediction phase. Interestingly, using only one step to solve for the state-covariance pair yields the most
accurate outcome. This outcome is likely attributed to the system’s linearity and noise characteristics.
When the system exhibits approximate linearity over a short time interval, a single prediction step can
provide a sufficiently accurate approximation of its behavior. In contrast, multiple prediction steps can
introduce inaccuracies due to less precise linearization.
In the presence of noisy measurements, employing multiple prediction steps can lead to error accumulation
==PAGE:41==
4.5. RESULTS OF PEM
25
in predictions, resulting in less accurate parameter estimates. Fewer prediction steps are less susceptible to
this error accumulation.
It is important to highlight that the optimal number of prediction steps for achieving the most accurate
estimation can vary for different system models. The degree of nonlinearity in the models, noise levels,
system dynamics, observability and more all contribute to the accuracy of estimations.
4.5.2
Euler methods
In terms of the two Euler methods, explicit and implicit, we observe that the implicit version is signif-
icantly more computationally intensive. This computational burden arises from the root-finding problem
associated with the implicit method, which becomes especially pronounced with an increasing number of
prediction steps.
Additionally, the results indicate that the implicit version tends to be slightly more accurate. This dis-
tinction is often linked to the stiffness of systems. In the case of highly stiff systems, the explicit Euler
method may encounter stability issues and exhibit higher error accumulation. However, for the specific
problem under consideration, stiffness appears to have a minor impact, as both the explicit and implicit
versions yield very similar results.

5.2.1
Simulate model
The simulate_model function is, as mentioned, one of two core component of the toolbox, designed
to simulate the behavior of dynamic systems. It takes into account system dynamics, introduces stochasticity
via a Wiener process and generates simulated measurements.
Inputs
• fun_jac_drift: Function handle for the drift function of the system. This function characterizes
the deterministic part of the system’s behavior and provides both the drift function and its Jacobian.
• fun_diff: Function handle for the diffusion function of the system. This function models the
stochastic component of the system.
• meas_fun_jac: Function handle for the measurement function and its Jacobian. This function
describes how system states are measured.
• U: Input trajectory for the system. If the model has no input, provide an empty array, [].
• time_interval: Time interval [t_start, t_finish] for simulation.
• N: Total number of time steps for simulation.
• x0: Initial state vector for simulation.
• p: Struct of system parameters.
• seed: Seed for the random number generator (optional).
Process
1. Integration of system dynamics: The simulate_model function integrates the system dynamics
using the provided drift and diffusion functions (fun_jac_drift and fun_diff) via the Euler-
Maruyama method. This process generates a simulated state trajectory.
2. Stochasticity: To introduce stochasticity into the system, a standard Wiener process is employed. This
process generates random noise that is added to the state trajectory to mimic real-world uncertainties
and disturbances.
3. Measurement simulation: The function simulates measurements by applying the measurement
function (meas_fun_jac) to the state trajectory. Measurement noise is introduced to replicate
real-world measurement inaccuracies.

4. Outputs: The function returns three key outputs: Y, which represents the simulated measurement
data; X, which is the simulated state trajectory; and T, which contains the corresponding time points
for simulation and measurement.

The estimate_parameters function is the other core component of the toolbox. It estimates model
parameters using measured data. It incorporates a continuous-discrete extended Kalman filter (CD-EKF)

Inputs
• fun_jac_drift: Function handle for the drift function of the system, providing both the drift
function and its Jacobian.
• fun_diff: Function handle for the diffusion function of the system.

Process
1. State estimation with CD-EKF: The estimate_parameters function employs the CD-EKF
to estimate the system’s state trajectory. This state estimation process utilizes the drift and diffusion
functions, measurement function, available measurements (Y) and the parameter structs.
2. Parameter optimization: Using the optimization algorithm fmincon, the function estimates the
model parameters by minimizing an objective function. The type of objective function (’ls’, ’ml’, or
’map’) is specified by the obj_fun_type input.
3. Outputs: The function returns p_est, which represents the estimated parameter struct. If the Hessian

In the pursuit of advancing parameter estimation methods for dynamic systems, we venture into the realm
of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) which are described in (Rumelhart D. 1986) and (Manaswi 2018).
Specifically, we explore the capabilities of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, a specialized variant
of RNNs renowned for their prowess in handling sequential data. This chapter delves into the integration of
LSTM networks into our parameter estimation framework and examines the outcomes of this novel approach.
6.1
Introduction to recurrent neural networks
Recurrent Neural Networks, or RNNs, are a class of artificial neural networks well-suited for processing
sequences of data. Unlike traditional feedforward neural networks, RNNs possess internal memory, allowing
them to maintain a hidden state that captures information from previous time steps. This temporal memory
makes them an ideal choice for tasks involving sequences, such as natural language processing, speech
recognition and, relevant to our research, parameter estimation for dynamic systems. RNNs has already been
used for parameter estimation in financial data (Lee 2023).
6.2
The LSTM architecture
The LSTM network (Chen 2016), a sophisticated variant of RNNs, addresses one of the primary
challenges faced by traditional RNNs: the vanishing gradient problem. In vanilla RNNs, gradients can
become extremely small during training, leading to difficulties in learning long-range dependencies. LSTMs
overcome this issue by introducing a gating mechanism, which enables them to capture and propagate
information over extended sequences. How to setup multi layered LSTMs is well described in Pascanu et al.
(2013)
LSTM key components:
Cell State: The cell state acts as an information highway, allowing data to flow across time steps. It can
retain information over long periods, selectively update or forget information, and pass essential information
to the output.
37
==PAGE:54==

Hidden State: The hidden state serves as the network’s memory at each time step. It retains and
propagates information while selectively updating based on the cell state.
Input Gate: The input gate regulates the flow of new information into the cell state. It determines which
values from the input should be added to the cell state.
Forget Gate: The forget gate decides what information from the cell state should be discarded or kept.
It controls the retention of long-term dependencies.
Output Gate: The output gate defines what information from the cell state should be exposed as the
output.
These components collectively empower LSTM networks to capture intricate patterns and relationships
within sequential data.

Data Preparation and Training the LSTM
In the process of training the LSTM network for parameter estimation, we undertook a comprehensive

This approach allowed us to explore a wide range of parameter combinations and system behaviors,

The training procedure entails exposing the LSTM network to the prepared dataset. During this phase,

training epochs. These hyperparameters play a pivotal role in steering the learning process and fine-tuning
the network’s performance.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
In assessing the performance and suitability of our mathematical models, we conducted simulations
employing both a one-state and a three-state model to capture the behavior of the adiabatic continuous
stirred tank reactor (CSTR) system. As was described in Wahlgreen et al. (2020) and section 2.2.4, and
as was shown in figure 3.1 the one-state model only has a small discrepancy within the first minute of the
simulation compared to the three-state model. This suggests that the simplified one-state model, which offers
computational advantages due to its reduced complexity, remains an effective representation of the system’s
dynamics.
To further investigate the utility of these models, we employed the Prediction-Error-Method (PEM) for
parameter estimation. Notably, the estimated parameters obtained from both the one-state and three-state
models were found to be in close agreement as was evident from the tables 4.5 and 4.6. This convergence of
parameter estimates reaffirms the suitability of the one-state model for practical applications, as it provides
estimates consistent with those obtained using the more complex three-state model.
These findings underscore the robustness of our modeling approach for the adiabatic continuous stirred
tank reactor (CSTR), demonstrating the practicality of utilizing the dimensionally reduced one-state model
for real-world system analysis without any substantial loss of accuracy. This observation suggests that
dimensional reduction may offer suitability for a wide range of systems beyond the CSTR, thereby expanding
its potential applicability.
In the state estimation process utilizing the Continuous-Discrete Extended Kalman Filter (CD-EKF), we
specifically employed the implicit Euler method, a numerical integration technique known for its suitability
in handling both stiff and non-stiff systems. Although it is computationally heavier than the explicit Euler
method, which was also tested, it showed slightly more accurate parameter estimations and the implicit
Euler method’s unconditional stability and robustness make it well-suited to handle much more stiff systems
without requiring stringent time step constraints.
While the Implicit Euler method proved effective in our state estimation process, it is worth acknowledging
the availability of alternative numerical methods for the prediction phase. For instance, explicit numerical
methods, such as the Explicit Singly Diagonal Implicit Runge-Kutta (ESDIRK) or fully implicit Runge-Kutta
(RK) methods, could have been viable choices. These methods offer different numerical characteristics and
computational trade-offs. In particular the ESDIRK methods combine explicit and implicit features, making
them a potential choice for stiff systems. However, their computational overhead may increase with the
number of stages, potentially impacting real-time estimation.
The fully implicit RK methods provide excellent accuracy but often at the cost of increased computational
complexity. As the number of stages in RK methods increases, the computational burden grows even more

than for the ESDIRK methods.
The choice of the implicit Euler method for the prediction phase balanced computational efficiency and
accuracy in our specific application. Importantly, in the context of the PEM within the CD-EKF framework,
we explored both explicit and implicit Euler methods. Interestingly, the implicit Euler method demonstrated
slightly better precision in estimating system states for the CSTR problem.
Our implementation of PEM, employed Matlab’s fmincon function using its default finite difference
method and it yielded commendable results. However, it’s crucial to note that more efficient options exist.
Research by Boiroux et al. (2019) suggests that gradient computation using sensitivities can offer several
advantages. Firstly, it enhances numerical accuracy by providing precise gradient information, reducing
the risk of convergence issues. Secondly, it leads to significantly lower computation times compared to
finite differences. The latter often requires a considerable number of function evaluations, which can be
computationally expensive, especially for complex models or large datasets.
One noteworthy consideration is that sensitivity-based gradient computation requires the availability of
state sensitivities and state covariance sensitivities. While this approach can offer significant benefits, it
is essential to highlight that not all parameter estimation scenarios may readily provide these sensitivities.
Thus, implementing sensitivity-based gradients in our toolbox could be a user’s choice. Users can opt for
this approach when their specific parameter estimation problem and available data make it feasible and
advantageous.
Future investigations could explore the implementation of sensitivity-based gradient computation within
our parameter estimation framework, aiming to achieve a balance between accuracy and computational
efficiency while keeping the user’s requirements and data constraints in mind.
Our experiments with the PEM also highlighted an essential aspect of parameter estimation: the reliance
on prior knowledge. The success of parameter estimation, to a large extent, hinges on the accuracy of initial
parameter guesses and the appropriateness of optimization boundaries. Small deviations or inaccuracies in
these aspects can lead to suboptimal or even erroneous results.
This observation underscores the importance of thorough system understanding and preliminary investiga-
tions before embarking on parameter estimation tasks. It emphasizes the need for domain expertise in order
to select initial parameter guesses, optimization boundaries, and even the choice of estimation methods.
The parameter estimation toolbox of this project was developed in Matlab, which for computationally
intensive simulations or optimization tasks might not be the most ideal choice. A C/C++ implementation
utilizing parallel computing techniques could would be way more efficient. This would enable users to
harness multi-core processors or cloud-based resources, potentially reducing computation time for large-scale
problems. The toolbox could then be made with interfaces to Matlab, Python, etc.
Expanding the toolbox to include a user-friendly graphical interface would also enhance its accessibility to a
broader audience.
The implementation of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks within this project, as a novel
approach to parameter estimation, has displayed considerable promise. While the LSTM models exhibited
commendable performance, it is noteworthy that their precision did not match the levels achieved by the
Prediction-Error-Method (PEM) estimations, as discussed in Chapter 4.
The landscape of artificial neural networks is rapidly evolving, with new network architectures emerging

of requiring only measured data as input features for parameter predictions. However, it’s important to
acknowledge that the creation of synthetic training data for these models demands a deep understanding of

Similar to the PEM approach, generating effective training data for LSTM models necessitates a certain

It’s worth noting that for this project, MATLAB was chosen as the platform to demonstrate the capabili-

parameter estimation in complex systems.
As demonstrated in Table 6.1, the LSTM models exhibited a decent Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)
across the entire validation set. Further inspection of the predictions revealed that some came remarkably
close to the target values. However, it’s essential to highlight that there was also a noticeable degree of
fluctuation in the results when compared to the relative stability seen with PEM. This fluctuation suggests
that while LSTM models can capture certain aspects of the system dynamics effectively, they may require
additional refinement and more extensive training to achieve the same level of precision and consistency as
traditional PEM-based methods.
While PEM remains the preferred choice for relatively simple systems, like the Continuous Stirred Tank
Reactor (CSTR) examined in this project, there are systems characterized by a much higher dimensionality
where Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), such as LSTMs, may prove to be more suitable. The adoption of
ANNs in parameter estimation represents a growing field of exploration, offering the potential to unlock
improved modeling capabilities for intricate systems with higher-dimensional parameter spaces.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In the pursuit of understanding and estimating parameters for dynamic systems, this thesis has explored
two distinct yet complementary approaches: the traditional Prediction-Error-Method (PEM) and the emerging
realm of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), exemplified by Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.
Through these investigations, we have endeavored to shed light on the nuanced complexities of parameter
estimation while uncovering some unique strengths and limitations of each approach.
Initially the project commenced with an examination of an adiabatic Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor
(CSTR) with an exothermic reaction — a system chosen for its illustrative potential. This foundational study
allowed us to scrutinize the influence of key parameters, such as the Arrhenius constant, activation energy,
and temperature coefficient, on system dynamics. While the chemical reaction within the CSTR served as a
specific case study, our focus transcended this domain, emphasizing the broader significance of parameter
estimation for differential systems.
Subsequently, we delved into the stochastic extension of the CSTR, acknowledging the inherent uncer-
tainty that accompanies real-world systems. The incorporation of stochasticity underscored the importance
of robust parameter estimation techniques capable of accommodating and mitigating uncertainties.
Our exploration transitioned to the traditional domain of parameter estimation, where PEM was studied
and implemented in a Matlab toolbox. We leveraged the PEM within the framework of the Continuous-
Discrete Extended Kalman Filter (CD-EKF) to estimate system parameters. The results reinforced the PEM’s
efficacy in capturing parameter values, even in the presence of stochasticity, highlighting its suitability for
systems with well-understood mathematical models and a manageable number of parameters.
However, the landscape of parameter estimation underwent a paradigm shift when we introduced ANNs,
particularly LSTMs, as a novel approach to parameter estimation. The LSTM models showcased their
adaptability, harnessing sequential data to predict system parameters.
The LSTM models exhibited promise, offering an exciting alternative for parameter estimation. Yet,
their precision, while commendable, did not quite match the levels achieved by the established PEM method.
This observation underlines the evolving nature of the field, where ANNs continually evolve and expand in
sophistication. Further research in this realm promises to yield even more precise models, advancing the
capabilities of parameter estimation.

potential of ANNs to maybe revolutionize parameter estimation. The choice between these approaches
depends on the nature of the system, the availability of data, and the desired precision.
