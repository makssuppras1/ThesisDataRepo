Block tearing of eccentric loaded bolted connections
I
Abstract
Bolted connections are commonly used to connect steel members in a structure. Block tearing is one of
multiple possible ways in which failure of a connection can occur. However, current block tearing design
in DS/EN 1993-1-8 is not dependant on the scale of eccentricity, despite this being a crucial factor when it
comes to the bearing capacity, due to the increase of moment force. Instead, a simple reduction factor of 0,5
is used for tensile stress area, regardless of eccentricity size. Therefore, this approach is not deemed suitable
for higher values of eccentricity, due to the lower capacity that comes with increased eccentricity.
A test setup was planned, where the intent was to incrementally subject a bolted connection to eccentric
loading until failure. Furthermore, the rotation of the test plates during loading was measured/calculated
using three diﬀerent methods: distance gauges, angle gauges and test machine piston movement. In cooper-
ation with 3 other students, three C-cutout tests were carried out for each of 4 diﬀerent eccentricities (274,
316, 358 and 400 mm) for this project, resulting in a total of 12 tests. During the test, a method was used to
experimentally estimate the center of rotation. After loading the specimens until failure, the test plates were
inspected in order to examine the deformation traits.
The yield load was estimated using a procedure described in [1] and the ultimate load capacity was the
simply the highest measured load, before any bolt crack took place. Plotting the average load capacity vs.
eccentricity showed a trend closely resembling a linear relationship, where load capacity decreased with
increasing eccentricity. The decrease in capacity when increasing eccentricity conﬁrms that the capacity is
dependant on the size of the eccentricity, which would be due to the applied moment scaling proportionally
to the eccentricity.
The deformation of the test specimens showed that block tearing was apparent. Compared to outer bolt
holes, deformation of inner bolt holes were barely visible, suggesting yielding occurs at the outer holes. Of
the outer bolt holes, the largest deformation occurred for the corner holes in the bolt column closest to the
point of loading. This seems to suggest an uneven loading distribution along the yield lines.
Ultimate loads of all conducted tests were always measured shortly before a bolt crack happened in the
connection, which suggests that the measured ultimate loads are more likely to reﬂect the ultimate capacity
of the bolts rather than that of the connections block tearing capacity. This was not intended, and it was
noted that bolt cracking always happened at the bolts weaker threaded cross section, due to being directly
subjected to loading at this point. Bolt failure likely happened due to a combination of shear force from
loading, normal force from bolt pretensioning and also internal moment.
Calculation of rotation using three diﬀerent methods showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in change of the rota-
tion itself at yield and ultimate load when changing the eccentricity. What did change though, is the amount
of force required to cause the same amount of rotation.
This seems to suggest that bearing failure is dominated by the moment on the bolt connection rather
than the shear forces, regardless of eccentricity (atleast for eccentricity values between 274 and 400 mm). If

Sammenfatning
Boltesamlinger er ofte brugt til at forbinde stål elementer i en konstruktion. Blokforskydning er en af ﬂere
måder hvorpå brud i en boltesamling kan forekomme. Dog er det tilfældet, at nuværende blokforskydning
design for excentrisk belastede samlinger i DS/EN 1993-1-8 ikke er afhængig af størrelsen af excentricitens,
selvom dette er en afgørende faktor når det kommer til bæreevnen, pga. øgelsen af tilført moment. Istedet
er en simpel reduktionsfaktor af 0.5 er brugt for træk arealet, uafhængig af excentricitets størrelsen. Derfor,
er denne fremgang ikke vurderet tilpasselig for højere excentriciteter, pga. den lavere kapacitet som øgede
excentricitet medfører.
En test opsætning war planlagt, hvor formålet var at løbende forsage excentrisk belastning indtil brud
forekommer. Derudover, så blev rotationen af test pladerne målt under belastningsforløbet ved brug af tre
forskellige metoder: afstands målestok, vinkel målestok og test maskine stempel forskydning. I samarbejde
med 3 andre studerende, tre C-cutout test blev udført for 4 forskellige størrelser af exctentricitet (274, 316,
358 og 400 mm) for dette projekt, som resulterede i 12 forsøg totalt. Under testen, blev der brugt en metode
til at estimere rotations centeret for test pladen. Efter test pladen blev belastede indtil brud, blev paldernes
deformation visuelt undersøgt.
Flydelasten blev estimeret ved brug af en procedure beskrevet i [1] og den ultimative last kapacitet er simpelt
lig med den højeste last målt, før noget bolte brud skete. Plot af gennemsnitlig last kapacitet som funktion af
excentriciteten viste et sammenhæng som havde en høj grad af linearitet, hvor last kapacitet formindskede
med stigende excentricitet, hvilket will ske pga. det tilførte moment som der skalerer proportionalt med
excentriciteten.
Deformations af test pladerne viste synligt blok forskydning. Sammenlignede med ydre bolt huller, så havde
indre bolt huller ingen synlig deformation, hvilket tyder på at ﬂydning sker ude ved de ydre bolthuller. Ud
af de ydre bolthuller, skete den største deformation ved hjørnehullerne i bolte søjlen tættest på det punkt
hvor lasten tog sted. Dette tyder på at der er en ujævn lastfordeling i ﬂydnings linjen langs de ydre bolte huller.
Ultimativ last/brudlast for alle udførte test var altid målt kort før et bolte brud skete i samlingen, hvilket
tyder på at den målte brudlast ikke reﬂektere test pladernes bæreevne, men derimmod det punkt hvor boltens
bæreevne er nået. For tidlig bolte knæk var ikke planlagt, og det blev bemærket at bolte brud altid skete
ved boltens svagere tråd tværsnit, pga. en direkte belastning på dette område. Bolt brud skete nok pga. en
kombination af forskydningskraft fra lasten, normal kræft fra bolte forspænding, samt internt moment.
beregning er rotationen ved at bruge tre forskellige metoder viste ingen betydelig forskel i ændring af selve
rotationen for ﬂydning og brud lasten, når excentriciteten blev ændret. Hvad der varierede, var lasten der
skulle til for at forsage den samme rotationen som en plade med mindre excentricitet.
Dette peger på at brud er dominerede af det tilførte moment på boltebelastningen istedet for forskyd-
ningskraft, uafhængig af excentriciteten (i det mindste indenfor det testede excentricitets interval). hvis
forskydnings kræfter havde mere indﬂydelse når det kommer til deformationen, så ville dette højst sansynligt

1
Introduction
Bolted connections are a commonly used method to connect steel members in construction. Failure of
a bolted connection can occur in diﬀerent ways and it is important to make sure the appropriate bearing
capacities are checked in order to avoid this. Current design of such connections are based on the Eurocode
standards which often are simpliﬁed conservative solutions. Current block tearing design for eccentric
loaded bolted connections as speciﬁed in DS/EN 1993-1-8 [2], does not adequately take into account the size
of the eccentricity, despite this having a large inﬂuence on the capacity due to the applied moment scaling
with eccentricity.
An alternative theory regarding design for block tearing capacity has been developed by the head of the
structural engineering section at DTU Byg, Jeppe Jönsson. The approach is based on simple assumptions
regarding stress distribution, combined with knowledge obtained from previous research in this area regard-
ing yield line stress and location.
The purpose of project is to investigate validity of this alternative theoretical approach, by conducting multi-
ple tests in cooperation with three other students regarding eccentric loading of bolted connections, with the
objective being to cause failure by block tearing. The test setups are largely based on similar tests conducted
in [3], which also investigated failure by block tearing.
Two report were written for this project. A test report which explicitly focuses on the details regarding test
setup and test results and a theoretical report which covers the relevant theory aswell as compares the ex-
perimental results with the theoretical equivalent capacities, based on the Eurocode and the alternative theory.
This report is the experimental part of the project titled "Block tearing capacity of eccentric loaded bolted
connections - Test Report".
The theoretical report of the project is titled "Block tearing capacity of eccentric loaded bolted connections
- Theoretical Report".

Test setup and concept
2.1
Setup
The purpose of the tests is to incrementally apply an eccentric load to a bolt group, in order to create failure
by block tearing. During a test the increasing rotation of the plate is measured using diﬀerent tools in
order to gather information about yielding, ultimate strength and the corresponding loading/rotation to these
key points. More speciﬁcially, angle gauges, distance gauges and test machine piston movement is used to
calculate rotation. Details regarding these are explained later in the report. It was also originally intended
to have strain gauges attached to the setup, however, complications with space resulted in this being scratched.
The test setup itself is largely based on a similar test setup outlined in Nissen (2014) [3], which also
investigated block tearing capacity. An example of the mounted test setup without any measuring equipment
attached pre-testing is shown on ﬁgure 1 and an example of the setup post-testing is shown on ﬁgure 2. The
test machine, machine control, datalogger and crane used to mount a specimen are shown on ﬁgure 3.

yielding point for each test was expected to occur somewhat around the phase 1 limit, based on theoretical

Calibration of distance/angle gauges
Before any testing is done, calibration of the distance and angle gauges should be performed. During the test,
measurements from the devices are given as a values in voltage, which has to be converted into displacement
values.
When performing calibration, the equipment should be connected to the data logger. Calibration of a device
the two devices is carried out by manually inducing a measured movement (displacement of piston for
distance gauge and rotation for angle gauge) and then perform a measurement where the voltage value is
noted (along with the corresponding displacement).
For this project, 10 measurements were performed for values spread out across the range of displace-
ment/rotation for all devices. Each data set was then plotted and given a linear ﬁt in Excel, providing the
equations used for converting the measurements into displacement/rotation. The linear ﬁts were checked
for linear correlation where 푅2 values of 1,00 and 0,999 were obtained for the distance and angle gauges
respectively (where 1,00 means perfect linear correlation). Based on this correlation, 10 measurements for
each device were deemed suﬃcient. An example calibration plot is shown on ﬁgure 20.

Test B1-1 - Estimation of yield load
The load vs. piston displacement of test B1-1 is shown on ﬁgure 40. The ultimate load 퐹푢is determined as
the highest load achieved before failure, whereas the yield load 퐹푦has been estimated using the following
steps, as described in [1]:
1. A constant slope is assumed for a speciﬁc interval in the linear elastic part of the curve (in this report,
chosen to be between 3-5 mm piston displacement). The E-modulus 퐸is assumed to be equal to this slope,
and the E-modulus is plotted as a linear line intersecting the data points deﬁning the interval, shown by the
blue dashed line on 40.
2. A linear line is plotted with a slope equal to one tenth of that of the E-modulus. Using visual estimation,
the y-axis intersection of the line is adjusted so that the curve is plotted tangential to the upper part of the
load curve, shown by the red dashed/dotted line on 40.
3. The yield load is now estimated as the intersection point of the curve representing 퐸from step 1 and
the curve representing 퐸/10 from step 2, and is determined by solving the simoutaneosly set of two linear
equations.
Using this method described above, the yield load for test B1-1 has been estimated to 퐹푦, = 121.28 kN while
the ultimate load is found to be 퐹푢= 154.20 kN. The speciﬁc yield load and ultimate load for all tests are

The neglecting of horizontal movement for the distance gauges and test machine piston means that these
results will have the highest accuracy for small rotations, due to the fact that larger rotations will have more
horizontal movement. Furthermore, it should be noted that deformation of the B-plate happens primarily in
the form of rotation due to the moment. However, if the eccentricity is low and shear forces will be more
dominant, deformation would take place in the form of vertical displacement instead rather than rotation.
Therefore it should be noted that the calculated 훼푝푖푠푡표푛will also be aﬀected by any potential vertical dis-
placement of the B-plates, as opposed to 훼푑푖푠푝and 훼퐵+훼퐵which only measures rotation. Looking at ﬁgure
44, it is noted that simply displacing the B-plate vertically would cause the pistons to extend, but it would
not cause any diﬀerence in extension, which is what the rotational calculation is based on. Only rotation of
the B-plate causes diﬀerence in extension.

4.5.2
Summary: Bolt failure & deformation
The bolt cracks always happened at the threaded part of the bolt, due to the thread having a weaker cross
section than the shank.
A used bolt believed to have been close to point of cracking based on test experience, is shown on ﬁgure
118 next to an unused bolt. A close-up picture of bolt 5d and 6d from test B4-2 is shown on ﬁgure 119. The
sudden transverse deformation at the start of the thread for the top bolt on both pictures seems to be caused
by shear shear forces, while the overall bending of the bolts indicates a moment. Moment inside the bolts
would be due to the gap between the B-plates of 30 mm (The A-plates are 15 mm thick each). Furthermore,
the bolts are also pretensioned using a torque of 57 kN, which would cause tension inside the bolt.
Therefore, it seems that bolt failure happened due to a combination of shear force, normal force and
moment. This topic is further investigated in the theoretical part of this thesis.

5
Conclusion
The purpose of the tests conducted for this project was to investigate the eﬀect of the eccentricity scale, for
bolted connections exposed to eccentric loading. 12 tests were conducted, where a bolted connection was
subjected to eccentric loading incrementally until failure.
The test setup was largely based on similar tests conducted in [3]. The experimental yield load capacity was
determined as described in [1], using the estimated E-modulus. The tests revealed a decrease of bearing
capacity when eccentricity was increased, based on four diﬀerent eccentricity values between 274 and 400
mm (distance from point of loading to the geometrical center of the bolt group). Experimentally determined
yield loads for these tests shared a similar trend. However, the points at which ultimate load was measured
are not believed to reﬂect the block tearing capacity of the bolted connection being reached, but rather the
capacity of the heaviest loaded bolt of the connection, due to a bolt crack occurring in every test performed,
shortly after ultimate load was reached. The ﬁrst bolt crack of each test was always followed by a sharp drop
in load after which the load would steadily rise again until the next bolt eventually cracked.
Post-testing examination of plates revealed that failure seems to partially have happened by the intended
block tearing, due to rotation of the entire block being apparent when examining grid lines inside and
outside the block. Based on the overall block deformation, the yield line seems to be located somewhere
along the outer bolt holes. Furthermore, when the bolts were pressing against the block, yielding seems to
happen behind the bolts (i.e. outside the block), which suggests that the yield line is located outside the block.
Bolts cracks always happened at the start of the threaded part of the bolt, most likely due to the weaker cross
section area being exposed to a combination of shear, moment and normal forces. Post-testing examination
of test plates supported the idea of particular bolts experiencing heavier loads than other bolts, due to a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence in visual deformation of the bolt holes. Speciﬁcally, the deformation of outer bolt holes
was quite apparent, compared to that of inner bolt holes where deformation was barely noticeable. Further-
more, the two corner bolt holes in the bolt column closest to the point of loading had larger deformations
compared to that of other outer bolt holes. These corner bolts being subjected to the heaviest load is also
supported by the estimated locations of the center of rotation.
Calculation of rotation using three diﬀerent methods showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in change of the
rotation itself at yield and ultimate load when changing the eccentricity. What did change though, is the
amount of force required to cause the same amount of rotation. This seems to suggest that bearing failure
is dominated by the moment on the bolt connection rather than the shear forces, regardless of eccentricity
(atleast for eccentricity values between 274 and 400 mm).
Three methods were used to measure the rotation: angle gauges, distance gauges and test machine piston
movement. In the elastic regime, all three methods showed similar results. However, as more yielding took
place the angle gauges started measuring larger rotations compared to the other two methods. This is likely
due to some assumptions made regarding neglecing any horizontal movement, for the distance gauges and

Angle gauges are therefore considered the most optimal way of measuring the rotation, whereas the
distance gauges and test machine piston movement can be regarded as approximations, with highest accuracy
for smaller angles.
