Abstract 
 
Ultrafine particles (UFPs), defined as aerosols with a diameter less than 100 nm, are an emerging topic that 
encompass largely overlooked aspects of interior and exterior air quality. Airports, as year-round operational 
hubs of combustion-related activities, have been identified as major sources of UFPs across different studies. 
The aim of this scoping review is to identify and categorise the available research on airport-produced UFPs, 
while simultaneously identifying regulatory gaps and examining the current guidelines and research efforts. 
After retrieving peer-reviewed literature from different databases, utilizing tailored keyword searching, 
following the PRISMA-ScR guidelines, and cross-checking using artificial intelligence, this study produced 
a compilation of results from 70 distinct studies across the globe that examine UFP pollution, the connection 
between UFP pollution and airport environments, the exposure patterns of passengers and airport staff, and 
the effects on human health and urban air quality. Most of the included studies employed a fixed measurement 
approach—the most commonly used method in existing UFP literature—providing consistent high-resolution 
data with limited spatial and operational coverage. Dynamic environments, such as airports, require tailored 
monitoring protocols to capture spatial, temporal, and meteorological variations. Various experimental settings 
have been used to record UFP pollution, resulting in inconsistencies in recording protocols. A very limited 
number of studies relied on participant feedback over the year to assess exposure levels. Current mass-based 
air quality monitors inaccurately portray UFP concentrations in ambient air. 
Modern aircraft are the key contributors to airport UFPs, with their various kerosene-based fuel mixtures 
producing the majority of UFPs, especially during the landing–take-off (LTO) cycles. Key findings related to 
external UFP pollution highlight that apron workers and baggage handlers are exposed to some of the highest 
UFP concentrations, often exceeding 500,000 particles/cm³. External UFP pollution has the potential to 
translocate to the interior of airport terminals and to the surrounding areas, as airport-produced UFPs were 
detected several kilometres downwind, affecting neighbouring urban and suburban areas, with the range of 
detection varying between different locations and operational attributes. On the other hand, interior airport air 
pollution—a significantly understudied area—presents elevated levels of UFPs, confirming the translocation 
effect, with levels reaching 11 times higher than the typical indoor urban level. 
Short-term cardiovascular and respiratory effects are evident, with varying levels of statistical confidence, 
while long-term effects remain speculative due to sparse longitudinal data. Finally, across all the regulatory 
frameworks reviewed, binding limit values and standardised monitoring protocols for airport UFPs are absent. 
This review therefore concludes with the need for universal measurement strategies, regulatory limits, and 
mitigation strategies in order to establish an adequate understanding of airport-produced UFPs, and to protect 
airport workers, passengers, and the surrounding communities.

1 Introduction 
 
Ultrafine particles (UFPs) are aerosols characterized by an aerodynamic diameter of 0.1 μm (100 nm) or less. 
Although their mass is minimal, UFPs dominate in terms of particle number concentration in ambient air. 
Their small size enables them to effectively infiltrate the respiratory system, bypassing primary airway 
defences and translocating to different organs (1) and the most distal regions of the lungs, such as the alveoli 
(2,3). Furthermore, their high surface area and exceptional capacity to adsorb toxic organic compounds make 
UFPs significant carriers of hazardous substances. Beyond their direct health impacts, UFPs also present 
environmental impacts, with potential areas of influence including meteorological processes and the 
hydrological cycle, like the ability to alter the spatial and intensity distribution of precipitation.(4,5)  
Under the microscope of current environmental research, UFP pollution has emerged as a critical concern due 
to its potential implications for human health. The effects of UFP on health, concerning both short-term and 
prolonged exposure, are being extensively studied, highlighting its capacity to pose significant health risks. 
There has been robust research trying to identify those effects with results ranging both in impact areas, with 
findings predominantly highlighting cardiovascular (6–8) and respiratory impacts (9–12), as well as varying 
degrees of statistical correlation. While the long-term effects remain insufficiently substantiated due to a lack 
of comprehensive follow-up studies, short term effects are well documented, particularly among high-risk 
groups such as children (3,9,12,13) the elderly (3) and individuals with asthma (9,12,14).Finally, employee 
groups related to UFP sources, are considered a high-risk group, necessitating rigorous monitoring and 
protective measures that need intensive monitoring. 
A close correlation has been identified between UFP pollution and aviation activities, with aircraft emissions 
being a prominent contributor. Airports, as hubs of intense aviation activity, have been a principal point for a 
number of UFP emission studies (13,15–18). Research has extensively analysed the emission of UFPs into the 
outdoor environment surrounding airports, while emphasising on assessing the exposure levels experienced 
by outdoor airport employees. These studies encompass various aspects, from the dominant UFP emission 
phases during landing and take-off cycles (LTO) and the spatial distribution of UFPs with their corresponding 
concentration peaks (12,19,20).  
Additionally, the multifactorial production of UFPs in airport settings, primarily driven by ground support 
activities such as taxiing, which includes the movement of the aircraft from and towards the runway, parking, 
and auxiliary power unit (APU) operation (21–23) as well as passenger movement that can trigger UFP re-
circulation, highlights the importance of understanding and mitigating UFP pollution in aviation-dominated 
environments to safeguard public health and occupational safety. 
However, the transfer of UFPs into the interior of the airport halls, such as the departures and arrivals halls, 
has been largely overlooked, evident by the lack of relevant studies. The lack of recorded scientific oversight 
extends to the exposure of interior employees, Passenger Service Agents (PSAs) as well as the passengers 
who can potentially spent significant number of hours in airport interiors. Given the statistical evidence of 
correlation between acute symptoms and short exposure, this overlooked aspect has the potential to yield 
valuable scientific results (6, 9, 12).  
Finally, given the commercial nature of airports and their subsequent placement near urban and suburban 
centres, an expected influence on the urban levels of UFP is occurring (20, 24–26). Throughout studies in 
various parts of the world, examining airports of different scales (9, 20, 26, 27) the influence of airport 
produced UFP in the levels of urban UFP is consistently demonstrated, underscoring an additional aspect for

The aforementioned lack of research forms the primary motivation for this scoping review, which aims to 
assess current studies on UFP pollution within airports.  At the regulatory level, strict guidelines for UFPs 
have yet to be established (6, 11, 14, 28)  despite their well-documented adverse effects and recognition by 
the World Health Organization (WHO) as air quality components requiring defined thresholds (28, 29). In 
airport level, given the proven significance of these facilities in the formulation and release of UFPs, systemic 
monitoring and detection remains scarce and insufficient. Finally, given the link between airport emissions 
and urban air pollution, comprehensive monitoring of their contribution to overall air quality is still lacking 
These factors combined, highlight an emerging field of research that addresses the existing gaps left by 
previous studies focused on UFP in exterior settings, while at the same time opening new avenues to explore 
the quality of passenger journey, indoor air quality in airports and the contribution of airports to urban air 
quality. 
1.2 Objectives of Study 
The aim of this study is to answer the questions that emerge on the described field of study. How well is airport 
sourced UFPs monitored and given the spatial distribution of air pollutants, what is the level of influence that 
those have on the surrounding urban centres. Concluding, taking into consideration the evidence of negative 
impacts of UFPs in human health after both long and short-term, how well are those impacts researched and 
how is employee and passenger exposure assessed.  
1.2 Rationale 
A scoping review was chosen for this study as it enables the identification of available research on UFP 
pollution in airport environments. Unlike systematic reviews, which focus on providing specific answers, a 
scoping review allows for the highlight of regulatory gaps that may have been overlooked due to the narrower 
scope of individual studies. Given that UFPs are an emerging area of research, in contrast to other particulate 
matter (PM2.5, PM10) that are already well-monitored and regulated globally, a comprehensive synthesis is 
necessary to assess their health effects and exposure pathways. Finally, the evolving regulatory landscape 
surrounding UFPs underscores the need for a broad examination of existing guidelines and research efforts, 
thus providing a foundation for future policy development and mitigation strategies.

2.2 Inclusion & Exclusion Criteria  
In order to search the available databases, the following guidelines were followed to ensure coherence in the 
search results as well as, impactful contribution of the selected literature to this review. Inclusion of records 
was made by initially searching on the different databases using selected keywords and conducting preliminary 
selection of results that encompassed the main topic of this review (eg. airport UFPs) as a primary scope. On 
the other hand, studies that include the main topic of this literature in secondary or lower scope and studies 
that went in depth to a particular aspect (eg. health analysis of UFP in the respiratory system, chemical analysis 
of UFP in different jet fuel) were excluded.

3 Results & Discussion  
3.1 Measurement Techniques and Data from Literature 
Commonly Used UFP Measurement Techniques 
The vast majority of studies rely on the fixed measurement approach, making it a common method compared 
to mobile monitoring. Fixed sampling refers to measurements taken at a fixed location for a set period of time. 
While this method provides consistent high-resolution data over time, it is inherently limited to the said 
specific location, falling to accurately assess spatial variability and potentially misrepresenting personal 
exposure levels in dynamic environments, such as airports. On the other hand, mobile monitoring records 
measurements while shifting between locations, capturing real-time variations in pollution levels and personal 
exposure, simultaneously offering a more comprehensive assessment of spatial distribution. 
Most studies rely on central ambient monitors, which may not accurately reflect individual exposure due to 
spatial variation, which would provide more accurate assessments but at the same time having major 
drawbacks like failing to control for co-pollutants like NO₂, which may confound results (13).The spatial 
distribution of UFP concentrations in this measurement settings, is further influenced by time-dependent wind 
fields, leading to a non-stationary concentration field across urban areas (31). Wind direction plays a critical 
role, with concentrations peaking when the wind direction shifts towards the measurement site (29). 
Monitoring studies addressing the contribution of aircraft emissions to local air quality have generally 
followed one of two approaches: either conducting the sampling under experimental settings or monitoring 
the ambient air in or around a normal operating airports to reflect the real-world pollution level (19). An 
example of the latter is the study by Samad et. al. (2022), that demonstrates this strategic placement of fixed 
measurement sites in the vicinity of STR1 airport across the different phases of measurements (32). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In some cases, like the research conducted by Wu et. al (2021), mobile monitoring campaigns are used as 
validation method for the spread of pollutants record by fixed measurement sites. In the particular study, 
mobile monitors were able to confirm the spread of airport (LAX) derived UFPs across a 60 km² area. (33) 
Comparing the limited available studies on UFP exposure in airports, is challenging due to the inconsistencies 
in in experimental protocols, variations in UFP size distribution, exposure duration, and differences in 
statistical models and calculations (8). Some studies have relied on controlled exposure setups, with 
participants being exposed in controlled environments (7), while others have utilised self-reported symptoms,

as the most exposed, as also discussed in the previous section. (16)

assessment, as demonstrated by Kerckhoffs et. al. (2020). In this study the advantages of mobile monitoring 
for capturing spatial variations in pollutant concentrations are highlighted. Traditional air monitoring networks 
may miss local hotspots of pollution, which is critical for airports where emissions vary by location and 
activities. To address this limitation, the study developed a mixed-effects that integrates land-use regression 
(LUR)3 models with real-time measurements, to improve predictions. Before concluding that this approach 
was more accurate than either data-only mapping or LUR models, the study compared different air pollution 
measurement techniques, including data-only mapping (direct measurement of pollution hotspots), LUR 
models and the mixed-effects models. The findings underscore the necessity of combining mobile 
measurement strategies with advanced modelling, to improve exposure assessment and support more effective 
policy interventions.(38) 
Challenges in accurately quantifying UFP concentrations in dynamic environments. 
UFP exposure shows high temporal and spatial variability, complicating accurate personal exposure 
assessment. This variability is further enhanced by the presence of indoor sources and differences in 
infiltration rates between indoor and outdoor environments, that complicate further the efforts to distinguish 
health impacts of ambient and indoor-generated particles (39). Of all the indoor production locations, 
restaurants and cleaning activities are marked as the greatest producers of indoor UFPs. Both of the 
aforementioned origin sources can be found in abundance in typical airport environments.(40,41) 
While health impacts from different sources are hard to differentiate, there are multiple methods of distinguish 
origin of UFPs. One of these methods is the OC/EC ratio, with values in the range of 2.5 - 3.5 being unique 
to airports (21). Other methods include statistical corelation with BC, with airport derived UFPs showing weak 
corelations. (12) 
Additionally, concentrations present high spatial variability, with levels rapidly decreasing as the distance 
from the emission source increases (13), making both fixed and mobile measurements challenging due to the 
potential underestimation of exposure due to the potentially vastly different levels within short time frames.

3.2 Sources and Characteristics of UFP Pollution in Airports. 
Aircraft operations linked to UFPs 
Modern aircraft operate using various kerosene-based fuel mixtures to meet their operational requirements 
both airborne and while conducting typical ground procedures included in LTO (2). The produced gas 
emissions are predominantly composed of nucleation mode particles and UFPs, typically ranging from 5 to 
30 nm in size. The majority of those produced gases are carbonaceous, consisting of elemental and organic 
carbon, similarly to diesel exhaust emissions (2,11,27). A study conducted at Los Angeles International Airport 
(LAX) identified the peak UFP concentration occurring at 14nm (42). Another research isolating and focusing 
specifically on the start-up stage, from the rest of the LTO, recorded a significant 19-fold increase in UFP 
consecration, compared to the baseline levels measured at the aprons (18). 
A key role in these emissions, is attributed to the composition of aircraft fuel, with particular emphasis on the 
presence of aromatics and sulphur, both of which enhance UFP formation (43).While analysing the LTO cycle, 
high-emission phases were identified with the most significant contribution occurring during taxiing, standby 
and take-off, influenced heavily by engine thrust settings and ground handling operations (19). Measurements 
taken during take-off by Bendtsen et. al. (2021) recorded concentrations as high as 4.8X106 particles/cm3 (2). 
Taxiing, which is defined as the aircraft’s movement between the runway and parking stand, and standby, 
during which the aircraft remains in idle position in the parking stand with the APU enabled, are two phases 
that contribute substantially to the aforementioned emissions. Although the temporal profile in taxiing and 
idling varies substantially with specific events, the overall pattern of decay associated with take-offs, appeared

UFP spikes, likely due to more efficient engines and emissions controls. Amongst the commercial jet aircraft,

3.3 Other airport sources of UFP emissions 
Ground support equipment  
Studies examining commuter exposure to UFPs have consistently identified heavy-duty diesel vehicles as the 
largest emitters among different vehicle categories (31). Given that Ground Support Equipment (GSE) in 
airports (ambulifts4, loaders, push-back5 etc.) have similar configurations with heavy-duty vehicles, it 
reasonable to assume that they are also among the major contributors to UFP emissions in airport tarmacs 
(10).A study conducted in two French airports Charles de Gaulle (CDG) and Marseille (MRS) (18) confirmed 
that diesel-based GSE operating within 100 m of the aircraft, contributed significantly in the produced UFPs. 
The median size of emitted particles from these vehicles ranged from 20 to 100 nm, in contrast with aircraft 
generated particles that exhibited a smaller median of approximately 15 nm. The distinction of the sources of 
the particles was achieved through the distinct particle profiles, with diesel-powered GSE emitting more 
carbonaceous and sulfur compounds compared to the gasoline-powered GSE.(18) 
Terminal activities  
UFP emissions from external airport sources have the potential to influence indoor air quality within airport 
terminals, with air filtration and ventilation systems playing a crucial role in regulating indoor UFP levels. 
Nonetheless, exterior sources are not the sole producers of UFP emissions. Airports contain a vast range of 
indoor sources that have a high potential of contributing to those particular concentrations. 
While combustion is the primary activity of UFP generation, indoor activities such as cooking, smoking and 
cleaning are recognised as some of the dominant sources (5,45,46). In some cases, indoor UFP levels are able 
to surpass outdoor levels, especially in poorly ventilated environments (5). Given that airport terminals usually 
accommodate considerable amount of food providing services, as well as sustain high passenger traffic, they 
operate under strict hygiene standards that result in frequent cleaning. With those factors combined, it safe to

3.4 Spatial Distribution of UFPs in Airports 
High-exposure zones  
The temporal and spatial variability of UFPs is a significant challenge in accurately mapping exposure 
potential within the airport vicinity. Nevertheless, several studies have examined different sections of airports 
in an attempt to assess the spatial distribution of these pollutants. 
Higher concentrations being recorded during take offs, consequently creating higher concentration zones 
downwind of airports (2), while simultaneously emphasizing the importance of wind direction in exposure 
assessments (22).Concentration levels appear to reach higher values close to the combustion sources but

maximise at 300 – 600 m departure altitude. (49)

3.4.1 Temporal variations in UFP levels  
Given the emerging field of study that is UFP in airports, relatively few studies have explored real-time UFP 
concentrations in order to comprehensively assess their temporal variability. 
One of the few conducted studies, examined the conditions at Findel Airport (LUX), a mid-sized airport in 
Luxembourg. According to the study, UFP concentrations were 9,200 particles/cm³ in summer and 7,800 
particles/cm³ in winter, with the seasonal difference attributed to increased flight activity and lower wind 
speeds. UFP levels peak in the early morning (6:00–7:00) and late evening (22:00–23:00), coinciding with 
increased flight activity and a stable nocturnal boundary layer that trapped the pollutants. Despite heavy 
daytime activity, UFP levels declined, possibly due to turbulent atmospheric mixing, which dilutes the 
pollutants, a factor that is strongly influenced by the airport's elevated location on a plateau. The unique 
elevation of LUX at 374 m above sea level, contributed to stronger atmospheric dispersion compared to low-
altitude airports. This study highlights the key role of mixed layer development and near-surface turbulence, 
in dispersing aircraft emissions, as well as the correlation of flight activity and temporal variation of UFP 
concentrations. (11) 
The results observed at LUX were comparable to those measured at other midsized airports of Paphos, Venice 
and T. F Green (51,52,53). In contrast, major hubs like LAX and LHR9 exhibit significantly higher and 
relatively stable UFP levels, likely due to the consistently high traffic volume and more extensive airport 
operations.(11,27,28,42)

3.6 Health Effects of UFP Exposure  
These high surface area particles, with a significant toxicological potential, have the ability to, bypass airway 
defenses, penetrate and deposit in different systems of the human body, by translocating to vital organs (4,5). 
From the human health perspective, UFP exposure has been linked to various adverse effects like 
cardiovascular diseases, DNA damage, and oxidative stress (54). Epidemiological studies examining those 
effects, suggest potential associations with adverse outcomes, even though the findings remain inconsistent 
(55).Air quality degradation in proximity of airports, has been identified as an evident health hazard, with 
studies linking aviation activities with a variety of adverse health impacts (56).  A systematic review conducted 
by Ohlwein et. al. (2019) indicates increasing evidence that short-term exposure independently affects 
inflammation, astronomic tone and blood pressure regulation. However, the evidence for long-term effects 
remains insufficient to draw firm conclusions, with the presence of suggestive trends. (55) 
This exposure primarily impacts two major human physiological systems: the respiratory and the 
cardiovascular system (57). However, there is also emerging evidence of a potential link between UFPs and 
impacts on the nervous system with conditions such as dementia, as well as the endocrine system, with 
conditions like diabetes (43,58). Nonetheless, the respiratory and cardiovascular systems remain primarily 
effected, remarkably for older adults due to their weakened immune and respiratory systems (59) and to 
younger children due to their increased respiration rates and developing respiratory systems.(60) 
In the respiratory system, a study conducted by Habre et. al (2018), exposure to airport UFPs are linked to 
acute systemic inflammation, indicated by the increased level of IL-618 (9). The increase of this marker, 
particularly strong with airport UFPs, suggests that the inflammatory effects are linked with certain 
characteristics of the pollutant rather that PNC. Despite those acute responses, no immediate changes in lung 
functions were detected, implying that chronic exposure might be more relevant for health impacts. This 
concern is significant for vulnerable populations like individuals with respiratory conditions like asthma and 
children. After examining 17 Massachusetts communities within a 8 km radius of Boston Logan International 
Airport (BOS), results yielded that children in these areas were 3 to 4 times more likely to experience

Short-term effects

significantly associated with significant decreases in statistical measures SDNN and RMSSD25 in HRV indices 
within 6hr of exposure indicating immediate UFP effects on autonomic regulation (8). Despite these 
immediate effects, no significant associations were found for acute or delayed (lag ≥ 1 day) exposure effects, 
suggesting that the impacts on HRV pronominally manifest shortly after exposure. (8)

exposure. Together with a relatively large body of evidence from studies on HRV, these findings suggest that 
short-term exposure to UFP can alter autonomic tone and influence arterial BP an important risk factor for 
cardiovascular disease (55). Finally, short-term exposure has been linked to temporary increases in systolic 
blood pressure (SBP), which typically return to baseline levels after a few hours. Changes in SBP are generally 
more pronounced than changes in DBP, suggesting a stronger cardiovascular effect on arterial stiffness.(8)

Long-term effects 
The long-term effects UFP exposure have been explored in several studies, with some highlighting occurring

3.8 Mitigation Strategies and Policy Responses 
Regulatory Frameworks and Gaps 
After reviewing the available literature, it is safe to conclude that UFP pollution is an emerging field of 
research within the field of air pollution. Short term effects are documented with varying correlation, while 
long term impacts require further investigation. Given those health risks, it is of imperative importance that 
those pollutants are subjected to regulatory oversight 
As of February 202534, there are no routine monitoring programmes or regulatory limits for UFP in ambient 
air, unlike PM2.5, PM10 – US-regulated at 35μg/m3 24h avg. and 150 μg/m3 24h avg. respectively (66) - and 
NOx making exposure assessment challenging (6,33,37).The EU has strictly regulated, similarly to the US, 
only PM2.5, PM10 with a 25μg/m3 on a 24h average for PM2.5 and 50 μg/m3 for PM10 (67).The International 
Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO), responsible for reporting airport related emissions, has been found to 
underestimate real-world UFP pollution, particularly for condensable particles, possibly due to lack of UFP-
specific measurement protocols. (27) 
Air quality regulatory limits worldwide fail to explicitly address UFPs. This gap includes all the reviewed 
regions in this research like China, the European Union and the North America. Finally, the only 
characterisation available comes from the World Health Organisation (WHO), which defines high PNC counts 
for UFPs as those exceeding 10,000 particles/cm3 for a 24 h mean or 20,000 particles/cm3 for a 1 h mean. (68) 
Research Gaps and Existing Mitigation Strategies 
Considering the aforementioned gaps in regulations, it comes naturally that few mitigation strategies have 
been developed targeting airport derived UFPs, as the lack of regulatory frameworks reflects the limited 
research depth in the field. 
Several technologies have shown potential in reducing UFP emissions in heavy duty vehicles, including 
adapted particulate filtration systems and lubricant formulation adjustments (69). Similar reductions in 
emissions have been observed in  electric vehicles, hydrogen cell vehicles and vehicles operating on bio fuels 
(70). Within terminal environments, adapted filtration has demonstrated effective results in reducing indoor 
UFP levels (47,21). Nonetheless, evaluating the adaptation of experimental mitigation technologies, in a vastly 
unregulated field, presents inherent limitations in estimating their potential impact.

To develop effective mitigation strategies, it would first be necessary to routinely monitor UFP levels in airport 
environments, to establish emission and exposure patterns as well as identify pollution hotspots. This would 
allow the formulation of targeted regulatory thresholds, which would be addressed by tailored mitigation 
strategies. Given the variability in airport environments, as discussed through the aforementioned examples 
in earlier sections, any monitoring guidelines should be standardized yet adaptable to accommodate site 
specific and operational conditions. 
4 Strengths & Limitations 
This scoping review highlights the strengths and limitations in the emerging body of research on UFP 
pollution in airports. 
Strengths 
The primary strengths of this review is the inclusion of vast variety of airports, underlining the significance 
of proper and consistent UFP monitoring in airports on global scale. The selection includes major international 
hubs like LAX and FRA as well as small scale airports and training facilities CIA and TXL. This broad 
spectrum of study sites confirms the necessity for UFP monitoring across different operational settings, 
establishing that results are representative of the diverse aviation environments. 
Limitations 
Despite the inclusion of a wide range of samples, this review stumbles across a number of limitations that 
highlight areas that necessitate further development. 
The absence of a standardized UFP monitoring methodology, renders direct comparisons between studies 
complicated and thus the robustness of the conclusions in this review is a limited area. Furthermore, the 
selected literature provides a limited coverage of UFP exposure among the entire spectrum of airport 
personnel. The available studies predominantly focus on outdoor staff, like baggage handlers and ramp agents, 
leaving interior staff and exposure levels understudied. Finally, the general air quality guideline from WHO 
provides a general guide for UFP exposure levels, but no UFP-specific threshold is available for urban 
environments nor airports, making air quality assessment difficult and health risk assessments inadequate.

5 Conclusion 
Research Gaps and Future Directions 
After reviewing the available literature, there are key findings that highlight the importance of this emerging 
field. While the importance of UFPs is widely accepted, the relatively limited research as well the early stage 
of assessment, inheritably create gaps and answered questions regarding the fate of the pollutant in airport 
environment and their urban surroundings. 
Although measurement campaigns have transpired across multiple regions, a standardised real-time 
measurement protocol is yet to be established. This would allow for a unilateral approach of monitoring, 
incorporating standardised instruments and methodologies, that would enable the production of unbiased and 
comparable data across different airport settings. Additionally, multiple urban measurement campaigns have 
consistently confirmed the significant contribution of airport derived UFPs to the urban network. These 
findings complement extensive measurement efforts in urban centres, solidifying this area as the most well 
researched aspect of airport related UFP pollution. 
A potential assessment of this indoor exposure is by creating a “passenger journey map”, which would outline 
the regions of the airport terminal most frequently visited by passengers during their stay. The significance of 
this mapping extends beyond merely identifying passenger exposure patterns; it also offers valuable insights 
into the exposure risks faced by indoor airport personnel. Given that PSAs are closely linked to passenger 
activity, a single monitoring campaign could provide extensive data on exposure pathways for the two affected 
groups.  Furthermore, this mapping includes a high degree of adaptability, as measurement locations and 
protocols can be adjusted based on operational conditions and seasonal variations unique on each airport. In 
terms of human health, short term effects including BP alterations, HRV effects and effects on autonomic 
regulations have been monitored with varying statistical corelations, with increases in systolic blood pleasure 
showing the highest correlation and statistical significance. Conversely, while long-term effects to appear to 
be evident, no firm statistical significance has been established. This lack of confirmed link between UFP 
exposure and long-term effects is primary attributed to the limited follow up periods in the health studies that 
have been conducted in airport environments. This highlights the urgent need for long term health studies that 
would include extended investigation periods. Simultaneously, the assessment of exposure among indoor 
personnel and passengers, remains be vastly understudied, despite the known impact of indoor air quality to 
human health. Given that short-term effects have been confirmed, further investigation is needed to evaluate 
the associated risks, considering the extended time airport employees and passengers can potentially spend 
inside terminals.  
Finally, the lack of regulatory frameworks has resulted in limited development and implementation of 
mitigation measures. This gap creates a void in the available research, resulting in the effectiveness of potential 
UFP reduction strategies largely understudied.  Establishing clear regulations and exposure limits, would be a 
vital step in driving future research and policy development.
