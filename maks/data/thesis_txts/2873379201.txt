Abstract
This study evaluates the effectiveness of three advanced deep learning architectures - Trans-
former, xLSTM, and Mamba - for conditional generative modeling of symbolic polyphonic mu-
sic. Central to this research is the introduction of a novel encoding strategy that combines
instrument and pitch into single tokens, which enables more nuanced learning of instrument-
specific musical structure. The models were trained on the poly-instrumental MIDI Classical
Music Dataset, with conditional generation implemented by prepending metadata embeddings
representing Composer, Time Period, and Genre to each input sequence. Model performance
was assessed using both quantitative metrics, such as token-level prediction and classification
accuracy, and qualitative listening tests designed to evaluate musical quality and realism. The
results show that the xLSTM model consistently received the highest subjective ratings; how-
ever, a statistically significant quality gap remains between artificial and authentic music. Both
the xLSTM and Transformer models made effective use of conditioning metadata - for improved
conditional generation and stylistic authenticity - validated by listening tests and downstream
composer classification. In contrast, the Mamba model made only limited use of the condi-
tional information during inference, indicating a more forceful conditioning mechanism might
be needed to conditionally generate music using Mamba. In terms of computational efficiency,
both xLSTM and Mamba offered reduced parameter counts and lower memory requirements
compared to the Transformer. Overall, xLSTM, leveraging the proposed encoding and condi-
tioning strategies, demonstrated the strongest results for conditional symbolic music generation,
though a noticeable gap in expressiveness and realism persists relative to human-composed
music. This work highlights key challenges and advances in the domain, identifying promising
directions for future improvements in both model design and evaluation strategies for conditional
deep learning-based music generation.

Introduction
The past decade has experienced remarkable progress within the field of music generation, driven
mainly by the appearance of the Transformer model capable of capturing complex dependencies
within sequential data. While Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their variants - most no-
tably the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network - pioneered the field by modeling temporal
dependencies, they ultimately proved limited in scalability and in their ability to capture long-
range relationships. These limitations became particularly evident when modeling the extended
sequences inherent to symbolic music.
The introduction of the Transformer architecture represented a breakthrough via self-attention
mechanisms that excel at modeling complex, non-local dependencies ( Vaswani et al., 2017).
Transformers quickly achieved state-of-the-art results in natural language processing and were
subsequently adapted to music generation in systems such as Google Magenta Project’s Music
Transformer: Generating Music With Long-Term Structure ( Huang et al., 2018) where they
modeled classical piano music through capturing the long-range dependencies of MIDI-like data,
and later OpenAI’s Jukebox: A Generative Model for Music ( Dhariwal et al., 2020) that
implemented artist and genre conditioned music generation through raw audio. In 2020 the
paper Pop Music Transformer (Huang and Yang, 2020) expanded upon the encoding, moving
from the previous MIDI-like event representation to one more in line with the representation
of a musical score called REMI, standing for revamped MIDI-derived events, leading to better
rhythmical structure in the models. While the quadratic complexity of Transformers with respect
to sequence length has been attempted mitigated through means such as the memory efficient
implementation of relative position-based attention in Music Transformer, the issue still persists
to a degree and presents a critical obstacle for modeling long-form music, where coherent global
structure is all-important.
In response to scalability and modeling challenges in sequence modeling, a new generation of
neural network architectures has emerged. The Mamba model, introduced in 2024, is based on
selective state-space models (SSMs) and offers a fundamentally different approach to that of
the Transformer, achieving linear complexity in relation to context window size while maintain-
ing the ability to capture long-range dependencies ( Gu and Dao, 2024). This makes Mamba
particularly well-suited for symbolic music, where musical sequences can contain thousands to
hundreds of thousands of events, requiring a large context window to capture overarching themes
and structure.

Building on Mamba, a more recent paper, MusicMamba: A Dual-Feature Modeling Approach
for Generating Chinese Traditional Music with Modal Precision ( Chen et al., 2025), proposed a
hybrid model that combines Mamba and Transformer blocks for music generation. Trained on
a variant of the REMI representations of traditional Chinese music, their results suggest that
Mamba outperforms the Transformer-based Musical Transformer models in this context.
Additionally, the xLSTM architecture, introduced in 2024, extends the classic LSTM by im-
proving its gating, normalization, and memory mechanisms ( Beck et al., 2024). These advance-
ments significantly expand its effective context window, enabling context scaling on par with the
Mamba architecture. Notably, the xLSTM paper offers a direct comparison between Mamba
and xLSTM on formal language tasks, demonstrating that xLSTM consistently outperforms
Mamba in these benchmarks. However, given the recent introduction of xLSTM, there have
been no reports, to our knowledge, of its application within the domain of music generation.
Nonetheless, since music generation often relies on techniques similar to those used in natural
language processing, xLSTM is an intriguing candidate for exploration in this field, motivating
its inclusion in the present study.
Many of the worlds leading tech companies are also actively researching within the field of music
generating models. With a surge of interest and investment in AI-driven music creation from
platforms such as Google’s Magenta Project (Google Research, Magenta Team, 2016), OpenAI’s
Jukebox (Dhariwal et al., 2020), Meta’s AudioCraft (Meta AI AudioCraft Team, 2023), and
commercial initiatives like Suno.com (Suno AI, 2023) demonstrating the field’s rapid expansion.
Despite achieving impressive generative results, most current systems however emphasize end-
to-end automation with minimal user input, typically limited to a text prompt.
While lowering the barrier to entry for non-musicians, such approaches do little to enhance the
workflows of composers, producers, and musicians who require tools to support, rather than
replace, the creative process.
The motivation for this study thus arises from the increasing
demand for next-generation music production tools that combine high-quality generative capa-
bilities with interactive, user-guided creative control.
Accordingly, this research investigates the capabilities of both Mamba and xLSTM models as
potential improvements over Transformer-based approaches for symbolic music modeling. Spe-
cial attention is given to methods for conditional generation, with the goal of advancing models
that not only produce expressive music, but also allow for user-guided generation.

Research questions
• What are effective symbolic encodings of MIDI data for multi-instrumental music, and
how do Mamba, xLSTM, and Transformer models trained on these encodings compare in
terms of generative musical quality, computational efficiency, and their ability to model
long-range dependencies?
– How this is examined: This study expands upon the REMI representation to
encode multi-instrumental structure, and made preliminary comparisons with the
MIDI-like encoding used in Music Transformer. The Transformer architecture from
Music Transformer serves as the baseline, with additional experiments conducted by
replacing its backbone with Mamba and xLSTM architectures. Generative musical
quality is assessed using token-wise prediction accuracy and qualitative listening tests.
Computational efficiency is evaluated through metrics such as model storage size, al-
located memory, and inference speed. The capacity to model long-range dependencies
is analyzed using token-wise accuracy across varying context window lengths.
• What is an effective conditioning mechanism for controlling music generation with Mamba
and xLSTM models, and how does this mechanism affect the quality of the generated
output?
– How this is examined: To assess each model’s ability to generate stylistically accu-
rate conditioned continuations, a multi-label classification model is trained to predict
track metadata from generated music. The proposed conditioning approach involves
prepending a learned metadata embedding to the input sequence. The effectiveness
of this method is evaluated by generating music in three scenarios: (1) with composer
conditioning, (2) with ’null’ token conditioning, and (3) without metadata. The clas-
sification model is then used to estimate the success rates and to what degree the
models generate in the conditioned style.
• What combination of quantitative metrics and qualitative user studies can effectively eval-
uate the performance of Mamba and xLSTM models in generating polyphonic music, as
well as the effectiveness of conditional generation techniques?
– How this is examined: The multi-instrumental encoding scheme implemented in
this study enables direct evaluation of each model’s ability to generate polyphonic mu-
sic. Quantitative evaluation is conducted through the comparative loss experienced
during training, as well as using token-wise accuracy metrics across three scenarios:

musical quality and perceived realism. This approach provides a comprehensive eval-

Report Structure

encoding scheme implemented in this study as a method of polyphonic music modeling, and data
augmentation strategies. Section 3 describes the methodologies, including detailed overviews of
the Transformer, xLSTM, and Mamba architectures, as well as the approaches adopted for

research.

Data
This chapter describes the data gathering and processing methods used in this study. It details
the MIDI format, describes the dataset of both single- and multi-instrumental classical music,
and outlines the acquisition of metadata employed for conditioning. Additionally, it explains
the implemented data augmentation strategies and presents the encoding scheme used in this
study.
2.1
Dataset Overview
2.1.1
Overview of the MIDI Classical Music Dataset
The dataset utilized in this study is the MIDI Classical Music Dataset (drengskapur, 2024),
which consists of 4,796 MIDI tracks representing works by 146 distinct composers amount-
ing to approximately 400 hours’ worth of data. This dataset provides a rich representation of
classical music, encompassing both single- and poly-instrumental tracks. The diversity of instru-
mentation and compositional style across such a wide range of classical composers makes this
dataset suitable for training and evaluating deep sequence models’ ability to generate polyphonic
composer-conditioned symbolic music.
For this thesis, the pretty midi Python library (Raffel and Ellis, 2023) is used for efficient parsing
of MIDI files - facilitating extraction of per-note events across instruments.
As a note on the MIDI Classical Music Dataset, not all composers were equally represented with
the numbers of tracks present in the dataset. Given the varying popularity and productivity
of classical composers, some, such as Johann Sebastian Bach, are heavily featured, while others
are represented by only a few pieces. The top five composers in terms of track count are:
• Bach: 887 tracks
• Unknown Artist*: 708 tracks
• Scarlatti: 692 tracks
• Mozart: 254 tracks
• Beethoven: 246 tracks
* Tracks without a known composer were designated as ”Unknwon Artist”.
As part of the pre-processing, all composers with only a single track were removed, and any files
containing fewer than 180 notes were discarded. This filtering ensured that all sequences were

2.1.2
High-Level Musical Abstraction in MIDI
The utilized MIDI format encodes music as discrete musical events rather than as time-wise
amplitude of raw audio. These events include note activations and deactivations, instrument
changes, dynamics, and tempo modifications, among others.
This abstraction is critical for
symbolic modeling, as it condenses the musical information into a compact, structured, and
interpretable form. Unlike raw audio, which consists of tens of thousands of amplitude samples
per second (e.g., 44,100 samples/sec at CD quality) and quickly leads to extremely long and
unwieldy sequences to capture even tiny snippets of music, MIDI offers a high-level and compact
abstraction of musical content. A few seconds of music in MIDI format may correspond to only
a few hundred discrete events, particularly for single-instrument or simple poly-instrumental
passages.

2.2
Symbolic Music Encoding
2.2.1
MIDI-Like Format
In 2018, the paper Music Transformer (Huang et al., 2018) successfully trained the Transformer
architecture on a piano MIDI dataset using a MIDI-like encoding scheme composed of Note On,
Note Off, Set Dynamics, and Time Shift events, closely mirroring the standard MIDI format.
While this representation effectively demonstrated the impressive capabilities of Transformers
for music generation, it diverged significantly from the way music is structured in conventional
sheet music. Unlike MIDI, where notes are defined by separate Note On and Note Off events,
sheet music typically represents each note as a discrete event with an explicit duration, speci-
fied through fractions of a bar. Additionally, the MIDI-like encoding omits important musical
events such as Set Tempo or Instrument Change, thus limiting the model’s expressiveness to
single-instrument, fixed-tempo compositions. To address some of these limitations, the REMI
(REvamped MIDI-derived) format was introduced.
2.2.2
REMI Format
As part of the 2020 paper Pop Music Transformer (Huang and Yang, 2020), the REMI represen-
tation was introduced, being directly derivable from MIDI-data. Unlike the previous MIDI-like
symbolic encoding, which relied on separate Note-On and Note-Off events, REMI represents
each note with a start time and duration. Additionally, time-shift events in REMI are quantized
to 32nd-note intervals, yielding a structure more closely aligned with conventional sheet music,
building the music up bar-wise. In order to really align the notes in a bar-centered format similar
to sheet music, positional information is note-wise given as a measure of time-steps from the
begining of the current bar.
By making the time-steps into resolutions of the bar length, helps the model to generalize across
varying tempos. As an example, where the previous MIDI-like format would have seen a time-
shift of 300 ms for the tempo 100 and a time-shift of 150 ms for the tempo 200 of the same
sequence, the REMI would format both tempos as time-shifts of a 4th-note length. Furthermore,
REMI adds tempo and chord as distinct event types, enabling models to capture finer musical
details. As part of the Pop Music Transformer paper it was shown that models trained with
REMI have been shown to outperform those using more traditional MIDI-like representations
in generating expressive music.
Since chord events are not explicitly encoded in MIDI data, chord recognition algorithms were
applied to identify chords by analyzing groups of simultaneously pressed keys. This process

be added to the encoding.
2.2.3
Poly-Instrumental Music Representation
Taking inspiration from the REMI representation designed for single-instrumental encoding, we
organize note events at 32nd-note intervals, and when multiple notes start together (e.g., chords,
harmonized instruments), they receive a time-shift token of zero.
To enable the model to distinguish between notes of similar pitch played on different instruments,
our tokenization scheme combines pitch and instrument information into a single token (referred
to here on out as Instrument Pitch). This approach ensures that notes performed by different in-
struments at the same timestep are represented distinctly, preserving instrument-specific context
throughout the sequence.
Preliminary experiments with an encoding scheme that represented Instrument and Pitch as sep-
arate token events revealed that models trained on this format struggled to capture instrument-
specific characteristics. These models frequently defaulted to common instruments and gener-
ated notes outside the realistic pitch ranges for each instrument, thus leading to the coupling of
the two events.
As will later be described, the primary reason this token type constitutes the majority of the
vocabulary - being responsible for 16,512 out of 17,862 tokens as is shown in Section 2.2.4 -
is that it is designed to capture the full range of possible instrument and pitch combinations
in the MIDI specification. Specifically, there are 128 MIDI pitches and 128 MIDI instruments,
plus one additional instrument designated as universal drums. To comprehensively represent
every possible combination, we require 128 × 129 = 16, 512 unique tokens, which accounts for
the substantial size of this portion of the vocabulary.

3.1
Transformer Model
In 2018, the Music Transformer paper (Huang et al., 2018) marked a significant advance in
sequence-based music generation, demonstrating that the self-attention mechanism is highly
effective for modeling single-instrument piano music, particularly through its use of relative
positional encodings.
In this study, the Music Transformer (MT) architecture serves as the
foundational backbone for implementing the conditional music generation model. This backbone
can subsequently be replaced with more recent architectures, such as Mamba and xLSTM,
facilitating a direct comparison of model performance and advancements beyond the Transformer
approach.
3.1.1
Introduction to Transformers
The Transformer architecture, introduced in the seminal paper Attention is All You Need (Vaswani
et al., 2017), marked a breakthrough in sequence modeling. Unlike prior models that relied on
recurrence (RNNs, LSTMs) or convolutions, the Transformer is built entirely on attention mech-
anisms, specifically self-attention. This innovation enabled the model to capture long-range de-
pendencies efficiently and to process sequences in parallel, drastically accelerating both training
and inference.
Transformers rapidly became the backbone of state-of-the-art models in natural language pro-
cessing (NLP), speech, vision, and, more recently, music generation. Their ability to model
complex relationships within sequences, irrespective of their distance to the latest token, makes
them well-suited for structured, temporally rich data such as symbolic music.
For this reason, in late 2018, the Transformer architecture was successfully applied in the Music
Transformer paper to generate continuations for given inputs and accompaniments conditioned

3.1.7
Modified Attention Mask for Metadata Conditioning
Since the Transformer architecture processes all positions in parallel, it is necessary to explicitly
specify which information each position can utilize during prediction. This is typically accom-
plished using a causal mask, which restricts each position to attend only to the tokens that
precede it, thus preserving the autoregressive property required for sequential modeling.
In this study, however, the attention mask is modified from the standard causal mask to ensure
that every token in the sequence can always attend to the metadata embeddings located at the
beginning of the sequence. This adjustment guarantees that the conditioning information con-
tained in the metadata remains accessible at all positions throughout generation. Apart from
this change, the mask maintains its regular autoregressive behavior: tokens are still prohib-
ited from attending to future positions during training, thereby preserving the causal structure
essential for sequence generation.
Additionally, it is important to note that the model’s output does not include the initial six posi-
tions, which correspond to the metadata embeddings. As a result, this conditioning mechanism
does not interfere with the model’s ability to predict subsequent musical tokens.
These adaptations ensure that the model conditions its predictions on the provided metadata
at every step, allowing generation to be guided by high-level descriptors such as composer, time
period, and genre.

3.2.5

In the standard state-space model (SSM) formulation, all parameters are time-invariant; that is,
they remain fixed for every position in the input sequence. While this makes the model efficient,
it limits its flexibility to adaptively focus on the most relevant information at different positions
in the sequence. For example, in symbolic music modeling, the prediction of the next note may
depend more on the pitch of previous notes than on their dynamics, motivating the need for a
mechanism that can selectively weight different features based on context.
The Selective State Space Model (S6), as introduced in the Mamba architecture, addresses this
limitation by allowing certain parameters - specifically B, C, and the discretization step ∆
- to be dynamic functions of the input sequence.
Instead of being fixed, these parameters
are generated at each time step, conditioned on the current or recent inputs.
This input-
dependent parameterization enables the model to ”select” which aspects of the input sequence
are most relevant at each position, thereby increasing its expressive capacity and ability to
capture complex, context-dependent dependencies.

3.2.6
Hardware-Aware Algorithm
Efficiently implementing the selective state-space model (SSM) on modern hardware introduces
unique challenges. Unlike time-invariant SSMs, which can leverage fast convolution algorithms
such as the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), selective SSMs require parallel associative scan algo-
rithms for sequence processing. While both the recurrent mode, with computational complexity
O(BLDN) (where B is the batch size, L is the sequence length, D is the input embedding
dimension, and N is the model’s state dimension), and the convolutional mode, with complex-
ity O(BLD log(L)), are theoretically efficient, actual performance on GPUs depends heavily on
memory access patterns and hardware utilization.
To address these challenges, the Mamba implementation incorporates several hardware-aware
optimizations:
Kernel Fusion.
A naive approach would store all intermediate scan states in high-bandwidth
GPU memory (HBM), resulting in substantial memory input/output (IO) costs, as each step
involves reading and writing O(BLDN) elements. To reduce this overhead, the discretization,
scan, and final multiplication with C are fused into a single GPU kernel. Key parameters (∆, A,
B, C) are loaded into fast on-chip SRAM, where all intermediate computations are performed,

and only the final outputs are written back to HBM. This fusion reduces memory IO by a factor
of O(N), yielding practical speedups of 20–40× over the naive implementation.
Chunked Processing for Long Sequences.
For very long sequences where the working
memory required exceeds on-chip SRAM capacity, the algorithm splits the sequence into smaller
chunks. Each chunk is processed independently, with intermediate states passed between chunks
as needed, maintaining both correctness and efficiency.
Memory-Efficient Training via Recomputation.
To further reduce memory usage during
training, especially for the backward pass, Mamba utilizes recomputation. Instead of storing
all intermediate activations and scan states, they are discarded during the forward pass and
recomputed as needed during backpropagation.
Since the critical inputs and gradients are
much smaller, this approach minimizes high-latency memory access without incurring significant
computational overhead.
Each selective SSM can be implemented as a block, comparable to the self-attention block in
a Transformer decoder. As with Transformers, these Mamba blocks can be stacked, with the
output of one block serving as the input to the next, enabling deep and expressive sequence
modeling.
3.3
xLSTM Model
The Extended Long Short-Term Memory (xLSTM) architecture, introduced in (Beck et al.,
2024), was developed as a scalable alternative to Transformer-based large language models and,
like the Mamba model, seeks to address the limitations of traditional sequence modeling ap-
proaches. The xLSTM paper reports that xLSTM even outperforms Mamba on certain formal
language tasks, making its comparative performance with Transformer and Mamba architec-
tures particularly interesting to investigate. However, to the best of our knowledge, the xLSTM
architecture has not yet been applied to the task of symbolic music generation in published
research.
Building upon the foundational Long-Short Term Model (LSTM), xLSTM introduces several key
innovations to overcome challenges inherent to traditional LSTMs. These include the adoption
of exponential gating, advanced normalization and stabilization techniques, and the implemen-
tation of novel memory structures that significantly expand the capacity and flexibility of the
classic LSTM framework.
Specifically, the xLSTM introduces two types of xLSTM-blocks: the sLSTM, which employs

3.3.4
xLSTM Architecture
Given that the sLSTM is designed to efficiently model long-term sequential dependencies in the
data, while the mLSTM acts as a ”slow” pathway - being updated less frequently and enhancing
the model’s ability to retain information over extended periods - the xLSTM architecture can be
implemented by residually stacking these two types of blocks to achieve a desired balance between
their complementary strengths. As suggested in the xLSTM paper, a common configuration is
to stack two mLSTM blocks for every sLSTM block, a setup that has also been adopted in this
study.

Music Generating Models
To achieve the goal of music generation, three distinct model architectures were implemented:
Mamba, xLSTM, and Transformer with relative attention and modified masking. Each model
operates as a next-token predictor, receiving an input sequence of encoded MIDI data and being
trained to predict the subsequent token at each step. By learning to match input sequences

to their corresponding target continuations, these models are able to generate coherent musical

The architectures and parameter settings for the models used in this study are summarized in

3.4.1
Conditional Generation
To facilitate conditional music generation, we introduce a dedicated Metadata Embedding layer.
In addition to the standard tokenized MIDI input mapped to the input embedding, each model
also receives a separately tokenized metadata vector composed of six elements: one token for
the composer, one for the time period, and up to four tokens representing musical genres. These
metadata tokens are separately embedded and concatenated with the input sequence, ensuring
that the metadata occupies the first six positions in the embedded sequence processed by the
models.
The approach of prepending Metadata Embeddings to the input sequence enables conditional
music generation by leveraging the recurrent and attention-like capabilities of the xLSTM and
Mamba models. Unlike previous recurrent architectures - which relied on a single learnable
parameter applied uniformly across all sequence elements and could not effectively capture
information from tokens at the beginning of long sequences - these models feature individualized,
position-sensitive mechanisms.
As a result, they are able to retain and attend to relevant
metadata tokens, across great distances from the current prediction step. This allows the models
themselves to determine the importance of the metadata and flexibly incorporate it into the
generation process. The experimental setup for testing the conditional generation is mentioned
further in section 4.2.

Generating Music
The models generate music by auto-regressively expanding the input sequence, as outlined in
Algorithm 4.
The generation process takes as input the initial sequence, the corresponding
metadata, and an integer specifying the number of tokens to generate. Whenever the expanded
sequence xtemp exceeds the context length, only the most recent tokens (up to the context size)
are provided as input to the model.
At each step, the model outputs a logit vector, from which the final logit is selected and pro-
cessed by our proposed PenalizeRepetitivity function. Preliminary experiments revealed that
sampling solely the most probable token often caused the model to fall into repetitive loops, re-
sulting in the generation of either a single note or a short, endlessly repeating sequence-leading
to poor musical quality. Likewise, basic top-k sampling strategies frequently caused the models
to diverge quickly from the input context, further degrading musical quality. While classical
music does feature repeated passages, authentic compositions also vary over time. To address
these challenges, the PenalizeRepetitivity function was designed to encourage diversity in
generation without compromising musicality.
This function examines the tokens present in the most recently generated 16 bar window, equiv-
alent to 384 time steps measured through the Time Shift tokens, and applies penalties to the
logits of tokens found within this window, with both the magnitude and upper limit of the
penalty depending on the token type and recent frequency. The penalty values, hand-tuned for

optimal results, are as follows:
For Instrument Pitch, the penalty is given by min(1.02 · count, 1.2); for Dynamics, it is
min(1.03 · count, 1.2); and for Length, the penalty is min(1.02 · count, 1.2). No penalty is
applied to Time Shift and Tempo tokens.
For Tempo, this is because real musical pieces tend to maintain a stable tempo; meaning that
artificially introducing changes to the tempo would lead to abrupt and unnatural shifts in the
generated music. Whereas, penalizing Time Shift tokens - particularly Time Shift 0 tokens that
mark chords and simultaneouslt playing instruments - would undesirably suppress polyphony
and result in overly sparse melodies. The penalties applied are intentionally minimal, attempting
to strike a balance between promoting musical variety and preserving the natural structure of
the music.

4.1
Experimental Setup - Generative Musical Quality
4.1.1
Quantitative Testing
To quantitatively assess the music generation quality of each model, we measured the cross-
entropy loss between the output and target sequences using both the training and validation
datasets, with the validation dataset constituding 20% of the total MIDI data. Training loss
was logged every 10 batches, while validation loss was recorded once per epoch.
Additionally, to assess the models reliance on the metadata during inference, we evaluate each
architecture’s accuracy in predicting various token types within the validation dataset.
We
repeat the experiments but where all metadata is replaced with ’null’ tokens, effectively removing
metadata and conditional information from the generation process.
To provide a baseline comparison, we train separate models without any metadata input and
measure their token prediction accuracies. By directly comparing these results to those of the
conditionally trained models, we obtain an objective assessment of how metadata conditioning
impacts musical quality of the generated output.
4.1.2
Qualitative Testing
To evaluate the quality and realism of the generated music, qualitative testing was conducted
across eight composers: Beethoven, Brahms, Chopin, Debussy, Liszt, Mozart, Vivaldi, and
Wagner.
For each composer, three distinct variations were generated from the same initial
fragment of a test set piece, alongside the original (human-composed) continuation, resulting
in a total of four variations per piece. Each variation was produced by continuing a 200-token
sequence from the dataset, using each of the models to generate an additional 1024 tokens,
thereby ensuring all variations shared the same starting context and enabling direct comparison.
When decoded, these musical sequences ranged from 15 to 40 seconds in length, depending on
the number of instruments.
The generated dataset comprised both single-instrument (solo piano) and more complex poly-
phonic orchestral samples. Importantly, the generative process placed no restrictions on which
instruments could be used, allowing for spontaneous variation in which instruments were in-
cluded between generated samples, even within different continuations of the same initial con-
text.

Experimental Setup – Conditional Generation
To assess the models’ ability to generate music conditionally, we developed a separate classi-
fication model based on the xLSTM architecture, designed to predict metadata tokens from a
given input sequence. This classifier mirrors the standard xLSTM architecture, except its out-
put is linearly projected into the metadata vocabulary, producing a logit vector indicating the
likelihood of each metadata token.
Given the method of metadata implementation, the task of classifying metadata from a musical
sequence can result in three to six labels per sequence: one for the Time Period, one for the
Composer, and between one and four for Genres. This approach uses a multilabel classification
framework, where the standard cross-entropy loss is replaced by BCEWithLogitsLoss during
training. This loss function, which integrates a sigmoid activation with binary cross-entropy,
then predicts the presence or absence of each metadata label. The classifier was trained on the
entire dataset but due to all data being of the classical genre and metadata defined composer-
wise, its performance was primarily evaluated based on composer prediction accuracy.

5.1
Generative Repetitivity Penalization
This is a short section on the necessity for the PenalizeRepetitivity function during gen-
eration. Figure 5.1 illustrates the differences in generative quality between different sampling
methods and highlights the issues associated with not penalizing upon sampling. It compares
a musical sequence generated by the Mamba model using the PenalizeRepetitivity function
with results from pure argmax and top-k sampling. As can be seen, the sequence generated
using repetition penalization appears as a nearly seamless extension of the original segment. In
contrast, the sequence without this function falls into a repetitive loop of the same seven pitches
within the first few generated notes. This occurs because the model identifies a ’most-likely’
continuation that, when repeated, outperforms other variations, leading to monotonous and
non-musical sections. Additionally, while top-k sampling was explored as a potential solution to
repetitiveness, it can be seen to introduce excessive variation or ’noise,’ rendering the sequence
chaotic and incoherent. Using this visual inspection as basis, the proposed penalization function
was utilized for all music generation throughout this study in order to apply some controlled
variation within the generated music.

Discussion
Initial tests of the generated sequences revealed that all three models tended to produce repetitive
outputs, often converging on stable, static sequences or even repeating the same chord and can
be equated to musical collapse. The first attempt to overcome this musical collapse, was in
the form of introducing top-k sampling as a decoding strategy which would help diversify the
output, but proved to introduce too much noise and severely degrade the musical quality of the
output. Thus, the PenalizeRepetitivity function was introduced, penalizing the repetition
of specific token-types within the most recent 16-bar window. While this intervention mitigated
issues of stagnation and musical collapse, it also introduced an external bias that occasionally
reduced model accuracy and led to artifacts in the output, such as the appearance of unnatural
piano trills.
To enable conditional generation based on external metadata, the models’ ability to retain
information across long sequence distances were utilized by prepending a six-token metadata
embedding to the input sequence.
This design allowed each model to learn the impact of
metadata autonomously and to determine the extent to which it relied on this information during
prediction. Experimental results revealed that both Transformer and xLSTM models effectively
utilized the conditional metadata, demonstrating significant conditionability. In contrast, the
Mamba model made only limited use of the metadata during inference, indicating a reduced
capacity for conditioning.
The reduced conditioning of the Mamba model might have been
mitigated by more forceful approaches to conditional generation, which are further explored in
Section 8.4.
Qualitative evaluation conducted in this study revealed that the backbone Transformer model
achieved the lowest scores in the listening test, while both Mamba and xLSTM models per-
formed better, with xLSTM achieving the highest ratings overall. Nevertheless, none of the
models on average matched the musical quality of real music, indicating a clear gap between
artificially generated and authentic compositions despite good generative performance for cer-
tain composer-styles. Similarly, the certainty test demonstrated that the outputs of all models
generally exhibited distinctive characteristics that set them apart as artificial music.
Quantitative evaluation of musical quality showed that while the xLSTM and Transformer
models achieved the highest token-wise prediction accuracy, the Mamba model performed only
marginally worse. Prediction accuracy was also found to vary significantly across token types.
Most notably, Instrument Pitch tokens reached only about 45% accuracy during inference, sug-

gesting that they are both the most challenging and arguably the most crucial for the models’
generative capabilities. This reduced accuracy could also be caused by the much larger sample
space associated with Instrument Pitch prediction compared to the other token types, making
accurate prediction more difficult.
Comparisons with non-conditioned generation further showed that both the Transformer and
xLSTM models effectively utilized metadata to inform their predictions, whereas the Mamba
model relied much less on metadata. This limited use is thought to stem from the Mamba
model’s slight signs of overfitting, reducing its reliance on metadata and leading it to reproduce
training data more directly rather than generalizing to conditioned generation.
The comparison with models trained without metadata revealed a slight decrease in predictive
accuracy relative to those trained with metadata. This suggests that incorporating metadata -
serving as additional contextual information - can enhance the musical quality of generated out-
puts, as it enables the models to make more informed predictions during inference. Interestingly,
trained upon the absence of metadata, the Mamba model outperformed both the xLSTM and
Transformer models, aligning with recent findings in music generation literature that highlight
the superior performance of the Mamba architecture over the Transformer (Chen et al., 2025).
In the classification test evaluating conditioned generative performance, the xLSTM and Trans-
former models again scored similarly, with the Transformer narrowly outperforming xLSTM.
The Mamba model’s generated sequences registered the lowest classification accuracy - almost
4% behind the other two models. Given the Mamba model’s demonstrated underutilization of
metadata, it follows logically that its conditioned generation would be less effective, as measured
by the pretrained classifier’s ability to correctly identify the target composer.
Overall, these results indicate that, within the context of this study, xLSTM and Transformer
models are more effective at conditional generation and producing music that adheres more
closely to the characteristics of a specified composer compared to the Mamba model.
To effectively handle multi-instrumental MIDI data, this study proposed combining pitch and
instrument information into a unified ‘Instrumental Pitch’ token, enabling models to better learn
relationships between pitch ranges and instrument-specific musical tendencies. However, this
approach introduces an inherent trade-off: the expanded vocabulary size required for expressivity
and musical nuance increases computational and memory demands, while also causing data
sparsity - especially for less common instruments, which may be underrepresented in the dataset.
As a result, certain sequences risk being over-simplified or inadequately modeled. Ultimately, it
was a balancing act between achieving expressive power and robust representation of all token

types within the dataset.
In computational efficiency tests, it was found that Mamba’s inference during evaluation is 2.7
times slower than the Transformer and the xLSTM 2.12 times slower than the Transformer. This
difference arises from the model architectures with the Transformer being fully parallelizable,
the Mamba model in recurrence mode being non-parellizable, and the xLSTM experiencing only
partial parallelization.
However, this dynamic changes during training, where Mamba leverages a convolutional rep-
resentation, enabling full parallelization similar to the Transformer, while requiring fewer com-
putations O(LD log N) for Mamba versus O(L2D + L2) for the Transformer. Consequently,
Mamba becomes 55% faster than the Transformer during training. For both the xLSTM and
Transformer, the modest difference in performance between evaluation and training is largely
due to the computational overhead introduced by backpropagation during training. This shows
that at least epoch-wise, the Mamba model remains the fastest of the three models to train,
only improved upon increasing batch sizes.
The current approach of auto-regressively generating conditioned continuations based on an
input sequence leads to gradual error accumulation, which causes the generated output to in-
creasingly diverge from the original theme over time. This issue is particularly pronounced in the
Instrument Pitch token, where the model’s prediction accuracy hovers around 45%. However,
one could argue that such evolving continuations during the generative process are a desirable
feature of artificial music, allowing for exploration of new musical directions. It is also worth
noting that, on average, the original input sequence will fall outside the model’s context window
after approximately 30 seconds of generated continuation. Proposals to combat this accumula-
tive error are further explored in Section 8.3.
With the MIDI Classical Music Dataset’s lack of metadata, the method of generating metadata
through the use of ChatGPT, can be critiqued due to the lack of verification for its validity, as
well as the metadata being assigned at the composer level rather than piece level. This means
that all tracks attributed to a particular composer share identical metadata, including Composer
Name, Time Period, and Genres. Since many classical composers in the dataset had long careers
and produced works spanning multiple genres and time periods, this metadata assignment results
in reduced expressivity and may not accurately reflect the diversity of individual pieces. This is
discussed further in Section 8.1.
At the beginning of this study, additional datasets were considered to train upon, including the
LAKH MIDI Dataset (Raffel, 2016), which offers 45,129 MIDI tracks spanning a wide range

classical dataset. It should however be noted that these experiments were conducted prior to the
implementation of the PenalizeRepetitivity function; thus, revisiting the inclusion of such
datasets for training may still be worthwhile. This possibility is also discussed further in Section

Finally, it is important to consider the societal impact of conditioned generative music technolo-
gies, both in their current state and in the event that they reach a generative quality resembling
authentic human composition. One of the most promising aspects of this technology lies in its
potential as a creative tool for composers and musicians. By leveraging models fine-tuned on
specific genres, individual artists, or custom datasets, musicians can generate music that not
only spans genres, but also captures the stylistic essence of their favorite composers. For in-
stance, an artist seeking inspiration or new directions for a composition could use a conditional
music generating model to create multiple variations on a particular sequence, then selectively
integrate the most compelling parts into their own work. This capacity to offer diverse musical
ideas on demand can both stimulate creativity and help overcome creative blocks.
Given the output of symbolic music representation is directly decodable back into the MIDI
format, conditioned generative models can be directly integrated into Digital Audio Workstations
(DAWs) which would enable musicians to access and incorporate the models as tools seamlessly
within their existing workflows.
Unlike current fully-automated music generation aimed at
non-musicians, such as those seen at Suno.com, this technology is positioned to augment and
refine the skills of practicing musicians rather than to replace them. In this way, conditioned
generative music stands to enhance the creative process by providing musicians with new tools
and expanded possibilities.
Conditioned generative systems also open fascinating opportunities to revive and build upon the
styles of historical composers. With carefully curated training data, these models could authen-
tically replicate the techniques of deceased artists, potentially even assisting in the completion
of unfinished works or offering “what-if” reinterpretations of their best works, that enrich both
creative exploration and music education.
However, these advancements are accompanied by significant societal and ethical challenges. As
generative models approach human-level musical quality, their ability to convincingly replicate

Music generating models trained on extensive music datasets may unintentionally generate ma-

a model is trained to emulate the style of a living artist. Questions surrounding consent, fair

artificially generated music grows in prevalence.
Nevertheless, by acknowledging and addressing these challenges, we can ensure that artificial
intelligence develops as an integral and responsible part of future music creation tools - supple-

AI has the potential to expand creative possibilities, empower musicians, and inspire new levels
of musical expression and innovation. Ultimately, embracing this technology responsibly can
enrich the musical landscape for both creators and audiences alike.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to evaluate the effectiveness of advanced deep learning architectures-namely,
Transformer, xLSTM, and Mamba models-for the conditional generation of symbolic polyphonic
music. The investigation was structured around three central research questions: the design and
impact of symbolic encodings for multi-instrumental MIDI data; the implementation and utility
of conditioning mechanisms; and the establishment of robust evaluation protocols combining
quantitative and qualitative measures.
A novel musical encoding scheme was introduced in this study, building upon the REMI format
by adding an explicit Instrument event and merging it with the Note On event to form a
unified Instrument Pitch token. This innovation enabled the models to better represent the
complexities of multi-instrumental music and imposed an instrument-centric structure on the
encoded sequences. However, in striving to capture the expressive potential of the full range
of MIDI instruments, this approach significantly increased the size of the token vocabulary and
exacerbated data sparsity - particularly for less common instruments. This outcome underscores
the persistent trade-off between enhanced expressivity and practical model capacity.
Comparative analysis of the three architectures revealed that both the xLSTM and Transformer
models consistently outperformed the Mamba model in generating conditioned music, as mea-
sured by quantitative metrics (such as token-wise accuracy) and supported by listening test
results. Notably, in the absence of any conditioning mechanism, the Mamba model surpassed
both xLSTM and Transformer on these same evaluation criteria. Nevertheless, within the scope
of this study, xLSTM proved to be the strongest overall performer, achieving the highest subjec-
tive ratings for realism and musicality. Although the Mamba model’s accuracy was only slightly
lower in general token prediction, its weaker performance on the most critical tokens constrained
its expressive capability. Importantly, all models - including xLSTM - still exhibited a significant
gap compared to the musical quality of genuine human compositions.
In terms of computational efficiency, the xLSTM and Mamba models required significantly fewer
parameters and less memory than the Transformer, validating their attractiveness for resource-
constrained applications. Notably, the Mamba model was the fastest to train due to its fully
parallelized convolutional representation in training mode, but it was slower at inference due to
non-parallelizable recurrent computation. These results emphasize the ongoing trade-off between
efficiency and output quality in sequence modeling for symbolic music.
A key contribution of this work involved implementing conditional generation via prepending
Page 61 of 70
==PAGE:66==
Deep Learning Based Sequence Models for Music Generation
30.07.2025
learned metadata embeddings-including composer, period, and genre. This strategy permitted
all models to learn the stylistic influence of metadata. The comparative experiments, utilizing a
pretrained classifier to evaluate the success of conditioned generation, revealed that xLSTM and
Transformer effectively employed metadata to produce compositions stylistically consistent with
the target conditioning, as reflected in both improved classification accuracy and higher-quality
subjective listening scores. Conversely, the Mamba model demonstrated weaker utilization of
conditioning information resulting in lower classification accuracy and less compelling stylistic
adherence.
This thesis demonstrated the value of using a hybrid evaluation methodology.
Quantitative
assessment using token-wise and classification accuracy offered clear, objective indicators of
model performance and abilities to generate conditional music. Yet, these metrics alone could
not fully capture perceived musicality. Hence, qualitative listening tests were crucial in revealing
artifacts such as repetitiveness and lack of expressiveness, aspects otherwise overlooked by purely
numerical evaluation. The design of comparative listening tests provided nuanced insights into
the relative realism of model outputs.
To conclude, this study finds that among evaluated architectures, xLSTM with the proposed
token encoding and conditional metadata offers the best balance of expressive power, conditional
controllability, and computational efficiency for conditioned polyphonic symbolic music genera-
tion. While the current approach narrows the gap toward convincing artificial music, significant
challenges remain, particularly in addressing musical realism and expressive diversity.
The conditioning mechanism of prepended metadata embedding proved robust for Transformer
and xLSTM, but further innovation may be needed for models like Mamba, having proven
to make only limited use of the conditioned information during inference. Finally, the multi-
faceted evaluations introduced as part of this study establishes a comprehensive template for
future music generation research, encouraging continued advancement toward models that can
not only generate music efficiently and accurately, but also create pieces that stylistically capture
the authenticity of real-life composers.

Future Research
8.1
Expand Dataset And Metadata
Given the limited range of instruments and genres represented in the MIDI Classical Music
Dataset, a promising direction for future work is to expand the dataset to include sources such
as the LAKH MIDI Dataset, which features a broader variety of modern music spanning diverse
genres and time periods.
This expanded dataset would allow models to learn from a more
representative sample of musical styles beyond the classical domain.
The current approach also assigns metadata at the composer level rather than the track level,
which can result in oversimplification - particularly for composers whose works span multiple
styles and eras. Introducing track-level metadata would more accurately capture the nuances
of each individual piece, potentially enabling finer-grained conditional generation and stylistic
control.
Furthermore, the present inference setup allows models to introduce new instruments arbitrarily
during generation, which may not always be musically appropriate. To address this, it could
be beneficial to include track-specific instrumentation in the metadata, providing explicit guid-
ance about which instruments should be used in a given generation. Alternatively, the decoding
process could be refined to restrict the sampling to only those instruments present in the meta-
data, thus enhancing control over the ensemble used in the generated music. The choice of
how strictly to enforce these instrumentation constraints can be adapted based on the desired
balance between creative freedom and user control in the generation process.
8.2
Expand Encoding
Given that our encoding builds upon the REMI representation, a promising direction for future
work would be to incorporate the currently omitted Chord event. This could be achieved by
introducing instrument-specific chord tokens, which would expand the vocabulary by 129 × 60
tokens to accommodate 60 different chords for each of the 129 instruments. Alternatively, a
multi-instrument chord token could be implemented, which would increase the vocabulary by
only 60 tokens but at the cost of losing instrument-specific detail. Given the success of the
Pop Music Transformer (Huang and Yang, 2020) and Music Mamba (Chen et al., 2025) both
including the chord event, it feasibly could prove to outperform the current encoding scheme
proposed in this study.

8.3
Auto-Regressive Generation
Given that auto-regressive generation relies on predicting each new token based on previously
generated tokens, even small mistakes can accumulate over time, causing the generated sequence
to gradually diverge from the intended style or overarching theme of the original music. As a
result, the generation of extended sequences is prone to exposure bias and increased repeti-
tiveness. To address these issues in sequence generative modeling, the 2024 paper ”Diffusion
Forcing” (Chen et al., 2024) proposes a novel approach: during training, a diffusion model learns
to denoise sequences by recovering original tokens from contexts with varying levels of token-
specific noise. At inference time, the model at planned intervals applies this denoising process
to steer the sequence toward more musically plausible and coherent outputs, thereby preserving
thematic consistency and reducing repetition over long generations. Exploring this method in
future work on music generation could offer a solution to exposure bias and potentially eliminate
the need for heuristic techniques such as the PenalizeRepetitivity function.
8.4
Conditional Generation
The current approach of prepending metadata embeddings - representing Composer, Time Pe-
riod, and Genre - to each input sequence allowed the models to autonomously determine the
extent to which they incorporated this conditional information during generation. While this
strategy yielded strong results for the xLSTM and Transformer models, the Mamba model made
only limited use of the provided metadata, resulting in less effective conditional generation. To
enhance the conditioning capacity of the Mamba architecture, more explicit or “forceful” con-
ditioning methods should be explored. For instance, instead of supplying metadata only at the
input, the conditional information could be concatenated or injected at every layer of the model,
ensuring that the metadata consistently influences the generative process throughout all stages
of computation.
