Abstract
Offshore wind technology plays a major part in accelerating renewable energies to
forefront and make carbon neutral goals achievable. Cost effective studies in opti-
mising the future offshore wind farms is the need of the hour, with this in mind the
shared mooring and anchors were proposed under the CoreWind project.
This Master‚Äôs thesis presents a study on floating wind turbines with shared mooring
design. Initially, the dynamics of such a model are established through a MATLAB
model with 4 DOFs focused on unidirectional wind and waves load cases and decay
tests to determine the natural frequencies of the mode shapes involved. Following
this a HAWC2 model for the same is setup to validate and compare the fidelity of
the MATLAB model. In doing so the load case responses are analyzed. Further,
the single turbine baseline results of HAWC2 simulations are compared with that of
shared mooring design with two turbines to map the differences.
The key takeaways from this study was that the controller setup for single turbine
is slow when it comes to mitigating instabilities in the shared mooring design as the
turbines are coupled. This would require a more robust controller that takes input
from both floater displacements and acceleration to better regulate the motions. Also,
the preliminary fatigue analysis shows no excessive DELs when compared to that of
the single turbine model.

1.1
Motivation
Europe is striving and exploring on how to be carbon-neutral by the end of 2050. The
European green deal is at the heart of this aim in becoming the first climate neutral
continent. This would also be a major contributor to the Paris Climate Agreement
to restrict the worldwide temperature rise due to global warming to under 1.5 ‚ó¶C
[4]. Achieving this goal requires major transformation of the energy and electricity
system by replacing conventional sources with renewables (wind, wave, solar, hydro,
tidal etc.) wherever feasible. Offshore wind energy is the most promising way to assist
this objective. The target is to scale up the existing
25 GW offshore installations
upto 450 GW making it a crucial factor alongside onshore wind [5]. The North Sea
is the most ideal candidate for such large scale wind resource extraction. But, this
also requires the utilisation of Floating wind turbines (FWT) to cover regions that
are beyond the scope of traditional monopiles.

On average only 7 % of the ocean is classified as shallow i.e. under 200m that is
suitable for bottom fixed wind turbines [].
Hence, the necessity for expansion of
FWT technology plays a crucial role in attempting to reach the aforementioned goals.
Not only are FWT much larger in capacity compared to the onshore counterparts they
can be located much further away from society. The wind resource is significantly
higher as well further away from mainlands as well as large spanning areas to reduce
wake effects. Specifically in case of Europe the North sea has large areas of potential
sites for floating offshore wind farms despite the exclusion zones and nature reserves.
A lot of research and investigation is being done into FWT to optimize and make
the turbines more cost eÔ¨Äicient alongside this, the advent of better technologies for
transport facilities and heavy lift cranes has made them even more feasible.
On
a commercial scale two floating wind farms exist in europe: The 30 MW Hywind
Scotland and the 24MW Windfloat Atlantic project in Portugal. These wind farms
have a really high levelised cost of energy (LCoE)
‚ā¨180/MWh while that of fixed
offshore wind farm is much lower at ‚ā¨60-70/MWh. Future predictions expect the
LCoE of floating wind farms to drop to under ‚ā¨100/MWh. With the use of optimal
O&M strategies the potential for floating wind farms is highly promising.
Cost of floating structures however still remains a major cause for high LCoE due to
the necessity for more compliant support structures to control the dynamic motions
of wind turbine. The Shared mooring lines concept between adjacent wind turbines is
a novel strategy that aims to cut down on the LCOE of FWT. This not only reduces
the cable costs for mooring lines but also reduces the overall number of anchors
required in case of a windfarm [6]. But, the dynamics of such configurations need
to be properly analysed also, the response of such a system to different load cases is
important in understanding the stability and performance of the system.

CHAPTER 2

2.1
State of the art in FWT
In comparison to land, the ocean wind resources are significantly greater and one
study postulated that 3 million sq.km of open ocean area in the windy North Atlantic
could produce enough energy to supply the entire world‚Äôs electricity needs [7]. To
undertake such projects to setup wind farms further away from shore it becomes
essential to utilize the growing technology of FWT which can offer several major
benefits over the fixed bottom wind turbines.
FWTs offers significant expansion
possibilities further into the oceans with higher wind potential and at the same time
it also negates visual clutter that onshore and near shore turbines cause. The potential
low environmental impact and ease of assembly and installation as they can be built
on land and transported easily into the ocean. This is mainly possible due to already
available technologies in the marine oil and gas industry.
2.1.1
Principle behind floating wind turbines
A FWT is able to float chiefly due to a floater which is based on the Archimedes
principle-‚ÄĚa body totally or partially submerged in water experiences a vertical up-
ward thrust equal to weight of the water displaced‚ÄĚ. This floater is the concrete, steel
or hybrid substructure on which the wind turbine is installed, providing the FWT
with buoyancy and stability. Alongside the floater further stability is provided by
the mooring and anchor system as well as the aerodynamic damping caused when
the aerodynamic thrust on the wind turbine creates overturning moment. In addition
to floating, wind turbines must maximize energy production for this it is important
to have as high a stability as possible. Minimising movement and optimal operating
conditions are essential to proper FWT functioning, this is where the different types
of floating platforms come into play which will be discussed below.

Types of Floating Platforms
The choice of a floating platform of one type or another will depend on the sea and
seabed conditions as well as the wind turbine capacity, wind climate, infrastructure
at the nearby harbour and material and equipment availability. The main types of
floating substructures are shown in Fig. 2.1 and detailed as follows:

2.1.3
Mooring systems
The mooring system for FWT are a derivative of the oil and gas industry technology
just like the offshore platforms. Mooring systems set floating platforms at specific
locations, transferring external forces to the seabed to prevent displacements and
provide damping to the motions of wind turbines.
The various types of mooring
systems are described below and shown in Fig. 2.2.
‚ÄĘ Catenary Line. This mooring line consists of a steel chain ideally suitable for
shallow to moderately deep water. In this system part of the chain rests on the
seabed with anchor point that is designed to withstand horizontal forces and
weaker in vertical direction. The restoring force is achieved through the weight
of the chain due to this the length of chain is longer than the water depth as
seen in the figure above. To overcome the issue of heavy chains synthetic ropes
were developed which extended the depth range for catenary mooring lines.
‚ÄĘ Taut line mooring.
Taut mooring lines are pre tensioned synthetic ropes
with an aim to reduce the footprint of the seabed area compared to catenary
mooring as well as the cable length and weight. The stiffness overall for this
mooring line is higher increasing the restrictions on the movement of the floating
platform at the same time reducing the cost and weight of the mooring. Hence,

2.2
Floating wind turbine motion and dynamics
When transitioning from land based wind turbine model to a floating wind turbine the
range of motions of the wind turbine are increased. They are unique in that regard
in having rigid body motion as the structure is not fixed or restricted in any manner.
Thus there are 6 rigid body modes of motion i.e. 6 degrees of freedom (D0F) which
is demonstrated in the below Fig. 2.3 namely translational modes surge, sway and
heave as well as rotational modes pitch, roll and yaw. These 6 DOF are never fully
independent motions, there is a certain extent of coupling that exists which influences
secondary motions in response to the primary rigid motion. The linearized equation
of motions from Newton‚Äôs second law can be expressed in matrix form as follows :

2.2.1
Hydrodynamics and Wave Forcing
The primary means of wave generation in seas is through wind, when a gust of wind
blows across the sea surface it induces ripples/waves. One method to capture and
observe sea state/waves is to consider a single point in time on the sea surface and
measure the surface elevation at this point over time to generate a wave signal. Some
important wave parameters can be established using the time series as well as the
wave spectrum. This type of wave modelling is called the linear wave theory or airy
wave theory which gives a linearized description of gravity wave propagation wherein
the fluid flow is inviscid, incompressible and irrotational.

Irregular waves
A more realistic sea state driven by wind is one in which waves are irregular in
nature. Such irregular waves are essentially a superposition of many different regular
waves with varying frequencies. A power spectral density (PSD) or spectrum is a
representation of wave signal in the frequency domain obtained by performing fast
fourier transformation (FFT). PSD is usally plotted against frequency to represent
how the energy from waves is distributed in a wide range of frequencies. It is possible
to reconstruct the wave signal from the PSD using inverse FFT but such generation
of time series is never the same copy but it retains the same spectral parameters.
A wave spectrum can be fitted to match specific curves, one such spectral shape is
the Pierson-Moskowitz(PM) wave spectrum. The PM spectra was generated by long
period of measurements conducted in the Atlantic ocean under constant sea states.

5.1
Single turbine load case comparisons
This section deals with the comparison and analysis of the response attributed to
linearized dynamic model of the FWT versus a fully fledged practical HAWC2 model.
HAWC2 is an aero-servo-elastic wind turbine analysis code.
It implements linear
timoshenko beams for multi body dynamic formulation of the wind turbine structure.
The floater properties are defined through WAMIT . The aerodynamic loads are
computed through BEM formulation [9]. The controller and mooring line dynamics
are computed by a dll also developed by DTU.
To start with decay tests are performed in surge and pitch motions with the intent
to capture and investigate the responses of the two systems and verify if the models
have converging natural frequencies. Subsequently, the behaviour of the numerical
model is validated by comparing it with the HAWC2 single turbine floater response
under wind, wave and step wind load cases.
In all load cases the wind and waves approach from the surge direction and thus the
main response channels analysed are surge and pitch. Under static equilibrium the
windcrete FWT is not located at the orgin, but instead has a surge offset of -1.01
m and pitch of ‚ąí0.64‚ó¶. This can be attributed to the fact that the COG of RNA is
-7.01925 m, this overhang results in the negative static pitch. Also the tension in the
mooring line contributes to the surge offset as well.
5.1.1
Free Decay tests
The objective is to determine the dynamic properties of the floater mainly in surge
and pitch as these are relevant DOFs to compare with the dynamic model from the
previous two sections. One additional factor that can be tweaked in the numerical
model is that the linear damping of the structure is adjustable, for the following
the decay tests the damping was adjusted in order to match the response of the
HAWC2 simulation.
In addition the initial surge and pitch offset is negated for
better comparability. An initial transient time of 300s is truncated to ensure valid
responses are recorded.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Wind energy industry has seen rapid growth and needs to ensure new studies are
conducted to contribute to its expansion. Crucially FWT play a key role in promoting
this sector.
Optimisation of FWT turbines with focus on cost reduction was the
driving factor in establishment of the shared mooring concept. Thus, shared mooring
designs can contribute to even better economic viability of offshore wind turbines.
This Master‚Äôs thesis aimed to study and discern the dynamics involved in a single
turbine floater, to then allow for creating a 4 DOF shared mooring concept with two
turbines in MATLAB with the focus of understanding the response of this model. In
this regard, preliminary decay tests and load cases were conducted to set a baseline
through the MATLAB code. The load cases involved unidirectional wind and sea
states with primary goal to accurately predict the natural frequency of the system in
surge and pitch DOF.
The IEA 15 MW turbine was used as reference wind turbine throughout this project.
The next stage setup a preliminary single turbine HAWC2 model in order to validate
the studies from the analytical model. Upon comparison, these two results showed
that for simple load cases and decay tests the approaches corroborate one another.
But, under turbulent and step wind cases the two models diverge in results. This was
mainly due to a simple wind forcing model adopted for the numerical model which
does not account for aerodynamic damping. Also, the assumption that the structural
damping of turbine is linear has its flaws as it cannot replicate the real inertia of the
HAWC2 model.
For the next step the 4 DOF shared mooring design‚Äôs validity was tested by comparing
it with the HAWC2 version of the design. In this case the numerical model was able
to tally very well in terms of decay tests and concerned natural frequencies of surge
and pitch were predicted fairly accurately.
Under wave load cases the MATLAB
model roughly estimates the same response as that of the HAWC2 simulations. The
complete morison model in HAWC2 and MATLAB are very similar and hence, their
responses are well matched. The MATLAB model does not incorporate the negative
aerodynamic effect which leads to floater pitch instability and neither does it employ
the BEM formulation for wind forcing. Thus, step wind simulations diverge in this
regard. Thus the fundamental dynamics of a shared mooring design are established.
The next part involved comparing and evaluating the shared mooring design with its
baseline single turbine response. Under wave loading the two systems‚Äô responses are
fairly similar. The real difference is observed under step wind simulation where the
