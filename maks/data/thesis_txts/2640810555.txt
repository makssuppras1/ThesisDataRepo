ABSTRACT
This study uses size based ecological theory to describe the asymptotic size structure of the North Sea
ﬁsh community. In Sections(2-4) data from IBTS-survey hauls, outputs from the Stochastic Multispecies
Model, and ICES stock assessments is compared with the Extended Sheldon Conjecture which predicts a
linear, slightly decreasing asymptotic size spectrum in log-log-space. Contrary to the theoretical prediction,
the data is best described by a quadratic function with 1st and second order parameters a = 0.27±0.22
and b = −0.16±0.05, corresponding to a maximum biomass at asymptotic size Win f,max =3.4kg with a
factor 3 uncertainty. The analysis was challenged by the low number of species making up the majority
of the biomass, which means that the spectrum is dominated by single species rather than community
effects, even at fairly low resolutions. The deviation of the spectrum from the expected linear slope
may be explicable by confounding of the ﬁsh community with the macrobenthos for lower size ranges.
Focusing only on the range from 0.5 kg to 60 kg - which was argued to be less confounded with the
macrobenthos - showed a linear decrease in the asymptotic size spectrum in the one data set that was
viewed. The slightly negative slope was consistent with a modeled scenario, but based purely on visual
inspection. It was also shown that the current asymptotic size spectrum is very similar to the one that
would result if and when all North Sea stocks are at MSY reference levels. Finally, the data showed a
sharp decline in biomass of several orders of magnitude for asymptotic size classes larger than cod. The
trend was visible in both IBTS and SMS data sets that include both mammals and ﬁsh larger than cod. In
Section(5) a ﬁshing mortality map is applied based on high resolution (to ICES C-square) trawl effort data
with the only other required input variable being a species/gear speciﬁc catchability. It was found that the
mean and median mixed ﬁsheries mortalities of ﬁshed areas in the North Sea are Fmean = 0.24±0.05/year
and Fmedian = 0.05±0.01/year based on catchabilities of q = 0.5±0.1. These values may be overrated by
up to ≈30% due to missing 0-values in the trawl effort data set. The large difference between mean and
median ﬁshing mortality shows the highly uneven distribution of ﬁshing effort, highlighting the need for
knowledge of species habitat use for their effective assessment. In Section(6), it was shown that trait-
and individual based population theory predicts a maximum viable asymptotic size for species in an area
of mixed ﬁsheries based on two variables: ﬁshing mortality and gear selection size. The model was
tailored to incorporate both teleosts and elasmobranchs. Apart from a sanity check in which three stocks
of cod were predicted to be viable under MSY ﬁshery, the model was not calibrated, and so predictions
are highly uncertain. Using realistic input values for the North Sea, it was predicted that mostly stationary
teleosts without speciﬁc habitat requirements are not limited in maximum asymptotic size under current
mixed ﬁshery mortality levels. For highly mobile teleosts, predictions of maximum viable asymptotic size
ranged from >1000kg to ≈25kg depending on their catchability. Mostly stationary elasmobranchs could
be viable for asymptotic sizes up to 16kg for very low catchabilities (q = 0.2), whereas highly mobile
elasmobranchs were predicted to be unviable for sizes above ≈2kg.

1 INTRODUCTION
In a game of 1 question for the professor in which we are allowed to ask only a single question about a
ﬁsh, hidden to us beneath a shimmering sea surface, which question would we ask to obtain the maximum
amount of information about this ﬁsh and its interaction with the environment? A good bid is how big
is it? because the answer to this question alone tells us a) what (and how many) other ﬁsh is it likely to
eat and b) what other ﬁsh will try to eat it? In Darwinian terms we get access to information about two
of three main imperatives of an organism, namely feed and survive. Suppose we were then allowed to
ask just one more question, what would that question be? By making the next question how big can it
get? we would now gain information about the third Darwinian imperative of reproduce, because a ﬁsh at
some large enough fraction of its maximum potential size is most likely fecund and vice versa. This is the
central idea behind size-based theory as synthesized in the book Fish Ecology, Evolution, and Exploitation
by [Andersen (2019)]. The book is an application to ﬁsheries science of the broader theoretical framework
of trait-based ecology, in which the key modeling units are individual organisms and their interactions
with each other based on traits rather than taxonomically identiﬁed groups and prescribed interactions
[Kiørboe et al. (2018)]. The theory revolves around the simple assumption that big ﬁsh eat small ﬁsh. This
may sound trivial at ﬁrst listen, but is actually a profoundly structuring relationship in all food webs, but
marine food webs in particular [Jennings and Mackinson (2003)]. On land it would be extremely unlikely
to see a fox eat a lion, but in the ocean, a herring may very well eat a blueﬁn tuna. That is because even
the biggest teleost ﬁsh start their life stages as microscopic ﬁsh eggs and larvae, exposed to predation
even from plankton! Because of the preference of ﬁsh for eating other ﬁsh at a particular (smaller) ratio
of their own body size, the amount of organisms in one trophic level determines the possible amount in
the next and so on. Through size based theory, the organisms’ energy requirements, predation rate etc., all
expressed in terms of individual size, tell us with surprising accuracy how many predators a given prey
abundance can support. In other words, individual organism interactions and physiology, describe the
size spectrum, which is the abundance or biomass B of organisms as a function of their body size W. In
1972, Sheldon conjectured, that the combined biomass of all organisms of a particular size group was
approximately the same as the combined biomass of any other size group. In other words, if you ﬁnd a ton
of life in the ocean, it might almost as likely be a whale as a thousand 1 kilo ﬁsh, which is expressed as:
log(B) = −A·log(W)+C
where A is a small, positive constant and C is an ecosystem speciﬁc scaling factor for the overall biomass
in the system [Sheldon et al. (1972), Boudreau and Dickie (1992), Thiebaux and Dickie (1992)].
By describing organisms with a few well chosen traits such as size and asymptotic size, population,
community and even ecosystem dynamics with its myriad organisms and myriad-squared interactions,
can be described as surprisingly low dimensionality problems. To some extent, the ﬂow of information
can also be reversed, that is, by looking at community and ecosystem metrics we can deduce information
about populations or individuals that inhabit them. For instance, if we look at a size spectrum of a ﬁsh
community and notice a very high abundance of large ﬁsh and a low abundance of small ﬁsh, we can
deduce that small ﬁsh experience a high predation pressure, which applies to both juvenile ﬁsh of large
species and mature ﬁsh of small species alike.
1.1 Fisheries and size spectra1
Because the size of organisms in marine communities are so tightly linked to trophic interactions,
altering the size structure of a community can have serious consequences for ecosystem functioning. In
extreme cases, depletion of large predatory ﬁsh, such as by ﬁshing, can lead to trophic cascades where
disappearance of large ﬁsh lead to lowered predation pressure on midrange size ﬁsh, which in turn lead
to increased predation on small ﬁsh and so on. Such effects may cause not only changes in species
composition, but may even shift taxonomic dominance (ie. ﬁsh to jellyﬁsh), and alter physical habitat
properties (higher turbidity due to lowered algal grazing) [Daskalov et al. (2007), Casini et al. (2008),
M¨ollmann et al. (2008), Ramcharan et al. (1996)]. In the less extreme case, depletion of older and larger
ﬁsh of individual populations, can lead to increased turnover rates in marine communities making them
more volatile to interannual variation and environmental changes [Jennings and Blanchard (2004)].

Unfortunately it is the case that ﬁshing has a tendency to alter the size spectrum of marine communities
towards smaller sizes and higher volatility. Based on a modeling study, [Christensen et al. (2014)] reports
a global decline of 54% in large predatory ﬁsh over the last 100 years. For the North Sea [Jennings and
Blanchard (2004)] report a 99% decline in ﬁsh over 16 kg compared to a modeled unﬁshed baseline.
Based on historical landings and quantiﬁcations of testimonies of British ﬁsheries, reductions of several
large species such as Halibut and Cod are estimated around 90% since the onset of industrial ﬁshing
[Plumeridge and Roberts (2017), Thurstan et al. (2010)]. While such numbers are inherently uncertain
due to the limited availability of high quality data prior to industrial ﬁshing, they do however paint a
convincing picture of a general decline of large ﬁsh.
One of the most commonly cited reasons for the decline of large ﬁsh is that of ﬁshing down marine
food webs [Pauly et al. (1998)]. This is mainly described as a culturally driven phenomenon where large
ﬁsh are targeted ﬁrst for their higher economic and cultural value as well as for their ease of capture
leading to biomass declines that mainly affect higher trophic levels.
While the single stock management regime based on maximum sustainable yield (MSY) has been
instrumental in the successful management and recovery of ﬁsh stocks around the world [Hilborn et al.
(2020)], the implementation of this framework in most places ironically also promote the alterations of
community size structure discussed above [Garcia et al. (2012)]. The reason is the implementation of
minimum conservation reference sizes (MCRS) in most places, which means that large ﬁsh are landed
and small ﬁsh are left in the sea. Some studies have even shown that unmanaged ﬁsheries are actually
better at preserving community size spectra than traditional management, at least in a non-commercial
setting [Zhou et al. (2019)].
1.2 Balanced harvest and trait-based models
Ecosystem based ﬁsheries management (EBFM) is a management approach that seeks to manage natural
resources while conserving ecosystem integrity, and is formally inscribed in the European Common
Fisheries Policy (CFP) [EU (2014)]. While the intentions are there, it should not be controversial to say that
EBFM still has not been fully implemented. A study by [Trochta et al. (2018)] found large discrepancies
between the conceptions of what EBFM even mean for scientists, managers, and stakeholders. Since
the CFP states that EBFM needs to ”... safeguard the composition, structure and functioning of the
habitats...”, and since it is well documented that size structure is the main factor determining the food
availability and predation risk for ﬁsh in a habitat, EBFM must seek to preserve size structure [EU (2014)].
Balanced harvest is a branch of EBFM which seeks to do exactly this. In a deﬁnition by [Zhou et al.
(2019)], balanced harvest seeks to:
(1) minimize signiﬁcant adverse ﬁshing impact on the relative size and species composition
of aquatic ecosystems, thereby minimising impacts on ecosystem structure and functioning,
and (2) increase or maintain sustainable yield from such ecosystems (with no attempt to
maximize yield by ignoring the ﬁrst goal).
Because of the simplicity and broad applicability of size spectrum models, it is not surprising that they are
key components of a balanced harvest framework. Particularly the heights and slopes of size spectra are
relevant, especially (but not necessarily) when they can be combined with reference values for preserved
community integrity [Garcia et al. (2015a)]. Enter stage, Sheldon Conjecture.
While the Sheldon Conjecture does provide a baseline for ﬁsh community integrity, reality is always
messier than theory. A number of empirical and theoretical studies have shown that the linear trend of size
spectra are often perturbed by or constructed from periodic dome-like shapes . This can occur naturally
or be the sign of potentially dangerous overexploitation and onsetting trophic cascades [Shin and Cury
(2004), Andersen and Pedersen (2009), Jung and Houde (2005), Sprules and Goyke (1994), Thiebaux
and Dickie (1992)]. Telling one scenario from the other requires knowledge of the system at hand, and is
hard to put in a one size ﬁts all framework. Another drawback of size based inicators (SBIs) to which
size spectrum parameters belong, is that they are relatively slow to respond to changes in ﬁshing patterns
(5-10 years’ lag) and therefore are generally considered more as strategic (long term) rather than tactical
(short term) management tools [Daan et al. (2005), Shin et al. (2005), Garcia et al. (2015b)].
1.3 The Extended Sheldon Conjecture
In a 2006 paper, Andersen and Beyer makes an extension to the Sheldon Conjecture by showing that
the size based theory from which it emerges, predicts another structuring relation. Not only is the
5/45
==PAGE:6==
biomass contained within logarithmic size classes constant across orders of magnitude, the same is true
for asymptotic sizes Win f , which is the size that individuals of a particular species will grow towards,
given a very long life [Andersen and Beyer (2006)]. That means
log(B) = −Elog(Winf )+D
where E ≈0.05 is a constant determined by species independent physiological parameters and D is an
ecosystem speciﬁc constant. The potential discovery of such a profound structuring property in marine
ecosystems would in and of itself be highly valuable with implications still open to discover. For this
reason one of the main aims of this study is to
Test and apply the Extended Sheldon Conjecture to the North Sea ﬁsh community
(Speciﬁc research objectives follow at the bottom of the section)
Furthermore, assuming that the Extended Sheldon Conjecture does in fact provide a good underlying
theory for the trait-structure of an unexploited ﬁsh community, it will be investigated how the North Sea
ﬁsh community would compare to its predictions if all stocks were at BMSYlevels.
In a management context, it is still highly speculative what the potential new insights that the Extended
Sheldon Conjecture could provide relative to ordinary size spectrum theory. After all, trophic interactions
are governed by size, not asymptotic size. For the sake of argument an observation is presented on
admittedly speculative grounds. Where the Sheldon conjecture presents an immediate snapshot of the size
spectrum, it gives us no information about where the size spectrum is headed. In combination with the
Sheldon Conjecture, the Extended Sheldon Conjecture may provide some kind of size potentiality that
can tell us where the size spectrum is going. Suppose for instant a size spectrum with very few large ﬁsh,
where the large species suddenly start improving due to highly succesful recruitments. This would not be
visible in an ordinary size spectrum because the big ﬁsh would need several years to grow all the way
across the size spectrum and into the high end. In the asymptotic size spectrum, the succesful recruitment
and growth of the individuals would be visible much earlier on, because they do not need to grow into the
high end of the spectrum to be counted. Again, on speculative grounds, this could actually compensate
for one of the before mentioned downsides to ordinary size spectra, which is their slow response time
[Daan et al. (2005), Shin et al. (2005), Garcia et al. (2015b)].
1.4 Trait-based models and large asymptotic size ﬁsh
Apart from the overall focus on the asymptotic size spectrum of ﬁsh communities, speciﬁc attention is
directed towards large asymptotic size ﬁsh. These would be expected to have been asymmetrically highly
affected by the discussed changes in ﬁsh community size composition. Furthermore, several studies have
shown that age at maturation is a good predictor in marine vertebrate vulnerability and even extirpation
risk, and so by correlation, body size as well [Juan-Jord´a et al. (2015), Anderson et al. (2011), Dulvy et al.
(2003)]. In a mixed ﬁsheries setting, such as the North Sea, large ﬁsh have yet another disadvantage of
being fully selected by ﬁshing gears through a longer part of their lifetime (particularly if not employing
bycatch reducing measures). Finally, cartilagenous ﬁsh are known to be especially vulnerable to ﬁshery
and make up an increasing part of the asymptotic size spectrum towards its high end [George (2009),
Andersen (2019), p. 138].
What all these observations have in common is that they relate particular traits to vulnerability.
Therefore, it appears highly sensible to use trait- and individual based population theory to try to describe
the underlying dynamics that render certain ﬁsh more vulnerable than others, and possibly affect the
(asymptotic) size spectrum. Lastly, since the species that grow to be as large as cod or larger, are of
comparatively small economic value due to low abundances, they are also much more data poor. This
makes trait-based theory further attractive since all relevant individual and population metrics can in
principle be inferred from traits. For single species assessment, this would of course be extremely
unreliable, but as the derivation of the Sheldon Conjecture shows, it can work very well on a community
level. Investigating the impact of mixed ﬁsheries on large data poor species in the North Sea therefore
motivates the second major objective of this thesis:
Can we predict a maximum viable asymptotic size in the North Sea for teleosts and elasmobranchs
using trait-based theory and a realistic mixed ﬁsheries scenario?

1.5 Research objectives
The aim of this thesis is to apply trait-based theory to the North Sea ﬁsh community to
1. Test whether the North Sea ﬁsh community is in accordance with the Extended Sheldon Conjecture
2. Test if the North Sea ﬁsh community would be in accorance with the Extended Sheldon Conjecture
if all stocks were at MSY-levels
3. Run a size based ﬁsh community model (Mizer [Scott et al. (2014)]) based on the theory which
predicts the Extended Sheldon Conjecture to see if it aligns with observations
Furthermore, particular attention will be paid to large asymptotic size ﬁsh by
1. Hypothesizing a background ﬁshing mortality for the North Sea Using VMS-based trawl effort
maps [ICES (2019)] and a mechanistic individual ﬁsh capture model.
2. Using trait-based modeling and the previously derived background ﬁshing mortality to predict a
maximum viable asymptotic size for teleosts and elasmobranchs in the North Sea.

2 IS THE NORTH SEA FISH COMMUNITY IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE
ESC?
The ﬁrst part of this thesis is dedicated to the testing and application of the Extended Sheldon Conjecture
and its discussion in a management context. In below section, the predictions of the theory is held up
against data for the North Sea ﬁsh community. While the Sheldon Conjecture has been demonstrated
empirically numerous times, in fact it was ﬁrst discovered as an empirical ﬁnding [Sheldon et al. (1972)],
the Extended Sheldon Conjecture has yet to withstand the test of empiricism [Andersen (2019), p. 188].
Holding the Extended Sheldon Conjecture up to real data from the North Sea is the objective of this ﬁrst
section.
2.1 Methods and materials
Three different data sets with species speciﬁc catch data from the North Sea were collected and used under
the assumption that they provide representative samples of the North Sea ﬁsh community (this assumption
will be discussed below). The data sets are listed below. All data is taken from the period of 2006 to 2016.
1) North Sea International Bottom Trawl Survey (NS-IBTS) Catch entries for 7997 NS-IBTS hauls
between 2006 and 2016 were extracted from the DATRAS database [ICES (2019a)] using R-
package ”DATRAS” [Berg (2018)]. 12 species make up 95% of the biomass. For an overview of
the data see Appendix(A) Figure(13 - 15).
2) ICES stock assessments 157 stocks of 54 different species labeled with ”Greater North Sea Ecore-
gion” as presented by the European Environmental Agency (EEA) as part of their environmental
indicator assessment [EEA (2020)]. This data set contains SSB as opposed to total biomass. The
data was labeled with ecoregion from the ICES SID database [ICES (2019c)] by the use of R-
package ”icesSD” [Millar et al. (2017)]. Most of the stocks were labeled with at least one other
Ecoregion label besides ”Greater North Sea”, for instance ”Celtic Seas Ecoregion” or ”Baltic Sea
Ecoregion”. As Sandeel was not labeled to a species level, ”Ammodytes Marinus” was used for all
stocks. For an overview of the data see Appendix(B) Table(6).
2.1) ICES stock assessments SSBMSYtrigger The same data as above, but this time only the stocks for
which SSBMSYtrigger was included. This data set contained 28 species and 82 stocks. For an overview
of the data see Appendix(B) Table(7).
3) Stochastic Multi Species (SMS)-model Total biomass outputs for all 17 ﬁsh species in the SMS-
model as presented by the ICES Working Group on Multispecies Assessment methods (WGSAM)
[ICES (2019)]. For an overview of the data see Appendix(C) Table(8).
For all data sets each species was labeled with with a maximum length by scraping the World Registry of
Marine Species (WoRMS)-database and extracting the maximum value listed under ”body size”. The
scraping was done using the worrms package [Chamberlain (2020)] in R [R Core Team (2013)]. Maximum
lengths were converted to maximum sizes using the canonic formula of W = b·La ﬁtted to 436284 ﬁsh in
the NS-IBTS dataset for which lengths and weights were available. See Appendix(D) Figure(16) for a
plot of the length weight ﬁt and ﬁtted parameters. Table(8 and 6) and Figure(13) in the appendix lists
Win f for the dominant species. All species were put into logarithmic bins based on their asymptotic size,
and the biomasses within each bin was summed. This gave 11 observations per bin, one for each year of
the study (2006-2016). The logarithmic bases used were 4, 2, and 1.5.
Combining the three different data sets
I aim to reduce biases in the results and compensate for the
speciﬁc weaknesses of either data set. The IBTS data set has the upside of covering both commercial
and non commercial species, and even non ﬁsh taxa, but has low selectivity for small ﬁsh. SMS and
assessment data sets cover fewer species, and only commercial ones, but since the assessments also
estimate the biomass of the smallest yearclasses, the problem of selectivity in IBTS is reduced. Looking
to Figure(14) in the Appendix, we see that commercial stocks make up the majority of the catches for
the IBTS as well. While the assessment data set covers more stocks than the SMS data set, it is more
eclectic as the ﬁsh are only partly conﬁned to the North Sea and most of the stocks have some unspeciﬁed
afﬁliation with other ecoregions as well. Combining the three data sets I expect to have a representative
sample of the North Sea ﬁsh community in terms of biomass.

Model ﬁt
The data sets were tested against three different empirical models namely 0th, 1st and 2nd
order polynomials. The best ﬁt was evaluated using two different goodness of ﬁt metrics, namely AICc
(Akaike Information Criterion corrected for few observations [Hurvich and Tsai (1989)] and adjusted
R2-value [Yin and Fan (2001)], both of which penalize overﬁtting. The models were ﬁt to the means
of the yearly observations for each bin and a weighting corresponding to the inverse of the variance of
observations between years.
Simple mean model - test for ﬂatline signal
If the Extended Sheldon Conjecture applies, we expect
a best ﬁt to a 0th order polynomial. However, a best ﬁt to a 0th order polynomial could mean anything
from a perfect low noise ﬂatline signal to random bin-speciﬁc biomasses with no correlation between bins
(both have R2 = 0). For this reason we perform an analysis of variance to test if the observations between
bins are drawn from the same distribution (equal means). Since ESC does in fact predict a slight decrease
as opposed to a complete ﬂatline, the data is transformed before analysis so that it would be completely
constant given ESC:
log(B)∗= log(B)+0.05·log(Wmax)
The range of the spectrum
that we will mainly focus on for our analysis is the range between the
smallest and the largest species included in all three data sets, namely sprat (60g) and cod (54.4kg). On
this range the asymptotic sizes fall well within the part of the size spectrum normally presumed to be
dominated by ﬁsh, which is 1g to 100kg [Andersen (2019), p. 135].

2.3 Discussion
The results indicate that the North Sea ﬁsh community is not in accordance with the ESC on the range
from 60g to 100kg, or if it is, it is masked in higher order effects. The choice between no model and a
parabolic model with 2nd and 1st order terms a = 0.27±0.22 and b = −0.16±0.05, depends on how
hard overﬁtting is judged. AICc suggests no model whereas adjusted R2 suggests a 2nd order model.

across the entire spectrum, I report a nonlinear negative effect of highly uncertain size and shape.

in the introduction. For a shelf sea such as the North Sea, one potential reason for the decrease in biomass
towards the low end of the spectrum could be coupling with the benthic community. [Greenstreet (1997)]
estimates that macrobenthic production is 3.8 times higher than that of ﬁsh in the North Sea and that the
macrobenthos consitutes up to 40% of ﬁsh diets in the benthos-to-demersal food web. Midrange size
ﬁsh would thus be able to appear in larger abundance than the availability of lower size classes sugest
since there is an alternative source of food. In a study by [Jennings et al. (2002)], benthic macrofauna was
found to constitute a signiﬁcant part of the biomass per m2 for size classes all the way up to 180g. In a
size spectrum study of the North Sea by [Blanchard et al. (2009)], predatory macrobenthos was included
in the size spectrum analysis along with ﬁsh. Thereore, investigating the coupling between the benthic
and ﬁsh communities would be an obvious next step in qualifying the results. While the IBTS data set
technically allows us to include more of the benthos in the study, selectivity is likely very low in this
range of the size spectrum. As a quick ﬁx, but expensive in data, we could also drop all size classes up to
the bin containing 180g, after which there was no more reported macrobenthos in the study by [Jennings
et al. (2002)]. Based on purely visual inspection of the range from 0.5kg to 100kg in Figure(2), it seems
plausible that there could be a linear trend. However, it is based on very few observations, and I did not
ﬁnd the time to verify it more rigorously.
The marked decrease in biomass for asymptotic sizes above cod in the IBTS data set is remarkable2.
Since there are teleost, cartillaginous, and mammalian species in this range, we have no a priori expectation
of why the Extended Sheldon Conjecture should not be applicable in this range (or the ordinary Sheldon
Conjecture for that matter). If anything it would be the contrary. [Jennings and Blanchard (2004)] argues
that the largest predators often have a comparatively larger predator to prey ratio (such as tuna feeding on
herring), and so if anything we would expect the slope to be less steep in the jump from large to very large
species since they have a comparatively higher availability of prey. Since marine mammals are expected
to make up an increasingly large part of the (asymptotic size spectrum from around Winf ≈100kg),
the biomasses of harbor porpoises and grey seals in the SMS model were extracted. These biomasses
combined turned out to be 2.6, 2.1 and 1.4 orders of magnitude lower than the lowest bin with ﬁsh using
log-binning with bases 4, 2, and 1.5. Considering that some of the bins are already almost empty for low
bin sizes (reﬂecting the ﬁnite number of species in the data set), I argue that the biomass of species larger
than cod in the SMS model is ⪆2 orders of magnitudes lower than the least dominant asymptotic size
class of ﬁsh. The IBTS and SMS data therefore agree that there is no biomass of any marine organism
larger than cod worth speaking of.
In terms of using the Extended Sheldon Conjecture as a size based indicator, a big drawback is
certainly the limited number of species making up the majority of biomass in each of the size classes. The
SMS data set is constituted by only 17 species, and so any high resolution binning is prone to depend
much more on single species effects rather than community effects. As can be seen from Figure(13-15) in
Appendix(A), even the most species rich data set (IBTS) is dominated by 10-15 species. This could very
well explain the high level of noise that we observe in the data set when decreasing bin size.

4 CAN A TRAITBASED MODEL DESCRIBE THE ASYMPTOTIC SIZE SPEC-
TRUM IN THE NORTH SEA?
As previously discussed, the size spectrum can assume various shapes apart from strictly constant decrease.
Especially under the inﬂuence of external factors such as ﬁsheries. Due to the obvious connections between
size spectrum and asymptotic size spectrum, it is reasonable to look for similar patterns in asymptotic size
spectrum as seen in size spectrum. In order to see if ﬁshery could cause the asymptotic size spectra to
attain the dome like shape as seen in the previous sections, a size based ﬁsh community model was run
with a realistic North Sea ﬁshing scenario and compared with data.
4.1 Methods and materials
A ﬁsh community model for the North Sea was implemented in the multispecies size spectrum model,
Mizer [Scott et al. (2014)]. The model was run with the below scenarios:
1. Scenario 1, Self contained ﬁsh community : Fish are consumers feeding on each others, and
small ﬁsh also prey on a resource spectrum with a maximum body size of 0.5g. Mortality comes
mainly from modeled predation. A universal background mortality is set to 0.01/year. There is no
starvation.
2. Scenario 2, Embedded ﬁsh community : The resource spectrum spans the entire width of the
ﬁsh size spectrum meaning that also big ﬁsh prey on it, thereby simulating feeding on the benthos.
External mortality is size dependent based on [Andersen, 2019, p. 169] with µ0 = 0.16g1−n/year.
This corresponds to a ﬁsh of 100g having a background mortality of 0.05/year. There is no
starvation.
Apart from the above, all parameter values used were the same as those reported by [Hartvig et al. (2011)],
which are based on the North Sea. Unfortunately, using the exact same values produced unstable results
(lots of extinctions), which was the main motivation of changing them to the values listed above. For
reference, [Hartvig et al. (2011)] uses a size dependent background mortality set at 0.1 year−1 for ﬁsh
of 5 kg [for details see Andersen (2019) p. 169], and a starvation mortality which kicks in after energy
reserves of 10% of body weight have been used. For details of models, see Appendix(F) Figure(18-19).
The model was run before the SMS and assessment data sets got included in the thesis work, so the results
are only compared to IBTS.
Modeling ﬁshing scenario
A ﬁshing scenario was implemented in which the ﬁshing mortality F depends both on size (modeling
selectivity) and asymptotic size (modeling ﬁsheries targeting speciﬁc species). The ﬁshing scenario was
implemented based on three assumptions:
1. Winf <57.7kg : The majority of ﬁsh within this size range are subjected to a targeted ﬁshery, and
F(Win f ) can be interpolated from ICES estimated values of ﬁshing mortalities of the dominant
species within each bin
2. Winf >57.7kg : These asymptotic size classes are caught as bycatch in the demersal ﬁshery and
their ﬁshing mortality can be estimated based on bottom trawl swept area ratio (SAR) for the North
Sea
3. NS-IBTS catches provide representative samples of the North Sea ﬁsh community asymptotic size
spectrum
Assumption 1 and 3 : F(Winf <57.7kg) can be interpolated from known ICES values
The assumption is that the majority of the biomass within these size classes are made up by commercially
important species, which are subjected to a targeted ﬁshery with a known ﬁshing mortality. That this is
indeed the case is indicated by Table(4) where the dominant species of NS-IBTS catches can be seen
along with ﬁshing mortalities based on stock assessments. Until a bin upper limit of 57.7kg, the majority
of the catch is made up by a single species for which an ICES estimated ﬁshing mortality is available.
The interpolation of ﬁshing mortalities from commercially important stocks was done at an early stage
of the study where the lower bins were not yet separated. Therefore the lowest bin in Table(4) actually
contains 8 bins in the range from 58g to 1.5kg. The bins were separated for the actual modelling, but with
all 8 lower bins sharing the same ﬁshing mortality.

4.2 Results
The modeling study showed two qualitatively different results based on the chosen scenario. Both models
are compared only to the IBTS data, as the SMS and assessment data sets were not yet included in the
study at the time of the modeling.
Scenario 1
where the ﬁsh only feed on each other, except for the smallest size classes that also prey on
a resource spectrum, can be seen in Figure(6). It shows very small deviations between ﬁshed and unﬁshed
scenario with the notable exception of very large asymptotic size ﬁsh (bigger than cod). These were
markedly depressed. For the rest of the spectrum, the trend follows a small linear decline as predicted
by the Extended Sheldon Conjecture. The linear part ﬁts data reasonably well for midrange size ﬁsh,
especially for large bin size.
Scenario 2
where all ﬁsh are allowed to feed on the resource spectrum (signifying feeding on the
benthos by large ﬁsh such as cod and ﬂatﬁsh), gave rise to trophic cascades in the ﬁshed scenario. At the
lower bin sizes, the model apparently ﬁtted data better, whereas the ﬁt appeared worse at higher bin sizes.
The unﬁshed scenario also produced asymptotic size spectrum oscillations, but smaller.
4.3 Discussion
Due to the large discrepancies in the two scenarios and the fact that one ﬁts better for low bin sizes and
another ﬁts better for high bin sizes, I decided to look for complimentary data sets to the IBTS. The idea
being that I needed a more reliable data source to compare the model to. This is when the SMS and ICES
assessment data sets got included in the thesis work. Unfortunately, I did not have the time in the end to
return to the modeling study, and conclusions drawn from it should be taken very cautiously.
One interesting observation in Figure(6) is that the data and modeling scenario 1 (the one where
large ﬁsh only prey on ﬁsh) suggest a linearly decreasing trend on the range from around 0.5kg to 64 kg.
This is the same range that was discussed in Section(2) as the one most surely unconfounded with the
macrobenthos. This could suggest that the linear decrease in this part of the asymptotic size spectrum is
actually a manifestation of the Extended Sheldon Conjecture.
In parallel with this, scenario 2, where also big ﬁsh are allowed to feed on the resource spectrum
(benthos) produces oscilations in the spectrum, but at a higher frequency than the parabola most reliably
observed across all three data sets. Returning to the discussion about the confounding with the mac-
robenthos from Section(2), instead of the resource spectrum running the entire range of Winf , it would be
interesting to run a new modeling scenario with a cut-off at 180g, corresponding to the upper limit found
by [Jennings et al. (2002)].
It is also worth noting that both scenarios produced orders of magnitude lower biomasses of asymptotic
size classes larger than cod when ﬁshing was applied relative to the unﬁshed case. The ﬁrst scenario
however does not predict an actual decrease in biomass in this part of the spectrum, but rather a leveling
of what would otherwise have been a positive slope. The depression of large ﬁsh might be exaggerated
due to an overestimation of ﬁshing mortality and catchability as discussed in Section(5).
Apart from a new resource spectrum cut-off, going forward, it would make sense to include the SMS
and assessment data sets as model targets and try to calibrate the models to the observed patterns. It could
then be discussed whether the range of parameters that agree with the data is broad and realistic enough
to consider the model a useful underlying theory.

5 DERIVING A DEMERSAL FISHING MORTALITY FOR BIG FISH IN THE
NORTH SEA
In this section I apply a high resolution ﬁshing mortality map of the North Sea based on trawl effort maps
and a probabilistic individual ﬁsh capture model. The method is motivated by our need for assessing
the ﬁshing mortality of large asymptotic size ﬁsh in the modeling study in Section(4). While the use
case in this scnario is large ﬁsh, the model is general enough to be applicable to smaller ﬁsh as well.
With up to 94% of mixed ﬁsheries catches originating from bottom towed ﬁshing gears [ICES (2019b)],
such a map may be useful in assessing the effect of ﬁsheries on data poor species as necessitated under
an ecosystem approach to ﬁsheries management. Similar models have been presented, but usually at a
resolution of statistical rectangles [Piet et al. (2009), Walker et al. (2019)]. The recent availability of
effort maps at C-square resolution [ICES (2019)], presents an exciting opportunity for the advancement
of ﬁshing mortality maps. Apart from assessment of data poor species, use cases include habitat speciﬁc
stressor evaluation and protection of vulnerable species.
5.1 Methods and materials
Consider a population of ﬁsh within an ICES C-square. Each year a fraction of the total area within
the square gets swept by bottom towed ﬁshing gears. This fraction, called SAR for swept area ratio, is
calculated by ICES every year based on VMS data and compiled into a map as can be seen in Figure(8)
[ICES (2019)]. As we shall see SAR is equivalent to ﬁshing mortality for gear/species combinations with
100% catchability.3 We will now use SAR to calculate the probability of ﬁsh being captured within the
C-square and use this result to estimate a ﬁshing mortality.

5.3 Discussion
The factor 5 deviation between ﬁshing mortalities obtained when using mean vs. median of SAR highlights
the uneven distribution of trawl effort in the North Sea. It also shows that the applicability of this method
for the assessments of datapoor species is greatly enhanched if knowledge of species habitat use is
available. A highly mobile ﬁsh that frequently visits all levels of the trawl gradient would most likely
experience a ﬁshing mortality in line with F = qSARmean. A more stationary ﬁsh would of course
experience the mortality based on SAR of its exact location, but without knowing what that location is, our
best guess would be F = qSARmedian. While SAR takes on values between 0 and 31/year, q is conﬁned to
the range between 0 and 1 and may further be approximated by values of similar well studied species and
the addition of gear speciﬁc effort information currently available [Piet et al. (2009), ICES (2019b)]. It is
therefore suggested that the greatest potential source of error is species habitat preference. This is also
evident from the very steep contour lines in the colorplot of F = qSAR, Figure(10), which is plotted over
the whole range of q and only 1/3 of the range of SAR.
Overestimation of F for model input in Section(4)
The ﬁshing mortality F = 0.48/year that was used in the modeling in Section(4) is most likely an
overestimation. Firstly because we use upper range estimates for both input variables, that is q = 1 and
SAR = SARmean. Using q = 1 is probably not a big exaggeration since we are modelling fully selected

6 PREDICTING A MAXIMUM VIABLE ASYMPTOTIC SIZE FOR NORTH SEA
FISH
With the ﬁshing mortalities derived in the previous section, we can now go forward and use trait-based
methods to study the effects of mixed ﬁsheries on (large) data poor demersals. In a similar effort, [Walker
et al. (2019)] used ﬁshing mortality maps and reproductive models to assess the status of speciﬁc data
poor populations. Taking a slightly different approach, I will use traits to estimate an upper asymptotic
size that can be viable in a mixed ﬁsheries setting with SAR, q and gear selection size as variables.

Elasmobranch viability
In the above, R0 was derived for teleosts, but Eq(3) can be quite simply converted to apply to elasmo-
branchs. As shown by [Andersen (2019), p. 137], all traits (parameters) used in this modeling framework
are not signiﬁcantly different between teleosts and elasmobranchs except for offspring size w0 and the
chances of eggs surviving to hatch εR. Elasmobrachs produce eggs (or live offspring) at a size proportional
to their body size. This relationship was found by [Olsson and Gislason (2016)] to be w0 = 0.0044w∞.
Note that w0 will now have to remain within the integral in Eq(3), since it is no longer constant in w.
Since elasmobranch eggs are bigger (if not born live), often wrapped in a tough shell and placed carefully
in the environment by the mother, it is not surprising that they have a higher chance of surviving to hatch.
[Andersen (2019), p. 144] ﬁnds εR ≈0.3 to optimize ﬁtness given the empirical ﬁnding of w0 = 0.0044w∞.
Using εR = 0.3 in this study, however, did not produce viable species (R0 < 1 for F0 = 0). However,
choosing εR ≥0.6 gave stable species, and so εR = 0.7 was chosen.

6.3 Discussion
The results show that we can theoretically predict an upper limit for asymptotic ﬁsh size in a mixed
ﬁsheries scenario. The results at this point are still highly uncertain as evident by the high sensitivity
to input variables q and SAR, which were in themselves already highly uncertain (see previous section).
It is encouraging that the model passed the sanity check and predicted viable cod populations under an
MSY ﬁshery for it. The model also produced the qualitatively expected differences between teleosts and
elasmobranchs.
For a continued use case, the model would have to be calibrated. One way of doing this could be
extending the sanity check for cod into an actual ﬁtting scenario where the model would have to predict a
binary variable called collapsed using ﬁshing mortality, asymptotic size and elasmobranch/teleost as input
variables. Data sources could be known viable mortality-species combinations like the cod example. For
training data with 1 in the collapsed variable, post-collapse analyses of important stocks such as Atlantic
Cod [Myers et al. (1997)], could be considered. This method coud be extended to data poor species such
as halibut and common skate where status on the IUCN redlist could be translated into the collapsed
variable. The ﬁshing mortality would in this case come from our F0 model. Upon such calibration, the
model could be used to create a viability map of the North Sea similar to [Piet et al. (2009)], who predicted
”sink” areas where upwards of 90% of biomass for certain species were removed annually.
The mixed ﬁshery scenario
revealed striking differences in maximum viable asymptotic size stemming
from using either mean or median of SAR. For teleost species, no choice of catchability rendered any
species unviable if SARmedian was used, whereas viability under SARmean was highly dependent on
catchability. For elasmobranchs neither SARmedian nor SARmean yielded any species over 16kg and in
most cases the cutoff was much smaller. This prediction is overall in line with [Walker et al. (2019)]
who assessed data-poor population status based on spatial mortality analysis and found that 57% of
elasmobranch species were under reference levels, which was only true of 10% of all species (majority
teleost). After a model calibration as discussed previously, we could make more quantitative comparisons
with estimates produced by [Walker et al. (2019)]. At least on cross-species level.
While the exact values are highly uncertain, the results do make plausible that there is an upper limit
of viable asymptotic size in a mixed ﬁsheries setting where selection size is ﬁxed to some relatively
small target species. As argued in the previous section, knowledge of species habitat use is of primary
importance for this model. Again, it would seem that more mobile ﬁsh are more vulnerable as they are
more likely to move considerably across the trawl gradient and thereby experience the mean SAR rather
than the median. That is so long as stationary ﬁsh do not require any habitat that is (or was) within the
bounds of high exploitation areas.
Returning to the asymptotic size spectrum
There could be many factors contributing to the observed
biomass cutoff for organisms larger than cod. These preliminary and highly uncertain results suggest that
under the current mixed ﬁsheries mortality scenario:
• Elasmobranchs would not be able to ﬁll in this part of the size spectrum
• Highly mobile teleosts with low to midrange catchabilities might be able to ﬁll in this part of the
size spectrum

7 SUMMARY
Summing up the main discussion points for Sections(2 - 6) it was argued that:
Section(2-3)
• The North Sea asymptotic size spectrum on the range from 60 g to 60 kg is best described by a
parabola with 1st and second order parameters a = 0.27±0.22 and b = −0.16±0.05, corresponding
to a maximum biomass at asymptotic size Winf,max =3.4kg with a factor 3 uncertainty.
• The biomass spectrum up to asymptotic size classes of around 180g might be confounded with the
macrobenthos making results in this area less certain
• There is a sharp cut off in biomass of organisms with asymptotic sizes larger than cod that has not
been accounted for. Both ﬁsh and mammals were investigated
• Asymptotic size spectrum theory is limited in its practical application due to the low amount of
species making up the vast majority of the biomass. This makes the spectrum very susceptible to
single species rather than community effects
• The currently observed asymptotic size spectrum is very close to the one that would be observed if
all stocks were at BMSYtrigger levels
Section(4)
• The asymptotic size spectrum on the range from 0.5 kg to 60 kg is roughly linearly decreasing as
predicted by the Extended Sheldon Conjecture. This result was seen in a modeling scenario where
ﬁsh only preyed on ﬁsh and in the IBTS data set in the range where we most surely do not expect
confounding with the macrobenthos. The observation is based on pure visual inspection of one of
three data sets, and the modeling study was not subjected to rigorous testing such as sensitivity
analysis.
• A new modeling study was suggested that cuts of (benthic) resource spectrum at 180g to simulate
realistic benthos-demersal food web dynamics
Section(5)
• Fishing mortality for the North Sea can be expressed to a resolution of ICES C-square with available
maps of swept area ratio SAR and species/gear speciﬁc catchability estimates q
• Mean and median ﬁshing mortality in North Sea mixed ﬁsheries were found to be Fmean = 0.24±
0.05/year and Fmedian = 0.05±0.01/year based on catchabilities of q = 0.5±0.1. This indicates
a very uneven distribution of trawl effort, which also means that highly mobile species are more
vulnerable than more stationary species without speciﬁc habitat requirements.
Section(6)
• Maximum viable asymptotic size for elasmobranchs and teleosts in a mixed ﬁsheries scenario can
theoretically be described as a function of ﬁshing mortality and gear selectivity.
• Using realistic values for the North Sea mixed ﬁsheries, it was predicted that mostly staitonary
elasmobranchs can be viable up to asymptotic sizes of 16kg provided that their catchability is low,
whereas highly mobile species cannot exceed 3kg. Highly mobile teleosts can be viable up to
>1000 kg provided that their catchability is low, but may be limited to 25kg for high catchabilities.
No upper limit was found for mostly stationary teleosts without speciﬁc habitat requirements.

The Sheldon Conjecture which is now a mainstay in various approaches to ecosys-
tem based ﬁsheries management, was ﬁrst discovered empirically and only later described in terms of
its underlying quantitative relations. With the Extended Sheldon Conjecture it is the other way around.
Through the observant, creative, and tedious study of the life processes and interactions of individual
organisms - both hypothesised as abstract agents in mathematical formulas and observed in laboratories
and ﬁeld studies - populations, communities and entire ecosystems arose in the line art paintings of
mathematical formulas. In this thesis, we may have seen a manifestation of Andersen’s Extension to
the Sheldon Conjecture on a select range of asymptotic sizes in the North Sea ﬁsh community. And we
have run head strong into the challenges of drawing the connections between theory and observations
and received many new questions. Why do we observe a curved spectrum? Why is the cut off so sharp
for organisms larger than cod? As for the older sibling of the Extended Sheldon Conjecture, it is highly
inspirational to see that the observation of Sheldon almost 50 years ago - that if you ﬁnd a ton of life in
the ocean, it may as well be a whale as it may be a thousand ﬁsh - is informing scientists and managers
to set new ambitions in the management of the world’s oceans. These days, balanced harvest and other
tools of ecosystem based ﬁsheries management are being developed into serious frameworks with solid
theoretical and empirical underpinnings. Every year the capabilities of ﬁsheries science and management
are being extended and allow us to continue onwards, from the remarkable achievement of single stock
management in a world of unlimited ﬁshing power, to the trophic restoration of the world’s marine
ecosystems. Applying the Extension to the Sheldon Conjecture, and presenting a model for the viability
of species in a mixed ﬁsheries scenario have been my minuscule workings toward this goal.
I am sincerely grateful to my supervisors, Alexandros Kokkalis and Asbjørn Christensen, for their
guidance on the open seas of marine quantitative ecology and the (sometimes painstaking) ways of
seeking, synthesising and spreading knowledge, and to Ken Andersen for lengthy and detailed emails
about my application of the theory that he has taken such a big part in developing.
