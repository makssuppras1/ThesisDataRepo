Abstract
Solid Oxide Cells (SOCs) are capable of converting excess renewable electricity to green gas which
can be stored. SOCs, however, degrade over time during operation which lowers the performance.
Previous studies have shown the possibility of reactivating degraded SOCs. This can be done by
oxidising the fuel electrode and subsequently reducing it. This is known as redox cycling. Re-
cent studies show that redox reactivation can restore the performance of Gd-doped Ceria (CGO)
electrode materials and Lanthanum doped Strontium Titanate, La0.49Sr0.31Fe0.03Ni0.03Ti0.94O3
(LSFNT), electrode materials. What happen to the materials that cause reactivation is unknown
though. For LSFNT it is speculated if it is caused by exsolution of Fe or Ni particles, or an Fe-Ni
alloy. This study seeks to understand the process of redox reactivation of degraded SOCs, and
how to optimise it.
This was done by investigating microstructural changes to the electrode by varying time and
temperature of a redox cycle.
The microstructural changes were examined through scanning
electron microscopy and scanning transmission electron microscopy. Changes to the surface ox-
idation state was analysed with X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) and Electron Energy
Loss Spectroscopy (EELS). The results for CGO showed no structural changes. The surface oxi-
dation state did indicate an increase in Ce4+, and an unchanged Ce3+ concentration when redox
cycling compared to degraded CGO. EELS measurements showed a higher concentration of Ce3+
at the surface compared to bulk CGO in a reducing atmosphere. This might indicate that it is
possible to reactivate CGO by controlling the surface oxidation state.
Studying the change in morphology for LSFNT electrodes, during a redox cycle, did not yield a
useful answer as many of the samples had an abnormal backbone structure. XPS measurements
revealed the presence of Ni but not Fe. Performing a redox cycle enhanced the presence of Ni
slightly which might be an indicator for reactivation of LSFNT electrodes. Due to the many
samples having an abnormal backbone structure it is advised to redo the experiments before
concluding too much.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Changing the infrastructure from being reliant on fossil fuels to renewable energy sources poses
new challenges. Meeting the energy demand based on the volatility of renewable energy sources
call for methods to store and extract energy on a large scale when needed. Solid Oxide Cells
(SOCs) are capable of converting excess renewable electricity to green gas which can be stored,
and act as an energy carrier until there is a need in supply. A SOC works in both electrolysis and
fuel cell mode which means that the conversion of gas back to electricity will help minimising the
ﬂuctuations in energy production, and meet the energy demand.
The challenge with SOCs is, however, too high costs associated with fabrication, operation of
the cells, and degradation of the electrodes. Lowering the cost is paramount for the introduction
of this technology. Studies have shown the possibility of prolonging the life time of SOCs by
reactivation. This is done by oxidising the fuel electrode, and subsequently reducing it - a redox
cycle [1] [2]. The following sections introduce the technology along with current state of the art,
and narrows it down to the scope of the project.
1.1
Introduction to Solid Oxide Cells
Solid Oxide Cells have the ﬂexibility to work in two diﬀerent modes. As a Solid Oxide Fuel Cell
(SOFC) and as a Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cell (SOEC). In fuel cell mode the SOC is used to
produce electricity from inlet gases, and in electrolysis cell mode the SOC is used to produce a
fuel from electricity. SOCs, in its basic principle, consist of two porous electrodes separated by
an oxide ion conducting electrolyte in-between, as can be seen on ﬁgure 1. The ﬁgure is sketched
for a cell working in fuel cell mode. Here, hydrogen acts a fuel and reacts on the anode with
oxygen ions to form water, and an excess of electrons. At the cathode these electrons react with
oxygen to form oxygen ions.
Figure 1: A solid oxide cell in fuel cell mode. The ﬁgure illustrates a single stack and the reaction
happening at the anode and cathode for oxidising hydrogen.
The generated current is then
utilised in an external circuit, and the product, water vapour, can be used in other processes. [3]

seen on equation 1-3. If operated in electrolysis mode, oxygen evolution and hydrogen evolution
occur at the cathode and anode respectively, and the reaction happens towards the right. When
operating in fuel cell mode, the reactions happen towards the left where hydrogen is oxidised,

In order for the reactants to react at the anode and cathode, the right conditions have to be
present. From reaction 1-3 it is evident that the area exposed to the reactants has to be both an
electronic and ionic conductor to facilitate the reaction. The conductivity of the electrodes must
be suﬃcient to provide an electromotive force. At the same time, the electrolyte has to be an
electronic insulator, or poor conductor to ensure the cell is not short circuited. Yttria Stabilised
Zirconia (YSZ) is a widely used material as the electrolyte due to the ionic conductivity. As
an electrode, a cermet of YSZ combined with the electronic conductive Ni is used. Doing this
creates regions named Three Phase Boundaries (3PB) where the reactions can take place as
seen on ﬁgure 2. The eﬀective area from which the reactions can take place can be enlarged by
choosing a Mixed Ion Electronic Conductor (MIEC). Here cermets are both electronic and ionic
conductors [3]. This means that the reactions taking place only requires an interface between
the mixed ion electronic conducting material, and the reactant gas. This creates what is called a
Two Phase Boundary (2PB) instead of the 3PB.

material. Right: The larger area caused by 2PB-regions is illustrated when using MIEC materials.
This eﬀectively enhances the reactive area. [3]
During operation a cell degrade over time worsening the performance. For Ni-YSZ, and other
cermets, this is due to many diﬀerent side reactions. Sulphur poisoning is among the potent side
reactions for degradation. A concentration of 50 ppb H2S in H2 at 750 ◦C is suﬃcient, and as such
ﬁltering the fuel gas is required to minimise this poisoning. If one feeds dry hydrocarbon based
fuels, carbon deposition on the electrode becomes a problem [5]. These degradation mechanisms
block the active reaction sites at the 3PB-region causing the performance to drop.
Ni based electrodes are also intolerant to cycling between oxidation and reduction. This is due
to the high expansion rate of the particles when Ni is oxidised to NiO. Doing this aﬀects the
mechanical strength and performance [5].

CGO is a MIEC material which increase the active electrode area from where electrochemical
reactions can take place. Having the large 2PB make such materials less susceptible to blockage
from impurities compared to 3PB-based electrodes [7]. Comparing the electronic conductivity
of CGO with cermets, such as Ni-YSZ, it is still too low to be an eﬃcient current collector.
Using CGO as a single component in fuel electrodes is therefore limited [8] [5].
In order to
use CGO as a fuel electrode, the electronic conductivity has to be improved. Inﬁltrating CGO
with SrFe0.75Mo0.25O3−δ (SFMO) enables one to optimise the electronic conductivity at elevated
temperatures in a reducing atmosphere [8]. Inﬁltration is done by introducing a desired compo-
nent into the backbone structure - in this case SFMO. This can be done by dripping a solution
containing SFMO onto the backbone, and subsequently evaporate the ﬂuid. What is left is an
inﬁltrated backbone structure. Inﬁltrating with SFMO consequently results in poor catalytic
activity. Therefore CGO can be inﬁltrated on top to regain the high catalytic activity [9].
Ceria is known to expand when changing the oxygen stoichiometry, δ, in CeO2−δ. The phase
change from CeO2 to CeO1.8 creates a lattice expansion of about 2 % at 900 ◦C [5]. The oxidation
state of Ce in CeO2 is Ce4+, while the oxidation state of Ce in Ce2O3 is Ce3+. Introducing defects
as Ce3+ on the account of oxygen vacancies thereby expand the lattice. It is generally believed
that Ce3+ is the catalytic active component when reducing water at the anode [5].

Along with CGO cells, Lanthanum doped Strontium Titanate, La0.49Sr0.31Fe0.03Ni0.03Ti0.94O3,
also known as LSFNT, is examined, but to a less degree.
This electrode material has been
reported to be stable in reducing and oxidising environments, and corrosion resistant towards
impurities compared to Ni-YSZ electrodes. The electronic conductivity is reportedly high com-
pared to other ceramic materials, and the electrocatalytic active particles are supposedly Fe and
Ni. Lanthanum doped Strontium Titanate crystallise in a perovskite structure with an A-site
deﬁciency [10].
This A-site deﬁciency is exploited in a process called redox exsolution. In this case, Fe and Ni
are substituted into the host oxide lattice in oxidising conditions. Upon reduction the metal
nanoparticles are released (exsolved) on the surface [7]. Contrary to inﬁltrated nanoparticles,
exsolved nanoparticles are immersed about one-third into the host lattice. It has been shown that
the exsolved nanoparticles are conﬁned in the host lattice at the original A-sites. This conﬁnement
has shown to stabilise the electrocatalytic nanoparticles, and thereby be more resistant towards
impurity build-up [7].
1.3
Regenerative Behaviour by Redox Cycling
Enhancing the activity of degraded cells by regenerative methods have been reported in literature
to improve the lifetime of cells. Diﬀerent approaches to this exists and has been studied to a
certain degree. Among them is redox cycling. Performing a redox cycle entails changing the
atmosphere, to which the SOC is exposed to, to an oxidising environment followed by a reducing
environment. Studies of the eﬀect of redox cycling Ni based fuel electrodes and Ni/YSZ have
shown the possibility of reactivation by lowering the internal resistance. Redox cycling Ni based
electrodes have shown to increase the porosity by examining SEM images before and after redox
cycling. This eﬀectively increase the previously degraded 3PB area from where the reactions
occur. One has to be careful, though, as the Ni-YSZ structure will otherwise fracture [1][11].
Regenerative behaviour has also been observed when using CGO as an electrocatalyst coated on
a backbone [1].
A study by David M. Bierschenk et al. [2] studied the regenerative behaviour of mixed anodes
consisting of Pd-substituted (La,Sr)CrO3−δ mixed with 50 wt% Ce0.9Gd0.1O2−δ by redox cycling.
During operation the anode performance degraded. The study clearly showed a regenerative be-
haviour when performing a redox cycle. During each redox cycle the anode performance was
restored back to the initial performance. This study explained the regenerative behaviour by
re-dissolving of Pd nanoparticles into the host lattice upon oxidation, and re-exsolution when
reducing.
In general, redox exsolution has been studied in terms of reversibility. When the performance
of the electrode is lowered, due to degradation, redox reactivation has shown to increase the
performance. Upon oxidation the electrocatalytic nanoparticles may be re-dissolved into the host
lattice, and upon reduction they re-exsolve on the surface [7]. The better performance can be
described by the formation of new 3PB and surfaces where the electrocatalytic nanoparticles are
re-exsolved [1]. This ability has also been studied on LSFNT electrodes. Theis L. Skafte showed
that redox reactivation is possible with LSFNT electrodes without the presence of an inﬁltrate
[9]. During redox reactivation of LSFNT, Fe and Ni are thought of being the electrocatalytic
nanoparticles who are dissolved into the host lattice, and re-exsolved again. But the underlying
mechanism might be diﬀerent [9].

Theis L. Skafte has studied the regenerative behaviour of CGO by redox cycling, and tried to
isolate the mechanism causing the reactivation of the electrode. Firstly, it can be seen on ﬁgure 5
how redox cycling lowers the polarisation resistance of the cell. Polarisation resistance, Rp, is the
resistance occurring when the potential is pushed away from the ideal potential at open circuit
[12]. This happens when electrochemical reactions at the electrode surface changes the potential
that must be applied to cause an electrode reaction at a certain rate. An increase in polarisation
resistance is therefore caused by degradation of the cell resulting in a lower reaction rate [5]. At
each redox cycle on ﬁgure 5 the cells were oxidised in air for 1 hour. The result of each redox
cycle is a signiﬁcant drop in Rp leading to a Rp close to that of the initial value after more than
5000 hours of continuous running.
The cells tested on ﬁgure 5 consisted of an electrode of CGO inﬁltrated with SFMO, and another
layer of Pr-doped Ceria (CPO) on top. This study diﬀers from the others by showing the ability
of reactivating CGO where CGO is not an inﬁltrate on a diﬀerent backbone, or mixed with
other anode materials. To isolate the mechanism causing reactivation, another experiment was
conducted with pure CGO, and without any inﬁltrated layers. Performing a redox cycle on pure
CGO also resulted in a lower Rp after redox cycling thus eliminating inﬁltration as the cause
of reactivation [9]. This clearly indicates that reactivation cannot be associated with inﬁltrated
layers, but CGO itself. The exact mechanism for redox reactivation of CGO is still unknown.

1.4
Scope of the project
The background of this study is based on results that showed the possibility of reactivating SOCs
resulting in improved performance and durability. Doing this redox cycle multiple times allowed
for performance close to that of the initial state. The future prospect is SOCs with prolonged
lifetime and performance, but a systematic approach to this phenomena is currently absent. The
purpose of this thesis is to improve the durability and lifetime of SOCs by understanding the
process of redox reactivation of degraded solid oxide cells. With increased understanding of the
mechanism, one is able to utilise it better.
The approach to this study has been to investigate reactivation of CGO and LSFNT electrodes
by the following means:
• The micro structural changes of the electrode are investigated by systematically varying
time and temperature when performing a redox cycle.
• The eﬀect of redox cycles on the surface oxidation state is analysed with XPS and EELS.
• Changes to the micro structure during redox cycling is documented with SEM and E-STEM.

2. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE
2
Experimental Procedure
In this section the approach to study redox reactivation is presented along with the methods for
characterising the SOCs. The samples presented in this study are listed to provide the reader
with an overview.
2.1
Redox Procedure with Tube Furnace
Doing a redox cycle requires the ability to adjust temperature and to change the atmosphere
between an oxidising and reducing environment. To simulate those conditions a Scandia tube
furnace is used to do the experiments. The tube has a length of 89 cm, and a diameter of roughly
8 cm. The samples were placed in the middle of the furnace to ensure the cells were exposed to the
same temperature. The furnace was programmed to ramp the temperature at a rate of 60 ◦C/h.
During a redox cycle, wet H2:N2 containing roughly 3-4 % H2 is used when reducing, and dry air
is used when oxidising. The pO2 was not monitored with the setup used, and it was not possible
to monitor the exact ﬂow. To ensure the redox reactions are not limited by the gas ﬂow, the ﬂow
is set to maximum during the experiments. The CGO and LSFNT cells used in this study are
symmetrical cells as can be seen on ﬁgure 6 with the electrolyte Scandia-Ceria-Stabilized-Zirconia
functioning as a support. The size of the tested cells is approximately 1 cm2.
Figure 6: Illustration showing the two diﬀerent SOCs being subjected to redox reactivation. Top:
Cross section of the symmetrical cells. Bottom: Top view showing the size of the cells tested.
The approach to study the process of redox reactivation of degraded SOCs is based on vary-
ing the variables time and temperature at diﬀerent stages of a redox cycle. By doing so, it is
examined where, or if there is a limit towards reactivating SOCs. If one can observe the ongo-
ing reactivation and the mechanism behind. To simulate degradation of SOCs, pristine CGO
and LSFNT are aged in the tube furnace in wet H2:N2. Ageing is thus the procedure used to
degrade the SOCs. As the redox cycle entails doing an oxidation of the electrode followed by a
reduction, it becomes relevant to study the eﬀect of those two parameters isolated from each other.

2.3
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy
X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) is a frequently used technique for characterising sur-
faces, and can be used to obtain information on the elemental composition, and the oxidation
state of the elements. With XPS one is also able to quantify the elements present. The standard
X-ray sources used to generate the X-rays are a combination of Al Kα (hν = 1486.3 eV), and Mg
Kα (hν = 1253.6 eV) [13]. The working principle of XPS is illustrated in ﬁgure (8), and is based
on the photoelectric eﬀect.
Figure 8: The interaction with an incoming X-ray is illustrated on the left side, and the following
Auger process upon relaxation is illustrated on the right side. Reprinted from [13].
Photons with energy hν hits, and interact with the sample surface. An atom absorbs the energy,
and ejects a valence or core electron with a given binding energy, Eb. The electron escapes the
surface with a certain kinetic energy, Ekin. The kinetic energy of the escaped electron depends
on the initial photon energy, the binding energy, and the work function for the spectrometer, Φ.
The kinetic energy of the electron is what is detected, and used to calculate the binding energy
as given in equation (6) [13].
Ekin = hν −Eb −Φ,
(6)
A secondary process called the Auger process occurs simultaneously as a consequence of the emit-
ted photoelectron, as seen on ﬁgure 8. The photo emission of a photoelectron leaves the atom
as a highly excited ion. Upon relaxation, an electron from a higher state occupies the hole at a
lower energy state. The excess energy during this process is absorbed by another electron called
the Auger electron which leaves the sample with an element speciﬁc kinetic energy that can be
detected as well.
The binding energies calculated from the detected photoelectrons are characteristic for each el-
ement, and thus functions as a unique ﬁnger print that can be used to interpret the recorded
spectra. Because the binding energies are unique for each element, the choice of diﬀerent X-ray
sources will not alter this fact. As stated by equation 6 a change in photon energy will change

the kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectron. On the other hand, the Auger electron has a
constant kinetic energy with varying binding energy dependent on the X-ray source. The peaks
observed in the recorded spectra will thus shift, when plotting the binding energy, with varying
X-ray source. This is also a means to recognise which peaks are due to Auger electrons.
XPS is known to be a non-destructive and surface sensitive characterisation technique.
The
surface sensitivity of XPS might initially sound contradictory as the penetration depth of the
generated photons is in the order of a few micrometers. The generation of photoelectrons and
Auger electrons at this depth would blur the detected events taking place a few nanometers from
the surface. The probability that an electron interacts with matter is far greater than with a
photon. So, although the photoelectric eﬀect also happens deeper within the sample, the mean
free path is too low for the electrons to escape without energy loss. This ensures that electrons
originating within a few nanometers below the solid surface can escape without energy loss [14].
The surface sensitivity can be explained by the universal curve seen on ﬁgure 9. The universal
curve depicts the mean free path of the electrons escaping the solid as a function of their kinetic
energy. As the X-ray sources AlKα and MgKα emit photons with energies hν = 1486.3 eV, and
hν = 1253.6 eV respectively, the kinetic energies of photoelectrons roughly lie in the range of 0-1
KeV [13]. The mean free path of electrons with those energies is within 2 nm or a few atomic
distances. Electron kinetic energies at 50-200 eV produce the optimum surface sensitivity.
Figure 9: The universal curve showing the mean free path of an electron as a function of its
kinetic energy. Reprinted from [13].

2.4.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron microscopy (SEM) and Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) are tools
that enable one to analyse the SOC on a nanoscale level to visually pinpoint changes to the mor-
phology of the electrode when performing a redox cycle. Figure 11 illustrates how the electron
beam is accelerated, in accordance with the applied voltage, and focused with several apertures
and lenses before it collides with the sample surface.
The basic principle of performing measurements with a SEM-microscope is moving the electron
beam across the sample in a pattern.
This is done to scan the surface of the chosen area.
The electrons can either interact with the nuclei (which are positively charged), or with the
electrons (which are negatively charged), thus resulting in, respectively, backscattering electrons
or secondary electrons that can be detected [16].
The beam interacts with the sample creating a volume known as the interaction volume, as
illustrated in ﬁgure 12. The shape of the interaction volume is a consequence of a cascade of
scattering events taking place as the beam hits the sample. The greater the acceleration voltage,
the larger the interaction volume within the sample becomes. This means that one can obtain
information from deeper in the sample, or closer to the top most atomic layers dependent on the
acceleration voltage chosen.

Backscattered electrons have undergone one or multiple scattering events within the sample. Do
to the elastic scattering, the backscattered electrons have higher energies than secondary elec-
trons. For the chosen acceleration voltage, this means the escape depth is larger for backscattered
electrons. The lateral resolution is therefore not suﬃcient if one wants to obtain information on
the surface topography of the sample. On the other hand, backscattered electrons can be used to
gain atomic number contrast. Elements with higher atomic numbers have a nucleus with a higher
positive charge. As a consequence, the likelihood of electrons to be scattered is higher creating
brighter areas on images [16].
Secondary electrons are generated when the primary beam interacts with the sample surface
which ionise the atoms. This cause electrons to be emitted. Due to the inelastic interaction, the
energy of secondary electrons is lower than the energy of backscattered electrons. This means the
escape depth is limited to within a few nanometers of the sample surface. One can thus obtain
images with higher topological contrast by detecting secondary electrons [16]. These points are
also illustrated in ﬁgure 12.
Due to the elastic interaction of backscattering electrons, the detector is placed directly above
the sample, as illustrated in ﬁgure 11. The Everhart-Thronley detector for detecting secondary
electrons is placed to the side. Applying a small bias voltage will attract the low energy secondary
electrons, and ensure backscattered electrons will not be detected. If the working distance (dis-
tance between the column head and sample) is suﬃciently small it is beneﬁcial to use an in-lens
detector instead. This detector is placed inside the lens as the name suggests [16].
The diﬀerence between detecting secondary electrons and backscattered electrons on a LSFNT
cell was examined as seen on ﬁgure 13. This was done to analyse how it aﬀects the level of
topological contrast on CGO- and LSFNT-cells. The acceleration voltage is 5 kV to minimise
the interaction volume. Comparing the two methods, it is evident that edges, contours and the
particles present are more visible when detecting secondary electrons.

2.4.3
Transmission Electron Microscopy
Where SEM detects the scattered signal above the sample, TEM detects the signal transmitted
through the sample. TEM can therefore be used to provide a higher resolution of the examined
structure at high magniﬁcation. Because TEM detects the penetrated electrons, one has to choose
regions of particles that are suﬃciently thin for the electrons to penetrate. This is necessary to
get the resolution required to separate the individual particles from each other. The electron
beam can be shaped in two diﬀerent ways. In TEM imaging, the beam is shaped to produce a
parallel beam. In Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy (STEM) mode, the electron beam
is shaped to a ﬁne spot on the sample surface, and rastered to create an image as is the case for
SEM imaging [18].
14
==PAGE:21==
2. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE
The STEM images are taken in Dark Field mode (DF). This is done by using a donut shaped
detector. When the primary electrons interact inelastically with the sample, the electrons are
scattered with a given angle beneath the sample. This ensures the scattered electrons are collected
on the detector. When the primary electrons do not interact with the sample, the electrons pass
right through the middle of the detector. As a consequence, the bright areas on the STEM images
correspond to an interaction with the sample examined, and vice versa [13]. The major advantage
of this method compared to using SEM is the possibility to obtain images at higher resolution.
2.4.4
Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy
With TEM it is possible to perform Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (EELS). EELS is a tech-
nique that measures the change in kinetic energy of the electron beam after interaction with
the sample. As the electrons pass through the sample, some will pass right through, and some
will interact with the sample atoms. Upon interaction, the electrons undergo inelastic scattering
with the sample atom, and loose some of the initial kinetic energy. Dependent on the material
examined, the conﬁguration of empty states varies, and is unique for each element and oxidation
state. This is reﬂected in the electron energy loss that is detected after passing the sample [19].
EELS spectra have three distinct regions, as can be seen on ﬁgure 14. A zero loss peak, low
loss excitations and core level excitations.
The zero loss peak arise when the beam interact
elastically with the sample, and therefore does not loose its kinetic energy. This signal reﬂect a
combination of the signal from the primary electron beam, and the signal for elastic scattering.
The low loss excitations occur due to inelastic scattering of valence or conduction electrons. As
these electrons are more loosely bound it does not require as much energy to excite them. The
core level excitations occur due to inelastic scattering of core electrons. It requires more energy
to excite these electrons resulting in a higher electron energy loss. It is the uniqueness of core
electron binding energies that enables identiﬁcation of elements and oxidation state [20].
Figure 14: EELS spectra illustrating the three main regions of information from a specimen.
Reprinted from [20].

Comparing this technique to XPS reveals similarities. The fundamentals of both spectroscopy
methods rely on the binding energy of the atom examined to identify the element or oxidation
state. In XPS one detects the escaped photoelectron from within the sample, whereas one detects
the energy loss of an electron from the primary beam in EELS. These methods thereby supplement
each other and can be used to verify oxidation states independently of each other.
2.4.5
Characterising Samples with TEM and EELS
In this study, TEM is used to document changes to the micro structure, and oxidation state
during a redox cycle. This is done by operating the microscope in in-situ Environmental STEM
mode (E-STEM). This allows one to change the atmosphere surrounding the sample, and tem-
perature. In-situ E-STEM thereby enables one to mimic the conditions needed for performing a
redox cycle while being able to study changes. The experiments were done by Søren B. Simonsen
while I was situated next to him.
The experiments conducted were performed on a FEI Titan E-Cell 80-300ST TEM in STEM
mode. The sample used was aged CGO for 300 hours at 750 ◦C. The sample was prepared by
scraping of the CGO electrode from the electrolyte with a scalpel. The CGO particles were then
grinded ﬁnely in a mortar with ethanol. The solution of ethanol and CGO is then dripped onto
the sample holder using a pipette. When the ethanol has evaporated, ﬁnely grinded CGO parti-
cles is left behind on the grid of the sample holder.
During experiments the pressure was kept at 3 mbar O2 in an oxidising atmosphere, and at 3
mbar H2 in a reducing atmosphere. When switching between an oxidising and reducing atmo-
sphere, the temperature is lowered to room temperature before evacuating the chamber to avoid
temperature spikes. After introduction of a new atmosphere the temperature is raised again.
After obtaining the EELS spectra the Gatan DigitalMicrograph software was used to analyse
them. A common method of analysing the oxidation state is by calculating the white line ratio.
The white line ratio is the ratio of area between the distinct peaks (white lines) for the element of
interest. This can be done as variations in oxidation state will result in slightly diﬀerent spectra.
The calculations were done using a script called ”Double Atan EELS Background” developed by
D. R. G. Mitchell[21]. This script creates a stepped continuum function under the white lines to
subtract the background and calculates the white line ratio.
An example of the process can be seen on ﬁgure 15. Firstly, the ratio of the step height of the
double atan EELS background is set to 2:1 [22]. Afterwards one has to manually deﬁne the peak
positions and the width of the area used to calculate the white line ratio. The width of the
integrated area is set to a default of 4 eV if not described otherwise.

3.1.7
Side Study of Inﬂuence from Carbon Coating of Samples
The poor conductivity of CGO made it diﬃcult to produce sharp and clear images when using
SEM at high resolution. Consequently, it was necessary to coat the CGO samples with carbon
as speciﬁed earlier. This ensured a higher conductivity, and thus the ability to prevent charging
from the electron beam.
This, on the other hand, introduced a variable that would make it
diﬃcult to evaluate systematic changes to the structure at nano-scale level from the actual exper-
iments of interest. To uncover the eﬀect of carbon coating in imaging a side study was performed.
The approach was to design an experiment with a CGO cell due to the poor conductivity. The
experiment was designed where one half of the cell got covered, and the other half would remain
exposed when evaporating the carbon thread, as seen on ﬁgure 29. This would result in two areas

3.2
Surface Analysis of CGO Cells using XPS
In this section the CGO cells are analysed using XPS. As XPS is a surface sensitive technique
changes in oxidation state at the surface can be measured. This is crucial as the electrochemical
reactions happen at the surface. In the following, the eﬀect of ageing at diﬀerent temperatures
and time, oxidising for diﬀerent periods of time, and redox cycling at diﬀerent temperatures and
time periods are examined. The obtained XPS spectra are all compared and analysed to the main
reference spectra of Ce4+ and Ce3+ as given on ﬁgure 10. To ease the analysis further each peak
has been given a name and colour. The red letters correspond to the same peak names given in
the reference spectrum for Ce4+. Likewise, the blue letters correspond to the same peak names
given in the reference spectrum for Ce3+. This will provide indications on what eﬀect time and
temperature has on the surface oxidation state during a redox cycle.
3.2.1
Varying ageing parameters
In order to simulate the eﬀect of degradation, pristine CGO was aged in three diﬀerent batches
with varying temperature and time. Comparing ageing of CGO with pristine CGO on ﬁgure 35
it can be seen that the surface composition changes when ageing. The characteristic peak e, from
the reference spectrum for Ce4+ on ﬁgure 10, disappears to a large degree on the aged samples.
So does peak f and the intensity of peak c. Comparing the aged samples, with the reference
spectrum for Ce3+ on ﬁgure 10, it is clear that peak d and a are the main peaks who develop
as a result of ageing CGO. Peak b does not seem to change signiﬁcantly though. It is therefore
clear that a reduction takes place as expected.
Looking at the binding energy at 919 eV it is clear that a peak appears to a varying degree
when reducing compared to oxidised CGO. This area is not mentioned in the cited literature, but
the signiﬁcant diﬀerence in spectra at 919 eV between oxidised and reduced CGO suggests the
presence of an additional peak at 919 eV when reducing.
Among the reduced samples diﬀerences in peak intensities do also occur. Reducing for longer
periods of time, i.e. 300 hours instead of 100 hours at 750 ◦C, produce a larger peak d and a peak
at 919 eV. The presence of peak e, f and c, from the Ce4+ spectrum, are slightly less apparent,
and peak d2 and b2 do not change when ageing CGO. This indicates that reducing for longer
time results in a higher concentration of Ce3+. Reducing for 300 hours at 850 ◦C instead of at
750 ◦C does not seem to reduce CGO even further as the spectrum is comparable to ageing at
750 ◦C for 100 hours. In all, it is only minor diﬀerences between the aged samples that set them
apart.

3.2.7
Side Study of Beam Damage from XPS
Using experimental equipment always introduces unknown variables and measurement uncertain-
ties. This small side study tries to enlighten one of the possible eﬀects that using XPS has on
the sample being studied, and not least if the eﬀect is signiﬁcant. It has been reported by E.
Paparazzo [25] among others that XPS induce damage on CeO2 surfaces from X-ray irradiation.
In doing so, CeO2 would partially reduce to Ce2O3 after being exposed to Al Kα x-rays. As the
entire study to a great degree evolves around analysing the formation of Ce3+ and Ce4+ during
a redox cycle, knowing the inﬂuence of x-rays from XPS is of great importance.
To perform this study, oxidised CGO for 20 minutes at 650 ◦C has been used to maximise the
potential eﬀect. By using oxidised CGO it is assumed that the concentration of CeO2 is dominant
and therefore any reduction caused by x-rays should be noticeable. Figure 41 show the resulting
measurements.
Looking at the spectra two regions at a binding energy of 910-900 eV and 890-880 eV point out
as where some diﬀerence between the two spectra occur. More speciﬁcally at 903 eV and at 884
eV do the peaks develop. Comparing these BE to the reference spectra on ﬁgure 10, and the
peak positions for Ce3+ and Ce4+, it can be seen that these peak developments correlate with a
development of peak a and peak d on the Ce3+ reference spectrum. This clearly indicates that
the oxidised CGO has been slightly reduced as a result of exposure from Al Kα x-rays.
With that in mind and as a precaution the Ce 3d spectrum was the second spectra after the
survey scan to be recorded. This was done for each measurement to minimise the reducing eﬀect
from x-rays. However, the eﬀect is minor and not consequential for the analysis conducted here.

nanoparticles after oxidation from SEM images is also conﬁrmed by the XPS measurements. This
is not according to theory, and might be because of the abnormal samples.

4. DISCUSSION
4
Discussion
This study was motivated by results showing the ability to reactivate degenerated SOCs by redox
cycling. Unpublished results from Theis L. Skafte [9], and results from two previous students [29]
show the possibility of redox reactivating CGO and LSFNT. This redox reactivation was done at
650 ◦C for 1-2 hours by measuring a polarisation resistance after redox cycling that was lower
than, or close to the initial polarisation resistance. The gas mixture was H2:H2O in the ratio
0.8:0.2 which is a higher ratio of H2 than in this study.
However, what causes reactivation of CGO and LSFNT has not been examined in detail. This
section seek to discuss what might cause the reactivation based on the results obtained in this
study.
4.1
Redox Reactivating CGO Electrode
The SEM images of pristine, aged, oxidised and redox’ed CGO did not show any changes to the
morphology regardless of varying time and temperature for diﬀerent redox cycles. It is, however,
more diﬃcult to conclude whether changes happen on a nano-scale level when oxidising or reduc-
ing CGO. This is due to the fact that it was necessary to coat the surface with carbon to enhance
the conductivity to obtain SEM images with suﬃcient resolution. The deposited thickness is
around 5 nm. This could cover minor changes in surface morphology i.e. around edges and same
sized surface particles. By comparing to STEM images, though, the morphology does not change
during a redox cycle. The idea of measuring the size distribution of the CGO particles were
abandoned after initial attempts. Deciding where the grain boundaries are for the particles on a
3D-image introduce a signiﬁcant uncertainty. It was decided that what deﬁnes a particle becomes
too biased. Although the size distribution of CGO particles appear to be comparable for aged,
oxidised and redox’ed CGO from a qualitative assessment, it cannot be pointed out with certainty.
When analysing the measured XPS spectra of CGO with the reference spectra for Ce4+ and
Ce3+, it becomes clear that it is increasingly diﬃcult to deconvolute the spectra with all the
reference peaks. Separating the 6 peaks in the Ce4+ reference spectrum from the 4 peaks in the
Ce3+ spectrum becomes dependent on several things. The appearance of the spectra separately
i.e. relative peak intensities, characteristic peaks and peak positions. Especially the analysis of
oxidation state in the region 905 eV to 895 eV has to be looked at with a portion of scepticism.
That small region contains 4 diﬀerent peaks whereas 3 of them are within 2 eV. Deconvoluting
those peaks in a qualitative manner therefore introduce some uncertainty, and with the general
analysis as well.
Linking the redox reactivation performed at 650 ◦C in the previous studies [9][29] with the XPS
spectrum for redox cycled CGO at 650 ◦C on ﬁgure 39, the cause of reactivation might be found.
The intensity of the Ce3+ peaks after redox cycling on ﬁgure 39 seem to be comparable to aged
CGO. Therefore it does not seem to be what causes reactivation, although Ce3+ is believed to
be the catalytic active component as described in the introduction [5]. A quantitative analysis is
required to determine whether this is the case. Oxidising aged CGO enhance the Ce4+ concen-
tration as seen on ﬁgure 39, and produce some distinct Ce4+ peaks when redox cycling. This is
contrary to what can be seen for aged CGO. This, on the other hand, indicates that the oxidation
step in a redox cycle is important for reactivation. Figure 36 also reveals that oxidising for 1 hour
enhance the Ce4+ concentration more than oxidising for 20 minutes.
Redox cycling at 550 ◦C also proved to reactivate the CGO electrode, but not as well compared
to doing a redox cycle at 650 ◦C [9]. Comparing redox cycling at 550 ◦C, and at 650 ◦C on ﬁgure

The results from the EELS measurements add another layer to the XPS results. Here, diﬀerences

that the oxidation state changes from the surface to bulk after reducing CGO. The concentration
of Ce3+ is higher at the surface than in bulk CGO, and this diﬀerence is larger after redox cycling
compared to aged CGO. As mentioned in the introduction, forming Ce3+ on the account of Ce4+
expands the lattice. This will most likely induce some strain at the grain boundaries between the
two phases. If this has implications on reactivation is unknown. Furthermore, minor indications
showed that the surface contain a similar amount or slightly more Ce3+ after performing a redox
cycle compared to aged CGO. This could support the theory of Ce3+ being the active catalyst, as
mentioned in the introduction, and the higher concentration after redox cycling be the cause of
reactivation. This is, however, a bold statement based on measurements of a single grain. Further
experiments are required to examine whether this result is reproducible. Comparing temperature
and time spend redox cycling with in-situ E-STEM, and in the tube furnace is not possible. The
in-situ E-STEM experiments were done at 3 mbar which is signiﬁcantly lower than redox cycling
at ambient pressure in the tube furnace.
In chapter 2 where the characterisation methods are presented, XPS is mentioned as a strong
tool to quantify the elemental composition. In this study the spectra are analysed by means of
comparing to reference spectra, and looking at the development. As such, it would make sense to
utilise this strength to quantify the concentration of Ce3+ and Ce4+ in CGO during the diﬀerent
experiments. Therefore an attempt was made to do so using the peak ﬁtting software CasaXPS.
In CasaXPS one has to manually create a peak model with a background, peak position, peak
shape, peak area and many other parameters dependent on complexity. The model is based on
the reference spectra from Christopher M. Sims et al. [15], and the approach to peak ﬁtting
in that article. It has to be said that this article does not provide any speciﬁc details on the
ﬁtting parameters used, nor the basic peak positions. This add an uncertainty to the results
when estimating the correct values.
The result from creating a basic peak model can be seen in appendix B. Due to the limited
available information, only the background, and peak positions were used. When ﬁtting the cre-
ated model, CasaXPS is designed to minimise the standard deviation from the original spectrum.
As a consequence, some peaks get suppressed or disappear dependent on how strict the peak
positions are constrained. The resulting Ce3+ concentration therefore varied between 26 % and
35% dependent on peak position constraints. The Ce 3d spectrum are known to be complex to
deconvolute, and several studies have tried to explain the reason behind along with what ﬂaws
peak ﬁttings of Ce 3d spectra [30]. To mitigate the issues encountered in this study, a more
thorough model has to be developed which was deemed out of the scope of this study.

4.2
Redox Reactivating LSFNT Electrode
The SEM images of pristine, aged, oxidised and redox’ed LSFNT showed the presence of exsolved
particles on all samples except for pristine LSFNT. As described in the introduction, exsolved
particles re-dissolve into the host lattice upon oxidation which is contrary to what can be seen
on ﬁgure 54. Here, exsolved nanoparticles are still present when oxidising for 1 hour at 650 ◦C
in dry air after being aged at 750 ◦C for 300 hours. None of the studies by Theis L. Skafte[9] or
the two students[29] show images of oxidised LSFNT making it diﬃcult to verify whether this is
reproducible.
Most LSFNT samples, who have been aged for 300 hours at 750 ◦C, have a radically diﬀerent
backbone structure from the observed LSFNT samples who has been aged for 100 hours at 750
◦C, and from current studies [10][9][29]. The reason behind this diﬀerence is unknown as it can-
not be described to a single LSFNT cell, but several. As it only occurred for the same batch of
samples it might be due to the fact that ageing for 300 hours compared to 100 hours decompose
the backbone into smaller particles. Alternatively, it could be due to some unknown event when
ageing in the tube furnace. However, the same batch of CGO cells, who were aged at the same
time, did not show any similar signs. The fact that the LSFNT sample, who has been oxidised
for 1 hour, and the LSFNT sample, who has been redox’ed for 1 hour in oxidising atmosphere
and reduced for 1 hour, showed an intact backbone similar to those being aged at 100 hours has
not been attempted reproduced.
With XPS the surface composition of LSFNT is examined to study what happens during a redox
cycle, and the elemental composition of the exsolved nanoparticles. Time constraints only allowed
for XPS measurements of aged LSFNT for 100 hours at 750 ◦C, oxidised LSFNT for 1 hour after
being aged for 300 hours at 750 ◦C, and redox’ed LSFNT for 1 hour in dry air, and reduced for
1 hour after being aged for 100 hours at 750 ◦C. As discussed above, it was only pristine LSFNT
that did not have exsolved particles on the surface, although one would expect the oxidised sample
to be similar to pristine LSFNT. It would therefore be relevant to include XPS measurements
of pristine LSFNT in order to analyse exactly what/which elements are exsolved during redox
exsolution. From literature it is known that the exsolved particle in LSFT is Fe and in LSNT it
is Ni [10]. Studying LSFNT it is expected that either Fe and Ni are exsolved, or an alloy of Fe
and Ni.
Comparing the XPS spectra, the most signiﬁcant diﬀerences occur for the Ni 2p and La 3d spectra
on ﬁgure 56. Metallic Ni has a peak at 852.7 eV, and La has a peak at 853 eV [14][26]. From
the spectra it is clear that the exsolved nanoparticles are not solely metallic Ni as a second peak
is present at around 855 eV. From literature a peak at 855 eV indicates the presence of a Nickel
Oxide, NiO [27][28]. This does, however, seem counter intuitive as the intensity of the NiO peak
at 855 eV is less intense when oxidising compared to aged and redox’ed LSFNT. The peak at
852.7 eV, for metallic Ni, is less intense when oxidising though. After a redox cycle the intensity
of both peaks are higher compared to aged and oxidised LSFNT. The presence of more metallic
Ni, due to the higher intensity, might be the reason behind the reactivation. Due to long storage
time before examination with XPS, the exsolved Ni particles might have reacted with air causing
the more intense NiO signal for aged, and redox’ed LSFNT. It is noticeable that a signal for Fe
could not be obtained from any of the samples treated diﬀerently. The amount of the dopants Fe
and Ni is equal in LSFNT. As a signal can be detected for Ni, one would expect to detect a signal
for Fe as well. It would therefore be interesting to do the measurements again in order to verify
the results, and examine whether the NiO peak at 855 eV is a result of too long storage time
before the XPS measurements were done. One could also try to perform the redox exsolution by
heating up in air to e.g. 850 ◦C, and then switch to a reducing atmosphere.

5. CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
The making of this study and thesis was motivated by results showing the ability to reactivate
degenerated SOCs by redox cycling. Prolonging the life time of especially the electrodes is of great
interest in order to improve the durability. Results from Theis L. Skafte showed the possibility
of redox reactivating CGO and LSFNT resulting in a performance close to that of the initial
state. However, what causes reactivation of CGO and LSFNT has not been examined in detail.
This study examined what happens to the morphology and surface chemistry during a redox cycle.
During a redox cycle the morphology of CGO does not change when comparing to the mor-
phology of degraded CGO. This result was consistent throughout the diﬀerent experiments with
varying temperature and time. The reactivation of CGO during a redox cycle can therefore not
be described by structural changes that would lead to an enhanced active surface area where the
reactions can take place.
When studying what happens to the surface chemistry during a redox cycle, observable changes
do occur compared to degraded CGO. The oxidation state of Ce in CGO changes during a redox
cycle. Oxidising aged CGO enhance the Ce4+ concentration, and produce some distinct Ce4+
peaks when redox cycling. This is contrary to what can be seen for aged CGO, and therefore
indicates that the oxidation step in a redox cycle is important for reactivation. This means that
it is possible to reactivate CGO by controlling the surface oxidation state.
The results from EELS reveal diﬀerences in oxidation state from bulk CGO to the surface of
CGO when being subjected to reduction. The concentration of Ce3+ is higher at the surface than
in bulk CGO, and this diﬀerence is larger after redox cycling compared to aged CGO. If this
diﬀerence in oxidation state has implications on reactivation is unknown. This study does not
examine the inﬂuence of all the studied redox parameters on cell performance. As such, further
optimisation is necessary.
Studying the change in morphology for LSFNT electrodes, during a redox cycle, did not yield
a useful answer. It was not possible to observe whether the exsolved particles get re-dissolved
into the host lattice when oxidising as theory predicts, and too many of the LSFNT samples had
an abnormal backbone structure upon examination. To verify changes to the morphology, new
experiments have to be made in order to conclude anything.
When studying the surface chemistry it seems to be Ni that has exsolved to the surface, and
not Fe. Doing a redox cycle enhance the presence of Ni slightly which might be an indicator for
reactivation of LSFNT electrodes. However, the presence of NiO on reduced samples is contrary
to what is expected, and so is the presence of Ni after oxidation. Because some of the samples
have an abnormal backbone structure, it is advised to test if the results are reproducible.

6. OUTLOOK
6
Outlook
• The ability to study SOECs in situ and in operando is of great importance to eliminate un-
known variables. In the case of characterisation of SOECs with XPS the cells are removed
from a known controllable environment. Lengthy time between the actual experiment and
characterisation with XPS implies storage time in containers/bags, contamination and ox-
idation from the sorrounding air resulting in many uncontrollable variables. Being able to
work ”in situ” with XPS would eliminate that concern.
Taking it a step further, ”in operando” mode would be the next step. In this study char-
acterisation and testing of SOECs are seperated and solely linked by the assumption that
by having the same environment and parameters the two can be correlated. Doing ”in
operando” experiments with e.g. XPS and E-TEM would also minimise unknown variables
and thereby provide a more accurate image of cause and eﬀect.
• In conjunction with the discussion of quantifying the measured XPS spectra it would be
beneﬁcial to develop a peak model for Ce 3d. This would enable one to quantify the con-
centration of Ce3+ and Ce4+ in CGO during the diﬀerent experiments with more precision.
• The results from EELS revealed a diﬀerence in oxidation state from the surface to bulk
CGO. This diﬀerence increased after a redox cycle. If this has an impact on reactivation
is unknown. Experiments where a systematic approach to this i put forward could be of
interest.
• Perform XRD to examine the crystal structure of CGO. If oxidising is the main cause of
reactivation, the impact it has on the structure might reveal new information.
• Perform a new set of experiments with LSFNT to determine if it is in fact Ni, and not Fe
or an Ni-Fe alloy that exsolves during redox exsolution.
