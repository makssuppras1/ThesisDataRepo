Abstract
This study explores how retinal ganglion cells respond to electrical stimuli by identifying and counting
overlapping (compound) action potentials, a critical step in evaluating electrode designs for retinal
implants. Inspired by the work in Kusumanchi et al. 2025, where two distinct electrode designs for
retinal implants are introduced and assessed using experimental data, this study aims to enhance the
counting methods used for performance evaluation. Due to the extreme noise and irregularities in the
experimental data, synthetic datasets were created, designed to emulate the characteristics of the ex-
perimental data, allowing the use of supervised methods. The 12 synthetic datasets with varying noise
levels enabled model evaluation under different conditions, addressing the unknown noise level in the
experimental data.
To count action potentials in retinal ganglion cells, a two-phase solution pipeline is proposed. The
first stage involves classifying whether cellular activity is present or not, while the second estimates the
number of action potentials in the sample using regression. Both baseline and advanced methods are
employed for these tasks. The results demonstrate that advanced models outperform baseline methods
in both classification and regression. For classification, the state-of-the-art model HIVE-COTE-V2 per-
formed significantly better than Random Forest with an F1-score of 0.96 ± 0.01 across all simulated
datasets, albeit with a higher computational cost.
For the regression stage, the study investigated if a modified version of InceptionTimeE designed with
specific alterations to suit the requirements of spike counting better, could outperform off-the-shelf
state-of-the-art methods for time series extrinsic regression. The proposed method was on par with
the advanced models recommended by the literature and performed especially well in low-noise settings
(RMSE 6.23). While it performed well in low-noise conditions (RMSE 5.90), it did not significantly
outperform similar models. As a result, FreshPRINCE, a feature-based rotation forest regressor with
the best overall performance (average RMSE 9.65), was selected for the final solution pipeline.
Lastly, this study applied the solution pipeline (HIVE-COTE-V2 and FreshPRINCE) to the experi-
mental data, where it was observed that the simulated data does not perfectly replicate experimental
conditions.
As a consequence, the solution pipeline failed to predict action potentials in line with
expectations. This suggests that either the experimental data’s noise levels should be decreased or
the simulated dataset should better capture the variability in the experimental data to ensure reliable
spike-counting performance in practical applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Retinal degenerative diseases like Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) and Age-related Macular Degeneration
(AMD) are major causes of irreversible vision loss, with both conditions leading to the progressive
degeneration of photoreceptors. AMD primarily affects central vision while sparing peripheral vision,
whereas RP often begins by impairing peripheral vision while central vision remains intact. These dis-
eases significantly impact patients’ quality of life, making the development of retinal implants a focus
in medical research today (Flaxman et al. 2017). Retinal prostheses aim to restore partial vision by
stimulating retinal neurons by bypassing or replacing damaged pathways in the eye. Proper assessment
of the benefits of these implants is essential to ensure they meet clinical needs and improve the lives of
those affected by these degenerative diseases.
This work was inspired by the paper Kusumanchi et al. 2025, which introduces two distinct electrode
designs and manufacturing procedures for future retinal implants. The electrode design plays a cru-
cial role in electrode-tissue interaction and the overall effectiveness of neural stimulation. The paper
proposes a three-dimensional (3D) pyrolytic carbon electrode as an alternative to traditional retinal
implant designs, which predominantly utilize two-dimensional (2D) electrodes that lie flat against the
retinal surface. The effects of 2D and 3D carbon pillar electrodes upon stimulation of retinal ganglion
cells were evaluated using experimental data. The data were collected by recording the reactions of
porcine retinal tissue to electrical stimuli from the different electrode designs via a Micro Electrode
Array (MEA). The research showed a substantially higher amount of activity in the Retinal Ganglion
Cell (RGC)s when stimulated with 3D carbon electrodes, compared to 2D carbon electrodes, indicating
that the 3D design is promising for electrical stimulation of the retina. The eﬀiciency evaluation of
the electrode design was measured using spike counts, which measure the neural activity generated
by RGCs in response to electrical stimulation. A higher spike count indicates more effective neural
activation.
Previous research in the domain has attempted to evaluate these types of recordings by analyzing
various aspects of the electrode-tissue interaction by quantifying spikes; their shape, location, and
magnitude in relation to the stimulus.
The methods typically include several preprocessing steps,
such as filtering out specific frequencies and removing artifacts of the recording environment, such
as the Stimulation Artifact (SA).
Detection of spikes is typically performed using unsupervised or
semi-supervised ”spike-detection” algorithms, which aim to detect individual Action Potentials (AP)s
but struggle with Compound Action Potential (CAP)s, which are spikes where neurons spiking near-
simultaneously.
Associating a number (count of APs) to a recording is called Time Series Extrinsic Regression (TSER),
for which established supervised methods exist. To be able to obtain labels of such recordings, simulated
data can be used, which is created by either biophysical modeling (based on specific cell characteristics
and recording devices) or by combining existing AP templates.
The counting of APs in extracellular recordings is a challenging task due to their overlapping nature and
the inevitable corruption of the signal by noise from various sources, including the systematic noise in
the recording hardware and the microelectrode tip being surrounded by multiple neurons as expressed
in Shahid, Walker, and Smith 2009. Consequently, the electrode detects the electrical activity of many
neurons, with the closest neurons producing the strongest signals, leading to altering the spikes’ am-
plitude and shape. This also results in the device picking up distant neuronal activity, which often
appears as noise due to its smaller amplitude.

1.2 Problem Statement
2
1.2
Problem Statement
Identifying electrically-evoked CAPs in extracellular recordings is critical yet challenging due to the
presence of APs from multiple neurons and various noise sources. The literature review revealed that
most existing spike-counting methods are unsupervised, making it diﬀicult to validate results without
extensive input from domain experts.
In Kusumanchi et al. 2025, the authors have attempted to estimate a proxy for the number of CAPs in
each recording so that RGC activity levels caused by different electrodes can be assessed. The focus of
this thesis is thus to improve upon the approach presented in the paper using state-of-the-art Machine
Learning models.
Therefore, the research questions are:
• RQ1: How can retinal ganglion cell responses to electrical stimuli be assessed by identifying and
counting overlapping action potentials?
• RQ2: How can such methods be reliably evaluated and compared against each other?
• RQ3: How does the proposed solution compare to the one presented in Kusumanchi et al. 2025?
1.3
Approach
Due to the extreme noise in the data and irregularities that do not align with the experimental pro-
tocol, it was decided to answer the research questions by creating synthetic data resembling that of
Kusumanchi et al. 2025 (hereafter referred to as the experimental data). The aim is to create a dataset
with a known number of APs, in line with the biological and mechanical phenomena occurring in the
experimental dataset. The simulated data allows for the use of supervised methods, thereby allowing
inference on the experimental data. This has the added benefit of accurate assessment by supervised
metrics. The project flow is illustrated in Figure 1.1 and explained in the section below.
Figure 1.1: Overview of the project flow. Synthetic datasets are generated through simulation based
on analysis of the experimental data and expert knowledge in neurophysiology. These
datasets are used to train a two-stage solution pipeline designed to count (overlapping)
APs. The pipeline’s performance is then evaluated using both synthetic and experimental
data..
To count overlapping APs in RGCs based on responses to electrical stimuli, the proposed solution is a
two-stage pipeline as illustrated in the gray part of Figure 1.1. The first step is classifying whether cel-
lular activity is present. This closely aligns with differentiating between ”with stimulus” and ”without
stimulus” (control) data of Kusumanchi et al. 2025. If cellular activity is deemed present, the samples
are passed to a regressor that solely focuses on predicting the number of APs.
For both the classification and regression steps, baseline and advanced methods are deployed. The
baseline model is Random Forest (RF) for classification, and the advanced method is HIVE-COTE
2 (HC2). The bake-off paper Middlehurst, Schäfer, and Bagnall 2024 argues that HC2 performs overall
better than other approaches on times-series classification problems. In contrast, RF is more computa-
tionally eﬀicient than HC2 and is a well-known classifier capable of operating directly on raw data.
Three baseline methods are used for spike counting, each serving a specific purpose. The threshold-
ing method, introduced in Kusumanchi et al. 2025, acts as an unsupervised baseline and provides a

benchmark for comparison with results from the experiments in the study. However, this method counts
”spikes” and not APs like other methods used in this project. The average prediction is the simplest
baseline, acting as a benchmark to assess the complexity of features learned by advanced models. If a
model fails to outperform the average prediction, it indicates that it has not successfully learned any
meaningful features from the data. Linear Regression is chosen as the last baseline method to have a
supervised method that is based on the input time-series.
To estimate the number of APs, four advanced models are selected based on the literature review,
incorporating insights from Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024 and aiming to cover a diverse range of approaches.
As shown in Figure 1.2, two of the top three regressors are tree-based algorithms (Diverse representation
Canonical Interval Forest (DrCIF) and FreshPRINCE). The third is a Neural Network (InceptionTimeE,
an ensemble variant of InceptionNet). Unlike fields such as language processing and computer vision,
deep learning methods have yet to outperform classical approaches for time series data (Middlehurst,
Schäfer, and Bagnall 2024). The selected models represent state-of-the-art methods, as highlighted in
Tan et al. 2021 and Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024, and encompass diverse techniques. DrCIF is an interval-
based method, while FreshPRINCE is primarily a time-series-feature-based approach.
These three
models are used alongside a fourth advanced model: a modified version of InceptionTimeE designed
with specific alterations to better suit the requirements of spike counting.
Figure 1.2: Critical difference diagram (Demšar 2006) of the benchmark study by Guijo-Rubio et al.
2024. The horizontal line represents the average ranking of models across all datasets
(19 benchmark datasets) and runs (5 repetitions with resampling) with critical difference
bars connecting indistinguishable performing models. (Ranking is in terms of RMSE, and

A range of metrics and statistical analyses is applied to compare the performance of different methods.
These include Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and F1 score in assessing predictive performance and
the Wilcoxon signed-rank test to evaluate the significance of differences between methods. These choices
are motivated by their use in Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024. This approach helps determine whether the
advanced models provide meaningful improvements over the baselines and relative performance of each
method. Furthermore, by showcasing the performance of fundamentally different models, this analysis
highlights the strengths and limitations of each approach.
The outcome is an end-to-end solution
pipeline, trained on the simulated dataset, that can be applied to spike counting in real-world data
scenarios. Based on these results, insights into the experiments and the data obtained in Kusumanchi
et al. 2025 can be provided.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
2.1
Retinal Implants
Over the years, various retinal implant designs have been proposed and tested, spanning wired and
wireless systems and epiretinal and subretinal approaches, each offering distinct features and potential
benefits. The ArgusTM II epiretinal implant (Zhou, Dorn, and Greenberg 2013) is a currently in use
wired prosthesis design, where visual stimulation is not linked to eye movements. The system features
an MEA implanted in the eye, an inductive coil for power and data transmission, an external belt-worn
video processing unit (VPU), and a camera mounted on glasses. The camera captures visual informa-
tion, which the VPU processes in real-time, generating stimulus pulses. These pulses are transmitted
to the MEA via specialized circuits and a superior-temporal inductive coil for wireless communication
between the implant and external components. The implant is silicone-based with platinum electrodes
for better mechanical stability and safety. It is 200 µm in diameter and features 60 electrodes laid out
in a 6x10 grid for localized stimulus of near-infrared light (NIR) for safer and deeper penetration into
the retinal tissue.
A notable wireless retinal implant is introduced in Wang et al. 2012, where photovoltaic arrays trans-
form NIR light into electric current to stimulate the nearby retinal neurons. However, having to wear
a device may still inconvenience the patient. The photodiode arrays are fabricated with lightly boron-
doped silicon-on-insulator wafers for better control over the conductiveness of the device. The arrays
are designed with a thickness of 30 µm to be able to absorb enough light (around 70%) while being thin
enough to be implanted under the retina. For large animals, a 1.9 mm2 array, and for rats a 0.8 x 1.2
mm2 was fabricated with three different sizes of pixels (280 µm, 140 µm, 70µm with active electrodes
of 80 µm, 40 µm, 20 µm) of which all were able to stimulate the retinal tissues in-vitro and in-vivo. The
design’s main contribution lies in each pixel’s return electrodes, which ensures a localized stimulation
crucial for vision restoration. While a flat design was tested in this paper, a ”pillar-style” implant with
improved anti-reflection coating and anodal stimulation is suggested for more eﬀicacious stimulation.
It was established in Flores et al. 2018 that a 3D pillar design for the above photovoltaic arrays with
dome-shaped caps is able to more effectively stimulate the nerve tissue due to the increased surface
area of the implant. The 10 µm version protruded into the middle of the inner nuclear layer (INL),
while 22 µm pillars reached the upper portion of the INL, and the 30 µm pillars were most effective at
stimulating the RGCs of rats.
The authors of Kusumanchi et al. 2025 compare a 2D- and a 3D pyrolytic carbon pillar design. The flat
design has 2 µm tall (40 µm wide) electrodes standing on a silicone base, while the 3D design features
30 µm tall carbon pillars on top of the same structure. The pillars could stimulate porcine retinal tissue
ex-vivo (via non-photovoltaic stimulation) better than the flat design.

2.2.2
Removal of Stimulation Artifact
The idea of using Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to split the recorded data into separate
sources is applied in the paper Akhoun, McKay, and El-Deredy 2013.
The data is recorded with
cochlear implant electrodes and consists of Electrically-evoked Compound Action Potential (ECAP)s of
the auditory nerve. To get a sense of the adequate number of independent sources, Principal Component
Analysis (PCA) was applied to the raw ECAP recordings. The paper showcased that the electrical SA
signals and the ECAP amplitude may be considered independent signals, especially from the point of
view of an intracochlear recording electrode. However, the method also has drawbacks as it sometimes
underestimates the ECAP amplitude if ICA doesn’t fully separate the ECAP into a single source, or
overestimates the amplitude if the artifact remains in the ECAP source. Determining the optimal num-
ber of independent components is also left as an aim for future studies.
To reveal spikes with a short latency (< 2 ms) and make those spikes obscured by the SA visible
in the recorded signals, Sekirnjak et al. 2006 suggests the approach of digital subtraction. Just below
the stimulation current threshold for spikes, approximately half of the stimulus trials showed notice-
able shape changes, indicating a potential spike hidden within the SA. To uncover the spike, a digital
subtraction was performed between a trial containing both the SA and the suspected spike and a trial
containing only the SA. Since the SA is nearly identical in both trials, the subtraction should cancel out
the SA and reveal the hidden spike. To improve the accuracy of this technique, multiple traces with and
without suspected spikes were averaged before subtraction, which led to an increased Signal-to-Noise
Ratio (SNR). This method yielded comparable or better results than those achieved by conducting
artifact suppression by local curve fitting as reported in Wagenaar and Potter 2002.
2.2.3
Detection of Spikes
This section provides a broad perspective of current methods for spike detection. The majority of these
methods are unsupervised, making evaluation challenging. They can generally be divided into three
categories based on their methodological concepts.
The first category is thresholding, which relies on the amplitude of the spike. In Kusumanchi et al. 2025,
amplitude thresholding with self-adaption of the threshold for identifying spikes from pre-processed data
is presented. For a data point to be counted as a spike, it must exceed the baseline noise (defined as
the root mean square) by some factor within that data recording. Another algorithm within this cate-
gory is described in Maccione et al. 2009. The precision timing spike detection algorithm relies on the
absolute voltage difference between a local maximum and minimum within a predefined time period.
The difference is classified as a spike if it exceeds a predefined threshold. A refractory period is also
implemented to ensure that no other peak can be found within this period.
The second category is template-based correlation, which is based on the actual shape of the spike.
Within the category, a commonly used method is the wavelet transformation. According to Quotb,
Bornat, and Renaud 2011, wavelet analysis is more robust for extracting action potentials in noisy
or unstable environments. The paper presents an adaptive threshold wavelet-based detection method
tailored for neural signals in extracellular recordings, which performed well on simulated data of the
human cerebral cortex. In Natora, Franke, and Obermayer 2010, the Hybrid Blind Beamforming algo-
rithm is introduced. This algorithm uses template spike forms computed through higher-order statistics
directly from the data, which are then convolved with the original signal to detect spikes.
The principle behind the methods in the third category is the transient nature of a spike, implying
that spikes can be detected by measuring some quantity describing the fluctuation of data. In Lieb,
Stark, and Thielemann 2017, two algorithms within this category are presented. The first is based on
a stationary wavelet energy operator, and the second is based on spikes’ time-frequency representation.
Both algorithms perform superior to results achieved using the methods described in Natora, Franke,
and Obermayer 2010 and Maccione et al. 2009, regardless of the noise level.

In recent years, new methods, including neural networks and hidden Markov chain models, have been
introduced within the field of spike detection. In Cheng et al. 2022, it is argued that despite their
widespread use, traditional spike detection algorithms, such as amplitude thresholding and wavelet
transformation, have several limitations that hinder their practical application. An Artificial Neural
Network (ANN)-assisted amplitude thresholding algorithm is proposed for more accurate detection.
The ANN validates whether the waveforms detected by the thresholding algorithm represent real spikes
or above-threshold noise. Using raw signals collected from the primary somatosensory cortex and pri-
mary motor cortex of macaques, the paper shows that incorporating an ANN enhances the performance
of amplitude thresholding, as indicated by the F1 score. It is important to note that the ground truth
data used to train the ANN comes from spike sorting results provided by Maccione et al. 2009.
In J. Li, X. Chen, and Z. Li 2018, the use of a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) for spike detection
is explored. The HMM-based spike detector does not require a threshold. Instead, it identifies spikes
by selecting the time-bin with the highest posterior probability. This method was evaluated on both sim-
ulated data and experimental neural recordings from the primary motor cortex of two Rhesus monkeys,
yielding promising results compared to traditional methods.
2.2.4
Spike Sorting
Extensive research has been done on attributing spikes to individual neurons, commonly referred to as
”spike sorting”. Once a candidate spike is elicited, different feature extraction methods are employed
to create a feature space adequate for subsequent clustering methods. These could be hand-crafted or
learned features. The clusters are then attributed to individual neurons or groups of neurons depending
on the application, experimental setup (such as measuring device), and objective.
An extensive review of spike sorting algorithms still in use today is presented in Lewicki 1998. Instead
of setting the cluster boundaries manually, clustering algorithms like K-means (Bishop and Nasrabadi
2006) and Bayesian clustering (Cheeseman et al. 1988) are suggested. The clusters can be used to
classify new spikes for outlier detection, and for template creation by averaging the cluster members’
shape. Templates can also be used to classify new spikes by measuring their similarity to the template
shape, and template matching can be further improved with interpolation. The number of neurons
studied should dictate the number of clusters; however, depending on the application, that might be
diﬀicult to determine.
Overlapping spikes (CAPs) are addressed by a template-based algorithm, in which the best match-
ing templates are repeatedly subtracted from the compound spike. This creates a search-tree based on
residual errors of subtracted templates, and the best-matching combination of templates is chosen as
the full description of the APs. This method assumes that CAPs are linear and can become unreliable
when dealing with many neurons firing together.
ICA is mentioned in Lewicki 1998 too, as a way to disambiguate multiple neurons.
This method
has been applied successfully in spike sorting; however, theory dictates that the number of sources
(neurons) must equal the number of simultaneous recordings (electrodes), which is rarely the case.
Nevertheless, convolutive Independent Component Analysis (cICA) has been employed for spike sort-
ing with results comparable to supervised methods (Leibig, Wachtler, and Zeck 2016).
It is based
on simulated brain tissue recordings with high-density MEAs (64x64 electrodes, where the neurons-
to-recording-channels ratio might be over 1, making the problem practically over-determined). In this
method, spacial correlations are exploited such that electrodes further away from the neuron may exhibit
temporal shifts of the same waveform, indicating that the same AP is picked up by multiple sensors.
This is modeled either by having multiple mixing matrices, each corresponding to a source shifted by
a certain lag (Auto-Regressive cICA), or by modeling the observed signals as a vector containing the
lagged versions of each observation (embedded cICA). The authors find that the embedded cICA results
in a better SNR than the auto-regressive version, while it also offers better computational eﬀiciency (as
it allows for the use of FastICA algorithm).

2.2.5
Time Series Extrinsic Regression
Many of the methods for TSER problems are adaptations of methods from the realm of Time Series
Classification (TSC), where the final task of predicting a discrete value has been switched to predict
a continuous value instead. A review, Middlehurst, Schäfer, and Bagnall 2024, found that a variant
of RandOm Convolutional KErnel Transform (ROCKET) (Dempster, Petitjean, and Webb 2020), a
convolution-and feature-based ridge-classifier and an HC2 (Middlehurst, Large, Flynn, et al. 2021), an
ensemble of radically different estimators, were the best models across 112 datasets.
The first benchmarking on TSER problem was introduced in Tan et al. 2021, which consists of 19
datasets, 4 univariate and 15 multivariate. In Tan et al. 2021, a comparison of the experimental perfor-
mance of 13 different approaches was laid out. This comparison showcased the Rocket algorithm from
Dempster, Petitjean, and Webb 2020, when adopted for regression, achieved the highest overall accu-
racy compared to classical machine learning algorithms such as XGBoost, Random Forest, and Support
Vector Regression, as well as other adaptations of TSC algorithms. However, the paper also states
there is no significant difference between the state-of-the-art time series algorithms like ROCKET and
InceptionTime (a deep learning model) from Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020 and classical regression
algorithms regarding RMSE.
An extension to this study was presented in Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024. The number of problems was
increased to 63. The comparison was extended to include a wider range of standard regressors and the
latest versions of TSER models from the previous study. Two new TSER tree-based algorithms, Fresh-
PRINCE and DrCIF, were introduced. The study illustrates that FreshPRINCE and DrCIF, along
with an ensemble InceptionTimeE, perform better than the other tested regressors. RMSE was used as
the performance estimator. DrCIF achieved the best score on the 63 regression problems and was the
only one that significantly outperformed a standard rotation forest regressor. The study also revealed
that InceptionTimeE (an ensemble of 5 Inception networks) delivered significantly better performance
than a single Inception network. However, the performance of InceptionTimeE varied across different
problems. While it was ranked first on 13 problems, it performed poorly on many others.
2.3
Simulation of AP data
Having ground truth spike positions and counts allows researchers to estimate and compare the eﬀiciency
of spike sorting algorithms. Simulation of APs is, therefore, a powerful tool for evaluating the eﬀiciency
of these methods. Below, two approaches are presented.
2.3.1
Simulation by Templates
In Leibig, Wachtler, and Zeck 2016, the authors used previously recorded multichannel templates of
rabbit RGC activity. Templates were derived from 19 clusters on a cluster-by-cluster basis via center-
of-mass temporal alignment of waveforms and averaging. They simulated 196 neurons arranged on a
rectangular grid (1141 neurons / mm2). The spikes were drawn uniformly from the 19 templates, and
each time a neuron firing was used, the templates were drawn from a cubic spline interpolation with
random offsets. On each channel of the simulated data, the spike was the sum of all firing neurons with
added Gaussian noise. Spike amplitudes were further noised according to different desired SNR levels,
and neuron-wise signal-to-noise ratios were drawn from a uniform distribution. Inter-spike intervals
were exponentially distributed with firing rates of 10-50 Hz and a refractory period of 5 ms. The total
dataset became 5 seconds long with 4366 channels.
2.3.2
Simulation by Biophysical Modelling
MEArec (Buccino and Einevoll 2021), a python library, offers a comprehensive simulation pipeline based
on the LFPy python package (Hagen, Næss, et al. 2018) made for simulating local field potentials and
individual extracellular potentials. LFPy is based on NEURON (Hines, Davison, and Muller 2009), a
software package designed to simulate individual action potentials and neural networks.
Unlike earlier attempts to generate hyperrealistic extracellular spikes (Hagen, Ness, et al. 2015), MEArec
also models the recording device. The data generation pipeline consists of two steps: the generation

intracellular modelling, followed by the projection of the extracellular behavior of the individual cells

3.1
Anatomy of the Eye
The human eye is a spherical organ with a diameter of around 24 mm. Its primary function is to capture
light and convert it into electrical signals that the brain interprets as visual information. The eye is
surrounded by six muscles - one above, one below, one on each side, and two rotating slightly around
it, allowing it to move freely within the eye socket.
Figure 3.11 illustrates the anatomy of the human eye. The eye’s outermost layer is called the sclera,
a white protective layer. The cornea, at the front of the eye, is a transparent layer that is bent by
the anterior chamber, an area filled with fluid called the aqueous humor. Just behind it, the colored
part of the eye called the iris ensures that an adequate amount of light enters the eye by contracting
or relaxing. The iris surrounds the pupil, a transparent area that lets light through. Right behind the
iris and the pupil is the lens, another transparent structure that is responsible for focusing light on the
retina. This is the part that determines the focal length (the distance at which objects are in focus),
and it is adjusted by the muscles protruding into the eye (culinary muscle). Behind the lens, there is
the vitreous chamber filled with vitreous humor, a transparent liquid.

3.1.1
Comparison with Other Mammalian Eyes
Preclinical studies in vision research are often performed on rodents and pigs. These initial investiga-
tions serve as a foundation for more complex experiments, enabling researchers to test hypotheses, refine
methodologies, and gather data in controlled settings before translating findings to human studies. It
is also important to note that there are differences between human and other mammalian eyes. A few
of these are noted here:
Mice are commonly used because of their availability and because some strains of mice are trans-
genic, allowing researchers to study the molecular and cellular processes of vision. (Other types of mice,
called ”wild-type” strains, have no specific genetic defects but are very similar genetically, making ex-
periments with these types very repeatable.) The center of the mouse retina has more photoreceptors
and a thinner membrane compared to the edges, just like in humans, but these differences are more
extreme in humans. Mice have a much higher number of photoreceptors overall, which puts more strain
on their retinal cells, which help clean up waste. In areas that match the human macula’s outer part,
the rod-to-cone cell ratio is pretty similar in both species. This suggests that while mice don’t have
a macula, their retina can still be useful for studying parts of macula degeneration, especially in the
outer regions where it often starts. (Volland et al. 2015)
Pigs are often favored for their eye size and anatomical similarity to the human eye, making them
ideal for translational studies. The pig retina is composed of ten layers, just like the human retina.
It has special areas where photoreceptors connect with other nerve cells to send visual information,
and Müller cells, which help support the retina, as they are prevalent across it. These cells keep the
retina together by connecting the retinal pigment epithelium to a layer underneath, using proteins
called integrins. In the center of the pig retina, there’s a cone-rich area called the foveal streak, which
is similar to the human macula and helps with sharp vision. The pig retina also has a similar balance
of cones and rods to the human retina (Chandler, Samuelson, and Mackay 1999), although pigs don’t
have a reflective layer called the tapetum, a structure most developed in animals relying on night vision.

The nerve cell or neuron is a type of cell responsible for transmitting information throughout the body.
It consists of a cell body, an elongated projection called the axon, and branchlike growths called den-
drites located both on the cell body and the end of the axon, which is called the axon terminal as
illustrated on Figure 3.33.

The incoming chemical signal from other neurons arrives in the form of neurotransmitters, which bind
to receptors on the dendrites. The binding causes ion channels to open, resulting in a change in the
cell’s membrane potential, which can ultimately lead to the generation of an action potential (AP) in

terminals, where it triggers the release of neurotransmitters into the synapse, exciting or inhibiting

Without external stimulus, the cell’s resting potential around the outer layer of the cell (resting mem-
brane potential) is governed by two types of ion channels (called gated and non-gated respectively), and
by the selectively permeable cell membrane. This selectivity (also caused partly by the ion channels)
leads to differences in the distributions of ions inside and outside the cell called chemical and electrical
gradients. The influence of chemical concentration and electrical potential makes ions flow in or out of
the cells. To maintain a steady state in both electrical potential and chemical concentrations there are
ion ”pumps”, eg. the sodium-potassium pump, that counterbalance these natural forces. However, this
requires the expenditure of energy through hydrolysis of Adenosine TriPhosphate (ATP). An overview
is presented in Figure 3.5. Below, the workings of the resting membrane potential of a neuron are
presented.
Overall there is a net negative charge inside the cell relative to the outside of the cell membrane.
Outside the nerve cell, mainly Na+ and Cl- have high concentrations, while inside the cell, K+ (and
some organic anions) have high concentrations. These ions may travel in or out of the neuron according
to their gradient via ion channels. Na+ is driven into the cell by both the chemical concentration gradi-
ent and the electrical potential difference on the two sides of the membrane. This depolarizes the cell,
and K+ flows out due to its electrochemical gradient. Although the negative electrical potential inside
the cell would typically attract K+, the concentration gradient of K+ is higher inside the cell, leading
to its outward movement. The electrical potential of the system is thus balanced, and the result is a
resting membrane potential of -65 mV, but there is a net influx of Na+ and a net efflux of K+. This
leakage of ions would even out concentrations inside and outside the cell had there not been pumps to
transport these ions back in the opposite direction. The sodium-potassium pump transports 3 Na+ out
of the cell and 2 K+ into the cell in each cycle. (This also contributes to the negative charge inside the
cell as it is the net outwards movement of one positive ion.) The system is therefore in a constant flow,
a steady state rather than still, at equilibrium.
Ions with much lower concentrations affect the system minimally via, e.g. Ca2+ pump and the K+-Cl-
cotransporter, a mechanism that uses the energy of the electrochemical gradient of an ion rather than
ATP to transport to the opposite side of the membrane.

Signal Processing
Signal processing is a field of electrical engineering that analyzes and manipulates signals, such as
time-series signals. Analog signals are continuous in both time and amplitude, while digital signals are
discrete in time and quantized in amplitude. First, filtering is introduced briefly, and then a frequency
analysis tool, power spectrum analysis, is detailed.

Filtering
There are different types of filters. Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filters use both current and past
input samples as well as past output samples to calculate the current output.
In contrast, Finite

filters include Butterworth, Chebyshev, Elliptic, and Bessel filters, while examples of FIR filters include
those designed using windowing methods. Other types of filters include Kalman filters and wavelet
filters. Frequency-based filtering, such as the ones below, is particularly useful in biomedical settings.
• High-Pass Filter: Allow signals with frequencies higher than a certain cutoff frequency to pass
through while attenuating frequencies lower than the cutoff.

A times series is a sequence of observations denoted xt recorded at successive points in time t, often

”Boosting” is an idea worth mentioning, as an ensemble of trees is frequently used in the popular frame-

subsequent tree ”learns from each other’s mistakes”. This is achieved by repeatedly sampling from the
dataset and giving higher importance (weight) to the training samples that were incorrectly classified
or, in the case of regression, according to the error size. This way, the observations that are diﬀicult to
classify have an increased influence. The final prediction is then a weighted average of the individual
trees, in which earlier models weigh less than later ones. Gradient Boosting is a variant of this technique,
in which the subsequent models (eg. trees) are fit to the residuals (y −ˆy) of the previous output. Thus,
the final prediction is the sum of the model outputs.

from a local region of the feature map is selected.

learning increasingly abstract features of the time series. The last convolutional layer is a fully con-
nected layer, where the learned features are flattened to perform tasks such as classification or regression.
Another concept in deep CNNs is the receptive field of a neuron in a certain layer of the network
(Luo et al. 2017). Unlike fully connected networks, where each neuron is connected to the entire input,
neurons in CNNs only depend on a localized region of the input, known as the receptive field. The size
of the receptive field increases as the network’s depth grows, enabling the model to capture patterns
over larger spatial areas. Additionally, factors such as the size of convolutional kernels, strides, and
pooling operations significantly influence the receptive field.

4.2.2
Power Spectral Density Analysis
The PSD was estimated using Welch’s method (Welch 1967) with a segment size of 512 data points
(approximately 17 milliseconds). This configuration was deemed adequate as it can capture the impor-
tant frequencies of the data without SAs with a high enough frequency resolution. At the same time, a
subsequent peak detection algorithm can still find well-defined peaks in the PSD (see A.3). Note that
the prominent frequencies of the SA overpower other frequencies in the data with stimulus. Moreover,
512 data points are a long enough period to cover an entire SA.
In the following, the Hann window was used (Virtanen et al. 2020, Welch 1967), with 50% overlaps.
The PSD analysis shows that the individual channels behave approximately the same way in the same
eye within the same experiment on a normalized scale; however, there is a distinct difference between
experiments with and without a stimulus. The experiments with stimulus have a smooth, dissipating
wave behavior in most channels, which can be due to the presence of harmonics. Some channels show a
very pronounced harmonic behavior. Figure 4.5 is an example of the normalized PSD of the data with
stimulus (and the TTX data has the same behavior). Note that without normalization, some channels
exhibit significantly higher power, which is also evident in the data distribution, as discussed above.
The form of the power spectrum density and the prominent frequencies, however, are comparable on a
normalized scale.

Methodology
This section outlines the methods employed in this study to address the research questions presented in
Section 1.2. As introduced earlier, it primarily focuses on the ”Simulate Data” and ”Solution Pipeline”
blocks from the project flow overview. The first part describes the process of creating synthetic data. It

5.1
Synthetic Data Generation
This section introduces the data generation procedure. The aim is to generate time-series samples that
closely resemble the experimental data. The data can be divided into two main components: clean data
and noise. Creating the components involves using templates from the true data or similar data from
external resources.
First, the selection of SA templates is explained, followed by a framework for generating clean data
as well as the noising procedure. As the aim is to evaluate models trained on multiple noised versions
of the same data, the noising procedure allows for different noise levels to be set.
5.1.1
Stimulation Artifact Templates
In section 4.4.1 and further detailed in A.1, it was found that the SA does not always have the same
shape but instead varies depending on the eye and channel. However, in the TTX data and with stim-
ulus data, the SA shape remains consistent within the same channel. The SA templates are based on
the TTX data, as it is considered more consistent than the stimulus data.
To identify distinct SA shapes in the TTX data while accounting for natural variability, K-means
clustering was applied. For each eye in the TTX data, the average SA for each channel was computed.
PCA was then performed on the channel averages and used as input for the K-means algorithm. The
number of clusters, determined using the elbow method as well as the Davis-Bouldin method, varied
between 2 and 4 depending on the eye. Figure 5.1 shows the average SA for each channel in the PCA
space for Eye 5. Channels were grouped into their respective clusters based on their K-means labels.
Figure 5.2 presents the three distinct clusters for Eye 5, along with the average SA for each cluster.
The cluster average (shown in black) forms a final SA template. This procedure was repeated for all
eyes, resulting in a total of 18 SA templates. The templates are displayed in E.1.1.

In the following, Algorithm 2 (along with Table 5.1) is detailed in words. The signal (one channel) is
modelled as a single time series of N consecutive stimulations. Each stimulation begins with an SA,
which is the same template across a recording as described in A.1.1. This template is modulated by
drawing an amplitude scaler α from a Gaussian distribution. After the SA, the cells start to spike.
The number of potentially active cells Nc is Poisson distributed with mean λc, and each cell has an
exponentially distributed start time with mean λstart, which marks the beginning of a spike train. A
spike train is defined as a series of templates that are modulated versions of the same base AP template.
The number of spikes in a spike train is Poisson distributed with mean λs. The inter-spike interval is
exponentially distributed with mean λt. The AP templates are modulated by drawing an amplitude
(βa) and length (βd) scaler from a Gaussian distribution.
This way, overlapping and approximately simultaneous activity (CAPs) can be modelled, in which
spikes have a tendency to appear close to the SA. Note that with a suﬀicient number of spikes within a
==PAGE:51==

spike train and suﬀicient inter-spike intervals between spikes, it is possible to observe a spike during the
next SA. To further randomize the templates, they are interpolated to capture different points along
the same curve. This process is referred to as time-jitter.
The function interp(template, time[ms]) stretches a given template to a given time in seconds
such that the resulting template corresponds to a 30 KHz sampling rate. Note that a minimum tem-
plate length is defined as a safeguard for the rare cases when the randomly drawn template length is
negative. The function length(x) returns the length in number of data points of a template or time
in seconds (again, on a 30 kHz sampling rate).
Once the ”clean” data has been generated, noise is added with two sets of SNR levels independent
from each other. One source of noise is Gaussian (white) noise; another source is the ME noise. The
white noise is drawn from a Gaussian distribution with µ = 0, and the standard deviation is scaled
according to the SNR level before being added to the clean data. The ME noise is added by repeating
the template and rolling it with a random offset (Uniform(0, half ME size)). This is to ensure that a
model doesn’t learn a specific value at a particular point within the sample but rather the time-invariant
pattern or ME frequency.
A final source of noise is the spontaneous firing of the cells1. This is modelled by placing AP tem-
plates across the entire recording according to a uniform distribution. The non-evoked firing rate of
the cells is 10 Hz and is present regardless of the number of cells Nc as this is due to light entering the
device or mechanical stimulation rather than electrically evoked stimuli.
5.1.3
Noise Components Overview
The synthetic recordings used in this study contain several sources of noise, which can be broadly cate-
gorized into two types: noise components related to the data-generating process and noise components
that are added afterward. The noise components related to the generating process are the placement
of templates, overlap of templates, different AP and SAtemplates, amplitude, duration, interpolation
(template jitter), and cut-off of at the beginning and end of a segment. In addition to these noise com-
ponents inherent to the data generation process, deliberate noise is added afterward to better simulate
real-world data conditions. White noise is one of those components. Another added noise component
is ME noise that is specific to the recording setup. Finally, spontaneous firing of neurons is also a form
of noise. This background firing activity can interfere with spike counting, as it introduces additional
events that are not related to the external stimuli.

5.2
Solution Pipeline
The data generated exhibits clear separation into two distinct clusters: one where the number of
cells is Poisson(0)-distributed and another where the number of cells is Poisson(30), Poisson(50) or
Poisson(70)-distributed.
The distinct division in the data also divides the labels into two distinct
clusters: samples with spikes and the other with little to no spikes. The clear separation of the data
offers the opportunity for a two-phase solution pipeline that leverages the natural separation in the
data, in contrast to addressing the problem purely as a regression task. The proposed solution pipeline,
illustrated in Figure 5.4, consists of two main stages: classification and regression.
In the classification stage, the model determines whether any cellular activity is present. This step
eliminates unnecessary computation for samples without activity and provides a clear cutoff for the
regression model. Samples classified as ”without activity” are immediately assigned a prediction of
0, whereas samples classified as ”with activity” are passed to the regression model. In the regression
stage, the regressor predicts the number of spikes occurring in a sample ”with activity”, allowing it
to specialize in modeling the variability of spike occurrences. Below, the classification and regression
models explored in this study are explained. For the regression models, a distinction is made between
baseline and advanced approaches.

Classification
Two models are deployed as classifiers to determine whether cellular activity is present given response
period. In this context, the two classes are ”with activity” and ”without activity”. The classifiers are

5.3.1
Random Forest
Random Forest is introduced in section 3.4.6. The raw simulated data is used as input to the model, so
the features are the values of each sample’s timestep. For classification, the prediction of RF is deter-
mined using majority voting, where the class predicted by most models is chosen as the final prediction.
The relative contribution of each feature to the predictive performance of a RF, also known as feature
importance, can be determined using Gini importance (Louppe et al. 2013). This measure computes
a feature’s importance by summing the impurity reduction at each split where the feature is used,
weighted by the number of samples reaching that split. This process is repeated for all trees in the
forest, and the feature importance scores are averaged across all the trees to compute the final impor-
tance scores for each feature. The values are then normalized, so they sum to 1, thereby measuring how
significantly each feature contributes to the model’s predictions.

5.3.2
HivecoteV2
The state-of-the-art classifier for classifying windowed time series HC2, an ensemble classifier (Middle-
hurst, Large, Flynn, et al. 2021), is composed of 4 classifiers:
1. The Shapelet Transform Classifier (STC) method from Hills et al. 2014 is designed for times series
classification. STC is a classifier that identifies shapelets in the training data, transforms time
series into vectors using distances to a filtered subset of shapelets chosen for their information
gain, and then constructs a classifier, a Rotation Forest (Rodriguez, Kuncheva, and Alonso 2006)
based on these transformed vectors.
2. Temporal Dictionary Ensemble (TDE) (Middlehurst, Large, Cawley, et al. 2021) is a dictionary
method in which short subseries are slid over the sample, but instead of using a distance-based
feature as with shapelet-based methods, it discretizes the subqueries (called ”words”) and uses
counts of these as features.
The final classifier is an ensemble of 1-Nearest Neighbors (NN)
classifiers that transform each sample into a histogram of word counts using Symbolic Fourier
Approximation (SFA) - a similarity search (Schäfer and Högqvist 2012).
3. Arsenal is an ensemble of ROCKET classifiers. ROCKET randomly chooses a set of kernels with
random length, values, and dilation level, then extracts the max value and the proportion of
positive values from the resulting convolutions. These features are then fed into a Ridge classifier
(where the L2 penalty term α is found via cross-validation).
4. DrCIF (Middlehurst, Large, Flynn, et al. 2021) is an interval-based classifier that uses phase-
dependent randomly selected intervals of fixed offsets and computes summary statistics on these
intervals.
This method extracts the catch22 statistics (Lubba et al. 2019), from the original
sample, periodograms and the first-order difference. These features are then fed to a time series
forest classifier (Deng et al. 2013). DrCIF (for regression) is explained in details in section 5.5.1.
HC2 decides on a final prediction using the Cross-validation Accuracy Weighted Probabilistic Ensem-
ble (CAWPE), which uses an accuracy estimate of each classifier formed on the train data to weigh
the probabilities of each component. The weights are found either through an internal estimate for
each classifier or by a ten-fold cross-validation. When making the final predictions, the weights are
exponentiated such that differences in sub-models are more pronounced.

5.5.1
DrCIF
DrCIF (Diverse Representation Canonical Interval Forest) is a tree interval-based ensemble model orig-
inally designed for time series classification. It can be adapted for regression tasks by replacing the base
tree algorithm with a regression tree. DrCIF extends the Canonical Interval Forest (CIF) framework
(Middlehurst, Large, and Bagnall 2020) by using different summary features and data representations.
DrCIF builds an ensemble of CART regressors, each trained on the output of different random interval
transformations of the input times series (see Figure 5.5(a)). The tree’s optimal splitting point is based
on absolute error, and splits are made until there is just one sample left in a partition.
The transformation process consists of three steps, as illustrated in Figure 5.5(b). First, the times
series is split into three distinct representations: the original times series, the first-order difference, and
a periodogram of the series. For each base regression tree, a different transformation is performed using
a random forest-based approach to select features from the time series. The selection of intervals and
extracting of features from their intervals are unsupervised. For each of the three data representations
and each individual tree, a subset of a features is randomly chosen from a pool of 29 possible features
for each individual tree. The feature pool is composed of the 22 features from catch22 (22 CAnonical
Time-series CHaracteristics) and seven additional summary statistics candidates: the mean, standard
deviation, slope, median, interquartile range, min, and max.
catch22 is a minimally redundant and computationally eﬀicient set of time series features (Lubba et al.
2019). catch22 is using a filtered version of the HCTSA (highly comparative time-series analysis) Mat-
lab toolbox that consists of 4791 features presented (Fulcher and Jones 2017). Each of the 22 features
is selected based on its ability to capture time-series properties of the distribution of values, linear and
nonlinear temporal autocorrelation properties, differences, and others. A full overview of the features
of catch22 is available in F.1.
Furthermore, k phase-dependent intervals are extracted for the times series where positions and lengths
are determined randomly. The selected features, a, are then computed for each of these intervals. The
features derived from all representations and intervals are concatenated to form a combined feature
vector for each time series with the dimension 3 · k (random intervals) · a (unsupervised features). Each
base regressor generates a prediction based on its distinct combined feature vector. The final prediction
of the model is found by averaging the prediction of each of the base regressors.

5.5.2
FreshPRINCE
FreshPRINCE is a pipeline algorithm that combines a TSFresh transformation and a Rotation For-
est (RotF) regressor. TSFresh, as described in Christ et al. 2018, is a feature extraction algorithm that
transforms the input times series into a feature vector. TSFresh extracts 794 time series features from
the input. These features can be used directly, or a feature selection that removes irrelevant filters can
be applied. Only a subset of these features (called the ”eﬀicient”-set) was used to speed up the feature
extraction process, totaling 777 features. The feature vector is then fed into RotF regressor, which is
a tree-based ensemble. The final model used 100 such regressors. RotF was originally developed for
classification tasks but is adaptable for regression. As presented in Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024, the key
differences between RotF for classification and regression (Rodriguez, Kuncheva, and Alonso 2006) are
the removal of class subsampling and the usage of the CART model as a regressor. The set of parame-
ters used in this work (the recommended defaults) is summarized in F.2.2.
The pseudo-code of the training and prediction phase of a rotation tree for regression is shown in
Algorithm 3. For each of the L tree regressors, the input features are split into K subsets. For each
subset, random sampling of 50% is performed to create a new subset, on which PCA is applied. The
results from the K PCA transformation are combined and rearranged to align with the original fea-
tures. This matrix, known as the rotation matrix, is used to transform the original input data. The
transformed data, along with the corresponding targets, are then used to train the L different CART
models. A detailed explanation of the working of a CART model is given in 5.5.1. After training, the
final prediction for a sample is computed by averaging the outputs of all L trees.

5.5.3
InceptionTimeE
The original InceptionTimeE model (Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020) was designed as a computer vision-
inspired classification model for time series. It is an ensemble model where n individual InceptionTimes’
(or sub-models) output is averaged for the final prediction (with equal weight). In Guijo-Rubio et al.
2024, the original classification model is adapted for regression by using mean square error loss instead
of cross-entropy loss combined with softmax output activation. The individual InceptionTime models
draw inspiration from the Inception modules originally introduced in the GoogLeNet architecture for
image analysis. The inception modules specified in Szegedy et al. 2017 were adapted to time series by
changing the 2D kernels to 1D with adjusted kernel sizes and changing the 2D average pooling to global
average pooling that averages the multivariate time series over the time dimension. (In univariate time
series this corresponds to a flattening of the channels’ dimension.) Figure 5.6 illustrates the inception
module used in InceptionTimeE.
Figure 5.6: Illustration of the inception module in InceptionTime using a multivariate time series.
The coordinate system to the left shows the number of channels, the number of timesteps,
and the number of filters. (For illustration’s sake, only one is being shown.) The bottle-
neck layer is a channel-wise procedure. The 3 kinds of convolutions, together with the
max-pooled and ”bottlenecked” input, produce the output of the multivariate time series.
(Figure from Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020).
The overall architecture of one of the InceptionTime models in InceptionTimeE is illustrated in 5.7.
The core of InceptionTimeE consists of multiple Inception modules arranged in parallel, as showcased
in the middle block of the figure. Residual connections, like in InceptionV4 (Szegedy et al. 2017), are
also introduced, where the current output of an inception module is added to the residual version of
the previous block. Such a module is called a residual module, where the input is processed using a
”bottleneck” layer and the batch normalization procedure. The bottleneck layer is a channel-wise linear
layer whose purpose is to compress the input to a lower dimension. The output of the residual and the
output of the most recent inception module are added together and passed through a ReLU activation.
Following the paper, a depth of 2 (also referred to as a depth of 6 after the number of purely inception
modules) was used, with every third inception module followed by a residual module. Therefore, the
InceptionTimes have 2 combined inception-residual blocks as illustrated in Figure 5.7.

The architecture of the inception modules is illustrated in the rightmost final block of Figure 5.7. These
modules are designed to capture features at different temporal scales by applying convolutional layers
with varying kernel sizes (e.g., 10, 20, and 40, as used in the original paper). This enables the model
to learn patterns of varying lengths within the time series data. To improve computational eﬀiciency
while retaining essential information, a bottleneck layer is applied before the main convolutional layers.
Each module also includes a max pooling layer alongside the convolutions. The outputs from the convo-
lutional layers and the max pooling layer are concatenated to create a comprehensive representation of
the input data. This combined output is then passed through a batch normalization layer and a ReLU
activation function before being forwarded to the next block.
Following the original work (Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020), the model is implemented with pa-
rameters consistent with those used in the paper, except for the number of epochs. The architecture
consists of five independent models, each containing a total of six inception modules (referred to as
depth = 2) with 32 filters per convolutional layer. The kernel sizes are retained from the original paper,
set to 10, 20, and 40, as illustrated in the right block of Figure 5.7. Each of the five InceptionTime
models trains for 800 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 and learning
rate decay. A batch size of 64 was utilized. In the original configuration, the models were trained for
1500 epochs. However, during initial trial runs, the validation loss curves consistently flattened around
300-400 epochs (see Appendix D Figure D.5). To improve computational eﬀiciency and to match the
experiments conducted for IncpetionTimeE II, the number of epochs was then reduced to 800.
5.5.4
IncpetionTimeE II
InceptionTimeE II builds upon the foundation of InceptionTimeE but introduces specific alterations to
the original architecture, enabling it to better suit the requirements of the task of counting spikes.
Different regularization techniques have been introduced to InceptionTimeE II to prevent overfitting,
including early stopping, weight decay (L2), and dropout. Early stopping aims to identify the optimal
point to stop the training process of a deep learning model. Early stopping is implemented using a
hold-out validation set and the moving average of the validation Mean Square Error (MSE) loss. Dur-
ing training, the model’s performance is evaluated on both the training set and a separate validation
set. When the validation set’s performance stops improving and begins to degrade, early stopping halts
further training. The validation set constitutes 15% of the total data samples, matching the size of the
test set. To assess the model’s performance on the validation set, the moving average of the validation
MSE loss is used as the performance metric. To allow for a burn-in period, early stopping only begins
==PAGE:61==
5.5 Advanced Regression Methods
53
monitoring after the first 50 or 800 epochs (minimum epochs is a parameter). The patience for early
stopping is set to 10 epochs, meaning that if the moving validation MSE loss does not decrease within 10
epochs, training is stopped. The moving average is applied to the validation loss to mitigate volatility,
with a smoothing factor based on the last 5 epochs.
Additionally, AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter 2017) is used as the optimizer as opposed to Adam
used in the original InceptionTimeE, which decouples the weight decay (L2) from the optimization
steps taken with respect to the loss function. Finally, dropout has been implemented after each incep-
tion module (red blocks in Figure 5.7) to prevent the network from becoming too dependent on certain
nodes and encourage it to learn more generalized features.
The concept that a larger receptive field is required for detecting larger patterns from very long one-
dimensional time series data is also hypothesized in Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020. It is known from
computer vision problems that a larger receptive field is required to capture more context for object
recognition, as stated in Luo et al. 2017. An initial CNN module is introduced to downsample the
input data before it gets passed to the Inception modules. The initial CNN module aims to increase
the receptive field such that an entire AP can be captured by the majority of the neurons in the last
convolutional layer before the global average pooling layer in InceptionTimeE II. The length of the AP
templates is 134 data points (∼5 ms on 30kHz); however, due to the data generation process, the AP
templates often overlap, extending the length of a CAP to more than 134 data points. InceptionTimeE
is a multi-path network, meaning that there are multiple paths from the input to the final output
feature map.

5.6.1
Test of significance
To determine if two models’ performance is significantly different from each other, the Wilcoxon signed-
rank test is used. In case of multiple testing, the Holm correction is used, following the method presented
by Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024.
The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is a non-parametric statistical test used to compare two dependent
samples (Garcia and Herrera 2008). It evaluates whether the median of the differences between paired
observations significantly differs from zero (two-sided), making it the non-parametric counterpart to
the t-test. The null hypothesis of the Wilcoxon test is that there is no difference between the two
groups in the population. Given it is non-parametric, the test does not assume a particular probability
distribution of the dependent variable in the analysis. The samples are required to be dependent (e.g.,
models are tested on the same datasets), and values are assumed to be continuous (e.g., the metric
RMSE). The test statistic is computed in the following way:

6.5
Predictions on Experimental Data
In this section, prediction on the experimental data is described, using the full solution pipeline. As it
is established in chapter 4, different channels of an eye behave approximately similarly, so it is suﬀicient
to consider a single channel within each eye. 100 samples of length 2700 were cut out from the signal
between SAs such that approximately all of the channel’s data was considered, and the samples fit the
number of timestamps expected by the solution pipeline. The best model from the classification stage
HC2 is determined in section 6.2.1. The best model for regression, FreshPRINCE, is elicited in section
6.4.1. The noise level of the experimental data is unknown, and therefore all 12 solution pipelines are
evaluated on it. (One solution pipeline with one classifier and one regressor per simulated dataset.)
Figure 6.13 shows the distribution of predictions on Channel 1 for all experiment types and all eyes of
the experimental data with the 3D design based on models trained on all 12 noise levels. Figure 6.14
shows the same for the 2D design.
In general, the predicted AP counts are higher than the distribution of the training data, with a
mean of around 70 rather than 50. This indicates that either the simulated data failed to capture some
aspects of the experimental data or that the model is correctly showing a higher number of APs than
anticipated. Assuming that one of the 12 noise levels corresponds to the experimental data’s noise level
and that the simulated data reflect the experimental data well, predictions around 50 should be the
most common, which does not align with the figure.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
7.1
RGC activity levels as counts
In section 5.4.3, a threshold-based spike-counting method was introduced. The intention of the authors
in Kusumanchi et al. 2025 was to introduce a value as a proxy for either the true AP or rather CAP
count. Theoretically, the number of spikes should correlate with the number of CAPs, and the area
under the spike curve should correlate with the number of APs’. This way, the difference in spiking
activity between the 2D and the 3D designs can be assessed and used to evaluate the retinal implants.
Counting APs instead of CAPs might be a diﬀicult task if the AP templates align in a way where
the depolarization segment cancels out the hyperpolarization part of the template. This could very
well happen, but the underlying biological process dictates approximately the same start spiking time
close to the SA, effectively encouraging the creation of a CAP. The individual templates might also be
learned later in the recording, where overlaps are less likely. (See section 5.1.2)
Besides the potential for increased accuracy, directly counting APs has a clear relation to neural ac-
tivity, allowing for a more detailed understanding of how the stimulating device’s design, the level of
stimulus, and the tissue interact. For instance, given knowledge of RGC density in a tissue sample, an
exact AP count could provide insights into the proportion of responsive versus unresponsive cells in that
tissue area. Exact counts would also facilitate a clearer comparison across research projects, recording
devices, and experimental conditions. This would enable direct benchmarking between studies and a
more standardized assessment of neural activity across different methods (e.g., Lewicki 1998, Cheng
et al. 2022, J. Li, X. Chen, and Z. Li 2018), devices and experimental setups (e.g., Buccino and Einevoll
2021, Sekirnjak et al. 2006), as well as retinal implants (e.g., Kusumanchi et al. 2025, Flores et al. 2018).
7.2
Simulated Data
The experimental data presents an inherently unsupervised problem as the true number of APs (or
CAPs) is unknown. Although there exist unsupervised methods to estimate the location and thus the
number of CAPs, such as the unsupervised methods presented in 2.2.3, it can be diﬀicult to assess
the performance of such methods. Evaluation using unsupervised methods could have been performed
through manual expert assessment. However, due to resource constraints, this was not feasible in the
context of this project and was unsustainable in the long term. Additionally, obtaining a pre-trained
spike sorter and combining its output with expert assessment (as it is the approach in Cheng et al.
2022) was not an option, as no well-established spike sorter has been trained on the experimental
data at hand. Moreover, using an existing sorter could propagate an unknown level of errors into the
model. The literature suggests that established spike sorters are primarily used to refine specific aspects
of sorting of newer models while maintaining benchmark accuracy levels as stated (Maccione et al. 2009).
Due to the extreme noise in the data and irregularities described in section 4.4.2 and to allow for
assessment by supervised metrics, synthetic data resembling that of Kusumanchi et al. 2025 was cre-
ated. The noise ranges from more noise than signal through as much noise as signal (when SNR is zero)
to almost entirely clear signal (when SNR is 80). The metrics allow for comparison of the performance
of multiple models and assessing a single model across different noise types and levels.
7.2.1
Simulated Dataset Design
To replicate the real biological processes and the experimental setup used in Kusumanchi et al. 2025 as
closely as possible, the simulated data was designed in collaboration with domain experts, particularly
==PAGE:83==
7.3 Models
75
those who recorded the experimental data. The approach of using simulated data may be inaccurate if
the parameters used for simulation differ significantly from the experimental data. Parameters where
expert guidance was insuﬀicient to specify a correct value, such as AP-shape-related settings, were
randomized.
Moreover, different datasets were created to cover a broader range of possibilities for
parameters that were completely unknown by experts, such as the noise level. While efforts were made
to align the simulation with real conditions, uncertainties still remain regarding the data generation
process. Still, it was done as accurately as possible within the limitations of the project.
7.2.2
Template Based Simulation
In section 2.3, two synthetic data generation methods were presented. While simulation by biophysical
modelling theoretically would have allowed for the usage of more relevant AP templates, the currently
available toolboxes do not contain (porcine) RGC models, nor the Utah Array (or any similarly sized
extracellular recording MEAs). Moreover, the creation of intracellular measurements necessary for a
full biophysical profile of porcine RGCs was outside of the scope of the thesis.
Instead, the use of readily available AP templates from Höfling et al. 2020 allowed for an increased
focus on model selection and development. Note that the data generation framework described in 5.1.2
supports any number of templates, with any length and sampling rate, allowing for future incorpora-
tion of biophysically modeled templates if needed. The final templates used in the project were derived
from continuously stimulated wild-type mice RGCs, which exhibit variability shape and are modulated
in amplitude and duration. These factors are drawn from distributions to account for this variation
and increase generalizability beyond a specific set of AP templates. However, comparing the project
templates with individual templates derived from porcine and human recordings is out of this project’s
scope.
7.2.3
Mains electricity artifact
The mains electricity artifact is a highly complex harmonic signal that contaminated the cellular activity
underlying the experimental data to various degrees even though the recording device included a (weak)
high-pass filter at 50 Hz (see section 4.1). In the data description chapter, it was stipulated that this
signal in most eyes and experiments had more energy than the CAPs (See Figure 4.16). It might seem
straightforward to filter away this noise (e.g., with a band-pass filter or with a series of notch filters
at the harmonic components), as the literature suggests. However, it was impossible to do so without
establishing what level to which it should diminish, as the energy and location of CAPs were unknown
ahead. Therefore, rather than trying to filter the ME signal out to an arbitrary level, it was decided
to model the data as a whole as part of the regression problem. This is also why the ME template was
added to the simulated data on two different levels.
7.3
Models
For both the classification and regression, the choice of advanced methods is motivated by the literature.
In the times series classification bake-off paper (Middlehurst, Schäfer, and Bagnall 2024), 40 different
approaches for TSC were evaluated on 144 datasets. The results demonstrated that the two classi-
fiers, MultiROCKET+Hydra (MR-hydra) and HC2, generally perform the best and are in a clique
that performs significantly better than the other classifiers. HC2 ranked higher than MR-Hydra on
most metrics across all datasets. In the paper, it is postulated that HC2 do better with longer series,
which is the case of both the experimental data and simulated data of this project. However, HC2
was found to be less effective on imbalanced datasets than MR-Hydra. Thus, a trade-off exists be-
tween handling longer time series and managing class imbalance. Additionally, a drawback of HC2
is its high computational cost. The baseline model, RandomForest, was selected as it is a well-known
classifier that can work directly with the raw data while being more computationally eﬀicient than HC2.
The selection of advanced regression methods is primarily based on the findings of Guijo-Rubio et al.
2024, which demonstrated that DrCIF, FreshPRINCE, and InceptionTimeE outperformed the other 18
regressors tested across 63 datasets. The study acknowledges certain limitations, such as some dataset

76
domains being overrepresented. Another criterion for model selection was to ensure a diverse range of
approaches was covered. According to the paper, DrCIF is interval-based, FreshPRINCE is feature-
based, and InceptionTimeE is deep-learning-based. However, DrCIF also incorporates an unsupervised
method for extracting features from intervals. The last advanced model selected was an adapted version
of InceptionTimeE, tailored specifically for spike counting.
A deep-learning-based approach was chosen for adaptation because, unlike fields such as natural lan-
guage processing and computer vision, deep learning has yet to consistently outperform classical meth-
ods in time series analysis1. Additionally, deep learning is more suited for real-life inference scenarios,
such as those presented in Kusumanchi et al. 2025 than methods with feature extraction. For example,
extracting even the ”eﬀicient-set” features in FreshPRINCE takes considerably longer than performing
inference with an InceptionTimeE-based model. However, this may not be a major concern for spike
count, as real-time predictions are not required. Finally, the focus on deep learning-based adaptation
is also influenced by the authors’ academic interest in Deep Learning.
Average prediction, linear regression, and threshold-based methods introduced by Kusumanchi et al.
2025 are selected as baseline models. Average prediction serves as an unsupervised benchmark, assess-
ing how complex the features learned by the advanced models are. If a model fails to outperform average
prediction, it indicates that it has not successfully learned meaningful features from the data. Linear
regression is selected as a simple supervised method based on input features. One limitation of such
threshold-based spike count methods is if the base noise level is much higher than the spiking activity,
no peaks will be classified as spikes. Moreover, the base noise level is established based on the entire
recording, so the model is sensitive to varying noise levels within a single sample. Another limitation is
the constant multiplier factor of 4.5. This value is established based on recordings produced by devices
other than the one used in the study, so its validity cannot be assessed based only on a single study.
The methods also count spikes and not APs, and the results are thereby not directly comparable to the
other regression methods.
7.4
Solution Pipeline
Section 5.2 introduces a two-step solution pipeline. The motivation for a two-step solution approach
stems from both the separation between TTX/”without stimulus” and ”with stimulus” and the simu-
lated data itself. The simulated data exhibits clear separation into two distinct clusters: the number of
cells spiking in a sample is approximately either 0 (non-active) or 30, 50, or 70 (active). As the classifi-
cation results in section 6.2 show, the classification step is relatively straightforward, often achieving an
F1 score of 100% (depending on the noise level). By focusing solely on active samples, the regression
step can better model the variability in spike occurrences. The division also aligns well with the larger
purpose of the study in Kusumanchi et al. 2025, namely, assessing spiking activity when testing retinal
implant designs. In some cases, the tested tissue might be dead, exhibiting little to no biological activity,
which shouldn’t be used to evaluate the performance of a retinal implant. The classification step helps
identify such cases early, as a high proportion of samples without activity could signal potential issues
with the tissue.
Another approach would have been to model the entire problem as a regression task, where the la-
bel for the ’non-active’ samples is close to 0 (spontaneous spiking can still occur). This approach could
have allowed for more time to fine-tune the regression models to the task at hand rather than relying
on one set of established settings. However, as shown in the results section 6.4, the customized version,
InceptionTimeE II, did not significantly improve upon the InceptionTimeE model with the default
settings. One drawback of the two-step approach is the potential propagation of classification errors
into the regression step. This issue will most likely arise in high-noise datasets where the classification
model does not achieve an F1-score of 100%. Since the regression models are only trained on ”with
activity”-samples, any misclassified samples would become outliers in the regression problem and likely
result in a high error rate.
When evaluating the performance of the solution pipeline, two approaches were considered: reporting

7.4.1
Classification
Two models were tested for the task of classifying whether or not cellular activity is present in the
recordings, where it was found that achieved HC2 better or comparable results on almost all metrics
than RF. Dealing with an imbalanced dataset, accuracy, even though the most common, might not be
the most representable metric as it yields misleadingly high performances that result from systemati-
cally predicting the majority class, which is ”with activity”. For the simulated datasets, only 28% of
the samples in the test set had the label ”without activity”, thereby making the datasets imbalanced.
Multiple metrics were therefore applied to evaluate the performance of the models. To ensure that the
imbalance was considered when performing the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, F1-score was used.
Both models process raw data as input; however, HC2 employs various feature extraction methods.
While it would have been possible to apply RF to extracted features like Catch22, potentially yielding
results more comparable to HC2, this approach would have the increased computational time and model
complexity. However, using features like Catch22 as input to RF could improve the model’s explain-
ability by significantly reducing the number of features and transforming them into more interpretable
representations. This could potentially have provided further insights into performance variations, par-
ticularly in data affected by ME noise, as analyzed in Section 6.2.1, however with the risk of losing
temporal information. Additionally, training and predicting with HC2 is on average 270 times slower
than RF, further emphasizing the trade-off between model complexity and performance.
7.4.2
Regression
When training the regression models, simple filtering was used to eliminate samples without stimulus.
This was done to evaluate the effect of regression alone rather than the compound effect of classification
(error) and regression (error). This was mainly important in the case of the noisier datasets, where classi-
fication had worse results. Note that the full pipeline is used when inferring from the experimental data.
To evaluate the individual models’ performance, the metrics RMSE, MAPE, and R2 were used. RMSE
is a standard error measure used in the field of TSER (eg. Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024). While RMSE gives
an absolute sense of the error, it penalizes large errors (such as outliers) more than MAPE. MAPE gives
a relative sense of the error, and thus, it might be better suited for count values with a natural minimum
of zero. However, it can be misleading when used with a larger span of output values. Together, they
give a nuanced sense of the error as the counts have a large span of values but a natural minimum of
zero. R2, on the other hand, is scale-invariant and interpretable, representing the proportion of variance
explained by the model. While R2 might be an ill-fitting measure for non-linear models, such as Neural
Networks, it might still show some goodness of fit, with the (reasonable) expectation that the residuals
are normally distributed and by only comparing similar model architectures (model complexities) to
each other.
Overall, all models performed better on the cleaner datasets and worse on the noisier ones, with the
three basic baseline models doing worse than the advanced models. The random forest-based methods
(DrCIF and FreshPRINCE) demonstrated the best overall performance, consistent with the findings
of Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024. This suggests that a model leveraging relevant time series features is suf-
ficient to achieve an RMSE below 10.76, which was FreshPRINCE’s worst performance. However, as
InceptionTimeE (original) and InceptionTimeE II had better performance in cleaner settings, directly

7.5
Prediction on Experimental Data
To summarize the findings of Section 6.5, the final solution pipeline seems to give incorrect predictions
for the experimental data. There is evidence that the simulated data doesn’t reflect the experimental
data well, and thereby, the models trained on the simulated data are biased. This is especially promi-
nent in the case of the models trained on cleaner datasets.
The expectation that the TTX data contains no APs and that the data without stimulus only con-
tains spontaneous spikes is not proven by the predictions of the final solution pipeline. Similarly, the
expectation that the 3D design is able to stimulate the tissue better than the 2D design is also not
proven by the predicted results. Ideally, one of the noise levels in this study is similar to the noise level
of the experimental data, and thus, one of the solution pipelines is relevant to it. (It is theorized that
the White Noise SNR level is close to 0 or below zero in the case of some eyes, and it is wildly different
in ME SNR between experiments.) However, no noise levels aligned with the above expectations.
This is likely due to discrepancies between the simulated data and the experimental data. Therefore,
patterns learned well in the simulated data (especially when using a model trained on cleaner datasets),
which are different from the experimental data, might lead to overfitting, which is not captured by the
test set as it is also simulated. Overall, the inconsistencies in the predictions on the experimental data
suggest that the models of the solution pipeline are unreliable for this dataset.
7.6
Limited resources
Limited resources in memory and time meant that certain steps of the research and parameters of
models had to be curtailed. Firstly, models with random components, such as DrCIF, FreshPRINCE,
and all InceptionTimeE variants, were trained only three times. RandomForest, HC2, DrCIF, Fresh-
PRINCE, the original InceptionTimeE, and InceptionTimeE II. were run 3 times on each dataset. This
is considerably lower than, e.g., the 30 runs presented by Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024. Adjusting the many
parameters of DrCIF and FreshPRINCE could have caused model training to increase (number of
estimators, tree parameters, number of extracted features, etc.). Time limitations dictated that the
number of estimators in both cases was reduced from 200 to 100. Since one goal of the study was to
explore the adaptation of InceptionTimeE for AP counts in noisy settings, considering one version (set
of parameters) of these models is suﬀicient.
The adaptation of InceptionTimeE was restricted to a single training run per parameter combination
due to the high number of models (72 models on 12 datasets) and the training time of the models (0.5-8
hours)3. Increasing the number of sub-models to stabilize results in the ensemble would have resulted
in a linear increase in training time. Nevertheless, InceptionTimeE, an ensemble model, provides ac-
ceptable stability across runs with the recommended 5 sub-models (Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024).
Secondly, memory usage was an issue for all models as the more memory was requested on the Technical
University of Denmark’s High-Performance Computing cluster, the more queuing time was expected.
Therefore, expansion in model parameters was minimized as much as possible, especially for DrCIF,
FreshPRINCE, and InceptionTimeE, due to their large number of features. Finally, to further validate
the results, a larger train and test set or cross-validation would have been beneficial. However, the
former was limited by memory constraints, while the latter was restricted by time.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
8.1
Conclusion
This work has examined how spike counting in retinal prosthetics research can be assessed and im-
proved under unknown noise levels.
Specifically, it has proposed a solution pipeline for evaluating
neural activity across the two different carbon electrode designs proposed by Kusumanchi et al. 2025
(RQ1). Synthetic datasets with varying noise levels were created to enable the use of supervised mod-
els, thereby allowing for assessment of the performance of the selected state-of-the-art TSER models,
providing a controlled framework for reliably evaluating these methods (RQ2).
Classifying the presence of neural activity proved significantly easier than quantifying the exact num-
ber of action potentials, as evidenced by the strong performance of HC2 across a wide range of noise
levels. However, none of the regression models achieved satisfactory performance in estimating the true
number of APs. Although all advanced models outperformed the baselines, the improvement, while no-
table, was insuﬀicient, highlighting the fundamental challenge: reliable spike counting under high noise
remains unsolved, making precise quantification inconclusive with the current approaches. However,
the current regressor exhibits a percentage error of 10–20% depending on the noise level, which is too
high for exact spike quantification. Despite this limitation, the pipeline remains valuable for comparing
relative differences in neural activity across experimental conditions.
FreshPRINCE, an ensemble model leveraging extracted time-series features, performed best across
all noise levels. DrCIF was most effective in noisy settings, while InceptionTimeE with the architecture
and hyperparameters proposed by Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020 and Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024 showed
better results in clean settings. This indicates that IncpetionTimeE has the potential to learn the shape
of individual AP templates in MEA recordings but is unreliable under realistic settings such as those in
Kusumanchi et al. 2025. Moreover, the adaptation of InceptionTimeE was unsuccessful as none of the
proposed architectures yielded significant improvements over the original. Notably, the differences in
performance among advanced regression models were statistically insignificant from each other across
all noise levels (RQ1).
The thresholding method proposed by Kusumanchi et al. 2025 was not designed to count APs, making
it unsuitable for this purpose. Additionally, since CAPs themselves are ill-defined, the method also
lacks interpretability. Counting spikes does not directly correspond to identifying individual APs, and
attempting to do so is unreliable in noisy settings. The method performed significantly worse than
the solution pipeline in estimating true AP counts in the simulated data. However, it is important to
note that the synthetic data generation process does not perfectly replicate experimental conditions,
meaning the simulated data cannot capture all noise sources present in the experimental data (RQ3)
Going forward with the research in Kusumanchi et al. 2025, reducing noise levels in the recordings,
especially that of the ME artifact, is recommended.
Another recommended approach is accurately
recording or obtaining a set of prototypical porcine APs templates and adjusting them in an informed
manner to create simulated datasets using the proposed synthetic data generation framework. Training
the proposed solution pipeline’s models on data more closely resembling the experimental conditions is
expected to enhance inference accuracy beyond current methods.
This thesis contributed to retinal implant development by advancing methods for detecting neural
responses, a crucial step in improving prosthetic vision. Enhancing spike detection reliability supports
the long-term goal of restoring sight to the blind through more effective stimulation strategies.

8.2
Future work
While this study provides valuable insights into the assessment of retinal implants by AP counting,
several areas remain unexplored and need further investigation.
Although the synthetic data generation presented in this work allows for using any type and amount of
AP templates, the actual data used for the simulated dataset came from continuously stimulated mice
APs. For a better approximation of the APs in Kusumanchi et al. 2025, porcine APs templates obtained
with discrete, bi-phasic 500 mV stimulus would have been more adequate. (A similar principle applies
to research with human tissue.) For maximal precision, one can also employ biophysical modelling for
obtaining these templates (Buccino and Einevoll 2021). This was, however, outside of the scope of
the thesis as an extensive set of intracellular AP measurements are needed. In this study, the SA was
defined as a 300-point template and was removed for convenience, but as there is no clear boundary
between the SA and the response period and spiking can be concealed by the ”SA template”. It might
be better to attempt to remove it by other means, e.g., ICA or template removal (Akhoun, McKay, and
El-Deredy 2013).
Evaluating model results with more repetitions of initializations and training would be highly appropri-
ate for future work. Although more time-intensive, training on an even larger dataset would yield more
stable results and allow for a greater diversity of samples. This would be especially important when
there is uncertainty about the data generation parameters, such as the expected number of vital cells.
To further the research of Kusumanchi et al. 2025, one should consider reducing noise sources, such as
those associated with handling the retinal tissue and artifacts of the recording environment (ME arti-
fact), as both supervised and unsupervised methods benefit from cleaner data. If the interplay between
the cells is important to measure, a MEA with lower than 500 µm pitch is recommended.
Regarding the specific models used in this work, FreshPRINCE and DrCIF performed well under various
noise levels. Since both are feature-based ensemble methods utilizing trees, an in-depth investigation
into the specific estimators and features that contributed to their success would be beneficial. Specifi-
cally, in DrCIF, it would be interesting to see which individual trees, thus which intervals, are accurate
and evaluate the relative importance of the three different input time series. With FreshPRINCE, one
might investigate the contribution of certain PCA directions of each estimator to the final output. Note
that this study focused on exploring and adapting NN-based solutions; thus, such exploration was be-
yond the main objective.
A moderate hyperparameter search was conducted to adapt InceptionTimeE to the simulated data.
The range of hyperparameters, as well as the number of architectures, was not suﬀicient to find a model
better than the setup proposed by Ismail Fawaz, Lucas, et al. 2020 and Guijo-Rubio et al. 2024. In
order to find one, a more extensive search could be performed. A flattening-out effect can be seen in
the loss curves of many of the tried architectures, indicating that a more complex architecture can be
employed. Using even larger kernel sizes, for instance, provides another way of extending the recep-
tive field of the network. On the hind side, this would require adequate regularization, parameters of
which would also need to be determined, and the training and inference time of such a model might
also increase substantially. On the other hand, a more appropriate initial CNN might decrease the
model’s overall size while potentially maintaining the level of initial down-sampling achieved in this
work. Another way in which InceptionTimeE could be adapted is to use the ensemble as a Bayesian
deep ensemble (Lakshminarayanan, Pritzel, and Blundell 2017). Considering the set of predictions as
a posterior distribution would provide a natural way of estimating epistemic uncertainty.

A.1
Stimulation Artifact
The SA is defined as a 300 data point (0.01s) period (to ensure that the whole SA is captured), which
is followed by a 2700 point (0.09s) response period that might contain CAPs (with stimulus data) and
might only contain noise (TTX data). All subsequent stimulation artifacts have a start point exactly
3000 points away from the first. The first SA’s starting point was found manually, and it is the same
across channels of the same experiment but is different between experiment types and eyes. Therefore
2 × 6 × 2 starting points were found ([2D, 3D]× [6 eyes] × [TTX, with stimulus] ).
