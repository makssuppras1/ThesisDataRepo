Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the possibility of using electroenchephalog-
raphy (EEG) signals to decode the brain state. We have designed an experiment to
collect data, where subjects view images. Furthermore, the objective is to mind read
by generating captions of the content on the images that the subjects view. Mind
reading is conducted by first predicting image semantics, which are vectors including
2048 features, from the EEG signals. The true image semantics are created by feeding
the images through the Inception-v3 model, which is a convolutional neural network.
The predicted image semantics are fed through a caption generating recurrent neu-
ral network, that ideally writes semantically correct captions to the images that the
subjects view during the experiment.
The EEG experiment was conducted with 15 subjects viewing 690 different images
of common objects within 23 different categories. The EEG signals were analyzed to
understand if we were able to decode what subjects viewed from their brain activity.
Using the raw EEG signals and a convolutional neural network, we were able to decode
the brain state by predicting categories with an accuracy of 19% ±2%, where baseline
was 4.35%. Through similarity analysis, we realized that some subjects had strong
temporal effects, which might have affected the classification. Canonical correlation
analyzes showed that the temporal effects could be distinguished by the first canonical
components when comparing the EEG data to the image semantics.
In terms of mind reading we investigated the predictable features of the image
semantics predicted by a ridge regression, and found that the features we were able to
predict not necessarily held information about the 23 categories. We also discovered,
that a dense neural network was unable to predict the images semantics. Finally,
we generated captions from the image semantics predicted by the ridge regression,
which resulted in captions, but not semantically correct ones. The closest we got to
mind reading was to predict correct categories from the predicted image semantics
using the last softmax layer of the Inception-v3 model, and randomly pick captions
generated by the caption generating network from the true image semantics.

Resumé
Denne afhandling undersøger muligheden for at anvende EEG signaler til at afkode
hjernens tilstand. Vi har designet et eksperiment til at indsamle data, hvor forsøgsper-
sonerne kigger på billeder. Desuden er målet at tankelæse ved at generere billedtekster
til de billeder, som forsøgspersonerne kigger på. Tankelæsning udføres ved først at
forudsige ’image semantics’, som er 2048 lange vektorer, fra EEG signaler. De rigtige
’image semantics’ genereres ved at køre billederne igennem Inception-v3, som er et
convolutional neuralt netværk. De forudsagte ’image semantics’ er input til et tek-
stgenererende neuralt netværk, der ideelt skriver semantisk korrekte billedtekster til
de billeder, som forsøgspersonerne ser under eksperimentet.
EEG eksperimentet blev udført med 15 forsøgspersoner, der i alt så 690 forskellige
billeder af almindelige objekter inden for 23 forskellige kategorier. EEG signalerne
blev analyseret for at forstå, om vi var i stand til at afkode det forsøgspersonerne
så fra deres hjerneaktivitet. Ved hjælp af de rå EEG signaler og et convolutional
neuralt netværk kunne vi afkode hjernens tilstand ved at forudsige kategorier med
en nøjagtighed på 19 % ±2%, hvor tilfældigheden var 4,35%. Gennem dataanalyse
indså vi, at nogle forsøgspersoner havde stærke tidsmæssige effekter, hvilket kan have
påvirket klassifikationen. CCA viste, at den tidsmæssige effekt var tydelig i de første
komponenter ved sammenligning af EEG signaler og ’image semantics’.
Med hensyn til tankelæsning undersøgte vi de forudsigelige elementer i ’image
semantics’ forudsagt af en ridge regression, og fandt ud af, at de elementer, vi
kunne forudsige ikke nødvendigvis indeholder informationer om de 23 kategorier. Vi
fandt også ud af, at et neuralt netværk ikke kunne forudsige ’image semantics’. Til
sidst genererede vi billedtekster fra ’image semantics’, forudsagt af ridge regressionen,
hvilket ikke resulterede i semantisk korrekte billedtekster. Det nærmeste vi kom på
at tankelæse, var at forudsige korrekte kategorier fra de forudsagte ’image semantics’
ved hjælp af det sidste softmax-lag i Inception-v3 og tilfældigt udvælge billedtekster,
genereret af det tekstgenererende neurale netværk fra de rigtige ’image semantics’.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Wouldn’t it be cool if we could read people’s minds? If we could actually decode what
people were thinking based on recorded signals from the brain. The last decade’s de-
velopment within measuring brain signals have created advantages within many fields
of research such as epilepsy, brain disorders, marketing analysis and mind reading as
well. There are many advantages in understanding the human brain and its processes.
Being able to understand what people are thinking would be more than just cool,
but would create immense value within communication for paralyzed people and
hearing impaired people.
If we could mind read, we would, for example, be able
to communicate with paralyzed people who cannot talk and write or deaf children
who cannot talk and have not learned to communicate by reading and writing yet.
However, systems like this could also benefit able-bodied people. Facebook announced
at their F8 conference in San Jose April 2017 that their secretive Building 8 research
group is looking into typing with the brain. The goal is to type 100 words per minute.
The technology methods that Facebook is planning to use is still unclear, but it will be
non-invasive [50]. So far, typing with the brain has a record of typing eight words per
minute using an invasive implant in the brain [64]. In terms of non-invasive methods,
the latest research was done in 2008 where ALS patients typed between 1.5 and 4.1
correct characters per minute which is around 0.3 to 0.82 words per minute [60]. With
these state of the art results, Facebook’s 100 words per minute typer is probably not
right around the corner. According to Facebook, over time, their research could result
in a way to ’think’ a message rather than typing it, which is more aligned with the
mind reading idea [50].
In an interview about Facebook’s announcement, Thomas Naselaris says that
the understanding of the way words and their semantic attributes are encoded in
brain activity is quite good currently, but this is mainly due to functional magnetic
resonance imaging (fMRI) studies of the brain. Even though fMRI is non-invasive, it
is very slow and not portable at all [75], which is clear in figure 1.1(a).
From fMRI studies we know that it is possible to decode objects and categories
from brain activity. In 2012, Huth et al used voxelwise models to examine the cortical
representation of 1705 objects and action categories [27]. This later lead to showing
that the semantic system in the brain is organized into patterns. These patterns seem
to be shared across individuals [29]. From brain activity signals measured by fMRI it
has been possible to decode object and action categories that the subjects have been
viewing in a movie [28], as well as making scene classification with 72% accuracy [83].

1 Introduction
(a) fMRI scanning (image from UC San Diego [32])
(b) Our EEG experiment with Enobio 32
Figure 1.1: fMRI is very cumbersome compared to EEG.
The research within this field is dominated by fMRI, whereas only a few studies
are using EEG to decode brain signals ([38], [73], [54], [35]). EEG is a portable and
cost effective method compared to fMRI [85]. fMRI scannings are very noisy which
potentially could affect the subject’s brain activity, and subjects with claustrophobia
would not be able to attend. EEG is easy to use, especially with an EEG cap system
as shown in figure 1.1(b).
Furthermore, experiments can be conducted anywhere.
The time to prepare the subject for an experiment with the EEG cap is very short.
Another advantage of EEG is that noise, also called artifacts, are easier to remove
from the signals compared to noise in fMRI signals. If looking at data gathered per
second, the fMRI and EEG are comparable. fMRI measures 50.000 numbers every
two seconds, which is a sampling rate of 25 kHz. An EEG cap with 32 electrodes
sampling with a frequency rate of 500 Hz, like the system used in this thesis, gives an
overall sampling rate of 16 kHz. This means that there is almost the same amount
of information in EEG data as in fMRI data. With the high temporal resolution of
EEG there might be a better chance of decoding semantic meaningful sentences from
brain activity. We hypothesize that EEG may be as successful in decoding the brain
and be as relevant as fMRI within this field of research.
To investigate this hypothesis, we conduct an EEG experiment where subjects
are looking at images of different objects. We want to investigate if we can decode
what people are looking at from their brain signals using different data analyzes. The
optimal goal of this thesis is to actually mind read. But how are we going to read
the mind of people? We know that neural networks can create text narratives from
image input. Can we create text narratives from EEG signals using neural networks?
An image pipeline is set up to create image semantics of images and train a caption
generating neural network to generate captions with image semantics as input. In this
thesis, an EEG pipeline is created with the purpose of predicting the image semantics
from the EEG data recorded while subjects are looking at the real images. The image
and EEG pipelines are visualized in figure 1.2.

Structure of the thesis
The chapters in this thesis are organized as follows:
• Chapter 2 provides the background of this thesis by presenting state of the art
articles within mind reading giving an overview of ’the story of mind reading’.
• Chapter 3 explains the theory behind the data analyzes conducted in this thesis.
• Chapter 4 describes the EEG experiment conducted in this thesis including the
experimental design and setup.
• Chapter 5 gives an overview of the data sets, the methods and data analyzes,
including how they have been applied.
• Chapter 6 presents the results of the EEG experiment and the data analyzes.
• Chapter 7 discusses the results presented in Chapter 6 including ideas for future
work.
• Chapter 8 concludes this thesis.

CHAPTER 2
The story of mind
reading
This chapter focuses on state of the art throughout history within decoding infor-

2.1
Initial steps towards decoding of brain state
Some of the initial steps towards decoding of the brain state were taken at the Tech-
nical University of Denmark (DTU).
The first study used positron emission tomography (PET) images where only one
parameter differed; the frequency of saccadic eye motion. It was conducted in 1994.
The aim of the study was to predict the frequency using a neural network. It is an
extremely ill-posed problem, because there are more parameters to estimate in the
model than number of samples. They showed that by using principal component anal-
ysis (PCA) to reduce the dimensions of the inputs, the problem can be transformed
to a marginally ill-posed problem, which can be handled with regularization. They
presented an alternative regularization method called pruning of the network weights.
Only using 48 images they were able to capture the one-parametric rule and predict
the frequency using a neural network [41].
The second study was a comparison of linear and non-linear models, in this case
artificial neural networks (ANN). They introduced the concept of generalization using
functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and PET data, where subjects were
conducting a simple motor task. They showed that generalization is affected by N, the
number of samples, and that a crossover appeared when plotting the generalization
error as a function of N. This means that for small datasets, the generalization error
was lowest for simple, more biased linear models, but for larger datasets, the more
flexible non-linear models performed best.
They argued that the flexible models
are more complex in terms of parameter estimation due to the non-linearity of the
parameters. However, the flexible models generalize better than the biased models,
where parameter estimation is less complex due to the linearity of the parameters

2.2
Identification of objects from brain activity
A lot of research focus on identification of different object categories from brain ac-
tivity. In 2001, Haxby et al [22] were able to predict objects and faces, that subjects,
while fMRI scanned, had been viewing. The predictions were based on patterns of
brain responses in ventral temporal cortex using pair-wise correlation comparisons.
The method was tested using all regions of cortex (96% mean accuracy), and all re-
gions except the ones with maximum response to the object categories being compared
(94% accuracy). The results indicated that not only maximum responsive areas carry
information about the object category, but the representation of faces and objects
are widely distributed and overlapping even in small-responsive regions. This was
the start of something interesting, even though only faces, cats and five man-made
objects were included in the study.
In 2012, Huth et al [27] showed that a continuous semantic space that reflects cat-
egory similarity describes representation of 1705 object and action categories across
the brain. This contradicts that each category is presented in a distinct brain area.
They used fMRI scans from subjects watching natural movies, WordNet [52] to label
the objects and actions in the movies and regularized linear regression for each voxel
resulting in 1705 weights indicating how each object or action influence the BOLD
responses in each voxel. They used PCA to recover the semantic space where the
first four principal components (PC) were significant for all subjects indicating that
subjects share broad aspects of semantic representation, whereas finer-scale repre-
sentations are specific to each subject. The full model (including all 1705 weights)
correctly predicted 76% of stimuli on average across subjects, whereas the one using
the four significants PCs had an accuracy of 49%. In 2016, Huth et al [28] presented a
new study where they decoded the semantic content of natural movies to extend their
previous studies. They discovered that the semantic hierarchical relationships defined
in WordNet, did not seem to be present in the brain activity when the example was
an atypical one (plant) compared to the general category (organism).
Horikawa et al [26] used a different technique to decode objects from brain signals.
They called it ’generic object decoding’, because they decoded object classes that were
not included in the training set. They used fMRI signals to predict visual features.
From these features they identified the object that the subject had been viewing
or imagining. This approach enabled them to decode object classes that were not
included in the training set, but included in the feature vector database. The object
was identified by calculating the correlation between the category-average feature
vectors (1000 different ones including the correct category) and the predicted feature
vector.
The object identified was the one with the highest correlation coefficient.
They managed to identify the object within top six highest correlation coefficients for
most of the test set.

2.3
Decoding words and sentences from brain activity
In 2011, Pereira et al [68] wanted to generate verbal description of mental content.
They used fMRI data where subjects had been scanned while reading concrete concept
names and viewing line drawings of what they read. Using a topic model, trained
in unsupervised manner, they were able to predict words related to the concept that
the subject had been reading and looking at. However, this way of generating words,
ignored syntax and word order. They developed their work further in 2016 [69] where
they used GloVe word embeddings [67] instead to build a forward model that decoded
semantic information and generalized to topics not seen in training. The forward
model is a mapping between the brain activation and the stimulus represented as
GloVe embeddings. By inverting the forward model they could estimate the word
embedding and find the best matching word embedding to the estimated, thereby
predicting words about the concrete and abstract concepts that subjects had been
reading or viewing. The argued that their approach can be extended to even decode
complex stimuli such as sentences.
Extending the studies using line drawings and static images ([68] [69]) Vodrahalli
et al (2017) [83] used natural movies to create a bidirectional mapping between fMRI
data and natural language annotation embeddings. The embeddings used weighted
combinations of vectors representing each word in the annotation. Using an indepen-
dent component analysis (ICA) based Shared Response Model (SRM) for aggregating
fMRI reponses from all subjects and using the previous time step information when
predicting current time step, they got an accuracy of 72% in classifying scenes when
decoding from fMRI →Text. Poorer results were obtained when encoding from Text
→to fMRI.
In close relation to [83] Nishida et al (2017) [61] did a similar experiment, decoding
distributed representation of words from natural movies. A word vector space was
created beforehand using Wikipedia word corpus and the skim-gram algorithm [51]. A
scene vector matrix was generated by projecting annotations of the movie scenes into
the word vector space. This matrix was estimated using a regularized linear model,
where weights were fitted during training. When decoding new scenes the output
was the estimated scene vector where correlations were calculated between the scene
vector and word vectors, and the words getting the highest scores were more likely to
be in the scene. They managed to decode different types of words (nouns, verbs and
adjectives), and were also able to decode words that were not included in the training
set.
Going back to Pereira et al [70] in 2018, they used their decoder from previous
study [69] to decode sentence stimuli. The output was a decoded semantic vector
which was compared to the text semantic vector representing the true sentence stim-

2.4
Relation to our work
The study by Matsuo et al [48] is very similar to the goal of this thesis. We want
to write captions to images that people have been viewing during an experiment
by feeding image semantics predicted from brain activity into a caption generating
recurrent neural network. The main difference is that we are using EEG data instead
of fMRI. Decoding the brain state from activity in the brain is a field that primarily
has used fMRI, potentially together with magnetoencephalograpgy (MEG) as Cichy et
al did in 2014 [8], where they investigated the temporal resolution of object recognition
in the brain. Only a few articles have used EEG signals.
Decoding semantics from EEG is more challenging because EEG opposite to fMRI
does not have high spatial resolution, but better temporal resolution, which we hy-

decoding, early studies have shown that semantic sources of category-specific effect,
when viewing images of different objects within categories, can be distinguished by
EEG [38].

got the best performance when using drawings (89%), but only 77% when predicting
on an unseen exemplar (one of the items left out of training).

and 30 work tools presented six times each giving 360 trials for seven subjects. The

3.1
EEG signals
The data in this thesis is recorded using electroencephalography also referred to as
EEG. Activity in the brain, neuronal communication, is associated with electrical
currents that result in electrical potentials on the scalp. These electrical potentials
can be measured using EEG.
The first non-invasive EEG signals from a human were recorded in the 1920’s by
Hans Berger [2]. Compared to the Neuroelectrics cap used to record EEG signals in
this thesis (see figure 4.1(a)), Berger’s setup was primitive. He used platinum wires,
zinc-plated steel needles and large plate electrodes connected to a Siemens double-
coil galvanometer. The output of the EEG signals was recorded onto photographic
paper. This was how Berger discovered the alpha rhythm in the brain, when recording
signals from two electrodes, one located over the frontal scalp and the other located
over the occipital scalp. He also discovered that the alpha rhythm was affected by
loud noises, mental effort, painful stimuli and eye opening. In 1934 Lord Adrian and
Matthews [1] proposed the ’idling’ hypothesis, when they discovered that the alpha
rhythm appeared when not much was happening in the cortex. They also showed that
the alpha rhythm appeared from posterior parts of the cortex most likely the visual
cortex, because the alpha rhythm appeared when the eyes were open in a dark room,
but disappeared when the eyes were open in light. Later on, more brain rhythms were
discovered, each of them representing different states of the mind. The alpha rhythm
is e.g. strongest when the eyes are closed [21]. Table 3.1 below gives an overview of
the different brain rhythms and when they were first described.
Besides the spontaneous brain activity, whether it is one of the above mentioned
brain rhythms or not, EEG can show evoked responses to various stimuli. These
responses are often referred to as event-related potentials (ERPs) and are small and
noisy signals in the EEG. The signals recorded as EEG are generated when activations
of neurons are happening in the brain.
The main source currents come from the
cortical pyramidal neurons. A pyramidal neuron consists of a soma (the cell body),
apical dendrites (receiving inputs from other cells) and an axon (delivers the neuron’s
impulse to other neurons). The net current that can be recorded by EEG, results
from a summation of post-synaptic potentials happening in any depth of the cortex
at a given time. Only when large populations of these pyramidal cells are firing in a
synchronized pattern the generated electrical activity is recordable on the scalp.

When recording EEG signals, electrodes are fixed on the scalp i.e. using an EEG
cap and potentials are measured between two electrodes. The amplitude of the scalp
EEG signal is often between 50-100 µV . EEG is recorded with millisecond temporal
resolution.
This very high time resolution gives insights into the brain processes
and development of brain activity and is useful when investigating rapidly changing
behavior in the brain. This is an advantage when conducting experiments with image
or video stimuli, because EEG allows for recording of processes that occur shortly
after the onset of visual stimuli.
There are consistent brain processes 50-100 ms
post stimulus [21]. The spatial resolution of EEG is not as strong as in e.g. fMRI
or MEG. One reason is that the EEG potentials on the surface are generated by
electrical currents of different orientation and depths in the brain. Another reason
is the electrical inhomogeneities of the layers in the brain. This leads to a difficult
problem with EEG signals; to localize the brain sources, where the activations given
rise to the electrical signals have taken place. This is often referred to as the inverse
problem, because it does not have a unique solution.
By using source modeling
based on the EEG signals and an additional assumption about the conductivity of
the different head-tissue layers, it is possible to estimate the source locations. One of
the reasons why source modeling of EEG is difficult, is smearing caused by the scull.
The electrical potential distribution on the scalp is widespread, and the differences
in electrical conductivity for the different brain parts such as scull and scalp tissues
result in lateral spread, also called smearing of the electrical potentials.

3.1.2
Artifacts
Artifacts are disturbances in the EEG signals from various sources. Artifacts can be
hard to avoid when recording EEG signals, but being aware of them increases the
chances of avoiding them. If EEG signals contain artifacts, different methods are used
for removing them such as filtering and using independent component analysis (ICA)
based approaches. Filtering of EEG signals, as part of the preprocessing, is always
necessary. Different kinds of filters can be used, the most common being high-pass,
low-pass, band-pass and notch filters. ICA can be used in terms of identifying eye
movement and other artifacts. Below, the most common and relevant artifacts for
this thesis are described.
3.1.2.1
Biological artifacts
Overall, the biological artifacts are eye-related artifacts, muscle artifacts, cardiac
artifacts and sweating. The last one is easily avoided by keeping a nice temperature
in the room where the experiment is conducted. Eye-related artifacts, such as blinking
and eye movement, are best avoided by designing the experiment so the subject can
blink at special times and is not likely to move the eyes around. This only apply when
subjects are watching static things on a screen. Muscle artifacts can be avoided by
instructing the subject not to move during the experiment, relax in the muscles, and
setting up an environment where the subject can relax. For example, some people
have a tendency to tighten their jaw while focusing or clamping the eyes if they cannot
see properly. Cardiac artifacts are harder to avoid during the recording, but all three
types of artifacts can be removed using special software or potentially an ICA based
approach after data is recorded [21].
3.1.2.2
Environmental artifacts
Environmental artifacts refers to artifacts created by the settings around the experi-
ment. The most common one is power-line noise. This arises from the electrical wall
outlets and occurs at either 50 Hz or 60 Hz depending on the country. In Denmark
it is 50 Hz. Power-line noise is difficult to avoid in an EEG recording, but optimal

3.1.3
Recording of EEG signals
When recording EEG signals, there are a lot of practicalities to keep in mind. This sec-
tion will briefly touch upon these practicalities, but the actual experiment conducted
in connection with this thesis is described in Chapter 4.
First of all, there are different methods and instruments for recording EEG. Exam-
ples are intracranial EEG (iEEG), where electrodes are placed directly on the surface
of the brain e.g. during surgery, Biosemi which is non-invasive and can have up to
280 electrodes, which often includes gel electrodes to improve conductivity. Finally,
easy to get on portable EEG caps exist, often with a lower number of electrodes than
the Biosemi.
No matter which instrument the EEG signals are recorded with, some very general
recommendations apply to ensure satisfying results. First of all, it is very important
that the subject is relaxed, but alert and not tired. The subject should not be given
caffeinated drinks to become more awake. To relax the subject, the settings of the
experiment have to be nice and cozy e.g. with soft lightening and a comfortable chair.
A sympathetic and professional experimenter can also help relaxing the subject. The
subject has to be prepared for the experiment as well. It is a good idea that the subject
has washed the hair with shampoo, but without conditioner before the experiment,
that the hair is dry without any wax or hair spray. If the subject is wearing contact
lenses normally, and gets dry eyes easily it might be an idea to suggest that they wear
glasses for the experiment. Another consideration is the reference electrode and how
to measure the EEG signal. With the software that exists nowadays, it is smartest to
record the signal with a reference electrode e.g. in the earlobe and not recording as
average referencing, since this can always be changed after the signals are recorded.
Normally, experiments are conducted in the subject’s native language including
the actual experiment and the instructions to the subject before the experiment. It
is important that all subjects receive the same instructions before the experiment,
and therefore it is recommended that the experimenters follow a fixed protocol. Dur-
ing the experiment, the experimenter should monitor the EEG signals in case the
impedance is increasing for the electrodes or the subject needs more instructions. It

3.2.1
Principal component analysis (PCA)
Principal component analysis (PCA) can be used to reduce the dimensions of data.
Imagine a dataset X ∈RNxDX where N number of samples is 300 and D number of
features is 100. How would we plot these 100 dimensions? Humans can maximum
understand three dimension and it would only be possible to plot the 300 data points
using three of the features. This plot would not represent the whole truth, but only
represent the three features plotted. In cases like this, PCA becomes very useful.
PCA can also be useful in terms of pre-processing data for data analysis. If a dataset
is very large and computer power is limited, a smaller dataset will decrease the run-
time. Explained in words, the principal component analysis creates as many principal
components as there are features in the dataset. The first principal component repre-
sents most of the variance in the dataset, the second principal component represents
second most variance and is orthogonal to the first, and so on as illustrated in figure
3.1 [3].

direction accounts for maximum variance, and the second principal direction vector
is orthogonal to the first representing the direction of second most variance in the
dataset. Projecting the original data X onto the principal directions V, gives the
principal components:

3.3
Neural networks
This section focuses on neural networks and how they work. Neural networks were
first discussed in the 1940’s where the field was called cybernetics. The popularity and
usage of neural networks have been limited since then, but around 2006, a new wave
of the field referred to as deep learning started. Today, it is often considered as a new
exciting technology because of the major breakthroughs during the last decade. The
reasons behind the breakthroughs are mainly the amount of data we have now a days
and the increased computational power of computers [17]. Historically, computers
have been very good at solving complex math problems that humans cannot grasp.
Computers, however, have a hard time solving tasks like identifying objects in an
image which comes very easily to a human. These problems are problems that are
intuitive for humans, and hard to describe formally. The purpose of deep learning is to
make computers able to solve these intuitive problems by learning through experience
and concepts. Computers can learn complex concepts by building them from simpler
ones [17].
Simply described a neural network model contains an input layer, a number of
hidden layers and an output layer. Each layer consists of a set of weights and an
activation function. Figure 3.3 shows the structure of a feed-forward neural network,
which is the simplest type of neural network. There are other types, such as convo-
lutional neural network (CNN) and recurrent neural network (RNN), which will be
introduced later. The next topics are explained in relation to the feed-forward neural
network.
The purpose of a neural network model is like any other machine learning model
to predict the output based on some input. The goal is to get a low error or high
accuracy. In order to achieve the best result, the model is trained by using as much
training data as possible. In each layer of the model, there are weights and biases.
Depending on the size of the network, the network can potentially have thousands
of trainable weights and biases. The goal of the training is basically to find the best

3.3.4
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have revolutionized the state of the art within
object recognition and processing within images, videos, speech and audio [45]. An
example of a CNN is the Inception-v3 model developed by Google, which has a
top-5 error of 3.58% in terms of object classification from images [76]. The CNN
architecture was proposed by Yann LeCun et al in 1989 [44] where it was used to
classify handwritten digits. This section will focus on how a CNN works in terms of
extracting image features from images.
A CNN uses convolution in stead of general matrix multiplication in at least one of
its layers. When data is fed through a CNN layer it typically happens in three stages.
First stage performs convolutions in parallel to create linear activations, which in the
second stage are run though activation functions such as ReLU and finally, a pooling
function is used in the third stage to modify the output of the layer further [17].
Figure 3.6 demonstrates the architecture of a typical CNN for image classification.
The input to a convolutional layer is a 3D-matrix, called the input feature map,
e.g. an image of size 299 pixels x 299 pixels x 3 rgb colors. The convolution operation
extracts patches from the input and performs the same transformation on all patches,
which results in an output feature map. The transformations performed are linear
and called the convolution kernel, which is the weights that are learned while training
a convolutional neural network. The parameters of a convolutional layer is the depth
of the output, which is also the number of features, that encode specific aspects of the
input and the size of the patches extracted from the input. The patches are extracted
by sliding a patch-sized window across the input [7]. A common patch size is 3 x
3, which is the case in the example in figure 3.7, that demonstrates the convolution
operation.
As seen in figure 3.6, a convolution is often followed by a pooling. The pooling
reduces the dimensions of the output feature map of the convolution operation. A very

3.4.1
Dataset
The image captioning pipeline uses images from the Microsoft Common Objects in

3.4.2
Caption preprocessing
The captions are preprocessed in four steps. First all letters are set to lower case,
special characters except [.,] are removed from the sentences and [!?] are transformed
to dots. To use the words in the model, the vector representation of the words are
needed. In this case, GloVe has been used as the ’word-to-vector’ tool [67]. The 400K
vocabulary of words represented as 100 dimensional vectors from GloVe has been
used, which can be found as a text file on the GloVe website2. Second step consists
of removing the captions that include words which cannot be found in the text file
of GloVe words. The remaining sentences are appended a <start> and an <end>
token. Finally, the captions that contain words which occur less than three times in
the entire sentence corpus are removed. The reason for this is to reduce the size of
the vocabulary and to ensure that weird sentences are removed.
To be able to use the captions in the model, a corpus of all the words in the
Microsoft COCO captions is created after the preprocessing of the captions. The
corpus is a vector that is 13458 long representing an index for each word. It is used
to represent the target word for a prediction and is encoded as a one-hot vector (1 in
the index representing the actual word and zeros on all the other indexes).

3.4.2.1
GloVe embedding
GloVe stands for Global Vectors for Word Representation and is developed at Stanford

Shortly explained, GloVe is an unsupervised learning algorithm that

and local context window methods, which are the two most popular model families

network. The feature vectors capture the semantic content of the image. The result

3.4.4
Model setup
The model that the image pipeline wants to optimize, is a recurrent neural network

of an image concatenated with the matching GloVe embedded caption up until just
before the next word that the RNN is suppose to predict. This means that the caption
can either be one word long, and then the network has to predict the second word
in the caption, or it can be e.g. four words long and the network has to predict
the fifth word in the caption. The maximum number of words in a caption is 59.
The final layer of the network is a softmax layer which results in a vector of length
13458 representing all the words in the corpus with probabilities summing to 1. This
softmax for the predicted next word and the true next word are compared in the cost
function to backpropagate through the network and update the weights. Categorical
cross entropy loss is used as the cost function.

4.3
Experimental design
There are many aspects to consider when designing an experiment. Discovering what
works and what does not work throughout the design phase, makes it an iterative
process. The initial experiment design was inspired by [8] and [5]. Pilot experiments
were conducted during the design phase when trying the set up, testing the blinking
and wearing the Enobio cap while concentrating for a certain amount of time. This
section will describe the overall experiment design and includes the thinking behind
designing the experiment.
In the experiment the subjects are presented with visual stimuli. A gray back-
ground is overlaid with probe word, stimuli, fixation cross, ’break’, ’ready’ or ’done’
(see figure 4.2(b)). The latter is shown when the experiment is over, ’ready’ before
the experiment starts and after a break, and ’break’ before a break. The experiment
is designed to run in blocks of image categories. In total there are 23 different im-
age categories. Figure 4.2(a) shows the entire experiment including the categories in
random order. Each block consists of a probe word (5 seconds) follow by 30 images
(stimuli) within the category of the probe word separated by an interstimulus interval
(ISI). In the interstimulus interval a fixation cross appears on the gray background.
Figure 4.2(b) is a small part of the entire experiment and demonstrates how all blocks
start with a probe word and then a fixation cross before the first stimulus.

The duration of each stimulus is 1 second. In [8] it is found that exemplar-level
information can be decoded in around 100 ms, but more abstract information takes
longer. According to [78], a great deal of visual processing will occur before 150 ms.
The reason why we show stimulus for an entire second is that we want to potentially
capture more than what just happens initially in the visual cortex [5]. The ISI is
variable between 1.85 and 2.15 seconds, which is randomly sampled from a fixed list
of ISI values between 1.85 and 2.15 ensuring an average of 2 seconds. A variable ISI is
proved to be more efficient than a fixed ISI [9]. If the subject blinks in the beginning
of the ISI, the blink will not affect the ERPs, because a blink lasts between 200-400 ms
[21]. Initially, the ISI was around 1 second and between every third stimulus there was
a blinking break. While testing the experiment, we realized that potentially, subjects
would be thinking one, two, three, blink, one, two, three, blink. The setup with no
specific blinking break, but longer ISI for blinking makes the blinking more natural
for the subject. Between every fourth block, there is a 35 seconds break. Initially,
we planned to just have one longer break mid-experiment, but conducting the pilot
experiments we realized that breaks were needed more often. In the break the subject
is allowed to move the head and shoulders, stretch a bit and potentially drink some
water, but not remove the cap or leave the chair.
A final consideration, in terms of the experiment, was the language. Since the
Microsoft COCO captions and categories are in English, we initially had probe words
in the experiment in English. Normally, experiments are conducted in the subjects’
native language so for the actual experiments it made sense conducting them in
Danish if all subjects were Danish.
This led to concerns about whether the fact
that subjects have been thinking in Danish during the experiment would affect the

4.3.1
Stimuli
Stimuli consist of images from the Microsoft Common Objects in Context (Microsoft
COCO) dataset [47]. The experiment has 23 categories holding 30 images each giving
690 images in total. The images contain common objects like animals and food items
(see figure 4.2(a)). All images are novel and not repeated throughout the experiment.
The approximate number of images to include in the experiment was based on how
long time we expected a subject could bear sitting still wearing the cap, and the time
duration of stimuli, interstimulus intervals, probe words and breaks. The objective
was to include as many images as possible to ensure a larger dataset. The blocks
and the images within the blocks appear in random order, which is also the case for
the experiment designs in [5] and [8]. This is to ensure that the signal we eventually
get is not due to subjects getting tired towards the end while looking at e.g. zebras.
Instead we hope to distribute potential variance due to tiredness across the images.
To minimize eye movements during the experiment the stimulus when presented is 4
x 3 degrees of the visual angle and 0 degrees from the fixation point, which gives best
effect in brain signals [12].

to as source localization. Figure 4.12 shows a simple overview of the relation between
the forward problem and the inverse problem, which represent the source localization
problem. The forward model maps each source location in the brain to each electrode,
and the inverse problem localizes the cortical sources that produce the EEG signal
that the electrode measures [19].

sparse basis functions. The algorithm used for this, is the time-expanded Variational

5.3.3
Canonical correlation analysis
As part of the analysis, we have conducted a canonical correlation analysis (CCA) to
compare the EEG data with the image semantics of each image. The reason for this
is to understand if there is correlation between the image semantics and the EEG
signals that we have recorded from the subjects’ brain while they were looking at the
same images. The CCA used in this analysis is the canoncorr function in Matlab. It
returns the weights, the correlation coefficients and the canonical variates. We use the
CCA to compare the raw EEG data (Xraw) and the image semantics (XI) as well the
cortical signals represented as basis functions (XBF ) and the image semantics (XI).
Before running the CCA with Xraw, the data has been stabilized using the inverse
hyperbolic sine (asinh in Matlab). Each ERP is transformed to the power signal by
calculating the standard deviation across samples (time dimension), and the matrix
transposed to 690 ERPs x 32 channels. When running CCA with XBF , the data
is transformed to the power signal as well and transposed to 690 ERPs x 438 basis
functions. The dimensions of the image semantics XI are reduced using PCA on
the centralized data. On average, the first 20 principal components each accounts
for more than 1% of the variance explained. The data projected on to the first 20
principal directions gives a matrix of size 690 x 20 when transposed.

Permutation tests
To check if the correlation coefficients of the canonical components are significant
5000 permutation tests are conducted. In each permutation test the EEG data is
shuffled by shuffling the order of the ERP blocks corresponding to an image category
block of 30 images. The canoncorr function is run again on the shuffled EEG data
and the image semantics. The permutation tests are testing the null hypothesis, that
the differences between the correlation coefficients of the CCA with the actual data
and the correlation coefficients of the CCA with the shuffled data are random. We
want to test if we can reject the null hypothesis at a α = 0.01 significance level
for each component. The significance level must be low because we are running a
high number of permutation tests. The first canonical component has the highest
correlation coefficient. We calculate the probability of the correlation coefficients of

Experiment: α optimization
Before fitting the model to the data, the α parameter of the L2 regularization term in

with α values from 0.0001 to 100000 are tested. The results from the experiment

δEj for α = 10000 and α = 100000 in figure B.4 in Appendix B.4.2 shows that the
predictions from α = 100000 are very regularized around the mean compared to α =
10000. We choose to use α = 10000.
5.4.2.2
Experiment: Normalization and test set division
The training, test and validation sets, previously described in section 5.1.0.1, are
generated before the ridge regression by testing different normalizations, and different
ways to combine the ERPs in a test set. The experiment is set up as crossvalidation
running 30 times for the 30 locations that an ERP within a category can have. Each
time, we include 23 ERPs per subject in the test set, one for each category located at
the same location. The experiment is run three times for no normalization, Python
StandardScaler and the inverse hyperbolic sine explained in section 4.5.5. This results
in a mean δE of 1.217, 1.045, 2.028, a mean MSE E of 0.123, 0.093, 0.324 and a mean
number of predictable features of 0.0, 154.3, 0.0 respectively across the 30 folds. This
indicates that using Python’s StandardScaler is best. The StandardScaler subtracts
the mean and scales to unit variance. The number of predictable features is very
variating depending on the ERPs included in the test set (see table B.3 in Appendix
B.4.1). This indicates that randomizing the ERPs included in the test and validation
sets, as described in section 5.1.0.1, is the right way for generating the test and
validation sets. Thereby, we have not predetermined anything based on which test
set that gives the best results.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
In this section, we discuss the experiment and the results presented in Chapter 6.
7.1
EEG experiment
Figure 6.1 show clear positive visual evoked responses at 100 ms post stimulus onset,
which is the P100 ERP component.
This is followed by a visual N1 around 150
ms after stimulus onset, which is in alignment with other studies of visual evoked
responses ([5], [74], [73]). The figures in Appendix C.1 show that each subject has
clear visual evoked signals, but it also show that subjects’ ERPs look different.
In figure 6.2 we see a tendency that animals like bear, cat, dog, elephant and horse
trigger long P300 ERP components from 200 ms to around 400 ms post stimulus onset.
However, a motorcycle shows a similar long signal. The latter can be caused by the
fact that the majority of subjects are male. In general, it is best practice to conduct
experiments with the same number of male and female subjects. One of the reasons
for this is that males and females have different evoked visual potentials as shown in
[71].
The topographic maps in figure 6.4 show that subject 11 has high positive activity
from 200 ms after stimulus onset in electrodes located in the occipital area, which is
what we would expect. The signals are not as clear for subject 12 due to very negative
signals from the electrodes in the front. This, indicates artifacts related to the eyes.
During pre-processing of the EEG signals, we decided not to remove any signals
(artifacts) or EPRs, because we wanted as much data as possible. The main arguments
for not removing artifacts are that the experiment design is controlled to the extend
that eye blinks will rarely happen during stimulus, and we collect so much data
that the artifacts that we potentially get in the signals are systematically distributed
across the entire dataset. Even though the experiment design was controlled in terms
of eye blinks, we did not consider that the Neuroelectrics cap is so tight that it
provokes muscle tensions around the eyes, which is hard to avoid. For the reasons
discussed above, supported by the fact that some of the independent components
were classified as muscle artifacts, e.g. for subject 12 in figure 4.11(b), could indicate
that we potentially should have removed EPRs with artifacts. However we did not
want to reduce the dataset.
Even though the design of and plan for the experiment was well thought through,
unplanned surprises still happened; like subjects with wax in the hair resulting in
==PAGE:98==
82
7 Discussion
electrodes that did not work. Lessons learned is definitely to capture more informa-
tion during the experiments to be able to identify outside events that can explain
unexpected trends in the signals.
To validate the quality of the EEG signals recorded with Enobio 32 and the
Neuroelectrics cap, it could be useful to conduct a couple of experiments with more
advanced EEG equipment and compare the signals. Finally, we should have followed
some guidelines for placing the cap on the head. This probably did not affect the
signals that much, and even using guidelines, placement of the cap is simply difficult
because head shapes of people are different.
7.2
Similarity analysis
The distance matrix Dmean in figure 6.6(b) shows a stronger signal within the blocks
in the diagonal for the EEG signals. This is supported by the calculated ratio of 0.8014.
This was promising, however, when plotting the categories in the original time order
in figure 6.9(a), we see a strong temporal effect. This is an issue because it indicates
that the block structure is not present due to similarity in the EEG signals within
categories. The temporal effect seems to be stronger for some subjects than others
which we see in figure C.6 in Appendix C.2.2. For some subjects, the effect could
likely be caused by bad electrodes when recording EEG signals. For example, figure
6.5 shows that all channels look good for subject 11, but two channels are drifting a lot
for subject 12 indicating that something was wrong with the two electrodes. Another
potential reason could be tiredness of the subjects.
To avoid this, we should not
have conducted experiments with subjects after a long day of school. We could have
considered running the experiment over several days and stopping when the subjects
got tired, however, this would make the experiment more cumbersome to conduct,
and would create more variation in the signals even within one subject. Alternatively,
compromising on the number of images, and shorten the EEG experiment could be
an option.
To mitigate the issue of the temporal effect, we could consider to remove the
subjects with strong temporal effects.
In EEG studies, it is common to remove
subjects with bad signals from the data analysis, for example in [85], they only use
data from 26 out of 30 subjects in the final analysis.
We removed five subjects
before calculating Dmean in figure C.7 in Appendix C.2.2. The block structure is still
visible and the ratio is below 1 (0.8946). In figure 6.9(b), the bootstrapping interval
decreases, which is because there is less variation within the included 10 subjects.
All subjects are included in the rest of the thesis because we were not aware of the
issue and we wanted to have as much data as possible. Since the experiment is very
easy to conduct and can be conducted within an hour, we should have conducted more
experiments. This would have given us more freedom to exclude subjects without
compromising on the amount of data included in the analysis.
Alternatively, we could try to remove the temporal effect from the signals. We
would have to investigate how to do this, but potentially PCA or ICA could be useful,
==PAGE:99==
7.3 Classification of categories from raw EEG signals
83
if we could identify components that hold the temporal effect and project the data
onto all other components. Using CCA to remove the temporal effect will be discussed
in section 7.4.
To sum up the block structure that we see in the diagonal in figure 6.6(b), is due
to strong temporal effects mainly in some subjects. Removing these subjects, leaves
some block structure in Dmean, however, this is potentially still caused by temporal
effects from the rest of the subjects. To conclude if there are blocks in the diagonal
caused by the categories, we would have to investigate the signals further. This should
be done after either removing the temporal effects or removing subjects with strong
temporal effect and potentially conduct more experiments with ’good’ subjects.
7.3
Classification of categories from raw EEG signals
Initially, we tried to classify animal vs.
non-animal, but we discovered no clear
clustering of animal vs.
non-animal according to figure C.10 in Appendix C.2.3.
This can partly be explained by the temporal effect, but another reason could be
that many of the images of animals are holding landscapes as well, which thereby
make them more similar with other types of images, where e.g. a train or a bus has
a landscape in the background. Defining a percentage of the image that the object
must cover could be an addition to the image selection criteria.
Another point of discussion is the division of the data into training, test and
validation sets. Within research, different approaches seem to be popular and the
most challenging way is to hold out one subject and train the model on the other
subjects. We believed that this task would be too difficult because of the variation
in the subjects’ ERPs as we see in the figures in Appendix C.1. Instead, we divided
the sets as described in section 5.1.0.1. In [73] and [54] they perform single subject
classification, which is training and testing on signals from the same subject. This is
an easier task, and could probably have increased performance of our classification,
but we would then have benefitted from subjects viewing more than just 690 images
once.
The baseline accuracy of the classification task is 1/23 = 4.35%.
The logistic
regression with 8% accuracy and the neural networks with 19% and 16% are all
predicting better than random guessing, and they are not predicting only the same
class. Looking at the confusion matrices in figure 6.11 and figure 6.12, the logistic
regression seems to have a tendency of random guessing and the neural networks a
tendency of predicting some categories more than others. For model 1, it is bus and
model 2, it is cat and boat. Model 1 which includes the searched hyper-parameters is
performing better on the test set than model 2 where dropout and L2 regularization
are increased. This is most likely a coincidence, since model 2 was performing better
on the validation set, however, model 2 might be too regularized. It is encouraging
that hyper-parameter search does help improve a neural network, giving a reliable
framework for optimizing the models when they are built.

7 Discussion
The results of the category classification indicate that we can decode brain state
from the EEG signals.
Our results compared to other EEG classification studies
mentioned in section 2.4 are acceptable. We cannot directly compare our results with
all of the studies mentioned, but Kaneshiro et al [35] and Zafar et al [85] performed
multiclass classification. They got 40.68% accuracy with six classes (baseline 16.67%)
and 40% accuracy with five classes (baseline 20%) respectively. Compared to those
results, an accuracy of 19% ± 2% with a baseline of 4.35% is acceptable.
The temporal effect discussed the in previous section might also have an effect
when classifying categories. Potentially, we have modeled the temporal effect instead
of actual category distinction, meaning that when we predict a category, it is predicted
based on what it comes after. We would need to investigate this further to understand
if this is the case. An option could be to look at the features that the network is
extracting, to see if we would discover anything from that. Another option could be
to do an experiment where we try to predict the next category in time based on the
previous category. If this proved to be equally successful, it would indicate that the
models are modeling the temporal effect.
It could be interesting to see if prediction of categories would be as successful if
we redesigned the experiment. For example, if we had shown all the 690 images in a
completely random order without the block structure and the probewords. We would
expect that the task of classifying categories would be more difficult, which is the
primary reason why the experiment has the block structure.
Finally, instead of using the raw EEG signals as input to the classifiers, it would
be interesting to investigate other features of the EEG as well as other classifiers.
Stewart et al [74] used independent components of ICA and SVM to classify objects.
SVM was also used to classify objects by Taghizadeh et al in [77], where wavelet
transformations were used as feature extraction. At DTU, Vilamala et al [81] used
multitaper spectral analysis to create visual images of sleep patterns that were used
as input for a CNN to classify sleep stage scoring. Potentially, feature extraction of
the EEG signals will be able to improve decoding of the brain state.
7.4
Canonical correlation analysis
The idea of comparing the EEG signals with the image semantics using CCA, was
to get an indication of whether it would be possible to predict the image semantics
from the EEG signals.
The correlation coefficients were tested with permutation
tests to understand how significant the components were. Initially, we permuted by
shuffling all images, which resulted in very low p-values and thereby many significant
components. Since the experiment was run in blocks of images, we realized that we
had to permute in blocks as well to get a more fair estimate of whether we could
reject the null hypothesis or not. In table 6.1 we see that this gave less significant
components, especially with XBF . With 5000 permutation tests, we would have liked
to see more significant components at a 0.01 significance level. Overall, we got more
significant components with Xraw, which is why we continued using the raw EEG
==PAGE:101==
7.4 Canonical correlation analysis
85
signals in the other analyzes in this thesis. However, investigation of the cortical
signals as basis function should be done to further analyze whether they could yield
better results.
In figure 6.14, showing the significant components of subject 5, the first component
seems to be temporal dependent whereas the 13th component seems to represent the
categories. This, is especially clear for the image semantics. Ideally, we would have
seen the same division of categories in the EEG data, but it is not the case. However,
by running the CCA on the EEG signals and the image semantics, the weights that are
found show that the images within categories are correlating together, and it indicates
that e.g. component 13 can distinguish the categories for the image semantics. This
result is obtained by finding the correlation with the EEG signals.
Initially, we
thought that the first component was the most interesting. That is not necessarily
the case, because the first component represents a temporal effect, which seems to
be caused by the electrodes close to the ears. This, can be seen on the topographic
map in figure 6.15. The topographic map of component 13 in figure 6.15 looks a
bit noisy, so it is not possible to conclude that it shows neural activity of visual
processing, even though component 13 in figure 6.14 seems to hold information about
the category division.
As mentioned in the previous discussion about the temporal effect, CCA could
potentially be used to remove the effect from the data. In figure C.12 in Appendix
C.3, we see that at least the first two components of subject 5 hold temporal effects.
By projecting the data onto the rest of the 20 components avoiding the temporal
ones, we would have a new dataset holding less temporal effect. With this method, it
would not be possible to project the data back to channel space. De Clercq et al [10]
use CCA to remove muscle artifacts from EEG data. Here they use a mixing matrix,
setting components holding artifacts to zero and multiplying it with the canonical
variates representing the EEG signals. Using their approach, we could potentially be
able to reconstruct the EEG signals without the temporal effects.
Finally, we looked at the correlation between subjects. This was mainly to get
an idea of whether the EEG signals of one subject correlated with the EEG signals
of another, and if the ERPs within categories affected the correlation together. The
canonical variates for subject 2 found by running CCA between subject 2 and 15
in figure C.14 in Appendix C.3.1 show that no clear temporal effects are captured
and that the categories are not separated. Figure C.15 in Appendix C.3.1 indicates
that some subjects seem to be more correlated with the others. For example, subject
4 has significant components with four other subjects when comparing Xraw. The
components are significant at a 0.01 significance level, but only 13 pairs using Xraw
and 9 pairs using XBF have significant components indicating variance in the signals
between subjects. However, this was also expected. ERPs within categories do not
seem to affect the correlation altogether.
To sum up, some correlation exists between the EEG signals and the image se-
mantics, indicating that we potentially can predict the image semantics. In the next
section, we will discuss a supervised approach to predict the image semantics using
ridge regression and neural networks.

7.5
EEG pipeline
This section discusses the predictable features found by the ridge regression and the
performance of the image semantics predicted by the ridge regression and the neural
network models, in terms of the ability to classify correctly and generate correct
captions.
7.5.1
Predictable features
The normalized MSE δE of the ridge regression is 1.024 indicating that we cannot
predict the image semantics, because the value is not lower than 1. Therefore we
found the normalized MSEs of the features, δEj, separately and got 264 predictable
features. We investigated the predictable features by analyzing the ability to predict
categories using two softmax classifiers. We created 10 test sets with 264 random
features and used true image semantics to compare.
The top-1 accuracy of the true image semantics is just as good when using the Mi-
crosoft COCO softmax classifier trained on the 264 random features as when trained
on the 264 predictable features. This, together with the fact that the accuracies are
not much lower for random features compared to predictable features in the top of
table 6.4, indicates that the predictable features are not holding information about
our 23 categories. If the predictable features were the interesting ones, in terms of
our categories, we would have expected a clear difference in the ability to predict cat-
egories for random and predictable features. In table 6.3 with Inception-v3 metrics
the results are more aligned with what we expect. The accuracies of classification
with the 264 predictable features are in all cases higher than the accuracies of the
random features, and in most cases, except one, higher than the accuracies of the full
features. However, for top-1 predictions mostly zebra is predicted (see figure C.17 in
Appendix C.4.1) with the predicted image semantics including the 264 predictable
features. The reason for this must be that the predictable features are the ones where
the error is low, because the predicted value is around the mean. Substituting the val-
ues of the non-predictable features with the mean of the separate features across the
training set, will then give very similar image semantics that hold values around the
mean for all 2048. This, mean that it will predict the same category first. However,
top-5 predictions can hold information found by the differences in the 264 predictable
features.
Overall, it seems that we might be able to predict 264 features, however, these
features are not necessarily the ones holding information about our 23 categories.
This is also indicated by the low number of features overlapping between the test and
validation set. This is of course unfortunate, because we had expected that since the
features were predicted by EEG signals, where subjects had been looking at e.g. a
cat, the features that we would be able to predict would also be representing a cat,
and thereby the 264 features would represent our 23 categories. We need other or
potentially more predictable features in the image semantics to be able to predict
more categories correctly and in the end generate correct captions.
Even though

it is not clear that the 264 predictable features are the interesting ones, the metric
number of predictable features seems to be a good indication of whether the predicted
image semantics can classify meaningfully. We could have considered calculating the

7.5.2
Performance of the predicted image semantics
In this section, we discuss the performance of the predicted image semantics with full
features predicted by the ridge regression and the neural network models.
7.5.2.1
Classification of categories using predicted image semantics
The image semantics predicted by the ridge regression have a top-1 accuracy of 4.9%
(baseline: 4.35%) and a top-5 accuracy of 23.2% (baseline 21.7%) with the Microsoft
COCO softmax classifier trained on full features.
The results are not significant,
and in figure C.20 in Appendix C.4.2 we see that correct predictions are cat, dog,
bird, bench and teddy bear. These predictions, except from teddy bear, tend to be
the categories with many training samples (see figure B.5 in Appendix B.4.3). This
indicates that to get the ridge regression to result in predictable features, it needs
to be very regularized with a high α parameter. This, has the effect of making the
weights small and thereby predicting values that do not differ much from the mean,
as we see in figure 3.2. This will affect the classification results by predicting the
’mean’ categories.
The results from the Inception-v3 classifier looks more promising. The metrics
are higher than the permuted baseline and significant for accuracy 1 top-5 (14.2%),
accuracy 2 top-1 (12.5%) and the mean semantic score for top-5 (0.52). The classifier
can predict 1000 different categories and for the ridge regression 50.1% of the predicted
categories are within our scope of 23 categories. It is predicting 9 different categories
within our categories, so it is not because it is predicting the same category, which is
the case for the neural networks models where 100% of the predicted categories are
within our scope.
The image semantics predicted by the neural networks are only used to classify
with Inception-v3. This results in predictions of only one category (zebra) for all test
samples, which can be seen in figure C.18 in Appendix C.4.1. This, supported by the
fact that the neural network models only have 7 and 8 predictable features, shows
that the models are really bad.
It is difficult to train fully connected dense neural networks [16] and they might
not be the best suitable models for predicting vectors. The models have too many
trainable parameters compared to the amount of training data, which is an extremely
ill-posed problem as we learned in [41]. The attempt to improve the models using
hyper-parameter optimization were not successful, which indicates that the model
structure in itself is not suitable for the problem. Matsuo et al [48] are partly suc-
cessful with caption generation using a 3-layer neural network, but they obtained the

same results as us with the 5-layer neural network; predicting the same caption for all
test samples. Their success with the 3-layer model can potentially be because they
have more brain activity data, or because they use fMRI data.
It could be interesting to use the CNN, described in section 5.3.2, to predict

It would need an extra convolutional layer to get the 2048

7.5.2.2
Generated captions from predicted image semantics
Finally, we have generated captions using the predicted image semantics with full
features from the ridge regression in table 6.7. The captions show that the image
semantics are not good enough to generate captions of what is actually on the image,
even though they were able to predict the category correctly with Inception-v3. If
we look at the semantic scores between the generated captions and the true captions,
they are pretty high. This indicates that the way we calculate the semantic score
is not optimal. For future work, when we get better predicted image semantics, we
should investigate how to calculate a more meaningful semantic score, and potentially
include a syntactic metric by e.g. using parsing. One option could be to use another
representation of words than GloVe e.g. Word2Vec which is trained on more data. In
GloVe ’ox’ and ’cow’ only have a semantic score of 0.47. Instead of using the sum of
all word vectors in a caption, we could calculate the semantic scores for the different
types of words separated by parsing and thereby better compare on type of word
level. Another idea for future work with the caption generating RNN is to fine tune
it by retraining it using the predicted image semantics and true captions. However,
this requires that predicting image semantics is improved.
Lastly, we have included the closest we get to mind reading in this thesis in table
6.8. We use the predicted categories from Inception-v3 accuracy 1 top-1, as indicators
of what we can decode from the EEG signals, to retrieve captions generated by the
true image semantics with the caption generating RNN. How close to mind reading
this is, is up to our own assessment. We conclude that there is still some work to be
done before we can actually mind read from the EEG signals.

There is a lot of work to be done to improve the EEG pipeline, and we have already
discussed opportunities above. To sum up we need to understand the temporal effect
in the EEG signals and find a good way to remove it, or exclude subjects holding
strong temporal effects. To avoid reducing the amount of data the best option would

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
We conducted an experiment with 15 subjects, each viewing 690 different images
within 23 categories.
We learned that even though the experiment was designed
to avoid blink artifacts, tension of muscles are hard to avoid for some subjects when
wearing the Neuroelectrics cap. Some subjects are more suitable for EEG experiments.
Thorough data exploration of the EEG signals in earlier stages of the project is
important to understand if the data is affected by, for example, temporal effects. The
discovered temporal effect in the signals can potentially be removed using ICA, PCA,
or most promising CCA, because CCA seems to find components that capture the
temporal effects. Further analysis is needed to conclude if category specific signals
are present in the EEG signals, when the temporal effects have been removed.
Furthermore, we used the EEG signals to predict image semantics of images fed
through the Inception-v3 network. We found that CCA could divide the categories for
the image semantics, by finding weights that maximize the correlation between EEG
signals and image semantics. However, the division was not clear in the canonical
variates of the EEG signals. Using a ridge regression to predict the image semantics,
we found that 264 features were predictable with δEj lower than 1, however these
features turned out to not necessarily be the features holding information about the
23 categories included in the experiment. Using the predicted images semantics as
input to the caption generating RNN did not create semantically relevant captions
to the images.
However, using the final softmax layer of the Inception-v3 model,
the predicted image semantics managed to predict the categories with accuracies
that were a bit higher than the permutation based baseline. We found that fully
connected dense neural networks performed worse than the ridge regression in terms
of predicting image semantics, so to be able to generate more semantically correct
captions, we need to investigate other models for predicting the image semantics from
the EEG signals.
Finally, we were able to decode the brain state from the raw EEG signals by
predicting the category that a subject had been viewing using a CNN with an accuracy
of 19% ± 2%. This was much higher than baseline (4.35%) and in alignment with
other multiclass classification studies using EEG ([85] and [35]). To be on the same
level with studies conducted using fMRI, there is still some work to be done.
