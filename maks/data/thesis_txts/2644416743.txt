Abstract
An approach is presented to model dynamic source and listener directivity for interactive
wave-based simulation methods. Two methods for extracting spatially encoded signals from
the Spectral element method (SEM) were derived, implemented, and analyzed. Binaural
responses are produced from the convolution of the wave amplitude density decomposed
using spherical harmonics with a head related transfer function (HRTF). The time-domain
encoding technique presented is based on the relation between spherical harmonic encoding
and the sound ﬁeld’s local pressure gradients. The ﬁrst method solves this relation by
using ﬁnite diﬀerences, and the second by using its weak formulation. A directive source
is implemented in Yee’s FDTD algorithm, modeled through a Dirac distribution shaped
from diﬀerential operators, representing a particular spherical harmonic function. Both
encoding methods were veriﬁed against the initial spatial encoding technique developed for
the Finite diﬀerence time domain method (FDTD). The encoded signal’s accuracy in two
SEM-based methods is analyzed in terms of the number of elements per wavelength and
the spacing between the grid points utilized in the encoding. Finally, numerical results
of diﬀerent source directivity patterns are presented, and the eﬃciency of the method
for diﬀerent frequencies is discussed. Both source and receiver directivity models run
locally and in the time domain, and they support interactive auralization, with moving
and rotating source and receiver.
Keywords: ﬁnite diﬀerence time domain method, spectral element method,
binaural auralization, source directivity, spherical harmonics

1 Introduction
Room acoustic simulations have been extensively used in diﬀerent industries over the last
ﬁve decades. Some recent such examples are the gaming [1, 2], the building [3, 4], the
hearing [5], and the automotive [6] industries.
A crucial concept in acoustic simulations is auralization, a term used to describe the
rendering of audible sound ﬁelds in direct analogy to light ﬁeld and visualization. This
spatialization step brought a revolution in communicating the simulated acoustic envi-
ronment with the customer or end-user. It can include the excitation of the simulation
model with a source of arbitrary directivity pattern (spatial aspects of sound on source’s
side), calculation of the room’s impulse response, the spatial encoding of the sound ﬁeld
at receiver’s position (spatial aspects of sound in listener’s side), and the audio playback.
Evaluating simulation realism and accuracy does not need to be based only on technical
acoustic parameters anymore but can be supplemented by a real hearing experience.
The quality of the auralization depends heavily on the simulation method, the boundary
conditions [7], the space model under examination, the encoding process, and the source
directivity pattern. The two last components will be the main interests of this report.
Diﬀerent encoding methods are known to reproduce the experience of a spatial sound scene.
They could be organized into categories, with the extremes be the binaural techniques
and the sound ﬁeld reconstruction [8]. The binaural response aims at incorporating the
frequency and directional characteristics of the human head, and sometimes of the torso
as well, in the free-ﬁeld recordings. It is known that the human auditory system renders
directional cues to recreate the impression of the space around the listener [9]. In recent
years, virtual and augmented reality had raised the interest in binaural systems [8]. The
source directivity pattern controls how space is excited. It is an essential factor as most
sound sources, from human voices [10] to musical instruments [11], radiate with a speciﬁc
directivity pattern, and the ability of humans to distinguish sources of diﬀerent radiation
patterns is known [12, 13].

Chapter 1.
Introduction
1.1
Background
Geometrical methods such as the ray tracing method [14], the image source method [15],
and the beam tracing method [16] approximate the sound wave as a ray or particle. They
provide cost-eﬃcient and yet trustworthy solutions but under conditions and limitations.
Some indicative examples of including source and receiver directivity in the methods
mentioned above are the following. Source and receiver directivity patterns were included
in the ray tracing method by utilizing spherical harmonics base functions applied at
the post-processing stage [17]. Directional sensitive source and receiver were applied for
the image source method in [18], while sound sources’ directivity properties utilizing
ﬁlters was presented in [19, 20]. Source and receiver directivity were accounted in the
closed-form image-source model by convolving each pulse with the source and receiver’s
impulse response at exit and incident angles [21]. Recent implementations of the beam
tracing method have achieved accurate interactive auralization with moving sources and
receivers by reducing the problem’s dimensionality[22, 23].
Nevertheless, the approximations employed in the geometrical methods are not generally
satisﬁed at low frequencies. Therefore, the wave-based methods, where the governing
physical equations are solved numerically, achieving high precision, and inheriting all
the wave phenomena, but with a high computational cost. From 1966, when K. S. Yee
suggested the Finite diﬀerence time domain method (FDTD) to solve Maxwell’s equations
in the time domain [24], this method had been extensively investigated and used for
acoustic simulations. Its usability stems from its ability to handle transient phenomena
[25] and operate in a wide-band range [26]. It is simple to implement, it is cost-eﬃcient
compared to other wave-based methods, and with suﬃcient precision in low geometric
complexity cases. Also, successful attempts for parallelism of the computing lead to
accelerating the simulation by orders of magnitude [27].
A ﬁrst approach to compute binaural responses in a ﬁnite diﬀerence model was to embed
a geometric model of a listener’s head in the grid and evaluate interaural time and level
diﬀerence cues [28]. However, modeling the pinna’s ﬁne geometric structure requires a
considerably higher grid resolution than modeling interaural cues [29, 30, 31]. As the
morphological characteristics are unique for each head, the use of personalized head related
transfer function (HRTF) had been suggested [32], acquired from magnetic resonance
imaging [30] and stereoscopy [33]. An additional burden of including a head model in the
solver is the need for recomputing the whole sound ﬁeld every-time the listener is moved
[34], making the method impractical for interactive auralization.
Extracting the sound ﬁeld’s plane-wave components and spatially integrating them with
pre-measured HRTFs forms a second approach for binaural rendering. In [35], a spherical
array of receivers was used to locally sample the grid, from which a plane wave density
function was computed and integrated with free-ﬁeld HRTF in the spherical harmonic
functions domain. In [36], a diﬀerential microphone processing was suggested, where

based on the array processing paradigm, but the relationship between spherical harmonic
encoding and the local pressure gradients of the sound ﬁeld.
Source directivity has been explored in FDTD methods in terms of omnidirectional
sources [39] or dipoles [40]. More recently, complex directivity patterns were achieved by
employing multi-poles [41] in an FDTD scheme. A more natural approach was suggested
in [42] where spatial diﬀerential operators in the Cartesian grid are associated with a
series of spherical harmonic functions.
Despite the potentials of the FDTD, its foundation on staggered grids makes it unattractive
for complex geometries. Even though curved geometries had been approximated as zig-zag
shapes [43] or by utilizing a locally conformal boundary modeling [44], the equilibrium
between added complexity and numerical errors is still deterrent.
A family of time-domain element methods like the Spectral element method (SEM) [45]
and the discontinues Galerkin Element Method (DGFEM) [46], which are two closely
related variants of high-order-accurate ﬁnite element methods, has attracted increased
interest recently. They achieve high-order accuracy, they can handle complex geometries
as they operate on unstructured, adaptive meshes with curvilinear mesh elements [47],
and parallel eﬃciency had been demonstrated [48, 46].
A thorough search of the relevant literature yielded no known methods for including
spatial characteristics for the source and the receiver for the before mentioned methods.
Nevertheless, such research in related wave-based methods had attracted interest lately.
Spherical harmonics had also been used in the pseudospectral time-domain method to
model source and head-related directivity [49]. A virtual microphone array was used for
plane wave decomposition based on the Finite Element method’s solution and an inverse
method to calculate the amplitudes of the plane waves [50]. A framework for a directional
source expressed as a linear combination of elementary spherical harmonic sources for the
Boundary Element Method and the Equivalent source method was proposed in [51], as
well as a plane-wave decomposition approach based on higher-order derivatives of the
sound ﬁeld.

1.2
Scope and structure of the thesis
This project’s scope was to develop a model for binaural reproduction in the time-domain
SEM. First, a method for decomposing the sound ﬁeld into its plane wave components
using higher-order derivatives of the sound ﬁeld [51, 52, 38] was implemented in Yee’s
FDTD scheme. Two variations of this method were developed and implemented in the
SEM. The ﬁrst one utilizes ﬁnite diﬀerences to solve the ambisonic equations. The second
one utilizes the weak formulation, making encoding an inherent part of the SEM solver.
This encoding technique was selected since it supports dynamic HRTF-based listener
directivity at run-time and be applicable even for unstructured grids. An additional goal
was to investigate a generalized partial diﬀerential equation model of a point source with
arbitrary directivity in the FDTD [42, 53], which will constitute the foundation for a
similar model in SEM. The choice of this method is justiﬁed as its authors suggest its
functionality even in irregular grids. All the above implementations were developed in
MATLAB.
Chapter 2 includes the formulation of Yee’s FDTD algorithm, a method for spatial
encoding, and a method for creating a source radiating with a selected directivity pattern
in this scheme. The SEM is established, and two methods for extracting spatially encoded
signals are developed.
Chapter 3 presents and compares the spatially encoded signals extracted from the three
methods (FDTD, SEM-FD, SEM). The relation between grid resolution, the spacing of
points utilized in the encoding and encoding accuracy are analyzed. Moreover, numerical
results illustrating directivity patterns in FDTD are presented.
Chapter 4 discusses the results, and further interpretations are given. Furthermore,
suggestions for future work are included.
1.3
Summary of main contributions
The main contributions of this project are
• the veriﬁcation of a spatial encoding technique employing spherical harmonic
functions, in Yee’s FDTD algorithm,
• the implementation of the above technique in the SEM,
• the derivation and implementation of a novel way of solving the ambisonic equations
in the SEM which integrates the encoding process directly in the solver,
• the analysis of the accuracy of the two encoding techniques in the SEM, in terms of
grid resolution and spacing of grid points utilized in the encoding,

2 Theoretical background
This chapter establishes the theoretical framework for extracting spatially encoded signals
by formulating the ambisonic equation. Numerical discretization of the space-time partial
diﬀerential equation, which relates the time derivatives of the ambisonic channels with the
sound ﬁeld’s spatial derivatives, is derived for the FDTD and the SEM schemes. The ﬁrst
encoding method is suggested by Bilbao et al. [38] for an FDTD scheme and utilizes ﬁnite
diﬀerences to solve the partial diﬀerential equations (PDEs). The second mimics how
ambisonic channels are computed in the ﬁrst one using ﬁnite diﬀerences but computes the
sound-ﬁeld in the SEM. In the third method, the sound ﬁeld’s governing equations and
the ambisonic equations are solved using the weak formulation, leading to an SEM model
that inherently spatially encodes the signal. A model for a directive source in the FDTD
is also included. Finally, some tools like modiﬁed pulse sources used in the simulations
and an analytical solution of the sound pressure are also brieﬂy described.

2.3
Time-domain spectral element method
As already mentioned, the SEM has been reputed in acoustic simulations for achieving
high accuracy in solving complex geometries as it can operate on unstructured, adaptive
meshes with curvilinear mesh elements [47, 45]. A numerical scheme based on the SEM

Two ways of extracting spatially encoded signals are described. The ﬁrst one solves
the ambisonic equations by utilizing ﬁnite diﬀerences, as suggested in [38]. The second

The linearized Euler and continuity equations for sound propagation in a lossless medium

2.3.6
Spatial sound ﬁeld encoding using ﬁnite diﬀerences
As a ﬁrst intuitive approach to spatial sound ﬁeld encoding in the SEM, the analysis
based on ﬁnite diﬀerences described in subsection 2.2.2 was adapted. However, the FDTD
presented in (2.23) discretizes the space through a Cartesian grid, whereas the SEM
through triangular elements. Therefore, the grid points set for each encoded signal at
the Cartesian grid should not necessarily coincide with the triangular elements’ nodes.
We overcome this disagreement by calculating the desirable positions’ pressure through
interpolation (subsection 2.3.5). As can be seen in Figure 2.2, p1 is the receiver point,
while the neighbor points for second-order harmonics are the p2-p6, which are not placed
at nodes.
First, the pressure is calculated in the whole physical domain using the SEM. The pressure
at neighbor points at positions as indicated from the ambisonic channels calculations is
calculated using interpolation. Finally, the ambisonic channels are calculated through
ﬁnite diﬀerences as in subsection 2.2.2. This method that calculates the sound pressure
ﬁeld using the SEM and solves the ambisonic channels using ﬁnite diﬀerences will be
referred to as the SEM-FD.

3.4
Investigation of grid resolution and spacing between
neighbor points in the SEM-FD
One question that now arises is the minimum grid resolution that ensures a precise spatial
encoding of the receiver’s signal. One interconnected question is, how far should the
neighbor points be placed from the receiver.
As a starting point for these questions, a signiﬁcantly coarser grid than the 20 points
points per wavelength (PPW) was used to simulate the same physical domain presented
in Figure 3.1.
In Figure 3.17 the ambisonic channels for 17, 15, 11, 8.5 PPW are calculated. Sampling
frequency at 48kHz, and the highest frequency of interest is considered the 700Hz. One
eﬀect that becomes apparent is that as the grid becomes coarser, oscillations of the
ambisonic channels arise. This is the general tendency and is not strict as an increase
from 8.5 to 11 PPW actually introduces more oscillations. For grids with less than 8.5
PPW numerical errors started appearing in the pressure calculation. Increasing the PPW
above 17, did not change the result of the ambisonic channels.
The eﬀect in the binaural response of these oscillations is negligible, as shown in Figure 3.18.
Furthermore, the diﬀerence was not audible from informal listening tests, even though a
more systematic investigation is needed for a deﬁnite conclusion.

4 Discussion
The main question of extracting spatially encoded signals in the SEM was addressed
successfully in this report. A method for calculating the ambisonic channels was adapted
from previous work [38]. It was implemented in a diﬀerent FDTD scheme than the one
initially suggested and, then it was included in an SEM scheme. Finally, a new way to
calculate the ambisonic channels in an SEM scheme was presented, developed inherently
in the scheme, and avoided the ﬁnite diﬀerence approximations used initially.
Among the three methods for calculating the ambisonic channels (using FDTD, SEM-FD,
SEM), a high agreement was achieved, indicating accurate solutions. Informal listening
tests were conducted where the source was placed in diﬀerent azimuth angles around the
receiver, and the binaural response synthesized was listened by the author. The listener
could resolve the sound wave’s direction of arrival in all three methods.
An exact comparison of the ambisonic channels calculated using the three methods against
the literature results [38, 52] was not possible because the formulation of the source used
was not given.
An attractive property of the SEM-FD method is the freedom of selecting the spacing
between the neighbor points used for the encoding. By increasing the spacing, oscillations
do not appear even for coarser meshes. Nevertheless, the spacing should be kept in small
values representing points around the receiver. In the SEM, the spacing is not chosen
explicitly, and it is bounded to the mesh’s resolution.
The plane-wave decomposition technique presented enables dynamic HRTF-based listener
directivity at run-time. The formulation is applicable in interactive applications and
supports listener head rotation without recomputing the sound pressure ﬁeld. This is
a comparative advantage against the previous array processing techniques [63, 64, 35],
which pre-compute the encoding ﬁlters and require recalculation of the whole domain in
every head movement.

Chapter 4.
Discussion
Even though the spatial encoding was used for binaural playback, spherical harmonics
make the two methods suitable for other audio reproduction techniques, such as the
higher-order Ambisonics [65].
Also this method avoids introducing an inversion employed from the plane wave decompo-
sition techniques that utilizes microphone arrays [37, 35], which is known to be unstable
for ill-conditioned matrices.
Finally, combining the directivity patterns created in FDTD using spherical harmonics
constitutes the basis for achieving sources with arbitrarily shaped radiation. This method
was proven time-eﬃcient as the discrete Dirac approximation in (2.35) is local and sparse.
Furthermore, the model for the directive source is locally deﬁned and operates entirely in
the time-domain. Therefore, it is applicable for moving sources and rotating sources, key
functionality for real-time auralization.
A limitation in calculating the ambisonic channels using the SEM-FD and the SEM is
the pressure estimation. The number of PPW should be considered among the spacing
between the neighbor points to avoid oscillations in the ambisonic channels. 15 PPW for
highest frequency of interest at 700 Hz and a spacing of 0.025 m were proven suﬃcient in
the SEM-FD, while for the same result roughly 17 PPW were needed in the SEM.
Initial benchmarks of non-optimized implementations MATLAB of the three methods, for
up to second-order ambisonic channels calculations, gave the following qualitative results.
The FDTD scheme, including the spatial encoding procedure, increases the computation
time insigniﬁcantly, in agreement with the literature [38]. In the second method, the
extra computational load is again insigniﬁcant. In the third method, where the ambisonic
equations were solved using the weak formulation, the computational load almost doubled.
Such a performance should be expected as the current formulation does not calculate the
wave amplitude density at the receiver position but in the physical domain.
It is essential to mention that the source model presented in the FDTD suﬀers from
numerical dispersion eﬀects, combined with truncation error in the discrete approximations
to the operators Dl,m, distorting the directivity pattern signiﬁcantly at high frequencies.
This distortion was also observed in [42]. However, no such eﬀect was stated in the initial
implementation of this model in the Boundary Element Method, and the Equivalent
Source model [51]. It is to be investigated whether or not this eﬀect will be present in
the SEM model. Moreover, in the literature, the source model presented requires very
high-order spherical harmonic expansion to simulate sharp directivity patterns [51].
4.1
Future work
Even though a complete framework for spatially encoding the sound ﬁeld using the SEM
was presented, open problems arise in the pursuit of generalized and usable results.

4.1.
Future work
• Expand the encoding to the 3D space. Ambisonic equations had initially been
solved in 3D [38]. Therefore the inclusion of the method in the 3D SEM solver is
expected to be straightforward.
• Having established a functional 3D acoustic simulation routine using the SEM with
receiver directivity included, the next step should be a systematic listening test to
conclude the encoded binaural cues’ precision. Depending on the precision needed,
higher than second-order ambisonic equations can be included.
• To ensure a usable 3D SEM model, the up now MATLAB implementation should
be optimized by locally solving the ambisonic equations. Furthermore, transcription
the code to a low-level programming language like C would substantially decrease
the computation time.
• The discrete recursive integration yields a prohibitive polynomial drift in the
ambisonic channels.
Filtering the ambisonic channels to control this inherent
property of encoding has already been suggested in [52], and its usage in the SEM
is an open problem.
• The initial spatial encoding technique is suggested for free-ﬁeld spaces. The eﬀect
of reﬂections on the technique should be investigated to pursue usage in room
acoustics.
• Include a directive source in the SEM, following the approach presented in FDTD,
to achieve Decrease the numerical inaccuracy of the source’s directivity patterns at
high frequencies by either increasing the space-time resolution or following a more
sophisticated design [66].

5 Conclusions
The main goal of this thesis was to suggest a method for including listener’s directivity
in the SEM. For this reason, a time-domain encoding technique based on the relation
between spherical harmonic encoding and the local pressure gradients of the sound-
ﬁeld previously implemented in the Boundary Element Method and the FDTD was
employed. Two derivations of this technique were developed. The ﬁrst method solves
this before mentioned relation by using ﬁnite diﬀerences, and the second by using its
weak formulation. Binaural responses were produced from the convolution of the wave
amplitude density decomposed using spherical harmonics with a head-related transfer
function. The two methods were implemented in MATLAB and were compared against
implementing the encoding method in Yee’s FDTD algorithm. Agreement among the
three methods was achieved when comparing the resulted binaural responses. The encoded
signal’s accuracy in two SEM-based methods was analyzed in terms of the number of
elements per wavelength and the spacing between the grid points utilized in the encoding.
Finally, a source with arbitrary directivity was presented in the FDTD. The source was
modeled through a Dirac distribution shaped from diﬀerential operators, representing a
particular spherical harmonic function. Adaptation of this model in the SEM fell out of
the ﬁve months of this project.
All source and receiver directivity models presented run locally and in the time domain,
making them applicable for interactive auralization. A future investigation towards the
directions indicated in section 4.1 aims at providing a complete framework that will
include source and receiver directivity in the SEM.
