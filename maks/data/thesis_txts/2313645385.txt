Abstract
In-situ measurements methods for the acoustic characterization of surfaces have been
proposed for years.
Their demands on accuracy trying to emulate laboratory results
usually incurs in complicated setups. In addition, the eﬀect of spurious reﬂections has
a notable impact in the studied frequency range. In the present paper, a new approach
for an in-situ measurement technique has been proposed by combination of automatic
detection of acoustic sources and enhancement of the directivity, based on impulse response
measurements. Special emphasis is put on the ease of use of the method, automating every
step under physical assumptions, which inevitably leads into inaccuracies. Introduced the
called Delay and Sum Beamforming (a coherent sum of impulse responses at random
positions), a rough estimation of the shape of a room and the absorption coeﬃcient of its
surfaces are calculated automatically. During the process, a new acoustic source detection
method was found for impulsive signals and the reduction of spurious reﬂections fulﬁlled.
MATLAB simulations and measurements in real rooms are conducted to test the method,
ﬁnding its strengths and limits.
It has been shown fair agreement between calculated
absorption coeﬃcient and theoretical results for frequencies above 300 Hz and no overlap
conditions.

Chapter 1
Motivation
The acoustic characterization of surfaces is an usual task to understand the behavior of a
room in terms of sound and adequate the room to the necessities of its use. The param-
eters that usually provide most valuable information are the reﬂection coeﬃcient R, the
surface impedance Zs and the absorption coeﬃcient α. They are frequency and angular
dependent, and there exist several measurement techniques to characterize them.
Laboratory measurements for acoustical properties of materials are well known such as
the impedance tube and the reverberation chamber. The impedance tube allows to mea-
sure the impedance Zs and the absorption coeﬃcient α of a material at normal incidence.
This method has some frequency constrains and the restriction of the angle of incidence
is also to be considered. Its pros are that the results are precise in the valid range and the
environment is well controlled; The reverberation chamber uses the statistical approach
of energy balance, trying to emulate a diﬀuse sound ﬁeld [1]. The random incidence ab-
sorption coeﬃcient can be calculated under this assumption. However, the edge eﬀect of
the samples inside the chamber can make the absorption coeﬃcient to be higher than 1.
The limitation in the deﬁnition of the acoustical properties for normal or random inci-
dence encourages to deﬁne new techniques based on the measurement of reﬂections. A
summary of these procedures is that the incident sound and the reﬂected one from the
studied material can be isolated and treated. Under this assumption all the acoustical
parameters mentioned before can be calculated at diﬀerent angles of incidence. However,
as it can be deduced, anechoic or semi-anechoic chambers are needed for this purpose.
The main problem of laboratory measurements is that these facilities are not accessible
for any acoustician and can be very expensive. In addition, reverberation and anechoic
chambers are complete buildings which cannot be transported. All these encourages to
develop other techniques which are commonly known as in-situ techniques. The loss of
precision of these techniques is countered by the inclusion of the exact conditions that the
material is going to be exposed to. Two main techniques are used in in-situ measurements.
First, the characterization of the materials based on models: using statistical approaches
again, and knowing the shape of the room, it is possible to try to match the properties of
the materials by tuning their absorption coeﬃcients using existing measurements until the
overall acoustic behavior of the room is the same than the measured one (usually, rever-
beration time). The main problem here is that there are inﬁnite combinations of material
properties that create the same global response of a room. In addition, the frequency
range that can be studied is dependent on the size of the room.
Secondly, in-situ models based on reﬂections: The idea in this case is the same than the
one explained for anechoic chambers. The closer to anechoic conditions, the better the
results. Therefore, the main problem of this technique is the eﬀect of spurious reﬂections
in the total signal. The frequency range in which it is usable is deﬁned by the time of
arrival of the ﬁrst spurious reﬂection.
==PAGE:12==
2
In addition to all the previous reasons, a good understanding of signal processing is needed
from the acoustician to be able to deal with these techniques and achieve reliable results.
In this paper a new in-situ technique for the characterization of acoustic properties of
room surfaces is developed, exploiting the inherent information in the impulse responses
about the room shape and its acoustical behavior. In the following the main goals of the
system are explained and the necessary theory is presented. Then the method developed
is explained through its diﬀerent steps, showing the strengths and weaknesses. Several
sets of simulations are computed to anticipate possible constrains of the method and two
real scenarios are measured to test its viability. Finally, the work is summarized with the
most relevant conclusions.

State of the Art and Goals
In-situ measurement techniques have been proposed for years. Most of the approaches
assume a plane wave propagation, simplifying the characteristic impedance of a spherical
wave. In addition, the surfaces are usually considered inﬁnite and point reacting. Yuzawa
[2] developed in 1974 a method called the cancellation method (Figure 2.1). Using two
microphones, one close to the surface (MB) and another one far away (MA), both at the
same distance from the loudspeaker, it is possible to determine the plane wave reﬂection
coeﬃcient by subtracting the pressure of MA one to MB.

Allard and Sieben [6] used an intensity probe to calculate the impedance of the surface.

heated wires. The temperature diﬀerence between both sensors is directly related to the
particle velocity in a linear matter.
Based on this previous work, the main objective of this thesis is to develop a system
that automatically localizes the main surfaces in a room and calculates an
approximation of the absorption coeﬃcient of such surfaces. This has to be done
without knowing the physical shape of the room, from impulse response measurements at
random positions and emphasizing the ease of use. To fulﬁll this process two well deﬁned
objectives are diﬀerentiated.
• Localization of impulsive sources in the space.
• Calculation of absorption coeﬃcient from impulse responses.

Chapter 3
Theoretical Background
The development of this method comprehends several engineering ﬁelds from acoustics to
pure signal processing. In this chapter the main theory necessary to understand the prob-
lem is presented, structured in 3 main sections. First, the Absorption Coeﬃcient Estima-
tion from Impulse Responses is explained, considering plane and spherical waves, including
the eﬀects of oblique incidence. Then, Automatic Detection of Reﬂections presents the
main techniques that will be used during the process to estimate the time of arrival of the
reﬂections. Finally, the Surface Localization section covers the translation from time of
arrivals of a signal to actual point sources in the space and its application to the detection
of surfaces.

3.3
Surface Localization
Localization of sources in acoustic engineering is usually focused on the detection of Di-
rection Of Arrival (DOA) [21] (e.g. beamforming). This study is done in the frequency
domain, analyzing a steady state source. However, some approaches have been developed
when the source is producing a transient excitation (e.g. an impulse). Usually, the de-
tection of the Time Of Arrival (TOA) of this impulse to diﬀerent sensors allows to relate
them to the source in a geometrical sense. In a bounded environment such as a room, each
boundary can be interpreted as a source and their reﬂections, the impulses. Dokmani´c et
al. explained in [22] how this can be applied for the detection of the shape of a room with
impulse response measurements. His work is summarized in the following sections. This
idea can be extended to any scenario where the sources to be detected generate transient
excitations.

Proposed Method
In this chapter the method is explained step by step. Results from a simulated rectangular
room will be used for illustration, presenting six surfaces. The subindex l refers to the
surfaces and the subindex i to the microphones.
The main objective of the thesis presented in Chapter 2 implies to solve several problems
found in the literature.
• The automatic detection of reﬂections from impulse responses is not covered in the
literature, being a manual procedure in all the cases.
• The localization of surfaces is usually based only on temporal queues (TOAs).

dependent on the surrounding reﬂections.
The solution proposed is a 5 step method which tries to detect automatically the reﬂec-
tions, translate into surfaces in the space and estimate the absorption coeﬃcient attenu-

It is impossible to completely avoid this problem but it is possible to reduce its eﬀects.
The ﬁrst main cause is the error introduced in the detection of the incoming directions.
As the trilateration is calculating a ”projection” of the selected direction into the space,
increasing the number of points on the sphere will reduce this error by increasing the
possibilities of hitting the right direction while increasing the computational cost. The
main process that the method goes trough is the delay and sum calculation. Doubling G
doubles the time of computation.
A second option is to use the procedure suggested in [28] for all the microphones and try
to match the highest amplitude delaying and summing for diﬀerent fs. Once the image
sources have been estimated, it is possible to focus on each detected direction and realign
the signals. This post processing has been included during the measurements.
One last option is to add the reﬂections in the frequency domain instead of the time
domain, which allows to sum only the absolute value of the reﬂection coeﬃcient and,
therefore, to neglect the eﬀect of the phase. This last solution leads into problems with
noise, which main way of reducing its eﬀect is by averaging including phase information.
Due to the noise’s randomness it will tend to cancel itself.

7.2.3
The Double Reference Measurement
During the measurements of the big room scenario, two reference positions, one nearby and
one far away from the sources were considered. This decision comes from the experience
of the ﬁrst scenario, where the high frequencies attenuated faster than the low frequencies
due to propagation eﬀects. To counteract this problem a second reference was considered
at furthest distances for both loudspeakers. They are deconvolved by the closest ones
and then used to compensate this potential problem in the measurements. In the small
room, the direct impulses recorded in the full impulse response measurements ppr, s, tq
were windowed and used for this purpose. All the results shown during Chapters 6 and 7
were corrected with this procedure.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The principal goal of this study was to develop a system that automatically detects the
main surfaces of a room and calculates the absorption coeﬃcient of them. Motivation
was found from existing in-situ methods for acoustic characterization of surfaces, which
try to reproduce laboratory conditions losing perspective in the ease of use. To develop
the proposed system two problems were found in the existing literature: localization of
acoustic sources and reduction of the eﬀect of the spurious reﬂections. The extraction of
the absorption coeﬃcient from impulse responses was thoroughly studied understanding
the problems of simpliﬁed assumptions such as plane wave propagation and inﬁnite surface
size. The localization of impulsive sources only based on times of arrival of the pressure
signal was also presented.
The new proposed method consists on ﬁve well deﬁned steps which were physically ex-
plained, tested under simulation conditions and proved in real scenarios. According to the
impulsive characteristics of the reﬂections in a room, a solution was proposed combining
Standard Deviation (STD) and Spectral Flux (SF) to automatically detect the Times
of Arrivals (TOAs) of the reﬂections, ﬁnding balance between high time resolution and
frequency spectrum information and solving the problem for low amplitude reﬂections. To
translate the TOAs to coordinates in the space, a method called Delay and Sum Beam-
forming based on an array of microphones and plane wave assumptions was developed
adding spatial queues to the existing Multidimensional Scaling. Problems in the combi-
nation of these two approaches led to a more relaxed solution only using the Delay and
Sum approach, releasing part of the constrains of MDS with the TOAs and focusing the
design in the enhancement of reﬂections from absorptive walls by the use of time windows.
Trilateration technique was applied to localize the surfaces in the space, calculating an
image source model of the investigated rooms. Exploiting the properties of the Delay and
Sum method, a possible solution to the problem of the spurious reﬂections was discovered.
The coherent sum of the signals recorded by the array of microphones used during the
localization of surfaces was also applied in the calculation of the absorption coeﬃcient,
enhancing the reﬂection under study and attenuating the spurious ones.
Simulations were implemented in MATLAB to test the assumptions made in the design
of the method. Random arrays of microphones were placed in rectangular rooms with
absorptive surfaces. In terms of detection of surfaces, a spread distribution of the micro-
phones led to worse results due to the loss of agreement between plane wave and spherical
wave propagation. The number of directions considered during the Delay and Sum process
highly inﬂuences the estimation of far surfaces. During the absorption coeﬃcient calcula-
tion, an inherent problem of the method consisting on the cancellation of high frequencies
was found due to misalignments during the delay and sum process. Increasing the sam-
pling frequency and the number of directions helped to reduce the problem.
Several sets of measurements were conducted in two rooms of diﬀerent size and with a dif-
ferent setup. The characteristics of the chosen sources and receivers were found crucial for
==PAGE:88==
78
the well behavior of the method, specially during the detection of the surfaces. Directivity
and frequency response were studied and discussed, concluding that the deconvolution of
the response of the source is generally a must. The frequency working range of the micro-
phones also conditions the detection of reﬂections. The localization of surfaces showed a
good rate of success and proved that the combination of SF and STD was a good choice
to detect absorptive surfaces. The eﬀect of non-ﬂat surfaces and scattering objects was
observed, reducing the detection. A comparison with existing MDS method concluded
that the relaxation of the condition in the TOAs leads to a better rate of success and
higher probabilities of convergence of the trilateration process.
The estimated absorption coeﬃcient fairly agreed with theoretical results under several
conditions. The microphones involved during the calculations have to be outside of an
overlap area to avoid interferences that lead into wrong estimations. The attenuation of
spurious reﬂections was proved under this condition and the absorption coeﬃcient could
be approximated for ﬁrst and second reﬂections. Further surfaces couldn’t be character-
ized. A clear low frequency limit was found due to the sphericity of the problem and
the diﬃculty of attenuating low frequencies from surrounding spurious reﬂections. Again,
disturbing elements such as lamps and ledges were considered, ﬁnding the results case
dependent and the necessity of choosing a good measurement spot in the room.
==PAGE:89==
Chapter 9
Future Work
Several aspects of this study that wasn’t solved could be considered as future research.
The automatic absorption coeﬃcient calculation can be improved. Once the image sources
of the surfaces are calculated, it can be deﬁned which microphones present overlap and
which ones don’t. According to results, it wasn’t always necessary to use all the micro-
phones in the calculation of the absorption coeﬃcient.
A more detailed study of the performance of the absorption coeﬃcient estimation should
be done under laboratory conditions. The comparison with existing methods and measur-
ing well known materials will provide a better understanding of the limits of this method.
The case of surrounding elements should be taken into account, deﬁning more thoroughly
the eﬀects on these calculations.
The detection of sources could be tested with loudspeakers instead of surfaces. The total
control of the reﬂections will end in a better comprehension of the localization error. If
this error is bounded, a deeper understanding of the extended reaction of the materials is
possible and in which cases the image source solution doesn’t apply.
