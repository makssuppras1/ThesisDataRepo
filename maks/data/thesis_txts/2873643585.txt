Abstract
This paper presents an NMOS based voltage regulator, designed using a 180 nm technol­
ogy and the GM/ID design methodology. Voltage regulators are used to convert voltage
from the main voltage supply, down to the desired voltage level. They are also used to
isolate noise sensitive circuit from the main supply rail, which can be noisy due to various
factors. The designed voltage regulator has an input of 3V­5V and is able to output 1.8 V
with 1% error margin. The regulator has a current consumption of 7.1µA and a total area
of 59.49µm × 56.82µm. It is able to reach a maximum phase margin of 86.06°and ­37.73
dB in PSRR−1. The NMOS based voltage regulator was designed to be able to handle a
supply rail shared beween error amplifier and regulator input.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
As the performance of portable devices are becoming better, smaller and more efficient
power­management systems are also becoming more crucial. On a system level, circuit
designers would have to balance between performance and power efficiency. By having
a more power efficient power­management system, the designers are able to achieve
better performance and higher power efficiency from the designed circuit.
System­on­chip (SOC) will require multiple voltage lines, as different circuit blocks would
need different levels of voltage. Low dropout regulators (LDO) are essential blocks in
power management systems, as their role is to convert the voltage level from the main
power supply down to the desired voltage level required by the load block [1]. While
integrated switch mode power supplies (SMPS) are able to efficiently regulate and step
down the voltage level, they are more complex and use more area.
Figure 1.1: Block diagram on LDO usage
Another reason for using LDO is to suppress and isolate noise. If all circuit blocks with
the same voltage requirement were to share the same supply line, certain circuit block
would generate substantial noise on the supply line due to internal switching. The SMPS
introduces noise at their output supply line, also due to switching. These factors would
degrade the performance of noise and power supply variation sensitive blocks, for exam­
ple RF blocks. Which is why LDOs are used as a final power supply regulation stage, as
they provide clean and low noise output, even with a noisy input voltage source.
1.2
State of the art
Voltage regulators typically use PMOS transistor as their pass devices, due to PMOS
characteristics allowing for simpler drive circuits and lower dropout voltage. However,
NMOS pass device based regulators have emerged as an alternative due to their superior
performance in noise regulation and loop stability.
The gain of the error amplifier has a large role in the noise regulation of the op­amp.
Single stage high gain op­amps such as cascode and folded cascode amplifiers are com­
monly used[2] [3]. Being single stage, the op­amps introduces fewer low frequency poles
compared to multi stage op­amps, enhancing the loop stability. The downside of this and
NMOS based LDOs in general is that the gate voltage required will be higher than than
the op­amp could provide. The benefits from NMOS LDOs comes as a cost of increased

design complexity. More complex drive circuits will be required, as NMOS based LDOs
often require the error amplifier and input of the LDO to have different supply rails[4]. If
the input and error amplifier are required to share the same supply rail, charge pumps
are implemented to boost the voltage level for the error amplifier[5]. The problem with
implementing a charge pump is the previously stated increase in circuit complexity and
requirements. Charge pumps require external digital signals to function and the added
capacitors would increase the footprint of the LDO tremendously.
Another way to allow for error amplifier and input to share the same supply rail is to use a
a rail to rail operational amplifier for the error amplifier, as shown in [6]. This ensures that
the error amplifier is able to output a high enough voltage swing for the pass device. The
downside of using a more complex amplifier like rail to rail op­amps is that the stability
they provide is lower and a more robust compensation network is needed[7]. Which in
turn increases the footprint of the LDO.
As more complex circuits are used, better state of the art compensation techniques are
used to improve the loop stability of the regulator. One common techniques is the volt­
age buffer compensation circuit[8] [9]. This compensation method add a voltage buffer
between the error amplifier and pass device. This isolates the high output resistance of
the error amplifier from the high gate capacitance of the pass device, preventing them
from forming a low frequency pole. Note that this technique is more prevalent in PMOS
based LDOs, as the output pole is often the chosen dominant pole due to PMOS pass de­
vices having a higher output resistance. For LDOs with the pass device gate pole as the
dominant pole, compensation capacitors will need to be used to ensure that the gate pole
is at a low frequency. One method to avoid this, and have a cap­less LDO altogether,
is to use a dual­loop circuit presented in [10]. The dual loop circuit uses a secondary
stage, consisting of an NPN transistor and a secondary feedback loop, that amplifies the
capacitance and resistance at the gate node. Making the gate pole the dominant pole.
To improve power efficiency in LDOs circuit, adaptive biasing were implemented [11] [12].
Adaptive biasing changes the bias current of the error amplifier based on the situation.
When high performance is needed, such as load transients, the bias current increases
which improves the transient response of the circuit. At DC or low load situations, the
bias current is reduced which increases the power efficiency of the circuit. Allowing the
LDO to run at low power most of the time and only switch to high power usage when
needed.
A table of state of the art NMOS based LDOs performances is presented in figure . In sum­
mary, while significant advancement have been made with NMOS LDOs the downsides
still persist. As the requirement for a higher supply voltage requires more complex circuit
such as charge pumps or rail to rail amplifiers, if a separate supply rail is not available for
the error amplifier.

LDO background
2.1
Function and applications of an LDO
As stated in the introduction, the LDO is used for 2 applications. Regulation and isolation.
The main role of a low dropout regulator is to convert an input voltage to a lower stable
voltage level. While a voltage divider can fulfill this function, it is sensitive to changes in
input voltage and output current because the input­output ratio and resistance are fixed[1].
As such the LDO is used instead. The LDO is able to output a stable output voltage,
regardless of change in input voltage and output current.
Due to its size, LDOs are mainly used to convert voltage from the main power line down
to more specific voltage levels required by various blocks in the circuit. This will in turn
isolate the output circuit from the main power line. Allowing the output circuit to be isolated
from the noise generated from other circuit blocks and the main power converter.
2.2
Basic LDO Design
The basic design of an LDO consist of 4 components.
Figure 2.1: Block diagram of the LDO
• Pass device: This is component acts as a variable resistor. Allowing the LDO to
control the voltage drop over the pass device and will output the correct voltage,
regardless of input voltage. This component is either an NMOS or a PMOS.
• Reference: This component inputs a stable constant voltage into the error amplifier.
• Feedback: This component is a voltage divider that will feedback the output voltage
into the error amplifier.
4
Design of a voltage regulator
==PAGE:13==
• Error amplifier: The error amplifier will take the reference voltage and feedback
voltage and output a fitting gate voltage. Which will ensure a proper transient re­
sponse and stability in the circuit.
First the LDO will take the unregulated input voltage and apply it to the pass device.
The output voltage is then sensed by the feedback circuit and fed into the error amplifier
together with the reference voltage. Note that the reference voltage and the ratio of the
feedback voltage divider will set the output voltage. Based on the error between the
reference and feedback voltage, the error amplifier will adjust the gate voltage of the pass
device, controlling the dropout voltage of the pass device and in turn the output voltage
of LDO. As either the input voltage or the output current changes, the output voltage will
in turn change. The feedback loop will adjust the pass device until the set output voltage
is reached.

3.2.1
Theory
Stability
Comparing the stability performances, the choice of pass device will depend on where
the dominant pole will be located. The output pole l depends on the load capacitor and
the output resistance. NMOS pass device LDOs will exhibit lower output resistance, com­
pared to PMOS pass devices[4]. Meaning that it will be harder for NMOS based regulators
to compensate for stability, due to the output pole being at a higher frequency. Which is
why the pass device gate pole is better suited to be the dominant pole for NMOS based
regulators. While if the output pole is chosen to be the dominant pole, it is better to have a
PMOS pass device due to a higher output resistance. Note that it is more power efficient
to have a dominant pass device gate pole. As to push the gate pole to a higher frequency,
the error amplifier must be faster and have a higher bandwidth. Which results in higher
current draw and power consumption[17].
Regulation and noise
In a voltage regulator circuit, an NMOS pass device would behave as source follower[4].
This improves the PSRR of the circuit because the output voltage will depend on the gate
voltage, with the noise from the input voltage having minimal effect. Although noise from
the gate voltage will freely propagate to the output in this case. PMOS pass devices would
behave as common gate amplifier, which mean that the input voltage noise will have an
effect on the output voltage. Which means the NMOS has better noise performance.
Figure 3.6 presents the path noise can take, 1 through the pass transistor and the other
through the error amplifier. The noise propogation through the amplifier will be discussed
in section 3.3.1.

For this project an NMOS pass device was chosen. This is due to superior performance
in both stability and noise. While PMOS is able to have lower dropout voltages, this is
not a strict requirement for this project. Note that by choosing NMOS, the error amplifier
power supply requirement will put design restrictions on the circuit.

3.3.1
Topology
There are 2 main requirements the error amplifier needs to fulfill in order for the LDO to
function to the required specifications. The op­amp needs to deliver a specified amount
of gain to achieve to wanted PSRR. This relation is further analyzed in section 3.3.3.
The second requirement relates to the restriction placed by using an NMOS pass device,
stated in equation 3.5. The error amplifier needs a high enough upper output range to
turn on the pass transistor and put it into saturation, this limits the topology choice for the
error amplifier. Op­amp gain can be describe with equation 3.11.
AV = gm × rout
(3.11)
To increase gain, op­amp topologies would stack transistors between supply and output
to increase the output resistance and therefore gain, for example cascode op­amp topolo­
gies. But in return the upper bound of the output range will go lower, depending on the
amount of transistors stacked as describe with equation 3.12, with N being the amount
of stacked transistor. Note that each stacked transistor can have a different overdrive
voltage.
VOUT,max = VDD −

VOVi

For this reason, only op­amps with 1 stacked transistor were considered, as this is the
minimum required of stacked transistors for error amplifiers. Also the topology will require
that the tail current source should be NMOS, to increase the upper range further.
One way to bypass these topology requirements and the 1 stacked transistor limitation, is
to consider a rail to rail op­amp. As these op­amps will allow the output to reach VDD. But
this will come at a cost of a higher complexity circuit, which could lead to lower stability
and higher circuit footprint.
The topologies considered for the error amplifier are the single stage 5T OTA and the 2
stage amplifier, presented in figure 3.9 and figure 3.10, both using NMOS input pairs. As
both of these circuits upper output voltage is only limited by 1 PMOS transistor, allowing
for higher output voltage:
VOUT,max = VDD −VOV,PMOS
(3.13)
Comparing the 2 circuits, the 2 stage op­amp has higher a gain which will allow for better
noise rejection. Comparing the stability, the 1 stage op­amp is expected to have a phase
margin of 90°. In contrast to the 2 stage op­amp which will have a lower phase margin,
due to introducing an extra pole. The 2 stage op­amp will also have a higher footprint due
to an extra stage. Due to its superior stability, the 1 stage op­amp was chosen.
It is important to note that using the combination of NMOS input 5T OTA and an NMOS
pass device has a disadvantage. A PMOS input 5T OTA will have a PMOS tail current
source, this tail current source is effective at preventing noise leakage from the supply to
the output, giving this op­amp topology a higher PSRR, compared to its NMOS counter­
part[1]. The NMOS input 5T OTA only has the active load between VDD and output, while
it acts as a resistor and will attenuate noise, it is less effective than a current source. In
section 3.2.1, it was stated that the NMOS is connected and behaves as a source follower.
This results in the noise freely propagating from the gate to the source of the pass device,
which will reduce the overall PSRR of the circuit.

phase margin.

lower transient response, and a risk of the pass device falling into the triode region. Apart
from the PSRR failing at 3V and minimum current conditions, the LDO is able to function
at the targeted 18 pF load capacitance requirement.

6
Future work
In this chapter, considerations for improvement of the circuit are presented and discussed.
6.0.1
Pass device
With the size of the layout, a higher GM/ID could have been used for the transistor. This
would result in a larger transistor size and would improve the phase margin according to
calculations. Looking at the gate voltage test of the transistor, a higher GM/ID would also
have resulted in lower gate voltage requirements, which will improve the loop gain of the
circuit and in turn the PSRR.
6.0.2
Compensation
To bring stability to the circuit, a gate capacitor had to be implemented. This came at a cost
of the dominant pole frequency being lowered, which lowered the gain and thus PSRR
at the wanted voltage. To avoid this a compensation technique would be needed, that
is able to push the output pole to a higher frequency. Another alternative is use output
pole as the dominant pole. This allow the error amplifier to have a higher bandwidth
and additional compensation circuits will not have to affect the performance of the error
amplifier. Although as stated in a previous section, higher bandwidth error amplifier would
result in higher quiescent current.
6.0.3
Error amplifier
To allow the error amplifier and input to share the same supply rail the 2 stage amplifier
and rail to rail amplifier were considered. With the phase margin and size achieved, these
2 considered amplifiers would improve the performance of the regulator. Both amplifier
would be able to achieve a higher open loop gain, which will increase PSRR. The rail to rail
amplifier would increase the upper voltage range to VDD, and allowing for a stronger gate
drive for the pass device. Although these 2 circuits would decrease the phase margin,
and with the minimum current and maximum load capacitance condition already having
a degraded phase margin, a more robust compensation circuit would be needed.
To increase the PSRR without adding more complexity to the circuit, a PMOS input 5T
OTA can be implemented. But for this to work, either the input voltage range is reduced
from 3V­5V to 4V­5V or the output voltage is lowered.
6.0.4
Feedback circuit
To further improve the feedback circuit, the resistor could be replace with diode connected
transistors. The circuit is presented in figure 6.1.
Using diode connected transistor as resistor would allow for a lower current draw and a
smaller footprint. In an isolated simulation, with minimum size NMOS transistor, the circuit
drew 76.59 nA. Doing a Monte Carlo simulation with 200 samples and minimum size tran­
sistor, shows that this feedback circuit only manage to pass the set target of 1.2V output
with 1% tolerance in 61.5% of the samples with a standard deviation of 15.1mV. A higher
success rate and accuracy could be achieved by increasing the size of the transistor to
minimize process variations.

7
Conclusion
To conclude this report a fully integrated NMOS voltage regulator in 180nm technology
was designed using the GM/ID design method.
The designed regulator was able to
meet the target specifications for output voltage error, quiescent current and tempera­
ture. Phase margins and load capacitance were able to meet target specification only
under certain speciations. Compared to norm, the NMOS based voltage regulator is able
to function with the error amplifier and input sharing the same supply rail. Allowing for
the lack charge pump and multiple different supply rails in the circuit. Although this came
at a cost of a degraded PSRR and lower performances at 3V, due to topology and de­
sign choices. The layout has an area of 59.49µm × 56.82µm, meeting the targeted area
requirements.
Comparing the designed voltage regulator with the state of the art regulators in table 7.1
shows comparable phase margin and maximum load capacitance. Note that compared
to the designed regulator, the other state of the art regulators are used at a lower voltage,
with lower dropout voltage and an output current in the mA range. Compared to other
regulators the PSRR is slightly lower, but is able to function with the error amplifier and
input voltage having the same supply rail . Comparing the area, the designed voltage
regulator is much smaller than [10] with the same technology, and is comparable to [2],
which uses 16 nm technology.
