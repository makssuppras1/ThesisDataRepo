Abstract
The methyltrophic yeast Pichia pastoris has gained popularity as a cell factory. It is especially prominent
in protein production, where the current method of creating the P. pastoris cell factories is very time-
and labour consuming. Plasmids are not capable of achieving stable or high enough expression, and
therefore current methods involves integrating many copies into the genome and screening. Moreover,
the integrated copies can still be lost during long-term cultivation resulting in decreased production of
the desired compound.
Yet, despite of the weaknesses mentioned, P. pastoris is still widely used as
a protein production host. Moreover, the popular CRISPR-Cas9 tool is not widely established in P.
pastoris. Therefore, better tools are needed for fast and eﬃcient genetic editing in P. pastoris, in order
to better utilise its potential for bioproduction. In this study, a novel Cas9-based editing tool for P.
pastoris was successfully developed and implemented. This was done using a single-strand oligo as repair
template which, due to its simplicity, could signiﬁcantly speed up genetic modiﬁcations in P. pastoris.
Additionally, tRNA spaced gRNA successfully guided CRISPR-Cas9 in P. pastoris, a system compatible
with multiplex genome edits of several sites. This expands the P. pastoris genetic engineering toolbox.
Moreover, the now broadened P. pastoris Cas9 based tools can be applied for construction of platform
strains such as CASCADE; that allows for gene over-expression for cell factory creation. The expansion
of the P. pastoris tools can pave the way for a faster and more sophisticated approach when genetically
engineering P. pastoris.
For example you could potentially do multiple gene edits in in signiﬁcantly
shorter time span with these tools.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1 | Aim of the project
The aim of the project is to implement and test for genetic engineering in Pichia pastoris, and furthermore
develop these tools for engineering P. pastoris. This is done by implementing and developing new tools
for CRISPR-Cas9 in P. pastoris. Additionally, start the process of implementing the CASCADE platform
in P. pastoris.
The platform aims to create strains of commonly used expression organisms, which can fast and easily
reach relatively high expression of the desired product. For this purpose the CASCADE system was
made, which in a 5-step protocol can integrate up to nine copies of a single gene at high expression
regions of the genome. The CASCADE platform has already been implemented in the commonly used
Saccharomyces cerevisiae[1]. Other projects are currently working on the implementation in ﬁlamentous
fungi, but this project focuses on the yeast P. pastoris.
The scope of this project is to implement and test the CRISPR-Cas9 system for eﬃcient gene editing
in P. pastoris, while also testing additional strategies within the CRISPR-Cas9 system. Furthermore, in
parallel; design, construct, and validate plasmids for the integration sites used by the CASCADE platform.
Additionally, test markers and protocols for P. pastoris to pave the way for the full implementation of
the CASCADE platform. A sketch of the plan can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1

Chapter 2
Background
2.1 | Microbial cell factories
As our knowledge of the world grows, the problems with the simple solutions are quickly eliminated, and
the problems with complex solutions are left. Many of the world’s chemicals are produced by chemical
reactions, however, some complex molecules, such as proteins, which currently cannot be chemically
synthesised, can be produced in microorganism cell factories.
Bacteria and especially Escherichia coli can be a powerful cell factory, as it is fast growing, and has a large
genetic toolbox. However, the downstream process can be costly as E. coli has little to no secretion, and
maybe more importantly E. coli lacks the ability for some of the important complex post-translational
modiﬁcations (PTM)[2]. This is especially a problem in the production of medication, if the protein
produced lacks or has a diﬀerent PTM than intended, the protein might not function properly. However,
in yeast the PTM processing is more similiar to humans, and more protein products are therefore viable[3].
With the viability of many potential protein products, the creation and optimization of the cell factories
can be taken into consideration.
2.1.1 | Yeasts as a cell factory
Yeasts are thought to be the ﬁrst microorganism humans utilised, the ancient Egyptians discovered that
yeasts were useful in food production and in creating alcoholic beverages[4]. Saccharomyces cerevisiae
also known as baker’s yeast, was of great interest and was studied heavily. The greater knowledge of
S. cerevisiae over other yeasts led to the ﬁrst heterologous protein production from yeast around the
1980’s[5][6][7].
Furthermore, S. cerevisiae was the ﬁrst eukaryotic organism to have its full genome
published in 1996[8]. The scientiﬁc community considers S. cerevisiae to be a model organism, from
which we have derived many theories covering: aging[9], cell cycle [10], apoptosis [11] etc. However, as
the toolbox expanded for other yeasts, alternative yeast hosts became available for genetic engineering.
While yeasts have great potential, they also have some obstacles to overcome. Plasmid-borne expression
of genes is fast and easy when compared to genomic integration. Little to no genomic knowledge of
the organism is required, and most commonly used organisms will have optimal growth conditions and
transformation protocols described in literature. However, expression of desired genes by plasmid have

2.1.2 | P. pastoris as a cell factory
The methylotrophic yeast P. pastoris has become a popular choice as a protein producing cell factory.
It can: produce high amounts of intracellular protein[15], grow to high cell densities on cheap media,
express heterologues genes, perform complex post-translational modiﬁcations, secrete product, and its

Especially the powerful protein production machinery makes it a strong production host. P. pastoris
is used for production for hundreds of diﬀerent proteins including Human interferon gamma[3][14]. P.
pastoris has been reclassiﬁed as Komagataella pastoris, but is mostly referred to by the former name.
There are several commercial vector system available for P. pastoris for the expression of heterologous
protein (see table 2.1). However, these vectors mostly result in low-copy numbers. Furthermore, the
vectors will only target one locus at a time, making the process of building an economically feasible cell
factory extremely laborious. They do however come with signal sequences targeting the desired protein
for secretion.

Chapter 2. Background
2.2. DNA damage repair systems in yeast
The yeasts high production potential combined with the cheap production cost, makes it an obvious
candidate when looking for a cell factory host for protein production.
2.2 | DNA damage repair systems in yeast
All organisms are susceptible to DNA damage, both from the environment and internal mechanisms.
Therefore, DNA damage repair mechanisms are crucial for a cells longevity. Most severe are the double-
strand break (DSB), if not tended to, these breaks are fatal to the cell. In yeast there are two main
pathways of DSB repair; Non-homologous end-joining, and Homologous recombination. Models are used
to describe the events of these pathways, but the exact mechanisms are not fully understood. These
models are described below, and later it is discussed how we can utilise these pathways.
2.2.1 | Non-homologous End-Joining
Non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) is one of two major DSB DNA repair mechanisms for yeasts, The
S. cerevisiae homologs Ku70p/Ku80p heterodimer facilitates (along with other factors) the ligation of
the DSB in P. pastoris[19]. NHEJ is error-prone sometimes causing small insertions or deletions at the
DSB[20].
When targeted integration of a gene is attempted, random integration of the desired gene can occur[21].
If the ﬁtness is not aﬀected by the integration this might not be a problem; it can even be desirable.
However, when highly speciﬁc integration is desired, random integration is a problem. Deletion of genes
involved in NHEJ has been shown to reduce the frequency of random integration. Speciﬁcally a group of
researchers created a ∆Ku70 which reduced the frequency of random integration to 1/268 transformants
compared to the 236/268 with 650bp homology regions at the HIS4 in P. pastoris locus[22]. Working
with a ∆Ku70 mutant can therefore be highly advantageous when doing targeted integration of desired
genes.
The same group of researchers found that a deletion of the Ku70 homolog in P. Pastoris results in lower
growth rate (10-30%), and lower resistance to UV-radiation, therefore, it is advised to reintroduce the
Ku70 gene once the desired genetic manipulation has been achieved[22]. If this key part is knocked
out, most DSB repair will be done by the other major DNA damage repair mechanism; homologous
recombination.
2.2.2 | Homologous recombination
Homologous recombination (HR) is the other main DNA repair mechanisms for yeasts. HR has been
described by three main models regarding crossing-over events mainly in meiotic cells: The Holiday
model[23], The Meselson-Radding Model[24], and The double-strand-break repair (DSBR) model[25].

However, in mitotic cells cross-over events are far less common, therefore the Synthesis-dependent strand
annealing (SDSA) mechanism has been used to describe the event where a DSB, is repaired without
reciprocal genetic transfer[26][27].
When a DSB occurs an endonuclease complex, along with supplementary proteins, is formed in order to
create 3’ overhangs by degrading the 5’ strand (5’-3’ end-resection)[25]. The complex is then removed
and another protein binds (mediated by other protein(s)) to the 3’ end. Strand invasion can now occur
where the 3’ end strand searches for a homologous strand nearby. When strand invasion is performed a
D-loop occurs and the invading 3’ strand primes onto the homologous strand and is extended by DNA
polymerase (Strand invasion and DNA synthesis). At this point one of two events can occur[28]:
1) the invading strand is displaced, and the remaining gap is synthesised from its own newly repaired
strand. The strand is ligated, resulting in a non-crossover event (SDSA)[28].
2) The second broken end is captured by the homologous template.
The strand is synthesised and
ligated. Two holiday junctions are formed, depending on the resolution of the junctions it results in
either a non-crossover- or cross-over event (DSBR)[28]. An outline of the events can be seen in ﬁgure 2.1

2.3. Engineering of P. pastoris
The ﬁrst genome sequence of a P. pastoris strain was published in 2009[30]. Genome sequences not only
expands our general knowledge of the organism by observing genes similar to our model-organisms. But
also enables genetic engineering of the organism without having to investigate the sequence of a gene of
interest. Since 2009 the genome sequences have been reﬁned to more accurately predict; centromeres,
annotations, open reading frames etc[31][32]. The published genomes provide a powerful tool not only
for genetic work, but also for understanding which systems in P. pastoris might be similar or diﬀerent
compared to model organisms. Models of systems in other organisms can be assumed to be somewhat
similar in P. pastoris, at least until proven diﬀerent, if the genes of this system is also found in the yeast.
The current methods for the creation of production strains of; multicopy plasmid-based systems or by
targeted integration into highly conserved and expressed regions, each have their own weaknesses. Yeasts
have the tendency to lose plasmids over generations when selection pressure is not applied. Therefore, in
a fermentation a portion of the population of cells will produce little or no product. Yeasts also have a
tendency to loop-out repeated genomic sequences, especially if the repeats are in close proximity of each
other. However, this can be avoided by choosing sites with essential genes in between.
2.3.1 | CRISPR-Cas9
CRISPR-Cas9 (Cas9) is an RNA mediated nuclease which causes targeted DSB in DNA. The Cas9 protein
originated in bacteria as an adaptive immune system response to viral DNA, but has been adapted to
perform genetic engineering[33]. To understand how we can utilise Cas9, we must ﬁrst understand the
mechanism.
Cas9 is mediated by RNA of which there are a couple of parts to consider. CRISPR RNA (crRNA)
consists of two parts: 1) varying complimentary (to the target) RNA sequence (cRNA) 2) a consistent
Short Palindromic sequence (the CRISPR sequence). The trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA)
consists of a sequence complimentary to the CRISPR sequence. The Cas9 protein works by binding
crRNA and tracrRNA, and causing a DSB in DNA identical to part of the cRNA sequence[34]. This
was later adapted to a single RNA piece by Jinek et al. when designed and implemented for engineering
purposes, these are termed guide RNA (gRNA) or single-guide RNA.
The Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) sequence is one of the two parts of the target sequence which
the Cas9 protein recognises, the other being the compliment cRNA sequence. The PAM binding site is
not RNA mediated but rather coded into the protein itself. The PAM sequence is short, three for Cas9,
and can vary for diﬀerent CRISPR proteins. For the Cas9 protein the PAM sequence is NGG. Therefore,
a limiting factor to the Cas9 technique is the presence of an NGG motif in the region of interest[35]. A
ﬁgure of the Cas9 technique can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2.

After the Cas9 mediated DSB the cell will attempt to repair the broken DNA either through NHEJ or

nucleotide mutation or deletion will most likely occur. In the case of a single or double nucleotide deletion
a frame shift would occur rendering the gene product inactive.
In the event of HR DNA repair the cell would attempt to repair the DSB using a repair template.
Such a template can be delivered to "trick" the HR pathway that the template is it’s own genetic code.
Homologous regions up- and downstream of the DSB can ﬂank a desired integrated sequence.
This
technique can be used to insert virtually any sequence at the DSB allowing for: Gene integration, gene
deletion, point mutation etc[37]. While Cas9 can take some work to eﬃciently implement, Cas9 papers
are regularly released spanning a wide range of organisms, making it easier and faster to implement in a
chosen organism. However, companies are mostly not looking to use the strategy as there is an ongoing

2.3.2 | Genetic engineering of P. pastoris
In order to eﬃciently genetically modify P. pastoris there is a need for selection markers. Antibiotic
resistance markers have been established conferring resistance to drugs such as: Zeocin, Geneticin (G418),
blasticidin S[17], Nourseothricin (NTC)[39], and Hygromycin B (Hyg)[40]. Furthermore, auxotrophic gene
markers have been identiﬁed: HIS4, MET2, ADE1, ARG4, URA3, URA5, and GUT1[17]. The URA3
marker is widely used in many yeasts because of the ability to counter-select for the presence of the gene
with 5-Fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA)[41]. The marker can also be highly useful in P. pastoris. However, even
in the presence of Uracil a ∆URA3 mutant will grow slowly, probably due to slow uptake[42], increasing
the time to form usable colonies[43].
The discovery of the Cas9 system provides a powerful tool to genetic engineering, which can enable
marker- and scar-less genome editing. The Cas9 system was ﬁrst introduced into P. pastoris in 2016
by Weninger et al[44].
The same study observed that P. pastoris had a much narrower window for
optimal editing as compared to S. cerevisiae. They theorized this was mainly due to S. cerevisae’s higher
HR frequency. They found that the Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase’s promoter (pGAP) and
the Homo sapiens codon optimized CAS9 (hsCAS9) was a viable combination for gene targeting in P.
pastoris[44]. This is also the promoter-Cas9 combination used in this study. Furthermore, they discovered
that using the standard RNA III polymerase[37] promoters for gRNA expression did not perform eﬃcient
gene targeting. However, a gRNA ﬂanked by; the Hammerhead ribozyme , and the hepatitis delta virus,
could yield gene targeting at a rate of 80-90 %.
Another gRNA expression strategy is to use a single promoter and utilise the endogenous tRNA-processing
system. This is especially useful when looking to do multiplex gene edits[45]. With this method gRNA
can be spaced by precursor tRNA, which will be cleaved by the organisms own tRNA processing system
in the nucleus. This enables the expression of multible gRNA available to the Cas9 protein within the
same construct[46]. As far as the literature research done in this project, none has successfully performed
Cas9 gene targeting with gRNA spaced by precursor tRNA, as is the strategy/goal of this study.
When Cas9 causes a DSB the method for obtaining desired genetic edits is to provide a repair template.
The repair template consists of the desired edit ﬂanked by homology regions. In S. cerevisiae a synthetic
single-stranded oligo repair template can be used to create genetic edits at usable frequencies[47]. However
this has not been reported in P. pastoris yet.

2.3.3 | CASCADE
CASCADE is a gene expression platform for yeasts developed by Strucko et al. It aims to create an
alternative to current yeast cell factory development, speciﬁcally the paper published in 2017 implemented
the CASCADE system in S. cerevisiae. The implementation of the CASCADE system in S. cerevisiae
was very time- and labour intensive [personal correspondence], therefore a powerful genetic engineering
toolbox could save a lot of trouble. This study aims to establish genetic engineering tools for P. pastoris,
by integrating and testing the Cas9 system, and designing and creating plasmids for genomic integration
of the CASCADE cassette.
The CASCADE platform allows the user to integrate a desired gene into diﬀerent loci of high expression
ﬂanked by essential genes in the genome.
CASCADE does this by integration of the Kluyveromyces
lactis_URA3 gene ﬂanked by regions for direct repeat recombination (DRR) loop-out termed A and B
regions. Furthermore, an I-SceI cut site is located between the upstream A region and the Kl_URA3
gene.

the CASCADE strain. The strain is selected on tryptophan depleted media to select for an HR event
replacing CASCADE cassette for the gene of interest cassette. 2) counterselection for the TRP1 marker

2.4. Cloning/plasmid assembly
2.4 | Cloning/plasmid assembly
The plasmid assembly in this study, was performed by Uracil-Speciﬁc Excision Reagent (USER) cloning.
USER cloning is not widely used, however it does have some advantages compared to the more traditional
Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) overlap ampliﬁcation cloning (eg. Golden Gate cloning). Mainly
reduced price due to shorter oligo primers needed, and only one PCR-amplication step which reduces the
error-rate[55]. The concept of USER cloning is described below.
2.4.1 | USER cloning
USER cloning works by creating 3’ overhangs by a single uracil nucleotide (nt) included in the primer (see
ﬁgure 2.5. The ﬁrst step is to design primers with 7-9 nt overhangs with a single uracil nt complimentary
to either the desired vector, or if cloning multiple fragments, another PCR fragment. The fragments
are PCR ampliﬁed using a uracil compatible DNA polymerase. Mixing these PCR fragments and pre
cut vector with the USER enzyme mix (New England Biolabs), will remove the uracil nt and nick the
remaining nts upstream, creating 3’ sticky end overhangs. The compatible fragments will anneal in the
mixture, and can be transformed into competent E. coli cells, where the cells machinery will assemble
the plasmid.

Chapter 4
Results and discussion
The goal of this project was to improve on the genetic engineering toolbox of P. pastoris. Since only a
small project had been done with the organism, many aspects of working with the yeast had still not
been determined/optimized. While transformation had been performed, the process was still somewhat
unreliable, and CRISPR-Cas9 had not yet been implemented or tested.
Therefore, two goals of this project were deﬁned: 1) implement and test CRISPR-Cas9 to inactivate the
URA3 gene, and determine whether the Kl_URA3 marker used by the CASCADE could successfully
restore P. pastoris’s ability to grow on uracil depleted media. The Cas9 system will also be useful in later
stages of the project for the integration of CASCADE cassettes. 2) in parallel, pave the way for future
projects by constructing plasmids to be used when CRISPR-Cas9 and the Kl_URA3 marker worked (IS-
and gRNA plasmids).
4.1 | Constructing strain/plasmids
To implement a single CASCADE cassette three diﬀerent elements would have to be present in the
cell: Cas9 enzyme, gRNA for the target locus, and a repair template containing the CASCADE cassette
ﬂanked by homologous regions up- and downstream of the induced DSB. The plasmids have therefore
been sectioned into; integration site plasmids, and gRNA with or without Cas9.
4.1.1 | IS plasmids, as a repair template for CASCADE cassette integration
A goal was to construct CASCADE plasmids which targeted speciﬁc loci of the genome in P. pastoris.
Therefore, plasmids were constructed with the CASCADE fragment (A-Kl_URA3-B) ﬂanked by ∼1
kb homologous up- and downstream regions for the desired integration locus. By varying the up- and
downstream regions, the same CASCADE fragment could be integrated at multiple locations in the
genome.
CASCADE fragments were to be integrated through HR after a Cas9 mediated DSB. The
plasmids were cloned from the FBF007 plasmid. The plasmids were named: FBF008, FBF009, FBF018,
and FBF019, and two were tested for each plasmid. With the designation of the designed IS: 1, 3, 5, 7,
respectively. A plasmid map of FBF008 can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1, and a simpliﬁed table of the plasmids
can be seen in table 4.1.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to construct plasmids for the CASCADE platform implementation, and
furthermore to implement, test, and improve on the Cas9 system in P. pastoris.
The integration plasmids were created, and are ready to be tested. The sites chosen for integration were
picked from transcriptional data, looking for high expression regions. However, these sites might interfere
with gene promoter or terminator of adjacent genes, or simply not be highly expressed. Therefore, some
or all of these sites might not pass growth or expression tests, luckily, there are additional sites which
could be viable options.
Furthermore, a functioning Cas9 system was successfully established in P. pastoris. The system uses the
pGAP-hsCAS9 combination and gRNA spaced by tRNA. The spacing of gRNA with tRNA has not been
reported in P. pastoris before, as far. The gRNA expression cassette design is setup to be compatible for
multiplexing.
The Cas9-induced URA3 gene editing created a uracil auxotrophy strain, and a highly reduced growth
rate on media with uracil. Furthermore, the sequencing results of four transformants showed one translo-
cation event, two colonies with double bp deletions, and most importantly one 100 bp deletion by HR
mediated by a 90 bp oligo repair template. In all four cases the DNA DSB was induced at the intended
locus, but was repaired in three diﬀerent ways. As only four transformants were tested, not much can
be concluded about the overall frequency of these events. If eﬃcient oligo repair gene editing can be
established, it would simplify gene diting in P. pastoris.

Chapter 5. Conclusion
5.1. Perspectives
5.1 | Perspectives
The implementation of the CASCADE platform for P. pastoris does not have the same advantage of
predeﬁned integration sites as the implementation in S. cerevisiae had[1][53].The data published on such
sites is sparse or hard to ﬁnd. Therefore Vogl et al suggested: "A promising approach would be to collect
high-expressing strains reported in the literature and to sequence them as a community eﬀort."[50] Such
eﬀorts could ﬁrst of all help researchers and industry quickly identify previously discovered high-expression
sites. It could also help predict new sites via bioinformatics or machine learning. Furthermore, with the
emergence of next-generation sequencing the cost of whole-genome sequencing has been reduced[61],
reducing the cost of such a project.
The data used for IS selection in this study was based on expression on glycerol and glucose media. The
goal was constitutive high expression, however it is only based on the two carbon sources. Most notably
methanol is often used as media for P. pastoris in protein production, therefore a CASCADE strain
optimised for expression during growth on methanol might be an additional project.
The prospect of implementing the CASCADE platform in P. pastoris looks bright. With a functioning
Cas9 system and ready-to-use constructed plasmids, investigation of insertion sites can soon begin. How-
ever, ﬁrst it must be tested if the Kl_URA3 gene can supplement the native P. pastoris URA3 gene.
It must also be tested whether a Kl_URA3+ strain will die on 5-FOA, as 5-FOA counter-selection is
needed for the CASCADE protocol.
Furthermore, two new tools for genetic engineering in P. pastoris were tested. The tRNA spaced gRNA
might be a powerful option when looking to do multiplex gene edits. This could be tested by cloning
multiple gRNA-tRNA iterations into the same plasmid and transforming.
Finally, oligo repair was performed. No conclusion could be drawn to the frequency of the repair event,
because of the sample size, however, more colonies are ready to be sequenced. Additionally, the process
could be optimised by looking for optimal DNA portioning in transformation, and by using a ∆Ku70
mutant, to add another tool for engineering P. pastoris.
