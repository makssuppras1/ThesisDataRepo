Abstract
Background: Porous membrane is widely used in organ-on-chip devices for mimicing barrier tissues.
Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) is a commonly used elastomer but it has cytotoxicity and can absorb small
hydrophobic molecules. Styrene-ethylene-butylene-styrene (SEBS) has been proposed to be a good alternative
of PDMS. Direct ink writing 3D printing method, instead of soft lithography, enables multi-material using and
fast prototype design.
Purpose: Develop SEBS based ink for 3D printed porous membrane. Then develop a printing procedure for a
fully printed transwell insert with porous membrane for cell barrier function tests.
Method: After choosing the porogen, print the membrane and make pores with porogen leaching and
immersion precipitation methods. Then design a suitable structure and printing procedure for the insert wall.
Afterwards choose the best combination for cell function tests.
Results: Lab-made microsized salt particles are chosen as porogen. The membranes printed using 1:5
SEBS:BA with 40% (v/v) or higher salt particle contents have pores going through the membrane. It shows a
Lucifer Yellow rejection of 60.77%. A double layer design of the insert wall is proven to have stable output.
Both HUVEC and Caco-2 cells grow well on fully printed inserts with SEBS-based membranes. But none of
the cells show barrier functions.
Conclusion: This thesis describes inks for porous membrane printing and designs a procedure for printing
transwell insert. Although still needs optimization, this project proves that fabricating a fully printed transwell
insert is feasible. And the insert shows good biocompatibility.

1 Introduction
1.1 Problem statement
Organ-on-chip (OOC) devices has received lots of attention and shown promising applications in biomedical
areas. By integrating different compartments on a small chip, OOC devices are very useful in quick on site
test, screening study. They can save lots of money and time in testing and modeling. By mimicking human
organ units or tissues on chips, it can show drug effects or biological functions.
Porous membrane is already widely used in bio-separation, drug delivery, bio-sensor and other areas [1]. It is
also an important part in OOC devices to model barrier tissues in human body, such as blood vessels, lung
aveoli and intestinal tissues. Porous membrane can be used as scaffold in tissue engineering, or help separate
different targets like cells and platelets in a microfluidic chip [2].
Soft lithography is a well developed fabrication method to make microfluidic chips and OOC devices. It
advantages in high resolution and low cost in mass production. However, this method mostly rely on silicon
based elastomer, such as polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), which carries inherent drawbacks of drug adsorption
and cytotoxicity [3]. As an alternative material, thermoplastic elastomers such as styrenic block-co-polymers
has been proposed [4]. They show good bio-compatibility and low drug adsorption in past studies, while still
being elastic and transparent.
Moreover, soft lithography is limited in material kinds used because of the making procedure. As a potential
emerging alternative to soft lithography, direct ink writing 3D printing method (DIW) enable users to print
with multi-material in one device [5].
The ultimate vision behind this project is to establish a multimaterial 3D printing method for generating
PDMS-free lab-on-chip and organ-on-chip devices. To realize this, new 3D printing materials and methods
must be established. In particular, this study will focus on generating 3D printed, porous elastomeric
membranes based on flexible styrene-ethylene-butylene-styrene (SEBS) elastomers. And extrude based DIW
will be applied to form the needed structures. After developing permeable barriers and compartmentalized
culturing systems, following cell tests of barrier function will be done on these systems.
1.2 Aims
As stated before, we want to make some printable stretchable membranes and the whole culturing system.
This is a key component in OOC devices application, especially in barrier tissue models. So the experiments
will be focused on the following 3 aims:
(1) Developing a printable ink for porous membrane based on SEBS;
(2) Developing a fabrication procedure for fully-printed insert with porous membrane;
(3) Evaluate viability and barrier function of endothelial and intestinal tissues in fully printed inserts.

2 Background
2.1 Organ-on-chip devices
Organ-on-chip is a kind of lab-on-chip device which can culture cells on a microfluidic chip. It is quite
different from tradition 2D or 3D cell culture methods. 2D cell culture is a well-developed cell culture
approach. It is comparably easy and simple, but can lose some cell functions or gene expression because of
morphology change when culturing cells on flat surface [6]. 3D cell culture is able to use hydrogels keeping
the shape of cells, and grow organoids in vitro. Cell-cell and cell-extracellular interaction will not be omitted
in 3D culturing, which can help a lot in tissue function study and drug tests. But irregular size of organoids
and difficulty in separating cells from 3D culture media bring some limitations in 3D cell culture [7]. Lack of
mechanical force and only one cell kind also restrict the application of both traditional 2D and 3D cell culture
methods.
With
continuously
perfused
chambers,
the
microfluidic
chip
overcomes
some
of
the
limitations listed above. Actually, it has been
demonstrated as particular valuable for modelling
barrier tissues of the body. Barrier tissues such as
intestine, lung and liver, always have cell layer
with tight junctions and undertake the substance
exchange
functions.
In
OOC
devices,
porous
membrane with seeded cells are used to create
barrier tissue for further studies. For instance,
Dongeun Huh et.al. have developed a biomimetic
microsystem that can model the lung alveolus and
capillaries functions [8]. As shown in figure 1, by applying vacuum in side chambers, the epithelium cells on
porous membrane can be stretched to mimic the movement of alveolar-capillary barrier when people breathe.
And Hyun Jung Kim et. al. make a gut-on-chip device to mimic the absorptive and mechanical function of
human gut [9]. Similar as lung-on-chip designs, the elastic channels and porous membrane provide the
prerequisite for having micro-environment with stresses and strains. Others like Tae-Eun Park et. al. describe
an two-channel organ-on-chip device to model blood-brain-barrier (BBB) [10]. This help to study the
selective barrier function of BBB for drug molecules. Therefore, porous membrane is an necessary part in
OOC devices.
Besides OOC devices, transwell insert (shown as
figure 2) is a kind of static barrier device which
also seed cells on porous membranes to get barrier
tissue in vitro. Although it cannot provide forces
like shear stress and stretch as OOC devices do,
they can be used for passive transportation study.
Tissues like intestines and vessels always has a
layer of polarized tissue with one side being
exposed to air-liquid or liquid-liquid interface [12].
They can be modeled by transwell insert with
porous membranes. Cells tend to form a tight layer
on the membrane surface and have selective barrier
functions [12]. This kind of modeling is less
complex than OOC devices to design and to make,
which is good to begin with.

2.2 Porous membrane and porogens
As stated before, porous membrane is an indispensable part in OOC devices and transwell inserts. Normal
inserts with porous membrane can be easily bought from companies. For example, transwell inserts with
polyester (PET), polycarbonate (PC) and polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) porous membranes are already
commercial available. But they doesn’t fit the need of prototype design. There are also many different ways of
making porous membranes, such as electron-spinning, soft molding or particle leaching. In this project,
making membrane with particles is chosen to develop printable inks for 3D printing.
In particle leaching (figure 3a), the polymer is usually first mixed with solvent to form a solution. Then it is
mixed with some particles which is called porogens. These porogens cannot dissolve in the solvent, but can
dissolve in a non-solvent of the polymer. Then after solvent evaporation, only polymer and porogens are left.
With non-solvent soaking and washing, polymer will remain the same morphology but porogens will be
eliminated, which provide a porous structure.
Figure 3 (a) particle leaching process (b) immersion precipitation process [13]
Immersion precipitation, which is also called non-solvent induced phase separation (NIPS), is another
common method to make porous membranes. As shown in figure 3 (b), the polymer is still dissolved in
solvent. Then the solution will be cast into a membrane and soaked in a non-solvent when it is still wet. Since
the non-solvent has very high affinity with the solvent, the solvent will tend to mix with non-solvent. With the
exchange of solvent and non-solvent, pores can be created. This method can be used with particle leaching as
well. For example, adding salt particles can provide small holes on the surface and make the whole membrane
permeable [14].
In order to get ideal membrane properties, porogen selection is needed in many cases. The solid porogens
(particles) often should be inert and stable during the whole process [15]. It should be insoluble in solvent and
soluble in non-solvent. The size, dispersion and shape of the particles can directly affect the pore size and
porosity. Different sizes of salt, sugar, ice, metals or metal oxide particles all can be used as porogens and
water or acid can be used as non-solvent to remove them from the mixture.
2.3 Soft-lithography
The conventional microfluidic chip making method is soft-lithography. Figure 4 shows the procedure of that.
Silicon wafer is usually used as a base. Then with photo etching, high resolution patterns (nm to µm) [7] will be
made on a photoresist layer. After pouring PDMS on the pattern, PDMS stamps with the same surface structure
will be obtained. Different parts can finally be assembled together, often on a glass slide base, to form microfluidic
chip with multi-channels. In this process, lots of manual work is required, which limits reproducibility [3]. For
example, the PDMS stamps usually needs to be carefully peeled off from the mold by hand. Researchers
sometimes need to cut, align and stick the replicas manually, which can lead to errors. The inlets and outlets
sometimes also need to be made with needles, which introduces another uncertain factor. Figure 5 shows an
example of assembling PDMS layers to make a microfluidic device. Besides, soft-lithography can restrict the

2.4 Materials
As written before, PDMS is a common used material in soft lithography microfluidic chip making. It is a kind
of elastomer which can be easily shaped into the wanted micro- or nano- structure. It is generally non-toxic
and is used in other fields like food and daily necessities [16]. The transparence, gas-permeability and bio-
compatibility of PDMS allow it to be a general choice of microfluidic device. With real-time inspection,
people can study how specific molecules can affect cell growth and expression with time line. The elasticity
of PDMS also enables people to apply different forces and deformation on microfluidic device. Thus give the
chance to know cell responses to mechanical factors.
However, PDMS shows cell toxicity in long-term cell
culture studies. In Janko Kajtez et. al.’s study, necrosis is
observed, which can be caused by the PDMS oligomer
leaching (toxicity) and nonspecific absorption [3]. PDMS
can absorb small hydrophobic molecules into its bulk
material, which means that they cannot be released
thoroughly afterwards. Many drugs are hydrophobic
molecules, so they can be absorbed by PDMS devices
[17]. This absorption can influence the accuracy of
experiment, which needs to be avoided.
Polystyrene (PS) is a kind of natural polymers. It has been
widely used as a replacement of glass in cell culture for
many years because of its transparency and low cost[18].
Its biocompatibility has also been well-proved. Unlike
SEBS, it is a hard thermoplastic which doesn’t have
elastic properties. So it is not a good choice to mimic
stretchable porous membrane in this project.
SEBS seems to be a suitable choice of material. SEBS is a
kind of thermoplastic elastomers. It can provide elastic
properties which PS doesn’t have, and still being cheap
and biocompatible. In addition, SEBS doesn’t show such
great absorption of hydrophobic small molecules like

2.5 Direct-ink-writing (DIW) 3D printing
3D-printing method has already been mature in many fields. It is quite easy to draw the model on computer
and have machine to print that layer by layer. Compared with soft lithography, 3D printing doesn’t require
clean room and can sometimes save time for prototype design and test.
DIW is a kind of extrusion based 3D printing method. It uses an ink deposition nozzle to give out material to
form an aimed architecture [20]. The whole procedure is controlled by computer and the material can come
out by droplets or filament [20]. This method requires ink to support themselves during printing [20]. The ink
should be able to stack during printing and liquid parts need to be removed fast. Therefore the printed
structure can solidify themself and doesn’t need to have the mode first as indirect printing.
There is another extrusion based 3D printing method - fused deposition modelling (FDM). It is a traditional
method which often use thermoplastic filaments like polycaprolactone (PCL) [21]. After melting, plastic will
be printed out through nozzle and then solidify very fast under room temperature, thus giving a stable
structure.
In this project, DIW will be used because of
the
muti-material
requirement.
Figure
8
shows the differences between FDM and
DIW. DIW directly extrude material out
from a nozzle with air pressure and the ink
itself keeps its shape because of material’s
own rheological properties. While FDM
needs to melt the material during printing.
Since all ink in DIW depend on the mixture
loaded, the material will not be limited in
thermoplastic
polymers.
Many
different
materials
like
hydrogels,
muti-polymer-
solvent mixtures, solution with insoluble
particles and so on can be tried. Different
materials can be loaded at the same time and printed into one structure as well. So, DIW simplify the
procedure of prototype design and optimization in lab. In addition, sensors can be integrated into the organ-
on-chip device with DIW. By printing sensor loop in the structure, Johan U. Lind et. al. realized non-invasive
measurement of in vitro cardiac tissue contractile stresses [5]. The embedded sensor allows data tracking
during long experiment periods (such as weeks) and simplify the procedure because it is non-invasive [5].
Complex OOC designs can be realized with DIW. In return, DIW method is a bit demanding in material
properties. The ink need to have shear-thinning behavior, uniform flow during extrusion, and ability for
viscosity recovery (>80%) after deposition [22].
2.6 Plasma treatment
Membrane modification is another important part in this project. SEBS/PS surface is very hydrophobic and
cell-inert, but cell attachment and spread is better on hydrophilic surfaces [24]. Although pores can add
roughness to the surface, cells still might tend to be prevented from adhesion. As a result, surface treatment
like oxygen plasma is often employed to provide some hydrophilicity, and make the surface become cell-
active polymer surfaces (Figure 9). After oxygen plasma treatment, PDMS surface can recover very fast (in
several hours) to hydrophobic, and SEBS/PS surface can remain hydrophilic for a longer time, providing cells
with a more stable growing environment, which gives another reason to use SEBS for OOC devices [4].

2.7 Cells and bio-tests
Cells are always used for material toxicity tests. Stem cells, primary cells and cell lines are often used in cell
tests.
Stem cells can differentiate into different functional cells. This is affected by the cell growing environment.
Stem cells can be used to test the factor’s influence on cell differentiation and proliferation. Embryonic stem
cells (ESC) can be used for in-vitro embryo-toxicity assessing. But stem cells are usually hard to get,
especially ESCs, and stem cell studies are costly.
Primary cells are cells taken from donor or biopsy. They can be cultured in an environment with enough
nutrients. But after certain times of passage, their grow speed will slow down and finally stagnant [25].
Seldom primary cells can keep proliferating, which is the immortalization. These immortal cells and cancer
cells, which can keep passaging, are cell lines. Cells taken from cell lines grow faster than primary cells, and
they are easy to get with lower cost. But primary cells have high relevance and maturity. For static barrier
tissue culture on porous membrane on transwell insert, both primary cells and cell lines are enough.
Apart from doing cell viability assay to test biocompatibility, permeability assays can be done to check the
barrier functions. Dextran or fluorescent dye assay can directly show the permeability result. By adding
Dextran or dyes in the apical side and check concentration on both sides after some time, passive
transportation ability can be tested. If there is barrier function, then different concentration change will be
observed between inserts with cells and controls. Using different sizes of Dextran can also help show the pore
size of membrane of transport load of the barrier.
Transendothelial electrical resistance (TEER) measurement is another way for barrier function test. Figure 10
shows how it works. It measures the electrical resistance across the cell layer to show the ionic conductance of
paracellular pathway [26]. It can also show the membrane integrity [26].

Inks:
Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) ink is made from 30% wt PVA, 60% wt Milli-Q water and 10% wt 2-propanol.
SEBS ink is made from mixing 3g SEBS solution stock, solvent and porogens. SEBS solution stock is 1:2
SEBS:BA (w/w) or 1:2 SEBS:HA (w/w). For instance, 1:5 SEBS:BA (w/w) means using 3g SEBS:BA stock
to mix with 3g butyl acetate. Here SEBS:HA is only used is solvent precipitation method. More details will be
shown in results.
Glass ink is made from mixing 2.7g SEBS:BA (1:2 w/w), 1.2g PS:HA (2:1, w/w) and 4.5g silane treated glass
spheres.
Silane treated glass protocol:
(1) weigh 40g-50g hollow glass sphere and wash 15min in Toluene; (2) use filter paper to filter Toluene away;
(3) add Toluene and 2% v/v trichloro(hexyl)silane; (4) leave them overnight; (5) rinse the glass spheres 3
times in Toluene; (6)rinse the glass spheres 3 times in 96% ethanol; (7) dry them on heat plate (100°C) for 3h.
Membrane surface measurement
Dektak 150 (Sol Voltaics) in DTU nanolab is used for membrane surface measurement. All samples are put
on glass slides when they are still wet, and dry in room temperature overnight. Then ethanol is sprayed on the
samples for cleaning. Force is set to 1mg and resolution is set to about 0.800µm/sample. Only hill mode and a
range of 655kÅ is used for measurement. Every time when measuring a new sample, the same measurement
will be done on the glass slide of at least one side of the sample. Then the whole result will be leveled with
glass slide measurement. Only middle linear part will be taken in average thickness calculation. The measure
routine should be as near to center as possible. If there is obvious defects (bubbles or holes) that cannot be
avoid, then the part affected by the defects will not be considered in final results.
Spincoat
Spincoating is used at the very beginning of this project to make membranes. Salt, sugar and powdered sugar
are mixed with certain ratio of SEBS:BA, then be dropped on PVA coated glass slide or silicon slide. 30 to 40
second and different spin speed are chosen for spin coating. After drying for 20min, the whole slide is put into
water for 2 days. The water is changed once every day.
Micro-sized salt particles
Micro-sized salt particles are made according to the method in Kim A. Luetchford et.al.’s paper [04]. (1)
dissolve too much salt in Milli-Q water (76g in 200ml), stir overnight; (2) wait until all undissolved salt
becomes sediment at the bottom of the bottle, take out 25ml supernatant and add 1.25ml (5% v/v) Milli-Q
water; (3) mix them and pour into a 500ml glass bottle; (4) use dry ice to freeze the liquid; (5) add 500ml
absolute ethanol (-18°C) into the bottle and shake vigorously; (6) use filter paper and vacuum filtration to
remove the ethanol; (7) carefully scratch the salt into a tube; (8) put the tube in liquid nitrogen to freeze all
liquid and use the freeze dryer to dry the particles.

All structures are designed in BioCAD software (regenHU) After generating, 3D Discovery HMI is used to

Most 3D printing procedures follow figure 12. Some special ones will be shown in detail afterwards.
After mixing the materials with mixer, a spatulas or syringe (depend on the viscosity of material) is used to
load material into a cartridge. Usually glass ink is loaded with spatulas, and membrane inks are loaded with
syringe. Then a chosen type of tip will be screwed on the cartridge, and the whole structure will be put on the
printer. Before printing, pressure is applied and adjusted. The material should be extruded out continuously. In
addition, surface measurement and needle measurement are done to get suitable distance between the tip and
the printing surface. PS plates or glass slides are used as printing base, and the distance should be 75%-80%
of needle diameter.
The printing design has been changed many times during this project. Normally, a layer of PVA circle is first
printed. It is a circle with 5.6-6.6 mm radius, and been filled with vertical lines with offset and line space of
0.15 or 0.2. It is printed with 200µm plastic or metal needle, using pressure of 70-85kPa (plastic, normally
70kPa), or 110-150kPa (metal needle, normally 135kPa), and a speed of 20mm/s. Then wait 30 minutes for
PVA to be totally dried.
For membrane without solvent precipitation, the SEBS membrane is printed directly on PVA layer. After it
dries, glass ink is printed on that to be the insert wall. After reaching certain height, which is 32 to 33 layers,
radius of insert wall printing circle begin to grow. The final circle needs to have a largest diameter of 12 mm
to ensure that the insert can be hanged ion 48 well plate.
Immersion precipitation
In immersion precipitation, SEBS are dissolved in hexyl acetate and printed. Right after printing, when the
printed membrane layer is still wet, the whole PS plate or glass slide is put in 2-propanol or absolute ethanol
for 5min, then taken out and dry in fume hood. When printing in 12-well-plate, right after membrane printing,
2-propanol or absolute ethanol is added into the wells until it fills 80% of the well. After about 1h, the solvent
is taken out and the whole structure will be dried in fume hood for at least 3h.
Under all conditions, the walls are printed with 400µm plastic nozzle with a pressure of 90kPa. Then the
whole structure will be dried in fume hood overnight and then soaked in water for 2 days to dissolve release
layer. The water is changed once every day. After that the released inserts will be soaked in 2-propanol
overnight (at least 8h) to remove all solvent and dry in fume hood for at least 3h.

3.3 Permeability tests
Before tests, plasma treatment is done to all inserts without cells. Lucifer yellow CH dilithium salt (LY,
Sigma-Aldrich, L0259) is used in all permeability tests. The stock is concentrated to 2.2mM, HBSS is used as
buffer. After warming HBSS (made by Asli, protocol see part above) to 37 °C, Lucifer yellow stock is mixed
with HBSS (3:107) to get 60µM Lucifer yellow solution. All inserts are put in 48 well plates. Then different
amount of HBSS and LY solution are added in the outside and inside of transwell insert according to the
insert design. Ruler is used to keep liquid height of both sides of the membrane to be the same. For inserts
with cells, 300µL LY solution and 1mL of HBSS are used. For inserts without cells, 250µL LY solution and
650µL HBSS are added. Then whole well-plate is put on the shaking bed, speed 100. After 3h, 90µL of liquid
is taken from both sides of the membrane and plate reader is applied for fluorescent intensity reading.
Right after getting the sample, another simple test will be done to quickly see if the membrane is permeable or
not. The insert will be put on a piece of tissue, and drops of water will be added into the insert. Then the insert
will be quickly shaken up and down with a very small amplitude, or the membrane will be pressed through the
tissue with a small force. If the tissue becomes wet, then the membrane is probably porous.

4.2 Porogen choosing - size measurement
Besides membrane printing, porogens are another important component of porous membrane making. For this
part, I begin with normal salt, sugar and powdered sugar as porogens. Calcium carbonate nanoparticles
(CaCO3 NPs) and microsized salt are also tried as candidates.
All particle sizes are measured with microscope as figure 15. The salt has a diameter size ranged from about
100 to 1000µm, sugar particles range from 300 to 1200µm, and powdered sugar is about 10 to 30µm in
diameter. All of these three particles have uneven sizes and large size ranges. CaCO3 NPs has an average size
of 90±15 nm, so it cannot be seen clearly under microscope (20X is the biggest magnification). The micro-
sized salt is about 3-5µm. Powdered sugar particles, CaCO3 NPs and micro-sized salt particles are measure
after dispersed in butyl acetate. Salt and sugar crystals are not good choices because they are much bigger than
the printing nozzle. Therefore, the other three particles are tried as porogens.

4.3 Spincoated membranes
Only powdered sugar is used as porogen in spincoated porous membrane. 50% v/v powdered sugar is mixed
with 1:7 and 1:10 SEBS:BA. Figure 16 (b) shows the rough membrane surface after soaking in water for 2
days, which means powdered sugar dissolves in water. Theoretically, if there is enough porogen dispersed in
polymer solution, a channel might be able to form inside the membrane. However, it is very hard to say if
there is really any pores that connect both sides of the membrane, which makes the membrane permeable.
Furthermore, figure 16 (a) shows that spincoated membrane originally has some holes, which makes it even
harder to tell if the chosen membrane area doesn’t have any crack or damage, but only pores made from
powdered sugar dissolving. These holes might be caused by the dragging of powdered sugar. Although
powdered sugar has a similar density as butyl acetate and SEBS, it tends to aggregate, which makes some big
particles. These big particles can have larger centrifugal force because of the large weight. So they are more
likely to move out. During spincoating, these particles can move faster than polymer solution, and drag some
solution with them, which reduce the amount of polymer in a certain area. Lack of strength can finally cause
cracks and breaks.

4.4 Printed membrane with CaCO3 nanoparticles
Salt, sugar and powdered sugar, despite of easy to be got, can block the printing nozzle and shows large
variation in particle sizes. This is not good for membrane making even if the ink is printable, because it adds
variation to the finally formed pores.
As a result, CaCO3 NPs is considered and tested. I used 25% v/v and 33.3% v/v of CaCO3 NPs and SEBS for
printing, but they also tend to block the nozzle after 1-2 prints. According to literature, small particles will
have high surface energy, which can lead them to aggregate and lower the system free energy [27]. Qiming
Xu et. al. wrote about using PVP as dispersants for indium tin oxide (ITO) nanoparticles in butyl acetate [28].
However, I tried 3.5% and 7% (wt.%, related to mass of the dry nanoparticle powder) which are the best
concentration and max tested concentration that they use, both doesn’t work. The CaCO3 NPs keeps blocking
the nozzle.

Finally, I used 400µm plastic nozzle and low layer height (150µm) to print 25% (v/v) CaCO3 NPs with 1:6
SEBS:BA. This method seems more printable than using 200µm plastic/metal nozzle, but all membranes
break after soaking overnight in acetic acid as shown in Figure 17 (a). In contrast, membrane printed without
wall in Figure 17 (b) doesn’t break at all.
The break might be caused by the aggregation of CaCO3 NPs or gas release, and the effect of glass walls. Acid
dissolving is a relatively fast procedure. Once CaCO3 be in touch with acid, it begins to dissolve and release
CO2. It can be the fast disappear of aggregated CaCO3 NPs that request very few SEBS network to keep
mechanical strength. If the force on some thin SEBS filament is too high, they will break and then cause the
whole network to crash. Or the gas which squeezes out from SEBS network gives the network more stress,
and then make SEBS membrane break.
There are also other explanations for this phenomenon. When there is wall, the whole membrane is dragged,
but if there is no wall, the membrane can shrink to handle the stress. Since pure SEBS membrane doesn’t
show any change after overnight in acetic acid, it shouldn’t be the high concentration used in figure 17(a) that
cause the membranes to break. Membrane thickness also matter in this situation. In figure 17(b), the ink was
actually too diluted, so there were more material printed to form the membrane. So those membranes are
thicker than the broken ones. Maybe increasing the layer numbers or pressure can help, but I didn’t test this.

of LY molecules to pass through. And the small concentration reduction of LY can also be caused by

the membrane changes from almost not permeable to permeable.
For cell function test, we hope the cell grow and cover all the pores on the membrane, then it can show barrier
functions. 40% samples have the lowest concentration to provide a porous membrane. It is more likely to have
less big pores than 50% ones, which means that cells are more possibly to cover the pores. Therefore, 40%
(v/v) salt membrane is chosen for seeding cells.

4.6 Immersion precipitation membranes
With microsized salt, we can successfully make porous membranes. However, that method consumes lots of
salt and the pore sizes are too big. For the purpose of using less salt and making membrane with small, even
sized pores, immersion precipitation is tried.
4.6.1 The main printing procedure
Before doing the immersion precipitation, different solvents are screened for selection. But only hexyl acetate
(HA) is able to dissolve SEBS. Also still very volatile, HA evaporates slower than BA. The preferred solvent
should be as less volatile as possible to keep the printed material wet for enough time until it is put into the
non-solvent.
At beginning the same printing procedure as before is used - first print a PVA release layer, then print
membrane on it. But the adhesion between SEBS and PVA are so bad that SEBS membrane float up from
PVA layer right after soaking it into either pure ethanol or 2-propanol. in addition, PVA can slightly dissolve
in pure ethanol. After only 5min soaking in pure ethanol, the edges dissolve as shown in figure 21 (a). So the
unused PVA layer will be destroyed.
Then a new printing procedure is designed as figure 21 (b). To make it easy for soaking wet printed
membrane into non-solvent, the whole printing procedure is done in a 12-well-plate. And the membrane is
design to be the last printing step, so the non-solvent can be added right after the print.
Figure 21 (a) PVA layer dissolves in pure ethanol (b) one printing procedure designed for immersion
precipitation. All printing in this procedure is done inside a 12-well-plate.
First still a PVA layer is printed. Then a circle is printed with arc line from inside to outside as shown in
figure 22 (a) then about 10 layers of walls are printed on the frame after it dries. When the wall totally dries
(normally overnight), long metal nozzles are used to print the membrane inside the wall. The membrane size
is designed to be 3.8mm in diameter so that it will have 1mm overlap with the frame. But this printing method
is not very manageable because it is hard to see how the printing goes inside well plate, and it is hard to see
how the membrane print goes inside the walls.
Some membranes are got with this method, but most of them breaks near the overlap part between the
membrane and the frame. This might be caused by the forces that holds the membrane during precipitation.
When the solvent goes out rapidly, the membrane might tend to pull back. So the overlap connecting part
bears much stress and becomes likely to break. Another reason for this might be the quick evaporation of HA.
The whole procedure of membrane printing takes about 1 min. The material used in printing is not very less,
and it will dry during printing. Since the membrane is filled in with filaments as shown in figure 22 (b), the
different drying degree at different parts of the overlap might cause different adhesion levels. Places that dry
earlier can adhere better with the frame. At the point with different dry levels on both sides, it is more likely to
break because the dryer part bears more force. Besides, the finishing point on the right can be another

Then the difference between dry level can cause problems.

4.7 Insert wall designs
Insert wall structure design has been changed and optimized many times throughout this project. The basic
design is drawing circles layer by layer to form an insert which fits 48 well plate.
The transwell insert can be divided into two parts: the straight part and the upper part. The straight part is used
to hold apical liquid needed in cell culture and following experiments. The upper part is used for hanging the
insert in 48 well plate. The straight part is composed of layers with the same design, and the hanging part is
composed of layers with increasing size. Figure 28 shows the two different parts.
The whole insert is designed to have a height of about
12.6mm inside the well (about 3mm above the well
bottom) and about 2mm hanging on the well plate. The
biggest diameter of the final layer should be larger than
the well diameter top of 48 well plate, which is 12mm.
But in real experiments, the designed diameter will need
to be about 13.6mm to have the function of hanging
because of the material shrinkage during drying. This
characteristic is caused by a high solvent content (26%)
in the glass ink. and it affects a lot in the wall design
and the printing settings. All designs need to take this into consideration otherwise there will be some
deviations.
The straight part is usually 9-10mm high. But the exact height depends on how many layers is needed for the
hanging part. For 250µm plastic nozzle, each layer will grow 0.1mm in radius than the previous one, and for
400µm plastic nozzle, each layer will grow 0.15mm in radius. The radius increasing speed decides the size of
first layer and the layer height of hanging part. It should be smaller than half of the nozzle size, so every layer
can be stably supported by the previous layer. If the radius grow with half of the nozzle size, the actual
support area will be less than that, which means sometimes the printed line might drop down, because the
small contacting area cannot provide enough support.
There are mainly 3 kinds of defects in the wall printing. Figure 29 shows examples of them. The holes on the
wall is usually caused by small bubbles inside the ink. The glass ink is very viscous - almost solid like. When
loading it with spatus, it takes more than one time to put the material inside the cartridge. As a result, it is very
hard to say that there is no air bubbles inside the material. And since it is very viscous, air bubbles cannot
come out themselves, and when pushing the material to the cartridge outlet, it cannot fill all the space. There
will be some space near the cartridge outlet which can also create small air bubbles during printing. Once
there is a lack of material in the nozzle, a hole will appear on the route. Although the glass material can fall
down to fill this gap, it will always stay hanged until dry or doesn’t stick tightly with the layer under the gap.
Then a hole on the wall is created. This problem sometimes can be solved by printing two layers or by
sticking it afterwards.

attach the other side of the gap. This can help hanging but still leave big holes on the wall.

change the shape of the insert. Another origin of shape change is the weight. Though the printed material can
support themselves and the upper layers, they are not rigid enough during the printing process. If too much
layers are stacked together and the material cannot afford such weight, it will change shape. Figure 29 (d) is
more or less caused by this reason.

3.21mm. But 10% of the estimated shrinkage height will be subtract. So the actual set initial printing height of
the 11th layer would be 3.18mm.

5 Discussion
5.1 Two different membrane making methods
In this project, porous membrane is made with two different methods - porogen leaching and immersion
precipitation. Porogen leaching use lab-made microsized salt particles to successfully make porous membrane.
But the pore size are very big - some can even be as big as about 100µm. CaCO3 nanoparticles and powdered
sugar are gave up because of aggregation, but at that time I didn’t realize that ultrasound can help dispersion.
Powdered sugar doesn’t have an even size so is not considered, but CaCO3 NPs might provide a printable ink
after ultrasound treatment. Although calcium carbonate particles have a higher density than salt (2.9 g/cm3 ,
salt density is 2.1g/cm3), it has a size which is much smaller than salt particles. So even nano-sized particles
are more likely to aggregate, it might still be able to provide a porous membrane with smaller pores.
The immersion precipitation needs very less salt particles. According to Kim A Leutchford, any amount of
salt particles can result in porous membranes [14]. In the paper 40% (w/w) is reported to be the best ratio [14].
It is converted into 15.6% (v/v) with calculation. N-Methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) is used as solvent and 1:4
PS:NMP is used as material mixture [14]. Water is applied as non-solvent [14]. The microsized salt particles
they use have an average diameter of 4.7 ± 1.3 µm, which is similar as what I get [14].
Comparing the SEM images got in this project (figure 27) with those in the literature (figure 38), obviously
there is difference between the inside structures. In the paper they got finger-like internal structure, but in this
project I only get sponge-like internal structure. As written before, this might be caused by the lack of affinity
between non-solvent and solvent. In that paper they also get more dense surface holes as shown in figure 38
(l). With almost the same salt concentration and particle size, the reason for my getting less surface pores
might be the sedimentation as written before.
Anyway, SEM image tells that immersion precipitation can be a promising method for making membrane
with less salt. The decrease of required particle amount can make printing much easier. Usually it is the
particles that add viscosity to the mixture which arise demand for printing pressure. Moreover, less particles
means less likelihood to aggregate, because their possibility to collide decrease with concentration. Then
smaller pores are much easier to be realized. In porogen leaching, it is very hard to reduce the pore size by
dispersing salt particles. In order to get enough pores that can form a internal network connecting both sides
of the membrane, more particles than ‘just right’ will be used. In this case, big hole are not avoidable. So
immersion precipitation might help, yet the details for a feasible ink still need more study and optimization.

5.2 Factors that can affect printing quality
Lots of effort in this project is put in the wall design and many factors can affect the printing. For example,
the capillary rise effect can cause material accumulation at the tip of the nozzle [29]. This phenomenon will be
triggered under two situations. One is the delay between printing stop and pressure ‘off’. This means the
pressure is ‘off’ a bit later than the printing stop time, so that the material is still extruded during that period.
These extra material will be pushed up and stick to the nozzle tip if the tip touch anything else - for instance,
==PAGE:43==
35
print the next target. Another reason is the affinity between the tip and the material. If the affinity is high, the
material is likely to be built up at the tip of the nozzle [29]. Low viscosity of material will add to this effect
[29]. The building up problem in this project is mainly due to the first reason. And sometimes it is not the
extra extrusion, but the drag of material by nozzle at the end of the routine. This problem is solved by the final
two layer design.
The printing speed and printing height can also affect how the printed filament look like. Figure 39 and figure
40 shows how they matter. The height problem in this project is solved by adjusting initial printing height
every 10 layers as stated before. The speed is adjusted to 5mm/s instead of 7.5mm/s in the final printing to
provide continuous filament.

5.3 Permeability tests for membranes and cells
Comparing the LY permeability test results of membrane and membrane with cells, a disappointing
conclusion can be draw - the cells doesn’t show barrier function in the experiment. The one ‘less porous’
sample which seem to be partly covered by cells is a special case which is not convincing. Though stain image
tells that cell cover some ‘pore like’ areas, none similar sample without cell is tested for comparison. The high
LY rejection result is not enough to show barrier function.
Apart from that special case, all porous membranes with cells do not show much difference in LY rejection.
The LY rejection of Caco-2 monolayer in other study shows a LY rejection of 75.52% [31]. They use a
trtanswell insert with 0.4µm pores [31]. But in table 4, the LY rejection of porous membrane seeded with cells
are lower than 70%. Maybe culturing longer time can help but the time is limited in this project.
In all permeability tests, only LY is used as the transported molecule. It is a relatively small molecule which
cannot reveal the pore sizes. If Dextran is used, maybe pore size difference between 40% and 50% salt (v/v)
samples can be detected.

6 Conclusions
This project begin with porogen choosing aiming at developing printable ink for SEBS based porous
membrane. Microsized salt particles are found to be an available porogen and 40% salt (v/v) or higher is
found to be a good ratio for making porous membrane. Permeability test is done to see how porogen
concentration can affect porosity. Membrane made with higher salt content shows lower LY rejection, which
means higher porosity. SEM imaging is done to show the exact structure of porous membrane. Then different
wall designs are tested for a fully printed transwell insert. A double layer structure is finally proved to be
acceptable. Cells tests are done on the combination of these two parts - transwell inserts with porous
membrane made by 40% salt (v/v). Cell viability test shows a good bio-compatibility of SEBS membrane.
Cell function tests doesn’t show barrier functions. This maybe due to the short culture period, or the big size
or pores on the membrane which cells cannot cover.
Besides porogen leaching, immersion precipitation method with porogens is tested. It shows a possibility to
make membrane with less porogen and provide smaller pores. Although different tries including membrane
size, printing order change and release layer material change all fail in some aspect, this can be a good
direction to go. SEM images show the porous structure inside the membrane and a few holes on the surface,
which indicates this method is probably feasible but need optimization.
If we can stably produce fully printed transwell insert, biosensor and be integrated for barrier measurement. In
addition, the printable porous membrane ink and procedure might can be applied in OOC devices. A fully
printable OOC device simplify the procedure for prototype design and adjust in lab. DIW 3D can easily make
the device with different kinds of materials at one time.
