Abstract
Cooling of buildings and the increase of their thermal mass by using phase change ma­
terials is one of the latest widespread ways to achieve thermal comfort conditions while
minimizing the use of conventional energy. This project investigated the effect of different
conditions on a macro­encapsulated PCM panel (MEP)installed in an office room, through
experimental and simulation processes. Ventilation methods, increase of heat gains and
different positioning of the internal heat gains were tested. The increase of heat gains
resulted in 31% within the Category II, according to EN 16978. The heat flux to the pan­
els reached 15.7 W/m².The experiment of different positions of the internal heat gains
showed a negligible effect on the operative temperature and mean heat flux up to 16.37
W/m² during occupancy, however moving the internal heat gains from the centre of the
room to the side, had as a result longer discharge time by more than 4 hours. A compar­
ison between mixed(MV) and displacement ventilation (DV) showed small differences in
the time within Category II during the occupancy. The panels absorbed more heat in the
MV experiment with a total of 202.3Wh/m², than in the DV where it reached 175.4 Wh/m².
However, the heat extracted by the ventilation and the water circulation system was sim­
ilar. Displacement ventilation scenarios, recorded vertical temperature differences up to
3.5K, which within category III, according to EN 16978.The PCM model was created, us­
ing TRNSYS 18 in order to be validated compared to experimental results, according to
the VDI 6020 standard. The heat flux of selected panels and the operative temperature,
were within the limits given, with RMSE of 0.2kW and 0.17 °C.The validated model was
used for the investigation of different window­to­wall ratio. The ratio of 30% had more oc­
cupied time within Category II, according to EN­16798, while the 60% and the 90% ratio
provided extremely high temperatures up to 35.9 °C.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been an increase in the interest in both climate change and the
increasing energy demands of various human activities. In particular, due to the constant
use of hydrocarbons as fuel, the human factor is now considered to be the main cause
of climate change, due to the ever­increasing CO2 emissions in the atmosphere. On the
basis of this observation, the scientific community is now pointing out that a reduction in
CO2 emissions is urgent and necessary, at a time when reserves of fuels such as oil and
gas are becoming increasingly limited [1].
The interest is even greater in the field of buildings, as buildings have high energy require­
ments and therefore the effort to save primary energy is necessary. Humans spend more
than 90% of their everyday lives inside buildings. Economic and technological develop­
ment has resulted in a multiplication of energy needs. In particular, with the constant
improvement in living standards, energy consumption for the operation of buildings is
constantly increasing. According to an analysis conducted by the International Energy
Agency (IEA) concerning energy certification of buildings, it was found that the building
industry uses over 40% of all primary energy [2].
Furthermore, the design of buildings aims at designing buildings with a low or even zero
energy balance. Zero­energy buildings combine energy efficiency with renewable energy
generation to consume only as much energy as can be generated on­site from renewable
resources during a given time period [3]. Thus, in recent years there is a new tendency that
seeks to achieve thermal comfort conditions while minimizing the use of conventional en­
ergy, which produce CO2, by using phase change materials (PCM) in buildings. Through
latent heat, PCMs can store much more thermal energy compared to other conventional
materials that rely only on sensible heat and as a result, they can add much more thermal
mass in proportion to their volume.
In this thesis the performance of PCM­based radiant ceiling panels was investigated. Ex­
periments in office environment were conducted to determine the effects of varying venti­
lation methods, different positioning of internal heat gains and the increase of heat gains
on the panel’s performance. Additionally, a TRNSYS model of the macroencapsulated
PCM (MEP) panel was developed and validated. The validated model, was later used
for the investigation of the effects of the different window­to­wall ratio of an office, to the
indoor thermal environment and the cooling performance.

1.1
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to study the effects of an installation of radiant ceiling panels
consisting of phase change materials (PCM) for the cooling of a renovated office room,
with or without ventilation. Additionally, the impact of increasing the heat gains and the
changing the position of internal heat gains is tested.
This study consists of two parts, namely the experimental and the simulation parts. The
experimental part will assess the indoor thermal environment, with the target of remaining
inside the Category II according to EN­16798 [4], during changes in heat gains load and
different ventilation methods.The heat flux from the office room to the panels , the ver­
tical temperature differences and the heat extracted from the water circulation, and the
ventilation system will be analysed.
The simulation part will focus on the validation of the PCM model created in TRNSYS 18
and the analysis of different wall­to­window ratios in the cooling of an office.

2.1.1
Thermal Environment Assessment
Requirements for the thermal environment may be stated as overall thermal comfort index
values Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) and Predicted Percent Dissatisfied (PPD) or as a
range of values between minimum and maximum operative temperature [6]. The majority
of standards combine both criteria, whereas some employ only one. In addition, local
discomfort is taken into account for a comprehensive description of the indoor thermal
environment [6].
Predicted Mean Vote
Predicted Mean Vote or PMV [7], is one of the most widely used indices in moderate
thermal environments. It predicts the mean value of the votes of a group of people on a
scale based on their overall thermal sensation. The scale divides the thermal sensation
into 7 points, ranging from ­3.0, which represents a cold environment, to +3.0, which
indicates a hot environment, as it can be seen in Figure 2.1.

Cooling of Buildings
During warm weather or when there are considerable thermal gains (solar gain, occu­
pants, equipment), it may be important to provide cooling for buildings. Cooling can be
divided into active and passive methods. Active cooling methods are described as the
use of mechanical equipment to meet the cooling demands of a structure without being
provided by nature. Passive cooling refers to technology or design features intended to
cool buildings without or with low energy use. Whenever energy consumption occurs,
it is negligible compared to that of active methods. [10]. As this thesis examines office
buildings, some of the most common cooling solutions will be described below.
3.1
Ventilation
Ventilative cooling depends on convection­based thermal energy exchange by providing
cold air to indoor spaces through active or passive strategies. Passive cooling can be
provided by natural ventilation, while active ventilation is provided with the aid of me­
chanical equipment. Currently, there are eight commonly applied ventilation strategies in
buildings. These strategies are mixing ventilation, displacement ventilation, personalized
ventilation, hybrid air distribution, stratum ventilation, protected occupied zone ventilation,
local exhaust ventilation, and piston ventilation [11].
3.1.1
Mixing Ventilation
The purpose of mixing ventilation is to dilute warm, polluted air from the occupied zone
with clean, cooler supply air. The goal is to attain a uniform temperature and distribution
of contaminants in the room [11]. In mixed ventilation, the air is supplied with a relatively
high velocity outside the occupied zone, usually from the ceiling or the wall (see Figure
3.1). Mixed ventilation is mostly unaffected by outside influences and can be used for
both heating and cooling needs.
Figure 3.1: Mixed Ventilation [12].
3.1.2
Displacement Ventilation
Displacement ventilation relies on the displacement of contaminated room air with fresh
air, which is provided at or near the floor at low velocity. As a result, the air rises due to
momentum and buoyancy forces [11]. Due to this stratification of the air, displacement
ventilation has a higher ventilation efficiency than mixed ventilation.

3.2
Radiant Cooling
Radiant cooling system refers to a system that absorbs heat from the conditioned area and
the surrounding environment through radiation. There are many subcategories of radiant
cooling, including radiant ceiling panels, embedded surface systems, and thermally active
building systems [11], [13].
3.2.1
Radiant ceiling panels
Radiant ceiling panels (RCP) refer to panels that are cooled with circulated water, with the
heat exchange taking place through or on top of the panels. RCP are suspended from the
ceiling, creating a plenum between the panels and the ceiling. The construction is usually
made from aluminium panels, connected to copper pipes with a heat­conducting profile,
in order to increase the heat conductivity between the pipes and panel [14].
3.2.2
TABS
Thermally Active Building Systems (TABS) is a water­based radiant system that utilize
the thermal mass of the building. In cooling applications, heat is removed from the indoor
space by TABS surface. Cooled water circulates through the pipes that are embedded in
the main concrete structure, which is exposed to the indoor space [15].
3.2.3
Embedded Surface Systems
Similarly to the TABS, embedded surface systems have pipes embedded in the screed,
but with no coupling with the main building structure. It is beneficial for refurbished build­
ings due to less work on the structure [16].

Thermal Energy Storage
Energy storage is a significant aspect of reducing and regulating energy use. Thermal
storage systems play a crucial part in several energy systems, including buildings, solar
heating, and waste heat applications, where they can enhance the performance of the
systems and is necessary for energy conservation [5].
In buildings, heat is absorbed in the structural materials during the day, when the heat
load is high and is released later. A structure with high thermal mass has a more stable
indoor thermal environment, which is less impacted by sudden variations in heat loads.
By reducing the building’s maximum temperature and raising the minimum temperature,
heat storage decreases the daytime temperature variations of the building, a phenomenon
known as the peak shaving effect [17]. The advantages include the reduction of energy
use, due to the decrease of cooling loads, better cooling system performance while func­
tioning in the nocturnal period, and the possibility of a downgrade of the existing cooling
system. From an economic perspective, it is financially beneficial for consumers to shift
their energy use from day to night, because energy prices are lower at night [18].
There are three methods of thermal energy storage (TES): Sensible, latent and chemical
storage. A schematic representation of these methods is depicted in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: TES methods: (a) Sensible; (b) Latent; (c) Thermochemical [19].
4.1
Sensible Heat Storage
Sensible heat storage (SHS) is the most common method of thermal energy storage.
Sensible heat appears when the heat that is applied and stored to an object changes its
temperature [19]. Similarly, when heat is removed from an object and its temperature
drops, the heat released is also called sensible heat. The amount of energy stored can
be quantified with Equation 4.1:
Q = m · cp · (Tf −Ti)

5
Introduction to Phase change materials
PCMs use the latent heat of phase change to regulate temperatures within a predeter­
mined range, which means that they work mainly as latent heat storage materials. The
phase change procedure can be categorized into four stages: solid­solid, solid­liquid,
gas­solid, and gas­liquid [20]. For practical purposes, only the solid­liquid variety can be
used for building cooling or heating because the other varieties have technical limitations.
5.1
Operating Principle of PCM
Figure 5.1: Solid­liquid PCM operating principle [22].
In Figure 5.1 the solid­liquid operating principle is depicted. While PCM is solid in a heating
process, the temperature exceeds a specific point, the chemical bonds in the material will
begin to break down and the material will absorb the heat through an endothermic process
in which it transitions from solid to liquid. The reverse process applies when there is a
drop in temperature and a change in the phase of the material from liquid to solid [23].
Energy and Indoor Environmental Quality Performance of Radiant Ceiling Panels with Phase Change
Materials
11
==PAGE:22==
Figure 5.2: Time evolution of the temperature [24].
Figure 5.3: Time evolution of the heat flux [24].
According to Kuznik [24], three stages may be identified in the phase transition process
of PCM while cooling. The PCM releases sensible heat (hS) during stage 1, which results
in a decrease in temperature until the freezing point (TF ).The conversion from liquid to
solid marks the transition to stage 2, while at a constant temperature, the material re­
leases the stored latent heat. The material continues to release sensible heat (hS) until
its temperature and surrounding temperature are equalized.
5.2
PCM Classification
In general, PCMs are divided into three categories, organic, inorganic and eutectic, ac­
cording to Figure 5.4.

5.2.1
Organic
Organic PCMs are subdivided into paraffins and non­paraffins.
They are resistant to
phase segregation and crystallize with minimal or no supercooling. Paraffins are available
in a wide temperature range and exhibit a low vapour pressure, but they are not compat­
ible with plastic containers. Non­paraffins (fatty acids and their esters, alcohols, glycols
etc.) are used broadly in buildings. However, both paraffins and non­paraffins have low
thermal conductivity levels (around 0.2 W/(m∙K) and 0.15­0.17 W/(m∙K) respectively). Ad­
ditionally, the fact that many organic PCMs are flammable, and that their burning products
are toxic, is a serious disadvantage that has a significant impact on the safety, when used
in construction applications [23].
5.2.2
Inorganic
Salt hydrates, salt solutions and metallics are commonly used as inorganic PCMs. Com­
pared to organic PCMs, they present relatively high heat storage density, due to the high
latent heat of fusion per volume, higher thermal conductivity, lower cost, and flammabil­
ity. Their disadvantages, which include super cooling phase segregation, lack of thermal
stability, corrosion, and decomposition, outweigh their advantages [23], [25].
5.2.3
Eutectic
Eutectic PCMs are a blend of organic and/or inorganic PCMs and act as a single com­
ponent [25]. Hence, eutectic PCMs can be made as either organic–organic, inorganic–
inorganic or organic–inorganic mixtures. Benefits of eutectic mixes include the possibility
to achieve more desirable qualities, such as a specified melting point or a greater heat
storage capacity per unit volume [23], as seen in Figure 5.5.

Gawron ([9], [23]), can be seen below:
• High specific heat and heat of fusion per unit volume and weight.

• macro­encapsulation
• micro­encapsulation

5.4.1
Impregnation
Impregnation is the oldest containment method, in which PCM is directly added to a porous
material like concrete or gypsum. However, the major drawback of this method is that
during the phase change transition, when PCM turns to liquid, it results in a continuous and
slow leakage. Additionally, the containing material shows reduced mechanical strength
properties [24], [25].
5.4.2
Macro­encapsulation
Macro­encapsulation is the process of enclosing the PCM into different capsules, that act
as a shell to prevent leakage, with a size in the range of 1­10 cm. The capsule can be
directly incorporated into the building materials. The disadvantage of this process is that,
occasionally, the PCM does not transition completely its phase and thus not all of the
latent heat storage potential is used [17], [25].
5.4.3
Micro­encapsulation
Microencapsulation is the second way of encapsulating the PCM and also the one with
the higher cost. The PCM is sealed into a thin layer capsule, ranging between 1 −100
μm, and can be added directly to the host material without risk of leakage [5].

Validation of Numerical PCM Model
This chapter describes the methodology followed to validate the simulation model against
experimental results.
9.1
TRNSYS 18
TRNSYS18 is a modelling software used to simulate the behaviour of transient systems.
It was used in order to create the model of chamber 6 from the experimental part [34].
TRNSYS is a multifunctional program that is commonly used in the fields of building sim­
ulation and renewable energy engineering. It consists of the kernel and the library. The
kernel is the core of the program from which the computational procedures are derived.
The library is a list of so­called Types, which represent components of the system. The
library contains a wide range of components such as weather archives, multi­zone build­
ings, heat pumps, etc.
9.2
Validation Methodology
To allow direct comparison with the experimental study, an identical construction model,
to chamber 6, was created. The model contains the chilled PCM ceiling as a module,
called Type 399.
9.3
Simulation Model
An identical to the construction of chamber 6 and its characteristics model was created
(see Fig. 6.1 and Table 6.1). The model consisted of modules that replicate the building
(Type 56) and the PCM panels (Type 399). All the characteristics of Type 56 module,
can be seen in Appendix B. The IHG loads of the experiment IncHG (Table 7.1) were
implemented as a module, with the same schedule (working hours 08:00­18:00).
9.4
Water Discharge

temperature setpoint of 21 °C, which were the same control as the measurements. The
water circulation was scheduled to activate after 18:00, when the operative temperature
was above 21 °C. The system was turned off when the operative temperature fell under
21 °C or at 08:00 (Figure 9.1).

9.6
Type 399
The PCM panel was simulated with Type 399, a component developed by Dentel [35].
It represents a wall construction, in this case, the ceiling, that hosts the PCM layer and
can simulate the passive and active behavior of PCM with water pipes. Type 399 uses a
Crank­Nicolson algorithm for one­dimensional heat transfer and an elimination approach
to solve the heat conduction equation. The hysteresis of the PCM can be considered, as
it uses two different curves (one for solidifying when cooling and one for melting when
heating), but the super­cooling effect is not taken into consideration.
Koschenz et al. [36] established a specialized approach for the computation of thermo­
active construction element systems that are incorporated in Type 399 and Type 56. The
modelling utilizes a resistance network approach [35].
Figure 9.3: Structure of the thermo active construction element system [35].
The PCM ceiling is represented as TABS with PCM, which requires some specific dimen­
sional constraints to be met in order for the simulation to run. Therefore, according to
Yasin [37], it is necessary to determine a thermally equivalent cross­section that would

12
Discussion
In the previous chapters, different experiments and simulations were conducted in order
to analyze the effects of different parameters and scenarios on the cooling process of a
room. In this chapter, the results for each experiment and the simulation will be discussed.
12.1
Experiments
12.1.1
Increased Heat Gains
The experiment showed that increasing the heat gains had a major influence on the
room temperature as well as the performance of the panels. It was shown that with in­
creased thermal loads,the indoor thermal environment could not be maintained within
Category II of EN 16798 [4].This means that the panels could not shift the heat load to
off­peak hours .The operative temperature increased above 26 ºC in a short period of 4
hours.Implementation of ventilation be used in order to solve that issue.
With higher internal heat gains, the heat flux to the panels reached up to 15.7 W/m². After
the occupancy, in the IncHG scenario, it was observed that panels were releasing heat to
the room.With the absence of the occupants, while the panels where charged due to the
high heat gains, they started releasing heat to the room.
12.1.2
Different Positions of Internal Heat Gains
Changing the position of the heat gains, moving them away from the centre of the room to
distribute the loads across the room panels,did not have any improvement to the thermal
environment according to the EN­16798 [4]. However,that change had as a result a longer
discharge time in the operating time of the water circulation system by 4.4 hours and
higher heat extraction from the water circulation 4.7 W/m² for the Position scenario . The
operative temperature after the occupancy, was higher compared to the IncHG scenario,
while the surface temperatures of the panel were also higher for that period. Calculating
the external heat load would provide a more clear image for the results, as the registered
lab temperatures were not the same for experiments.
12.1.3
Ventilation
Displacement ventilation exhibited slightly lower temperatures compared to mixed ventila­
tion, while IncHG had significantly higher temperatures. The implementation of ventilation,
while IHG were present, had a positive impact on the thermal environment of the room.
The panel in mixed ventilation had higher total energy absorption than in displacement
ventilation.The mean heat flux from the room was also higher for the mixed ventilation
(11.81 W/m²), than in displacement ventilation (9.66 W/m²).The case with no ventilation
used had greater heat absorption, because in the mixed ventilation and the displacement
ventilation cases, heat was also extracted by the ventilation system.
Displacement ventilation and mixed ventilation cases showed similar heat extraction from
the water circulation system as well as little difference in the heat extracted by the ven­
tilation system. Despite heat extraction from the water circulation system was higher at
the end of the occupancy for the mixed ventilation experiment at 115 W/m² compared to
69.5 W/m², the discharge period lasted shorter than the displacement. The panels in the
MVWC scenario were discharged in 6.6 hours, while in DVWC in 7.2 hours.
It also must be taken into account that displacement ventilation had a notable difference
in vertical temperatures, with greater temperature differences than Mixed ventilation and

IncHG scenario (no ventilation) due to stratification, which resulted in thermal comfort
between categories II and III according to EN16798 [4].
On the other way, ventilation only scenarios proved also that they could not to maintain
the indoor thermal environment within the limits of Category II [4].The implementation of
water circulation system can be provided as a solution.
The increase of the ventilation temperature showed undesirable effects.By increasing
ventilation temperature by 2K, the operative temperature reached almost 30 ºC.In these
scenarios, the measured heat flux values to the panels during the occupancy were the
lowest among all scenarios, between 5­5.5 W/m².
12.2
Validation
The validation needs further research. PCMs are considered by the TRNSYS as a single
surface area. Given that the experiments and the validation process have shown that
in a room the heat loads are not uniformly distributed,it was proved that the use of the
average of the heat flux values measured did not have the desired results. A combination
of different measures solved the problem , but the issue should be invastigated.
12.3
Simulation
The simulation tested the different cases of window size in proportion to the wall. The
results showed that the smaller the window the better the thermal comfort and the shorter
the time for water circulation for the discharge of the panels since they had received less
solar radiation.However, the heat flux to the panels from the room registered values be­
tween 4 W/m²­6.2 W/m², lower than the comparable experimental scenarios of MVWC
and DVWC, which had 11.8 W/m² and 9.6 W/m². respectively. The model with the small­
est window­to­wall ratio was out of class II mainly due to temperatures lower than 23
degrees, rather than overheating; the other models had significant issues with room over­
heating. Quantification of the solar gains would help. Possible solutions, in cases of large
windows, could be the installation of window shading or different window glazing, with a
view to reducing solar radiation.
12.4
Future Work
As only the study for the cooling season was investigated, future work could possibly
be broadening the investigation for a whole year, to analyse how would the performance
of the panels and the thermal environment of the room be affected during the heating
season. The optimization of the water circulation schedule according to the heat gains
would be beneficial.
Αnother aspect that can be studied is the construction and design of panels with different
phase change materials and the investigation of the effect of different amount of PCM in
the panels, to the indoor thermal environment.
Additionally, the comparison with similar software to TRNSYS can lead to an improve­
ment in that issue. The feasibility study compared with other cooling methods would be
beneficial.

Conclusion
Various scenarios were tested in order to study the effects of different ventilation methods
and positioning of the internal heat gains in the indoor thermal environment of an office
room according to EN­16798. Additionally, a parametric analysis of the window­to­wall
ratio was simulated on TRNSYS for a similar office, with a validated model. For both
experiments and simulation, the indoor thermal environment was evaluated. The cooling
power of a radiant ceiling panel containing PCM, the heat extracted from the water, and
the air system were also investigated.
Increased heat gains have negative results on the thermal comfort of the room. According
to EN16798, the indoor thermal environment of NHG was within category II for 88% of the
occupancy time. In IncHG the percentage fell to 32%. The maximum operating temper­
ature that appeared in IncHG was 28.6 °C, while in NHG 25.6°C. The average heat flux
from the room to the panels determined the cooling power of the panel at 15.69 W/m² in
IncHG and 9.53 W/m² in NHG.
The change of the position of the IHG resulted in an increase in the discharge time of the
panels. The placement of the IHG in the center of the room had as a result 9.6 hours of wa­
ter circulation with a mean heat system extraction from the water of 21 W/m². The change
of the position of the IHG around the walls resulted in a continuous circulation of 14 hours
with a mean heat extraction from the circulation system of 25.7 W/m² The comparison
between the two scenarios showed similar results in the indoor thermal environment.
The comparison between mixed ventilation and displacement ventilation resulted in sim­
ilar operative temperature trends during occupancy. Mixed ventilation had a maximum
temperature of 25.8°C and 92% within Category II, while displacement ventilation had
25.3 °C and 87% within Category II. The mean cooling power of the panel during occu­
pancy was 11.81 W/m² for MC and 9.66 W/m². Displacement ventilation provided higher
vertical temperature differences between the head and ankles with a maximum between
1.9 ­ 3.5K. The absorbed heat from the water and the air system provided small differ­
ences.
The combination of water circulation and ventilation had a significant impact on improving
the thermal comfort of the occupants and the absorption of the heat from the panels.
Increasing the ventilation temperature and decreasing the airflow had a negative effect
on the operative temperatures of the room, which registered up to 30 °C.
The simulation created in TRNSYS was validated according to the standard VDI 6020.
For the validation, the operative temperature and the cooling power of the panel were in
agreement between the simulation and the experimental results. A combination of the
measurements of two different panels was used, to mimic the process that the simulation
algorithm uses.
A parametric analysis of the window­to­wall ratio of an office was simulated using the
validated model. The average heat flux to the panel during the occupancy was 4W/m² for
the 30% ratio , 5W/m² for the 60% and 6.2W/m² for the 90% .
