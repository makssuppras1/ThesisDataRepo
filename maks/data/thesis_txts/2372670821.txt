Abstract
This thesis explores the dynamics of Fano lasers using laser theory and numerical
simulations.
The Fano laser is presented as a line-defect waveguide coupled to a
nearby point-defect waveguide in a 2D photonic crystal membrane, with embedded
quantum dot active material. The right mirror is realised through Fano interference
of the waveguide modes and the nanocavity modes. Such a structure has been stud-
ied extensively, so a review of the known mirror properties and threshold solutions
is presented, as well as derivations, descriptions and analysis of a dynamical model
based on ordinary diﬀerential equations and an iterative travelling-wave model, and
their applicability and limitations. These models show qualitative agreement, but
with some quantitative deviations due to the higher accuracy of the iterative model.
The ODE model is used to study a system with active material in the nanocavity,
which leads to self-starting pulse generation, likened to passive Q-switching with the
nanocavity functioning as a semiconductor saturable absorber mirror. A linearised
stability analysis shows how the main source of this instability is relaxation oscil-
lations becoming un-damped, as well as demonstrating how a region of bi-stability
exists, in which both continuous-wave (CW) and self-pulsing (SP) modes may be
found. This dual region is explored extensively through numerical simulations and
elements of non-linear systems theory, investigating the nature of the solution space,
as well as demonstrating how phase transitions from CW to SP may be induced by
dynamical detuning of the nanocavity resonance. It is shown that the likelihood of
phase transitions depends upon the rate of modulation of the resonance frequency,
and that some unusual power traces can be generated by suitable variation of the
temporal detuning.
A system with no linear absorption of the laser ﬁeld in the nanocavity is also stud-
ied extensively with a view toward pulse generation for applications in integrated
photonics, such as on-chip signal processing. In particular, two schemes of operation
are identiﬁed, which are analogous to the well-known cavity dumping and active Q-
switching pulse generation schemes, and these are analysed using the iterative model.
From this study, an analytical model of cavity dumping is formulated, which agrees
well with simulations for fast switching of the reﬂectivity. A suggestion is then given
for optical modulation control of the cavity dumping scheme, and the dynamics and
pulse characteristics of this are characterised using a rate equation approach. These
ﬁndings suggest that systems with a large value of the linewidth enhancement factor
may be useful for pulse compression, showing eﬃcient compression down to few-ps
pulses, whereas high-Q systems may be used to obtain transistor-like functionality,
where a high-energy output pulse is triggered by a low-energy control pulse.
An active Q-switching mechanism is demonstrated, leading to output pulses with un-
precedented characteristics, such as sub-picosecond pulsewidths and peak powers in
the hundreds of milliwatts. These exceptional numbers are explained as consequences
of gain and carrier density approximations that are unlikely to hold in practice, but
the simulations still indicate strongly that this conﬁguration has potential for micro-
scale generation of ultra-short, high-energy pulses. This suggests that this scheme
may be well-suited for transistor-like functionality and as a short-pulse source in in-
tegrated photonics. Particularly, the energy of the output pulses is in some cases
shown to be more than an order of magnitude larger than the input pulses. These
investigations also demonstrate how the relaxation dynamics are not well appropri-
ately described, exposing the limitations of the approximative iterative model and
underlining the need for improved models for studying large-detuning dynamics.
Finally, a suggestion for in-plane control of the nanocavity resonance and thus laser
output is given for the passive system, along with some suggestions and perspectives
for interesting conﬁgurations and future work.

Introduction
Semiconductor lasers have over the recent decades grown to become a centrepiece of mod-
ern technology, particularly in optical communications, but also as useful light sources for
a variety of other applications [1]. Given the ever-increasing amounts of data transmitted
and the requirements for more energy eﬃcient communication with higher bit-rates, the
continued development of new methods for signal-processing and optimisation of optical
communication schemes is essential for further technological progress [2].
One such potential improvement is the advent of integrated photonic circuits for on-chip
signal processing, where the conventional bandwidth limitations of electrical data pro-
cessing systems may be circumvented by the use of light as a signal carrier rather than
electrical currents. Successful implementation of such schemes requires a number of com-
ponents, including eﬃcient, integratable light sources [1]. Photonic crystal membrane
structures have been suggested as a platform for realisation of such light sources [3], be-
cause they allow realisation of ultra-small optical cavities with large quality factors. This
led to the ﬁrst demonstration of a photonic crystal laser [4], and since then, research on
these types of lasers has progressed signiﬁcantly [5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11].
In particular, electrically driven lasers [7, 8] have been demonstrated, as well as lasers
with ultra-low power consumption and gigahertz modulation rates [7]. Furthermore, the
system properties can be optimised for a number of applications through modiﬁcation
of the photonic crystal geometry, enabling ultra-high quality factors [12, 13], as well as
realisation of coupled-cavity structures with complex and rich dynamical properties [9].
Recently, a new type of photonic crystal laser, the Fano laser, was suggested [14] and
succesfully experimentally demonstrated [11]. The laser consists of a photonic crystal
membrane structure with a line-defect cavity coupled to a nearby point-defect cavity, as
shown in ﬁgure 1. The active material consists of quantum dots embedded in the photonic
crystal membrane, while the left mirror is realised by termination of the waveguide [15].
The right laser mirror is based upon Fano interference between the continuum of waveg-
uide modes and the discrete nanocavity mode, leading to a strongly resonant suppression
of transmission [14, 16], which eﬀectively functions as a highly-dispersive, narrowband
mirror. In [11] it was shown how this structure generates short optical pulses of picosec-
ond pulsewidths at a low GHz repetition rate, without the need for external modulation.
As such, this type of structure is of interest for e.g. on-chip signal processing, where
pulsed light sources are sparse.
This work aims to explore, review, and clarify the dynamics of these Fano lasers in a
variety of systems using theory and numerical simulations, in order to demonstrate new
potential applications, such as generation of ultra-short sub-picosecond pulses, as well as
transistor-like operation, pulse compression, frequency conversion and equaliser-like func-
tionality. Before these investigations, chapter 2 gives a brief review of the steady-state
properties, as well as the speciﬁcs of the Fano reﬂection coeﬃcient and its dependence
upon system parameters, including the design of an asymmetric reﬂection coeﬃcient.
Chapter 3 presents a review of the dynamical model used in [14, 17, 18], as well as the
development of a new iterative model.
Generally, two types of systems are treated in this thesis. Chapter 4 treats the ﬁrst,
which is similar to the one studied in [11], where the active material also extends to the
nanocavity, thus termed the ’active cavity system’, while in the second, there is no linear
absorption of the laser ﬁeld in the nanocavity, which is referred to as the ’passive cavity
system’ or just ’passive system’. This conﬁguration is explored in chapter 5.

The key property of the threshold gain curves is their asymmetry, which stems from the
ﬁnite value of the α-parameter in combination with the mirror phase. During oscillation

phase is changed through the amplitude-phase coupling of α, which forces the laser to
oscillate further from the reﬂection peak.
However, the mirror phase is an antisym-
metric function of detuning (dashed lines in ﬁgure 3a), so when the cavity resonance is
detuned towards lower frequencies, the change in mirror phase has the same sign as the
α-induced phase, leading to a large laser detuning and a swiftly increasing threshold gain.
Conversely, when detuning positively, the two contributions to the laser phase condition
have opposite signs, leading to a smaller adjustment in laser frequency, and thus a lower
threshold gain.

minimum threshold gain is obtained slightly, which follows directly from the changes to
the reﬂection coeﬃcient that were demonstrated in ﬁgure 3a, while not inﬂuencing the

damental lasing modes, and it is seen that as the cavity frequency is detuned towards

Since this ODE model is based upon ﬁrst-order Taylor expansion, the regime of accuracy
is limited by variations in both laser frequency and carrier density. These constraints
were explored in detail in [17] for a passive nanocavity, where it was demonstrated how,
for a constant expansion point, the accuracy of the model becomes critically poor for

Self-pulsing laser dynamics
For the parameter combinations deﬁned by the yellow region in ﬁgure 8, self-starting
laser pulsation is observed. This is demonstrated in ﬁgures 10a and 10b, which show the
laser dynamics for the same system as ﬁgures 9a and 9b, except here J = 1.2Jth. It is
noted here that self-pulsations have been predicted in nanocavities pumped by a strong
CW beam [34] and in coupled nanocavity structures [35], but that this conﬁguration is
signiﬁcantly diﬀerent due to the combination of a high quality factor cavity with a strongly
localised mode and the open-ended waveguide with an extended mode, although the
saturable absorption dynamics have similarities to [35], as demonstrated in the following.
A temporary increase in laser power results in a saturation of the cavity absorption,
leading to a larger mirror reﬂection, which in turn provides positive feedback for the
laser, resulting in pulse build-up (ﬁgures 10a and 10b). The pulsing may be understood
in terms of passive Q-switching, by viewing the active nanocavity as a saturable absorber,
since the cavity carrier density cannot exceed transparency, as demonstrated in ﬁgure 9b.
As the waveguide active region is continually pumped, the waveguide carrier density
will eventually reach the threshold corresponding to the nanocavity being unpumped, at
which point the onset of lasing occurs. As the waveguide ﬁeld starts building up, so does
the carrier density in the nanocavity, which results in the mirror loss decreasing because
of the decreased cavity absorption, so that the ﬁeld grows further.

4.1.3
Pulse characteristics
Figures 14a, 14b, 15a, and 15b show pulse characteristics of the laser under self-pulsing
operation, with the values in the areas outside the pulsing regimes being set to zero,
since these correspond to CW-operation for which pulse characterisation is nonsensical.
The parameters are identical to those used in ﬁgure 8. Figure 14a shows that the pulse
repetition rate is generally in the low GHz range, very close to the experimental values
reported in [11]. As for a typical saturable absorber system, the repetition rate increases
with pumping current, since the laser returns to threshold faster when the pump rate
is increased, meaning that there is less downtime between each pulse while the carrier
density in the waveguide is building back up [36]. Figure 14b shows the inverse of the
pulsewidth (FWHM), corresponding to ﬁgure 8.
Generally, the pulses obtained from this system are on the order of 10 picoseconds but
can reach the range of 3-4 picoseconds depending on parameter variations and inclusions
of non-linearities in the nanocavity, such as the Kerr eﬀect. However, the multitude of
relevant eﬀects also include e.g. free carrier absorption and two-photon absorption so
that the interplay between the diﬀerent phenomena is complex and analysis is beyond
the scope of this work. The bandwidth of the laser is limited by the linewidth of the
mirror, which is ≈200 GHz, depending on cavity quality factors, meaning that few-ps
pulses is not unrealistic despite the single-mode operation - as an example of this, a
transform-limited Gaussian pulse with an FWHM of 2 ps requires a bandwidth of ≈220
GHz [32]. For large detuning, the pulses are longer due to the slower saturation of the
reﬂection coeﬃcient, as demonstrated in ﬁgure 12).

4.4
Nature of the solution space
The location and size of the numerically obtained self-pulsing regime and the regime
of small-signal instability extracted from the stability analysis are close to identical, as
shown by the overlap of the yellow area and the dashed black line in ﬁgure 8, with the
only signiﬁcant diﬀerence being a small self-pulsing region near zero detuning.
The fact that the numerical self-pulsing regime exceeds that found by stability analysis
is in line with general mathematical theory of stability, where multiple types of instabil-
ities exist, while the linearised stability analysis only identiﬁes small-signal instabilities
(local bifurcations) [37]. This behaviour suggests an alternative source of instability in
the region where both the CW and SP modes exist (henceforth termed the dual region
for brevity). A number of laser instabilities are known [38], so the existence of one does
not seem unreasonable.
The following section examines the nature of the solution space near the dual region in de-
tail in order to characterise the dynamics of the bi-stable region. Figure 17 shows temporal

the eigenvalues of (4.11).

4.6
Perspectives
While the source of the instability in the dual region was not explicitly identiﬁed, the
previous explorations uncovered the nature of the solution space and the dynamics of the
mode transitions, including the selection mechanism in the dual region and the existing
stable and unstable modes. As such, the dynamics of the regime are now reasonably well-
understood, so that the knowledge gap here is mostly of a theoretical nature. Perhaps it
may be of interest in the future to carry out a more rigorous, mathematical analysis of the
system, but this is excessively complicated due to the high dimensionality and non-linear
nature of the system, which is also the primary reason for the numerical approach utilised
here. In any case, some interesting dynamics were also demonstrated - particularly the
potential trapping of the transient mode transitions of ﬁgure 27, for which some unusual
temporal power proﬁles were obtained, once again underlining the richness and complexity
of the system.

5
Passive cavity dynamics
In this chapter the subject of study is a conﬁguration for which there is no linear absorp-
tion of the laser ﬁeld in the nanocavity, which thus has the designation ’passive’ system.
Such a system has been extensively studied in [14, 17], demonstrating few-picosecond
pulse generation by active harmonic function modulation of the nanocavity resonance,
as well as a theoretical modulation bandwidth orders of magnitude larger than typical
semiconductor lasers (> 1 THz), due to the fact that the laser can be modulated directly
through the mirror, avoiding the relaxation oscillation limitations of typical modulation
of regular semiconductor lasers [14]. The following sections explore the consequences of
anharmonic modulation of the nanocavity resonance in order to investigate interesting
potential applications such as generation of ultra-short sub-picosecond pulses, transistor-
like operation and realisation of optical equaliser functionality.
Two modes of operation have been identiﬁed as promising, and these are analogous to
two well-known laser pulse generation schemes, namely cavity dumping and active Q-
switching, and thus will be referred to as such. They are explored separately in sections
5.1 and 5.2, but using the same theoretical approach. As these types of operation are
governed by large signal dynamics with signiﬁcant variations in both carrier density and
laser frequency, the iterative model is used to obtain a more accurate description of the
dynamics. Initially, the switching properties of these schemes are demonstrated by direct
temporal variation of the resonance frequency in the equation for the nanocavity ﬁeld,
(3.6), in order to clarify the pulse generation mechanisms. Subsequently, some suggestions
are given for optical modulation through variation of the nanocavity resonance frequency
by optical pumping of carriers in the nanocavity and this is characterised using a simple
rate equation approach.
5.1
Cavity dumping
The general concept of cavity dumping is relatively straight-forward. A laser is running
in CW operation with high-reﬂectivity mirrors, driven by a strong pump to generate a
large circulating intensity. The energy of the cavity is then ’dumped’ on a short time-
scale by rapidly decreasing the reﬂectivity of one or both of the laser mirrors, leading to
the circulating intensity coupling out on the time scale of the round-trip time, resulting
in a strong, short spike in output power [19, 20]. Thus, the eﬃciency of this scheme
is governed mostly by the mirror properties - particularly the ability to store a large
amount of circulating intensity (high mirror reﬂectivity) and the ability to quickly switch
oﬀthe reﬂectivity to a near-vanishing value. In the typical context of cavity dumping, the
pulsewidths produced are limited by the round-trip time because it is generally utilised in
macroscopic lasers, where the mirror reﬂectivity can be switched faster than the round-
trip time.

modulated through the refractive index in the nanocavity, allowing for eﬀective switching
of the reﬂection proﬁle - particularly for high quality-factor cavities, where the linewidth
becomes increasingly narrow, cf. ﬁgure 3, and for asymmetric reﬂection proﬁles where the

5.1.7
Perspectives and applications of cavity dumping
As has been demonstrated in the previous sections, the cavity dumping scheme shows
signiﬁcant promise. In particular, the readily realisable asymmetric scheme demonstrated
eﬃcient transistor-like functionality, where the output pulses have energies many times
larger than the input pulse, so that even when accounting for imperfect coupling into
the nanocavity and other degrading eﬀects, it is imaginable that the transistor-eﬀect re-
mains. Additionally, it was demonstrated how one can get periodic outputs, with the
repetition rate seemingly mostly limited by the nanocavity carrier lifetime, as in ﬁgure
43b. Furthermore, the output pulses are generally signiﬁcantly shorter than the input
pulses down to very low input pulsewidths, particularly for the high-α system, although
this comes with the qualiﬁer that changing the active material brings its own issues, as
described in section 5.1.5. In general, this scheme seems rather eﬃcient for pulse com-
pression, although it comes with built-in frequency conversion - which can be either an
advantage or a problem, depending on the intended application.
In all of these considerations one must keep in mind that large-signal eﬀects such as satu-
ration of the CW power with large current has been neglected, as have thermal eﬀects and
optical non-linearities, as well as spontaneous emission noise, so these simulations must
be evaluated with an amount of scepticism - or rather, taken as qualitatively indicative
of potential functionality rather than conﬁrmation of such.
However, the key point is that the structures investigated here are realisable and show in-
teresting potential for transistor-like operation, as well as also providing frequency down-
conversion and pulse compression, suggesting a wide range of applicability as building
blocks of integrated photonic circuits.

5.2
Active Q-switching
The self-pulsing dynamics of section 4.1.2 were explained in terms of passive Q-switching,
where the saturation of the nanocavity absorption served to continuously increase the re-
ﬂection coeﬃcient, leading to an increase in the ﬁeld intensity, and in this way establishing
a dynamical equilibrium for passive pulse generation. In this section, the subject of study
is active Q-switching, rather than passive.
A typical example used to explain active Q-switching is a laser equipped with a rotat-
able, mechanical mirror, so that by rotation the mirror loss coeﬃcient can be controlled
dynamically. Initially, the mirror is kept in the oﬀ-position so the mirror loss is large.
This, in combination with a strong pump, allows for a large carrier density to build-up
in the active region, but because of the near-unity mirror losses lasing is still not possi-
ble. If the mirror is then rotated to instead provide a sizeable reﬂection coeﬃcient, the
large active region carrier density results in a fast build-up of the laser ﬁeld intensity. As
the ﬁeld builds up rapidly, it strongly overshoots the steady-state value of the ﬁeld, and
thus depletes the carrier density well-beyond the steady-state level, resulting in the laser
falling below threshold as the mirror is rotated to the oﬀ-position and the mirror loss
returns to near-unity, thus generating a pulse of output power [19, 20].
The general principle is simply to start pumping while maintaining a large round-trip
loss (usually through manipulation of the mirror, but also from e.g. saturable absorbers
in passive cases), allowing for a large carrier density to build up. After this, the round-
trip losses are reduced strongly and lasing starts, releasing a large pulse, after which the
laser falls below threshold as the losses are switched back to the oﬀ-value and the carrier
density is allowed to build back up until the next switch, generating a train of pulses.
Crucially, unlike cavity dumping, the pulse characteristics typically depend mostly on
the amount of energy stored in the active material, rather than the switching dynamics
of the mirror, as long as the mirror switching speed is faster than the on-set of the pulse
build-up [20]. This may be understood through the fact that the more energy is stored
in the active material, the faster the laser ﬁeld can grow once the mirror is switched on,
and the faster the ﬁeld grows, the faster it can deplete the carrier density and return to
zero. As such, the pulsewidth and peak power are governed mostly by the stored energy
in the active material. Assuming that pumping and carrier decay processes are negligible
during pulse release, an approximative estimate for the pulsewidth is [19]
τpulse = τin

when studying cavity dumping.

5.2.3
Noise implementation and numerical challenges of Q-switching simu-

In experimental implementations the turn on of the laser will be started by ampliﬁed
spontaneous emission into the lasing mode, in order to utilise the large available gain as
the reﬂectivity is switched back on. In noise-less simulations this is somewhat challenging
to emulate, since if there is no noise the exponential decay of the iterative formulation
tends to bring the ﬁelds values so low that eventually they are rounded to zero, in which
case the laser is unable to turn on after the reﬂectivity is switched. As such, obtain-
ing meaningful, noiseless simulations requires careful balancing of the initial conditions,
drive current and turn-on delay, so that the carrier density reaches equilibrium without
the laser ﬁelds vanishing.

5.2.5
Perspectives and applications of active Q-switching
While the quantitative values presented in the previous section are questionable due to
the mentioned qualiﬁers on the assumptions and approximations used, the concept ap-
pears very promising, with the main idea being based on the solid foundation of the
threshold curve from the oscillation condition and the well-understood resonance shifting
properties of the nanocavity. This utilises the properties of passive Q-switching, where
the output pulse parameters depend mostly on how much energy can be stored in the
active material, as dictated by the shifting properties and the threshold curve, promising
ultra-short pulses and eﬃcient switching.
In particular, generating sub-picosecond pulses with GHz repetition rates in microscopic
lasers without utilising mode-locking seems extraordinary, as semiconductor lasers pro-
ducing femtosecond pulses appear to be exclusively based on mode-locking [40, 41, 42]. In
this context, however, it should be noted that these authors report generation of pulses in
the ≈200 fs range, whereas the pulses generated here, even with the used approximations,
do not go below 700 fs, and are probably longer in practice. In any case, the simplicity
and robustness of this conﬁguration makes it promising as a source of ultra-short pulses
for integrated photonics.
Due to the ability to store large amounts of energy in the active material and the nar-
row linewidth of the resonance, this conﬁguration appears well-suited for transistor-like
operation in on-chip signal processing, where a low-energy control pulse triggers a much
stronger output pulse, as investigated previously. One challenge for this is to control the
turn-on delay eﬀectively, so that uniformity and tunability of the output can obtained
- pulse timing jitter from spontaneous emission presents a similar issue that must be
considered. This application is particularly strengthened, if operation with an in-plane
trigger pulse can be demonstrated, since vertical excitation is not well-suited for inte-
grated applications. This option is brieﬂy explored in section 5.3.
As demonstrated in ﬁgures 55, 56a, 56b and 57a there is a sizeable range of input pulse
parameters for which the output pulses do not vary noticeably. In that case, one might
imagine utilising the system as a form of optical equaliser, where a signal of distorted
pulses is converted into a train of identical pulses through conversion by the Fano laser
conﬁguration.
Finally, the system provides automatic frequency conversion of the input pulse to an
input pulse corresponding to the nanocavity resonance frequency, so in that sense one
might imagine utilising the system for tunable frequency conversion by suitable choice of
the laser cavity length and nanocavity resonance frequency.
All of these properties serve to underline the rich dynamics of the system, and the sig-
niﬁcant variability in performance with design parameters demonstrates how the system
may be designed according to the envisioned application, warranting consideration for
several roles in the integrated photonics platforms of the future. Thus, there is plenty
of motivation for dealing with some of the challenges presented here, such as inclusion
of noise, obtaining accurate descriptions of frequency-detuned dynamics far from the ex-
pansion point, and implementing and modelling operation with in-plane control pulses.
Since the process depends so crucially upon the resonance change of the nanocavity, it
is also important to include all relevant eﬀects such as Kerr non-linearities, band-ﬁlling
and two-photon absorption in order to correctly model the resonance shift.

6
Conclusion and scope
In this work the Fano laser was presented as a photonic crystal laser consisting of a
line-defect coupled to a nearby point-defect in a 2D photonic crystal membrane, with
the active region made up of quantum dots embedded in the membrane. The mirror
properties and threshold solutions were brieﬂy reviewed, based on a transmission line
description and well-known coupled-mode theory. It was demonstrated how the reﬂec-
tion proﬁle may be tuned by variation of the system parameters, particularly the quality
factors, and by inclusion of a partially transmitting element in the waveguide.
An ordinary diﬀerential equation model and an iterative model were derived and analysed,
both based on expansions of an oscillation condition derived through the transmission
line description, including steady-state and stability properties, as well as considerations
of model limitations and applicability. Here it was demonstrated how the dynamics of the
two models were qualitatively similar, but with some quantitative diﬀerences, stemming
from the improved treatment of large-signal dynamics in the iterative model.
After this, a conﬁguration with active material in the nanocavity was analysed using
the ODE model, and it was demonstrated how this leads to passive pulse generation in
well-deﬁned parameter regions. This pulsing was likened to passive Q-switching, with the
nanocavity functioning as a dispersive semiconductor saturable absorber mirror. A stabil-
ity analysis was carried out, which demonstrated how the primary origin of self-pulsing
was relaxation oscillations becoming un-damped, but also that a region of bi-stability
exists, in which both CW and self-pulsing modes exist. This regime was explored ex-
tensively by dynamical detuning of the nanocavity resonance frequency, clarifying the
nature of the solution space and showing how dynamical phase transitions between CW
and self-pulsing can be induced using modulation of the nanocavity resonance, as well
as generating some interesting temporal power outputs by modulation back and forth.
While the source of the dual region instability is still not explicitly clear, these investiga-
tions revealed the nature of the solution space and how it may be manipulated, suggesting
that in order to obtain a deeper understanding, some rigorous mathematical analysis may
be necessary.
A passive-cavity conﬁguration was also analysed extensively with a view towards ap-
plications in on-chip signal processing, and two schemes of operation were identiﬁed
analogously to the well-known cavity dumping and active Q-switching schemes for pulse
generation. Each of these schemes was explored in detail, leading to an approximative
analytical model of cavity dumping, which agreed well with simulations for fast switching
of the reﬂectivity. High-α and high-Q systems were suggested as useful for cavity dump-
ing, based on their large reﬂectivity contrast and eﬃcient switching. The output pulses
were characterised numerically as functions of the input pulse parameters in a simplistic
model of optical control of the nanocavity resonance frequency, and it was demonstrated
how the high-α system may provide signiﬁcant pulse compression down to pulsewidths of
a few picoseconds, while the high-Q system lead to longer output pulses, but with much
higher energies, suggesting potential viability in a transistor-like scheme, where a small
input pulse triggers a larger output pulse from the laser.
The active Q-switching mechanism was demonstrated, leading to output pulses with
unprecedented characteristics, such as peak powers of hundreds of milliwatts and sub-
picosecond pulsewidths, resulting from gain-approximations that are probably unrealis-
tic. In any case, these simulations gave strong indications that this scheme may provide
ultra-short, high-energy pulses, suggesting viability in both transistor-like schemes and
80
==PAGE:86==
for short-pulse generation in on-chip signal processing, particularly due to the energy of
the pulses, which was in some cases more than an order of magnitude larger than the trig-
ger pulse energy. During these investigations, it was also demonstrated how the limited
model accuracy of the iterative model led to incorrect transient relaxation dynamics after
pulse release, once again underlining the need for higher-quality models for description of
large dynamic detuning of the nanocavity resonance - particularly with a view to noise
inclusion. Generally, the repetition rates of both cavity dumping and active Q-switching
appeared to be limited by the nanocavity carrier lifetime, because this lifetime governs
the decay of reﬂectivity switching. Finally, a suggestion was given for in-plane optical
control of the nanocavity resonance, including a CMT-based model. This would greatly
improve the applicability in on-chip signal processing, due to the practicality of in-plane
operation, where the 2D photonic crystal membrane structure may be utilised.
Based on these investigations, the Fano laser appears highly promising for a number of
applications in integrated photonics due to the rich physics of the system, which depend
strongly on a number of design factors, allowing for the system to be tailored for the
application in mind, such as pulse compression, transistor-like functionality, equalisation
and frequency conversion. From the investigations presented here, the cavity dumping-
functionality appears better-modelled, producing more reliable, but slightly less promising
results, compared to the Q-switched scheme, where the results appear quite extraordi-
nary, but as mentioned in the main text, these come with a number of qualiﬁers on the
quantitative values.
Thus, future work should aim to demonstrate eﬃcient operation of the cavity dumping
and Q-switching schemes using in-plane control of the nanocavity resonance, as well as
develop an improved model, in which the large detuning is well-modelled, particularly
beyond the oscillation condition mode cut-oﬀ, where the approximative models struggle,
as well as implementing spontaneous emission noise. Additionally, it would be highly rele-
vant to also include higher-order frequency shifting eﬀects, since both cavity dumping and
Q-switching depend crucially on the nanocavity resonance change. For these purposes
it would also be useful to obtain a reliable base of comparison during model develop-
ment, such as laser dynamics from Maxwell’s equations, e.g. by FDTD simulations of the
structure, as well as considerations of coupling to other system components. One might
also imagine even richer systems by further modiﬁcation of the membrane structure, such
as adding an additional point-defect cavity on the other side of the waveguide, creating
interference between two discrete modes and the continuum of the waveguide modes. It
is also of interest to study e.g. a self-pulsing Fano laser structure supplying the trigger
pulses for a passive structure, as outlined in section 5.3, as both of these conﬁgurations
are well-understood and their combination may then be investigated as an example of
in-plane optical resonance control.
