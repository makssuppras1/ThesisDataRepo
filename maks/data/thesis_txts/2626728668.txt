Abstract
The extracellular protease AprX, produced by psychrotrophic bacteria in milk such as Pseudomonas
spp. is responsible for the breakdown of casein, causing unpleasant ﬂavours and textures in e.g.
UHT milk. It is therefore highly desirable for the dairy industry to analyse raw milk for the presence
of the protein before the milk is processed. This can for instance be achieved through the detection
of the gene encoding the protease. However, several problems are associated with the extraction
and puriﬁcation of DNA from milk due to its content of e.g. fat, protein, and calcium.
One aim of this thesis was therefore to develop a method for DNA extraction from milk that
results in yields of high quality DNA for subsequent qPCR analyses. Three diﬀerent DNA extraction
methods of varying complexity and time requirements were assessed and evaluated. A qPCR
programme was furthermore developed with primers speciﬁc for the aprX gene in Pseudomonas
spp.
DNA was successfully extracted from milk and aprX was recovered from DNA extracted with
all three diﬀerent methods. Furthermore, it was found that limit of quantiﬁcation (LOQ), i.e., the
lowest CFU in a reaction detectable by this DNA extraction method and this qPCR programme,
was 15 with a Cq value of 38.98 ± 0.75. Moreover, metagenomic sequencing revealed that DNA from
Gram-positive cells and mammalian cells could additionally be extracted. Lastly, the possibility of
fully automating the DNA extraction protocol of the MagneSil® KF, Genomic System allows for a
standardised, fast, and cheap DNA extraction protocol which makes this method ideal to use by the
dairy industry.

1
Introduction
Ultra high temperature (UHT) treatment is a
technique widely applied by the dairy industry
to conserve milk. In contrast to low pasteurisa-
tion, which is usually performed by heating milk
to 72 °C for 15 seconds, UHT treatment requires
heating of the milk to 135 °C for 3-5 seconds
[1]. Both methods inactivate more than 99%
of microorganisms and UHT treatment further-
more inactivates some bacterial endospores [2–4]
which allows for long-term storage at room tem-
perature. In Denmark, an eﬀective cold-chain
enables the marketing of low-pasteurised milk.
However, in countries such as India and China,
which are among the largest consumers of milk
globally, this is not possible, and as such UHT
milk is a viable alternative [5].
Arla Foods amba is the largest producer of
milk in Denmark and furthermore produces more
than 200,000 tonnes of UHT products per year,
most of which is exported. In addition, more
than 60,000 tonnes of liquid milk (konsummælk)
is exported annually (in 2019), a number which
has increased by 278% over the last decade [6].
Despite being virtually sterile, one in 500
batches of UHT treated products will spoil over
the course of the extended shelf-life and must
therefore be discarded. Spoiled products lead
to recalls and represent a direct expense for the
dairy industry. Additionally, as the consumers
are likely to associate a brand with spoiled prod-
ucts, this will decrease the trustworthiness of the
brand, being even more costly to the producer.
Because of the high temperature during pas-
teurisation, UHT milk is seldomly spoiled di-
rectly by bacteria [7]. Instead, casein degrada-
tion is caused by heat resistant enzymes such
as plasmin or AprX. Plasmin is found in mam-
malian cells while AprX is produced by psy-
chrotrophic bacteria in milk. In order to avoid
plasmin-induced enzymal degradation of UHT-
treated milk, a pre-heating step of more than
90 °C for 30-60 seconds can be added to allow
β-lactoglobulin to inhibit the enzyme by binding
to and stabilising the unfolded structure accom-
plished by heating [8]. However, while this pre-
treatment method is eﬀective in the destruction
of plasmin, some proteases, such as AprX, can
remain intact following heat treatment [9].
1.1
AprX
AprX is a bacterial extracellular zinc metallopro-
tease in the serralysin superfamily. The enzyme
is capable of breaking down milk proteins such
as casein and alter the ﬂavour and texture of
the milk. Milk contains 3-6 µg zinc per ml and
thus harbours the coenzyme required for AprX
function. A study by Marchand et al. showed
that 42 out of 55 tested Pseudomonas strains
from milk harboured the aprX gene, encoding
AprX [10].

to minimise bacterial growth, fresh raw milk is
stored in large tanks at 4 °C for up to 5 days
before collection and pasteurisation. Certain
psychrotrophs including Pseudomonas spp. are,
however, able to grow at these temperatures

As such, the protein can successfully refold to its

Notably for AprX is that it does not contain any
cystein residues. Cysteine generally contributes
to the heat stability of a protein, as it has a
thiol group which enables it to form disulphide
bonds, thus stabilising a peptide structure. As
the ability of AprX to refold upon appropriate
temperatures depends on the ﬂexibility of its
structure, the lack of cysteine residues in the
protein can make the protein more heat resistant
[12, 16, 17].
AprX has been shown to be able to retain more
than 80% of its activity following treatment at
110 °C for 2 minutes, and 40% activity after
treatment at 130 °C for 3 minutes. However,
thermal stability may vary depending on the
version of the enzyme [16, 18, 19]. The enzyme
appears to be inactive below 5 °C and its activity
generally increases until it reaches its optimum
around 45 °C, after which it decreases until 55 °C
where it is no longer active [12, 18]. As UHT milk
is often stored at room temperature, the enzyme
is active and thereby able to break down the
proteins in the milk, causing bitter oﬀ-ﬂavours
and thickening of the milk [20].
AprX works by hydrolysing casein with a pref-
erence for κ-casein, from which it cleaves the pep-
tide bond between Phe105 and Met106. Calcium
in milk is found as calcium phosphate which
is important in e.g. bone formation. In order
to avoid aggregation in the milk, thousands of
casein proteins surround the molecule, forming
a micelle with calcium phosphate in the centre
[21]. There are four diﬀerent forms of casein; αs1,
αs2, β, and κ-casein. Due to their hydrophobic-
ity, the ﬁrst three forms surround the calcium
phosphate molecule but are generally located
inside the micelle. κ-casein is hydrophilic and
thus forms a hair-like layer on the surface of
the micelle (see ﬁgure 2a). If the Phe-Met pep-
tide bond of the κ-casein is broken, it can no
longer adhere to the surface of the micelle, leav-
ing hydrophobic sides exposed. This causes the
diﬀerent casein micelles to aggregate (ﬁgure 2b)
[14, 22].
The aprX gene is located on the aprX-lipA
operon which is controlled by a single promoter.

LipA, the gene for which is also located on the
aprX-lipA operon, is a lipase capable of break-
ing down the fats in the milk, causing rancid
and soapy ﬂavours [25, 26]. In order to min-

are located on the same operon, their gene copy
number should theoretically be the same [18,
23] and for simplicity, the focus of this paper is
going to be aprX.
1.1.1
Testing for presence of AprX
The current standard for AprX detection in the
dairy industry is the so-called "fast method".
This includes a plate count from milk that has
been incubated on agar plates. This method is
unreliable as it does not diﬀerentiate bacteria
that produce AprX from those that do not, and
additionally not all bacteria are culturable on
agar plates. Studies have, however, found that a
bacterial culture in the late exponential or early
stationary is necessary for AprX activity [27–33].
This number is well above the standards set by
Directive 92/46 of the EEC which states that
raw cow’s milk for consumption should contain
at most 105 CFU/ml based on plate count at 30
°C. The method can therefore be used to sort raw
milk ﬁt for consumption from that which is not,
though it is not sensitive enough to sort milk
ﬁt for UHT treatment from milk unﬁt for this
treatment. Furthermore, a much faster answer
is needed by the industry as processing time is
desired to be below 8 hours. Other methods
focus more on the detection of spoilage enzymes
in the milk. One such method involves screening
for protease activity on agar plates containing
milk, although this method is not AprX speciﬁc
and takes about 12-24 hours [34].
In recent
years, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) has been
implemented as a faster alternative to the above
mentioned methods. However, as the results of
a regular PCR need to be visualised on a gel,
the number of aprX -encoding bacteria necessary
for a positive result is relatively high and the
amount of aprX cannot be quantiﬁed [10, 35].
Moreover, extracting a high concentration of
pure DNA from milk can be challenging due to
the high content of calcium ions, protein, and
fat [36, 37]. To the best of our knowledge, no
studies have been conducted on the eﬀectiveness
of quantitative PCR (qPCR) on aprX detection
at the time of writing.
1.2
qPCR
Similarly to a traditional PCR, DNA is copied
based on the speciﬁc primers in a qPCR. How-
ever, qPCR enables real-time quantiﬁcation of
the DNA using a ﬂuorescent agent and thus elim-
inates the need for visualisation on a gel. The
ﬂuorescent agent in a qPCR can either be added
in the form of an intercalating dye that binds
to double stranded (ds)DNA or in the form of
a probe with a ﬂuorescent dye attached to it
that will only ﬂuoresce when separated from the
nearby quencher [38].
In intercalating dye based qPCR, the dye will
not be attached to the DNA during the initial
denaturation step as the DNA will be single
stranded and there will thus be no ﬂuorescence.
When the primers attach during the annealing
phase, there will be some ﬂuorescence due to
the small amount of dsDNA but this will be in-
signiﬁcant compared to the ﬂuorescence achieved
==PAGE:11==
Page 8 of 69
following the extension step where the DNA
is double stranded. As more DNA is copied,
more ﬂuorescence is expressed and this can be
measured by the qPCR machine. As the dye
will bind to any dsDNA, there might be some
unspeciﬁc binding in the form of e.g. hairpin
structures and primer dimers. To take this into
account, a dissociation curve can be made, mea-
suring ﬂuorescence at a gradient of temperatures
to show melting temperatures of the strands.
Ideally - if one gene from one organism is tar-
geted - there should be only one peak on the
dissociation curve [39].
Probe-based qPCR is more speciﬁc in that
the presence of a sequence-speciﬁc probe lim-
its quantiﬁcation from unspeciﬁc binding of the
primers. When the DNA polymerase extends
the single stranded DNA, the probe is hydrol-
ysed and the dye is separated from the quencher,
expressing ﬂuorescence only during the exten-
sion phase. Due to the requirement for a probe,
this method is, however, more expensive than
intercalating dye based qPCR [38].
According to the Minimum Information for
Publication of Quantitative Real-Time PCR Ex-
periments (MIQE) guidelines, that have been
issued in order to standardise the results from
qPCR, quantiﬁcation cycle (Cq) values above
40 can be unreliable due to spontaneous ﬂuo-
rescence. Nevertheless, the protocol does not
recommend arbitrary cutoﬀlevels [40].

1.4
Sequencing
Another method of analysing which genes or
organisms are present in a sample is via sequenc-
ing. The ﬁrst generation of sequencing emerged
in the 1970s with Sanger and Maxam-Gilbert
and was the golden standard until the beginning
of the 2000s [41]. Both methods rely on gel elec-
trophoresis for visualisation of the sequence and
they are thus relatively time consuming com-
pared to the size of reads that can be achieved.
The emergence of second generation sequencing
eliminated the need for gel electrophoresis and
furthermore enabled simultaneous generation of
millions of short reads, making the process faster
and cheaper but more diﬃcult to assemble due to
the shorther length of the reads. One commonly
used second generation sequencing platform is
Illumina, in which bases are read as they are
synthesised. Before shotgun sequencing (such
as Illumina sequencing), DNA needs to be frag-
mented into pieces of about 300-400 bp. This
is frequently done mechanically and will often
leave overhangs on one strand compared to the
complementary one. Following fragmentation,
ends will need to be repaired and adenylated to
prevent them from ligating to each other. Then,
adaptors containing primer binding sites, bar-
codes for sample identiﬁcation, and binding sites
to the Illumina ﬂow cell, are attached [42] (see
ﬁgure 5).

1.5
Aim
The aim of this project is to develop a method for
DNA extraction and a qPCR assay to screen for
the presence of aprX in raw milk. The method
will ideally not only eliminate the need for a
gel, thus saving time, but furthermore enable
detection of lower amounts of the protease gene
compared to standard PCR as well as real time
quantiﬁcation of the data to oﬀer a faster and
more optimal method of aprX detection for the
dairy industry. The eﬀectiveness of the DNA ex-
traction method will furthermore be elucidated
using metagenomics to assess the eﬀectiveness
of the DNA extraction method on bovine cells
as well as Gram-positive and Gram-negative
bacteria as well as illuminate the microbial com-
position in a raw milk sample.
2
Materials and Methods
A method for DNA extraction was developed,
that resulted in DNA of high enough concentra-
tion, purity, and quality to be used for qPCR
and metagenomics, both of which were applied
to test for i.a. the presence of aprX. Further-
more, bacterial strains and raw milk were tested
for proteolytic activity and the bacterial count
in raw milk was assessed. The diﬀerent methods
were compared in order to assess their respective
eﬀectiveness. An overview of the overall work-
ﬂow has been illustrated in ﬁgure 6. A detailed
description of the methods can be found below.
Unless otherwise stated, all samples were carried
out in triplicates.

2.2
Phenotypic Detection of AprX
2.2.1
CFU count in raw milk
Fresh raw milk was shipped from ARLA Foods
amba in an isolating container with cooling el-
ements and temperature loggers. Until time of
use, the raw milk was stored at temperatures
between 4 and 6 °C. Raw milk was diluted 1:10
to reach dilutions down to 10−9. The number
of CFU/ml in the raw milk was determined by
plating 1 ml raw milk or diluted raw milk onto a
3M™Petriﬁlm™Aerobic Count Plate (3M, USA.
REF: 6400/6406/6442) and incubating at room
temperature for 48 hours.
2.2.2
Screening for Proteolytic Activity
In an eﬀort to not only assess the number of
bacteria but also proteolytic activity, skim milk
agar (SMA) plates were prepared by dissolving
1% w/v skim milk powder (Sigma-Aldrich, USA.
Product no: 70166-500G) in a small amount of
demineralised water followed by the addition of
0.5% w/v yeast extract (Merck Millipore, Ger-
many, Product no: 1.03753.0500) and 1.5% w/v
agar (Th. Geyer GmbH & Co. KG, Germany.
CAS no: 9002-18-0) and demineralised water
was then added to reach the desired volume.
Two diﬀerent methods were attempted in or-
der to screen for proteolytic activity. In the ﬁrst
method, small wells (≈2 mm in diameter) were
punched into the SMA using a cut-oﬀsterile
pipette tip and 10 µl bacterial suspension was
added to each well. The plate was left to incu-
bate at room temperature for 72 hours. The sec-
ond method involved pipetting 5 µl drops onto
the surface of the SMA, letting these dry fol-
lowed by incubation at room temperature for 72
hours. Enzymatic activity was seen as clearing
zones. Though both methods were eﬀective, the
latter was faster and easier. This method was
therefore used in the succeeding experiments.
The four strains P. ﬂuorescens DSM 50090, P.
weihenstephanensis DSM 29166, P. ﬂuorescens
DSM 50120, & P. panacis DSM 18529 were
screened for proteolytic activity by adding 3
droplets of 5 µl each for each strain according
to the method described above. The experiment
was repeated using raw milk and plates were
incubated at room temperature for 72 hours as
well as at 5 °C for 6 days.

2.3.2
DNA extraction
Three diﬀerent methods were compared for DNA
extraction.
A brief overview of these meth-
ods can be seen in table 2 and the methods
have been explained in further detail below. Ex-
tracted DNA was stored at -20 °C until further
use. DNA extracted using the MagneSil® KF,
Genomic System (Promega Corporation, Madi-
son, WI, USA) was used for further analysis.
2.3.2.1
DNeasy® PowerFood® Kit
After pre-treatment, DNA was extracted ac-
cording to manufacturer’s instructions which
can be found in Appendix B. In short, pellet
was dissolved in lysis buﬀer and transferred to a
bead tube, in which it was vortexed for 10 min-
utes to further lyse the cells. Cell debris, beads
etc. was removed from the DNA-containing su-
pernatant via centrifugation.
The DNA was
then transferred to a silica membrane spin col-
umn, from which further non-DNA material was
removed before the DNA was eluted in TE (Tris-
ethylenediamine tetraacetic acid) buﬀer.
As DNA concentration was low, several adjust-
ments - listed below - to the protocol and the
pre-treatment were made to increase concentra-
tion.
1. In order to reach a higher DNA concentra-
tion, the volume of starting material was
increased.
2. In order to increase purity of the DNA, pel-
let was washed with 0.9 % NaCl to remove
fats and other impurities.
3. Centrifugation time in the pre-treatment
was adjusted to assess whether or not this
would give a higher DNA yield.
4. In order to maximise the eﬃciency of the ly-
sis and thus possibly increase yield of DNA,
samples with lysis buﬀer were heated to
65 °C for 10 minutes prior to mechanical
lysing according to the protocol of the kit.
2.3.2.2
MagneSil® KF, Genomic System
This kit was used in combination with a King-
Fisher 700 Magnetic Particle Processor (Thermo
Labsystems, Finland) machine in order to au-
tomate the method. Pre-treated pellet was dis-
solved in lysis buﬀer and transferred to a King-
Fisher plate along with magnetic beads that
bind DNA but not non-DNA compounds. A salt
wash was added to neutralise the charge of the
DNA, making it less soluble in water while mak-
ing proteins more soluble in water. Then, an
ethanol wash was added to remove the salts. Fol-
lowing ethanol wash, the ethanol was allowed to
evaporate as this would otherwise inhibit down-
stream processing. Lastly, the DNA was eluted
in nuclease-free water. As the original method
resulted in a highly variable DNA concentration
and purity, the following methods were applied
in an attempt to stabilise and increase DNA
concentration and purity.
1. In order to reach a higher DNA concentra-
tion, the volume of starting material was
increased.
2. More eﬃcient lysing was attempted by com-

Page 12 of 69
bining the chemical lysing with mechanical
by using a bead beater for 10 minutes (see
section 2.3.2.1).
In order to investigate the consistency of the
method, three plates were set up with milk
spiked to a ﬁnal concentration of 107 CFU/ml
added to all columns of the KingFisher plate.
Consequently, the pre-treatment method was
modiﬁed from that described in section 2.3.1;
following initial centrifugation, the supernatant
was poured out and the samples were centrifuged

supernatant was then removed by pipetting and
the MagneSil kit protocol was followed.
A detailed protocol with updated steps for
DNA extraction using the MagneSil kit com-
bined with the KingFisher machine can be seen
in Appendix C.
2.3.2.3
Quick & Dirty
Following pre-treatment, pellet was centrifuged
an extra time and residual supernatant removed
with a pipette. Pellet was dissolved in 30 µl
0.9 % NaCl and 100 µl Chelex 100 was added to
remove Mg+-ions that can otherwise inﬂuence
downstream processing. The samples were incu-
bated at 99 °C for 10 minutes followed by imme-
diate cooling on ice to accommodate heat-shock-
induced lysing.
The samples were then cen-
trifuged at 17,000 x g and the DNA-containing
supernatant was transferred to a clean tube.
Unlike the DNeasy® PowerFood® Kit and
the MagneSil® KF, Genomic System, non-DNA
material is not removed during this method with
the exception of Mg+-ions.
Due to the high
content of inhibitors such as fat, and calcium,
this might prevent aprX detection during qPCR.
In order to compensate for this, the following
additional steps were added to the protocol.
1. 0.9 % NaCl wash of pre-treated pellet to
remove additional fat and protein.
2. Ethanol precipitation to wash away proteins
and other non-DNA compounds.
Ethanol precipitation was implemented by
adding 1:10 v/v 3M Na-Acetate (pH 4.8) to
the samples followed by 2.5:1 v/v ice cold 100%
ethanol. This was incubated at -20 °C for 1 hour
and then centrifuged for 15 minutes at 17,000
x g. The supernatant was discarded and pellet
was washed twice in 70% ethanol in order to
wash away the salts. Lastly, pellet was dried
and dissolved in TE buﬀer as it did not dissolve
in nuclease free water.
2.3.3
DNA concentration and quality
To assess the concentration and quality of the
DNA, three diﬀerent methods were used on all
samples. The Invitrogen Qubit® 3.0 Fluorom-
eter (Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc, Malaysia) was
used to measure DNA concentrations as this can
measure very low concentrations relatively accu-
rately. It does not, however, measure the purity
of the DNA. The NanoPhotometer NP80 Mobile
(IMPLEN, Germany) was used to quantify both
DNA concentration and purity in the form of
A260/A280 ration using spectrophotometry but
is not accurate when measuring concentrations
< 10 ng/µl. An A260/A280 ratio between 1.6-
1.9 indicates pure DNA [44]. Lastly, selected
samples were analysed on an Agilent DNA 1000
(Agilent Technologies, Germany) chip using the
Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer G2938B (Agilent Tech-
nologies, Germany) according to the protocol in
Appendix D. Lower limit was 15 bp and upper
limit 1500 bp.

2.3.5
Sequencing
DNA was extracted from 30 ml raw milk which
had been pooled and centrifuged in a 1.5 ml Ep-
pendorf tube at 17,000 x g. Additionally, DNA
was extracted from from a falcon tube with 50
ml pasteurised milk spiked with P. ﬂuorescens
DSM 50090 and L. lactis subsp. cremoris FD13
to a ﬁnal count of 107 CFU and 106 CFU, re-
spectively. This tube had been centrifuged at
4256 x g for 10 minutes.
Following centrifu-
gation, the MagneSil kit was used to extract
DNA from both samples. The DNA was then
metagenome sequenced by Euroﬁns Genomics in
2 x 150 bp paired-end sequencing using Illumina
with random primers to target as many organ-
isms as possible. 20 million bp were sequenced
per sample.

2.3.5.1
Statistical analyses
When possible, data is presented as arithmetic
means with their standard deviations. Signiﬁ-
cance level cutoﬀwas set to P ≤0.05. Normality
was assessed using omnibus K2 D’Agostino &
Pearson tests. For comparison of DNA extrac-
tion methods and attempted improvals, one-way
ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests for multiple
comparisons were applied. Two-way ANOVA
was used to evaluate diﬀerences between the
columns in the KingFisher 700 Magnetic Parti-
cle Processor and one-way ANOVA was used to
compare runs overall, not taking inter column
variation into account.

4
Discussion
It is highly desirable for the dairy industry to
be able to sort protease-containing milk from
protease-free milk and as such this issue has laid
grounds for extensive research. Previous studies
have succeeded in developing a method for DNA
puriﬁcation and PCR to detect the aprX gene in
raw milk [10, 62]. However, as many successful
DNA extraction protocols require harsh chem-
icals such as phenol chloroform, they are not
attractive to the dairy industry. Others have
evaluated methods that do not require these
kinds of harsh chemicals [35] but to our knowl-
edge, a fully automatated method has yet to be
described. In this study, a method that does
not involve harsh chemicals and which can be
fully automated following the pre-treatment was
developed for the extraction of DNA from raw
milk. Furthermore, a qPCR programme was
developed to test for the presence of aprX and
applied to evaluate the eﬀectiveness of the DNA
extraction method.
Due to its high content of e.g. calcium, fat,
and protein, milk can be a challenging medium
from which to extract DNA of suﬃcient qual-
ity for PCR. Several studies have showed that
calcium ions can act as inhibitors for the DNA
polymerase during PCR by competitively bind-
ing to the polymerase instead of magnesium ions
[36, 63, 64]. Fat and protein have furthermore
been implicated in PCR inhibition [37, 65]. A
general tendency does, however, indicate that
the inhibitory eﬀect of these compounds is only
relevant in notably higher concentrations than
what is present in milk [36, 66]. In this study
we used an environmental master mix designed
to work in the presence of high inhibitor levels.
Nevertheless, in order to ensure the best results
during PCR and the lowest detection limit pos-
sible, it is important to remove as many of these
inhibitors as possible.
The three diﬀerent DNA extraction methods
applied in this study were compared for their
ability to isolate DNA with an acceptable pu-
rity for qPCR detection of the aprX gene. The
PowerFood kit returned relatively low DNA con-
centrations with a high purity, while the Quick
& Dirty method resulted in high DNA concentra-
tions and low purity. The MagneSil kit produced

DNA of varying concentration and purity. In a

DNA concentrations were around 0.9 ng/µl with

found to be 0.7 ng/µl and the A260/A280 ratio
was 1.23. Similar to what was observed in the
current study, Quigley et al. found large varia-
tion in the concentration of the DNA obtained
using the MagneSil beads [67]. However, DNA
concentration was found to be higher for Power-
Food than for Wizard in the Quigley study. This
is in contrast to what was found in the current
study. Furthermore, the large variation in DNA
concentration discovered in the current study
was partially contributed to inconsistency of the
magnets in the KingFisher machine. However,
taking the ﬁndings made by Quigley et al. into
consideration, other factors seem to further in-
==PAGE:27==
Page 24 of 69
ﬂuence the results. Fat has been associated with
disturbances of magnetic resonance imaging [68]
and as such, one possible explanation could be
the fat in the milk preventing the paramagnetic
beads from binding optimally to the DNA. Al-
ternatively, some DNA might not be washed oﬀ
the paramagnetic beads, which could account
for the lower DNA concentration.
The Quick & Dirty method involving a ther-
mal shock to lyse the bacterial cells is a widely
applied method on pure bacterial suspensions
[69–71] but generally not on a food matrix, pos-
sibly due to the high content of non-cell material.
In order to overcome the presence of fat, protein,
carbohydrates etc. in milk, ethanol precipitation
was necessary in order to apply this method on
the milk.
The aprX gene was retrievable with the de-
veloped qPCR programme for all three DNA
extraction methods.
Furthermore, while the
variation in Cq level was less pronounced than
that in DNA concentration within the MagneSil
kit, the variation was nevertheless signiﬁcant
and more pronounced than from the PowerFood
kit. Doubling the amount of starting material
resulted in approximately a doubling in DNA
concentration for both the PowerFood and the
Magnesil kit as measured by both Qubit and
NanoPhotometer. For a qPCR with an eﬃciency
of 100%, doubling the DNA concentration will
result in 1 decrease in Cq value. As expected,
this eﬀect was observed for DNA extracted with
the PowerFood kit, though surprisingly, this was
not the case for DNA extracted with the Magne-
Sil kit. Increasing the sample volume from 1.5
ml to 3 ml resulted in a 5 unit decrease in Cq
value. This could perhaps be explained by the
fact that some DNA might be left on the para-
magnetic beads, and as such there is a deﬁcit
in the extracted DNA. This could be overcome
by the addition of more DNA as the amount
of lost DNA would theoretically be constant.
This could furthermore explain the 1.2 Cq value
decrease observed when going from 3 ml to 9
ml.
As UHT milk can be stored for up to 6 months
[72], even small amounts of proteolytic enzymes
can be enough to spoil the milk. It is there-
fore essential that the milk used for UHT treat-
ment contains as few proteases as possible. We
found that the lowest possible concentration of
aprX -harbouring bacteria detectable by qPCR
was 15 CFU/reaction which corresponds to 100
CFU/ml extracted from 1.5 ml whole milk. It
should, however, be noted that the gene was
only recoverable in 5 out of 9 replicates and the
Cq value was relatively high. Nevertheless, this
detection limit is notably lower than the detec-
tion limit of 105 CFU/ml found in a previous
study. Furthermore, the DNA used in that study
was extracted from reconstituted skim milk pow-
der using phenol-chloroform [62]. Another study
was able to achieve detection limits of 102 and
104 CFU/ml for P. fragi and P. ﬂuorescens, re-
spectively. The DNA was similarly extracted
from reconstituted skim milk powder followed
by a chloroform wash [10]. The fat content in
skim milk is remarkably lower than that found
in unprocessed raw milk, and the properties of
the milk might furthermore have been altered
upon dehydration.
A comparison between DNA extraction from
cream and from skim milk showed that about
double the amount of DNA was extracted from
skim milk compared to cream [73], making it
diﬃcult to compare detection limits from the
two matrices. Another factor that makes these
method less desirable by the industry is the fact
that they rely on conventional PCR which is
more time and labour consuming than qPCR
due to the need for gel visualisation. The num-
ber of bacteria necessary for high AprX activity
is still debated but are generally believed to be
between 104 and 107 protease producing bacte-
ria/ml [29–33]. The detection limit found in the
current study is thus considerably lower than
==PAGE:28==
Page 25 of 69
that required for optimal AprX activity and fur-
thermore lower than the limit set by Directive
92/46 of the EEC.
The main producers of AprX in raw milk have
been found to be within the Pseudomonas genus,
in particular P. ﬂuorescens, P. lundensis, and
P. fragi [74]. The aprX gene is highly heteroge-
nous between species and variations between
63% and 99% have been reported within P. ﬂu-
orescens [10]. This is in accordance to what was
found in the current study. Furthermore, studies
have found the protein AprA, produced by Pseu-
domonas panacis in milk, to similarily be able
to cause spoilage of UHT milk by denaturation
of e.g. casein, causing increased viscosity and
bitter ﬂavours. MSA revealed that there was
generally a high identity between the primary
structures of aprA and aprX, indicating that
they could be the same protein. However, it did
also reveal a large variation between species with
less than 50% identity from both P. aeruginosa
strains to all other strains. This supports pre-
vious ﬁndings that P. aeruginosa varies greatly
from e.g. P. ﬂuorescens [75]. The large hetero-
geneity of the heat resistant proteases found in
milk can thus make it diﬃcult to design primers
that eﬀectively discover all aprX genes.
The forward primer SM2F used in this study
was designed based on MSA of aprX genes from
5 strains to target conserved regions of the gene.
However, the gene was only recovered in about
75% of the Pseudomonas strains tested and 80%
of the strains that exhibited proteolytic activity
[10]. The primer was furthermore designed for
conventional PCR and the PCR product was
around 800 bp. Generally, amplicon lengths are
longer for conventional PCR as these results
need to be visualised on a gel. For qPCR, how-
ever, a shorter amplicon length is necessary as
long amplicons are diﬃcult to eﬃciently quantify
during qPCR [76]. In the current study, forward
primer SM2F was combined with the JHOO-
R1 reverse primer and the JHOO-P1 probe de-
signed by Jeﬀrey Hoorfar [47] in order to create
a shorter amplicon sequence more suitable for
qPCR. However, out of the four Pseudomonas
control strains, aprX was only detected in P.
ﬂuorescens DSM 50090. Taking this into consid-
eration, we hypothesised that the reverse primer
JHOO-R1 and probe JHOO-P1 might not bind
to the most conserved regions of the gene. Fol-
lowing WGS, possible primer binding sites were
found on each strain. While there was one mis-
match on the probe JHOO-P1 and two mis-
matches on the reverse primer JHOO-R1 for P.
ﬂuorescens DSM 50090, both primers and probe
shared 100% identity with the P. panacis DSM
18529 strain. This was surprising as aprX had
not been retrieved using qPCR on this strain.
In order to assess whether or not the right strain
had been isolated, an API 20 NE test was used.
This revealed that all four strains belonged to
the Pseudomonas genus as expected. Neverthe-
less, only DSM 50090 was identiﬁed correctly
on species level. This is, however, a common
problem with API tests [77] and as such, a more
thorough examination, such as re-sequencing the
genome of P. panacis DSM 18529, would be nec-
essary in order to safely rule out that the correct
strains were used.
Interestingly, while it was the only strain, from
which aprX could be detected using qPCR, no
proteolytic activity was detected from P. ﬂuo-
rescens DSM 50090 following screening for pro-
teolytic activity on SMA at room temperature
or at 5 °C. Marchand et al. and Dufour et al.
similarly reported that several strains did not
exhibit proteolytic activity at room temperature
while the aprX gene was detectable from PCR
[10, 12]. While a varying structure of the gene
could potentially inﬂuence its proteolytic activ-
ity, another explanation could be that expression
of aprX is regulated at transcriptional or transla-
tional level, depending on extrinsic and intrinsic
factors such as quorum sensing, temperature,
and ion content [9, 33, 78, 79]. In a study by
==PAGE:29==
Page 26 of 69
Caldera et al. P. ﬂuorescens DSM 50090 was
found to only exhibit proteolytic activity at 5 °C,
while P. ﬂuorescens DSM 50120 was proteolytic
at both 5 °C and 25 °C [80]. Furthermore, P.
panacis DSM 18529 and P. weihenstephanensis
DSM 29166 have been shown to express prote-
olytic activity at 20 °C [19, 81]. However, all
samples in these studies were incubated in UHT
milk or minimal salt medium while the strains
in the current paper were incubated on SMA
plates.
The LA-F1 and LA-R1 primers and the LA-P1
probe were designed based on conserved regions
of the aprX gene in 5 Pseudomonas strains.
However, they were not able to detect aprX in
any of the four positive control strains. The P.
ﬂuorescens DSM 50120 strain generally varied
notably from the remaining strains and as such,
there were several mismatches from this strain
to all of the primers which could explain the
lack of signal in the qPCR. Unfortunately, the
sequence for P. weihenstephanensis DSM 29166
was not received and as such, it was not possi-
ble to investigate possible primer binding sites
for this strain. However, for the two remain-
ing strains (P. ﬂuorescens DSM 50090 and P.
panacis DSM 18529) ), there was 100% similar-
ity between the strains and the forward primer.
There were three mismatches on the probe for
both strains, as well as 2 mismatches in the 3’
end of the reverse primer in DSM 50090 and 1
mismatch for in the middle of the reverse primer
for DSM 18529. Furthermore, there was a high
G/C content in the probe, which could cause
problems during ampliﬁcation. However, nei-
ther analysis of the qPCR product on a gel or
qPCR using intercalating dye revealed a prod-
uct, indicating that the problem lies elsewhere.
Studies indicate that mismatches in the 3’ end
of a primer generally inﬂuence amplicons more
than mismatches in the 5’ end [82, 83] and as
such, the LA-R1 primer is perhaps not ideal for
P. ﬂuorescens DSM 50090.
In order to quantify the number of bacteria
in the milk, part of the 16S gene was ampliﬁed
using qPCR. While the number of 16S genes
in the sample with 30 ml raw milk was lower
than the number of colonies found on aerobic
count plates, the diﬀerence was not outspoken
and could perhaps be explained by dilution and
rounding errors, although 16S is often notably
higher than plate count due to e.g. the lack of
distinction between live and dead cells [84]. In
the sample with 50 ml spiked pasteurised milk,
the bacterial count should be 2.2 · 105 CFU/ml
according to the spiking level. However, qPCR
revealed that only 1300 16S copies were found.
One explanation for this could be that the 50 ml
of milk were centrifuged at once in a 50 ml Falcon
tube. This repeatedly generated lower amounts
of DNA than pooling milk in an Eppendorf tube,
possibly due to the lower centrifugation speed
and the shape of the tube and as such, part
of the cell-containing pellet was removed along
with the supernatant. The fact that the number
of 16S copies is lower than CFU count for both
the raw milk and the spiked pasteurised milk
sample could indicate that some DNA is lost
during DNA extraction.
The dissociation curve for this sample revealed
two diﬀerent peaks, indicating two diﬀerent 16S
regions. This corresponds to the fact that the
sample was spiked with bacteria from two dif-
ferent phyla. Metagenomic sequencing further-
more revealed that 66% of the bacterial DNA in
the sample with spiked pasteurised milk origi-
nated from Pseudomonas, mainly P. ﬂuorescens.
Surprisingly, no lactococci were found, while
instead 28% of the DNA was classiﬁed as Mi-
crococcus spp. One possible explanation for this
could be that the strain had been incorrectly
identiﬁed or possibly contaminated. Neverthe-
less, the data revealed that the MagneSil kit
could successfully be applied to extract DNA
from mammalian, Gram-positive, and Gram-
negative cells. Generally, extraction of DNA
==PAGE:30==
Page 27 of 69
from Gram-positive bacteria is more diﬃcult
than from Gram-negative bacteria due to the
thick peptidoglycan layer of the Gram-positive
cell wall. In order to overcome this, mechanical
cell lysing is often necessary [85, 86]. DNA ex-
traction by the MagneSil kit did not, however,
seem to diﬀerentiate between Gram-positive and
Gram-negative cells. In fact, while only 10%
of the added bacteria were Gram-positive, al-
most 1/3 of the bacterial DNA was predicted
to be of Gram-positive origin by CCMetagen.
One possible explanation could be that some
Pseudomonas reside in the fat layer which was
removed. Previous studies have demonstrated
that a large fraction of bacteria were recover-
able from the fat layer. In fact, up to 80% of
the bacteria have been found to have separated
into the fat layer within 5 minutes of spiking
[73, 87, 88]. Another explanation might lie in
the fact that the sequencing depth was not suf-
ﬁcient. Nonpareil curves for both forward and
reverse reads from spiked milk revealed that the
sequencing depth of 20 million bp only covered
about 80% of the metagenome. Meanwhile, for
the raw milk sample, only about 75% of the
metagenome was covered. Part of the reason
why the coverage is so low is most likely the
size of the bovine genome; similar to the human
genome, the bovine genome is about 3 · 109 bp
[89].
Sequencing of raw milk resulted in an even
lower coverage and furthermore, only 0.009%
of the metagenome was classiﬁed as bacteria.
A large portion of the microbiome consisted of
lactic acid bacteria in the Firmicutes phylum.
Other dominating phyla included Actinobacte-
ria and Proteobacteria. This supports previous
observations by Quigey et al. who additionally
found Bacteroidetes as a major phylum in the
raw milk. While there were some Bacteroidetes
in the raw milk sample in the current study, this
phylum accounted for only 0.05% of the bacte-
ria in the sample. While previous studies have
found the Pseudomonas genus to be a dominat-
ing component of the microbiome of raw milk [74,
90–92], interestingly, only 2% of the microbiome
in the raw milk sample in the current study con-
sisted of pseudomonads. Furthermore, for both
the raw milk sample and the pasteurised sam-
ple, a large part of the metagenome consisted
of Rhabditida, an order of nematodes which is
often found in milk; especially in temperate cli-
mates [93, 94]. Surprisingly, the majority of the
nematode DNA originated from Wuchereria ban-
crofti which is a human parasite. A recent study
compared the genomes of this organism to that
of Setaria digitata, which is a common parasite
in cattle, and discovered a large similarity be-
tween the two genomes [95]. Based on this, the
species might simply have been misidentiﬁed.
The current study have presented three pos-
sible methods for extraction and puriﬁcation of
DNA from full fat milk including raw milk. We
have furthermore proven that the aprX gene
was recoverable through qPCR. Nevertheless, in
order for the methods to be more widely ap-
plicable, more work is still needed. It was not
possible to design a multiplex qPCR during the
time span for this thesis. However, future work
could include several sets of primers, targeting
a variety of diﬀerent aprX genes in order to
achieve maximum detection of the gene. Fur-
thermore, given the fact that several studies
have found that a notable fraction of the bac-
teria resides in the fat of the milk [73, 87, 88],
removing the fat from the milk could thus po-
tentially decrease the sensitivity of the assay,
resulting in a false sense of security. As such,
future work would need to additionally focus on
the extraction of DNA from milk fat as well as
from the milk itself. Moreover, as proteolytic
activity varies highly, depending on many diﬀer-
ent factors [24], the presence of the aprX gene
does not necessarily translate to the presence
of the protein. In order to examine the pres-
ence of AprX, several diﬀerent methods can be
==PAGE:31==
Page 28 of 69
used, including an enzyme-linked immunoassay
(ELISA) designed with antibodies speciﬁc for
AprX. This has been done previously, although
detection limits were found to be relatively high
(more than 106 CFU/ml) [96, 97]. Future work
could therefore include the development of an
ELISA protocol with a lower detection limit.
5
Conclusion
DNA was successfully extracted using all three
DNA extraction protocols.
The aprX gene
was, however, only recoverable from the P. ﬂuo-
rescens DSM 50090 strain using the developed
qPCR protocol. Due to the large variation in
the gene, a multiplex qPCR with several sets
of primers to target a wider range of the Pseu-
domonas spp. could advantageously be devel-
oped in the future. Metagenomic sequencing of
spiked milk revealed that the MagneSil® KF,
Genomic System could successfully extract DNA
from Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and so-
matic cells. Although some work is yet needed
in order to optimise the method, it could prove
a valuable addition to the toolbox for detection
of spoilage enzymes.

STORAGE CONDITIONS 
The strips and media should be stored at 2-8°C until the 
expiration date indicated on the packaging. 
SPECIMENS (COLLECTION AND PREPARATION) 
API 20 NE is not for use directly with clinical or other 
specimens. 
The microorganisms to be identified must first be iso- 
lated on a suitable culture medium (e.g., Trypticase Soy 
agar) according to standard microbiological techniques. 
INSTRUCTIONS FOR USE 
Oxidase test 
The oxidase test must be performed according to the 
manufacturer's instructions for use. The result should be 
recorded on the result sheet as it is an integral part of the 
final profile (21st identification test). 
Selection of colonies 
API 20 NE should only be used with non-fastidious Gram-
negative rods which do not belong to the Entero-
bacteriaceae.  
NOTE 1 : Some non-enteric Gram-negative rods are 
oxidase negative (S. maltophilia, Acinetobacter...). These 
microorganisms may also be identified with API 20 NE but 
their selection must be based on other bacteriological or 
clinical criteria. 
NOTE 2 : Fastidious organisms having demanding 
nutritional 
requirements 
and 
requiring 
appropriate 
handling precautions (i.e. Brucella and Francisella) are 
not included in the API 20 NE database. Alternative 
procedures must be used to exclude or confirm their 
presence. 
Preparation of the strip 
• Prepare an incubation box, tray and lid, and distribute 
about 5 ml of distilled water or demineralized water  
[or any water without additives or chemicals which may 
release gases (e.g. Cl2, CO2, etc.)] into the bottom of the 
tray to create a humid atmosphere. 
• Record the specimen number on the elongated flap of 
the tray. (Do not record the number on the lid as it may 
be misplaced during the procedure.) 
• Remove the strip from its individual packaging. 
• Place the strip in the incubation box. 
Preparation of the inoculum 
• Open an ampule of API NaCl 0.85 % Medium (2 ml) as 
indicated in the paragraph "Warnings and Precautions" 
of the package insert for this product, or use any tube 
containing 2 ml of 0.85 % physiological saline without 
additives. 
• Using a pipette or PSIpette, pick up 1-4 colonies of 
identical morphology from the agar plate, either by 
suction or by successive touches. It is recommended to 
use young cultures (18-24 hours old). 
• Prepare a suspension with a turbidity equivalent to 
0.5 McFarland. 
This 
suspension 
must 
be

DNA extraction - Standard Operating Procedure
Purpose
The MagneSil® KF, Genomic System (Promega Corporation, Madison, WI, USA) kit can be used for DNA extraction
and puriﬁcation of milk to screen for the presence of the aprX gene. When combined with the KingFisher 700
Magnetic Particle Processor (Thermo Labsystems, Finland), this method can be used to extract DNA from 24
samples simultaneously. Puriﬁed DNA extracted from milk generally has a A260/A280 ratio of between 0.6 and 2.2.
The following protocol describes the steps required for pre-treatment and extraction of bacterial DNA from milk.
Materials
• Eppendorf tubes (2 per sample)
• Microcentrifuge
• MagneSil® KF, Genomic System kit (Promega Corporation, Madison, WI, USA)
• KingFisher 700 Magnetic Particle Processor (Thermo Labsystems, Finland)
• KingFisher plate + comb
• 10 µl inoculation loops
• Micropipettes and tips
Important notes before starting
• All steps should be carried out at room temperature.
• If using spiked milk, make sure to centrifuge and remove fat and supernatant shortly after spiking.
• If using spiked milk, spike to desired concentration before protocol starts.
Isolation of bacterial DNA from milk
1. Transfer 1.5 ml milk (spiked or not spiked) to a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube
2. Centrifuge samples at 17,000 x g for 1 minute.
3. Carefully remove fat layer with a 10 µl inoculation loop by inserting it under the fat in the top of the tube and
then scoop out the fat in one smooth movement.
4. Pour out supernatant to only leave pellet.
5. Centrifuge pellet and remaining supernatant at 17,000 x g for 1 minute.
6. Carefully remove remaining supernatant using a pipette.
7. Add 200 µl lysis buﬀer to pellet and mix by pipetting up and down until pellet is dissolved (about 10 times
depending on size of pellet).
8. Shake paramagnetic beads to evenly distribute them and transfer 25 µl to the three top wells of a column on a
KingFisher plate for each sample. Make sure to shake magnetic beads in between transferring to ensure even
distribution of the beads.
9. Transfer 75 µl of a sample with lysis buﬀer to each of the three top wells in a column.
10. Transfer 100 µl salt wash buﬀer to wells in rows 4 and 5 for each sample.
11. Transfer 100 µl alcohol wash buﬀer to wells in rows 6 and 7 for each sample.
12. Transfer 100 µl nuclease free water to wells in row 8 for each sample.
==PAGE:48==
13. Place the plate in the KingFisher 700 Magnetic Particle Processor, add comb and start the machine with the
programme described below:
(a) The sample is incubated with particles in well A for 3 minutes.
(b) The sample is incubated with particles in well B for 3 minutes.
(c) The sample is incubated with particles in well C for 3 minutes.
(d) The particles are washed in well D.
(e) The particles are washed in well E.
(f) The particles are washed in well F.
(g) The particles are washed in well G.
(h) The puriﬁed DNA is released into distilled water in well H.
(i) The particles are removed and returned into well F.
14. After the machine is ﬁnished, transfer the DNA from wells in row number 8 to a clean, nuclease free 1.5 ml
Eppendorf tube (one for each sample).
15. Following DNA extraction, the DNA should be stored at -20 °C until further use.
