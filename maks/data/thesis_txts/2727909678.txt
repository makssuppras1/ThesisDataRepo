Abstract 
The escalating demand for protein, coupled with the adverse environmental consequences associated 
with feed and food production, presents significant challenges in the face of population growth. These 
challenges impose immense pressure on natural resources, exacerbating their scarcity. To address this 
pressing demand, the exploration of innovative processes becomes imperative. The product alfalfa 
brown juice that is derived from the processing of the forage legume alfalfa thanks to its physico-
chemical properties stands out as a material that could be used for alternative protein production.  
This master's thesis presents a comprehensive life cycle assessment conducted to assess the 
environmental sustainability of three scenarios for the valorization of alfalfa brown juice. The three 
scenarios considered are its use as a substitute fertilizer, energy recovery, and soybean substitution 
through microbial protein production. The primary objective of this thesis is to determine the most 
environmentally beneficial option among the three scenarios by evaluating their respective life cycles. 
These life cycles are simulated using the EaseTech software, employing data obtained from laboratory 
studies and scientific reports. 
The characterization of the results and a thorough contribution analysis show that all three scenarios 
bring environmental benefits to the overall systems. Specifically, scenario 1 shows the highest savings 
in carbon emissions, amounting to -98.62 kg CO2. However, it raises concerns regarding emissions 
after the 10-month storage, contributing to soil degradation and agricultural non-point source pollution. 
Scenario 2, involving energy recovery, exhibits savings in climate change and the use of water and 
energy demand, mainly due to the displacement of more energy-intensive processes. However, certain 
energy-demanding steps, such as anaerobic digestion, introduce environmental burdens related to 
ecotoxicity and water use. Scenario 3, the soybean substitution, proves highly beneficial across multiple 
impact categories, offering low CO2 emissions and reduced land use changes. Additionally, it mitigates 
freshwater eutrophication by decreasing the use of P-containing fertilizers and minimizes freshwater 
ecotoxicity by reducing excessive S and Cl- in the soil. 
Based on the aforementioned results, the energy recovery scenario emerges as the most sustainable 
and viable option for the effective utilization of alfalfa brown juice. It not only achieves energy savings 
but also demonstrates reductions in water usage while effectively contributing to climate change 
mitigation without burdening the quality of the ecosystem. 
Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis is conducted to assess the impact of various parameters on the 
results. It reveals the significance of nutrient distribution in the soil and CH4 production efficiency in 
Scenario 2. On the other hand, scenario 3 demonstrates minor sensitivity to soybean substitution and 
microbial protein production efficiencies. 
Lastly, two scenario analyses explore the potential environmental impacts  by alternating different 
factors. The first scenario examines nutrient distribution by changing soil types, and the second 
explores wind energy implementation to supply energy-demanding processes. All of them present 
changes compared to their initial scenarios, showcasing once more how much the selected parameters 
contribute to the overall system.  
 
Keywords: protein demand, environmental impacts, valorization methods, alternative protein 
production, biofertilizer, anaerobic digestion, microbial protein production, contribution analysis, 
sensitivity analysis, scenario analysis, sustainability.

1. Introduction  
Intensive livestock production heavily relies on protein meals as essential feed ingredients. The global 
demand for protein meals is primarily met through soybean meal production, with an anticipated annual 
growth rate of 1.6 % until 2027 (Spiller et al., 2020). It is known that the most prominent soybean meal 
producers worldwide are the USA, Brazil, and Argentina. However, the expansion of soybean 
cultivation, has raised concerns regarding the environmental consequences associated with land use 
change. These concerns encompass greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and biodiversity loss (Spiller et 
al., 2020). 
The projected increase in protein demand and the accompanying environmental impacts related to 
feed and food production presents significant challenges due to population growth. These challenges 
place immense pressure on land and other natural resources, exacerbating their scarcity. Addressing 
this mounting demand necessitates the exploration of innovative processes (Bákonyi et al., 2020; 
Spiller et al., 2020). 
Acknowledging these concerns, the European Commission has actively supported the advancement 
of bio-based products, with specific emphasis on the production of leaf protein concentrate (LPC) 
derived from green biomass (European Commission, 2022). The Commission has also taken substantial 
strides in assessing the progress made in implementing the 2018 EU Bioeconomy Strategy, updating 
its strategic program in 2022, to align with the latest policy developments under the European Green 
Deal (European Commission, 2022). These dedicated support mechanisms and research initiatives have 
yielded promising results, leading to a notable increase in the number of green biorefineries operating 
at pilot or commercial scales across Europe (Barna et al., 2022). 
Among the suitable crops for LPC production, alfalfa (Medicago sativa) stands out as a prevalent 
forage material that can be easily adapted to this purpose (Barna et al., 2022).The isolation of leaf 
protein, in the form of LPC, involves the extraction of both insoluble proteins (such as those found in 
mitochondria, chloroplasts, nucleoprotein, and cell walls) and soluble proteins (including the soluble 
fraction of mitochondrial proteins, chloroplast matrix, and cytoplasmic proteins). To achieve this, 
thermal treatment of the green juice obtained by pressing the fresh biomass is necessary to coagulate 
these protein types. Consequently, this process generates a by-product known as "brown juice" or 
deproteinized plant juice (DPJ), which accounts for a significant portion, approximately 50% of the 
processed green biomass (Bákonyi et al., 2020) 
Brown juice valorization is currently a significant concern, as it is frequently viewed as a waste 
product and underutilized. Despite being rich in proteins, it is often discarded or utilized for bio-
fertilizer, or animal feed due to its limited commercial value. The lack of efficient utilization of valuable 
resources and the generation of waste can cause environmental and economic inefficiencies. 
Additionally, it can hinder the development of circular and sustainable value chains for the production 
of alfalfa. By enhancing awareness and conducting research on the potential applications and benefits 
of brown juice valorization, new revenue streams, and industry-wide improvements can be realized 
(Bákonyi N. et. al., 2020).

2. State-of-the-Art and research objectives 
The field of biorefinery research has seen relatively few studies focusing on the various valorisation 
methods of DPJ and its production process, particularly in relation to microbial protein production.  
For instance, Leonardi (2020) explored the potential of brown juice derived from grass protein 
extraction within the context of a green biorefinery. The study primarily investigated the anaerobic 
digestion of the product, resulting in the production of methane (CH4) and digestate, which are valuable 
sources of carbon (C) and nutrients. Furthermore, batch tests were conducted to assess the feasibility 
of utilizing the digestate as a nutrient source for methane-oxidizing bacteria (MOB), aiming to generate 
alternative microbial proteins. Similarly, Mundeling (2021) evaluated the viability of cultivating 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae kveik yeast and MOB, using both raw and digested brown juice. 
Based on the foregoing, the aim of this research study is to conduct a comparative life cycle 
assessment (LCA) and comprehensively assess three different strategies for managing brown juice in 
the green protein industry, with a focus on their potential environmental implications. By evaluating 
the effectiveness and feasibility of alternative approaches, this study aims to provide valuable insights 
into promoting environmentally friendly practices within the industry, and seeks to answer a 
fundamental question: which method offers the most promising and sustainable way to utilize alfalfa 
brown juice effectively? The following processes will be assessed: 
• Fertilizer use: this represents the conventional approach to utilizing brown juice, where it is used as 
a bio-fertilizer. 
• Energy recovery: the organic matter present in brown juice can be utilized for biogas production 
through anaerobic digestion. This process offers an eco-friendly way to treat waste and generate 
renewable energy that can be further valorised. 
• Microbial protein production: alfalfa brown juice contains residual proteins that can be extracted and 
refined to produce high-quality protein ingredients. They have the potential to be used as additives 
in food products or as nutritional supplements in animal feed (Kobbi et al., 2017). 
 
Specifically, the main objectives are to assess the environmental performance and overall 
sustainability of each valorization pathway for alfalfa brown juice and determine which one of them is 
the most beneficial in terms of sustainability. Moreover, the study aims to identify potential hotspots 
within the pathways that require process optimization and improved data collection, through sensitivity 
analysis and also evaluate the scenarios to determine the robustness of the LCA. Lastly, the study seeks 
to provide recommendations for improving the sustainability of the valorization pathways, including 
identifying potential practices and addressing research needs. An introduction into LCA is  presented 
in section 3.1.

3.1 LCA 
LCA is a widely accepted methodology that enables the evaluation of the environmental impacts 
associated with a product or service. LCA involves measuring the consumption of resources and the 
release of emissions and pollutants throughout the product's entire life cycle, including the extraction of 
raw materials, production, use, and disposal. The assessment can be conducted either for the entire 
product lifespan or within a specific time frame (Varling, 2022).  
A thorough LCA usually includes evaluations of the ecological, social, and economic impacts of the 
product, including impacts on human health and resource depletion. However, the specific impact 
categories may vary depending on the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) methodology used in the 
analysis. By conducting a comprehensive LCA, companies and individuals can identify the areas where 
the product's environmental performance can be improved and make informed decisions to reduce their 
environmental impact (Varling, 2022). LCA consists of four key stages that are interconnected and 
iterative in nature. They are as follows (Figure 1): 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1st Stage - Goal and scope: The first phase is the goal and scope definition: the goal definition is the 
basis of the LCA and defines among other things the study’s application, limitations, decision context, 
target audience and influential actors. The scope definition identifies the product or system under 
investigation and i.e., defines and describes the FU, physical and temporal system boundaries, data 
requirements and modelling choices (Muralikrishna et. al., 2017). 
2nd Stage - Life cycle inventory: The second phase is life cycle inventory (LCI), which is responsible 
for collecting data and constructing models in line with the goal and scope definition established in phase 
one. There are two main types of LCA modelling: attributional and consequential, and the modelling 
choices must be applied consistently (Muralikrishna et. al., 2017; Varling, 2022). 
3rd Stage - LCIA: The third phase is  the LCIA, which converts the data collected in the LCI phase 
into impact category results (Muralikrishna et. al., 2017).  
4th Stage - Interpretation: The final phase is the interpretation phase, where the results of the LCIA 
are analysed and interpreted within a broader context. This often involves a contribution analysis, 
sensitivity analysis, and uncertainty analysis, in addition to a discussion of the results (Muralikrishna et. 
al., 2017; Varling, 2022).

3.2 Goal and scope 
This LCA aims to assess the environmental implications  of three valorization methods applied to 
alfalfa brown juice and determine the most sustainable practice amongst them.  
According to the European Commission (2019), the study falls under the consequential LCA (C-
LCA), allowing for the examination of systemic potential changes and the wider environmental effects 
of implementing each valorisation pathway.  
The FU chosen for this study is one metric ton of alfalfa brown juice treated using the valorization 
processes, in the course of one year. The study focuses on the geographic and temporal boundaries 
specific to Denmark. The characteristics of the brown juice, including its composition and other 
relevant details, are provided in Section 3.5.1. 
The primary focus of this study revolves around assessing different utilization methods of brown 
juice. The investigation begins with the alfalfa brown juice as an initial product and concludes with the 
attainment of the final product (fertilizer substitution, energy recovery and the substitution of soybean). 
It is worth noting that the production process of alfalfa brown juice is excluded from the scope of this 
study, as it does not align with the study's objectives. Detailed illustrations depicting the system

3.4 LCIA interpretation  
The interpretation of the results begins with their characterisation and a contribution analysis, which 
includes all the impact categories as mentioned in Table 1. They are further categorized based on their 
respective fields to climate change, human health  ecosystem quality, and resources management. The 
contribution analysis helps identify the key factors that have the most significant influence on the 
overall environmental impact of each scenario. But in order to see the full effect of the results it is 
imperial to upscale all the findings according to the annual production of alfalfa brown juice in 
Denmark, which is 200,000 m3. 
Furthermore, to gain further insights into the results, a sensitivity analysis is conducted. The main 
objective of conducting a sensitivity analysis is to assess the influence of variations in input parameters 
on the overall system. This analysis helps identify the key variables that have the greatest impact on 
the results. By understanding the sensitivity of the system to these variables, further investigation can 
be conducted to explore their underlying factors and implications. (Parpia et al. 2020). 
Furthermore, it should be highlighted that the sensitivity analysis covers both robust parameters in 
addition to the system's uncertain parameters. This is due to the fact that even seemingly insignificant 
ones  may have a substantial effect on the outcome.  
The sensitivity analysis is carried out with the help of a one-at-a-time perturbation analysis of  +10% 
of the initial values of the most influential parameters. The selection for the parameters investigated is 
directly based on the results of the contribution analysis so some of them might appear in more than 
one scenario, showcasing the interconnectedness between the processes. The results are then analysed 
according to the sensitivity ratio (SR). The following Equation 1 is used to estimate the SR (Varling, 
2022).

3.5 System modelling per life cycle stage 
3.5.1 Alfalfa brown juice 
The alfalfa plant, known as Medicago sativa and commonly referred to as lucerne, is a perennial 
flowering legume belonging to the Fabaceae family. Renowned as the "king of forage," alfalfa is highly 
regarded for its numerous advantages, including its exceptional leaf protein quality, adaptability, 
nutritional value, palatability, and productivity. In comparison to other crops such as peas, soybeans, and 
wheat, alfalfa exhibits a remarkable capacity to produce crude protein content that is two to four times 
higher, making it an extremely promising candidate for the production of  LPC (Bákonyi N. et al., 2020).

To obtain LPC, the solid and soluble proteins from the leaves are extracted, requiring thermal 
treatment of the green juice obtained from the fresh biomass (Figure 2). This process results in a 
brown liquid by-product referred to as brown juice, which can yield up to 500 gr/kg of fresh alfalfa 
biomass. 
 Brown juice, as also known as DPJ  contains high levels of protein, phenols, and micronutrients, 
and can be utilized in several ways such as a fertilizer, animal feed, microbial growth medium, and 
in vitro rhizogenesis (Bákonyi N. et. al, 2020). The utilization of brown juice in animal feed and 
health supplements offers significant benefits, including waste reduction, decreased environmental 
impact, and the creation of a new revenue stream for the alfalfa green protein industry. Denmark, for 
instance, produces around 200,000 m3 of brown juice annually (Thomsen et al., 2004). 
 Brown juice is also employed as a fertilizer for green crops due to its nutrient-rich composition, 
particularly containing valuable elements like potassium (K) and nitrogen (N). However, excessive 
N application poses environmental challenges when not absorbed by plants during the autumn and 
winter seasons, leading to potential groundwater contamination from nitrate (NO3-) runoff. 
Consequently, more stringent regulations have been implemented to govern the use of these residues 
as fertilizers. In Denmark, there is a prohibition on applying brown juice as a fertilizer between 
October 1st and February 1st  (Thomsen et al., 2004). 
The brown juice assessed in this study is derived from the grass alfalfa which is supplied by the 
company Biotest Aps in Middelfart, Denmark. The extraction and production procedures of this 
brown juice have been detailed by  Leonardi, 2020.  A list of the physico-chemical properties is 
presented in the following table (Table 2).

3.5.2 Scenario 1 – Use as a bio-fertilizer 
 
 
In this scenario, brown juice is employed as a bio-fertilizer, with its macro-nutrients, namely N, 
phosphorus (P), and K substituting the need for conventional chemical fertilizers. Organic fertilizers, 
which are naturally occurring mineral sources containing essential plant nutrients, offer several 
advantages over synthetic fertilizers. They mitigate issues associated with synthetic fertilizers, reduce 
the frequency of fertilizer application to maintain soil fertility, and ensure a gradual release of nutrients 
into the soil solution, thereby promoting balanced nutrient availability for healthy crop growth (Shaji 
et al., 2021). 
The utilization of by-products and waste materials as fertilizers constitutes a fundamental element of 
sustainable agriculture. It is noteworthy that alfalfa is a perennial crop harvested multiple times per 
year, usually from early spring to late October, depending on weather conditions. To maintain a 
continuous supply of alfalfa, a consistent production of brown juice distributed evenly throughout the 
months from March to October has been assumed. Therefore, it is recommended to apply brown juice 
as a fertilizer during the months of March and April, as studies indicate a significant increase in crop 
yield during this period (Landbrug et al., 2018; Leonardi, 2020). 
For effective utilization of 1 ton of brown juice (FU),  25% of the total production is allocated as 
fertilizer during the specified period, while reserving the remaining 75% for future use (Leonardi, 
2020). The duration during which alfalfa can be utilized is depicted in the table below (Table 3):

To ensure the optimal preservation of the brown juice, it is stored in stainless steel tanks that have 
a capacity of 5 m3 each. These tanks effectively prevent any potential losses and are used to store 
both the portion intended for immediate use, as well as the portion intended for future use. Once the 
brown juice is stored, it is transported to an agricultural land, assuming a distance of 50 km and no 
potential leaks during the process. 
The remaining 75% of the brown juice, which is intended for future use, is transported to a separate 
storage facility after being stored in tanks. These tanks are kept at the facility for 10 months until 
their next use. It is considered that the storage facility is conveniently located near the farm where 
the fertilizer will eventually be applied. This proximity eliminates the need for any further 
transportation following the 10-month storage period. 
During the 10-month storage period, natural breakdown processes occur in the brown juice. This 
biological decomposition leads to the transfer of nutrients, particularly N and P, between different 
fractions and chemical forms. As a result of this degradation process, emissions of CH4, ammonium 
(NH4+) and dinitrogen monoxide (N2O) are released into the atmosphere when the storage tank is 
opened. For the purposes of this study, these emissions were assumed to be similar to those observed 
in the decomposition of other biodegradable materials, such as animal manure (Girard et al., 2013). 
Similarly, for the simplifications of the simulations,  the physical and chemical characteristics of the 
brown juice remain unchanged during this degradation process. The specific emissions resulting 
from this degradation will be discussed in detail in the “Results and discussion’’ section of this study. 
In conventional chemical fertilizers, nutrients are in a mineral form for easy absorption by plants. 
On the other hand, organic fertilizers, like brown juice, require the nutrients to be biodegraded in the 
soil before plants can use them. This process can lead to nutrient losses and may not fully substitute 
all the nutrients provided by traditional fertilizers (Mundeling, 2021). 
Organic fertilizers, particularly in relation to N, present a challenge as a portion of it can transform 
into potent GHGs like N2 or N2O, which are released into the atmosphere. Thus, according to 
Mundeling, 2021 it is assumed that the P and K nutrients in brown juice could be effective 
alternatives to conventional fertilizers, at a 1:1 ratio. However, approximately 70% of the N content 
in brown juice could potentially replace traditional N sources. 
The significance of soils has gained increasing recognition among planners, land users, and 
researchers when addressing global challenges such as food security, climate change, biodiversity 
decline, and land degradation (Adhikari, 2013). The type of soil plays a crucial role in the distribution 
of nutrients within the soil and the subsequent emissions from plant uptake into the air. In Denmark, 
sandy clay is the dominant soil type, occupying a substantial portion of the central and southeastern 
regions, while coarse sand prevails in the western part of the country (Adhikari, 2013).

The initial simulations in this study are conducted with the sandy clay soil type in mind,

gain insights into the behavior and impact of these nutrients in the sandy clay soil type. 
 
3.5.3 Scenario 2 – Energy recovery  
Following the transportation of 1 ton of alfalfa brown juice to the anaerobic digester facility (with 
an assumed distance of 50 km for the purpose of simulation simplification), the brown juice serves 
as the feedstock for the anaerobic digestion process conducted at 35°C, mesophilic conditions. 
During this process, the organic matter present in the brown juice undergoes degradation, resulting 
in the production of two main products: biogas and digestate, as biogas is a renewable energy 
resource with high energy content, while digestate functions as a plant bio-fertilizer in agricultural 
practices. It is important to note that the simulations do not consider the uptake of nutrients by the 
biogas-producing bacteria, assuming that the entire nutrient content of the brown juice is transferred 
to the digestate (Mundeling, 2021; Leonardi, 2020). 
The digestate is collected in a stainless-steel tank and transported for a distance of 50 km to the 
designated agricultural area, where it is utilized as a fertilizer. The utilization of digestate as a 
fertilizer promotes nutrient recycling and serves as a substitute for mineral fertilizers. This 
substitution helps reduce the GHG emissions associated with the use of chemical fertilizers, as 
highlighted by Santamaria-Fernandez et al. (2020). The application process for digestate on land is 
similar to that of raw brown juice, but it should be noted that the behavior of digestate differs. 
According to the regulations enforced by Danish Ministry of  Food Agriculture and Fisheries, in

The biogas produced from the anaerobic digestion process consists of 60% CH4, while the 
remaining 40% is composed of impurities such as carbon dioxide (CO2), hydrogen sulphide (H2S), 
and N2 in minor amounts. These impurities, particularly H2S and N2, are toxic and highly corrosive, 
posing a potential risk to the combined heat and power (CHP) units (Angelidaki et al., 2018). The 
biogas yield, based on previous lab-scale anaerobic digestion experiments conducted under 
mesophilic conditions using brown juice, is reported to be 120.02 mL CH4/g VS. 
After leaving the anaerobic digester, the biogas is transferred to a biogas upgrading facility, where 
water scrubbing is employed to increase the calorific value of the biogas and convert it to a higher 
fuel standard by removing the biogas pollutants (Angelidaki et al., 2018). It should be noted that a 
certain percentage of the produced biogas (2.7% according to Khoshnevisan et al., 2020b) is lost 
during transportation to the biogas upgrading facility. 
To achieve high-quality CH4, the water scrubber uses water as a solvent at high pressure to remove 
CO2 from the biogas. The aqueous CO2 solution is then regenerated by rapidly reducing the pressure, 
allowing the CO2 to be released back into its gaseous form. The water used in the process is 
recirculated, resulting in overall low water usage. The biogas upgrading process achieves a CH4 
purity of 98% with an overall CO2 removal efficiency of 98.3%. However, it is important to note that 
there is a 1.3 % pure CH4 loss during the upgrading process (Varling et al., 2023b). The CO2 is 
emitted into the atmosphere. 
The purified CH4 obtained from the biogas upgrading process is then utilized as a substitute for 
natural gas. It is estimated that the substitution rate of CH4 is 91%, indicating that the amount of the 
energy demand previously met by natural gas is now supplied by the purified CH4. The remaining 
9% accounts for distribution losses during the transportation and delivery of the gas (Varling et al., 
2023b). 
Before being utilized for energy recovery, the purified CH4 is compressed to a pressure of 80 bars. 
This compression is necessary to match the high-pressure natural gas grid in Denmark, ensuring 
compatibility and efficient distribution of the gas throughout the network (Varling et al., 2023b).

3.5.4 Scenario 3 - Microbial protein production 
 
In the third scenario, the digestate produced from the anaerobic digestion plant is employed for the 
cultivation of microbial proteins. To gain a comprehensive understanding of the process, it is 
recommended to refer to Leonardi’s (2020)  master thesis, which provides a detailed and thorough 
description.  
The first steps of the process are the same as in the initial phase, the untreated raw brown juice is 
transferred to the biogas production facility, where it undergoes anaerobic digestion under 
mesophilic conditions (refer to Section 3.5.3.). The raw biogas produced then undergoes a water 
scrubbing process to remove impurities, mainly CO2. This purification step is necessary to obtain 
purified CH4, which is suitable for the growth of Methane Oxidizing Bacteria (MOB). MOB are a 
group of aerobic organisms that have the unique ability to biochemically oxidize CH4. As they carry 
out this oxidation process, they internally accumulate N in the form of proteins (Leonardi, 2020). 
According to Mundeling (2021), approximately 97.2% of the produced digestate is allocated for 
use as an organic fertilizer. The remaining 2.8% undergoes an initial pre-treatment process, which 
involves centrifugation and pasteurization. The supernatant obtained from the centrifugation step is 
collected and transported a distance of 50 km to the designated agricultural land, where it is utilized 
as a bio-fertilizer. 
During the pasteurization process, the liquid is filtered and subjected to heat at 70°C. Following 
pasteurization, the product is diluted with water to achieve a desired N-NH4 concentration of 28 
mg/L. This concentration is aimed at matching the ammonium level found in the dAMS control, 
which is a synthetic medium that uses ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) as its N source.

The subsequent phase of the process involves the production of microbial protein. This step relies 
on aeration, where ambient air serves as the source of oxygen (O2) for the aerobic reactor. As a result, 
there is no need for the separation or purification of air. During the microbial protein production, 
phosphate is introduced in the form of  monopotassium phosphate (KH2PO4), with the assumption 
that the quantity matches that used in Mundeling's experiment, specifically 1.36 kg. 
Moreover, CH4 is easily oxidised by methanotrophs using an enzyme called methane 
monooxygenase (MMO), but in order to have a faster oxidation process the particulate MMO 
enzyme requires the presence of copper (Cu) in concentrations greater than 1 µmol/g (cell dry 
weight) (Leonardi, 2020). Aeration is facilitated by compressed air, as well as the CH4 generated in 
the earlier stages of the process. It is important to note that for simulation simplification purposes, 
no transportation burdens and energy losses associated with CH4 are considered. 
After the cultivation phase, the biomass undergoes centrifugation and spray drying as the final 
processes before it can be used as a substitute for soybean meal. During centrifugation, the rejected 
water is collected and recirculated back into the system for reuse in the dilution of the digestate. This 
recycling process eliminates the need for external water inputs, promoting resource efficiency within 
the system (Mundeling, 2021; Leonardi, 2020). 
Following the completion of all the processes, an amount of 3.7 kg of biomass is now available for 
use as a feed alternative to conventional soybean cultivation, in the production of soybean meal. This 
substitution contributes to reducing reliance on traditional soybean farming practices. Notably, the 
biomass exhibits a protein content of 47.9%. The Ecoinvent process used to simulate the substitution 
is named  ‘Soybean, soybean production, Row’,  and land use changes are  taken into consideration.

4.2. Contribution analysis 
4.2.1 Climate change impact category  
In the context of the climate change impact category, all three scenarios show overall environmental 
savings/FU, as depicted in the figure above (Figure 6). Notably, the energy recovery scenario 
demonstrates an overall reduction of -65.08 kg CO2-eq. While certain processes within this scenario 
necessitate the consumption of electricity from external sources (conventional energy-demanding 
technologies) and other resources such as H2O for biogas upgrading, resulting in CO2 and CH4 emissions, 
the substitution of natural gas (- 45.22 kg CO2-eq) and the utilization of the resulting brown juice 
digestate (-145.83 kg CO2-eq) contribute to the overall environmental benefit of the system. 
Specifically, the energy recovery aspect of the system generates resource savings, primarily through 
displacing more energy-intensive methods, like fossil fuel-based energy generation. By using electricity 
and heat generated by gas as a renewable energy source, the climate change impact is mitigated (Styles 
et al., 2022). 
On the other hand, the soybean substitution scenario exhibits savings, amounting to -20.21 kg CO2-eq. 
This outcome is expected, as the substitution process, in addition to anaerobic digestion, involves 
electricity consumption for pre-and post-treatment, as well as the production of microbial protein  
(Leonardi, 2020). However, the acquisition of the final product (-59.34 kg CO2-eq) and the use of the 
produced digestate as fertilizer (-142.77 kg CO2-eq) contribute to the overall benefit of the system. The

==PAGE:29==

substitution of soybean primarily stems from its ability to reduce reliance on conventional agricultural 
practices associated with soybean cultivation (use of  fertilizers), contributing to lower GHG  emissions. 
For scenario 1, the emissions resulting from the degradation of brown juice over the 10-month period 
are minimal, amounting to 3.93 kg CO2-eq. But the substitution of brown juice from chemical fertilizers 
brings savings in the climate change impact category (-167.38 kg CO2-eq). 
Furthermore, it is clear from Table 4 that using raw brown juice as a fertilizer provides greater 
substitution benefits compared to using the digestate. This discrepancy can be attributed to the varying 
N substitution rates in different scenarios, as mentioned previously . This observation calls for further 
investigation, particularly in terms of sensitivity analysis. For detailed information regarding the results, 
refer to Table 4. 
 
4.2.2 Human health impact category 
 
The two graphs presented above (Figure 7) illustrate the environmental impacts of the assessed 
scenarios in two health-related categories: cancer and non-carcinogenic effects. Upon observing the 
graphs, both categories display a similar pattern, with consistent values across all three scenarios. 
Specifically, the human health impact category related to cancer demonstrates values, ranging from -
1.89 to -2.54*10-7 DALYs. In the non-carcinogenic impact category, the total values range from 1.17*10-
6 to 1.67 * 10-6 DALYs. 
The values can be attributed to the fact that the substitution of soybean reduces the use of pesticides 
and other chemicals associated with conventional soybean cultivation, promoting soil health, and

4.3 Comparative assessment of the three scenarios  
The comparative assessment of the three scenarios sheds light on their respective environmental 
impacts, enabling us to conclude which scenario is the most promising in terms of sustainability. 
Scenario 1 demonstrates overall environmental savings in the climate change impact category; however, 
it falls short in other impact categories, particularly ecosystem quality. The excessive use of brown juices 
as a bio-fertilizer in this scenario could potentially lead to ecotoxicity risks in freshwater ecosystems. 
On the other hand, scenario 2 displays substantial savings in energy demands through efficient biogas 
generation and utilization. By substituting natural gas, scenario 2 significantly reduces the climate 
change impact. Additionally, the utilization of brown juice digestate as a substitute for chemical

After conducting a comprehensive assessment of all three scenarios, the energy recovery scenario 
emerges as the most sustainable option for brown juice valorization. It achieves savings in energy 
demands, demonstrates water use savings, and contributes to reducing the climate change impact, all 
while avoiding substantial burdens on ecosystem quality. 
 
4.4 Sensitivity analysis 
A one-at-a-time perturbation analysis is conducted to examine a total of 7 parameters. Their 
selection for analysis was based on the previous contribution analysis, which identified parameters 
with significant impacts on the overall results. It is worth noting that some of the parameters are the 
same in some scenarios, to mostly identify the interconnectedness between the processes. For a 
detailed description of the methodology used in the sensitivity analysis, please refer to Section 4.5. 
Overall, the outcomes of the systems are influenced by several parameters, particularly the 
distribution of C, N, and P in the soil. The composition of the soil plays a crucial role in this regard, 
and a detailed discussion on this matter will be presented in the scenario analysis. However, prior to 
delving into the scenario analysis, it is essential to determine the sensitivity of these parameters. 
Furthermore, it is necessary to explore the substitution rate of N due to the observed disparity 
between the substitution rates of raw brown juice and the digestate produced during anaerobic 
digestion. Hence, these parameters are significant for investigation in the sensitivity analysis, as they 
have a direct impact. 
Moreover, the contribution analysis reveals a positive contribution of soybean substitution to the 
overall system. This outcome primarily arises from the protein content generated during microbial

4.5.1. Implications in the distribution of nutrients in the soil 
The findings from both the contribution analysis and sensitivity analysis revealed that the 
distribution of N, C, and P within the soil and other parts of the atmosphere plays a significant role 
in the overall environmental impact of all three scenarios. These parameters have a notable influence 
on the entire system, indicating their importance in shaping the outcomes and potential 
environmental consequences. 
Considering the initial simulations were conducted with sandy clay soil in mind, it becomes 
intriguing to explore the distribution of chemicals from biofertilizers in a different soil type, which 
leads to variations in the distribution of key chemicals. The simulations are performed using the 
EaseTech process "Mineral Fertilizer Use on Land, Coarse Sand Soil," where the distribution 
percentages of N, C, and P were simulated using a one-dimensional deterministic agro-ecosystem 
model. 
 
The results of these changes reveal that in all three scenarios, the application of either raw brown 
juice or digestate as a fertilizer in coarse sand soil leads to an overall  increase of the load within the 
climate change impact category (Figure 16). Specifically, for scenario 1, the total CO2 emissions are 
equal to  -52.8 kg CO2/FU, compared to -98.62 kg CO2-eq/FU in the initial scenario, resulting in 
approximately a 55 % increase (28% for Scenario 2, and 19.4% for Scenario 3). 
These alterations can likely be attributed to differences in organic matter content, as clay soils have 
different organic matter content, providing them with a greater nutrient-holding capacity and 
enhancing their ability to retain and exchange nutrients compared to coarse sand soil (Dong et al., 
2021). Additionally, variations in particle size contribute to different water retention capabilities, 
which, in turn, affect nutrient retention in the soil (Dong et al., 2021).

4.5.2. CH4 recovery and transition towards renewable energy sources 
The green transition is the process of shifting from an economy, heavily reliant on fossil fuels and 
non-renewable resources, to one that embraces renewable energy sources, circular economy 
principles, and sustainable practices. This transformation is motivated by the urgent need to curtail 
greenhouse gas emissions, which drive climate change, and to safeguard natural resources for future 
generations. The pursuit of a secure, reliable, and eco-friendly energy supply has prompted nations 
worldwide to strive towards achieving those sustainable goals (Jensen, 2022).  
Denmark, known for its strong commitment to renewable energy development, is currently 
experiencing a notable shift towards valorising wind energy. Despite being one of the smallest Nordic 
countries, the country presents favorable conditions such as a suitable climate, flat terrain, and an 
extensive coastline, which contribute to substantial wind resources. As a result, wind energy has 
become a prominent component in the danish renewable energy landscape, leading to an impressive 
60 % increase in wind energy capacity between 2010 and 2021. The trajectory indicates that wind 
power will continue to be the primary contributor, especially with the expansion of offshore wind 
energy (Jensen, 2022; International Trade Administration, 2022).  
By 2015, wind power already accounted for over 40% of Denmark's electricity consumption, and 
the country aims to increase this figure to 50% by 2030. To address the urgent challenges posed by 
global warming and energy security, the danish government took  significant steps in 2020 by 
enacting the Climate Act, which sets ambitious targets for reducing GHG emissions by 70 % by 
2030, using 1990 as a baseline. Additionally, the country strives to maintain its leading position in 
various low-carbon technologies and systems. And ultimately the danish government has established 
the long-term goal of achieving complete self-reliance on renewable energy by 2050, including all 
energy demands such as electricity, heating, industry, and transportation (Danish Energy Agency, n. 
d.).  
Thus, the transition to transitioning to wind power for the methane recovery systems is an 
intriguing development to assess in this section. It is known that wind power stands out as one of the 
cleanest and most environmentally friendly energy sources, producing no carbon emissions or air 
pollution. Using wind power exclusively in Scenario 2 and 3 could potentially reduce the carbon 
footprint and contribute to mitigating climate change impacts. Moreover, transitioning away from 
fossil fuels, as previously mentioned in the contribution analysis, can alleviate the burden on the 
overall system posed by conventional marginal electricity and heat sources (Energy Information 
Administration, n.d.). 
In both scenario 2 and 3, all energy-intensive processes involved (such as anaerobic digestion, 
biogas upgrading, and centrifugation etc.), which were previously fuelled by conventional electricity 
and heat sources from average Danish power plants, are now replaced with energy derived from 
wind power. Specifically, the external process utilized in EaseTech  is “Electricity from wind, DK 
2010 [KWh]”, which takes into account the average contribution of wind power in Denmark during 
2010, comprising 66% onshore and 34% offshore wind sources.

5. Limitations and future research  
There are several limitations that may have influenced the findings of our study. Firstly, it is important 
to note that, while a majority of the data used for simulating processes were obtained from studies 
conducted in Denmark, many ecoinvent processes lacked geographical limitations or focused generally 
on a global scale. This could introduce a certain level of uncertainty when generalizing our findings to 
other regions or contexts. 
Furthermore, it is important to highlight that the alfalfa brown juice, which was utilized in our research, 
remains relatively understudied. Limited research has been conducted on the composition and behavior 
of alfalfa brown juice under different conditions, as well as the resulting digestate. Acquiring a more 
comprehensive understanding of this substance would significantly enhance the quality and reliability 
of future LCA studies. 
In light of this, it should be noted that certain assumptions were made during the simulation process. 
Specifically, it was assumed that all elements present in the raw brown juice were fully transferred to the 
digestate. These assumptions may have influenced the outcomes related to its effectiveness as a fertilizer 
and its potential substitution from chemical alternatives. 
Moreover, it is important to highlight that microbial protein production is a promising and innovative 
technology that holds potential for addressing various challenges related to food scarcity and 
environmental concerns. Our study revealed savings associated with soybean substitution. However, it 
is crucial to acknowledge that at the time of our research, microbial protein production utilizing alfalfa 
brown juice had not yet been fully established at an industrial scale. Therefore, the assessment of large-
scale production was conducted primarily based on laboratory-scale data and literature sources regarding 
microbial protein production using other renewable sources, constantly assuming  a linear relationship 
for material use. Thus, upscaling the production process to real-life settings may yield different results, 
given the complexities and unique considerations associated with alfalfa brown juice. 
Indeed, as a future research opportunity, investigating the change in protein content when the CH4 yield 
is increased, would be highly intriguing. Understanding how adjustments in the yield influence the 
microbial protein content, would provide valuable insights into the interplay between biogas production 
and protein quality. 
While this study successfully evaluated the environmental implications of the three valorization 
scenarios, it is important to acknowledge that a comprehensive assessment should also consider 
economic and social aspects. 
Overall, this research contributes to the growing body of knowledge on the sustainable utilization of 
agricultural residues. The outcomes offer valuable insights for stakeholders, policymakers, and 
researchers seeking to adopt environmentally friendly practices and optimize resource use in the 
agricultural and energy sectors.

6. Conclusion  
This master's thesis aimed to assess the environmental sustainability of three scenarios for the 
valorization of alfalfa brown juice: its use as a substitute fertilizer, energy recovery, and soybean 
substitution through microbial protein production, and determine which one of them is the most 
beneficial in terms of sustainability. 
According to the contribution analysis, all biorefineries were found to be environmentally friendly, 
with savings in most of the investigated impact categories. Specifically, Scenario 1 showed benefits 
regarding CO2 emissions, but its N2O and NH4 emissions after the 10-month storage, as well as the 
possibility of the excessive use of organic fertilizers, showed to contribute to soil degradation and 
significant agricultural non-point source pollution. 
 For Scenario 2, energy recovery showed to yield savings to climate change and  the majority of the 
resource management impact categories, mainly due to the displacement of more energy-intensive 
methods like fossil fuel-based energy generation. Moreover, all the energy-demanding processes 
involved such as anaerobic digestion and water scrubbing, showed to impose environmental burdens to 
ecotoxicity and water use, mainly because of the marginal electricity and heat used, and the indirect 
water use because of them. But the use of the digestate as an organic fertilizer proved to have overall 
similar benefits as the raw brown juice. 
In Scenario 3, soybean substitution proved to be a highly beneficial biorefinery process across various 
impact categories. It contributed to low CO2 emissions and land use changes. Additionally, it helped 
mitigate the impact of freshwater eutrophication by reducing the use of P-containing fertilizers 
associated with conventional soybean cultivation. Moreover, it minimized the impact of freshwater 
ecotoxicity by reducing excessive S and Cl- to the soil.  
Upon thorough comparison of the three scenarios, the energy recovery scenario stands out as the most 
sustainable and viable option for brown juice valorization. This scenario not only achieves savings in 
energy demands but also demonstrates reductions in water usage. Moreover, it effectively contributes to 
mitigating the climate change impact, all the while avoiding burdens on ecosystem quality.  
Based on the contribution analysis, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis was conducted to evaluate the 
impact of various parameters on the results. This analysis took into consideration the distribution rate of 
nutrients in the soil, which is primarily influenced by the soil type. The substitution rate of N into the 
soil, biogas production efficiency, protein production efficiency, and soybean substitution efficiency 
were also assessed. 
Among all three scenarios, the distribution of N in the soil exhibited high SR values in most of the 
impact categories. Moreover,  for scenario 2 the CH4 production efficiency showed relatively higher SR 
values in the water use and resource use, energy carrier impact categories. However, other parameters in 
scenario 3, such as soybean substitution efficiency and microbial protein production efficiency, showed 
relatively minor sensitivity within the  +10% range. 
Finally, two scenario analyses were conducted to further investigate the potential environmental 
impacts under different factors. The first scenario focused on the distribution of nutrients in the soil and 
the environment, specifically by changing the soil type from sandy clay to coarse sand. The second 
scenario explored the implementation of wind energy to supply all the energy-demanding processes of 
scenario 2. Both scenarios presented differences compared to their initial scenarios, especially in the 
climate change impact category. Moreover, scenario analysis 2  showcased  noteworthy benefits in the 
resources management impact category.
