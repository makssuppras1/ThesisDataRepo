Abstract 
 
Fungal secondary metabolites represent valuable biologically active compounds with potential 
pharmaceutical, biotechnological, and industrial applications. In this project, the yeast 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae was employed as a microbial host for heterologous expression of two 
fungal biosynthetic genes, atrA and apvA, responsible for the production of atromentin and 
aspulvinone E respectively. Three methods were employed: genomic integration of a USER-
assembled construct expressing atrA, episomal expression from a high-copy 2μ plasmid, and 
mating of an apvA-expresisng strain with a chaperone overexpression library. Even after successful 
transformation and confirmation of appropriate insertion, no pigment was observed from the atrA 
expression strategy, suggesting underlying issues in gene dosage, precursor availability, post-
translational activation, or protein folding. The episomal approach yielded nonviable clones, likely 
the result of poor assembly or instability issues. In contrast, the apvA expression approach yielded 
weak but detectable fluorescence, which reflected low-level production of aspulvinone E. 
Quantitative fluorescence assay across a chaperone library identified several overexpressed 
chaperones to increase yield, and it can be concluded from this that protein folding represented a 
major bottleneck. In summary, although there were challenges encountered, this project showed 
the potential of modular synthetic biology approaches in yeast for reconstitution of fungal 
metabolites and provides a valuable model for pathway optimization in the future.

1 Introduction 
The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is a well-used model organism and microbial cell factory, 
that is valued for its genetic tractability, stress resistance, and scalability to industrial-level 
fermentation. In addition to its traditional uses, it is increasingly employed as a host for 
heterologous production of fungal secondary metabolites, biologically active compounds of 
biotechnological and pharmaceutical relevance. Of these, atromentin and aspuvinone E are 
particularly remarkable for their unique chemical characteristics and origin from non-ribosomal 
peptide synthetase (NRPS)-like enzymes [11, 15]. 
 
To allow heterologous pathway expression and screening in yeast, a collection of molecular tools 
has been developed. USER cloning (Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent cloning) is used to assemble 
several DNA fragments efficiently and scar-free using uracil-containing primers and enzymatic 
digestion [38]. CRI-SPA (CRISPR-mediated Selective Ploidy Ablation) enables high-throughput 
chromosomal transfer of genetic elements into yeast libraries through mating followed by selective 
removal of the donor genome with the use of the enzyme Cas9 [44]. These resources can be 
employed to study libraries such as the YKO (Yeast Knockout) library [47], a genome-scale library 
of single-gene deletion strains, or targeted libraries such as the yeast chaperone overexpression 
library, which is designed to explore the influence of folding and processing factors on 
heterologous protein expression [63].  
 
This project aimed to express the atrA and apvA genes in S. cerevisiae with the objective of 
evaluating their biosynthetic capability and developing workflows for pathway optimization 
through high-throughput screening. Strategies included genomic integration of atrA, episomal 
expression by a 2µ plasmid and apvA integration followed by mating with a chaperone library and 
fluorescence-based screening. This established the necessary tools, workflows, and expression 
strategies, which offers a clear pathway that can be used for future research targeting similar 
biosynthetic systems.

2 Project Objectives and Hypothesis 
2.1 Objectives 
The objective of this project is to explore the potential of S. cerevisiae as a microbial cell factory 
host for the heterologous production of fungal non-ribosomal peptide-like secondary metabolites. 
Specifically, this research aimed to explore the expression of two biosynthetic genes, atrA and 
apvA, in yeast strains that have been engineered and to explore the impact of co-expression of 
molecular chaperones on pathway output.  
 
The objective of the initial stage is the heterologous expression of the atrA gene encoding a NRPS-
like synthetase that is responsible for atromentin biosynthesis, a polyphenolic basidiomycete-
pigment produced by basidiomycetes naturally. The purpose is to clone the gene into an expression 
plasmid via USER cloning and subsequently transform it into the CEN.PK113-3B yeast strain. 
The goal is to successfully create an atrA expression strain, followed by the CRI-SPA methodology 
for introducing this construct into the yeast deletion library in order to have the ability to 
investigate in a high-throughput manner the effect of specific gene deletions on atromentin 
production. CRI-SPA allows chromosomal integration of the construct into recipient strains and 
subsequent 
selective 
elimination 
of 
the 
donor 
genome.  
 
A supporting strategy is aimed towards aspulvinone E, a fluorescent natural fungal metabolite, as 
a second biosynthetic target. The goal is to express the apvA gene of A. terreus in S. cerevisiae and 
mate the resulting strain with a chaperone overexpression library composed of 68 yeast strains, 
each containing a genomically integrated chaperone gene. The process attempts to find chaperones 
that enhance ApvA folding, solubility, or enzyme activity in yeast. Diploids are then chosen using 
SC-Ura + NTC media, which exploits the KI.URA3 chaperone library marker and NatMX marker 
of the construct. 
2.2 Hypothesis 
This project hypothesizes that heterologous production of fungal secondary metabolites in S. 
cerevisiae can be maximized with the help of some genomic and proteomic support mechanisms. 
With atromentin, use of the CRI-SPA system to transfect the atrA construct into the yeast knockout

3 Literature Review 
3.1 Cell Factories  
The industrial manufacturing world is transforming its paradigm from the traditional chemical 
synthesis to greener, biologically mediated processes, and this is primarily achieved by Microbial 
Cell Factories (MCFs) [2]. Such micro-sale powerhouses, encompassing both prokaryotic and 
eukaryotic microorganisms, are genetically modified biological systems capable of delivering a 
wide variety of useful products from bulk chemicals and biofuels to advanced pharmaceuticals and 
food ingredients [1]. Known as the “chips of biomanufacturing”, MCFs play a central role in 
addressing rising environmental challenges, fossil fuel dwindling, and the need for a sustainable 
economy [3]. Microorganisms, being the workhorses of these bio-factories, also have built-in 
advantages over conventional chemical processes such as less toxicity, more cost-effective 
methodologies, a friendly environmental nature, and can be cultivated quite well on large-scale 
fermenters [2]. 
 
MCFs have evolved from the earliest unmanipulated natural microbes (MCF 1.0) used in natural 
fermentation to highly engineered artificial factories (MCF 5.0) of the future [3]. The new versions 
are more dependent on digital synthetic biology and artificial intelligence, which needs an intimate 
understanding of microbial chassis cells, including specific genome sequences and sophisticated 
metabolic and regulatory networks [3]. Advances in molecular biology, recombinant DNA 
technology, and powerful genome editing tools like CRISPR-Cas9 have made it possible to 
genetically manipulate such organisms more easily, facilitating exact engineering and optimization 
of bio-synthetic pathways to create efficient and stable MCFs [1].

Among the wide variety of microorganisms deployed as cell factories, S. cerevisiae or baker’s 
yeast has become a star eukaryotic host [4]. The eukaryote unicell has millenia-long industrial 
experience that goes back to centuries with significant applications in bakery and brewing 
processes [5]. Its robust constitution, relatively straightforward genetic tractability, and sensitivity 
to numerous environmental stresses like temperature and pH make it a great platform for 
biotechnology [4]. The sheer amount of experience that has been accumulated over decades of 
examining the biology of yeast, coupled with a vast set of highly sophisticated genetic engineering 
tools, makes S. cerevisiae as a choice for host organism for production of a wide range of 
molecules, particularly complex secondary metabolites and heterologous proteins [4]. 
 
The potential of S. cerevisiae as a powerful host has been profoundly demonstrated by visionary 
research, including de novo assembly of an entire yeast chromosome (the Sc2.0 project), which 
illustrated its flexibility for large-scale genetic alteration [4]. This has enabled one to engineer 
complex metabolic pathways [4]. Genetically modified S. cerevisiae strains, for instance, have 
been effective in anti-malarial drug precursor production, artemisinic acid [1]. Similarly, this yeast 
was genetically engineered to produce stilbene analogs like resveratrol, a polyphenolic compound

with therapeutic potential, through addition or modification of stilbene synthetase (STS) enzymes 
[2]. Another central engineering strategies applied in S. cerevisiae include the expansion of its 
substrate scope for utilizing lower-cost and renewable biomass feedstocks like lignocellulose-
derived pentoses like xylose, a sugar with which native yeast metabolizes slowly [4]. Whereas 
traditional strategies focused on adaptive evolution towards the improvement of xylose utilization, 
the introduction of heterologous routes such as bacterial xylose isomerase has significantly 
improved ethanol production from such non-food plants [4]. Furthermore, advances in genome 
editing technology, especially the CRISPR-Cas system, have facilitated unprecedented efficiency 
and accuracy for site-directed alteration and multiplex deletion in the S. cerevisiae genome [4]. 
This allows for the effective introduction of entire heterologous metabolic routes or the deletion 
of multiple genes in one step, circumventing such historical time-consuming limitations [4]. The 
development of orthogonal systems towards independent genetic modules with well-understood 
behavior is also a critical research focus in order to have precise control over designed pathways 
for S. cerevisiae [4]. 
 
Whereas S. cerevisiae represents an excellent eukaryotic platform, a range of other microbial hosts 
are being exploited as MCFs with some unique advantages and are contributing to the growing 
bioeconomy [5]. 
 
Pichia pastoris, a non-conventional yeast, is a strong alternative to S. cerevisiae for recombinant 
protein production due to its high protein secretory capacity, high cell density fermentation, and 
simplicity of downstream processing [1]. It is increasingly being explored for the production of 
metabolites other than proteins [5].  
 
Yarrowia lipolytica, an oleaginous yeast, is well renowned for its ability to accumulate high levels 
of lipids and is therefore an excellent candidate for the sustainable production of polyunsaturated 
fatty acids (PUFAs), biofuels, and other lipid-derived bioproducts [1].  
 
The bioeconomy era is thus fostering the investigation and engineering of an ever-increasing array

3.2 Biosynthetic Pathways of Secondary Metabolites in Fungi 
Fungi consist of an exceptional chemical diversity reservoir and actively manufacture a broad 
spectrum of secondary metabolites, which play no direct role in essential primary metabolic 
functions like growth or reproduction. These often structurally elaborate compounds are central to 
fungal ecological success, controlling critical interactions like resource competition, predator 
defense, and communication in their complex environments. The outstanding bioactivities inherent 
in these metabolites have profoundly impacted human society, serving as valuable resources for 
diversified applications in medicine, agriculture, and biotechnology. The most common examples 
are the β-lactam antibiotics penicillin, effective cholesterol-lowering agents such as lovastatin, and 
strong immunosuppressants like cyclosporin A. As the chemical creativity leaders, fungi have 
gone on to continue stimulating the discovery of new bioactive compounds with pharmaceutical 
and industrial potential [7] 
Secondary metabolites produced by fungi can be classified into different chemical classes 
depending on the precursors they have, such as either acyl-coAs, amino acids or prenyl 
diphosphates, and they also depend on the enzymes used for their biosynthesis [9]. Compounds 
like polyketides, non-ribosomal peptides, terpenes and some alkaloids all have in common the 
same enzymes that synthetize them which are core enzymes called polyketides synthetases (PKSs), 
non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs), terpene cyclases (TCs) and dimethylallytryptophan 
synthetases (DMATSs), respectively. PKs and NRPSs are multidomain enzymes with complex 
catalytic activities [7].  
Fungi use two main types of polyketide synthetases (PKSs) for the construction of heterogenous 
polyketides: iterative type I PKSs and type III PKSs. Type I PKSs are giant multi-enzymatic 
complexes that iteratively build the polyketide framework, primarily from acetyl- and malonyl-
CoA [10]. They are classified according to their reducing activities as non-reducing, reducing, or 
partially reducing types, depending on the presence of some of the catalytic domains. The final 
polyketide product is released by a specialized domain or in some cases by co-evolved export 
enzymes. To date, although advances have been made, accurate prediction of the resulting 
==PAGE:16==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
8 
compound from iterative type I PKSs continues to be challenging by virtue of complex 
programming. Nevertheless, type III PKSs are small single-domain enzymes that also iteratively 
add starter units and malonyl-CoA, and are tolerant of high substrates. While significantly 
versatile, fungal type III PKs have been less studied than type I PKSs [7]. 
Non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs) are multi-modular, large fungal enzymes that 
assemble heterogeneous non-ribosomal peptides. The enzymes typically contain adenylation, 
condensation, and thiolation domains in their modules, which catalyze amino acid activation, 
formation of peptide bond, and transport of the growing chain, respectively. Peptide release 
typically occurs by cyclization through a terminal condensation domain. While mainly most fungal 
NRPSs operate sequentially, predicting the peptide characteristics by module order, some others 
are iterative, reusing modules for amino acid addition. Patterns and module structures of modules 
interacting amino acids are still hard to predict. A homology family, NRPS-like enzymes, have 
modules with insertion of adenylation and thiolation domains but no condensation domain, instead 
they feature a C-configuration terminal thioesterase or reductase domain. These NRPS-like 
enzymes are able to catalyze the dimerization of α-keto acids to create compounds such as 
benzoquinones. Notably, the NRPS-like enzyme encoded by the atrA gene in A. terreus is the one 
responsible for the synthesis of atromentin. Fungi also produce hybrid enzymes which combine 
PKS and NRPS modules, along with some other pathways for the biosynthesis of secondary 
metabolites which are less common [11].

Fungal biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) consist of physically connected genes carrying the 
enzymes and accessory components required for the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. 
Clusters are often made up of genes that encode backbone-formation enzymes such as polyketide 
synthases, non-ribosomal peptide synthetases, or terpene synthases, along with genes that are 
involved in tailoring, transport, regulation, and self-resistance [8]. Genome mining is now the 
major approach towards identification and characterization of such clusters in fungal organisms. 
Simultaneously, S. cerevisiae has also been a good microbial host for heterologous expression of 
such biosynthetic pathways depending on workability ease and metabolic engineering potential 
[11]. 
Understanding how fungi produce secondary metabolites puts the atromentin biosynthetic pathway 
into perspective and is also able to guide research into similar systems. This type of information 
provides opportunities to devise novel pathways and combine different biosynthetic elements. 
However, natural fungal producers often synthetize a plethora of other secondary metabolites, 
which leads to complex metabolomes that can hinder the isolation and characterization of a specific 
compound. Moreover, many native producers are difficult to genetically manipulate or slow 
growing, making their manipulation an inconvenient for research and industrial applications. This 
is precisely why S. cerevisiae represents a great platform for the study of metabolites of interest, 
it naturally produces considerably few secondary metabolites, resulting in cleaner background for 
heterologous expression of a given biosynthetic pathway and easing downstream analysis.   
3.3 Biological and Pharmacological Relevance of Atromentin 
Atromentin can be defined as a polyphenolic fungal secondary metabolite that can be found in 
basidiomycetes fungi, such as Tapinella atrotomentosa, Paxillus involtus, and Hydnellum peckii. 
Notably, it also occurs in the filamentous ascomycete Aspergillus terreus [12], which served as 
the source organism for atromentin gene extraction in this project. Besides having a well-
characterized biosynthetic pathway, it also possessed different biological activities that make it a 
valuable compound in many different areas, mainly the biotechnological environment. Amongst 
these biological activities it can be found that atromentin can act as a smooth muscle stimulant, it 
==PAGE:18==

10 
exhibits anticoagulant, antibacterial and antineoplastic properties, and it can act as an apoptosis 
inducer in some leukemia cells. Its molecular formula is C18H12O6, and it has a molecular weight 
of 324.3 g/mol [13]. 
 
Figure 2. Atromentin structure. (Taken from [14]) 
Atromentin biosynthesis is a well-established enzymatic reaction that converts the amino acid L-
tyrosine into secondary metabolites known as terphenylquinones. This is initiated by the action of 
the AtrD aminotransferase, which catalyzes the transamination of L-tyrosine [15]. In this 
transamination process, this amino group is transferred to 2-oxoglutarate and yields L-glutamate 
and the corresponding α-keto acid, 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid (4-HPP) [20], which is the 
immediate precursor to atromentin production [15].  
The following step in the pathway is carried out by AtrA, this is an enzyme classifies as a non-
ribosomal peptides synthetase (NRPS)-like enzyme, which is responsible of the dimerization 
reaction, which is key to the biosynthetic pathway [11]. AtrA is composed by three functional 
domains which resemble the NRPS system, it contains: an adenylation domain (A), a thiolation 
domain (T), and a thioesterase domain (TE) [16]. The adenylation domain is the one responsible 
for recognizing and activating the 4-HPP by forming and adenylate intermediate [17]. The 4-HPP, 
already activated, is then covalently transferred to the thiolation domain, which includes a 
phosphopantetheinyl arm that links the substrate. The final step, which consists of binding 
everything together, is carried out by the thioesterase domain. The thioesterase domain is 
responsible for the catalyzation of a C-C bond-forming reaction between two 4-HPP to get as a 
result the production of atromentin [11]. 
==PAGE:19==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
11 
The adenylation domain activates the precursor substrate 4-HPP, and it is subsequently transferred 
to the thiolation domain. The final step is catalyzed by the thioesterase domain that produced the 
product. The most significant aspect regarding the difference in atromentin biosynthesis is that it 
done via the dimerization of two identical 4-HPP molecules, unlike typical NRPS pathways [15]. 
Additionally, the atromentin biosynthetic pathway is included in a broader metabolic network 
which generates many different types of pigments and bioactive compounds, such as pulvinic acids 
[9]. Atromentin acts as a precursor molecule from which various structural modifications, such as 
hydroxylation, cyclization, and oxidative cleavage, can create a variety of secondary metabolites 
with their own different ecological and pharmacological functions [15]. 
 
Figure 3. Atromentin biosynthetic pathway. (Taken from [15]) 
Atromentin has many different biological properties, which can be relevant to several areas. Some 
studies have shown that atromentin exhibits selective antibacterial activity which targets

inhibitor of FabI, the isoenzyme of E. coli and S. aureus, showing specificity to FabK-containing 
pathogens. This selectivity places atromentin in the position of a promising lead compound 
towards the developent of narrow-spectrum antibiotics against pathogens that are entirely reliant 
on FabK for fatty acid biosynthesis [18].

apoptosis induction of human leukemia U937 cells. Atromentin exposure reduces the cell viability

3.4 Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a Model for Metabolic Engineering 
S. cerevisiae, also known as baker’s yeast, is nowadays the most extensively researched eukaryotic 
microorganism, which helps our understanding of the biology of the eukaryotic cell grow. For a 
long time, S. cerevisiae has been used to produce food and alcoholic beverages, but since recent 
times it has also been used in the pharmaceutical industry. S. cerevisiae has become a highly 
attractive organism to use given that is it a nonpathogenic organism, and due to the many uses it 
has been given, it is now considered as a GRAS (generally regarded as safe) organism. Another 
reason for its applicability in biotechnology is its susceptibility to genetic modifications by 
recombinant DNA technology, which has been an option since its whole genomic sequence was 
made available in 1996 [22]. 
For compounds such as polyketides and non-ribosomal peptides manufacturing processes can be 
challenging due to their complex molecular scaffolds. Recently, microorganisms like S. cerevisiae 
have been used as heterologous hosts for the production processes of many different chemical 
compounds. The capability of transforming exogenous DNA easily into S. cerevisiae along with a 
highly efficient homologous recombination ability positions it as a top choice for recombinant 
production [15].  
Over the years, different DNA assembly methods have been developed, and they can be divided 
in four main groups: restriction enzyme-based methods, in vivo and in vitro sequence homology-
based methods, and bridging oligo-based sequenced approaches. Given to the homologous 
recombination (HR) capability of yeast a wide range of in vivo homology-based DNA assembly

3.5 USER Cloning Method: Mechanism and Applications 
Molecular cloning, defined as the set of experimental techniques required to the assembly of 
recombinant DNA molecules and its insertion into a vector for propagation [32, 33], is a key and 
indispensable process in contemporary biological science and biotechnology. Such capability is 
vital for a wide variety of applications ranging from the functional analysis of genes to the effective 
production of proteins as well as the intricate engineering of metabolic pathways for a range of 
industrial end purposes [34]. Previously, cloning methods were mostly dependent on precise 
==PAGE:24==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
16 
cutting and splicing of DNA segments via restriction enzymes and DNA ligase [35]. While it is a 
traditional approach, it tends to have some limitations, such as its dependence on the frequency 
and strategic placement of certain enzyme recognition sites or the risk of introduction of unwanted 
sequence insertions into ligation junctions [36], leading to challenges in the assembly of multiple 
DNA fragments into a single coherent structure efficiently. The increasing requirement for more 
sophisticated and desired genetic constructions and the increasing demand for high throughput 
cloning methods have thus significantly accelerated the development of more efficient and flexible 
methodologies.  
 
To address this evolving demands, Uracil-Specific Excision Cloning (USER) has been extremely 
powerful and a highly efficient tool for the seamless assembly of DNA fragments [37]. USER 
cloning is different from conventional methods as it removes the use of restriction enzymes as well 
as in vitro DNA ligase in the assembly process [38], thereby delivering a much more simplified 
method of genetic engineering. This method is particularly good at enabling the correct and 
directional combination of more than one DNA fragments into a single vector [34], effectively 
eliminating the limitations encountered in conventional cloning approaches. Its novel enzymatic 
mechanism and established efficacy have solidly positioned USER cloning as a prominent and 
fundamental instrument in contemporary molecular biology. Enabling more streamlined and 
versatile genetic manipulations for diverse research and industrial processes 
 
The intrinsic process of USER cloning relies upon the strategic incorporation and subsequent 
enzymatic elimination of uracil bases, hence generating highly specific single-stranded overhangs 
that allow for precise and directional annealing of DNA fragments [39]. This fine-tuned control of 
junction sequences ensures that the ligated DNA molecules are seamless, meaning no irrelevant 
nucleotides are incorporated during ligation sites. Scarless cloning of this type is of critical 
importance to maintaining the integrity of open reading frames and for the preservation of exact 
spacing between regulatory elements and coding regions [38]. This method is particularly valuable 
for constructing intricate architectures, such as plasmids specially designed for targeted gene 
replacement, promoter-regulated overexpression of genes, or precise fusion of multiple coding 
sequences [34]. For instance, in applications such as targeted gene replacement in filamentous 
fungi, USER cloning has demonstrated remarkable efficiency achieving success rates averaging 
==PAGE:25==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
17 
84% for gene replacement and 80% for targeted overexpression in multi-fragment assemblies [34]. 
Its efficiency plus the inherent flexibility in primer design renders USER cloning a highly 
attractive alternative for both high-throughput large-scale genetic engineering applications and for 
routine laboratory procedures [37]. 
 
The USER cloning process is orchestrated with precision through a series of enzymatic steps that 
precisely prepare the DNA inserts as well as the target vector for proper and directional assembly 
[37]. The multi-enzymatic pathway ensures the formation of compatible single-stranded overhangs 
that are capable of spontaneously annealing and ultimately be subjected to in vivo ligation [35]. 
The initial step is the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) amplification of the DNA fragments to 
be cloned [37]. Most significantly, the oligonucleotide primers for these PCR reactions are 
designed to contain 5’-deoxyuridine (dU) extensions in the PCR primers [39]. These dU residues 
are positionally located at specific, usually 6-10 nucleotides from the 5’ end of the template DNA 
homologous reaction [39]. During PCR, an editing DNA polymerase, which has deoxyadenine 
opposite to a dU, synthetizes DNA strands containing the uracil bases at the same positions [37]. 
Incorporation of dU is important, as it identifies the correct sites for subsequent enzymatic 
cleavage [39].  
 
Following PCR, the dU residues containing amplified DNA fragments undergo a treatment using 
a specialized USER enzyme mix. This proprietary enzyme blend typically consists of two crucial 
enzymatic activities: Uracil DNA Glycosylase (UDG), which specifically identifies and removes 
the uracil base from the DNA backbone and leaves an abasic site, and DNA Glycosylase-Lyase 
Endonuclease VIII (Endo VIII), which cuts the phosphodiester bonds at the 3’ and 5’ edges of this 
abasic site [37]. This two-way cleavage results in a single-strand breakage and the resulting 
separation of a small 5’ part of the initial primer, resulting in the PCR fragment having a distinctive 
3’ single-stranded overhang of known length, typically around 9 base pairs [36]. The exact 
sequence and length of these overhangs are directly defined by the original primer structure only, 
thus high specificity and directionality during the resulting assembly process are guaranteed [34].  
 
While the DNA inserts are being prepared, the recipient plasmid vector is also being prepared to 
receive these fragments [34]. This involves linearizing the vector and generating complementary 
==PAGE:26==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
18 
single-stranded overhangs that can perfectly anneal with those inserts [37]. This is generally done 
by digesting the vector at predefined USER Cloning Sites (UCS) using a mixture that has a 
standard restriction enzyme (PacI) and a nicking enzyme (Nt.BvCI) [34]. The strategic position of 
such recognition sites in the UCS exactly controls the length and sequence of the resulting 3’ 
single-stranded overhangs on the vector, which are carefully engineered to be distinctive and fully 
complementary to overhangs that are formed on the DNA inserts, thereby giving directional 
cloning [34].  
 
Lastly, the prepared inserts and linearized vector, each with compatible single-stranded overhangs, 
are mixed together. Because of the exact complementarity of their overhangs, the fragments 
spontaneously anneal, and a stable, recombinant circular molecule with nicks (discontinuities in 
the phosphodiester backbone) is produced [37]. The annealed molecule, which does not need in 
vitro ligation, is then transformed into competent E. coli cells [34]. In the E. coli host, the cellular 
DNA repair machinery efficiently recognizes and seals such nicks by covalent phosphodiester 
bond formation [34]. This in vivo ligation reaction finishes off the plasmid circularization and 
makes the plasmid complete and functional, eliminating the need of a prior ligation [38].

3.6 CRI-SPA Method: Mechanism and Applications 
The area of biotechnology and biological research has seen a revolution with the discovery of 
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) and its association to the 
protein Cas9 [45]. This novel genome editing tool makes it possible to precisely and effectively 
modify genetic material across a wide variety of species [41]. The CRISPR-Cas9 system uses 
universal principles that come from the bacterial adaptive immune system at its fundamental level 
[41]. It makes use of one guide RNA (sgRNA) to direct the Cas9 nuclease to the target DNA 
sequences in the genome and create targeted double-stranded breaks [42]. These double-stranded 
breaks are subsequently repaired by the cell’s own repair machinery, making it possible to 
introduce genetic modifications of preference, such as substitution, insertion, or deletion [42]. Its 
efficiency, cost-effectiveness, and convenience have established CRISPR-Cas9 as a standard tool 
in genetic engineering [41]. Its versatility has found its way into various applications, from basic 
biomedical research, where it can be utilized to learn about the functions of the genes and disease 
pathogenesis through the execution of precise gene knockouts and disease modeling, to 
agriculture, environmental science, and biotechnology [41]. The huge capacity of this technology 
in functional genomics research provides a powerful tool for the simultaneous editing of several 
genes [45]. However, with every powerful technology, CRISPR-Cas9 poses substantial ethical 
concerns which are still under debate and investigation [41]. 
 
Amongst all the organisms CRISPR-Cas9 has made a significant impact, one of them is the yeast 
S. cerevisiae, which is an essential model organism and the primary production host in industrial 
biotechnology [45]. The suitability of the yeast towards genome editing is emphasized as it 
possesses its highly efficient process of homologous recombination, due to which the addition of 
heterologous genes to its genome becomes extremely accurate [42]. Prior to the advent of CRISPR, 
there were crucial bottlenecks in yeast engineering, including the lack of available selection 
markers for transformation, plasmid instability, and the labor-intensive nature of existing 
techniques for gene integration [43]. CRISPR use in yeast engineering has greatly alleviated these 
issues, greatly accelerating stepwise construction and analysis of genetic variants [46]. 
Furthermore, the simplicity, effectiveness, and flexibility of CRISPR-Cas9 have rendered it 
compatible with high-throughput, systematic gene function analysis at unprecedented scale [45]. 
This capability is particularly valuable to recreate complex metabolic pathways in yeast since

many important drugs and plant-derived nutraceuticals possess multigene biosynthetic pathways

genes since it contains effective transformation procedures, limited endogenous specialized 
metabolisms, some overlapping primary metabolism networks with plants, and its capacity for 
post-translational modifications [43]. The ability of CRISPR-Cas9 to combine many genes of 
interest in a single transformation makes the reconstruction of these complex pathways and hence 
makes yeast an even more efficient and effective tool in functional genomics research on plant 
specialized metabolism [43]. More recently, it has also been demonstrated that CRISPR genome 
editing is transferable to several species of yeast, some of which possess inherent traits beneficial 
to industrial biotechnology [45]. The recent availability of nonconventional yeasts, which are less

motivated by the general availability of CRISPR-Cas systems compared to genome editing [45]. 
Some studies have proved there are efficient Cas9-based methods for the creation of pools of 
mutants with desired genetic alterations (deletions, substitutions and insertions) at a conventionally

repair of a double-stranded break within a target locus [46]. This method allows basic questions in 
biology to be explored in a high-throughput way with precision [46].

traditional methods like Synthetic Genetic Array (SGA) rely on arrayed mating, meiosis, 
sporulation, and marker selection [44]. SGA’s meiosis requirement is typically time-consuming 
and slow, normally requiring 4-7 days for sporulation, and its efficiency is difficult to control [44]. 
Moreover, classical methods of generating variant libraries have been hard to make, with even 
well-used collections containing undesired secondary mutations and suffering from the presence 
of selection markers [46]. Furthermore, meiotic recombination in SGA can lead to undesired donor

CRISPA eliminates these limitations effectively by integrating mating, CRISPR-Cas9-guided gene

the technique are the donor and recipient yeast strains with customized characteristics and

conversion [44]. The CRISPR-Cas9 expression vectors, typically plasmids, are engineered to 
encode a guide RNA (gRNA) that directs Cas9 to a genomic location, thereby allowing the precise 
insertion of a gene feature of interest into the target genome by the method of homologous 
recombination-mediated transfer [44].  
 
The advantages offered by CRISPA are numerous with significant impacts on high-throughput

extremely suitable for large-scale genetic screens [44]. Moreover, the entire process of CRISPA 
can be accomplished within a period of approximately one week, which is a huge acceleration 
compared to traditional methods [44]. This method has also shown high efficiency and

3.7 Yeast Knockout (YKO) Collection 
The budding yeast S. cerevisiae is a notorious model organism used in cell biology and molecular 
genetics, this due to its genetic tractability and well-defined biological processes. Its compact 
genome and straightforward genetic manipulation have made it possible to carry out genome-wide 
==PAGE:31==

studies, greatly influencing fields from fundamental research to industrial biotechnology. One of 
the most notorious advancements is the Yeast Knockout Library (YKO), a core resource which 
represents the first and only complete, systematically constructed deletion mutant strain collection 
available for any organism [47]. 
 
The concept of the YKO collection was born as a direct result of the completion of the S. cerevisiae 
genome sequencing project in the mid-1990s that provided a complete genetic map but left the 
biological functions of many open reading frames (ORFs) undefined [48]. For this, researchers 
developed a yeast-genome wide collection of loss-of-function mutants by systematically 
producing nearly 6,000 ORFs in the yeast genome [48]. Every deletion was achieved using a PCR-
based gene disruption technique, in which, a selectable marker, like the KanMX cassette that is 
resistant to G418, flanked the upstream and downstream sequence of the ORF [48]. These 
disrupting gene cassettes were introduced into yeast cells, and the homologous recombination 
machinery of the cell itself was exploited to swap the target gene for the marker [48]. Every strain 
underwent a confirmation step following transformation to confirm that the desired gene deletion 
had occurred at the correct locus, preserving the integrity and validity of the entire collection [49]. 
 
One of the key benefits of the YKO collection is the overall heterogeneity, with heterozygous and 
homozygous diploids, as well as MATa and MATα haploids [48]. S. cerevisiae can be in a stable 
haploid (single set of chromosomes) and diploid (double set of chromosomes) state [50]. Haploid 
yeast cells are divided into two distinct mating types: MATa and MATα [51]. These mating types 
are controlled genetically by the possession of the specific allele at the MAT locus on Chromosome 
III [52]. When haploid cells of opposite mating types (MATa and MATα) are placed under 
optimum conditions, they conjugate, or “mate”, and form a diploid cell [53]. Diploid cells with a 
complete set of chromosomes donated from each parent can be made to sporulate and enter 
meiosis, giving rise to haploid spores, half of which will be MATa and half MATα [54]. This 
sexual cycle and the distinct mating types of yeast make it necessary for most genetic approaches 
and advanced genomic studies.

3.8 Chaperones and Chaperone Libraries 
Chaperones play a crucial role in cellular proteostasis by acting as intermediates for protein 
folding. This activity has been extensively utilized to promote protein folding and optimize 
heterologous protein production in host organisms such as S. cerevisiae [63]. Such a strategy has 
not been explored for small molecule (SM) production, since SM routes rely on the involvement 
==PAGE:34==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
26 
of multiple enzymes, such as multi-domain synthases or synthetases, that may benefit from 
assistance in folding to augment pathway catalytic proficiency [63]. 
 
Molecular chaperones are an evolutionary conserved group of proteins critical for correct folding 
of new proteins synthetized and the prevention of misfolded protein structures during both normal 
and stressful conditions like exposure to elevated temperatures [61]. Molecular chaperones bind 
to partially folded or nonnative proteins, preventing aggregation and guiding them onto productive 
folding pathways [60].  
 
The major families of general chaperones are the Hsp40, Hsp60, Hsp70, Hsp90, and Hsp100 
families, in addition to small heat shock proteins [60]. General chaperones often interact in 
cooperative networks with protein cofactors [62, 60].  
 
Cytosol contains a broad range of protein folding and reconstruction molecular chaperones [62]. 
Some of the principal cytosolic chaperones include: The 70-kDa heat shock proteins (Hsps70s) 
constitute an extremely conserved family that recognize hydrophobic determinants on misfolded 
proteins. They are controlled by binding and hydrolysis of ATP [62]. In S. cerevisiae, the members 
of the Hsp70 superfamily are the Ssa, Ssb, and Sse families [62, 61]. There are supplemented by 
other nascent chain-binding proteins like prefoldin in stabilizing elongating protein chains on 
ribosomes against aggregation [64]. Hsp40/J proteins, homologs of E. coli Dnaj, stimulate the 
ATPase activity of Hsp70s. In S. cerevisiae, they include Ydj1 and Sis1 [62]. Nucleotide Exchange 
factors (NEFs) enable ADP release from NBD of HSP70 to reinstate its cycle for new ATP binding 
[62]. The chaperone machine of Hsp90 is a highly conserved molecular chaperone that is primarily 
devoted to the “final” maturation of proteins and assembly of macromolecular structure [60]. The 
Hsp100 family member Hsp104 is both an unfoldase and disaggregase [62]. Small heat shock 
proteins, such as Hsp26 and Hsp42, are ATP-independent “holdases” that prevent nonnative 
protein aggregation [62]. Chaperonins are also giant, rod-shaped structures, such as GroEL of 
bacteria, with an interior cavity for a single chain of a protein to fold without interference from 
aggregation. Folding in the cytosol can be following regulated chain release from starting factors 
like Hsp70 or following transfer of newly synthetized proteins to downstream chaperones like the 
chaperonins [64].

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is a central compartment for the biogenesis of the majority of 
secreted proteins, including factors required for their import and proper folding [65]. It provides a 
unique environment for folding, more oxidizing than the cytoplasm, that favors the formation of 
disulfide bonds and generally including N-glycosylation for secretory proteins [65]. Because of 
these specialized requirements, the ER also possesses some specialized chaperone systems [65]. 
Some of the key ER chaperones include: The ER Hsp70 (BiP/Kar2) homolog is abundant in the 
ER lumen and essential for post-translational protein import and folding [62, 65]. Proteins, such 
as Scj1, Jem1, and Sec63, activate ER Hsp70 ATPase activity and play roles in translocation and 
protein folding [62]. Protein Disulfide Isomerases (PDI) catalyze rearrangement of disulfide 
bonds, a process critical for proper folding in the ER oxidizing environment [62, 65]. Calnexin is 
an integral membrane-bound chaperone protein that is involved in quality control of protein 
folding for glycoproteins [62, 65]. Its soluble homolog, Calreticulin, is also involved in 
glycoprotein folding and quality control in the ER [65]. 
 
Mitochondria also possess a distinct set of chaperones, high similarity with bacterial homologs as 
predicted by the endosymbiont theory [60]. These include Mitochondrial Hsp70 (Ssc1, Ssc3, 
Ssq1), which are involved in protein folding and translocation in the mitochondria [62]. 
Mitochondrial J proteins (Mdj1, Mdj2, Jac1, Pam16, Pam18) activate mitochondrial Hap70s and 
function in protein import [62]. Furthermore, mitochondrial chaperonins (Hsp60, Hsp10) form a 
double-ring protein and have protein folding in its interior cavity in ATP-dependent manner, with 
the help of the cochaperone Hsp10 [62, 61]. 
 
Heterologous pathway production in a foreign organism like S. cerevisiae can be challenging due 
to poor protein folding of non-native proteins, which can decrease yields [66]. Overexpression of 
endogenous chaperones, such as cytosolic Ssa1 and Ydj1, is a proven way to enhance levels of 
secreted proteins [67].  
 
In order to overcome heterologous small molecules production bottlenecks, a powerful resource 
exists in the shape of chaperone overexpression libraries [63]. The library collection described 
Vestergaard et al., [63] consists of 68 S. cerevisiae strains, and each strain has been particularly 
==PAGE:36==

28 
engineered to overexpress a particular endogenous cytosolic chaperone or co-chaperone [63]. 
These overproducing strains are constitutively overexpressing these chaperones, creating a library 
of yeast cells with varying proteostasis conditions [63]. The central purpose of such library is to 
screen systematically which single chaperones, upon overexpression, are able to enhance the 
folding and activity of the foreign enzymes involved in the biosynthetic pathway for the production 
of small molecule [63]. 
 
This is typically accomplished through a mating-based method, where the library can be screened 
at high-throughput for increased production of small molecules [63]. In this process, a “query” 
yeast strain, engineered to include the biosynthetic pathway to produce the small molecule of 
interest, is mated with each of the 68 chaperone-overexpressing strains of the library [63]. This 
combination results in diploid cells that each contain both small molecule biosynthetic pathway 
and one or more library overexpressed chaperones [63]. By culturing these diploid strains and 
quantifying the target small molecule, researchers can successfully identify the specific chaperones 
or combination of chaperones that are responsible for high yields [63]. For instance, with 
aspulvinone E as a model compound, overexpression of Ydj1 and Ssa1 together in a production 
strain led to substantial improvement in the production of small molecules, suggesting that specific 
chaperones can be used for functional expression of multi domain enzymes involved in these 
pathways [63]. Such a systematic screening approach offers a valuable method for optimizing S. 
cerevisiae cell factories towards enhanced biosynthesis of small molecules. This is a valuable 
approach given that overexpression of chaperone genes to increase heterologous small molecule 
production in S. cerevisiae is not common and only few cases have been reported in the literature 
[63], such as improvement of terpene biosynthesis [68, 69].

3.9 Biological and Pharmacological Relevance of Aspulvinone E 
Aspulvinone E is a significant natural compound, a derivative of the furanone, predominantly 
purified from the fungus A. terreus [70]. It is biosynthesized via an interesting process by non-
ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS)-like enzymes [72]. In A. terreus, specifically, two NRPS-
like proteins, ApvA and AtmelA, are involved in the production of aspulvinone E as a common 
intermediate [72]. The apvA gene targets expression in fungal hyphae to encode for aspulvinone 
E, which can be further prenylated by the enzyme AbpB to yield other forms of aspulvinone [72]. 
Such two-gene production is a sign of a highly sophisticated spatial control within the fungus, 
allowing the same precursor, aspulvinone E, to be funneled into different downstream pathways 
[72].

The scientific study of aspulvinone E is commonly linked with atromentin due to the analogy of 
their biosynthetic origins from NRPS-like enzymes, regardless of their structural differences. 
Atromentin is a benzoquinone derivative while aspulvinone E is a furanone, both of which are 
typically derived from the same α-keto acid precursors. The point of comparison is the enzymatic 
machinery: the two compounds are produced from the action of NRPS-like enzymes, which are 
responsible for catalyzing the dimerization of α-keto acids [60]. The potential has been explored 
by scientists for inter-converting the biosynthetic activities of aspulvinone E synthetase to a 
quinone-producing atromentin synthetase via domain-swapping or site-directed mutagenesis [60]. 
While straight amino acid replacement or broad domain exchanges have proven challenging for 
complete conversion, such efforts do reflect a common similarity in their biosynthetic pathway 
[60].  
 
This underlying similarity suggests that successful research approaches utilized on one compound 
may be able to be replicated or transferred to the other. For example, strategies for the heterologous 
expression of aspulvinone E biosynthetic enzymes in hosts like S. cerevisiae could be replicated 
to produce atromentin. Similarly, strategies to optimize pathways or genetically modify that are 
successful for one compound could be excellent starting points for optimizing the biosynthesis of 
the other compound. The shared enzymatic class means that insights obtained in protein folding 
bottlenecks or enzyme efficacy in one system can then be used as a valuable starting point for 
optimizing production of the other, advancing the overall field of natural product biosynthesis.  
3.10 State of the art 
Recent advances in synthetic biology and metabolic engineering have enabled the heterologous 
production of fungal biosynthetic pathways in model organisms such as S. cerevisiae. These efforts 
aim to replicate intricate natural product biosynthesis in genetically tractable hosts for high yield, 
stability, and scalability. For the intent of this project, focus was laid on engineering yeast strains 
for expression of fungal non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS-like enzymes) involved in the 
biosynthesis of atromentin and aspulvinone E. The following sections review previous work 
concerning the heterologous reconstruction of such pathways in yeast, providing both background 
information as well as a comparative framework for the present study.

3.10.1 Heterologous Expression Strategies for Atromentin Production 
Atromentin has been the target of several synthetic biology efforts aimed at expressing its 
biosynthetic pathway in S. cerevisiae. The Desings differ in their strategy, ranging from episomal 
plasmids to chromosomal integration, but they all highlight accessory enzymes and host 
compatibility as being essential for productive production.  
 
The research by Schneider et al. (2008) delineated the atromentin biosynthetic pathway in 
Tapinella panuoides, with the central role being given to the NRPS-like atromentin synthetase 
(AtrA) and an accompanying related aminotrasnferase (AtrD), and the cloning of their respective 
encoding genes atrA and atrD. Using a heterologous expression system in Aspergillus nidulans, 
they were able to demonstrate that AtrA was singly capable of producing pigment when co-
supplied with the tyrosine derived precursor 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate (4-HPP). The secreted 
enzyme contains minimal A-T-TE structure, which activates, transfers, and dimerizes 4-HPP to 
atromentin. Their work established the biochemical and genetic basis for further attempts to 
reconstitute this route in other hosts [8]. 
 
Linna (2024) pursued chromosomal expression of a codon-optimized version of AtrA (ATRO-opt) 
in S. cerevisiae, integrated via CRISPR-Cas9 into a specific genomic locus and under the strong 
constitutive TDH3 promoter. The objective was to develop a stable production host using this 
integration into the chromosome. After integration and expression validation, no pigment 
production was observed. This correlated with the absence of a phosphoantetheinyl transferase 
(PPTase), essential for post-translational activation of the thiolation domain of NRPS-like 
enzymes. Linna complemented the experimental approach with metabolic modeling using 
enzyme-constrained genome-scale models (ecYeastGEM), which predicted possible flux 
bottlenecks. The models proposed that overexpression of shikimate and tyrosine biosynthetic 
pathway genes (ARO1, ARO2, TYR1) and eliminating competing branches like PHA2 could 
increase 4-HPP availability and optimize pathway output. Even though production was not 
achieved, this study provided a rational design platform for future engineering [79].  
 
Functional production of atromentin was made possible by Hühner et al. (2018), who cloned an 
NRPS-like synthetase from A. terreus (given the annotation ATEG-03090) in combination with 
==PAGE:41==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
33 
NpgA, a PPTase. The constructs were introduced into S. cerevisiae using plasmids, and LC-MS 
analysis detected the presence of atromentin in the culture broth. Interestingly, the experiment 
identified the failure of pigment production in the absence of co-expression of a PPTase, further 
confirming that cofactor compatibility is the key to heterologous expression. In this study, a 
production titer of approximately 35 mg/L was achieved, making this a benchmark for future yeast 
pathway construction [20]. 
 
Together, these studies show both the promise and difficulty of heterologous atromentin 
biosynthesis in yeast. Schneider et al. (2008) established the enzymatic template, Linna (2024) 
demonstrated the limitations of minimal expression systems and applied predictive metabolic 
modeling, and Hühner et al. (2018) accomplished pathway reconstitution under optimized 
conditions of co-expression. Collectively, the results emphasize the functional expression of fungal 
secondary metabolites in yeast often entails more than just the central biosynthetic gene, it requires 
controlled expression of accessory enzymes, strict precursor balancing, and host strain 
optimization. 
3.10.2 Heterologous Production of Aspulvinone E 
Guo et al. (2015) investigated aspulvinone E biosynthesis in Aspergillus terreus under natural 
conditions and showed that two separate genes, apvA and atmelA, are responsible for its production 
in different part of the fungus. While heterologous production was not investigated in this study, 
it was essential in exposing the spatial and gene-specific regulation of aspulvinone E biosynthesis, 
which offers required groundwork for metabolic engineering approaches. In hyphal cells, apvA 
activates aspulvinone E synthesis, which is further modified by a prenyltransferase enzyme (abpB) 
to generate other derivatives of aspulvinones. Conversely, in the spore-producing conidia, atmelA 
is converted into aspulvinone E, and this is then converted into melanin withing the assistance of 
a tyrosinase enzyme. This shows that the same fundamental molecule can be used differently 
depending on where it is located in the organism. Contrary to expectations, the research further 
revealed that these genes are not grouped together in the genome, which is unusual for fungal 
biosynthetic gene clusters and helps to demonstrate the mechanisms by which metabolic 
complexity arises even when genes are not clustered [72]. 
 
==PAGE:42==

Building on this foundational knowledge, Vestergaard et al. (2025) transferred the aspulvinone E 
biosynthetic pathway into S. cerevisiae in order to explore its potential for heterologous 
production. They provided the fungal gene melA, an apvA analog, and npgA, required for enzyme 
activation. For increase yield, they mated the created yeast strain with a library of 68 different 
strains, each overexpressing a different molecular chaperone. The diploid resulting strains were 
screened with UV light to measure the aspulvinone E yield by fluorescence. Some chaperones, 
such as YDJ1 and SSA1, enhanced fluorescence significantly, with improved enzyme folding and 
function [63]. 
 
Overall, these experiments both underscore the natural complexity of aspulvinone E regulation in 
fungi and the challenge mimicking such pathways in yeast. They demonstrate that heterologous 
production not only works with the fundamental biosynthetic genes but also with the aid of the 
host cellular machinery to generate correct enzyme function and product formation.  
3.10.3 Summary and Perspectives 
The atromentin and aspulvinone E production approaches highlight raising potential and 
sophistication of heterelogous production of secondary metabolites in S. cerevisiae. Though the 
biosynthetic pathways of both compounds have their basis in filamentous fungi, their 
reconstruction in yeast has yielded controlled, modular exploration of gene expression, enzyme 
activity, and pathway optimization. In atromentin, early attempts were difficult to achieve due to 
the requirement of the co-expression of phosphopantetheinyl transferases and extensive pathway 
optimization to achieve detectable amounts. Similarly, yeast production of aspulvinone E has 
demonstrated the importance of host factors, such as chaperones and folding assistance, which 
may be essential in stabilizing NRPS-like enzymes. Together, these examples indicate that 
successful heterologous production of fungal natural products might require more than the 
minimum biosynthetic genes, it requires coordinated assistance from the host system, including 
transcriptional regulation, availability of precursors, and post-translational modification. These 
findings inform the engineering of future cell factories and the approaches taken in synthetic 
biology to produce complex metabolites.

was aimed at establishing if integration by Cas9-mediated homologous recombination would be

donor strain that would be ready for downstream future CRI-SPA mating, where the donor strain 
(ScHO20 + construct + gRNA) would be crossed with a deletion recipient strain to enable targeted 
gene conversion and selective ploidy elimination.

4.5 Strategy 3: Heterologous Expression of apvA for Aspulvinone E 
Production in Yeast and Evaluation of Chaperone Co-Expression 
 
This approach redirected the experimental focus to heterologous production of aspulvinone E and 
incorporated a sophisticated high-throughput screening approach consisting of mating with a pre-
existing chaperone overexpression library. This approach was employed to identify specific 
chaperones that have the potential to enhance the production of the target molecule.  
 
The construction of the plasmid pCFB2909-npgA-apvA for heterologous apvA expression in S. 
cerevisiae was carried out using the same protocol as for the atrA construct up to the linearization 
of the plasmid. The apvA gene, with an expected size of approximately 2,770 bp, was obtained 
from A. terreus and used for PCR amplification. The npgA-pTEF2-pTEF1 and tENO2-NatMX

electrophoresis was used to verify the products, and assembly of the fragments into previously 
prepared backbone pCFB2909 via USER cloning. Transformation into E. coli DH5α cells and 
subsequent plasmid purification were also performed as for the atrA construct.  
 
Nevertheless, various essential steps in this pathway were carried out differently, including 
linearizing the plasmid, and yeast transformation method. In addition, while atrA construct work 
was stopped after transformation, the process of apvA proceeded and included strain mating with 
the chaperon library, selection, and screening for fluorescence. These downstream processes are 
described in the sections that follow.

5 Results 
 
This section outlines the experimental outcomes of the construction and analysis of different 
strategies for the fungal genes atrA and apvA in S. cerevisiae. A range of molecular biology 
techniques, including PCR amplification, USER cloning, plasmid linearization, colony PCR, yeast 
transformation, high-throughput mating with a chaperone overexpression library, and 
fluorescence-based screening, were used to assemble and evaluate the constructs. All experimental 
routes were intended to ensure proper assembly and examine potential improvement in metabolite 
production.  
 
Results are reported in the sequence in which each approach was carried out: beginning with the 
atrA integration route, followed by the episomal plasmid-based strategy, and finally the apvA 
expression and screening route.

6 Discussion 
6.1 Heterologous Expression of the atrA Gene for Atromentin Production 
in Yeast 
 
The first experimental strategy used for the heterologous production of atromentin in S. cerevisiae, 
which aimed at the genomic integration of a construct containing the atrA gene, the npgA-pTEF2-
pTEF1fragment, and the tENO2-NatMX fragment. This construct was successfully transformed 
into two yeast strains, CEN.PK113-3B and ScHO20. Transformation was validated through 
selective growth on SC and YPD + NTC media, with appropriate negative controls confirming 
selection specificity. Moreover, colony PCR confirmed the successful integration of the construct 
at the XII-5 chromosomal site. These results constitute a technically robust outcome: they 
demonstrate that the USER cloning approach guaranteed the precise and efficient assembly of 
multiple biosynthetic elements, and successful chromosomal delivery was achieved through 
transformation. Additionally, the use of the ScHO20 strain, a strain which inherently expresses 
Cas9, provided a basis for future implementation of the CRI-SPA methodology, in case a pigment-
producing phenotype was achieved. Although phenotypic output was not observed in this initial 
route, this strategy established a reliable framework for the stable genomic expression of fungal 
biosynthetic genes in yeast.  
 
However, despite successful integration and selection, no visible pigment phenotype, an indicator 
of atromentin production, was seen in any of the transformed yeast colonies. Lack of output 
suggests various underlying issues often encountered in heterologous expression of fungal 
secondary metabolites. One major limitation is likely insufficient gene dosage. Genomic 
integration typically results in single-copy insertion, which may be inadequate for the generation 
of significant levels of enzymes in order to generate sufficient metabolic flux [83]. Previous studies 
have demonstrated that gene copy number increase, through multi-genomic integration or 
episomal plasmids, can vastly improve yields [84]. Biosynthesis of artemisinic acid in yeast was 
greatly improved through elevated copy number of key biosynthetic genes, demonstrating that 
reaching a threshold of enzyme activity is crucial for the synthesis of product [85]. 
 
==PAGE:81==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
73 
However, another critical bottleneck is phosphopantetheinylation. AtrA, an NRPS-like enzyme, 
must be post-translationally activated by a phosphopantetheinyl transferase (PPTase) in order to 
be catalytically active [86]. Although the npgA gene encoding a fungal PPTase was included in 
the construct, it is possible that the expression was insufficient or that NpgA was not compatible 
with AtrA. Even minor inefficiencies in post-translational activation can severely compromise 
enzyme activity and reduce overall performance.  
 
Precursor availability could also be a key limitation. In native basidiomycete producers, AtrA is 
co-located with AtrD, an aminotransferase that catalyzes conversion of L-tyrosine to 4-
hydroxyphenylpyruvic acid (4-HPP) [9]. The endogenous metabolic pathway for L-tyrosine and 
its subsequent conversion to 4-HPP may not be enough to meet the requirements of a recently 
introduced high-flux pathway in a heterologous host such as S. cerevisiae. Yeast’s native shikimate 
pathway which mediates aromatic amino acid biosynthesis is tightly regulated and flux through 
this pathway is limited [87]. Without metabolic engineering to upregulate L-tyrosine biosynthesis 
or increase 4-HPP availability, substrate limitation may prevent downstream pathway activation, 
regardless of enzyme expression levels. Metabolic modeling suggests that some strategies such as 
overexpression of certain enzymes within the shikimate pathway (ARO1, ARO2, TYR1) could 
potentially be employed to increase precursor availability for target compounds [79]. 
 
Lastly, AtrA is a multi-domain complex enzyme whose expression in yeast could suffer from 
improper folding or extremely rapid degradation [66]. Heterologous expression of such proteins 
often requires co-expression of targeted molecular chaperones to ensure functionality [66]. The 
yeast chaperone system may not be fully compatible with fungal NRPS-like enzymes [88]. 
Misfolding of AtrA could result in low catalytic activity even when transcription and post-
transcriptional activation are successful. In more complicated systems, co-expression of 
chaperones has been shown to improve activity of heterologous enzymes in S. cerevisiae [63]. 
This alternative approach was explored in the apvA expression route with the purpose of enhancing 
correct protein folding and improving expression.  
 
 This first approach demonstrated that complex fungal constructs can be assembled and transferred 
into S. cerevisiae using a combination of USER cloning, yeast transformation, selective plating, 
==PAGE:82==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae 
74 
and colony PCR verification of integration. While no pigment was detected, this approach helped 
characterize potential limitations typically encountered in heterologous expression such as low 
gene dosage, limited precursor availability, or enzyme folding problems. Despite there not being 
an observable phenotype, the procedure described provides a good basis which could be adapted 
in future efforts towards the reconstruction of other fungal biosynthetic processes in yeast.  
6.2 Episomal 2µ Plasmid Approach for Atromentin Production 
Following low phenotypic output observed from the genomic integration route, a second strategy 
was attempted employing a high-copy episomal plasmid pRS423 for atrA gene, the npgA-pTEF2-
pTEF1 fragment, and the tENO2-NatMX fragment. The purpose of this approach was to increase 
the gene dosage and consequently improve metabolite yield, given that episomal plasmids having 
an origin of replication 2μ are able to replicate in higher copy numbers compared to 
chromosomally integrated plasmids [89]. The PCR-amplified fragments were cloned into the 
pRS423 plasmid using USER cloning, and E. coli transformation was attempted for its propagation 
and subsequent plasmid extraction. However, no colonies were observed on LB plates 
supplemented with ampicillin, suggesting that assembly may not have been successful.  
 
This outcome can be attributed to a number of reasons. Firstly, large and complex inserts, 
especially those encoding biosynthetic enzymes that can cause metabolic burden, can destabilize 
plasmids or E. coli transformation efficiency [90]. The atrA and npgA cassette combined 
constitutes an insert of considerable size, and high expression potential under constitutive 
promoters can result in reduced growth caused by metabolic stress or toxicity [91]. Second, 
improper USER cloning assembly or poor DpnI digestion may have been the cause of non-
functional plasmids. The absence of colony growth on selective media may have resulted from 
inefficient ligation or low transformation efficiency.  
 
This episomal strategy, despite not advancing to yeast transformation, gave valuable insights into 
the challenges of constructing and maintaining large fungal assemblies in E. coli. Such limitations 
emphasized the complexity of episomal cloning for secondary metabolite pathways and the 
necessity for optimizing this assembly strategies. Though functional expression was not achieved,

75 
the outcomes from this route help clarify practical limitations and pave the way for future research 
focused on optimization of episomal strategies in fungal pathway engineering.  
6.3 Heterologous Expression of apvA Gene for Aspulvinone E 
Production in Yeast and Evaluation of Chaperone Co-Expression 
 
The third strategy aimed to enhance aspulvinone E production by mating the engineered apvA-
Expression strain with a chaperone overexpression library, which consists of 68 separately 
engineered S. cerevisiae strains overexpressing single or combinations of molecular chaperones. 
This was grounded in the hypothesis that proper folding and stabilization of the heterologous 
ApvA enzyme, a complex NRPS-like protein, could be one of the central bottlenecks in pathway 
functionality. This was a different strategy from those that were previously attempted by focusing 
on post-translational optimization rather than transcriptional or genomic integration strategies.  
 
The mating strategy was successful, yielding a full set of heterozygous diploid colonies (figures 
40-42). Colonies produced were plated on SC selection media and screened for fluorescence under 
UV light, which occurs naturally on aspulvinone E. Fluorescence was detected, despite being 
weakly, it demonstrated that the heterologous pathway was expressed functionally to a certain 
extent. This represents a promising outcome, as neither the episomal expression nor genomic 
integration alone had previously yielded phenotypic output. The weak but apparent fluorescence 
suggests that although pathway activation was successful, one or more biosynthetic steps were 
suboptimal.  
 
To further compare strain performance, corrected mean fluorescence intensity values were 
compared for all colonies and sorted by chaperone combination. Two complementary plots were 
generated. First, a boxplot (figure 44) indicates the distribution and range of fluorescence for each 
strain across replicates. While most strains showed tight distributions around low intensities, 
several of them, such as SSA2-SSA1, HSC82-HSP82, and HSC82-STI1, showed both higher 
medians and larger interquartile ranges, reflecting consistent but modest enhancement of pathway 
output. Outliers in these distributions probably reflect the variability in colony growth or local 
overexpression effects. 
 
==PAGE:84==
High-Throughput Screening Methods for Improving Small Molecule Production in Saccharomyces cerevisiae

Second, a summary line graph (figure 45) ranks the strains by average corrected fluorescence 
intensity. The highest-scoring pairs also mirrored those with the broadest intensity distributions, 
supporting the significance of chaperone pairing. SSA2-SSA1, HSC82-HSP82, and HSC82-
HSP42 were among the highest-scoring candidates, which is consistent with previous findings that 
members of the Hsp70/Hsp90 families, such as SSA1/SSA2 and HSC82, are critical in stabilizing 
large heterologous proteins and biosynthetic enzymes [63]. These findings validate the overall 
hypothesis stating that the increased chaperone capacity can facilitate functional folding and 
activation of NRPS-like enzymes in S. cerevisiae.  
 
Despite promising signals, fluorescence was relatively low overall, suggesting that overexpression 
of chaperones is insufficient for optimal aspulvinone E biosynthesis. This aligns with the 
previously discussed challenges of reconstituting fungal secondary metabolism in S. cerevisiae, 
such as precursor limitation, potential domain interaction incompatibilities, and lack of essential 
post-translational modifications. Furthermore, as the strains used in this strategy were diploid and 
heterozygous, allele balance of expression may have affected performance [92]. Future analyses 
might benefit from using homozygous diploids, which could yield clearer and more distinguishable 
outcomes. 
 
Overall, this pathway confirmed the potential of folding-directed optimization of non-native 
biosynthesis. While not definitive, the detectable fluorescence and top-performing strains 
constitute a worthwhile strategy for follow-up engineering. Together, with the previous routes, 
these findings highlight the complexity of reconstituting secondary metabolite pathways but also 
the value of modular, iterative strategies to improve expression, folding, and pathway flux.

7 Conclusion 
 
The aim of this project was to recreate the atromentin and aspulvinone E biosynthetic pathway in 
S. cerevisiae via heterologous expression of atrA and apvA, respectively. A range of synthetic 
biology tools, including USER cloning, yeast transformation, colony PCR, and strain mating, were 
combined to establish foundational methodologies that could be replicated or optimized in future 
research. 
 
The genomic integration of atrA confirmed effective delivery and stable integration of a multi-
gene construct, as confirmed by selection and colony PCR. However, the lack of visible pigment 
means that one or more limiting factors, hampered the reconstitution of the pathway. The episomal 
strategy, designed with the purpose of increasing gene dosage, was unsuccessful due to possible 
issues in plasmid assembly or instability, suggesting challenges in the expression of large fungal 
genes in bacterial systems for cloning purposes.   
 
In comparison, the apvA strategy using a strain harboring the gene mated with the chaperone 
overexpression library showed limited success. Weak but reproducible fluorescence was seen, and 
the signal was improved with some combination of chaperones, confirming that protein folding 
plays a crucial role for pathway expression of functional protein. Fluorescence quantification in 
68 overexpression strains allowed the selection of candidate targets for optimization. 
 
Overall, the project demonstrated that successful metabolite production involves more than just 
gene integration, while also confirming potential of modular pathway engineering in yeast. 
Important factors include post-translational modification, enzyme folding, expression levels, and 
precursor availability. This study emphasizes the complexity of fungal biosynthetic pathway 
recreation and the need for comprehensive optimization strategies.

8 Future Perspectives 
 
For continued work on this research, future studies should aim to enhance metabolite production 
through both atrA and apvA expression systems. Gene dosage elevation, through techniques such 
as multi-copy genomic integration, can alleviate expression limitations that are inherent in single-
copy plasmids. The utilization of inducible or tunable promoters could provide improved control 
over expression, preventing toxicity and reducing metabolic burden on the host. Genetic 
manipulation of the upstream biosynthetic pathway, particularly of L-tyrosine and 4-HPP 
represents a promising route to address precursor scarcity. If post-translational activation remains 
a bottleneck, screening alternatives or codon-optimized PPTases may increase enzyme activity. 
The preliminary results of the chaperone overexpression screen suggest that stable co-expression 
of specific folding assistants, or synergy-testing of combination of chaperones, may enhance AtrA 
or ApvA activity and should be investigated systematically. Further research must involve 
metabolomic approaches such as LC-MS or HPLC to directly detect and quantify atromentin and 
aspulvinone E, enabling improved assessment of pathway performance. Finally, for the atrA 
strategy, execution of the CRI-SPA process, including high-throughput mating with the YKO 
deletion collection and resolution and subsequent ploidy resolution, could expose deletions that 
enhance pathway flux, enhance cofactor availability, or promote metabolite export. Notably, this 
method can also be employed for the apvA expression system, where implementing CRI-SPA 
would not only allow systematic selection of beneficial host changes but also ensure homozygous 
integration in all diploid strains with reduced gene dosage imbalance. These combined future uses 
present a targeted and scalable mode for fungal biosynthetic expression optimization in S. 
cerevisiae
