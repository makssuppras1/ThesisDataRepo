Abstract
With the increasing need to decarbonise the energy sector, the concept of placing wind
farms far offshore with an installation that allows efficient transmission of energy to shore
is gaining attention. However, these projects, some of which involve the construction of
an artificial island, still have many uncertainties.
This thesis focuses on a risk assessment for events that could potentially jeopardize the
entire system and a techno-economic analysis to develop a cost model for an energy
island depending on the size of the project in the range of 2 to 10 GW and assuming the
construction of an artificial island.
The first part of the work proposes the events that pose a risk to the whole previously de-
fined system and then, using the simplified Failure Mode Effects and Criticality Analysis
approach, a risk assessment is made. In the second part of the study, the available knowl-
edge on the individual components of the energy island was gathered and, by combining
them, a methodical framework was developed that allows project costs to be estimated
with a high degree of flexibility regarding the energy island’s sub-systems.
The aim of this work is to fill a gap in the currently available research and to provide
a starting point for more detailed analyses of the risk and techno-economics of energy
islands.

1
Introduction
The energy island in the sense analyzed in this paper is still at the concept level. In the
scientific literature, the term energy island very often means i.e. isolated villages, small
cities, urban districts, rural areas with weak or non-existing grid connections or physical
islands [1]. In this study, risk and techno-economic assessment of energy island projects
similar to the project of the Danish government will be carried out.
1.1
Motivation and background
Danish government plans to build two energy islands, one on Bornholm and the other an
artificial one in the North Sea. The islands are intended to be a hub to which offshore wind
farms will be connected and from which energy will be exported to Denmark and neigh-
bouring countries. In addition, the island in the North Sea should allow more convenient
use of wind resources located far from the shore. The next steps in the development of
energy islands include the expansion of the islands with energy storage facilities and the
use of electrical energy transferred by the islands for its conversion into other forms of
energy (Power-to-X). The scope of these modifications will be defined by the future state
of knowledge and technology.
This ambitious initiative, which is expected to bring green electricity to more than 13 mil-
lion households, raises the question of whether the chosen locations and project design
exhaust the potential pool of such projects. It should be remembered that the energy
island project is still in the conceptual stage, there are no working equivalents of such
a system and therefore one can venture to say that this topic is not yet widely studied.
However, the energy island concept is interdisciplinary and its various aspects have been
the subject of numerous studies. Thus it should be possible to attempt to find answers to
the questions that arise in connection with this project. What conditions must be met for
this type of project to be possible? Are such projects also possible in other parts of the
world? Is only offshore wind energy suitable for such a project? These are not the only
examples of issues the answers to which would help accelerate the green transition. As
far as possible, this work will try to dispel some of the doubts about energy islands and
thus contribute to the energy transition.
1.2
Objectives
The purpose of this work is not to make another technical report. For this reason, the
Danish government’s project will be one of the options under consideration and not the
definition of an energy island. It will be examined what else an energy island could look
like, what the various solutions entail, and what costs are to be expected. The scope of
this study would be divided into two main parts. In the first part, a risk assessment will be
carried out. The work will be divided as follows:
• The system/s that are the subject of this work will be defined.
• Technical risks associated with the construction and operation of the energy island
will be defined and analyzed.
• A criticality analysis will be carried out.
In the next step, a techno-economic analysis will be performed. It will include:

1.3
Structure of this work
The thesis is structured as follows:
• Literature review discussing the state of current research on energy islands;
• Risk assessment according to the Failure Mode Effects and Criticality Analysis stan-
dard where risks affecting the operation of the whole system are analysed. The
chapter consists of a definition of failure modes and their possible causes, an as-
sessment of probability, criticality and concludes with a discussion;
• Techno-economic analysis in which the costs of the energy island project are cal-
culated according to size, and a case study is presented. This chapter consists of
an analysis of the available solutions and the definition of the cost of those selected
depending on the size of the project, the selected formulas are implemented in a
case study and the chapter ends with a discussion;
• Conclusion part containing a summary of the entire work.

2
Literature review
This section is divided into three parts, the first of which reviews the literature on energy
islands the second of which reviews literature that focuses on risk assessments from the
scope covered by an investment such as an energy island and the third of which reviews
the techno-economic literature about technologies that can be applied to the energy island
project.
2.1
Energy island
The scientific literature on energy islands is not very extensive but is updated with new
articles and studies from month to month. The report, titled ”Cost benefit analysis and
climate footprint of energy islands in the North Sea and Baltic Sea” [2] prepared by COWI
analyzes in detail the scenarios, costs, technology and amount of materials needed for
the construction of two energy islands. The report focuses on two localisations (Bornholm
and the artificial island in the North Sea) under consideration by the Danish government
and currently provides one of the most comprehensive descriptions of the energy island
concept as an energy hub. Klomp W. together with his associates in their conference pa-
per [3] analyze challenges in the construction of energy island in the North Sea. The paper
examines 3 artificial island concepts in terms of cost, constructability and CO2 emissions.
The amount of materials needed was also calculated. The purpose of the document is to
give support to authorities and developers in the process of choosing the final solution.
In the next conference paper [4] Juhl J. and Kristensen A. on behalf of potential investors
of the energy island in the North Sea, comparing this project with other artificial islands
analyze it in terms of possible technologies of construction and implementation. Finally
Nicolaos A. Cutululis et al. in their work [5] point to the very serious challenges associated
with integrating the energy island into the grid and with the operation of a non-inertia en-
ergy generation system of such a scale. This was part of a publication focused on projects
implemented by the Danish government.
The next group of publications are those on the North Sea Wind Power Hub project. In
the report ”Unlocking the North Sea as a Green Powerplant” [6] they present their concept
of developing the resources of the North Sea. According to the idea, the energy island is
to be a small part of the future 180 GW energy system. The work, which is divided into
5 parts, addresses such topics as offshore hydrogen production, energy hubs and the
integration of energy markets. Next North Sea Wind Power Hub consortium document
[7] is a continuation of the work on the concept of energy development of the North Sea
and is a feasibility study of the project. Specific technological solutions and ideas on how
to realize such an ambitious project are discussed. These are not the only publications
available under the project, and further work and development is announced.
It is possible to distinguish a group of works dealing with the subject of integration of such
projects into European energy systems. Alexandra Lüth et al. investigate ways to con-
nect future energy islands to the energy system [8]. They are considering two options:
integrating energy islands via electricity versus hydrogen infrastructure. They check the
advantages and disadvantages of both solutions and end up concluding that electricity
system benefits more from connecting close to shore wind farms via power cable and in
turn, for energy islands far from the shore, electrolysis allows for greater benefits. A sort

are examined as it is a main driver for political support of, or resistance to, the project.
Martin Kristiansen et al. in their work present the concept of a power link island [12] which
is an artificial island for transnational power exchange and distribution of offshore wind re-
sources. Further in this work, the economic benefits and system implications under three
different case studies were evaluated. Presented assumptions of the power link island
are similar to the one implemented by the Danish government (VindØ) but their scale is
different.
João Gorenstein Dedecca with his team, in two works, further develop the topic of an inte-
grated offshore European transmission network. In the first of their articles [13] they study
transmission expansion pathways of the grid, which are non-optimal and path dependent,
using a novel and open-source simulation model for offshore transmission investments.
In the second of their articles [14] they analyze the governance of the North Sea offshore
grid expansion using the dimensions of level, implementation obligation, and implemen-
tation discretion. Through their work, they identified 5 challenges for the governance of
the integrated offshore grid.
The publications discussed above do not exhaust the literature on energy islands and
there are also studies that should be classified in other categories. Malte Jansena et al.
instead of evaluating hypothetical designs of energy islands present a bottom-up capi-
tal cost estimate to compare the Power Hub located in the North Sea with conventional
point-to-point connection for offshore wind farms [15]. According to their work, such in-
vestment exceeds the threshold of profitability when more than 10 GW will be connected
to the energy hub and will reduce CO2 emissions by 10 Mt per year thanks to an increased
number of interconnections. Nevertheless, this is another work drawing attention to the
uneven distribution of benefits related to the operations of the energy hub. In a slightly
different line of work Hongyu Zhang et al. model and analyze offshore energy hubs [16].
By means of a mixed-integer linear programming model, the role of offshore energy hubs
is investigated in the transition of an offshore energy system toward a zero-emission en-

that the system with offshore energy hubs can potentially reduce CO2 emissions by 49%
and energy losses by 10%, compared to a system with only offshore renewables, gas

In the literature, numerous studies consider the energy island as an isolated energy sys-
tem. Selected studies with the potential to contribute to this work are listed below. Karl

fossil fuels and base its power generation on the wind. He checks not only the processes

using Santiago, Cape Verde island as an example [20]. The work highlights the benefits
that come from making energy sources more flexible that is cheaper energy and the pos-

if necessary. However, it is impossible to list all research on energy islands in this sense
due to their often local nature.
2.2
Risk assessment
Methods for assessing risk are well researched and described. This subsection lists pa-
pers examining risk in areas that, due to the interdisciplinary nature of the energy island
project, can provide valuable input. Ge Hou et al. in a review of risk assessment meth-
ods for offshore wind turbine foundations [21] provide an up-to-date overview of methods
for risk assessment but also potential data for analysis (FMEA or HAZOP) in the field of
offshore wind turbine foundations. Suggest ways to make analysis more effective and
highlight possible challenges. Yunna Wu et al. presented a risk assessment of a renew-
able energy-based island microgrid using the HFLTS-cloud (hesitant fuzzy linguistic term
sets) model method [22]. Analysis conducted for technical, economic, environmental and
social factors expands the set of events considered and assesses their possibility, sever-
ity and uncontrollability. A. Abramic et al. studying the impact of offshore wind farms on
the environment [23] determine a list of environmental risks and carry out their assess-
ment. Work is based on the EU standard: Good Environmental Status. This work as well
as other EIA reports provides a broad definition of the environmental risks of building and
operating offshore structures. Hervé Corvellec and Åsa Boholm in their work consider
how risk is defined in reports such as the EIA [24]. Using the EIA for the Kriegers Flak
wind farm project (2004) as an example, they analyzed the ways by which the scale of
the risks can be distorted and noted the need for precision and unambiguity to ensure the
quality of such studies. DNV report on Navigational Risk Assessment of Hesselø Offshore
Wind Farm [25] is an example of how to define and determine risks associated with marine
traffic around offshore structures. Such reports do not count as scientific literature, but
nevertheless raise very relevant issues and allow for even more accurate identification
of risks. Pascal Lienert et al. Study public acceptance of high-voltage power lines [26].
The study examined how attitudes toward high-voltage lines change depending on the
information available to the respondents. In the context of determining social risks in an
investment such as an energy island, the article shows how public perception of energy
infrastructure is sensitive to public awareness.

2.3
Techno-economic assessment
As in the case of risk assessment, every year there are many new techno-economic anal-
yses of projects related to energy sector. The following are those that could allow to
deepen the work on certain branches of energy island projects. Ingo Drummen et al.
propose the concept of Design of a Modular Floating Multi-Purpose Island [27]. Using a
heuristic approach, they determine the rough dimensions, costs and loads. X. Xiang et al.
in their article [28] compare available technological solutions for power transmission from
offshore wind farms, their advantages and disadvantages as well as costs. Then, in their
next article [29], they further expand this topic to include onshore wind turbines and also
update the data relative to their previous work. Ellen Jump et al. in a report dedicated to
substations [30] conduct techno-economic analysis on floating and fixed solutions. Their
study focuses on HVAC (high voltage alternating current) substations and covers a ca-
pacity range of 400 to 700 MW. Nouri B. et al. in their work compare network codes for AC
and HVDC technologies for renewable energy sources [31]. It is an overview of solutions
applied in different synchronous areas in Europe and a set of suggestions for better inte-
gration of renewable sources into the power system. Finally, studies by agencies such as
IRENA or the IEA provide a thorough overview of available technological solutions, the
economic situation and the costs of individual technologies.
2.4
Current state of research
From the section above, it is possible to conclude that while some research in terms of
the Danish government’s projects has been done, the concept of an energy island as an
energy hub itself has not yet been thoroughly investigated. The available studies mainly
focus on the North Sea Energy Island project and analyze various options for its technical
implementation. Preliminary estimates of costs and quantities of materials for construction
are also available. The second main group are documents on energy hubs and meshed
grids. However, they do not exhaust the available research directions on such concepts
because these studies again mainly focus on technical issues. There is a lack of studies
exploring the impact of such investments on the environment or society. There is a lack of
research on the potential risks associated with such investments and finally, there is a lack
of research on potential energy island configurations. Danish projects have narrowed the
discourse on the subject to a hub transmitting energy from offshore wind farms to land.
This work by its scope should contribute to a more thorough study of the energy island
concept.

3
Risk assessment
In this chapter, an analysis of the risks associated with the construction and operation of
energy islands will be presented. The system in question will be defined, the risk assess-
ment method will be presented and the results will be discussed.
3.1
System definition
The main object of the analysis will be a 3GW energy island considered in two variants:
an artificial island and an ”energy island’s” infrastructure located on land (most likely an
island as the goal is to gain access to wind resources far offshore through an energy
hub). Since an energy island is a very large and complex energy system, instead of
focusing on individual system components such as a wind turbine, several key elements
are highlighted in this analysis, below the list main design intentions of each element are
presented.
• Energy island as a whole system,
• Artificial island/big floating structure,
• Cluster of wind turbines (radial connection),
• Substation with MV/HV transformer,
• HVAC export cable,
• Energy island’s (HV)AC/HVDC converter station,
• HVDC transmission link,
• HVDC/HVAC converter station onshore.
Energy Island plays the role of an energy hub for three 1GW offshore wind farms, allow-
ing the use of far-offshore wind resources, can connect different synchronous areas, and
also increases the energy security of the countries connected to the system by increasing
the number of transmission links. Artificial island will be a structure allowing to locate
energy conversion equipment offshore, a platform allowing for later expansion of the in-
vestment, a port for vessels related to the construction and operation of wind farms, and
a warehouse. Cluster of wind turbines is the smallest system under consideration, as
the failure of a single turbine has a negligible impact on the operation of the entire energy
island. The cluster under consideration counts up to 6 turbines with a power of 15MW
connected by a 66 kV cable. Substation with MV/HV transformer is another compo-
nent of the system, necessary when the wind farm is located far from the energy island,
wind turbine strings are connected to it via medium voltage cables. On the substation
are located transformers and other technical equipment that allow to raise the voltage
and ensure safety. HVAC export cable provides export of generated energy if a wind
farm is too far from the energy island, part of this system are also reactive power com-
pensators. (HV)AC/HVDC converter station according to its name, it is used to change
current from alternating to direct current. This makes it possible to transmit power in a
way that provides advantages over AC technology, which makes the energy island con-
cept worth considering. HVDC transmission link is part of the system responsible for
exporting power to land, the cable located on the seabed. HVDC/HVAC converter sta-
tion onshore is the last part of the system, responsible for converting current from DC to
AC and integrating power to the local grid.

3.2
Methodology
In order to design an energy island in a safe and feasible way on a concept stage there
should be an attempt to identify the risks so as to include them in the project stage. For
this purpose, a method based on Failure Mode Effects and Criticality Analysis (FMECA)
was used. Below is a description of the method and the individual steps.
• System definition and function description - description of the individual elements
with their functions are in section 3.1;
• Failure Mode - describes an event that leads to the occurrence of a failure;
• Failure Cause - describes the cause of failure mode;
• Likelihood - in this step, the probability of failure is defined. Due to the fact that
the energy island has not yet been built, it is not possible to use quantitative and
semi-quantitative methods. For some components that have been in operation for
a long time, if possible additional data will be used, but in general, the qualitative
method will be applied to assess the likelihood of occurrence of failure scenarios.
Matti Niclas Scheu et al. faced a similar problem and it was decided to use their
proposed solution [32]. Their approach is presented in Table 3.1;
• Failure Effect - describes the final state of the device/system after failure mode in a
realistic worst-case scenario;
• Consequence - describes consequences of failure effect and the importance of a
particular scenario. Consequences are analyzed for each defined system but the
main goal is to study how the failures of the individual systems affect the operation
of the entire energy island. The criteria and description of the consequences are
shown in the Table 3.2;
• Criticality - describes a quantified result combining the earlier collected information
about the potential failure mode, the likelihood of occurrence and the consequences
of the effect. The formula used for the calculation of the criticality is presented below:
Criticality = Likelihood · Consequence

• Fire on artificial island - It is assumed that the fire protection systems did not func-

3.7
Discussion
A total of 35 unique events were defined, almost all of which pose little or no risk to the
energy island system.
By far the greatest threat is posed by a cyber attack and taking control of the system. This
is due to the main component of VSC converters, the IGBT, and the fact that it exhibits
high heat loss. If not properly controlled, it can reach dangerous temperatures within
milliseconds and become damaged. Such an event would result in the need to replace
the converters and, as these devices are not readily available, would mean that the energy
island would be out of service for a longer period of time. It can be argued whether such
considerations are valid, but in light of, for example, the blown-up Nord Stream pipeline, it
was decided to take up the subject. Next, the greatest threat is from fires in the electrical
infrastructure. They can also cause very serious damage, and since it is not possible to
control the atmospheric factors, the only thing that can be done is to try to adequately
install the lightning protection system.
Risk and techno-economic assessment of energy island projects
17
==PAGE:24==
Next on the scale of defined risks are those related to disruption in control systems. There
are basically as many potential causes as there are signals entering the control system,
and while it is assumed that emergency systems will work correctly, such failures can
still be dangerous. The last medium defined risk is the subsidence of the artificial island.
This is a very unlikely event but the consequences would be very serious and even more
difficult to neutralise. The remaining incidents can be classified as low risk. still, as a
consequence of some of them, the impairment of the energy island’s functionality could
be serious, but these incidents are unlikely and, moreover, thanks to professional action,
can be completely prevented.
The risk assesment methodology deserves a separate paragraph. All the work was done
by one person. Typically, such studies are carried out in a group of experts; it is not
possible to have adequate knowledge and experience for such a multibranch project. It
is therefore important to bear in mind that risk assessments may be subject to error, and
that access to statistics or other documents that are not part of publicly available studies
could have a positive impact on the accuracy of the results. An additional difficulty is the
analysis of a system that has not yet been developed anywhere in the world, which means
that a certain part of the study has to be based on assumptions, e.g. regarding some of
the technical solutions. This is also affecting the quality of results.

Techno-economic assessment
In this chapter, a techno-economic model allowing to analyze the properties of an energy

4.1
Scope definition and methodology
The energy island concept is very interdisciplinary and, depending on the project, can
be even more complex.
Each project will also be different in many ways, e.g.
wind
and weather conditions, sea depth, accessibility to land infrastructure or condition of the
seabed. For this reason, in techno-economic analysis, it would be necessary to choose
a specific project with specific conditions to analyze. However, the subject could be ap-
proached differently. The design of the energy island will be divided into modules and
each of them will be described and, if possible, parameterized. This will allow, with the
assumptions about the revenue of an energy island’s project, to make a simplified finan-

• Analysis and selection of existing technologies for the energy island from a techno-

• Division of selected solutions into modules and development of their implementation

• Determination of the necessary area for the energy island depending on the chosen

• Case study with calculation of basic economic indicators

4.2.4
AC/DC converter stations
For an energy island, the best solution is to choose DC technology. This is due to several
reasons:
• It is possible to transport energy over long distances with less losses than with AC
technology - this is especially important for the energy island due to the scale of the
projects. This is because there are no reactive losses.
• Fewer cables can be used to transmit the same power, again due to the scale of the
project this is both a design simplification and a reduction in CAPEX.
• The power flow can be fully controlled and non-synchronous areas can be con-
nected. In this way, the energy island can further enhance energy integration be-
tween the consumers connected to it. An additional advantage is a black start ca-
pability(VSC).
These were only the technological advantages of choosing DC. In the case of energy
transmission via an underwater cable, the break even distance is about 60 km [28]. In
case of energy island, choosing DC is simply cheaper.
For direct current transmission a distinction is made between two main technologies. Cur-
rent Sourced, Line Commutated Converter(LCC) and Voltage Sourced Converter (VSC).
The topic of these two technologies has been extensively researched and described in
the literature, so only the arguments for choosing VSC technology in the context of an
energy island will be presented here.
• VSC provides more control (turn on/off) than LCC
• Allows bi-directional power flow and current reversal
• Takes up less space
• VSC has a black start capability
• VSC almost/ doesn’t require AC filters (high degree of space saving)
4.2.5
Array and export cables
The difference between array cables and export cables is only the intended use. Array
cables will be intended for AC operation usually at 33/66 kV whereas export cables will
be intended for DC operation at voltages even above 500 kV. However, the method of
installation is the same and the process of trenching the seabed and laying the cable in it
is very costly and means that every kilometre of cable, irrespective of its size and purpose,
is carefully planned to minimise its length and eliminate the need to cross it.

4.3.1
Platform or Artificial island
During the process of writing this paper studies on artificial islands giving some general
relationship to estimate the cost of the island was not found. In addition, determining a
general relationship is challenging because the cost of an artificial island depends on a
large number of variables:
• Water depth
• Island surface
• Island design (wave conditions)
• Distance from shore
• Availability of sand and other building materials
However, project-specific data are available. These data were therefore collected and a
summary was prepared from them so that the order of magnitude of the costs of the arti-
ficial island could be estimated. Unfortunately, due to the complexity of the issue and the
scarcity and accuracy of the data, it was not possible to construct even a simplified model
of artificial island costs which, once implemented, would be at least marginally compatible
with the available data.
Available cost data on offshore platforms on which HVDC equipment can be placed indi-
cates that the artificial island is a cost-competitive solution even for small projects of 3 GW
in size. The additional functionality that can be obtained with an artificial island makes the
use of an offshore platform justifiable only in very specific situations.

The equations presented have allowed the cost of the energy island to be estimated for
projects ranging in size from 2 to 10 GW, and by keeping the analysis modular, it is pos-
sible to adapt this work fairly flexibly to other reasonably similar projects. In terms of the
projects analysed, it can be seen that the cost of the artificial island for increasingly larger
projects represents a smaller and smaller share of the total CAPEX. This even prompts
the construction of a larger island than necessary in order to maximise modernisation
potential.
When more than half of the cost is accounted for by the turbines including foundations, it
is very clear that the larger the turbine, the more cost-effective the project will be. In such
a large project, where several hundred turbines will be required, it can be expected that
economies of scale would allow noticeable savings, however, this is an element that is
not included in this work. Another cost worth commenting on is the export cables. The
assumption made results in a linear increase in price, but it is easy to see that depending
on the project this price can vary greatly and the only potential saving is that resulting from
economies of scale.
When it comes to operating costs, it can be seen that basically all of it is due to the
operation of wind farms. Even though they are lower thanks to the artificial island, they are
still dominant. Work is currently underway and systems are being developed to allow for
cheaper inspections and thus make preventive management more cost-effective, which
should result in a decrease in these costs in the future.
When analysing these costs, it should be borne in mind that they have been calculated
using equations that do not take account of the situation in the materials market, avail-
ability, etc. where possible, an attempt has been made to verify the calculated costs with
the latest data from completed projects, but there is still a wide margin of error. This is
particularly evident in larger projects, where while the cost of the 3GW project calculated
by COWI [2] and in this work are similar(6,23 b€and 7,78 b€), the discrepancy is much
greater for a 10 GW project(21,08 b€and 28,18 b€). Obviously the projects analysed are
different from each other, but given the distribution of costs by category and the increasing
divergence with size, it can be assumed that there is scope for refinement of the formulas
used.
The analysis of the case study shows that the project is feasible even though the price
of electricity is assumed to be very low which is undoubtedly due, among other things,
to the high Capacity Factor 80% of the project is financed with cheap credit, so it can be
expected that if the WACC would increase, it would affect the profitability of the project,
but it is a very good forecast. Despite the fact that the project in its essence does not
differ much from other wind projects in terms of cost-effectiveness (i.e. a small margin
for changing conditions), the other benefits of the project and the impact on accelerating
decarbonization are undeniable.

5
Conclusions
In conclusion, this thesis has delved into the analysis of risks to the security of the en-
ergy island system and the development of a methodological framework for the techno-
economic analysis of energy island depending on size of the project.
Risk assessment
The energy island project is not exposed to very serious risks, the most serious of which
is related to cyber security. The rest of them are manageable and, apart from locating the
investment on an artificial island, are nothing new that individuals looking after the safety
of existing power plants have had to deal with. In addition, most of the risks discussed
in this work can be minimised by using redundant sub-systems, which, given the cost of
the project, seems reasonable. It should be borne in mind that when performing a full
risk assessment, rather than a simplified one as in this work, the importance of individual
events may be distributed differently than presented.
Techno-economic assessment
Existing technology makes it possible to realise a cost-effective investment such as an
energy island. moreover, trends in the development of wind energy suggest that the costs
of this type of investment will only decrease. These conclusions, however, only apply to
projects in relatively shallow waters - up to about 50m. The potential of energy islands,
to be fully assessed, needs to wait for further development of the floating technology.
However, within the range developed, mainly due to access to windy sites the energy
island presents a very competitive LCOE and positive NPV in addition to its non-carbon
advantages. The ratio of the cost of the artificial island to the other components of the
project suggests that such projects will not only be energy hubs, but that the opportunity
to increase the available space relatively cheaply and gain additional functionality will be
exploited.
Avenues for future research
This work could be developed through a detailed risk analysis carried out by a group of
experts and further extended to include environmental and social aspects. In addition,
energy storage and/or conversion solutions located on the island may be included in the
analysis. From a technoecomic point of view, the work could be expanded to include a
more precise definition and update of costs. In addition, a more in-depth study of the
construction of artificial islands and additional energy storage/conversion facilities would
help to assess the viability of different configurations of energy island projects with more
functions than just being an energy hub.
