Abstract
This thesis aims at recommending an appropriate runoﬀfunction for urban pervi-
ous areas, with the intent of implementation in the distributed hydrologic model
SCALGO. The method employed to attain this goal is a comparison of runoﬀgen-
eration results from three hydrologic models; the SCS-CN, T/A and kinematic wave
model. Each model is applied to a catchment in Skanderborg, Denmark and results
are obtained for both pervious and impervious areas separately, as well as for the
total catchment. Four diﬀerent rain events of 10, 20, 70 and 110 mm are applied.
Results from the 70 mm rain event show that the kinematic wave model predicts
runoﬀvolumes 4 to 4.5 times greater than the SCS-CN and T/A model. For the
110 mm rain event the kinematic wave model predicts runoﬀvolumes 24 and 5.6
times greater than the SCS-CN and T/A model respectively.
It is concluded that the T/A model overestimates runoﬀgeneration by smaller rain
events, and underestimated runoﬀfrom larger rain events. The results from the
kinematic wave model represent an upper limit, due to the prerequisites of the
model not being fully met.
The ﬁnal recommendation is a runoﬀfunction for urban pervious areas based on
the SCS-CN model, including an adjustment of the parameter lambda from 0.2 to
0.1, decreasing the size of the initial loss from 32.5 to 16.2 mm.
An analysis of the soil properties input parameters for the SCS-CN and kinematic
wave model, indicates that this is the most signiﬁcant parameter in regards to mod-
elling pervious areas. Consequently, the determination of this is an essential task.

Introduction
One of the earliest attempts to scientiﬁcally describe the relationship between rain-
fall and runoﬀis more than 345 years old. It was conducted by Pierre Perrault,
and born out of a curiosity to investigate whether the amount of precipitation was
enough to feed the ﬂow of the Seine river running through Paris. Pierre Perrault
conducted quantitative studies, and even though his methods of measuring and
estimating would seem rather primitive to the eye of the modern hydrologist, his
approach of conducting quantitative scientiﬁc experimentation and describing his
results mathematically was very progressive and became a major stepping stone in
the ﬁeld of hydrology (Nace 1974). However, not much further progress happened
in the ﬁeld of rainfall-runoﬀmodelling until the beginning of the 20th century. In
the ﬁrst half of the century, a new understanding of the relationship between rain-
fall, runoﬀand the sustainability of agricultural land emerged. This understanding
brought with it an increased focus and economic funds for research, in order to
predict areas of concern and prevent negative consequences by the means of opti-
mized land use practises or instalment of measures for ﬂow retarding. In the second
half of the century the digital evolution and advancement in technology meant an
acceleration of discoveries and rainfall runoﬀmodels representing various levels of
complexity (Dˇzub´akov´a 2010).
Today, many places in the world are more vulnerable to ﬂooding as a result of
increasing urbanisation, deforestation and changing weather patterns. Predicting
the hydrologic response of a catchment is therefore as relevant as ever.
Conse-
quently rainfall-runoﬀmodelling is an integral part of water resource assess- and
management and is widely used in many diﬀerent ﬁelds such as; the design and
dimensioning of hydraulic structures, forecasting and predicting the impact of cli-
mate change, conducting environmental impact assessments, determine ﬂood risk,
wastewater management and city planning. Diﬀerent tasks calls for diﬀerent levels
of complexity and today there are wide variety of models available, however, until
recently - most of these where so called lumped models. In a lumped model the
catchment is represented by one unit, and spatial variability is therefore disregarded
(Dˇzub´akov´a 2010). However, with the continuing technological advancements, and
increasing accessibility of data, mapping spatial variability in high resolution is be-
coming more and more attainable, making distributed hydrologic modelling possible.
Distributed models do not treat the whole catchment as one unit, but rather divides
it into units, cells or pixels, this allows for rainfall and runoﬀpatterns to vary over
space and time, and increases the accuracy of the model by performing the hydro-

logical computations individually for each cell, routing the potential ﬂow or runoﬀ
from cell to cell (Vieux 2016).
SCALGO is a digital tool for creating high resolution distributed models, based on
Digital Elevation Maps (DEM’s), representing the topography. The structure of
SCALGO, combined with topographical data in high resolution, allows for predict-
ing the routing of the surface runoﬀwith high accuracy. At the time of writing
however, SCALGO neglects any hydrologic abstraction, and thus treats each pixel
as having a runoﬀcoeﬃcient equal to one. As a result, the total volume of runoﬀ
equals the total volume of precipitation, naturally leading to an overestimation re-
garding ﬂooded areas. In order to improve the results, further geophysical data,
such as a runoﬀfunction for each pixel is needed. This is a task proven to be more
complicated in terms of pervious than impervious areas, due to a highly non-linear
relationship between rainfall and runoﬀ.
Today there is no commonly acknowl-
edge ’best practise’ for estimating runoﬀfrom pervious areas, which has often been
generally completely neglected in relation to urban hydrologic models (DHI 2014).
However, the signiﬁcance of runoﬀcontribution from pervious areas has recently
gained increased recognition (Nielsen 2019) (Davidsen et al. 2018).
This thesis seeks to assess the applicability of the following hydrologic models for
generating an appropriate runoﬀfunction for urban pervious areas: The T/A model,
representing the most commonly applied modelling option for danish urban condi-
tions, the kinematic wave model representing a structural model speciﬁcally mod-
elling inﬁltration capacities and lastly the SCS-CN model. The SCS-CN model is an
empirical based conceptual model developed in the Unite States, as the application
and awareness of this model is limited in Denmark, a comprehensive review of this
method is prioritised.
The ﬁnal goal is to recommend a runoﬀfunction for urban pervious areas, for im-
plementation in a distributed hydrological runoﬀmodel, such as SCALGO with the
intention to increase the accuracy of the surface runoﬀpredictions. The basis for this
is both a critical review of the model structure, and an analysis of runoﬀgeneration
results for a typical danish urban area, from the three mentioned models.

The road to runoﬀ
Water is an integral part of life on earth. The hydrological cycle describes the con-
tinuous circulation of water between the earth and the atmosphere, many diﬀerent
processes are involved in this circulation, as illustrated in ﬁgure 1. Runoﬀis part of
the hydrological cycle and the turning point of this thesis. This ﬁrst section aims to
describe the essential processes and complexity involved in runoﬀgeneration. The
intention is to create a foundation for understanding and analysing the diﬀerent
approaches to modelling runoﬀdescribed in this thesis.

When the surface of the earth is heated by the sun, water turns from its liquid state
into water vapour and evaporates into the atmosphere. Water evaporates mainly
from oceans, but the process also occurs on the Continents, where water evaporates
from the land, forests and ﬁelds, from the surfaces of lakes and rivers, and transpires
from vegetation.
When air containing water vapour becomes cooled, often initiated by uplift as a re-
sults of weather fronts, orographic eﬀects or convection, the water vapour condenses

back into liquid state in the form of tiny water droplets. The condensation process
and droplet formation is initiated by a condensation nuclei in the form of dust, salt
or ice, and as more and more droplets are formed, clouds develop (Fleisher 2010).
Clouds can carry water droplets for many kilometres and they are important for the
redistribution of water on the earth. When clouds become thick, the water droplets
aggregate and at some point become heavy enough to fall as precipitation in the
form of rain, snow, hail or sleet.
2.1
The many possible fates of precipitation
As precipitation falls towards earth by the force of gravity, there are many possible
fates awaiting. Some of the precipitation never reaches the ground, but evaporates
midair, some land in the oceans, rivers or lakes, which, for the purpose of this report,
is not of interest, and some land on the ground. However, not all water reaching
the ground turns into surface runoﬀ; interception, depression storage, evapotranspi-
ration and inﬁltration are hydrologic abstractions that intercepts rainfall before it
becomes runoﬀ. The magnitude and inﬂuence of these abstractions depend on the
catchment characteristics such as the topography, soil condition, vegetation, level of
urbanisation and percentage of impervious surfaces, the two last mentioned being
of particular interest for the purpose of this report.
2.1.1
Hydrologic abstractions
Below, the hydrologic abstractions and their potential inﬂuence for both pervious
and impervious areas will be described in further detail.
2.1.1.1
Interception
Interception, in the context of the hydrologic cycle, de-
scribes the process where precipitation adheres to, or is absorbed by surface vege-
tation cover and is returned to the atmosphere through evaporation without ever
touching the ground (Wagener et al. 2004). The ﬁnal volume of water intercepted
is highly dependent on characteristics of both the catchment area the rain event. In
the case where the duration of the rain event exceeds the time it takes to ﬁll the
storage capacity of the catchment vegetation, stemﬂow and canopy throughfall will
start taking place (Singh 2003). The longer the rain event continues hereafter, the
smaller the percentage of water is intercepted, concluding that interception plays
the biggest role in non urban environments and during rain event of short duration.

the soils within urban environments. Compaction lowers the permeability and hence
the inﬁltration capacity (Endreny 2006). The inﬂuence of the above mentioned fac-
tors, leads to the conclusion that urban catchments will, in most cases, allow for less
inﬁltration than rural or agricultural catchments. Firstly, urban environments are
to a greater degree covered by impervious surfaces in the form of roofs, pavements
and roads. Secondly, pervious areas often consist of more compacted soils decreasing
inﬁltration capacity.
The water that is inﬁltrated is inﬂuenced by gravity, however, the hydraulic connec-
tivity of a soil proﬁle tends to decrease with depth, and this can lead to a lateral
interﬂow, also known as subsurface runoﬀor throughﬂow (Hornberger et al. 2014).
In an urban setting, the occurrence of interﬂow is increased due to a higher hydraulic
conductivity around underground cables and pipes surrounded by sand and gravel.
Interﬂow can contribute as a baseﬂow to rivers and streams or even resurface in
other parts of the catchment. This phenomenon will be further described in section
2.1.2. Some of the inﬁltrated water will not become interﬂow, but will percolate
further down and become part of the groundwater reservoir where the residence
time can be very long (Singh 2003).
2.1.1.3
Depression storage
In the case where the intensity of the rain event
exceeds the local inﬁltration capacity, water will accumulate and pond on the sur-
face, where is will ﬁll any natural or artiﬁcial depressions. The water retained in
these depressions will eventually either evaporate, or contribute to soil moisture
by inﬁltration (Singh 2003). The fraction of water being contained by depression
storage depends on the texture and unevenness of the surface. Both urban and
rural, pervious and impervious areas can facilitate depression storage. The capac-
ity however is greatest in the case of uncultivated pervious ground, as agricultural
cultivation and urbanisation tends to eliminate natural depressions and promote
drainage. Typical values for depression storage capacity can be seen in table 2.

2.1.1.4
Evaporation
Heat from the surroundings, mostly in the form of solar
energy, can evaporate precipitation before it contributes to surface runoﬀ. The evap-
oration rate of a given catchment is inﬂuenced by weather and climatic factors such
as solar radiation, sunshine hours, vapour pressure, humidity, air and water surface
temperatures, wind velocity, and atmospheric pressure (Singh 2003). Evaporation
rates are higher in hot and arid regions, compared to more humid regions, as evap-
oration happens only if the surrounding air is not already saturated. Additionally,
evaporation rates from pervious areas are naturally higher as these are often covered
by vegetation capable of containing more moist and free water surfaces than urban
structures (Weyman 1978).
2.1.1.5
Transpiration
Most of the water taken up through the roots of plants,
is transpired out into the atmosphere again through pores in the stem and the
leaves. The rate of this process depends on factors such as temperature, humidity,
wind, soil-moisture availability and the type of plant (Singh 2003). As in the case
of evaporation, transpiration plays a bigger role in the case of pervious areas simply
because these often comprise of more vegetation.
2.1.2

As described in section 2.1.1, there are several abstractions intercepting rainfall from
becoming surface runoﬀ. However, as dictated by the hydrologic cycle, if the amount
of precipitation exceeds the capacity for hydrologic abstractions, surface runoﬀis
created.

3
Classiﬁcation of Hydrological Models
The nature of urban developments reduce the inﬁltration capacity dramatically and
thus increases the risk of downstream ﬂooding, damaging infrastructure, property
and even endangering lives (Wagener et al. 2004). As the capability of performing
direct hydrological measurements is limited, extrapolation from existing data via
hydrological models is highly relevant (Beven 2011). Estimations predicting runoﬀ
ﬂows and volume are crucial, as the need for ﬂood resilient cities and developments
increases. Predictions assist in the most optimal land use distribution, infrastructure
and planning of new city development in a way that best accommodate and help
prevent damages caused by excessive runoﬀ(Singh 2003).
3.1
”All models are wrong, but some are useful’
The above quote is attributed to George Box who was a statistician, however, it
can be applied to many ﬁelds of science including hydrology. A hydrological model,
or any model at all, is an approximate representation of reality. They do not pro-
vide a completely realistic picture of the reality, however, an appropriate model can
still provide important and valuable predictions that help understand hydrologic
processes and support the most optimal solutions for safe and sustainable manage-
ment.
So what is an appropriate model? Hydrology and modelling of hydrologic processes
have been characterised by great simpliﬁcations for many years. The involved factors
have often been drastically simpliﬁed, and parameters have been lumped together
(Vieux 2016). Today, advances in computing power, coupled with a growing under-
standing of the many complex hydrological processes, have resulted in a broad range
of approaches to rainfall runoﬀmodelling, representing diﬀerent levels of complexity.
The choice should be based on the objective of the modelling, understanding of the
system and the available data, computational power and level of expertise (Sitterson
et al. 2017).
3.2
Structural and spatial classiﬁcation
As mentioned, there is a broad range of hydrological RRM’s available today, and
as follows, there is a range of diﬀerent ways of classifying them. This section will
propose one way of classifying RRM’s, based on model structure and spatial struc-
ture. The aim of this section is to create an overview of the complexity, strengths
and weaknesses associated with each type of model listed. It should be noted that

arguably belong in two, or between two categories. Model hybrids and overlaps

3.2.1
Model structure
The classiﬁcation of model structure presented here, is based on the extent to which
the model is build on physical principles. Three categories are included, data-based
models, conceptual models and physics based models. Figure 4 shows a simpliﬁed

3.2.1.1
Empirical models
The empirical model, also known as black box model,
is the least complex of the three structures mentioned. The structure of empirical
models is based on data from experiment and observation. Various predicators such
as precipitation and runoﬀfor a speciﬁc catchment are recorded, and an input-output
correlation is derived, often based on statistical analysis (Vaze et al. 2011). This
method allows an empirical rainfall-runoﬀmodel to determine the runoﬀgenerated,

without any reference to the internal physical hydrological processes (Sitterson et al.
2017). An example of an empirical model is the rational method, ﬁrst proposed by
the Irish engineer Thomas James Mulvaney in 1851 (Beven 2011). The model relates
the ratio of runoﬀto rainfall by assigning a runoﬀcoeﬃcient, based on catchment
size, rainfall intensity and duration. The runoﬀcoeﬃcient is a dimensionless fac-
tor, ranging from 0 to 1, a high value represents greater runoﬀvolumes, while low
value corresponds to less runoﬀ. The simplicity of the method was beﬁtting in a
time of limited data and computational techniques. However, the method entails
certain limitations. A runoﬀcoeﬃcient comprises all the hydrological abstractions
and catchment characteristics in one constant parameter, and does not account for
the nonlinear relationship between these and the proﬁle of the rain event, or the
spatial distribution of the rain. This means that the runoﬀcoeﬃcient might over
or underestimate the runoﬀvolume generated by a speciﬁc rain event. This is es-
pecially prevalent in the case of pervious areas where the hydrologic abstractions,
such as inﬁltration capacities, is highly dependent on the amount of precipitation.
The use of runoﬀcoeﬃcients in rainfall runoﬀmodelling is still common today, and
in many cases the simplicity of implementation combined with fast computational
time is an advantage. Table 3 shows values for proposed by (Winter et al. 2011),

The conceptual model is more complex than the

between these. The level of complexity with which these interactions are described
can vary greatly from model to model. The conceptual representation of these in-

complexity. However, unlike physics-based models, not all parameters are a direct
physical interpretation, some are determined empirically or through calibration, and
several parameters can be aggregated into one (Solomatine & Wagener 2011).
The SCS-CN method, described in detail in section 5, is a great example of the

model is empirical. However, the governing assumptions can also be interpreted as a
conceptual structure, based on empirical evidence. Both deﬁnitions are acceptable.
Conceptual models have the advantages of being simple to implement and having a
fast computational time.
3.2.1.3
Physics-based models
The physics-based model, also known as process-
based, mechanistic or white box model, is based on the laws of physics. The govern-
ing equations and functions are built on physical principles regarding the hydrologi-
cal responses of the catchment, and so, an in depth understanding of the individual
hydrological processes and their interconnection is of the essence.
The structure of a physics-based model is very similar to the real-world and can pro-
vide information regarding both spatial and temporal variations within the catch-
ment (Sitterson et al. 2017). However, the requirements when setting up a physics-
based model are a high level of precise data. Running a physics-based model also
requires more time and computational power compared to empirical and conceptual
models. For these reasons, the use of physics based models is only relevant if a
high level of spatial detail is necessary, if the main focus is total runoﬀ, this level of
complexity is not needed (Solomatine & Wagener 2011).

3.2.2.1
Lumped spatial structure
The lumped approach, represented by ﬁg-
ure 5 a, considers the drainage area as one homogeneous unit. This means that
all data regarding the model, such as hydrologic parameters, input and output, is
represented by averaged values, constant in space and time. In reality rainfall and
resulting runoﬀvary, these variations can however not be modelled using lumped
models. Lumped models are often used to predict the total runoﬀamount, and do
not provide insight to speciﬁc ﬂows within the catchment (Sitterson et al. 2017).
The simplicity of a lumped model is both the advantage and the limitation. Lim-
ited data is required to run the model, however, the accuracy of the results can be
questionable, especially for large catchments, as a large number of assumptions is
associated with the model.
3.2.2.2
Semi-lumped spatial structure
A semi distributed subdivided catch-
ment, as can be seen in ﬁgure 5 b, in this case, the spatial variability within the
catchment is considered on a smaller scale than the lumped model. The catchment
can be divided according to e.g. slope, soil condition or surface cover and output
can be computed for each sub-catchment. This model requires more data than the
lumped model, however, the accuracy is likely to improve, given the quality of the
input data.

A fully distributed model take
the spatial heterogeneity into account by dividing the catchment into pixels. The

hydrological processes included in the model is then computed for each pixel, incor-

and spatial information is made available.
For many years, lumped models have been the preferred tool for predicting rainfall-
runoﬀ, compared to distributed models, partly due to limitation in data availability
and computational power.
Today the options for gathering high-resolution geo-
spatial data have expanded considerably due to a revolution in mapping and sensor
technology, making it possible to create 3D maps of the surface of the earth and con-

in the terrain surface. Furthermore, due to advances in technology and computer
power, the computational time of distributed models is no longer an obstacle of the
same magnitude (Vieux 2016). As a result of these technological advancements the
use of distributed models are becoming more and more common.

SCALGO Live
SCALGO Live is an online software tool that allows the user to visualise the ﬂow and
accumulation of water on terrain through a fully distributed digital high resolution
topographic map. This sections aims to describe SCALGO Live and the structure
on which it is based, in order to create a foundation for assessing the possibilities
and challenges associated with the implementing of a runoﬀfunction to a distributed
RRM.
4.1
The basis for SCALGO Live
The basis for SCALGO Live is geo-spatial data in the form of high resolution digital
elevation models (DEM’s). DEM’s represent the land surface elevation. In Denmark
the Danish Geodata Agency provides a freely accessible DEM which is continuously
updated. This highly detailed model is generated by laser scanning done from an
airplane where the return time, from when the laser reaches the ground and is re-
ﬂected back, is recorded and converted into distance reﬂecting surface elevation.
In Denmark 415 billion data points have been measured, and together these rep-
resent a topographical map with a horizontal resolution of 0.4 m. (Styrelsen for
Dataforsyning og Eﬀektivisering 2018). These maps are the basis on which terrain
data-processing technology and algorithms routes water on the surface of the terrain
(SCALGO 2019).and the level of detail provided by these, makes it possible to make
very precise analysis of the water movement on terrain (Rasmussen 2015).
SCALGO Live is the result of more than 20 years of groundwork and research in the
ﬁeld of big data and geometric algorithms conducted at the Center for Massive Data
Algorithms (MADALGO) at Aarhus University, Denmark, and at Duke University,
U.S. (SCALGO 2019).
4.2
SCALGO Live - a ’glass-plate’ model
The high-resolution DEM’s enables SCALGO to represent the catchment in great
life-like detail when it comes to topography, however, not all aspects of the model
bear true representation of the actual conditions. SCALGO is, at the time of writing,
designed as a ’glass-plate’ model, meaning that the runoﬀcoeﬃcient is always equal
to one, and the model routes 100% of the precipitation from one pixel to the next.
Neither the surface of the terrain, or the inﬁltration capacity is taken into account,
this becomes evident in ﬁgure 6, where a rain event of only 3 mm leaves multiple
ﬂooded areas. If inﬁltration capacities were included, the resulting ﬂooded areas

method
The, rather long, heading of this section is the name of one of the most popular
ways of determining surface runoﬀfrom agricultural or urban catchments (Shi et al.
2009) (Mishra et al. 2004). Normally it is referred to as the SCS curve number,
method or simply SCS-CN, as will be the case in the remainder of this report.
The SCS-CN method is an empirical based method that converts total rainfall into
total runoﬀby assigning a runoﬀcurve number, based on various factors such as soil
type and hydrologic conditions, to a catchment. The method is based on empirical
data from extensive ﬁeld experiments completed in the United States during the
late 1930s and early 1940s (Singh 2003). The method was ﬁrst published in the
National Engineering Handbook of Hydrology (NEH) in 1956, since then it has
undergone several revisions, and is still widely used throughout the world. Despite
the popularity of this method, it is not commonly applied in Denmark, neither
is it very well known. For these reasons, a comprehensive and detailed review is
prioritised in the following sections of this thesis.
5.1
A look at the historical background
The Soil Conservation Service (SCS), now Natural Resources Conservation Service
(NRCS), was established as a permanent agency in 1935 by the United States De-
partment of Agriculture (USDA). The establishment of the SCS happened as a
response to a new awareness of soil erosion happening as a consequence of weather
conditions such as ﬂooding and wind. The erosion of soil impacted the sustainabil-
ity of agricultural productivity, and thereby compromised the livelihood of the rural
farmers and communities (States Department of Agriculture 1999).
In 1936 the Flood Control Act was passed in the United States. Surface runoﬀ,
ﬂooding and soil erosion was recognised as a national priority, and the USDA, was
put in charge of research and action plans in order to prevent downgrading of the
soils (Williams et al. 2012). This meant greater funds were available to the SCS
and with this, also greater possibilities of experimental research and gathering of
empirical data. At the time of establishment there had been very little research
conducted in the ﬁeld of hydraulics, soil erosion and ﬂood control, and it soon
became clear that a need for extensive hydrologic data was present (Helms 2007).
Through the late 1930s and into the early 1940s the SCS pioneered doing extensive

5.2

Applying the SCS-CN method in practice can be divided into two steps, the ﬁrst is
to determine the curvenumber(s) for the catchment in question. The second is to,
based on the curvenumber, either calculate or read of a graph, the corresponding
runoﬀvolume for a given rain event. Both of these steps will be further explained
in the following sections, starting with the determination of the curve numbers.

5.5
Limitations of the SCS-CN method
There are many advantages and reasons for the wide use of the SCS-CN method. It
is uncomplicated and based on simple equations. There is only one parameter, the
CN, that needs to be established in order to apply the method. This parameter is
a function of the catchments characteristics; soil type, land cover, hydrologic condi-
tions and treatment. The relation between the CN and these characteristics is well
documented and based on numerous empirical studies. Through it was originally
37
==PAGE:52==
5.5
Limitations of the SCS-CN method
developed for agricultural its application range is broad, it can be applied in both
rural and urban areas and is suitable for ungaged watersheds.
However, there are also certain limitations associated with the use of the SCS-CN
method that one should be considerate of when applying it. These limitations will
be described below.
5.5.1
Rainfall, intensity and temporal distribution
Since the method was designed to estimate the total runoﬀvolume caused by a
total rainfall volume, one of the main limitations is the inability to include rainfall
intensity and temporal distribution. Rainfall intensity and temporal distribution are
often inversely correlated and might impact the inﬁltration, and thus the generated
runoﬀ, greatly. A short rain event with high intensity can cause inﬁltration capacities
to be exceeded and thereby result in inﬁltration excess overland ﬂow. In this case
the SCS-CN method might underestimate the runoﬀgenerated.
Another aspect of this limitation is that, in regards to urban areas, the drainage
system is often designed according to runoﬀgenerated from rain events with diﬀerent
return periods. Results for these design purposes are not attainable using the SCS-
CN method.
5.5.2
Size and slope of a catchment
The method was primarily developed for usage on areas ranging between a few
hectares and 60 km2. The reason for this is that the variety of factors aﬀecting the
CN’s becomes to diverse (Gribbin 2013). Furthermore, as mentioned in the previous
point, there is a strong possibility that rainfall patterns will vary considerably within
a larger catchment making the results unreliable. In regards to this thesis, the size is
not a limiting factor. The aim is to develop a runoﬀfunction for a typical pervious
urban area, thus ﬁeld scale data would be preferably to point scale, however, the
ﬁeld scale does not need to exceed a few hectares.
Another limitation in regard to the geography is that it is not possible to include
slope. The slope can again aﬀect the inﬁltration capacities and potentially have
great consequences. One should reconsider if another method of computing runoﬀ
would be more appropriate in an area that for example is very mountainous. The
reason that slope has not been included as an inﬂuential parameter is that agri-
cultural land in the United States is most often <5%. Independent studies have
formulated extrapolations of the SCS-CN model to include slope, e.g. (Sprenger
38
==PAGE:53==
5.5
Limitations of the SCS-CN method
1978). However, it has not been possible to ﬁnd other studies applying these meth-
ods, and thus the foundation for including them in this thesis, is not considered solid
enough.
5.5.3
Subsurface runoﬀ
The runoﬀresults generated by the SCS-CN method only accounts for the direct
surface runoﬀ. However, as described in section 2.1.2, subsurface runoﬀcan some-
times contribute considerably to the ﬁnal amount of runoﬀ. This would often apply
in catchments or sub catchments with a high inﬁltration capacity, stratiﬁed soil pro-
ﬁles, or urban soil proﬁles including pipes. If the occurrence of subsurface runoﬀ
is known, or estimated, for an area the CN could be adjusted to account for this
(Cronshey et al. 1985).
5.5.4
Initial abstraction
The initial abstraction is an empirical derived parameter, based on data from agri-
cultural watersheds. It estimates a proportionality factor of 0.2 between the ini-
tial abstraction and the maximum potential storage of the catchment. Two things
regarding this parameter should be considered.
Firstly, as ﬁgure 11 shows, the
correlation is not strong, this is explained by great uncertainties in both observa-
tional methods and determination of parameters such as inception. Secondly, the
proportionality factor might diﬀer for another type of catchment. The uncertainty
regarding this parameter will be further analysed in section 7.2.1.2 in this thesis.
Lastly, the method can not account for runoﬀgenerated from snow melt and rain
on frozen ground.
On a closing note it should be mentioned that various modiﬁcations, reinterpre-
tations and extensions of the method have been developed with the intent to ac-
commodate the limitations associated with the SCS-CN method. Many of these
extensions have been criticised for not generally improving the predictability of the
method, or being inadequate in terms of theoretical support (Bartlett et al. 2016).
The author of this report has been unable to identify any broadly or internationally
accepted modiﬁcations, and therefore focus is limited to the method as published
by the USDA.

5.6
Could the SCS-CN method be applied in Denmark?
5.6
Could the SCS-CN method be applied in Denmark?
Reviewing previous sections, the applicability of the SCS-CN method in Denmark
is evaluated.
The simplicity of the method makes it very transparent, there are no parameters
included that are geographically speciﬁc for the United States. Therefore, in theory,
the method could be applied anywhere outside the United States, as long as the
catchments falls into one of the categories valid for application; agricultural, urban or
rangelands. Naturally all limitations mentioned in section 5.5, should be considered
regardless of the geographic locations. The initial abstractions factor for example
could vary, however, this could also be the case within the United States, and is
not seen as a constraint for application elsewhere. In fact, based on the assessment
of the SCS-CN method performed in this thesis, the transferability across countries
does not raise as many questions as the applicability for urban areas. The empirical
studies forming the foundation for the method have been performed on agricultural
sites. Documentation supporting the generalisation of these results, or additional
empirical studies performed in urban conditions, has not been found. Considering
the extent of interconnected hydrological processes, and the number of inﬂuential
parameters, such as compaction and potential disturbance of the soil proﬁle, the
validity of presumed extrapolations is an essential aspect.
In conclusion, the method itself is presumed as transferable, but as would be the
case anywhere, the accuracy of the runoﬀpredictions depend on the accuracy of the
determination of catchment characteristics.

detected. This is essential to keep in mind, seen as the ﬁrst meter of the soil proﬁle
is crucial with regards to the inﬁltration capacity. Furthermore, another aspect,

Applying the T/A model
The reason for including the T/A model in the comparison, is that it represents the
most commonly used RRM for design purposes in Denmark Nielsen (2019). This
is by no means a validation of the suitability of the model in context with the aim
of this thesis, however, it provides an insight to what the results would be in case

a representation of a purely empirical modelling approach, a very simplistic black
box, where no hydrological processes are simulated.
The T/A model computes runoﬀvolume based on initial loss, a reduction factor
representing a continuous hydrological loss and the size of the catchment (DHI 2019).
Referring section 3.2.1, this representation corresponds to an empirical model, as
the parameters are based on statistics and empirical observations, and there are
no physical or conceptual descriptions of the hydrological processes. This model
is not designed to compute runoﬀfrom pervious areas, and does not comprise a
speciﬁc method for computing inﬁltration losses. If modelling catchments entailing
a considerable fraction of green areas, the recommended approach by (DHI 2014) is
to divide the catchment into sub-catchments representing the fractions of pervious
and impervious areas respectively. The inﬁltration capacity of the respective sub-
catchments is then modelled by increasing the reduction factor.
This approach
is pursued in the following, and the Skanderborg catchment is divided into sub-
catchment 1 and 2, representing the impervious and pervious fraction respectively.
The two sub-catchments are assigned diﬀerent model parameters, these are described
below and listed in table 16.

8
Discussion
Results from three diﬀerent models, all predicting runoﬀvolumes from the Skander-
borg catchment, have been compared. As evident in section 7.1, the results from
the impervious areas are relatively similar, while the results from the pervious areas
shows a much greater diﬀerence in results. This outcome is not surprising. Modelling
runoﬀfrom pervious areas is a task involving a much higher level of complexity than
modelling runoﬀfrom impervious areas. The logic of this assertion becomes evident
when considering the amount of hydrological abstractions associated with pervious
areas, and the interconnected processes between these. Soil type, vegetation cover,
antecedent moisture, soil compaction etc. are just some of the parameters inﬂuenc-
ing runoﬀand most of these parameters present a non-linear relationship between
rainfall and runoﬀthat are hard to measure and quantify. In addition, the spatial
variation within these parameters for pervious areas is often high.
As mentioned in the beginning of this thesis, there is no generally acknowledged
best practise for estimating runoﬀfrom pervious areas, and it is very hard to ver-
ify hydrological models.
However, based on the results generated in this thesis,
and an understanding of the diﬀerent model structures, advantages and limitations
regarding the applied models will be discussed in this section. Based on this, a
recommendation for implementing a runoﬀfunction for pervious areas in a typical
danish urban environment will be proposed in the end of this section.
8.1
Advantages and limitations regarding the diﬀerent mod-
els
Below advantages and limitations of the three applied models in regards to generat-
ing a runoﬀcurve for pervious and impervious areas, in connection to a distributed
model like SCALGO, will be reviewed.
8.1.1
The T/A model
The T/A model represents a purely empirical approach to modelling rainfall-runoﬀ.
Out of the three models evaluated in this thesis, this is the most simple approach,
as the hydrologic abstractions are all expressed as a static initial loss and reduction
factor. This means that the non-linear relationships between rainfall and runoﬀis
not accounted for, except at the very beginning where an initial loss is included.
As mentioned in section 6, the reasoning for including the T/A model in the result
62
==PAGE:77==
8.1
Advantages and limitations regarding the diﬀerent models
section, is not to determine whether or not the model is a relevant option for gen-
erating a runoﬀfunction for pervious areas. Due to the model structure and the
simplicity, it is almost safe to determine the inadequacy of this model in advance.
Instead the intended purpose is to provide an insight to what the results would be
in case of ’default’ modelling.
The T/A model is well known and widely used to design drainage systems in com-
pliance with service levels in Denmark. As mentioned in section 6.4, the model, as
originally intended in MIKE Urban, neglects runoﬀgenerated form pervious urban
areas completely. This is a choice based on the assumption that all precipitation on
pervious ares will inﬁltrate. Increased focus on the potential contribution from per-
vious areas and the importance hereof, have lead to an extrapolation of the method
including runoﬀfrom pervious areas. Extrapolation is the keyword here, as the
model builds on the conclusion that the computation method for impervious areas,
is also applicable for pervious areas. Keeping in mind the complexity and non-linear
relationship associated with runoﬀfrom pervious areas, this is of course a gross sim-
pliﬁcation. Runoﬀfrom pervious areas is simulated by an initial loss of 1 mm and a
static reduction factor of 0.10. This means that the decreased in inﬁltration capacity
over the course of a rain event, due to saturation, is not accounted for. As a result,
runoﬀgeneration from small rain events will tend to be overestimated, and runoﬀ
from larger rain events will tend to be underestimated. The results presented in
ﬁgure 17, supports this theory. The T/A model is the only one producing runoﬀ
from the 10 mm rain event. This points towards the fact that the other models
account for the increased inﬁltration capacities at the beginning of, or in the case of
a small rain event. The T/A model on the other hand always assumes a reduction
factor of 0.10, leading to runoﬀin every case where the rain event exceed initial loss.
In the case of largest rain event, of 110 mm, runoﬀgeneration computed using the
T/A model, is signiﬁcantly less, a result likely caused or inﬂuenced by the lack of
decrease in inﬁltration rates, and other hydrologic abstractions.
Seen as no actual measurements have been conducted in connection to the sim-
ulations performed, a direct comparison to actual runoﬀamounts is not possible.
However, the assumption that runoﬀwill occur as a result of a 1 mm rain event
seems unlikely. The same conclusion can be drawn for a continued reduction factor
of 0.1 in the case of large rainstorms.
The fact that the T/A model is not designed with the intend to handle runoﬀfrom
pervious areas, and does not account for the non-linear relationship associated with
hydrologic abstractions, suggests that the modelling approach represents a crude

simpliﬁcation. This becomes evident when considering the results in comparison to
the other models, and leads to the conclusion that the T/A method is not suitable
for developing runoﬀfunctions for pervious areas.
8.1.2
SCS-CN
The SCS-CN represents a conceptual approach to modelling rainfall runoﬀ. Com-
pared to the T/A method, it includes a conceptual interpretation hydrologic abstrac-
tions, and the governing equation accounts for the non-linear relationships between
total rainfall and total runoﬀ.
The SCS-CN method is based on an extensive amount of empirical research, and
includes many inﬂuential parameters associated with the generation of runoﬀ, such
as soil type, cover type and hydrologic conditions. This is an advantages, as runoﬀ
functions can be developed to match relevant conditions. However, as the empirical
data is not generated in Denmark, the question of transferability is prevalent. Orig-
inally, the research forming the foundation for the SCS-CN model was conducted
for agricultural sites in the United states. This means that both the geographic lo-
cation, and the type of catchments which the model is applied to, is divergent from
the original intent. No literature on the applicability for danish conditions have
been found, however, there are numerous examples from other countries where the
SCS-CN model have been adopted. Along with these follow various modiﬁcations,
the relationship between the initial abstraction and the maximum potential storage,
deﬁned by the factor λ, especially have received a lot of attention. Many studies
suggests an alternative value based on site speciﬁc investigations, similar research
could potentially optimize the model for use in Denmark, or at speciﬁc types of
sites.
When looking at the results in ﬁgure 16, it shows that the SCS-CN model is the
only one not resulting in runoﬀgeneration from the 20 mm rain event. This results
is a direct consequence of an increased initial loss compared to the other models.
As the size of the initial loss is based on equation 5 including λ, this result could be
an indication that the value of λ needs to be revised, or at least that a discrepancy
exists between the magnitude of initial loss according to the SCS-CN method and
a more common danish modelling approach.
The fact that the SCS-CN model is designed with the intend to handle runoﬀfrom
pervious areas, and does account for the non-linear relationship associated with hy-
drologic abstractions, suggests that the modelling approach is suitable for develop-

8.1.3
Kinematic wave
The kinematic wave model represents, like the SCS-CN model, a conceptual ap-
proach. The governing equation is based on empirical data from inﬁltrometer exper-
iments. Horton’s equation, computes inﬁltration based on minimum and maximum
inﬁltration capacity of the soil. This approach sets the kinematic wave model apart
from the other models, as this is the only method directly computing the inﬁltration,
and not the total runoﬀgenerated from total rainfall.
A key issues regarding the kinematic wave method, is that time is an intrinsic
property of Horton’s equation. Equation 9 shows an exponential decay with regards
to the inﬁltration capacity. However, this decay is dependent on a time factor, and
not the actual amount of accumulated rainfall. This leads to Horton’s equation only
being valid in the case where the rate of precipitation exceeds the inﬁltration rate.
This fact represents a signiﬁcant drawback, both in the case of smaller rain event
where the rate of precipitation never actually exceeds the inﬁltration rate, but also
in the case of larger rain events where the rate of precipitation is smaller in the
beginning. Comparing table 19, showing the intensity of the applied rain-series, to
the maximum and minimum inﬁltration rates applied in the kinematic model, table
17, shows that the rate of precipitation does not exceed the inﬁltration capacity for
any of the rain events.
Considering the results presented in ﬁgure 17, the kinematic model clearly produces
the greatest amount of runoﬀfor the larger rain events of 70 and 110 mm. This
outcome is likely inﬂuenced by the inﬁltration capacity decreasing ’too fast’, based
on the model assumption that precipitation is always ponding on the soil surface,
and thus contributing to soil saturation.

from pervious areas, and does account for the non-linear relationship associated

8.2
A recommendation for SCALGO
When recommending a model, it is important to remember the objective of the
modelling task, likewise, when recommending a model as a basis for developing runoﬀ
curves for SCALGO, the requirements from section 4.4 are carefully considered.
Starting from a structural point of view, the T/A model is perceived by the author,
as too great a simpliﬁcation. Basic knowledge of hydrological abstractions and non-
linearity quickly reveals the shortcomings of an empirical model based on a static
reduction factor. This conclusion is in line with the already mentioned presumptions
regarding this model. The SCS-CN and kinematic wave model both represent a
conceptual model, which would be the overall recommended model structure for a
runoﬀfunction. The reason for this is that a conceptual model can account for non-

linearities and reﬂects more physical characteristics about the catchment than an
empirical black box model. The conceptual model is also preferred over the physical
model structure, due to the amount of data required, the challenges known to be
associated with this, and the fact that a spatial variability in great detail is not the
ambition.
Considering the two conceptual models presented in this thesis, the kinematic wave
models has some limitations compared to the SCS-CN model. One is the empirical
basis on which the model is created. In the opinion of the author, the point scale
experiments, undergoing up-scaling to ﬁeld scale, represent a less solid foundation
than data collected at ﬁeld scale. The other is the intrinsic time factor, thought
to have great inﬂuence on the results produced especially from larger rain events.
According to ﬁgure 17, 66% of the total amount of precipitation is converted to
runoﬀapplying the kinematic wave model at a 110 mm rain event. This result
seems to overestimate the runoﬀgeneration.
The SCS-CN model generates runoﬀvolumes of a magnitude between the T/A and
kinematic wave model. Seen as the T/A model is presumed to underestimate runoﬀ,
and the kinematic model is presumed to overestimate runoﬀvalues, the anticipated
runoﬀvalues lie somewhere in between. The extensive empirical foundation on which
the SCS-CN model is formed, including both point and ﬁeld scale experiments,
and the wide use and acceptance, support the credibility of this model. The most
apparent limitation is the lack of previous application and validation for danish
conditions.
Considering both runoﬀgeneration results and structure of the three diﬀerent models
applied in this thesis, my recommendation for implementing a runoﬀfunction for
pervious areas in a typical danish urban environment is the SCS-CN model. In
terms of parameters, HSG B and good hydrologic conditions, as applied in this
thesis is still recommended. This choice is based on boulder clay being the most
common danish soil type (Klint et al. 2011), thought to be represented best by HSG
B, and a rather unsupported assumption that most pervious areas are covered by
minimum 75% vegetation. In terms of the proportionality factor λ, a default value
of 0.1 instead of 0.2 is recommended, consequently decreasing the initial loss from
32.5 mm to 16.2 mm. This recommendation is based on the following:
• The results presented in section 7.2.1.2, including the vast amount of studies

Conclusion and future perspectives
Estimating runoﬀfrom pervious areas is a complex task. This thesis provides a fun-
damental understanding of the hydrologic possesses associated with the generation
of surface runoﬀ, and the general structure of the models commonly applied to pre-
dict runoﬀvolume. This insight provides a necessary foundation for understanding
the complexity involved with mathematically describing runoﬀfrom pervious and
impervious areas, and forms the basis for assessing generated runoﬀresults from
hydrologic models, with the goal of recommending a runoﬀfunction for urban per-
vious areas. Results estimating runoﬀfrom a pervious and impervious surface, has
been produced using three diﬀerent hydrologic models; T/A, kinematic wave and
SCS-CN.
A comparison of model results for impervious areas shows that all models performed
relatively similar. This results was expected, as the associated number of hydrologic
abstraction are limited, and are expected to entail a certain degree of linearity.
In terms of runoﬀgeneration from pervious areas, the results vary signiﬁcantly
more. This was expected due to the diﬀerence in model properties and computa-
tional approaches to the non-linear relation of hydrologic abstractions associated
with pervious areas. The inclusion of the T/A model serves mostly as a measure
of comparison to the default modelling state. An understanding of the simpliﬁed
model structure allows for a ascertained presumption about the results in advance.
The static reduction factor causes the model to overestimate runoﬀfor smaller rain-
events, and underestimate runoﬀfrom larger rain-event.
Comparing the resulting runoﬀgeneration from the SCS-CN an kinematic wave
model, the diﬀerence was bigger than initially expected. The magnitude of this
discrepancy is considered to be caused, partly, by the time dependent decrease in
inﬁltration capacity associated with Horton’s equation. knowing that the rainfall
intensities did not exceed the inﬁltration capacities, the results from the kinematic
wave model are compromised to some degree, as the prerequisites for applying the
model are not fully met. However, the results still provide valuable information, as
they are interpreted as a deﬁnite overestimation, and thus indicate an upper limit.
The analysis of parameter choice in section 7.2 illustrate a core challenge related to
both the SCS-CN and kinematic wave model; the determination of soil properties
and inﬁltration capacities. As the analysis show, the eﬀect of this parameter on
the ﬁnal results is signiﬁcant. Inﬁltration is the most essential hydrologic abstrac-
tion, however, it is also very diﬃcult to quantify due to the extensive amount of
70

determining factors and spatial variability. Our lack of knowledge regarding the soil
properties, and the challenges associated with quantifying it, lies at the very core of
modelling pervious areas.
The initial results from the SCS-CN model, based on a proportionality factor,
lambda, of 0.2 were considered too conservative.
This perception was based on
the diﬀerence between results from SCS-CN and kinematic wave model, the diﬀer-
ence in presumed initial loss between the SCS-CN model and commonly applied
values in Denmark and the extensive amount of studies suggesting a decrease of
lambda for improved results. Based on this, lambda was adjusted from 0.2 to 0.1.
Before changing the proportionality factor lambda, the kinematic wave resulted in
4.1 times more runoﬀfor the 70 mm rain event, and 2.4 times more for the 110 mm
rain event. After adjusting lambda this diﬀerence was reduced to 2.1 and 1.8 times
respectively. The size of the initial loss for the SCS-CN model decreased from 32.5
to 16.2 mm In conclusion the recommended runoﬀfunction for urban pervious areas
is based on the SCS-CN model, assuming HSG B, good hydrologic conditions and
lambda = 0.1.
This conclusion supports the theory that runoﬀfrom pervious areas can contribute
a signiﬁcant amount to the total runoﬀgeneration, especially in the case of large
rain events.
9.1
Future perspective
One very apparent questions is why the time dependent version of Horton’s equation
was chosen, as the prerequisites could not be properly met. The reason for this is
simply that awareness of this property was not discovered until relatively late in
the process. Had it been known earlier, or had more time been available, a model
applying an integrated version of Horton’s equation would have been prioritised.
In terms of additional further work the optimum would be to conduct ﬁelds scale
studies, similar to the ones forming the foundation for the SCS-CN, for danish urban
areas. On this basis new CN’s could be developed especially for relevant conditions.
This approach is however very resource demanding. A more moderate approach
could be to generate results on the basis of historical rainfall records to provide a
deeper insight to the response of a pervious surface.
Lastly, a highly relevant topic, is the impact of including a routing model for the
generated runoﬀ. this element is likely to change the ﬁnal runoﬀresults, as runoﬀ
from an upstream pixel could inﬁltrate, in the case of a down stream pixel having
