Abstract
This master thesis is conducted in order to contribute to the development of software, which is capable
of processing multi layer geophysical and geodetic problems. We want to express gravitational data
referring to an ellipsoidal Earth model as a reference surface. Furthermore, we want to develop tools
for performing geological stripping and setting up an inverse gravimetric problem (IGP), in order to
isolate the gravitational signal from "unknown sources" in the subsurface. The data we have used is
depth data, and is generated from seismic data, and is provided by the Geological Survey of Denmark
and Greenland (GEUS).
The geology of the subsurface is bound by a top and a base depth grid, which we denote as geolog-
ical units. The depth grids are fundamental for determining the gravitational attraction from each
geological unit. We detected inconsistencies with the depth grids, which had to be ﬁxed in order to
proceed with our work process. The work process with the depth grids was very time consuming and
is therefore very exposed in this master thesis. The MATLAB routines are developed based on depth
data from the Danish Central Graben and the Danish Basin. The purpose with the software design
is to gain new subsurface information at any given location in Denmark. I accomplished with devel-
oping MATLAB routines used in an overall software design, put together by my supervisor Gabriel
Strykowski. The overall software design computes the gravitational attraction of the subsurface re-
ferring to an arbitrary Earth model (ellipsoidal, spherical, or ﬂat Earth). The MATLAB routines
include a routine to localize the common area, by using bounding polygons, for all the depth grids in
the Central Graben and in the Danish Basin separately. Furthermore, I contributed with develop-
ing A MATLAB routine that expresses the gravitational attraction referring to an ellipsoidal Earth
model, by using change of basis of a Cartesian coordinate system. This is essential for computing the
gravitational attraction as described in this master thesis. We succeeded with correcting the depth
grids, and developing MATLAB routines to compute the gravitational attraction referring to an ellip-
soidal Earth model as a reference surface. Due to the time consuming work process with the depth
grids and the time constraints, we did not perform geological stripping. Therefore, we could not use it
as a tool (as constraints) to set up an inverse gravimetric problem. The inverse gravimetric problem
should have computed the mass density distribution in the subsurface, and then enable us to isolate
the residual signal from "unknown sources" in the subsurface. But we have developed a software
design that can compute the gravitational attraction from the geological units we work with. The
computational time is just too time consuming for us to present those results in this master thesis. It
needs optimization, and we want time to think about how to do it. The ﬁrst geological unit we work
with is the Chalk unit (unit 3) in the subsurface. Therefore is missing computations of the gravita-
tional attraction from the air (unit 0), sea water (unit 1), and quaternary sediments (unit 2). This
master thesis makes possibilities for future work

Introduction
This master thesis is part of a research project by my supervisor Gabriel Strykowski, where we agreed
to work together. I help in solving subproblems, which are part of larger problems, and Gabriel solves
the more advanced programming and software design problems. It is important to emphasize that
this master thesis was bound by an overall software design, but I was free to come up with ideas on
how to solve the subproblems. In order for me to make this master thesis, I had to learn about a, to
me, new scientiﬁc ﬁeld called gravity data for geophysical applications. Not all of our ambitions with
this master thesis were accomplished within the duration of the project. But the numerous problems
we did solve were very satisfying.
The subsurface of the Earth consists of different types of geology, different geological layers from
different time periods. Each with varying mass densities within the layer, which depends on the
type of rock, compaction and post-depositional processes in the subsurface. Some processes cause
changes in the mass density due to pressure of the overburden and temperature. When measuring
gravity at the surface of the Earth with a gravimeter, we are measuring the gravitational attraction
of all the masses in the subsurface plus the centrifugal acceleration from the Earth’s rotation. Partial
information about some of these masses (sources) is given to us as depth grids. We are therefore
able to compute part of the gravitational signal associated with some of the "known sources" in the
subsurface. This signal from the "known sources" can be subtracted from the measured gravitational
signal, which results in a residual interpreted as the gravitational signal (see sec. 2), associated with
"unknown sources" (masses) in the subsurface. Following the classiﬁcation of geologists, the masses
in the subsurface are divided into what we denote as geological units (see sec. 4).
In this master thesis we work with geological structures in the Danish subsurface, and how the
distribution of these masses are reﬂected in the measured gravity ﬁeld. This has previously been
attempted using the geodetic software GRAVSOFT (Fortran based) [3]. It is not impossible to solve
geophysical problems in GRAVSOFT, but it requires a complicated setup. The problem with GRAV-
SOFT is, it cannot easily express data in a natural ellipsoidal Earth geometry i.e. expressing the data
in relation to the Earth’s natural form. This means orienting a Cartesian coordinate systems for a
point on the Earth’s surface, where the z-axis is placed along the ellipsoidal normal. Another problem
is, it only corrects the data from ﬂat Earth geometry into spherical Earth geometry. Furthermore,
GRAVSOFT can only handle one layer (the topopgraphy) of data. Therefore, the ambition with the
geophysical software called GEOGRAV (MATLAB based) is to be able to handle multi layers of data,
as the real subsurface consists of many different geological units. We want to transform the previous
work into the GEOGRAV software, which should make these computations easier. The ambitions
with GEOGRAV is to be able to address both geophysical and geodetic modelling problems, and to
express the data referring to an ellipsoidal Earth. This is convenient since GNSS (GPS) data are
given in ellipsoidal coordinates, so position coordinates of longitude and latitude are easily included
into the GEOGRAV software.
The inclusion of depth information from the Danish subsurface was very useful in order to develop
ﬂexible software that captures the complexity of real world modelling problems. The geometrical
extent of different geological units in the subsurface of the danish Central Graben and the Danish
Basin is given as depth grids. The depth grids are generated from seismic data and provided by the
Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS). Together a top and base depth grid bound
a geological unit. The depth grids depicts major seismic discontinuities (which are possibly mass
density discontinuities) (see sec. 4.1). We assume (as a ﬁrst guess that can be modiﬁed subsequently)
that the mass density variations correlate with the geometry of the geological units. However, the
mass density is not necessarily constant within a given geological unit. It was a very time consuming
process to clean these depth grids, because previous student work showed to be inconsistent [3].
This master thesis is therefore a continuation of the work I (the author) made in the synthesis project
Geological stripping in preparation for gravity inversion with seismic constraints in Denmark [8].
In this synthesis project we initiated our work with correcting the inconsistent depth grids, which

Theory: Gravity, gravitation and elementary bodies
Gravity on the Earth’s surface is deﬁned as the sum of the gravitation (i.e. the Newtonian attraction
of masses in the subsurface) and the centrifugal acceleration from the Earth’s rotation. The Earth’s
gravity ﬁeld is studied as a deviation of the true Earth gravity ﬁeld from the normal Earth model’s
gravity ﬁeld. The normal Earth model is a rotating reference ellipsoid with the same mass, mean
axis of rotation, and mean angular velocity as the actual Earth. It approximates both the actual
Earth’s gravity ﬁeld, and the geometrical shape of the Earth. The gravity measurement techniques
can commonly be classiﬁed as either absolute or relative. Currently, the absolute measurements
techniques by the state-of-the-art free-fall instruments are rare and time consuming. In order to
cover large areas with the gravity data, the relative measurements techniques are being used. The
results of relative gravity surveys are commonly expressed as gravity anomalies, i.e. a deviation of
the absolute gravity value from the corresponding normal Earth model value. Below, we argue that
the most common type of gravity anomalies, the free-air gravity anomalies, can almost be viewed
as gravitational anomalies and, thus, depend almost exclusively on the distribution of (anomalous)
masses in the subsurface.
The gravity potential W is a scalar ﬁeld and is deﬁned as
W = V +Φ
(1)
where V is the gravitational potential and Φ is the centrifugal potential.
The gravity potential for the normal Earth model is deﬁned as

(2)

(1) and (2) the centrifugal potential Φ, because the normal Earth model has the same mean axis of

T is also the anomalous gravitational potential:

the gravitation is left i.e. T is the signal generated by the masses in the subsurface. This absence of
the centrifugal potential is inherited to other quantities of the Earth’s gravity ﬁeld e.g. the gradient
of the anomalous gravity potential, and the anomalous gravity gradient tensor.

2.1
Gravitational attraction of some elementary bodies
The mathematical description of the gravity ﬁeld is part of Potential Theory. It is customary to start
the derivations from a scalar ﬁeld; the gravity potential. Other types of quantities include the gravity
attraction vector; a vector ﬁeld, and the gravity gradients; a gradient ﬁeld, which are all derived
from the gravity potential by gradient operators. These are mathematically classiﬁed as the zeroth,
ﬁrst and second order tensor. The zeroth order tensor is the gravitational potential, the second order
tensor is the gravitational acceleration, and the third order tensor is the gradient of the gravitational
acceleration. These will be used as denotation further on. As argued above, we will neglect the
centrifugal potential and deal with the gravitational potential, which only reﬂects the attraction of
geological sources in the subsurface.
The gravitational potential, V, can be computed for any bounded volume of mass. It is computed
by approximating a speciﬁc volume of mass by a ﬁnite set of small elements of volume. Then we
compute the attraction from each element, and superimpose these partial attractions in a ﬁeld point
P - a superposition principle. This is described in equation (9)

In the synthesis project Geological stripping in preparation for gravity inversion with seismic con-
straints in Denmark [8], a MATLAB routine for tensor rotation (change of orthonormal base) of the
zeroth, ﬁrst and second order tensor has been developed and veriﬁed by a numerical experiment. The
results from the synthesis project will be fundamental for this master thesis.
In the change of base routine two different methods were compared; the compute-and-rotate and the
direct method. This was done for point masses and homogeneous rectangular prisms. In the compute-
and-rotate method, we start by computing the zeroth, ﬁrst and second order tensor of a homogeneous
rectangular prism Q in a ﬁeld point P. The Cartesian coordinate system in ﬁeld point P is aligned
with the prism sides. Afterwards, we use change of base on all three tensors, so the gravitational
attraction is expressed in a natural local East-North-Up (ENU) Cartesian coordinate system in ﬁeld
point P (see ﬁg. 4b). When using a homogeneous rectangular prism as a source point Q, the local

routine the gravitational attraction is rotated into a local ENU Cartesian coordinate system in the
ﬁeld point P (as shown in ﬁgure 4b). In this Cartesian coordinate system the z-axis (Up) is aligned
with the ellipsoidal normal (approximately the direction of the plumb line) through the ﬁeld point
P. This is not a problem for point masses, and is therefore much simpler to compute, but a lot more
computations need to be done due to their small coverage. In the direct method, all three tensors are

2.3
Ellipsoidal Earth approximation
From physical geodesy, we know that a point on, below or inside the Earth’s masses can be described
by different coordinates - global cartesian, local cartesian, spherical and ellipsoidal. The different
choosing of coordinates makes us able to chose a natural coordinate system for every ﬁeld point P. We
make use of a Cartesian coordinate system where the z-axis is parallel to the ellipsoidal normal. So,
the depths of a geological unit in the subsurface will be the depth according to the ellipsoidal normal.
Therefore, the orientation of the homogeneous rectangular prism sides are aligned with the local
ENU Cartesian coordinate system. Still, we have to use the change of orthonormal base MATLAB
routine in order to express the gravitational attraction in a local ENU Cartesian coordinate system
that is natural for a given ﬁeld point P.
It is very convenient and natural to express our data referring to an ellipsoidal Earth, because the
longitude and latitude data we get from GNSS (GPS) is given in either ellipsoidal coordinates or
global Cartesian coordinates.

The techniques residual/regional separation and geological stripping are both used in gravimetric

2.4.1
Source geometry
The technique of geological stripping requires knowledge about the geometrical setup of each geolog-
ical unit in the subsurface, in order to compute the gravitational attraction of each geological unit.
We have seismic depth information from GEUS that suggest major seismic discontinuities, which
possibly, but not necessarily, could be mass density discontinuities. These depth grids illustrates the
structure of the subsurface geology. The depth information from the geology can be very varying, due
to for example faults and salt domes, which can lead to interpolation errors. The depth grids from
GEUS is affected by some uncertainties, but it is the best model we have. The depth grids presented
in this master thesis constrains the geometry of the geological units i.e. the depth to the top and the
base of each geological unit. An intuitive assumption about the structure of the geological units, is
that an older geological unit is laying below a younger geological unit. The ﬁrst geological unit we
work with is the Chalk unit. Above this unit is the air (0), sea water (1), and quaternary sediments
(2). So, we start with geological unit no. 3.
2.5
The inverse gravimetric problem
To solve the inverse gravimetric problem, means to determine the distribution of sources in the sub-
surface from gravitational data. In the litterature the problem is often solved mathematically by
making assumptions and getting answers based on these assumption e.g by assuming constant mass
for the source. However, the mathematical assumption may not necessarily reﬂect the true conditions
in the subsurface. Geological stripping can help to formulate a more realistic inverse gravimetric
problem (IGP), where the direct knowledge about the subsurface is included. In section 2.5.1 we ex-
plain how geological stripping can be set up as an inverse gravimetric problem, where the geometry
of the geological units is ﬁxed, while the mean mass density of the units is unknown. In section 2.5.2
we explain how the results of geological stripping can be used to reveal the gravitational signal from
"unknown sources". These residuals can be used for setting up local inverse gravimetric problems to
model these "unknown sources". A generalized method is described in the article [5].

The solutions I provided and assisted with to solve the subproblems has been implemented in the

In the bounding polygons MATLAB routine we localize the geographical area that all the depth grids
in the Central Graben, and in the Danish Basin, separately, have in common. We start by deﬁning all

all of the depth grids, we are able to compute the gravitational attraction, by using homogeneous
rectangular prisms as described in sec. 3.1.
In the change of orthonormal base MATLAB routine we compute the gravitational attraction from a
homogeneous rectangular prism (the zeroth, ﬁrst and second order tensor) in a ﬁeld point P, where
the axes of the coordinate system are aligned with the prism sides. Then, we use change of base to
express the three tensors referring to an ellipsoidal Earth model. This means, the z-axis in the local
ENU coordinate system in ﬁeld point P is placed along the ellipsoidal normal (see sec. 2.2 for further
explanation). So, the computations is still given in Cartesian coordinates, the coordinate system is
just oriented so it refers to an ellipsoidal Earth model.
These MATALB routines are implemented in the overall software design. The gravitational attrac-
tion (the zeroth, ﬁrst, and second order tensor) of a geological unit is computed as depicted in ﬁgure
9 in both Cartesian, spherical and ellipsoidal coordinates.
All of the programming has been time consuming and challenging. There were challenges with the
overall software design. One of the challenges we encountered was the very large data ﬁles. The data
computed in the software design took up so much space on the computer, that it would take a long
time to load it into MATLAB. Therefore, we had to split the data, both ﬁeld point data and source
point data, in order to create ﬁles that do not occupy as much space on the computer, when the data is

the ﬁeld point P and the source point Q, the global coordinate system (X, Y, Z), ect. Another challenge
at the moment is also that the computation of the gravitational grids are very time consuming. It
takes a lot of time for the software to compute the gravitational attraction for all the prisms. There
are 53001 grid points, and these grid points are computed for 5 heights above the ellipsoid - 0 m, 100
m, 200 m, 500 m and 1000 m. The gravitational attraction is computed for three prisms for each grid
point for both the top and the base depth grid. As it is programmed right now, it takes ca. 5 minutes

my supervisor, this is not an unfamiliar problem, this problem also occurs when computing terrain
effects in physical geodesy. A way of working around this problem is to make prisms larger (lower

computations in ellipsoidal geometry, but it is difﬁcult. So, right now it appears to be an impossible
task due to the time consuming computations. But we know the software can handle to compute the
gravitational attraction step-wise, so the computations can be initiated, turned off and then resumed
at the point where the computations last ended.

ACCOMPLISHMENTS, CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
s151727
6
Accomplishments, conclusion and future work
A lot of fundamental work has been accomplished in this master thesis. We have developed a soft-
ware design that can express the gravitational attraction of the subsurface referring to an arbitrary
Earth model (ﬂat Earth, spherical, or ellipsoidal). The work is fundamental for performing geological
stripping, and to set up an inverse gravimetric problem, in order to isolate the gravitational signal
from "unknown sources" in the subsurface. Many of my contributions have been included in the
overall software design in the GEOGRAV software package. The MATLAB routines were developed
based on depth data from the Danish Central Graben and the Danish Basin, but the purpose with
the software design is to be able to use it to gain new subsurface information at any given location in
Denmark.
One of our goals was to express the distribution of masses in the subsurface referring to an ellipsoidal
Earth. Therefore, it is a great accomplishment that we are now able to compute the gravitational at-
traction from each geological unit referring to an ellipsoidal, spherical or ﬂat Earth model as reference
surface. In order to compute this, we have used the consistent depth grids to determine the geometry
of each geological unit. Then we developed the routine to compute bounding polygons for all the depth
grids in the Central Graben and in the Danish Basin in order to locate a common area. After locating
the common area for the depth grids, we are able to form homogeneous rectangular prisms to ﬁll out
the arbitrary geometry of the geological units. For each geological unit, the prisms are formed from a
reference level, laying 200 m below the base, reaching up to the top and to the base, respectively (see
ﬁgure 9 in section 3.1). Then we compute the gravitational attraction from each prism and add them
together for the top and the base separately. The gravitational signal from top and the base is then
subtracted, resulting in the gravitational attraction of the geological unit bound by the top and the
base.
Another goal was implementing the change of orthonormal base MATLAB routine into the overall
software design. This implementation, enable us to represent the gravitational potential, the grav-
itational acceleration, and the gravitational gradient in a local Cartesian coordinate system, where
the z-axis is placed along the ellipsoidal normal. We were not able to include all of the results from
the software design, because the computational time is very time consuming. But I was able to com-
pare the results from the change of orthonormal base MATLAB routine with partial results of the
gravitational attraction grid from the main loop. This is where the change of base MATLAB routine
is implemented in the overall software design. By comparing the results, I was able to verify that the
implementation was correct. The results can be seen in section 5.
Our most fundamental accomplishment, and also the most time consuming, was correcting the depth
grids, because we could not proceed without consistent depth grids. Afterwards, I was able to develop
a MATLAB routine to compute the bounding polygons (common areas) for all of the depth grids in the
Central Graben and in the Danish Basin separately. This accomplishment made it possible to form
homogeneous rectangular prisms (as described in sec. 3.1), and thereby compute the gravitational
attraction from each geological unit. We conclude that the depth grids we received from the student
synthesis project [3] had errors due to use of a wrong interpolation technique. This was corrected by
locating graphically and diagnosing the errors, and then use an interpolation technique to solve the
problems.
Due to the time consuming work process with the depth grids, we were not able to ﬁnish the ﬁnal
tasks set up for this master thesis. We wanted to perform geological stripping, and use it as a tool
(as constraints) to set up an inverse gravimetric problem for computing the mass density distribution
in the subsurface from measurements of the external gravity ﬁeld. This was only if time allowed
it. But now we are able to model parts of the regional signal by using the "known source" geometry
(the consistent depth grids). Parts of the regional signal can be computed in the overall software
design. By stripping from the total surface gravitational signal the partial gravitational signature
of identiﬁed sources and connect them with speciﬁc part of the subsurface, we are able to isolate the
residual signal (the "unknown sources").
