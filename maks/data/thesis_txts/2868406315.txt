Abstract 
One of the by-products of anaerobic digestion is digestate, a biomass with a high moisture 
content often used as fertiliser. A promising utilisation of digestate is as biomass source in 
the process of pyrolysis, where biomass is decomposed into valuable products such as 
biochar, bio-oil, and syngas. However, the high moisture content of digestate still poses a 
challenge. Before pyrolysis, the digestate must be dried from 85% to 10% moisture 
content. This thesis investigates the integration of air drying and steam drying in pyrolysis 
processes, focussing on efficiency and economic viability under limited heat availability.   
 
The drying methods were modelled and simulated in Aspen Plus v14. The analysis 
focuses primarily on two aspects: energy performance, where key aspects such as energy 
efficiency, heat recovery, and energy balance are analysed, and the economic 
performance, assessed based on the CAPEX and OPEX of the two methods.  
 
The results highlight that while the steam dryer has a higher CAPEX due to the hardware 
required, it offers a higher efficiency, and more heat recovery options compared to air 
drying. The economic analysis shows that despite the higher CAPEX, steam drying offsets 
its initial investment within 16 years, because of lower operational costs, making it a more 
economically viable option under current moisture conditions.  
 
The findings of the thesis highlight the importance of selecting the appropriate drying 
method in a biomass pyrolysis process to optimise energy consumption and reduce long-
term costs. Under different circumstances, the air-drying could still be the more energy- 
and cost-effective option.

Glossary 
Air drying 
A drying method that uses ambient or heated air to remove moisture from a material. 
 
Anaerobic digestion  
A biochemical process in which microorganisms break down organic material, in the 
absence of oxygen, to biogas and digestate.  
 
Biochar 
A solid and carbon-rich product obtained in for example pyrolysis. It has applications in soil 
improvement and energy production.  
 
Bio-oil  
A liquid product composed of oxygenated hydrocarbons obtained from pyrolysis. It can be 
used as a fuel or in chemical industry.  
 
Composite curve 
Graphical representation of the hot and cold streams in a process or heat exchange 
network, used to analyse the heat recovery potential.  
 
Digestate 
A residual material left after anaerobic digestion, it contains organic matter and nutrients 
and is usable for bioenergy and as fertiliser. 
 
Steam drying 
A drying system method that uses steam to remove moisture from a material.   
 
Pyrolysis 
Heating a carbonaceous material in the absence of oxygen so it decomposes. 
 
Thermal conversion 
A process in which a material is converted into other forms using heat, such as combustion 
and pyrolysis.

1. Introduction  
The global search for sustainable energy sources has intensified, driven by concerns 
about climate change and diminishing fossil fuel reserves. In this context, pyrolysis of 
biomass has gained attention as a promising technology for producing sustainable fuels. 
These fuels have some benefits over other sustainable sources such as solar and wind, 
which require infrastructural changes. The biofuels are particularly advantageous in difficult 
to decarbonize industries like aviation and heavy trucking. As these fuels are compatible 
with existing infrastructure and engine technologies, allowing for easier and more cost-
effective implementation. Additionally, pyrolysis has the potential of enabling carbon 
capture, as the process also produces biochar, a valuable byproduct that can be used to 
enrich soil quality. Moreover, the technology is flexible in feedstock, as it can use a wide 
range of biomass materials, from agricultural waste to forestry residues. This adaptability 
makes pyrolysis an interesting technology for supporting a circular economy by 
transforming bio-waste into energy or other products [1, 2].  
 
In pyrolysis, biomass is heated in the absence of oxygen, causing it to decompose into 
several products: bio-oil, biochar, and pyrolysis gas. These products can then be used as 
a fuel or a base for other biochemicals. The proportion of each product depends on the 
type of biomass and the pyrolysis conditions. Pyrolysis systems can generally be 
categorized into two main types, fast and slow pyrolysis. In fast pyrolysis the heating rate 
in the process is high (>200°C/s) and the biomass has a short residence time in the 
reactor. These conditions promote the production of bio-oil, allowing bio-oil yields up to 
75%. Slow pyrolysis uses lower temperatures (<500°C), and the biomass spends a longer 
time in the reactor. These conditions promote the production of biochar and reduces the 
production of bio-oil. The production of biochar is favourable when the aim of the operator 
is to capture carbon [1, 3].  
 
Pyrolysis has historically been used for charcoal production, but its potential for producing 
bioproducts from biomass is less well-known. Despite its promise, there are still significant 
challenges to making pyrolysis of biomass commercially viable on industrial scale. To 
address these challenges, pyrolysis facilities of various sizes are built to gain knowledge 
about scaling up the process. One of these large-scale commercial pyrolysis facilities is 
Skyclean from the Danish company Stiesdal. The company has built a 20 MW pyrolysis 
plant in Vrå, Denmark. The pyrolysis plant of Stiesdal is built next to a biogas plant and the 
pyrolysis plant of Skyclean uses the biogas digestate fibres as a biomass energy source. 
The Skyclean plant aims to produce mostly biochar to use as a fertiliser and for carbon 
capture. The facility serves as the reference case for this thesis project.  
 
Before entering the pyrolysis process, the digestate fibres need to be dried. The digestate 
fibres must be dried to enhance pyrolysis efficiency and for the desired end-products. First, 
the fibres are mechanically dewatered by a screw press to 70% moisture and then 
thermally dried in a pressurized disc steam dryer, which operates at 130°C. The steam 
generated by the disc steam dryer can be reused in the anime plant. However, the high 
capital expenditure (CAPEX) of the pressurized steam dryer has led Stiesdal to explore 
air-drying as a more cost-effective alternative.  
 
This leads to the research question of this master thesis: How does the integration of air-
drying compare to steam drying in terms of energy efficiency and economic viability in 
biomass pyrolysis processes with limited heat availability? The aim of the project is to 
compare the air drying to the steam drying method on efficiency, energy consumption, 
impact on the rest of the pyrolysis plant, and the technical and economic feasibility of the

2. Methodology  
System boundaries  
The primary focus of this master thesis is the comparison of air and steam drying methods 
for biomass within the context of pyrolysis. Although other biomass drying techniques 
exist, they were not included in this research. The drying methods were integrated and 
simulated in a model of the Stiesdal pyrolysis plant in Aspen Plus. The Stiesdal Skyclean 
pyrolysis plant was used as a real-world reference to provide the required data to compare 
the drying methods. Providing Stiesdal with advice on whether implementing air-drying 
could lower the costs and increase efficiency of the plant is one of the objectives of this 
thesis. However, in the master thesis itself a more general comparison between air-drying 
and steam-drying is presented. The goal of the thesis is to compare the air drying to the 
steam drying method on efficiency, energy consumption, impact on the rest of the pyrolysis 
plant, and the technical and economic feasibility of the drying methods.  
 
Data 
The data for the project came from multiple sources. Primarily, data was sourced from the 
company Stiesdal and their pyrolysis plant measurements, including the parameters used 
in the Aspen Plus model like the technical and economical parameters. Not all data could 
be sourced from Stiesdal Skyclean. Literature review was conducted to find additional data 
about drying methods, economical parameters, and experience of other pyrolysis plants. 
Where specific data was unavailable, realistic assumptions were made based on company 
data or literature.  
 
The data for the economic analysis were based on the investment costs of the steam dryer 
from Stiesdal and information provided by Dryer One [4].  
 
Aspen plus 
Aspen Plus V14 is the software tool used to develop and simulate the thermodynamic 
models of the drying methods and the pyrolysis plant. Aspen plus is chosen for this study 
due to its capability to model the energy and mass balance of complex processes. Also, 
Aspen Plus offers a wide range of features that enable advanced analysis, such as the 
feature to create composite curves. 
 
Modelling 
As mentioned earlier, the software used to build the thermodynamic models of the drying 
methods is Aspen Plus. First, the pyrolysis plant of Stiesdal was set up with the company 
data, and information gathered in the literature review. When the model was set up, it was 
validated with the experts of Stiesdal and DTU. After validating the model of the pyrolysis 
plant, simulations were run for both steam- and air-drying methods. The models made are 
shown and explained in section 4, the models. 
 
Model Validation  
Validation of the models is essential to ensure the models accurately represent the real 
system. The validation process involved three key steps: identifying critical parameters, 
comparing the results of the Aspen model to the data from Stiesdal, and iteratively refining 
the Aspen model to minimize discrepancies. The key parameters assessed in the 
validation process, include moisture reduction, energy consumption, and outlet air 
temperature.  
 
Stiesdal uses a different simulation software than DTU, called AVEVA. Data from the 
pyrolysis plant and the simulation software AVEVA of Stiesdal were used to validate key

AVEVA, so Stiesdal can accept and trust the results from the Aspen software. The

Analysis 
The analysis focused on two main aspects: evaluating the performance of the two drying 
methods based on the results of sensitivity analysis and model simulations, and assessing 
the economic performance of the methods based in terms of the CAPEX and OPEX.  
 
In the energy analysis, key aspects such as energy efficiency, energy consumption, and 
energy balance of the drying methods were assessed. The analysis began with an 
assessment of the composite curves of the pyrolysis plants to explore the heat recovery 
potential and evaluate the thermodynamic behaviour. This was followed by examining the 
performance of the dryers, including their load and efficiency under drying moisture 
content and biomass input. This was performed to determine whether the dryer could 
manage the required biomass load and to compare the energy consumption and efficiency 
of both systems.   
 
Heat recovery was analysed at multiple levels. First, simulations were conducted to 
evaluate heat recovery individual components of the system, followed by an assessment 
of the complete system. This approach helped to explore the feasibility of heat recovery 
and assess the impact of the different components on the heat flow of the overall system. 
The heat recovery was carried out by using composite curves and detailed calculations in 
Excel. See the results of the energy analysis in paragraph 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3.

Sensitivity analysis  
To assess the robustness of the Aspen model, a sensitivity analysis was conducted. The 
sensitivity analysis provided insight into how the model was affected by variations in key 
parameters. The sensitivity analysis focused on the following parameters: initial biomass 
moisture, drying temperature, heat availability, and the effect of heat recovery. Each 
parameter was varied ±10% from the base case, based on realistic operating conditions. 
The output parameters assessed include energy consumption, energy efficiency, and heat 
recovery, all of which were evaluated based on percentual changes.  
 
The results proved that the model is robust enough to simulate effectively within the 
ranges and could keep up with results, the results are analysed in paragraph 5.3.

3. Literature Review 
3.1. 
Drying Biomass 
Most untreated biomass, such as wood or bio-waste, have a high moisture content, which 
presents several disadvantages when using biomass as an alternative energy source. 
High moisture content leads to lower efficiencies in thermal processes, unwanted products 
in gasification, and increase in transportation costs. To address these problems, some pre-
treatment such as drying or dewatering are necessary to lower the moisture content and 
enhance the thermal characterises [5]. 
 
The biomass source assessed in this thesis are digestate fibres. Digestate is the slurry 
residue from anaerobic digestion of biomass sources like manure or straw. While already 
processed, the digestate still contains organic and inorganic materials, which still can be 
utilised as a biomass source for thermal conversion [6]. However, digestate exits the 
anaerobic digestion plant with a moisture content up to 90%. However, the optimum 
moisture content is around 10-15% for thermal processes [7]. Given these characterises of 
digestate, significant pre-treatments are required to reduce the moisture level.  
 
At the Stiesdal Skyclean plant, the digestate fibres are first mechanically dewatered by a 
screw press to a moisture content of 70% and then thermally dried by pressurized steam 
dryer to a moisture content of 10%. There are various methods to dry biomass, but this 
study mainly focuses on steam and air drying. Both drying techniques have their 
advantages and disadvantages. 
 
3.2. 
Challenges drying  
As addressed in 3.1, pre-drying of biomass is needed to reduce the moisture content to 
increase the processes efficiency. However, adding drying to a pyrolysis plant poses 
several challenges. In this paragraph, the research question, "What are the challenges of 
integrating air-drying into a pyrolysis system?" is addressed. The objective of this sub-
question is to gain a deeper understanding of drying technologies and the problems of 
integrating them in a process. Additionally, the objective is to gain knowledge in the 
advantages and disadvantages of using air-drying in comparison with steam drying. 
 
High energy consumption  
Thermally drying biomass is an energy intensive process. A large amount of energy is 
needed to reduce the moisture content of the biomass. Thermally drying has a large 
impact on the total energy efficiency of the pyrolysis process. When drying is not properly 
integrated into the system, a lot of energy is wasted, and the total energy efficiency will be 
negatively impacted. Putting off the advantage of better combustion of dried biomass. 
 
Emissions 
The drying of the bio digestate can lead to the emission of odours and volatile organic 
compounds (VOCs). These emissions need to be minimized to decrease the 
environmental impact of the air and steam dryers. Digestate from manure is a nitrogen rich 
biomass, and as this material dries, ammonia can volatilize into the air, particularly in 
warmer conditions and when the pH is above neutral [8].  
 
Although ammonia is not a greenhouse gas, but it poses a significant risk to both the 
environment and human health. Environmentally, ammonia contributes by alkalinity of the 
soil and nitrogen accumulation, which can negatively impact the biodiversity. Certain fast 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
growing and more common plant species adapt quickly to the nitrogen rich environment. 
These species thrive under these conditions and out-compete the other more sensitive 
species. [9] 
 
Ammonia exposure can also have serious health effects. At high concentrations, ammonia 
causes immediate burning to eyes, nose, throat and lung, and can result in death. In lower 
concentrations, ammonia can still cause health issues and has a strong, unpleasant odour. 
In areas where ammonia is used or released, this strong odour can be source of 
discomfort for people nearby. While the odour is less harmful, it still requires regulation to 
prevent nuisances. [10] 
 
These VOCs and odorous emissions must be mitigated to prevent environmental and 
health risks. One of the most effective technologies, and already used in the current plant, 
is the use of scrubber technology. Scrubbers are an effective measure to remove air 
pollutant from gas streams. There are various types of scrubbers, but the most used 
scrubber is the wet scrubber. In a wet scrubber, water is sprayed over the polluted gas, 
effectively washing out the particles of a dirty air stream. This mitigates odours and VOCs 
emissions and ensures compliance with environmental regulations. [11] 
 
Stiesdal had conversations with Technic One about minimizing the emissions of the air 
dryer. The company Technic One is working on a concept where an air scrubber is placed 
in the centre chimney of the dryer. The scrubber should be able to catch all the VOC and 
ammonia out of the air. Stiesdal does not have much experience with air scrubbers. So, 
they will have to rely on external experts on this subject. From an energy point of view, it 
would put the blowers under substantial high-pressure, and the air maybe needs to be 
reheated after the scrubber, or else the air will leave the scrubber at dew point 
temperature, and it would form a mist or rain in the local area.  
 
Heat availability 
The process of pyrolysis with drying cost requires a lot of heat to operate. This needs to be 
generated or obtained in some way. If the system would only use fossil fuel as a resource 
or electricity from the grid, the system would probably not be economically valuable. The 
dryer, and other parts of the process should be integrated with each other to make the 
system more efficient. How this can be done is further researched in paragraph 3.3 
 
Scale  
The dryer plays a critical role in determining the potential scale and efficiency of a 
pyrolysis plant. The drying requires significant energy input to reduce the moisture content 
of the biomass. As the scale of the plant increases, the energy demand for drying further 
increases, potentially leading to an energy imbalance. This energy imbalance may limit the 
potential of scaling up the system without additional energy to sustain the process. Making 
the system less economical viable. Despites its high energy consumption, drying is 
required for achieving the desired product yield in the pyrolysis process, as excessive 
moisture content can make the process of pyrolysis less efficient.   
 
Pressurised steam dryer  
The Stiesdal plant currently uses a pressurised steam dryer. The hot steam enters the 
dryer at 15 bar and the biomass is pressurised to 5 bars, when it enters the dryer. A 
significant drawback of this system is its mechanical complexity. Both streams require 
pressurization, the system needs costly valves to maintain system pressure. This has led 
to numerous operational issues for Stiesdal with the system. Not only are the valves itself 
expensive, but their maintenance also demands considerable time and costs. According to 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
Stiesdal, the investment costs of the valves are around two million euro of the totals eight 
million euro to invest for the system.  
 
3.3. 
Integrating drying  
The dryer and pyrolysis use a lot of energy and negatively impact the overall efficiency of 
the pyrolysis system. Thermodynamically integrating the technologies into the system 
would enhance the overall efficiency of the system. There are several possibilities to 
integrate the pyrolysis system, air dryer, and steam dryer into the pyrolysis system. In this 
paragraph the possible solution for integrating the systems into the pyrolysis process are 
explored.   
 
Current situation 
Stiesdal uses now a pressurised, indirect steam dryer at Stiesdal plant. This steam dryer is 
thermodynamically integrated into the pyrolysis plant. The steam dryer is integrated in two 
ways. First, it recovers the heat generated by combusting the produced pyrolysis gas. 
When the digestate fibres are heated in the absence of oxygen in the pyrolysis reactor, the 
digestate fibres decompose among other compounds into pyrolysis gas, which contains 
hydrocarbons, mostly CH4, and CO, and H2. These gases can be combusted after exiting 
the pyrolysis reactor. The heat generated by combusting these gases is usable to heat the 
pyrolysis reactor, steam dryer, and potentially an amine plant. When the process is in 
energy balance the process can be effectively be self-sustainable. [12] 
 
The steam dryer has two output streams, one being the evaporated moisture of the 
biomass and the other being the dried biomass with 10% moisture content. The 
evaporated moisture content exits the steam dryer exhaust at a temperature of 152°C, 
said Stiesdal. This means that the evaporated moisture content still contains a lot of heat. 
This heat can be reused to heat other processes or units.  
 
At the pyrolysis plant and biogas plant are different processes and units, which need heat 
to operate, but not every unit is suitable to use the heat. The pyrolysis reactor operates at 
500°C, so the heat out of the steam exhaust is too low to heat the pyrolysis reactor.  
 
Amine plant 
The plant also contains a so-called amine plant. The amine plant is used to clean the 
biogas produced in the digestion plant of acids like hydrogen sulphide. The process cleans 
the biogas from impurities (H2S and CO2) with adding amine solutions as an absorber. 
The amine plant works at a temperature of 140°C and has a heat demand of 3500kWh 
according to Stiesdal. This means that the amine plant is a viable option for integrating the 
exhaust of the steam dryer with the amine plant. The steam dryer provides at least 
provides heat at the right temperature, if the amount of heat energy is also sufficiently 
needs to be simulated [13]. 
 
Air drying 
The other technology under investigation is the air dryer, which also needs to be integrated 
into the pyrolysis plant to enhance energy and cost efficiency. Heat recovery from the 
combustion of the pyrolysis gas is viable option for the air-drying process. In the current 
system, this heat is recovered by a boiler and hot-water system, this can also work for the 
air drying. Heat recovery from the heat generated from the combusted pyrolysis gas is the 
most logical choice, as the pyrolysis reactor requires high temperature heat. This level of 
heat cannot be supplied by other means, other than gas from the grid or from combusting 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
pyrolysis gas. According to Stiesdal, under normal conditions, the pyrolysis gas will 
generate more energy than the pyrolysis reactor requires.  
 
The hot air, combined with moisture removed from the biomass, exits the dryer at 30 °C. 
This temperature is too low to effectively heat the amine plant, like the steam dryer. The 
amine plant could potentially be heated using energy from combusting pyrolysis gases. 
However, this requires simulation to assess that the combustion of non-condensable 
gases produces sufficient energy to heat all three processes: the amine plant, the pyrolysis 
reactor, and the air dryer. If the combustion process does not cover the heat demand, 
alternative solution would need to be explored.   
 
A common approach to recover low temperature heat is using it for district heating or other 
industrial heating applications. This solution is already investigated by Stiesdal, but this 
appears to be unfeasible. According to Stiesdal, the investment cost would be too high. 
The pyrolysis plant is in Vrå, a small town in rural Denmark, where building the necessary 
infrastructure to connect the nearest residential areas would be too expensive.

Pyrolysis plant  
In this master thesis research, the pyrolysis plant of Stiesdal is used as the main test case.

section, in this thesis only the pyrolysis side is modelled with the relevant components. In

Pyrolysis plant  
This paragraph focuses on the general stats and performances of the pyrolysis plant, 
specific stats about the drying methods are mentioned later.  
 
The process flowsheet begins with biomass, the biomass used in the process are bio-
digestate fibres. These fibres are by products from the biogas plant operated next to the 
plant of Stiesdal in Vrå. The characteristics of the bio-digestate fibres are presented in 
appendix 2 and 3.  
 
To lower the initial moisture content, the digestate is mechanically dewatered by a screw 
press. The screw press lowers the moisture content to 70%. The slightly dryer biomass is 
transported into the air dryer to be dried to 10% moisture content.  
 
When the biomass is dried to 10% moisture content, the biomass enters the pyrolysis 
plant. In the pyrolysis reactor the biomass is heated up to 550°C in the absence of oxygen. 
In these conditions, the biomass does not combust, because of the absence of oxygen, 
but the biomass decomposes into biochar and combustible gases. The combustible gases 
can be condensed to so called bio-oil.  
 
So, the main three products of pyrolysis are bio-oil, biochar, and syngas. The proportion of 
the products depend on the feedstock and operating conditions. As mentioned in the 
introduction, Stiesdal aims to make biochar to use as fertiliser and for CO2 capture. Not all 
the biomasses can be converted into biochar, around half of the biomass is converted into 
pyrolysis gas, not all the gases can be condensed (H2, CO, CO2, light hydrocarbons), some 
of these can be combusted for heating. This heat can then be recovered to heat the 
pyrolysis reactor and dryers. This makes the pyrolysis plant more efficient. The pyrolysis 
reactor is shown in appendix 4 and the characteristics of the biochar in appendix 3. 
 
As shown in figure 1, the pyrolysis gas exiting the pyrolysis reactor is separated before the 
combustor. A portion of the gases, such as CO and H2, are to be recycled back to the 
pyrolysis reactor. These gases help to stabilise the pyrolysis reactor and maintain 
efficiency by providing heat or creating a reducing environment. The remaining gases are 
to be combusted, primarily gases like CH4 and CO.

5.3. 
Sensitivity analysis  
In the sensitivity analysis three parameters, moisture content, biomass load, and drying 
temperature, are assessed on the impact they have on the dryer load and heat recovery. 
The input parameters are to be varied individually to -10% and +10% from the base case.

The next section focuses on the sensitivity analysis of heat recovery potential at a 
pyrolysis system with air dryer. The heat recovery is quantified as the additional energy 
required to sustain the process; this is calculated by: 
 
 Additional Energy =   𝐸𝐹𝑙𝑢𝑒𝐺𝑎𝑠 − 𝐸𝐷𝑟𝑦𝑒𝑟 − 𝐸𝑃𝑦𝑟𝑜𝑙𝑦𝑠𝑖𝑠 –  𝐸𝐴𝑚𝑖𝑛𝑒𝑃𝑙𝑎𝑛𝑡 
 
When the additional energy is calculated and the result is negative, this indicates that 
supplementary energy is required to sustain the process. Conversely, a positive result 
means that no additional energy is needed to maintain the process and that surplus 
energy is available for heat recovery. The results of the analysis are presented in figure 25. 
 
As illustrated in figure 25, the moisture content (red curve) has the most significant 
influence on the heat recovery of the system. A 10% increase in moisture content results in 
an additional energy requirement of 7100kW, which is double the initial amount of extra 
energy required to sustain the process. In contrast, the biomass load shows a more 
moderate impact on the energy recovery of the system. A 10% increase in biomass load 
leads to a 21% in the energy required to sustain the process. Finally, the temperature of 
the dryer (green curve) has an inverse relationship compared to the moisture content and 
biomass load. A 10% increase in dryer temperature reduces the additional energy 
requirement by 5%.  
 
When comparing the effects on heat recovery with the effects on the dryer load, an 
important observation can be made. The impact of the biomass load and moisture content 
on the additional heat required is more significant than on the dryer load. This observation 
further underscores the importances of maintaining the biomass load and moisture content

5.6. 
Optimising Energy Efficiency Air Dryers 
The results show a lower efficiency and higher operational cost for air dryer than the steam 
dryer. However, as discussed in the economic analysis, there is an extra 2 million to invest 
for the air dryer, showing potential for extra investments to optimise the drying system. 
This paragraph explores various options to determine if the air dryer can be made more 
efficient and to evaluate potential methods to lower energy consumption of the air drying.  
 
Straw 
Stiesdal considers addition of straw to the dried biomass to further enhance efficiency or 
cover the additional heat needed. In a scenario where straw is added after the drying 
process, the digestate does not have to be dried as much. Also, the addition of straw 
increases the thermal properties of the biomass, enhancing performance. The covering of 
additional heat with straw for the air dryer is already calculate in paragraph 5.5, proving to 
be effective.  
 
Reducing moisture content 
In the future, the initial moisture content, before entering the dryers, can be reduced by 
other pre-treatment technologies. Study show that other mechanical and chemical 
technologies are available to decrease the initial moisture content potentially even more. 
For example, vacuum preloading and electroosmosis dewatering [18].  Additionally, 
Stiesdal expect that the operators of digestion plant can deliver the bio digestate with a 
lower moisture content. 
 
Figure 30 presents a table illustrating the cost of a drying system with a moisture content 
of 60%, the process does operate without requiring additional energy. When comparing 
annual expenses and the aggerated cost, it becomes apparent that the yearly operating 
cost of the steam dryer system are €400 thousand higher than the cost of the air dryer. 
Over time, the air-drying system proves to be less expensive, offering savings of €10 
million. This highlights an opportunity to invest in extra pre-treatment technologies, such as 
extra screw presses of vacuum preloading.

Heat pump 
One of the most interesting technologies is a heat pump dryer, this technology is Stiesdal 
also exploring. To estimate the energy requirements for a heat pump dryer, the coefficient 
of performance (COP) and the moisture content of the biomass are considered. Stiesdal is 
exploring a heat pump with the company Alumichem, they advised a heat pump sludge 
dryer from Drying Matter [19]. This heat pump has a COP of 5. According to Alumichem, a 
heat pump requires around 250 kWh to remove 1 ton of water.  
 
To compare the heat pump to the steam dryer and air dryer, the heat required to dry 20MW 
of biomass with a moisture content of 70% is calculated. In this case the biomass has a 
mass of 14374 kg and contains 9500 kg of water. To remove this amount of water the heat 
pump would require: 
 
9,5 𝑡𝑜𝑛∗250𝑘𝑊ℎ= 2400 𝑘𝑊ℎ 
 
When analysing the performance table of the Alumichem heat pump, each unit consumes 
496kWh of electricity, assumed is that 350kWh is allocated for generating heat. To meet 
the required drying capacity of 2400kWh of electricity for water removal, 7 heat pump units 
from Alumichem are needed. Together, these heat pump systems consume a combined 
amount of electricity of 3472 kWh. Energy-wise, this is lower than the air dryer and steam 
dryer. The air dryer uses for the same amount of biomass and moisture content 8800kWh, 
and the steam dryer uses 7700kWh, but this is thermal heat. 
 
In the current system, the dryers use the heat generated from combusting the gases 
produced in the process of pyrolysis. The electricity consumed by the heat pumps must be 
from the grid, or locally solar or wind parks. This means that Stiesdal must invest in extra 
infrastructure.  
 
Assuming the electricity price of the economic analysis in paragraph 5.5, the electricity 
price per kwh is 0,10 euro. The electricity consumption of the steam dryer is 734 kWh and 
for heat pomp dryer is 3472 kWh. This means that the steam dryer cost 590 thousand euro 
per year and the heat pump cost 2.8million euro each year in electricity consumption.

Atmospheric steam dryer  
In the current situation, Stiesdal uses a pressurised steam dryer for the drying of the 
digestate. However, the use of pressurised steam is complex, and because of this Stiesdal 
now encounters operational issues at the plant. An alternative approach would be to use a 
steam dryer operating at atmospheric pressure. This would reduce the CAPEX of the 
steam dryer, as rotary valves to pressurise the steam would not be required. Also, the 
evaporation temperature would be lower (100°C), allowing the dryer to operate at a lower 
temperature.  
 
Operating at a lower pressure and temperature would make the steam dryer easier to 
integrate with district heating, as it operates within the same temperature range as district 
heating. By contrast, pressurised steam would need to be depressurised to match the 
pressure levels for district heating. While the lower temperature would also reduce the load 
of the dryer, it would also decrease its efficiency. The implications of operating at a lower 
requires further investigation in future research.

6. Conclusion  
The objective of this thesis was to answer the research question: How does the integration 
of air-drying compare to steam drying in terms of energy efficiency and economic viability 
in biomass pyrolysis processes with limited heat availability? 
 
The results indicate that air drying is less energy-efficient than the steam dryer and 
consumes more energy to dry the same amount of biomass. Due to this higher energy 
demand, a pyrolysis plant with an air dryer requires additional energy to sustain 
operations, further increasing overall energy consumption.  
 
In the economic analysis, the capital and operational expenditures of both systems were 
assessed. The steam dryer requires €2.7 million more in capital expenditures, because of 
the additional components required for its operations, such as rotary valves. The 
operational expenditures for the air dryer were €200 thousand higher per year than for the 
steam dryer, because of the significant amount of additional energy needed for sustaining 
the process. While the steam dryer has higher upfront expenses, the cost savings from its 
lower operational expenditures result in a break-even point after 15 years, after which the 
steam dryer outperforms the air dryer, making it the more economically viable option in the 
long term under the studied conditions.  
 
The findings of the thesis highlight the importance of selecting the appropriate drying 
method in a biomass pyrolysis process to optimise energy consumption, heat recovery, 
and reduce long-term costs. Under different circumstances, the air-drying could still be the 
more energy- and cost-effective option.  
 
Although the steam dryer has higher capital expenditures and is more complex to operate 
than the air dryer, steam drying is the more valuable option in terms of energy efficiency 
and long-term economic performance.

7. Discussion 
The study demonstrated that the steam dryer system is more efficient than the air dryer 
system in the current situation, this is primarily due to better heat transfer characteristics of 
the high-pressure steam dryer. Table 20 compares key features of the air-drying and 
steam-drying methods, highlighting the strengths and limitations of each system.  
 
With the air dryer being less efficient, the operating cost of the air dryer are higher than the 
steam dryer, this is unexpected. Stiesdal showed interested in the air dryer because of 
supposedly better and cheaper operating characterises. However, this study indicates that 
its performance may not meet expectations under current conditions.  
 
However, in future scenarios where the moisture content of biomass can be reduced, air-
drying could be the most valuable option. The economic analysis revealed a gap of 2 
million euros in favour of the air dryer, which could be used to additional investments in 
reducing moisture content of the biomass or increase the efficiency of the system. Also, 
Stiesdal anticipates that the moisture content of digestate fibres will improve in the future, 
potentially rendering the air-drying system as the more favourable option.

A strength of the study is the use of realistic data in the simulations, derived from the 
Stiesdal plant and from Dryer One. This ensures the results provide with a realistic 
perspective on the challenges and opportunities of integration of dryers into a pyrolysis 
system. While the performance data of Dryer One is credible, it also highlights a limitation

Additionally, the validation process revealed some discrepancies in airmass flow between 
the models of Aspen and AVEVA, which limits the results and do not make the model a 
one-to-one comparison with actual operations, but the energy balances align, making the 
results viable, as the study focusses on the thermodynamic behaviour rather than the 
specific mass flows of the dryers. It is acknowledged that no software model can perfectly 
align with real-world conditions.  
 
The research revealed that the addition of straw to the digestate can enhance the 
efficiency of the dryer system, also Stiesdal considered this method. This requires further 
investigation into how this can be implemented into the process. Key areas to study

feasibility.

The study showed the importance of properly integrating drying systems into a pyrolysis 
process, considering the unique characteristics of each pyrolysis plant and its specific 
location. Effective integration of the dryer not only minimized energy consumption and 
efficiency but also demonstrated the potential to make the process self-sustaining. 
Additionally, optimizing dryer integration enhances the economic feasibility of the process 
for stakeholders like Stiesdal. Further research into integration of drying technologies 
could contribute to standardising solutions and expanding their applicability to diverse 
pyrolysis systems.

8. Recommendations  
The results indicated that the steam dryer is most efficient and cost-effective option. 
Therefore, it is recommended that Stiesdal continues using the steam dryer in cases of 
high moisture content, despite the steam dryer having the higher investments cost and 
added complexity.  
 
As discussed, the investments costs for the air dryer are 2 million lower than those of the 
steam dryer. This presents an opportunity to invest toward other technologies aimed to 
reducing the moisture content and increase efficiency. Further research is needed to 
evaluate the required investments and the potential efficiency gain, such as those 
achievable through recycling of the low-grade heat out of the air dryer or implementing 
other mechanical dewatering techniques.  
 
This study shortly highlights the potential emissions associated with drying in the literature 
review. However, Stiesdal is currently lacks the experience in the technologies to mitigate 
the emissions of the air dryer. Further research in mitigating emissions is required to 
implement and operate an air dryer.  
 
At the current Stiesdal plant in Vrå, the nearest residential areas are located too far away 
for district heating to be an effective heat recovery option. However, in future cases, this 
could become a viable option to further improve the overall efficiency of the pyrolysis 
systems, especially if the initial moisture content is reduced below 70%. In such case, 
residual heat from combusting pyrolysis and the dryer exhausts could be better utilised.

1. Appendix: Model Development and Refinement 
Model validation 
In this paragraph, the validation process with Stiesdal is reflected on. The models of the 
thesis are compared with the models of Stiesdal in AVEVA. The simulation results from 
Aspen must be comparable to the data from the AVEVA, the software that Stiesdal uses, 
so Stiesdal can accept and thrust the results from the Aspen software. The key parameters 
assessed, include moisture reduction, energy consumption, and outlet air temperature. 
The validation process was conducted for a large part via email. For reference, please see 
the e-mail thread: Re_ Dryer Validation Mail Thread  
 
Initial comparison  
In the initial attempt to compare the results of the air dryer and steam dryer to the results 
between AVEVA and Aspen, discrepancies were observed for the air dryer and steam 
dryer. This was due to a misunderstanding regarding to use of the LHV or HHV for the 
biomass thermal load. Additionally, the Aspen results show the total energy required to 
heat the hot air and AVEVA displayed the heat transferred from the hot air to the biomass.  
 
Second Iteration: Steam dryer 
In the second iteration for the steam dryer, the results for the temperature and biomass 
load match for both platforms but there is a slight mismatch between the heat used in both 
dryers, this is because of the difference in steam mass flow. This mismatch is because of a 
difference in calculating the water properties between AVEVA and Aspen. According to 
Stiesdal, AVEVA uses the IAPWS Industrial Formulation (2007 revision) methods [20]. 
Aspen uses for free-water calculations the Steam-TA method, which also refers to the 
same version of the IAPWS regulations, but only for transport properties. For 
thermodynamic properties Aspen refers to the ASME steam tables [21].   
 
Second Iteration: Air Dryer 
In the second iteration, the results of both software tools aligned closely in terms of heat 
consumption and temperature. The mass flow rates of the biomass-in, dry biomass, and 
wet-air match were consistent between both platforms, but the air flow rates were different. 
See figure 32 and 33 for the results, in both figures, the air dryer is simulated for 20MW to 
70% moisture content. In energy consumption, the systems have a difference of 0,14%. 
The temperatures show a discrepancy of 1,25%  
 
Further research revealed that both software tools calculate the vapor saturation pressure 
differently, resulting in a difference in airflow. The vapour saturation pressure is calculated 
with different calculation methods in the tools, but all the calculations did not match. 
Despite the results in airflow did not align, the air-drying model is accepted by the Stiesdal 
experts and the DTU supervisor. These differences were deemed negligible in terms of 
impact on the energy balance and results of heat recovery.
