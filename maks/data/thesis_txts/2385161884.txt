Abstract
As the transition towards a more sustainable electrical energy system progresses, the con-
tribution of distributed renewable energy sources is continuously increasing. While the
environmental bene๏ฌts of this transition are obvious, it is also bringing about new unfore-
seen challenges in the distribution networks where these generation sources are located.
Two of the most critical challenges relate to increased active power losses and increased
reactive power ๏ฌows between the transmission and distribution networks. This MSc thesis
proposes a multi-objective mathematical optimization framework to simultaneously mini-
mize the active power losses and the reactive power transfer between a 60kV distribution
network and a high voltage transmission system, utilizing the inherent reactive power
capabilities of wind power plants installed in the given system.
The analysed network is a 150/60 kV Danish distribution system operated by Eniig Forsyn-
ing A/S. Through the inspection of a full year of historical measurements, hourly samples
are selected to illustrate conditions of high wind power production and high active power
losses in the system. Furthermore, a full day of power generation and load variations is
also inspected. Throughout the report, various single and multi-objective optimization
methods are implemented, analysed and compared. The impact of the wind power plant
reactive power limits are also studied and compared for two scenarios. The ๏ฌrst de๏ฌnes
the reactive power limits as per the grid code requirements established by the system
operator, whereas in the second scenario the real wind turbine reactive power capabilities
are used to estimate potential reactive power support. The optimization and load ๏ฌow
simulations are conducted in various packages in the Python programming language.
The study concludes that through the control of wind power plants, local provision of
reactive power support in the studied distribution network can reduce both the active
power losses and the reactive power transfer between the distribution and transmission
systems. For the selected time interval, the proposed methodology provides a Pareto front
for the multi-objective optimization, and reaches single objective optimization values of
up to 5.23% of active power loss reduction and 100% reactive power transfer minimiza-
tion. Nevertheless, as these results are closely related to the wind power generation and
consumption levels of the system at the speci๏ฌc point in time, further studies with more
representative time samples and increased controllable wind power penetration are sug-
gested.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Denmark has historically been at the forefront of the development and integration of
wind power technologies. The countryโs commitment to reach a sustainable energy future
has been translated into ambitious political targets to reach 100% of the total energy
supply covered by renewable energy sources by 2050, and a 50% share of wind energy in
electricity consumption by 2020 [6]. Last year (2016), 37.6% of the total Danish electricity
consumption was covered by wind power, compared to a 2% in 1990 [7]. To achieve this,
distributed generation has and will keep playing a key role.
Figure 1.1: Growing share of distribution grid electricity generation in Denmark [1]
Grids located at distribution voltage levels currently provide more than half of the Danish
electricity production , covering a mix of distributed sources such as wind turbines (WTs),
solar cells and combined heat and power (CHP) plants[1]. As seen in Figure 1.1, a visible
trend in the last thirty years shows the increasing role that distributed generation plays
in providing electricity to the Danish grid. The transition away from centralized, fossil
fueled energy systems and the increasing share of variable renewable energy sources in the
grid bring challenges, such as voltage and frequency stability, to both distribution and
transmission system operators as they endeavour to ensure system stability, security of
supply and reliability to consumers.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.2
Motivation
The displacement of traditional power generation sources equipped with automatic gover-
nor and automatic voltage regulation controls will require the use of supplementary control
actions to meet a diversity of system requirements such as frequency and voltage stability
[8]. Moreover, local network companies now face new challenges in minimizing the addi-
tional power losses occurring in the distribution grid due to increased levels of active power
transfer from wind power plants (WPPs) and other variable generation sources. In partic-
ular, reverse power ๏ฌow to the transmission network caused by this distributed generation
has an impact in the systemโs voltage quality. Traditionally, to deal with the imbalance,
reactive power sources such as capacitor banks and static var conpensators are installed
locally where the reactive power consumption or generation is required. Unfortunately,
this implies investment costs in additional equipment to ensure the systemโs reliability,
which could be avoided if other means of reactive power compensation are used.
Wind turbines can now behave as active controllable components in the power system in
a similar way as traditional power plants. Due to the interfacing of modern WPPs with
the grid through power electronic converters, WT now have the ability to independently
control both active and reactive power. This means that they can be used to actively
support the distribution network, e.g. in relation to active and reactive power regulation.
[8]. Recent studies, both through simulations and physical tests, have demonstrated the
potential provision of a range of ancillary services from variable renewable power plants.
Last year, Californiaโs Independent System Operator (ISO) in cooperation with First Solar
and The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) conducted a series of tests on an
operational 300 MW solar plant to explore its ancillary service provision abilities. Through
this study, they demonstrated the active and reactive power control capabilities of inverter-
connected power plants, and showed how renewable energy generation can be leveraged
from a simple intermittent energy resource to a provider of a wide range of ancillary
services, including reactive power compensation [8] . With this in mind, if existing WPPs
can be used and optimized to minimize the active power losses and the MVAr ๏ฌow from
the transmission system, added costs in compensating equipment could be avoided.
1.3
Project Objectives
The goal of this project is to propose a multi-objective, mathematical optimization method
to assess the potential bene๏ฌts of using the inherent reactive power capabilities of pre-
existing wind turbine generators to support a 60 kV distribution network in steady state
conditions. Throughout the report, di๏ฌerent deterministic optimization approaches are
investigated aiming to minimize the 60 kV active power losses and the reactive power ๏ฌow
(also referred as MVAr ๏ฌow hereafter) with the transmission network. This optimization
has been performed by operating the wind power plants at their available wind power
while satisfying the constraints of the wind power capabilities, grid code requirements,
distribution feeder congestion and voltage limits. The project examines the possibilities to
optimize a non-linear, multi-objective function using mathematical optimization methods
to provide reactive power setpoints for wind power plants, which is achieved through the
completion of the following tasks:

system optimization problems.

Chapter 2
State of the Art
This chapter presents a literature review of di๏ฌerent power system optimization formula-
tion and solution methods that have been studied through time. During the last decades,
the power system optimization has seen an evolution hand in hand with the development
of computational technology. Throughout the ๏ฌrst half of the 20th century, power system
optimization was approached solely by the empirical studies using mainly experience and
judgment of operators and engineers [10]. The ๏ฌrst formulation of an optimal power ๏ฌow
(OPF) problem was created by Carpentier [11] in 1962. Since then, OPF has become one
of the most studied non-linear optimization problems [12]. For the solution of this com-
plex problem, di๏ฌerent formulations and methods have been studied to optimize power
systemsโ operations. This chapter presents and compares some of these methods.
2.1
Formulation of Optimization Problems
A power system optimization problem is de๏ฌned to optimize a given objective be it gener-
ation cost, planning or reliability of a system by controlling the power ๏ฌow in an electrical
network, staying within network power ๏ฌow constraints as well as system and equipment
operational limits [12]. Similar to a standard load ๏ฌow, optimization of power systems
seeks a state of operation by determining voltages, currents, injected powers and other
control variables in the electrical system.
Both the solution and the accuracy of a power system optimization are highly dependent
on the problem de๏ฌnition. A few ways to formulate the optimization problem will be
brie๏ฌy de๏ฌned as a contextual introduction to the solution methods, based on a thorough
and complete analysis by Stephen Frank [12].
2.1.1
Non-linear Programming
The original optimal power ๏ฌow formulation ๏ฌrst introduced by Carpentier [11] is a Non-
Linear Programming (NLP) problem. Non-linear problems, like OPF problems, have both
non-linear objective functions and constraints. In this approach, discrete variables such
as the transformer tap changers are usually approximated as continuous to simplify the
problem. System variables are separated as control and dependent variables, which are
associated to the power ๏ฌow equations [13]. Two main advantages of this formulation are
an accurate representation of the power system behaviour and characteristics, and the

2.2.1
Deterministic Methods
The ๏ฌrst methods implemented for the solution of OPF problems belong to this group of
โconventionalโ optimization, which rely on mathematical programming.
Simplex Method.
The simplex method could be attributed as one of the ๏ฌrst methods
to be studied for OPF problems [20]. Nowadays, even after the development of varied
deterministic and heuristic methods, still holds as the most widely used of all optimization
tools [15].
It takes advantage of the convexity of linearized formulations to achieve a
solution by exploring the vertices of the feasible region until the objective function can
not be improved any further [12]. On the down side, its accuracy is compromised due
to the function linearization [21]. Throughout the years, its demonstrated reliability and
speed have made of the Simplex Method commonly used in industry.
Gradient Descent Methods.
These methods are ๏ฌrst-order iterative optimization al-
gorithms that use the gradient of the NLP objective function to de๏ฌne the improving
direction each of the search iterations [18]. They are reliable, relatively simple and ensure
convergence in well-behaved functions , but are generally slower than higher order meth-
ods [12]. Peschon et al. [22] develops and describes an OPF formulation using a gradient
descent method.
Newton/Quasi-Newton Methods.
The Newton method require the calculation of the
second derivatives (Hessian) of the objective function, the power ๏ฌow equations and some
constraints to ๏ฌnd the search directions[12]. This is done by the use of the Second Order
Taylor series expansion [15]. Quasi-Newton methods follow a similar path but employ a
Hessian approximation to reduce the computation intensity required for the calculation
and inversion of the second derivatives, therefore reducing convergence time for complex
problems [12]. Nevertheless, all Quasi-Newton approximations of the Hessian require to
store and manipulate dense matrices, which could be expensive in large-scale problems [15]
. Housus et al. [23] present and compare Quasi-Newton methods for OPF formulations.
Two well known Quasi-Newton methods are:
โข Truncated Newton Method(TNC). The TNC method is extensively described by
Nash in [24] and [25]. This algorithm does not require the explicit knowledge of the
Hessian, and uses a preconditioned conjugate gradient calculation [26] to indicate
the search direction. The conjugate gradient calculation is one of the most popular
iterative methods for solving large systems of linear equations [27].The approximated
search direction is then used to determine the line search. The line search can be
done using iterative methods such as the one described by Morยดe [28].
โข Limited Memory BroydenโFletcherโGoldfarbโShanno (L-BFGS). This Quasi-Newton
iterative method also utilizes a Hessian approximation to determine the search direc-
tion. The algorithm is fully explained in [29] and [30]. The Hessian approximation
is used to de๏ฌne a quadratic model of the objective function, which is solved to ob-
tain the search direction. Finally, the line search also uses the previously subroutine
described by Morยดe [28].

5.1.1
Control and Dependent Variables
In power system loss minimization problems, the control variables predominantly used are
the generator voltages and the transformer tap ratios [12]. For simpli๏ฌcation purposes and
to independently study the e๏ฌect of the MVAr capabilities on the systemโs active power
loss reduction, the transformer tap ratios are not considered. Consequently, the control
variable vector is de๏ฌned as x = [VK], consisting of the voltages of the WPPs in which
the reactive power capabilities can be used by the distribution system operator.
Additionally, a vector of dependent variables u(x) = [VL, QGK] is established, where L is
the discrete set of all the load buses, hence VL represents the load bus voltages and QGK
the WPPsโ reactive power output.
The values of these dependent variables are obtained through a conventional power ๏ฌow
analysis. All the mentioned variables are de๏ฌned as continuous. Continuous problems are
usually easier to deal with since the smoothness of the function at any point can help
deduce information about other points close to it [64].

โข From the sensitivity study explained above, it is determined to aggregate the active
and reactive power production from WPP1 and WPP3 so that WPP1 = WPP1 +
WPP3. This helps mathematically to deal with the nonexistent relation between
changes in the voltage of WPP3 and all other load buses. The main drawback of
this is that, since the production of the two wind power plants is merged, there is
no individual control of their reactive power capability. This presents a problem
since this simpli๏ฌcation can not be achieved in real circumstances by the DSO, but
does help to avoid false readings during the simulation and optimization process.
Additionally, this also impedes the consideration of the line losses between WPP1
and WPP3. Nevertheless, the losses in this line only represented 0.38% of the total
system losses from the initialized load ๏ฌow, which would not impact drastically the
๏ฌnal results.
โข To linearize the equality constraints, a nested load ๏ฌow is proposed within each
optimization iteration, simplifying the complexity of the problem de๏ฌnition.

Weighted Sum Method
A multi-objective optimization is now carried through the weighted (or weighting) sum
method earlier described in Chapter 2, and formulated in equation 5.14. Though this is
a basic approach to study the simultaneous minimization of both objective functions, it
helps to have an overview of the trade-o๏ฌs that giving a higher importance to each of the
objectives has in the overall results and generatorsโ reactive power setpoints. Since the
particular costs for a unit of the reactive power generation (MVAr) or the active power
losses (MW) are not de๏ฌned on grid codes, a normalization of the two objectives provides
an equal relative weight to both values and eliminates the problem of big di๏ฌerences in the
magnitude of both numbers. To provide a good spectrum of the Pareto optimal solutions,
the weighting factor is changed from 0 to 1. Results shown in Figure 6.7 demonstrate that,
for this particular scenario and having scaled the values of the objective functions, there
is a visible trade-o๏ฌwhen bigger weight is given to either the Ploss or the MVAr transfer
minimization.

It should be clari๏ฌed that the goal of this method is not to give one particular solution,
but to provide su๏ฌcient information of the range of values and combinations that can
be covered when optimizing these two functions simultaneously. Figure 6.8 shows a plot
with a set of non-dominant Pareto optimal solutions (also known as Pareto Set) as a
concluding ๏ฌgure of this optimization, where the red line represents the combination of

Case 4:
Sensitivity Analysis for Voltage at
150/60 kV Transformer.
As an addition to the previously developed multi-objective optimization and the full day
single objective study, a sensitivity analysis is conducted to study the impact of controlling
the voltage at the 150/60 kV transformer to improve the distribution system operation.
During all previous scenarios, the voltage at the 60 kV side of the transformer was kept
constant at a level of 1 p.u. to ensure there were no considerable voltage variations in
the system. Though this is not within DSOโs regulatory area, being able to establish a
voltage setpoint for this transformer could also help optimize the lines losses and MVAr
๏ฌow. Low voltage levels mean that higher currents are required to transport the same
amount of power, which as a consequence increase the line losses. Therefore, having the
ability to set a voltage target by using the automatic tap settings of this transformer could
be considered as an option to optimize the system performance. This would also have
the added advantage of avoiding the installation of other equipment such as capacitor
banks. Nevertheless, recurrent changes in the tap settings could reduce the lifetime of
the transformer, so economical impacts of this option should also be considered. It is
important to clarify having the ๏ฌexibility to change the voltage setpoint at the 60 kV side
of the transformer would probably require a DSO/TSO interaction, but the sensitivity
analysis is done to demonstrate the possible bene๏ฌts that varying this voltage level (by
means of the automatic tap changer) could bring to the overall objective functions in
question.

Conclusions
This thesis develops and implements a multi-objective formulation to optimize the active
power losses and the reactive power ๏ฌow between the transmission and distribution systems
in a real 60 kV distribution network, by using the reactive power capabilities of controllable
IEC Type 4 wind turbines. The main conclusions and outcomes that can be drawn from
this work are as follows:
โข The software interface created for this project provides an e๏ฌective method to mi-
grate the network data from Digsilent PowerFactory to the Python programming
language, and potentially to other power system analysis tools such as Pypower.
The creation of this software interface provides the ability to increase optimization
performance and reduce processing time by solely using Python-based packages to
conduct the multi-objective optimization. Furthermore, adapting the network de-
tails and topology to the Pypower power system analysis software enables access to
all codes and relevant load ๏ฌow data such as the admittance and Jacobian matrices.
A limitation of this method is that automatic transformer tap changers can not be
modelled.
โข One of the main advantages of using deterministic instead of arti๏ฌcial intelligence
optimization methods is the ability to access and track relevant information through
the optimization process, such as individual line losses in the system. This gives
a better understanding of the steady state conditions of the network, as well as
particularly loaded areas in the grid.
For instance, through the single objective
optimization for the high WPP production study case, it is clearly seen that most
of the system power losses correspond to the lines between the WPPs and the bus
connecting to the transmission system. For the high WPP production case, these
lines shown in Figure 6.1 generate 87% of the total system line losses. The reason
for this is not only because of the WPP active power production being exported
to the transmission network, but also due to the high reactive power demanded by
some of the buses located in between the WPPs and the 150/60 kV transformer, e.g.
bus 22. Having knowledge of these details helps to assess if the obtained results are
realistic. A better understanding of the network and its characteristics helps provide
more speci๏ฌc insights regarding the location of the power loss reduction.
โข According to Chapter 6, the independent minimization of the active power losses and
the reactive power transfer in the network is performed through the SLSQP method
and two Quasi-Newton Methods, namely the Truncated Newton Method and the L-
BFGS-B. The comparison of these methods shows similar numerical results, but the
TNC method falls behind in computational processing time. This can be attributed
53

to various factors such as the solverโs parameters tuning and di๏ฌerences in the search
methods. Though the L-BFGS-B method provides very promising results, it is not
considered for the multi-objective optimization since it can only work with box-type
bounds and does not have the capability to handle constrained problems. SLSQP
method con๏ฌrms its fast convergence and ability to optimize constrained problems
throughout this study.
โข Single objective optimizations for Ploss and MVAr transfer show that multi-objective
optimization is not relevant for all system conditions since con๏ฌicting results are
required to make the multi-objective study useful. For the high WPP power pro-
duction hourly sample, when the controllable WPP reactive power limits are set per
the grid code requirement imposed by the TSO, all generators provide as much reac-
tive power as possible for both objective functions. Through all methods, Ploss and
MVAr ๏ฌow are reduced by 3.12% and 33%, respectively, regardless of the objective
function being optimized. This shows that, for certain cases such as the high WPP
production sample, the two objective functions are not contradicting each other with
the aforementioned limits.
โข Increasing the WPP reactive power limits from the Danish grid code requirement to
a resemblance of the actual IEC Type 4 wind turbine reactive power capability curve
results in higher reductions of the active power losses and the MVAr transfer. For
the selected sample, single objective optimization of the active power losses reaches a
5.23% of Ploss minimization, and the reactive power transfer optimization provides
a reduction of 85% of the total MVAr ๏ฌow. In this scenario, the reactive power
setpoint of the controllable WPPs does not reach the limit during the Ploss mini-
mization, showing con๏ฌicting results between the Ploss and the MVAr ๏ฌow individual
optimization. This makes the case suitable for a multi-objective study.
โข The multi-objective optimization is implemented through the weighted sum and E -
Constraint methods to compare the resulting Pareto fronts. It is important to note
that since there is no determined preference or cost for the two objectives, objective
functions are normalized and the system is optimized for a varied set of weighting
factors ranging from 0 to 1 to show the set of Pareto optimals. For the weighted sum
method, active power losses reduction ranges from 1.98% to 5.23%, while the MVAr
๏ฌow reduction ranges from 43.38% to 84.72% depending on the chosen weighting
factor. The E -Constraint Method delivers similar results, having the advantage for
the decision maker that instead of ambiguous weighting values, the Pareto front is
determined by the limits placed to the objective function selected as the problem
constraint, in terms of the actual objective function units. It should be pointed out
that the used methods only ensure local optima and results might vary if control
variable limits are modi๏ฌed, as other local minimas could be found.
โข The analysis of a full day of 24 hourly samples shows that local provision of reactive
power by the controllable WPPs has a favorable impact in reducing the active power
losses even if total losses are not at the highest levels. For a randomly selected day, a
single objective optimization is conducted to minimize the active power losses with
results showing a daily average of 2.5% Ploss reduction. This translates to a 0.41
MWh of saved energy from a total of 15.41 MWh of energy lost in the system lines
during that day.
โข The active power losses in the studied network are mostly reduced when a combi-
nation of high aggregated reactive power consumption at the buses and high wind

โข Increasing the voltage level at the 150/60 kV transformer setpoint could support

voltage is not within the DSO regulatory area, therefore a DSO/TSO interaction

โข The proposed methodology for multi-objective optimization for real medium voltage
distribution networks has provided a good insight of the systemโs potential to use
the WPP reactive power capabilities to reduce the active power losses and the MVAr
๏ฌow. In di๏ฌerent aggregated bus loading and wind power generation conditions, the
performed optimization was able to reduce both objective functions individually and
in parallel. Furthermore, it is concluded that the extent of the minimization of the
objective functions relies heavily on the reactive power limits of the WPP, the overall
system power demand and the WPP generation.
