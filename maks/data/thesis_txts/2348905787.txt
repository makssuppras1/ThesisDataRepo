ABSTRACT 
Plastics are widely used and known to have negative impact on the planet. Many com-
panies work on solutions to replace plastics and one of them is ecoXpac, that focuses on 
the research and development of environmental friendly packaging solutions. The Green 
Fiber Bottle project is one of the most significant projects run in the company and it 
aims to develop a paper bottle for beer. 
 
Key goal of this master thesis is to analyze and optimize the paper molding manufactur-
ing process, focusing on the manufacturing parameters of temperature, pressure and 
time. These parameters are investigated in regards to how they influence the quality of 
the final product. Quality assessment is done through the analysis of geometrical 
replicability of surface characteristics such as roughness, step height and width by using 
optical measurement instruments such as Alicona Infinite Focus and Olympus LEXT. 
 
Three sets of experiments were conducted and analyzed in this research for the purpose 
described above.  
The first set of experiments conducted using Alicona Infinite Focus microscope shows 
that the best replicability of paper samples to the mold is achieved at the tempera-
ture/pressure of 160°C /100 bar and 160°C/60 bar for the roughness, and the tempera-
ture/pressure of 160°C /60 bar and 110°C /60 bar for the step height.   
The second set of experiments was conducted and measured using Olympus LEXT. A 
new mold, that contain different measurement areas on its surface (e.g. for roughness, 
step height and width) was applied. The results showed that the most optimal process 
parameters to ensure replicability are the temperature of 130°C, holding time of 5 se-
conds and vacuum time of 15 seconds. However, the results indicate that these parame-
ters can be further optimized. Therefore, the new set of experiments was proposed. 
The new set of experiments was conducted using the same manufacturing machine and 
measurement instrument as in the second one. However, the mold that was used in the 
second set of experiments was coated with Teflon. This was done in order to see how it 
can improve the replicability of the features, hence, quality of the end product. In addi-
tion, new process parameters were defined and applied, based on the analysis from the 
second set of experiments, in order to optimize the process and improve replicability. 
The result of this experiment showed that Teflon coating and new process parameters 
have improved the replicability. The most optimal process parameters are the tempera-
ture of 110°C, holding time of 8 seconds and vacuum time of 8 seconds.  
However, the results indicate that additional analyses and work need to take place to 
further improve the manufacturing process of molded pulp products.

INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background 
People have been using plastic for decades. Plastic has been considered a cheap and 
convenient material used for various purposes, including packaging. However, today 
people are more informed about the true costs of plastic for the planet, the oceans and 
for society in general. Plastic has a very long period of degradation which means that 
many of the plastic products produced still exist. Each year, tons of plastics end up on 
the land fields and in the oceans having a negative impact on the environment and on 
the health of people and animals. In addition, the production process is extremely re-
source heavy as it requires tons of oil to produce plastic products [1]. 
 
Many organizations and companies have been working on increasing awareness about 
the above described problems and working on developing solutions to find environmen-
tally friendly alternatives to plastic. One of such companies is ecoXpac. 
EcoXpac is a Danish based company established back in 2005. The company works 
with environmentally friendly packaging solutions for various industries and products. 
EcoXpac works together with its customers to develop packaging that would have min-
imum negative impact on the environment. The primary focus is on molded pulp solu-
tions for packaging [2]. 
Already back in 2013 ecoXpac has been successful in producing its first fiber - biode-
gradable bottle [2]. It opened up vast potential and new opportunities for development. 
The new bottle has shown to have comparative advantages to the traditional plastic and 
glass bottles. The most significant advantage is of course the biodegradability. The bot-
tle is able to degrade in the environment in about seven weeks according to the study 
conducted by the Danish Technological Institute [2]. Moreover, it can also be recycled 
together with other paper products. These benefits are valuable, but in order to be able 
to produce and market the bottle successfully, an efficient production process has to be 
developed and established.  
 
1.2 The Green Fiber Bottle 
One of the most significant project run in the company is the Green Fiber Bottle (GFB) 
project. The project is set up as a partnership between ecoXpac, Carlsberg Group, the 
Danish Technical University (DTU) and BillerudKorsnäs. In addition to the recourses

from the partners, the project is funded by such funds as Horizon2020, the Danish Mar-
ket Development Fund, The Danish Innovation fund, and the Danish Eco-innovation 
program [2]. 
 
One of the greatest ambitions of the GFB is to develop a bottle for beer made out of 
paper. The vision is to make a paper bottle that is biodegradable and can be recycled [3], 
and thus be environmentally friendly. An example of a paper beer bottle is shown in 
Figure 1.1. 
 
 
Figure 1.1: Paper bottle for beer [4] 
 
It is not only important to make a bottle more environmentally friendly compared to 
plastics or glass bottles, it is also essential to develop a manufacturing process competi-
tive to the traditional bottle production processes.  
 
The first machine developed by ecoXpac to produce the bottle is of the thermoformed 
type, but improvement needs to be introduced to improve the production cycle time and 
lower energy consumption. Figure 1.2 shows the machine owned by ecoXpac and used 
for the production of the paper bottle.

Figure 1.2: Machine for making paper bottles at ecoXpac 
 
The process steps are listed below:  
Step 1: Forming process. Injection of the pulp into the mold 1 which is porous (see Fig-
ure 1.2). As soon as the pulp is injected, the vacuum inside the mold is applied that 
leads to water removal from the pulp and the paper substrate is collected on the mold 
net. The result of this step is a product that resembles a bottle but still contains a lot of 
water which usually accounts of 50% of weight [5]. 
Step 2: Drying process. This process aims to remove the remaining water from the bot-
tle. First of all the wet bottle is moved from mold 1 to mold 2 which is hot (see Figure 
1.2). Then, a silicone balloon is placed inside the bottle to develop a certain pressure 
inside the bottle. Trapped in the hot mold and pressed by the silicone balloon, the water 
turns into steam and leave the product. Thus, the fiber substrate inside the mold dries 
out. The end product of this process is a paper bottle which then will undergo quality 
control. 
 
Today, the described above paper bottle manufacturing process takes minutes to go 
through. In order to compete with the traditional processes of glass bottle manufactur-
ing, the cycle time needs to be lowered down to approximately 10 seconds [3]. 
 
1.3 Research goals and scopes 
The key goal of this research is to analyze and optimize the paper molding manufactur-
ing process. Main process parameters are temperature, pressure and process time [3]. In 
this master thesis the influence of these parameters on the quality of the final product is 
analyzed using a design of experiments (DOE).  
 
In addition, the product quality analysis encompasses the study of the geometrical 
replicability of the features through various optical measurements such as roughness, 
step height and width of the mold and paper samples.

In order to accommodate for complexity of analyses and to scope the project appropri-
ately, a subset of the process parameters has been carefully chosen and investigated by 
applying empirical and analytical analysis methods. To monitor process conditions sta-
tistical methods are applied. Minitab® is used in order to analyze the output data of the 
experiments.  
 
Based on the conclusions and observations of the analyses conducted throughout the 
master thesis, a new set of experiments is proposed for further investigation and optimi-
zation of paper molding processes. 
 
1.4 Structure of thesis 
This thesis consists of five chapters and it is organized as follows. 
 
Chapter 1 provides the introduction to the subject of the study, its background and 
goals.  
 
Chapter 2 comprises the literature reviews on relevant topics around paper manufactur-
ing and related quality control processes. It begins with an overview of molded pulp 
products' manufacturing processes and then comes with a brief description of the sur-
face metrology. Surface metrology includes the most important paper surface character-
istics and measurement methods of these characteristics including the comparison of 
different approaches to surface roughness measurements in paper industry. 
 
Chapter 3 presents the experimental part of this thesis, which is subdivided into two 
parts: 
• The first set of experiments includes methodology description, Alicona measurements, 
SPIP™ analysis, result observation and conclusion. 
• The second set of experiment includes methodology description, Olympus LEXT 
measurements, SPIP™ analysis, Minitab® analysis, result observation and conclusion. 
 
Chapter 4 discusses the possibilities of the process optimization based on the results 
from the previous experimental part of this thesis. 
 
Observations, recommendations for further research as well as final conclusions are 
presented in Chapter 5.

STATE OF THE ART 
2.1 Molded Pulp Products (MPPs) 
As mentioned in the introduction part the need for more sustainable packaging solution 
is increasing. It is further boosted by increasing regulations around oil based forms of 
packaging [6]. To live up to these regulations and meet increasing customer demands 
for sustainable solutions many companies explore opportunities for paper based solu-
tions. Very often, these solutions are referred to as molded pulp products (MPPs) or 
molded fiber products. MPPs are primarily used in handling and packaging of manufac-
turing products. They are also used in food packaging, that goes beyond egg trays that 
historically are the most known paper packaging solution and the utilization of such 
products are increasing rapidly [7]. However, there are ways to go in terms of making 
manufacturing processes of paper more efficient both in terms of resources consump-
tion, production time and even more standardized practices to manufacturing and quali-
ty control. Some literature exists on this topic however, it is somewhat divers. In order 
to have a more holistic understanding of the pros and cons of MPP, collection and anal-
ysis of data and information in these different sources is key for further development of 
MPP. 
 
2.1.1 Classification of MPP 
According to the International Molding Fiber Association (IMFA) there are four differ-
ent types of molded pulp products [8]. These types are defined based on the manufactur-
ing process and quality of input materials [9]. 
  
Thick wall is a first type of MPP [8] (Figure 2.1). This type of products is manufactured 
by using a single mold and the product is simply dried in an oven. The result is a prod-
uct where one side of the surface is relatively smooth while the other one is rough. 
Moreover, the wall thickness is between 5-10 mm. Such products are mainly used for 
support packaging of heavy items, e.g. vehicle parts and furniture. In addition, they can 
also be used in floral and nursery pots and containers. 
 
==PAGE:17==
 
State of the art 
6 
 
 
Figure 2.1: An example of thick wall molded pulp product [10] 
 
Transfer molded is a second type of MPP [8] (Figure 2.2). The end product is dried in 
an oven too, so this process is much alike the one described previously. However, the 
difference is that in transfer molded MPP two molds are used. The first mold is used to 
form the product and is called forming mold. The second one is called transfer mold and 
is used to move the product. The benefits of using two molds are the reduction of the 
wall thickness to 3-5 mm and improvements in the surfaces quality. Most common ap-
plications are cartons for eggs, beverages and fruit trays as well as packages for elec-
tronics and other light weight items. 
 
 
Figure 2.2: An example of transfer molded pulp product [10] 
 
Thermoformed is a third type of MPP [8] (Figure 2.3). Thermoforming is the newest 
molded pulp process [11]. Thermoformed molded type of products are manufactured 
using multiple heated molds which means that the product is dried inside the mold. 
When the process is completed, the product is ejected from the heated mold in its fin-
ished state, and no further oven treatments are needed. The result is a product with a 
smooth and well detailed surface with minimal draft angles. Moreover, due to the hot 
mold pressing process, the walls are somewhat more dense and the wall thicknesses is 
normally between 2-4 mm. For these reasons, thermoformed molded pulp products look 
very similar to the plastic products in their completed state. They can be used in packag-
ing for products such as molded plastic tableware.  
 
==PAGE:18==
 
State of the art 
7 
 
 
Figure 2.3: An example of thermoformed molded pulp product [10] 
 
Processed is a fourth type of MPP [8] (Figure 2.4). Processed molded pulp products are 
those that have undergone a secondary treatment. This could be a second hot pressing, 
edge cutting or cutting out, perforation, printing, adding additives or coatings. This type 
of products can be used for growing seeds. 
 
 
Figure 2.4: An example of processed molded pulp product [10] 
 
2.1.2 Manufacturing process of MPP 
Most paper-based packages are made starting from a flat sheet then bent and assembled 
into a three-dimensional package. In contrast, mold pulp products are made from cellu-
lose fibers that are mixed into water and then formed, drained and dried [12].  
 
The production process of molded pulp products is depicted (see Figure 2.5) and de-
scribed in the following. 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
State of the art 
8 
 
 
Figure 2.5: Manufacturing process of traditional MPP [13] 
 
The process starts from putting raw materials into a so called pulper, seen in step two in 
the above figure. A giant blender mashes everything up and blends it with water that is 
43-65°C [14]. The hot water swallows fibers, causing them to break apart. After 20 min 
pulp is ready for the next step [14]. The pulp then exits the bottom of the pulper through 
a filter that screens out plastics and other contaminants. Adding bleaches and additives 
to the slurry storage, such as sizing agents or fillers can improve a particularly product 
functionality [15]. However, producers of molded pulp are concerned about the poten-
tial negative consequences additives might have on the environment. Thus, they are 
very careful and selective to what and how much additives are used in the process. 
 
The next step is forming. The sample is shaped in the specially designed formation 
mold. The mold is made of bronze, plastic or aluminum, depending on how the factory 
intends to use them. The mold is covered by a stainless steel mesh. Sample shaping is a 
fully automated process. Formation mold descends into the pulp mixture and the water 
is pulled through a metal mesh by applying vacuum. This mesh ensures an even vacuum 
through the mold, so that the pulp spreads evenly. Suction from the vacuum helps bind 
the fibers. The transfer mold presses the formation mold, helps remove most of the wa-
ter and shapes the sample. Afterwards, the transfer mold grabs the sample and moves it 
further to the conveyer. Water based lubricants are applied with a brush on the transfer 
mold before it grabs the sample. This ensures that the samples do not stick to the mold. 
After forming, the sample is wet and needs to be dried. Therefore, the wet sample goes 
into an oven for several minutes to completely dry out. The 205°C heat evaporates the

2.2 Surface metrology 
The world of modern technologies, such as nanotechnologies and micro technologies 
are developing rapidly and bring about new opportunities, challenges and requirements 
to the instruments and tools applied. These technologies enable producers to continu-
ously downsize the products, producing smaller and smaller mechanical and electronic 
products [24].  
 
As the product size is reduced to micro and nano scales, there are new requirements for 
measuring instruments. Traditional dimensional metrology, which primarily measures 
the physical size of an object or product, struggles to meet these requirements, and so 
new instruments may have to be used. 
 
Micro and nanotechnologies require achieving characteristics and tolerances in nano 
and micro scales. These characteristics obviously can no longer be measured with old 
dimensional equipment and instruments. This is where the new optical instruments 
come into play. These instruments are known for their precision and flexibility. In the 
newest technologies, the most important task is no longer to measure the physical char-
acteristics of an object, but rather to analyze the surface of an object through the appli-
cation of surface topography [25]. Surface topography is a field that engineers have 
been studying for about 15 years now [26] in order to meet the requirements of the new-
est technologies. Surface topography is defined as local deviations of a surface from a 
perfectly flat plane. It can be described in two ways. The first one is the measurement of 
a line across the surface and can be represented as a height function, z(x). The second 
one is the measurement of an area on the surface which can be represented as a height 
function, z(x; y) defined across the plane. The second measurement method has more 
advantages due to its ability for complete surface characterization in addition to having 
more statistical value [27].  
 
In paper industry surface topography, also known as a surface texture, is different and 
depends on the product materials, production conditions and techniques that are being 
applied. The texture of the surface is extremely vital and it has to be defined as precisely 
as possible [28][29]. Surface topography, using optical instruments, can be applied for 
quality assurance of MPPs [30]. Optical instruments have a scale of precision down to 
micro scale and are flexible compared to the traditional ones. In addition, they are more 
appropriate for 3D measurements.  
 
To be able to ensure proper and efficient production processes and to fully understand 
and analyze the surfaces of the samples or production components, the surfaces are 
measured in two (2D) and three (3D) dimensions. 3D type of measurement can fully 
describe the surface enabling a more precise and realistic picture of the surface. In addi-
tion, it has more statistical value compared to the 2D type of measurement [27]. In order 
to evaluate the surface texture of the product, 3D standardized parameters are selected. 
==PAGE:23==
 
State of the art

These parameters are the roughness Sa and Sq, step height h and width w. Further de-
tailing of these parameters can be found in the standard ISO 25178-2 [31] and ISO 
5436-1 [32].

2.2.2 Step height and width 
Step height, according to ISO 5436-1 [32], can be defined as the average distance be-

there is no ISO that defines the measurement of the step height, ISO only exists for cali-
bration standards. These standards are defined in ISO 5436-1:2000 [32]. In addition, a 
number of various software analysis packages exist that can calculate step height and 
width. A good example of such a software package is SPIP™.  
 
SPIP™ is a software that processes images for the use in nano and micro scale analyses 
[36]. This software is able to calculate step height by applying the Z-calibration function 
which is based on height distribution histogram.

function, which, as seen in its name, is based on the ISO 5436-1 standard [32].

characteristics such as slope angles of a line or trench. The figure below illustrates the

2.2.3 Measuring of paper roughness  
Several ways of measuring paper surface roughness exist. When all surface elements are 
in one plane, paper surface roughness is identified as a deviation from this flat plane.  
Paper surface roughness measuring devices function in a way so that they compare pa-
per surface to the flat surface. Nowadays, three different methods for measuring paper 
surface roughness applied in the paper industry. These methods are shortly described 
below. 
 
The first one is an air leak method. Air leak method is a standardized measurement 
method of paper roughness [38], and it is one of the most reliable techniques in paper 
industry. There are plenty of techniques, using air as a source in defining the paper sur-
face roughness. The ones are mainly used are Bekk, Bendtsen and Parker Print Surf 
(PPS) [39]. These techniques is about measuring the ability of paper to stand air that 
flows in-between two surfaces where one is a paper surface and another is a metal ring 
called "annulus" (Figure 2.9). However, in a Bendtsen technique, besides metal ring a 
flat glass plate is used so the paper sample is clamped between those. The result or the 
output of such methods is affected by air pressure, specific clamping pressure between 
annulus and paper surface which is normally between 198 and 490 kPa [39] and the area 
of the measuring head [40]. When this method is applied, one can see that the bigger the 
rate of air leakage ml/min, the rougher then the surface is.  
 
The basic principle of the Bendtsen and Bekk methods is shows in figure below. 
 
 
Figure 2.9: Illustration of the basic technique of air leak method [39]

The main disadvantage of these methods is that they are only suitable for laboratory 
measurements and give roughness in only one parameter which is not enough to have a 
full information about the surface texture. Therefore, these tests are insufficient. The 
tests have poor resolution and can therefore only characterize roughness at a macroscop-
ic level, thus requiring a micro-roughness instrument for further analysis [41]. 
 
The other two are the non-contact (optical) profilometry and contact profilometry  
methods. A profilometer is an instrument that measures paper roughness by measuring 
the surface variation. Profilometer can be contact (mechanical), where the physical sty-
lus moves along the surface or it can be non-contact (optical) profilometer that use laser 
beam to analyze the surface. The way the stylus moves is highly dependent on the sur-
face it moves on and the path it makes.  
It has been concluded that non-contact methods report higher values of roughness com-
pared to the contact one [42]. This may be explained by the fact that contact methods 
are restricted by the size of the stylus that then restricts the resolution to 10 μm as a 
minimum [42], so the variations below this resolution are "low pass filtered". 
An non-contact profilometer functions in a different way compared to the contact one. 
This profilometer uses a laser beam light that is applied to the surface of the paper. A 
special detector then analyses the reflection characteristics of this light and any devia-
tions of the defined focus position are analyzed [42]. 3D image of the surface typogra-
phy is calculated given the position, direction and the illumination of the light sources.

2.3.1 Focus variation instrument 
One of the newest technologies in the field of modern measurement instruments is a 
focus variation instruments. These instruments gained high market recognition and high 
demand as these instruments have the ability to measure simultaneously the roughness 
and the 3D form [45].  
 
In order to provide a topographical and color information of a surface, focus variation 
[46] is applied by combining the small depth of the optical system’s focus and vertical 
scanning. The measurements are undertaken by finding most optimal objective position 
in regards to the analyzed sample [27]. In order to be able to rebuild the surface entirely, 
the above described process is repeated for each optical element.  
 
In Figure 2.11 key elements of the focus variation instrument are illustrated.  
==PAGE:28==
 
State of the art 
17 
 
 
 
Figure 2.11: Schematic representation of a basic focus variation instrument [47] 
 
The system works in the following way: Light emerges from the white light source (3 in 
Figure 2.11). This light is splitted by a beam splitting mirror (4 in Figure 2.11) then it 
goes into the optical path of the system through the objective (5 in Figure 2.11) and then 
reaches the specimen (6 in Figure 2.11).  
When the light goes into the objective and reaches the specimen, it is being reflected 
into many directions, which is dependent on the topography of the specimen. If diffused 
reflective properties of the topography are seen, it indicates that the light is dispersed 
equally strong into different directions. Contrary, if specular reflections are observed, it 
indicates that the light is primarily dispersed into the same one direction.  
A light sensitive sensor that is situated behind the beam splitting mirror, captures rays 
that emerge from the specimen going through the objective lens and that are being gath-
ered together in the optics. The depth of the optics field is rather limited therefore only 
small areas of the sample surface are clearly captured. To compensate for this limitation 
and to ensure that all sample areas are clearly captured, vertical movement along the 
optical axis of the optic is performed. This enables full measurement of the surface 
depth.  Collected set of data are then converted into the 3D true color image information 
[48]. 
 
This measurement method has its cons. Translucent materials are difficult to measure 
using focus variation instrument. In addition, focus variation instruments do not capture 
very small topography variations because the array detector requires contrast in order to 
conduct the measurement. The last limitation is a rather long measurement time, espe-
cially when high magnification objectives and high vertical resolutions are applied.  
 
One of such a focus variation instruments is utilized in this research. The name of the 
specific instrument is Alicona Infinite Focus and it will shortly be described later in the 
experimental part of the thesis.

2.3.2 Confocal Laser Scanning Microscope (CLSM) 
Another type of optical instruments that can be used in surface measurements is a con-
focal microscope, and there are three main types of these microscopes, that are listed 
below: 
• laser scanning microscopes,  
• disc scanning microscopes 
• programmable array scanning microscopes [27].  
 
In this subchapter, the Confocal Laser Scanning Microscope (CLSM) is described as the 
only confocal microscope used in this research. 
This microscope uses a laser light and acquire point by point high resolution images 
which are reconstructed by an electronic system. These high resolution images are cre-
ated by filtering out-of-focus light in specimens that are thicker than the focal plane. 
Additionally, confocal microscopes can take in-focus images from any depths of the 
specimen surface and this is the key feature of these type of microscopes. 
 
In the following paragraph the functionality of the CLSM is described. In the normal 
optical microscope image, the veil is created outside of the focus plane. The CLSM re-
moves this veil. 
A laser goes through the lights source pinhole and reflects by a dichromatic mirror, fo-
cuses on the image plane of the objective. Then, the light reflected by the point which is 
placed in the focal plane of the specimen pass back through the dichromatic mirror and 
is focuses at the detector pinhole without any loss. The working principle of the CLSM 
is diagrammatically presented in Figure 2.12. 
 
 
Figure 2.12: Architecture of the CLSM [49]

On the other hand, the light reflected by the points that are placed outside the focal 
planes is dimmed or completely blocked by the detector pinhole. This means that the 
out-of-focus regions that are placed outside the focal planes do not affect the image 
quality of the points placed on the focal plane [27].  
This type of microscope enables high numerical aperture of the objective, which ensures 
high measurable maximum slope up to 75° [50]. 
The limitation of these microscopes is that they are very sensitive to the vibrations. To

Tuning the microscope settings 
To obtain high quality and realistic measurements using Alicona Infinite Focus a num-
ber of settings are set. These settings are exposure (brightness), contrast, light source, 
gain, and vertical and lateral resolutions. The first four above mentioned parameters are 
set depending on the sample material and color. So, the higher the roughness the higher 
should be the brightness set in order to lighten the valleys correctly. The last two pa-
rameters are the same for all the measurements and are equal to 20 nm and 1,5 µm for 
vertical and lateral resolutions respectively. Neither polarized light, nor the ring were 
used in the process of mold measuring. However, the polarized light was used in order 
to measure the roughness of the paper samples. The white color of the paper samples 
makes the surface more reflective to the light leading to spikes and void areas on the 
image acquired, thus the need to apply polarized light occurs.  
When utilizing the polarized light the results are worse compared to not using it. This is 
because an additional element in the form of polarization filter is added in the optics. 
This addition has a negative effect on the measurements and it may lead to higher 
roughness of the sample comparing to the sample measured without polarized light ap-
plication [51].  
 
3.1.2 Measurement results and analysis 
During the measurement of paper samples, a few challenges occurred. It was difficult to 
find the right settings, especially when measuring the roughness with 50x lens. The rea-
son might be the fact that when measuring paper samples with 50x lens or higher, the 
numerical aperture is increasing (see Table 3.2) . The higher the numerical aperture the 
more difficult it is to capture side walls. Therefore, it leads to unreadable zones, hence 
voids pixels appear on the acquired image after the measurements. 
Additionally, the samples that were made with the temperature of 210°C were measured 
but not included into the analysis. Because with high temperature the disk is stuck in the 
upper mold while unloading. Thus, the disk was unloaded manually. This led to the oc-
currence of fringes on the sample. These fringes were seen more clearly when measur-

3.2.2.3 Width 
A number of steps in the SPIP™ software need to be applied in order to find the width. 
• Open the SPIP™ software. 
• Open the image that is acquired by the microscope.  
• Use a function called global leveling in the "plane correction" tab in the ribbon.  
• Apply zero background function. 
• Apply and distribute horizontal cross section on the entire surface.  
• Use quadrangle fit function which builds periodic structure (square wave) on the 
cross section profile. 
 
The quadrangle fit function (square wave) is part of the SPIP™ calibration module 
which is based on ISO 5436-1 standard and is very important for evaluation of various 
nano or micro fabricated structures.

PROCESS OPTIMIZATION 
Quality of the end product is in large influenced by the manufacturing process of the 
product. To ensure high quality of the product, process parameters need to be adjusted 
in a way that ensures reduction of variability due to changes in the process parameters. 
In addition, deep understanding the full production process and parameters influencing 
the process can ensure reduction of the manufacturing time to the minimum.  
 
Design of Experiment (DOE) is a systematic method for analyzing these key effects that 
influence the manufacturing process. The description of the DOE is presented earlier in 
the thesis, but this is a quick reminder. And an experiment is a number of steps that are 
conducted and that differ in conditions that are predefined. Combining and analyzing 
these steps is what leads to identification of key conclusions and observations. These 
conclusions are based on the analyses of the input variables and output results.  
 
All the observations, conclusions and reflections in this work are based on various ex-
periments, understanding how and why these experiments were developed and carried 
out is of outmost importance. This is why a proper DOE development is not only vital, 
but can lead to such benefits as proper resource use and cost savings. These benefits 
may occur as a result of constructing the most appropriate sequence of the experiments, 
in addition to applying most appropriate software for data analyses [52].

the same processes and steps as the second set of experiments that is described in the 
detail in chapter 3.2. It is therefore only differences and new information and analyses

of experiments new process parameters are applied. These parameters are chosen be-

tion to reduction in process time. This assumption is investigated in this set of experi-

5 
CONCLUSION 
The key goal of this research was to analyze and optimize the paper molding manufac-
turing process based on paper product quality control. Three different sets of experi-
ments were conducted at the industrial partner ecoXpac A/S. The main process parame-
ters were the temperature, pressure, holding and vacuum times and influence of these 
parameters on the quality of the final product was investigated by empirical and analyti-
cal methods. These methods include the study of the geometrical replicability of the 
mold features on the paper samples. This was done through optical measurements and 
comparison of roughness, step height and width using two different optical instruments, 
Alicona Infinite Focus and Olympus LEXT.  
 
• In the first set of experiments, roughness and step height were measured using 
Alicona Infinite Focus microscope. The results were analyzed by calculating the 
average values, the standard deviation and the coefficient of variation. Then the 
relative deviation from the average values measured on the mold to the average 
values measured on the paper samples have been calculated, in order to find the 
process parameters at which the features on the paper samples have best 
replicability. It can be concluded that the manufacturing process was highly de-
pendent on the temperature. Paper samples made with the temperature of 160°C 
have lower surface roughness, hence better quality, compared to those made 
with the temperature of 110°C. Alterations of pressure seem to have minimal 
impact on replicability. In contrast, comparison of the step height of the mold 
and paper samples showed that the average values of the paper samples have rel-
atively close results to those measured on the mold, this indicates that the 
replicability was quite good for all sets of parameters. The best replicability was 
achieved in samples made with temperatures of 110°C for step height h(x; 0) and 
with the temperature on 160°C for step height h(-x; 0). Based on these observa-
tions the second set of experiment was conducted.  
 
• In the second set of experiments, the roughness, step height and width were 
measured using Olympus LEXT microscope. This microscope showed better re-
sults compared to Alicona Infinite Focus, when working with paper samples. 
Moreover, its fast settings adjustment plays important role when big amount of 
samples need to be measured. In addition above mentioned, a DOE plan was car-
ried out, to observe the influence of each process parameter on the replicability. 
==PAGE:90==
 
Conclusion 
82 
 
In this set of experiments new mold, that contain different measurement areas on 
its surface (e.g. for roughness, step height and width) and new set of parameters 
based on the observations from the first set of experiments were applied. These 
parameters were the temperature of 130°C and 170°C, holding time of 5 and 15 
seconds and vacuum time of 5 and 15 seconds. The results from the second set 
of experiments, based on the statistical analysis and observations, showed that 
the most optimal manufacturing parameters to ensure replicability are the tem-
perature of 130°C, holding time of 5 seconds and vacuum time of 15 seconds. 
However, the results indicate that these parameters can be further optimized.  
 
• Based on the conclusions and observations of the analyses conducted throughout 
the second set of experiments, a new set of experiments is proposed for further 
investigation and optimization of paper molding processes. In order to optimize 
the process the new set of parameters were applied which were the temperature 
of 110°C and 150°C, holding and vacuum times of 2 and 8 seconds each. More-
over, it was proposed to coat the mold with Teflon. This was done in order to 
see, if the Teflon coating would improve the replicability, hence the quality of 
the final product. Process optimization used to find optimal process parameters, 
showed that different temperatures, holding and vacuum times had an influence 
on the replicability. Moreover, it can be concluded that Teflon coating increased 
the values of the step height and width measured on the paper samples. In addi-
tion, the roughness average values also increased, as the roughness of the mold 
increased as well following Teflon coating. However, the difference of the 
roughness average values between the mold and paper samples decreased, com-
pared to the roughness difference measured in the second set of experiments. 
This showed that Teflon coating helped improve the replicability, hence quality 
of the end product. Based on the experimental evidence and statistical analyses 
the most optimal parameters for the new set of experiments were the temperature 
of 110°C, the holding time of 8 seconds and  the vacuum time of 8 seconds. 
Even though some improvements have been achieved in the new set of experi-
ments, these results are still not optimal and further work and investigation need 
to be done. 
 
• Lastly, in order to find a model that can describe and predict the behavior of the 
surface parameters by using different process parameters, the comparison of the 
statistical analyses from the second and the new sets of experiments was done.  
It can be concluded that these analyses are not reliable since the visual observa-
tion and comparison of the main effect plots did not show any trends. When 
comparing main effect plots for the same features in different sets of experi-
ments it is impossible to see any trend and hence to find a prediction model, 
based only on the comparison of the main effect plots. Therefore, only empirical 
observations of the data was taken into consideration in order to find the most 
optimal parameters for the second and the new sets of experiments. However,

more robust. 
 
5.1 Future work 
Based on this experimental work it is suggested that further investigation should be 
done to find possible correlations between the different surface parameters. This should 
be done in order to make a reliable model in which the changing of the process parame-
ters will lead to predictable output results. To achieve this, further experiments can be 
suggested in which the number of measured features is increased. Moreover, in this re-
search only one type of pulp material (Birch pulp, see Figure 2.7) was applied, which 
made difficult to rely on the results. Therefore, new types of pulp material can be ap-
plied and the results of using different types of pulp material can be compared.
