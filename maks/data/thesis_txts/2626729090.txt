Abstract  
This project investigates wave-run-up on dikes and cylinders by numerical simulations. The 
aim is to make a reliable OpenFOAM model and understand the kinematics in wave run-up. A 
mesh conversion test is made both for the run-up on dikes and on a cylinder. The dike 
conversion test also included a Courant number. The test shows the importance of a fine 
mesh and of the number of cells. A compromise has to be made to have an acceptable 
computational time. It was found that for dikes, which is a 2D case, the results match 
experiments very well, whereas it was harder to match the run-up on a cylinder. Overall, the 
mesh needed to be rather fine to get accurate results, which was a limitation in 3D cases. The 
dike experiment concluded that it was possible to produce precise results with OpenFOAM 
compared to physical experiments. It was furthermore seen that the slope decreases the run-
up height and the time. The result from wave run-up on a cylinder showed an overall 
agreement with experiments. The error varied depending on the wave steepness. For the 
steepest wave, the simulation did not achieve a useful comparison. Kinematics in the wave 
run-up is mainly investigated by looking at the velocity fields. For the cylinder in irregular 
waves, Bernoulli's principle was utilized. The main conclusion for irregular waves was that the 
standard equations overestimate the vertical velocity. The simulation's vertical velocities 
were much smaller in the run-up and was almost constant compared to the calculated 
velocities. The velocities were overestimated in the simulation compared with Dean's stream 
==PAGE:7==
v 
 
function theory for the incoming wave. Bernoulli's principle showed the importance of the 
pressure distribution in the run-up. It also showed that the streamlines were parallel to the 
surface. Therefore, the forces of secondary structures can be reduced, with an improved 
expression for the vertical velocities in the wave run-up. The expression is not derived as part 
of this research. Nonetheless, the research suggests the coupling of the vertical velocities to 
the incoming wave's horizontal velocities, instead of the run-up height, as is done in current 
practice.

1. Introduction  
Wave run-up is a phenomenon that occurs when waves interact with marine or coastal 
structures. Wave run-up (R) is defined as the distance from the mean water level to the water 
column’s maximum elevation. This thesis only investigated non-breaking waves, and no mix 
of water and air is observed. Wave run-up plays a significant role in both design of coastal 
protection and offshore structures such as wind turbines. Wave run-up was greatly 
underestimated at the Danish windfarm Horns Reef 1, which destroyed many platforms and 
boat landings. This case sparked a series of experiments and design guides for calculating 
wave run-up. The guidelines for calculating wave run-up are made primarily from the 
experiment by De Vos et al. (2007). 
 
Figure 1: Wave run-up at Horns Reef 1 (Lykke Andersen et al., 2010). 
 
These experiments, which lie as background for the design guidelines, are appropriate. 
Nevertheless, numerical simulations give other possibilities, such as looking at the velocity 
and pressure and freely changing the physical setup. For example, the velocity in the run-up 
is given as a very simplistic equation, presumably because it is difficult to measure.  
This study has two interlinked research objectives: the first is to verify that OpenFOAM can 
simulate accurate wave run-up. The second is to broaden the understanding of the kinematics 
of wave run-up. The objectives are pursued by examining the wave kinematics and the 
velocity and pressure in the run-up. Firstly, wave run-up on a dike with different slopes will 
be calculated. Wave run-up on a dike is a common study, and therefore a good base case 
since it can be simplified to a 2D case. Secondly, wave run-up on a cylinder is calculated, both 
for regular and irregular waves. Regular waves are to be tested against Kriebel’s experiments 
(David L. Kriebel, 1992) to verify the model. It has been chosen to focus on four experiments 
==PAGE:14==
2 
 
from Kriebel. Two additional tests have been carried out with regular waves, one with varying 
diameters and varying wavelengths. Lastly, irregular waves have been investigated. Two 
irregular tests have been made. The first was an analysis of one irregular wave. This wave was 
part of a short time series, which were simulated on a fine mesh. Then a statistical analysis of 
irregular waves was conducted on a less fine mesh. The irregular test both looks at the run-
up but also at the velocities of the run-up. The run-up height should match the experiments. 
It is challenging to attain new knowledge concerning run-up heights when using numerical 
tools because the model is validated against experiments. Still, the recommended way of 
calculating the velocities in run-up is very simplistic and based on the concept of potential 
energy transforming kinetic energy. It is, therefore, of great interest to see if this assumption 
is valid. The velocities are used to calculate the forces on secondary structures.  
1.1 Motivation 
Wave run-up is a crucial design parameter when designing secondary structures on wind 
turbines as it governs the platform’s height. Furthermore, the forces can be significant. On a 
specific project in the North Sea, the wave run-up force at 20 m above sea level was 279 kPa. 
The maximum crest height was 11 m, and the total run-up height was 38 m above the mean 
water level. These forces are calculated with the standard guidelines provided (Lykke 
Andersen et al., 2010). With these forces, it is necessary to build platforms at very high levels, 
and a very long boat landing is needed. Therefore, it is of interest to the offshore industry to 
better understand wave run-up and reduce the force of wave run-up if possible.

2. OpenFOAM 
OpenFOAM is an open-source CFD (computational fluid dynamic) program, and it stands for 
"Open-source Field Operation And Manipulation." OpenFOAM is programmed in C++ and 
runs in Linux. It has no GUI (graphical user interface) and is therefore controlled in the 
terminal. Three branches of OpenFOAM exist. In this paper, OpenFOAM by Ltd is used, which 
can be found on openfoam.com.  
The solver used in this project is Interform. Interfroam can solve the Navier-stokes equation 
for two isothermal (constant temperature), immiscible (fluids don't mix, but stay separated 
as two fluids), incompressible (no change in density) fluids using a VOF (volume of fluid) 
phase-fraction method. VOF method gives one fluid an α value of 1, in this case, water, and 
another fluid α =0. The free surface is located in between an α value of 1 and 0. It is chosen 
to specify this at the location of the free surface as α=0.5.

3. Wave run-up on a Dike  
 
 
 
 
 
3.1. Introduction  
Wave run-up on a dike is investigated to understand wave run-up mechanisms better and 
validate the OpenFOAM model. Wave run-up on a Dike can be simplified to a 2D problem, 
making the computational time smaller and easier to test out different ideas. It was chosen 
to look at wave run-up from solitary waves to simplify the case even further. When working 
with wave trains, an extended time series is needed before the solution stabilizes. With 
solitary waves, there is only one wave, so there is no interference with other waves. Three 
cases have been conducted, with different slopes of 15, 20, and 45 degrees. The result has 
been compared with experiments, numerical simulations, and empirical equations. There 
exist multiple theories to describe a solitary wave. Two approaches have been used in this 
project, as follows in the next section.

3.3 Courant number 
The Courant number (Co) is defined as: 
𝐶𝑜= 𝑑𝑡 𝑈𝑚𝑎𝑥
𝑑𝑥
 
 
Eq. 12 
Where dt is the length of the time-step 𝑈𝑚𝑎𝑥 is the maximum velocity in the simulation, and 
dx is the length of the smallest cell. The Courant number describes how far a particle travels 
through a cell in a one-time step. It is essential to have a Courant number below 1 to ensure 
stability. As shown in Larsen et al. (2019), it is sometimes necessary to have an even smaller 
Courant number to achieve good results. In OpenFOAM, the maximum Co number is usually 
specified instead of the time-step. Specifying the Co number ensures that the simulation is 
stable. This can lead to time-steps lower than needed because the highest velocities are often 
found in the air, which is mostly out of interest.  
3.4 Case Setup 
This section will clarify how the model was set up. The effect of Schemes, mesh size, Courant 
number, and boundary conditions were investigated.  
3.4.1 Boundary conditions  
The boundary conditions can be seen in Figure 4. The sides had the boundary condition 
empty, which prevents anything from happening in the normal direction of that boundary and 
are therefore a perfect 2-D boundary condition. Wall boundary condition is a no-slip 
condition, which means that the velocity at the wall is zero, and there is a zero gradient in the 
pressure. Since there is no turbulence in the model, the wall is 100% smooth. The inlet 
condition is a wave inlet condition created by wave2foam and used when making waves. 
When dealing with solitary waves, a starting point is specified for the solitary wave. The 
starting point was at least equal to the wavelength. This means that the solitary wave does 
not start at the inlet but instead starts at a specified location. Atmosphere is a typical 
wave2foam boundary condition that is self-stabilizing. It can handle air traveling in and out, 
and it will keep a uniform pressure.  
 
Figure 4: Sketch of the domain with boundary condition. 
3.4.2 Schemes  
This section will introduce the schemes used and how they work. Schemes specify how the 
different derivatives shall be calculated. The schemes influence the quality of the result, the 
simulation's stability and affect the computational time. Not all schemes work for all terms. A 
==PAGE:21==
9 
 
mix of higher and lower order schemes are therefore used. The schemes can be found in the 
file fvSchems, which is in the systems folder.  
Multiple Schemes were tested as an iterative process. The wave profile was then evaluated 
both in skewness and smoothness. The effects of different schemes were larger on coarse 
meshes. The finer the mesh and time-steps, the smaller difference was seen between the 
schemes concerning the result, but more considerable differences in computational time. No 
systematic convergence study was done on the Schemes because there were too many 
parameters to adjust. It was chosen to use well-tested schemes, which also gave the 
smoothest and most symmetric wave profile. A systematic convergence study was performed 
when the schemes were chosen, including mesh size and Courant number. The schemes used 
in the end are very close to the waveTank tutorial schemes by wave2foam. The only difference 
is the cellLimited fourth 1 scheme used for grad schemes. The cellLimited fourth 1 scheme 
was out commented in the tutorial file, and therefore an obvious choice. The cellLimited 
fourth 1 schemes produce a smoother wave profile than the Euler scheme, which was the 
standard option, but it increased the computational time.

schemes were tested, but it was slower, crashed more often, and the impact was minimal.

3.7 Locating the free surface  
As mentioned in the OpenFOAM section, the VOF method is used. The cells with water are 
given an α value of 1 and the air cells a value of 0. At the interface between water and air, the 
cells will have a value varying between 1 and 0. The surface is located somewhere in that 
range. There is a function called wave gauges in OpenFOAM, which determines the surface 
location. The problem is that they can only be specified to follow one of the principal axes. 
The wave gauges can, therefore, not follow the dike slope. One option was to put wave gauges 
in a very fine discretization up along the shore. This was not the most elegant solution and 
not very flexible whenever changing the setup. It was therefore chosen to find the surface in 
the post-processing. The surface was chosen to be placed at α =0.5. Three different methods 
were tested to locate the surface. Firstly, the surface has been interpolated between the 
closest values around 0.5, as seen in Figure 13. Secondly, the surface has been found by a 
linear fit between the cells with an α value between 0.15 and 0.85, as seen in Figure 14. Lastly, 
the cells on the end wall have been integrated, from the bottom to the top. This is practically 
done by multiplying the α value by the length of the cells. This produces the length of the dike 
with water on it. This value is then multiplied with the sinus to the angle to project into the 
vertical axis. The cells can be seen in

3.9 Summarization of solitary wave run-up on a dike.  
In this section, wave run-up on a dike from a solitary wave was measured. From the 
conversion test, can it be concluded that a Co=1 was too large with these schemes.

surface locating section, it can be concluded that the integration method was the best way to 
locating the surface. The result section compared the simulated wave run-up in OpenFOAM 
to an identical experiment by Han et al. (2015) and different numerical expressions. The result 
of wave run-up showed good agreement with the mentioned experiment by Han et a. (ibid.) 
The simulated run-up showed a much more clear tendency than the experiments. 
Furthermore, turbulence was not included in the model. This had some minor influence when 
H/h increased because the run-up length increased, and surface roughness therefore had an 
increasing effect. In conclusion, appropriate results were produced in OpenFOAM using a 
laminar turbulence model for solitary wave-run-up on a dike with a slope of 15-, 20-, and 45 
degrees. The velocity plot validates the laminar model’s choice because very little flow is 
observed in the boundary's perpendicular direction, and the velocity field in general looks 
smooth. For engineering purposes, the laminar model is therefore recommended.

4. Regular wave run-up on a Cylinder  
4.1 Introduction  
This section will investigate wave run-up on a cylinder in regular waves. This section's purpose 
is to create a good 3D setup, which produces results that resemble physical experiments. It is 
easier to investigate the impact of specific parameters in regular waves compared to irregular 
waves. The validation of the model was partly done in the previous section. However, since 
the case presented herein is 3D and the geometry differs, another conversion test and 
experimental comparison are carried out with some of Kriebel’s (1992) findings on regular 
waves.  
The model setup and convergence study will first be presented and compared to Kriebel’s 
experiments (1992). Lastly, it is seen how wave run-up changes depending on the diameter 
and the wave’s steepness. 
4.2 Setup 
4.2.1 Schemes and boundary conditions  
The Schemes used are the same as for the 2d case and can be seen in Table 1.  
In the boundary conditions, the sides are changed from empty to a wall with no-slip. The 
cylinder and end wall are also a wall with no-slip. There are implementer relaxation zones at 
the start and end of the domain. The relaxation zones ensure there is reflection from the end 
walls. The relaxation zones are shown later. 
The regular waves are generated with stokes fifth-order theory and generated with  
waves2foam (Jacobsen, 2017). It was chosen to use Stokes fifth-order to achieve accuracy. 
Kriebel uses stokes second order in his experiments.  
4.2.2 Meshing strategy  
The mesh is governing for the precision of the results, but also for the computational time. If 
a cell is halved in all three directions, it creates eight new cells, and the time-step needs to be 
halved to have the same Co number. Therefore, it is important not to make the mesh too fine 
to avoid long computational times. It was observed with the 2D case that it is essential to 
have orthogonal cells in the domain. Orthogonal cells cannot be accomplished with a round 
cylinder in the domain. By making multiple blocks around the cylinder, the mesh quality can 
be optimized.  
The mesh is built from the tutorial case “Flow around a cylinder,” which is a 2D case. The 
mesh is made into a 3D, with an x-axis along the domain’s length, the y-axis in height, and the 
z-axis in width. 
The mesh was set up parametric, as mentioned earlier. In this case, it implies that the length, 
height, width of the domain was controlled by the diameter (D), wavelength (L), wave height 
(H), and depth (h). The height of the domain was given as the h+Hs1.6 the width was 24 
Diameters, and the length was 8 wavelengths, calculated from first-order theory. This can be 
seen in Figure 22: Domain overview. The cells were scaled in x and z direction, so the finer 
==PAGE:39==

mesh was obtained around the cylinder. In the y-direction, the mesh was scaled such that it 
was finer in the top. In the transitions between different blocks, it was strived to have 
approximately the same cell size.

4.2.3.1 Convergence study overview  
Mesh convergence was done in multiple steps. The process is described in the steps below:  
1) Firstly, an appropriate mesh is chosen. The requirement is that it did not have too 
many cells but still produce appropriate results.  
2) Secondly, the size of the domain had to be determined. How many wavelengths did it 
have to be long, and how many diameters wide?  
3) Thirdly, the mesh was tested in refinement in the x, y, z direction independently.  
4) Fourthly, the best result from the x-direction from step 3, was tested again with a new 
refinement in the y-direction.  
 
==PAGE:41==
29 
 
Step 4 was included to verify that a finer mesh in the y-direction did not influence when the 
x-direction had changed. It was not chosen to recheck the z-direction since x and z are coupled

4.3 Comparison with Kriebel  
In this section, wave run-up on a cylinder from regular waves is of interest. A comparison with 
Kriebel (1992) is made of the wave run-up around the cylinder. It was chosen to include four 
cases for this study. Kriebel’s experiment was carried out in a wave basin, which ensured 
reflection or blockage from the side walls. A grid was drawn on the cylinder, with vertical lines 
every 15 degrees and horizontal lines every 5 cm. A high-speed camera measured the wave 
run-up. Kriebel used these experiments to test a first and second-order theory derived in an 
early paper (1990). This showed that the second-order theory did a much better job predicting 
wave run-up than the first-order theory. The second-order theory outperformed the first, but 
the second-order theory did a poor job for very steep waves. The steeper waves had the 
higher wave run-up.  
Kriebel produced Second-Order Stokes waves. Kriebel did 22 experiments. It was chosen to 
validate the model against four of these 22 experiments. The kH values of Kriebel’s 
experiments can be seen in Table 14. The wavenumber and wave height were both specified 
by OpenFOAM and measured in the simulation. Early in the process, when the measured and 
the specified values aligned, better results were achieved. The diameter was 0.362 m, the

4.4 Summarization of wave run-up on a cylinder 
In this section, wave run-up around a cylinder was investigated. First, a mesh convergence 
study was performed. With this study, a mesh and domain were selected. The mesh could 
have been more refined, but the number of cells had to be kept down, so the computational 
time did not become too long. Four cases were then compared with Kriebel's experiments 
(1992). The comparison showed that the simulation captured the run-up's overall picture, 
except for the steepest wave. The mean error was between 5.8 and 9.7 for the three smallest 
waves, which was in the expected range. The greatest wave had a mean error = 18%. Finally, 
the run-up was illustrated for the simulations and the experiments together. The simulation 
had a logical tendency when increasing the wave height, whereas the experiments showed a 
less clear trend for increasing wave height.

5. Irregular 
5.1 Introduction to Irregular waves 
In this section, irregular waves have been tested. Irregular waves are the most realistic waves 
conditions an offshore structure will experience and therefore have great importance. 
Irregular wave series take a long time to compute numerically for three reasons. Firstly, 
irregular waves consist of a summation of regular waves, depending on how many regular 
series included the computational time increases, as shown in the appendix of the wave2foam 
manual (Jacobsen, 2017). Secondly, the domain must be fine enough to handle small waves, 
high enough to handle the highest waves, and long enough to handle the longest waves. 
Lastly, it must run for a long time to produce enough waves before making statistics on it.  
The irregular wave study’s focus was to make a deterministic and statistical analysis. The 
deterministic analysis includes a detailed description concerning the wave of interest. The 
result consists of comparing the simulated run-up to what is specified in the guidelines by 
Lykke Andersen et al. (2010). Furthermore, is the maximum vertical velocity is compared to 
the recommended way for calculating the vertical velocities. The vertical velocity is essential 
since it is used to calculate the forces on the substructures. To better understand the forces 
in the run-up, Bernoulli’s principle is utilized in the run-up process.  
The statistical analysis focuses on the whole time series. It investigates how the run-up 
matches De Vos et al. (2007) experiments. Furthermore, it is of interest to see which wave 
generated the highest run-up.   
The experiment produced by De Vos et al.(2007) includes both regular and irregular waves. 
There are 25 irregular waves with varying wave conditions. De Vos et al. used two depths and 
two structures (a monopile and monopile with a cone foundation). Wave run-up was 
measured with wave gauges and with cameras to inspect the measured run-up. The 
experiment's result is the backbone of the guidelines by Lykke Andersen et al. (2010). They 
reanalyze the data generated by De Vos et al. and develop the current guidelines. The 
guidelines prescribe the run-up for the two percent highest wave Ru,2% Eq. 20, and the maxim 
wave run-up Ru,max. Eq. 21. It furthermore suggests how to calculate the vertical velocities.

5.2 Model setup 
Two meshes were used for irregular waves—one for the statistical approach and a finer for 
the deterministic approach. The reason for making two setups was that some instabilities 
were noticed after the long time series was generated. The time available for the current 
project did not permit another lengthy time series to be developed and analyzed. Therefore, 
it was chosen to make a shorter analysis and do the deterministic research on that time series. 
The setup for the statistical approach was an edited version of the regular wave setup. The 
mesh was modified to the same dimensions used in their experiments by De Vos et al. The 
domain was made higher since the height of the waves varies, and the simulation often 
crashed if the waves hit the top of the domain. The cylinder’s diameter was scaled, so a replica 
of experiments by De Vos et al. (ibid.) was made. The effect of D/L on regular and irregular 
waves might not be the same. Linear theory prescribes that diffraction is influential at 
D/L=0.2; the influence of diffraction might be more extensive for irregular waves. Therefore, 
it was chosen to have the same D/L ratio to dismiss different variables from the original 
experiment. The domain was made slightly shorter. It was seen in the regular waves that this 
influenced the waves, but it was needed to save computational time. Due to a shortage of 
time, a convergence study was not performed on the new mesh. Nonetheless, it is important 
to point out that instabilities were present in the new mesh, as will be elaborated later in this 
section.  
The same mesh for the regular waves was used for the deterministic approach since it was 
proven to be good.

5.4.2 Summarization of the deterministic analyses  
The deterministic analysis showed the properties of a specific wave. The wave was highly non-
linear, and a large difference in zero-up crossing and zero-down crossing values was observed. 
By using zero up-crossing values, the run-up was well predicted with Eq. 20. The run-up was 
compared to the regular waves, with the same kH value. The comparison showed that 
irregular wave had significantly higher run-up compared to the regular waves. Furthermore, 
the perception of the run-up changes depending on the normalization. Many different ways 
of normalizing the run-up are used, but R/eta provides the best intuitive understanding of the 
run-up.  
Then vertical velocities alongside the cylinder were investigated. It was seen that Eq. 22 
provided a wildly inaccurate description of the velocities. Therefore the Bernoulli’s principle 
was utilized. Here the streamline was seen to be parallel with the free surface. This further 
indicates that the velocities in the run-up and in the incoming wave are most likely linked 
together. Creating an equation where the run-up velocity is a function of the incoming wave 
velocity would probably make a better estimate of actual run-up instead of using the principle 
that potential energy is transformed into kinetic energy. It was furthermore seen that the 
potential energy was mostly transferred into pressure energy. The velocity alongside the 
cylinder was rater constant. And the maxim velocity alongside the cylinder was much lower 
than the recommended practice.

mean spectrum showed that the correct frequencies were in the simulation, but there was a 
lack of energy in the frequencies that held the most energy. The spectrum became much

known from FFT analysis. The results regarding wave run-up included the Rmax, R2%, and Rs.

incoming amplitude. The wave producing the highest run-up was, therefore, also the wave 
with the highest incoming wave-height. This wave was rather steep, which also has a positive 
influence on the run-up. This follows the findings from regular waves, which shows that the 
relative run-up increases with H/L.

6. Conclusion  
This thesis has investigated wave run-up on dikes and cylinders. The run-up on dikes showed 
that it was possible to generate very accurate run-up predictions for multiple angles. The 
convergence study confirmed that a Courant number 1 was too high. It was furthermore seen 
that a more refined mesh did not always produce better results. Three ways of locating the 
free surface were tested because wave gauges could not be used. Of the three methods 
integrating through the water column produced the most reliable result. Making a linear fit 
or interpolating between the closest values both had unreliable results. The simulation did 
not include turbulence, and precise results were still achieved. For engineering purposes, a 
laminar model, therefore, seems optimal. It reduced the complexity and uncertainties and 
provided slightly conservative results. For lower angles, turbulence has a more significant 
influence, and it is not recommended to use laminar models on milder slopes without testing 
the effect beforehand.  
After run-up on a dike, the study continued to include wave-run up on a cylinder from a 
regular wave. A new conversion study had to be made, considering the added complexity of 
the 3D case and the cylinder's geometry. This showed that the optimal domain had to be 
surprisingly large and rather fine. The run-up was different between the cases that were 24 
and 36 diameters wide and with the cases that were 6 and 12 wavelength long. The mesh also 
had to be rather fine, but compromises were taken to keep the number of cells down. Wave 
run-up on a cylinder from regular waves was compared with Kriebel's (1992) wave run-up 
experiment. Four cases were chosen for the comparison. The kH value for each case was kH= 
0.186,0.317 0.438 and 0.53. The comparison showed agreement on overall tendencies, but it 
did not capture the exact run-up in all locations around the cylinder. The mean error was 
below 10% for kH= 0.186,0.317, and 0.438, which was expected. The mean error from 
kH=0.53 was 18%; this simulation can therefore not be seen as a representative for the real 
run-up. The simulations were then plotted on the same figure, which showed a clear tendency 
in the run-up with increasing kH. By examing the simulations together, it is unlikely that they 
will follow the same path as the experiments, considering the more random pattern seen 
when the experiments were plotted together. The run-up generally decreases around the 
cylinder until approximately 135 degrees, where it increases again. The increase on the back 
is due to positive interference between the water going around the cylinder on each side. The 
location of the increase was difficult to capture precisely and often lead to a large difference 
between the simulation and the experiment in that location. It was seen from the 
convergence study that it would have been possible to optimize the result using a larger 
domain with more cells.  
Lastly, run-up from irregular waves were analyzed. A deterministic and statistical analysis was 
conducted. The deterministic analysis was performed on the mesh from regular waves. From 
the deterministic analysis, it was seen that the run-up calculation did a great job predicting 
the run-up when using the zero up-crossing values. Due to the wave's non-linearities, a 
significant difference between zero-down and zero up-crossing values was seen. The 
velocities in the incoming waves were higher than the Dean stream function theory. 
Afterward, the velocity around the cylinder was investigated. Here the velocities were 
==PAGE:68==
56 
 
significantly smaller and did not follow the theoretical pattern prescribed in the guidelines. 
The analysis was extended to include Bernoulli's principle, which showed the streamlines' 
were parallel to the surface. Bernoulli's principle also showed the importance of the pressure 
distribution in the run-up. Bernoulli's principle was also utilized alongside the cylinder. Even 
though it did not follow a streamline, the pressure term and the elevation term in Bernoulli's 
principle worked opposite. They illustrated how the velocity term was relatively constant in 
comparison and generally smaller. Therefore to predict correct velocity in the run-up, the 
pressure must be included, or the velocity has to be a function of something else than the 
run-up height. Another function was not proposed due to limited data for irregular waves. 
The statistical analysis problematized how difficult it is to generate irregular time series 
numerically. Instabilities arose when the wave interacted with the cylinder, which resulted in 
large uncertainties in the simulation. The instabilities were not present when using the set-
up from the regular time-series. The power spectrum did show that the specified frequencies 
were present in the simulation, but a lack of energy around the peak frequency was observed. 
The wave generating the highest relative run-up also had the highest wave height. It was 
therefore seen that generating irregular time-series numerical is difficult. 
In the thesis, it is seen that the largest waves produce higher relative run-up, which is one 
factor explaining why run-up in the ocean can reach tremendous heights. The possibilities of 
decreasing the run-up height with simulations seem limited. However, reducing the run-up 
velocity and thereby reducing the run-up force seems realistic considering the simplistic and 
conservative practice utilized today.  
The original contribution of this research is two-fold. From a research perspective, the 
contribution is that it shows how the run-up process velocities have an almost constant value 
in the run-up, which deviates greatly from current practice. This was further illustrated with 
Bernoulli's principle utilized in the run-up, which demonstrates the pressure energy's 
importance. From an applicative perspective, this research can significantly reduce the forces 
on secondary structures from wave run-up. The current forces calculated from the velocities 
are enormous and are often design governing for boat landing and working platforms. The 
amount of steel used in secondary structures could be significantly reduced if an alternative 
expression was developed for the run-up velocities. With the offshore industry's expansive 
growth in mind, this is a considerable amount of steel on a global basis.  
 
 
==PAGE:69==
57 
 
7. Future work and perspective  
The main finding in the thesis concerns the velocity in the run-up. Due to limited irregular 
time-series, an equation for the velocities in the run-up could not be established. Future work 
could aim to create such an expression. For this, a better irregular time-series is needed. The 
first step in creating a better irregular time series would be to increase the domain size. That 
was the main difference between the mesh used in the deterministic and in the statistical 
analysis. Coupling the velocity in the run-up to the incoming wave would be the next step. 
The coupling could be done to the velocities in the incoming wave. Nevertheless, it always 
has to be bound in Hs and Tp, since they are the only know parameters in irregular time series. 
Physical experiments would be needed to verify the findings. It could be interesting in later 
experiments to include the working platform to validate the forces, which are the final result. 
Collaborating with the industry and applying measurement equipment on offshore wind 
turbines could further verify the theories. Such an approach would generate necessary 
knowledge, which could be used to generate more realistic simulations.
