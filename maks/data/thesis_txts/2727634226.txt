Abstract
This Master’s Thesis attempts to evaluate the potential benefits of using 3D Navigation
techniques for autonomous robots and program a 3D path planning algorithm. The project
has been developed in collaboration with the DTI, taking part in the European CANOPIES
project.
Throughout the report, firstly the purpose of the CANOPIES project will be explained,
followed by their objective and connection with the Thesis. Later, the main 3D Navigation
techniques are described, giving an insight into their theoretical performance, benefits, and
the reason for choosing the best technique for the development of this project.
Finally, after illustrating the data processing steps, the most common path planners will
be detailed. Then, the developed path planning algorithm will be analyzed, showing its
mode of operation and performance. To conclude, the results of the project will be shown.

Introduction
Robotics is an engineering field that involves the development of machines controlled by
computers, that can help and assist humans, clone human actions, or improve certain tasks.
Current research focuses on the development of mobile robotics, a subfield of robotics that
allows robots to move around the environment, not being fixed to one physical location.
While certain robots require user input to operate, other robots (Autonomous Mobile
Robots - AMR) have the capacity of functioning autonomously. AMR relies on a wide
variety of sensors to navigate unknown environments, define paths, and operate without
the need for external guidance or human intervention. The project will dig a little bit into
the world of autonomous systems.
This Master’s Thesis has been carried out in collaboration with the DTI. The objective of
this project is to be involved in the CANOPIES project, with the purpose of improving
some current features and capabilities.
1.1
CANOPIES project
The aim of the CANOPIES project [1] (part of the European Horizon 2020 innovation
funding program [2]) is to build a collaborative paradigm for Human Workers and Multi-
Robot Teams in Precision Agriculture. The duration of the project will be 4 years (2021 -
2024), in which 10 partners (6 universities and 4 SMEs) are globally involved. In the next
Figure 1.1 the logo of the project is illustrated.
Figure 1.1: CANOPIES logo
The application will be developed in the vineyards’ scenario. As can be seen in Figure
1.2, the robots will have to work together with humans with the purpose of grasping and
collecting table grapes from the trees. The main focus of the project is based on improving
precision agriculture, establishing a Human-Robot and Multi-Robot collaboration, and
improving the harvesting and pruning in the Agri-food area.

The goal can be explained by the Box-Exchange-Mechanism (BEM) observed in Figure

Having said that, multi-robot coordination strategies will be implemented to synchronize

A

Concerning the logistic robot, two conveyors covering most of the top plate of the yellow

Regarding the farming robot, this will be in charge of collecting the grapes from the trees

1.1.2
Problem statement
Several ways to improve the multi-robot collaboration were discussed in the early stages
of the Thesis, but the one with the biggest capacity for improvement and necessity was
focused on enhancing the autonomous navigation of the robot’s base. As has been ex-
plained, the DTI is in charge of developing the navigation of the mobile base, so there
were some features with potential improvements to be made.
First of all, is important to mention that the current Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV)
of the CANOPIES is navigating well around the vineyard’s environment. The UGV is
able to map the environment, locate itself in the field and go to the target destinations
using path planning. It uses a 3D SLAM (Simultaneous Localization And Mapping) but a
2D basic navigation package, so all the data is squeezed and leads to a loss of information
about the surroundings.
In the vineyard’s scenario, the outdoor environment introduces numerous challenges that
are not encountered in an indoor setting.
Factors such as changing seasons, wind, or
varying incident light through the day can create complex conditions. Consequently, just
relying on a 2D environment may result in the loss of critical features, potentially leading
to errors in perception and poor performance, especially in the case of unexpected obstacles
on the path. Therefore, upgrading the whole architecture to a 3D setup will give additional
feature extraction of the environment.
Secondly, in the autonomous robots industry, 3D navigation is the near future. Many
big companies are driving the development of 3D technologies due to all the advantages
it carries regarding robustness and performance. Thus, the DTI is very concerned about
expanding in this direction, considering the development of 3D navigation for most of their

2.1
ROS
The Robot Operating System or ROS [3] is an open-source framework widely used in the
world of robotics. It provides a flexible and distributed platform for building and control-
ling autonomous systems, and if it is combined with Linux, facilitates the communication
between different components of the robot system.
One of the key features of ROS is the messaging system it has, which allows different
components of a robot system to communicate with each other.
The communication
is based on a publish-subscribe model, where nodes (individual software modules) can
publish information, such as sensor data or commands, to specific topics, and other nodes
can subscribe to those topics to receive the published data. On top of this, the ROS master
acts as a centralized coordination hub for communication, it serves as a central point for
managing and tracking the nodes, topics, and parameters within the ROS network. Thus,
all the nodes and topics are registered by the ROS Master, announcing its presence and
providing the necessary information to communicate in the environment. See Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Communication between ROS nodes
In ROS, there are two important communication mechanisms used for interaction between
different nodes: services and actions. Regarding services, these are synchronous, it is a
request-response communication between nodes in ROS. They are designed for one-to-one
==PAGE:18==
8

communication, where a client node sends a request to a server node, which then processes
the request and sends a response back to the client. Services are typically used for tasks
that require a direct interaction or immediate response, so time matters.
Concerning actions, these are asynchronous, based on a goal-driven communication mech-
anism.
They are designed for more complex and long-running tasks that may require
feedback, state updates, and cancellation capabilities. Actions involve two main compo-
nents: a client, and a server. The client node sends a goal request to the action server,
which is responsible for executing the goal and providing feedback, updating it on the
progress or status of the ongoing task. Actions can also be canceled by the client if needed
and the communication flow in actions is non-blocking, allowing the client to perform
other tasks while waiting for updates or completion of the action. See Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Actions in ROS
There are two major versions of the ROS framework, ROS, and ROS2. ROS Box Turtle
[4] was the first ROS distro or version released in 2010. Since that time, there have been
13 different ROS distros within ROS, the last one launched in 2020 called ROS Noetic
[5], which is the one used for the development of this Thesis. The ROS Noetic distro has
further advanced ROS capabilities compared to the previous ROS distros, emphasizing
better Python3 support, Windows capability, and improved packages. Secondly, ROS2
has brought several significant improvements and advancements to the framework, in
which the first distro was called Ardent Apalone [6] launched in 2017. Since then, the
Iron Irwini [7] it is been launched in 2023 as the last distro version, summing a total of 8
ROS2 distros until the date.
ROS2 has several improvements compared to ROS, such as a new communication system,
improved real-time capabilities, easier integration in robots, implementation of multi-
language support, and higher security features.
ROS2 will be the future of robotics,
implementing a very robust framework with many stronger features.
Although many
companies have started the transition to ROS2, there are still a lot of robots operating
with ROS, as has been the case in this Thesis. In this particular work, both the robots
in the simulation and real-life operate with ROS, so using this previous framework was
required. A full transition towards ROS2 in the industry will take some time.

2.2.1
RGB-D camera
RGB-D cameras, also known as depth cameras or 3D cameras, are sensors that combine
traditional RGB (Red, Green, Blue) color imaging with depth sensing capabilities. Un-
like regular RGB cameras that capture only color information, RGB-D cameras provide
additional depth information for each pixel in the captured image.
The camera consists of a set of sensors, including a depth sensor, an RGB sensor, and
an infrared sensor, which work together to measure the distance of objects in the scene
and produce a detailed depth map. Sometimes, the camera counts with an IMU to be
more precise, as it will be the case of the Thesis. The RGB camera captures the color
images and gives information about the texture and appearance of the objects in the
frame. Especially this RGB camera is key for object detection. Secondly, the active depth
sensor uses structured light technology to measure the distance between the camera and
the target objects. The sensor beams an infrared light pattern to the environment and
measures the deformation of these patterns to calculate the distance. Thus, the depth
sensor is useful for object tracking and 3D mapping. Finally, if the model counts with an
IMU incorporated, it will be in charge of measuring and registering the movements of the
camera in the world. Thanks to the gyroscope and the accelerometer, more information
about the camera’s movement is captured, in other words, useful data transferable to the
robot’s pose.
Figure 2.3: RGB-D components for an Intel RealSense camera [8]
Thus, a comparison between the depth and infrared stream can be observed in the next
Figure 2.4. In this particular case, the data is captured from a UAV. The closest points
appear in blue, while the further ones are represented in red and black. This pixel visual-
ization technique indicates the distance from the camera to the objects, making it easier
for the user to know how far the objects are.

2.2.2
LIDAR
A LIDAR is a remote sensing technology that uses laser light to measure distances and
create detailed 3D maps of the surrounding environment. The basic principle relies on
the time-of-flight operation: the LIDAR emits short pulses of laser light and measures the
time it takes for the light to travel to an object and return to the sensor. As the speed of
the light is known, the sensor can calculate the distance to the object, based on the time
it took for the light to go back and forth.
The core components of a LIDAR system include a laser source, scanner, receiver, and tim-
ing/positioning system. The laser source emits laser beams, typically in the near-infrared
spectrum, which are directed to the surroundings. The scanner controls the direction and
orientation of the laser beams, allowing them to cover a wide field of view. The receiver
captures the reflected laser light, and the timing/positioning system synchronizes the laser
==PAGE:21==
CHAPTER 2.
PROJECT DESCRIPTION
11
emission and the reception of the reflected signals. See Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.6: LIDAR’s components [10]
When the laser beams hit an object in the environment, a portion of the light gets scattered
or reflected back towards the LIDAR sensor. The receiver detects these reflected signals,
which are known as echoes. As explained above, by measuring the time it takes for the
echoes to return, the LIDAR system can calculate the distances to the objects in the scene.
By scanning the laser beams across the environment, the LIDAR generates a dense set
of distance measurements, creating a point cloud representation of the surroundings. In
Figure 2.7 an example of the data obtained from a LIDAR is appreciated.
Figure 2.7: Data obtained from a LIDAR [11]
For the Thesis, the LIDAR model to use will be the Ouster SO1-64 (see Figure 2.8). This
is a mid-range LIDAR sensor with a vertical resolution of 64 channels and horizontal res-
olution equal to 512, 1024, or 2048, allowing to elaborate 1,310,720 points per second. It
guarantees an operating range of 120 m and a vertical field of view of 45°, with preci-
sion ±0.7 – 5 cm. The Ouster SO1-64 provides a ROS driver which facilitates software
integration.

3
3D Navigation Representation
Techniques
As the field of autonomous robotics continues to advance, the ability to navigate through
complex environments is becoming increasingly important. One key aspect of this is the
use of 3D navigation techniques, which allow robots to move through and interact with
their surroundings in a more effective way. In this section, some of the best 3D navigation
approaches will be explored, as well as their potential benefits and challenges of using
them.
The development of 3D navigation in robotics can be traced back to the late 1970s and
early 1980s, when robots were first introduced in industrial settings [18]. At that time,
robots were mainly used for tasks such as welding and painting, which required a high
degree of precision and repeatability.
However, as robots became more advanced and
capable, researchers began to explore the possibility of using them for more complex
tasks, such as navigation in dynamic environments.
The early approaches to 3D navigation in robotics were based on traditional sensors such
as cameras and ultrasonic sensors. These sensors could provide information about the
robot’s environment, but they were limited in their ability to provide a detailed 3D map
of the surroundings. As a result, researchers began to experiment with 2D range scanners,
which could provide a more comprehensive view of the environment.
The first 2D range scanner was developed in the early 1980s, and it was quickly adopted
by researchers in the robotics community [19] [20]. This technology allowed robots to
navigate in complex environments, and it paved the way for the development of more
advanced 3D sensors such as LIDAR.
Today, 3D navigation is a critical component of many robotics applications, including
autonomous vehicles, drones, and industrial robots. It continues to evolve and improve,
with new sensors and algorithms being developed to enable robots to navigate in increas-
ingly complex and dynamic environments. Thus, the three main navigation representation
techniques will be explained: point clouds, elevation maps, and octomaps.
3.1
Point clouds
A point cloud is a set of 3D points in space that represents the geometry of the real world.
This technique is important in 3D navigation, since it provides a detailed and accurate
representation of the environment that can be used to guide the robots through complex
and dynamic spaces. The main advantage of this method is the high eﬀiciency, flexibility,
and quick coverage of a large unknown environment. See figure 3.1.
==PAGE:30==
20

Figure 3.1: Point cloud representation [21]
Early approaches to this method involved capturing consecutive sets of laser scans from
discrete viewpoints and merging them into a single frame [22]. This process aimed to
create a more comprehensive representation of the environment by combining multiple
perspectives. However, these initial methods often faced challenges in achieving accurate
alignment and registration of the individual scans, obtaining inconsistencies within the
merged point cloud.
To address these limitations, subsequent advancements focused on improving the accuracy
of the point cloud reconstruction through the probabilistic fitting of the sampled data
within the frame. By incorporating probabilistic modeling techniques, such as iterative
closest point (ICP) algorithms or Gaussian mixture models, researchers aimed to improve
the alignment and reduce the effects of noise, outliers, and occlusions. This probabilistic
approach enabled a more robust merging of individual scans, leading to enhanced accuracy
and overall quality of the resulting point cloud representation [23].
While early efforts primarily relied on dead-reckoning for estimating the sensor’s position
and orientation between consecutive scans, researchers soon realized that this approach
alone was inadequate for generating continuous 3D point clouds [24]. Dead-reckoning, just
based on motion sensors like accelerometers and gyroscopes, suffers from accumulating
errors and drift over time, which can significantly affect the accuracy of the resulting
point cloud.
However, this drawback can be effectively overcome by integrating data
from additional sensors, such as GPS, IMUs, and visual odometry, through sensor fusion
techniques.
Sensor fusion combines information from multiple sensors to improve the accuracy, ro-
bustness, and consistency of the generated point cloud. By integrating data from diverse
sensors, such as cameras and inertial sensors, researchers can leverage the strengths of each
modality to compensate for individual sensor limitations and provide more reliable and
precise navigation and mapping capabilities. Sensor fusion algorithms, such as Kalman
filters, facilitate the fusion of sensor measurements and enable the generation of continu-
ous, high-fidelity 3D point clouds, even in challenging environments with dynamic changes
and sensor uncertainties.

3.2
Elevation maps
3.2.1
Previous researches
The 3D elevation maps navigation technique involved the utilization of height information
in outdoor or non-flat environments. This technique employs a 2D discrete grid, where
each cell stores the corresponding surface height, enabling eﬀicient representation of the
terrain and surroundings. See figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Elevation map example
To enhance the capabilities of elevation maps, researchers have explored various combina-
tions with stereo vision techniques for object detection and tracking [25]. By integrating
stereo vision with elevation maps, it becomes possible to extract additional depth infor-
mation and accurately perceive the three-dimensional structure of the environment. This
fusion of height-based representation with stereo vision contributes to improved perception
and understanding of the surroundings, enabling better obstacle detection and tracking
during navigation.
In order to mitigate errors or noise caused by robot motion, some researchers have in-
corporated tilted laser scanners into the elevation maps navigation technique [26]. The
additional tilt-enabled laser scanner serves as a filtering mechanism, allowing for the reduc-
tion of unwanted distortions caused by robot movement. By utilizing the complementary
capabilities of elevation maps and tilted laser scanners, the accuracy and reliability of the
navigational system can be improved, particularly in dynamic environments.
The construction of elevation maps can be achieved through various means, including
stereo images [27] and 3D stereo vision techniques [28]. By using stereo imaging or 3D
stereo vision, researchers can reconstruct the terrain’s height information, enabling the
creation of detailed and accurate elevation maps. These techniques utilize the principles
of triangulation and disparity estimation to derive the height information from stereo-
image pairs or multiple viewpoints, respectively. The resulting elevation maps provide a

3.2.2
Gridmap package description
Before starting with the explanation of how the elevation map library works, some concepts
about grid maps are important to explain. A grid map is a probabilistic representation
that indicates the occupancy of a given area in an environment, indicating the state of
occupancy (occupied, free, or unknown). It divides the environment into a grid, where
each cell represents a small area or volume. In the next Figure 3.3 an example of a 3D
occupancy grid can be observed:
Figure 3.3: 3D occupancy grids [29]
The ETH Zurich University has created an open-source library based on grid maps for
general representation and mapping of the environment in the world of robotics, providing
eﬀicient ways to store the data structures of the occupancy grid-based maps [30]. This
library’s source code can be found on GitHub [31] and it has been programmed in C++.
Thus, in the following chapter, the general overview of how the library works is going to
be explained, but more details can be found in the research paper.
This ROS-compatible library is developed for online surface mapping and rough terrain
evaluation. Data storage is based on the Eigen linear algebra library to be eﬀicient and
flexible with many mathematical algorithms. Elevation maps are one of the most popular
3D navigation techniques, where each cell of the 2D horizontal plane is associated with a
height value. With the purpose of simplifying the process, this 2.5D representation of the
environment will be saved and stored as grid maps, so that each cell in the grid holds a
height value.
A popular ROS package that also works with grid maps is the costmap_2d package, used
for 2D navigation. Here the data is stored in unsigned char arrays, with integers between
0-255. Thus, as this Gridmap library is used for more complex processing applications,
the map data is stored in matrices of type float. This enables more precision and flexi-
bility when working with heights, variances, and normal vectors. Nevertheless, to ensure
compatibility with ROS, all the data can be transformed to Occupancy grids (same as the
costmap_2d package), Gridcells and Pointcloud2 type messages.

3.2.3
Robot-centric elevation mapping package description
The robot-centric elevation map library is designed to create a terrain map that incorpo-
rates uncertain localization. It considers parameters such as drift, uncertainties in state
estimation, and sensor noise, indicating a lack of complete trust in the robot’s localization.
This research conducted by ETH Zurich introduces the concept of a robot-centric formu-
lation, where the surface map is associated with the robot’s current pose. While more
detailed information about the procedure can be found in the paper [32], a basic summary
of the approach will be provided here. The same as with the Gridmap library, all the
source code can be found on GitHub [33].
The core idea of the library relies on the following statement: during the time the robot
is observing the environment, newer measurements have higher accuracy, while the older
ones accumulate uncertainty that might lead to errors. As can be observed in Figure 3.7,
the robot is going upwards, so more uncertainties can be found in the right part of the
image, observing wider boundaries and lower predictability.

3.3
Octomaps
3.3.1
Definition
Octomaps are a highly effective 3D navigation mapping technique based on octrees, a
type of tree data structure where each node represents a cube-shaped region in 3D space,
known as a voxel. Octrees offer a flexible representation by dynamically adjusting voxel
size based on the complexity of the environment. This adaptive approach allows for a
detailed representation of interesting features while using larger voxels to represent less
relevant parts of the environment, optimizing memory consumption (see Figure 3.11).
Figure 3.11: Octree representation
In the past, occupancy grids were widely used for navigation mapping.
This method
employed a multidimensional random field to estimate the occupancy of spatial cells.
However, occupancy grids suffer from certain disadvantages. Firstly, they require memory
allocation for every cell in the grid, leading to significant memory overhead. Secondly,
they impose a fixed geometric grid decomposition, which may not be suitable for all
environments. To overcome these limitations, octrees were introduced for 3D mapping,
along with quadtrees for 2D mapping.
Octrees offer several key advantages over occupancy grids. Firstly, they allow for object
representation down to the smallest cube (as mentioned earlier), resulting in eﬀicient mem-
ory consumption. Secondly, manipulation and analysis algorithms for objects in octrees
are simplified due to the hierarchical structure. Thirdly, objects within octrees are in-
herently pre-sorted, enabling eﬀicient search and traversal operations. Fourthly, Boolean
operations such as union, intersection, subtraction, translation, scaling, and rotation can
be easily performed on octrees, as the algorithms traverse input trees to generate output
trees. Additionally, octrees can operate at the required level of resolution, making them
versatile for various applications.
The computational eﬀiciency of octrees is another advantage, as they offer eﬀicient imple-
mentations of geometric operations. Union, intersection, subtraction, translation, scaling,
and rotation operations are not only well-defined but also computationally eﬀicient. This
allows for fast and accurate processing of spatial operations on octrees.

In summary, octomaps, based on the octree data structure, provide a powerful and
memory-eﬀicient approach for 3D navigation mapping. The adaptive voxel representation,
along with the advantages of hierarchical organization and eﬀicient geometric operations,
make octrees an effective choice for various robotic navigation tasks.

Most of the work for building this Octomap library is due to the work of Harmin Hornung,

Path Planning
Path planning plays a crucial role in robotics, determining the most eﬀicient route be-
tween a given start and goal positions. This capability is vital for autonomous robots as it
enables them to navigate their surroundings independently. The ability to adapt to unex-
pected obstacles and dynamically changing environments improves the robot’s autonomy.
Therefore, in this chapter, an explanation of several common path planning algorithms is
going to be analyzed, as well as the practical implementation for this Thesis.
6.1
Path Planning fundamentals
These algorithms come with a range of techniques and approaches, each with its own
strengths and limitations.
They aim to compute the shortest path while considering
factors such as obstacles or dynamic environments. However, this process can be handled
in many different ways, each one with its strengths or drawbacks. Now, a brief explanation
of some of the most common path planning techniques will be described: A∗, Dijkstra,
and RRT algorithm.
6.1.1
A∗algorithm
The A∗algorithm is a widely used and highly eﬀicient path planning in the field of robotics,
first used in 1968 [40]. This algorithm will be developed and implemented in the Thesis.
The singularity of the A∗is that the core idea relies on three mathematical functions:
The heuristic function h(n) provides an informed estimate of the cost required to reach
the goal from a given node n. It helps guide the A∗algorithm by prioritizing nodes that
appear more promising based on their estimated costs.
h(n) = Estimated cost from node n to the goal
(6.1)
In the case of the Thesis, this heuristic function will be the Euclidean distance from the
goal node to the current node n.
h(n) =
√
(xgoal −xn)2 + (ygoal −yn)2 + (zgoal −zn)2
(6.2)
The cost function g(n) calculates the cumulative cost of the path from the start node to a
particular node. It accumulates the cost as the algorithm explores the graph, considering
factors such as distance, terrain diﬀiculty, or any other relevant metric. Thus, in this
particular case, the cost function is the distance traveled from the starting node to the
current node:
g(n) = Cost of the path from the start node to node n
(6.3)
The evaluation function f(n) combines the cost function g(n) and the heuristic function
h(n) to determine the overall evaluation score of a node. The A∗algorithm uses this score
==PAGE:50==
40
CHAPTER 6.

to prioritize nodes for exploration, with lower evaluation scores indicating more favorable
paths. The path option with the lower f(n) will be preferred.
f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
(6.4)
Having said that, the start node will be initialized and the closest nodes will be found.
Thus, between those nodes, the f(n) function will be calculated for every one of them, mov-
ing towards the one with a lower evaluation function. This iterative process will continue
until the goal position is found. More information about the practical implementation will
be explained in the following section.
6.1.2
Dijkstra algorithm
The Dijkstra algorithm is the path planning algorithm partly used in the ROS Navigation
Stack [41] for selecting the shortest path from the start to the goal. This approach is very
similar to the A∗, but they have a big difference: the Dijkstra just focuses on minimizing
the cumulative cost, ensuring that all the nodes have the lower cumulative cost possible,
while the A∗has into account both the cumulative cost and the remaining cost to the goal.
The algorithm starts by selecting the start node and exploring the distances to the closest
nodes, calculating the cost to reach each of them. Once the closest nodes are visited and
evaluated, the algorithm will determine which node is closest to the current node and
go toward that direction. Later, from the actual node, all the costs of the surrounding
nodes will be evaluated again: if the found node is new, the current cost function will
be initialized. However, if one of the nodes is re-visited again, it will check if the current
cost is lower than before, and if that is true, the new cost will be updated. This iterative
process will continue until the goal is found, ensuring that for every node the optimal path
is established.
The biggest drawback of this algorithm is the high memory consumption it requires for long
paths. In this scenario, there are many intermediate nodes that must be updated every
iteration, so this can cause computational overhead. Thus, the ROS Navigation stack
does not only the Dijkstra algorithm, it also combines their algorithm with a heuristic
calculation based on the A∗. Their implementation of the algorithm will be explained
later in the report.
6.1.3
RRT algorithm
This path planning technique is based on the expansion and building of random tree
structures in the environment [42], which is applied in the quadruped robot from ETH
Zurich shown in the ”3D Navigation Techniques” chapter. After initializing the first node,
the algorithm will expand by randomly sampling and connecting new configurations to the
existing tree. The algorithm continues until a goal configuration is reached or a termination
condition is met. It eﬀiciently explores high-dimensional spaces and finds feasible paths
with very little computational power.

quality and eﬀicient paths.

6.3.4
Closest neighbors
This function is probably the most important one inside the algorithm, all the logic of the
A∗is developed inside here. The closest neighbors function will start initializing 2 sets:
the open set and the closed set. The first one refers to those nodes that are candidates
for further exploration, in other words, they have been discovered but not fully evaluated
yet. This means that the nodes in this open set are implemented as a priority queue,
where nodes are sorted based on their evaluation scores f(n) = g(n) + h(n). The node
with the lower f(n) is selected for expansion first. On the other hand, the closed set is a
set of nodes that have already been evaluated and visited. Once a node is expanded, it is
added to the closed set to ensure that is not revisited again.
Having said that, is important to mention that all the g(n) and f(n) cost functions are
initialized to infinite, making the algorithm’s logic easier. Thus, the first step is finding
the starting node, adding it to the closed set (represented in black in the next Figure

7.4
Strengths and limitations of the algorithm
After observing how the algorithm has been developed and its performance in simulation
and real scenarios, some reflections about the whole system will be detailed.
7.4.1
Strengths
Using the self-programmed A∗path planning algorithm offers several potential benefits
over other alternatives. Firstly, its ease of implementation, clarity, and comprehensive
understanding of the algorithm contribute to its flexibility and adaptability in various
scenarios. By simply adjusting two main constants (the neighbor’s range distance and the
point cloud density), the algorithm can rapidly adapt to different environments, making
it highly versatile. On top of this, the path smoother can be added, so even better results
can be achieved.
Secondly, all the code is divided into two clean Python scripts, being very accessible to
everyone that wants to use it.
Other path planning algorithms have a more complex
architecture, making use of external libraries in which their content can not be easily
accessed. In these scripts, all the lines are made as simple as possible, in order to be
understandable for everyone.
Moreover, the algorithm’s real-time functionality allows it to respond to new or unexpected
obstacles in the environment. This capability enables dynamic obstacle avoidance, ensur-
ing the algorithm remains effective and eﬀicient even in dynamic or changing scenarios.
The ability to detect and navigate around obstacles in real time enhances the algorithm’s
reliability and robustness.
Finally, the algorithm not only shows to trace trajectories on flat surfaces, but also is able
==PAGE:82==
72
CHAPTER 7.
RESULTS
to find the path through small hills. In the vineyard scenario, there might be some not-flat
environments around, but it is proven that this algorithm will not have any issues going
over them.
7.4.2
Limitations
As has been shown in the Thesis, this algorithm still needs some development and there
are many tasks to be improved. First of all, the most important one is that after a collision
is found in the path, the algorithm warns the user, and the robot is stopped. However, it
does not provide any alternative route, so it will stay there until a new non-collision path
is provided. At least, the robot would remain safe and avoid any collision, but for future
implementations, this feature needs to be improved and provide a route available for that
scenario.
Secondly, the action of moving the base in mountainous scenarios is not robust. If the
robot gets stuck somewhere, the link between the Gazebo and Rviz will be affected, leading
to errors in the action. In flat surfaces, as there are no collisions with the environment,
the base moves and executes the routes, but one collision is enough for the system to start
failing.

Conclusions and Future Work
8.1
Conclusions
The MSc Thesis has concluded many important aspects concerning the initial objectives
established. This project involved engagement with a European real project, allowing for
practical application and research within the field. This aspect gives a better understand-
ing of a real robotic application, in which top companies and universities work together.
Having real data to try the algorithms makes the project more reliable and allows the
reader to observe what kind of problems are tackled in the nowadays industry.
Secondly, one of the most important objectives was to evaluate whether 3D navigation
techniques would make an impact in the future and if they were feasible to implement.
As it has been demonstrated, all these 3D navigation capabilities show huge potential,
capturing a good level of detail in the surroundings. Traditional 2D navigation tends to
squeeze the data, so for certain complex environments, that loss of data might cause a worse
performance of the system. Especially elevation maps play a crucial role in 3D navigation,
providing detailed information about the vertical dimension of the environment.
The
terrains are well captured in a quick response time, allowing fast transitions in dynamic
scenarios. The future of robotics strongly relies on the advancement and adoption of 3D
navigation techniques to unlock new levels of autonomy, eﬀiciency, and adaptability in
diverse environments.
Furthermore, the ability to use ROS and process all the data has been successfully achieved.
As it has been shown, ROS is a powerful framework that enables interaction with many
features of the robot at the same time, so putting it all together and sticking to the nec-
essary data was a task required. Especially in the early stages of the project, connecting
many different libraries from other developers was demanded, in order to get the Husky
working with the Elevation maps and with the Octomaps. Later on, the competence for
managing all the data, such as filtering the existing information and combining it with
self-programmed algorithms was needed. At the end of the Thesis, a combination of li-
braries is been used for the development of the project, and without ROS it would not be
possible.
Another key objective was the capacity to build a path planning algorithm from scratch
that would navigate through the 3D navigation environment, establishing an ”innovation”
stage inside the Thesis. This task was achieved by building a 3D A∗planner solution that
indicates the trajectory from start to goal. Overall, it works well in both simulation and
real life, having all the code in two simple scripts accessible to everyone. However, the
time dedicated to this path planning algorithm was limited, leading to some characteristics
that still need to be improved.
All these aspects from the MSc Thesis will not be implemented in the CANOPIES project,
as the whole system needs to be finished in 2024 and some more work must be done in
the developed algorithm in order to build a reliable and robust architecture. However,
it has been demonstrated that the 3D navigation techniques are very powerful and could
improve the current 2D systems, so probably the theory and features explained in this
Thesis will be used and applied to other future projects at DTI.

8.2
Future Work
This project has been started from scratch, so the first stages of the MSc Thesis were
based on doing research about 3D navigation techniques and the different path planners.
Thus, the time for programming and implementing code was restricted, and many features
regarding the algorithm’s code must be enhanced. If the project would last longer, these
three aspects would be considered as the next improvements:
The first and most important task is to improve the collision avoidance situation. Right
now, when the robot finds a collision, it will stop and listen to new routes, instead of pro-
viding new alternatives given that same scenario. This feature makes the system ineﬀicient
and will prevent a good performance. Consequently, changing the different constants for
the variables automatically might make a difference, but this is an alternative that needs
to be tested.
Secondly, improving the system’s robustness when moving the base would be beneficial,
especially in hills and irregular setups. The success rate in flat surfaces is acceptable,
but not in mountainous scenarios, leading to errors and bad functioning of the system.
As a result, a possible option would be trying different actions for moving the base or
re-adjusting the robot’s control parameters, but the same as before, these last suggestions
need to be tested.
Lastly, once the system is working perfectly on simulation and these two aspects are
improved, the final step would be trying everything on a real robot.
The sensors are
proven to work fine in the real-life setup, so in that regard, the system should not behave
much differently from the report’s scenarios. Furthermore, the ROS algorithms applied in
the simulation environment would be the same as in the real-life case, so new commands
will not appear. However, nothing can be taken for granted until is not proven.
With these advancements and testing, the entire architecture will gain robustness, enabling
its deployment in various robotic platforms. The future of robotic systems holds great
potential for 3D path planning algorithms in the field of 3D navigation, so all the research
established in this area is more than welcome.
