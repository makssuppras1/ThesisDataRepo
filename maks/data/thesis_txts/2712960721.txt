Abstract
To achieve an effective shear connection of timber┬¡concrete composite (TCC) structures,
an interlayer connector method must provide composite action while simultaneously keep
costs low. Hence, notched connections in combination with mechanical fasteners have
been recognized as a favorable connector method, both in respects to erection and struc┬¡
tural performance. To further reduce costs, it is believed that a fully prefabricated TCC
system, a so┬¡called ÔÇ£dry┬¡dryÔÇØ solution, should be implemented. If achieved, this would
eliminate time┬¡consuming concrete curing at the building site and allow for disassembling
of the structure. The results of an experimental campaign of 30 notched TCC specimens
with varying parameters are therefore documented and analyzed in this study.
As the influence of parameters regarding the mechanical fasteners are poorly understood,
this was, in combination with the competitiveness of prefabricated concrete elements, an
important scope of this experimental campaign. In addition to the primary focus area of the
fastenersÔÇÖ prestressing degree, the presence, inclination, and position of the fasteners are
all parameters examined in this study. Finally, the influence of in┬¡situ cast concrete and the
notch length are found as well. The report presents and compares the specimensÔÇÖ load
carrying capacities, slip modulus, and failure modes, as well as tendencies concerning
displacement, rotation, and its fastenersÔÇÖ behavior.
Based on the results of this experimental campaign, it is believed that a fully prefabricated
TCC connection that ensures an adequately ductile failure mode is feasible. Further, im┬¡
plementing prestressed fasteners, it is argued that additional beneficial properties can be
achieved.

Introduction
In 2020, 37% of the worlds energy and process┬¡related carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions
originated from the construction industry, where manufacturing building materials and prod┬¡
ucts such as steel and cement accounted for 10% (United Nations Environment Pro┬¡
gramme, 2021). Hence, increasing the use of timber, a carbon┬¡neutral material, in build┬¡
ings and construction elements exhibit a great potential to reduce emissions. In many
residential houses and low┬¡rise buildings, timber decks and timber frame flooring provide
great results. But pure timber elements do have technical limitations concerning load car┬¡
rying capacity, fire resistance, acoustic and thermal properties, as well as deflection and
vibration (Yeoh et al., 2011). These problems can be resolved by using timber┬¡concrete
composite structures, hereby referred to as ÔÇÿTCCÔÇÖ structures.
TCC structures are constructed as a timber beam or deck connected to an upper con┬¡
crete flange using different types of connectors. This way, the timber composite is mainly
subjected to bending and tensile forces while the concrete topping resists compression,
utilizing the beneficial properties of both materials. Comparing TCC structures to traditional
reinforced concrete sections, several advantages appear. As the concrete layer can be
reduced by approximately 50%, lowering the structures self┬¡weight, reduced load imposed
on the foundation and reduced seismic actions are obtained (Lukaszewska et al., 2008).
And according to Yeoh (2011) because of the low┬¡emitting properties of timber, lower em┬¡
bodied energy and CO2┬¡emissions are achieved. Additionally, because the concrete slab
acts as protective layer, delaying the charring of the timber, the fire performance of a TCC
floor is significantly improved when compared to a timber┬¡only joisted floor (Yeoh et al.,
2011).
However, for a TCC system to be efficient, Yeoh (2010) argues that three fundamental
design criteria must be satisfied. Firstly, the neutral axis of the composite should be posi┬¡
tioned as close to the interlayer as possible in order exploit the beneficial properties of both
composites. Following, the method used to connect the two elements must be strong and
stiff enough to transfer the design shear force and provide an effective composite action,
which is defined below. Lastly, in order to resist the forces from gravity loads applied on
the TCC structure, the timber element must be strong enough. Hence, a design that fulfills
these criteria is desired.
The degree of composite action tells a story about how well the TCC structure acts as
one element. Complete composite action gives no interlayer slip between the two com┬¡
posites, and the TCC structure bends as one element (Gutkowski et al., 2004). Contrarily,
in the case of no composite action, no horizontal forces are transferred between the two
composites and the layers of the TCC structure moves separately, which consequently
gives disadvantageous tensile forces in the concrete and compression forces in the timber
(Yeoh, 2010). When connectors are implemented between the concrete┬¡ and timber ele┬¡

2004). Some interlayer slip is still present, and the elements are exposed to both ten┬¡
sion and compression. Nevertheless, the structural performance is significantly improved
compared to the situation of no composite action.
Most TCC systems involve in┬¡situ cast concrete, which gives an effective composite action
between the two materials. However, a structure where the concrete slab is prefabricated
off┬¡site bear several practical and some structural benefits. Rapid erection, easy recycling
of waste, close to no shrinkage when the two composites are connected, and higher mod┬¡
ulus of elasticity are some of them (Lukaszewska et al., 2008). Further, a large number
of different connection types have been researched throughout the years, and, ultimately,
these studies have revealed the notched connection as a favorable connection method
concerning both stiffness and strength. This is further talked about in Section 2. In addi┬¡
tion to the grooves cut into the timber composite, mechanical fasteners are often included.
Zhang (2020) states that the use of mechanical fasteners is mainly to prevent uplift of the
concrete when subjected to shear forces and gap openings between the two composites
due to shrinkage of the concrete and drying of timber. In order to maintain an adequate
sound insulation, such gaps must be avoided as much as possible (Zhang et al., 2020).

If fully prefabricated TCC structures are to become an established construction system
within the building industry, awareness and a greater understanding of its behavior is es┬¡

configurations of a notched TCC connection. A sketch of this connection is illustrated in
Figure 1.1. The main objective of this paper is to present the results of these tests and
provide an analysis of the influence several parameters have on the connectionÔÇÖs behavior
and performance, with a particular focus on the influence of fasteners and their degree of
prestressing. A comprehensive investigation on how the test specimens with prefabricated
concrete elements compares to the specimens with in┬¡situ casted concrete elements will
also be presented.

Literature study
Because of its beneficial properties such as stiffness, capacity and sound insulation, there
is an increasing motivation within the industry for implementing TCC structures into com┬¡
mon building techniques (Dias and Sch├ñnzlin, 2018). To realize this, a set of rules and
design models must be developed and implemented into the standards, subsequently, es┬¡
tablishing standardized solutions. As the choice of an effective shear connection is key
to obtain a good composite structure, several connection types have been developed and
tested by researchers throughout the years. In addition to easy manufacturing, it is es┬¡
sential to achieve a stiff and strong connection, while simultaneously maintaining ductile
and predictable behavior (Lukaszewska et al., 2008). Both glued connections and long┬¡
notched connections with dowels display beneficial properties regarding stiffness and load
bearing capacity (Dias, 2005). However, due to multiple drawbacks of the glued connec┬¡
tions, such as complexity of application, extensive quality control, and expensive waste
management, the notched connection is favored (Yeoh et al., 2011).
Push┬¡off tests are usually carried out to examine shear connections (Kuhlmann and M├Ânch,
2018). For instance, 5 series of push┬¡off tests were conducted by Lukaszewska (2008) to
investigate different connection methods embedded into a prefabricated concrete slab.
Within these series, a notched connection in combination with a vertical head coach screw
was included. Although the slab used in the notched connection method was prefabricated,
it was manufactured with a hole in the area of the notch which was filled with concrete mix
at a later stage. Thus, even though the notched connection displayed the highest strengths
of all connections, Lukaszewska expressed concerns regarding the manufacture process
of the slabs due to their holes, and the mix design of the concrete used to fill these holes.
However, in later years, several more push┬¡off tests of the notched connection have been
performed to gain a better understanding of the connectionÔÇÖs load capacity, stiffness, and
failure modes. In 2008, Yeoh and his colleagues (2008) carried out 30 tests, whereas
10 was of a rectangular notch connection vertically reinforced with a ├ÿ16 mm lag screw.
Further, 20 push┬¡off tests were conducted by Kudla (2017) in 2015 and an additional 12
tests were conducted by Kuhlmann and M├Ânch (2018) in 2017. While Yeoh (2008) reported
that the specimens failed mainly due to shear of the concrete, compression of the timber
fibers and shear of the timber in front of the loaded edge were the governing failure modes
experienced by Kudla, M├Ânch and Kuhlmann.

push┬¡off tests in previous studies restrict the specimens to lift during testing with the use
of supports (Zhang et al., 2020═¥ Ballerini et al., 2002). This is, however, believed to also
restrict some failure modes, and, consequently, important knowledge on the specimensÔÇÖ
behavior is lost. Hence, a test setup allowing the concrete element to lift was designed.
To achieve pure shear at the notched connection, the attacking point of the actuator and
the passive support at the opposite side needed to be located at the level of interface.
Hence, great consideration was given to the leveling of the specimens, resulting in four
beams stretching across the test setup, carrying the roller supports, which consecutively
supported the test specimens. Once the test initiated and some load was applied to the test
specimens, the roller supports moved freely, indicating no forces were lost due to friction
in these supports. Custom┬¡built pinned horizontal supports at the active┬¡ and passive side
of the test setup were made to allow for unrestricted displacement in the vertical direction.
The active side is defined as the side where the actuator is actively applying load, and vice
versa.

The experimental campaign is divided into three stages, pre┬¡linear┬¡, linear┬¡, and post linear
stage, where the latter two are the essential stages when investigating the notched con┬¡
nectionÔÇÖs behavior. In Figure 4.1, the three stages are illustrated in the force┬¡displacement

4.1.1
Slip modulus
Initially, the slip modulus is found conventionally following the formulas for slip modulus
(ks) provided by DS/EN 26891, which states that the slip modulus is to be defined within
the load range of 10%┬¡40% of Fest. Although this is done, the force┬¡displacement curves
reveal that most specimens did not establish a fully linear behavior before later in the
loading procedure. Therefore, an additional examination of the slip modulus at a load
range of 40%┬¡80% of the maximum load Fmax is carried out as well. In this study, the
stiffnesses at the two load ranges are referred to as k04 and k08 respectively.
Figure 4.2: Slip modulus of load range 1 k04, and slip modulus of load range 2 k08
Comparing the two stiffnesses of all specimens, a clear tendency that the slip modulus
increases throughout the loading procedure is seen, as demonstrated in Figure 4.2. Here,
specimens located above the diagonal line experience an increase in stiffness, referred to
as hardening. Thai and his colleagues (2020) did a similar investigation. However, in his
study, both stiffnesses were found at 10%┬¡40% of Fmax, but the second slip modulus was
determined from the second load cycle. Nevertheless, a significant increase in stiffness
from the first to the second load cycle was recorded, and it was also noted that the second
slip modulus was more consistent. Thai stated that this was because the specimens had
been stabilized after the first load cycle.
A similar phenomena is believed to cause the hardening reported in this study as well. It
is reasonable to assume that the timber┬¡ and concrete elements were not in 100% contact
at the loaded notch edge when loading procedure initiated. And further, because of the
fasteners, the two composites were restrained from being pushed towards each other dur┬¡
ing the pre┬¡linear stage of the experiment. Thus, the specimens hardened throughout the
linear stage of the loading procedure. It is worth to mention that because of tolerances con┬¡

timber groove. Thus, 2 mm were cut from the unloaded notch edge, which, consequently,
created some wiggle room in the notch.

having full contact when the tests were initiated is further supported. In Figure 4.2, it
is seen that the specimens without fasteners stay relatively close to the diagonal line,

Discussion
The experimental campaign raised some unexpected concerns that is addressed in this
section. First, it proved to be difficult to ensure 100% alignment of the test setup. The
center of the passive support was measured a couple of millimeters off relative to the
actuatorÔÇÖs center. Additionally, the surface of the steel cube on the passive side was not
completely parallel to the actuator plate. As discussed in Section 4.1.2, it is believed that
these unalignments caused a slight horizontal rotation of the concrete element relative to
the timber when applying load.
Further, when the timber groove and the concrete tooth are both prefabricated and manu┬¡
factured separately, it proved difficult to achieve small enough tolerances so that the two
composites fit perfectly. Hence, if the concrete tooth is too big, as was the case in this
study, the timber grooves must be increased. This is a complex and time┬¡consuming pro┬¡
cess, especially if it is not discovered before the elements arrive at the building site. More┬¡
over, if the grooves are bigger than the concrete tooth, it is difficult to ensure full contact
at the loaded notch edge between the two materials, as discussed in Section 4.1.1. This
could ultimately lead to an insufficient slip modulus during the serviceability stage. Another
consequence of the concrete being prefabricated in its entirety, including the toothed part,
is that custom┬¡made moulding forms would be needed, resulting in extra cost.
When the different levels of the fastenerÔÇÖs prestressing degree were determined, design
models given by Eurocode 5 and characteristic withdrawal capacity from the fasteners
European Technical Assessment were used. However, as both the withdrawal tests and
the main tests revealed, the KOP Coach screws possessed a much higher capacity than
estimated. Consequently, the prestressing levels could have been altered, possibly re┬¡
sulting in more obvious influences of the prestressing degrees. However, as presented in
Appendix D, the fasteners with higher prestressing degrees exhibited a greater relaxation
effect. Hence, over time, the degrees of prestressing will not differ as much as initially
estimated.
From the test results presented in Section 4, some features of the notched shear connec┬¡
tion are seen to affect its behavior, such as the presence, inclination, and prestressing
degree of fasteners, and the casting method of concrete. First and foremost, as the pres┬¡
ence of fasteners provided the only form of vertical reinforcement in the notched area,
fasteners proved essential in order to avoid premature failure. However, although internal
shear reinforcement in the area of the notch would not prevent lifting, it should be investi┬¡
gated if this could improve the connectionÔÇÖs load bearing capacity or ductility at failure.
Moreover, implementing an inclination of the fasteners increased the capacity with approx┬¡
imately 30 kN relative to specimens with perpendicular fasteners. Because of hinges in
the fasteners and the design of the test specimens, these fasteners had to be cut at the

41
==PAGE:50==
interface to be removed from the timber, which complicated the dismantling process of the
composite structure. However, this problem would not be transferred to full size floor ele┬¡
ments, as, in that case, fasteners are not to be loaded so that hinges occur. A drawback of
the prestressed inclined fasteners is that it requires a triangular cut in the concrete element.
Hence, a custom moulding form would be needed, which would increase expenses.
Concerning the different degrees of prestressing of the fasteners, no signs of improved
performance regarding load bearing capacity or slip modulus were seen. Nevertheless,
other beneficial effects could be seen for the specimens with higher prestressing degree.
The process of prestressing the fasteners do not require any special equipment other than
a torque wrench or a nut runner. However, it is recommended to use fasteners that avoid
being inflicted to torsional moment when they are prestressed. Hence, fasteners where
only axial force is applied during prestressing, as used in this study, is suggested. Con┬¡
sequently, custom┬¡made fasteners would have to be manufactured, which could lead to
additional costs.
Although the in┬¡situ specimens displayed great properties concerning stiffness and simi┬¡
lar post linear behavior, some drawbacks were observed. The loss of cohesion appeared
at different loads, which makes the force┬¡displacement behavior during the linear stage
difficult to predict. Thus, as the cohesion loss, and, consequently, the jump in slip docu┬¡
mented in this study should be avoided during the serviceability stage, there are concerns
implementing this connection configuration to a full size TCC element. However, because
the behavior during the post linear stage and the capacities within the in┬¡situ series were
so similar, design models are easier to implement.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this paper was to investigate the influence different variables have on the
behavior and structural performance of a notched TCC connection. The effect of differ┬¡
ent fastener configurations, such as prestressing degree and inclination, were especially
given great consideration, in addition to the performance of prefabricated concrete ele┬¡
ments compared to in┬¡situ casted concrete. Hence, seven series of push┬¡off tests were
conducted, where a total of 30 specimens were loaded to failure and further until approx┬¡
imately 15 mm horizontal notch displacement was reached. The different series included
specimens with in┬¡situ casted┬¡ and prefabricated concrete elements, varying notch lengths
and fastener positions, as well as specimens without┬¡, inclined, and prestressed fasteners.
Although the load bearing capacity of the specimens with prefabricated concrete elements
were competitive to the specimens with in┬¡situ casted concrete, the in┬¡situ specimens dis┬¡
played a greater slip modulus, and a more ductile failure. However, some specimens with
prefabricated concrete elements did display initial stiffness higher than the what is sug┬¡
gested in previous studies, and throughout the loading procedure, more specimens devel┬¡
oped a stiffness higher than this recommended slip modulus value. Further, the cohesion
and the unpredictable composite action it resulted in, raised concerns regarding difficulties
anticipating in situ casted TCC systemsÔÇÖ behavior during the serviceability stage.
Concerning the specimens without fasteners, due to its lack of vertical reinforcement, only
a fraction of the capacity of the specimens with fasteners was reached. Additionally, the
most brittle failure modes were observed during the no fastener┬¡series. The results of the
push┬¡off tests with varying degrees of fastener prestressing did not show any effect on the
connectionÔÇÖs capacity nor its slip modulus. Nevertheless, other beneficial characteristics
such as less initial vertical rotation and smaller force drop at failure were achieved.
Based on this study, it is believed that a fully prefabricated TCC notched connection with
prestressed mechanical fasteners could be introduced as a favorable connection method
in a big scale TCC floor element. The advantages of a fully prefabricated system com┬¡
pared to a system with in┬¡situ casted concrete are many, including faster construction,
less expenses, allows disassembling and better waste management. Additionally, a de┬¡
sign where buckling of the timber fibers governs the failure mode will provide an adequately
ductile failure mode. This can be ensured through a long notch and selection of concrete
compressive strength higher than the timbers. To further guarantee a ductile connection
with sufficient load bearing capacity, vertical reinforcement must be implemented. In order
to achieve multiple beneficial effects such as improved sound insulation and less verti┬¡
cal rotation, it is suggested that this vertical reinforcement is realized through prestressed
mechanical fasteners.
