Abstract
In this paper, we have illustrated the implementation of a co-processor for a RISC-V
architecture. In particular, we have exposed the integration of a Fused Multiply-Add
(FMA), a system able to do oating point calculations, inside the Rocket Chip. The
interface used is the RoCC (Rocket Custom Co-processor). The following thesis wants
to show the design ow of the whole system, starting from the HDL le generation to
post-synthesis tests. One of the main objectives was the comparison between the FMA
and the oating point unit integrated into the processor in terms of speed, power and
energy. The paper was the result of the collaboration between the DTU (the technical
university of Denmark) and the University of Rome Tor Vergata.

Chapter 1
Introduction
"The complexity of a microcircuit, measured for example by the number of transistors per
chip, doubles every 18 months (and therefore quadruples, therefore, every three years)".
In 1965, Gordon Moore made a prediction that would set the step for the modern digital
revolution. From a careful observation of an emerging trend, Moore extrapolated that the
power of computing would increase dramatically, and the relative costs would decrease at
an exponential rate. This insight, known as Moore's law, has become the golden rule of
the electronics industry and a potent stimulus for innovation. As a co-founder, Moore set
the stage for Intel to make ever faster, smaller and more aordable transistors that are
found today in modern devices and toys. Even over 50 years later, the impact and lasting
benets continue to felt in various ways.
Currently, the dimensions of a transistor are of the order of nanometers. Although the
technologies are very advanced, for some years now the integration of these has become
increasingly dicult. Today, and for several years now, there has been a need to cover a
broad distribution of services. For this reason, electronics has also developed in dierent
elds. The main reasons for the development of the dierent architectures come from
power consumption, speed and are of System on Chip(SoC).
In fact, for each application, it is necessary to satisfy dierent technical specications.
The three main types of commonly known hardware architectures are microcontrollers,
FPGA, and ASIC.
The processor is the most versatile system among the three systems just mentioned. In
computer science and electronics, it is a type of hardware device of a computer that
distinguished by being dedicated to the execution of instructions, starting from an in-
struction set. The reconguration is accessible for dierent applications, so it has allowed
great dissemination in all consumer electronics. Notwithstanding the excellent computing
power, often higher processing speeds requested. For this reason, multi-core processors
have developed. The advantage of the processor is its low power consumption and easy
re-programming. The main disadvantage is a small clock frequency.
In contrast, an application specic integrated circuit (ASIC), in digital electronics, is an
integrated circuit created specically to solve a special purpose calculation application.

The specicity of the design focused on the resolution of a single problem allows reaching
performances in terms of processing speed and electric consumption that are dicult to
obtain with the use of general purpose solutions.
The last type of architecture is the FPGA (Field Programmable Gate Array). It nds
implantation in some applications where you search a tradeo between processing speed,
power consumption, costs, and recongurability. To starting from HDL languages, it is
possible to develop specic and recongurable applications.
Over the years, hybrid architectures consisting of processor and FPGA or between proces-
sor and ASIC has developed. In particular, in this thesis, we discuss hardware co-processor
integrated into the processors. It is a hardware component for computers designed to im-
prove PC performance in specic areas (generally oating point computation or graphics
processing). It allows you to download the CPU from all those activities that would oth-
erwise occupy most of the computational resources and which can be left free to perform
other tasks more eciently.
We can distinguish accelerators from co-processors. In particular, a coprocessor executes
instructions (sent by the CPU), while an accelerator appears as a device on the bus (con-
trolled by registers).
Each processor has an instruction set architecture (ISA). It is, in fact, the set of basic
instructions that the processor can perform and which therefore constitute its machine
language, starting from which the corresponding programs have written in the various
programming languages at the highest level of abstraction.
Computers with dierent
microarchitectures can share the same instruction set. Naturally, available data includes
instructions, registers, addressing methods, memory architecture, interrupt and exception
handling, and any external I/O. Dierent types of ISAs are available, but they are al-
ways subject to new changes along with the progress of information technology; however,
there is a vast amount of microprocessors and microcontrollers that implement ISAs of
all characters.
In this thesis, a co-processor we have developed for the processing of oating point cal-
culations. The architecture chosen is the Rocket Chip processor based on a particular
instruction set called RISC-V (reduced instruction set computer).
The paper has divided into the following chapters:
• chapter 2: We introduced RISC-V by seeing the four main instruction set types
(R-I-S-U) and how the register les divided. We analyzed the extensions that it
provides for the integration of a custom co-processor. We then discussed the Rocket
Chip environment, the components that makeup it and the pipeline stages. Finally,
we described the design ow from the sources in chisel language to test outputs.
• chapter 3: We have extensively discussed the RoCC (Rocket Custom Co-processor)
interface by analyzing all the subclasses that it uses. In particular, we focused on the
exchange of information between processor/co-processor and co-processor/memory
D-cache. In the last section of this chapter, we have set the class extension for
co-processor integration. Finally, we saw, with a simple example, the call of the
processor to the co-processor starting from a C program.

It is an open source hardware based on established Reduced Instruction Set Computer
(RISC) principles. Based on this instruction set, some microprocessors soft intellectual
property(IP) have been developed. The main advantages of this initiative are the opti-
mized instruction set focused on mobile phones and portable embedded systems require-
ments and the independence between the software toolchains and the hardware imple-
mentation.
The instructions are interpreted by the computer as a series of high and low electronic
signals. These signals represent numbers. Each part of instruction can be considered as
an individual number. RISC-V foresees 32 registers(Fig.2.1) with 32 bits of the word.
The RISC-V instructions are all 32 bits and this favors the regularity of all system. This
32 bits layout is called instruction format. To distinguish it from the assembly language
we will call these numeric instructions as machine language2. Since the size of the num-
bers inside the personal computer is almost all four powers, then we can represent the
instructions also in hexadecimal (base 16). We can easily convert from hex to binary to
get the machine language.
Multiple addressing formats are called addressing modes. As previously mentioned, RISC-
V ISA provides 32 xed instruction bits, however, the RISC-V scheme has been designed
to integrate variations on its length. In the ISA base, there are mainly four instruction
formats(R/I/S/U)(Fig.2.2)[10]. The four bytes must be perfectly aligned in the memory,
where the instruction will subsequently be read.

One of RISC-V's design goals is to provide support for custom extensions. For this pur-
pose, the instruction set is organized starting from an integer base ("I") plus the optional
extensions. Among these are:
• "M": standard extension for integer multiplication and division instruc-tions;
• "A": standard extension for atomic instructions;
• "F": standard extension for single-precision oating point instructions;
• "D": standard extension for double-precision oating point instructions;
• "C": standard extension for compressed instructions.
The "IMAFD" extensions are indicated with the letter "G" and the resulting ISAs are
called RV32G for the 32-bit version and RV64G for the 64-bit version. In addition to all
these standard extensions, the ISA also enables the presence of non-standard extensions.
There are many ways to extend RISC-V ISA. Among all the possibilities, the main and
the simplest, is the use of four opcodes, in the format of 32-bit instructions, which have
been reserved for custom extensions. The rst two of these optional codes, known as
custom-0/1, will no longer be implemented by future standard extensions, while opcodes
marked as custom-2/3 are reserved for future use by the 128-bit ISA extension(Fig 2.4).
These four opcodes are those used by the Rocket core processor in the RoCC interface.

2.2
Architecture
Many hardware architectures can implement the RISC-V5 ISA standard. Some of these
cores can be extended with custom accelerators. For the purpose of the thesis, this par-
ticular was a strong element of selection. Some of the architectures that can support
the ISA instruction format are: Rocket-Chip, LowRISC, PULPino, Ariane-SoC, OPEN-
PULP, HERO, Riscy, etc. Rocket-core architecture was chosen for this project.
2.2.1
Rocket Chip Generator
Rocket Chip is an open source System-on-Chip(BSD:Berkeley Software Distribution, li-
cense on Github6) design generator developed at UC Berkeley suitable for research and
industrial purposes that emits synthesizable RTLs. It uses the chisel[3] programming lan-
guage (hardware) that allows you to describe the core, caches, and interconnections to
generate a single chip.Rocket Chip generates processor cores for general use that use the
open RISC-V ISA. The main advantages of this processor are:
• Customizable system;
• Accelerator integration (in the form of instruction set extensions,.coprocessors, or
fully independent novel cores).
For more modularity, many of the components of Rocket Chip are available as indepen-
dent subfolders and use git sub-models to keep track of compatible versions. Rocket Chip
is a stable system that allows working silicon prototypes. Berkeley continues to expand
the space of projects it can express with new features. Rocket Chip is implemented in
Chisel, an open source hardware construction language incorporated in Scala[8]. Chisel
directly describes synthesizable circuits (it is more similar to traditional hardware de-
scription languages such as Verilog than to high-level synthesis systems). As it makes the
complete Scala programming language available for the generation of circuits, it allows
functional and object-oriented descriptions of the circuits (this simplies programming a
lot).
Chisel also has additional features not present in Verilog, such as a rich type system with
support for structured data, inference for wires, high leveling of state machines. Chisel
generates a synthesizable Verilog code that is compatible with FPGA and ASIC design
tools. One of the main features of the Chisel language is that you can generate a simulator
implemented in C ++. This unlike commercial Verilog simulators is much faster and can
be used to simulate an entire instance of Rocket Chip.
The Rocket generator (written in Chisel) consists of a set of parametrized chip construc-
tion libraries that we can use to generate dierent System on Chip variants.

About a nite number of digits, it is possible to represent a rational number that approx-
imates the given real number with an error. Two notations are used.
Fixed-point notation: use part of the digits in the integer part and the others in the
fractional part (the position of the point is xed on a preset bit);
Floating-point notation: use part of the digits to represent an exponent of the base(it
indicates the order of magnitude of the represented number);
Floating point representation extends the range of numbers represented with the same
number of digits respect to the xed point notation.
The IEEE standard for oating point arithmetic (IEEE 754) is a technical standard for
oating point calculation that was established in 1985 by the Institute of Electrical and
Electronic Engineering (IEEE).
A oating point unit (FPU, colloquially a mathematical coprocessor) is a part of a com-
puter system specially designed to perform operations on oating point numbers[1]. Typ-
ical operations are addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, square root, and bit-
shifting. In general purpose computer architectures, one or more data processing units
can be integrated as execution units within the central processing unit (as in the rocket
chip).
If a CPU is running a program that requires a oating point operation then it needs
to integrate an FPU. There are several ways to represent the oating point number but
IEEE 754 is the most ecient in most cases.
The oating-point representation of a real number x is:

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this chapter, we retrace the work done in this thesis, highlighting some aspects of it.
We will analyze the architectural and design choices that have been made and then we
will discuss the positive and negative issues of these decisions. We will talk about some
possible integrations that integrate into the system and what advantages they would bring.
The thesis aimed to design a custom co-processor on RISC-V architecture. The Rocket-
chip processor has proved to be the right choice as it allows for numerous parameteriza-
tions, so the user can decide to generate a processor specic to his needs. Another goal
was to succeed in separating the chisel design ow, being able to work only with HDL les.
We had several possibilities for the processor extension. The RoCC Interface has allowed
us to integrate our accelerator quite merely. In fact, by extending the interface class, we
connected the co-processor.
Starting from the custom conguration in chisel language, we moved on to work with the
Verilog behavioural les generated. Much of the work was organizing a new environment
consisting of makeles, directories, libraries and archives that allowed us to create exe-
cutables (for verication) starting directly from the synthesized les. This result must
highlight as it aims to integrate custom starting accelerators directly from HDL les. The
wrapper (customizable) developed in a chisel environment serves precisely this purpose.
In chapter 5 we showed the post synthesis design ow. We have synthesized all the Rocket-
core modules, the FPU and the co-processor. We then tested the system following the
new ow and executing the matrix product (4-24 bit variable precision for the mantissa).
The results obtained in terms of power, area and energy are satisfactory. We managed to
implement a oating point calculation system about 40 per cent faster than the internal
oating point unit. The total power consumed is higher than the FPU, but considering
the clock cycles, we have gained in terms of energy. The area occupied by the accelera-
tor/wrapper system is remarkable, but if compared with the entire system, it is acceptable.
Let us now discuss some of the disadvantages of the RoCC interface and show a possible
alternative. The bottleneck, in this project and many systems of this type, is the exchange

6.1. FUTURE DEVELOPMENTS
6.1
Future Developments
We assume we always consider the Rochet-chip architecture (in fact, as we have seen,
many architectures uses RISC-V instructions). There are two ways to connect an accel-
erator to the processor core:
• Tightly-Coupled RoCC Accelerator (used in our thesis);
• MMIO Peripheral (a.k.a TileLink-Attached Accelerator).
These approaches dier in the method of communication between the processor and the
custom block. With the TileLink-Attached approach, the processor communicates with
MMIO peripherals through memory-mapped registers.
Extending the Rocket chip with the peripheral MMIO, we can use the AXI (Advanced
eXtensible Interface) bus to communicate directly with external memory banks. It is
undoubtedly lead to a more ecient and faster exchange.
Essentially, IO devices or
accelerators (like a disk or a network driver), we may want the device to write directly to
the memory system coherently, eciently.
