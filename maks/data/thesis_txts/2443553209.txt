ABSTRACT
The master thesis has been elaborated with the purpose of providing a reference for analysing
Power-to-Fuel energy systems, distinguished in Power-to-Methane and Power-to-Ammonia. Each
of these plant has been further decoupled into two operating modes, depending on the deployment
of low-temperature electrolyser (Alkaline cell) or high-temperature electrolyser (Solide Oxide
cell).
All four energy systems have been investigated under thermo-economic and exergy principles in
order to develop a complete study based on multiple aspects and criteria.
Results obtained after the design and simulation process of Power-to-Fuel systems by means of a
software called DNA (Dynamic Network Analysis) are the following ones.
In terms of overall eﬃciency, Power-to-Methane alkaline mode and solide-oxide mode achieve
values equal to 0.56 and 0.73, respectively. Regarding Power-to-Ammonia, eﬃciency for alkaline
and solide-oxide mode are found to be equal to 0.36 and 0.44, respectively.
For a better heat integration, heating and cooling components have been implemented in the
form of district heating and absorption-chiller units. Heating modules allow to cover up to 50.7%
of the space heating demand of an average Danish household while cooling modules can supply
between 3 to 5 entire households.
Desalination units are an additional element to improve the management of heat sources generated
within Power-to-Fuel systems. A range of 46-75 household can be sustained with the distilled
water produced.
Moving to the exergy study, it has been revealed that the most destructive and ineﬃcient
components are usually electrolyser, methanator/ammonia reactor and a group of heat exchangers.
To optimise the exergetic performance of Power-to-Fuel systems, the mentioned components
should be ﬁrstly modiﬁed.
Finally, concerning the economic section, it has been found out that high total investment cost
in the range of 29-37 M$ make all these projects unfeasible, even for a long period of 60 years.

INTRODUCTION
This ﬁrst chapter is an introduction to the master thesis, which includes the rationale for this
study as well as an overview of the current energy scenario in order to outline which resources
and associated technologies are deployed for powering our society.
Particular attention is posed on the eﬀects on climate change caused by the intensive exploitation
of fossil fuels and how the increasing penetration of RES (Renewable Energy Sources) in the
energy mix has contributed so far in maintaining grid balance, while reducing CO2 emissions.
After this, a new section is proposed to introduce the role of energy storage inside the energy
sector as a link for eﬃcient integration between RES and fossil fuels.
Diﬀerent storage technologies are then described in terms of key operating conditions and their
potential suitability for power/heat production.
Power to fuel is then presented as interesting and promising energy storage solution due to its
strength coming from the possibility of converting renewable electricity, the one generated from
RES, into chemical energy in the form of a synthesized fuel.
1.1
Background motivation
The worldwide energy context is rapidly changing and moving forwards from the old one, which
saw the conventional fossil fuels such as coal, petroleum and natural gas prevailing over any
other energy source. The over exploitation of these limited quantity energy reserves not only
puts the future security of supply at serious risk, but it has the negative side-eﬀect of being the
responsible for the high concentrations of GHGs (Green House Gas emissions) released to the
environment.
A positive awareness and mindset around themes like carbon footprint1, sustainability principles
and methods are key tools for the transition towards decarbonised, decentralized and ﬂexible
energy networks, characterized by abundance of RES . Within this projected energy framework,
an essential part will be covered by storage systems due to their ability to better integrate
RES, especially VRES (Variable Renewable Energy Sources) like wind and solar, whose power
generation management is diﬃcult because of the ﬂuctuating nature.
Power-to-Fuel storage scheme oﬀers a valuable option for an eﬃcient utilization of these clean
resources from seasonal storage to interesting capacities storage. Therefore, this thesis is developed
with the objective to disclose the potential of this recent technology.

1.2
Current and future energy scenario
The existing European energy infrastructure has been undergone signiﬁcant and rapid
transformations during the last few decades, after having set up strategies to ﬁght against
the drawbacks brought by the excessive deployment of fossil fuels. The latter are still seen as
important conventional sources of energy, whose non reproducible nature entails the problem of
scarcity of assets. Furthermore, the responsibility for high levels of pollution is related to the
release of toxic pollutants such as CO2 and NOx into the air, that are products of processes
with fossil fuels used as raw materials.
This major transition towards a more sustainable and cleaner future for the energy sector is
driven by a common environmental awareness which is reﬂected into several targets, on the short
and long term, being written down in diﬀerent documents.
Starting from the Paris Agreement of 2015, the main goal is identiﬁed in "holding the increase in
the global average temperature to well below 2◦C above above pre-industrial levels and pursuing
eﬀorts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5◦C above pre-industrial levels"[1].
Global warming eﬀects can be seen in Fig.1.1, in which global temperature anomaly2 is depicted
with an upward trend from 1850 (reference year, pre-industrial period) to 2016, being the warmest
year observed so far with a value of +0.94◦C. This graphic shows that the increase is still below
the ﬁxed goal of +2◦C, at the same time the rising slope until 2016 is a clear warning signal
indicating the need of a greater share of RES in the power mix to keep under control global
warming phenomena.
Figure 1.1. Global average temperature. Source: Berkeley Earth
The European Commission has launched projects under the common name of Energy roadmap,
which include a series of policy frameworks aimed to create pathways towards a secure, stable
and decarbonised energy system with RES becoming its core. Each of this project contains
targets to be achieved during diﬀerent time frames, for instance 2020, 2030 and 2050.
By 2020, EU aims to [2]:
• increase the share of renewables to at least 20% of consumption
• reduce GHGs by at least 20%
• obtain energy savings of at least 20%

1.3
Key role of energy storage
The diversiﬁcation of sources, as a result of the combination of fossil fuels and RES, leads to
a greater complexity in the energy sector which aﬀects the management of balance between
electricity supply and demand.
Especially, more penetration of VRES augments the risk of instability for the grid, which is
notiﬁed by the deviation of the frequency beyond the limits of a predeﬁned range for the steady
state condition, that for European countries like Denmark is set to be 50 Hz.
The problem arises from the ﬂuctuating and unpredictable nature of VRES, that might result in
a mismatch of supply and demand. The real time balance between the two is a fundamental
prerequisite that has to be always maintained in a power system, otherwise overproduction of
electricity causes increase in the frequency, whereas overconsumption induces a decrease. For this
reason, frequency regulation is a procedure controlled by ENTSO-E 3, whose task is to assure
grid stability through activation of additional generation or disconnection of loads 4 [6]. An
example of typical regulation is depicted in Fig.1.4, in which a normal operation is restored after
the sudden drop in frequency by means of primary and second response of generating units that
are required to produce more power to compensate the fault.
Imbalances between supply and demand can be avoided with the introduction of a key element
for an energy system, the storage. This component works as a buﬀer to provide ﬂexibility to
the ﬂow of energy. It can be identiﬁed as a sink for the generation side and a source for the
consumption.
The main beneﬁt derived from its implementation consists in adding a structure where the
energy carrier can be saved up during times when it is not used (excess of renewables), solving
the technical issues related with curtailed electricity generation when a unit is disconnected.
Afterwards, this stored electricity can be re-utilized whenever the production bulk is not suﬃcient
compared to the demand.
The combination of more RES with better energy saving performances entails surpluses of power
output over demand with more frequency in time, which indicates a clear necessity of storage.

1.3.1
Classiﬁcation of energy storage applications
Over the course of the years, diﬀerent storage technologies have been designed and improved
through intensive R&D works, proving that storage is recognized as essential tool for smart and
eﬃcient energy infrastructures characterized by increasing integration of RES [7, 8].
At the moment, a wide range of energy storage technologies have been developed to diﬀerent
extents and they can be classiﬁed into three main categories based on the energy carrier involved
[9]:
• P2P (Power-to-Power) in which only electricity is involved in charging and discharging
cycles
• Conversion of power to heat in which electricity is transformed into heat to be stored
and be used for ﬁnal consumption
• Conversion of power to hydrogen in which electricity is transformed into H2 to be
stored and be employed as fuel for mobility, industry sector or as reactant for the synthesis
of diﬀerent types of fuels/gases to be injected into the gas grid (P2F, Power-to-Fuel)
The choice of the type of storage for a speciﬁc installation depends on several variables which are
the amount of electricity to be stored, the storage time and the cost of the technologies involved.
The organization World Energy Council [10] has collected data regarding emerging storage
technologies in order to set up valid comparisons related to some key parameters.
Fig.1.5 provides insight of the available energy storage applications, divided in several classes.

PHS is the most mature technology, meaning that the majority of the present installed storage
capacity runs with hydro power, which produces electricity from the energy potential of running
water pumped through turbines connected to generators [10]. In Eq.1.1 Q is the volume of ﬂow

pumped back from lower to upper reservoir.
PHS is then a well established technology, commercially viable on large scale. However, it has
topographical constraints due to the availability of valid water reservoir and land use [11].

CAES is a mature option, commercially viable and capable to store energy at relatively low
cost. It does not have a large scale size like PHS. Electricity is utilized to compress air at high
pressures, around 70 bar and stored in geological structures like salt caverns. The compressed
air is then released, pre-heated and expanded in a turbine to produce electricity [10, 11].
LAES is a promising storage at a demonstration stage which does not require any particular
geological site. Air is compressed, cooled down to liquid inside a refrigeration system and then
directed to insulated tank. Afterwards, it is converted back to gas, heated and moved to a
turbine for electricity generation [10].

Batteries are electrochemical storage tools in which chemical energy is converted to electricity.
Operational voltage and current are important parameters for the operating principles of a
battery [11]. These technologies have been studied for long times, but only in the recent years
their utilization has shifted from small to medium/large scale as a result of greater integration of
RES in the energy sector [10].
The most common types of batteries are lead-acid and lithium-ion. The ﬁrst ones are produced
in large quantities with a diﬀerent sizes and designs. They allow to obtain high performances
characterized by high cell voltages. The second ones have high energy density and fast charging
periods. Losses are very small (5% per month) so that they require almost no maintenance [11].
Flood batteries are a recent prototype using ions dissolved in liquid electrolytes. This conﬁguration
allows to achieve long life cycles (40 years) but they are expensive and still in development phase
[11].

Thermal storage can be distinguished in three classes that are sensible (SHS), latent (LHS) and
thermochemical (THS). The term sensible indicates that a change in the temperature for the
storage medium is observed during operation, while latent points out that a phase change is
involved for medium such as molten salt or paraﬃn. For thermochemical a reversible chemical
reaction occurs. In one direction the reaction is energy demanding and the other is energy
yielding [10]. Materials used for THS ensure 8-10 times higher storage density than SHS and two
times over LHS for same volume. In addition, it presents advantageous features like low heat
loss and lower charging temperatures. A detailed comparison for materials and other parameters
related to SHS, LHS and THS can be found in [12].
Hydrogen refers to the ﬁnal product of a conversion system having electricity as source of energy
for a water electrolyser, which releases output in the forms of H2, O2 and remaining H2O. This
scheme is part of Power-to-Fuel plants with other types of fuels such as methane, ammonia and
methanol.
Produced H2 can be utilised for electricity generation from fuel cells or directly injected into
existing natural gas grids, but with a limitation on the H2 concentration (around 10% by volume
of the admixture)[10].
SNG or methane is a fuel synthesized from H2 and CO2 as reactants in a process called
methanation. Unlike H2 injection to the natural gas grid, CH4 is not subjected to any restriction
regarding the maximum allowable amount that can be introduced inside the existing network.
This is seen as one of the major advantages of Power-to-Methane systems together with the
positive environmental aspect of consuming CO2, captured from air. By doing this, it contributes
in the ﬁght against the global warming and in general climate change.

Pumped hydro and compressed air are appreciated as large-scale storage since they can store
power in the magnitude of thousands of MW with almost zero losses (self-discharge). They can
last for several years (range of 20-60 years) before shutting down and they are characterized by
good eﬃciencies, around 0.4-0.85.
Flywheels are capable to accumulate power up to 20 MW which can be released in few
seconds/minutes. A drawback consists in high losses that can be added up to 10% of the
stored capacity. However, energy and power density are greater than PHS and CAES, while an
eﬃciency of around 0.8 can be achieved.
Li-ion and Lead-acid batteries have in common similar power rating, from small decimals to 100
MW and discharge times. Losses do not have much inﬂuence on the their performances which
feature high values of eﬃciency in the range 0.8-0.95.
Flow batteries have much less energy and power density compared to the above mentioned
batteries as well as slightly smaller eﬃciency.
Hydrogen and SNG belongs to the group of Power-to-Fuel technologies, whose speciﬁcations
include power rating up to 100 MW, the highest values for energy density (measured at pressure
of 200 bar) and the longest discharge period. Eﬃciency are found to be from 0.25 to 0.45, which
are quite low with respect to other storage devices.
Finally, technical data for thermal storage is given for molten salt medium which can save power

1.4.1
Current projects and applications
Power-to-fuel plants can play a major role in the upcoming future energy scenarios since it
combines the beneﬁt of utilizing surpluses of RES, that otherwise would be curtailed, to provide
grid balancing with the reduction of CO2 emissions for schemes with methane or methanol
production.

H2 to yield CH4. For this case of study, H2 source was identiﬁed in sea water for which speciﬁc
electrolysers had to be developed and CH4 was intended to be liqueﬁed and be transported to
Japan for ﬁnal utilisation.
In 2009 Sterner conducted a study aimed to outline diﬀerent plant conﬁgurations based on which
CO2 source (pure CO2, syngas or biogas) is used for the methanation process. [14]

METHODOLOGY
This chapter is meant to give an overview of the conceptual structure of the considered energy
system and to provide some information regarding the simulation tool used during the design
and the analysis step.
2.1
Power-to-Fuel block diagram
A Power-to-Fuel represents a category of energy storage which is identiﬁed by some essential
structural elements. In this section, a conceptual block diagram of the plant is presented with
the intention of highlighting the ﬂow stream of energy carriers and the main components.
In general, a P2F scheme includes inlet energy sources which undergo a series of processes, at
the end of which the principal outlet and the by-products are diﬀerent from the initial ones.
In Fig.2.1 is shown the Power-to-Fuel block diagram that is going to be designed and assessed in
this study. There are four main elements that represent speciﬁc processes and several energy
carriers, denoted by the arrow shape.
The ﬂow of energy begins with water which is fed into the electrolyser, whose function is to
operate the split of H2 and O2 molecules thanks to the external source of electricity. The latter
is drawn fron the grid and its nature can belong to diﬀerent resources such as renewables or
fossil fuels. For this case, it is identiﬁed in wind power.
Afterwards, a gaseous mixture of H2 and residual H2O is directed towards a separator, namely a
condenser to eliminate traces of H2O based on the diﬀerent condensation temperature of the
two species. During this step, heat is released as eﬀect of temperature reduction.
Now pure H2 can be compressed and mixed with the required substances, N2 or CO2 to create
the mix that becomes the feed stream for the last and important process. The compression is
needed to set up an appropriate level of pressure for the next component, which usually requires
higher pressure for a good yielding.
The conversion of H2 and N2 or CO2 occurs in the ending part of the system, during an extremely
exothermic process with NH3 or CH4 respectively as ﬁnal product with additional non-converted
reactants.
As described above, Fig.2.1 is valid for Power-to-Fuel systems for the ammonia or methane
production.

2.2
DNA modelling software
Diﬀerent Power-to-Fuel conﬁgurations have been designed through the usage of an energy system
and process simulation software called DNA (Dynamic Network Analysis). It was developed at
the Department of Mechanical Engineering at DTU1 with the purpose of providing the user a
versatile program for modelling systems in both steady state and dynamic operations [32].
The user is required to create the energy plant through an editor window based on the GNU
Emacs2, so that DNA can be run interactively.
The text editing procedure occurs through the call of the components needed to build up the
energy system in the editor window. An already existing library of useful components is available,
among which there are models of boilers, heat exchangers, pumps, separators and more [32].
It is also possible to add a new model of a component in DNA, but it needs to be previously
deﬁned in a FORTRAN3 code within the directory where the library is located.
In the text editor interface, each component has to be programmed using speciﬁc syntax while
complying with the physical and thermodynamic constraints during the development of the
system. In DNA, the components are connected with node numbering. Based on these numbering,
the system is solved by mathematical equations that include the mass and energy conservation
for all components as well as the relations of thermodynamic properties of the ﬂuids involved.

Wind turbines
The ﬁrst component to be introduced is the wind turbine, a technology which allows to take
advantage of the wind energy content. Looking at Fig.1.3, which is proposed in subsection 1.2, it
is possible to visualize the signiﬁcant potential of solely wind for power generation in the EU
context. More than third of the projected electricity mix by 2040 will derived from wind with

The modelling process starts from the knowledge of working principles and structure of a wind
turbine technology, which is composed of several parts as shown in Fig.3.1 [33]. An aerodynamic
rotor captures and converts wind speed into kinetic mechanical power, which is applied to a low
speed shaft. Next, a gearbox is required to increase the rotational speed from low to high speed
shaft, before getting fed to the generator. This element transforms mechanical into electrical
power that passes through a power electronic interface for controlling purpose. At this point, a
transformer is needed to modify the voltage level, either by stepping up or down depending on
the point of the grid in which it is located. For this case, it will ensure a step up in the voltage
before electricity ﬂows into transmission lines of the grid.

3.1.1
Wind turbine concepts
Wind turbines can be gathered into a variety of classes with respect to what kind of features the
assessment is based on.
One of the main criteria is associated to the spacial arrangement of the axis of rotation, referring
to the possibility to have a horizontal (HAWT1) or a vertical axis (VAWT2). It can be pointed
out that majority of the current technologies present the installment of horizontal axis of rotation
due to the fact that the design of the vertical ones is more complicated. In reality, the blades of
a VAWT are operating at least for each half of revolution, so that they are more aﬀected by load
ﬂuctuations. Thus, the aerodynamic fatigue loads are greater for VAWT than HAWT which
result in the need of more maintenance [34].
Another classiﬁcation is associated with the ability or inability to modify the rotor speed. Based
on this parameter, a wind turbine can be distinguished between Fixed Speed (FSWT) or Variable
Speed (VSWT). The former does not change the rotor speed according to the wind ﬂuctuations
but it remains ﬁxed to the grid frequency (50 Hz), whereas the latter is decoupled from the
grid frequency and it can follows changes in wind loads through power electronics. The main
advantage owned by VSWT comes from the possibility to achieve the optimal aerodynamic
eﬃciency for a wide range of wind velocities; a condition achievable at only one wind speed for
FSWT [33].
Last category is related to the option of changing the pitch angle. Therefore, there are Fixed
pitch or Variable pitch wind turbines. This operating element aﬀects the aerodynamic eﬃciency
because allowing to change the pitch angle is equivalent to modify the angle of attack of wind to
the blades [33].

3.2
Electrolyser
The electrolyser is a core component inside a Power-to-Fuel system as it represents the source of
hydrogen, the energy carrier of the ﬁrst part of the storage plant. Its typical structure mainly
consists of an electrolysis stack, an ion exchanger, H2 and O2 channels for the separation of
the two products into separated streams and a converter. The stack, in turn, is composed of a
set of cells connected in series so that the overall voltage is equivalent to the sum of single cell

3.3
Methanator
Methanator is a reactor designated to operate the methanation process for the synthesis of
methane, CH4, as the product of Power-to-Methane storage system. Two diﬀerent models of
26
==PAGE:35==
3. Components modelling
methanator are currently used, namely catalytic and biological. The point of diﬀerence consists
in which elements are deployed as catalyst to help the reaction to take place.
First, catalytic methanation occurs with the presence of metals such as Ni, Ru, Rh and Co4
which eﬀectively decrease the amount of activation energy. Ni is the most common metal due to
its high activity, good CH4 selectivity and low cost.
Typical operating temperatures for catalytic methanation are between 200 and 500◦C and
pressures up to 100 bar [41].
Second, biological methanation requires the presence of biocatalyst in the form of microorganism
called archae. By anaerobically metabolising H2 and CO2, these microorganisms ﬁnd nutriments
for their growth. Therefore, biological methanation proceeds under anaerobic environments at
temperatures 20-70◦C and ambient pressure [41].
Methanation or also called Sabatier reaction was ﬁrst studied by Sabatier and Sendersens in the
beginning of the last century. Initially, the Sabatier reaction was applied as pure gas cleaning
technology, with the intention of removing traces of CO2 and CO from the inlet gas for the
ammonia synthesis. In fact, both acts as a catalyst poisons in the ammonia production process.
Recent interest in the implementation of large share of RES in the generation mix has given a
new perspective for the potential utilization of this reaction. Motivations behind this renewed
approach derives from the advantage of providing a sustainable path for fuel production coupled
with the ability of reducing CO2 emissions, since carbon dioxide is one of the reactants required
for methanation.

3.4
Ammonia reactor
Ammonia is a chemical specie that can be artiﬁcially obtained through Haber-Bosch process.
This reaction was invented around the 19th century when a German scientist named Haber was
able to synthesis of ammonia from the ﬁxation of nitrogen and hydrogen gas, elements that
are inexpensive and abundant. Following his discover, another scientist named Bosch was the
responsible for scaling-up the process to industrial applications.
Nowadays, the worldwide utilisation of ammonia is associated with one third of the population
because of its deployment for the production of fertilizers.
The main nitrogen reserve is air with a concentration equal to 78% by volume, whereas the
remaining part is mainly consisting of O2 equal to 21% by volume. The separation of N2 from air
can be performed through Pressure Swing Adsorption technology where an adsorbent material
with good selectivity is used to draw nitrogen from the feed gas mixture [26].
3.4.1
Thermodynamics of Haber-Bosch process
Haber-Bosch process stoichiometric reaction is presented in eq.3.24 where it can be noticed that
it is a reversible reaction with a negative enthalpy, meaning that it is also an exothermic reaction
like methanation with considerable amount of heat released.
1
2N2 + 3
2H2 ⇌NH3
∆H = −45.7kJ/mol
(3.24)
The conversion of reactants into products sees a reduction in the volume as the number of moles
goes from 2 to 1. According to Le Chatelier´s principle to enhance the ammonia production rate
there are two possible options: increasing the pressure since there is a pressure drop during the
development of the reaction, decreasing the temperature since the reaction is exothermic.
This process does not occur at ambient temperature as N2 requires high energy for dissociation,
around 3000◦C in gas phase. On the contrary, raising the temperature to those high values
will favour the reverse reaction. To solve this issue, an iron catalyst is used so that the energy
activation drops down. By doing this, Haber-Bosch optimal conditions are related to a range of
temperatures around 400-500◦C and high pressures, approximately 150-200 bar [44].

Desalination plant is an energy system designed for the production of fresh and desalinated
water from sea water. There are various devices that can be exploited for this purpose such as
multi-stage ﬂash or reverse osmosis. However, direct contact membrane distillation (DCMD) is
the technology selected for this study.
DCMD is based on the concept of membrane distillation, a thermal separation process which
works on the principle of vapor-liquid equilibrium. The vapor partial pressure diﬀerence caused
by temperature gradient is forced between liquid-vapor interfaces. This is the key factor of this
process in which water vapor moves through a microporous hydrophobic membrane [47]. Other
particles such as salt grains are blocked when trying to pass through the membrane due to the
their bigger size compared to porous channels.
A schematic of a shell-and-tube DCMD is given in Fig.3.13 from which it is possible to notice
the counter-ﬂow streams of "hot" feed sea water and "cold" permeate water, indicated as Fluid
In-Out and Process Fluid In-Out respectively. The permeate stream is the ﬁltered pure water
which it is then directed to a storage tank or immediately used while salted water can be sent to
sea or recirculated for further distillation.
DCMD corresponds to only one element of the entire desalination plant, whose conﬁguration is
completed with the addition of pumps and heat exchangers for sea water pre-heating.

3.7
Absorption chiller
Absorption chiller is a refrigeration plant which exploits an external source of heat to provide
the energy needed to drive the cooling process. The operating characteristics of this technology
are aﬀected by the amount of heat given to the system as the more the heat is introduced, the
greater the cooling phenomena is. Therefore, there are diﬀerent heat options that can be assessed
for this kind of application like solar energy, waste heat from industrial sector or district heating
stations.
The concept this system relies on is about ﬁnding a proper refrigerant to be able to absorbs an
absorbent at low temperature and pressure, while releasing the absorbent at high temperature
and pressure [48]. For this study, water is chosen as refrigerant and Lithium Bromide (LiBr) as
absorbent.
In Fig.3.14 a representation of the schematic of absorption chiller is given to better understand
how it works and which devices are involved during operation mode.
Figure 3.14. Schematic of absorption chiller system
A rich solution is input for the desorber in node 86, being the place where the heat source (node
14) is utilized to boil the refrigerant from absorbent. By doing this, only water comes out in node
81 to continue the ﬂow of process, while LiBr exits at node 87 to move towards an expansion
valve in order to be reassorbed by the refrigerant in the absorber.
Weak solution (only water) goes to the condenser to be cooled down before moving to an
expansion valve for pressure decreasing. This is the point at which the solution is at lowest
temperature and pressure conditions.
In the evaporator the refrigerant undergoes evaporation. A cooling ﬂow is included in this part
of the system, which is the product of the absorption chiller (nodes 74-75).
The evaporated refrigerant in node 84 is now at low temperature and pressure, prerequisites for
the absorption of the LiBr. The absorber sucks the stream of refrigerant due to low pressure
which results in the condensation of vapor into liquid water.
The loop is closed when the new rich solution goes through a pump to increase the pressure
before being fed again to the desorber.

The location of the designed Power-to-Fuel system should comply with two essential prerequisites:

1st condition addresses the topic of exploiting a VRES for electricity feed of the electrolyser so

Wind is chosen as sustainable source due to projected potential in the EU context that is shown

the addition of this element narrows all the possible locations to the ones which are not too
distant from the sea/ocean in order to minimize the cost of pipelines for sea water transportation
to desalination plant.
Taking into account these variables, a good location for the installation of Power-to-Fuel system
could be Denmark for which wind electricity generation is already a well established sector.

5.2
Investment study
Prior to any investment project, it is necessary to have the required knowledge about its economic
viability, especially when it involves signiﬁcant cash ﬂows like in the case of construction and
operation of large energy systems of several MW in size. The economic feasibility of a project is
related to the possibility of achieving the break even point1 of the capital investment within a
reasonably long period of time. After this stage, the capital investment should be mature enough
to induce proﬁts. If these requisites are not to be met, then the negative economic outcome is
indicative of the unfeasibility of the investment plan.
Diﬀerent tools are designed to provide valuable support during the strategic decision-making
process. For instance, it should be known the present value of the future investment (NPV2)
followed by the payback time (PBT3) and the discount rate (IRR4). By means of all these metrics,
the ﬁnal result should give a proper idea whether the future capital investment would represent
a proﬁtable decision or not.
First of all, NPV is deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the present value of cash inﬂows and the
present value of cash outﬂows. Formula for NPV calculation is:

to be studied among the set of available ones. For this study, the designed Power-to-Methane
and Power-to-Ammonia are an examples of plants whose conﬁgurations allow to be examined by
changing a relatively high numbers of conditions. Having said that, the sensitivity analysis is
carried out by focusing on the performance of two key elements: electrolyser and the synthesis
reactor (methanator and ammonia reactor). These are acknowledged as the basis process of a
Power-to-Fuel system for the realization of the electrical-chemical conversion.
Regarding the electrolyser, it is chosen to variate the mass ﬂow of feed water for alkaline modes.
By doing this, it will primarily aﬀect its working characteristics in terms of voltage and heat. As
a matter of fact, cell voltage of the electrolyser is inversely proportional to the feed water mass
ﬂow, while the heat balance is in turn depending on the cell voltage as shown in Fig.3.8. In this
graph of polarisation curve, it can be noticed that by changing the voltage from thermoneutral
point, for which Qelectrolyser = 0, a heat surplus (Qelectrolyser < 0) or demand (Qelectrolyser > 0)
would occur based on the voltage change.
The thermoneutral cell voltage for alkaline is set to be around 1.496 V, represented in Fig.6.2
by a vertical line which marks the change in the background color from magenta to green. In
this graph alkaline modes for both Power-to-Methane (P2M) and Power-to-Ammonia (P2A)
are considered. Increasing the mass ﬂow of water results in lower voltages with corresponding
higher heat demands from thermoneutral point, up to 1200 kW at 1.098 V. On the other hand,
decreasing mass ﬂow of water results in higher voltages with downward trend of negative heat
values, reaching -1050 kW at 2.2 V.
Since each energy system being designed consists of several components connected to each other,
varying operating characteristics determines consequences on other elements and on the entire
system. It can be observed that eﬃciency for both P2M and P2A alkaline mode tends to decrease
as the voltage augments. Their downward trends do not have a constant and uniform slope but
it is irregular and segmented.
For P2M, eﬃciency goes from around 0.7 for a range of 1.098-1.4 V to 0.42 for 2.2 V while for

6.6
Exergy analysis
An exergy study of the Power-to-Methane and Power-to-Ammonia systems provides a new
perspective to the traditional analysis of a plant, based on the calculation of energy and eﬃciency.
Exergy corresponds to the maximum possible amount of technical work that can be done by
a given amount of energy under the conditions ﬁxed by its surroundings/environment. By
its deﬁnition, to conduct an exergy analysis is necessary to determine the characteristics of
environment or surroundings, a system to which the analyzed closed system (Power-to-Methane,
Power-to-Ammonia) exchanges energy in order to reach an equilibrium condition. To do so, it
has to reach the dead state conditions of the surroundings that are pressure p0 and temperature
T0. It is generally assumed them to be T0 = 25◦C and p0 = 1.013 bar.
DNA simulation tool oﬀers the option to calculate exergy for each node included in the energy
system by declaration of dead state condition, which are chosen to be 10◦C and 1 bar. These are
reasonable values for a possible installation of the designed energy system in Denmark, where
average temperature for water and air sources does not exceed 25◦C over a year.

the process of identifying the related fuel and product. A total of 5 diﬀerent types of formulas

CONCLUSION
The study carried out in this master thesis has involved relatively new energy systems, Power-to-
Methane and Power-to-Ammonia, that are still in the R&D phase. The interest in this type of
systems derives from the possibility of applying conversion from electricity to chemical energy of
the fuel. This is a versatile and useful concept to store excess of variable renewable electricity
(generated from wind, solar energy sources) that is then available to be exploited when it is more
needed. Based on the type of fuel synthesized, it can be used for electricity generation or as
vehicle fuel or in the industrial sector. This indicates its wide potential usage in diﬀerent areas
which makes these energy systems even more attractive.
After the design process, these Power-to-Fuel systems have been analysed under several aspects in
order to obtain a better understanding of their potentials. It is found out that Power-to-Methane
plants are able to achieve a molar fraction of CH4 equal to 0.32 while Power-to-Ammonia plants
can reach a molar fraction of NH3 equal to 0.31, at the exit of the respective methanator and
ammonia reactor.
When looking at the overall system eﬃciency, Power-to-Methane alkaline mode presents
ηPTM = 0.56 and ηPTM = 0.73 for SOEC mode.
Instead, Power-to-Ammonia achieves
ηPTA = 0.36 for Alkaline mode and ηPTA = 0.44 for SOEC mode. These can be assessed
as positive outcome since most of these systems are still in pilot stage.
Heating and cooling potential are further parameters that allow to evaluate these energy plants.
As for heating, it is possible to provide up to 50.7% of the space heating demand for a Danish
household while for cooling between 3 to 5 households can be supplied.
Moreover, another important feature is the production of distilled water which enables to provide
fresh and clean water from sea water for a number of minimum 46 up to 75 households. These are
quite impressive ﬁgures considering the fact that distilled water is a by-product of Power-to-Fuel
systems.
The exergetic study has identiﬁed the most destructive and ineﬃcient components in the
electrolyser, methanator/ammonia reactor and a group of heat exchangers. To achieve better
exergetic performances, it is necessary to primarily target these three component which are
responsible for a signiﬁcant amount of irreversibilites. For instance, heat exchangers can be
improved with a better matching of "hot" and "cold" streams which can be determined by means
of pinch analysis.
The economic feasibility study has highlighted the extremely high investment costs required to
ﬁnance these types of projects. They are in the range of 29-37 M$ among all the systems. With
such similar costs and with the calculated net annual cash ﬂow, it turns out that even in long
time period of 60 years there is no chance of making these system proﬁtable. At the current state
of development, it is not thinkable to identify Power-to-Fuel as key elements in the transition
towards a more decentralised and completely decarbonised energy network because of their high
78
==PAGE:87==
7. Conclusion
costs. Policies and supporting schemes should provide more subsidies and incentives so that net
annual cash ﬂow could have more impact in the calculation of net present value and pay back
period. Furthermore, more eﬀective materials and technologies could be discovered through an
intensive R&D that would need increasing ﬁnances during the whole process.
To conclude, the designed Power-to-Methane and Power-to-Ammonia energy systems have shown
interesting potential as future resources for renewable energy transition. However, the deployment
of these systems relies on the availability of adequate supporting schemes that could contribute
in making these plants economically feasible.
