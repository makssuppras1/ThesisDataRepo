Abstract
An accurate estimate of the damping ratio is important to determine the dynamic loads of
elastic structures and ensure their proper operation. In addition to requiring output data
measured over longer time periods, current damping estimation methods from output-only
measurements involve assumptions about the system and the input excitation that may result
in inaccurate estimates. While time series analysis methods such as FS-TARMA modelling
have been investigated for short-term damping estimation of different structures, they do not
appear to have been implemented specifically for estimating nonlinear damping. As a result,
the short-term damping estimation methods to be applied to operating elastic structures are
investigated with an emphasis on estimating nonlinear damping.
The more complex damping estimation method, based on FS-TARMA modelling, is preceded
by a simpler exponential fit method. A nonlinear damping model is implemented to generate
simulated data. An experimental setup is also used, consisting of a shear frame structural
model equipped with a Tuned Liquid Damper (TLD) to provide nonlinear damping. In each
case, free as well as forced excitation is considered. The exponential fit method is used to
estimate the damping ratio from free responses, and FS-TARMA models are mainly used to
obtain estimates from forced responses.
Overall, the damping ratios estimated from the two methods seemed to generally coincide
with the expected values when applied to simulated data. In addition, even though the re-
sults of the nonlinear case may inaccurate due to the effects of filtering, the experimental
free decay damping ratios estimated from the exponential fit method and FS-TARMA model
parameters were also similar. The experimental responses to forced excitation resulted in
more uncertainty in the estimated damping ratio, though the residual analysis showed flaws
in the FS-TARMA models possibly originating from the measured responses. As a result, it
appears that the accuracy of the short-term damping estimation methods when considering
nonlinear damping is highly dependent on the quality and characteristics of the input excita-
tion. Therefore, although the exact input signal does not need to be measured or assumed, as
is the case for long-term estimation methods, ensuring an adequate excitation may improve
the accuracy of the estimated damping ratios.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Predicting dynamic loads in elastic structures is important to ensure their optimal and safe
operation. These loads are related to the amplitude of vibrations of the structures, governed
by their inherent damping characteristics. Therefore, an accurate estimate of damping is
beneficial for suitable operation. Current methods for estimating damping from output-only
measurements, such as Stochastic Subspace Identification (SSI), use long-term measurements
and a number of assumptions regarding both the system (linearity, time-invariance) and the
input (stationary conditions, white noise excitation) [1, 2].
However, these assumptions
may not always be fulfilled, resulting in inefficient and inaccurate damping estimates. In
wind turbines, for example, the excitation forces stem from aerodynamic, gravitational, and
structural loads [3], which not only are they different from white noise, but they cannot be
measured. As a result, estimation methods based on output-only measurements are needed.
In addition, nonlinear dampers such as Tuned Liquid Dampers (TLDs) can be installed
in wind turbines, amplifying nonlinear behavior and thus limiting the accuracy of damping
estimates. Therefore, it would be beneficial in practice to use short-term damping estimation
methods in elastic structures such as wind turbines.
To improve parameter estimation in wind turbines beyond the conventional modal analysis
methods, parametric modelling under realistic conditions such as non-stationary input and
time-dependent parameters has been performed using time series analysis methods. Time-
varying autoregressive (TAR) models have been implemented [3], although with little em-
phasis on damping estimation.
Furthermore, time-varying autoregressive moving average
(TARMA) models have been used to estimate modal parameters for a bridge-like experimen-
tal setup with three non-stationary responses. However, the estimated damping ratios did
not correspond to baseline values from stationary experiments [4]. Earthquake ground mo-
tion has also been modelled with Functional Series TARMA, and although the models were
deemed appropriate representations of the signals and validated through statistical tests, the
damping ratios were not independently verified [5].
Regarding nonlinear dampers, several experiments have been performed to determine the
effectiveness of TLDs on different structures [6–8].
However, only a few studies involve
damping estimations. Sun et al. [9] used force and displacement data from shaking-table

which was shown to depend on the excitation amplitude. This was done by equating the

where an expression is given for the total equivalent mass, was then used to obtain the

The actual damping ratio is then estimated by

experimental input force and displacement. Wakahara et al. [11] also estimates equivalent

by [12], but this was based on linear theory.
As the investigations on TLDs suggest, damping estimations involve either linearity assump-
tions, which is unrealistic for TLDs at large excitation amplitudes, or both input and output
measurements, which is not feasible for large structures such as wind turbines. Meanwhile,
short-term estimation methods from output-only measurements eliminate the need for the
current modal analysis assumptions of time-invariant modal parameters and an excitation
equivalent to white noise. As a result, methods based on TAR/TARMA will be used for
short-term damping estimation from output-only measurements. The methods will then be
tested with simulated data from single-degree-of-freedom models with nonlinear damping,
as well as with experimental data from a simple laboratory setup. The experimental setup
includes a one-story shear frame structure mounted on a shake table and equipped with a
3D-printed TLD. The numerical methods developed should provide insight into performing
suitable damping estimations from operational output-only, short-term measurements, and
could therefore be employed in industrial applications. These methods could be applied to
elastic structures in general; their implementation on wind turbines is used as a motivating
example.
To this end, Chapter 2 offers a background on AR/ARMA models and their extensions, as
well as a description of TLDs. Chapter 3 then presents the damping estimation methods.
Chapter 4 describes the simulation and analytical modelling, while Chapter 5 outlines the
experimental procedure. The results are then discussed in Chapter 6. Lastly, conclusions are
provided in Chapter 7.

Chapter 2
Background
Before presenting the damping estimation methodology, an account of some relevant back-
ground concepts is offered. The Tuned Liquid Damper (TLD) is first introduced and the
relevant theory is described. ARMA models are subsequently presented, as they constitute
the basis for the Functional Series Time-varying ARMA (FS-TARMA) models described in
Chapter 3.
2.1
The Tuned Liquid Damper (TLD)
The Tuned Liquid Damper (TLD) is any damper where a liquid is used to dissipate energy
and reduce the vibrations resulting from dynamic loads [13, 14]. Similarly to the Tuned
Mass Damper (TMD), a mass is added to the structure, generating an inertia force that
counteracts the vibrations of the primary system [14]. This makes the basic mechanism of
the TLD analogous to the TMD. However, as the added mass is mostly a liquid, normally
water, nonlinearities are introduced [9].
On their own, TLDs are passive dampers: they react to the vibrations of the structure
and external power is not required to operate them. Along with their simple design and
composition, this aids in maintaining low initial and maintenance costs.
Unlike TMDs,
TLDs are activated even at low excitation amplitudes, do not require an external damping
element, and a single device can be used to control vibrations from multiple directions [14].
The simplest configuration is a Tuned Sloshing Damper (TSD), where the liquid is con-
tained inside a tank. Newer, more complex types include the Tuned Liquid Column Damper
(TLCD), which uses a U-shaped container instead of a tank, and the Compliant Liquid
Damper (CLD), where a flexible system is used to attach the container to the structure.
Most literature has used the more general term TLD to designate the TSD configuration
[14], and this same practice is used here throughout.
TLDs of the sloshing type can consist of rectangular tanks, though circular or annular tanks
may be a better choice for axisymmetrical structures to dampen vibrations in multiple direc-
tions [9]. To adequately dampen vibrations, the fundamental frequency of the TLD is then

2.3
Summary
In this chapter, some background principles were presented, offering a foundation before the
more specific concepts of subsequent chapters. The TLD was described, and formulas for its
shape-dependent theoretical fundamental frequencies were revealed. Furthermore, the theory
of the nonlinear damping force due to liquid sloshing in the TLD was also outlined, where
the damping force is seemingly quadratic and in accordance with the sign of the horizontal
velocity. The theory of TLD dynamics will be used in Chapter 4 to generate simulation
models, and in Chapter 5 to design a TLD to use in the experiments. ARMA models, used
to represent time series, were also described, as they establish the basis for the FS-TARMA
model presented in Chapter 3.

Chapter 3
Damping estimation methods
This chapter describes the damping estimation methods to determine linear and nonlinear
damping from the different vibration responses. Firstly, a simple exponential fit method is
presented. This method can be applied to estimate the damping ratio from free vibration
responses. It is an extension of the standard exponential decay damping estimation method,
which can be deduced from vibration theory. The second damping estimation method is
based on the Functional Series TARMA (FS-TARMA) modelling approach, where the ARMA
coefficients vary with some selected functions of time.
The FS-TARMA method can be
used to model a time series, such as a vibration response, and the modal parameters can
be extracted from the model coefficients, which vary with time. This damping estimation
method can therefore be used to estimate the time-dependent damping ratio from forced
vibration responses.

3.2.6
Model structure selection
The selection of the model and basis orders presents a difficult optimization problem. Includ-
ing additional terms in the FS-TARMA model (3.6) by increasing the model and basis orders
na, nc, pa, and pc would improve the representation of the signal yt and reduce the magnitude
of the residuals. On the other hand, this also increases the complexity of the model, and
could lead to overfitting. Very high model and basis orders could also result in very complex
models with only marginally improved performance. Therefore, the selection of the model
structure is important to provide a suitable but efficient model.
The two conditions are
assigned into a single value in the form of the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) [23, 24]:
BIC = −ln L(θ | y) + ln N

The function estimates the likelihood of the unknown coefficients in θ given the signal samples

represents the observed data, but to discourage overfitting, a second term is added to the
BIC in (3.25) to penalize a high number of coefficients. The optimal model is then found by
minimizing BIC.
The chosen procedure for minimizing the BIC, allowing to select a model structure, is based
on the concept of backward regression described in [24]. Firstly, the MA model order nc and
the AR and MA basis orders pa and pc are set to suitable high numbers. The AR model order
na is then varied and subsequently set to the order resulting in the minimum BIC. This is
repeated sequentially for nc, pa, and pc. Stabilization diagrams, where na is plotted against
the resulting mean frequencies, are used to further validate the selected AR model order.
Once the model structure has been selected, the resulting model is validated by residual
analysis.
The normalized residuals are plotted against time, ensuring that no structure
remains. The correlation of the residuals at different time lags is also computed with the
autocorrelation function (ACF). High autocorrelation at a specific time lag could mean that
there could be a periodicity in time series that has not been accounted for in the model.

Chapter 4
Simulation and analytical modelling
Simulation models of systems with different damping formulations are used, as they grant the
opportunity to control input parameters and rapidly generate different sets of data. Three
different damping formulations are thus presented. Firstly, linear, constant damping, is used
as the simplest test for the exponential fit damping estimation method as well as for extracting
the damping ratio from estimated FS-TARMA parameters. Linear but time-varying damping
is then considered for an intermediate modelling complexity. Lastly, simulated data with
nonlinear damping is generated to test the estimation methods with more complex data. An
analytical model of the free vibration response with nonlinear damping is derived to compare
to the estimated damping ratios in Chapter 6.

Summary
The different damping formulations used for simulating vibration responses have been pre-
sented in this chapter. The linear, constant damping is the simplest case, where the input
to the basis functions of the FS-TARMA model is simply time. The linear, time-varying

the input to the basis functions is then the absolute value of the velocity. An analytical solu-

used to compare with estimated damping ratios from the simulated as well as experimental
responses.

to configure the data acquisition device. The appropriate signal can then be sent to the
shake table with the HIL Write block, and the HIL Read Encoder block is used to read the
encoder signal. The Inverse Modulus block is used to unwrap the encoder signal, avoiding
discontinuities [32].
Since the Q2-USB has 16-bit counters [33], the modulus was set to
216 = 65536. Furthermore, the encoder counter goes through 49152 counts when moving the
shake table stage from one end to the other, or 24576 counts when moving from one end to
the center, corresponding to a precision of about 820 counts/mm. Thus, a gain of 1/24576
is used to match the amplitude of the encoder signal to that of the input signal.

Chapter 6
Results
This chapter presents the results of damping estimation using both the exponential fit method
and FS-TARMA. Free vibrations are first considered, primarily to validate the exponential fit
method. Both simulated and experimental responses are used, each with linear and nonlinear
damping examples. Harmonic excitation is then applied in both simulation and experiments

time-varying) and nonlinear damping, and in experiments with the shear frame with and

estimates obtained with the responses of the shear frame only, as these were an order of mag-
nitude apart. The discrepancies could be explained by the drift of the shake table platform
from the center position in these responses. This may have led to inaccurate models, as the
residual plots deviate from the expected white noise structure.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
Throughout the last few chapters, short-term damping estimation methods were formulated
and investigated for linear and, specifically, nonlinear damping. These methods, the first
based on the standard exponential decay estimation and the second based on modal param-
eter estimation from FS-TARMA models, were applied to free or forced vibration responses,
in simulated as well as in experimental data. The simulated data was obtained from linear
damping models including constant and time-varying damping, as well as from a nonlinear
damping formulation pertaining to liquid sloshing. The free vibration response with non-
linear damping was used to obtain an analytical solution of the equivalent damping ratio
as a function of the vibration amplitude. A laboratory model was also set up to produce
experimental data. The setup was based on a shear frame structure equipped with a Tuned
Liquid Damper (TLD) to provide nonlinear damping.
The estimated damping ratios were obtained for free and forced vibration responses from sim-
ulated as well as experimental data, where in each case both linear and nonlinear damping
were considered. Although both with their limitations, the damping ratios estimated from
the exponential fit and FS-TARMA modelling methods mostly seemed to agree when consid-
ering free responses with nonlinear damping. In addition, FS-TARMA models of white noise
excitation on simulated responses with linear damping led to accurate damping ratio esti-
mates. However, the damping ratios estimated from equivalent experimental responses were
an order of magnitude greater than those estimated from free vibration responses with the
exponential fit method. When considering nonlinear damping, the estimated damping ratios
from both simulated and experimental responses to white noise excitation showed significant
variability between the different realizations.
The bandpass filter used on the free responses with nonlinear damping might have affected
the data, resulting in inaccurate damping ratios. In addition, the accuracy of the FS-TARMA
model may be limited in these responses, as the short time duration increases the uncertainty
of the estimated parameters. However, the two damping estimation methods seem to more
or less coincide when applied to the same free vibration responses, suggesting that they could
both be accurate depending on the data. Nevertheless, depending on the response signal, the
results of the estimation methods do not seem very precise. Despite the apparent accuracy of

different time series are considered, even if the underlying system is the same.
In general, an experimental model consisting of a single-degree-of-freedom system but still
providing nonlinear damping, for example by applying an external air flow, could be used to
test and validate the estimation methods for free vibration responses without the effects of
filtering. In addition, nonlinear damping could be estimated from systems with a higher num-
ber of degrees of freedom, producing more complex models to further validate the short-term
damping estimation methods. Lastly, data from commercially operating elastic structures
could be used to test the effectiveness of these short-term damping estimation methods in
industrial applications.
