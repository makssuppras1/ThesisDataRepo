Abstract
Floating wind energy is under growth and validated numerical models of ﬂoating wind turbines
are crucial for this development. In this project a numerical study modelling a 1:60 scaled
ﬂoating wind turbine was examined in terms of agreement with experiments. The wind turbine
was the DTU 10MW RWT and it was mounted on the spar conﬁguration of TetraSpar ﬂoater.
Firstly, the wind ﬁeld was calibrated in order to determine the mean wind speed experienced
in hub height as well as the turbulence intensity according to the experiments. Afterwards, the
rightness of the damping level in the model was investigated and further calibration was done
in order to replicate the basin tests. A trial and error method was applied by changing the
hydrodynamic damping in ESYSWamit module of HAWC2.
The numerical model was simulated for wind-only, waves-only (regular, irregular and focused)
as well as the combination of wind and waves. The calibration focused on the cases of irregular
waves, with and without wind, which are more representative for a real environmental condition.
Moreover, misaligned cases with a wave heading of 30◦were tested and further calibrated when
wind loads were present. The duration of the aforementioned simulations was similar to the
experiments apart from the case of irregular waves, in which the duration was half the time due
to software limitations. However, the cases of wind and aligned irregular waves were simulated
once more for 1500s (equal time to the experiments), which is equivalent to 3h sea state in full
scale. The comparisons between model and test were illustrated with time series, power spectral
density and probability exceedance plots. The environmental conditions were also selected in
order to comply with the experiments, having ﬁve diﬀerent load cases. All the simulations are
presented in the appendices, while the most important results are included in the main part
of the report, emphasizing in plots for evaluating both the eﬀect of wind and misalignment.
Although, the ﬁrst approach was simulated without second-order hydrodynamics, these forces
were included by using the Newman approximation and both the calibration and the presented
results are related to the latter. Some comparisons between the motion responses with and
without second-order loading are described.
The numerical model seemed to underpredict the in-plane motions and this phenomenon was
ampliﬁed for smaller sea states. Furthermore, the behavior in rated wind speed may indicate
uncertainties in wind speed inputs or controller tuning, indicating the importance of further
research on these issues. The followed procedure of damping calibration was able to provide a
good match between high-ﬁdelity aeroelastic code HAWC2 and the derived data from the test
campaign, opening up new areas of research and pointing out crucial improvements and checks
for further investigation of either related numerical studies or the existed one.
Keywords: Floating wind turbines, TetraSpar ﬂoater, Numerical study, HAWC2

Oﬀshore Wind Energy
All the above information supports the increasing trend of using renewable sources for electricity

1.1.2
Floating wind turbines
In general, it could be said that ﬂoating wind turbines are not as popular as monopiles and
jackets,as it was observed in Figure 1.4.
The reason is mainly the high costs and the low

1.1.2.1
Barge platform
The barge ﬂoater has a very low draft and it is a waterplane-stabilized ﬂoater type.
It is
anchored to the seabed with catenary mooring lines. The ﬁrst installation of this ﬂoater was
done in 2018 on the Atlantic coast of France, from the ﬁrm Ideol (Floatgen Demonstrator).
The second installation of barge ﬂoater was in Japan (the same year from the same company).
The name of the project is Hibiki and a 3MW two-bladed wind turbine is mounted on the
top of the ﬂoater. According to the company, Ideol’s ﬂoating wind turbine technology is fully
validated and conﬁrms its outstanding performance with a big list of future commercial farms
in France, Scotland, Japan and many other geographies [6]. Regarding this ﬂoater, some of the
main advantages could be the low draft as well as the Ideol’s patented inner pool that acts as
a damper for the system. On the other hand, the barge is likely to be subjected to higher wave
loading [7], resulting in an increasement of the motions. Thus, maybe a higher cost is required
compared to other solutions.

1.1.2.3
Spar buoy
Concerning spar buoy, it could be characterized as a ballast stabilized. On this type of ﬂoater,
the center of gravity is signiﬁcantly lower in the water than the center of buoyancy.
It is
achieved by placing some ballast weight in the lower part of the ﬂoater. It is also fact that
the draft of spar platform is quite big. It implies that the wind turbine cannot be mounted
on harbor, resulting in a more expensive installation procedure by implementing the need of
crane vessel usage. It is also a limitation for repairments in comparison with the previously
presented ﬂoater (semi-sub). Concerning the beneﬁts, it could be stated that the simple design
acts positively for the fabrication processes.
One of the demonstration projects with high signiﬁcance was done in 2009 by Equinor (Statoil)
in Norway. The demo version of the project experienced wind speed of 40 m/s and a maximum

1.1.2.4
Tension leg platform
The TLP is a mooring stabilized ﬂoater, which is semi-submerged and anchored to the seabed
with taut mooring lines. Its stability can be gained through the use of the mooring line tension.
Regarding this type of ﬂoater there are some projects (Figure 1.7), but none of them has been
installed until now. In the list of the project of TPL are the Pelastar [11], Eco TLP [12] and
TLP from Gicon-Sof [13].
Figure 1.7: Pelastar project [11], Eco TLP [12] and TLP from Gisoc-Sof project [13]
The advantages of TLP ﬂoater are the low structural mass and the ability of onshore turbine
assembly, while the high loads on the mooring and anchoring system could be considered as a
drawback [14]. However, taut leg mooring system could be a better cost-eﬀective solution for
deep-water compared to the catenary system [15]. Thus, TLP substructures could be a good
choice for deeper seas.
1.2
Statement of the problem
As it has also been referred earlier, FOWT are complex structures compared to other existing
types of substructures for oﬀshore wind energy. Furthermore, ﬂoating wind energy is in its
infancy, since only a few full-size prototypes exist and signiﬁcant research eﬀorts are still needed
to make the technology mature.
To start with, numerical models of ﬂoating wind turbines are quite essential on the improve-
ment of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind industry for various reasons. Their main purpose is the design
veriﬁcation through load analysis. A numerical model can be demonstrated and validated by
comparisons with experimental results (lab-scale tests) and by providing good agreement could
push the wind industry to invest more in this speciﬁc ﬁeld. In addition, numerical models,
6
==PAGE:19==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE
in general, can save a huge amount of money from companies, since the cost is much less
compared to experiments. However, as it has been said, experimental results are needed for a
proper validation.
More speciﬁcally, for the dynamic analysis of a FOWT, a numerical model is needed. It contains
the aerodynamics, hydrodynamics, mooring, structural response and the applied controller. All
these inputs are set in the appropriate aero-hydro-servo-elastic code, such as HAWC2 (Horizon-
tal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation). For this project, the aforementioned
code was used, making comparisons with the experimental results in terms of the in-plane
ﬂoater motions (surge, heave and pitch).
The aim, as well as, the inspiration for the project is to match, in the most appropriate way,
the numerical model with the data derived from the test campaign in order to help for further
progress of similar simulations.
The response of the ﬂoater was tested for cases of wind-
induced, wave-induced and the combination of these loads.
Diﬀerent types of waves were
examined (regular, irregular and focused) for diﬀerent environmental conditions, according to
the experiments. Apart from the improvement of similar numerical models, one outcome of this
project is the obtained knowledge to be used for future experimental setups of scaled ﬂoating
wind turbines.
In that way, a deeper understanding of underlying physics could be achieved through the
validation process and better numerical models can be acquired in order to increase the trust
on numerical analyses.
After that, a much lower safety factors could be used, resulting in
cheaper ﬂoaters and more competitive prices compared to other alternatives. Thus, ﬂoating
wind turbines would be more cost-eﬀective, providing a solution to exploit the abundant wind
resource in deep water.
To address the above-mentioned problem, this report is structured in six chapters, including the
introduction and concussion parts. In the main part, in chapter 2, some of the basic theory, as
an introduction to the reader, is provided with some related previous studies on ﬂoating wind
turbines and the most important outcomes of them. Chapter 3 elaborates the experimental
setup as well as the diﬀerent environmental conditions, followed by chapter 4 which describes
brieﬂy the followed procedure for the calibration method during this numerical study as well as
how each case was handled. The last before conclusion, chapter 5, presents some of the results
and explains in more detail both the illustrated results and the followed procedures during this
project.

terms of surge and heave, whilst some discrepancies were observed for pitch, nacelle acceleration
and line tension.

Scaling in experiments
Scaling in the experiments is a topic of high importance with a lot of research [33] [34]. Con-
cerning the experiments of ﬂoating wind turbines the topic of scaling is more complicated. The
ideal way of experiments for ﬂoating wind turbines would be a proper scaling for both hy-
drodynamic and aerodynamic loads. These loads are more dependant on two non-dimensional
numbers, Froude and Reynolds number, respectively.
To start with, the equations that characterize the aforementioned non-dimensional numbers are
presented below (Equations 3.1, 3.2), and the physical meaning of them is described.

• ρ: is the density of the air
• ν: is the dynamic viscosity of the ﬂuid

The Froude number deals with the relationship between inertial forces and gravitational forces,
whilst the Reynolds number is determined as the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces in a
ﬂow. The similarity requirements posed by the Froude and Reynolds numbers can typically
not be satisﬁed simultaneously. It is then necessary to decide which are the dominant forces
according to which the scaling must be done. It may then be necessary to compensate for the
eﬀect of the other force. Until now, it is quite diﬃcult during the experiments of ﬂoating wind
turbines, both numbers to be preserved. For this reason, it is more common to preserve the
Froude number [33], while some adjustments are done for the Reynolds one [34].

used during the experiments of TetraSpar ﬂoater.

Calibration of turbulence intensity
By analyzing the wind speed signals derived from diﬀerent positions, diﬀerences in terms of
turbulence intensity were evident. In the existed numerical model the inputs regarding turbu-
lence intensity indicated high discrepancies compared to the experiments. The previous work
on TetraSpar ﬂoater [27] was mostly focused on wave loading. For this reason, a research about
the wind speed in terms of spectra was decided as the best choice in order to determine the
inputs used in wind module of HAWC2.
The software HAWC2 uses the Mann model format. The fundamentals of the Mann model is
isotropic turbulence in neutral atmospheric conditions and the energy spectrum is given based
on the Von Karman spectrum [35]. For this reason, in the following comparisons of wind speed
spectra between numerical model and experiments, the theoretical Von Karman spectrum is
also illustrated. The main aim was the determination of the turbulence parameters in HAWC2
software in order to obtain a good match between wind speed spectrum from experiments and
the numerical model, keeping in mind that the agreement of the ﬂoater motions is of highest
importance for an accurate numerical model. Before describing the followed procedure and
illustrating the obtained results, for the sake of information, some basic theory of turbulence is
explained below, focusing on the parameters used as inputs in the numerical model.
More speciﬁcally, the whole process was done in order to determine the needed turbulence
parameters to be inserted as inputs in HAWC2.
The Mann turbulence format was chosen
and for this reason the determination of both length scale L and αε2/3 parameter was needed.
According to the HAWC2 manual [35], typical values of L and αε2/3 are 29.4 and 1, respectively.
Furthermore, another parameter in Mann turbulence model Γ is recommended to be equal to
3.9 for non-isotropic turbulence and normal atmospheric conditions.
In this case, this does not apply for a number of reasons. First of all, it is a lab-scale numerical
model and for this reason these suggestions cannot be used directly in the model. In addition,
the conditions during the experiments were considered with isotropic turbulence. Thus, the
properties of the turbulence like variance and turbulent length scale are identical for all three
directions, corresponding to vortex structures being circular instead of a more elliptic shape
for non-isotropic. Furthermore, the dont_scale HAWC2 command was used [35], changing the
turbulence intensity in an indirect way with changes of the parameter αε2/3.
To start with, the deﬁnition of turbulence intensity (Equation 4.3) is the ratio of the wind
speed standard deviation to the mean wind speed, taken over a speciﬁed period of time [37].
The mean wind speed is the averaged wind speed over 10 minutes.

4.4
Hydrodynamic damping
As it was stated in section 4.3 and it can be also observed from the initial presented simulations
in Appendix A, the numerical model was modiﬁed for a better reproduction when the ﬂoating
wind turbine is exposed to wind-only loads. After calibrating the wind, further calibration of
damping matrix B was necessary in order to obtain a good match in the ﬂoater responses.
The hydrodynamic damping was changed in the corresponding module in HAWC2. The aim of
these changes was to obtain an error below 5% in the standard deviation of the signal responses
[20]. The calibration was done for surge, heave and pitch and this is why the matrix elements
B11, B33 and B55 were changed, anticipating an agreement of the numerical model compared
to the experiments.
Although, all these cases are wind-induced, the hydrodynamic damping still aﬀects the re-
sponses. According to research such as [39] and [40], BEM code may not compute correctly or
in the most eﬃcient way the aerodynamic loads for FOWT and this is why changes in matrix
B were implemented, resulting in a better converge between the numerical and experimental
results.
Nonetheless, this strategy was also followed for wave-induced cases. The damping calibration
was diﬀerent for each sea state as it is also described below.
The followed procedure was
in accordance to previous studies in this ﬁeld [19]. According to a previous study [23], by
increasing the environmental condition a higher damping is observed and this is why each sea
state has to be calibrated separately.
Afterwards, the add of damping from wind-induced cases and wave-induced cases was used for
the cases of wind and wave load combination as an initial approach to the combined loads. It was
expected that it could give a good converge but further calibration was needed by observing the
statistics of the obtained results. The procedure is brieﬂy described in this section but further
details, as well as the results of these simulations are presented in the following chapter 5 and
the corresponding, to this chapter, appendices.
The linear hydrodynamic damping in ESYSWamit part of HAWC2 was manually changed and
the way the ﬂoater responded to these changes was evaluated. The procedure of damping cali-
bration started for wind-induced cases followed by wave-induced and the combination of wind
and waves. Generally, the numerical model, after calibrating the wind speed, underestimated
the responses in comparison with the experimental data. This fact led to the decision to de-
crease the hydrodynamic damping values in order to achieve as accurate results as possible.
This kind of calibration was also applied for all the diﬀerent environmental conditions. As it
was denoted earlier, the agreement of the obtained results was evaluated by keeping in mind
that the error between the standard deviation of the signal responses (surge, heave and pitch)
from HAWC2 and experiments is below 5%.

Second-order hydrodynamics
Regarding the cases with wave loads, the ﬁrst-order wave forcing was the initial approach, but
the comparison between HAWC2 responses and the corresponding experimental ones resulted
in the fact that the contribution from sub-harmonics was crucial and this is why the Newman
approximation was used in order to take into account the second-order loads. The results after
implementing the Newman approximation in the model are presented in the following chapter 5.
The ability of using this method in this speciﬁc case has been elaborated in chapter 2. By taking
into account the second-order hydrodynamics the system is able to capture any excitation in low
frequencies range. In the following Figure 4.9 a wave-induced case of EC03 is illustrated.

6
Conclusions and further research
6.1
Conclusions
A numerical model of a 1:60 scaled DTU 10MW RWT [18] mounted on TetraSpar ﬂoater [16]
[17] was investigated and validated against experimental data from 2017, in order to estimate
the ability of the model to replicate the basin tests. The structure was excited to diﬀerent
loads, such as wind, waves and the combination of them. Especially for the wind speed, a
calibration in terms of power spectral density was established so as to obtain a compliance
with the measurement turbulence intensity. Regarding the wave loads, diﬀerent cases were
examined, such as regular, irregular and focused waves. The data were obtained by using the
corresponding wave gauges for either aligned or misaligned cases and the appropriate input
was used for the numerical model in HAWC2. Further investigation was done, for wind-only,
irregular-waves-only, as well as wind with irregular waves, in terms of system damping. The
combined loading cases were calibrated for both aligned and misaligned waves. The followed
methodology was described in chapter 4, while the results and comparisons between model and
tests are illustrated in both chapter 5 and the corresponding appendices (Appendix A to E). It
should be noted that both ﬁrst-order and second-order hydrodynamics, by using the Newman
approximation, were investigated and the majority of the presented results contain the second-
order forces. It was described how the aforementioned loading beneﬁts and validation of the
numerical model, especially for lower environmental conditions (chapter 5).
In the ﬁnal chosen numerical model, with the additional second-order hydrodynamics, a fur-
ther calibration was applied, by implementing changes on the linear diagonal elements of the
damping matrix. The procedure was based on a trial and error method and the calibration aim
was to obtain a lower than 5% error in standard deviation of in-plane motions signals. This
aim was able to achieved for all examined cases but misaligned ones, in which the model pitch
motion was highly over-predicted compared to the experiments. Diﬀerent damping coeﬃcients
were used for diﬀerent loading scenarios, as well as for each sea state separately. In general,
it could be said that the model was able to replicate the measured responses after damping
matrix calibration. Some damping values were examined in terms of their inﬂuence in the
structure and compared to the corresponding decay tests for qualitative checks. Nevertheless,
it should be done for each diﬀerent scenario in order to be able to evaluate the calibration
method in a better way, having in-deep knowledge of the behavior of the structure. Finally,
the obtained results and comparisons denote a good agreement and some main ﬁndings for
wind-only, waves-only and aligned combination of them are presented below.
For wind-only loads, a good match was obtained. This agreement was much better for EC03 and
ECmax, while for sea state with the rated wind speed the highest discrepancies were observed.
The EC05 shows the highest deviations in terms of surge mean value and for this reason further
investigation in both mooring lines and controller details, or even tuning, are recommended.
The selected damping values for EC05 had an opposite trend, by comparing them with below
53
==PAGE:66==

and above rated wind speed sea states. Both EC03 and ECmax indicate that there is missing
forcing, which is not the case for EC05. The uncertainties of BEM model for aerodynamic
calculations in ﬂoating wind turbines [39] [40], may explain this trend.
Regarding wave loads, although, regular, irregular and focused waves were simulated, some con-
clusions about the irregular ones are presented. This kind of waves were the cases which further
calibrated against the experimental data, while both regular and focused waves are areas of
further investigation. A good match between model and tests was acquired. Small sea states
had energy content in motions’ natural frequencies, while for EC64 and EC11 energy content
was also notable in wave frequency. In general, these latter environmental conditions appeared
a quite better match in comparison with the others. According to this behavior, it could be
concluded that second-order hydrodynamics clearly inﬂuence the system for small environmen-
tal conditions and maybe, Newman approximation or assumptions during the calculation of
these loads, such as the contribution of ﬁrst-order motion, could aﬀect the accuracy in more
severe sea states. This assumption during the calculation of second-order loads may lead to
underprediction of second-order loads for higher environmental conditions as it is observe from
the obtained results, fact that is also stated in [23]. Moreover, the fact that higher environ-
mental conditions have a stronger damping inﬂuences the observable peak in low frequencies.
For wave-only cases a conclusion about the selected diagonal damping values is not easy to be
obtained. However, the mostly negative values of damping compensate for the missing wave
excitation forces in the model.
Concerning the combination of wind and irregular waves, it could be deduced that a good
match was obtained. Again, the big diﬀerences in time series plots (in terms of mean values)
may indicate that the wind ﬁeld needs a diﬀerent and probably more accurate approach so as
to even more decrease any possible uncertainties. In general, the presence of wind increased the
deviations between model and experiments, leading to the recommendation of a more elaborated
approach. Regarding damping values it was noted that a higher value than the addition of the
obtained damping from wind-induced and wave-induced cases was needed in order to achieve a
good agreement between model and tests. Nevertheless, EC05 did not follow this trend, having
an arbitrary correlation as it was observed in the illustrated bar charts in section 5.5.
Finally, for misaligned cases, the combination of wind and irregular waves was simulated and
a damping calibration was done. It could be said that higher discrepancies were observed for
pitch motion and the calibration procedure was not as eﬀective as for the rest of the in-plane
motions. However, the eﬀect of misalignment indicates that the aerodynamic damping is not
so eﬀective, resulting in higher loads experienced by the ﬂoating wind turbine.
6.2
Suggestions for further research
In this section some suggestion for further development of numerical models or even the existed
one are presented.
To start with, some general suggestions related to this speciﬁc numerical model could be the
investigation in the external DLL ﬁle used for wave kinematics in HAWC2 software. As it has
been referred in the main part, due to software limitation, the full duration of the experiments
could not be simulated in HAWC2. Although, a way for full simulations was found, and it
was also applied for some of the presented results in subsection 5.3.1. This method was quite
time-consuming skyrocketing the required time from 1h to 60h. Hence, a calibration process,
lasting similar to the experiments time, is problematic for further development of this numerical
54
==PAGE:67==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER RESEARCH
model. However, full simulations could provide more accurate results as it was also noted from
the related comparisons of wind and irregular waves cases and thus it would be a preferable
approach.
Additionally, the existed numerical model could be enhanced by elaborating the mooring line
tensions as well as the controller behavior in each diﬀerent scenario. Further analysis on the
aforementioned issues will improve the numerical model, making it more robust and reliable.
Moreover, analyses of other existed experimental measurements, such as misaligned waves with-
out the presence of the wind, could be investigated, having the change to conclude in more
prosperous statements during these cases.
Furthermore, the obtained results regarding the rated wind speed (EC05) leads to uncertainties
related to the estimated wind ﬁeld. Moreover, it creates thoughts about the proper calculation of
aerodynamic loads through BEM model and if it is applicable for ﬂoating wind turbines. Thus,
elaboration of these issues and use of diﬀerent models, such as CFD are recommended.
In addition, assumptions about the calculation of second-order forces were earlier discussed
and a diﬀerent way to calculate them could further improve the obtained results. However,
the required costs of each approach in terms of time and money have to be taken into account,
since there is not always a positive ratio or the need of a more detailed analysis. Although,
the use of Newman approximation seems to be acceptable for this speciﬁc project, a full QTF
analysis of second-order hydrodynamics may provide a more accurate numerical model.
To sum up, research on numerical models of ﬂoating wind turbines could enhance this ﬁeld,
feeding developers with more diﬀerent cases and expanding the literature of ﬂoating wind
turbines. It helps in the expansion of this ﬁeld and push the scientiﬁc community one step
further for easily assessment of ﬂoating wind parks, making them competitive in terms of
prices.
