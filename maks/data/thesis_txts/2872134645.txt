Abstract
This thesis introduces a novel pipeline, RiverSeg. Designed to generate dynamic river
graphs from topological encouraged water extent masks. These masks are produced by a
U-Net model trained on remote sensing imagery using weakly supervised labels, derived
from a threshold applied on the Normalized Difference Water Index. The RiverSeg pipeline
transforms raster based segmentation masks into vectorized graph representations.
The U-Net models where trained using Sentinel 1, Sentinel 2 and DEM data, with a focus
on encouraging topological connectivity through the use of the clDice function. Exper-
iments suggest the standard U-Net trained using clDice, without including DEM data,
provided the best trade-off between segmentation accuracy and topological connectivity.
The predicted mask were spatially stitched together to from a large water mask covering
a large area. The large water masks were refined to have a cleaned water mask, that is
transformed into a vectorized representation, using a series of graph construction tech-
niques.
These graphs were evaluated using the weakly labelled, pseudo, ground truth
masks, and compared against the SWORD representation of the rivers. Results indicate
that RiverSeg captures finer details, at the cost of introducing more fragmentation com-
pared to SWORD.
Finally a case study utilizing SWOT elevation data, demonstrates RiverSeg’s ability to
support local slope estimation around river islands. Something impossible with SWORD
graphs. The findings highlight RiverSeg is a viable and flexible tool for generating de-
tailed dynamic river graph representations, relevant for remote sensing and hydrological
analysis.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As the impact of climate changes intensifies, monitoring and understanding surface water
bodies such as rivers, has become increasingly critical. Rivers are essential components for
the global water cycles and play a crucial role in flood monitoring, hydrological modelling
and ecosystem health. Accurate maps of river extent and structure are foundation for
a wide range of applications from sediment transport studies to water surface elevation
analysis.
Traditionally, such maps are acquired using optical satellite imagery.
In recent years,
optical satellite imagery has become easily available due to European Space Agency’s
(ESA) Sentinel 2 missions. While optical imagery provides high spatial resolution and is
effective under clear sky, it suffers from several limitations. Optical imagery, is unavailable
at night, and it often fails in persistently overcast or foggy regions due to the weather
conditions.
To overcome these limitations, Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery offers a valuable
property.
One of ESA’s other missions has been to send up satellites capturing SAR
imagery, making it easily available. Unlike optical sensors, SAR can pierce cloud cover
and operate independently of daylight.
These properties make it ideal for monitoring
during all conditions at any time of the day. Combining Sentinel 1’s SAR and Sentinel 2’s
optical imagery potentially along with a Digital Elevation Model (DEM), opens for more
robust and consistent monitoring of river systems.
While ESA’s Sentinel missions aim to provide continuously updating imagery for monitor-
ing Earth’s surface, NASA has simultaneously been developing the global river database
SWORD (Surface Water and Ocean Topography River Database).
SWORD provides
global river centerlines as a precomputed graph, consisting of vertices and edges. While
SWORD is useful for different hydrological studies and applications, such as estimating
river slopes demonstrated by Normandin et al (2024)[2], it is static. I.e. each river segment
is defined only once and does not change over time. As such, this limitation makes the
river graphs less useful for studying temporal changes, such as seasonal morphology shifts,
flood events or local slope estimation in braided or meandering rivers.
This limitation, motivate the need for an alternative method to generate dynamic, topo-
logically accurate river graphs, that can adapt to changing river conditions. Existing seg-
mentation methods either rely solely on Sentinel-2 imagery or combine SAR and optical
data, yet both approaches typically overlook the importance of topological connectivity.
This lack of focus on the connectivity accuracy limits the usefulness in downstream ap-
plications such as slope analysis, or flow modelling. A well connected river segmentation,
enables more meaningful graph based representations of river systems, which can support
a wider range of research topics.
1.2
Problem statement
Despite advancements in water monitoring from optical and SAR imagery, segmenting
rivers accurately, from the topological point of view, remains a challenging task, espe-

Related work
2.1
Remote sensing based surface water segmentation
Surface water monitoring has long relied on imagery from optical or radar-based satellites.
Accurate monitoring is crucial for fields like hydrological studies, ecological studies and for
flood management. Optical sensors like the ones used on Sentinel 2 provide high resolution
multispectral bands, that can be used for water monitoring [3]. Optical data is, however,
sensitive to weather conditions, making the imagery useless in cloudy cover. Sentinel 1’s
SAR imagery provide robustness under such cloudy conditions. (An example of this can
be seen in fig. 5.3) Many early works [4] use these modalities individually, often relying
on traditional simple indices or other classic machine learning techniques.
2.1.1
Traditional methods using optical imagery
Prior to the emergence of deep learning, surface water mapping using optical imagery was
often addressed through thresholding of spectral water indices, such as the Normalized
Difference Water Index (NDWI) (McFeeters, 1996)[5]. The idea for such indexes is based
upon the fact that the different sensors, measuring the different optical bands, capture
different amounts of reflected wavelengths. For instance, higher values in the green band
indicate a more vegetated area. Two indices where proposed by McFeeters (1996)[5] and
Xu (2006)[6].
The NDWI and MNDWI are two indices that based on these physical
properties are able to separate water and non water from each other. The indicators work
on a per pixel level and as long as no clouds cover water bodies, they should be able to
identify them. By nature the indices values are in the range [-1,1], where a positive number
indicates a pixel is likely to be water. A more thorough run-through of the indices is given
in the theory section. (See section 3.2.1)
2.1.2
Traditional methods using SAR
In parallel to optical imagery, Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imagery exists, offering a
unique property. The sensor used for SAR imagery is not hindered by clouds, making it
useful for monitoring during any weather conditions. Additionally due to the way SAR
sensors functions (they emit a signal and measure the backscatter) the satellite works
during day and night regardless of light conditions. Unlike optical imagery it is not possible
to create an effective segmentation mask using an index similar to the ones above. SAR
measures backscatter rather than reflectance in specific spectral bands. That means there
is no straight forward physical relationship to exploit. In SAR imagery the appearance of
water depends on factors like, incidence angle and polarization.
An extensive overview of past and current water body segmentation methods is provided by
Guo et al. (2022)[4]. Segmenting water bodies from SAR imagery is done largely based on
classic machine learning approaches or simple traditional classic image analysis methods.
Classic machine learning methods, such as random forest, support vector machines and
Markov random fields, are all techniques that have been used to segment water bodies
from SAR imagery. Other classic image analysis methods like canny edge detection[7] and
Otsu thresholding[8], are also used techniques for segmenting water from optical imagery.
The key challenge with SAR data is the amount of speckle noise produced when capturing
the imagery. (An example of the speckle noise can be seen later in fig. 4.9) Machine learning
techniques try to group water pixels together but due to the speckle noise present in SAR
imagery it makes it diﬀicult to group pixels correctly together[4]. With the increasing

2.2
Deep learning approaches to water segmentation
In recent years, deep learning has quickly become the dominant approach for many types of
segmentation tasks, including surface water segmentation. Convolutional neural network
(CNN) based models such as U-Nets and DeepLab, have shown great results due to their
ability to learn complex spatial features from large training datasets[4]. Along with the
rise of deep learning, increased attention has been paid to multimodal fusion of SAR and
optical imagery.
Konapala et al. (2021)[9] and Peña et al. (2023)[10] have explored this direction. Konapala
et al. combined SAR imagery with features derived from optical data, such as NDWI and
HSV transformed bands, using a U-Net for segmentation.
Their results indicate that
fusing SAR and optical imagery improves the segmentations during cloudy weather, while
for clear day the segmentation masks remain mostly unchanged.
Peña et al.
(2023)
proposed a self-supervised approach in which a deep learning model was trained using
knowledge distillation. NDWI was used as a teacher model to generate pseudo labels,
which were used to supervise a CNN, trained exclusively on SAR imagery. Their results
showed improved performance compared to other unsupervised segmentation methods.
As for all other deep learning tasks, a major hurdle is the vast amount of data needed to
train robust models. The method proposed by Peña et al. (2023) did not use manually
annotated data. Another approach, focusing on limited data, was explored by Pai et al.
(2020) [11]. They explored the idea of using generative models to create more synthetic
data to train a segmentation model on. In their paper they propose a GAN based ap-
proach to generate synthetic SAR like imagery and improve surface water segmentation
performance that way. Although the method was limited to SAR data only it showed
improved performance, compared to models only trained on real data.
2.3
Topology aware segmentation methods
A well known challenge when segmenting small, narrow structures, like roads, blood vessels
or rivers, is the tendency for CNN based models to produce fragmented outputs. In other
words, objects that should form a single, continuos structure appear as broken segments in
the resulting mask. Although each pixel prediction is informed by a large receptive field,
the classification is performed independently per pixel. In certain applications, preserving
the topology, that is the connectivity and continuity, is as important as achieving a high
pixel-wise accuracy. For instance, in road networks, blood vessel structure or river systems,
understanding how different parts connect is important for downstream tasks.
Zeng et al.
(2015) [12] presents a rule-based post-processing method to connect river
segments after water body detection. Human made structure like bridges or dams can,
in optical imagery, disturb the natural flow of water, making it such that what should
have been a continuos segmentation ends up being split into disconnected fragments. The
method works by rescaling the mask to various smaller resolutions. Based on four criteria;
gap width, river direction consistency, river width consistency and minimum river seg-
ment length, the mask is modified in an attempt to increase the topological structure of
the segmentation mask. The method proposed work well, when reconnecting fragmented
river segments into a topologically consistent network. While the algorithm improves the
continuity of river segmentations, it requires tuning four hyperparameters for every single
segmentation. This makes it a tedious and timely method. Hence, for large amounts of

Beyond post-processing methods, different types of topological-aware loss functions have
also been researched. The goal with such loss functions is to encourage better connectiv-
ity in predicted segmentation masks during model training. TopoSeg presented by He et
al. (2023)[13] and clDice presented by Shit et al. (2021)[14] are two such loss functions.
TopoSeg was developed for instance segmentation of densely packed and overlapping nuclei
in medical images. TopoSeg relies on two components: a topology-aware module (TAM),
that encodes structural features (i.e. interior, boundary and background) using persistent
homology, and an adaptive topology-aware selection (ATS) strategy highlighting regions
with topological errors. While TopoSeg is effective in its intended domain, it is a compu-
tationally heavy loss to compute. This makes it an inappropriate loss for structures like
rivers. In contrast, clDice is a more relevant topology-aware loss for segmenting elongated
connected structures such as blood vessels or rivers. clDice calculates a similarity score,
based on the intersection of segmentation masks and their morphological skeletons. The
authors propose a differentiable version of clDice, soft-clDice. Soft-clDice was made such
that it could be integrated into the training of neural networks, to preserve topological
structure. A detailed explanation of clDice is provided in section 3.5.2.
2.4
Graph based representations of river networks
Rivers are naturally formed systems that can contain complex branching, and flow dynam-
ics. Rasterized segmentation masks can capture such complexities. A more convenient
and structured way to describe the river, is by representing the river as a graph. Graphs
provide a framework for modelling aspects like reach length estimation, connectivity anal-
ysis and water routing analysis. Graph representations also allow for integration with
other hydrological datasets, such as the frequently updated Surface Water and Ocean
Topography (SWOT) data, provided by NASA. This enables the association of physical
measurements with discrete river segments. These graphs can thus be used as the back-
bone for river science and other remote sensing applications.
Several datasets provide graph-based representations of river networks. Notably, NASA’s
SWORD (Surface water and ocean topography river database) offers global coverage of
all rivers wider than 30 m[15]. SWORD covers over 200.000 reaches1 and consists of more
than 10 million nodes. Nodes and edges contain attributes such as reach_id, sinuosity,
reach_length and many more [15], all used to describe the river. The graph representa-
tion provided by SWORD are designed in such a way that complex river structures are
simplified. In braided river systems, rather than representing each braid, SWORD ap-
proximates the system with a single, average path. An example of this is shown in fig. 2.1.
Additionally, the river graphs in SWORD are static, i.e., not updated over time, they do
not reflect physical changes in river morphology.
2.5
Applications of river graphs and SWOT data
With the launch of NASAs SWOT mission, new opportunities for elevation profiling of
surface water bodies have been introduced. Graph based representations of river networks
can be leveraged to align SWOTs water surface elevations along predefined river paths.
The aligned elevation profiles enable estimation of metrics such as river slope.
Normandin et al. (2024)[2] demonstrated one of the first practical uses of SWOT data
for slop estimation across rivers in the Congo Basin, a large river basin in central Africa.

2.6
Gaps in existing work
While progress has been made in both segmentation accuracy and in fusing SAR and
optical imagery, gaps still remain unexplored. Few studies focus exclusively on river seg-
mentation as opposed to general water bodies. As a result, datasets such as S1S2-Water
presented by Wieland et al. (2024)[16] contains all types of water bodies, i.e. lakes, wet-
lands and rivers.
Second, topological connectivity in segmentation masks is often overlooked. Current meth-
ods mainly focus on pixel-wise accuracy and neglect the focus of topological connectivity.
While topology aware methods like clDice [14] exist, their application in surface water
mapping remains unexplored. Current approaches for remote sensing data is to apply
hand tuned post-processing steps as in the work by Zeng et al. (2015). The existence
of prior work on topology for river segmentations suggest that topological structure does
matter in practice. The tuning of 4 parameters makes the method unsuitable for auto-
mated uses.
Lastly, there is a lack of methods for automatically generating river graphs directly from
segmentation outputs. Existing approaches rely on static river network databases such
as SWORD, which do not capture temporal changes to the river morphology. In prior
studies they do not integrate segmentation results into downstream applications. Often
the masks are used directly for, e.g. flood monitoring. This disconnection between seg-

3.1
Remote sensing satellites
3.1.1
Sentinel 1 SAR
The Sentinel 1 mission part of ESA’s Copernicus programme, currently consists of two
satellites (Sentinel 1A and Sentinel 1B) equipped with a C-band synthetic-aperture radar[17].
The satellites operate in the same orbit but are positioned 180° apart to ensure more fre-
quent coverage. With a two satellite constellation the revisit time at the equator is six
days, i.e. it is possible get new imagery every sixth day[18, 19]. Sentinel 1 operates us-
ing a Interferometric Wide (IW) swath mode, the IW mode offers a spatial resolution of
approximately 10 meters [17]. SAR create images by emitting radio wave pulses towards
earths surface and measuring the returned signal. The wave is possible to transmit in two
different types of polarisations: Horizontal or vertical [20]. Along with the transmission
polarisation it is also possible to choose the polarisation of the received signal. Typically,
the same polarization is used for both transmitting and reception. This signal, or backscat-
ter, varies depending on the surface the wave hits. Properties like moisture content and
roughness of surface, affect the backscatter giving different surfaces different intensities,
which then can be translated into a 2D image. A key benefit of SAR imagery, is its inde-
pendence from weather and its invariance to time. This makes the imagery particularly
useful for analysis of things like flood detection and river mapping, where cloud cover often
obstructs optical sensors. A downfall with SAR imagery is that it is subject to speckle
noise. Speckle noise from in the imagery, must be handle prior to any actual work using
the data. Later in the data section my preprocessing steps are described. (Section 4.1.6)
3.1.2
Sentinel 2 optical imagery
Another mission part of ESA’s Copernicus programme is the Sentinel 2 missions. Like
Sentinel 1 it consist of two satellites (Sentinel 2A and Sentinel 2B) orbiting earth with
a revisit time of 5 days[21].
The satellites are equipped with a multispectral imaging
instruments. The multispectral instrument captures 13 different bands, four of which are
visible bands, 6 are NIR and the remaining 3 are short-wave infrared (SWIR) bands [3]. In
contrast to Sentinel 1, Sentinel 2 captures optical imagery instead of SAR imagery. Rather
than transmitting a signal and measuring the backscatter, the instrument measures the
reflected sunlight. Figure 3.1 illustrates this, optical imagery relies on reflected signals
which means in the presence of clouds, the clouds, will cover parts of the imagery. As
optical imagery measure the reflected signals, optical imagery must be captured during
the day under great light conditions. In low light conditions the reflected signals does not
return strong enough.
The 13 multispectral bands, highlight a variety of things, making different bands useful
for different applications. The spatial resolution of the bands also vary, the most detailed
resolution is 10 m, available for four bands.

3.2
Surface water indicators
3.2.1
Normalized difference water index (NDWI)
A common way to segment water from optical satellite imagery is to use the normalized
difference water index (NDWI). Originally proposed in The use of the Normalized Differ-
ence Water Index (NDWI) in the delineation of open water features McFeeters (1996)[5],
the NDWI is a simple method to generate a layer describing the intensity of water presence
at each pixel location.
The NDWI is computed using the green and near-infrared (NIR) spectral bands defined
in the spatial domain Ω⊆R2. Where Bgreen : Ω→R and BNIR : Ω→R. The NDWI is a
pixel-wise value, for each pixel x ∈Ωthe NDWI is computed as:
NDWI(x) = Bgreen(x) −BNIR(x)
Bgreen(x) + BNIR(x),
Bgreen(x) + BNIR(x) ̸= 0
By the definition of the NDWI its values are bounded within the closed interval:
NDWI(x) ∈[−1, 1]
These two bands are selected due to their physical properties. A property of optical bands
is that they measure light waves reflected from the surface of the earth. Different sensor
capture different wave lengths like the visible green band or the NIR band. This means
that the reflectance in the green band over water captures low to moderate reflectance
values, as water absorbs most of the green wavelengths. NIR bands are not possible to
see with the human eye. A key property of NIR is that it is especially good at capturing
biomass and vegetation, making it suitable for mapping shorelines [3].
The key to NDWI’s effectiveness, lies in the unique spectral properties of different surfaces.
Water absorbs most NIR waves, but reflects some green light. This leads to a high NDWI
value (close to 1).
Vegetation reflects both green and NIR waves, resulting in low or
negative NDWI. Soil and urban areas generally have moderate reflectance in both bands,
producing NDWI values near zero or slightly negative. The relationship is summarized in

3.3
River network datasets: SWORD and SWOT
The surface water and ocean topography river database (SWORD) provides, in the form
of graphs, global representations of river networks. The database is constructed using
different global hydrography databases, that provide different information [15]. In the
oﬀicial introductory paper for SWORD Altenau et al. (2021) [15] state that: The goal for
reach definition in SWORD is to maximize SWOT observation coverage along a reach,
while respecting natural and anthropogenic hydrologic boundaries and barriers. In other
words, SWORD on its own is not the main product rather its a product used together
with SWOT.
The surface water and ocean topography (SWOT) mission, launched in 2022, provides data
and information about the oceans, lakes, rivers or wetlands [24]. SWOT measures water
elevation in high resolution, within 1 km from land. Over land SWOT enable observations
of water surfaces for rivers wider than 100 m [24]. There is frequently new observations
available from SWOT. On average, each location is observed once or twice within a 21
day cycle [25].
3.4
Deep learning for segmentation
3.4.1
U-Net
A widely used deep learning architecture for image segmentation tasls is the U-Net, orig-
inally proposed by Ronneberger et al.
(2015) [26].
Since its introduction, the U-Net
architecture has become one of the most cited architectures in the field of deep learning,
with over 100.000 citations according to Google Scholar.
U-Nets are particularly well-suited for pixel-wise prediction tasks due to its structure.
The architecture consists of two main parts:
• Encoder (contracting path): Performs downsampling to extract increasingly ab-
stract feature representations.
• Decoder (expanding path): Performs upsampling to gradually reconstruct the
spatial resolution and produce a segmentation map.
The contracting part consists of a sequence of convolutional layers followed by ReLU
activation and max-pooling operations. With each step the spatial dimension of the feature
maps are reduced, while its feature channels increases. This enables the network to learn
high-level representations of the input.
The expanding part in contrast restores the spatial dimension using up-convolutions. At
each upsampling step, the network concatenates the corresponding feature maps from the
contracting part with skip connections. The skip connections help preserve spatial details

The final output layer of the U-Net is a n-channel prediction map. To produce pixel-wise

3.4.2
Dynamic U-Net
While the standard U-Net is a widely used network architecture, many modified variants
have been proposed to improve its flexibility, self-configuration, and overall performance.
Some of these works include: nnU-Net: Self-adapting Framework for U-Net-Based Medical
Image Segmentation [27], Automated Design of Deep Learning Methods for Biomedical
Image Segmentation [28], and Optimized U-Net for Brain Tumor Segmentation [29].
Implemented in the MONAI framework the network architecture ”Dynamic U-Net” (Dy-
nUNet), is implemented based upon some of the advancements made in the work above.
The implementation integrates some of the key concepts from said, work to enhance
adaptability and performance for image segmentations. While all three papers focus on
medical image segmentation, the different advancements are generalizable to all types of
segmentation task including segmentations from satellite imagery.

3.5.2
Topologically aware Loss function
A common challenge encountered in segmentation models, is the lack of connectivity in
the predicted segmentation masks. In particular, objects that should have been segmented
as a single continuous structure, may be broken into multiple disconnected parts. This
is especially problematic when, e.g,. segmenting blood vessels in medical imagery, where
capturing thin well connected structures, is crucial for research tasks [14].
The same
can be said for segmentation of rivers.
To understand the behaviour of a river, it is
crucial to obtain a well connected segmentation. When a segmentation is fragmented, the
resulting mask can misrepresent flow paths and connectivity, which ultimately limits the
usefulness for downstream tasks. One approach addressing the issue with the topology is
to incorporate a loss function that penalizes disconnected or fragmented predictions. Shit
et al. (2021) proposes such a method with the introduction of centerline Dice (clDice).
clDice is a topology aware loss function aiming to preserve the connectivity of segmented
structures.

Skeletonization
Skeletonization is a way to reduce a structure to its simplest form.
Unlike the soft-
skeletonization proposed by Shit et al. (2021) [14], whose goal is to make a differentiable
approximation of the skeleton structures.
Widely used skeletonization techniques can
automatically skeletonize an image to the thinnest form possible. A widely used skele-
tonization technique is proposed by Zhang et al.
(1984) [33].
The method preserves
topology i.e. pixel connectivity and is fast. The method works by iteratively removing
boundary pixels from the binary image while preserving the topology of the structure. For
each iteration the algorithm examines each pixel, and applies a set of logical conditions
to determine whether the pixel can be removed without removing endpoints or breaking
the connectivity. It is done in two sub steps, where specific pixels patterns are identified.
The algorithm also ensures that the object is thinned symmetrically meaning that the
remaining skeleton is also the centerline of the object. When the context is not about
clDice or soft skeletonization, it can be assumed that the skeletonization algorithm used
is the one proposed in Zhang et al. (1984) work.

To address this two distinct strategies are used to construct the dataset. The strategies are
intended to capture diverse weather conditions. The two acquisition strategies are referred
to as the tightly constrained strategy and the loosely constrained strategy.
Tightly constrained strategy
The first strategy is designed to provide as precise as possible segmentations, at the cost of
ignoring cloudy weather. The strategy is subject to strict constraints, primarily regarding
the time period ∆t between the acquisition times of Sentinel 1 and Sentinel 2 bands. The
constraints used for the strategy are as follows:
• Clear sky: Sentinel 2 imagery must be acquired on a day with cloud cover κ = 0%
cloud cover, ensuring completely clear-sky satellite imagery.
• Temporal consistency: The corresponding Sentinel 1 images must be captured
within ∆t = 1 day of the Sentinel 2 images.
• Multiple timestamps per location: Each location may include multiple times-
tamps of Sentinel 1 and Sentinel 2 images, allowing seasonal variations.
Each of the three constraints serves a distinct purpose. First, by requiring cloud-free im-
agery, it is ensured that the extracted water masks accurately reflects water bodies and
it avoids misclassifying clouds as water. Second, by enforcing a minimal gap of at most
∆t = 1 day, between the capture of Sentinel 1 and 2 imagery. It ensures that both sensors
capture the same scene under similar conditions. Finally, by incorporating imagery from
different seasons of the same area, natural variability is captured. An example of natural
variability is the colour of leaves changing.
The tightly constrained strategy, provides the optimal conditions for good segmentation
masks, that can be used as ground truths. The strategy ignores an important aspect, it
does not include imagery where there is clouds covering the optical bands. For practical
applications, it would be desirable to have a model that is robust to different weather con-
ditions, including clouds. This limitations is the motivation behind the second acquisition
strategy.
Loosely constrained strategy
The second strategy is designed to construct a dataset that includes both usable segmen-
tation masks and cloud covered Sentinel 2 imagery. In contrast to the tightly constrained

strategy, where a strict temporal alignment of ∆t = 1 is enforced, this strategy relaxes

relaxation helps include diverse weather conditions, it also increases the chance of discrep-

follows:
• Initial Sentinel 2 imagery: An initial Sentinel 2 image must exits with cloud

• Cloud covered imagery: A second Sentinel 2 image must be available within

• Sentinel 1 imagery: Within the same ∆t = 1 week period, there must be a

• Multiple timestamps per location: As in the tightly constrained dataset, mul-

river morphology can have changed during the time period.
To ensure valid pseudo ground truths, the first Sentinel 2 image should remain mostly
cloud free. However, as κ = 15% it does mean that minor clouds present can introduce
misclassification in the ground truth. The second Sentinel 2 image is the one with the
actual cloud cover present. By introducing actual clouds, the trained models are forced
to learn river features from Sentinel 1, otherwise it wont learn to segment during cloudy
weather. As in the tightly constrained strategy, this strategy includes seasonal variation
by including multiple timestamps throughout the year per location.
By using both strategies to obtain data for the dataset, the dataset ends up being more
diverse, robust and well-suited for the task of river segmentation in different weather
conditions.
Pseudo ground truth generation
Training a model for river segmentation requires ground truth labels to supervise learning.
While manually annotating masks is an option, and would provide the most reliable masks,
it is not feasible due to the size of the dataset and the limited time available. Instead the
NDWI is used as a pseudo ground truth, where pixels with a value greater than 0.01 are
classified as water and the rest as non-water. As these automatically generated masks are
not manually verified, they are refereed to as pseudo ground truths.
Note that although NDWI is an effective way to separate water from non-water, it has
some limitation. It is sensitive to cloud cover, may misclassify urban area or shadows as
water, and most notable the index often struggles to detect narrow river channels. These
limitations introduce noise and bias in the training data, which must be considered when
evaluating the model performance.

mations of the original samples.
However, not all augmentations are suitable to satellite imagery. For instance Zhu et al.
(2021) [37] describes that applying rotation-based augmentations to SAR data, can lead
to physically implausible patterns. Such augmentations should be avoided when working
with Sentinel 1 imagery.
In this work three augmentations are applied to the training data:
• Scaling:
Patches are randomly scaled between 0.9 and 1.1 of their original size.
This encourages the model to learn scale invariant features. The augmentation is
inspired by Wieland et al. 2024 [16].
• Horizontal flipping:
A horizontal flip of the data is applied with a 50% probabil-
ity. Since it is flipping and not a rotation it does not introduce unrealistic distortion
in the Sentinel 1 SAR imagery. This technique is also applied in Wieland et al. 2024
[16].
• Artificial cloud overlay:
An experimental idea, involving the overlay of synthet-
ically generated clouds onto cloud-free Sentinel 2 patches. The goal is to increase
the robustness of the model to varying cloud conditions. This augmentation is only
applied to data from the tightly constrained data, where the optical imagery avail-
able is cloud-free.

4.2
Model architecture and training
For this thesis two architectures are used: the standard U-Net and the Dynamic U-Net
variant described in section 3.4.2. U-Net has demonstrated strong performance across
various segmentation tasks, making it a natural choice. The models were configured to
handle multimodal satellite imagery from Sentinel 1, Sentinel 2 and DEM data as the

without the DEM included.
To address the topology of the segmentations, a combination of standard loss functions
and topology aware losses where used. More specifically, clDice was used to encourage
connectivity. The performance of the clDice loss depends on a parameter controlling the
skeletonization.

4.2.3
Training details
The models trained in this thesis were trained using the Adam optimizer, with limited
hyperparameter tuning. Most hyperparameters were either selected heuristically or left as
the default parameters. Across all experiments, the hyperparameters where kept constant.
Models were initially trained for 50 epochs, however experiments showed that the models

the models seemed to be converged.
Training for more than 100 epochs was considered, but was deemed impractical. Each
model required between 11-13 hours to train. A total of 16 different models were trained,

• Center line extraction (Skeletonization): The first stages involves reducing the

and applying a length threshold, short spurious branches can be pruned. The result-

4.5
Evaluation methodology
Ideally, for evaluation of the model, three different aspects should be evaluated. 1.) The
accuracy of the segmentations. 2.) The topological quality of the segmentations, and
finally. 3.) Comparing the quality of the generated river graphs with SWORD. For each
of the three parts, different evaluation metrics should be used.
4.5.1
Segmentation quality
Evaluating the quality of the segmentation mask is crucial, as the segmentation accuracy,
directly impacts the effectiveness of downstream tasks. A poorly performing model, com-
promises the overall utility of the proposed pipeline, whose goal is to generate dynamic,
accurate river graphs. It is important to note, that no manually labelled ground truths
exists. Instead, pseudo ground truths have been employed, with the addition of processing
them to filter out non-river water bodies. Hence, if the model learns to segment water
bodies rather then strictly river water, it will affect the reported accuracies. It is still
possible that the masks yield great results for the downstream application. Multiple eval-
uation metrics have been selected, each chosen to highlight different aspects of the model
performance.

the 25th, 50th and 75th. If the quantiles are close to each other it suggests the graph
closely follows the river.

To select a final model, used from now on, different factor must be considered: Should the

without DEM, trained using the clDice function.

realistic choice. It provides strong accuracies while encouraging the model to preserve

of including it, thus it was excluded.

The last section laid the foundation for which model, that should be used for creating

Discussion
6.1
Training and hyperparameters
6.1.1
Loss function composition
The choice of loss function changes the model’s way of learning, and dictates what the
model should prioritize during training. In this work, a combined loss was used equally
weighting the Dice loss and clDice loss, to balance segmentation accuracy and topolog-
ical connectivity. This weighting was selected early in the project and was not further
optimized. This raises the possibility that other weightings could yield additional improve-
ments to the results, although this depends on the intended applications of the model.
Additionally, for the clDice loss, the iteration parameter k must be determined. In this
project both a dynamic selection of k and a hardcoded k = 20 were explored. The dynamic
approach was based on a sigmoid-like mapping determining k based on the ratio between
water amount and the patch size. This method was heuristic and not validated against
other alternatives. Other approaches could be used to determine k. One such approach
could be to base k on the maximum river width or to make it a learnable parameter in
the network.
6.1.2
Hyperparameters
While hyperparameter tuning is usually a major component of deep learning experimen-
tation, only limited tuning was conducted in this project due to scope and time. Most
parameters were selected heuristically, or kept at default values throughout training. For
example, the weighting parameter α was selected early on and not revisited. Similarly,
the learning rate was chosen based on initial experiments, without doing any optimization
of the parameter. A grid search or random search, could have been used for this.
Following initial tests, the number of epochs was raised from 50 to 100, which provided
better convergence, without overfitting, as illustrated in fig. 6.1. Longer training could
potentially have improved performance, however, 100 epochs took approximately 11 −13
hours to train. Given 16 models were trained times 3, training any longer would be infea-
sible.
A batch size of 32 was chosen as a balance between memory constraints, and batch diver-
sity. As tuning was limited in this project, it remains possible that further improvements
could be achieved through a more extensive hyperparameter search.

6.2
NDWI based pseudo ground truths
6.2.1
Reliability of NDWI for river mask generation
The NDWI is an effective, fast, and cost-free method for detecting surface water from
optical satellite imagery, which is why it was used for generating the pseudo ground truths
used in this project. The NDWI made it possible to create a large dataset, without any
manual annotations.
However, the NDWI derived masks does not come without limitations.
First, NDWI
performs poorly in narrow rivers, where the contrast between water and land is low. As a
consequence, these narrow rivers are often misclassified as non-water, causing the models
to undersegment narrow rivers. This effect is shown in the left most panel of fig. 5.13, where
the narrow river, is entirely missed by RiverSeg, but annotated by SWORD. Second, NDWI
produces false positives in bright urban regions or around clouds, where the reflectance
values mimic the reflectance values for water.
Alternative water indices, or a different thresholding technique, could have been used, in
an attempt to improve mask quality. This, however, would have meant more time would
have been spend on annotating the data, defeating the point of relying on weakly labelled
data.
Despite the limitations, the pseudo ground truths were used for evaluation of the models.
This choice can be justified for two reasons.
First, evaluation is performed on labels
acquired under the same assumptions as the training data. Meaning that, the difference
in performance, reflects architectural and training choices, rather than inconsistencies
in label quality. Second, the goal of this project was not to achieve the absolute best
segmentation accuracy, but to develop a method improving topological quality in the
predicted river masks, and use the the mask to to create a dynamic river graph pipeline.
The NDWI pseudo ground truths, are suﬀicient to differentiate trends in segmentation
and graph structure across models and locations.
6.2.2
Mask filtering for river segmentation
The primary goal of this project was to improve topological quality and use the extracted
river mask, to generate a graph representation of the river. The pseudo ground truths,
however, contained all types of water bodies, not only rivers. To have the model focus
on learning river features, rather than general water body features, a filtering step was
applied to remove components, which skeletons, were either too short or too long, based
on two predetermined thresholds.
The filtering was applied on individual patches, rather than the larger areas. This decision
introduced biases. River segments that briefly entered a patch, were often removed as they
were too short. As a direct consequence, the models may have learned that river presence
is associated with large continuos components. An example of this is shown in fig. 6.2,
where part of a valid river is missed in the prediction, despite it being easily distinguishable
in the Sentinel 1 image.

6.3
Topology preservation and clDice
As one of the primary goal of this project, we wanted to improve topological quality and
correctness in the segmentations. This was accomplished by integrating clDice into the loss
function, it showed a noticeable impact. As previously shown in table 5.2 and table 5.1,
models trained with clDice consistently produces fewer fragmented components and better
connectivity.
These improvements are a result of how clDice is designed, which penalizes discrepancies
between the skeletons of predictions and ground truths, encouraging preservation of thin
structures.
However, clDice is not without limitations. Models trained using clDice often oversegments
around thin narrow structures, leading to an increase in false positives. For our intended
use this is acceptable, but it may be more problematic for other applications where greater
precision is more important.
Furthermore, clDice does not guarantee topological correctness, it only encourages it.
Figure 5.10 show an example where the RiverSeg graph is fragmented in the narrow
∼40 −50 m wide river channels. This limitation is a result of the pseudo labels used,
which also struggle with narrow channels, as discussed earlier.
In summary, clDice proves to be a valuable addition when topological quality is prioritized.
It reduces the number of fragmented components and improves the connectivity, but it
does not ensure topological correctness, especially for narrow, challenging cases.

Topology evaluation metrics

of the predicted river masks. Unlike pixel-wise metrics, which are misleading in case of

use of a ground truth.
The metrics are also limited. As they are purely quantitative measures, they treat all
components equally, i.e, a small break in an offshoot is weighted equally to a main river

6.5
Post-processing pipeline
The post-processing pipeline developed consists of 4 stages. 1) Generation of a large pre-
diction mask, 2) cleaning the mask for noise and unwanted objects. 3) Skeletonization
and pruning, and 4) construction of a graph.
Through the stages 5 parameters are introduced that in the end influence the final graph:
θ area ratio threshold (to remove objects that are to compact), mk morphology kernel
(gap closing), τ hole area threshold (Filling small internal holes), ℓmin minimum allowed
branch length (spur removal) and δ graph resolution (node spacing and edge collapsing).
While the flexibility of five tunable parameters could imply the need for local adjustments,
the pipeline is designed to function with minimal configuration. A key advantage of the
pipeline is that most of the parameters are optional, and can be left disabled or as the
default values, depending on the amount of time one is willing to spend, tuning the pa-
rameters.

Overall, the pipeline is modular and extremely flexible.
Its possible to fine tune the

Future improvements could be to dynamically select the parameters based on some rules,

RiverSeg graph vs SWORD
6.6.1
Strengths and weaknesses
Using RiverSeg over SWORD comes down to what the actual application it needs to be
used for. Selecting one comes with some pros while it has other cons.
The strengths of SWORD is that it is topologically consistent across all rivers available
in the database, this makes it a ”plug and play” solution where a graph is easily selected

from the database and used for the actual application. In addition to this, all the graphs
are already available in their database, to use it does not require much setup. The graphs
also includes different attributes that help describe the river, and might be important for
the intended use.
SWORD does have its limitations. First, the database is static meaning it is not updated
based on changes to a river.
Additionally, the graphs does not follow the river very
closely, the centerline is an average representation of the channels close by. As a result
the centerline goes through islands located in rivers.
RiverSeg on the other hand follow the actual river very closely. Depending on the distance
between nodes, the graph will follow the river more closely. The RiverSeg pipeline makes
it such that two different graphs can be made for the same area, for two different days.
This allows for graphs that change with the river. Because RiverSeg follows the river it
also implies that if a river splits into multiple branches, each branch is followed. A final
strength is that the entire pipeline is not strictly dependant on the models proposed in
this project. If another mask is available e.g. the NDWI, then this mask could also be
used to generate a graph.
RiverSeg also comes with its own limitations. The topology of the graph is dependant
on the mask.
If the mask is not topologically correct, the same will be true for the
graph.
Additionally, the graphs have to be made per location and timestamp, i.e.
it
is not a download the graph and get on with it solution, as SWORD is. Compared to
SWORD, there are a very limited amount of attributes available. In some applications
these attributes could be highly valuable, and it would not make sense to use RiverSeg.
6.6.2
When to use what?
Depending on the application, either SWORD or RiverSeg makes sense to chose. For large
scale applications where the general flow of the river is the most important part, SWORD
is the go to solution. For localized analysis of a specific river section, RiverSeg offers the
best advantages. It can create dynamic graphs, that follow the river as well as capture
different flow paths.
Overall there is not a ”one solution fits all” and it depends on the application at hand,
what river representation makes the most sense to use.

Conclusion
This thesis presents two contributions. First, a deep learning based U-Net was used to
segment river extent from remote sensing data. The models incorporated Sentinel 1 SAR
imagery, Sentinel 2 optical imagery, and, in some configurations, a Digital Elevation Model
(DEM) to enhance segmentation robustness across diverse environments.
Models trained without DEM input were found to perform better than those that included
it. The combination of SAR and optical imagery proved effective for improving model ro-
bustness to varying weather conditions. Although not perfect, the models are capable of
predicting simple river structures even under cloudy conditions, something that had not
been possible using optical data alone.
The entire dataset used in this project was created without manual annotation. Instead,
the NDWI index was used to generate pseudo ground truth masks. While NDWI is ef-
fective at identifying larger water bodies, it fails on narrow river channels, leading to an
under-representation of narrow rivers in the ground truths. As a result, the models fail to
predict narrow rivers.
The introduction of the clDice loss function significantly improved the topological struc-
ture of the predictions. Compared to models trained using Dice and BCE loss, clDice
trained models produced better more connected predictions, especially around complex
river regions.
The second major contribution is the post-processing pipeline, RiverSeg, which transforms
rasterized masks into a vectorized river graph. The pipeline offers a dynamic alternative
to NASA’s static SWORD database. A comparison between RiverSeg and SWORD show
that, while both are graph representation, the have distinct use cases.
RiverSeg produces detailed, highly flexible graphs that can capture temporal changes in
the river. RiverSeg, does in contrast to SWORD, not guarantee topological correctness.
RiverSeg often produces fragmented networks for narrow rivers. This highlights an im-
portant limitation with RiverSeg: The quality of the resulting graph is directly dependent
on the quality of the segmentation mask.
As a way to show RiverSeg as a practical utility, a selected graph was used through a case
study, using SWOT elevation data, to estimate local river slopes. The case demonstrated
the major difference between SWORD and RiverSeg. Where SWORD is limited due to it
being an averaged centerline, RiverSeg does not have this limitation and follows the river
naturally. The case study showed that with the RiverSeg generated graph it is possible
to collect points from SWOT, where it previously would not have been as easy to do so.
The primary contributions of this thesis includes. A pipeline for generating a robust topo-
logically improved river segmentation model, using remote sensing imagery, while relying
on pseudo labels as the ground truth. A new pipeline for creating dynamic river graphs,
overcoming the static limitations in datasets such as SWORD. And finally a practical
application, showing how the river graphs can be used in other applications, such as esti-
mating the river slope using SWOTs water elevation data. This thesis provides a pipeline
that can be used for future scientific work, where the focus is on precise and detailed
representation of river networks for hydrological and environmental analysis.

- Does not operate well under cloudy conditions.

D
Artificial cloud augmentation explained
The artificial clouds are added using the oﬀicial implementation of the SatelliteCloud-
Generator by Czerkawski et al. (2023) [38]. The library enables synthetic clouds to be
included on cloud free optical satellite imagery. The augmentation follows a structured
pipeline made up of several steps, transforming the cloud free imagery into cloud covered
imagery. The initial step is to generate the clouds shape. This is done using Perlin noise,
a random noise pattern with natural characteristics. Perlin noise varies depending on a
scaling parameter, combining multiple scales creates cloud structures similar real clouds.
Once the shape of the clouds is made, the density and opacity of the clouds are adjusted.
The clouds can be adjusted such that the cloud cover can vary from sparse thin clouds to
dense cloud cover.
With the clouds in place the shadows of the clouds are also mimic. Again it is done using
Perlin noise at various levels and layering them together. Instead of making this noise
brighter to mimic cloud they apply some darkening technique to make the shaded areas
appear darker. Having added both clouds and shadows makes parts of the satellite images
covered, limiting the usefulness of the optical bands.
