Abstract
Recent years has seen an enormous increase in the focus on human caused climate change
and ways to avoid the continued temperature rise caused by greenhouse gases. A large
part of this problem is the transition to an energy supply from sustainable sources.
The transition to a sustainable society includes the transportation sector. As of now,
the solution seems to be electric vehicles with batteries as energy storage.
This thesis aims at analyzing the Nordhavn power grid in the case where one or more
500kW+ fast-chargers for E-buses are deployed. A model for the relevant part of the
Nordhavn grid is implemented in MATLAB Simulink.
The chargers will draw a minimum of 500kW of power for short periods of time, making
a load and voltage constraint analysis for the local grid necessary. A Simulink model
for the charger is developed and implemented in the simulations along with load profiles
and grid specifications provided by DONG.
The effects on the grid caused by the fast-charger is investigated in terms of current and
voltage level, both during normal operation and faults.
The effects of supporting the implementation of fast-chargers with a Battery is investi-
gated. Several charging patterns, charger locations and setups are explored to find the
constraints for successful integration of a fast-charger.

Resumé
I løbet af de seneste år har verden sat et øget fokus på de menneskeskabte klimaforan-
dringer og bekæmpelsen af temperaturstigninger som skyldes øgede mængder af drivhus-
gasser i atmossfæren. En stor del af udfordringen er omstillingen til bæredygtige ener-
giresourcer.
Omstillingen til et bæredygtigt samfund omfatter også transportsektoren, som i dag er
baseret på olie. Den mest lovende løsning ser i øjeblikket ud til at være batteridrevne
elektriske køretøjer.
Målet med dette projekt er at analysere Nordhavns elnetværket i det tilfælde at en
500kW hurtiglader til E-busser bliver installeret. En model af netværket er udviklet og
indsat i MATLAB Simulink for at undersøge problemstillingen.
Laderen vil som minimum påtrykke en belastning på 500kW i korte tidsperioder, dette
nødvendiggør en analyse af belastnings- og spændingsgrænser og niveauer.
Der er udviklet en model for laderen samt belastninger på 10/0.4kV transformerne i
netværket, derudover er en Batterimodel også udviklet. Netværksmodellen er udviklet
på en basis af specifikationer leveret af DONG Energy.
Det er undersøgt hvordan implementationen af en hurtiglader påvirker strøm- og spænd-
ingsniveauer på to radialer in elnettet. Der er undersøgt hvordan et batteri kan støtte
under driften af hurtigladere. Sidst er der undersøgt hvad det vil kræve for et batteri
for at varetage fuldstændig ”peak shaving” for forskellige lademønstre.

This report is a 30 ETCS point thesis and a requirement to complete the Master pro-

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Readers guide
This chapter explains the motivation of this Masters thesis and the effect of integrating a
fast-charger into an existing power grid along with the potential and possibilities offered
by having a grid scale Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) providing support in
fault and peak demand situations. The scope, limitations and outline of the thesis is
presented. It is assumed that the reader possesses knowledge of electrical energy systems
in addition to an understanding of power system operation, electrical components and
the network administrator regulation and governance surrounding the operation of a
power grid.
1.2
Background
Recent years has seen an enormous increase in the focus on human caused climate change
and ways to avoid the continued temperature rise caused by greenhouse gases. A large
part of this problem is the transition to an energy supply from sustainable sources.
In this context, Denmark has an ambitious goal of being completely fossil fuel free by
2050[1]. This transition does naturally include the transportation sector, where an al-
ternative to oil as primary fuel has to be implemented.
As of now, the solution seems to be electric vehicles with batteries as energy storage.
Of the battery types available, Lithium-ion has become the most dominant due to its
relatively high energy density, power output and acceptable durability. Li-ion batteries
have seen a quick maturation over a few years as the demand for the technology has
increased rapidly. Current batteries offer significantly better performance, both in ab-
solute terms and in performance per investment[2].
This has opened the opportunity to electrify the public bus network in cities to decrease
point pollution, noise level and help complete the transition to a fossil fuel free society.
Multiple cities across the world are already running projects where some bus lines are
serviced by E-buses[3].
The continued transition to an electrification of our society poses new technical chal-
lenges for both Transmission System Operator (TSO) and for the Distribution System
Operator (DSO) as the infrastructure has to be upgraded to be able to handle the new
system with decentralised plants, Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) and increased

E-buses adds a different challenge than personal EVs or PHEVs as they have a very

1.3
E-Buses
The goal of being fossilfuel free is not reached without including the transportation sector,
both private and public. Since the S-train and metro net are already fully electric, the
regional trains are planned to be electrified in the coming years, the next step is to
electrify the bus network.
Electrified buses (E-bus) offers some technical advantages over diesel buses, among these
are regenerative braking where power is regained to the battery from using the motor
as a generator to brake[4], no point pollution, options for different fuels depending on
what method is used to generate the electricity.
1.3.1
Charging methods
Several methods are available to implement this, the three major types being trolleybuses,
battery powered buses with large capacity and lastly battery powered buses with smaller
batteries. Each offers its own set of advantages and disadvantages.
Trolleybuses
A trolleybus does not rely on on-board energy storage as it is constantly connected to
the electrical grid by overhead wires. This eliminates the need for charging stations, but
strictly limits its movements to the route of the overhead wires. The dependence on
wires makes this a rigid solution with low rerouteing possibilities if road work, accidents
etc. requires it.
E-bus with opportunity charging
This type of bus carries a smaller battery or supercapacitor, which needs to be recharged
after a shorter distance eg. 30km[5]. The advantages of this design are the lower weight,
which results in a higher efficiency in terms of km/kWh and higher reliability[5]. Disad-
vantages are the need for frequent recharging, lower energy density if using supercapac-
itors and higher investment cost for charging equipment as it has to be more powerful
and installed at more locations.

Night-/Slow charging
Similarly to a conventional EV, this type of bus relies on a large battery which allows
for longer trips between charging periods. The large batteries will be charging for longer
periods, especially during night time, which makes it possible to use them as network
utility and for the owner to profit on the trading of power when the buses are idle and
therefore available to provide system services on the same level as a dedicated BESS in
terms of voltage regulation, frequency support etc.
1.4
Scope
The focus of this thesis is to investigate the effects a fast-charger may have on a MV
distribution grid in a residential area. The MV grid is chosen due to DONG Energy,
the largest DSO in Denmark [6], being a collaborator in the thesis.
DONG has provided data for two of the 10 kV radial lines in their grid in Nordhavn.
The angle of investigation is from the DSO point of view, meaning the consumption
on the 0.4kV side of the 10/0.4kV transformers are aggregated and following the same
24-hour load profile with scaling corresponding to the number of registered customers
per 10/0.4kV transformer.
The connection to the 60kV grid is regarded as an infinite source connected by 60/10kV
tap changing transformer. This means that the grid frequency does not play any role,
both in terms of the added consumption affecting the grid frequency and fast-charger
behavior during frequency abnormalities.
As part of the full scale energy development project, EnergyLab Nordhavn[7], it seeks
to identify the challenges that may arise when installing fast-chargers in an existing grid
and what countermeasures can be taken to avoid overloading the grid or a feeder in both
current and future load patterns. The primary factors used for evaluation of the grids
response to a fast-charger are line current and voltage level.
The thesis seeks to first discover what part of the grid the fast-charger should be installed
at to cause the worst performance in terms of current levels in feeders and the voltage
level at the load farthest from the 60/10kV transformer on the respective feeder.
The connection point found in the aforementioned scenarios will be used as point of
investigation when determining the possible risks of directly integrating a fast-charger
into the power grid. This is investigated with respect to the grid still being able to com-
ply with normal operation criteria for voltage level and feeder load percentage. Another
constraint it must comply with is to not overload a supporting feeder during a fault on
the feeder carrying the fast-charger.
Alternatives to grid upgrades by installing larger feeder cables is explored, as future
power grids will likely rely on energy storage services in order to cope with the fluc-
tuations of DERs.
It is intuitive to make use of these technologies, leading to the

investigation of the possible interaction between a fast-charger and a Battery Energy

The BESS is introduced to alleviate the grid from the large load spikes associated with
the bursts of power the fast-charger delivers.
To make a more general picture of the effects of fast-chargers, scenarios with two units
will be investigated to explore more severe cases of fault operation and a higher peak

Finally an investigation of the capacity requirements for a BESS to keep the load of
a feeder below normal operation constraints will be carried out to give an impression
of the possibility for this type of flexible support as an alternative to conventional grid
upgrades.
Since a grid scale BESS is already planned to be installed in the Nordhavn grid, the
specifications used in the Simulink grid model resembles the parameters for the actual
Nordhavn BESS.
The battery is modelled as a programmable load with state of charge determining the
amount of power available. The parameters for the BESS are based off the actual BESS
in terms of in-/output power and battery capacity. It is assumed that the BESS is
completely dedicated to supporting the fast-charger, so battery degradation and other
services the BESS might be able to utilize has been omitted in this investigation.
To summarize the goals of this project:
• Adapt and upgrade the existing model of the Nordhavn grid to include residential
loads with a varying demand over a 24-hour cycle.
• Determine the power and charge length for a fast-charger that can supply a city
bus’ consumption.
• Develop a BESS model that can support the operation of a grid when one or more
fast-chargers is integrated.
• Determine the capacity requirements for running multiple fast-chargers within grid
code limits.
1.5
Project and approach
In order to investigate the effects of installing a 500kW fast-charger in the existing power
grid in Nordhavn, an existing model[8] of two radial branches has been adapted and ex-
panded to include controllable loads, real physical parameters for cables and a BESS
with a power flow driven controller.
The consequences of the fast-charger, seen from the DSO perspective, are measured in
the individual line power flows, currents and voltage level.
Simulations are done to discover if the implementation will violate any of the current
==PAGE:17==
1.6 Project limitations
5
rules in the grid operation code, mainly if 50 % line load is exceeded during normal
operation and if the maximum line capacity is exceeded during fault scenarios.
Furthermore, the potential synergy with a grid size BESS has been investigated in terms
of peak shaving and faul situation power support.
A successful integration of a fast-charger in the power grid will meet the following criteria:
• Will be able to supply the power need for an E-bus to finish charging before a new
arrives.
• Deliver the power without overloading the transformer it is connected to.
• Be able to run at all times of the day without exceeding the corresponding 10kV
feeders 50% capacity during normal operation.
• Not overload a feeder during fault operation where the remaining feeder has to
carry two feeder loads.
• drag the voltage of the farthest load below the regulatory threshold.
Furthermore, a successful integration of a fast-charger in combination with a BESS will,
in addition to the above, meet the following:
• Significantly reduce the current on remaining feeder during fault.
• Be able to displace load during high demand hours.
1.6
Project limitations
As the scope of this thesis is to investigate and present the possible challenges in regards
to line loads and congestions. The fast-charger model is modelled as a PQ load and its
transient behavior is not investigated.
The location and power of the charger is assumed to be of insignificant influence on the
high voltage grid and the frequency is therefore omitted as a factor in the operation
criteria for the charger. In reality, a charger of this size will likely be subject to a set of
grid codes.
All loads are modelled as resistive loads, as detailed load data has not been available for
the project. The model used limits the load analysis to 3-phase connected, symmetrical
components which excludes investigation of phase voltage unbalances.
The actual placement of the physical BESS are not included in this model as the BESS
used has been placed at the same bus as the charger to show the optimal synergy effect
it may have on the operation of the fast-charger. The BESS is assumed to be dedicated
to the charger, which would not be the case in practice as it would be required to provide
grid services in order to make profit.

2.1.1
Simulink and SimPowerSystems
MATLAB Simulink is used to simulate the power grid in this project. Simulink uses
a block interface, where all mathematical functions are packaged and connected in a
block diagram. This allows for a graphical overview mimicking the schematics of a real
power grid. Simulink contains the library Simscape Power Systems which has defined
blocks for the most common elements in power systems such as generators, line sections,
transformers and RLC loads.
The model used uses the built-in blocks for transformers and π-line segments which has
been modified with parameters provided by DONG[10].
The model from [8] has been extended with loads on each 10/0.4kV transformer, an
externally controllable load has been developed for the simulations done in this thesis.

2.2
Battery Energy Storage System model
The BESS technology has been suggested as an opportunity to provide utility services
on a grid level such as frequency support and voltage support[11]. A BESS unit is also
planned to be part of the Nordhavn project[12], this BESS will be dimensioned to be
able to produce or absorb 1MW of power with a capacity of 0.5MWh[12].
Using this as a design point, the BESS has been modelled and integrated in the simula-
tion to explore the opportunities for synergy it may provide with a fast-charger.
The BESS is used when investigating fault situation support and peak shaving by load
shifting. It is controlled by a proportional controller which uses the magnitude of the
power flow at the beginning of the relevant 10kV feeder as input and an externally speci-
fied set point which is changed depending on the desired ”aggressiveness” of the battery
control strategy. The BESS control equation is created as:
Pout = (Pset −Pmeasured) · G · PBESS
(2.6)
Where Pset is the specified operation point for the BESS, Pmeasured is the measured power
flow, G is the gain of the controller, PBESS is the maximum power output and Pout is
the commanded output by the BESS, if this is negative it will put the BESS into charge
mode and it will absorb power from the grid.
It is assumed that the BESS is dedicated to supporting a fast-charger and it is placed on

2.4
Fault situations
A major part of the work presented in this thesis is the effect a failure of one of the
feeders has on the operation of the grid. A fault can arise from multiple reasons, a cable
being cut, a breaker triggering, a short circuit etc.
The fault itself is not analysed as this is outside the purpose of this work. Normally
when a fault occurs there will be a period of time where the segment behind the fault
is completely disconnected before the fault procedures are activated and the power is
rerouted through the neighbouring feeder(s).
The simulation of a fault situation is handled as instantaneous, meaning when a fault is
introduced it is handled as if a breaker is tripped and the switch at end of the lines is
closed in the same time step.
2.4.1
N-1 criterion
The dimensioning rule for power grids are as minimum to be able to operate if the biggest
unit in the system trips and disconnects. This rule is dictated by the European TSO
ENTSOE [14] and by extension, the Danish TSO Energinet.dk [13]. The rule carries
over to the design criteria for MV grids where DONG has specified the limits as 2/3 of
the maximum capacity. This is due to DONG’s grid design choice where two neighboring
feeders are set to cover for one if a fault occurs, splitting the load in two instead of one.
Since the model used for this thesis only contains two feeders, a constraint of 50% load
during normal operation has been used as evaluation parameter.

2.5 Peak load mitigation by loadshift

2.5
Peak load mitigation by loadshift
Peak load mitigation or peak shaving is an option to defer or postpone traditional
grid upgrades where the method is to reinforce the grid by installing additional/larger
transmission lines. The concept has been visualised in figure 2.3
Figure 2.3: Peak shaving example [15].
The BESS described earlier allows for an alternative solution where the grid is made
flexible in terms of decentralized regulation units, in this case the BESS, supports the
load flow in the local area instead of a power plant having to push the power through
the whole grid.
In this case, the purpose of the BESS is to attempt to keep the load of the feeders below
the threshold of 50% during normal operation. The BESS is to discharge during peaks
and to exploit the excess line capacity to charge during off peak hours.
Equation 2.8 is used to govern the behavior of the BESS, with the State Of Charge (SOC)
and maximum Power as limits to its operation. It is of course not possible to utilize
all excess capacity, which is leads to the problem of battery size which is investigated
further in the load shift simulation case.

Choice of charger dimensions
The fast-charger specifications has been chosen on the basis of bus frequency in table

2.8
Summary of modelling assumptions
The assumptions made in order to simulate and evaluate the scenarios for an integration
of a fast-charger in a MV power grid are summarized here:
• Grid assumptions
– Cable length on 0.4kV side is assumed to be negligible
– 10/0.4kV transformer at charger bus is assumed to be upgraded if load exceeds
capacity
• Load assumptions
– Load scale is made on a background of Denmark as a whole
– Is almost purely resistive
• Charger assumptions
– Transient behavior is not investigated
– Power electronics are assumed to be able to maintain unity powerfactor
– Total harmonic distortion is assumed to be negligible
– ramp up is assumed to be near instantaneous
• BESS assumptions
– BESS is dedicated to supporting fast-charger
– Is located at same bus as fast-charger
– Has the same properties as the planned Nordhavn BESS in terms of capacity
and max power

CHAPTER 3
Case study
This chapter describes the scenarios used to determine the impacts of the integration of
a fast-charger in the power grid. The effects are investigated in terms of feeder load and
voltage quality.
The investigation is done to ensure that the operation of fast-chargers does not break
any grid codes or entirely overload the system during a fault on a feeder.
Synergy with a BESS is being investigated to see if it is possible to reduce the stress on
the grid caused by a fast-charger by supporting during a fault or by supplying power
locally when demand is high.
After showing the effects of a single fast-charger, another one is introduced in order to
explore a more hypothetical scenario where the grid proves too weak to operate with
the fast-chargers within the grid code boundaries.
Finally it will be explored if it is technically possible to stay within normal operation
boundaries with the help of a BESS when two 500kW fast-chargers are installed.
3.1
Scenarios
A series of scenarios has been simulated to investigate the consequences of installing
a fast-charger in an existing residential power grid and to give a picture the actions
that can be taken to avoid overloading the grid or violating normal operation criteria
described in the previous chapter.
The scope of the project is to investigate the load change on the grid during different
operation modes. The scenarios are focused on normal operation and operation during
a single fault on one feeder at a time.
The simulations has been conducted in an order with increasing complexity and fault
severity.
First, the integration of a single 500kW charger is simulated at several locations to
determine the worst case location to be used when simulating other scenarios with
different configurations.
Second, the grid response to a fault on one feeder is investigated with both one and two
500kW units. During 1000kW scenarios, the capacity of the transformer connecting the
fast-chargers has been increased from 630kVA to 1.5MVA to be able to handle the extra
load.
The aforementioned scenarios are used as base cases for comparison to scenarios where

3.1.4
Peak shaving by load shifting with BESS
Fault situations occur rarely compared to demand peaks which happen daily, therefore
it would be very costly to install a grid sized BESS just to mitigate fault loads.
Another purpose for a BESS could then be to ensure that the normal operation limits
are not exceeded during peak hours. This is done by letting the BESS inject power pro-
portional to the amount above the criteria limit on the respective feeder. The discharged
energy is then replaced during off peak hours and thus the load will be ”shifted”. The
load shift capability is of course limited by the physical specifications of the BESS in
terms of maximum power and energy capacity.
The simulation is carried out for both one and two 500kW units. The BESS is not ex-
pected to be able to handle all of the excess demand, the capacity required is investigated
after.

3.1.5
Estimation of BESS capacity required to fully cover
peak shaving demand
The final aspect of this investigation is an estimation of the total battery capacity needed
to fully cover the load shift demand shown in previous simulations. This is done by
measuring the current magnitude at all time steps where the feeder current exceeds 50%
of max and then measuring the gap when there is excess capacity.
The two resulting vectors are then plotted as cumulative sums showing the total power
required as well as the available power for charging the BESS back up.
The slope of the discharge cumulative sum reveals where the critical time period is, the
steeper the curve, the more power is needed.
Subtracting the charge and discharge cumulative sums will reveal when there is a deficit
of charging opportunities.
To show the significance of charge time length, this investigation is carried out for charge
time lengths of 5, 4 and 3 minutes.

in the feeders is well below 50% during normal operation on the heaviest loaded feeder
1 and the same is seen when feeder 2 has to carry both loads during a fault on feeder 1.
It has been shown that the worst case placement during normal operation is the end of
feeder 1. This manifests as a 0.005 p.u. larger decrease in voltage during a load step as
seen in figure 4.7.
The current and power flow differences observed in the simulations are all minuscule and
will not have any effect on a decision about the location of such a unit.
Fault operation analysis shows the worst case location for the fast-charger is at the
beginning of feeder 1 with a fault on the same feeder, forcing the power to flow through
the longest possible path. Again the difference in current is almost non-existent as seen
from figure 4.8. This makes the voltage level a deciding parameter in identifying the
worst case location. From figure 4.9 it is seen that the start of feeder 1 is the worst
location for the charger during fault situations.
This leads to the decision that the start of feeder 1 is used as location for further
investigations.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the integration of a 500kW fast charger into an existing medium
voltage power grid. To fully integrate the fast-charger the grid must be able to operate as
before the introduction of a fast-charger. The operation criteria for successful integration
has been specified as:
• No feeder is to supply more than 50% of its rated power during normal operation.
• All voltage levels must remain within +/- 10% on the low voltage side of the
10/0.4kV transformers in all situations.
• The grid will not experience overloading on any feeder during a fault.
A series of scenarios have been constructed to determine the worst case location of the
fast charger during normal operation and fault situations.
Then the BESS is introduced and the benefits it provides to feeder load and voltage
level are demonstrated.
An additional 500kW fast charger unit is introduced on the same load bus as before to
investigate the behavior of the grid in a situation where the normal operation grid rules
are being violated.
Finally an investigation of the peak shaving capabilities of the BESS is carried out. The
BESS capacity and power requirements for complete peak shaving is investigated. The
analysis is done for several charging lengths to discover the influence of charging time
per cycle on the required BESS storage capacity.
Through the simulation of the scenarios, a worst case location was found and used for
further simulations. The worst case simulation was chosen on the background of voltage
level at the transformer farthest from the tap changing transformer.
The simulation of a single fast charger unit at the worst location showed that the grid
was able to support it without any need for upgrades. This was proven true for both
normal operation and during fault.
An additional unit was the added to show what could be done if an integration of fast
chargers actually violated operation criteria.
The synergy with a BESS was demon-
strated as it successfully operated as a support for the fast chargers with clear improve-
ment of feeder load and voltage level.
The simulations showed the need for BESS energy capacity exceeding the limits of the
implemented model.
==PAGE:62==
50
5 Conclusion
The requirements for the BESS to completely remove the peaks breaking the 50% load
level has been estimated by summing all of the need through a 24 hour cycle and com-
paring it to the recharging opportunities for the BESS. The capacity needed was found
to be a factor of 5 larger than the currently planned battery.
A model of a part of the Nordhavn medium voltage power grid, containing two parallel
10kV feeders with 10/0.4kV transformers connected along their length has been adapted.
The model has been expanded to also include loads on the 0.4kV side of the transformers
connected on each feeder.
A basis load profile was developed for the 0.4kV loads by making an averaged 24-hour
load profile from Energinet.dk consumption data and scaling it by the number of con-
sumers per transformer.
The consumption per consumer were increased in order to reflect a future load level.
The model has also been upgraded with the true parameters for the line segments for
each feeder using cable data provided by DONG.
The model has been implemented using MATLAB Simulink with the Simscape Power
Systems toolbox to simulate the three phase network.
The parameters for the fast charger has been developed in terms of charging cycle and
power. The charging cycle frequency follows the pattern of Movia bus line 3A which
terminates in Nordhavn. The pattern varies through a 24 hour cycle. The charger power
has been determined to be 500kW based on E-bus consumption data and available tech-
nology.
A Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) model has been developed and implemented
into the Simulink model. The BESS has a max in/output power of 1MW and a capacity
of 500kWh, its parameters are based off the planned BESS to be installed in Nordhavn.
The battery has been used as a dedicated support to the fast charger. in the scenarios
where it is present it is located on the same load bus as the fast charger.

5.1
Future Work
To further improve the precision of the model, load profiles for the 0.4kV loads could
be modelled from real world data measured at each transformer. This would also give a
more precise ratio of active to reactive power. An expansion of the model to include all
the branches of the 10kV Nordhavn grid would also have to be developed to give a full
picture of the effects a fast charger may have on the grid as a whole.
A full model of the fast charger could be developed to see the impacts the charger will
have on transient behavior of the grid. This would show the ramp up, voltage spikes
and start up current draw which may affect the operation of the power grid.
