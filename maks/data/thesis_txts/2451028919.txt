Abstract 
Since the first introduction to the market in the 1920s, PCBs had been widely used in various 
commercial and chemical industries products for a wide range of application due to their 
special and unique physicochemical properties. Their widespread use in the building materials, 
coupled with their high persistence in the indoor environment, makes the PCBs exist in the 
indoor environment for a long time. In addition, PCBs exert adverse impacts on humans, such 
as inducing various cancers. Therefore, this study aimed to develop and apply an equilibrium 
sampling approach to determine the exposure to indoor air PCBs. It was further tested whether 
the increased wind speed could shorten equilibrium time or standardize the sampling conditions. 
 
The results showed that increased wind speed indeed accelerated the uptake kinetics. 
Comparing with the reference results reported by Katrin Vorkamp, the time for PCB28, 52, 
and 101 required to reach their thermodynamic equilibrium stage was reduced by at least three 
times. More specifically, the t90% of PCB28, 52 and 101 were shortened and controlled nearly 
within two days. There was no comparable data available for PCB 118, 138 and 153 in the 
reference case, but it still took them less time in this case to reach the thermodynamic 
equilibrium state compared to PCB 28, 52, and 101 in the reference case. This could help to 
infer that the equilibrium time for those heavier PCBs (118, 138 and 153) could also be reduced 
due to elevated wind speed. But additional experiments were needed to confirm and quantify 
this inference.  
 
There was no adequate data to accurately determine whether the increased wind speed could 
exert the standardized effects on the sampling condition throughout the whole experiment, 
because the choice of analytical methods, coupled with other associated uncertainties, had great 
influences on the consistency of experiment data. When the same samples were analyzed via 
different analytical methods, the equilibrium concentrations of PCBs were three to six times 
different, and it also led to that the PCB concentration ratio between the PDMS and the indoor 
air had 0.6 log unit difference. Oppositely, when the same analytical methods were applied to 
analyze the different samples that collected at different locations or different time, it gave the 
similar concentration ratios between the media. Generally, it is of a challenge to experimentally 
determine the partition coefficients, even under a well-controlled lab condition. Those 
experimentally determined PCB concentration ratios between the PDMS and the indoor air 
were very similar to the value of calculated partition coefficients from Gilbert et al.

Chapter 1  
Introduction 
1.1 Background 
1.1.1 Chemical structure 
Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs, CAS number: 1336-36-3) is a class of synthetic organic 
compounds with two connected benzene rings (biphenyls), where the replacement of hydrogen 
atoms by chlorine atoms occurs. The trade-off between the number of chlorine and hydrogen 
atoms in the benzene rings is clearly revealed by its chemical formula (C12H(10-n)Cln), which 
means one to ten hydrogen atoms can be substituted by chlorine atoms. The positions of 
replacement can be further illustrated by its general chemical structure shown in Figure 1-1. 
 
Figure 1-1. General chemical structure of PCBs. The “x” and “y” stand for the number of chlorine atoms in each benzene 
ring, ranging from 0 to 5 in each biphenyl ring. The number from 2 to 6 (2’ to 6’) means the available positions for 
substitution. 
 As shown in Figure 1-1, only position 2 to 6 and 2’ to 6’ have replaceable hydrogen atoms, 
and it also indicates there are in total ten hydrogen atoms available for substitution by chlorine 
atoms. Without considering the difficulty of replacement reaction or the possibility of 
synthesis, there is a total of 209 PCB congeners possible owing to the variation in number and 
position of chlorine in the benzene rings. However, in practice, only around 130 PCBs have 
been found in commercial products (DHA., 2013).  
1.1.2 Physical-chemical properties 
PCB congeners generally show great similarities in their physicochemical characteristics. For 
instance, those PCB mixtures are hydrophobic in the water, but they have relatively high 
solubilities in organic solutions, fats and oil. They are inert, inflammable, chemically stable,

electrically insulating and good conductors of heat.  Apart from those general similarities, 
chlorine content and position normally lead to the differences in some specific properties within 
PCB congeners. Along with increasing chlorine content, the melting point, vapor pressure and 
hydrophilicity increase, whereas the solubility and volatility decrease.  It should, however, be 
noted that this is not applicable for the whole PCB congeners, some isomers are exceptions. 
Moreover, some PCBs consisting of four or more than four chlorine atoms with one or no 
replacement at the ortho position are termed as dioxin-like PCBs, and those PCBs exert similar 
toxicity as dioxin (Reddy et al., 2019). The orientation of two benzene rings concerning each 
other also affects the toxicity of PCBs, coplanar PCBs have been thought to be more toxic than 
the nonplanar congeners (Kimbrough & Krouskas, 2003).

1.1.3 Use pattern 
Their distinct and useful properties have certified PCBs as widely-used compounds in various 
commercial and chemical industries for a wide range of applications, such as dielectric fluids 
in electronic applications, various building materials and conductors in heat systems 
(Frederiksen et al., 2012; Jensen et al., 2013; Reddy et al., 2019).  The total accumulated sale 
of PCBs in the whole world reached 1.3 million tons since its first introduction in 1920 to 1980 
(Breivik et al., 2002; Jensen et al., 2013; Vorkamp et al., 2016). Especially in the years the 
==PAGE:21==

1950s to 1970s, this period experienced the most extensive use of PCBs as a result of high-
intensity construction activity in some developed countries. For example, in Denmark, around 
1100 to 2000 tons of PCBs were consumed, among which over 600 tons PCBs were used in 
electrical appliances (Frederiksen et al., 2012).  Corresponding to its high construction 
activities, around one-third of building stock in Denmark has been thought to be built during 
this period, and it means there are still considerable PCB-containing products remaining in the 
environment. The construction materials are the main sources of PCBs in the indoor 
environment. 
1.1.4 Human exposure 
 People are exposed to PCBs via various pathways, consumption of foodstuffs contaminated 
by PCBs has been confirmed as the primary route of PCBs exposure, and it accounts for 
approximately 96% over the total human exposure (Ivanescu, 2015; Reddy et al., 2019). Owing 
to their high solubility in the oil and lipids, those PCBs are prone to accumulate in the lipid 
parts of creatures, thereby magnifying through the food chains and finally resulting in the high 
level of PCBs in the fatty parts of human body (Barrs et al., 2004; Jensen et al., 2013). The 
accumulated PCBs in the human body exerts adverse impacts. 
 
Over the past few years, inhalation has also been demonstrated as another important exposure 
pathway of humans to PCBs in the indoor environment.  In different cases that not taking the 
dietary intake into consideration, the contribution of inhalation to overall intake of PCBs in 
human body was estimated to fluctuate between 6 and 64% with mean at 25%. As a result of 
government efforts and related regulations, the concentrations of PCBs in the foodstuffs have 
decreased in the past few decades, and it directly results in the reduction in the proportion of 
dietary intake. Then PCB intake via inhalation will contribute more to the total PCB intake and 
the proportion of PCB intake through inhalation will increase as well.  
1.1.5 Effects on humans 
In the past few decades, the time people spend in the indoor environment has significantly 
increased and it was estimated that the percentage of time spent in the indoor building 
accounted for 91.7 % (Currado & Harrad, 1998; Owen et al., 2010). Considering the long 
exposure time of humans to the PCBs and increasing indoor PCBs level, it is of prime 
importance to identify the adverse impacts of PCBs on humans. There is adequate evidence 
showing the direct correlation between the exposure of PCBs and its carcinogenicity in 
==PAGE:22==

humans. PCBs were categorized as carcinogenic to humans by both The National Toxicology 
Program (NTP) and the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC). Various 
toxicological studies including those from IARC have demonstrated that PCBs exposure 
significantly increases the induction of liver tumors in rodents, brain cancer, liver cancer and 
breast cancer (Hemming et al., 1995; Loomis et al., 1997; Gallagher et al., 2011; Lauby-
Secretan et al., 2013; Brauner et al., 2014).  
 
Additionally, PCB exposure has been thought to interfere with the adult reproductive system. 
Low sperm mobility and decreasing sperm motility with increasing PCBs concentrations have 
been found in the toxicological studies of PCBs in the male reproductive system. The women 
exposed to PCBs during their pregnancy experienced a reduced duration of pregnancy, 
reduction of fetal growth. The exposure to PCBs might double the risk of early pregnancy loss 
in women. In children, worse mental and psychomotor development, such as short-term 
memory loss and lower IQ, have been attributed to the prenatal PCB exposure. Besides, 
dysregulation of the immune system, incidence of asthma, elevated blood pressure, obesity and 
diabetes have also been assumed to be related to the exposure of PCBs (Kreiss et al., 1981; 
Faroon et al., 2003; Everett et al., 2008; Tsuji et al., 2012; Jensen et al., 2013; Brauner et al., 
2014). 
 
PCB congeners have been chosen as target compounds not only because of their adverse effects 
on the humans but also because they are very hydrophobic and the conservation choice of 
model compounds. If the equilibrium sampling of PCBs could be applied to measure the 
exposure to PCBs in indoor air, this method is almost applicable to every volatile organic 
compound. Therefore, the research of applying equilibrium sampling method on PCBs can 
provide meaningful reference and foundation for studying other volatile or semi-volatile 
organic compounds in the future. 
1.2 Sampling methods 
It is well known that the selection of the sampling method affects the choice of samplers, 
experimental setups, subsequent detection procedures and even the final analytical results.  The 
choice of the sampling methods is mainly subject to the aims of the study or experiment. In 
general, there are two primary sampling methods applied to collect contaminants in air: active 
sampling and passive sampling. 
==PAGE:23==

1.2.1 Active sampling 
In this conventional approach, an electrical pump is deployed to collect a known-volume air 
through the filter, adsorbent bed, or the combination over a specified time. After extraction, 
extracted samples are used for subsequent detection in the analytical instruments. This 
approach is a mature technology and has been considered as the most accurate and precise 
method in measuring air concentrations of PCBs (Melymuk et al., 2014).  
 
However, it is worth noting that the PCB concentration obtained via this method is a total 
concentration of the particle and gaseous PCB, which can be interpreted as the active sampling 
is unable to distinguish the differences between freely-dissolved and particle-bound PCB 
(Mayer et al., 2003). This method can, therefore, only provide information about the presence 
of PCBs, rather than providing information for analyzing the exposure and pathway of PCBs 
in the air. Besides, there are various parameters, such as environment variables, sampling 
velocity, affecting the overall performance of active sampling. In addition, it is not handy in 
practical experiments (Tuduri et al., 2006). 
1.2.2 Passive sampling 
Unlike active sampling, passive samplers do not require any electricity or power sources, and 
they show various advantages in the sampling stage. When applying passive sampling method, 
the chemicals are not forced to filter and retain on the absorbent materials as in the conventional 
method, but they partition from a media with the high chemical potential to a media with low 
chemical potential until the corresponding thermodynamic equilibrium state is reached.  
 
Normally, the exchange of chemicals between two media with different chemical potential 
experiences three uptake stages: linear stage, curvilinear stage and equilibrium stage. The 
generalized uptake curve of chemicals is shown in Figure 1-2. Initially, the uptake proceeds in 
linear regime within a relatively short time. Afterward, it moves to the intermediate stage 
before reaching the steady state. In general, passive sampling is either controlled within the 
linear regime or in the equilibrium stage, rather than in the curvilinear stage, because this 
intermediate stage is affected not only by the factors affecting the linear stage but also by the 
factors affecting the equilibrium stage. The uptake profile of chemicals can be described by a

been proposed to quantify the sampling rate precisely, including introducing performance 
reference compounds, agitating the sampler to minimize the boundary layer (Vorkamp &

determination of an accurate sampling rate, since other new uncertainties and errors are likely 
to arise from those new approaches (Melymuk et al., 2014). 
 
Considering those associated limitations of kinetic sampling, equilibrium sampling might be 
an alternative method, since it not only has some advantages over active sampling, but it is also 
not subject to the sampling rate compared to kinetic sampling.

Owing to its sound theoretical basis and those above-mentioned attractive advantages over the 
active sampling and kinetic sampling, the equilibrium sampling method has been applied to 
measure the hydrophobic organic compounds, semivolatile organic compounds and volatile 
organic compounds in various environmental matrices e.g. water, soil, sediment and sludge 
(Baltussen et al., 1999; Maenpaa et al., 2011; Jeong et al., 2018; Sjøholm et al., 2018a; Sjøholm 
et al., 2018b). However, there are still many challenges for successfully applying this method 
to measure the indoor exposure to PCBs. It is normally time-consuming to reach the 
thermodynamic equilibrium stage between the media, and long equilibrium time makes 
automated sampling impossible.  
 
The deployment time of passive sampling varies with the environmental conditions (e.g. 
airflow and temperature) and samplers. Melymuk et al. (2014) reviewed passive sampling 
techniques and reported the sampling time for double-bowl polyurethane foam (PUF) varies 
from weeks to months, and it even takes XAD passive air sampler years to reach equilibrium. 
Similar results have also been reported in other studies (Wania et al., 2003; Pozo et al., 2006; 
Pozo et al., 2009). Compared with active sampling, the equilibrium sampling devices generally 
require four or five-fold sampling time, so controlling and shortening the equilibrium time has 
become the major challenge for the successful application of equilibrium sampling approach 
in practice (Mayer et al., 2003). Shorter sampling time also reduces the risk of degradation or 
vandalism of the samplers.  
 
As Eq. 1 and 2 show, both kinetic and curvilinear uptake are subject to the sampling rate. It is 
possible to promote fast uptake by accelerating sampling rate, and it can further shorten the 
overall sampling time. Several parameters influence the sampling rate: the ratio between 
surface area and volume of the sampler (A/V), and the wind speed has been considered the 
most important.

The A/V ratio has been considered to play an essential role in increasing the uptake kinetics 
(Mayer et al., 2003), because high A/V ratio promotes the diffusive exchange of chemicals 
between the media with fugacity gradient significantly, and the uptake of chemicals can thus 
be accelerated. Mayer et al. (2003) confirmed the positive correlation between the sampling 
rate and A/V ratio by the positive slope of the regression when the uptake rate constants were 
plotted against the A/V ratio of four passive samplers. Besides, Tuduri et al. (2012) and 
Bartkow et al. (2005) further quantified the direct relationship between the rate constant k and 
the A/V ratio:

PMDS coating, corresponding to A/V ratio around 106 m2/m3. The submicrometer PDMS 
coating enables the baking paper to be the optimal equilibrium sampler with fast uptake 
performance. 
 
Numerous researchers have frequently reported the promoting effects of high wind speed on 
the sampling rates. For example, Soderstrom and Bergqvist (2004) carried out an experiment 
with five passive air samplers deployed in an indoor, unheated and dark tunnel with different 
wind speeds (6 to 50 m/s) and found the highest wind speed corresponded to the best 
performance within kinetic uptake stage and the sampling rate increased with the wind speed.  
Markedly increase in the sampling rate was also observed in PUF disk samplers when the 
airflow increased gradually from 0 to 0.9 m/s and a sharp increase was noticed when the wind 
speed was up to 1.75 m/s (Tuduri et al., 2006). Besides, elevated airflow might also minimize 
boundary-layer effects and thereby establishing standardized sampling conditions around the 
air-side boundary layer. The positive relationship between the wind speed and the sampling 
rate has only been confirmed for the kinetic sampling, and it is worth further verifying whether 
increasing the wind speed can promote fast uptake kinetics throughout the equilibrium 
sampling period, and also testing whether the sampling condition can be standardized by 
controlling wind speed.

After sampling, solvent desorption and thermal desorption are the primary methods to transfer 
the analytes from the samplers to the analytical instruments for the subsequent analysis. In 
solvent desorption, certain amount (mL) of organic solvent is added to extract the analytes, and 
the extraction is normally concentrated to 1mL for detection. However, this method sometimes 
results in the inability of the analytes to be detected because typically only 0.1% of concentrated 
solvent extract is injected into the instrument (Greenwood et al., 2017). Compared to solvent 
desorption, thermal desorption shows the better recovery and better ability to quantify analytes. 
The sample was placed into a vial, where the analytes are directly extracted by heating via a 
stream of inert gas. This method can simultaneously complete the work of the analytes 
extraction and transfer the analytes to the fiber without consuming any solvent. The partition 
coefficient of the analytes between the fiber and the sampler is needed to convert the analyte 
concentration on the fiber to the concentration on the polymers. However, it is challenging to 
accurately determine the partition coefficients through practical experiments, even under a 
well-controlled laboratory condition (Vorkamp et al., 2016). 
 
In view of the limitations and drawbacks of these methods, a new analytical principle has been 
proposed based on the thermal desorption to get rid of the restriction of partition coefficient. 
The polymer around the fiber is changed to the PDMS as the baking paper in this study. 
Appropriate temperature and desorption time can help to establish the thermodynamic 
equilibrium stage between the PDMS-coated baking paper and PDMS-coated fiber. This 
method can also increase the PCB mass injected into the analytical instrument for the detection. 
1.3 Objectives 
The overall objective of this study is to develop and apply an equilibrium sampling approach 
for measuring the exposure of PCB congeners in indoor air. In this study, the baking paper with 
submicrometer polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) is used for the equilibrium sampling of PCBs in 
the indoor buildings. Therefore, the specific aims of this study are: 
1) Testing whether PCBs mass transfer from indoor air to the baking paper can be 
accelerated by increasing wind speed during the deployment period and the equilibrium 
time can thereby be shortened. 
2) Testing whether uptake kinetics can be standardized through controlling wind speed; 
3) Applying a new analytical principle for transferring analytes from PDMS-coated 
baking paper to the analytical instrument.

All PCB congeners both at location 1 and 2 showed convergence to the equilibrium, and lighter 
PCBs (e.g. PCB28) generally reached the equilibrium stage faster than heavier ones 
(e.g.PCB153). In equilibrium sampling, the uptake of chemicals has been confirmed to be 
driven by chemical potential or fugacity gradient between the media, and those two 
thermodynamic parameters are directly related to the availability of chemicals in the media. In 
other words, the more available the PCBs are in the air, the higher the chemical potential or 
fugacity gradient between the air and the baking paper, the faster the uptake of PCBs. 
Compared with those heavy PCBs, light PCBs are more volatile and more easily release from 
the PCB-containing sources.  In addition, most of PCBs have really high persistence in the air, 
which makes they can exist in the indoor environment for quite a long time (Jensen et al., 2013; 
Zeng et al., 2013). All those enable light PCBs to be more available in the ambient air than 
those heavy PCBs, and it thus takes light PCBs less time to establish the equilibrium between 
the indoor air and the silicone-coated baking paper.

In equilibrium sampling, the mass transfer of PCB congeners from the indoor air to the silicone-
coated surface of baking paper could be viewed as a two-step process: transport from the 
ambient air to the air-sampler interface, from the interface to the PDMS coating. The mass 
transfer of PCB congeners from the air to the polymer-coated media has been confirmed to be 
air-side rate-limited (Harner et al., 2003; Vorkamp et al., 2016). Elevated wind speed would 
exert directly positive effects on the sampling rate via introducing the turbulence around the 
air-sampler boundary and thereby reducing its thickness. The reduction of the thickness of air-
sampler boundary would contribute greatly to the increase of diffusion exchange of PCBs from 
the indoor air to the silicone coating of baking paper. Various reports have also been reported 
the direct correlation between the wind speed and sampling rate by (Ockenden et al., 1998; 
Tuduri et al., 2006; Tuduri et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2013; Brauner et al., 2014; Melymuk et 
al., 2014). For instance, the sampling rate of PUF disk samplers increased sharply when the 
airflow was elevated up to 1.75 m/s (Tuduri et al., 2006). Therefore, the contribution of the 
elevated wind speed to the shortened equilibrium time accounted for a large proportion.

Chapter 4  
Conclusion 
This project aimed to develop and apply the equilibrium sampling method to accurately 
determine the indoor exposure of PCBs based on its solid and sound theories.  Since it generally 
takes equilibrium sampler weeks, months or even longer time to reach the thermodynamic 
equilibrium stage of individual PCB congeners in practice, shortening and controlling the 
sampling time within a relatively short time (1-2 days) has become the major challenge for its 
successful application.  The use of an electric fan during the sampling period was assumed to 
promote the uptake kinetics, which directly linked with a reduction of the equilibrium time. 
Besides, the utilization of the fan might exert influence on standardizing the sampling 
conditions around the equilibrium samplers (PDMS-coated baking paper). Moreover, the new 
analytical principle was successfully applied to analyze the PCBs on the baking paper of 
experiment II in this study.  
 
Although the wind speed of the fan showed the decreasing trend from hub to the tips as well in 
space, all baking paper was attached on the area, where had the steadiest wind speed 
distribution. Thus, all experiments in this study were carried out under the nearly stable 
condition with the wind speed up to 5.1 m/s. Generally, the indoor temperature at both 
experimental locations did not fluctuate as excepted, instead it was kept relatively steady due 
to the use of the heating system. Only a small temperature difference was recorded and 
observed for all experiments. In this study, the indoor temperature was not regarded as an 
influential environment parameter, and it had no impacts on the equilibrium sampling condition 
and some other relevant parameters.  
 
To a large extent, the uptake kinetics in experiment I was affected by the selection of analytical 
methods, which contributed markedly to the difference in the equilibrium concentrations of 
PCBs on the sampler when the same samples were analyzed in DTU and AU. However, the 
equilibrium time for most PCB congeners was considerably shortened to less than two days 
and even less than half day for PCB28 and 52. Because the elevated wind speed promoted the 
diffusive exchange of PCBs between the ambient air and the PDMS coating on the baking 
==PAGE:52==
Equilibrium Sampling with Baking paper for Measuring Exposure of Polychlorinated Biphenyls (PCBs) in Indoor Air 
34 
paper. In experiment II, it included more sampling points at the beginning of the experiment, 
and all samples were analyzed via GC-MS/MS. The shortened equilibrium time was also found 
for most of PCBs, but PCB 138 and 153 had much higher t90% than that in experiment I. It took 
more than two days for them to reach 90% of its true thermodynamic equilibrium stage.  
 
There was no adequate data or results to conclude whether the standardized sampling condition 
could be established due to the increased wind speed during the whole experiment period or 
the effects of other relevant factors ,such as selection of detection methods or the re-partition 
of PCBs, had such domain effects that the standardization effects of increased wind speed on 
the sampling condition could not be clearly observed. The choice of analytical methods had 
significant influence on the equilibrium concentrations of PCBs, but two detection methods 
gave the similar logarithmic value of CPDMS/CAir only with 0.5-unit difference. Those 
experimentally-determined concentration ratios between the PDMS and ambient air were very 
close to the calculated logKPDMS:air. Additional relevant experiments could be carried out to 
better investigate the effects of the wind speed on the sampling conditions and the uptake 
kinetics, and it can also popularize the application of this new analytical principle in the 
practical experiments.

Chapter 5 
Future Studies 
The application of baking paper as an equilibrium sampler under ventilation conditions showed 
distinct advantages in shortening the equilibrium time for PCB congeners. Suggestions for 
future investigation, which would help to better develop and apply the equilibrium sampling 
method are therefore provided in this section. 
 
Only one wind speed has been selected to investigate its effects on uptake kinetics as well as 
the sampling conditions, it was not enough to determine its effects for those heavier PCBs (e.g. 
PCB 153 and 180) especially when there was not enough comparable data available in the 
reference case.  Therefore, several groups of equilibrium experiments could conduct under 
different wind speeds, coupled with reliable analytical methods, could give several comparable 
results. Comparison between those data could help to determine and quantify the effects of the 
wind speed on the uptake kinetics, as well as their function of standardizing the sampling 
conditions around the equilibrium samplers. 
 
Before the elimination experiment, the PCBs on the PDMS-coated baking paper were released 
to the ambient air again, and it provides two possible options for the elimination experiment. 
One is to find a container or bags with better sealing performance to store the baking paper 
after completion of the uptake experiment, the other one is to start the elimination experiment 
as quickly as possible after the uptake experiment is completed instead of storing them in the 
aluminum bags for a long time.  
 
Considering the significant effects on the uptake kinetics of elevated wind speed, it would be 
good to include more sampling points at the beginning of the uptake experiment. because it 
could avoid the fact that the uptake of PCBs occurs too fast in the early stage to accurately 
present the actual uptakes curves based on the experimental data. In addition, several relatively 
longer sampling points are also necessary for double confirm the PCBs already reach the 
thermodynamic equilibrium stage between the media.
