Abstract
As the size of the projects in wind energy evolved from few kilowatts standalone turbines
to today’s wind farms with multi-megawatt turbines, the design and optimization of
wind farms appears to be an important research topic. A highly important phenomenon
to consider in this aspect is the wind turbine wakes. When a turbine is in the wake of
another turbine, it experiences a decreased power production and increased loading. To
mitigate the eﬀect of the wake, control approaches such as yaw-based wake steering or
turbine repositioning are studied and wake center position tracking will contribute to the
further studies in this topic.
In this thesis, a wake tracking method based on blade load strain gauge measure-
ments is developed. To set a relation between the wake position and the blade loads, a
simpliﬁed single wake system is considered. As the wake position changes dynamically,
a diﬀerent forcing is experienced by the downstream turbine’s blades at each rotation.
Transforming the out-of-plane blade moments into the ﬁxed frame rotor moments, a
relation is set up between the wake position and these rotor moments. Measurement data
needed for the development of the tracking method is generated through simulations
in HAWC2 aeroelastic code where DWM model is built in. Wake tracking method
is developed in Cartesian and polar coordinates. The latter is investigated through
a parametric study where various conditions of the meandering wake are considered.
Eﬀect of the changing parameters on the model is presented as well as the performance
of the method under changing wake conditions is examined. Ambient turbulence in-
tensity and the wind speed are found to have the strongest eﬀect on the developed method.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
“Ensure access to aﬀordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all”, states
the 7th Goal of the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals (UNSDG, 2015).
Following this goal, increasing the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix
by 2030 is given as a target. By the reduction of the cost and also development in the
renewable energy technologies, a strong growth in their deployment has been seen in
the last decade (IEA, 2019b). Even though renewables’ share has increased in the mix
recently, for instance its share in electricity generation reached 25% in 2018 (IEA, 2019a),
an acceleration of change towards renewables is still needed to meet these goals.
Wind energy can be counted as one of the key sources in the future of renewable
energies. In 2019, with a 59 GW of newly added amount, the total capacity of the
wind energy in the world increased to 623 GW, accounting for the 24.5% of the total
renewable energy capacity in the world (IRENA, 2020). In World Energy Outlook 2019
(IEA, 2019b), the prospect of capacity additions for 2019-2040 period shows a 1190 GW
of added capacity of wind energy in the world, which is 21% of the total added capacity.
The share of the wind in capacity additions is expected to be 39% in European Union for
the same period. Oﬀshore wind projects are seen as a promising source in wind category
as they reach larger capacity factors and also as they are becoming more cost-competitive
with the advancements in technology (IEA, 2019b).
As the projects in wind energy evolved from standalone turbines of few kilowatts
capacity to huge wind farms with multi-megawatt turbines, the design and optimization of
wind farms appears to be an important research topic. In order to be competitive, a wind
farm is desired to have a low levelized cost of energy, LCOE, which measures the lifetime
costs divided by energy production. Low LCOE levels can be obtained by optimizing the
wind farm for maximal annual energy production and minimal maintenance costs. An
important aspect to consider for this aim is that the interaction of the turbines within
the wind farm are dependent on the changing wind conditions as well as the operation of
the turbines and this should be considered both at the planning and operation phases of
the project.
In wind farms, the aforementioned interaction between the turbines can be deﬁned by
the wake eﬀect. Electricity generation by a wind turbine is based on extracting energy
from the incoming wind, therefore, the energy in the wind leaving the rotor is less than
the one entering. This causes the wind behind the turbine to be slowed down and have a

higher turbulence intensity, which is called the wake of the turbine. Following this, the
downstream turbine in the wake experiences decreased power production and increased
fatigue loads creating an obstacle for the design aim mentioned earlier (Kim et al., 2018).
In (Barthelmie et al., 2009), the average power loss due to the wake eﬀect is found to
be of the order of 10% to 20% of the total power output of a row of wind turbines in a
large wind farm operating as normal and without regulation of the power output. Hence,
the wind farm layout is optimized to minimize this eﬀect and lower the LCOE. In order
to optimize the farm’s production further during the operation under the wake conditions,
wind farm control strategies considering the eﬀect of wake have been studied where the
main focus is on wind farm power maximization. (Knudsen, T. Bak, and Svenstrup,
2015; P. M. O. Gebraad, P. A. Fleming, and van Wingerden, 2015a) present a review on
the wind farm control literature and a tutorial on wind farm control-oriented modeling
can be found in (Boersma, B. M. Doekemeijer, et al., 2017). In addition to these, the
recent study by (Ali C. Kheirabadi and Ryozo Nagamune, 2019) provides a quantitative
review of wind farm control literature with the objective of power maximization. From
these studies, it can be seen that the control concepts proposed and investigated in the
literature for mitigating the eﬀect of wake are grouped under three main categories:
power de-rating, wake redirection and turbine positioning.
Power de-rating is the concept where the velocity deﬁcit in the wake behind a turbine
is reduced by de-rating the turbine from its optimal operational point by decreasing the
axial induction. As it results in greater wind speeds experienced by the wind turbine
in the wake of the de-rated one, greater power output is obtained at the turbine in
the wake. To maximize the output of the wind farm, the power loss of the upwind
turbines need to be compensated by the gain at the downwind turbines. Various studies
have been performed on this concept predicted relative wind farm eﬃciency gain in
a range from -16% to 34%, see (Hoek] et al., 2019; Marden, Ruben, and Pao, 2013;
Jennifer Annoni et al., 2016; Goit and Johan Meyers, 2015; W. Munters and J. Meyers,
2018). In (Ali C. Kheirabadi and Ryozo Nagamune, 2019), it is stated that as the ﬁdelity
of the evaluation method used in the studies on power-derating concept is increased,
the range of the predicted gains reduces to 0% or less for the ﬁeld tests. Even though
the capability of raising the power production of wind farm is not found to be strong
for power de-rating concept, studies by (Schepers and Pijl, 2007; Boorsma, 2012) have
shown that the power loss from derated turbines are at least captured by their down-
stream neighbors therefore the concept can be used for load mitigation with low power loss.
Another concept for controlling the wake is wake redirection by inducing a misalign-
ment of the upstream turbine with the incoming ﬂow direction so that the downstream
turbine can result in greater power production as the wake is deﬂected away. Though it
is possible to apply the misalignment in nacelle yaw or tilt degrees of freedom of the wind
turbine, studies in literature focused on the yaw based wake steering as the modiﬁcation
of the tilt angle is not available for present turbines. Similar to the power de-rating
concept, there have been numerous studies performed on yaw based wake redirection, see
==PAGE:14==
1.2 Literature Review- Wake Tracking Methods
3
(P. Fleming, King, et al., 2019; P. Fleming, J. Annoni, et al., 2017; Howland, Lele, and
Dabiri, 2019; Campagnolo et al., 2016; Pieter Gebraad et al., 2017; Wim Munters and
Johan Meyers, 2018). The predicted relative wind farm eﬃciency gain in these studies
range from -5% to 45%. The review by (Ali C. Kheirabadi and Ryozo Nagamune, 2019)
shows that the potential eﬃciency gains reported for yaw based wake redirection studies
are rather found to be positive and signiﬁcant negative values are rare. Additionally,
the predicted potential is not as sensitive as the power derating studies to the ﬁdelity of
evaluation method.
As the ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines are in their development process, a control
concept which is applicable to it, namely turbine repositioning, is also being investigated.
The concept is based on altering the positions of the turbines within a wind farm in
real-time so that the wake overlap is minimized. The limited number of studies on this
concept such as works by (Rodrigues et al., 2015; Paul Fleming, P. M. Gebraad, et al.,
2015; A. C. Kheirabadi and R. Nagamune, 2019) showed substantial potential for the
power maximization of wind farm. Though, further research on the potential of this
concept is needed as well as on its practicality.
The existing studies used static open-loop wake control approaches, for instance the
yaw angle of an upstream turbine for steering the wake does not rely on the wake position
at the downstream turbine. These studies focused on the potential of the wake control
approaches and the development of the controllers. There is a lack of studies into closed
loop wake control. Few studies such as by (S. Raach et al., 2016; Campagnolo et al.,
2016), considers the implementation of the yaw-based wake redirection by using the
feedback from the state of the wind farm to adjust the yaw angle to the desired optimal.
As to perform the aforementioned control strategies, identiﬁcation of the wind farm state
in terms of wake, for instance tracking the position of the wake center, is necessary to
take required control actions such as yawing the turbine. Without measurements of the
wake state, wake is solely modeled and the control actions are based on these models with
assumptions. Supplying the wake position information to the wake control mechanisms
will improve the performance of the controller since correct location is used instead of the
model. Therefore this thesis will study on the development of a wake tracking method
which will contribute for further studies in closed loop control of wind farms.
1.2
Literature Review- Wake Tracking Methods
As the literature is surveyed for the wake tracking methods, it is found out that current
methods can be categorized in two groups: by lidars, by blade loads.
Shown in (Bingöl, Jakob Mann, and G. C. Larsen, 2010; Trujillo et al., 2011), one
can track the wake position by use of a nacelle-mounted lidar. LiDAR, Light Detection
and Ranging, is a remote sensing measurement device which uses a laser beam and the
Doppler shift as the light hits the aerosols in the air to measure the radial wind speed.
==PAGE:15==
1.2 Literature Review- Wake Tracking Methods
4
When it is mounted on the nacelle of a wind turbine and it faces downwind ﬂow ﬁeld, it
can be used for characterizing the wake dynamics and for tracking the wake. Example
studies on the implementation of wake tracking based on lidar measurements can be
found in (Steﬀen Raach et al., 2018; P A Fleming et al., 2014). Although, the scanning
lidars can be used for wake tracking, their implementation is still conﬁned to the research
studies and they are not available in most of the wind farms yet due to their cost and
complexity.
Alternatively, several methods based on the concept of using the wind turbine as a
wind sensor for wake detection are found in the literature. In (Bottasso, Cacciola, and
J. Schreiber, 2018), a methodology to detect the wake impingement on a wind turbine
caused by an upstream machine is described. The method is based on the local wind
speed estimation by the out-of-plane blade root bending moment. By deﬁning a cone
coeﬃcient similarly to the thrust coeﬃcient, a rotor equivalent wind speed estimator
is given. This estimator is then used for deriving the wind speeds in four quadrants
where the lateral ones are used to detect the wake shed. In order to indicate the left
or right wake impingement, relative wind speed diﬀerence is calculated between two
sides and compared to a predeﬁned threshold, the wake impingement is detected as
left/right-sided. The method’s ability of detecting the partial wake overlap, also in
the presence of a sinusoidal wake meandering, is shown through simulation studies of a
high-ﬁdelity aeroservoelastic model of a wind turbine exposed to the turbulent wake model.
Further in (Schreiber et al., 2016), the previously mentioned method is extended
such that the position of a wake is tracked relative to the aﬀected wind turbine rotor.
The same concept of cone coeﬃcient and sector eﬀective wind speed is used in this
study to estimate the horizontal shear and the rotor eﬀective wind speed from blade
root bending measurements. Then, by setting an optimization problem to match these
observed quantities and the modeled ones with an engineering wake model, the wake
position is determined. In order to deﬁne the wake shape and the speed deﬁcit, it is
assumed that the ambient turbulence intensity and wind speed are known. The method
is veriﬁed through experimental results obtained in a large boundary layer wind tunnel
with using two instrumented scaled wind turbine model.
A third method found in the blade load category is (Cacciola et al., 2016). In this
study, a simple linear steady model relating the hub loads (nodding and yawing moments)
to vertical and horizontal shears is presented as well as the least-square algorithm to
identify the model from wind turbine data. The hub loads are the transformed blade
out-of-plane root bending moments transformed into the ﬁxed-frame of hub. As the
hub load model is deﬁned and identiﬁed, horizontal shear can be estimated from the
measurements of the hub loads. By also estimating the rotor eﬀective wind speed with a
standard observer based on the torque balance equation, the wake position tracking is
performed similarly to the one described in previous method of (Schreiber et al., 2016).
Simulation studies performed within a high-ﬁdelity aeroservoelastic environment shows
that wake position can be tracked with this method.

The current state of wake tracking shows that the lidar-based wake tracking is the
commonly adopted method in the literature for wind farm control studies. On the other
hand, this method requires the use of an additional and expensive sensor when it is
implemented in a commercial wind farm. Therefore, the recent idea of using rotor itself as
a sensor is an interesting and potential alternative for wake tracking since when turbines
have the load sensors such as strain gauges at the blade roots, no additional cost will be
added. Aforementioned methods developed around this idea show the ability of wake
tracking. However, the studies reviewed earlier mainly show the performance of these
methods in static cases and do not consider the meandering phenomenon which is the
unsteady motion of the wake. Only in (Bottasso, Cacciola, and J. Schreiber, 2018), a
case with meandering wake is tested for the performance of the method, though the
performance is only based on the detection of wake as to left/right impingement and the
method does not track the wake position. With a motivation of tracking the wake with
blade load measurements at a high accuracy, it is important to include the meandering
dynamic of the wake in the method. Furthermore, methods in (Schreiber et al., 2016;
Cacciola et al., 2016) use engineering wake models in addition to the training of the cone
coeﬃcient through simulations, so that the lateral relative position of the wake center is
determined in numbers. These engineering models do not incorporate the meandering of
the wake center and the aforementioned wake methods track the wake center position
as an averaged value over a time period longer than the meandering period. Better
control actions can be performed if the wake center position can be delivered as the
average over shorter time interval since the meandering of the wake will be captured.
This need of including the meandering phenomenon in the tracking method considered,
dynamic wake meandering model is seen as a good option for developing a new wake
tracking method via blade load measurements. Lastly, the performance of the methods in
(Bottasso, Cacciola, and J. Schreiber, 2018; Schreiber et al., 2016; Cacciola et al., 2016)
are only given for few number of cases of wind conditions. In this study, a new method
is developed which includes more cases in order to see the limitations and feasibility of
the method.

2.1
Wind Turbine Wake
When a wind turbine is present in an airﬂow, it eﬀects the ﬂow in its upwind and
downwind regions. The upwind region is referred as induction zone where the main
impact of the turbine is reduction of wind speed as in (Medici et al., 2011). Though
the ﬂow is aﬀected by the turbine several rotor radii upstream of turbine, the eﬀect is
stronger closer to the turbine.
An important phenomenon appearing in the wake is the velocity deﬁcit which results
as a reduction in the power output of a downstream turbine. Another phenomenon is
the increased turbulence intensity which causes the unsteady loading on the downstream
turbines to rise. The wake is also divided into two regions where the ﬂow characteristics
are diﬀerent: near and far wake (Vermeer, J. Sørensen, and A. Crespo, 2003). The
near wake region can be referred as the initial wake region since it is considered as the
region starting just behind the rotor and extending in length to 2-4 rotor diameters, D
(F. Porté-Agel, M. Bastankhah, and Shamsoddin, 2020). The ﬂow in this region is highly
dependent on the rotor characteristics such as the number of the blades, blade proﬁle,
geometry of the hub and nacelle. It is in a complex, three-dimensional and heterogeneous
form with the presence of the pressure ﬁeld from rotor, and vortices shedding from the
tip and root of the rotor blades. As the velocities of the air inside and outside of the
wake are diﬀerent, a shear layer thickening with the downstream distance is present.
Turbulent eddies are formed in this shear layer and the turbulence in the wake helps with
the mixing of the low velocity ﬂuid in the wake with the higher velocity ﬂuid outside. As
the momentum added to the wake by this mixing, it expands and the velocity deﬁcit
decreases (Sanderse, 2009).
The stability analysis by (Okulov and J. N. Sørensen, 2007) shows that the helical
==PAGE:20==
2.1 Wind Turbine Wake

tip vortices in the aforementioned shear layer are unstable and tend to break down at
some distance downstream of the turbine. The study of (J. F. Ainslie, 1985) shows that
this breakdown occurs typically after four rotor diameters and in (Jens N. Sørensen
et al., 2015), an expression of the breakdown position is given as function of the ambient
turbulent intensity and other parameters depending on the turbine’s operational char-
acteristics. After the breakdown occurs, the point where the side shear layers thickens
such that they hit the central axis of the wake is considered as the end of the near wake
region.
The far wake region starts from the end of the near wake region and the azimuthally
averaged velocity deﬁcit proﬁle in this region is expected to follow a self similar Gaussian
shape with a decay of centerline velocity deﬁcit as moved further downstream (Jens N.
Sørensen et al., 2015). The ﬂow in the far wake is mainly determined by the turbulence,
which is a combination of the ambient turbulence, turbulence generated by the velocity
deﬁcit proﬁle and the turbulence from the mechanical mixing in the rotor plane (G. C.
Larsen et al., 2008). For this reason, the details of the wind turbine will not have a
considerable eﬀect on the ﬂow in the far wake and the inﬂow conditions as well as the
operational parameters of the turbine will be enough to predict the ﬂow in this region.
Another phenomenon to be considered in this region is the meandering of the wake due
to the large scale turbulence in the incoming boundary layer.

2.3
Multi-blade Coordinate Transformation
The measurements of the loads on wind turbine blades such as the root bending moments
are in general done with strain gauges which measures in the rotating reference frame of
its corresponding blade. Similarly, the dynamics of wind turbine blades are generally
expressed in their respective rotating reference of frame. Besides, the response of the
blades to excitations such as changes in wind state, wake meandering, control inputs or
dynamic motion of tower can be considered to be given as the whole of the rotor rather
than the individual blades.
Multi-blade coordinate (MBC) transformation facilitates the expression of the blade
dynamics in a non-rotating frame. This transformation, which can also can be referred as
Coleman-Feingold transformation, originates from the work by (Coleman and Feingold,
1958) on the vibrations of the helicopter rotor blades and it has been widely used in
the helicopter ﬁeld. As it oﬀers beneﬁts applicable to the wind turbine analysis such as
removing the azimuthal angle dependance and allowing the eigenanalysis on wind turbine
system, it is used in the wind turbine ﬁeld as well. Studies by (M. H. Hansen, 2003) and
(Riziotis et al., 2004) are examples for the use of MBC transformation for the stability
analysis of operating turbines. In study by (Bir, 2008), an MBC scheme is developed for
application to the variable-speed turbines which may have non-identical blades. MBC
transformation guarantees time-invariant system matrix when the rotor is isotropic, that
it has three or more blades and the blades are equally spaced (Skjoldan and M. Hansen,
2009). In addition to the stability analysis ﬁeld, MBC transformation is used for control

CHAPTER 3
Eﬀect of Wake Position on
Rotor Loads
Described in the previous chapter, the ﬂow in the wake of an upstream turbine is aﬀected
by diﬀerent parameters and can be considered to be dynamic rather than static. This
dynamic change on the ﬂow will then have a strong eﬀect on the loading of the turbine
exposed to this wake. The downstream turbine’s rotor being at a comparable height
level to the one causing the wake, is the part of the turbine strongly aﬀected by the
wake since it covers a large area under the eﬀect of the wake. The wake from upstream
turbine causes an increased loading of the downstream turbine’s rotor. As the eﬀect of
the wake can be sensed strongly on blade loads and considered that the blades of the
turbines are instrumented with sensors, for instance strain gauges at their root, blade
load measurements can be used for tracking the wake center position.
In this section, the idea behind the developed wake tracking method is presented by
showing the eﬀect of the wake position on rotor loads in a simpliﬁed form. First, the
single wake system used in this study is presented. It is followed by the outline of the
wake position eﬀect on rotor loads and the parameters possibly aﬀecting this relation are
listed.
As shown in Section 2.1, the ﬂow in the wake is rather complex than simple. For
this reason, several assumptions are made in order to simplify the relation between wake
position and rotor loads. The ﬁrst simpliﬁcation is about the system considered here. A
two-turbine system (a.k.a single wake system in this text) as shown in the next section is
used throughout this thesis.

single wake system is visualized in Figure 3.1 where the downstream turbine is in full

(ey, ez) when the wake center is at the position (ys, zs).

3.2
Parameters eﬀecting the Rotor Loads-Wake
Position relation
The idea presented previously is based on many assumptions and only reﬂects a fairly
simpliﬁed conﬁguration of the wake. It should be noted that there are various parame-
ters shaping the wake therefore possibly aﬀecting the relation between the wake center
position in lateral/vertical directions and the ﬁxed-frame loads. Below, a list of these
==PAGE:32==
3.2 Parameters eﬀecting the Rotor Loads-Wake Position relation
21
parameters are given.
• Ambient Wind Speed: The rotor eﬀective wind speed at the downstream turbine
is dependent on the ambient wind speed. This will also aﬀect the thrust force at
the downstream turbine which eventually changes the rotor loads. Another eﬀect
of the wind speed is on the meandering of the wake. As the stochastic modeling
of the wake meandering is based on the wind speed, the wake position will have
larger ﬂuctuation around the source point. Additionally, in DWM model,the large
scale structures are modelled with the cut oﬀfrequency in Equation 2.8. Therefore,
higher levels of wind speed will results as higher meandering frequency.
• Ambient Turbulence Intensity: Turbulent wind causes the blade loads to have a
broad spectrum of frequencies. In addition to the ambient turbulence intensity,
the turbulence from meandering of the wake and the added turbulence due to the
wake shear also contribute to the turbulence sensed at the downstream rotor. In
the near wake region, turbulence level increases with the increasing downstream
distance from the source turbine. At a certain point in the transition region, the
turbulence level reaches its maximum and start to decrease with further increasing
downstream distance. Increasing ambient turbulence level leads to a faster wake
recovery which happens to be at a closer distance from the source turbine. Higher
levels of ambient turbulence also results in larger decay of the wake deﬁcit and in
larger expansion of wake width.
• Atmospheric Shear: The presence of atmospheric shear creates a variation of wind
speed with height across the rotor. It is then a source of tilt moment on the
rotor and will eventually aﬀect the curves showing the relation of the ﬁxed-frame
moments to the wake position. At the same time, atmospheric shear contributes to
the wake deﬁcit development in the far wake region as it has a comparable order
of magnitude to the wake shear. In the study by (Keck, Maré, Churchﬁeld, Lee,
Gunner Larsen, and Helge Aagaard Madsen, 2015), DWM model is improved with
the inclusion of the eﬀect of atmospheric vertical shear on the wake deﬁcit evolution
compared to the version that the atmospheric shear is only added to the mean ﬂow
after the wake deﬁcit is calculated.
• Ambient Wind Direction: When the upstream turbine is not aligned with the
incoming wind direction, the wake center is deﬂected from the centerline of the
incoming wind. Another eﬀect of the misalignment of the upstream turbine is
on the operation which then reﬂects on the loads on the downstream turbine as well.
• Operational State of the Upstream Turbine: The eﬀect of the operational state
of the upstream turbine is in both the velocity deﬁcit and also in the turbulent
mixing downstream. When the upstream turbine is operated at a lower point
==PAGE:33==
3.2 Parameters eﬀecting the Rotor Loads-Wake Position relation
22
than its optimum for the given inﬂow condition, it will lower the induction of the
wind speed across the rotor and result as smaller wake velocity deﬁcit. The loads
on the downstream turbine is then aﬀected by the operation of the upstream turbine.
• Wake Meandering: The wake center meanders as a result of the large scale turbu-
lence structures. The changing wake position, coupled with the operation of the
wind turbine and conditions of the wind, will cause the blades to have a dynamic
loading.
• Wind Farm Layout: The spacing between the upstream and downstream, here
considered as the downstream distance from the upstream turbine, alters the result-
ing wind ﬁeld sensed at the downstream turbine which has an eﬀect on the loads
sensed as well. At the same time, the meandering of the wake is dependent on this
downstream distance. At a further downstream point, expanded wake diameter
will result in a larger meandering of the wake center point.
• Wind Turbine’s Angles (Tilt, Yaw, Cone): When the turbines have non-zero an-
gles of tilt, cone or yaw, it alters the wind speed paths that each blade element
is following. This change in the path results as a diﬀerence of the loads on the
turbine than it would directly face the incoming wind.
In order to test the idea presented above and develop a method for wake tracking,
measurement data of blade loads and wake positions is needed. For this purpose, a para-
metric study in simulation environment is designed considering several of the parameters
mentioned previously. This parametric study is explained in the following section.

4.1
Simulations in HAWC2
The data required for developing the method for wake tracking has been generated by
simulations in HAWC2 aeroelastic code where DWM model is built in. HAWC2 is an
aeroelastic code developed and distributed by DTU Wind Energy which can calculate
the wind turbine response in time domain (Torben J. Larsen, 2019). The code consists of
several models to simulate the wind turbine under various external conditions: external
eﬀect, applied loads, structural dynamics and connection to the control system. In
external eﬀects, the external factors such as wind, waves or the soil are modeled. The
interaction of these external eﬀects with the turbine is then modeled through applied
loads in aerodynamic, hydrodynamic and soil models in the code. The formulation of
the wind turbine structure is based on multi-body system where each component, for
instance the blades, the main shaft and the tower, is modelled with Timoschenko beam
elements. The control of the wind turbine is handled through external Dynamic Link
Libraries.
The aerodynamic part of the HAWC2 code is based on Blade Element Momentum
theory and extended with several models to handle for instance dynamic inﬂow, dynamic
stall, eﬀects from large deﬂections (H. A. Madsen et al., 2020). The models included in
the simulations of this study are given in section 4.3.

In this chapter, the development process of the wake tracking method is explained.
Before the experimental study described in the previous chapter is fully performed, a
representative data set is selected for the method development. Starting with the idea
given in Section 3.1.1, the method is improved as it is detailed in the following sections.
Selected dataset includes the outputs from simulations with the variables as follows:
• U = 8m/s, α = 0.2, TIambient = 0, TImeander = 0.1, Seed = 1000, x = 4D, all (yS,
zS) duplets
After the dataset is selected, the blade out-of-plane root moments are transformed into
ﬁxed-frame loads by use of the formulation in Equation 3.1 and 3.2, in each simulation
subset. This process can be seen in details in section A.1.
As explained in Chapter 2, the meandering of the wake is associated with the large
scale turbulence and lower frequencies are present in the wake position’s spectrum. At
the same time, the turbulent wind results in a broader frequency spectrum of blade
loads. As a result, the transformed moments’ signals have a higher frequency content
than the wake position signals. In the further application of the tracking method in wake
control mechanisms, such a high frequency of estimation is not required therefore the
data used for ﬁtting can be smoothed by using a ﬁlter. The ﬁlter used in this study
is selected as simple moving average ﬁlter with window size of 10 seconds which was
found to supply enough resolution for capturing the wake dynamics and for removing
the high frequency noise from the transformed moments for the given range of simulations.
A trade-oﬀof using a ﬁlter for a smoother data is the time delay introduced in the
ﬁltered data. During the development of the method, when the ﬁlter is applied to every
signal before any ﬁtting is performed then the delay does not cause a problem. However,
when the resulting ﬁts are applied to the real actual systems, it should be noted that
what the method predicts will refer to the wake center information from previous time.
Similar to the MBC transformation, the moving average ﬁlter is applied to each
simulation subset and since ﬁrst 10 seconds of the data in each simulation will not apply
the ﬁlter with information from previous stages, this initial period is discarded from each
simulation set. An example of the ﬁltering is presented in section A.2.

5.3
Condition for points in the limited region
The method developed previously is based on the dataset with the limit of r ≤1D
implying that the turbine is under the eﬀect of the wake in this region. Although this
limit can be used for ﬁtting purposes, in the implementation of the method to the real
life cases a mechanism is needed to detect if the turbine is in wake or not. A candidate
for this is to use a limiting value for the collective moment as Mcol,lim and deﬁne the data
point in or out of the wake based on this value. As seen in Figure 5.11 where r is plotted
against Mcol, the cloud forms into the tail shape for large r values. This results from the
fact that when the wake center is far out and do not have any eﬀect on the turbine, the
turbine will operate as in free stream conditions for any wake center position without
any eﬀect. Therefore the same revolution of Mcol is observed for the increasing r.
The limiting value Mcol,lim can be determined as the maximum Mcol value of the
points within a region of larger r values. In this study, largest 2000 points are considered
for deﬁning Mcol,lim value. Also a margin of 0.1% is removed from the found maximum
Mcol value. The points within the Mcol,lim limit for the dataset considered in this chapter
are shown in Figure 5.11 in red color.
The ambient wind speed of the dataset presented in this chapter is in the below rated
region. Considering the thrust curve of the turbine for steady conditions (see Figure
5.12), in the below rated region, the thrust increases with the wind speed. Therefore,
the wind speed deﬁcit due to the wake results as a decrease in the thrust. The greatest
wake deﬁcit is present when the wake position is at the center of the rotor, therefore, the
smallest thrust is observed there. As the thrust is the main driving factor of the Mcol,
the smallest Mcol absolute value is observed at r = 0. This relation then results as the

The change in the meandering turbulence intensity do not cause a primary eﬀect as the

in the training simulations in HAWC2 when the method is applied to realistic conditions.

6.3
Limiting moment in turbulent cases
In Section 5.3, the limiting moment Mcol,lim is deﬁned in order to have a wake detection
feature of the method. As it is applied to the cases with TIambient, it is seen that this
feature cannot be used for turbulent conditions. As the smaller frequency content of the
turbulence causes the scattering of the Mcol moments along its axis, the vertical line used
for detecting Mcol,lim cannot be observed in turbulent cases therefore the selected limit will
introduce errors in wake tracking. In Figure 6.13, the application of the limiting value to
the case with x = 6D, Seed = 1000, Shear = 0.2, TIambient = 0, TImeander = 0.05, U = 8
m/s is shown. Red points indicate the data remaining within the limit while blue points
show the whole data. As it can be seen from the ﬁgure, a great portion of the data points is
rejected by this condition even though the wind turbine is under the eﬀect of these points.

Since use of this limiting moment would cause problems at the application, it is
disregarded in the further evaluation. In case no wake detection mechanism is applied
with the tracking method, there will be points where the wake center is out of the eﬀective

error in tracking the wake position will be higher at the cases with steep slope of the
linear ﬁt and higher turbulence intensity. As the moments’ ﬂuctuation increases with
turbulence intensity and the slope is steep, the gap between the actual and the estimated
point will be higher.

comprehensive study. Furthermore, a more detailed look up table is needed in order to
derive the sensitivity quantities more accurately.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to develop a method tracking the meandering wake based
on blade load measurements to supply the closed-loop wake control strategies.
As a starting point, a single wake system is considered in a simpliﬁed wake condition.
Out-of-plane blade root moments transformed into rotor loads by MBC transformation,
a clear relation between the rotor loads and the wake position was obtained. In order
to deﬁne this relation for changing wind conditions, a simulation study was designed
for DTU 10 MW RWT in HAWC2 aeroelastic code coupled with DWM. Changing
inputs of the simulations are selected as the ambient wind speed, ambient turbulence
intensity, atmospheric shear, meandering turbulence intensity, seed number of turbulence
generation, longitudinal distance between two turbines and the wake source position in 2D.
During the development process of the method, the ambient turbulence intensity is
assumed to be zero whereas the meandering turbulence intensity is present. In Method
1, the ﬁtting of the position y with data from Dlat and the position z with Dver disabled
the ability of the method to predict positions y and z at the same time. Introduction of
the polar coordinates of the wake position in Method 2 as well as using the whole dataset
then eliminated this problem. In two steps, using sine function instead of the angle itself
and r as the weights in the angle ﬁtting, Method 2 was improved and brought to its ﬁnal
form in this thesis.
As the method was developed to predict y and z through two functions, linear ﬁtting
of r to Mcol and non-linear ﬁtting of sin(θ) to Mtilt and Myaw, it was applied on the
datasets with changing input parameters. The parameters’ eﬀect on the ﬁtting constants
were inspected through a parametric study. Ambient turbulence intensity is found to
be the most eﬀective parameter on the ﬁts. Its inclusion in the system, deviates the
ﬁt constants from the excluded cases’ and also more scattering of the ﬁt constants was
observed. In addition to its own eﬀect, turbulence intensity also alters other parameters’
eﬀects on the ﬁtting. It is seen from the results of the constant a that the turbulent
cases with above rated wind speeds misﬁtted the data due to high noise level. The
larger ﬂuctuations of the wind speed at higher mean wind speeds reﬂect as the larger
deviations of the moments causing the misﬁtting. Similar to the wind speed, at larger
downstream distances, the data deviates from the assigned function’s shape and causes
worse performance. It initiates from the fact that the wake deﬁcit is recovered more at
larger distances therefore its eﬀect is not strong enough to decouple from the ambient
wind conditions.

8 Conclusion
60
The eﬀect of changing external conditions on the tracking method’s ﬁt constants
require the method to be scheduled for ambient wind speed, ambient turbulence intensity,
shear exponent and longitudinal distance between turbines. This can be performed via
using a 4D lookup table of the ﬁt constants p1, p0, a, b, c, and d as function of the
aforementioned variables.
The simulation data used in this study was collected with a high sampling rate. The
data is smoothed with a simple moving average ﬁlter of 10 seconds window since the
focus was on capturing the wake dynamics which is in the low frequency scale. As the
wake model suggests a formulation for the meandering frequency depending on the wind
speed and the wake diameter, a ﬁrst order discrete low pass ﬁlter is considered as an
alternative with this suggested cut-oﬀfrequency. A below rated and an above rated
wind speed are studied for the eﬀect of the ﬁlter. Low pass ﬁlter improved the goodness
of the ﬁt at the lower wind speed and did not yield a considerable eﬀect on the higher
wind speed case. The reason that no better ﬁt was obtained at the high wind speed
case is due to the low frequency content of the turbulent wind being smaller than the
cutoﬀfrequency given to the low pass ﬁlter. In order to cancel the eﬀect from this lower
frequency content, a lower cut oﬀfrequency is needed in the low pass ﬁlter. Use of a
lower frequency will also result in greater loss of the wake dynamics to capture which
opposes the objective of this study.
The method is developed with the assumption that the wake is sensed by the turbine
when r ≤1D therefore used this limit for ﬁtting the functions. The idea of using a
limiting Mcol moment in order to detect if the turbine is in the eﬀect of the wake or not
is presented. As this idea is tested with the results from the simulation study, it is seen
that the limiting moment could not be used for the detection of the wake in realistic
turbulence conditions. The tracking method can be supplied with another detection
method in order to reduce the error in the prediction of the wake position.
The method is capable of tracking the wake position in both y and z directions when
no ambient turbulence intensity is assumed. RMSE value of y prediction for these cases
ranges between 0.03D and 0.2D which is promising. It is found that the position z is
predicted with more error than the prediction of y, the maximum RMSE value of z pre-
diction is found to be at 0.33D. As the ambient turbulence intensity is introduced to the
system, it is seen that the error level in the wake position prediction has been increased.
The range of RMSE value for y prediction in TIambient = 0.1 wind conditions is found
to be from 0.13D to 0.48D. Introduction of the ambient turbulence to the system, not
only drops the performance levels but also disables the prediction of z at most of the cases.
The cross-validation study performed with the training and validation sets showed that
they have comparable RMSE values. For some low wind speed cases with TIambient = 0.1,
the RMSE of the validation set is 35% larger than the training set’s value. Sensitivity

8.1 Future Work
61
8.1
Future Work
The recommendations for the future work are summarized in this section.
In the development of the method, ambient turbulence intensity was omitted in order
to see the general trend of the relation between the wake position and rotor loads. In
order to account for the eﬀect of the ambient turbulence, it can be included in the
development process from the beginning.
The selection of the a cross grid for the wake source position in the simulations helped
with the development of the method at no ambient turbulence conditions. As it is seen
from the results with turbulent cases, tracking of the position z was disabled in many
conditions. The dataset used for training the method can be reduced to only lateral wake
source positions in order to improve the position y tracking capability of the method.
Also, a ﬁner grid for the wake source positions can be selected in order to compensate
the reduced number of points for the ﬁtting.
The ﬁtting of the wake position is performed in two separate functions: one for ﬁtting
r and another for the angle θ. In order to optimize the method for y prediction, these
functions can be combined into one to deﬁne y position.
A wake detection method can be implemented into the tracking method to reduce the
prediction error arising from the points which are predicted within the eﬀective region
even though they are out of the wake. The detection method could be developed based
on the rotor eﬀective wind speed compared to the ambient wind speed. Moreover, the
rotor eﬀective wind speed could be introduced to the function deﬁnitions used for the
ﬁtting to reduce the eﬀect of the turbulence in the ﬁtting.
The ﬁlter for smoothing the data can be selected as the low pass ﬁlter introduced in
this study. Additionally, several seeds can be used for the ﬁtting of the data to improve
the robustness of the method.
In this study, the angles of the turbine were not considered. An investigation can be
performed with the consideration of the tilt and cone angles of the turbine at its original
values. In addition to these, the eﬀect of the yaw misalignment and the ambient wind
direction can be studied.
In order to apply the method to realistic conditions, a broader range of the input
parameters are needed to create comprehensive look up tables. A more detailed grid of
ambient turbulence intensity is especially suggested since in this study, it is only used as
binary. The meandering turbulence intensity of the future cases can be considered same
as the ambient turbulence intensity.
