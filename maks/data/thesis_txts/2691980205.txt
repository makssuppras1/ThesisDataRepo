Abstract
One of the main obstacles in the building and construction sector is to reduce the
CO2-eq emission. The climate projection shows that the target of a reduction of 70%
for the total net emissions before 2030 will not be reached, with the present politics in
Denmark. In detail, the projection for the CO2-eq emission related to the cement indus-
try remains at the same level. In addition, the majority of road bridges in Denmark are
constructed with reinforced concrete. To propose new initiatives that can contribute
to reach the target in 2030, the purpose of this thesis is to investigate whether it is
possible to design a highway bridge with timber as the main construction material,
which is cost competitive compared to an equivalent concrete bridge.
The design process in this thesis is divided into a preliminary design phase and a
detail design phase. In the preliminary phase a parametric study with Grasshopper
and Karamba3D is conducted.
A combination of diﬀerent static systems, diﬀerent
types of superstructures and diﬀerent types of road decks are analyzed.
Based on
the parametric analysis a design with four timber girders in composite action with a
concrete deck was selected. In the detail design phase the construction is analyzed with
FEM-Design, however, special cases are assessed manually. The cost and the global
warming potential of the ﬁnal design of the bridge is compared to an existing timber
bridge and an existing concrete bridge. By an overall comparison, the timber-concrete
composite bridge has a cost at the same level as the concrete bridge, but a lower global
warming potential.
The estimations for the material cost and the CO2-eq emission are discussed to obtain a
more nuanced perspective to the result of the comparison between the three bridges. In
addition, proposals for optimizing the design of the bridge are presented. At last, it is
concluded that it is possible to design a cost competitive highway bridge with timber,
but it might not be the most cost eﬀective method of reducing the CO2-eq emission
related to them.

Chapter 1
Introduction
”The next big task will be a massive reduction of the CO2 emission regarding
our constructions and it will be diﬃcult to accomplish, if we are not ready
to walk new ways”,
said Niels Højgaard Pedersen, head of department of constructions at the Danish Road
Directorate [14]. The above citation clariﬁes the importance of thinking out of the box
in the construction sector to comply with the target of reducing the CO2-eq emission
by 70% before 2030 in Denmark.
In 2019 the total greenhouse gas emission in Denmark was 46.7 millions kiloton CO2-eq
per year [18], where an emission of 23.2 million kiloton CO2-eq is required to comply with
the target. The construction process and the manufacturing of construction materials
in the building and infrastructure sector accounts for 10% of the total CO2-eq emission
[37].
In the projection for the development in the CO2-eq emission in the building sector, it
is expected that the main reduction is found in the additional manufacturing industries
over the next 10 years. The reduction of the main contributor, the cement industry,
which accounts for about 50% of the total CO2-eq emission from the sector is expected
to remain unaltered [19].
In addition, since 2010 the volume of the traﬃc on the roads and highways has increased
by 25% [64]. Due to the increased volume of the traﬃc new bridges need to be built
and many of the existing bridges need to be replaced or extended. With starting point
in that most road- and highway bridges in Denmark are built of reinforced concrete and
with the projection for the CO2-eq emission in the building sector it seems obvious that
there is an unexploited potential by reducing the amount of concrete in constructions.
It is relevant to point out that this thesis only will focus on the design of a highway
bridge. An eﬀective method could be to replace concrete with timber, because as a
construction material, timber certainly has an environmental potential since it is a
renewable and carbon neutral resource. With the proper technique and design, timber
structures could be extremely strong and can achieve a lifespan as long as concrete’s.
Most timber bridges built today use glued-laminated timber due to its high strength
and ﬂexibility in relation to its shapes and sizes [51].

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.1. OUTLINE OF THE THESIS
With a high degree of prefabrication, the components can be assembled in parts, trans-
ported to the site and are almost ready to be installed. Prefabricated timber bridges
can be assembled quickly, where the preliminary work such as pre-drilling and surface
treatment can be done in the factory. This leads to a reduction of the construction
time, hence minimizing the disruption of the traﬃc ﬂow [67].
With all the mentioned advantages of timber, especially the environmental impact,
timber seems as an obvious material, but a previous project in Denmark from 2001 has
found that there is a huge extra expense related to a highway bridge constructed with
timber [65]. The above leads to the following problem deﬁnition for this thesis:
Is it possible to design a highway bridge with timber as the main construc-
tion material, which is cost competitive compared to an equivalent concrete
bridge?
Based on the problem deﬁnition and the information above, it is expected that the
outcome of this thesis is to provide a nuanced answer to whether it is feasible to design
highway bridges with timber as the main construction material. The thesis is carried
out as a practical study where a new timber bridge is projected. The total cost and
the global warming potential of the ﬁnal solution are compared with a similar concrete
bridge.
1.1
Outline of the thesis
This thesis is organized as follows: chapter 2 presents an overview of two existing bridges
that are considered as the baseline in this thesis. Chapter 3 presents an introduction
of timber as a structural material and a presentation of a series of special construction
elements made of timber. In addition, it contains an overview of the programs used
in this thesis and a small evaluation of how to create composite cross sections in the

3.1
Timber as a structural material
Wood is an organic material, which in construction terms can be divided into two
categories: softwood - needle shaped leaf and hardwood - broad leaves. Softwood is used
in most engineering aspects, either as single units or glued together. Having wood as an
engineering material presents some issues, since it is an anisotropic material. A simple
physical model which represent the properties of wood is a bundle of thin tubes glued
together. In general the strength of wood is highest in the ﬁber direction and inferior
when stressed perpendicular to the ﬁber direction. For shearing action the opposite
strength characteristic applies. Actions in the ﬁber direction easily delaminates the
wood, while transverse actions needs to ”cut” through the material [40].
Generally, the direction of the ﬁber is indicated with a subscript 0 and the transverse
direction is indicated with a subscript 90.
Normal stresses are denoted by σ with
subscript c (compression) or t (tension). Shear stresses are denoted by τ with subscript
v (transverse shear) and subscript r (rolling shear). The diﬀerent notations and terms
are illustrated in ﬁgure 3.1.
The strength and stiﬀness of construction timber are speciﬁed in [30] for solid timber
and [21] for glued laminated (glulam) timber. In this thesis the axis along the structural
member is denoted as the x-axis, while the y-axis and z-axis is the principle axes of the
cross section. Bending around the stronger axis is assumed to be around the y-axis.

3.1.3
Weather protection
To increase the durability of timber constructions, there is in general two diﬀerent
directions: mechanical protection and chemical treatment. Actions should always point
towards protecting the wood with mechanical installations. Studies has shown that
when relying on either natural durable wood species or chemical treatments it is not
possible to achieve 100 years lifetime of the bridge [54]. With a proper design and
mechanical protection, timber bridges can last over 100 years.
An example is the
Ponte Timber bridge over the river Finn at Loom, Norway, shown in ﬁgure 3.2. In
the design of the bridge there has been a strong emphasis on actively protection the
bearing wood elements [11]. When protecting the timber construction with cladding,
it can be beneﬁcial to use an open design, which allow for easy close up inspection of
the bridge. With a closed cladding it should be removed at every inspection [54].
Figure 3.2: Ponte timber bridge [11]

for the load bearing elements. In the design process, increasing the durability of the
wood can also be done by passively protecting the wood with the deck. A research has
found that when the timber structure is placed underneath the deck there is a higher
preservation of the wood than compared to when the timber structure is placed on the
sides or above the road deck [38].

3.2.2
Cross laminated timber
Cross laminated timber is a timber construction element made of timber boards which
are glued together crosswise. CLT is in general used for load bearing walls and slabs
in multi-story timber buildings. In the recent years, CLT has also been used for bridge
decks. For example Hundorp Bridge in Norway [44] and Mistissini Wooden Bridge in
Qu´ebec [39], where CLT is used as the bridge deck.
Most CLT panels comprise at least three and usually a maximum of seven layers of
boards. The layers of boards are usually glued perpendicularly together. The indi-
vidual boards are ﬁnger-jointed in order to create larger elements. The thickness of
the boards varies from 20 to 40 mm. The size of the CLT elements varies depending
on the manufacturers [56]. CLT is orthotropic structural material, thus possess dif-
ferent characteristics for actions in both directions compared to other timber products
which normally carry the load in one direction. The basic calculation of design proper-
ties (stresses and displacements) of CLT panels are often based on mechanical jointed
beams theory (Gamma method) or Timoshenko beam theory [9].
The Gamma method is normally used for cross section with 3-and 5-layer panels and
is limited to single span structures subjected to uniformly distributed loads [4]. In the
method, only the eﬀective bending stiﬀness is used for the calculation. For the Gamma
method, the net cross-section values are dependent on the span of the elements. This
means that the eﬀective bending stiﬀness is greatest in the main direction along the
ﬁber direction, where the angle is 0◦[56]. Figure 3.5 illustrates the bending stresses σ
and stiﬀness E of a CLT slab of 5 layers based on the Gamma method.
Figure 3.5: Design parameters of a 5-layer CLT slab based on Gamma methode
In ﬁnite element analyses (FEA), the mechanical behavior of a CLT panel is considered
as a composite plate/shell. In FEM-Design, the Reissner-Mindlin plate theory is applied
for CLT.

Programs
To model and analyze as many diﬀerent bridge designs as possible, the thesis relies on
the use of design and analyze software. The geometrical model of the bridge is created
with the visual programming language Grasshopper, which runs within Rhinoceros 3D.
The main advantage of using Grasshopper is that it is possible to create the bridge as
a parametric model. Furthermore, Grasshopper is compatible with the other programs
used for analyzing the bridge.
3.4.1
Karamba3D
For the structural analysis of the early bridge designs the ﬁnite element program
Karamba3D is used. The main advantage of Karamba3D is that it is built as plugin
for Grasshopper, thus it is possible to directly analyze the parametric model and make
changes based on the results and use optimization algorithms available in Grasshopper.
To get a deﬂection which represents that actual behavior of the construction as precise
as possible the elasticity of joints is incorporated into the model by the use of springs.
Spring elements in Karamba3D allow for setting the stiﬀness relation between two nodes
in the model [48]. An evaluation of how the spring elements in Karamba3D are used
can be found in appendix A.
Limitations
Karamba3D has limitations compared to traditional FEA-tools. The limitations centers
mainly around the utilization of the elements. Some of the limitations and how they
impact the result of the parametric study are presented below.
Stiﬀness properties can be set for an orthotropic material but strength properties can
only be speciﬁes as a homogeneous material. This leads to errors when using timber as
the structural material due to the ﬁber oriented strength properties.
For shell elements the stresses are calculated as the Von-Mises stress and not as normal
stresses related to the orientation of the shell and shear stresses. Furthermore, with the
”Utilization of Elements” component the Von-Mises stress is directly compared with
the speciﬁed yield strength of the material.

3.6
Topology optimization
The fundamental theory about the optimal layout structural frameworks were devel-
oped by Michell (1904). The theory was especially based on optimization of trusses
and frames structures. The aim is to ﬁnd a structural layout with the lowest volume in
a given system of external forces. The stresses in the structural members are subject
to the conditions: −σc ≤σ ≤σt, where σ is the axial stress in a single structural mem-
ber. The solution of this problem is also known as plastic optimum design [10]. When
solved computationally, structure layout optimization problems are using the ground
structure approach that was proposed by Dorn et al (1964) and further developed by
Gilbert and Tyas (2003). The ground structure is generated and formed by potential
members by connecting nodes that are distributed in the design domain as illustrated
in ﬁgure 3.14. The most eﬃcient structure is identiﬁed by solving linear programming
problem. By using linear programming algorithms it is ensured that the global opti-
mum (absolute minimum volume) for the given problem can be found [31]. An example
of layout optimization is illustrated in the ﬁgures below. Here, the design domain is
shown in ﬁgure 3.12, while the optimized structure is shown in ﬁgure 3.15.

Chapter 4
Method
This chapter presents the methodology used for the preliminary design process. Fur-
thermore, a more detailed presentation of the three main design phases of the bridge
are presented.
4.1
Design methodology
The preliminary design stage is probably one of the most important stages in the design
of a bridge. At this stage the important aspects of the bridge is chosen e.g. static system
and materials [8]. The importance of the preliminary design phase is less present in
the actual design workﬂow which often involve a point-based-design (PBD) process. In
the PBD process the design of a structure is conceived through an iteration of single
decisions and evaluations of the found solution until a satisfying design has been found
[43].
To put a stronger emphasis on the preliminary design phase a diﬀerent approach to
the PBD process is used in this thesis. There is used a set-based-parametric-design
(SBPD) methodology where various design alternatives are analyzed together.
The diﬀerence between the two diﬀerent design methods is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1.
The outer circle symbols the feasible design solution space, meaning all bridge designs
which falls inside are possible solutions. The solution space is deﬁned by the designer by
constraints, and could include parameters such as support conditions, loads, material
properties etc.
For a more systematic overview of the solution space it can be divided into systems,
chosen by the designer, e.g. diﬀerent static systems. The number of solutions in each
category is dependent on the number of variables in the present design. Variables could
for example be the height and the width of a beam, or the numbers of beams in a deck
construction.

For both methods the solutions are compared to each other, where design solutions
close to the perimeter of the circle performs poorly, while solutions close to the center
performs favorable. The performance is based on chosen design attributes, e.g. price,
CO2-eq, mass etc. If the chosen attributes changes so will the ”best performing” design,
thus performance is not an absolute value, but a guidance to compare various designs.
The diﬀerence between the PBD process and the SBPD process is in how the solutions
are found. In the PBD method the designer choose a system and the design variables
within. One design is illustrated as a dark grey dot in the solution space. The per-
formance of the chosen design is then analyzed based on the chosen design attributes.
The designer can then either change the variables of the present design, start over with
a diﬀerent system or accept the present design as the ”best” solution. In the SBPD
process the designer ﬁrst chooses diﬀerent systems and the variables within. By the
use of parametric design, all the diﬀerent variations of a system can be analyzed. This
is illustrated as the light grey region in the design space.
One of the issues with the SBPD process is the high number of possible designs. If
there for example are four variables in a system and each variable has a range of 10
steps, the total number of design solution is equal to 104 = 10000 solutions.
One
method for reducing the calculation time is to use a heuristic optimization method to
ﬁnd the most favorable solution. Though the calculation time is reduced as a result of
the algorithm, the method has a issue. The starting value used in the algorithm is often
chosen randomly but can have an eﬀect on the ﬁnal output, which results in a local
optimum is being found [31]. This issue is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1 where the solution
space for the truss system has two peaks, but one of them is closer to the center.

4.2. DESIGN PROCESS
CHAPTER 4. METHOD
4.2
Design process
To ensure the most optimum design for the bridge is found, the design process is divided
into three phases: Preliminary design, Parametric design and Detail design.
4.2.1
Preliminary design
In the ﬁrst phase the physical domain that constrains the design space of the bridge
and the support conditions are speciﬁed.
To ﬁnd a low volume design for the bridge a topology optimization is performed. The
optimization is conducted with the aid of the plugin Peregrine for Grasshopper. There
are made two diﬀerent situations for the topology optimization. One where the domain
for the structure is limited to only be placed under the road deck, and one where the
domain for the structure is extended to the sides of the road deck and above it.
In extend to the solutions from the topology optimization other bridge designs are
analyzed. All of the new bridge designs are based on a few diﬀerent static systems and
constructions designs for the superstructure. Each of the static systems and type of the
superstructure are chosen as they possess diﬀerent beneﬁcial characteristics e.g. the
possibility to shelter the entire construction under the road deck. To keep an overview
of the diﬀerent solutions they are categorized based on their static system and the
design of the superstructure.
To have an objective evaluation of each of the bridge designs, two parameters are chosen.
The ﬁrst parameter is the overall cost of the bridge and second parameter is the global
warming potential (GWP) of the bridge superstructure. The cost of the bridge will
take into account which materials are used and an evaluation of how the bridge are
joined together to give the most precise results. The global warming potential is based
on the Danish Environmental Product Declaration data and the volume of the bridge
superstructure.
4.2.2
Parametric design
In the second phase a parametric study of the bridge is conducted to evaluate as
many diﬀerent bridge designs as possible. The parametric study is performed by using
Grasshopper and the plugins Karamba3D and Octopus. Grasshopper is used to create
the parametric geometrical model, Karamba3D is used to create the FE-analyze of the
parametric model and Octopus is used to optimize the construction.
Due to the described limitations of Karamba3D there are speciﬁed unique material
properties for the three timber materials used: glulam, CLT and SLT. To reduce the
computation time of the parametric study only a few of all the relevant load combi-
nations are used. The chosen load combinations are those which are assumed to be
governing for the design of the bridge.

the saved time.

Topology optimization
For the topology optimization there is evaluated two diﬀerent constraint scenarios.

The optimization is performed as a 2-dimensional problem, where the solution is a
frame design.
There is used diﬀerent load cases with a high point load placed at

combination with the high point load there is used a uniformly distributed line load.

Poisson’s ratio = 0. For both cases the structure is assumed to be protected against

6.3
Design categorization
The designs of the bridge are divided and categorized based on three diﬀerent factors:
the overall static system of the bridge, the type of superstructure and the type of load
distributing road deck. The diﬀerent factors for the categorization are presented in the

6.3.1
Static systems
In the design of the bridge three diﬀerent static systems are assessed, which are assumed
to be possible systems based on the giving constraints of the building site. The three
static systems are: (A) an arch bridge, (B) a strut frame bridge and (C) a continuous
beam bridge. The three diﬀerent static systems are illustrated in ﬁgure 6.4.
The beneﬁt of an arch bridge static system is that the main structure is mainly in com-
pression as opposed to bending. This allow for a more material eﬃcient construction.
One of the disadvantages of this static system is that the bearing arch is unprotected
against rain and will need protecting cladding. In addition, due to the curvature of the
arch they are cumbersome to transport as one piece.
The strut frame static system is a more simple alternative to the arch bridge, where the
members are still in compression to a degree due to the inclination of the columns. One
of the advantages is that the structure in placed under the road surface, hence more
simple to protect against the weather. The advantage of the continuous span static
system is the simplicity. All the construction members are placed either vertical or
horizontal and only vertical forces are transferred at the connections from dead loads.

6.3.3
Road deck types
At last, the diﬀerent types of the road deck for each supporting structure are used to
categorize the solutions. The three diﬀerent types of road decks are: (1) cross laminated
timber, (2) stress laminated timber and (3) concrete. The three diﬀerent types of road
deck are illustrated in ﬁgure 6.6.
The function of the bridge deck is to provide support to local traﬃc loads and transmit
the loads to the primary superstructure of the bridge. The three diﬀerent types of
road deck are chosen based on individual advantages. The CLT deck has boards in
two direction allowing to redistribute the load to the superstructure in the transverse
direction as well. A disadvantages is lower stiﬀness and strength in the main direction
due to the cross layering boards.
The SLT deck is able to transfer the shear stress that occurs between the slats and the
tensile stress caused by the transverse moment, making the deck acts a plate. In addi-
tion, the dimension stability of the SLT helps to prevent rupture of the waterprooﬁng
membrane, subsequently problems related to moisture. On of the disadvantages for
the SLT deck is that a relative large minimum thickness of 315 mm for the deck is
recommended [12].
The concrete deck type is the most common bridge material and has a high stiﬀness
compared to a timber deck.
Furthermore, the cost of concrete per square meter is
lower compared to timber. In addition, it is expected that a timber concrete composite
structure will result in a material eﬀective construction where compression is carried
by the concrete and tension by the timber. The disadvantages are the higher density
and higher CO2-eq emission compared to timber.

factors kc,2 and kcrit. The issue with the excessive buckling length is solved when the
cross beams are added to the construction. To prevent lateral torsional buckling caused
by negative moments at the columns, a bracing is introduced.

ﬁctitious bar at the columns. In the existing FE-model the columns are modeled as
a rod element that attaches to the rod element of the beam. This result in that the
columns attaches to the top of the beam if the dimensions of the cross sections are taken
into account. To correct this a ﬁctitious bar is introduced. A detail of the connection

Lateral torsional buckling
The lateral torsional buckling veriﬁcation of the main girder is calculated with an
eﬀective length which is based upon the distance between the cross beams. Because of
the eccentricity of the main girder the result from FEM-Design is prone to errors as a
result of the location of the cross beam relative to the cross section of the main girder
is not taken into account. At mid-span there is no error as the cross beams are placed
at the top of the main girder. Furthermore, with the shear connection to the concrete
it is not be possible to have lateral torsional buckling caused by compression at the
top of the main girder. The problem occurs near the columns where negative moments
causes the bottom of the beam to be subjected to compression.

girders need suﬃcient capacity to carry the dead load of the wet concrete and the

the concrete needs suﬃcient bearing capacity.

calculations. The density of the wet concrete is deﬁned in chapter 5. The characteristic
surface load of the wet concrete is determined as following:

cost and GWP values on the ﬁnal design are presented. It is analyzed how the cost of
the projected bridge would have been aﬀected if a lower cross section height was used.
At last, suggestions for further work and optimization of the ﬁnal timber concrete
composite bridge are presented.
9.1
Assumptions
Throughout the thesis assumptions and simpliﬁcations are made. This includes dis-
regarding load cases, specifying the design domain, assuming material prices and the
global warming potential for the construction materials.
9.1.1
Loads
Nature loads
The snow load and wind load are deselected based on a guidance for loads on bridges
[61].
Furthermore, load related to expansion and contraction due to change in the
temperature is deselected based on the statement that they in general are negligible
compared to expansion and contraction due to change in the moisture content [52].
Based on the presented literature it is expected that these three nature load cases may
not have an huge impact on the ﬁnal design of the TTC bridge if they were included.
Concrete creep
The eﬀect of creep and diﬀerent expansion rates of timber and concrete in timber-
concrete composite constructions are not covered in this thesis. The choice is made to
reduce the time spend on the detail design. In [46] and [1] it is stated that the eﬀect
can be unfavorable for composite constructions. Thus, further analysis in this area is
needed to ensure proper durability of the bridge. Though the eﬀect of creep should be

examined, it is expected that the magnitude of the stresses related to the creep will
not result in that the cross section of the main girders needs to be changed.
Accidental load and LM2
The special load cases for the accidental load and LM2 are disregarded in order to
narrow the extent of the detail design phase. The main purpose of this thesis is the
design of the superstructure of the bridge and not the secondary elements. With the
projected TCC bridge it is expected that the reinforced concrete deck is suﬃcient to
transfer the horizontal load to the abutment from the accidental load and have suﬃcient
punching capacity for the high concentrated load from LM2. Thus, including the two
load cases, they might not have a huge impact on the design of the superstructure, but
require special attention to the design of the road barriers and expansion joints.
Fatigue loading
The load case for fatigue load is not examined in this thesis. The choice is made to
simplify the veriﬁcation of the bridge in the detail design phase. EC5 for timber bridges
provides a method for verifying structures for fatigue loading. In table A.1 of [29], the
values for the coeﬃcients ”a” and ”b” are only provided for connections with dowels
or nails. In [5] a fatigue test of the HBV-connector is conducted and it is compared
to the results of an analysis made with the more advanced fatigue veriﬁcation method
based on the cumulative linear fatigue theory of Palmgren-Miner.
It is found that
with the fatigue theory of Palmgren-Miner a conservative fatigue load capacity for the
HBV-connector can be calculated.
9.1.2
Costs
Uncertainties of the cost of the superstructure might aﬀect the total cost of the bridge
design, thus a diﬀerent design might prove superior, or the comparison to the existing
bridges might be inaccurate. In chapter 6 it is found that the cost estimation for the
timber materials has a great correlation to the cost of the superstructure of the existing
timber bridge. Contrary to the cost estimation of the timber materials, the cost esti-
mation of concrete and reinforcement resulted in a deviation of 40%, when comparing
the theoretical material cost and the actual price for the concrete superstructure.
It is assumed that the 40% lower cost is a result of uncertainties regarding the expenses
related to the construction process. In the evaluation of the secondary expenses for
designs with a concrete deck an additional 0.3 millions DKK is added. The residual
diﬀerence of 0.7 millions DKK is assumed to be allocated for the cost of the scaﬀold
and form-work for the in-situ casting.
But it is acknowledged that the cost of the
construction process is a considerable uncertainty in the cost estimation of the timber-
concrete composite bridge.
Another uncertainty in the ﬁnal comparison between the three bridges is the cost of
the existing concrete bridge. According to a statement of the Danish Road Directorate
[62] the average cost for road and highway bridges in Denmark is estimated to be 20000
DKK/m2. This cost estimation is based on the sum of the unit cost of the individual
road bridges and the total road bridge area.

The price per square meter of the existing concrete bridge is 9215 DKK/m2 which is

Other cost assumptions
Assumptions that could aﬀect the result of the parametric study is estimation for
the amount of steel in the connections and the calculation of the required amount of
reinforcement in the concrete slab.
The amount of steel used in the connections in the parametric study is based on a pre-
liminary design assumption. In the ﬁnal design of the bridge it is found that 4.3 kiloton
steel is needed, while the assumed amount of steel was 3.9 kiloton. The diﬀerence be-
tween the assumed material consumption and the actual material consumption is 10%.
It is assumed that the other assumptions made for the diﬀerent types of connections
are as equally valid, thus there is a low possibility that another solution would have
been more economical.
In the parametric study the amount of steel used in the road deck is based on the
Karamba3D component ”Optimize Reinforcement”. For the solution chosen from the
parametric study to the detail design phase, the total reinforcement is estimated to
12.5 kiloton. In the ﬁnal design 42.6 kiloton reinforcement is required. The four most
economical solutions in the parametric study utilized a combination of timber girders
and a concrete deck (see ﬁgure 7.14 and 7.15). If the cost of the additional reinforcement
of 0.53 millions DKK is added to these solutions they are still the most economical.
CO2 emission
In the previous chapter it is found that the TCC bridge is around 300 DKK/m2 more
expensive and has a GWP which is about 10 times lower compared to the existing
concrete bridge. With the low carbon footprint of the TCC bridge it might appear
that the small increase in the square meter price is worth the saved CO2-eq emission.
But if the cost of carbon capture and storage (CCS) is taken into consideration it can
lead to another ﬁnding. The cost of CCS is estimated to be in the range of 595-1160
DKK/kiloton CO2 in the report [49]. The average of the estimation for the cost is 878
DKK/kiloton CO2 and is used for the calculation below.
It is calculated how much it would cost per square meter to compensate for the CO2-eq
emission of the concrete bridge if it should be at the same level as the emission of the
timber-concrete composite bridge. The diﬀerence in the emission per square meter for
the two bridges is 0.4 kiloton CO2-eq/m2. The cost of compensating for the increased
emission of the concrete bridge with CCS is 334 DKK/m2. This gives a total square
meter price of 9549 DKK/m2 for the existing concrete bridge, almost equal to the
square meter price of the TCC bridge.

9.2
Further work
The ﬁnal design of the timber-concrete composite bridge is simple in many aspects.
The timber elements are straight and only four diﬀerent cross sections are used in total.
All the timber connections are made as bolted connections. The concrete deck has a
constant height across the entire road surface and the reinforcement is placed with a
repetitive pattern, where only the area above the columns has additional reinforcement
in the top layer. Proposals for interesting areas of the bridge that can be optimized
further are presented below.
9.2.1
Post tension
As shown for the TCC with the lower cross section height, the deﬂection is the governing
factor when the girders become lower. It would be interesting to look into how the
bridge could be designed if the height of the bridge is similar to the existing concrete
bridge, without the need of excessive amounts of timber.
One of the methods for
increasing the stiﬀness of the cross section without making the cross section wider is
to introduce a post-tension system to the construction. Experiments presented by [45]
show that the deﬂection of a post-tension timber beam is 70% lower compared to an
equivalent timber beam without the post-tension.
The reduction of 70% is found for a continuous span static system, which indicates that
the technology is suitable for the type of bridge analyzed in this thesis. A principle
sketch for the placement of the post-tension tendon along the beams is illustrated in
ﬁgure 9.1, where the red line illustrates the tendon.

Material optimization
An interesting ﬁeld to investigate further is to optimize the material used for the design
of the bridge. One area could be to optimize the cross section of the girders and another
area could be to optimize the reinforcement design in the concrete slab.
Girder optimization
For the timber girder the highest utilization is found for the shear eﬀects near the
columns and at the abutment. Figure 9.4 shows that for the rest of the failure modes
the highest utilization is about 65% for tension at the mid-span and about 55% for
bending at the columns. In general, the utilization is under 30% for the rest of the
span length.
Figure 9.4: Utilization summary of the main girder
To reduce the utilization of shear eﬀects, inclined threaded screws could be inserted to
the girder acting as shear reinforcement. In [15] the technology is described as state
of the art, hence a low amount of information is available. In the same paper a low
increase of the shear capacity is found but it is mentioned that an increased shear
capacity of up to 38% is achieved in another study. To achieve the high increase of the
shear capacity, a great amount of reinforcement is required. Thus, general advances
in this ﬁeld is essential to utilize the full potential of timber in bridge and large span
constructions.
With an increased shear resistance of the girder it is possible to reduce the cross section
height signiﬁcantly, regarding ULS. With an utilization for SLS of 80% it is likely that
the deﬂection of the bridge will be governing before the minimum height regarding ULS
is found. To have a more balanced utilization for both ULS and SLS a pre-cambered
design of the girder could be a solution, which might be worth the extra cost in the
manufacturing process.
Reinforcement design
In the present reinforcement design an almost constant amount of reinforcement across
the concrete slab is applied. Only the area above the columns has additional reinforce-
ment in the top layer.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
With the ﬁnal design of the timber-concrete composite bridge it is found that it is
possible to design a highway bridge with timber as the main construction material,
which is cost competitive compared to a similar concrete bridge. It is found that the
square meter price of the timber-concrete composite bridge is 9534 DKK/m2 whereas
the price for the concrete bridge is 9215 DKK/m2.
Moreover, the global warming
potential is about 10 times lower for the timber-concrete composite bridge per square
meter compared to the concrete bridge.
If the height of the girders in the timber-
concrete composite bridge is limited to 1.0 meter to match the cross section height of
the existing concrete bridge, the material cost of the timber-concrete composite bridge
is increased with about 1.5 millions DKK.
A considerable uncertainty in the cost estimation related to the expense of the con-
struction process of the timber-concrete bridge is present. In addition, a substantiated
uncertainty in the cost of the existing concrete bridge is established. If the estimation
for cost of the timber-concrete composite bridge is underestimated and the cost of the
concrete bridge is valid, it is not cost eﬃcient to substitute concrete with timber, and
other technologies could be a more appropriate method of reducing the CO2-eq emission,
for instance carbon capture and storage.
However, if the cost estimation for the timber-concrete composite bridge is reasonable
and the cost of a concrete highway bridge in general is closer to 20000 DKK/m2 than
10000 DKK/m2, it is more economical to construct highway bridges with timber and at
the same time reduces the related global warming potential signiﬁcantly. At last, if the
cost estimation of the timber-concrete composite bridge and the price of the existing
concrete bridge are tolerable, it is hard to distinguish between which construction is
optimal from an economical perspective. Also, with the low cost for capturing carbon
dioxide they might have the same global warming potential level without a signiﬁcant
change in the price.
To summarize, the ﬁnal remark is that it is possible to design a cost competitive highway
bridge with timber, but it might not be the most cost eﬀective method of reducing the
CO2-eq emission related to them.

Spring elements
Connections in Karamba3D is modeled as spring elements.
Springs in Karamba3D
allow to deﬁne the stiﬀness relation between two nodes based on spring constants. For
the spring it is possible to deﬁne the translational stiﬀness and the rotational stiﬀness.
Though springs are modeled as a line elements the length of the spring is irrelevant, as
illustrated on ﬁgure 10.1. The red arrow is the force, the burgundy line is the spring
and the green arrow is the reaction. As shown, though the force is applied at one end
of the spring it does not cause a moment at the support, at the other end, but only
one reaction in the opposite direction of the force.

G. PARAMETRIC STUDY ASSUMPTIONS
APPENDICES
G
Parametric study assumptions
The thickness of the concrete slab used for the composite deck is set to be minimum
200 mm thick based on [41].
The minimum thickness of the SLT in the road deck is set to 290 mm.
In [12] it
is recommended to have a minimum thickness of 320 mm, so it has a suﬃcient stiﬀ
connection with the road barrier. As the thickness of the existing bridge is 290 mm,
this value has been used instead and assumed to be suﬃcient.
For construction parts made of CLT the maximum thickness is chosen to 320 mm and
the minimum thickness is chosen to 60 mm based on standard factory capacities. In
the diﬀerent sub categories the interval may be reduced further to reduce computation
time.
For the arch bridge static system the yield strength of the SLT material is set to the
bending strength of GL28h (28 MPa) instead. The altering is made as the SLT deck is
only subjected to bending with this static system.
G.1
Lattice girder assumptions
For the CLT and SLT road deck the upper cord of the truss is removed and the in-
termediate members of the truss is connected directly to the road deck. This design
choice has been made to save the material used for the upper cord. Furthermore, it is
assumed that it will ease the assembly as the force can be transferred directly to the
lattice girder instead of being transferred to the upper cord ﬁrst. For the concrete road
deck an upper cord in the lattice girder is added to secure the HBV-connector allowing
for composite action between the road deck and the girder.
With the lattice girder the dimensions for the elements is determined by the Karamba3D
component ”Optimize Cross Section”. As described in chapter 4, the component opti-
mize the individual elements in the girder based on the external loads. This will result
in that elements close to the ends are given a small cross section which is insuﬃcient if
the load was applied at these areas instead. To compensate for this problem an extra
load case is added. The load case is LM3 placed in the area between the end of the
bridge and the intermediate support. Furthermore, the mass of the lattice girder is
added with 10% to ensure the elements at these areas have enough bearing capacity
when the moving load passes across the whole length of the bridge.

O.3
Lateral torsional buckling
Due to the high negative moment near the columns in combination with that the cross
beams are placed at the top of the main beams, they may be prone to lateral torsional
buckling. In the danish EC5 only the correlation between the span length and the
eﬀective length for simple supported beams and cantilevered beams is provided. To
ﬁnd a more precise value for the eﬀective length of the outer span (from the abutment
to the column) and the inner span (from the column to the 0-moment point) of the
bridge there are used the tilting coeﬃcients presented in [50], which are taken from the
German national annex of the Eurocode (DIN EN 1995-1-1/NA:2013-08).
For the inner section, the span length is taken as the distance from the column to the
point where the moment shifts from being negative to positive. The assumption is
made as the main beam is restrained against LT-buckling for the positive moment by
the cross beams and the HBV-connector. An illustration of the buckling length of the
inner and outer section is shown in ﬁgure 10.70. Note that the values presented in the
ﬁgure are only examples, the values used in the calculation is based on the result from
FEM-Design.
