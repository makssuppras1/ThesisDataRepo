ABSTRACT 
Steel fibre reinforced concrete (SFRC) is a composite material combining plain concrete and discrete 
steel fibres. Properties of the composite are influenced by the components of the cementitious matrix, 
the structure of the fibres, fibre distribution and the fibre-matrix interactions.  
Steel fibres are used as a reinforcement to arrest the propagation of cracks and increase the residual 
tensile strength of the composite after the first cracking. With strong, well distributed and bounded fibres, 
SFRC can be submitted to large stresses even after it cracks. In general, the main role of using fibres 
in the composite is to increase the post-crack ductility and overall residual mechanical performance. 
The property of the concrete, which is mostly affected by addition of steel fibre reinforcement, is the 
post-cracking flexural tensile strength or the residual tensile strength. The residual tensile strength is 
expected to increase by adding fibre reinforcement.   
This project investigates the influence of the rotation on uniaxial tension tests of SFRC. Main objective 
of this research was thereby to elaborate on the influence of the rotation on uniaxial tension tests and 
contribute to finding a more suitable tension test method for future investigations, which could be widely 
used in the construction industry. A literature review initially summarises previous research about SFRC 
and various test methods to determine mechanical properties. Subsequently, experimental tests were 
performed to characterize the tensile and flexural behaviour of SFRC. The investigation focused thereby 
on the impact of rotation and overall stiffness of the test method on the residual tensile strength 
measured on uniaxial tension test systems. The uniaxial tension tests were performed with different 
confinement levels (full rotation allowed, partial rotation allowed and full rotation constrained) to allow 
for observations, examination and discussion on the impact of the rotation on obtained results 
Furthermore, results obtained from uniaxial tension tests were compared to a standard flexural test 
method, i.e. the notched three-point bending test.  
The experimental investigations undertaken in this project demonstrated that the rotation has a 
considerable influence on the determination of the mechanical performance, in particular the residual 
tensile strength, of SFRC. In general, it could be observed from the results that larger rotations lead to 
determination of higher residual tensile strength. Further, predictable formation and propagation of 
cracks were observed for test methods with restrained rotation. Moreover, different tests methods (i.e. 
with different confinement level) illustrated the influence of the fibre distribution on results. For example, 
for tests in which full rotation was allowed, the displacement in the specimens around the fracture 
surface tended to be controlled only by the fibres bridging the crack, whereas for the restrained uniaxial 
tension test fibre distribution was of secondary importance. Malfunctions were discovered using the test 
method in which partial rotation was allowed. Therefore, no representative results were derived from 
this test method. In general, results of the experimental investigations indicated a dependence between 
determined results and various test set-ups, emphasizing that the results are affected by the machine-
specimen interaction.

1. INTRODUCTION 
The traditional method of reinforcing concrete has not always been the most efficient. The conventional 
steel reinforcing bars tend to be troublesome to place, e.g. when the reinforcement may shift out of its 
casting position or etc. Moreover, placement of the steel bars is rather time-consuming and may cause 
significant delays during the construction phase. Therefore, over the last decades, new methods of 
concrete reinforcement, such as e.g. fibre reinforced concrete (FRC) have been introduced to address 
the issues mentioned above. The concept may be inspired by examples from ancient history, for 
example clay bricks reinforced with straw were used for construction e.g. high hill of Aqar Our (area of 
today’s Baghdad). 
Concrete is a brittle material with low tensile strength and the tendency to fail quickly as crack appears 
and propagates. Therefore, the addition of fibres to the concrete mix aims to improve the post-cracking 
performance. In addition, fibres in the composite can also arrest cracks, enlarge the tensile strength of 
the composite not only at first cracking, but also at ultimate limit state [1].  
The tensile strength in the post-cracking region is called the post-cracking flexural tensile strength or 
the residual tensile strength and is one of the most characteristic properties of FRC. The residual tensile 
strength is increased by addition of fibre reinforcement and most accurately described by the stress-
crack opening relationship (𝜎-𝑤). Thus, a variety of tests describing this relationship have been 
developed during the last five decades. Available tests can be generally divided into two groups: direct 
tests and indirect tests (e.g. flexural tests, panel/plate tests, wedge splitting test). Indirect tests are often 
recommended by standards to describe the tensile behaviour of the FRC as indirect tests are often less 
time-consuming (compared to direct test methods) and do not demand any advanced equipment or 
complicated preparation of the specimens. However, the results obtained from these tests are indirectly 
related to the stress-crack opening relationship and demand specific procedures (e.g. inverse analysis) 
to describe the tensile behaviour of material. Moreover, a number of assumptions are made in the 
analysis of flexural approaches often producing rather conservative results. Subsequently, conservative 
results used for the design of a structure may cause large over-dimensioning and overpricing. Direct 
tension tests present themselves as a more feasible solution that allow for the direct determination of 
the stress-crack opening dependence. However, the execution of available direct tension tests is highly 
demanding and according to multiple researches can be affected by the impact of rotation and overall 
stiffness of the chosen test set-up, as well as the machine-specimen interaction or fibre distribution. 
Among these factors, it has it has been demonstrated that the factor having the biggest impact on the 
accuracy of direct tests is the rotation [2]. 
Therefore, this project investigates the influence of the rotation on uniaxial tension tests of Steel Fibre 
Reinforced Concrete (SFRC). The mechanical properties, mainly residual tensile strength, of SFRC 
differ considerably depending on the used test method. The variations typically result in increased safety 
factors and over-dimensioning of structural elements. The influence of the degree of confinement on 
uniaxial tension tests has not been fully elaborated yet and a number of questions are still to be 
addressed. In this research, three types of uniaxial tension tests with varying confinement levels, i.e. 
different degrees of rotation are allowed, will be used to measure the residual tensile strength of a SFRC. 
The results will then be compared to observations obtained from standard flexural test methods, such 
as the notched three-point bending test. 
 
1.1 
Aim, objectives and hypothesis  
The aim of this project is to examine the tensile strength by means of various direct tension tests with 
different confinement levels (full rotation, partial rotation and full rotation constrained) and to compare 
the obtained results to the three point bending (3PB) notched test. 
The following objectives are defined:

2. STATE OF THE ART 
Fibre-reinforced concrete (FRC) is broadly applied as an alternative or/and addition to ordinary 
reinforced concrete due to its improved performance. FRC’s most appreciated advantage in the building 
industry is a significant reduction of construction time and cost due to major reduction of the most labour-
intensive activities on a construction site, such as placing and fixing steel reinforcing bars etc. 
Considering that the usage of FRC has a potential of impacting both time and cost of any construction 
project, the material is worth further detailed investigation. Therefore, potential advantages and 
disadvantages of FRC will be discussed in this section [3] [2]. 
First time when discontinuous steel elements, i.e. nails or wires, were used as concrete reinforcement 
in experimental trials and patents dates back to 1910. However, according to Löfgren [2], early patents 
on FRC were first introduced as early as in 1874. The use of commercial steel fibres in concrete started 
being studied from the 1960s in the USA. Fibres made from different materials, e.g. glass, were 
investigated even earlier [4]. Since then a great amount of papers describing the properties and 
behaviour of fibres has been published. However, FRC is still not widely applied in structures. One of 
the reasons for that is the lack of sufficient information on standardised tests and design codes. With 
existing testing standards, it is difficult to distinguish the difference in properties related to material from 
the ones related to performance of the structure. Such issues result in significant over-dimensioning of 
components and therefore higher costs. Moreover, structural engineers are reluctant to apply FRC due 
to lack of experience and unclear design procedures [2]. 
Nevertheless, there is a number of structures subjected to high and dynamic loads, where FRC can be 
used instead of conventional reinforced concrete, e.g. foundations, tunnels, retaining walls, slabs on a 
ground, thin-shell structures or repairs. Even though the popularity of FRC increases, for the time being 
it is not commonly practised to apply FRC exclusively in critical structural members such as large slabs 
or beams. In large slabs or beams conventional steel reinforcement may be partially replaced with steel 
fibres creating another method of reinforcement known as combined reinforcement systems. Use of 
fibres in ordinary reinforced or pre-stressed concrete has two significant structural advantages: it 
improves the tensile residual strength of the members and contributes to the bond created between the 
matrix and steel bars [5] [6]. 
In many cases, there is a potential for reducing costs and construction times of constructions projects 
replacing conventional concrete with FRC. Therefore, there is a need for a thorough investigation of the 
material and subsequently is a great number of on-going research on FRC properties, aiming at 
establishing guidelines and procedures for FRC usage. It is possible that in the near future the benefits 
of fibre reinforcement can reach a wider audience, hence an EN standard for the design of SFRC is 
currently being prepared. 
 
2.1 
Fibre reinforced concrete 
Fibre reinforced concrete (FRC) is a composite material combining plain concrete and discrete fibres, 
as shown in the sketch in Figure 2-1. The properties of the composite vary based on demand and are 
influenced by the components of the cementitious matrix, the structure of the fibres, their distribution 
and orientation in the matrix and the fibre-matrix interaction [1]. 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
4 | 127 
Figure 2-1 Sketch of a fibre reinforced cement composite as a two-component system [7] 
 
The most significant differences between conventional reinforced concrete and fibre reinforced 
composite are in relation to the dimensions, spacing and distribution of the reinforcement as well as the 
area of reinforcement. Conventional concrete is reinforced with steel bars that are typically of a 
significant length, of rather big diameters and placed according to thorough design requirements, 
meaning that there is a set distance in between bars. Whereas in case of FRC, the fibre reinforcement 
is usually randomly distributed in the entire cross-section and the fibres are short and of significantly 
smaller diameter. A large amount of steel is commonly needed to control cracks with conventional 
reinforcement, which can result in over-dimensioning of the structure, heavy, costly and time-consuming 
labour or problems with the execution of construction. Those issues can be avoided or reduced through 
the use of fibres. However, it is not possible to obtain as large reinforcement areas with fibres as it is 
achievable with steel bars. Therefore, large span construction elements designed with FRC often 
demand additional reinforcement with steel bars [2] [8]. 
Plain concrete is regarded as a brittle material of low ductility. The main role of fibres in the composite 
is to increase the post-crack ductility and overall residual mechanical performance. The most impacted 
mechanical property of concrete due to the addition of fibres is called the post-cracking tensile strength 
or the residual tensile strength. The residual tensile strength is also expected to increase with an 
increase of amount of fibre reinforcement.   
Fibre reinforcement is used to arrest the propagation of cracks and increase the residual tensile strength 
of the composite after the first crack appears. With this method, if the fibres are strong enough and well 
bounded, the FRC can be submitted to a large stress even after it cracks. Also, with the use of fibre 
reinforcement, cracks can be bridged and their width can be reduced [1]. Based on the stress-strain 
response in tension, FRC can be categorized as strain-softening or pseudo strain-hardening. The 
difference between both responses is presented in the Figure 2-2 with reference to plain concrete.  
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
5 | 127 
Figure 2-2 Classification of post-cracking behaviour of various types of concrete, from [2] 
 
As can be observed from Figure 2-2, considering strain-softening response, the highest load carrying 
capacity corresponds to the first crack and afterwards values of the stress will only decrease. On the 
contrary, a strain-hardening material has the ability to enlarge its post-cracking strength after a crack 
appears, resulting in a higher load carrying capacity than a material with a strain-softening behaviour. A 
pure strain-hardening behaviour, where the stresses in a material grow and stay constant after reaching 
a certain desirable level, characterizes mostly non-brittle metals and is also known as strengthening a 
material by plastic deformation. Though, some FRC composites exhibit behaviour similar to strain-
hardening, hence the title ‘pseudo strain-hardening’. The difference between these two responses is 
that unlike some metals, the ultimate tensile strength of the FRC material is reached when rupture 
occurs and the amount of stresses that the composite can withstand suddenly decreases [9]. A 
significant difference between strain-softening and pseudo strain-hardening behaviour is the number of 
cracks. In the first case a single crack is observed, whereas in the other multiple cracks are present.  
As an example of materials with pseudo-strain hardening response, high performance fibre reinforced 
concrete (HPFRC) can be adduced. One type of HPFRC is Engineered Cementitious Composite (ECC), 
a highly crack resistant composite, also called ‘bendable’ concrete that exhibits multiple cracking. Steel 
fibre reinforced concrete (SFRC) is an example of a strain-softening material [2] [10]. 
  
2.2 
Steel fibre reinforced concrete  
Steel fibre reinforced concrete (SFRC) can be described as a composite of two components: a matrix 
and discontinuous discrete short steel fibres, see Figure 2-1. The addition of steel fibres to the composite 
influences the properties of both fresh and hardened concrete. One of the advantages of SFRC, is that 
the procedures used for mixing, placing, finishing and quality control are similar to plain concrete and 
the same equipment can be used [2].  
When tension is applied, SFRC may fail only due to two different types of steel fibre behaviour: pull-out 
or breakage. The durability of steel fibre reinforced concrete can be modified depending on properties 
of the matrix or the bond. However, its performance is influenced mainly by various characteristics of 
fibres, like material properties of the fibre, its geometry or its volume. Some properties of steel fibres 
influencing the residual performance of SFRC are discussed further below [10].

2.2.1 Steel fibres  
The general definition of steel fibres describes them as discontinuous, discrete and short, of a length-
to-diameter ratio between 20 and 100, length between 25 and 60 mm and with a cross-section that 
allows for random distribution during mixing in a fresh, unhardened concrete [4]. 
According to [11], 5 primary types of steel fibres can be distinguished, according to the production 
process: 
 
Type I- cold-drawn wire, 
 
Type II- cut sheet, 
 
Type III- melt-extracted, 
 
Type IV- shaved cold-drawn wire, 
 
Type V- mill cut,  
The first experiments with steel fibres comprised round, straight fibres. However, this type of 
reinforcement quickly proved to be inefficient regarding utilization of the strength of steel.  Results of 
those tests showed that straight fibres did not bond enough to the matrix. Since then, there has been 
plenty of studies regarding the connection between the shape of fibres and anchorage to the matrix. As 
a result, a large number of fibres of various geometries has been created. Some examples of differently 
shaped fibres are shown in the Figure 2-3 [10] [12]. 
Dimensions of fibres vary based on the fabrication process. Round, straight fibres, that are usually cut 
from a wire, can have diameter of 0.25 up to 1 mm. Flat straight fibres, produced by cutting a sheet or 
cut from flatten wire, have a cross-section that can vary from 0.15 to 0.64 mm with a thickness of 0.25 
up to 2 mm. Fibres of diverse geometry are mostly fabricated with the use of straight round or flat fibres. 
Therefore, crimped fibres are manufactured by crimping or corrugating of a wire/sheet, while hooked-
end fibres are mostly bent at the end [4].

2.3.1 Compressive properties 
There is a large number of studies regarding the influence of steel fibres on the compressive strength 
of concrete. It is generally concluded that moderate dosages (<1%) of typical steel fibres added to 
concrete do not change its compressive properties [2]. However, according to research by Williamson 
[14], the increase in values of the compressive strength can vary from rather negligible in most cases 
up to 23% when the fibre volume in concrete has a value of 2% [15]. 
In another research [16], the effect of ratio and volume fraction of steel fibres on compressive properties 
of SFRC was examined by testing 9 SFRC replicates. Results of the test showed that the compressive 
strength of specimens increased by about 4-19% in comparison to a control concrete cube without 
reinforcement.  
Nonetheless, the State-of-the-Art’s report on FRC from 2002 [4] summarizes all the literature findings 
on the topic and defines the increase of the compressive strength in SFRC due to addition of steel fibres 
as negligible, with a value from 0 to 15% for composites with fibre volume up to 1.5% [2]. 
 
2.3.1  Tensile properties 
After the first crack is formed, transfer of any significant load through this crack in plain unreinforced 
concrete is no longer possible. However, due to usage of discontinuous steel fibres, SFRC material can 
still transfer load along the crack and the width of a crack can be reduced [17]. 
According to Williamson [14], by increasing the content of fibres in concrete to a value of 1.5%, the 
residual tensile strength can increase up to 40% compared to plain concrete. Furthermore, studies 
demonstrate that with the use of smooth, straight steel fibres (6% of concrete volume), aligned in the 
direction of the tensile loading, the tensile strength of SFRC can improve by 133%. However, as can be 
seen in Figure 2-4, with a 6% dosage of randomly distributed steel fibres, the increase in tensile strength 
may vary from negligible up to 30%. Therefore, the benefits of adding steel fibres may be limited if it 
was only to improve tensile properties. As all the studies conclude, the main benefits from the addition 
of fibres is the increase in ductility and resistance to expansion of longitudinal cracks.

2.4 
Fibre content and fibre distribution 
The volume and the distribution of fibres in concrete influences the toughness and the stress-
displacement (𝜎−𝑤) behaviour of SFRC composites. Toughness is a main parameter that distinguishes 
steel fibre reinforced concrete from plain unreinforced concrete. It can be explained as an ability of a 
material to withstand impact loading without immediate breakage, while maintaining structural integrity 
[4]. Moreover, the peak load as well as the cracking stress is highly affected by the fibre properties. 
Fibre content, orientation and distribution may vary due to many reasons, such as e.g. geometry of a 
specimen, size of fibres and aggregates, mixing and casting technique. Especially orientation of the 
fibres can greatly affect the post-crack behaviour of SFRC. According to Jannson [8], fibres that are 
placed in parallel direction to the loading plane, unlike fibres located along it, neither affect the tensile 
behaviour of composite after it cracks, nor influence the stress transfer across a crack. Therefore, 
judging by randomness of the fibre distribution a certain scatter in test results of SFRC specimens can 
be expected.

2.5 
Test methods to evaluate FRC 
As has been mentioned before, the most important property characterizing steel fibre reinforced 
concrete is the tensile behaviour. It is most accurately described by the stress-crack opening relationship 
(𝜎-𝑤). Thus, a variety of tests describing this feature have been developed during the last five decades. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
9 | 127 
Available tests can be generally divided into two groups: direct tests and indirect tests (e.g. flexural tests, 
panel/plate tests, wedge splitting test).  
Direct tests, allow for direct determination of 𝜎-𝑤 relationship. For this reason, it may appear as the 
preferred choice when deciding upon a test method. However, direct tests currently available require 
advanced apparatus and supporting features, which may not be available in standard testing 
laboratories and/or on construction sites. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that the obtained 
results are dependent on machine-specimen interaction and can vary due to different confinement levels 
e.g. full rotation allowed, full rotation constrained, etc.  Moreover, conducting such tests often requires 
a lot of time-consuming preparation and can be affected by a variety of factors, e.g. geometry of the 
specimen, fibre distribution.  
In general, indirect test methods allow for measurements of crack width during flexural loading. In this 
case the 𝜎−𝑤 relationship cannot be directly determined from the test results and an inverse analysis 
must be applied to determine mechanical properties. Following the article in Journal of Advanced 
Concrete Technology [21], the results obtained from indirect tests becomes the input of an inverse 
analysis, where materials parameters are determined in order to fit the experimental observations, e.g. 
load-displacement or load-crack mouth opening curve. In other words, in this procedure an analytical 
interpretation of 𝜎-𝑤 is needed to fit the results obtained from experiments. Fundamentals of inverse 
analysis, summarizing the above description, are shown in Figure 2-5 a).  
According to [22], different approaches for inverse analysis procedure have been developed, where 𝜎-
𝑤 relationship can be either bi-linear or multi-linear. Generally, a shape shown in Figure 2-5 b) can be 
computed by means of hand calculation. However, to obtain a curve similar to the one in Figure 2-5 c) 
usage of FE-analysis software is recommended.  
There is a number of problems that can occur during inverse analysis. For instance, various shape of 
𝜎−𝑤 curve can be observed due to different materials used in a mixture, or difficulties in finding the 
first slope of the curve caused by varying amount of fibres or lack of one, definite solution, etc. Due to 
the above mentioned reasons a multi-linear relationship has become more popular and many studies 
have focused on developing different approaches to ease the implementation of this method. Moreover, 
another reason for choosing multi-linear method over the bi-linear, is the larger number of points 
describing the 𝜎-𝑤 curve and therefore greater accuracy [21] [8]. 
Additionally, SFRC material characterized as tension-softening under flexural loading in indirect tests 
may show two different responses: deflection hardening or deflection softening (see Figure 2-6). Those 
different responses may occur due to sensitivity of SFRC to the distribution and orientation of fibres, as 
well as geometry of a specimen and casting procedure. Thus, caution with interpretation of the results 
of indirect tests should be maintained [2].

RILEM TC 162-TDF 
RILEM TC 162-TDF is a test method to determine the 𝜎−𝑤 relationship. The test is commonly used for 
SFRC materials. However, it can be applied for any concrete material that demonstrates a strain 
softening response. The test should be controlled by displacement on a specimen with a notch of a

diameter and length both equal to 150 mm. The test set-up should be stiff to the highest extent, therefore 
the specimen is glued to a metal block as shown in Figure 2-7. The block is then connected to the testing 
machine (through metal plates) with bolts, preferably pre-stressed. Minimum 3 devices should be 
installed to measure the crack opening in a position shown in the Figure 2-7 a). Moreover, in Figure 2-7 
b) a step-by-step procedure of fixing a specimen in a set-up is presented to give a better overview of 
the complexity of the test preparation. Due to its high inconveniency, the test is widely unpopular among 
the standardized tests methods evaluating SFRC and is not used on a larger scale. Moreover, it requires 
advanced testing equipment, which is often not available in standard testing laboratories [23].

The Brazilian test cannot be used for a direct definition of the tensile strength of FRC materials due to

Tensile dog-bone test 
The dog-bone test is used to examine FRC materials of strain hardening post-cracking behaviour. In 
the test, an un-notched 210 mm long section with a width of 50 mm and 22 mm thick is used as shown 
in Figure 2-10. The test is intended for materials with small aggregates (not larger than 0.18 mm). The 
specimen is fixed at both ends with hydraulic grips, which apply tensile force to the specimen. The 
recommended loading rate is 0.5 mm/min. Multiple cracks are expected during the test. This direct 
method is used to determine strain capacity, minimum, maximum and average crack widths at different 
stress level and material ductility. It is not recommended for materials in which single crack occurs, such 
as e.g. SFRC composites [10].

Single crack notched coupon test 
This test is used to examine the tensile behaviour of FRC material under direct tension. Two types of 
specimens are used depending on the expected behaviour of the tested material as presented in Figure 
2-11. For materials which are expected to exhibit strain-hardening, 100-120 mm long specimens of 50 
mm width and 12-16 mm thickness are used, whereas for SFRC composite 150 mm long specimens 
with a cross-section of 150 × 150 mm are recommended. The test method was developed at DTU. In 
the test a weak plane is created in which the formation of a single crack can be studied. The results of 
the test help to characterize the response of the material, whether it is predicted to be strain-softening 
or strain-hardening as load-crack opening and tensile stress-crack opening curves can be plotted 
directly from test results [10].

2.5.2 Indirect tests 
Two main flexural tests have been developed, for which the general difference is in the amount of 
loading points and presence of a notch. Both test methods have been extensively described in the 
literature and for both an inverse analysis has to be applied to describe the tensile properties of the 
investigated material. 
 
Three-point bending test 
The three-point bending test is a common method to define tensile fracture properties of SFRC. The 
method allows to determine equivalent and residual flexural tensile strengths. The complete 
recommendations for the test is given by RILEM in [26]. One of the reasons for the popularity of this 
approach may be the 𝜎−𝜀 (stress-strain) design method, suggested also by RILEM TC 162-TDF (2003) 
and based on the results from three-point bending tests. The two methods combined allow for the 
application of SFRC composites in structures [2]. 
The three-point bending test is performed on a specimen with a length of at least 550 mm and a square 
cross-section with a side length of 150 mm, notched at mid-span. Detailed measurements of a specimen 
are shown in Figure 2-12. The preparation of the specimen is thoroughly described in [27]. As can be 
seen in Figure 2-12, the beam is supported on two roller supports placed on the sides and the flexural 
load is applied through a loading device placed in the middle of specimen above the notch. As a result 
of the test, a flexural load-deflection or flexural load-crack mouth opening displacement (CMOD) curve 
is obtained.

Four-point bending test 
The four-point bending test is comparable to the three-point bending method. Similarly, this test 
determines flexural strength based on the load-deflection curve. As presented in Figure 2-13, the test is 
performed on a simply supported beam without a notch, in which the flexural load is applied through two 
point loads. There are several standards describing the method. Comparing to other standards, highest 
flexibility in choice of fibre properties is provided by ASTM C1609, in which beams with two different 
sets of dimensions are used: small 350 mm long beams of cross-section with a side length of 100 mm 
and 500 mm long beams, where h=b=150 mm. The distance 𝑎 marked in Figure 2-13 is equal to width 
and height of the beam. As for failure pattern, multiple cracking is expected mainly in between the load 
cells (in the area of the ‘constant moment’). Due to lack of a notch, cracks are not prescribed and crack 
occurrence cannot be controlled [28]. 
According to Paegle and Fischer [29], the four point-bending test is not suitable for examining SFRC 
composites, mostly due to the high spread of obtained results. Moreover, studies have shown that the 
values of strength defined in the test tend to be significantly smaller than the mean values of examined 
strengths. The test results can be affected by a large number of factors, e.g. specimen geometry or 
sensitivity of the beams supports, therefore other methods, such as e.g. three-point bending is 
recommended to use instead.

Wedge splitting test 
This test is utilized to examine the tensile fracture behaviour of FRC material. The recommendation for 
preparation of the test specimens can be found in NT Build report, see [30]. The specimen size can vary 
and it is recommended that height, width and thickness of the specimen should be 4 times as large as 
the maximum fibre length. Each replicate is pre-notched. As shown in Figure 2-14 a) the splitting load 
is applied via two steel loading devices with roller bearings placed on the top of the cube and into the 
groove [31]. The test is rather easy to execute as it is stable and can be performed using any standard 
mechanical testing machine with a crosshead through which the displacement can be applied [2]. The 
test is finished, when the replicate is split in half. According to the NT Build [31], a scatter of the results 
between of 5 and 40% depending on the properties of the fibres can be expected.

The round determinate panel test (RPD) is used to determine the flexural toughness of FRC and a 
detailed description of the test can be found in the standard [32]. As shown in Figure 2-14 b), a round 
panel specimen supported on three pivots (marked as ‘x’ in the figure) is subjected to a central point 
load applied by a steel piston. Load-deflection curve is obtained from the test [32] and cracking in the 
specimen is caused mainly due to bending stresses [10].

In this section, one of direct tension test methods, i.e. uniaxial tension test is compared with the most 
representative of the indirect tension tests, i.e. three-point bending test. The main reason for comparison 
of the two methods is their importance for this project.  
Results from an experimental program presented in [19], have been used to analyse the differences 
between the two test methods. In the investigation, five concrete mixes were studied, in which the fibre 
volume was between 0.5 and 1% of concrete. The composition of the concrete mix can be found in [19].

The experimental fibre efficiency factor  𝜂𝑏.𝑒𝑥𝑝 discussed in Section 2.4, is used to portray the fibre 
content and variation in fibres distribution. It can be observed in Figure 2-15, that the fibre distribution in 
UTT specimens is highly random. The number of fibres participating in bridging the crack (fibres found

This indicates that results from UTT tests are not entirely reliable and will have a rather large scatter. 
For the 3PB test values of the 𝜂𝑏.𝑒𝑥𝑝 factor are comparable with theoretical values for 2 and 3-D case

significantly more consistent and less affected by the number of fibres participating in the crack bridging 
process. The difference in consistency of those tests may be due to different geometry of specimens or 
casting and vibration process.

2.7 
Concluding remarks 
In this chapter a summary of findings from the literature is presented. A brief summary of SFRC 
properties shows that concrete reinforced with steel fibres is a material of significant importance in the 
construction industry. However, due to a lack of sufficient information on standardised tests and design 
codes, widespread use of SFRC is yet to come. Existing test methods still leave much to be desired.  
Direct tension tests are either time-consuming and demand advanced equipment and preparation or 
miss important data about e.g. post-cracking tensile behaviour of the replicates. Additionally, a high 
influence of fibre distribution can be observed on the available direct test methods. It is also visible that 
the results of uniaxial tension tests may be influenced by the impact of rotation. However, there is a 
limited number of research available investigating this matter. Moreover, the results of direct tests often 
depend on the machine-specimen interaction.  
On the other hand, indirect tests are stable and often a variety of machines (found in a standard 
laboratory) can be used to execute them. However, an inverse analysis shall be applied to use the 
results obtained during a flexural loading to describe tensile properties of the investigated material. The

3.4 
Fibres distribution 
As discussed before, steel fibres have a crucial influence on SFRC material. Two main properties of 
fibres can alter the behaviour of the composite, i.e. amount and distribution of fibres. Moreover, 
performance of the fibres in the loaded matrix is equally important for analysing the results from the 
tests. 
Therefore, all specimens, after testing, were split in half and the fracture surface of each half was 
analysed to assess the behaviour of fibres. The investigation was performed for two types of specimens:  
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
30 | 127 
beams and cubes of dimensions mentioned in Section 3.2.2 and 3.2.3, respectively (Figure 3-17).  
Firstly, the performance of each fibre in the surface of fracture was assigned to one of two categories: 
fibres that pulled out or snapped. To understand the behaviour of the fibres, the stress transfer in a 
SFRC composite should be briefly explained. When tension is applied to a concrete reinforced with a 
higher-strength fibres, in the matrix some of the load will be transferred to fibres and the rest will be 
taken by the matrix itself. When the first crack appears in the matrix, FRC does not fail like a plain 
concrete, but the crack is bridged by the fibres present in a fracture surface, which are pulled out by the 
tensile force.  
The failure in the specimen occurs mainly due to pull-out of the fibres, which for end-hooked fibres can 
be observed as straightening and plastic deformation of the fibre. Higher residual tensile stresses can 
be transferred through the matrix [2], the more fibres are pulled-out. However, it can happen that a 
failure in a fibre occurs before it is pulled out and this behaviour of a fibre is called snapping. The 
snapped fibres were marked in red, whereas pulled-out fibres are coloured in green. Afterwards, the 
cracked surface of each specimen was photographed and the fibres of each category were counted 
manually for each specimen, to conduct statistics regarding performance and distribution of fibres along 
the x and y axis. 
For convenience, the fibres in each picture were marked with red dots at the start of each fibre and the 
x and y axis were assigned to the area of fracture, together with an initial point (0,0) on the coordinate 
axes, as shown in Figure 3-18. Then a grid was applied to the pictures of the fracture surface, which 
divided the surface every 20 mm, first along x and then along y axis, so the fibres could be counted. For 
UTT tests point (0,0) corresponds to the position of clip gauge number 2.

4.3.3 Indirect Uniaxial Tension Test 
The results obtained from the indirect uniaxial tension test were not representative. As mentioned in 
Section 3.3.4, one extensometer was placed on each face of the cube diagonally to each other, while 
the cube was positioned in such a way that both phases were lying horizontally. Therefore, one clip 
gauge was on the top and other on the bottom of specimen in the test set-up. During the test, the top 
extensometer was measuring a positive crack opening displacement, whereas the bottom one was 
initially indicating values close to -1 mm and then opening slowly. Furthermore, despite the fact that high 
forces were applied to the cube and considerable rotation was already registered by the extensometers, 
a crack was not forming. From several tests a pattern was observed that specimens were not cracking 
in a way that was observed in other uniaxial tension test despite applying a large load. When the 
specimen finally cracked, crack propagation was too sudden to allow for the observation of any 
characteristic behaviour of SFRC material. The amount of energy released by the sudden crack was 
typically so large that both extensometers were falling off the faces of the specimen and the test could 
not be continued (presented by the steep lines in Figure 4-22). Therefore, a study on crack formation or 
of the post-cracking behaviour of the specimen could not be conducted.  
Table 4-5 gives an overall summary of the results from three tested specimens. For each cube, the 
value of load for 𝐸𝑙𝑖𝑚 and 𝐿𝑂𝑃 was measured for the corresponding mean value of the CMOD.

5. DISCUSSION 
In this section the results presented in Section 4 are compared to different tests and further discussed.  
5.1 Limit of Proportionality 
To start with, the definition and correctness of Limit of Proportionality should be debated. According to 
[26], 𝐿𝑂𝑃- Limit of Proportionality is a value of a stress at a particular point on the Load-CMOD curve, 
when the value of the load during the first 0.05mm of CMOD is the highest, as shown exemplary in Figure 
5-1. However, it may be debated whether standards describe the point of LOP correctly. According to 
the laws of psychics, a proportional limit is the greatest stress that a material can withstand, while the 
dependence between stress and strain or between load and displacement is still proportional. After the 
proportional limit is reached, the material can still exhibit elastic behaviour, until the elastic limit is 
reached. On the contrary, as seen in the Figure 5-1, the point that standards describe as LOP is found 
after the elastic limit is reached. It may be assumed that the method used by standards is not valid and 
should be revised. Nevertheless, in this project the term LOP was used as advised by RILEM [26] and 
described in Figure 5-1.

5.2 Impact of rotation on Uniaxial Tension Tests 
In Figure 5-2, results of two specimens with direct proximity to the mean values of the unrestrained and 
restrained Uniaxial Tension Test are presented for 4 characteristic points of the mean value of the 
CMOD, i.e. elastic limit and limit of proportionality for each specimen, 0.5 mm and 3.5 mm. Results from 
UTT_U specimen are presented on the left part of the figure, whereas UTT_R results are presented on 
the right part. At first, in both figures the rotation seem to follow the same pattern and presents similar 
values for the elastic limit. While the crack continues to open for the limit of proportionality, a sudden 
change in direction of rotation is observed for the UTT_U specimen, whereas the opening in the other 
cube follows a similar pattern of rotation and does not change until the average value of the CMOD 
reaches 3.5 mm.  As for the UTT_U specimen another change in direction of rotation is seen in the 
fracture surface for the mean value of the CMOD equal to 0.5 mm and the rotation pattern continues to 
shift as it becomes almost vertical for the mean value of the CMOD equal to 3.5 mm. The scatter in the 
values of the rotation remains similar for all figures on both the left and right side until an average value 
of the crack opening of 0.5 mm. Afterwards the discrepancy in the results continues to progress. At 
value of CMOD of 3.5 mm, the scatter for the UTT_R specimen has a value of around 2.5 mm, whereas 
the difference in most extreme values of the UTT_U replicate is twice as high as for UTT_R. 
The difference in the rotation for different test set-ups can be seen in the above example for specimens 
that exhibit a behaviour similar to the mean values of the corresponding test set-up. To further highlight 
the magnitude of rotation for the various test set-ups, box plots showing dispersion in the results of 
rotation for three characteristic mean values of CMOD are presented in Figure 5-3. Although the values

shown in the plot are the readings of extensometers placed as shown in Figure 3-20 and not directly at 
the corners of fracture surface, the plots are assumed to be representative. From the presented results 
the magnitude of dispersion in the rotation in each point can be observed, which confirms the 
observations from Figure 5-2 that the rotation in the unrestrained test was much larger than in the 
restrained. The difference can be especially seen in the elastic region of the results (Figure 5-3 a), in 
which the scatter in the UTT_R results is negligible, whereas the dispersion in UTT_U readings of the

All those observation lead to the conclusion that the rotation in UTT_U replicates is much higher than 
for UTT_R specimens and that the crack formation in the Uniaxial Unrestrained Test is more uneven

large rotations in UTT_U replicates result in larger amount of dissipated energy, therefore the work for 
UTT_U cubes may be larger than those for UTT_R tests.

5.4 Comparison of Uniaxial Tension Tests 
Figure 5-6 illustrates the dispersion in values of load for various crack openings for both restrained and 
unrestrained tension tests. Due to the overall scatter in the results from the replicates, in particular with 
an increase in the width of the crack, a detailed interpretation of the results is not possible. However, 
from the presented results it may be concluded that a larger dispersion is observed for UTT_R 
specimens throughout the entire test.  
Figure 5-7 presents mean values of CMOD versus Residual Tensile Strength for each test together with 
the corresponding confidence intervals. In the elastic region of the specimens (Figure 5-7 b)) higher 
mean values are observed for UTT_U tests. As can be observed from the presented results, for both 
tests the mean behaviour in the pre-peak region is linear for approximately two-thirds of the region. 
Afterwards, until the peak is reached, a non-linear response is exhibited at a crack opening of around 
0.01mm. Although the peak load is reached, the material can still transfer tensile stresses through the 
entire length of the tests and for large crack openings [2].  
Despite the higher mean values for the restrained test (black thick line in Figure 5-7 b)), the UTT_U 
replicates exhibit higher tensile stresses as can be seen from the confidence intervals. Moreover, Figure 
5-7 b indicates that the data acquisition frequency of 5Hz was not enough to capture the crack formation 
for both tests. This can be seen clearer in Figure 5-7 c), in which the crack propagates with a constant 
tensile strength from a mean crack opening of around 0.07mm. The most plausible explanation for this 
observation is that the clip gauges registered only two points during crack formation, i.e. one before and 
one after, as a result of the data acquisition frequency. 
Nevertheless, from the results presented in Figure 5-7 a) it can be seen that through almost entire width 
of the crack, UTT_U replicates transfer slightly higher amounts of tensile stresses than the specimens 
from the restrained test (see red thick line and red confidence interval curves (CI) in the figure). Potential 
reasons for exhibiting higher forces in unrestrained tests may be connected to the rotation and fibre 
distribution. Based on observation from Figure 5-8 and Figure 5-5, it can be concluded that the larger 
number of fibres in a specimen, the higher the tensile load that can be carried and the larger the amount 
of energy dissipated, which results in more work. 
Based on Figure 5-7, Figure 5-8 and Section 5.2 and 5.3 the tests may be defined instead as uniaxial 
tension and uniaxial displacement test. In the unrestrained test (uniaxial tension test) rotation is allowed, 
whereas, in the restrained test (uniaxial displacement test) rotation is restrained and the displacement

5.5 Comparison of Uniaxial Tension Tests and Flexural Test 
Initially, the dispersion of the residual tensile strength for the uniaxial tension tests is shown together 
with a figure presenting the scatter of the residual flexural strength for three-point bending tests in Figure 
5-9 and Figure 5-10. A large variation of values between uniaxial tension and flexural tests is observed 
from the results although the same material was tested. The reason for the scatter is that the results 
from those two types of tests cannot be directly compared. It should be stressed that the results are 
obtained through different types of loading, either tension or flexion. Flexion is a combination of tensile 
and compressive forces and the stresses that three-point bending replicates can withstand must not be 
compared with results from tests in which tensile load is applied. Therefore, for the sake of comparison, 
a linear model based on a linear post-cracking constitutive law (Figure 5-11) is used to define a stress-
crack opening dependence in uniaxial tension from the bending test results according to fib Model Code 
[39] and the CMOD-Residual tensile strength curves are plotted as shown in Figure 5-11. The plots are 
derived crack mouth opening displacement equal to 0.5, 1.5 and 2.5 mm. Therefore, the results may be 
considered independent of values of Limit of Proportionality dictated by standards (discussed in Section 
5.1) and may be considered representative for such an analysis.  
As seen from the standard deviation in Figure 5-11 the results obtained from the flexural test with the 
linear post-cracking constitutive law are still significantly higher than the ones obtained from direct 
tension tests (Figure 5-12 b)). This may be caused by multiple reasons, e.g. assumptions made with 
respect to the linear method to assess the tensile behaviour of the material, amount of fibres bridging 
the crack in the flexural specimens, which results in an increased toughness of specimens or poor 
distribution of fibres in tension test replicates. 
From the presented results it may be concluded that the uniaxial tension tests is suggested as the 
preferred method to determine the mechanical performance of SFRC in tensions, since desired results 
can be directly obtained, whereas a number of assumptions for flexural approach may lead to 
misinterpretation of the results. Additionally, the curves obtained from the linear analysis are consisting 
only of few points of data and therefore are not representative.  
However, both tests types have been created for different purposes. Flexural tests, such as the three-
point bending test, have been designed to test structural elements, e.g. beams, subjected to bending, 
in which both tensile and compressive forces are likely to occur. Therefore, use of the flexural tests to 
examine the properties of a material as if it was subjected to pure tension may not give satisfying results 
as they are derived indirectly through inverse analysis. Thus, the lack of representative results from 
flexural tests determining residual tensile strength of SFRC materials may be considered as yet another 
reason to continue the research on development of the direct tension test methods.

6. CONCLUSIONS 
This project focused on investigating the impact of rotation, overall stiffness and fibre distribution in 
SFRC on the residual tensile strength measured by means of uniaxial tension tests, and further to 
compare experimental results to standard test methods, i.e. the notched three-point bending test. The 
uniaxial tension tests were performed with different confinement levels (full rotation allowed, partial 
rotation allowed and full rotation constrained) to observe, examine and discuss the impact of rotation on 
obtained results. 
Review of the literature showed that the existing uniaxial test are not sufficiently developed and facing 
a number of problems hindering their introduction as valid, widespread methods. Therefore, non-
standardized direct tension test methods were examined in this thesis. Three of the most common 
issues in relation to testing with direct methods were addressed, i.e. the machine-specimen or test set-
up- replicate interaction, the influence of the fibre distribution on the obtained data and the correlation 
between results from direct and indirect tension tests. 
From the results presented in this report, the following conclusions can be drawn: 
 
A change of the material behaviour can be observed with the various test set-ups and different 
confinement levels. Replicates tested with free rotation allowed exhibited overall higher tensile 
strengths than specimens for which rotation was constrained.  
 
Moreover, the fibre distribution affected mainly unrestrained uniaxial tension tests, i.e. when the 
rotation was not restrained, the only level of confinement was provided by the distribution of the 
fibres. The fibre distribution affected the restrained direct test to a lesser extent, because the 
displacement was controlled and the rotation followed similar pattern in all replicates.  
 
The results obtained from the flexural test using a linear post-cracking constitutive law were 
significantly higher than the directly gathered stress-strain dependences obtained from uniaxial 
tension tests, which may be attributed to assumptions in the flexural approach.  
 
Furthermore, both direct and indirect tests are examining highly diverse structural behaviour of the 
material and therefore cannot be directly compared.  
Finally, it can be concluded based on this project that the results of direct tests are significantly more 
consistent and representative than indirect test when describing the post-cracking tensile strength of 
SFRC materials. However, direct approaches still have some limitations and can be affected by a large 
number of parameters, like rotation, fibre distribution, etc.  Although, it has been proved that the fibre 
distribution has a large influence on the results only if the replicate is free to rotate. When rotation is 
restrained, fibre distribution plays secondary role. Therefore it can be suggested for future research to 
focus on developing a uniaxial displacement test rather than a uniaxial tension test as a leading method 
of direct testing.

7. RECOMMENDATION FOR FURTHER RESEARCH  
Some recommendations for further research are made: 
 
Search for improved uniaxial tension test should continue based on restrained test as the conditions 
used in the test are closest to ones that can be observe in real structure. Thus, the focus of further 
research on direct test methods should be put on uniaxial displacement test, where a crack can be 
formed uniformly for all specimens and discrepancy in the rotation is reduced to minimum.  
 
In Restrained Uniaxial Tension Test additional improvements should be made to provide higher level 
of confinement and restrain eventual rotation, e.g. removing the empty spaces in between the 
replicate and test set-up.  
 
Indirect Uniaxial Tension Test should not be used for examining residual tensile behaviour of SFRC 
unless major improvements regarding mainly stiffness and level of confinement are implemented. 
 
More even fibre distribution was observed in larger specimens with larger area of cross-section, e.g. 
beams rather than in cubes, therefore it could be a good idea to examine further the influence of the 
size of the specimens on the distribution of the fibres. 
 
Data sampling rate of around 5Hz used in Unrestrained and Restrained Uniaxial Tension Test was 
not enough to properly capture the propagation and formation of the first crack in a specimen. 
Further, even increased data sampling rate of 102.4 Hz used in Indirect Uniaxial Tension Test and 
Three Point Bending Test also did not bring much improvement in observing the formation of the 
crack. Therefore, higher resolution that one used in the project are recommended for the future.  
 
There is still not enough precision in the procedure of cutting the notch in the specimens for direct 
tests, i.e. cubes. More precise and stable saw should be used for preparation of future specimens 
of preferably smaller thickness, so the incision could be better aligned and more uniform through 
entire cross-section of the specimen.
