Abstract
In the context of kinship recognition there are no open source multi-modal video datasets
available. Currently most research conducted on kinship verification is done using col-
lected images of individuals and their kin relationships. Furthermore, there is no way to
assess the credibility of existing uni-modal datasets in regard to their representativity of
the true population. This thesis is written in collaboration with Slovak University of Tech-
nology in Bratislava and Technical University of Denmark to develop the first open source
multi-modal dataset, named Families in the Wild Videos. The Families in the Wild Videos
dataset will be published to be used for kinship recognition tasks. Furthermore an exter-
nal dataset has been evaluated by training a Deep Fusion Siamese Network on Families
In The Wild dataset. Lastly, the trained model is evaluated on Families in the Wild Videos
dataset. This outcome of this study shows that there are limitations in the Families In The
Wild dataset where females get higher error predictions, indicating a lack of diversity of
women. Lastly Transfer Learning has been utilised to see whether pre-trained feature ex-
tractors can be used to train model with one dataset to be used to predict kinships another
dataset. With a precision of 0.61 it is possible train on Families In The Wild dataset to use
Transfer Learning to infer on Families in the Wild Videos dataset.

Introduction
Kinship refers to the relationship between individuals usually based on blood relations.
Kinship have been used throughout history to organize social relationships, and to create
groups of what have become known as families. A family is a collection of people that are
usually connected through blood relations. However, this can deviate as is the case with a
husband and wife relation. Families have traced their lineage using family trees. A family
tree provides a distinctive way to perpetuate an individual’s ancestry. For example, the
Danish Royal lineage can be traced back to 958 AD [1]. Families and relationships also
bring a variety of social factors in play. For instance, marriage between family members
is disregarded in various cultures while acceptable in others. Scientists suggest that the
reason for families having similar traits, that can span across generations, could be due to
key genes that are within the family [2]. This is apparent as to why many family members
look alike, with the often closest looking sibling pair being a twin. Since blood related
family members tend to have a higher ratio of similar facial features, significant research
have been conducted to find algorithms that can be utilised to estimate kin relationships
between two individuals, i.e. Kinship Recognition (KR).
Facial Recognition (FR) verifies whether an individual is an imposter or the actual person.
The field of Kinship Verification (KV) is similar to FR with the key difference being that KV
verifies whether two subjects are related or not [3]. The field of KV is a subfield of KR and
Facial Kinship Verification (FKV). The field of FKV started to surface in the mid 2010s and
can thus not be considered new [3]. FKV is relevant field of Computer Vision, as it can
be utilised in a variety of different fields such as forensic investigations, genealogy to help
trace family histories, biometrics & security for border control and access management
systems. FKV can be categorized into image and video based approaches. One of the
challenges faced in the research of KD is the lack of available datasets, specifically for
the video based KD tasks. Apart from the issues of determining kinship the collected data
can be used for assessment of other datasets and the models trained on them. Since
the datasets, described in chapter 2, are based on images collected in the wild1, they
simulate a comparable collection in this thesis. Evaluation of collected data and models
that utilise the dataset(s) need to be representative of the population being modelled [4].
The goal of this excerpt has been to study existing datasets in KR and look at limitations of
these. The resulting work consists of proposing a new framework for collection, storage,
and retrieval of Multi-Modal Data (MMD) to be used in various research such as linguistics
and genetics, because there is no open source MMD datasets available as of now. The
intention is to provide a well-documented and feature rich dataset to be used for research
in KR. The proposed framework is developed using videos sourced from the internet. Fur-
thermore, the framework contains instructions on how to contribute to the data collection.
Currently, the collected data includes 36 individuals and their relationships. Diversity of
the dataset in this context relates to inclusion of different races, languages, and accents.
This excerpt further looks at Transfer Learning (TL) to investigate whether existing datasets
as well as feature extractors can be used to train models for KV that can be used to predict
classes on the FIWV dataset. There are a number of different use cases within KV and
in this context the missing persons use case have been chosen as part of the motivation

1.1
Motivation
With the rise of computational power as well as the emergence of Artificial Intelligence (AI)
various Computer Vision (CV) challenges have come to light during the former years with
one of them being KV. A number of challenges are hosted on platforms such as Kaggle
to engage data scientists and researchers to solve the complications around KV with one
of the latest being in 2021: Recognising Families In The Wild (RFIW). With the enhance-
ment in specifically computational power the capability to train deep neural networks on
huge datasets have been propelled into new heights. Although there a number of open
source datasets with images collected to be used for the aforementioned tasks, there is a
general lack of video based datasets within the field of KV. This creates the basis for the
motivation of this project, to start the creation of the first known open source video based
dataset for KV. To address this issue, this research project has been conducted in collab-
oration with Data field Lab at Slovak University of Technology in Bratislava (STU) and the
department of Applied Mathematics and Computer Science at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU) and contributes to a broader collaboration between the two universities.
The outcome and motivation thus become to publish the first video dataset with labelled
data of kinship relationship to be used in various research fields to further develop the
overall field of Kinship Verification (KV).
1.2
Problem statement
There are no open source video datasets available for research in the field of Multi-Modal
Data (MMD) KD. Apart from a contribution to the research field, the ability to determine
kinship between individuals can be useful in various cases, for example usage of KD in
border control. The problem statement is therefore to see if it is possible to train a model to
be able to determine if two individuals are related or not. In addition, the issue of creating
an open source dataset will also be addressed in a framework for collecting, storing, and
retrieving video data. Lastly the challenge of validating models trained on other datasets
will be investigated. This is important to assess whether open source datasets used to
train a variety of models represents the biases and diversity of the population they are
modelling. This is crucial when inferring on subjects from the real world. In essence, the
problem statement can be divided into the following:
• How to conceptualise a standardised framework to collect, store and retrieve Multi-
Modal Data (videos) to experiment on different Kinship Detection tasks?
• How can Transfer Learning be used to detect kinship amongst subjects evaluating
on Deep Learning models?
• How well is the performance of models trained on Families In The Wild dataset
and evaluated on our own collected data? And can the results be used to evaluate
external datasets?

2.1
Biometric systems
Biometric Systems (BS) are described as “the automated use of physiological or be-
havioural characteristics to determine or verify identity” [5], e.g. facial features, finger-
prints, speech patterns, etc. to amongst other successfully identify a person. The field
of BS can be divided into uni-modal and multi-modal. Uni-modal is defined as using only
a single characteristic of a person for identification, for example using only one image
of a person to identify the person. Multi-modal on the other hand uses a combination of
different biometric markers to identify an individual. [6]
There are many different types of BS and they tend to be split into physiological and be-
havioural biometrics. The physiological biometrics focuses on the direct measurements
of body parts e.g., retina, face, fingerprints while behavioural biometrics are concerned
with the measurements and data that can be derived from an action an individual is doing.
Various biometric characteristics possess an inherent robustness. Robustness is defined
as “a measure of the extent to which the characteristic or trait is subject to change over
time” [5]. Several factors, such as age or injury, can influence this robustness. The degree
of distinctiveness is another measure that can be considered when evaluating a character-
istic. This measure indicates the extent to which individuals can be distinguished from one
another within the general population. For instance, retinal scans exhibit a high degree
of distinctiveness, whereas facial recognition exhibits a comparatively lower degree [5].
The physical characteristics are called modalities and can be categorized into the follow-
ing [5]:
Method
Characteristics
Hand geometry
In the hand geometry, the overall structure of the hand,
shape, and proportions are used to identify an individual.
Head and face
The head and face are primarily concerned with distinguish-
ing between the facial properties of individuals.
Behavioral characteristics
Behavioural characteristics are looking at the behaviours of
individuals rather than physical characteristics.
Other physical character-
istics
This category includes various physical traits that can be
used for feature extraction.

2.1.1
Face
The idea is to look at the distinguishable features of the face to identify an individual. One
of the advantages of using facial biometrics is that it does not require physical contact with
the subject. The data can be acquired easily as it only requires an image. The system
afterwards extracts features from the face: distance between eyes, width of nose, jawline,
etc. to compare with stored data of a person. The disadvantages are that it is less precise
than some of its counterparts such as fingerprint recognition. Therefore, the use case at
hand should be considered when choosing the correct modality. To enhance the reliability
of biometric systems multiple modalities can be combined to further enhance its precision,
such as incorporating voice to the facial biometric [5, 6].
2.1.2
Voice
As the name suggests this modality looks at the different features that can be derived
from an individual’s voice. This can be divided into both acoustic features which is related
to the anatomy of the person and behavioural features. The behavioural features are the
tone, accent, voice pitches and so forth and therefore voice is considered a behavioural
biometric. There are different approaches to do the actual match and they are: text-
dependent, text-prompted, and text-independent. Text-dependent looks at the person
saying some specific predefined passphrases, that are then used. The text-prompted is
used when there is a possibility of an intruder, and this can then pose as an extra layer
of security. Lastly, the text-independent does not have anything but the voice to check
for [7].
All BS consists of three key elements they follow: [5, 6, 8]
• Enrollment
• Templates
• Matching
The enrolment process consists of acquiring the biometric marker needed. This is done
by using some technology like a camera, voice recorder, fingerprint scanner, etc. The
templates makes sure to extract features from the data acquired through the enrolment
process and creates a template of the most significant features to act as the body that
future potential authentications will be compared against. The matching process takes
whatever biometric characteristics fed to the system and matches them with one or more
modalities stored in the database [5, 6, 8].
There are three failure types that should be considered: failure to enrol, false match and
false non match. The failure to enrol is when the system is unable to acquire the proper
features to do comparing. The false match is when an intruder is successfully able to
penetrate the system by getting a positive match. The false non match is when a person
who actually does exist gets denied by the system [5, 6, 8, 7].
This provides the basic layout for how BS works. The facial and voice properties have
been highlighted to provide a background for how the systems work. KD as compared to
biometric systems focusses on the issue of two persons being related and there is some
overlap - as some of the features may be the same but not all of them. Therefore, a direct
match is not intended e.g., an individual’s image matching exactly an image in a database.
This furthermore means that the acquiring of the biometric marker - in this case - is more
relevant regarding obtaining the persons needed to be checked. The actual acquisition
of the biometric marker is really training a model that can distinguish between families in
general.

Kinship Recognition
The rise of AI, has been a significant technological advancement with far-reaching im-
plications across various fields. AI refers to the development of computer systems that
can perform tasks that would require human intelligence, e.g. visual perception, speech
recognition, decision-, and problem-solving [9]. AI is a broad field that encompasses many
subfields, such as ML, DL, and CV. AI has been used in numerous applications, such as
self-driving cars, facial recognition, and natural language processing. One area where
AI has shown great potential is in the field of KR, where KV is a subfield. KV aims to
distinguish between kin and non-kin, while the art of Kinship Identification (KI) have tried
to determine the exact kin relation between two individuals images [10, 11]. KR can be
conducted in different ways but in this excerpt the FKV will be focussed on.
KR is a subfield of FR where FR focuses on identifying and verifying an individual’s identity
using visual features. This can be applied in various fields, including security measures
such as using FR to access personal electronic devices. The VKD aims to determine
whether a biological relationship exists between two individuals based on their visual fea-
tures. VKD encompasses several sub-problems, each addressing different use case. The
increase of computation capacity have allowed researchers to develop new techniques
for KR based on DL [11] For example, a novel pattern analysis technique for KV has been
proposed that uses a DL based approach. Given a pair of face images, deep features
are extracted from each image using Resnet50. Feature distances between each pair
of images are then computed, and to overcome the problem of unbalanced data, One
Hot Encoding for labels is utilised. The distances are finally fed to a deep neural network
to determine the kinship relation [10]. Extensive experiments have been conducted on
the FIW dataset, which contains 11 classes of kinship relationships. The results showed
promising results and pointed out the importance of balancing the training dataset. Fur-
thermore, the approach showed an interesting ability to generalize, performing better than
all existing approaches on grandparents-grandchildren type of kinship [10].

collection of images. This makes the verification more precise as there is a benchmark of
how a non-kin relationship looks like [14].
Search and retrieval
Search and retrieval refer to the process of verifying whether the subject or input facial
image exists in any given database where the search is performed. This problem can be
used for several purposes which include solving missing persons cases, forensic research
and more [12].

Tranfer learning
Traditional ML models are based upon the assumption that the training and testing data
is collected from the same domain. This essentially means that input feature space and
data distributions characteristics are the same. However, this assumption does not hold
in many real-world application areas [20]. Having enough data is often a limitation. TL
is a ML technique that aims to improve the performance of a model on a target task by
transferring knowledge from a related source task [21]. Figure 2.2, shows examples of
how TL can be used transfer knowledge across domains.
Figure 2.2: Intuitive examples about TL. Adapted from [21].
The goal of TL is to reduce the amount of data required from the target domain. There
are two common types of tasks for TL, feature extraction and fine tuning of a model.
The feature extraction task involves using the representations learned from a pre-trained
model to extract meaningful features from new samples. This can be achieved by adding
a new classifier on top of the pre-trained model, which will be trained from scratch, such
that the feature maps learned previously can be repurposed for the new dataset. [22, 21]
The fine-tuning task in TL, is based on unfreezing some of the top layers of the pre-trained
model and jointly training both the newly added classifier layers and the last layers of the
base model. This allows one to fine-tune the higher-order feature representations in the
base model to make them more relevant for the specific task. [22]

Siamese networks
Siamese Network (SM) was proposed in the 90s to solve signature verification as an
image matching problem [13]. SM revolves around a process in which two input samples
are provided that needs to be compared. Feature extraction is then performed on both
samples by identical subnetworks. The identical subnetworks are used to ensure that
similar inputs gets mapped to a similar feature space [13]. The feature representations
are compared using a similarity metric such as Euclidean distance or cosine similarity.
Lastly, the loss is calculated to quantify the dissimilarities between the inputs and can be
used to set a threshold for predictions [13].

The DFSN consists of a backbone used for feature extraction. The images served to
the model will be passed through an implementation of MTCNN to detect and crop the
faces for optimal use. The implementation will not be explained but used and is a part
of the facenet-pytorch model [25]. In the later implementation, the Inception-ResNet V1
was chosen as the main feature extractor. It was chosen because of the state-of-the-art
performance on various CV problems. The model was introduced in 2016 in the pa-

[24]. The model consists of Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture, combining
Residual Network (RESNET) and Inception blocks into the Inception-ResNet architecture.
The background for creating the aforementioned architecture can be found in the vanish-
ing gradient problem when constructing CNN with multiple layers. The vanishing gradient
problem was resolved by introducing residual conncetions or skip conncetion. The math-
ematical representation for a skip conncetion can be written as output = input+F(input).
It should be noted that the output is calculated based on the input from the former layer
plus the transformed input, and this thus creates the flexibility for the network to learn dur-
ing the forward pass and back propagation. This combined means that the gradient flow
of the network becomes optimised since the network is able to learn residual mappings1
This proves valuable as it directly impacts the ability to create deep NN.

introduced in the 2010s [24]. This module introduces different kernel sizes in each layer
to capture a variety of different features and a rich variety of both high- and low-level

The feature extractor provides a 512-dimensional embedding vector that needs to be in-
terpreted further. Since the backbone is identical for both input images, a concatenated
vector of both images is needed to calculate the feature fusion part of the model. Feature
fusion improves the nonlinear ability of the DFSN while encoding the feature represen-
tations abstractly. To achieve this, several operations can be performed on the feature
representations for the two inputs and concatenate these into one larger vector to be fed
through a NN. There have been described several different types of feature fusion oper-
ations. Suppose x and y are the extracted feature vectors then the following operations

are examples of operations that can be made to fuse them: (x ⊕y), (x + y) ⊕(x −y), (x +
y) ⊕(x −y) ⊕(x ∗y), (x2 −y2), (x −y)2 ⊕x2 −y2 ⊕x ∗y amongst others [17]. After the
features have been concatenated, the resulting vector has 1536 dimensions, which are

function maps an input value to an output in the range between 0 and 1, and is therefore
useful when calculating the similarity score.
For training the Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) loss function was selected. This is due to
the performance demonstrated in the DFSN paper. BCE loss is given by the following
formula:
BCE(ytrue, ypred) = −[ytrue · log(ypred) + (1 −ytrue) · log(1 −ypred)]
Where ytrue is the correct binary label 0 or 1, pred is the predicted similarity score. The
BCE loss function penalizes the error proportionally to its size, meaning a large error gets
penalized more than smaller errors. This incentivises the model to increase the prediction
capability by minimising the errors.

on both positive relationship types as well as negative relationship types. The negative
sampling could be done in a various of different ways. The most important factor to uphold
in the negative sampling was to get an equal number of negative and positive samples.
To ensure this the graph property was utilised to get the number of nodes in the graph
and then pair them with the nodes that were not connected - as connection meant real re-
lationships. This was used to generate equal pairs of negatives. Since the FIWV dataset
were videos and not images as opposed to FIW considerations towards the sampling of
video data needed to be made as well.
To start it was considered to create a pipeline that could provide continuous frames ex-
tracted from video data to get classification on those. It was instead decided to create
an algorithm that could be used to source frames per individual depending on how many
frames per second needed as well as the total number of frames wanted. This made
sense in research setting as compared to the continuous stream that would be more suit-
able in a production setting. Since it could be possible to want to sample more than one
extracted frame per individual this was chosen as the most viable option. The actual ex-
traction of the frames was also done by sampling through the videos by jumping though
them. This means that static frames were not extracted as that could defeat the purpose
during training or testing since four frames extracted from the same second would have
the same image. So, the extraction thus became more diverse in the sense that some
randomness was added to the extraction of the frame. This did create other issues since
there are times during the videos where the subjects have their eyes closed or in some

7.1
Data collection & Preprocessing
To start with the data collection will be discussed. For the collection the notion of coverage
was strived for. Coverage is as mentioned in the section 2.4 has the objective to get
representatives for each subgroup in the population and thus achieves a representative
sample of the population being modelled. When trying to model the world population to
some extent - as is the case for this thesis - the subgroups become relative to the definition
of what the researchers want. This means that there are no “correct” way of creating the
subgroups as that becomes dependent on several different factors such as availability of
data, time for collection and the end use case. In this case the overall strategy was to
start by completely getting one relationship sample in each age group as described in the
figure 4.1. The age groups thus became one way of subgrouping the population but as
could be noted in the chapter 6 further subgroups could be labelled such as race to further
get insights about amongst others model predictions trained on external datasets as well
as getting a representative dataset. Only the race “Caucasian” were thoroughly collected
for the relationships: ’bb’, ’sibs’, ’ss’, ’fs’, ’ms’, ’md’ and ’ms’ which were described as
the most critical relationships to model. It was also apparent to see that there could be
some biases present in the FIW dataset since the only “African American” sample were
wrongly predicted by DFSN model, but since more samples were not collected of races it
can biased to generalise from such a scarce prediction. It could also be noted that there
seemed to be a clear bias towards women being wrongly predicted by DFSN model as
compared to the male population. This could indicate a bias in the FIW dataset. Apart
from the race a few other factors could also be considered when collecting data to further
enhance the quality of the FIWV dataset such as: eye color and hair color which can be
useful when inferring on data to get insights. Nonetheless the currently FIWV dataset -
although not fulfilling the overall idea of coverage - still does follow the general idea and
since a big part of the creation of an open source dataset is to contribute and extend the
labels as well as collect data the data collection can still be considered representative of
the Caucasian race and the age groups collected.
The preprocessing step before serving the data to train models is also vital to discuss. As
described in chapter 5 a number of different dataloaders were made for the different mod-
els. The discussion will include the dataloaders acrshortdfsn-FIWV and acrshortrfiw2021-
FIW as the best performing model was DFSN. The biggest setback of the acrshortdfsn-
FIW dataloader is that it is not utilising all the samples in the FIW dataset. The primary
reason was that the model training started to halt due to the large amount of data. Also,
because of the way the images of the individuals were structured the control over sampling

was not as desired. The dataloader thus samples randomly for each relationship pair and

For the acrshortdfsn-FIW dataloader which sampled frames from the videos the relation-

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the problem FR for KD using supervised DL approach.
The
datasets FIWV and FIW were employed to conduct the research. The FIWV is proposed
as a open source MMD dataset to be employed in future research in this field. The models
DFSN and RFIW2021 were chosen for training and testing. The FIW dataset was utilized
primarily for training while FIWV was used for testing the usage of TL and whether FIWV
dataset could be used for benchmarking other datasets in the field.
It can be concluded that a MMD dataset and framework have been proposed for support-
ing future work in the field of KV. The collected dataset consists of 16 families, 36 individ-
uals with a total of 23 relationships. The collection process is extremely tedious and time
consuming, when the constraint of collecting minimum 10 seconds of video needs to be
upheld. This is due to the limited availability of the video data in the wild as well as the
available quality of the videos. Since the data collection was split amongst two individu-
als for the English speaking group, it is evident that there are quality misalignments in the
collected videos that will need to be aligned in the future. With the limited data collected
the ability to use this dataset to train for real-world applications is also limited. Additional
data collection is thus required to utilise the full potential of the FIWV dataset.
None the less the dataset has been used to assess FIW dataset in terms of represen-
tativity. From the FIWV dataset it was evident that models trained on FIW dataset was
not performing well on predicting the female gender. Although the strategy of coverage
was applied there is only one relationship of the “African American” race which shows the
scarcity of the collected data. To summarise, it can be concluded that an MMD dataset
was produced, however the multi-modality of the dataset was not utilised for this project.
Also TL was conducted by training a DFSN model on FIW dataset and inferred on FIWV
dataset with performance almost matching that of conducting tests on FIW dataset: ACC
= 0.66 on FIWV dataset and 0.69 on FIW dataset. This means that TL was successful
when using pretrained feature extractors and an external dataset to conduct training.
