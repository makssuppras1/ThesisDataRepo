Abstract 
Bottom culture, which involves creating structures resembling natural mussel beds, can influence 
the diversity of the local macrofauna. However, this method of aquaculture also faces various risks 
while aiming to increase mussel production. The study was conducted in Limfjorden, Denmark. 
They were separated into 8 experimental groups and 3 control locations according to the seed source, 
relaying density, relaying time, and relaying vessels. Moreover, four temporal stages (pre-relay, 1 
month, 6 months, and 1 year after mussel beds were relayed) were established. The study also 
monitored the environmental conditions in the area to examine the effects of the above factors on 
the local macrofaunal community and the performance of mussels. 
The findings indicated that the presence of mussel beds had a significant effect on species 
abundance. Areas with denser or larger mussels exhibited higher species abundance, but the 
diversity indices in the study site were generally lower. The dominant species in the area was the 
brittle star (76%), followed by the slipper limpet (7%). The presence of mussel beds also influenced 
the community composition, with the greatest dissimilarity found between C1 (control location) and 
WSD (Wild-Seed-Dense), particularly in the pre-relay and one year after mussel bed relaying. The 
brittle star was identified as the primary species responsible for these differences.  
Except for two groups that exhibited mediocre performance and experienced some mortality 
due to summer relaying, the remaining six groups demonstrated growth and aggregation. Among 
them, the WSD group displayed the largest shell length (44mm) and the highest production (15kg 
m-2) when harvested. The condition index exhibited noticeable spatial and temporal variations, 
which were significantly influenced by sampling month and dissolved oxygen. Moreover, the 
growth rates of mussels were greatly impacted by the relaying practices.  
Therefore, according to these culture practices, it is suggested that fishermen's vessels can 
jointly fish mussels to improve efficiency. The suggested relaying density is 5kg m-2, and relaying 
during the summer is discouraged. Sediment conditions can be incorporated into the site selection 
criteria in the future. Further research is required to explore the potential use of suspended seeds as 
an alternative to wild seeds in different conditions. To address the biosecurity concerns related to 
ascidian recruitment, a biosecurity plan is proposed, encompassing site selection, the relay process, 
and prevention and mitigation measures. 
These findings provide valuable insights into the study of subtidal mussel beds and associated 
macrofauna biodiversity. They hold significant importance in guiding relevant culture practices and 
promoting the sustainable development of aquaculture.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Blue mussel bed ecology 
Blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) filter particulate matter directly from the water and play an important 
role in the benthic-pelagic coupling of the ocean as a vital component of the marine ecosystem 
(Maar et al. 2015; Taylor et al. 2021). Mussels aggregate and provide a habitat for a variety of 
marine organisms by forming biogenic reefs known as mussel beds. Mussel beds are of high 
ecological significance in marine ecosystems, as they can improve species diversity and density 
compared to bare sediment or rock, while also yielding significant commercial shellfish production 
(Khalaman et al. 2020). Mussel beds may have a greater effect on species diversity in subtidal areas 
than in intertidal areas (Kristensen 2021). However, wild mussel beds are frequently reported as 
being unstable, being susceptible to external influences to undergo short- or long-term changes 
(Khaitov et al. 2016). For instance, climate change and predation by invasive green crabs (Carcinus 
maenas) have caused a sharp decline in mussel bed abundance in the Gulf of Maine (US) (Commito 
et al. 2019). Due to storms and starfish predation, a similar situation has been observed for mussel 
beds throughout the western Wadden Sea (NL) (Smaal et al. 2021). Both intertidal and subtidal 
mussel beds can be found in the natural environment, but their associated biodiversity is mainly 
influenced by local conditions, and disturbances will limit their species’ complexity and diversity 
(Craeymeersch et al. 2018). Furthermore, research has indicated that the mussel beds formed 
through bottom culture also have an impact on the abundance, diversity, and composition of the 
benthic fauna (Beadman et al. 2004; Murray et al. 2007; Ysebaert et al. 2009). Within the intertidal 
areas, Dolmer (2002) found that an increase in mussel abundance had a positive effect on the number 
of related species, however, Beadman et al. (2004) discovered that an increase in mussel abundance 
may reduce infaunal diversity. According to Murray et al. (2007), mussel cultivation triggered a 
transition in the subtidal area from oligochaete to polychaete-dominated worm communities. 
Presently, the majority of studies have primarily focused on the intertidal areas. Therefore, it is 
crucial to investigate the bottom culture mussel beds in the subtidal areas and their associated 
biodiversity. 
 
1.2 Shellfish aquaculture 
Meanwhile, mussel beds can also be established by active cultivation. Bottom culture is a method 
of producing mussels that can result in structures resembling those found in natural beds, which can 
develop large and diverse communities (Craeymeersch et al. 2018).  
In Europe, shellfish bottom culture mainly includes oysters (Crassostrea gigas, Ostrea edulis), 
mussels (Mytilus galloprovincialis, M. edulis), scallops (Pecten maximus), cockles (Cerastoderma 
edule) and clams (Ruditapes spp) (Robert et al. 2013; Smaal 2002; Strand et al. 2016; Benovic 1997). 
Besides bottom culture, bouchot (lines strung on a wooden pole) or suspended culture such as raft, 
and longline setups, represent the principal mussel culture methods in Europe (Strand et al. 2019). 
Bottom culture is most prevalent in shallow macrotidal waters such as the Netherlands, Germany, 
the UK, and Ireland (Maguire et al. 2007). The success of bottom culture is mainly related to natural 
conditions such as recruitment, food availability, and predation (Smaal 2002). Bottom culture is a 
recent development in Denmark, and at present, longline culture and fishery of wild beds are still 
the main methods of mussel production (Petersen et al. 2018). Different mussel bottom culture 
techniques have evolved as a result of the policies and traditions of different countries (Maguire et

Countries with a long tradition of bottom cultivation are chosen for the description of the cultivation 
practices: 
The Netherlands 
The largest mussel bottom culture area in Europe can be found in the Dutch Oosterschelde estuary 
and the western region of the Wadden Sea (Capelle et al. 2016). In the Netherlands, large fishing 
boats with two to four dredges and a flat bottom to allow fishing in shallow areas are typically used 
to fish mussel seeds from wild mussel beds (Strand et al. 2019). However, Dutch mussel farmers 
have gradually switched from collecting seeds from wild mussel beds to employing suspended seed 
mussel collectors (SMCs) (Kamermans et al. 2002) as a result of legal enforcement of substituting 
fishery of wild seed with SMCs (Strand et al. 2019). Large fishing boats also relay the collected 
mussels into subtidal or intertidal licensed culture areas, where they continue to grow for one to 
three years until they reach marketable size (Gosling, 2003). 
 
UK 
The Menai Strait in North Wales produces the most mussels annually in the UK (Gallagher et al. 
2008). Mussel seeds are collected from denser natural mussel beds by large fishing boats and then 
relayed on licensed bottom culture plots (Laing et al. 2006). In the Menai Strait, mussel seeds are 
first relayed on the muddy substrates of the intertidal area. The number of mussels that can be 
harvested is high because there are fewer predators, but there is not enough food due to tidal 
variations and it usually takes about 1.5 years of cultivation to reach 30-40 mm size. They are then 
moved and cultured for 0.5–1 year in subtidal areas until they reach commercial size (45 mm). Early 
September signals the start of the commercial size mussel harvest, which lasts through the winter 
until all marketable size mussels have been harvested. (Gallagher et al. 2008).

Denmark 
In Denmark, seeds originate in two ways: one is to collect them from natural beds and the other is 
to gather them from suspended farms. The former started in 2004, it includes transplanting seeds 
from areas of high density to lower density (Christensen 2012), transplanting seeds from deeper 
water with lower dissolved oxygen levels to shallow water (Petersen et al. 2018), and transplanting 
mussels from dredge fisheries but sorted with shell lengths less than commercial size (45 mm). Once 
the mussels reach commercial size they cannot be fished during the summer months following the 
mussel fishery regulation. The later, seeds from suspended cultures started on January 26, 2016, 
acting as a new source of seeds for the bottom culture. Those mussels, once they reach commercial 
size can be fished in the summer time, as they follow an aquaculture regime. In both cases, after 
reaching the culture area, the mussel seeds are washed out from the sides of the vessels according 
to the selected seeding density and transplanted to the bottom (Christensen 2012). So far there is 
only one active vessel similar to the Dutch and UK ones capable of both fishing and relaying the 
mussels in licensed plots. This is because the industry is still small in Denmark. Harvesting of wild 
mussels is also possible using traditional fishing vessels that can transfer their catch to the relaying 
vessel. Mussels are typically farmed in Denmark for two years before being harvested when they 
reach commercial size (shell length 45–55 mm) (Christensen 2012). 
 
1.3 Bottom culture risks and challenges 
Mussel bottom culture is susceptible to many risks, making it crucial to effectively mitigate these 
risks to achieve optimal production. One key aspect is site selection, which holds great importance. 
Bottom culture involves exposing mussels to environmental conditions resembling those found in 
natural mussel beds (Ysebaert et al. 2009). The mussels will inevitably face variations in the food 
supply, predator exposure, unfavorable weather, and marine pollution due to the environment of the 
selected site (Avdelas 2021). Research has demonstrated that mussel growth is closely linked to the 
biomass of phytoplankton (Page and Hubbard 1987). Therefore, it is essential to select a site where 
the concentration of chlorophyll-a in the surrounding water is sufficient to sustain mussel growth. 
Water currents play a vital role in delivering food to the mussels. Strong storm events or high water 
currents can displace bottom mussels in shallow waters, while slow water movement can impede 
bivalve growth by affecting the replenishment of food sources (Buck 2017). Studies have shown 
that reducing the number of predators can enhance mussel survival rates (Murray et al. 2007). 
Therefore, it is necessary to choose areas with robust water exchange capacity, appropriate flow 
velocity, and a limited amount of predators for subtidal bottom culture. On the other hand, when 
selecting a site, considerations need to be given to spatial limitations in the sea to avoid conflicts 
with potential interests such as nature reserves, recreational areas, economic utilization, and public 
acceptance (Smaal 2002). 
Implementing good culture practices is another crucial aspect. Research has indicated that 
providing complex substrates can enhance mussel survival rates (Christensen et al. 2015). 
Controlling predator populations can be achieved by using starfish mops (Calderwood et al. 2016). 
Varying seed density and selecting optimal timing for relaying and harvesting can increase 
production volumes and improve production efficiency (Beadman et al., 2004; Capelle et al., 2016; 
Strand et al., 2019). It is important to note that proposing effective culture practices should take into 
account national policies and long-term threats encountered during the culture process. In this study, 
we need to consider the specific situation in Limfjorden. The most significant natural predator of 
==PAGE:7==
5 
 
mussels is the sea star (Asterias rubens), which can directly cause mussel mortality by feeding on 
them (Burgaard et al. 2023) and is abundant in the Limfjorden. The Limfjorden fisheries are 
negatively impacted by infrequent harmful algal blooms (HABs), which can affect the condition of 
mussels by causing blue mussels to accumulate Diarrhetic Shellfish Toxins (DSTs) (Jørgensen et al. 
2008; Nielsen et al. 2020). Invasive alien species (IAS) can compete with native species for 
resources like food and space after being introduced (Joyce et al. 2021). Many European countries, 
including Denmark, have been invaded by Pacific oysters (Nielsen et al. 2022). The invasion of 
Pacific oysters introduced parasitic copepods (Mytilicola intestinalis), and the spillover of this 
parasite may indirectly affect blue mussels, causing a significant reduction in their body condition 
(Goedknegt et al. 2018). Based on Dutch experience (Dara 2017), the oyster drill (Ocinebrellus 
inornatus) is considered a potentially greater threat to the entire shellfish industry in Limfjorden 
than Mytilicola intestinalis. These snails eat young oysters and spats in addition to drilling holes 
through the flesh and shell of oysters. Addressing the risks to bottom culture posed by these 
biosecurity issues (predators, HABs, invasive species, and parasites) requires the development of a 
biosecurity plan. Biosecurity plans can mitigate threats by implementing treatments such as 
freshwater washing, aerial exposure, chemicals, and smothering (Cook et al. 2016). Therefore, it is 
of utmost importance to increase mussel production by considering the challenges that may arise 
during site selection and the culture process and to formulate corresponding biosecurity plans to 
effectively address these issues.  
 
1.4 Aims of the study 
The project focuses on the evolution of macrofauna diversity of bottom relayed mussel beds in the 
Limfjorden and associated mussel bed evolution from collected samples from four temporal stages 
(pre-relay, 1 month, 6 months, and 1 year after mussel beds were relayed). In this thesis, the 
following questions will be addressed: How can bottom culture impact the resident macrofaunal 
community in Limfjorden? How are mussels performing (growth, condition index, density, biomass 
production) depending on different experimental relay methods? How do environmental conditions 
affect mussel performance? The outputs from this project will provide information on changes in 
biodiversity with time in mussel bottom culture in subtidal areas and can serve as a reference for 
sustainable aquaculture practices. 
 
2 Materials and Methods 
2.1 Study site and design of bottom plots 
The study site is situated in the Limfjorden in the northern part of Jutland, which is the largest fjord 
system in Denmark and the primary region for mussel production. It is connected to the North Sea 
in the west and to the Kattegat in the east. The fjord has a total area of roughly 1600 km2 (Nielsen 
et al. 2022; Maar et al. 2021), and the experiment's area (Figure 1) is 1200×1200 m with a water 
depth of about 8 m. 
To test the effects of different seed sources, relaying densities, relaying times, and two types 
of fishing vessels on macrofauna diversity and mussel production, differences were compared 
between eight experimental groups and three control locations. The distribution of 11 plots and 
specific stations can be seen in the figure below (Figure 1). The fishing vessels are divided into two 
types: fishermen's vessels and a large fishing and relaying vessel called "Limfjorden". "Limfjorden"

2.2 Field Procedures 
Mussel sampling was done one month after seeding, there were subsequent samples taken about six 
months and a year later. Sampling stations were divided into "mussel beds" and "bare patch/without 
mussels". The former was sampled where mussels aggregated in the relay plots, and the latter was 
sampled between relay tracks close to where the mussels were sampled. The distribution of different 
types of stations (M for mussel beds and B for bare patches) are shown in Figure 1. The time of the 
first sampling is also the time when the project started, which is September 23, 2020. A SCUBA 
diver counted macrofauna in a 0.25 m2 quadrat on the seafloor. The macrofauna data were collected 
four times, the first sampling time was one month before the mussel seeds were relayed in each 
respective culture plot, and the next three sampling times were the same as for sampling mussels. 
When the diver finished counting macrofauna, all material within the quadrat was collected and 
transported back to land in the Nykøbing Mors DTU Aqua laboratory for measurement. The entire 
sample was weighed before and after the draining of water; subsamples were taken for sorting. 
Mussels were sorted from other species and shells; mussels were partitioned if < 1 cm and all sorted 
fractions were weighed. 100 mussels were weighed to estimate the number of mussels in the 
subsample, and the density was calculated by estimating the number of mussels in the total sample 
according to the ratio. The same approach as before was used to calculate mussel biomass. Finally, 
the 100 mussels were frozen（-20℃）after the measurements were completed and preserved for 
biometric analysis. 
 
2.3 Environmental monitoring 
From June 22, 2020, to August 22, 2022, a time series of the environmental conditions and 
hydrodynamics were gathered. Data collection gaps occurred between November 2021 and March 
2022 and February 2021 and March 2021, as a result of the removal of instruments in expectation 
of ice coverage and bad weather. At LOCATION (56.85108°N, 8.90477°E) (figure 1), 50 cm from 
the seafloor, an Aquatroll 600 sonde (In Situ Inc.) was fixed to a mooring to measure conductivity, 
temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen (optical), and chlorophyll-a fluorescence with logging 1-minute 
frequency averages every 15 minutes. An RD Instruments Workhorse Sentinel 600 kHz ADCP was 
collocated, and moored in a fixed frame on the seafloor, logging 10-minute ensembles in 50cm bins, 
with the first good bin at 1.6m. From March 2022, the mooring was moved to the south of the culture 
area LOCATION (56.84500°N, 8.91273°E) (figure 1), and an additional sonde deployed north of 
the culture area at LOCATION (56.85680°N, 8.91288°E) (figure 1). According to the predominate

Following the manufacturer's recommendations, the sondes were cleaned and calibrated every

2.4 Laboratory Procedures 
First, the weight of the 100 frozen mussels was measured, the number of mussels with epibionts 
was recorded, and the weight of these epibionts was measured. Following that, 10 representative 
mussels were chosen, and their shell length, height, and width were all measured with vernier 
calipers. The remaining 90 mussels, however, were only measured for their shell length. The tissue 
was then cut out using a scalpel, blotted out, and dried in an oven (80°C) for 36 hours before being 
removed to measure the tissue weight to derive a condition index.

2.5 Statistical analysis 
A Generalized Linear Model (GLM) analysis was used to evaluate the potential impacts of treatment, 
type (mussel beds and bare patch), time since relay, and temperature on species abundance (N), 
Margalef's species richness (d), Shannon Weiner Index (H', using log base e), and Pielou evenness 
index (J′). A linear model with an identity link was used to fit the data for these four dependent 
variables. The data distribution was normalized using a log2 (x+1) transformation for all dependent 
variables. The effects of mussel density and shell length on N, d, H', and J' in the mussel group were 
also examined using GLM. A linear model with an identity link was fitted to all of the data. The 
data distribution was normalized using a log2 (x+1) transformation for N, d, and J'. The effects of 
treatment, time since relay, temperature (T), dissolved oxygen (DO), and chlorophyll-a fluorescence 
(RFU) on mussel biomass, density, and growth rate were examined in mussel production using GLM. 
The three dependent variables were fitted to a linear model with an identity link. To standardize the 
data distribution, a log2(x+1) transformation was used for biomass and density. Meanwhile, the 
effects of treatment, sampling month, dissolved oxygen (DO), and chlorophyll-a fluorescence (RFU) 
on condition index were also examined in mussel production using GLM. An identity link linear 
model was fitted to this dependent variable. Condition index performed a log2 (x+1) transformation 
to normalize the data distribution. The lowest Akaike information criterion (AIC) was used to 
determine which model to use in each case. The software program Statistical Package for the Social 
Sciences was used to conduct the analyses (SPSS version 27). 
Based on the entire data set, a multivariate analysis was carried out to observe the variations in 
the benthic community composition between different treatments, time since relay, type (mussel 
beds and bare patch), and season. Using abundance data that had not been modified, the Bray-Curtis 
similarity measure was applied to the non-metric multidimensional scaling (nMDS) ordination 
model. A pairwise comparison of the significant term in a PERMANOVA main test was conducted 
once a significant main effect (time since relay, treatment, type, and season) was identified. The 
Similarity Percentage Analysis (SIMPER) was used to determine how much each species 
contributed to the similarity within and dissimilarity across the 4 factors (Avramidi et al. 2022).

3 Results  
3.1 Macrofauna diversity indices 
As can be seen in Figure 4, the species abundance fluctuated roughly in the range of 0-50 ind/m2 
among the different plots over time. On day 288 after the project's start (The start time of the project 
is the time of the first sampling, which is September 23, 2020), the WSD station with mussels 
recorded the highest abundance value of roughly 88 ind/m2. The results of GLM (Table 3, The 
definitions of the following significant impact factors can be found in the remarks to Table 3) 
showed that species abundance overall was significantly influenced by treatment (different plots, 
including JRL, JRF, MRL, MRF, SFS, SFD, WSS, WSD and C1-3), time since relay, type and the 
interaction of time since relay and type. The Margalef index (Figure 5) varied only between 0 and 
0.4. Here, the highest Margalef index was roughly 0.4, both at the C1 and MRF stations without 
mussels in the last time stage were found. Pielou evenness index and Shannon-Wiener index values 
also revealed comparable patterns (Appendix A). Again found at day 630 at the bare areas of MRF, 
the maximum Shannon-Wiener index and Pielou evenness index values were approximately 1 and 
0.78, respectively. All three diversity indices were significantly different in treatment, time since 
relay, and the interaction of time since relay and type, according to the GLM results (Table 3). Two 
factors (mussel density and shell length) were chosen for analysis to further explore whether there 
were differences between stations with mussels. Both factors revealed significant differences in 
species abundance, whereas only the shell length showed significant differences on all four diversity 
indices (Table 4). The findings showed that the diversity indices were relatively low, and the 
existence of mussel beds only had a significant impact on species abundance.

3.5 Mussel production 
Throughout the study period, no new mussel settlements were seen in any of the areas. Figure 13 
illustrates that the biomass (kg m-2) and shell length (mm) of MRL and MRF increase, accompanied 
by an initial rise in density (individuals m-2) followed by stabilization. These findings indicate that 
the mussels in these plots were experiencing growth and aggregating into small areas. Similarly, 
SFS, SFD, WSS, and WSD exhibited an increase in biomass, density, and shell length (Figure 14), 
implying that mussels in these plots are also growing and aggregating into smaller areas. However, 
the shell length of SF (Smartfarm) did not increase much, suggesting that mussels in those plots did 
not grow well. Regarding JRF (Figure 13), the biomass and shell length increased, while density 
decreased, indicating a combination of growth and mortality among the mussels, which was a 
mediocre performance. As for JRL (Figure 13), both biomass and density declined, implying a 
higher mortality rate for mussels, as evidenced by the lowest growth rates in the plot (Figure 15). 
Their growth rates (Figure 15) also support these results. Mussels that are usually aggregated into 
small areas have higher biomass at harvest, as in the case of MRF, WSS, and WSD, where their 
biomass increases from 4 kg m-2 to about 15 kg m-2.  
Compared to the initial relays, the shell length of each plot increased (Figures 13 and 14). In 
the year after the relay, the length of WSD's mussel shells reached 44 mm, which was close to the 
commercial size (45 mm), and its growth rate was faster than that of other plots. Although there was 
not a substantial difference in biomass between SF (Smartfarm) and WS (Wild seed) beds, SF had 
a higher density than WS due to smaller mussels. According to the results of GLM (Table 7), 
treatment had a significant effect on growth rate. The pairwise test results (Appendix E) indicated 
that JRF exhibited a considerably greater growth rate compared to the other experimental groups. 
When comparing experimental groups that only differed in a single variable (JRL and JRF, MRL 
and MRF, SFS and SFD, WSS and WSD), it was observed that JRF had a significantly higher growth 
rate than JRL, while SFS had a significantly higher growth rate than SFD. The condition index 
(Figure 13, 14) showed spatial and temporal variation, and a corresponding trend with seasonal

changes, where JR (June relay) initially had a lower condition index, perhaps as a result of the 
greater temperature at the time of the relay. Similarly, it is clear from the GLM analysis's findings

4 Discussion 
4.1 Macrofauna diversity 
The complex impact of human disturbance on marine ecosystems makes it crucial to comprehend 
the effects of mussel bottom culture on benthic environments (Dubois et al. 2006). The presence of 
mussels plays a crucial role in maintaining biodiversity and can have an impact on soft-sediment 
communities through processes like benthic-pelagic coupling and increased biodeposition (Sea et 
al. 2022). Previous research indicates that mussel beds lead to a significant increase in sediment 
organic matter, attracting species adapted to high organic matter and oxygen-depleted sediment 
conditions (Callier et al. 2009), such as the bivalve mollusk Theora lubrica, amphipods from the 
Phoxocephalidae family, and spionid polychaetes from the genus Prionospio (Sea et al. 2022). The 
==PAGE:27==
25 
 
SIMPER analysis revealed that the brittle star and common periwinkle were the species responsible 
for the differences observed between the presence and absence of mussel groups. According to the 
EU Habitat Directive, subtidal brittle star beds can be found on both gravel or mixed sedimentary 
substrates (sandbars that are always submerged in seawater) and hard substrates (coral reefs) 
(Hughes 1998). Similarly, the common periwinkle is commonly found in both hard substrate and 
soft bottom environments (Saier 2000). Similar species in different types (mussel bed or bare patch) 
likely contribute to the absence of significant effects on the Margalef index and Shannon-Wiener 
index.  
In this study, the distribution of species abundance is primarily observed in the range of 0-50 
ind/m2, which differs from the species abundance (1184 ind/m2) recorded by Saier (2002) during the 
survey of epifauna in subtidal mussel beds in the Wadden Sea (NL). Similarly, the Shannon-Wiener 
index obtained in this study is also lower compared to Saier's findings (Saier 2002). This disparity 
in results may be attributed to the relatively young age of the mussel beds examined in this study. 
According to the Ecological Time Hypothesis, communities that have been established for longer 
periods have a greater opportunity for colonization, leading to increased diversity (Veiga et al. 2022). 
Another possible explanation is that there is so much soft sediment in the experimental area, that 
relatively low substrate stability may be challenging for colonization and succession. Relayed 
mussels were observed to be buried, likely due to high sedimentation rates, sediment advection, or 
fluidization. In addition, eutrophication of seawater can lead to phytoplankton blooms. This will 
alter the abundance and diversity of benthic communities by raising the turbidity of the water 
column and exacerbating oxygen depletion (Meyer and Koester 2000). Consequently, this 
environment may not be conducive to supporting a thriving ecosystem. 
In natural mussel beds, mussel size has been identified as the primary predictor for both 
abundance and species richness (Veiga et al. 2022). Similarly, this study's results indicated that shell 
length had a notable impact on the four diversity indices. This finding is consistent with the 
increased diversity reported by Tsuchiya and Nishihira (1986) in larger mussels. The presence of 
larger individuals within the mussels likely contributes to a more intricate structure, creating 
sheltering cavities between mussels and shells, which may explain the observed patterns (Norling 
and Kautsky 2008). 
During the final period of the JRL (JuneRelayLimfjorden) mussel station, there was a notable 
shift in the dominant species to ascidian. The significant increase in the population of ascidian in 
the Limfjorden could be one of the contributing factors (pers. com. local fishman). Another possible 
explanation for the recruitment of ascidian is the higher mortality of JRL mussels, which reduces 
competition for space and resources (Palanisamy et al. 2018). This creates favorable conditions for 
ascidian to settle and utilize the vacant shells as substrates. Additionally, there was an evident change 
in SFD. Before and after the relaying of mussels, there was a substantial decrease in the number of 
slipper limpets (Crepidula fornicata). This could be due to sampling in plots with many slipper 
limpets (in special cases), or it may be linked to the high variability in the distribution of slipper 
limpets. 
 
4.2 Mussel production 
Our findings revealed that JRL exhibited a higher mortality rate and the slowest growth rate, 
potentially due to the recruitment of ascidian, which could decrease the growth and overall fitness 
of the mussels. Additionally, the sediment substrate in the study area was of poor quality, with high 
==PAGE:28==
26 
 
levels of sedimentation and resuspension, creating unfavorable conditions for mussel growth. 
Moreover, the mussels in the JRL plot were relayed in June, during the summer when high 
temperatures may have initially provided an unfavorable environment for their growth. Furthermore, 
oxygen levels were occasionally low during the relay period, which could further impede their 
growth (Figure 17). Moreover, within the first two months of harvest, there was an additional 
hypoxic event (Figure 18). The combination of these stressors likely contributed to the relatively 
poor performance of JRL.  
Another area, SF (Smartfarm), also exhibited slower growth, primarily attributed to sediment-
related issues. Another potential reason could be that expenditure on re-aggregation (continued 
increase in biomass and density) may occur at the cost of shell accretion or tissue due to sediment 
effects. Despite JRF exhibiting the highest growth rate in our results, this could be attributed to its 
smaller shell length during relaying, which provided it with greater growth potential. Consequently, 
the results strongly suggest that WSD exhibited the most positive performance metrics, as it not 
only demonstrated a good growth rate but also a higher biomass at harvest.

4.3 Environmental conditions affect mussel growth 
In an investigation of mussel growth rates, Almada-Viella et al. (1982) discovered that the rates 
follow a logarithmic rise between 3°C and 20°C but experience a significant fall above 20°C. The 
initially lower condition index found in JR (June relay) may be explained by this threshold at 20°C 
(Peharda et al. 2007). The temperature variation in our study was generally within this favorable 
range for mussel growth. Mussels have lower filtration rates when exposed to prolonged low salinity 
(15 ppt, 1ppt=1PSU) or high salinity (>50 ppt) conditions (Wilson and Seed 1974). A frequent 
problem that results in a decrease in salinity is the intrusion of freshwater brought on by surface 
runoff and rainfall (Buck 2017). Blue mussels can withstand salinity drops of 4-5 ppt for a short 
period, however, this environment still restricts their growth and can cause substantial mortality 
(Kautsky et al. 1990). In our results, the salinity ranged from 26.5-29.5 PSU and such extreme 
fluctuations did not occur. Studies show that lowering the pH of seawater to 7.3 may damage 
the mussels (Michaelidis et al. 2005), lower their metabolic rate, and affect the physiological status 
and function of their hemocytes (Bibby et al. 2008). Estuaries typically have relatively low pH (7.5 
and even 7). The pH range (7.8–8.4) at our estuary was similar to the typical seawater pH values in 
the open ocean (Ringwood and Keppler 2002), and lower pH unfavorable for mussel growth did not 
occur. Lowering dissolved oxygen levels from 9 mg/L to 2 mg/L only has an impact on mussel 
respiration and not filtration rate. Mussels cannot maintain their typical aerobic metabolism when 
oxygen levels are less than 2 mg/L (Tang and Riisgård 2018). Our results indicate a monthly average 
dissolved oxygen range of 6-12mg/L, which falls within an appropriate range. Food depletion can 
negatively affect mussel farm output during times of low phytoplankton abundance (Ogilvie et al. 
==PAGE:30==
28 
 
2000). High levels of Chl a (> around 8 μg Chl al−1) have also been shown in studies to inhibit 
growth (Larsen et al. 2018). Based on our monthly RFU averages, it appears that the food 
availability during January-March 2021 (chlorophyll-a concentration below 1 μg L-1) may not be 
favorable for the southern mussels. Figure 18 shows that there is a clear occurrence of food depletion 
in the north in the summer of 2022, but at this point, the mussels in the north have been harvested, 
so this study may not be much affected.  
Overall, the environmental factors mentioned above generally fall within the suitable range for 
mussel growth. Only during short periods (as depicted in Figures 17 and 18, which show daily 
environmental fluctuations), hypoxia, food depletion, and stratification are unfavorable for mussel 
growth. In our study, the influence of environmental factors was manifested in the significant effect 
of dissolved oxygen on the condition index of mussels. The fact that the environmental data 
imported into the analysis data is only the specific value corresponding to the sample day may be 
the reason why other environmental factors have no impact on the mussel growth. Because the 
length of our sampling interval makes it impossible to assess the effects of changing environmental 
conditions on mussel growth over a short period, there is a significant inaccuracy. 
 
4.4 Recommendations  
4.4.1 Culture practices 
Based on the observed lower condition index and higher mortality rate of JR (June relay) compared 
to other experimental groups, it is advisable to avoid relaying mussels during the summer months 
when high temperatures prevail. Numerous elements, including salinity, environmental pollutants, 
temperature, food accessibility, gonad development, and population density, have an impact on the 
condition index (CI) of marine bivalves (Hemachandra and Thippeswamy 2008). In this study, 
relatively high condition indexes were observed during the first MRL sampling in May 2021 (4.8 
mg mm-3) and the second WSD sampling in July 2021 (5.0 mg mm-3), which could be attributed to 
favorable food conditions in the weeks leading up to sampling (Dolmer et al. 2012). According to 
the available data (the average condition index of each month was not measured), it is advisable to 
refrain from harvesting during spawning periods and periods with significant salinity fluctuations 
(Landes et al., 2015). Instead, it is recommended to harvest for seasons characterized by abundant 
food and before the breeding season, as the condition index tends to be relatively better.  
According to our findings, significant differences were found only in the growth rates of SFS 
and SFD, but not in WSS and WSD. However, it is important to note that SF itself is heavily 
influenced by sediment, which introduces some inaccuracies in the results. So overall, two densities 
(2.7 kg m-2 and 5 kg m-2) did not significantly affect the performance of mussels (SFS, SFD, WSS, 
and WSD), which is consistent with Capelle et al. (2016) findings that size and age of the mussels 
rather than density affect subtidal mussel growth. Similar to the discovery that bottom culture 
mussel growth was not density-dependent in the study by Dolmer et al. (2012). However, an 
excessive density can also limit food and interfere with the mussels' filtering action and growth 
(Saurel et al. 2013). Therefore, there was sufficient food in our study to support a high density. In 
general, if the performance remains consistent, the higher density tends to lead to higher biomass at 
harvest.  
Using suspended seeds as a seed supply can help reduce the environmental harm caused by 
dredging fisheries, and is a more sustainable and stable option (Christensen et al. 2015). Despite the 
fact that this study revealed that SF (Smartfarm) growth rates are generally low, sediments have a 
==PAGE:31==
29 
 
significant role in this. Therefore, further study is required to determine whether suspended seeds 
can serve as a seed source for bottom culture under different conditions. 
Only the growth rates of JRL and JRF showed a significant difference when fishermen's vessels 
were compared to the “Limfjorden” vessel (JRL, JRF, MRL, and MRF). The difference in growth 
between the two groups can be attributed to the smaller initial shell length of JRF, which has a higher 
growth potential, and the longer initial shell length of JRL, which has a lower growth potential and 
is negatively impacted by ascidian. Therefore, it is inaccurate to compare the growth rates of these 
two groups. As a result, there are no significant differences between MRL and MRF's mussel 
performance. The idea is to progressively switch from fishing and relaying by "Limfjorden" 
vessel to fishing by fishermen's vessels and relaying by "Limfjorden" vessel. This mode can boost 
the effectiveness of production.  
Mussel bottom culture through site selection is important for the formation of mussel beds and 
the development of sustainable aquaculture. Many factors need to be considered in site selection, 
including the number of predators, chlorophyll concentration, current velocity, and pollution (Buck 
2017). Since mussels and other macrofaunal organisms may not be suitable for survival in the 
experimental area of this study due to the quality of the seabed sediment, the condition of the 
sediments could be utilized as an indicator when choosing future study sites.  
 
4.4.2 Biosecurity plans 
In the analysis of macrofauna, there are not many threats from invasive species and predators, but 
mainly from competitors. One of the potential causes of the greater mortality rate in the JRL is 
ascidian recruitment. The key to stabilizing mussel populations and creating sustainable aquaculture 
is understanding how to mitigate these risks. Here are some recommendations on how to control 
ascidian in mussel bottom culture by referring to existing biosecurity plans (Fitzsimons et al. 2020; 
Arzul et al. 2021; Spark et al. 2018): 
Firstly, it is advisable to avoid areas known for favorable conditions for ascidian breeding or 
choose sites with lower historical recruitment of ascidians. Secondly, to minimize the risk of 
ascidian invasion through the seed source, it is recommended to refrain from using wild mussel 
seeds obtained from areas inhabited by ascidians. And before the relay, mussels can be soaked in 
low-salt seawater or a weak acetic acid solution for a short period of time to eliminate any ascidians 
present on the mussel seeds (Fitzsimons et al. 2020). Ascidians exhibit seasonal variations in their 
growth patterns. The species Ascidiella aspersa, examined in this study, typically begins to multiply 
during the summer months and ceases growth when water temperatures drop (Mackenzie 2011). 
Since relevant research institutions have studied the environmental changes in the culture area 
(Taylor et al. 2021), it is recommended that mussel farmers and relevant research institutions 
cooperate. Before summer arrives, it can take preventive action by consulting research institutions 
to gather environmental monitoring data appropriate for a significant ascidians invasion. At the same 
time, during the process of bottom culture, it is advisable for farmers to regularly monitor the 
fluctuations in the ascidian population to avert widespread outbreaks. Upon detecting signs of 
ascidian recruitment and reproduction, immediate action should be taken by employing trawls to 
capture them and prevent their further spread. Site selection, the relay process, and the adoption of 
prevention and mitigation measures are the main ways to implement biosecurity plans. These 
measures are intended to serve as a reference for mitigating the risks posed by ascidians in mussel 
bottom culture. However, it is important to acknowledge that biosecurity plans may differ across 
==PAGE:32==
30 
 
regions due to variations in local regulations, environmental factors, and ascidian species (Turon 
and Becerro 1992). 
 
5 Conclusions 
To summarize, bottom culture has an impact on species abundance, with larger mussels having a 
positive effect on species diversity, but the overall diversity indices tend to be low. Bottom culture 
also influences community composition, with the most notable differences observed in plots C1 
(control location) and WSD (Wild-Seed-Dense). Temporally, the greatest disparities in community 
composition occur before and one year after relaying mussels. The dominant species in the study 
site are brittle stars, followed by slipper limpets. 
The growth rate of mussels is primarily influenced by the relaying practices implemented 
between different plots, while environmental conditions manifest their effect through the influence 
of dissolved oxygen on the mussels' condition index. Under the conditions of this study, mussels 
that typically aggregate together demonstrate higher biomass during harvest, and mussels from the 
WSD plot exhibit better growth and higher production.
