Abstract 
Dairy industry relies heavily on Streptococcus thermophilus, a key thermophilic lactic acid bacterium. 
While genetic modification could enhance these strains, EU regulations prohibit GMO use in food 
production. This research aims to develop a laboratory-based CRISPR-MAD7 genetic toolbox for two 
industrial S. thermophilus strains that will be strictly used for research purpose and to guide natural 
methods of strain improvement. 
The project was divided into three work packages. In work package 01, S. thermophilus strains, 
STCH_02 and STCH_09, were transformed with a high-copy plasmid pSTSJ014 to create fluorescently 
labelled variants. The transformed strains were analyzed using fluorescence microscopy (FM) and a 
flow cytometry protocol was optimized by establishing appropriate mCherry gating and conducting 
comparative experiments by cultivating the S. thermophilus strains in both microtiter plates and 
culture tubes with oxygen exposure. In work package 02, three different promoters were evaluated 
and attempted to construct ten different MAD7 plasmids consisting of different antibiotic resistance 
markers and promoters. 
In the work package 01, both S. thermophilus strains were successfully transformed with pSTSJ014 as 
well as two negative control plasmids (pHEEN035 and pHEEN036). A negative control integrative 
plasmid (pD-NC) was constructed, isolated and sequence verified. However, despite implementing 
three different approaches, attempt to construct strains with different fluorescence backgrounds (i.e. 
GFP labeling) were unsuccessful. For flow cytometry analysis, strain-specific mCherry gating was 
established for each S. thermophilus strain using pHEEN035 as the baseline control. Sample viability 
and mCherry signal intensity were significantly impacted by growth condition, with notable 
differences between microtiter plates and tube cultures. These variations also affected cell survival 
rates after cell sorting. 
In the work package 02, seven out of ten designed MAD7 plasmids were successfully constructed. Two 
plasmids (pRS002 and pRS003) were transformed into cloning host Lactococcus lactis MG1363, though 
whole plasmid sequencing failed due to low concentrations. Rolling circle amplification on plasmids 
containing tetracycline resistance gene was performed, but transformation into cloning host was 
unsuccessful. Plasmids with erythromycin resistance gene and rolling circle amplified plasmids were 
attempted to transform into the targeted S. thermophilus strains. Notably, only the negative control 
plasmid carrying erythromycin resistance gene (pRS011) was successfully transformed into the S. 
thermophilus strains. 
Differences in transformation efficiency between two S. thermophilus strains were observed, with 
STCH_02 showing superior plasmid uptake compared to STCH_09. FM and FACS analysis revealed 
differences in mCherry signals between chromosomally integrated and plasmid-based expression 
systems. While integrated strains exhibited uniform mCherry signals, strains containing the high-copy 
plasmid pSTSJ014 showed non-uniform expression. This heterogeneity likely reflects random 
segregation of plasmids during cell division. The chain-forming nature of S. thermophilus complicated 
the analysis of post-sorting samples, making it difficult to conclusively evaluate sorting efficiency. 
Additionally, significant variations in cell viability between microtiter plate and tube culture conditions 
for STCH_09, indicated the need for further optimization of FACS protocols for this strain. In the work 
package 02, promoter strength significantly impacted MAD7 plasmid stability and host viability. The 
unsuccessful transformation of MAD7 plasmids into S. thermophilus suggests the need to explore 
alternative Cas enzymes to address potential toxicity issues. 
The successful development of fluorescent labeling systems in work package 01 and the insights 
gained from MAD7 plasmid construction in work package 02 provide valuable foundations for future 
methodological refinements and guidance for the future experiments.

1. 
INTRODUCTION 
Consumers seek variety in their diets and increasingly prefer natural ingredients over artificial 
additives. While S. thermophilus strains are widely used in the dairy industry, there is a growing 
demand for new strains or improvements to existing ones with enhanced characteristics [1]. However, 
the use of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) in food production is heavily regulated, particularly 
under European Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) standards [2]. As a result, only strains improved 
through natural methods can be used in applications and products [3]. Insights gained through   
CRISPR-MAD7-mediated research can guide the identification and selection of naturally occurring 
variants with desired traits, thereby avoiding GMO restrictions while advancing strain improvement 
efforts. 
Despite the availability of numerous S. thermophilus strains, developing effective genetic tools for 
these industrial strains remains a challenge due to limited knowledge of their genetic characteristics 
[4]. Traditional genetic modification approaches for S. thermophilus, such as homologous 
recombination, have proven to be time-consuming and often lack the precision required for targeted 
modifications [5]. The emergence of CRISPR technology has revolutionized genome editing capabilities 
across various organisms. Specifically, the CRISPR-MAD7 system, a novel CRISPR nuclease, offers 
several advantages including high specificity, reduced off-target effects and flexible targeting 
capabilities [6]. This system presents a promising tool for precise genetic modifications in industrial   
S. thermophilus strains. 
In this project we aim to develop an optimized CRISPR-MAD7-based genetic toolbox for two industrial 
S. thermophilus strains. The approach includes generating fluorescently labelled S. thermophilus 
strains, optimizing FACS sorting protocol, evaluating MAD7 toxicity and delivering gRNA-MAD7 
plasmid to remove fluorescent signal from the labelled strains. Following sections will delve deeper 
into the theoretical background of this research. 
 
1.1. Streptococcus thermophilus: characteristics and applications in dairy fermentation 
Streptococcus thermophilus is a Gram-positive, thermophilic lactic acid bacteria characterized by its 
spherical to ovoid morphology and chain-forming growth pattern [7]. It grows optimally at 
temperatures between 40 to 45 °C [8]. The bacterium prefers anaerobic conditions to grow, however, 
it also shows aerotolerant properties, meaning they can grow in presence of oxygen [7]. 
The metabolic capabilities of S. thermophilus is primarily homolactic fermentation, where it utilizes 
lactose as its principal carbohydrate source [9]. During this process, β-galactosidase enzyme catalyzes 
breakdown of lactose into glucose and galactose. While glucose is metabolized via Embden-Meyerhof 
pathway to produce two lactate molecules, galactose is expelled into the surrounding medium [10]. 
Widely used as a starter culture in dairy industry [11], this bacterium plays a key role in producing 
yogurt and various cheese varieties including Feta [12], Mozzarella [13] and Cheddar [14]. It belongs 
==PAGE:10==
 
2 
to bacterial family Streptococcaceae, which includes both commensal and pathogenic species [15]. 
Through centuries of growing in dairy environments, the bacterium has lost many pathogenic traits 
[16], leading to its safety approval from both the U.S. FDA (GRAS status) [17] and European Food Safety 
Authority (QPS status) [18]. 
Despite possessing various peptidases that enable growth of S. thermophilus in media with hydrolyzed 
proteins or yeast extract, it lacks the ability to hydrolyze casein, the main protein in raw milk [19]. This 
limitation requires heating the milk or adding Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus to the starter 
cultures to make yogurt [19].  These organisms work symbiotically where L. bulgaricus breaks down 
casein into short peptides, which S. thermophilus absorbs and turns into amino acids for growth [20]. 
In return, S. thermophilus produces formate and carbon dioxide, helping L. bulgaricus growth [21]. 
During fermentation rapid acidification by S. thermophilus inhibits the growth of spoilage and 
pathogenic microorganisms [22] . Additionally, the bacteria produce exopolysaccharides (EPS), which 
enhance creamy texture and mouthfeel of yogurt [23]. The synergistic action of S. thermophilus and  
L. bulgaricus enhances flavor in yogurt through production of lactic acid, aromatic compounds and 
organic acids [21] [24]. 
Furthermore, S. thermophilus exhibits probiotic properties, particularly beneficial for lactose-
intolerant individuals [25]. When consuming yogurt containing viable S. thermophilus cultures, these 
bacteria, utilize their intracellular β-galactosidases to hydrolyze lactose into digestible components, 
effectively compensating for enzymatic deficiency in the small intestine of lactose-intolerant 
individuals [26].  
 
1.2. Natural methods of strain improvement for dairy cultures 
Development of new starter cultures can be approached through two primary strategies: a) natural 
strain selection and b) strain improvement of the existing strain [3]. Natural strain selection involves 
identifying and isolating naturally occurring strains that already possess the desired properties [27]. 
Alternatively, existing strains can be improved through various strain improvement methods, such as 
dominant selection, adaptive laboratory evolution or random mutagenesis [28][29].  
Dominant selection represents a targeted approach where mutants with specific desired traits are 
directly selected through carefully structured selection plans. This method, while effective, requires 
comprehensive knowledge of microbial physiology to implement successfully [3].  
Adaptive laboratory evolution, where strains are cultivated under controlled laboratory conditions for 
extended period to gradually develop mutant strains adapting to the specific conditions [30]. Although 
this method accelerates the process of natural evolution, it may also lead to unintended mutations 
[31]. 
Random mutagenesis has emerged as a particularly popular choice in food industry [32]. This method 
involves introducing random mutations into the genome of interest and selecting strains that display 
==PAGE:11==
 
3 
desired traits. However, this approach comes with inherent risks, as unintended mutations can occur 
alongside the desired changes [33]. These unexpected genetic modifications could potentially impact 
the strain's performance in industrial settings, raising questions about long-term stability and 
consistency in commercial application. 
 
1.3. EU regulations on GMO in Food industry 
The European Union (EU) maintains one of the world's strictest regulatory frameworks for GMOs in 
food industry [34]. Under Regulation (EC) No 1829/2003 and Directive 2001/18/EC, any GMO or 
product containing GMOs must undergo rigorous safety assessment before entering the EU market 
[35]. According to EU regulations, new starter cultures intended for food production must be          
GMO-free. However, methods considered as non-GMO include Natural selection, Adaptive laboratory 
evolution or random mutagenesis [28]. The key difference lies in the technique used, direct genetic 
manipulation (GMO) versus indirect methods (non-GMO). For instance, a dairy company developing 
a new yogurt starter culture could expose their existing strains to UV light (mutagenesis) and select 
strain that shows improved acidification properties. Despite the genetic changes that occur, this strain 
would be considered as non-GMO under current regulations. However, if they were to achieve the 
same improvement by directly inserting a specific gene, the resulting strain would be classified as 
GMO and subject to extensive regulatory requirements [27]. 
 
1.4. Bridging genetic insights and traditional methods 
Aligning with EU’s regulatory framework for GMOs, the aim of this project is to develop a                
CRISPR-MAD7 genetic toolbox for two industrial S. thermophilus strains, that will be strictly aimed to 
be contained in laboratory-research settings. These strains will be served as model systems to study 
genetic mechanism and exploring the development of desirable traits. This will create a knowledge 
base that subsequently guide the traditional non-GMO strain development method. Understanding 
how certain genetic elements contribute to improved acidification rates might lead to designing 
specific growth conditions that naturally select for strains with these characteristics.  
 
1.5. CRISPR system: bacterial adaptive immune mechanism 
CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a natural adaptive immune 
system found in bacteria and archaea [36]. This system enables bacteria to defend themselves against 
repeated infections by bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) [37]. When a bacteriophage 
attacks a bacterium for the first time, the bacterial cell takes some time to recognize and degrade the 
viral genome. However, the response will be faster and more efficient on second attack, allowing the 
bacteria to quickly degrade the viral genome. This process is known as adaptive immune response 
[38]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
4 
Bacterial genome contains specific regions known as CRISPR loci, which consist of multiple short, 
palindromic DNA repeats (30–34 nucleotides in length) arranged in a clustered fashion [39]. Located 
upstream of the CRISPR locus are Cas (CRISPR-associated) genes, which encode proteins responsible 
for carrying out the immune response [40]. One of the most well-known proteins in this system is 
Cas9, a nuclease enzyme that plays a critical role in DNA cleavage [41].  
The CRISPR immune response occurs in three main stages (Figure 1): 
• 
Adaptation phase: When a bacteriophage infects bacteria, bacterial defense system got 
activated, cas1-cas2 protein (conserved within all CRISPR-Cas system) complex synthesized 
and bind to the viral genome at a specific sequence called the PAM site (Protospacer Adjacent 
Motif) [42]. PAM is a short nucleotide sequence, typically 5’-NGG-3’ (specific for Cas9 
recognition), where "N" can be any nucleotide [43]. Bacterial genome rarely contains two 
consecutive guanine (GG) sequences, whereas they are commonly found in bacteriophage 
genomes [44]. After binding to the PAM site, cas1-cas2 cuts a fragment of double-stranded 
viral DNA, upstream of the PAM site [41]. This fragment known as a spacer (when present on 
viral genome, they named as protospacer), integrated into 5’-end of the CRISPR locus in the 
bacterial genome [45]. This newly acquired spacer serves as a genetic "memory" of the viral 
attack, allowing the bacterium to recognize the virus more effectively in future infections [46].  
• 
Expression phase: After spacer acquisition, bacterial RNA polymerase transcribes the CRISPR 
array into a long RNA molecule called pre-crRNA (pre-CRISPR RNA) [47]. RNaseIII which acts 
as an endonuclease, processes this pre-crRNA by cutting it into shorter RNA fragments 
(crRNA). Each of these short RNA sequences contains one spacer (derived from the viral DNA) 
and one adjacent repeat [48]. Another RNA molecule, called tracrRNA (trans-activating CRISPR 
RNA), binds to the repeat region and forms a complex with crRNA [47]. Once the                   
crRNA-tracrRNA complex is formed, the Cas9 protein binds to it, creating a functional 
molecular defense system [47]. This complex then scans the bacterial cell for any invading 
viral DNA that matches the stored spacer sequence [47]. 
• 
Interference phase: If same bacteriophage attacks the bacteria again, stored spacer sequence 
in the crRNA recognizes and binds to the complementary protospacer region of the viral 
genome and Cas9 binds to the PAM site on viral DNA. Since spacer sequence acts as a guide, 
it is often referred to as guide RNA (gRNA) [49]. Once correctly positioned, Cas9 functions as 
an endonuclease. Using its HNH and RuvC nuclease domains, Cas9 cuts both strands of the 
DNA, 2–3 nucleotides upstream of the PAM site and creates a blunt-ended double-stranded 
breaks (DSBs) [49]. This breaks result in the degradation of the viral genome, preventing 
further infection [47]. 
 
CRISPR-Cas systems classified into two classes: Class 1, which uses multi-protein complexes (Types 
I, III, IV) and Class 2, which relies on single effector proteins (Types II, V, VI)  [50]. Type I (Cas3-
based) is the most common, using a Cascade complex and Cas3 nuclease for DNA degradation 
==PAGE:13==
 
5 
[37]. Type III (Cas10-based) can target both DNA and RNA [37]. In Class 2, Type II (Cas9-based) is 
the most widely used for genome editing, with Cas9 recognizing a specific PAM sequence for 
precise DNA cleavage [51]. Type V (Cas12-based) targets DNA, offering alternative PAM 
recognition, does not require tracrRNA for activity [37]. Type VI (Cas13-based) is RNA-targeting 
and plays a role in RNA editing and viral detection [52].

1.6. Using CRISPR-Cas9 as a genome editing tool  
Genome editing refers to the process of modifying an organism’s DNA by either inserting a new gene, 
disrupting an existing gene or making targeted modifications within the genome [53]. CRISPR-Cas9 has 
emerged as a precise and efficient tool for genome editing, allowing targeted genetic modifications 
with high specificity [54]. In CRISPR-Cas9 system, the enzyme Cas9, a Type II nuclease sourced from 
Streptococcus pyogenes (SpCas9) [55]. 
The process begins by identifying a target gene to be modified. A guide RNA (gRNA) is then synthesized 
which is complementary to the specific DNA sequence of interest. Along with the gRNA, CRISPR 
repeats are also synthesized [49]. These repeats bind to tracrRNA, forming a complex with the Cas9 
protein. For successful editing, the target gene must contain a PAM site (Protospacer Adjacent Motif), 
which Cas9 can recognizes and binds to. Once bound, gRNA guides Cas9 to the target DNA sequence, 
where Cas9 introduces a DSBs in the DNA, typically 2–3 nucleotides upstream of the PAM site [56] 
[57]. 
When a double-stranded breaks occur, the cell initiates a repair mechanism through one of two 
pathways [58]: 
• 
Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ): This is an error-prone repair pathway where the broken 
DNA ends are directly ligated back together. However, this process often results in small 
insertions or deletions (indels), which lead to gene modification [59]. NHEJ is a predominant 
pathway for DNA repair [60]. 
• 
Homology-Directed Repair (HDR): This high-fidelity repair mechanism is used when an 
exogenous repair template is provided. HDR allows for precise insertion of new genetic 
material at the target site, making it ideal for adding or replacing specific genes [59]. 
 
1.7. CRISPR-MAD7 as a genome editing tool 
MAD7 is a CRISPR-associated Cas12a (Cpf1) endonuclease also known as ErCas12a, is an engineered 
variant of the Cas12a (Cpf1) enzyme, derived from Eubacterium rectale [6]. As part of the class 2        
type V-A CRISPR family, it differs from Cas9 by recognizing a different PAM site (5’-TTN-3’) [6]. 
Originally developed and released by Inscripta, MAD7 is a royalty-free alternative to Cas9, making it 
accessible for both commercial and academic research [61]. Structurally, it shares similarities with 
Cas12a but offers unique advantages particularly in its DNA cutting mechanism [62]. Instead of 
creating blunt-ended DSBs like Cas9, MAD7 generates staggered cuts by cleaving 19 bases 
downstream of the PAM site. MAD7 utilizes a single RuvC-like endonuclease domain to cleave DNA, 
creating 4-nucleotide 5ʹ overhangs [6]. This staggered cutting pattern is useful for precise genetic 
modifications and DNA insertions (Figure 2). 
Another key advantage of MAD7 is its simpler gRNA requirements. Unlike Cas9, which requires both 
crRNA and tracrRNA, MAD7 functions with a shorter (42- or 56-nucleotide) single gRNA, eliminating 
==PAGE:15==
 
7 
the need for tracrRNA [63]. Additionally, MAD7 exhibits both DNA and RNA nuclease activity, allowing 
it to autonomously process pre-crRNA into mature crRNA further simplifying the CRISPR system [64].

1.8. Fluorescent reporter system and their application for genetic tool development 
Fluorescent reporter systems are essential in genetic research, enabling real-time visualization and 
tracking of genetic processes [65]. Fluorescence signals can be measured using various methods, such 
as flow cytometry and fluorescence microscopy [66]. These systems rely on fluorescent proteins or 
molecules that emit light when excited at specific wavelengths [67]. By integrating fluorescent 
reporters into genetic constructs, researchers can quickly assess the efficiency of genetic 
modifications or identify successful transformations [68]. These reporters provide immediate,          
non-invasive and quantitative readouts, making them particularly valuable in CRISPR-based genome 
editing [69]. 
Common fluorescent proteins include green fluorescent protein (GFP), yellow fluorescent protein 
(YFP) and red fluorescent protein such as mCherry. In our study, we primarily use mCherry, along with 
GFP and PpFbFP (a flavin-based fluorescent protein). 
• 
mCherry: Derived from Discosoma sea anemones, mCherry consists of 236 amino acids. It 
absorbs light between 540–590 nm and emits fluorescence in 550–650 nm range [70] 
• 
GFP: First isolated from the jellyfish Aequorea victoria, GFP consists of 238 amino acids. It 
absorbs blue light at 488 nm and emits green fluorescence at 510 nm [71]. Both mCherry and 
==PAGE:16==
 
8 
GFP require oxygen for chromophore formation [72]. In GFP, the chromophore is generated 
through cyclization and oxidation of the tripeptide sequence Ser-Tyr-Gly [73]. 
• 
PpFbFP: Oxygen-independent fluorescent reporter, is composed of 100-140 amino acids and 
uses flavin mononucleotide as a cofactor. It absorbs blue light at 450 nm and emits cyan-green 
fluorescence at 495 nm [74]. 
 
1.9. Flow cytometry 
Flow cytometry is a powerful analytical technique used to study heterogeneous cell populations based 
on their size, complexity and phenotype [75]. The system consists of three main components: fluidics, 
optics and electronics [76]. 
• 
Fluidics system: Sample is suspended in a sheath fluid that directs cells to pass one by one 
through a laser beam in a downward direction. The sheath fluid creates a controlled flow, 
ensuring individual cell analysis [77].  
• 
Optical system: Consists of a laser beam that generates visible and fluorescent light signals, 
which are used to analyze the sample [76]. When cells pass through the laser, they scatter 
light in two ways:  
Ø Forward scatter (FSC): Measures cell size by detecting light scattered in the same 
direction as the laser beam. Larger cells scatter more light, while smaller cells scatter 
less.  
Ø Side scatter (SSC)/ Backward scatter (BSC): Measures cell complexity or granularity by 
detecting light scattered at a 90° angle. Cells with more internal structures or granules 
scatter more light.  
Importantly, light scatter is independent of fluorescence [76] and provides structural 
information about the cells. Specialized detectors capture both FSC and SSC signals.  
• 
Electronic System: Converts signals from the detectors into digital data that can be processed 
and analyzed by a computer. 
 
In addition to analyzing cell populations, flow cytometry can rapidly sort individual cells [78]. This 
technique, known as fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), separates fluorescent (FL) cells from 
non-fluorescent (non-FL) cells based on fluorescence expression [79]. The process involves several key 
steps [76]: 
• 
Sample preparation: Cells are either genetically modified to express fluorescent proteins (e.g. 
GFP, mCherry) or stained with fluorescent dyes that bind to specific proteins or structures 
within the cell. 
• 
Cell detection: The cell suspension passes through a flow cell, where cells move one by one 
through a laser beam. If a cell contains a fluorescent marker, it emits fluorescence, which is 
==PAGE:17==
 
9 
detected along with FSC and BSC signals. Detectors then identify whether a cell is FL or          
non-FL. 
• 
Droplet formation and charging: A vibrating nozzle breaks the stream into tiny droplets, each 
containing a single cell. The system assigns charge (either positive or negative) to each droplet 
based on fluorescence, i.e. 
§ 
FL cells (fluorescent): receive a positive charge. 
§ 
Non-FL cells (non-fluorescent):  receive a negative charge. 
• 
Sorting with deflection plates: The charged droplets pass through deflection plates with 
opposite electrical charges. Positively charged FL cells are attracted to the negatively charged 
plate and vice versa. This process physically separates FL and non-FL cells into different 
collection tubes. 
• 
Collection and further analysis: The sorted cells are collected in separate tubes or microtiter 
plates for further analysis, such as culturing.

AIM 
The primary objective of this project is to develop an optimized CRISPR-MAD7 genetic toolbox for two 
industrial Streptococcus thermophilus strains. To achieve this, the project is divided into three work 
packages (Figure 3). 
In Work Package-01 (WP-01), we aim at constructing fluorescently labelled versions of two                        
S. thermophilus strains using different fluorescence marker genes (mCherry, GFP and PpFbFP). These 
markers are expressed either through plasmid-based expression or chromosomal integration. 
Fluorescence signals is confirmed using fluorescence microscopy (FM) and quantified via flow 
cytometry (FACS). Additionally, we aim at optimizing the FACS protocol for these labelled                             
S. thermophilus strains.  
In Work Package-02 (WP-02), we intend to evaluate strength of three different promoters by assessing 
their ability to express mCherry gene using FM and FACS. Based on this, we design MAD7 expression 
plasmids incorporating these three promoters along with three different antibiotic resistance markers. 
These plasmids are tested for potential toxicity in those two S. thermophilus strains. If bacteria can 
tolerate the MAD7 plasmids, Work Package 03 is initiated. 
In Work Package-03 (WP-03), we construct MAD7 plasmids using the most effective promoter from 
WP-02 and incorporate a gRNA complementary to the fluorescence gene introduced in WP-01. These 
MAD7-gRNA plasmids are transformed into the fluorescently labelled S. thermophilus strains 
constructs in WP-01. The gRNA will guide MAD7 enzyme to target and cleave fluorescence gene, 
leading to loss of fluorescence signal, which will be confirmed through FM and FACS. 
This report comprises methods and results obtained from WP-01 and WP-02 and reveals perspectives 
for initiating WP-03.

3.7. Gel electrophoresis 
PCR products were analyzed using electrophoresis on 1% agarose gels prepared in 1x TAE buffer. 
GelRed was incorporated into the gel at a 1:1000 dilution by adding 30 µl of GelRed to 30 ml of

3.10.  Gibson Assembly  
Gibson Assembly was performed to construct plasmids using two different approaches. For plasmids 
requiring two fragments of near similar sizes (pRS004, pRS005 and pRS006), fragments were combined 
in a 1:1 ratio maintaining a total DNA concentration of 0.01-0.2 pmol in assembly reaction. The 
required volume of each fragment was calculated and provided in Appendix-04 and nuclease-free 
water was added to reach a final volume of 10 µl. Subsequently, 10 µl of Gibson Assembly master mix 
was added to the reaction mixture. 
For negative construct plasmids (pD-NC and pRS010) and pRS003 a single PCR-amplified fragment was 
used to ligate. 10 µl of purified concentration (with 0.01-0.2 pmol concentration) was added with        
10 µl of Gibson Assembly master mix (Appendix-04). 
All Gibson Assembly reactions were performed using NEBuilder hiFi DNA Assembly Master Mix and 
incubated at 50 °C for 1 hour in a Bio-Rad thermocycler. The required fragment concentrations were 
calculated using NEBuilder Calculator v1.1.3.

3.11.   Rolling circle amplification (RCA)  
Rolling Circle Amplification (RCA) was performed for pRS004, pRS005, pRS006 and pRS010 plasmids 
before conducting a second transformation into cloning host L. lactis MG1363. RCA is a unidirectional 
DNA amplification technique used to generate large amounts of circular DNA from a minimal starting 
template. 
To perform RCA, first 3 µl of the Gibson assembled mix was added to 10 µl of denaturing buffer and 
heated at 95 °C for 3 minutes. The sample was then cooled to room temperature and placed on ice. 
Next, 10 µl of reaction buffer and 0.4 µl of enzyme were added to the denatured sample. The reaction 
was incubated in a Bio-Rad thermocycler at 30 °C for 18 hours, followed by 65 °C for 10 minutes to 
inactivate the polymerase, then cooled to 4°C. The amplified samples were stored at 4 °C until further 
use. 
 
3.12.  Nanodrop spectrophotometry 
To determine concentration and purity of extracted plasmid and PCR amplicon after gel extraction, 
Nanodrop spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific) was used. A sample volume of 1 µl was loaded into 
the pedestal after taking blank with 1 µl of MiliQ water. Absorbance was measured at 260 nm (A260) 
to determine DNA concentration and at 280 nm (A280) to assess protein contamination. The purity of 
the samples was evaluated using both the A260/A280 ratio and the A260/A230 ratio.

4.1.3.   Generation of strains with diverse fluorescence background  
To create STCH_02 and STCH_09 strains with varying fluorescence signal backgrounds, three 
expression (pNSJ-based) and three integrative plasmids (pD-based) were selected for transformation 
(Table 2). First, negative construct (pNSJ-NC and pD-NC) of both plasmids (expression and integrative) 
were attempted to generate using in-house primers. 
4.1.3.1. pNSJ-NC: 
The goal was to construct a plasmid with pNSJ backbone without fluorescence marker gene by 
amplifying two linear fragments (1.5 kb and 2.5 kb) from pNSJ014 plasmid. These fragments had 
overlapping ends and were to be ligated using Gibson Assembly to form a complete 3980 bp pNSJ-NC 
plasmid. Two in-house primer pairs were used (Appendix-06): 
• 
primer pair-01: PAUS143_pNSJ-NC and SPR012 (for the amplification of 1.5 kb fragment) 
• 
primer pair-02: SPR013 and SPR018 (for the amplification of 2.5 kb fragment) 
With Q5 polymerase, 2.5 kb fragment was successfully amplified (Figure 18-a), where 1.5 kb fragment 
amplification was failed from template plasmid. Then previously amplified and isolated 1.5 kb linear 
fragment (without the overlapping sequence) used as template DNA and gradient PCR was performed

DISCUSSION 
5.1. WP-01 
5.1.1. Genetic variability and transformation efficiency  
Despite belonging to the same species, STCH_02 and STCH_09 strains exhibited significantly different 
transformation efficiencies under identical experimental conditions. STCH_02 demonstrated higher 
transformation efficiency in the uptake of pSTSJ014 and pHEEN035 plasmids compared to STCH_09 
strain (Table 7). 
5.1.2. Growth characteristics and expression analysis 
In presence of erythromycin in LM17 broth, STCH_02 containing pSTSJ014, pHEEN035 and pHEEN036 
plasmids exhibited robust growth. In contrast, STCH_09 strains harboring those plasmids showed 
slower growth rates. As expected, samples containing pHEEN035 and pHEEN036 plasmids showed no 
fluorescence due to absence of either mCherry gene and its promoter (pHEEN035) or the promoter 
only (pHEEN036). In contrast, strains harboring plasmid with mCherry and its promoter (pSTSJ014) 
showed fluorescence under the same exposure time (Figure 9). 
5.1.3. Plasmid distribution pattern 
Despite pSTSJ014 being a high-copy plasmid, mCherry signal was not uniformly observed across all 
cells in the culture (Figure 9). While some exhibited strong mCherry fluorescence, others did not. As 
high-copy plasmids are primarily segregated by random diffusion during cell division, leading to 
unequal plasmid distribution between daughter cells [80], which could explain the observed 
phenomenon of unequal mCherry expression.  Since S. thermophilus tends to grow in chains, dead or 
non-expressing cells could remain attached to fluorescent ones, making it difficult to distinguish 
between active and inactive cells under FM. 
5.1.4. FACS analysis 
5.1.4.1. Establishing gates for FL and non-FL cells 
A key objective of optimizing the FACS experiment and sorting these two strains was to precisely 
quantify fluorescence signals and effectively distinguish FL and non-FL cells from the fluorescently 
labelled strain developed in WP-01 . Here, a baseline fluorescence distribution for both plasmid-based 
and chromosomally integrated strains was defined. To ensure accurate mCherry gating, strains 
containing pHEEN035 plasmid were used as baseline control. This plasmid contained only ermR gene 
and does not have any promoter or mCherry gene. Using this plasmid as a control allowed for the 
distinction between background fluorescence and true mCherry expression (Figure 12). The 
established gates will facilitate future work on testing the effectiveness of CRIPSR-MAD7 system for 
targeting fluorescent proteins (WP-03). Specifically, by analyzing fluorescence levels before and after 
MAD7 activity, any observed decrease in fluorescence or increased proportion of non-FL cells, would 
indicate the effectiveness of the developed system. 
==PAGE:53==
 
45 
5.1.4.2. Cell viability 
Growth rates were significantly impacted with MTPs conditions for STCH_09-pSTSJ014 both before 
and after sorting (Figure 10-a). In MTPs, viability of the 0 h sample for STCH_09-pSTSJ014 strain was 
lower compared to STCH_02 containing the same plasmid. Additionally, the 3 h sample showed 
significantly decreased in growth, which also affected the subsequent sorting process. When 100 cells 
were sorted into each well as FL and non-FL populations, no growth was observed after overnight 
incubation (Figure 10-a). It likely indicates that MTPs conditions and oxygen exposure were not 
optimal for STCH_09-pSTSJ014 strain’s growth after the sort.  
Despite belonging to the same species, STCH_02 and STCH_09 strains exhibited significant differences 
in their characteristics which reflected in their ability to grow with oxygen exposure and surviving the 
sorting process in microtiter plates. 
5.1.4.3. FSC vs BSC plot: cell size and complexity 
Since the mCherry-expresssing variants of both STCH_02 and STCH_09 strains were successfully 
obtained (Figure 8), two distinct mCherry gates for two strains were established. To observe of the 
cells aggregation in the FSC vs. BSC plots and to detect any changes in cell shape due to the plasmid 
uptake, WT cells along with cells carrying the control plasmids pHEEN035 and pHEEN036 were 
screened on FACS.  
For STCH_02 strain, WT, pHEEN035- and pHEEN036-carrying strains clustered in a similar region in the 
FSC vs BSC plot (Appendix-01). However, a noticeable difference was observed between STCH_02-
integrated and STCH_02-pSTSJ014 strains. The integrated strains displayed a well-defined oval shape, 
with most cells concentrated in the upper-right corner of the plot (red area in Figure 13-a), indicating 
uniformity in cell size and complexity. This outcome was expected because mCherry and ermR genes 
were chromosomally integrated in this strain, which meant that each daughter cell carried these genes 
during cell division. Some cells were dispersed in the bottom-left corner (grey area in Figure 13-a). 
Cells in grey area indicates, cells smaller in size and complexity. Since this sample belonged to the 0 h 
time point, it likely indicates that the cells had reached their stagnant growth phase. This observation 
aligned with the fact that, at this stage, a significant number of cells were either dead or in a 
deteriorated state, leading to reduced size and complexity. 
For STCH_02-pSTSJ014 strain, a greater number of cells were found in the bottom-left corner (grey 
area, Figure 13-b), with a more widespread distribution compared to STCH_02-integrated strain. This 
is likely due to plasmid copy number in the individual cells. Plasmid pSTSJ014 is a high copy 
number plasmid. However, it distributed diversly in cells, which influenced their size and complexity. 
The broad distribution (creating two area red and grey in the FSC vs BSC plot) at 0 h, suggests a 
heterogeneous population in the sample where some cells retained plasmids, while others either lost 
them due to metabolic burden [81] or were dead. Despite these variations, STCH_02 strains 
maintained viable growth in both TCs and MTPs, indicating its ability to manage the metabolic 
demands of the plasmid maintenance. 
==PAGE:54==
 
46 
As opposed to STCH-02 strain, STCH_09 strain displayed a significant difference in cellular 
characteristics following plasmid uptake. While the wild-type STCH_09 cells initially showed an oval-
shaped distribution from bottom-left to top-right corner in the FSC vs BSC plots (similar to wild-type 
STCH_02), the introduction of plasmids (pSTSJ014, pHEEN035, and pHEEN036) caused a severe shift 
in cell morphology (Figure 14). All plasmid-containing STCH_09 strains tightly clustered in the bottom-
left corner, indicating a uniform reduction in both cell size and complexity. This morphological 
response can be correlated directly with the strains poor growth performance. The strain showed 
decreased viability at 0 h, significant growth reduction by 3 h and complete growth failure in microtiter 
plates after sorting (Figure 10). 
These findings suggest that STCH_09 strain is more sensitive to the metabolic stress caused by 
plasmids uptake [81], which affects both its cellular characteristics and growth. This also suggests that 
STCH_02 and STCH_09 handle the metabolic demands of plasmid maintenance differently. 
5.1.4.4. mCherry expression: integrated vs. plasmid-containing strains 
STCH_02-integrated strains maintained a consistently high percentage of fluorescent cells on FACS 
across all conditions, including post-sorting (Figure 15). Under FM, nearly all cells were detected as 
fluorescence cells from this population (Figure 16-a).  
In contrast, pSTSJ014 plasmid containing strains (both STCH_02 and STCH_09) showed a very low 
percentage of fluorescent cells during FACS analysis with their established gating parameters (Figure 
15). Under FM, only a small fraction of fluorescence cells was detected in the FOV (Figure 16-b). 
Notably, these few cells displayed intense fluorescence, suggesting that cells carrying multiple copies 
of the high copy pSTSJ014 plasmid resulted in stronger fluorescence. However, majority of cells in the 
FOV showed no fluorescence. 
FM analysis alone could not distinguish whether the non-FL cells carried the plasmids but were not 
expressing mCherry gene at the time of analysis or if they were plasmid-free cells or dead cells that 
remained attached in chains. To further investigate this, sorting was performed to separate FL and 
non-FL cells. 
5.1.4.5. Sorting strategy and post-sorting survival 
FL cells were incubated in media containing erythromycin to maintain plasmid selection. Non-FL cells 
were incubated with and without erythromycin to assess either they contain the plasmid or plasmid 
free or dead cells. If non-FL cells retained the plasmid but lacked expression, they would survive in Ery 
media and might later express mCherry. Cells without plasmids would not grow in Ery media, but chain 
forming nature in S. thermophilus might allow plasmid-containing cells to protect plasmid-free cells, 
leading to colony formation. Dead non-FL cells attached to viable chains would fail to form colonies. 
For the STCH_02-integrated strain, FL and non-FL populations were sorted and incubated under the 
same conditions. Since this strain carries chromosomally integrated mCherry and ermR genes, viable 
non-FL cells without these genes would not grow in Ery media but might form colonies in antibiotic-
==PAGE:55==
 
47 
free conditions. If non-FL cells exhibited fluorescence after incubation, sorting may not have 
effectively distinguished FL from non-FL. 
For STCH_02-integrated strain, post-sorting FL and non-FL cells exhibited robust growth in both MTPs 
and TCs (Figure 10). Percentage of fluorescent cells detected in FL population remained consistent for 
both conditions (Figure 15). In contrast, in non-FL population (in Ery media), a very small percentage 
of fluorescence cells were detected (0.54%) in MTPs, while it was 51.86% in TCs. As 100 cells were 
sorted in MTPs, in contrast to 1000 cells in TCs and as S. thermophilus tends to grow in chain, during 
sorting FL cells might inadvertently fall into the non-FL channel, which under antibiotic pressure grew 
overnight and displayed fluorescence (Appendix-02-c and d).  
For STCH_02-pSTSJ014 strain after sorting FL population exhibited robust growth in antibiotic 
containing media in both MTPs and TCs (Figure 10-a). However, non-FL cells showed reduced culture 
density in MTPs compared to TCs (in Ery media) and completely lost viability in MTPs but showed 
growth in TCs in media without Ery. Under FM (Figure 17-c), very few fluorescent cells were detected 
in FL population, with no fluorescence in non-FL population for MTPs. In contrast, both FL and non-FL 
population in TCs exhibited fluorescence under FM (Appendix-02-e and f). This data suggests that 
sorting 1000 cells might not be efficient for STCH_02 strains. However, in MTPs the growth of non-FL 
cells in media containing antibiotics indicates these cells might still carry plasmids but fail to express 
the fluorescence gene during sorting and under antibiotic pressure they survive and grow. 
Additionally, the complete loss of viability of non-FL cells in MTPs without Ery suggests that, in the 
absence of antibiotic pressure, non-FL cells lost their plasmids and were unable to grow, which further 
supports this hypothesis. 
For STCH_09-pSTSJ014 strain, growth on MTPs was very low (Figure 10-a) and FM analysis only 
performed from TCs samples. This strain did not survive the sorting process and due to time 
constraints, experiment was not repeated to check if they could survive sorting in TCs. 
In conclusion, sorting is efficient in microtiter plate for STCH_02 strain (both integrated and plasmid), 
but not optimal for STCH_09 strain. This suggest that a further analysis with FACS should be conducted 
in tubes with 100 cells sorting, this would confirm efficient sorting of cells into FL and non-FL 
population and grown in tubes with 1 ml media will provide optimal growth condition by allowing cells 
to recover after sorting process.   
5.1.5. Construction and amplification challenges of pNSJ-NC plasmid 
To construct pNSJ-NC plasmid, two linear fragments were attempted to amplify, each containing 
overlapping sequences, allowing them to be ligated into a single plasmid using Gibson Assembly. The 
plasmid was split into two fragments, to prevent potential folding if amplified as a single large linear 
fragment, which could hinder proper assembly (Paula Szymczak, personal communication). However, 
after three attempts, the 1.5 kb fragment failed to amplify successfully (Figure 18-a). This was likely 
due to the primer overhang sequences binding nonspecifically to other regions, preventing the primer 
to bind properly and inhibiting polymerase activity.

5.1.6. Transformation challenges with integrative and expression plasmids 
Despite two transformation attempts with electroporation into STCH_02 and STCH_09, 
transformations were unsuccessful for both integrative (pD-BB014, pD-BB017 and pD-BB024) and 
expression plasmids (pNSJ014, pNSJ017 and pNSJ024). However, multiple colonies appeared in the 
positive control plate (pSTSJ014), confirming that the experiment condition was optimal and the 
electrocompetent STCH_02 and STCH_09 strains were efficient. 
For pNSJ-based plasmids, chloramphenicol resistance gene may not have been effectively expressed 
due to an unoptimized promoter, preventing cell survival under antibiotic selection (Paula Szymczak, 
personal communication). Additionally, 10 µg/ml chloramphenicol was used in LM17 agar plate for 
selective colony growth, which may have been too high, as later from the MIC test during WP-02, 
determined chloramphenicol MIC value for S. thermophilus to be 5 µg/ml (Appendix-07). Since 
transformations were conducted early in the project, due to time limitation further optimization and 
repetition could not be possible. 
Additionally, S. thermophilus strains are known to commonly possess restriction-modification (RM) 
systems, which can recognize and degrade foreign DNA, potentially affecting the efficiency of 
transformation [82]. In this study, RM system sequence analysis was performed for both strains (Paula 
Szymczak, personal communication). It revealed the presence of RM systems that could target the   
pD-based plasmids at multiple sites. Although this was an in-silico analysis and may not guarantee 
complete accuracy, these finding suggests that both strains possess RM systems capable of degrading 
the pD-based plasmids upon its introduction into the cells.  
STCH_02-integrated strain used in this study was obtained by natural transformation (Paula Szymczak, 
personal communication). Thus, STCH_02 was used for natural transformation with four linear 
fragments from four integrative vectors (Figure 20). Despite two attempts, natural transformation was 
unsuccessful. 
 
5.2. WP-02 
5.2.1. Testing promoter strength 
Promoter strength analysis revealed that pHEEN003 promoter exhibited lowest activity among the 
tested promoters, as evidenced by the absence of a detectable signal under FM and a very low 
percentage (3.79%) of fluorescent cells detected in FACS (Figure 21). This suggests that pHEEN003 
promoter has minimal transcriptional activity and was used in the pRS003 plasmid to express MAD7 
gene. 
In contrast, pHEEN018 promoter exhibited fluorescence in a larger proportion of cells (51.04% 
detected in FACS), though fluorescence intensity per cell is relatively low (Figure 21). This suggests 
that pHEEN018 promoter facilitates broader transcriptional activity, but lower expression levels. 
pRS002 plasmid contained pHEEN018 promoter for MAD7 expression. 
==PAGE:57==
 
49 
Meanwhile, pHEEN027 promoter resulted fluorescence in a smaller fraction of cells (6.52% detected 
in FACS), but those cells exhibited a much higher signal intensity (Figure 21). This indicates that while 
pHEEN027 promoter is active in fewer cells, it leads to stronger expression per cell. pRS001 plasmid 
included pHEEN027 promoter for MAD7 expression. 
MAD7 plasmid library was constructed using three different promoters to evaluate the potential 
toxicity of MAD7 expression in S. thermophilus. If all three plasmids are successfully transformed and 
the cells exhibited normal growth, it would indicate that MAD7 expression is not toxic, regardless of 
expression level. However, if even the weakest promoter (pHEEN003) leads to noticeable negative 
effects, it suggests that MAD7 is toxic for the target strains, even at low expression levels. 
5.2.2. Transformation of pRS002, pRS003 plasmids into cloning host 
The transformation in L. lactis MG1363 results showed that pRS001 plasmid (containing pHEEN027 
promoter, used as PC) yielded highest number of colonies (356), while pRS002 (with pHEEN018 
promoter) produced the lowest (77 colonies) and pRS003 (containing pHEEN003 promoter) showed 
intermediate efficiency (135 colonies) (Appendix-03-c) and colonies were confirmed by colony PCR.    
Following plasmid extraction of pRS002 and pRS003, concentrations were extremely low (30.6 ng/µl 
and 48.1 ng/µl, respectively) despite multiple attempts. Nanopore sequencing of the whole plasmid 
failed due to the poor concentration. A PCR check of the extracted plasmid confirmed that the 
fragments were correctly assembled via Gibson Assembly, resulting in a full plasmid (Figure 23-a). In 
contrast, the control plasmid pRS011, which lacks both a promoter and MAD7, yielded concentration 
of 438.1 ng/μl. All three plasmids (pRS001, pRS002, and pRS003) contain the pRS011 backbone. 
Additionally, pRS001 maintained relatively high plasmid yields of 148.3 ng/μl. 
This pattern aligns with the distinct expression characteristics of each promoter. The pHEEN018 
promoter in pRS002, showing broad but low-level expression across many cells (51.04% in FACS), 
resulted in poorest transformation efficiency and purified plasmid concentration. This suggests that 
consistent low-level MAD7 expression across a large cell population might become toxic for the cell 
to survive thus affecting subsequent colony formation after transformation.  
In contrast, minimal promoter activity of pHEEN003 (3.79% cells in FACS) in pRS003 plasmid likely 
enables better transformation and purified plasmid concentration than pRS002 by reducing the 
toxicity effect and allowing for better tolerance of MAD7 expression, thus leading to a higher colony 
count compared to pRS002 (Appendix-03-c). 
Interestingly, pRS001, containing the pHEEN027 promoter which shows high expression intensity but 
in a limited cell population (6.52% in FACS), exhibited the best transformation efficiency and the 
purified concentration was higher compared to pRS002 and pRS003. During transformation period, 
having fewer cells express MAD7 strongly might be less detrimental to cellular survival than having 
many cells express it at lower levels.

5.2.3. Testing of cloning hosts for MAD7 plasmid with tetracycline resistance gene 
Since we were unsure of the ideal cloning host for the MAD7 plasmids with tetracycline resistance 
gene, we tested both E. coli DH5α and L. lactis MG1363.  For E. coli DH5α, two colonies appeared on 
plates transformed with pRS010 plasmid (Appendix-03-d) and no colony appeared on other plates.  
These two colonies were confirmed as false positive by colony PCR. 
For L. lactis MG1363, after two days of incubation multiple colonies appeared on all the plates 
including NC plate (Appendix-03-e). Despite colonies in NC plate, colonies from the plates transformed 
with pRS004, pRS005, pRS006 and pRS010 were checked with colony PCR, resulted no expecting band 
on agarose gel, suggesting that L. lactis MG1363 became resistant to tetracycline at 1 µg/ml.  
When these plasmids were transformed into L. lactis MG1363 after rolling circle amplification, the 
outcome was same as previous with L. lactis MG1363 became resistance after two days of incubation. 
Despite applying RCA which resulted increase in plasmid concentration, transformation efficiency did 
not improve for L. lactis MG1363.  
These results suggest that neither E. coli DH5α nor L. lactis MG1363 was an optimal cloning host for 
these plasmids. Due to time constraints, further testing with other cloning hosts (such as Bacillus) was 
not possible. 
5.2.4. Transformation challenges in S. thermophilus strains 
Transformation with pRS001, pRS002 and pRS003 was unsuccessful in S. thermophilus. However, both 
S. thermophilus strains were successfully transformed with pRS011 plasmid (negative construct) 
(Figure 23-b), indicating that the backbone was suitable for transformation, but S. thermophilus could 
not tolerate MAD7 expression under any of the tested promoters. 
For pRS004, pRS005, pRS006 and pRS010, false positive colonies appeared on the plates after two 
days incubation, suggesting that the tetracycline concentration needed to be increased for effective 
selection in Gram-positive strains.

CONCLUSION 
The aim of this project was to develop an optimized CRISPR-MAD7 genetic toolbox for two 
fluorescently labelled S. thermophilus strains (STCH-02 and STCH_09). The project was systematically 
divided into three work packages. 
Work Package 01 focused on constructing fluorescently labelled industrial strains (STCH_02 and 
STCH_09). This effort was partially successful, with one high-copy mCherry-expressing plasmid 
(pSTSJ014). However, attempts to label both strains with different fluorescent genes using various 
approaches and plasmids were unsuccessful. To compare mCherry signal intensity, an in-house strain 
with chromosomally integrated mCherry and ermR genes was analyzed alongside the high-copy 
plasmid strains using FACS and FM. A reliable FACS protocol was established, demonstrating that the 
strains could grow with oxygen exposure while maintaining fluorescent signals. Additionally, cell 
sorting was feasible under these conditions, but instead of microtiter plates, the strains needed to be 
cultured in tubes with at least 1 ml of media. Moreover, sorting efficiency improved when reducing 
the number of sorted cells from 1,000 to 100  ensuring better recovery. 
Work Package 02 involved the design and construction of MAD7 plasmids. While pRS002 and pRS003 
were successfully transformed into the cloning host L. lactis MG1363, their extraction yields were low. 
Other plasmids (pRS004, pRS005, pRS006, and pRS010) faced transformation issues, with bacterial    
(L. lactis MG1363) resistance leading to multiple colonies, including on negative control plates. Rolling 
circle amplification did not enhance transformation efficiency. Due to time constraints, plasmids from 
both low-concentration extractions and rolling circle amplification were transformed into                           
S. thermophilus strains. However, pRS004, pRS005, pRS006 and pRS010 exhibited the same 
transformation challenges as observed in the cloning host L. lactis MG1363, while pRS001, pRS002, 
and pRS003 failed to produce colonies. Interestingly, pRS011 successfully formed colonies in both 
strains, suggesting that while the plasmid backbone was suitable for transformation, the presence of 
MAD7 gene was not tolerated by S. thermophilus.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVE 
As this project marks the beginning of a larger study, several potential directions can be explored to 
further advance the research: 
Future work should focus on designing alternative plasmids to successfully label S. thermophilus 
strains with different fluorescent markers, particularly GFP, which was initially targeted. Achieving 
both mCherry and GFP labelled strains would enable the establishment of a two-channel system on 
FACS, allowing for co-culturing and evaluating the FACS ability to accurately distinguish and separate 
different fluorescently labelled cells. 
During FACS experiments, it was observed that microtiter plates were not optimal for STCH_09 strain 
survival. A follow-up experiment should be conducted where all strains culture in tubes before and 
after sorting. Additionally, reducing the number of sorted cells from 1,000 to 100 could help assess 
their recovery capacity after sorting.  
As MAD7 plasmids with tetracycline resistance gene failed to produce colonies and bacterial 
resistance developed within two days, a MIC test should be performed. This test should evaluate tet 
concentrations at 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, and 2.5 µg/ml to determine the appropriate MIC value of tetracycline 
for Gram-positive strains. Additionally other cloning host should be tested for these plasmids. 
The MAD7 plasmids with chloramphenicol resistance gene were not successfully constructed due to 
amplification failure of one fragment from pCEWO_tet template. Future efforts should focus on 
troubleshooting this amplification step to enable the construction and testing of MAD7 plasmids with 
chloramphenicol resistance gene. 
To better understand the toxicity effect of MAD7 on S. thermophilus strains, a transcriptomic approach 
can be conducted. Additionally, alternative Cas enzymes, such Cas12, could be tested on STCH_02 and 
STCH_09. If an alternative Cas enzyme is less toxic while maintaining efficient editing capabilities, it 
could serve as a better candidate for genetic modifications in S. thermophilus strains. 
Due to time limitations and the challenges faced in Work Package 02, where MAD7 transformation 
into S. thermophilus strains were unsuccessful, Work Package 03 could not be initiated as planned. 
Further optimization is required before proceeding with the next phase of the project.
