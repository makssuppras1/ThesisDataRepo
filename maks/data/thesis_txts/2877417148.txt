Abstract
Green hydrogen produced by grid-connected electrolysis is a key enabler of deep decarbonization, yet its
cost remains sensitive to both electricity-market operation and electrolyser durability. In particular, variable
renewable energy coupling and participation in ancillary service (AS) markets can introduce frequent load changes
and cycling that accelerate degradation, while most techno-economic optimization models neglect stressor-specific
lifetime impacts. This thesis develops a Power-to-Hydrogen (PtH) co-optimization framework that explicitly
integrates a control-oriented electrolyser degradation representation into a high-resolution scheduling model to
improve economic assessment and operational decision-making.
An Excel-driven workflow is implemented in Python and formulated as a mixed-integer linear program (MILP)
solved with OR-Tools. Degradation is incorporated via linear exposure counters that decompose voltage-rise
contributions into steady-operation and cycling-related terms, with additional cycling exposure linked to AS
provision through service-specific activation-frequency parameters. The resulting degradation cost is monetized
as lifetime consumption relative to an end-of-life (EOL) voltage threshold and added as a linear depreciation
proxy in the objective.
Results show that degradation-aware optimization materially changes both sizing and operation. In the base
case, including degradation reduces the optimal electrolyser size from 74.64 MW to 69.45 MW while maintaining
annual hydrogen production at ≈175.4 GWh. Cycling is nearly halved (3,534 to 1,861 equivalent cycles), and
total AS participation decreases from 63% to 35% of capacity, indicating a shift toward smoother dispatch and
more selective reserve provision. Despite reduced AS allocation, the levelized cost of hydrogen decreases from
4.10 to 4.00 EUR/kg, alongside lower fluctuation-induced voltage degradation and a higher estimated lifetime
(19.3 to 22.9 years). A sensitivity study on lifetime criteria demonstrates that the assumed EOL threshold is a
dominant driver: tightening the threshold from 0.72 V to 0.2 V raises LCOH to 4.94 EUR/kg and further reduces
AS participation to 15%. Overall, the framework highlights that explicit degradation integration can significantly
alter optimal flexibility utilization and techno-economic conclusions, and that transparent, well-justified EOL
assumptions are critical for credible PtH valuation.
Keywords: Power-to-Hydrogen (PtH), Power-to-X (PtX), PEM water electrolysis, electrolyser degradation,
degradation-aware optimization, ancillary services (FCR, aFRR, mFRR), levelized cost of hydrogen (LCOH).

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
The transition to a clean energy landscape critically relies on low-emission green hydrogen, primarily generated
through water electrolysis powered by variable renewable energy (VRE) sources. Electrolysis technologies, such as
Proton Exchange Membrane Water Electrolysers (PEMWEs) and Alkaline Electrolysis (AEL), offer rapid response
capabilities that are essential for successful coupling with intermittent VRE and for providing crucial ancillary
services (AS) to the power grid. While this synergy is vital, the cost of green hydrogen remains significantly higher
than that produced via natural gas reformation, necessitating advances in both durability and efficiency.
A major barrier to achieving cost-effective green hydrogen is the inevitable degradation of electrolyser components
[1]. Degradation leads to decreased stack efficiency and increased operational expenditure (OPEX) over time.
Operational stressors inherent to VRE coupling, such as fluctuating power input (fatigue) and particularly frequent
on/off cycling, are known to accelerate this performance decline [2]. For instance, in PEMWEs, degradation is
accelerated by load cycling and results in kinetic losses from catalyst dissolution and migration, alongside increases
in ohmic resistance due to membrane thinning or porous transport layer (PTL) passivation. Because maximizing
profitability often involves optimizing VRE consumption and the revenue stream from ancillary services, modern
Power-to-X (PtX) facilities must utilize sophisticated optimization models to minimize costs and determine optimal
investment sizing.
However, traditional energy management system (EMS) models often struggle to fully account for the complex,
long-term impacts of dynamic operation on component lifetime. While specialized research has shown that deploying
multi-stack control strategies based on degradation models can extend plant lifetimes significantly [2], these control-
oriented degradation models need robust integration into broader economic optimization frameworks. The lack
of explicit integration between degradation models which quantify the penalties associated with steady operation,
fluctuating loads, and cycling, and economic optimization models that maximize ancillary service inclusion, limits
the accuracy of techno-economic projections. Therefore, this thesis is motivated by the critical need to develop a
refined Power-to-Hydrogen (PtH) optimization model that rigorously integrates validated degradation modeling.
This comprehensive approach will facilitate the design of sophisticated control strategies that balance the economic
gains from providing ancillary services against the corresponding costs associated with degradation-induced efficiency
losses and asset replacement (CAPEX amortization), ultimately aiming to maximize the profit of the PtH plant.
1.2
Problem statement
Existing techno-economic optimization frameworks utilized for PtH systems typically rely on Mixed-Integer Linear
Programming (MILP) to minimize Equivalent Annual Operating Costs (EAOC) by managing energy balance and
maximizing revenue from AS participation. Crucially, these high-level EMS models often treat degradation either
implicitly (e.g., through a fixed lifetime estimate) or neglect the dynamic, stressor-specific damage accumulation
entirely. This systemic decoupling leads to optimization solutions that prioritize short-term revenue maximization
from dynamic AS provision without accurately penalizing the concomitant long-term costs associated with accelerated
degradation (stack replacement capital amortization and efficiency losses). Therefore, a critical methodological gap
exists in developing a robust PtH optimization model that explicitly and dynamically embeds a control-oriented
degradation model to truly inform operational scheduling and techno-economic decision-making over the asset’s
lifetime.
1.3
Research objective
The primary objective is to advance the state-of-the-art in PtX system optimization by developing and validating
a comprehensive computational framework that seamlessly incorporates dynamic, stressor-specific electrolyser
degradation modeling to minimize the total equivalent annual operating cost.

Specific objectives include:
• Modeling and Integration: To formulate a MILP model that integrates a control-oriented degradation function
(quantifying penalties from steady operation, fluctuating power, and on/off cycling) within the constraints of
VRE input and ancillary service dispatch.
• Trade-off Quantification: To utilize the integrated model to quantitatively analyze the economic trade-off
between maximizing instantaneous ancillary service revenue and mitigating the projected long-term costs
imposed by accelerated degradation (e.g., reduced efficiency, early replacement).
1.4
Research question
The central research question guiding this thesis is:
How does the explicit integration of a control-oriented degradation model into a Power-to-Hydrogen
optimization framework quantitatively alter optimal system sizing and economic performance when
participating in grid ancillary service markets?
Supporting research questions include:
1. How can the electrochemical degradation effects caused by specific operating stressors (steady operation,
fluctuating power, and on/off cycling) be accurately parameterized and integrated as dynamic constraints or
cost functions within a time-series MILP model used for optimal AS dispatch?
2. How does degradation-aware optimization change cycling exposure and the allocation of electrolyser capacity
across different reserve products?
3. Under the investigated market conditions, how does AS participation affect hydrogen production economics
relative to a no-AS strategy, and how is this interaction modified when degradation is modeled explicitly?
1.5
Thesis outline
The remainder of the thesis is structured as follows. Section 3 presents the system model, optimization formulation,
degradation representation, and data inputs, and defines the investigated scenarios. Section 4 reports quantitative
results for investment sizing, operational indicators, ancillary service participation, hydrogen production cost,
and degradation-related metrics. Section 5 interprets the results in relation to the research question, discusses
implications and limitations, compares the findings with relevant literature, and outlines research extensions.

Literature review
2.1
Ancillary services in the electricity system
Ancillary services are reserve products procured by the transmission system operator to maintain secure system
operation by correcting short-term imbalances between electricity supply and demand. In this thesis, the relevant
products are Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR), automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR), and
manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR), which differ primarily in purpose and activation mechanism.
FCR is used for frequency stabilization and is characterized by a fast response requirement (30 seconds) with
delivery that is triggered directly by frequency deviations, meaning the provider measures grid frequency locally
and responds to frequency drops or rises [3]. In contrast, aFRR is used for frequency restoration and is activated
through an automatic control signal from Energinet, communicated continuously via the control system, with a full
response time on the order of minutes (5 minutes) [3]. mFRR is used for balancing and is activated on demand
through a signal from Energinet, with a slower response requirement compared to aFRR (12.5 minutes) [3].
These products are relevant for electrolysers because consumption can be adjusted rapidly within technical limits,
allowing the plant to contribute reserve capacity by increasing or decreasing load when activated. In the market
framing used here, FCR is procured as a symmetric product, while aFRR and mFRR are procured as asymmetric
products with separate upward and downward directions, which affects both feasible operation and the implied
cycling exposure when participating in these services [3].
2.2
Electrolysis in VRE-coupled systems and the durability challenge
Renewable hydrogen production via water electrolysis is positioned as a key pathway for decarbonization in policy
and research roadmaps, which identify cost, efficiency, and lifetime as primary barriers to large-scale deployment
[4]. Within this context, PEM water electrolysers (PEMWEs) are frequently highlighted for applications requiring
flexible operation because their fast dynamic response enables load-following and potential provision of grid services
[4]. At the same time, experimental studies show that intermittent operation and frequent operating-point changes
can affect performance evolution, and that voltage changes observed during testing may include both reversible and
irreversible contributions depending on operating history and diagnostic procedures [5].
2.3
Degradation metrics and stressor-specific evidence
PEMWE degradation is commonly quantified as an increase in cell voltage over time at fixed current density (or,
equivalently, a reduction in current at fixed voltage), because voltage rise maps directly to efficiency loss and provides
a practical basis for end-of-life (EOL) definitions in techno-economic assessment [6]. DOE technical targets similarly
frame EOL in terms of an allowable performance loss defined through a voltage increase from beginning-of-life
operation at the same current density [7].
A key nuance is that cell-voltage rise does not necessarily represent purely irreversible degradation. Suermann et al.
argue that a substantial share of measured voltage increase can be recoverable depending on operating conditions
and diagnostic procedures, motivating caution when directly translating voltage rise into long-horizon lifetime loss
[6]. Differences between steady operation and cycling protocols have also been reported, with performance evolution
depending on the applied sequence of operating states and the presence of repeated transients [8].
Evidence from long-duration operation and accelerated stress tests (ASTs) indicates that distinct operating stressors
can contribute differently to voltage increase, motivating stressor-specific representations that separate steady-
operation effects from cycling- and transient-induced contributions [8]. In particular, cycling-based protocols (e.g.,
square-wave and triangle-wave profiles) have been shown to accelerate performance loss relative to steady operation
under comparable test durations [9]. Recent AST work further indicates that degradation rates depend not only
on the amplitude of fluctuations but also on their temporal structure (e.g., step time), implying that “dynamic
operation” is not a single stressor and should be parameterized accordingly [10].

2.4
Modelling rationale: control-oriented decomposition of degradation
System-level optimization models typically require reduced-order degradation representations to preserve tractability
while still capturing stressor dependence. Tully et al. propose a control-oriented degradation model that separates
degradation contributions from steady operation, fluctuating operation, and start/stop cycling to support controller
design and comparative lifetime assessment in multi-stack systems [2]. The present work adopts an analogous
decomposition by separating a steady-operation component based on utilization from a cycling-related component
based on power variability. The use of an explicit cycling-related term is motivated by experimental findings
showing larger losses under cycling protocols than under steady holds [9]. The need to distinguish operating regimes
is further reinforced by evidence that intermittent operation with idle periods can lead to distinct performance
evolution compared with operation that avoids open-circuit exposure [5].
2.5
End-of-life criterion and voltage threshold selection
A central modelling choice in lifetime estimation is the definition of end-of-life (EOL), i.e., the allowable degradation
margin before stack replacement. EOL criteria are not fully harmonized across PEMWE studies and vary by
application, protocol, and performance metric, motivating transparent reporting of the adopted threshold and
sensitivity to alternative definitions [6].
In a control-oriented framing, Tully et al. define stack replacement at 80% of the undegraded efficiency at rated
power and report that this corresponds to an EOL voltage-increase threshold of 0.72 V in their model [2]. In a
targets-based framing, DOE PEM electrolysis targets define end-of-life as a 10% voltage increase from beginning-of-
life operation at the same current density and state that degradation-rate targets are consistent with this EOL
definition [7]. At a representative nominal condition of 1.9 V/cell, a 10% increase corresponds to ∆V ≈0.19 V,
which is commonly rounded to 0.2 V for sensitivity-style comparisons [7]. Accordingly, this thesis reports results
under both ∆VEOL = 0.72 V and ∆VEOL = 0.2 V to illustrate how lifetime criteria influence optimal operation and
economics.
2.6
Parameterization of steady-state and cycling-related degradation
Siracusano et al. report a 1000 h steady-state durability test at 1 A cm−2 in which the post-conditioning voltage-rise
rate is approximately 5 µV/h for an optimized MEA configuration [11]. This observation motivates using a small
but non-zero steady-operation term to represent degradation accumulation under controlled steady conditions.
Based on this evidence, the present work sets the base voltage-rise rate to rbase = 5.00 × 10−6 V/h (5 µV/h). In the
optimization, the steady-operation term is applied using an equivalent full-load hours (EFLH) proxy, so that each
simulated hour contributes proportionally to the operating load fraction rather than being counted as one full hour
at rated power.
Dynamic operation is represented through a cycling-related voltage increment derived from cycling durability
evidence. Alia et al. report an overpotential increase of 142 mV (evaluated at 2 A cm−2) after 31.5k square-wave
cycles between 1.45 and 2.0 V [9]. The cycling-related voltage increment is therefore computed as

Methodology
3.1
System description
This thesis develops an Excel-driven optimization workflow for a PtH system with optional participation in AS markets.
The model is implemented in Python and solved as a mixed-integer linear program (MILP) using the OR-Tools
linear solver interface. Input data are stored in a macro-enabled Excel workbook (PtX_optimizer_Lin_Version),
which serves as both (i) the primary source of parameters and time series and (ii) the destination for model outputs
and key performance indicators (KPIs). The Python script reads the relevant sheets via xlwings, constructs and
solves the optimization model, and exports the results back to dedicated result sheets and a KPI log.

3.2
Base optimization model
This section describes the underlying optimization model used to co-optimize electrolyser investment sizing, hourly
operation, hydrogen supply and storage, and participation in AS markets. The model is formulated as a linear
optimization problem with continuous decision variables, implemented in Python using OR-Tools and solved with
the CBC solver backend.
The Excel workbook and the Python optimization script used in this thesis were originally provided by Energinet,
who collaborated in the project and supported the development and application of the modelling workflow. The
model structure presented in this thesis builds on that provided framework, with adaptations and extensions
implemented by the author to match the research objective and case-study scope (including data handling, scenario
configuration, and the integration of the degradation representation described in Section 3.3). The resulting workflow
should therefore be understood as a collaborative baseline provided by Energinet and further developed within the
context of this thesis.
3.2.1
Model formulation
The model is solved on an hourly time grid for a full year. Let t ∈T index hours, u ∈U index the modeled units
(in this thesis, only the electrolyser is active), and s ∈S index the ancillary service products activated in the input
data. These sets are constructed directly from the Excel inputs: T from the length of the time series table, U from
the unit columns in AvgUnit, and S from the active entries in the Ancillary services sheet.
The optimization includes investment, dispatch, reserve allocation, and system-level operational decisions. The
main decision variables are:
• Investment for each unit u: installed capacity Ku (MW), bounded by minimum and maximum capacity
limits from AvgUnit.
• Dispatch for each unit u and hour t: net power pu,t (MW) and hydrogen output qu,t (MWhH2/h).
• AS allocations for each unit u, hour t, and service s: reserve capacity ru,t,s (MW), bounded by unit-specific
participation limits and availability.

The objective minimizes total annualized cost by combining (i) hourly electricity-related costs, (ii) unit-specific
variable costs, (iii) net revenues from AS provision and optional hydrogen sales, and (iv) annualized investment
costs for installed capacities and infrastructure (storage and grid connection). In the base model, the objective
aggregates:
• Energy costs: electricity consumption valued at the hourly electricity price (and additional terms such as
heat-price coupling where applicable in the input).
• AS revenues: reserve capacity allocations multiplied by service-specific hourly reserve prices.
• Hydrogen sales and tariffs: optional hydrogen sales revenue (when market price is non-zero) and net-
work/truck tariff terms as defined in the Storage and TimeSeries sheets.
• Annualized CAPEX: linear investment costs for electrolyser capacity and other infrastructure (hydrogen
storage, battery, grid connection), using annualized cost coefficients provided in the Excel inputs.

When degradation is enabled, additional degradation-related cost terms are added to the objective; these are treated

Operational feasibility is enforced through unit-level constraints and system-level balance constraints. For each unit
u and hour t, dispatch and reserve provision must satisfy:
• Capacity and availability: dispatch is limited by installed capacity and hourly availability.
In the
implementation, the investment constraint links pu,t to Ku (with availability factors from the time series).
• Minimum load (electrolyser): the electrolyser must respect a minimum load fraction relative to Ku when
active, enforced as a linear inequality using the unit MinLoad parameter.
• Ramp-rate limits: hour-to-hour changes in power are bounded by ramp-up and ramp-down coefficients from
AvgUnit, scaled by capacity and availability.
• Power-to-hydrogen conversion: hydrogen production is modelled linearly as qu,t = αu pu,t, where αu
corresponds to the unit conversion coefficient (stored as aPQ in AvgUnit) and is consistent with the model sign
convention.
• AS provision bounds: reserve allocations ru,t,s are bounded by (i) unit capacity, (ii) an AS-specific
participation fraction, and (iii) unit availability.
For producing units, reserve provision reduces the headroom to maximum output and must remain compatible
with the scheduled dispatch level. For consuming units (electrolyser), reserve provision is constrained such that
activation in the up/down direction remains feasible relative to the consumption baseline and installed capacity.
System-level feasibility is enforced through electricity and hydrogen balances, storage dynamics, and connection
limits:
• Electricity balance: the net electricity balance combines unit dispatch, curtailment, grid import, and (if
enabled) battery charge/discharge to ensure an hourly balance equation.
• Hydrogen balance: hourly hydrogen demand is satisfied by electrolyser production and hydrogen storage
operation; if a hydrogen market price is present, additional hydrogen can be sold, and the balance is extended
accordingly.
• Storage dynamics: hydrogen storage state-of-charge follows an intertemporal balance equation with charg-
ing/discharging, subject to investment-based capacity limits and charge/discharge power limits. Similar
constraints exist for battery storage when enabled.
• Grid connection limits: import/export (including the effect of reserve activation in up/down directions) is
constrained by an invested grid connection capacity.
Finally, AS procurement is bounded by the available market volume. For each AS product s and hour t, total
procured reserve capacity across all resources is constrained not to exceed the available market size from the time
series input. This is implemented separately for up- and down-regulation directions via service-specific direction
parameters.
3.2.2
Implementation
The optimization model is implemented in Python using OR-Tools (pywraplp) and solved with the CBC backend.
Although the current configuration uses continuous decision variables, the CBC mixed-integer interface is retained
to remain compatible with potential future extensions that introduce integer decisions.
At runtime, the Python script opens the Excel workbook via xlwings and imports the required inputs into pandas
data structures. Specifically, it reads AvgUnit (unit parameters), TimeSeries (hourly prices and volumes), Storage
(infrastructure bounds and annualized costs), Ancillary services (active services and participation fractions),
8

Giovanni Aldegheri
1st of January 2026
Main (run configuration such as maximum solver time), and Degradation (toggle and coefficients). The time, unit,
and AS sets are constructed directly from these tables.
Model construction follows a modular structure. First, the unit block (_buildZoneUnits) creates investment
and hourly dispatch variables for each unit, as well as reserve allocation variables and the associated operational
constraints (capacity limits, minimum load, ramping, conversion, and AS feasibility). Second, the system balance
block (_buildEnergyBalance) introduces storage variables and intertemporal constraints, enforces electricity and
hydrogen balances, and adds connection limits and AS market-size constraints. Finally, the objective is assembled
in (_build_objective) by aggregating hourly cost and revenue components and annualized investment costs into
a single minimization objective; if degradation is enabled, the corresponding degradation-related cost terms are
appended as described in Section 3.3.
A solver time limit is read from the Main sheet and passed to OR-Tools before optimization. After solving, results
are exported as a full hourly dataframe containing dispatch, hydrogen production, AS allocations, storage states and
flows, import/curtailment, and cost/revenue terms. Outputs are written back to the workbook in either Results or
Results NO Degr depending on whether degradation is enabled, and selected KPIs are appended to a KPI log
sheet with timestamp and metadata.
3.3
Degradation Model Integration
This section describes how electrolyser degradation is represented and integrated into the optimization model.
The objective is not to reproduce a detailed electrochemical degradation model, but to introduce a control-
oriented, stressor-specific formulation that distinguishes steady operation from cycling-induced wear while remaining
tractable for co-optimization of dispatch and AS provision. Experimental studies report that dynamic operation
and intermittent profiles can accelerate performance loss relative to steady operation, motivating an explicit
representation of cycling-related exposure, as illustrated in Section 2.3.
3.3.1

Degradation in PEM water electrolysis is commonly observed as an increase in cell voltage over time, which

energy throughput (equivalent full-load hours), and a cycling metric based on power variability. These metrics
are used to compute degradation-related depreciation cost terms in the objective function (Section 3.3.2), while
efficiency feedback from degradation is not modelled explicitly (discussed as a limitation).

3.3.3
Activation-frequency estimation for ancillary services
To approximate additional cycling exposure caused by AS activation, the model uses service-specific activation-
frequency parameters (termed activation frequency or ramping factor in the implementation). These parameters
represent the expected number of full activations per unit time for each reserve product and are applied as multipliers
that translate reserved capacity into additional cycling exposure in the cycling counter.
Activation frequencies for aFRR and mFRR are derived from Energinet open datasets hosted on Energi Data
Service. Specifically, aFRR activation volumes are obtained from the AfrrEnergyActivation dataset and combined
with imbalance-market information using the ImbalancePrice dataset [13], [14]. The raw time series are processed
in an Excel preprocessing sheet. For each service, a threshold-based counting procedure is applied: given a chosen
MW threshold, the number of time steps in which the activated energy volume is sufficiently large to activate the
reserved capacity is counted. The resulting annual activation count is then converted into an activation frequency
(cycles per hour) used by the optimization model.
For aFRR and mFRR, activation is assumed to be binary at the chosen threshold (0% or 100%), meaning that when
the activated volume exceeds the threshold, the reserved capacity is treated as fully activated. This assumption
is adopted to remain consistent with the linear representation used for degradation costs and to avoid modelling
partial activation trajectories. In the base case, thresholds are set to 80 MW for aFRR and 100 MW for mFRR
Up, slightly below the yearly average activation levels for the respective services. This choice is motivated by the
assumption that the electrolyser bids near the middle of the merit order and is therefore activated when system
activation is moderately high.
FCR activation is treated differently from aFRR and mFRR because it is continuously linked to frequency deviations
from nominal frequency rather than being activated through discrete call-offs. Accordingly, instead of deriving a
threshold-based activation frequency from activated-energy volumes, a fixed activation frequency is assumed for
FCR. In this thesis, FCR is represented by an assumed activation frequency of 500 activations per week.
Using the threshold counting procedure described above, the following activation frequencies are used in the base
case: 100 activations per week for aFRR Up, 250 activations per week for aFRR Down, and 40 activations per week
for mFRR Up, while FCR is set to 500 activations per week. These values are converted to the model units (cycles
per hour) and provided as inputs in the Degradation sheet, where they scale the contribution of reserve allocations
to the cycling counter.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation
This thesis is motivated by the methodological gap between techno-economic PtH/PtX optimization models and
degradation-aware control models, and asks how explicit degradation integration quantitatively alters optimal
sizing and economic performance when maximizing participation in grid ancillary services. The results address this
question by comparing solutions with and without degradation modelling and by isolating the contribution of AS
participation under the same modelling framework.
Including degradation reduces the optimal electrolyser capacity from 74.64 MW to 69.45 MW while keeping annual
hydrogen production essentially unchanged (Table 3). This indicates that the degradation-aware formulation changes
the preferred investment decision toward a smaller unit operated at higher utilization rather than maintaining
a larger installed capacity. This shift is reflected in the capacity factor, which increases from 49% to 52% when
degradation is included (Table 4).
The degradation-aware solution also changes operational behaviour by substantially reducing cycling. Total cycles
decrease from 3,534 to 1,861, and average cycles per hour drop from 0.40 to 0.21 (Table 4). The representative
operating window (Fig. 1) illustrates this effect qualitatively, with fewer and less frequent hour-to-hour load changes
in the Degradation-On case, consistent with the lower cycle metrics.
Explicit degradation modelling further alters how flexibility is allocated across reserve products.
Total AS
participation decreases from 63% to 35% of electrolyser capacity (Table 5). The reduction is concentrated in
FCR, aFRR Down, and mFRR Up, whereas aFRR Up remains comparable across both cases, indicating that the
degradation-aware optimization preserves a subset of reserve participation while reducing exposure in services that
are more costly in terms of cycling or activation. The annual moving-average profiles (Fig. 2) and the stacked
snapshot (Fig. 3) show that this is not a short-lived effect but a persistent change in allocation over the year.
Economically, the base case LCOH decreases from 4.10 to 4.00 EUR/kg when degradation is included in the
optimization (Fig. 4). This improvement coincides with the smaller selected electrolyser size and the smoother
operational pattern observed in the degradation-aware configuration (Tables 3 and 4). At the same time, the
scenario comparison (Fig. 5) indicates that AS participation remains a major contributor to overall economics under
the investigated conditions: removing AS increases LCOH to 4.52–4.53 EUR/kg, despite slightly higher capacity
factors and lower degradation exposure. This highlights a central trade-off in the setup considered here: AS revenues
can materially improve hydrogen economics, but internalizing degradation leads the optimizer to pursue those
revenues more selectively.
Finally, the assumed EOL criterion strongly affects the magnitude of degradation-related economic effects. Under
the stricter EOL threshold (0.2 V), the estimated lifetime decreases from 22.9 to 6.9 years and LCOH increases
from 4.00 to 4.94 EUR/kg (Fig. 6 and Table 7). In parallel, total AS participation decreases from 35% to 15%
(Table 8), and the yearly profile (Fig. 7) shows reduced reserve provision across the year. These results indicate that
tighter lifetime definitions amplify the role of degradation-aware scheduling, both by increasing the effective cost of
cycling and by shifting the optimal reserve strategy toward lower exposure.
5.2
Implications
The results have several implications for degradation-aware operation of PtH systems and for the use of optimization
models as decision-support tools.
The observed capacity reduction (Table 3) suggests that modelling degradation explicitly can shift the cost-optimal
investment decision toward smaller electrolyser sizing, while maintaining hydrogen output through increased
utilization (Table 4). This supports the thesis motivation that implicit lifetime assumptions can yield different
investment conclusions than models that penalize stressor-specific degradation dynamically, even when the overall

The analysis also indicates that AS participation remains economically attractive in the investigated setup, but
degradation-aware operation changes how that value is captured. Figure 5 shows that AS revenues materially
reduce LCOH relative to the no-AS cases. At the same time, explicit degradation modelling reduces overall reserve
participation and changes the service mix (Table 5 and Figs. 2–3), implying that degradation-aware strategies
may require selective participation across products rather than uniform reserve provision. Practically, this points
toward prioritizing services that deliver revenue with lower cycling exposure or activation burden under the model

The sensitivity results further highlight that EOL assumptions should be treated as a key techno-economic driver.
The large changes in lifetime, annualized degradation cost, and LCOH under the stricter EOL threshold (Table 7)
show that lifetime modelling choices significantly affect the valuation of flexibility and the economic penalty assigned
to cycling. Accordingly, EOL criteria should be carefully researched and justified and, where possible, aligned with
relevant technology assumptions and the broader lifetime usage context of the intended application.
5.3
Comparison to Literature
The findings of this thesis support the view that durability becomes a binding constraint when PEM water
electrolysers are operated flexibly in VRE-coupled and market-driven settings. In the presented case studies,
internalizing degradation shifts the optimal solution toward smoother dispatch and a more selective allocation of
flexibility across reserve products, which provides a system-level counterpart to durability insights typically reported
at cell or stack level.
A recurring theme in PEMWE durability research is that performance trajectories depend not only on total
operating time but also on the temporal structure of operation. Weiß et al. show that intermittent operation with
idle periods can lead to distinct performance evolution compared to operation that avoids open-circuit exposure
[5]. In this thesis, the degradation-aware optimization substantially reduces cycling exposure (Table 4), indicating
that explicitly pricing lifetime consumption can discourage frequent operating-point transitions that are plausibly
associated with increased stress under intermittent regimes.
Cycling experiments further motivate penalizing power variability in system-level scheduling models. Jung et
al. report that ramping characteristics under triangular cycling influence durability outcomes, supporting the
interpretation that not only the amplitude of load variation but also its temporal structure can affect degradation
rates [15]. This aligns with the observed change in operating behaviour in this thesis, where degradation-aware
scheduling reduces effective cycles and yields a smoother power profile (Fig. 1), consistent with the expectation that
limiting transients can reduce cycling-related wear.
Voltage-rise metrics are widely used to quantify degradation in PEMWE, but they can mix reversible and irreversible
contributions. Suermann et al. argue that a major share of measured voltage increase can be recoverable depending
on operating conditions and diagnostic procedures, motivating caution when translating voltage rise directly into
long-horizon lifetime loss [6]. Blair et al. similarly highlight reversible loss contributions in PEMWE performance
assessments, reinforcing that voltage-based durability indicators require careful interpretation when used for lifetime
modelling and economic projections [16]. This perspective is relevant here because the degradation model uses
voltage-rise metrics to estimate lifetime and depreciation cost; consequently, the sensitivity of lifetime and LCOH to
the chosen EOL threshold (Fig. 6 and Table 7) should be interpreted in the context of how voltage-rise components
are defined and measured.
The strong dependence of lifetime estimates on the EOL voltage threshold observed in this thesis reflects a broader
methodological challenge: EOL criteria vary across studies and are not fully harmonized. DOE PEM electrolysis
targets define end-of-life as a fixed percentage voltage increase from beginning-of-life operation at the same current
density, providing a clear targets-based framing for a stricter threshold in comparative analysis [7]. Broader

complicate lifetime comparisons and can produce materially different EOL interpretations for otherwise similar

From a system and market perspective, the value of ancillary-service participation for electrolysers depends on
market design, activation intensity, and operational constraints. Johnsen et al. quantify that ancillary services can
contribute meaningful value to electrolyser economics under certain assumptions, while emphasizing the dependence
on operational context and market conditions [18]. The present thesis adds to this literature by demonstrating that
when degradation is priced within the optimization, reserve provision becomes more selective and the service mix
changes (Table 5 and Fig. 2), suggesting that degradation-aware operation may favour targeted participation rather
than uniformly maximizing reserve capacity.
A key contribution of this project to the literature is the quantitative link it establishes between experimentally
motivated degradation stressors and system-level techno-economic decisions when operating across multiple markets.
The framework integrates a control-oriented degradation representation into a scheduling and sizing model that
explicitly co-optimizes hydrogen production and ancillary service provision, allowing the trade-off between reserve
revenues and degradation related depreciation to be evaluated within one consistent formulation. The results further
show that lifetime and economic conclusions are highly sensitive to the chosen EOL criterion, underscoring the
importance of transparent EOL assumptions when interpreting degradation-aware operational strategies.
5.4
Limitations
The conclusions drawn from the results should be interpreted in light of modelling scope, parameter uncertainty, and
data representativeness. The framework is designed to quantify comparative effects under consistent assumptions
rather than to produce universally transferable absolute values.
The degradation representation is intentionally simplified. It aggregates steady-state and cycling-related mechanisms
and incorporates activation-frequency effects associated with AS participation (Table 2). This yields a tractable
optimization model and a transparent linkage between dispatch decisions, cycling exposure, and lifetime-related
costs. At the same time, the approach compresses multiple electrochemical and materials degradation pathways into
simplified penalty terms, and the chosen coefficients reflect a specific parameterization. Because reported degradation
behaviour varies with technology, operating regime, and test protocol, alternative parameter choices would change
the relative weight assigned to steady operation versus cycling. Consequently, the reported quantitative differences
(e.g., optimal sizing, cycling reduction, and LCOH changes) should be interpreted as conditional on the selected
parameter set and modelling assumptions.
The reported cycling indicators depend on the cycle definition adopted in the Methods section. Different empirical
definitions and non-linear interpretations of what constitutes a damaging “cycle” (e.g., threshold-based load changes,
inclusion or exclusion of start–stop events, or equivalent-cycle formulations) can change absolute cycle counts
and thus the implied cycling-related exposure. The most robust interpretation of the cycling results is therefore
comparative across scenarios evaluated under a consistent cycle definition, rather than as an absolute estimate of
physical cycle accumulation.
The scenario scope also limits external validity. The analysis focuses on a small set of configurations (with/without
degradation; with/without AS; two EOL thresholds), which isolates key mechanisms but does not cover the full
range of possible market conditions, activation patterns, plant configurations, or technology variants. The results are
based on hourly time series for 2024 and are specific to the assumed PEM electrolyser characteristics. In addition,
no sensitivity analysis is performed for the selected degradation parameters, so the conclusions should be interpreted
within the context of the defined environment and parameter set.
A further limitation is that the model does not explicitly represent efficiency degradation feedback as voltage rise

accumulates. In practice, voltage degradation increases specific electricity consumption (kWh/kg H2) and can reduce
hydrogen output for a given electricity input, affecting LCOH through both higher operating costs and reduced
production. Over a one-year horizon and the high SOH values observed here, the near-term impact of this feedback
is expected to be limited, but it can become material when operation approaches end-of-life. Efficiency feedback
is omitted to preserve linearity in the optimization and because the magnitude is expected to be small over the
simulated period. Nevertheless, excluding this mechanism may lead to conservative estimates of long-run degradation
impacts and may understate the value of degradation-aware operation under stricter lifetime assumptions.
Finally, the analysis relies on hourly time series and therefore cannot fully capture intra-hour activation dynamics
that contribute to cycling and wear, particularly for fast services. Outcomes may differ under alternative years
with different price levels, volatility, and reserve conditions. Extending the analysis to multiple years, longer
horizons (including full-lifecycle simulations), and stochastic evaluation (e.g., Monte Carlo resampling of price and
activation patterns) would improve the robustness of conclusions regarding optimal sizing, reserve allocation, and
cost performance under uncertainty.
5.5
Future Research Extensions
Building on the limitations identified in Section 5.4, several research extensions could improve robustness, external
validity, and physical representativeness while retaining the usefulness of the framework for decision support.
A natural extension is to refine and calibrate the degradation representation beyond aggregated penalty terms. This
can include operating-state dependencies (e.g., load-dependent degradation rates), a clearer separation between
reversible and irreversible contributions where supported by data, and calibration to a documented technology
baseline and operating regime. Such refinements would reduce sensitivity to parameter choice and strengthen
comparability across studies and applications.
Complementing model refinement, structured parameter uncertainty analysis would improve the interpretability of
economic conclusions. A practical approach is to vary key degradation parameters (steady-state and cycling terms,
as well as activation-frequency assumptions) within plausible ranges and report the stability of outputs such as
optimal capacity, cycling exposure, AS allocation, and LCOH. This would provide uncertainty bounds and identify
which assumptions most strongly drive economic outcomes.
Introducing an explicit link between degradation accumulation and efficiency (or specific electricity consumption)
would enable performance feedback on hydrogen production and operating costs. While direct inclusion may
introduce non-linearities, approximate treatments could be explored, such as piecewise-linear efficiency curves,
iterative re-optimization across multi-year horizons, or a two-stage workflow where dispatch is optimized first and
efficiency impacts are evaluated ex post. Extending the simulation horizon toward end-of-life would allow the
economic role of efficiency losses to be quantified more directly.
External validity can be strengthened by expanding scenario scope beyond the base configuration. This includes
evaluating additional price years, alternative reserve-product assumptions, and other electrolyser technologies or
parameter sets beyond the assumed PEM configuration. Such studies would help distinguish results that are
structural (driven by the optimization–degradation coupling) from results that are context-specific (driven by a
particular year or market regime).
Improving time-series representativeness and temporal resolution is also important. Multi-year datasets and
stochastic evaluation methods, such as Monte Carlo resampling with block structures that preserve seasonality,
would better capture uncertainty in market conditions and activation patterns. Where feasible, moving from hourly
to sub-hourly resolution would improve representation of reserve activation dynamics, particularly for fast services,
and allow a tighter link between activation and cycling exposure.
Model realism could further be improved by incorporating additional market and operational constraints that
influence practical feasibility. Examples include bidding constraints, product-specific availability requirements,

Conclusion
This thesis answers the research question by showing that explicitly integrating a control-oriented electrolyser
degradation model into a Power-to-Hydrogen optimization framework materially changes both optimal system sizing
and economic performance under ancillary service participation. When degradation is internalized, the optimum
shifts away from strategies that maximize short-term flexibility revenues at the expense of lifetime consumption
and toward operating policies that trade reserve provision against wear. In the investigated case, this manifests
as a smaller optimal electrolyser investment operated at higher utilization, a smoother dispatch trajectory with
markedly lower cycling exposure, and a corresponding change in the mix and extent of ancillary service provision,
with net consequences for hydrogen production cost.
Taken together, the results indicate that degradation-aware scheduling alters the economic logic of flexibility. The
degradation-on solution maintains essentially the same annual hydrogen output while selecting a smaller installed
capacity and operating it more steadily, reducing cycling intensity relative to the degradation-off case. This
operational smoothing is accompanied by a more selective reserve strategy: total ancillary service participation is
reduced and reallocated across products, indicating that not all reserve revenues are equally attractive once their
implied cycling burden is priced. In parallel, the scenario comparisons demonstrate that ancillary service revenues
remain a meaningful driver of hydrogen economics in the examined market setting, but that their value depends on
whether degradation costs are accounted for in the dispatch decision. The sensitivity analysis further shows that
lifetime and cost conclusions depend strongly on the end-of-life voltage criterion; a stricter EOL threshold amplifies
the economic penalty of cycling and leads to more conservative reserve participation and higher hydrogen cost.
The original contribution of this work is the development and demonstration of a tractable degradation-integrated
PtH co-optimization framework that links experimentally motivated degradation stressors to system-level investment
and dispatch decisions under multi-market operation. The formulation preserves linearity by representing degradation
through cumulative exposure counters for steady operation and cycling, and it embeds these terms directly in the
objective via an annualized depreciation proxy tied to an explicit end-of-life criterion. Beyond the formulation itself,
the implementation provides a transparent Excel–Python workflow that supports reproducible scenario definition,
consistent parameter management, and automated export of time-resolved results and KPIs, enabling systematic
comparison of degradation-aware and degradation-agnostic strategies within the same modelling environment.
The validity of the conclusions is bounded by the modelling scope and parameterization choices adopted in this
thesis. The degradation model is intentionally reduced-order and aggregates multiple physical mechanisms into
steady-operation and cycling-related proxies; the quantitative outcomes therefore depend on the selected coefficients
and the assumed activation-frequency mapping for ancillary services. The model operates at hourly resolution over
a single representative year and does not explicitly include a feedback from accumulated degradation to electrolyser
efficiency; as a result, the reported economics reflect degradation primarily through a depreciation proxy rather
than through evolving specific electricity consumption. These design choices preserve tractability and enable clear
attribution of effects across scenarios, but they limit direct transfer of absolute values to different technologies,
operating regimes, or market designs without re-parameterization and validation.
Within these boundaries, the implications are twofold. For theory and modelling practice, the results demonstrate
that treating degradation as an endogenous component of the optimization problem can change both capacity
decisions and operational policies, and that lifetime definitions should be treated as a first-order modelling choice
rather than a secondary assumption. For practical deployment and operational planning, the findings suggest that
profitability from ancillary services should be evaluated jointly with degradation costs, since degradation-aware
strategies may favor selective participation and smoother dispatch rather than uniform reserve maximization.
For future research, the framework provides a foundation for incorporating refined and calibrated degradation
representations, introducing explicit efficiency feedback through tractable approximations, extending analyses to
