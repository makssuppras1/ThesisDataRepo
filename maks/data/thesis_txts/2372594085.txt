Abstract
The need of transporting items to hard to reach places, usually brings high prices, un-
safe operations and inconvenience. This could be jobs like transporting equipment to
the top of a high tower, for example an antenna tower, or doing repair work of live
power bearing, high voltage transmission cables, just to mention a few. The work in
this thesis breaks ground in trying to innovate, on how to solve problems like these in
a more efficient way, and maybe even allow for a few of the jobs, to be extended with
some degree of autonomy in the future.
These jobs in general could be solved in a large number of ways, but this thesis will focus
on using collaborating Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) to solve the jobs, making for
a scalable team of relatively small UAVs, that can be tailored to fit each task. The idea
is to model the highly nonlinear dynamics of multiple quadcopter, carrying a rigid-body
payload in suspension cables, while trying to control the load position and attitude. The
control does not care of the quadcopter positions, with the exception of a few restrictions
on their position and attitude. Using this model, a number of controllers are synthesized,
capable of controlling the load attitude and position, in the presence of model errors and
wind disturbances. In order to plan the path from point A to B, a trajectory generator
is created. The generator puts together piece-wise polynomial paths, going through a
number of predefined keyframes, constrained by the physical properties of the system.
The result is simulated tests, successfully combining state estimator, controllers and a
trajectory generator, to solve two real life scenarios, with what is considered realistic
wind disturbances of up to 12 m/s. A disturbance of 12 m/s, applied for 0.2 seconds,
causes a slight load position swing of ± 10 cm for approximately 2 seconds, before fully
rejected by the controllers.

Resume
Behovet for at transportere objekter til steder som er svære at komme til, betyder som
regel høje priser, usikre arbejdsforhold og ulejlighed. Dette kunne være jobs som at
transportere udstyr til toppen af et højt tårn, som for eksempel et antennetårn, eller at
lave reperationsarbejde på strømbærende højspændingskabler, for bare at nævne nogle
få. Arbejdet i denne opgave ligger grund for, at nytænke hvordan man griber opgaver
som disse an på en mere effektiv måde, og måske endda gøre det muligt en dag, at
automatisere nogle af dem.
Denne type jobs kan generelt blive løst på rigtig mange forskellige måder, men denne
opgave fokuserer på at anvende samarbejdende droner til at løse problemet, hvilket giver
mulighed for et hold af relativt små droner, som kan skaleres og tilpasses den enkelte
opgave. Ideen er at modellere den stærkt ulineære dynamik, der findes i flere droner som
bærer en nedhængt last i suspensionskabler og så anvende denne model til at designe
controllere, til at styre lastens position og orientering, uden hensyntagen til dronernes
position. Der er dog nogle begrænsninger på dronernes position i forhold til lasten. Con-
trollerne der er blevet designet, skal kunne styre lastens position og orientering, selv
med modelleringsfejl og vindforstyrelser, der påvirker lasten. For at kunne planlægge en
kurve fra punkt A til B, er der lavet en kurvegenerator. Denne generator sammensætter
en stykkevis polynomiekurve, som går gennem en række prædefinerede nøglepunkter,
begrænset af de fysiske egenskaber i systemet.
Resultatet af dette er en stribe simulerede tests, som successfuldt kombinerer state
estimering, controllere og en kurve generator, for at løse to scenarier fra virkeligheden
omkring os, med realistiske vindforstyrelse på op mod 12 m/s. En vindforstyrelse på 12
m/s, som rammer lasten i 0.2 sekund, foresager en lille svingning i lastens position på
± 10 cm i omkring 2 sekunder, før den er helt afvist af controllerne.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In this chapter the motivation behind the project will be presented, as well as the
problem statement and a description of the actual project. The goals of the project will
be described and a short outline of the thesis will be presented.
1.1
Motivation
When the requirement for moving objects or parts to a hard to reach location arises, it is
either very time consuming or extremely expensive in the way it is done today. Usually
a job like this would be solved using either cranes, which normally comes in two ver-
sions; a stationary version which is cumbersome to assemble and only available on that
single location, or a mobile version which is limited in reach. Another way of solving
the problem today could be using a helicopter, but this is again expensive, not widely
available and extremely risky to operate in challenging environments. A proposed way
of addressing a problem like this, could be to use multirotor unmanned aerial vehicles
(UAVs) because they are cheap, mechanically simple, rapidly deployed and safer to op-
erate in challenging environments, due to their agility and stability. A multirotor offers
the same flight characteristics as a helicopter in terms of vertical takeoff and landing,
and stationary and lateral flight in a wide range of speeds, but with a much simpler
mechanical structure. They do however come with a somewhat limited lifting capac-
ity, as well as a limit to how far it makes sense to scale the drone in order to increase
payload capacity. This is where collaborative UAVs could give the capabilities a notch
up, as the payload weight of multiple UAVs collaborating to lift a single payload would
theoretically scale very well up to a certain point.
In the last couple of years, multirotors of all sorts are being put to the test in pretty
much every field conceivable. This is because the technology has reached a point, where
it is mature enough to actually perform a piece of work with the required stability.
While this is a good thing, this also means that a lot of rules and regulations has been
created by governments in the last couple of years, including in Denmark [13]. The
new regulations for unmanned aerial vehicles in Denmark means, that it is expensive to
obtain the rights to pilot an unmanned drone larger than 7 kilos. This restriction is one
of the arguments for the research conducted in this thesis, as this means that it will not
be allowed to scale the UAV in order to increase the maximum takeoff weight, without
getting a license. It is however still allowed to lift heavier objects using multiple UAVs,
as the rules currently applies to each drone on its own.

2
1 Introduction
1.2
Problem Statement
Controlling a system of multiple quadcopters with a single suspended payload, with the
goal of transporting the load to a given location is a tough problem. This problem is in-
vestigated with basis in two simple scenarios from the world around us. The investigated
scenarios are,
• Lifting equipment to the top of large towers, for example antennas to the top of
an antenna tower.
• Inspection and repair work of live airborne high voltage transmission cables.
both of which are conducted by helicopters with specially trained personnel today, en-
dangering the people working in these environments, while being costly.
This thesis proposes a way to approach the control issues of handling scenarios like these
in general using UAVs, with the possibility of extending the work with autonomy.
1.3
Project Description
The aim of this project is to,
• investigate the limits and capabilities of lifting suspended loads with multiple
quadrotors, in order to increase the payload capacity.
• model and simulate the complex dynamics of a nonlinear system like this.
• design and simulate controllers capable of tracking the payload position, velocity
and orientation, while controlling these through the number of UAVs available.
• design, simulate and evaluate estimators capable of estimating the unknown pa-
rameters of the system.
• create a trajectory generator, capable of generating smooth trajectories in multiple
dimensions from a number of keyframes.
• model and simulate different scenarios combining all of the above.

1.4 Thesis Structure

1.4
Thesis Structure
In Chapter 2 the previously conducted research in the field is presented and discussed.
The concept behind the quadcopter is introduced and the advantages and drawbacks
of the design is debated. The notion of differential flatness for nonlinear systems is
explained, as this will be used throughout the thesis and lastly the assumptions under
which the results of the thesis are conducted, is presented.
In Chapter 3 the kinematic model used throughout the thesis is presented, as well
as multiple ways of parameterizing the attitude of a three dimensional rigid body, with
a discussion of advantages and drawbacks of each, and a selection for the rest of the
project. The mathematical models of the full dynamics of four models are derived, as
well as the differential flatness calculations of each. The flatness calculations are tested
against a common reference trajectory.
In Chapter 4 a geometric PD-type attitude controller [10] is implemented, simulated

2.1
Previous Research
Aerial transportation of cable suspended payload has been widely researched in the con-
text of a helicopter [20], but is still relatively new for multirotors, where the payload is
often just modelled as disturbances to the system with varying results [26, 29, 45].
Back in 2012, Lee et al. designed a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) for a quadcopter
based on a linearized dynamics model [14]. This model was expanded to LQR control
of the position of a cable suspended payload by Alothman et al. in 2015 [1]. In 2015
Raimundez et al. proposed a combination between adaptive PD control and a neural
network to cancel out the unmodelled dynamics [27]. A nonlinear controller for the
quadcopter in the full SE(3) space was introduced by Lee et al. in 2012 [16].
Recently people have started researching cable suspended payloads for small-size sin-
gle and multiple autonomous vehicles [20, 22, 25], resulting in nonlinear controllers for
swing free stabilization of the payload [31] and trajectory generators optimized for pay-
load attitude control, minimizing payload swing and oscillation of payload [32, 34]. The
nonlinear tracking control of the quadcopter with suspended load has been expanded
to investigate cooperative transportation of suspended point-mass load by Lee et al. in
2013 [18].
The problem of transporting a rigid body payload using multiple quadcopters, while
being able to control the attitude of the load, as well as the yaw of each of the quad-
copters has been investigated using nonlinear geometric control, by Goodarzi in his PhD
dissertation in 2015 [9] and in [15, 43].

The Quadrotor Concept
The quadrotor as a concept for aerial vehicles is not particularly new, as a matter of
fact one was built as early as 1907, where the Breguet-Richet Gyroplane was successfully
flown for the first time [38]. The quadrotor, often referred to as a quadcopter, is a
rotary-wing aircraft due to its configuration as shown below.
The quadcopter consists of four individual motor-propeller pairs fixed to a body in a
plus formation as seen in figure 2.1. Two of the propellers spins in a clockwise fashion,
while the other two spins counter-clockwise, with the two spinning in the same direction
being opposite of each other in the cross as seen in figure 2.1. This use of the propellers
leads to a balancing of the total system torque, while cancelling the gyroscopic and
aerodynamic torque in stationary flight [4].
This design is extremely simple mechanically, compared to for example a helicopter, as
all of the propellers are fixed-pitch blades and there are no mechanical link between the
motors.

2.2.2
Advantages and Drawbacks of Quadrotors
Quadcopters, or multirotors in general has some advantages and some disadvantages
when compared to traditional helicopters, some of which will be presented here.
• An advantage of the quadcopter is the mechanically simple design, as mentioned
previously the design of the quadcopter, means that it uses four fixed pitch pro-
pellers, in comparison to the mechanically advanced variable pitch rotor of the
helicopter. This results in easier manufacturing and maintenance of the device.
• The symmetry of the quadcopter means that the gyroscopic effects, created from
the rotating blades are highly reduced compared to a helicopter, easing the control
of the vehicle.
• A quadrotor generally tends to have higher lifting capabilities, when compared to
a helicopter of the same size, due to the four rotors versus a single one [11].
• A quadcopter has vertical takeoff and landing (VTOL) capabilities just like a
helicopter does, but with much better maneuverability due to the four rotors and
the absence of a tail [19]. These capabilities makes quadcopters very attractive for
tight environments, like indoor building exploration [2].
• The design of the quadcopter makes for a more steady stationary hovering, due to
the motors shifted away from the center of gravity, as well as the symmetry of the
vehicle.
• One of the drawbacks of the quadcopter system is the coupling between the rota-
tional dynamics and the translational dynamics, making it impossible to move the
vehicle in the X,Y plane without rotating it at the same time.
• Another drawback is the increased power draw from having four motors compared
to the single motor of the helicopter [11]. At the same time they are usually heavier
due to the presence of four motors [3, 11].
• Lastly a huge drawback of the quadrotor design is its inability to scale beyond a
certain size, due to full-scale multirotors inability to use electric motors like the
small ones. The reason is that when you scale an airfoil up, the lift it produces
increases with its area, which grows with the second power of size, but its weight
increases with volume, which grows with the third power of size. Therefore small
models have much more lift for weight and can afford simple but relatively heavy
batteries, while full-size aircrafts need propulsion systems with higher power den-
sity.

Differential Flatness
Differential flatness is a system property of nonlinear dynamical systems, that extends
the notion of controllability known from linear systems. A system is said to be differen-
tially flat if a set of outputs exists, such that all the states and inputs of the system can
be explicitly expressed from the outputs and a finite number of its derivatives [8].
The following definition is from the book Nonlinear Control and Filtering Using Dif-
ferential Flatness Approaches [28].
Definition: A system ˙x = f(x, u), with state vector x ∈Rn, input vector u ∈Rm where
f is a smooth vector field, is differentially flat if there exists a vector y ∈Rm in the
form,
y = h(x, u, ˙u, · · · , u(r))
(2.3)
such that
x = ϕ(y, ˙y, · · · , y(q))
(2.4)
u = α(y, ˙y, · · · , y(q))
(2.5)
where h, ϕ and α are smooth functions and r and q are finite integers. This means that
the new system’s description is given by the m algebraic variables yi, i = 1, 2, · · · , m.
This flatness property will be used later when designing controllers for parts of the
following dynamical system.
It will only be used where a feedback term cannot be

2.4
Modelling Assumptions
Due to the systems modelled in this project being nonlinear, underactuated and unstable
systems, it would be impossible to consider and explicitly model all of the nonlinearities
and dynamical effects acting on the systems. Mainly because some of the nonlinearities
cannot be accurately modelled, and because some of the effects are so small that they
can be safely ignored. In this subsection, assumptions made in order to obtain a simple
yet useful model are explained. All of these assumptions are fairly common in many
quadrotor control systems [7, 36].
• The cable connecting the quadcopters with the payload is a massless inflexible
cable, without inertia.
• The structure of the quadcopters are rigid and symmetric.
• The origin of the body-fixed frame coincides with the center of mass of the quad-
copters.

Rotation Matrix
A rotation matrix R is a linear transformation, mapping from one linear space ∈R3

Attaching a payload in a cable-suspended fashion, rather than through a rigid gripper of
some sort solves the issue, that the load increases the inertia of the quadrotor, making

suspended payload does however present a number of new potential problems, due to
the under-actuated nature of a cable-suspended system. Attaching a suspended load to
the quadcopter significantly alters the dynamics of the system, as this is essentially the
same as attaching a pendulum to the quadcopter.

CHAPTER 5
Estimator Design
In this chapter the problem of state estimation will be addressed. A state estimator is
needed in order to implement the controllers discussed in chapter 4 in real life, because
it is not possible to measure all of the states in the system through hardware sensors.
A state estimator can address this problem, by estimating the remaining immeasurable
states in the system, through the knowledge of how the states affect each other, obtained
from the dynamics model derived in chapter 3. When it comes to state estimation, there
exists a number of different solutions, each with their advantages and disadvantages
with respect to different systems, for linear dynamical systems the kalman filter or the
Luenberger observer are often used, depending on the noise characteristics of the system,
while for nonlinear systems like the ones in this thesis, the particle filter, extended kalman
filter or the unscented kalman filter is the mostly used state estimators [37]. For this
project the extended kalman filter has been chosen for all four of the implementations,
mainly due to familiarity with the filter from previous experience, and because all three of
the mentioned estimators for nonlinear filters, would be able to deliver the performance
required in this project, given the correct models and noise characteristics.
5.1
Extended Kalman Filter
The extended kalman filter, which is arguably the most widely used Bayesian estimator
for nonlinear systems, is chosen for carrying out state estimation in the systems. It
is a sub-optimal estimator, because it works by creating an optimal estimate for the
linearized model in the current state estimate, making the estimate only optimal in
that single operation point. The extended kalman filter works in two steps, just as the
regular kalman filter, a prediction step and a correction step. Considering the nonlinear
continuous time system, with discrete time measurements, given in a standard state
space representation as,

5.1.1
Runge-Kutta method
In order for an extended kalman filter to perform the numerical integration of an ordinary
differential system, required to solve it numerically, an iterative solver is used. That is,
in order to solve equations (5.3) and (5.4) every time step. In this project the commonly
used method of Runge-Kutta [24] is used in its fourth order version, sometimes referred
to as RK4. The Runge-Kutta implementation uses the exact same model as derived for
the systems in chapter 3, to establish the ordinary differential system to be solved.

Sensor Models
In order to calculate the expected values of the sensors, to obtain the best weight between
the predicted and measured states, virtual sensors are modelled. For these systems an
accelerometer, gyroscope, magnetometer and an absolute positioning sensor is modelled
on each of the quadcopters.
5.1.2.1
Accelerometer
The accelerometer is a sensor that measures the translational acceleration in three di-
mensions. The acceleration measurement from the accelerometer is integrated once to
obtain the velocity. This velocity is calculated in the system model for the payload in
the simulation as ˙XL, and can be propagated to each quadcopter using the first deriva-
tive of equations (3.41) and (3.89), calculating for example the expected quadcopter
accelerometer measurement, in the rigid-body case can be done as,

where v ˙x is the zero mean, Gaussian measurement noise of the i-th quadcopters ac-
celerometer after integrating it, with standard deviation σ ˙x. This is a super simplified
sensor model, not including any pre-filtering of the signal, as it would in a real im-
plementation, but it serves to show how the extended kalman filter works with noisy
measurements. The thing to notice is, that when the tension in a cable goes to zero,
and the system becomes two separate systems consisting of the free-falling load and the
quadcopter alone, the load has no measurements and can thus only be predicted, while
the quadcopter acceleration is directly measurable.
5.1.2.2
Gyroscope
The gyroscope measures the angular velocity around each of the three axis, in the body-
fixed frame ⃗b. As the angular velocity of each of the quadcopters is part of the system
state vector already, the expected measured value is directly equal to the state, with
added measurement noise as,
z{Ωi} = Ωi + vΩ
(5.13)
where vΩis the zero mean, Gaussian measurement noise of the angular velocity, with
standard deviation σΩ.
5.1.2.3
Magnetometer
The magnetometer, often referred to as the compass, measures the magnetic field of the
earth in order to establish an absolute rotational measurement of the body-fixed frame ⃗b,

Absolute Position
One of the requirements for doing the kind of control done in this thesis, is a sensor

The position of the payload is directly one of the states in the system models, and can

5.1.3
Measurement Noise
The measurement noise is the noise added to the measured values, due to the sensor hard-
ware not being perfect. One of the assumptions about the extended kalman filter, is that
the measurement noise covariance R is known, or can be obtained from measurement
data. Another assumption of the EKF is that the measurement noise can be modelled
as a zero mean, white noise processes with a Gaussian distribution, i.e. v(t)∼N(¯0, R),
where R represents the measurement covariance matrix.

CHAPTER 6
Trajectory Generation
In this chapter the focus is on reference generation, also called trajectory generation.
Trajectory generation is an important part of the project, as the trajectory has to be
continuous up until the 6th derivative, in order for the controllers to be valid, because
they are making use of the differential flatness property. Because the systems are differ-
entially flat hybrid models, the trajectory generator can furthermore keep track of when
the models are switched, in order to correctly design trajectories for both the pieces
where the cable tension is zero, and the pieces where it is nonzero.
A keyframe is defined as the full set of differentially flat variables, at a given times-
tamp for the system, meaning that for example, for the quadcopter alone a keyframe
will consist of a position in space along with the quadcopter yaw (x, y, z, ψ) and the
first r derivatives of these, unless they are to be unconstrained. The problem of gener-
ating a trajectory is equivalent to finding a path through m + 1 keyframes. A simple
trajectory generator could be one that interpolates between these m + 1 keyframes with
straight lines, however this trajectory is not really feasible for a quadcopter, due to the
potentially infinite curvature at the keyframes. Instead it is convenient to consider these
trajectories, using a basis of piece-wise polynomial functions of order n over m intervals
[21]. That is, a piece-wise d-dimensional trajectory on the form X = [X1 X2 · · · Xd]
where,

6.1.2
Payload Constraints
So far the trajectory generation has been looking at a single quadcopter alone. By attach-
ing a suspended load to the quadcopter, the complexity and the number of constraints
dramatically increases. The continuity constraints as part of the equality constraints
remain the same, while the endpoint constraints changes.
The endpoint constraints
changes because the keyframes contains information about, where the load should be,
while the trajectory to minimize the derivatives of displacement in, is actually the quad-
copters trajectory.
Another reason for the number of constraints to increase rapidly, is that the quadcopter
with payload is a hybrid system, meaning that the trajectory planning contains con-
straints on, when they should act as two separate systems, and when not to. As such
the keyframes for the load also contains Tdes = [T0 T1 · · · Tm]T, describing the desired
tension in the cable as either zero or nonzero (unconstrained). Due to this rapidly in-
creasing number of constraints, the optimization of trajectories involving a payload, has
been kept at as few payload concerning constraints as possible, even though the possibil-
ities are almost endless. With payload constraints it is possible to constrain everything
from forcing the quadcopter to be above the load, or the opposite and keeping payload
as well as cable free from defined obstacles, to the tension in the cable, which way the
load should fall when the tension goes to zero, and when it goes from zero to nonzero
values. For the work in this thesis, the payload constraints are kept to maintaining a
nonzero tension in all cables, due to the processing time as a consequence of the poten-
tially large amount of constraints. This does however come with the downside that the
path returned from the optimization problem, does not contain all of the differentially
flat outputs. This is fixed by locking some of the differentially flat outputs to fixed
==PAGE:82==
62
6 Trajectory Generation
values, e.g. ignoring the control of the quadcopters yaw, by setting them to a constant
value. It could also be locking the cable direction of all but one cable to a constant
value, resulting in a single quadcopter doing the majority of the work. The tension can
be constrained to always being positive-definite [35] through an inequality constraint as
part of Aineq and Bineq.
One way of handling the rapidly increasing number of constraints, is to make the opti-
mization problem into a mixed integer quadratic optimization problem, and then make
use of a faster implementation of a solver, e.g. IBMs CPLEX Optimizer [12].
6.2
Summary
In this chapter a method of finding the optimal trajectory from point A to point B,
through m keyframes has been presented, with respect to minimizing a certain derivative
of the trajectory subject to a number of constraints. It has been presented how the
optimization problem can be expressed, as well as how to express the restrictions. A
method of creating corridor constraints has been shown, as well as a method of keeping
track of a suspended load through optimization limitations. One of the biggest issue of
using this method, is the amount of constraints in the optimization problem. As the
models increase in complexity, the number of restrictions increases even faster, leading
to very big computational requirements in order to solve the optimization problems.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis, has been to create a general purpose control method for
multiple quadcopters with a single cable-suspended, rigid-body payload, for transporta-
tion purposes. This involves creating an optimal trajectory to transport the load from
one point to another, through a number of well defined keypoints, while minimizing the
swing of the payload. The aim and motivation to pursue research in this direction was
explained in chapter 1, supported by the survey of previously researched methods in
chapter 2.
Two scenarios from the world around us, has been used as performance indicators for
the research conducted during this thesis. The two scenarios are transportation of equip-
ment to the top of a high tower, for example an antenna tower and doing repair work on
live power bearing high voltage transmission cables. In order to use collaborating UAVs
in these scenarios, it is necessary to model the dynamics of the system, design a state
estimator and controllers for the modelled systems, and create a continuous trajectory.
An extended kalman filter has been created, capable of estimating the states of each
of the four systems accurately, even with realistic process and measurement noise on
sensors. The estimated position state stays within ± 2 cm of the actual state, even with
sensor noise and unknown wind disturbances, with the same performance on the rest of
the states in the system.
A geometric controller for attitude and position control has been presented for all four
systems, capable of tracking a given trajectory of differentially flat output variables. The
controller manages to reject wind disturbances of 12 m/s for four quadcopters carrying
a rigid-body payload, causing a slight load position swing of ± 10 cm for approximately
2 seconds.
Because the trajectories must be continuous until at least the 6th derivative due to
diffrential flatness, a trajectory generator has been put together. The trajectory genera-
tor is capable of synthesizing an optimal trajectory going through a number of keyframes,
while minimizing the 4th or 6th derivative, depending on the model it is created for.
The generator is capable of setting corridor constraints on the trajectory, as well as
constraints on the cable tension for the payload.
