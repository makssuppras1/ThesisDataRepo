Abstract
By
S├Ėren Peter Nielsen
This thesis investigates how two di’¼Ćerent photovoltaic panels operate when subjected
to dynamic shading. One PV-panel consists of polycrystalline silicon modules, whereas
the second consists of CIS-based thin-’¼ülm modules. A well-de’¼üned dynamic shade was
constructed by physically erecting a vertical object in front of each of the panels. In
order to validate the position of the shadow, a dynamic Simulink model has been calcu-
lating its position on the panel. A detailed theoretical analysis presents how shading can
a’¼Ćect power characteristics. To assess the dynamic shadowŌĆÖs actual impact on power
characteristics, two mathematical models have been developed. The ’¼ürst model is a
further development of a Simulink PV-model made by IEEE member Mattia Marinelli.
The second model is a linear regression between several predictor variables, conducted
in Matlab. Both modelsŌĆÖ root mean square error on power production proved to be
below 2.1┬Ę10ŌłÆ4 p.u. The two models also demonstrated accurate energy prediction, with
a deviation less than 1%. Experimental veri’¼ücation showed that a dynamic shadow cov-
ering close to 3% of the total surface area, reduced the instantaneous power production
by more than 20% for the polycrystalline panel, and about 6% for the thin-’¼ülm panel.
Daily loss in energy was found to be close to 10% for the polycrystalline panel, and 4%
for the thin-’¼ülm panel. Findings suggest that the optimal way of mounting PV-modules,
exposed to a dynamic shadow, is to have the strings of the solar cells orientated verti-
cally, thereby reducing the time of critical shading. The thesis thus concludes that the
presence of dynamic shadows should be taken into serious consideration when deciding
where to place new PV-panels.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In a rapidly consuming world, the demand for renewable energy sources increases. Over
the last three decades three major concerns have contributed to raise the ever growing
awareness for power productions from renewable sources. These concerns are: the con-
tinued increase in global energy demand, speculations on the amount of remaining fossil
fuels and consequences due to climate change. Nowadays, power production around the
world is primarily based on fossil fuels, nuclear- or hydro power plants. However, con-
temporary trends indicate a strong increase in power production from especially wind
and photovoltaic (PV) [3], [4]. This solid increase in renewable energy sources is mainly
due to political ambitions.
In Denmark, the Danish Government (2011-2015) has a
long-term goal of covering the entire energy supply by renewable energy, no later than
year 2050. Photovoltaic is regarded as an essential supplement to energy from wind and
biomass. Due to the political ambitionŌĆÖs dependency on photovoltaic, it is extremely im-
portant to assess the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and potential risks embedded
in the technology.
Placing PV-panels in urban areas results in some con’¼éicts, such of aesthetics, space
consumption, and shading. Aesthetics is subjective and can therefore not be assessed
quantitatively. However, space consumption and shading can. Only places that are not
shaded by objects such as buildings, antennas, chimneys and ’¼éagpoles, etc. are consid-
ered ideal for harvesting energy from the sun. Since the amount of ideal, south-facing
rooftop-area is limited, itŌĆÖs important to assess how the limited rooftop-area should be
prioritized. Rooftop-areas in cities like Copenhagen are in high demand. The Technical
and Environmental Administration of Copenhagen is determined on constructing cli-
mate resilient neighbourhoods. The administration regards green roofs as being part of
the future of urban design [5]. Besides, sunny rooftop-areas are used for roof terraces
that provide leisure for the urban residents. Increased demand in PV-technology may

Power production from photovoltaic depends on several things, such as the level of

PV-panels exposed to partial shading are more likely to su’¼Ćer malfunction or even

In principle, as PV-cells are often connected in series, they are forced
to conduct the same current. The level of irradiance establishes how much current is
produced. If one or more of the cells are subjected to a distinct lower level of irradiance,
due to shading, it may have severe consequences. The shaded cells are only able to
conduct the higher current by changing the polarity of the voltage, causing the cells to
absorb power. If the PV-modules are not equipped with adequate by-pass diodes, the
cells may heat up and cause thermal breakdown [7].

3.3
Classi’¼ücation of shadows
Shading comes in various forms and is not simple to generalize. However, according
to [13], shadows may be categorized in two major groups, being Objective Shading
and Subjective Shading. Objective Shading is caused by uncontrollable variations in
weather conditions, such as clouds, aerosols, mist and haze. On the other hand, Sub-
jective Shading is caused by nearby objects that blocks out the Sun. The Subjective
Shading group consists of two subgroups and these are Static Shading and Dynamic
Shading. Static shading occurs when material constant blocks out the Sun, indepen-
dently of the movement of the Sun. Such shade could be caused by bird droppings, dust
or accumulated dirt on the PV-panels. Dynamic shade is de’¼üned by being subject to
the movement of the Sun. The shadow varies, dynamically, according to the azimuth
angle of the Sun. Dynamic Shade is typically caused by trees, buildings, antennas etc.
[13]. This thesis will only investigate in’¼éuence from dynamic shading.

3.5.1
Bypass diodes
To prevent hot-spots from occurring, diodes are placed in parallel with strings of solar
cells to bypass excess current, as shown in Figure 3.26.
Figure 3.26: Schematic showing how bypass diodes operate under varying irradiances
Figure 3.26 shows how the bypass diode is blocked when all the cells are subjected
to the same level of irradiance.
On the other hand, when the cells are exposed to
heterogeneous irradiance the bypass conducts current. The bypass diode has a greater
internal resistance than the string of solar cells when the cells are fully irradiated. The
current will run along the path with least resistance, making it ’¼éow through the cells.
However, when the solar cells are heterogeneous exposed the internal resistance of the
string will increase above the internal resistance of the diode, forcing current to ’¼éow
through the diode [18]. Bypass diodes are able to save the poorly illuminated cells and
at the same time increase the power output to the load, hence the shaded cells does not
absorb power [22].

Discussion
This chapter is set out to elaborate on some of the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities,

6.1
Strengths
The improved power models, labelled Model A and Model LASSO, proved to be even
more accurate than the bench-mark model, labelled Model Ref. Hereby, the improved
models provide more reliable power predictions, and they are even capable of predicting
the total energy production with a small deviation from actual measurements.
The irradiance based power model (Model A) has some great advantages over the linear
regression model (Model LASSO). First, it may directly be applied to various PV-panels
at di’¼Ćerent locations, and tilting. Secondly, it only needs three input-measurements in
order to compute power production. The required sensors are commercial and relatively
a’¼Ćordable.
As long as the sensors function pristine, the model does not need to be
calibrated or changed in any way, making the model very robust.
The linear regression model is superior when it comes to accuracy. Due to its wide
span of inputs it is capable of predicting both instantaneous power and total energy
production very precisely. The linear regression model is not limited by a number of
inputs. The model is able to account for almost an in’¼ünite number of inputs, and is
able to prioritize and use the most correlated of them. The LASSO model even proved
to accurately predict power production from one of the investigated PV-panels which
seemingly only delivered a fraction of its full capacity.
The applied theory gave an insight into why and how heterogeneous light, caused by
shading, causes PV-modules to either absorb power or to channel current through bypass
diodes. The simpli’¼üed equations showed how the solar cells may turn their polarity, if the

The experimental design is fairly straight forward to replicate, or even to expand for

The height of the shading object was designed to cover the entire height of the PV-

dynamic shadow. A vertical alignment of the modules will reduce the time in which the
dynamic shadow is most critical.
