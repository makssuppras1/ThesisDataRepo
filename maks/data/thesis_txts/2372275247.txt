SUMMARY 
Bioprocesses involve the use of living microorganisms or enzymes for the production of chemical and 
biochemical substances. Enzyme-based processes have many applications in both chemical and 
pharmaceutical industries and can act as possible alternatives for conventional synthesis routes. 
However, limitations of enzymes mostly related with product and/or substrate inhibition result in low 
product yields and highly diluted product, which leads in difficult downstream separations.  
The design of intensified reaction-separation schemes can be applied to overcome such shortcomings in 
bioprocesses. Continuous removal of the product from the reactor is known as a method that can increase 
the yield of a reaction either by preventing product inhibition or by shifting the reaction equilibrium to 
produce more product. 
The objective of this MSc thesis is to develop a systematic method for the design of reaction-separation 
schemes, where the product is continuously removed from the reactor and the separation is performed 
externally. The separation method is liquid-liquid extraction, therefore liquid-liquid equilibrium systems 
are taken into consideration in this project. The design method is given in terms of a workflow consisting 
of seven steps. The first steps include the problem formulation by defining the reaction system and 
collecting all relative data of the reaction and pure component properties. Based on the mixture analysis 
that occurs afterwards, the solvent selection method can be applied. Finally, the reactor is simulated and 
the feasibility of the applied method is evaluated according to performance criteria, such as the 
feasibility of the separation and the product yield. 
Six case studies are used to highlight the application of the design method. Oxidation of primary alcohols 
to aldehydes are the reactions, due to the interest of aldehydes, their instable nature as they are 
intermediate products, and the occurrence of liquid phase split with water.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
OVERVIEW OF BIOPROCESSES 
Chemical and bio-chemical industries produce valuable products, essential for every aspect of our lives. 
Economic and environmental concerns have driven industries towards the development of sustainable, 
efficient and cost effective methods of production to meet the great demands. Bioprocesses are important 
biological reactions and can play a vital role to address the challenges of limited sources of raw 
materials, pollution and cost (Brahmachari, Demain, & Adrio, 2016).  
Biochemical engineering involves the use of whole living organisms or part of them (enzymes) for the 
production of chemical and biochemical materials, which is called bio-transformation (Liese, Seelbach, 
& Wandrey, 2006). However, it is noted that there is difference between the processes carried through 
by microorganisms and those where enzymes are involved. In the present project, the term bio-
transformation will refer to processes catalyzed by enzymes. Such processes present many advantages 
and can actually substitute the conventional synthesis pathways. The relative success of biochemically 
catalyzed reactions can be mainly attributed to the nature of the catalyst (enzyme), which is related with 
characteristics like selectivity and biodegradability and mild process conditions. These are only some of 
the aspects that are beneficial from both an environmental and economic point of view (Koeller & Wong, 
2001). 
However, biochemical reactions also come with some limitations. These are mostly due to the sensitivity 
of the enzymes in many factors that have an effect in the process in total. Reaction conditions, such as 
temperature, pressure, pH, should be mild and carefully controlled, in order for the enzymes to maintain 
their activity. The substrate and/or product can turn out to be toxic for the enzyme if the concentration 
exceeds a certain limit, which leads in low product yields. Traditionally, in bioprocesses the products 
are produced in a dilute environment, resulting in low product concentrations and difficult downstream 
separations (Cen, 1993). The pharmaceutical industry is an indicative example of industry dealing with 
such concerns, as bioprocesses are the core, and issues of low yields and difficult downstream processes 
current problems. The use of organic solvents in a typical pharmaceutical process counts for about 80 
% of mass utilization, due to the difficulty of product recovery. This can be understood better by the use 
of metrics, such as the E-factor, which is defined as the mass ratio of waste to product. In pharmaceutical 
processes the E-factor is 25-100 kg waste/ kg product, whereas in fine chemicals is 5-50 kg waste/ kg 
product (Woodley, 2008).

MOTIVATION OF THE THESIS 
To overcome the limitations of bioprocesses, in order for them to be extended over synthetic chemical 
reactions, the integration of reaction and separation can be applied, as it can be beneficial in terms of 
the reaction yield and the downstream process separation. Coupling reaction and separation can be 
achieved via two process designs: in situ product separation and separation in an external recycling loop 
(Cen, 1993). The method can increase the yield of a given bio-reaction either by overcoming inhibitory 
or toxic effects or by shifting unfavorable reaction equilibria. ISPR can be introduced to different modes 
of operations (i.e. batch, fed-batch or continuous) and the separation step can be performed either in the 
bioreactor (internally) or outside in a separate loop (externally) (Freeman, Woodley, & Lilly, 1993). 
Membrane bioreactors and continuous stirred tank reactor are examples of applications of intensifying 
processes, which can be used in bioprocesses for the in-situ separation of the reaction products to 
enhance the yield (Vaghari, et al., 2015).  
 
OBJECTIVE OF THE THESIS 
The objective of the MSc thesis is to:  
 
Develop a systematic design method for reaction-separation schemes. The method aims to provide 
a step-by-step guide for the process design of two different scenarios; one steady-state scenario and 
one dynamic-state scenario.  
 
Highlight the application of the method through case studies. The method aims to be applicable for 
any enzyme with very low substrate concertation and highly diluted product. 
With respect to the design methodology: 
- 
The objective of the steady-state scenario is the separation of the product from the reaction 
mixture, whereas the objective of the dynamic-state scenario is the design of a reaction-
separation scheme, where the product is continuously removed from the reactor and the 
separation is performed externally. 
- 
The separation technique is liquid-liquid extraction, therefore liquid-liquid equilibrium 
systems are considered in this project.  
- 
The method includes a complete analysis of the reaction system, in terms of reaction kinetics 
and phase equilibria.

- 
A solvent selection method is included, and the selection is performed according to the 
results of the analysis.  
- 
The developed models should be able to describe the behavior of the system and provide

-

objective.

2.1.2. Reaction class 
Chemical reactions can be divided into several classes according to the type of compounds (reactants 
and products) that are part of the reaction. The class defines the type of reaction by specifying the type 
of the reactants and the products. For example, in esterification the reactants include alcohol and acid 
and the products are ester and water. Another example is acid-base reactions, where an acid and a base 
are combined and water is one of the products. The class of reaction used in this project to highlight the 
conceptual basis of the design method is oxidation of primary alcohols. Therefore, the reactants are a 
primary alcohol and oxygen and the products are an aldehyde and water (see chapter 4).

BIOREACTIONS   
Fully understanding of bioengineering and its concepts was a requirement, in order for the biotechnology 
industry to be developed and allow the implementation of new and sustainable production methods. A 
bioprocess involves the transformation of a raw material into some product by a biocatalyst (De Regil 
& Sandoval, 2013).  
2.3.1. Advantages of bio-catalyzed reactions 
Enzymes used in catalysis have advantages over the common catalysts. Catalysts can be pure elements, 
like nickel or platinum, a pure compound (e.g. silica) or a mixture. The most common catalysts are 
transition metals for redox reactions, proton acids for hydrolysis and platinum for reactions involving 
hydrogen. (Farrauto, Dorazio, & Bartholomew).  
 
Major advantage of the enzymes is their selectivity. Most enzymes are only active for a single 
reaction and as a result side-reactions are avoided and by-products are minimized.  
 
Moreover, the mechanisms of conventional catalysis, either heterogeneous or homogeneous, 
can be applied in the enzymatic catalysis (Kochi, 1981).  
 
Apart from the apparent advantage of bio-catalysis, which is the nature of the catalyst, it leads 
to a process with a low environmental impact. Enzymes or microorganisms require aqueous 
solutions, therefore, the use of solvents with a poor environmental profile is avoided.  
 
Furthermore, the reaction takes place under ambient conditions because of the sensitivity of the 
microorganisms (Kochi, 1981). As a result, the energy requirements are lower, which is 
beneficial in both of cost and environmental terms. 
==PAGE:24==
Intensified Reaction-Separation Schemes 
 
10 
 
 
Bio-catalysis is homogeneous catalysis, which has the advantage that both the reactant and the 
enzyme are present in one phase (usually the liquid phase) and as a result there are no problems 
of pore diffusion.  
2.3.2. Enzyme kinetics 
Enzymes are proteins with catalytic properties and they increase the rate of the reaction by decreasing 
the activation energy. Enzymes are classified into six different groups based on the type of reactions 
catalyzed: oxidoreductases, transferases, hydrolases, lyases, isomerases and ligases. In the present work, 
the reactive systems are considered to be catalyzed by Galactose oxidase, which belongs in the family 
of oxidoreductases (Larroche, Sanroman, Guocheng, & Pandey).  
The first to study the kinetics of enzymes were Michaelis and Menten, who proposed an expression for 
the rate of a reaction catalyzed by an enzyme as a function of the substrate concentration. According to 
equation (1), the reaction scheme represents a reaction with two substrates. The mechanism of kinetics 
in this case is called Ping Pong bi bi (Figure 2) (Pedersen, Rehn, & Woodley, 2015).  
 
Figure 2: Schematic representation of Ping-pong bi bi kinetics mechanism (Pedersen, Rehn, & Woodley, 2015). 
2.3.3. Enzyme inhibition and deactivation 
One of the main problems of bioreactions is the susceptibility of the enzymes to substances that might 
decrease or stop the enzymatic reaction. These substances are called inhibitors agents. Often the product 
has an inhibitory effect and is called a competitive inhibitor, leading to product inhibition. The 
expression of the reaction rate is also a function of the product concentration (Liese, Seelbach, & 
Wandrey, 2006). In such cases, there is an upper limit in the concertation of the substrate and/or product, 
which should not be exceeded, in order for the enzyme to maintain its activity. Similar considerations 
exist also in the operating conditions, as enzymes are also sensitive in temperature, pH, and aeriation, 
in terms of oxygen concentration ((NC-IUB), 1983).

DOWNSTREAM SEPARATION IN BIOREACTIONS 
Due to the inhibitory effect that the product might have on the enzyme, the majority of bioreactions are 
carried out in dilute solutions. High dilution of the product and the fact that the reaction is 
homogeneously catalyzed are two characteristics of the enzymatic catalysis. Both of these characteristics 
results in difficult separation of the product. Due to thermal sensitivity of the enzymes, the most common 
method of separation, distillation, cannot be used. Also, the removal of large amounts of water can be 
highly costly. The most common techniques include membrane filtration, liquid-liquid extraction, 
precipitation and adsorption. Chromatography is also a very commonly used separation technique in 
bioprocesses, which is usually applied in the end of the downstream processing, but it leads to loss of 
the biocatalyst (Aires-Barros & Azevedo, 2016).  
 
INTENSIFIED REACTION-SEPARATION SCHEMES 
Reaction-separation schemes is an approach considered to address the challenges of low yield and 
difficult downstream processing in bio-reactions.  
2.5.1. Advantages of reaction-separation schemes

technology. The integration of reaction and separation in one unit not only can increase the efficiency 
of the process by enhancing the yield and the productivity, but it reduces the steps of downstream

requirements can be reduced, due to the reduction of separation steps. Table 1 presents some of the

2.5.2. Design and operation of reaction-separation schemes 
According to what is mentioned in § 2.3.3 and § 2.4 with respect to the main limitations and difficulties 
of enzyme-catalyzed processes, it seems that simultaneous bioreaction and product separation might be 
an opportunity to improve the performance of the reaction and to reduce the production cost. The 
reaction-separation schemes can be classified into two categories: in situ product separation and 
separation in an external loop (Cen, 1993). 
The concept of intensified reaction-separation schemes usually involves the removal of the product as 
it is formed. Generally, selecting a technique to separate the product from the reactant mixture requires 
a driving force, which is the result of differences between the properties of the compounds. For example, 
large difference between the boiling points of the components indicate easy separation by distillation or 
evaporation, while differences in solubility are in favor of extraction. The bigger the difference of the 
properties is, the larger is the driving force and consequently the easier is the separation (Schembecker 
G., 2003). In situ product separation requires the existence of two phases. One phase is the reaction 
phase, where the reactants and the enzyme are dissolved, and the second phase is the transport phase for 
mass or energy transport. Usually, the second phase which is created is the one that removes the product 
from the reaction phase (Schembecker G., 2003). The second phase can be also named mass separation 
agent (MSA). The product will be partially transferred into the MSA, and by this a reduction of the 
product concentration in the reacting phase is achieved. A successful separation requires the use of a 
==PAGE:27==
Intensified Reaction-Separation Schemes 
 
13 
 
biocompatible MSA, as it comes into contact with the biocatalyst. In situ product removal can be 
operated either in batch or continuous mode (Cen, 1993). 
In the case of external product removal, a separator is placed in the external recycling loop and the 
product in the recycling stream can be separated, while the stream containing the reactants and the 
biocatalyst is recycled back to the reactor. With respect to the MSA, external separation of the product, 
the requirements of the MSA are not so strict, as the contact of the biocatalyst and the MSA can be 
avoided by adding a separator in the external recycling loop (Cen, 1993). 
In this project, liquid-liquid extraction is used as the separation technique (see chapter 3), so phase 
systems with two liquid phases are considered. Consequently, in this case the MSA is a solvent. In order 
for liquid-liquid extraction to be feasible the solvent must have the following characteristics: 
 
The solvent should be liquid. This means that the operating conditions are higher than the 
melting point of the solvent (𝑇𝑇𝑚𝑚) and lower than its boiling point (𝑇𝑇𝑏𝑏). 
 
The solvent should be substantially immiscible with water.  
 
The separation factor and selectivity for product should be as high as possible. 
 
Large values of the distribution coefficient between the two phases (i.e. organic and aqueous). 
The higher the distribution coefficient the less is the amount of the solvent required for a given 
degree of extraction. 
 
No azeotrope formation with the extract. 
 
Should be easily to be separated from the reaction broth (Cen, 1993). 
 
High boiling point. 
 
High resistance to thermal degradation. 
 
Non-reactive with the compounds involved in the system. 
 
Non-toxic, non-flammable and non-corrosive to the equipment. 
 
Biocompatible (i.e. not inhibit the bioreaction or cause any deactivation of the biocatalyst). This 
is essential for in situ separation (Cen, 1993). 
 
Not cause any degradation or deactivation of the product (Cen, 1993). 
 
Environmental friendly. 
 
Low cost.

SCENARIOS 
In the algorithm of the method presented in § 3.1, Step 0 involves the problem definition and the 
selection between two scenarios, which lead in different simulation of the system. Two operating 
scenarios were examined in this work: Scenario 1, in which the system is simulated in steady-state and 
Scenario 2, in which dynamic simulation of the system is considered. Each scenario serves for a different 
objective of the problem to be solved.  
3.2.1. Scenario 1: Steady state simulation 
Under Scenario 1, the system is in steady state. In general, simulation in steady state implies no 
variations of temperature, pressure, and composition and reaction rate with respect to time. A continuous 
stirred tank reactor (CSTR) is characterized of an inlet and an outlet that allow for a constant flow into 
and out of the tank. Therefore, the volume in a CSTR is constant and there is no accumulation. Stirring 
for well-mixing is also a condition in order for steady-state to be reached, as it ensures homogeneous 
temperature, pressure and composition. Certain time is needed, according to the reaction, for the steady 
state to be reached. Once steady state has been reached, full conversion of the substrate to the product 
can be achieved. 
Since full conversion of the substrate is attained, the outlet of the reactor will contain the diluted product 
and no substrate. Bio-reactions are considered in this project, meaning that the medium of the reactor 
contains large amounts of water, due to the enzyme, and as a result the product will be highly diluted. 
The objective of Scenario 1, is the recovery of the highly diluted product.

In Scenario 2, the dynamic state is studied. This involves the continuous removal of the product during 
the reaction. This technique can be described as external product removal. A continuous reactor is 
considered, but since the model simulates the dynamic state the inlet and outlet flowrates are not equal.  
Therefore, the model equation is derived from the differential mass balance. The separation method is 
the same (liquid-liquid extraction) as in the steady-state scenario. However, the difference in this 
scenario is that, since the separation is performed externally in a separate loop, a fraction of the medium 
(i.e. water) is also circulated for product removal. The efficiency of the configuration depends on the 
sufficiently fast circulation rate, in such a way so the product concentration is maintained below the 
inhibitory level. Also, the concentration of the substrate should always be higher than the product 
concentration, in order for the equilibrium to be shifted. In this case, the solvent should be able to extract 
the product from the aqueous phase and separate it from the reactants, while the reactants should remain 
dissolved in the aqueous phase. After liquid-liquid extraction, the product and the solvent are heading 
towards further separation, where the product is recovered and the solvent can be reused, whereas water 
with the dissolved reactants are recycled back to the reactor. In order for a successful separation to be 
achieved and the solvent to be able reusable, a heavy organic solvent should be used, with very low 
volatility and water immiscibility. The selection of the appropriate solvent is based on the suggested 
methodology in Step 4.

The development of the model is based on several assumptions. It is important of the assumptions to be 
clearly stated, in order for the model to maintain its validity when applied in other reaction systems. If  
 
The temperature and pressure are constant. It is important for the reaction to take place in steady 
conditions. The reaction is slightly exothermic, therefore, the produced heat can be removed 
by a cooling jacket, in order for the temperature to remain constant. Since the temperature is 
assumed to be constant, an energy balance is not included in the model. 
 
The liquid of each phase is incompressible. Density is a function of temperature (Appendix D), 
so this assumption holds, as long as the temperature is controlled to remain constant. 
 
 
The composition and properties are the same at all points of each phase. The lumped parameter 
model assumes that the composition and properties in a phase are spatially independent. 
 
 
Two phases, one liquid and one vapor phase, exist in the reactor. The vapor phase occur as a 
result of the aeriation, so no vapor-liquid equilibrium exists between the phases. 
 
 
Reaction occurs only in the liquid phase.

Due to their high chemical activity aldehydes are important intermediates for the manufacture of resins, 
solvents, plasticizers and pharmaceuticals (Brown & March, u.d.). Of all aldehydes, formaldehyde is 
produced on the largest industrial scale and is used in the productions of resins. Because of their 
tendency to oligomerize or polymerize their combination with other molecules results in many known 
plastics. Some sugars, hormones or vitamin derivatives are aldehydes (Smith & March, 2006). 
4.1.3. Methods of aldehyde productions 
There are several routes for the synthesis of aldehydes, with the most dominant one being 
hydroformylation, which is the ozonolysis of alkenes. Dehydrogenation of alcohols is a method widely 
used in industries and it involves the use of metal catalysts (Colquhoun, Thompson, & Twigg, 1991). 
The selection of the reaction systems in this project is based on previous experimental work (see § 4.2), 
therefore, the method for aldehydes production that is considered in this work is the oxidation of 
alcohols.

4.1.4. Oxidation of primary alcohols 
Primary alcohols (R-CH2-OH) can be transformed to aldehydes (R-CHO) or to carboxylic acids (R-
COOH) through oxidation, according to the conditions that the reaction takes place (Figure 9). 
 
Figure 9: Mechanism of oxidation of primary alcohols. 
During oxidation and under the presence of oxygen, two atoms of hydrogen are removed from the 
alcohol (dehydrogenation) and water is formed because of the existence of oxygen. Therefore, oxygen 
is the hydrogen acceptor. Selective oxidation of alcohols to aldehydes is a challenging reaction, as 
aldehydes are relatively unstable products and they tend to be transformed to carboxylic acids under the 
presence of water (Haines, 1988). 
 RELATED WORK AND DATA 
This project uses the bio-catalytic oxidation of primary alcohols to aldehydes as the class of reaction to 
highlight the developed design methodology. The starting point for the selection of the reaction systems 
was the experimental work of Pedersen et al., 2015. Their work (Pedersen, et al., 2015) was focused on 
the oxidation of benzyl alcohol to benzaldehyde by the enzyme Galactose oxidase (GOase). During the 
experiments loss of the product in the gas phase was noticed, which was attributed to the sparging air in 
the bioreactor. Their future work suggested that systems with such properties are candidates for the 
application of intensified reaction-separation schemes. 
4.2.1. The enzyme GOase  
Galactose oxidase (GOase) is a copper-dependent enzyme (Pedersen, et al., 2015) that has shown 
promising results for the bio-catalysis of the oxidation of primary alcohols to the corresponding 
aldehydes (Whittaker, 2003). Examples of oxidation reactions catalyzed by GOase are the D-galactose 
and the C6 hydroxyl group to their corresponding aldehydes, during which hydrogen peroxide is 
produced as a by-product. Since hydrogen peroxide is toxic for the enzyme and can inactivate it, a 
catalase is used, which disassociates hydrogen peroxide to one mole of water and half a mole of oxygen   
(Pedersen, et al., 2015). A very important aspect of employing the GOase as the bio-catalyst is that the 
==PAGE:53==
Intensified Reaction-Separation Schemes 
 
39 
 
selective oxidation to the corresponding aldehyde is achieved, avoiding in this way the overoxidation to 
acid (Orbegozo, et al., 2009).  This is of high importance considering the unstable nature and high 
reactivity of the aldehydes, due to which they oxidized rapidly to acids under the presence of oxygen. It 
has been showed (DeLu Yin, 2015) that GOase and a closely related member in the GOase family can 
potentially catalyze the oxidation of primary alcohols. Reasonable activity has been proved for the 
following substrates (DeLu Yin, 2015): 
 1-propanol 
 1-butanol 
 1-pentanol 
 1-hexanol 
 1-heptanol

In competitive product inhibition, both the substrate and the inhibitor compete for binding to the active 
site of the enzyme. The inhibition is most noticeable at low substrate concentrations, but can be 
overcome at sufficiently high substrate concentrations ((NC-IUB), 1983). On top of product inhibition, 
there is also product toxicity, which is severe above 50 mmol/l. Therefore, the product concentration 
should be kept below this value (Pedersen, et al., 2015). 
The enzyme is very susceptible to the process conditions and a number of additives is required in order

investigated. The following are noted:

application of the dynamic state model in the case studies.  
 
All six reaction systems (Table 6) are considered to be catalyzed by the enzyme GOase (see § 
4.2).  


consideration and they should not exceed 0.05 kmol/m3 (=50 mmol/l). 






kinetic parameters are given in Table 5 (§ 4.3). The rate of oxidation for all six reaction system 
is described by the same kinetic model. 


changes according to the reaction system, whereas the rest of the parameters are the same for 
all six reaction systems. 
 
The contribution of the density of the organics in the mean density is very small due to the small 
concentration of the organics in water. Therefore, the density of the liquid phase between the

6. DISCUSSION OF RESULTS 
In § 5.1 and 5.2 the design methodology was applied step-by-step in the selected reaction systems. The 
results of each step were shown at the respective section. This chapter analyzes the obtained results and 
their meaning.  
 Scenario 1 results 
 
The objective of each case study under Scenario 1 is the separation of the diluted aldehyde from 
water. The objective is achieved in all of the case studies by applying the methodology and the 
suggested configuration (Figure 4). 
 
The design methodology includes also a methodology for solvent selection, since the separation 
technique is liquid-liquid extraction. Following the method for solvent selection and by using the 
tool Pro-CAMD, heavy organic solvents were identified. 
 
The last step of the methodology (Step 6) is the evaluation of the results in terms of feasibility and 
the summary of the results of each step in a report. The evaluation of the separation feasibility with 
the identified solvent is performed by the ternary plots for each system of water + aldehyde + 
solvent. 
 
All ternary plots generated for each case study (Figure 11-Figure 16) confirm the occurrence of 
phase split between the solvent and water and the removal of the aldehyde from the aqueous phase. 
 
The last unit in the configuration of Figure 4 is an evaporator for the recovery of the aldehyde from 
the solvent. In order to examine the feasibility of using evaporation as the separation technique, the 
vapor-liquid equilibrium analysis for the binary mixtures aldehyde + solvent was performed 
(Appendix A). From the VLE plots (Figure 25, Figure 27, Figure 29, Figure 31and Figure 33) it can 
be seen that separation of the aldehyde from the solvent by simple evaporation is not feasible, as the 
driving force is not large, especially for the cases of heavy aldehydes. For this, heavier and non-
volatile compounds would be required. A solution to that might be the use of hydrophobic ionic 
liquids. 
 
In Step 3 the liquid-liquid equilibrium analysis is included, for which the binary plots of 
temperature-composition (T-xx) are shown in Appendix A (Figure 24, Figure 26, Figure 28, Figure 
30, Figure 32 and Figure 34). It is noticed that all the aldehydes are partly immiscible with water 
and that the heavier is the aldehyde the less miscible is with water. This is why a phase split between 
==PAGE:94==
Intensified Reaction-Separation Schemes 
 
80 
 
water and aldehyde occurs for very small concentrations of the aldehyde in the case studies 4, 5 and 
6 (Table 27, Table 33 and Table 39).  
 
Despite the phase split, a solvent needs to be used, as significant amount of the aldehyde was lost in 
the aqueous phase. At this point it is noted that cooling down the product and crystallization were 
examined as possible alternatives to use of solvent. However, the results were not satisfactory in 
terms of the lost amount of aldehyde in water. Crystallization was rejected, as the melting point of 
water is higher than the melting point of the heavy aldehydes (i.e. 1-hexanal, 1-heptanal, 
benzaldehyde), so water would be the one to be crystallized first.

7. CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK 
 
SUMMARY OF ACHIEVEMENTS 
In this project, the development of a method for the design and evaluation of reaction-separation 
schemes has been achieved. The following have been developed: 
 
A workflow presenting the design method step-by-step and specifying the methods and tools 
used in each step. 
 
Two different simulating scenarios, one under steady state and one under dynamic state. 
 
A generic model of the dynamic state, able to predict the composition of the mixture at each 
time in the reactor and calculate the performance indicator. 
 
A series of procedures describing how to generate the data by combining tools and methods. 
 
A method for solvent selection by using the tool Pro-CAMD.  
 
Simulations of the reactor in steady state in PRO/II. 
 
Simulations of the reactor in dynamic state by code implementation of the model. 
 
METHOD LIMITATIONS 
The implementation of the design method requires the use of the associated models, methods and tools, 
in order to generate the required data. Difficulties are likely to arise during the data generation, if they 
are not available to be collected, so then use of tools and/or model-based methods is required. The first 
limitation appears in the first step of the method, where the data regarding the reaction system need to 
be collected. The kinetic model is essential for the evaluation of the reaction performance and the 
optimization of the reaction. However, the kinetic model depends on the reaction pathway and the type 
of catalyst used, and there might be difficulties in finding available data in the literature. Experimental 
work and validation is required for the generation of the kinetic model and the parameters, which is the 
reason why data are hard to be found. Prediction of phase behavior (VLE, LLE) of multicomponent 
systems by using model-based methods or tools is another limitation of the method. After the selection 
of thermodynamic model the binary parameters interaction are required. The difficulty is greater for 
performance of LLE calculations, as data are hard to find and sometimes result in poor prediction phase 
behavior. Solvents play an important role in this project as liquid-liquid extraction is suggested as the 
separation technique. However, solvents need to fulfill also sustainability and economic criteria. In this 
project, a basic search was performed as the main objective was already achieved.

FUTURE WORK 
Taking into consideration the current limitations of the method some future perspectives are considered 
in this section. First of all, the reaction is considered to be the core of the process. Also, the output of 
the reaction determines the input of the downstream process separation. Consequently, it is of high 
importance to choose the best alternative pathway, in order to achieve a successful implementation of a 
reaction-separation scheme. For this, an extended database with reaction details or alternatives would 
be really useful. Similarly, with respect to the difficulty of applying the predictive model, expansion of 
the predictive model-based tools or addition of new ones to the already existing would facilitate the 
implementation of the methodology and reduce the required time to achieve solution. At this point it is 
important to be mentioned that a model has already been developed for the simulation of a reactor where 
the separation of the product occurs in the reactor, based on phase equilibrium phenomena. The method 
for this model included the transition of a single phase to a multiphase reactor by the addition of a phase. 
The phase systems considered is liquid-liquid equilibrium systems. The method includes the analysis of 
the reaction system and the selection of the second liquid phase. Prediction of liquid-liquid equilibrium 
make the application of the model difficult.

As it can be seen from the values, the organic components have similar values of parameters, while for 
water A significantly differs from the rest components. Moreover, the liquid density is calculated by 
assuming ideal mixing and depends on the composition. Therefore, it is obvious that the liquid density 
for all the systems have almost the liquid density of water calculated at T=298.15 K, taking into

results were given for all case studies.
