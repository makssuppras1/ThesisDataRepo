Abstract
A major limitation of the application of CRISPRôÙCas9 in gene therapy is the size of Cas9
(> 4 kb) and the potential of offôÙtarget editings. In this project, the incentive was to facilitate
the delivery of the enzyme into target cells by developing a splitôÙCas9 system, using the
split form of Cas9 nuclease together with a sortase enzyme enabling siteôÙspecific ligation
of proteins. To overcome the emergence of offôÙtarget effects, it was of interest to create
a remotely controllable splitôÙCas9 system mediated by magnetic induction.
To verify the ligation of the splitôÙCas9, HEK293 cells were transfected with the two plasôÙ
mids carrying the splitôÙCas9 genes together with the assembly plasmid containing sortase,
whereafter the successfully assembled splitôÙCas9 protein was confirmed by Western blot.
In the following, the aim was to apply the system to perform gene knockouts (KOs) by
targeting itgal and luc in Jurkat and HEK293ôÙluc cells, respectively. The KOs of luc perôÙ
formed with low amounts of the splitôÙCas9 and assembly plasmids proved to be the most
efficient. With the incentive to optimize the assembly of the splitôÙCas9 enzyme and its
delivery to the nucleus, nuclear localization signals were attached to the different genes
on the minicircle plasmids constituting the splitôÙCas9 system. Deducing from a luciferase
assay, the implementation of nuclear localization signals did not convey a higher luc KO
efficiency in HEK293ôÙluc cells compared to the original splitôÙCas9 system.
To create a system, which could enable the remote control of the splitôÙCas9, a paraôÙ
magnetic field was first used to induce an NFATôÙsensitive promoter coupled to the luc
gene in JurkatôÙluc cells transduced with three different calcium ion constructs. Exposed
to different intervals of a paramagnetic field, the TRPV4 ion channel mediated the highôÙ
est luciferase expression relative to preôÙinduction levels, as concluded from a luciferase
assay. Following induction using an automatic magnet, the JurkatôÙluc cells carrying the
same TRPV4 channel exhibited the highest luciferase expression when compared to uninôÙ
duced cells.
The conclusions drawn in this project contribute to the understanding of the splitôÙCas9
for gene KOs together with deeper insight into temporal regulation of gene expression
through magnetic induction, which emphasizes the potential of the splitôÙCas9 system for
genome editing in remotely controllable CARôÙT cells.

Introduction
Since 2017, six CARôÙT cell therapies have been approved for hematological cancers by
the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) [1]. A major restraining factor for the largeôÙ
scale clinical use of the autologous T cell is its costly and prolonged manufacturing proôÙ
cedure. Furthermore, the underlying onôÙtarget, offôÙtumor activity of CARôÙT cell therapy
is the pivotal cause of immuneôÙrelated adverse events (irAEs), such as cytokine release
syndrome (CRS) and neurotoxicity [1]. Henceforth, there is a great incentive to develop
universal allogeneic T cells with high antiôÙtumor activity. Clustered regularly interspaced
short palindromic repeat (CRISPR)ôÙCas9 is currently being employed to construct offôÙtheôÙ
shelf CARôÙT cells providing robust resistance to immune suppressive molecules. Owing
to its gene editing capability, CRISPRôÙCas9 presents an encouraging tool supporting a
paradigm shift in T cell immunotherapies, particularly in the field of nextôÙgeneration CARôÙ
T cells [2].
Due to the size of the Cas9 sequence and the underlying cumbersome delivery into tarôÙ
get cells, this project aims to develop a system based on the split form of the Cas9 proôÙ
tein together with a sortaseôÙmediated ligation of the two enzyme halves. Sortases are
epitopeôÙspecific enzymes that are powerful tools for siteôÙspecific ligation of peptides and
proteins. In this regard, the splitôÙCas9 enzyme is constructed with a specific amino acid
sequence integrated into the two parts of the Cas9 for sortase enzyme recognition. Only
upon coôÙexpression will the sortaseôÙmediated transôÙsplicing occur and the full Cas9 proôÙ
tein be reconstituted, as illustrated in Figure 1.1. Apart from the challenges presented by
the intracellular delivery of the large Cas9 cargo, the reduction in offôÙtarget effects assoôÙ
ciated with Cas9 activity remains to be solved. Hence, this project proposes to harness
Cas9 nuclease activity by implementing a paramagnetic field for remote control of gene
expression.

1.1
SplitôÙCas9
A major bottleneck in applying CRISPRôÙCas9 for therapeutic purposes, is the substanôÙ
tial size of the sequence, exceeding over 4ôÙ5 kb. Henceforth, finding an optimal delivery
system has become a formidable challenge [3]. While adenovirus has previously been
used as a delivery system for CRISPRôÙCas9, its immunogenicity makes it inappropriate
for gene therapy applications [4]. Although viralôÙbased transduction using a lentivirus
has proven efficient in delivering exogenous genes into T cells, potential insertional muôÙ
tagenesis raises concerns for its use in clinical settings [5]. Therefore, a recombinant
adenoôÙassociated virus (rAAV) is the standard delivery system for humans as it produces
very low immune responses. However, a limitation of this type of viral transduction is the
packaging capacity of the virus, which is confined to a maximum of 4.7 ã 5 kb [6]. Thus, an

rendering it inactive up until its reconstitution.
Prior research conducted by Truong et al. consisted in developing a splitôÙCas9 system usôÙ
ing inteins, protein splicing domains, which can excise themselves out of a sequence and

It was demonstrated that the activity of the intein splitôÙCas9 system, even when split into

1.1.1
SortaseôÙmediated ligation
SortaseôÙmediated ligation is a widely used biochemical method for protein modifications.
It is based on sortase A (StrA), a sequenceôÙspecific transpeptidase derived from StaphyloôÙ

NôÙpeptide. Following the release of the leaving group consisting of the glycine residue
and all downstream amino acids, a sortaseôÙbound thioester is concomitantly formed and
ligated to the nucleophile of the CôÙpeptide, usually presented by the öÝôÙamino group of the
NôÙterminal glycine [9] [10].

1.1.2
CARôÙT cells and research models
Chimeric antigen receptors (CAR)ôÙT cells are genetically modified T cells expressing enôÙ
gineered fusion proteins in the form of synthetic surface receptors, which allow to detect
and eliminate cancer cells through the identification of specific tumor antigens. Owing
to their ability to recognize surface antigens on cancer cells independent of the human
leukocyte antigens (HLA), CARôÙT cells are not limited by major histocompatibility complex
(MHC)ôÙrestricted antigen recognition and can therefore detect a broad range of antigen
targets [1]. Activation of CARôÙT cells is mediated by binding of the antigenôÙbinding domain
of CAR to the specific target, and the engineered cells can henceforth attack and eradôÙ
icate the targeted tumor cell. CARôÙT cells have proven to be a promising alternative to
conventional radiation, immunotherapy and chemotherapy for hematological cancer [1].
In order to investigate the proposed splitôÙCas9 system, a robust T cell model should be
employed. A model commonly used to study T cell biology and to develop T cell therapies

1.2
Magnetogenetics
Since its discovery, CRISPRôÙCas9 has proven to be a revolutionary technology in the
fields of genetic engineering and biotechnology. It has provided unprecedented opportuôÙ
nities for scientific research and medical application, especially in genomics and disease
research. Nonetheless, the CRISPRôÙCas9 system has wellôÙknown disadvantages such
as offôÙtarget effects due to doubleôÙstranded DNA breaks in untargeted genomic regions.
The consequences of offôÙtarget effects can lead to gene mutations, e.g. insertions, deleôÙ
tions and henceforth result in tumorigenic events. To overcome these challenges, reôÙ
searchers have developed a broad range of strategies aimed at enhancing the precision
of CRISPRôÙCas9 gene editing in recent years and many studies have addressed the toxôÙ
icities of the system in vivo by limiting the activity of the Cas9 with an inducible system,
hence reducing the probability of offôÙtarget effects [13].
1.2.1
Chemical inducers and optogenetics
To enhance the regulation of the CRISPRôÙCas9 system for potential clinical applications,
various approaches can be implemented. Among these strategies, one possibility is to
establish a remotely inducible system, i.e. an onôÙoff switch conferring temporal control
of gene expression. Currently, a variety of chemically inducible CRISPRôÙCas9 systems
have been reported since they allow for convenient and easy control of the system. HowôÙ
ever, in vivo applications exhibit severe challenges such as cytotoxicities as the chemical
agents can diffuse freely once inside the cells, thereby limiting the spatial resolution of the
gene editing. Another limitation of the in vivo application of a chemically inducible system
involves the residence time of the chemicals within the body, as some might persist for
a long time thus contributing to the complexity of regulating and switching off the activity
of Cas9. One approach taken by Zetsche et al. was to utilize the mammalian target of
rapamycin (mTOR) to develop a rapamycinôÙmediated inducible splitôÙCas9 system [14].
In the presence of rapamycin, the two fragments are able to dimerize and constitute an
active Cas9, due to a rapamycinôÙsensitive dimerization domain. While this approach sucôÙ
cessfully demonstrated the feasibility of an inducible splitôÙCas9 system in the presence of
the chemical agent rapamycin, its application in therapeutic settings is impractical due to
the underlying adverse events associated with these chemical inducers.
In recent years, multiple systems based on optical control of Cas9 activity have been unôÙ
der development, including photoactivatable splitôÙCas9 system based on blue and farôÙred
light [15] [8]. The former system has its limitations since blue light is unable to penetrate
deep tissues and can cause cytotoxicity if exposed for extended periods. On the other
hand, the farôÙred light splitôÙCas9 offers great advantages as it can penetrate deeper into
tissues as well as being a nonôÙinvasive method. Yu et al. designed a splitôÙCas9 sysôÙ
tem which relied on two splitôÙCas9 fusion proteins. Both splitôÙCas9 proteins had a highôÙ

1.2.2
Remote induction of gene expression
In the course of this project, an alternative approach for remote induction was explored
by investigating the effect of a paramagnetic field on the induction of gene expression
in mammalian cells. Three different calcium ion constructs were evaluated for their suitôÙ
ability and efficiency in gene regulation through magnetic induction. Ferritin iron redisôÙ
tribution to ion channels (FeRIC) is a magnetogenetic technique that employs magnetic
fields to activate transient receptor potential channels (TRPV), linked to cellular ferritins
[16]. The interaction between a magnetic field and ferritin has shown to elevate levels of
free cytosolic irons which in return initiates chemical reactions leading to the activation
of ferritinôÙcoupledôÙTRPV. Both Stanley et al. and Wheeler et al. demonstrated successôÙ
ful nonôÙinvasive in vivo temporal regulation of neural activity using magnetic fields, by
coupling a TRPV1 and TRPV4 to ferritin, respectively [17] [18]. The aforementioned ion
channels served as two of three constructs that were evaluated in this project. The third
construct consisted of a protein encoded by an electromagnetic perceptive gene (EPG),
derived from K. bicirrhis, which is thought to increase intracellular Ca2+ concentrations in
response to electromagnetic fields. It has been reported to be a membraneôÙassociated
protein, holding an NôÙterminal extracellular domain but its complete mechanism of action
remains unclear [19].
By combining these ion constructs with the sortaseôÙmediated splitôÙCas9 system, the ligôÙ
ation of the two Cas9 fragments could be under the remote control of a magnetic field.
The aforementioned could lay the foundation for a nonôÙinvasive approach with reduced
emergence of adverse effects when compared to optogenetics, such as deeper tissue
penetration, reduced phototoxicity and less heating.
For enhanced therapeutic efficiency, the inducible splitôÙCas9 system outlined in this report
offers a means to harness CARôÙT cell activity through remote control of gene expression.
Apart from mediating magnetically induced KO of exhaustion genes and thereby preventôÙ
ing a decrease in CARôÙT cell activity, the splitôÙCas9 system could also help to reduce
adverse events associated with excessive CARôÙT cell activity.

3.1
SortaseôÙMediated SplitôÙCas9 System
For the design of the splitôÙCas9 system, it was decided to split the Cas9 sequence at
amino acid position 573, i.e. between Glu573 and Cys574, based on research conducted
by Yuan et al [20]. To make the system compatible with sortase activity, a sortase recogôÙ
nition motif LPXTG was added to the CôÙterminus of the NôÙterminal Cas9 (Cas9(N)). FurôÙ
thermore, to make the CôÙterminal Cas9 (Cas9(C)) suitable for sortaseôÙmediated ligation,
an additional motif was placed at the NôÙterminus. This creates a TEV protease recogniôÙ
tion site, facilitating the cleavage of ENLYFQ/G and resulting in a glycine residue at the
NôÙterminus of the Cas9(C). These modifications allow for sortaseôÙmediated ligation of the
Cas9(N) and Cas9(C) upon coôÙexpression.
The herein employed plasmid constructs consisted of minicircle (MC) plasmids carrying
a scaffold/matrix attachment region (S/MAR) [21], thereby presenting an episomal vector
system with high stability and episomal retention in eukaryotic cells. The plasmids are
showcased in Figure 3.1, 3.2 and 3.3, and will in the following be referred to as MCCôÙ
Cas9, MCNôÙCas9 and MCTSôÙgRNA (assembly plasmid), respectively. The three conôÙ
structs constituting the complete splitôÙCas9 system are all under the control of a CMV
promoter and carry the antibiotic resistance gene KanR serving as a selectable marker.
Inspired by literature on inteinôÙmediated splitôÙCas9 systems [3] [20], the MCCôÙCas9 plasôÙ
mid carried a nuclear localization signal (NLS) downstream of the Cas9(C) gene. The
NLS mediates the transport of the translated Cas9(C) protein from the cytoplasm into the
nucleus, with the aim of transporting the fully reconstituted Cas9 into the nucleus after
ligation [22]. The MCTSôÙgRNA plasmid contained the gRNA and the enzymes involved in
the ligation of the two splitôÙCas9 halves, i.e. protease and sortase, as well as a mCherry
reporter gene.

4.1
SortaseôÙMediated SplitôÙCas9 System
Verification through both SDSôÙPAGE analysis and Western blot confirmed that the deôÙ
signed splitôÙCas9 system was able to form a reconstituted Cas9 through sortaseôÙmediated

spite achieving significant efficiencies of luc KO in HEK293ôÙluc cells with purified sortaseôÙ
mediated splitôÙCas9 derived from E.coli, a persistent challenge remains in matching the
efficacy of a fullôÙlength Cas9 nuclease when the sortaseôÙmediated splitôÙCas9 system is
expressed separately on minicircle plasmids.
Overall, exploring the potential of genetic editing with the proposed splitôÙCas9 technology
for gene KO in HEK293 and model T cells, i.e. Jurkat cells, has demonstrated a valuôÙ
able proficiency of the system in terms of delivery of the split form of the Cas9 enzyme,
thereby reducing the cargo and it presents an alternative approach for the delivery of the
large Cas9 sequence to mammalian cells.
4.2
Plasmid Promoter Optimization Library and Expression
in T Cells
Apart from investigating the application of the splitôÙCas9 system in mammalian cells, this
study sought to enhance heterologous gene expression in T cells for implementation in
the minicircle plasmid constructs of the splitôÙCas9 system. Chimeric antigen receptors, at
times, have proven to be difficult to express in T cells and it was thus of interest to optimize
the expression of a thirdôÙgeneration. Owing to the influence of the intronic region on
expression levels of transgenes carried on minicircle plasmids in T cells, a mutant library of
S/MAR minicircle plasmids, carrying eGFP downstream a CAR gene, was generated. The
assessment of GFP emission mediated by the promoter mutants relative to the standard
plasmid using FACS confirmed the successful establishment of promoter mutants with
enhanced CAR expression.
4.3

5
Future Perspectives
The evaluation of the sortaseôÙmediated splitôÙCas9 system for gene knockout and magôÙ
netically inducible gene expression in model T cells mark a significant milestone in the
development of sortaseôÙmediated splitôÙCas9 enzymes for genome editing in remotely
controllable CARôÙT cells. With the continuously growing insight into the intricate sortaseôÙ
mediated splitôÙCas9 system and the underlying molecular modifications enabling remote
control of this technology, there is still remarkable potential for refinement and optimizaôÙ
tion.
Further optimization of the splitôÙCas9 expression on minicircle plasmids in T cells, entails
the employment of the optimized CMV promoter containing the intronic region mediating
the highest expression levels. Following removal of the bacterial backbone, the stability
of the minicircle plasmids in T cells should be evaluated. To promote ligation efficiency of
the splitôÙCas9 system, new split sites could be generated and tested.
Having determined the best conditions for optimal gene expression in model T cells, the
CMV promoter in MCTSôÙgRNA should be replaced with the NFATôÙpromoter to enable
magnetic inducibility of the assembly machinery, while the two Cas9 halves are constituôÙ
tively expressed under the CMV promoter. Hereafter, it would be of interest to investigate
the KO efficiency of the splitôÙCas9 system when the assembly machinery is constitutively
expressed, while the two splitôÙCas9 fragments are under the remote control of a paramôÙ
agnetic field.
Additional optimization of gene expression could be performed by replacing the CMV or
NFAT promoter on the three plasmid constructs with other promoters activated by tranôÙ
scription factors, such as NFôÙö¤B, APôÙ1 and NFAT/APôÙ1. Further evaluations should be
conducted to assess the on/off kinetics related to magnetogenetics and to gain more inôÙ
sight into the durability of induced gene expression after being exposed to the paramagôÙ
netic field. Lastly, there is an incentive to investigate the effect of alternating paramagnetic
exposure times and intervals on the splitôÙCas9 efficiency, which would enable a deeper
understanding of the tunability of this technology.
