Abstract 
VII | P a g e  
 
Abstract 
The goal of this thesis was to model and analyze the performance of a solar heating plant in 
order to predict its energy output and outlet temperatures based on weather conditions. It 
was the aim to formulate an efficient and flexible model that could be integrated into energy 
system operation tools like Mentor Planner.  
The benefit of the developed model integrated in Mentor Planner is to optimize the operation 
of district heating systems including solar heating plants in terms of cost effectiveness, 
flexibility, stability, sustainability and security of supply.  
A reference plant in Gram, Denmark was used to develop the model and compare the results. 
Theoretical calculations of the thermal performance of a solar collector were conducted, 
which were the base of the model formulation. Two approaches were used to model the 
performance of the solar heating plant: one considering only a single collector and one that 
simulated solar collector rows as they are installed in large solar fields. The focus was on 
calculating time and accuracy. It was found that the single collector model could calculate the 
energy output much faster and in good agreement with the measured values of the reference 
plant. In addition, special attention was paid to the transportation inertia of the collector fluid, 
which plays an important role in modelling solar heating plants. A constant delay of 40 min 
for the fluid at the outlet was considered to be very accurate for the given plant. A sensitivity 
analysis of the main assumptions finalized the development of a base model that could be 
used to calculate the energy output of a solar heating plant. This model was validated for a 
period of 5 months (May вҖ“ September) in steps of 10 min. The energy output could be 
calculated for a maximum tolerance of Вұ0.3 MW in 82% of all cases. Adjustments on the 
validated model were performed in order to not only calculate the thermal performance of a 
solar heating plant, but also to predict it. The calculations were only based on plant specific 
parameters and weather data, which included solar irradiation on the collector tilt, ambient 
temperature and wind speed.  
 
In addition, investigations were performed to calculate the tilted irradiation on the collector 
based on radiation on a horizontal surface, which is more common accessible. Three different 
models: the isotropic sky model, the HDKR model and the Perez model were applied for these 
calculations. The results showed that all models overestimated the tilted radiation compared 
to the measurements. Days with stable weather conditions could be represented more 
accurate than days with unstable conditions. The main differences occurred due to cloud 
covers that could not be represented that well by the weather forecast. 
A discussion about the implementation of the model into Mentor Planner and the resulting 
advantages of operating this software as operation as well as planning tool finalized the 
research work. It was concluded that modern district heating systems could benefit from such 
software in order to increase cost effectiveness, competitiveness, stability and flexibility. 
However, more research is also recommended to develop the model but also the integration 
into Mentor Planner further.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Motivation 
Denmark has decided to be independent from fossil fuels by the year 2050. Instead of using 
coal, oil and gas, more and more green energy has been implemented to the market. By 
achieving this goal Denmark ensures its security of supply and contributes significantly to the 
reduction of greenhouse gases (Danish Energy Agency 2013). Sustainable solutions for 
producing power and heat, from e.g. solar, wind and hydro (wet and dry year), are more 
difficult to predict than conventional plants and are dependent on current weather 
conditions. Having various unpredictable technologies integrated within one system may lead 
to system instability and results into high fluctuation in prices. This applies especially to 
modern energy systems, which are presented in the next section. 
1.1.1 The modern energy system 
How power and heat will be produced in the future energy system is an important question. 
Many countries develop different solutions suitable for their needs in order to solve this 
issue. For instance, power could be provided by solar photovoltaic (PV) plants, but more 
common is wind power when looking at the Danish market. Big wind farms generate large 
amounts of electricity onshore as well as offshore. The development of wind power 
production in Denmark, but also from other sources is shown in the following figure:  
 
Figure 1: Development of electricity production in Denmark (C. F. B. Nielsen 2015) 
It can be seen that wind power plays an important role in the Danish power market. Already 
2014, the production of wind power was 39% compared to the total electricity consumption. 
The share on the market will even increase further in the next years as predicted in Figure 1. 
In addition, electricity is also generated by big power stations and local combined heat and 
power (CHP) plants. Especially local CHP plants are important for the future energy system to 
==PAGE:20==
Motivation 
2 | P a g e  
 
balance out the high fluctuation in production due to wind power but also to provide 
sufficient heat.  
Nowadays, a modern energy system includes usually one local CHP plant. In addition, it 
contains several production units to be able to provide heat at all times during a year at 
reasonable costs and in a sustainable way. Heat in Denmark is mostly supplied by district 
heating (DH) networks resulting in a contribution of 62% to the heat supply of the residential 
sector in 2010 (Li 2015). Apart from CHP plants, such local DH stations can include varies 
technologies. A good example of such system is located in Gram, South Jutland, Denmark. This 
modern DH system consists of a CHP unit, a gas boiler, a multi-fuel type boiler, an electric 
boiler, an electric heat pump, heat accumulation steel tank and use of surplus process heat. In 
addition, it has a large solar heating plant in combination with seasonal pit heat storage and 
covers a small amount of its electricity consumption by a solar PV plant. Due to its variety of 
sustainable solutions and its ability to respond to electricity prices (roughly +/- 8 MW), Gram 
was used as reference case for the investigations of this study to represent a modern DH 
system. 
Such a complex system makes it possible to produce heat at low costs as the heat production 
units compete in supplying the annual heat production. In summer, when the sun shines the 
solar heating plant can produce heat almost at no costs to cover the demand and more 
important to store the heat in the seasonal storage for usage in winter. In cases, where the 
electricity price might be very low or even negative, the electric boiler can use the access 
electricity and produce heat. When the electricity price is below a certain level, the heat pump 
can produce heat at a competitive price. On the other hand when the electricity price is above 
a certain level, the CHP plant can be used to produce heat at a competitive price. In winter, 
the solar heating plant cannot be used that much due to the lack of sunshine. In cases no 
production plants are competitive against the estimated maximal competitive price, the heat 
can be provided by the seasonal storage (J. E. Nielsen 2015). To control such system as 
described in an optimal way it is important to have accurate estimations of the energy output 
of the solar heating plant. This allows optimizing the operation of the remaining units and 
results in a cost effective and sustainable DH system.

The previous chapter has shown the necessity of solar heating plants and seasonal storages 
for the energy system to generate heat and power at low costs. Especially Denmark is front 
runner of installed solar heating plants as shown in Figure 2:

The boom of large solar heating plants in Demark can be explained by the fact that they can 
be connected to the very advanced DH network. Another very important reason is that they 
have been optimized in order to become cost effective and compatible with conventional 
plants. The technology is simple, lasts over 20 years and is very efficient (Dyrelund 2013b). 
This is especially noticed when building in large scale. The larger the solar heating field and

CO2 neutral and therefore reduce global warming, do not produce any noise nor smoke, 
ensures security of supply and are independent on fuel prices (ARCON Solar 2012). This 
underlines the importance of large solar heating plants for the future energy system, 
especially in Denmark.  
The latest information estimate that the total area of installed large-scale solar heating 
plants in Denmark in 2016 will be 1.3 million mВІ or 3.4 times more than the area in 2014 
(Danish District Heating Association 2016). It is estimated that the total potential in Denmark 
is around 8 million mВІ (Dyrelund 2010).

The solar heating plant in Figure 3 consists of several collector connected in series. These

capacity is much smaller than for a seasonal storage, but a high temperature level can be 
reached within shorter period.  
Controlling such DH system in a cost effective way can be very complex. In order to even 
optimize the operation further, several tools can be used. These take into account power 
prices, production costs or available capacities. One of them is Mentor Planner and is 
described in the next chapter. 
1.1.3 Operation optimization tool: Mentor Planner 
Mentor Planner is an optimization tool that ensures cost-effective production of power and 
heat as well as distribution for operators of DH systems. The centerpiece of this software is 
the prediction of the heat demand for up to 6 days. The calculations are based on a neuronal 
network using historical data and are performed in short time. In addition, the software takes 
into account the price development on the power market. Therefore, special attention is paid 
to the first day of forecasting due to the consideration of the day-ahead power market. This 
==PAGE:23==
Motivation 
5 | P a g e  
 
allows optimizing the operation schedule of production units depending on their running 
costs and expected revenue for heat and/or power. Also optimization of the flow temperature 
and peak load operation is part of Mentor Planner that makes it valuable for DH companies. 
вҖңThe planning is conducted on the basis of a visualization of SCADA-data, weather data and 
extrapolation of heat consumption. Power plans and expected gas consumption are 
automatically sent to the TSO, the gas supplier and other relevant third partsвҖқ (OE3i ApS 
2015).  
The technologies implemented in the optimization tool are CHP plants, heat pumps, electric 
boilers and short term heat storages. The software also integrates pure heat production from 
e.g. heat boilers, biomass plants (wood chips and straw) and waste-to heat plants. But it is 
desired to implement even further technologies such as district cooling (DC), aquifer storage, 
seasonal storage and solar heating in order to optimize modern energy systems.  
The corner stone of this ambitious plan shall be the integration of solar heating. Estimating 
the energy output of a solar heating plant can be difficult, because it depends on weather 
conditions that are unstable and not easy to predict. However, it is important to know the 
expected output, because it can improve the operation schedule and decision making of 
running the most suitable production units for certain periods in terms of cost effectiveness. 
Solar heating in combination with seasonal storages can also help to stabilize the system and 
to reduce fluctuation of power prices caused by unpredictable power production of e.g. wind 
or PV.  
Furthermore, Mentor Planner could also be used as planning tool. The great advantage would 
be that the same software could be used for planning and operation of an energy system. 
This would avoid discrepancies between different software used for these purposes. No time 
and cost would be lost by installing an additional operating system.   
1.1.4 Planning and calculation tools 
There exist several tools on the market that can be used for planning solar heating systems 
and performance calculations. Common tools are: F-easy, Fjernsol-II (v145), SOLID Concept 
tool, SDH Online Calculation Tool and SUNSTORE-4. An overview and more information is 
given in (Denn 2014). Most of them can be accessed for free and provide pre-sizing of solar 
heating plants as well as economical aspects. Another promising tool has been developed for 
simple calculations for solar heating plants with a seasonal storage (Guadalfajara 2014). This 
tool has been developed using the software EES (Engineering Equation Solver) and is based 
on simple calculations that require small amounts of data and calculating time. It can be used 
for вҖңanalysis, pre-design and performance evaluation of central solar heating plants with 
seasonal storageвҖқ (Guadalfajara 2014). In addition, more advanced software can be 
purchased: ENERGY PRO, POLYSUN or TRNSYS. Such commercial tools can consider more than 
only a solar heating plant and can be used to plan an entire energy system.

1.2 
Objectives 
The research question of this thesis is: How should an efficient and flexible model for 
predicting the energy output of a solar heating plant be formulated for the implementation 
into energy system operation tools like Mentor Planner? 
 
To answer this question a model will be developed to calculate the thermal performance of a 
reference solar heating plant and the results will be analyzed in relation to that plant. The 
purpose of this is to verify the model first and to investigate the accuracy of the model 
determining the energy output and outlet temperature of the reference plant based on 
measured input parameters. 
The analysis will be continued aiming to modify the model further to predict the energy 
output only from weather data. This includes ambient temperature, wind speed and solar 
irradiation on the collector tilt.  
In addition, investigations how irradiation on a tilted surface can be calculated based on 
global radiation on a horizontal plane will follow. This is done, because weather forecasts that 
include global radiation are more common.   
Finally, the suitability of the model for the software tool Mentor Planner and possible 
benefits of the integration will be addressed. Further discussions will point out advantages 
using the model integrated in Mentor Planner as planning tool for new solar heating plants in 
combination with CHP plants, heat pumps and heat storages etc..

The results of the model formulation were compared to a reference solar heating plant in 
order to analyze accuracy and applicability of the model. The reference plant in Gram was 
used. It is located in Jutland, Denmark. The solar heating plant is part of an entire DH system 
as briefly described in chapter 1.1.1. This includes a CHP plant (6.5 MWth and 5.5 MWel.), one 
gas boiler (4.5 MW), a multi-fuel type boiler (5 MW), an electric boiler (10 MW), an electric heat 
pump (0.9 MW, COP=4.5), a heat accumulation steel tank (2,300 mВі) and the use of surplus 
process heat. In addition, it has a large solar heating plant (32 MW, 44,836 mВІ) consisting of 
three solar fields (SF), which in combination with a seasonal pit heat storage (122,000 mВі) 
can cover around 50% of the annual heat production. A minor part of its electricity 
consumption can be supplied by a solar PV plant, but this is not relevant to this thesis, as the 
plant is connected to the electricity grid. An overview of the system configuration can be 
found in Figure 4: 
 
Figure 4: Schematic drawing of DH system in Gram

Figure 4 shows a basic drawing of the DH system in Gram and how the units are connected 
with each other. The SFs 1, 2 and 3 are shown exemplary for SF 1 in a simplified version with 
only 2 collector rows connected in parallel. One row consists of 13 collectors connected in 
series (Figure 4). SF 2 and 3 have instead even up to 17 serial connected collectors. This is 
very common in practice and the number of collectors in one row can be up to 20 (Per Alex 
SГёrensen 2012b). But there have been plants designed with even 24 collectors in a row, 
which is the limit (e.g. OksbГёl solar heating plant). There are several advantages of having 
collectors connected in series. Having a lower temperature level, increases the thermal 
performance of a solar collector (Furbo 2005b). This will also be shown later in Figure 7. This 
is why a low temperature is desired, which is the case at the first collectors of a row. The 
temperature increase in a row can be very high e.g. from 40В°C вҖ“ 85В°C (Bava et al. 2015). 
Therefore, it is recommended to use inexpensive collectors at the beginning of a row and 
more expensive collectors with an extra foil on the cover or a special cover treatment for 
collectors placed in the end of a row. The latter collectors reduce heat losses, which is 
especially important at high temperatures (Bava et al. 2015). On the other hand special foils 
reduce the amount of irradiance reaching the absorber. This is an additional reason why 
collectors without such foil should be used in the beginning (Bava et al. 2015). The 
combination of different collector types is applied for SF 2 and SF 3, but not for SF 1. Another 
advantage of having collectors connected in a row is a more homogeneous flow distribution 
due to a higher pressure drop in that row. A homogeneous flow results in a higher efficiency 
but also in a reduced total pressure loss (Knabl & Fink 2014). Uneven flows on the other hand 
can result in higher temperatures in certain parts causing stagnation. An irregular 
temperature distribution between collectors also reduces thermal efficiency. In addition, 
having rows reduce piping lengths and therefore investment costs (Knabl & Fink 2014). 
Having shorter pipes also reduces the heat loss (Furbo 2005b). 
When the solar heating plant shown in Figure 4 is in operation useful heat will be transferred 
to the DH system by heat exchangers called SF 1, SF 2 and SF 3 according to the number of 
field. The three heat exchangers divide the glycol-water cycle from the water cycle.  
It can be decided to either fill the seasonal storage or to transfer the energy further to the 
remaining DH system. For the first operation mode, SF 2 and SF 3 can fill the storage at two 
different heights depending on the temperature level. But also SF 1, the CHP unit or the heat 
pump (HP) are able to charge the storage when the energy is transferred via an additional 
heat exchanger and valves are in correct position. Filling the storage using the CHP unit or 
heat pump can make sense when the electricity price is very high and the heat demand as 
well as the capacity of the accumulation tank are already fully covered.  
The second operation mode is used to utilize solar heat directly. The fluid can be mixed with 
the output of the remaining units depending on the temperature level and needs. This can be 
done by three valves shown above the accumulation tank in Figure 4. The lower valve is open 
when the electrical heat pump is in operation, and the flow temperature is below a specified 
value. But it is also open for preheating the transmission line. The valve in the middle mixes 
the solar heat with the energy output of the remaining units. The upper valve is used to heat 
the inlet fluid of the gas boiler which already got preheated after passing the smoke gas 
condenser (Con).

2.1.1 Control of solar heating plant 
Each solar field in Gram can be controlled individually from each other. In order to start the 
operation of one field on the primary side (glycol-water cycle), the calculated energy output 
per mВІ based on Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 has to be above a set point of 50 W/mВІ. The calculated value 
is based on the measured radiation, the ambient temperature, the flow temperature and the 
installed solar collector as explained later. The operation of a field stops in the same manner 
when the value drops below 5 W/mВІ. The pump on the secondary loop (water cycle after heat 
exchanger) is activated or deactivated, when the outlet temperature is above or below a 
defined set point.  
The solar heating plant is controlled by a variable volume flow in order to obtain a constant 
outlet temperature. This set point can be changed manually on daily basis due to the weather 
and expected heat demand. In addition, a minimum volume flow rate is defined before the 
plant starts its operation. In order to obtain a homogenous flow distribution in the solar 
collector rows, a valve is placed at the beginning of each row. The valve at the end of a row is 
a three-way valve used for venting the collector rows during commissioning.  
An alternative method to control a solar heating plant in general is to keep the volume flow 
rate constant. This on the other hand leads to a variable outlet temperature (Furbo et al. 
2015). A general problem that occurs when operating a solar heating plant with rather simple 
controllers is the oscillation of the outlet temperature when this is the controlled parameter. 
This is caused by the transportation delay of the collector fluid over long distances (S. Zunft 
1995). This phenomenon was also noticed at the plant in Gram and was part of the 
investigations as described in chapter 2.6 and 3.2.

2.1.3 Accuracy of measurements 
The accuracy of the measurements used as reference is important. Information about the 
instruments for measuring the volume flow and temperature at the plant were not available. 
But most common devices to detect the volume flow are electromagnetic flowmeter. 
Alternatively, ultrasonic flowmeter, vortex flow meter or flowmeter with rotating impeller are 
used (European Solar Thermal Industry Federation 2007). The accuracy of an electromagnetic 
flowmeter is very high (0.2%) (Ke & Slattery 2014). Measuring the temperature of a liquid is 
usually done by thermocouples or platinum resistances. For solar heating plants above 1000 
mВІ sensors with an accuracy of no more than 2% are recommended. In addition, a correction 
factor has to be applied when a glycol water mixture is used. These correction factors have a 
tolerance of Вұ2% (European Solar Thermal Industry Federation 2007).  
In order to be able to calculate the useful energy output of a solar collector, it was vital to 
know how much solar radiation actually hits the surface of the collector. This could be done 
by applications measuring the solar radiation. Most common instruments used in Europe are 
pyranometers measuring the total radiation with an uncertainty of Вұ5% (Duffie & Beckman 
2013a). Integrating the data over a certain period of time defines the solar irradiation. 
Integration errors can even increase the inaccuracy. In addition, it was shown that calibration 
of pyranometers changes when measurements were conducted for tilted radiation. A 
correction factor of 1-2% was detected for such cases (Duffie & Beckman 2013a). 
It is important to understand that the measured data have a certain tolerance in accuracy 
when used as reference to compare and validate the developed model. 
2.2 
Performance of solar heating plants 
A flat plate solar collector as it was investigated for this study consists of a cover and 
absorber plate that are both within an aluminum frame. The absorber consists of several 
parallel copper pipes that contain the collector fluid. The collector is insulated to the back and 
the sides to reduce heat loss. An overview of a solar collector can be found on the datasheet 
in the appendix. The performance of solar heating plants and in particular of solar collectors is 
influenced by different factors: collector design, weather and operational conditions (Fan 
2015b). A well designed collector is the key role. It should include a good insulation, consists 
of an efficient absorber plate and cover glass. This ensures that maximum possible solar 
energy is absorbed and transferred further to the collector fluid. The influence of operational 
conditions is determined by fluid inlet temperature, flow rate, thermal properties, but also by 
the orientation of the plant and the collector tilt (Fan 2015b). Weather conditions are the

factors that cannot be influenced and consist of ambient temperature, wind speed, solar 
irradiation, diffuse fraction, incidence angle and sky temperature (Fan 2015b).

(Fan & Furbo n.d.) resulting in recommendations that the calculation of the mean fluid

temperature is used. (Furbo et al. 2015) have also shown that the actual thermal 
performance of a solar collector can differ up to 11% compared to the stated one based on 
the efficiency expression from the data sheet.

2.3.10 Dust and shading 
Dust and shading should be considered when investigating collector performances. However, 
since these factors are difficult to estimate and to generalize, assumptions are made based 
on (Duffie & Beckman 2013b). It is referred to various experiments on the effects of dust and 
shading. It is recommended to have a reduced absorbed energy S of 1% in moderate climates 
accounting for dirt and also 1% for shading. This is also done during the performed 
calculations for this work. In general, a high collector tilt of e.g. 45В° reduces the loss due to 
dust, because rain cleans the cover on regular basis. Shading mostly appears when the 
position of the sun is low at the horizon. This leads to shading effects from surrounded 
collector rows. 
The newly installed solar heating plant in Chile is in this respect an extreme case. The plant is 
close to the equator at the driest place on earth, which is very dusty. The production is 
estimated to be 4 times the production in Denmark and the collector tilt is only a few 
degrees. Therefore, production and shading is not a problem, whereas dust can reduce the 
production significantly unless it is cleaned by a special vacuum cleaner on regular basis 
(Ramboll 2013; Global Solar Thermal Energy Council 2013).

2.4.3 Reflection from the ground 
The reflection from the ground is often referred to as albedo ПҒg, which is defined as вҖңthe ratio 
between the solar radiation received by a surface and the radiation which is reflected backвҖқ 
(Dragsted & Furbo 2011). The albedo has an influence especially when the ground is covered 
by snow and its value is typically assumed to be 0.7. With no snow cover a constant value of 
0.2 is usually used. A detailed investigation of the albedo was done by (Dragsted & Furbo 
2011) for a solar heating plant in Greenland. The results suggest a monthly albedo depending 
on the difference between solar azimuth angle И–s and azimuth angle of the plant И–. However, 
the albedo was in a range of 0.1 and 0.2 for the summer months which were investigated for 
this study. This variation has a very small effect on the tilted irradiation. Therefore, a 
constant albedo of 0.2 was still assumed for this study.

2.5 
Method of developing a base model 
The software EES was used to calculate the energy output of the solar collector field based 
on the equations in chapter 2.3. The base model was built up in two ways. On the one hand 
calculations were performed with only one collector. The energy outcome was multiplied by 
the number of collectors in order to simulate the output of the entire solar field. On the other 
hand 13 solar modules were connected in series as it is the case at the plant in Gram. The 
energy output was then multiplied with the number of rows. The temperature along these 
modules increased steadily so that the outlet temperature of one collector was the inlet 
temperature of the next one. The last collector had the highest temperature comparable to 
the outlet temperature when considering only one solar collector. Both simulations were 
performed with the total volume flow rate and inlet temperature of the plant in Gram as 
inputs. The volume flow was divided based on the given situation for a collector or row. In 
this way the solar heating plant was simulated realistically.  
Subject of this analysis was the calculating time of the model and if simulating only one 
collector was sufficient to use it for further investigations. But also the energy output was 
compared with the real values measured at the plant and with each other. The deviation for 
each time step and for the accumulated heat is presented. Furthermore, the outlet 
temperatures are compared as well.  
Two days of each month were chosen from the entire period of available data (08.05.вҖ“
29.09.2015). One day represented a sunny day with steady weather conditions and the other 
one a cloudy day where the irradiation intensity changed rapidly. In this way it was looked at 
different irradiation profiles, but also at the influence of the time of the year. The following 
days in year 2015 were chosen for this investigation: 11.05., 25.05., 01.06., 12.06., 02.07., 
22.07., 06.08., 20.08., 06.09. and 20.09.  
2.5.1 Simulation of 1 collector 
The problem was formulated in a way that the model solved the equations in an iterative 
manner. First, an estimate was made for the mean absorber plate Tpm, mean fluid Tm and 
cover temperature Tc. The absorber plate temperature Tp was calculated as the mean 
temperature of the plate Tpm as it is done in Eq. 57. Then, the heat transfer coefficients h1-h4 
as well as the top heat loss coefficient were calculated based on Eq. 13, 16, 18, 19 and 75. 
Eq. 24 allowed calculating a new cover temperature. This value was used as new input to 
repeat the procedure until Tc remained unchanged with a tolerance of 10-6. The next step 
was to determine the remaining heat loss coefficients at the back and at the edge by means 
of Eq. 28 and 32. Finally, the total heat loss of the solar collector was calculated based on Eq. 
11.  
In the following, the fin efficiency factor F was found by Eq. 33 and the collector efficiency 
factor FвҖҷ by Eq. 40. Before determining FвҖҷ, the fluid properties had to be calculated as it is 
explained in Appendix D. This was necessary so that the convective heat transfer coefficient 
from the tube to the fluid could be calculated. The next parameters, the collector heat 
removal factor FR and the collector flow factor FвҖҷвҖҷ, were determined by solving Eq. 51 and Eq. 
52, respectively.

Now, the only parameter still missing to calculate the energy output of the collector was the 
absorbed solar energy S based on Eq. 10. The solar irradiation GSun was measured at the same 
level as the collector tilt, so that the incidence angle became zero and therefore IAM equaled 
1. An incidence angle of zero, also allowed to determine the transmission of the cover as 
described in chapter 2.3.1. In addition, the losses due to shading and dirt as stated in chapter 
2.3.10 were considered. Finally, the useful energy Qu of the solar collector was calculated by 
Eq. 53.  
In the end the new temperatures for Tm and Tpm and also the outlet temperature Tout were 
determined by means of Eq. 55, 57 and 47, respectively.  
The described procedure was repeated until the mean temperatures for the absorber plate 
and the fluid as well as the outlet temperature did not differ with a tolerance of 10-6 
compared to their values of the previous repetition. The energy output Qu times the number 
of collectors of the entire solar field gave the calculated solar heat Qu,tot.  
2.5.2 Simulation of collectors in a row 
The input parameters for the simulation of collectors in a row were the same as for a single 
collector. In addition, the number of collectors in a row was defined to be 13, which is the 
case for most of the collector rows placed in solar field 1 in Gram. Therefore, the total volume 
flow of the plant was divided by the number of collector rows when calculating the energy 
output of one row.  
Considering rows in the model formulation had as consequence that the entire procedure 
described in the previous chapter was repeated for each collector of one row. In addition, the 
inlet temperature only applied for the first collector of a row. The calculated outlet 
temperature after each collector was used as input for the next one while the volume flow 
remained constant. This process was repeated until the final outlet temperature of the last 
collector was found. In addition, the whole procedure was repeated until the absorber plate, 
mean fluid and outlet temperature did not change anymore considering a tolerance of 10-6. 
The useful energy output of the entire field was then calculated by the energy output of the 
rows times the number of rows. These values were then compared with the existing data of 
the plant and with the calculated ones for the single collector approach.

2.7 
Method of sensitivity analysis  
A sensitivity analysis of the main assumptions was conducted after the investigation of the 
fluid inertia was completed. This was done before the analysis of the entire period of 
available data was conducted (5 months), because otherwise it would require too much time. 
First, it was investigated if the properties of the air between the cover and absorber plate 
have a linear temperature dependency. This was done, because the simple mean value of 
cover and absorber plate temperature was used to describe the properties. The properties of 
the collector fluid on the other hand were calculated based on its actual temperature 
calculated by Eq. 55. Only for the implementation of a time delay, the specific heat and 
density were necessary and were based on the mean value between inlet and outlet 
temperature. But still, all fluid properties were investigated to verify the decisions made. In 
addition, assumed parameters were analyzed, which were the extinction coefficient K=12 m-1 
and the emissivity of the collector glass cover Пөc=0.88. Also, the absorptance and the 
emissivity of the absorber plate were varied, because their values are stated with a tolerance 
of Вұ0.02 on the datasheet of the collector, which can be found in the appendix.  
2.8 
Method of model validation 
In order to validate the model, the analysis of 10 selected days was extended to the entire 
period of available data from 08.05.2015 вҖ“ 29.09.2015. The results of the previous model 
approaches and from the sensitivity analysis were taken into consideration for the final 
model that was validated. The total number of investigated data points was 20,880 from 
which only 9,256 were considered, because either the calculated or the measured energy 
output were above zero. Values e.g. in the night were not taken into account. Input 
parameters to the model were only the measured irradiation on the collector tilt, the volume 
flow rate, the ambient temperature and inlet temperature besides the parameters describing 
the installed solar collector and plant size.  
The calculated energy output, the accumulated energy output over the entire period as well 
as the outlet temperatures were compared with corresponding values measured at the 
reference plant in Gram.  
2.9 
Method of prediction model 
After the model validation procedure over a period of 5 months was completed, the model 
was further modified, because the validated model used the volume flow rate and the inlet 
temperature from the plant in Gram as inputs. These are parameters that are usually 
unknown, when the useful energy shall be predicted.  
==PAGE:56==

38 | P a g e  
 
In order to avoid that the volume flow rate and the inlet temperature were inputs, the 
prediction model was changed in a way that the minimum volume flow rate of the existing 
plant was taken as initial start value. Then, the same procedure as it is described in chapter 
2.5.1 was used to calculate all values including the outlet temperature in an iterative process. 
If this temperature happened to be above a maximum level, then the outlet temperature was 
fixed at Tmax. Instead, the corresponding volume flow rate was calculated repeatedly until its 
value did not change anymore with a tolerance of 10-6 compared to its previous value. In this 
way either the minimum volume flow rate was fixed and the outlet temperature varied or the 
maximum outlet temperature was fixed and the volume flow rate was calculated. Finally, the 
calculated energy output could be extracted. The minimum volume flow rates and maximum 
temperatures were obtained from historical data of the solar heating plant in Gram and can 
be found in Appendix H. 
The prediction model was used to analyze the entire period from 08.05.2015 вҖ“ 29.09.2015. 
In addition, two more investigations were conducted: one with a correction factor in order to 
improve the accuracy and a second one with a variable correction factor depending on the 
time of the year. The latter one was used to count for possible effects occurring throughout 
the year. This could be shading but also the influence of the fluid inertia. Both change over 
the year, but were assumed constant for this investigation. The correction factor was applied 
at the absorbed energy S found in Eq. 10. All three model versions were compared with each 
other and the results are presented in the same time to avoid repetition.

2.10 Method of calculating tilted irradiation 
So far the measured irradiation on the collector tilt was used for the model validation and the 
prediction model as described in the previous chapters. But in order to be able to actually 
predict the thermal performance of a solar heating plant these values cannot be used as 
inputs. Instead, the tilted irradiation was calculated based on global radiation as described in 
chapter 2.4 where the following models were presented: the isotropic sky model, the HDKR 
model and the Perez model. These models were compared and their suitability analyzed for 
the plant in Gram.  
Forecasts of global radiation were available on hourly basis for Gram provided by the weather 
service StormGeo. Their predictions are based on a three dimensional mathematical model of 
the atmosphere considering the extraterrestrial radiation of the sun, air molecules, reflection 
from clouds and other surfaces and much more. The model formulation is very advanced and 
developed. But still, the calculations have also some weak points, which are mostly the 
representation of the cloud cover. The scale of the clouds is often smaller than the resolution 
of the model formulation. This information was received from StormGeo.  
The measured tilted irradiation at the plant was given in time steps of 10 min. As explained in 
chapter 2.1.3 these measured values can have an uncertainty of Вұ5%.   
The investigation was conducted for the entire period of 5 months between May and 
September 2015. The results present however only the 10 days previously used: 11.05., 
25.05., 01.06., 12.06., 02.07., 22.07., 06.08., 20.08., 06.09. and 20.09.2015.

3.1.3 Conclusion of developing a base model 
The previous chapters contain the comparison of two models to calculate the useful energy 
of a solar heating plant and the measured data of the solar field in Gram. One model 
simulated 13 in series connected collectors and the other one only one single collector. Both 
models simulated the energy output in a good way. However, certain aspects like the fluid 
inertia were not taken into account. Due to the calculating time the model simulating a single 
solar collector was preferred. To compensate the lack of series connected modules in this 
model a correction factor of 1.0234 was introduced. This corrected model was used for 
further investigations. The calculated outlet temperatures were higher than the measured 
ones. They also started to increase earlier in the morning and dropped faster in the evening. 
The overall pattern however was similar than the real one. It seemed that the temperature 
profile was shifted by approx. 1 hour compared to the measured values. As next step the fluid 
inertia was part of the investigation to improve the current model further.

3.3.7 Conclusion of sensitivity analysis 
The sensitivity analysis has shown that assuming the properties of air between the cover and 
absorber plate being a linear function of temperature is a good approximation for all four 
calculated parameters. Such dependency is also a reasonable assumption for the specific 
heat, density and thermal conductivity of the collector fluid, but not for the kinematic 
viscosity. Furthermore, the extinction coefficient has to be chosen with care, since it has a 
significant effect on the calculated energy output. The results were on the other hand less 
sensitive to the emissivity of the cover. Changing the absorptance and emissivity of the 
absorber in the given tolerance showed that the energy output was affected. Therefore, the 
measured useful energy at the plant could result from the stated values of the data sheet, 
but also from the lower or upper limits of the given tolerance. This is an uncertainty factor 
that could not be avoided. Overall, the model formulation for the investigated 10 days has 
shown its applicability.

Figure 35 and Figure 36 show that for stable and unstable weather conditions the prediction 
model is able to calculate an accurate energy output, while the temperatures and volume 
flows follow a similar trend than measured at the plant. It can be seen that the energy output 
follows the same pattern as the volume flow, which is reasonable.  
Looking at the outlet temperatures it is noticed that the predicted ones are lower and closer 
to the measured values. This is because of the defined maximum temperatures based on the 
plant configurations (Appendix H). This also explains why a correction factor of 0.96 was 
necessary. Lower temperatures result in a higher efficiency of the collector as shown in 
Figure 7. As consequence, the energy output increases. It is also observed that the maximum 
temperature for the 25.05.2015 could have been selected at 82.5 В°C instead of 77.5 В°C 
meeting the real outlet temperature. To compensate the lower temperature, the volume flow 
rate is higher compared to the real data. The energy output shown for the 11.05.2015 begins 
and stops earlier. To avoid this, additional start and stop criteria could be implemented. This 
was not done, but could be analyzed in future work. In the same diagram it is noticed that 
peaks are not represented as good as for the validation model, even though the time delay is 
considered, see 13:50 and 14:30 on 11.05. This is because the maximum temperature is set 
to a constant value. Therefore, smaller irradiation drops are not represented in the 
temperature profile. This happens only for unstable weather conditions in Figure 36 at e.g. 
13:10 and 14:20.  
The inlet temperatures are modelled in a satisfactory way for the 25.05. The 11.05. on the 
other hand differs from 11:00 вҖ“ 16:00 significantly by approx. 10 В°C. Having a too low inlet 
temperature affects the volume flow, while the outlet temperature is fixed at its maximum 
value. As consequence, the volume flow is lower than the measured one to compensate the 
higher temperature difference. It should be mentioned that the control function of the inlet 
temperature adjusts itself over time, but that the start date of the investigated period is the 
08.05.2015. Therefore, the effect of the control mechanism after just 3 days is not that 
developed yet, especially not for such a sudden increase as shown in Figure 35. Further 
research on optimizing the control of inlet temperature is desired.  
3.5.4 Conclusion of the prediction model 
The previous chapters have shown that the prediction model can be used to estimate the 
useful energy output of the solar heating plant in Gram within a certain tolerance. Three 
versions with no correction factor, a variable correction factor and a constant correction 
factor of cs=0.96 were investigated and the latter one was chosen to be most accurate of the 
investigated models. It means that the energy output, outlet temperatures and volume flow 
rates can be predicted when only the ambient temperature, wind speed and the measured 
irradiation on the collector tilt are known. The energy output could be calculated within a 
tolerance of Вұ0.3 MW in 75% of the cases. The calculated outlet temperatures were 
especially for energy outputs above 1 MW much more accurate than the previously validated 
model.

4. Discussion 
The previous chapters have shown how a model has been developed to predict the energy 
output of a solar heating plant. For this neither the volume flow nor current inlet temperature 
are necessary. The calculations were based on measured values of the irradiation on the solar 
collectors and gave good results. In combination with the knowledge of converting global 
radiation to a tilted surface, it seems feasible to predict the energy output of a solar heating 
plant based only on weather data that include ambient temperature, wind speed and global 
radiation. However, the accuracy clearly depends on the quality of the forecast for the global 
radiation. Especially cloud cover seems to have an immense effect on the calculations when 
not being considered in the predictions. Therefore, the accuracy of the developed model 
might decrease especially for days with unstable weather conditions.  
4.1 
Advantages for Mentor Planner 
The developed model has been successfully implemented into the software Mentor Planner 
by the company OE3i giving the same results when the same input parameters were used. 
Only adjustments due to the different programming language had to be done. Therefore, the 
compatibility is given. A next step would be to test the module integrated in the software 
further using a reference or fictive energy system. To simulate a solar heating plant in Mentor 
Planner the predicted energy output could be considered as a separate heat supply with no 
costs. Therefore, it would always have priority unless constraints would restrict otherwise. 
Knowing the output of the solar heating plant and the expected heat demand reduce the 
necessary supply of the remaining units, such as CHP plant, boiler, etc. Thus, less heat has to 
be provided by these plants. The optimization of generating the reduced amount of heat 
would be done in the traditional way of Mentor Planner. Such implementation seems simple 
but reasonable.  
Since the outlet temperatures of the solar heating plant are also calculated within the 
prediction model with good accuracy, Mentor Planner could also be used to optimize the 
control of that temperature. As described in chapter 2.1.1 the set point of the outlet 
temperature is defined on daily basis by the plant operator, who is usually engrossed in work. 
In order to find an optimum value, Mentor Planner could be used to calculate the expected 
heat demand of the DH network. When this is known, it can be compared with the expected 
energy output of the solar heating plant at a defined outlet temperature. In case 
supplementary heat is necessary, the software can check if additional units can produce 
energy at a higher temperature than needed for the DH network. In such cases, the outlet 
temperature of the solar heating plant could be lowered in order to increase its energy output 
shown on its efficiency curve (Figure 7). In the same time less energy has to be produced by 
the conventional unit at the constant temperature. The desired temperature for the DH 
network would be based on the mixture of the output of the conventional unit at high 
temperature and the solar heating plant at low temperature. 
The short calculating time of the developed model should also be mentioned, which is an 
important factor to consider when optimizing the real time operation schedule. Another 
benefit of the solar heating module is that Mentor Planner can be installed in energy systems

will reduce costs and the use of resources resulting in increased sustainability and

5. Conclusion 
The following can be concluded when looking at the results obtained during the work for this 
thesis: 
The energy output of a solar heating plant can be predicted and calculated based on 
simulating only one collector to reduce calculating time.  
In order to compensate the lack of simulating collectors connected in series, a correction 
factor was introduced. For the investigated solar heating plant in Gram, a correction factor of 
1.0234 for the absorbed energy in the model formulation was found to be suitable.  
The results have shown that the measured energy output could be calculated with an 
accuracy of Вұ0.3 MW for 82% of the cases over a period of 5 months (May вҖ“ September). Also, 
the accumulated energy over the entire time was in good agreement with the measurements.  
It was also possible to develop a model that was able to predict the useful energy output of a 
solar heating plant. An accuracy of Вұ0.3 MW was fulfilled for 75% of the cases. In addition, 
the outlet temperature could be calculated with a good accuracy and the volume flow rate in 
a satisfactory way. Input parameters were only the measured weather conditions: ambient 
temperature, solar irradiation on the collector tilt and for this case a constant wind speed.  
Moreover, investigations on calculating the irradiation on the collector tilt based on global 
radiation were performed. The results have shown that all models overestimated the incident 
irradiation, but were able to represent the measured data for good weather conditions in a 
satisfactory way. Unstable and fast changing weather conditions could not be modelled that 
good. The reason for that are most likely local cloud covers that are difficult to predict.  
Furthermore, it can be concluded from the results that considering the fluid inertia of the 
collector fluid is important. Good results were obtained when the inertia was simulated by the 
use of delayed outlet temperatures. A constant time delay of 40 min was most appropriate 
for the investigated solar heating plant. 
Assuming a linear temperature dependency of air properties between cover and absorber 
plate is a very good approximation for the typical temperature range solar collectors are 
exposed to. Such linear assumption is also reasonable for the specific heat, density and 
thermal conductivity of the collector fluid. However, the dynamic viscosity did not show a 
linear temperature dependency. This is why the calculation of the real mean fluid 
temperature is preferred rather than assuming a mean value of inlet and outlet temperature.  
The correct choice of the extinction coefficient is of great importance, because it has a 
significant influence on the energy output. It would be beneficial providing exact values by 
manufacturers of the solar collector or the producer of the cover glass. Also the absorptance 
and emissivity of the absorber plate had an effect on the calculated energy when these 
values were varied within the given tolerance from the data sheet of the solar collector. A 
small range of Вұ0.02 for each value clearly affected the calculated energy output.   
Integrating the developed model into Mentor Planner can have several advantages as 
discussed in chapter 4. Mentor Planner could optimize modern energy systems, first of all to

to reduce imbalances of the power market due to a high share of wind power. Another area of

production and storage.

Dependency of the transportation inertia of the solar collector fluid on the volume 
flow and/or solar irradiation.

Shading effects depending on the time of the day and year

Start and stop criteria when the prediction model should be active. Instead of

as it is done in real cases and also for Gram.  
пӮ· 
The control of the inlet temperature could be optimized in a way that more values 
from the past are taken into account for the prediction model. Also other rates of 
change could be investigated.

Accuracy of calculating irradiation on a tilted surface for instance by measuring the 
global radiation at the same location as the tilted one. Measurements could be 
conducted with several instruments at the same time to reduce systematic errors.

Further development of the prediction model in Mentor Planner. A neuronal network

future energy system: seasonal storage, district cooling and aquifer thermal energy 
storage.
