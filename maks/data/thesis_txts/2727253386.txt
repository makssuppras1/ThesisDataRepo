Abstract
The purpose of this master thesis is to investigate several ventilation configurations and meth­
ods in an occupied ISO 8 pharmaceutical cleanroom to detect efficient ventilation solutions and
the potential energy savings of reducing the air change rate (ACR). Current regulatory standards
regarding cleanroom ventilation recommend high ACRs that seem unnecessary and outdated.
The suggested ACR for cleanrooms in the pharmaceutical industry is 20 h−1 according to the
Food and Drug Administration, resulting in high energy consumption. Further, the guidelines
support mixing ventilation (MV) as the typical applied ventilation strategy to fulfil the cleanliness
requirements and achieve acceptable air quality, which may not be the most energy­efficient
option. Thus, literature has proven the potential of using other air distribution methods, such as
displacement ventilation (DV) and local ventilation (LV), to create more efficient contamination
removal but with some constraints. Therefore, this study strives to explore these ventilation
strategies at reduced ACRs through extensive full­scale experiments in a climate chamber and
energy simulations in IDA ICE. The primary experimental methodology used to analyze the
performance of the ventilation configurations was dosing the chamber with the tracer gas N2O
and measuring the concentrations throughout the room and in the air terminal devices (ATDs).
The chamber contained a table, two occupants, and simulated pharmaceutical equipment. The
ventilation effectiveness (VE) was used to normalize the results where a value of 1 was desir­
able in the critical area, imitating the manufacturing area. The results showed that an ACR of
20 h−1 was not necessary and can be reduced to 10 h−1 for DV and LV strategies. MV was
inefficient in this study, indicating that such arrangements required a higher ACR, which is not
energy­efficient. The ventilation configuration containing a box diffuser above the critical area
with a downward airflow combined with a supply diffuser in the middle of the room providing
mixing was the most efficient solution for removing the contamination. Also, the system did not
deteriorate by the extra heat load from the pharmaceutical equipment, and the configuration
resulted in a comfortable indoor environment without any local thermal discomfort. Overall, the
findings in this study indicated that it is possible to lower the ACR and perform effective ventila­
tion solutions to save energy without compromising air quality or thermal comfort. However, for
the current standards to revise the suggested ACR range and propose other ventilation strate­
gies than MV, future investigations with more realistic conditions are necessary to validate these
results.

1
Introduction
Cleanroom technology is essential for many industries, such as the medical, pharmaceutical,
and electronics sectors. For many years, the pharmaceutical industry has shown to be of great
importance and essential to human health. Especially in the last few years living with a pan­
demic, the production and allocation of COVID­19 vaccines indicated how significant the global
pharmaceutical market is [1]. It is a market in growth and has seen continuous expansion. The
demand and investments in pharmaceuticals have increased, and there is a global focus on
improved healthcare. In 2021, the pharmaceutical market was valued at $ 487 billion and is
expected to reach $ 958 billion in 2028, indicating a yearly growth rate of 10.2 % [2].
An important area is the manufacturing of sterile pharmaceutical products. The preparations
of these products and medicines require an antiseptic and clean environment. Therefore, it
transpires in a pharmaceutical cleanroom. It is a controlled environment to avoid pollutants and
create a clean, dust­ and germ­free space without microbiological contamination [3]. Here, the
ventilation system plays a crucial role in the cleanroom to fulfil its function. Cleanroom ventila­
tion is complex and significant. Without sufficient ventilation in the cleanrooms, many industries
would not be allowed to complete their products as they could negatively affect people’s health
and risk lives. The ventilation demand causes a huge energy use, up to 50 times higher than
for standard rooms [4], and life science industries like the pharmaceutical and biotechnical ones
emit more CO2 than all the other regular industries that usually emit high amounts as the semi­
conductor sector [5].
Energy use and efficiency are often not a concern for cleanroom operators. However, with
the current climate changes, global warming, and energy crisis, all industries need to optimize
their energy use and CO2 emission. Here, the pharmaceutical and biotechnical market has a
big potential to save energy, especially concerning cleanrooms. Facilitating designers tend to
oversize the ventilation system and supply more air than needed as a safety margin since the
actual particle level within the cleanroom is unknown beforehand. Therefore, there is a need
for more studies to improve the ventilation systems and validate whether the airflow can be
reduced in pharmaceutical cleanrooms without compromising cleanliness, air quality and other
environmental conditions. Due to the importance and size of the pharmaceutical industry and
the energy­intensive facilities, it has considerable potential for energy savings.

1.1. Cleanroom Definition
Chapter 1. Introduction
The following sections present a theoretical background for this master project and review pre­
vious studies concerning ventilation and contamination control in cleanrooms. The theory in­
cludes an insight into what the purpose and requirements of cleanrooms are, their history and
classifications. Furthermore, it introduces the essential theory about cleanroom ventilation and
the relevant ventilation strategies. The literature review contains key findings from other studies
used to define the aim of this master thesis and understand the problems in depth.
1.1
Cleanroom Definition
A cleanroom is an enclosed space where air hygiene and indoor environment quality are con­
trolled to ensure extremely high cleanliness levels. In the pharmaceutical industry, cleanrooms
are used as a technical solution to prevent contamination when preparing sterile medical prod­
ucts. Additionally, inside the cleanrooms, standard controlled environmental parameters are
particle levels, viable organisms such as bacteria, temperature, humidity, air pressure and mo­
tion, airflow, vibrations, noise, and lighting. These parameters should follow recommended
ranges and create a comfortable, hygienic, clean space. Naughton [6], a member of ASHRAE,
describes a good cleanroom as ensuring effective contamination control and guaranteeing the
required degree of reliability and productivity with a proper installation and operating cost.
1.2
Cleanroom History
Over the last decades, the cleanroom evolution has contained many noteworthy changes. In
the eighteenth century, the concept of a cleanroom started as a clean space which has devel­
oped into the modern cleanroom as we know it today. Technology has evolved significantly
since the first approaches, but the idea persists. The concept originated from concerns about
environmental cleanliness. Whether this was to keep manufactured products clean, avoid infec­
tions during surgeries or another need for contamination control, cleanrooms were the solution
[7]. Figure 1.1 gives an overview of the main technological innovations, standards and breaking
points in the history of cleanrooms.
Figure 1.1: Overview of Important Events in Cleanroom History
The cleanrooms started in the medical and military industry but later, in the 1960s and 1970s,
spread to applications like pharmaceuticals and biotechnology, microelectronics, aerospace and
various others [6]. Hospital spaces completed the first attempts to control the air near patients,
and from the beginning, the well­ventilated areas proved promising. From here, many studies
investigated this matter further, and several pioneers provided relevant information about their
breakthroughs, like sterilization of equipment, aseptic methods in surgery, and cleanable rooms.
Manufacturers concerned with contamination control embraced this new guidance to use it in
precision manufacturing operations.

These rooms were called ”white rooms” or ”ultra­clean rooms”. An example is during World
War II, with a demand for more complex military parts like guns, aircraft etc. and, therefore, a
need for a clean space for production. After the war, the use of cleanrooms increased again
in correlation to industrial developments, and especially, more advanced aircraft and nuclear
weapons were in focus [6] [7].
The first version of the modern cleanroom with “laminar flow” was by Willis Whitfield in 1961.
He supplied the air through the ceiling and removed it again through grills in the floor. Willis and
his team used the application of an earlier invention, the high­efficiency particulate air (HEPA)
filters, to create a unidirectional airflow. Hence, cleanliness could achieve a higher level than
before [7].
1.3
Particle Generation and Contamination Control
In general, the primary purpose of pharmaceutical cleanrooms is to effectively control contami­
nation since airborne particles and other impurities can significantly affect product quality. The
contamination in cleanrooms may come from different sources, both inside and outside of the
room, but people are the primary ones. The National Environmental Balancing Bureau de­
scribes that the workers in cleanrooms are responsible for 70­80 % of the contamination due
to, e.g., their hygiene, undergarments, and residual smoke [8].
If the workers smoke during their breaks or free time, residual smoke, containing particles, will
be left in the lungs and emitted into the air. Further, humans shed hair and replace the outer layer
of their skin every month and thus emit many particles in the form of skin flakes. An average
person shades between 200.000­600.000 skin flakes every minute. It corresponds to 30­90 mg
of skin flakes per hour [9]. Still, clothing materials and fibres, equipment, structural elements,
and outside airborne pollutants can also affect the contamination inside the cleanroom. The
contaminants can be viable as bacteria and viruses or nonviable as metals, dust, and organic
and inorganic compounds [6].
Particles divide into different categories based on their size. Small particles with a diameter <
0.1 µm are considered ultrafine particles. Particles between 0.1­2.5 µm are fine particles, and
the large particles > 2.5 µm are called coarse particles [9]. In general, skin flakes are coarse
particles but can vary in size. The average diameter is 10­20 µm [10], but they can be up to 100
µm, and some can be smaller than 1 µm. Human hair has an average size of around 70 µm [9].
Contamination control in cleanrooms consists of two main strategies; preventing contamination
from entering the room and minimizing the generation of contamination in the room. Therefore,
there is a protocol for people and equipment to be cleaned before entering the space. Further,
the personnel wear specific protective clothing. Inside the cleanroom, sufficient air filtration and
pressure reduce the contamination level. Here, the ventilation system plays a significant role,
and cleanrooms have an extremely high ventilation demand, driving the facilities to be very
energy­intensive establishments [6].

1.4. Cleanroom Standards and Classifications
Chapter 1. Introduction
In pharmaceutical cleanrooms, the challenges with particles are not only when they are airborne
but also when they deposit onto the surfaces of the products and equipment. Therefore, it
is necessary to gain knowledge of the mechanisms that make the particles deposit. In this
regard, many different processes explain particle deposition and the primary mechanisms are
gravitational settling, Brownian diffusion, turbulent deposition, electrostatic attraction, impaction,
interception, thermophoresis, and turbophoresis [11].
Whyte et al. [11] considered the deposition mechanisms of particles in a cleanroom based on
the particle deposition rate. They concluded that gravitational settling is the primary mecha­
nism for particles with a size above 0.5 µm. For particles smaller than 0.5 µm, the Brownian
diffusion showed to be the most influential one. Further, they found that turbulent deposition
and electrostatic attraction can be relevant in cleanrooms, and the two mechanisms can affect
particles of all sizes. There are other studies concerning particle deposition, but mainly for small
particles below 1 µm in the semiconductor industry [12] [13]. In cleanrooms, the most gener­
ated particles, as described above, are from humans, especially skin flakes, which are generally
bigger than the examined particle size [10]. Otherwise, the studies are only theoretical and do
not consider all mechanisms like the turbulent deposition, which was shown to be an influential
mechanism in cleanrooms [11].
1.4
Cleanroom Standards and Classifications
Due to all the innovative cleanroom technologies and a recognition of the need for controlled en­
vironments, the implementation of cleanrooms increased. As a result, demands for guidelines
and instruction occurred, and the U.S. Air Force Technical Order delivered the first standard­
ization of cleanroom cleanliness in 1961 [6]. Afterwards, in 1963, the U.S. General Services
Administration created the first Federal Standard (FS) named FS 209. From here, different
countries started to perform a national standard through the 1970s, and in 1971, the first Good
Manufacturing Practice (GMP) standard came out. During most of the 1980s, the standardiza­
tion of cleanrooms was in focus. In particular, the guidance for manufacturing processes, as
used in the pharmaceutical industry, increased. Later, the International Organization for Stan­
dardization (ISO) issued the first international standard, ISO 14644, in 1999. Since the first
standards, there have been many revisions, improvements and extensions [6] [14].
Today, air cleanliness regarding particle concentration categorizes cleanrooms into different
classes. Here, a threshold for the particle size and the maximum allowable particle concentra­
tion determine the specific cleanroom classification. There are different standards with varying
classifications of cleanliness. Generally, the most widely used standards and guidelines for
cleanrooms are ISO 14644, GMP Annex 1, and FS 209. The ISO 14644 classification ranges
from Class 1­9, whereas the FS 209 uses Class 1­100000, and the GMP guideline is from
Grades A­D. All these classifications concern the particle concentration inside the cleanroom.
The following tables indicate how the standards are connected and show the cleanliness re­
quirements according to the different standards. Table 1.1 shows the ISO classes, and Table
1.2 displays the GMP classes.

1.5. Ventilation in Cleanrooms
Chapter 1. Introduction
1.5
Ventilation in Cleanrooms
The ventilation in pharmaceutical cleanrooms should be sufficient to prevent airborne particles
and viable contaminants like bacteria from attaching to the manufactured products. These final
products need to be sterile and safe. If particles contaminate them, they can be damaged and
create a risk for the patients receiving them. Therefore, ventilation in cleanrooms is essential to
reduce contamination. Typically, the ventilation system supplies high air flow rates to fulfil the
cleanliness requirements and uses a lot of energy. The cleanroom HVAC system accounts for
≈70 % of a production site’s energy use [5] [18].
When designing the cleanroom ventilation system, there are many factors to consider. Some
of these parameters can be accounted for already in the design phase to ensure high efficiency
and low energy use. Choosing the correct cleanliness class from the beginning is essential since
it is associated with the needed air change rate (ACR) and air speed. A lower category than
necessary can result in too much contamination and damage to the manufactured products.
A higher class than required corresponds to higher airflows and can cause unnecessary high
energy use. Further, it does not always meet the demanded cleanliness class. By lowering the
ACR, the fan size can also be smaller, and the energy and cost decrease. The ACR defines
the amount of time, usually per hour, it takes to replace the total air volume in a space. The
fan power is proportional to the cube of the airflow, where a 30 % decreased ACR reduced the
power by ≈66 %. A lower airflow may even improve the cleanliness level since the turbulence
will be minimized [19].
A couple of guidelines indicating the needed ACR for a given cleanliness class exist. However,
many recommendations are based on rules of thumb or outdated guesses [20]. For cleanrooms
in the pharmaceutical industry, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recommends a value
of 20 h−1 [17]. Most guidelines recommend high values with no scientific arguments. Typically
a wide range is given, and there is no consensus on the optimal ACR, resulting in conflicting
recommendations. An example from a design guideline is a recommendation between 250­700
h−1 for ISO Class 5, but benchmarking shows that the actual operating ACRs in cleanrooms
with this classification range from 94­625 h−1 [19]. Table 1.4 shows another recommendation

Ventilation Systems and Components
Like in any other ventilation system, the air handling unit (AHU) is an important part. The usual
AHU function is to provide a comfortable environment by adding clean air to the room and ex­
tracting contaminated air. Further, it provides the needed air temperature and humidity in the
space and controls contamination and pollution levels through filtration [23]. Thus, another es­
sential innovation for cleanroom technology is the mechanical air filter. It is the barrier between
the channels connected to the AHU and the diffuser supplying air to the room. So, all air en­
tering the cleanroom travels through the air filter first. Here, HEPA filters are typically used.
Also, ultra­low particulate air (ULPA) filters are in demand when there is a more strict cleanli­
ness requirement. These absolute air filters trace back to World War II. Since then, HEPA filters
have evolved to have a 99.97 % efficiency of removing airborne particles of 0.3 µm and an even
higher efficiency for both smaller and larger particle sizes. For the filters to function optimally,
they should be replaced and cleaned regularly [24].
Unidirectional and Non­Unidirectional Airflow
There are two main airflow distribution methods used in cleanrooms. The air distribution method
can either be non­unidirectional (Non­UDAF) or unidirectional (UDAF).
When the airflow is Non­UDAF, the air comes through a diffuser in the ceiling equipped with
either a HEPA or ULPA filter. Afterwards, the supplied air mixes with the air in the room, and
the contaminated air flows towards the exhaust grills and leaves the room. This ventilation
method typically applies to ISO Class 5 or above. Here, the exhaust location is often at a low
level on the wall. However, sometimes the exhaust can be located in the ceiling, especially if
less strict cleanliness levels like ISO 8 are required. Usually, for ISO Classes 1­5, where more
stringent requirements apply, UDAF is used, which is also called laminar airflow in some cases.
For this airflow distribution method, more air is supplied to the room through the ceiling than
for the Non­UDAF. The air flows down vertically through the room and exits through the floor.
The UDAF method can be more energy intensive as more air is required to achieve a laminar
airflow. Figures 1.2a and 1.2b show examples of the ventilation concept of the Non­UDAF and
UDAF, respectively [25].

Another important factor to consider in cleanroom ventilation is the air jet released from the
supply diffuser. There are many different types of jets, such as free jets, wall jets, and swirl jets,
depending on the geometry of the supply diffuser and the airflow conditions.
The air jet can be divided into four regions; the initial region (core), the characteristic decay re­

area clean. If the approach is to avoid contamination in a specific part of the room, the solution
could be to supply air directly down towards the zone [27]. For the air to reach this critical area,
the length of the core is essential, which can be influenced by several factors, such as the out­
let cross­section and Reynolds number, depending on the jet type. Also, thermal plumes from
people and machinery can affect the air jet and potentially shorten the core length [26].

Other Airflow Distribution Methods
Besides UDAF and Non­UDAF, there are other ventilation methods to use in cleanrooms. Two
commonly used ventilation methods for many different buildings are mixing ventilation (MV)
and displacement ventilation (DV). MV is the most widely chosen principal in cleanrooms, but
in more recent studies, DV has also proved to be a relevant alternative [28].
If the entire room needs to be clean, MV is often chosen. MV supplies clean air at higher
velocities outside the occupied zone to ensure that the air fully mixes in the total volume of the
room and creates a more uniform temperature and contamination distribution in the space. MV
is suitable for many building types and can be found in schools, offices, and small restaurants
where comfort is highly demanded [29]. However, if only clean air is prioritized in a part of the
room, typically the occupied zone, DV is preferable. DV, also called stratification ventilation,
applies cold fresh air at low velocities into the needed zone. Typically, the air is supplied from
a low level forming a thin layer of air and taking advantage of the buoyancy forces that help lift
the supplied air, making it able to rise into the thermal plumes created by heat sources. The
polluted warm air will then be extracted by diffusers in the ceiling or at a high level. Therefore,
DV is often efficient at removing pollution while ensuring fresh air in the occupied zone since it
creates two areas with different air quality within the room [30]. DV is preferable in rooms with
a high ceiling height and where high ventilation is required, such as in auditoriums, conference
rooms, airports, and cafeterias [29].

Pressurization
The interaction between the air in cleanrooms and the adjacent rooms is also influential. Clean­
rooms can have different air movement controls, where the typical types are positive pressure,
negative pressure and cascade. Pharmaceutical cleanrooms usually utilise positive pressure.
As the name describes, the pressure inside the cleanroom is higher than in the external rooms.
This concept supplies more air to the space than extracted, so the air cannot circulate back into
the room again. Therefore, it creates an outgoing flow to avoid contamination from the outside
to flow into the room and keep the manufactured products sterile. The opposite type is negative
pressure which aims to keep the possible harmful contamination generated in the room from
flowing outside to the adjacent rooms. The air pressure is lower in the cleanroom than outside
the room to trap the contamination in the cleanroom. Here, the system extracts more air than
supplied to keep people outside of the room safe. Usually, this type is implemented in hospi­
tals and isolation rooms. Finally, cascade ventilation, is where the air comes from a ventilated
adjacent room and passes through the cleanroom and out into another adjoining space like a
corridor [31]. The following figures display the different control types, where the grey arrows

Temperature and Humidity Control
When preparing sterile products in a cleanroom, the temperature and humidity are vital to con­
trol, and their levels should be acceptable. Depending on the manufactured products, there is
a specification of the needed environment to ensure the proper function of the products. If the
temperature and humidity don’t follow protocol, it can affect the product efficacy and result in
damaged outcomes and waste.
A divergence in the temperature can influence chemical durability and stability. These changes
in the properties of the medicines can have serious consequences for the patients. Further,
the temperature can affect the personnel, where a too­high temperature can cause people to
sweat, and a too­low temperature can result in shivering. Both of these cases can result in a
higher release of particles [32]. It has been suggested that a temperature of 21 ◦C ± 2 ◦C is
optimal [33]. Therefore, it should be considered that the necessary conditions for manufactured
products are prioritized but thermal comfort is also important for the workers in pharmaceutical
cleanrooms [34].
If the humidity exceeds the requirements, the pharmaceutical products can absorb moisture,
which adversely may affect their properties. Therefore, the humidity level should not surpass
60 %. For the humidity range to also create a comfortable environment for the personnel, a
typical range is between 30­65 % according to ISO 14644: Part 4 [35].

Previous Studies
The theory showed that cleanrooms and the underlying ventilation method spread rapidly to
many industries, such as pharmaceuticals, aerospace, and microelectronics, and are today
well­used facilities worldwide. The contamination control technology proved promising from the
beginning to create clean spaces with minimal contamination. However, cleanrooms also cre­
ate challenges, especially concerning their high energy use due to the large ventilation demand.
Therefore, a lot of research has been conducted intending to optimize ventilation in cleanrooms
while complying with the contamination criteria. The following sections exemplify the main con­
cepts and fundamental variables that are provided by the literature.
Demand­Controlled Filtration, DCF
A phenomenon often met in literature is demand­controlled filtration (DCF). It is a strategy used
to save energy by reducing the airflow rate in cleanrooms during specific periods, e.g. when
the particle concentration is low or when the room is unoccupied [37].
In 1994­1995, David Faulkner et al. [37] investigated possible energy savings in a cleanroom
by introducing DCF and reducing the fan speed without adversely affecting the indoor particle
concentration. Since then, many studies have examined the DCF methods, which have proved
great results in reducing energy. Faulkner et al. [37] found that the control could save up to
60­84 % fan energy by reducing the airflow. Later, Faulkner et al. [38] examined two control
strategies and concluded an energy saving of 40 % with both methods when compared to a sce­
nario with constantly running fans. More recently, Loomans et al. [39] [40] investigated three
DCF strategies to reduce energy use in pharmaceutical cleanrooms by optimizing the ACR. The
examined methods were night reduction, control by particle concentration and by occupancy.
They found significant potential energy savings of up to 93.6 % and stated that especially the
occupancy­based DCF could be enforced without compromising the contamination and cleanli­
ness class. Another study by Loomans [41] experienced similar results and fan energy savings
of 70 % and more without adversely affecting the air quality.
These studies highlight that DCF methods can result in large energy savings while maintaining
an optimal environment inside the cleanrooms. However, the fan energy savings are correlated
with the time the cleanrooms are in operation mode [40]. Many pharmaceutical cleanrooms
are running almost constantly. Further, the ventilation system is rarely off, even if the room is
not in use, since it takes a while to get started again, and operators are not willing to take any
changes that can affect their products poorly [40]. Hence, the DCF will therefore not be ideal.
This result also complies with a statement by Faulkner [37] describing that the control is only
recommended for small cleanrooms that are not in operation all the time.

Air Change Rates, ACRs
Both theory and literature showed that the ACR is a crucial factor concerning contamination
control. Several studies have been conducted regarding ACR in cleanrooms and its influence on
pollution levels. Most literature has the same conclusion; a lower ACR than required can fulfil the
desired cleanliness level without compromising the products or the indoor environment. Further,
many studies agree that higher ACRs do not necessarily guarantee more efficient contamination
removal nor better thermal comfort or air quality. Therefore, a higher ACR is not optimal in every
case, but there is still no consensus on an adequate range.
Zhao et al. [42] described that higher ACRs may create dust near machines and equipment due
to high turbulence. In their numerical experiment, they investigated ACRs of 15, 20, and 25 h−1
in a pharmaceutical cleanroom with MV and a secondary return air system. They found that the
ACR of 25 h−1 caused high air speeds and created an uncomfortable draft inside the cleanroom.
The study showed that an increase in the ACR did not correspond to a decrease in the local
age of air around the equipment and critical zone. Further, a higher ACR did not significantly
affect the air change efficiency (ACE). In another study by Zhao et al. [43], they conducted
experiments with micro­porous supply panels and different ventilation schemes. Additionally,
three ACRs of 16, 22, and 30 h−1 were examined. They found that a higher ACR may improve
the cleanliness since for the ACR of 30 h−1, the removal efficiency was higher than for the
others, but it also created a bigger risk of thermal discomfort.
In 2019, a survey by Ogunsola et al. [44] showed that most ISO Class 7 and 8 cleanrooms
operate with lower ACRs than recommended but, at the same time, are cleaner regarding par­
ticles. The study investigated particle production in cleanrooms with different activities. They
suggested that standards should look into the specific kind of cleanroom and the processing
activity performed as a lower ACR can be used in rooms with work that produces fewer parti­
cles. Also, Zhao et al. [43] recommended that the existing numerical methods of determining
the ACR should take the characteristics of the ventilation strategy into account. A similar sug­
gestion was made by Mičko et al. [45] in 2022, stating that the ACR should be determined from
the room characteristics and not only empirical formulas. Their numerical study examined air
velocities between 0.15­0.25 m/s in operating rooms and the effect on particle contamination.
They concluded that lower ventilation air speeds in the range of 0.15­0.175 m/s proved to be
more efficient at removing particles at the critical area, in this case, the operating table. Fur­
ther, another advantage of a lower air speed was a decrease in the cost since the system could
operate with lower ACRs, and an increased airflow led to undesired turbulence.
A numerical study by Cetin et al. [46] examined how ACRs affected particle decay. The project
showed that an increase in ACR and the decay rate of the particle concentration are not directly
proportional. Their CFD simulations showed that when the ACR increased, the decay time
decreased. Although at the high ACRs, the decay time did not decrease as much as for the lower
ACRs, and between 25 and 43 h−1, the difference in the particle decay was almost negligible.
They concluded that an ACR of 10 h−1 was sufficient concerning the recovery time. However,
the model was simulated as a non­occupied room, and the results could vary for a room in
operation.

The 10 h−1 has shown to be adequate in other studies as well. In 2022, an experiment by

an operational cleanroom containing pharmaceutical equipment. The equipment was modelled

The literature mostly considers the supplied air into cleanrooms as contamination­free and that
the pollution sources have a constant generation. However, this is often not the case. Several
studies concerning air filtration [47] [12] [48] concluded that the filters can pollute the air instead
of cleaning it, especially if not maintained properly. Regarding particle generation, the airflow
can either be constant or varying. In 2022, Cetin et al. [49] did a reduced­scale and numerical
study in an empty cleanroom on different ACRs and particle generation rates on particle depo­

1.73, 2.88, 5.76, and 7.68 h−1 were tested. The study concluded that if the particle flow rate has
a constant generation and does not change with the airflow velocity, increasing the ACR can
provide a cleaner room. However, a system without a proper filtration unit results in dynamic
particle generation, depending on the ACR and air velocity. Hence, increasing these parame­
ters will lead to higher contamination within the room. The study pointed out that in practice,
particle generation is often dynamic. Therefore, the study recommended assessing the ACE
instead of only focusing on raising the ACR. The study considered different particle sizes but
did not compare their findings with cleanroom requirements.
Overall, many studies demonstrated how different ACRs affected the particle concentration in
various cleanrooms. Multiple studies showed that cleanrooms can comply with the required
cleanliness class with a lower ACR than recommended [17] [42] [46] [44]. Regardless, many
investigations have several limitations and keep other parameters fixed that may influence the
results. Further, the literature indicates that the relationship between contamination and airflow
is not well acknowledged and needs further validation.

Ventilation Strategies and Supply­Exhaust Layout
Studies have examined different layouts of the supply and exhaust air terminal devices (ATDs) in
cleanrooms and proved their considerable effect on ventilation effectiveness and contamination
removal.
Whyte et al. [50] conducted an experimental and a CFD study of a cleanroom, where they
tested different variables and their effect on the particle concentration. They examined the type
of supply diffuser, air supply velocity, the temperature difference between the supplied air and
the room, and the contamination release position. From all these variables, they concluded the
most influencing parameter to be the type of supply diffuser based on the performance index.
The study indicated that using an air inlet with no diffuser led to an increase in cleanliness below
the inlet area, but it also decreased the cleanliness in the rest of the room. Instead, the use
of a 4­way diffuser showed to be preferable for mixing the air and creating a uniform particle
concentration throughout the cleanroom.
Cetin et al. [51] investigated ten different supply and exhaust configurations with three different
types of contaminant release. The configurations were studied based on the air velocity, con­
tamination distribution, ACE, the removal rate of contamination, and particle decay. The study
agreed that the supply and exhaust positioning significantly influenced the ventilation perfor­
mance, especially regarding the airflow pattern and, therefore, also the contaminant distribution
and air age. The study figured that the exhaust close to the pollution source was efficient at
removing particles and not letting the contamination disperse to the rest of the room. They also
found that different configurations have different advantages, and no arrangement performed
best on all parameters. For example, the highest ACE was reached with an inlet and outlet
opening located on the same wall, while a free air jet supplied from the ceiling gave the shortest
decay time of particles. These findings comply with their recommendation stating the ventila­
tion layout should be determined based on the use of the specific cleanroom, including the area
of concern and pollution source position. The study was limited to only examining one supply
opening and one exhaust opening for each case. Multiple openings may influence the findings.
Also, the simulated room was non­occupied, and the heat generated from occupants can affect
the air distribution and the conclusions.
The study by Loomans et al. [39] discussed ventilation efficiency in cleanrooms and described
potential improvements by the position and type of the supply and exhaust diffusers as well
as the source of contamination. They examined 13 cases from six parameters, being the dif­
fuser type, swirl angle, ACR, supply position, extract position and source position. The cases
with swirl diffusers indicated a homogeneous particle concentration within the cleanroom, which
complies with findings demonstrated by other studies as [17] [52] [53]. Loomans et al. [39] also
found that localized ventilation resulted in high local ventilation effectiveness. Here, they rec­
ommended that the supply air should be provided close to the required clean area, and the air
extraction should be near the personnel’s working area. However, this proposal is not recom­
mended by guidelines since they concern high requirements for air quality in the whole room
and not only for a specific area. Thus, most cleanrooms still use MV.

the room and, therefore, a higher ventilation performance and more efficient contamination re­

a significant role regarding ventilation effectiveness and particle contamination. This result es­

they are more affected by the airflow, according to Bouilly et al. [55]. The exhaust resulted
in the highest effectiveness and removal rate at a low level near the pollution source [51] [54].

ventilation (LV), where the system supplies air close to the required area, resulted in high ef­
fectiveness. Also, DV can be efficient at removing pollution. However, these methods do not
provide clean air in the whole room, and MV is still the typical ventilation choice in cleanrooms.
The thermal loads in cleanrooms and the occupant position can affect the air distribution and
ventilation performance. Therefore, they are essential to account for. Furniture and other ob­
stacles have also been mentioned in the literature to influence the airflow and contamination
distribution [54]. The amount of space used for equipment and other tools in the cleanrooms is
essential, according to Tung et al. [56], who examined different ventilation configurations and
their effect on the airflow and particle concentration. The study used three tool coverage ratios,
describing how much space is for tools. The investigated values were 0 %, 38 % and 60 %.
Findings showed that the coverage ratio influenced the results significantly, and an increased
tool coverage ratio adversely affected the particle removal efficiency.

Particles vs. Tracer Gas
The use of tracer gas as a substitute for particles in controlled experiments has also been
discussed and investigated. Tracer gas is easier to measure and usually more effective as it
can collect data in multiple locations simultaneously. Instruments for particle generation and
collecting is often more expensive and can also disturb the airflow during the experiment. A
study by Bivolarova et al. [57] examined whether tracer gas can be used to model particles
in their investigation of ventilation rates and free convection flow from a thermal manikin. The
study measured gas and particle concentrations in a test room with MV. Generally, the findings
showed that the tracer gas method was reliable and not even an increase in the ventilation rate
or the room surface area (thermal manikin, furniture) affected the distribution between the tracer
gas and particles. It can therefore predict the occupant exposure to particles in a cleanroom
with similar conditions. There are also some restrictions since gas molecules are lighter and
smaller than particles in real life and thus move differently. Particles can have many different
sizes, and the tracer gas cannot predict the behaviour of all. The particle sizes investigated in
the study were 0.07 µm, 0.7 µm and 3.5 µm. Larger particles act differently and are generally
more affected by gravitational force. Therefore, the study only proves using tracer gas as a
reliable method for particles below 3.5 µm.
A study by Ai et al. [58] also examined the use of tracer gas instead of particles. Their hypothesis
stated that “tracer gas is a suitable surrogate of exhaled droplet nuclei for studying airborne
transmission in the built environment [58].” The study accepted their hypothesis and proved
that tracer gas can be used to study airborne transmissions for particles up to 5 µm. However,
it also provided some counterarguments as to why tracer gas can be different from particles.
For example, tracer gas cannot imitate the resuspension of particles which is the state when
particles leave a surface into the space again.

1.7. Justification

1.7
Justification
Literature proved the possibility of reducing the ACR and improving ventilation efficiency and
contamination removal in cleanrooms without compromising thermal comfort or air quality in sim­
ilar settings as this study. There are several adjustable strategies and parameters to achieve
these optimizations, as described in the literature. However, all studies have limitations, and fur­
ther investigations are suggested to validate existing findings, which this study aimed to provide
through a broad experiment. The experiment included different ventilation strategies, supply­
exhaust diffusers and arrangements, and reduced ACRs in an occupied pharmaceutical clean­
room due to existing findings in the literature and their constraints.
MV with swirl supply diffusers was identified as the typical ventilation type in pharmaceutical
cleanrooms. Multiple studies, as [17] and [53], proved that the swirl diffusers created a homo­
geneous air distribution and good air mixing. These findings made MV the ideal strategy for
initial experiments, including swirl diffusers.
Loomans et al. [39] and Cheong and Phua [54] highlighted that LV and laminar airflow were
efficient measures for removing contamination near the critical area. Berlanga et al. [53] [28]
examined MV and DV strategies and concluded that DV was more efficient than MV on con­
tamination removal for cases with ACRs below 12 h−1. These solutions are not recommended
by standards today, but it can be discussed whether MV is necessary in all cases. Therefore,
the project included cases of DV and further, a new box diffuser with UDAF was designed to
hang down from the ceiling to get closer to the critical area.
Studies, such as [54] and [53], proved that low­level wall exhaust was the most efficient so­
lution for removing contamination. Both studies were limited to being examined in a hospital
with negative pressure. Therefore, experiments were performed with the exhaust at a low­wall
level and in the ceiling to compare the results and check whether similar findings applied to a
pharmaceutical cleanroom.
Many of the provided studies in the literature section were limited to non­occupied empty clean­
rooms, which is unrealistic. The influence of occupants and other obstacles, such as furniture
and equipment, was proved by Cheong and Phua [54] and Tung et al. [56] to be influential.
Thus, the cleanroom examined throughout this study contained two occupants, a table, and
simulated pharmaceutical equipment.
Most ventilation improvements stated by the literature will result in energy savings, as they
concern reduced ACRs and more effective ventilation design. However, most studies did not
assess the actual energy use and did not consider the potential energy savings. Therefore
finally, energy simulations were conducted to explore the energy­saving potential of reducing
the ACR.

1.8. Objectives
Chapter 1. Introduction
1.8
Objectives
This study aims to explore potential improvements of the ventilation system in an occupied
ISO Class 8 pharmaceutical cleanroom to save energy. Therefore, different ventilation con­
figurations, types of supply and exhaust diffusers, and ACRs were examined regarding the
contamination distribution and ventilation effectiveness. The main objectives are specified as
follows:
• To investigate the potential of reducing the ACR to a lower value than recommended to
save energy while still providing the required air quality.
• To examine the influence of different supply and exhaust diffusers and their locations on
the contamination distribution and ventilation effectiveness and detect the most efficient
ventilation configuration.
To investigate the objectives, the following hypotheses are presented based on the findings
from the literature.
• The ACR could be reduced in the cleanroom to save energy without compromising the
cleanliness level.
• Different supply and exhaust types and layouts would affect the contamination control and
ventilation effectiveness in the cleanroom.
• Positioning the exhaust at a low wall level would provide higher ventilation effectiveness
than ceiling exhaust when using supply diffusers in the ceiling.
• Using a DV air distribution method would result in higher ventilation effectiveness than an
MV strategy.
• Pharmaceutical heating equipment would influence the air distribution and ventilation ef­
fectiveness.

2
Method
Experimental measurements in a climate chamber and energy simulations were performed. The
aim was to explore the influence of different ventilation configurations and ACRs on contaminant
removal, performance and energy use in a pharmaceutical cleanroom. The main approach for
the experimental part was using tracer gas to investigate the pollution exposure around the room
and near the critical area to determine the ventilation effectiveness (VE). Other measurements
regarded the indoor environment and thermal comfort, where the primary parameters measured
were air speed, relative humidity (RH), and air temperature. Concerning the energy use to
check whether decreasing the ACR saved energy, IDA ICE was used to simulate simple building
models. Lastly, data analysis methods examined the gathered data and visualised it. Here,
the programs used were Excel and the software R Studio. The following sections explain the
procedure more carefully.
2.1
Full­Scale Experiment
A full­scale mock­up of an occupied pharmaceutical cleanroom was conducted to examine the
performance of different ventilation configurations with different ACRs concerning the contam­
ination level inside the room. In this regard, a comparative analysis was made to examine the
VE for all the supply/exhaust layouts.
2.1.1
Experimental Climate Chamber
The experiments were performed in Climate Chamber No. 7 in Building 412 at DTU Lyngby
Campus. The chamber size was 4.7 m x 5.3 m and had a height of 2.6 m. The room was
considered to represent a pharmaceutical cleanroom with ISO 8 classification, or GMP Grade
C ”in operation”. The room layout was flexible and allowed for easy changes regarding the
ventilation system and diffuser. To model a pharmaceutical cleanroom with a typical setup, it
contained an area with a table, two occupants, and simulated pharmaceutical equipment. The
simulated equipment was considered the critical area. It indicated the manufacturing area of
the pharmaceutical products performed by workers. For this cleanroom size, two workers were
assumed to be adequate as it was similar to the size of the room used in the study by Behrens
et al. [17]. One side of the room was primarily a glazed wall so the users could look inside
the room during experiments. The chamber was placed within a room in the basement with no
windows, so the glazed wall had no heat load from the sun and was only for the view.

2.1.2
Pharmaceutical Equipment
Other than occupants, equipment may also be a heat source in pharmaceutical cleanrooms and
contribute to heat gains. Therefore, some of the experiments included heating equipment. De­
pending on the activity conducted inside a cleanroom, different types of pharmaceutical equip­
ment and machinery may be present. The shape and size of the machinery vary from cleanroom
to cleanroom, and so does the heat gain. An ASHRAE handbook states that the heat from lab­
oratory equipment ranges from 50­270 W/m2 [59]. Further, the handbook gives examples of
typical equipment and their heat gains. Based on this information, a heat load of 300 W was
chosen in the experiments with the equipment turned on.
The equipment was modelled using three metal cylinders, each with a light bulb inside to gen­
erate heat and a thin metal plate on top to help distribute the heat. The heat generated from
the three bulbs was 95.5, 96, and 97.5 W, respectively. Materials of metal were chosen to sur­
round the bulbs since it is a good heat conductor with high thermal conductivity. The first metal
plate used was before Experiment 10 changed to a more narrow plate. Figure 2.4 shows the
simulated heating equipment.

In reality, pharmaceutical cleanrooms have a positive pressure indicating that the ventilation

Tracer Gas Dosing
The ventilation system should effectively remove potential contamination within the room to
keep the manufactured products in a pharmaceutical cleanroom clean and safe. The reviewed
literature showed that the prominent pollution source in such spaces is people. Therefore,
the performance of the different ventilation configurations and diffusers was investigated using
tracer gas to simultaneously measure the pollution released from the two occupants at multiple
locations around the room.
The tracer gas N2O mimicked the behaviour of particles within the cleanroom and how the
particles moved from the pollution source towards the critical area, which was the product man­
ufacturing area on the table. The pollution sources were around the right wrist of the personnel.
This location was assumed to be a realistic area where particles were emitted from in reality
due to the space between the skin and closing. The right wrist was due to most people being
right­handed and was, therefore, the hand that the workers most likely would use when they
manufactured the products on the table. During the experiment, a flow rate of 0.7 LN2O/min
entered the room to ensure a tracer gas concentration within the measuring range of the moni­
tors. A gas cylinder outside the room released the gas through two connected tubes, each tube
attached to one of the occupants as shown in Figure 2.25. A mass flow meter from Alicat [73]
kept the flow constant and was checked several times during the experiments.

2.3.4
Ventilation Effectiveness, VE
The VE indicated the ventilation quality in the cleanroom. It describes how well the room is
ventilated compared to a perfectly mixed room. In such a case, the VE equals 1. Therefore, a
value of 1 or higher indicates proper mixed ventilation conditions. The VE was calculated using
Equation 2.13 based on the average concentrations from the tracer gas measurements.
ε = CExhaust −CSupply
CPoint −CSupply
(2.13)
where
CExhaust
is the concentration of the tracer gas, here N2O in the exhaust
CSupply
is the concentration in the supply diffuser
CPoint
is the concentration in the individual measuring point within the room
If the effectiveness is ≥1, the air is more clean than polluted, and the opposite applies if ε <
1. Then the air contains more contaminated air than clean air. Therefore, for the experiments
to be considered effective, the VE should ideally be ≥1. The effectiveness was calculated for
each measuring point in each experiment.

Figure 3.31: Air Speed Measurement under the Box Diffuser
Figure 3.32: TU under the Box Diffuser
Laminar flow occurs when the airflow has uniform conditions independent of time, e.g., for ve­
locity and Tu, and moves in the same direction in parallel layers. Generally, the flow moves at
low air speeds and with a low Tu. Turbulent flow is the opposite and occurs with high velocities
and property changes over time. Here, the airflow layers mix and create vortexes etc. [88].

0.8 m/s. This outcome is related to the high ACR and airflow from the diffuser. The air speeds

represented by the orange line, had an average value of 0.46 m/s. At the higher levels, the air

were not equal throughout the air jet. Closer to the diffuser at the heights of 0.24 m and 0.59

air distribution. Also, the starting point, X1, may not have been exactly in the middle of the
diffuser due to measurement errors connected to using a ruler and marking the points by hand.
Further, the rectangular box diffuser was wide and connected to a circular duct. Thus, more air
came through the middle of the diffuser. There was no filter between the duct and box diffuser
to create a pressure difference, which may have helped create a more even distribution.
The Tu also varied and was unevenly distributed, again indicating that the flow from the diffuser
was not laminar. The values ranged from around 7­12 %. The Tu on one side of the table,
X4­X5, and in the middle were relatively similar, but on the other side, X6­X7, and X2 and X3,
the Tu varied more. The Tu increased as the measuring points were away from the centre in all
directions at 1.14 m. Since the mean air speeds were similar here, this uneven Tu distribution
was due to the SDs being higher further away from the middle. Generally, higher velocities
resulted in lower Tu since they did not fluctuate as much, and the level of 0.59 m from the
diffuser had the lowest Tu values.
The results show that the box diffuser did not provide a completely laminar airflow but more
of a transition to turbulent airflow due to the high velocities and Tu’s and dissimilar conditions
throughout the core. So, the box diffuser generated a downward airflow, but it was not laminar.
The box diffuser was tested with a 20 mm thick, coarse filter from Camfill with the air speed and
Tu results shown in Appendix A.11.1 to see if the filter helped create a more even distribution.
The results showed that with the filter, the velocity profile was more stable, independent of
the distance from the diffuser at the longest axis (X7­X5). The air speed closer to the diffuser
decreased slightly due to the filter; however, a high velocity of 0.66 m/s was still measured. The
velocity profile on the short axis (X2­X3) remained similar with or without the filter, although the
air speed was reduced slightly near the diffuser. The drop in the air speed 10 cm from the centre
at X2 and X3 was initially assumed to be caused by disturbances from the thermal plumes of the
dummies. However, measurements were also conducted for X2 and X3 without the dummies,
and the results were almost identical.
The Tu was generally lower with the filter. The points 0.24 and 0.59 m from the diffuser followed
the same distribution in both directions, while 1.14 m below the diffuser had the highest Tu. The
higher Tu and drop in air speed at X2 and X3 were most likely caused by the air reaching the
equipment, spreading to the sides and mixing with the surrounding air.
Overall, the filter created a more even velocity profile, but the flow was still not laminar.

Discussion
The main objective of this master thesis was to examine possible energy savings in a phar­
maceutical cleanroom by improving the ventilation system and determining efficient ventilation
strategies. According to the results from the climate chamber experiments, the ACR and venti­
lation configuration, including the type of supply and exhaust ATDs and their locations, proved
influential on the contamination distribution and performance of the ventilation system. Different
air distribution methods were tested, and overall, DV and LV with a UDAF resulted in high VEs,
while MV did not provide efficient results.
For the examined ventilation configurations to be perceived as effective, they should efficiently
remove the generated pollution in the critical area and around the room. The VE indicates
the air distribution performance, and local values of 1 or above were desirable. The main aim
was to achieve this threshold for the measuring points above the equipment, especially the one
closest to the table. For the rest of the locations in the room, a value below but still near 1 was
acceptable.
According to other guidelines, such as the Camfill recommendation document about cleanrooms
and energy optimization, different thresholds can also be used. They state that, for turbulent
mixing cleanrooms, an overall VE of 0.7 indicates good contamination removal [89]. Therefore,
according to this, the ventilation configurations may also be efficient if the points in the room
exceed a value of 0.7. However, with the aim of this study, it is still essential to keep the critical
area on top of the table above 1.
Figure 4.1 summarizes the results and presents the overall VE for each experiment throughout
the project. The results are divided into two categories to compare the local VE for the critical
area with the average VE in the room. It is noticeable that in this figure, the critical area only
includes the measuring point 10 cm on top of the equipment and not the one 65 cm above. This
point was used to examine the particle distribution near the manufacturing area but is assumed
not to be at the level where pharmaceutical manufacturing occurs.
The figure contains two red lines: the dotted line represents the VE threshold wanted for the
critical area, and the solid line is the 0.7 value desired for the points in the room. The y­axis
was limited to a maximum VE of 2 to explore the interesting points of 1 and 0.7. Therefore, VE
values above 2 were cut off, as indicated by the two black arrows on the bars.

Mixing Air Distribution
Overall, the MV air distribution methods in Experiments 1­7 were inefficient, both at the reduced
ACRs and the reference ACR of 20 h−1. The air mixed well in most cases, and the VE in
the room was above 0.7, but none of the VEs on the equipment reached a value of 1. The
supplied air entered horizontally along the ceiling before mixing with the air in the room, and
some air was short­circuited. Thus, the air did not reach the critical area adequately, and the
ventilation strategy only achieved an effective contamination removal outside the occupied part
of the room. MV is the typically used ventilation method in pharmaceutical cleanrooms since
standards state that the entire space should be clean. However, in this study, the MV strategy
was not efficient, as it enhanced the pollution dispersion on the table and created short­circuit.
The short­circuit was, especially, when using the ceiling exhaust and was minimized with the
low­level wall exhaust. In practice, MV configurations may cause even more short­circuit, as
higher ACRs and more supply diffusers are typically used to fulfil the criteria provided by the
standard, making the strategy energy inefficient. For ISO 8, a ceiling coverage of 5­15 % is
recommended, stating the % of the ceiling area occupied by supply diffusers with filters [90].
This parameter was not accounted for in this study. If considered, the MV cases should include
extra diffusers, as the ceiling coverage in Experiments 1­5 was around 4 %.
A comparison between the low­level wall exhaust and ceiling­mounted exhausts was analyzed
for one of the MV cases, showing that the low­level exhaust was more efficient at contamination
removal on the table. Here, it was beneficial for the low­level wall exhaust to be close to the
room setup and pollution sources. However, this conclusion was not tested for other ventilation
strategies and can therefore only be recommended for MV cases similar to the one performed
in Experiments 1 and 2. Thus, additional studies should validate this finding for other situations.
The most efficient MV cases were Experiments 4 and 5 with the moved setup, utilizing three
small swirl diffusers in the ceiling and two and one low­level wall exhaust, respectively. Since
the setup was the only parameter adjusted from Experiment 2 to 4, the change improved the
performance of the ventilation configuration. This outcome demonstrated the importance of
considering the relationship between the ventilation arrangement and the room setup.

In literature, such as the study by Loomans et al. [39], the swirl diffusers and MV often ensure
a homogeneous particle concentration throughout the room and result in small variations in
the local VE. This was also the case in Experiments 3 and 6, with evenly distributed VEs in the
room except on the table. These two experiments had an ACR above 10 h−1, but when the ACR
decreased in the other MV cases, the VEs varied more. So in this study, the distinct advantage
of MV, being the uniform indoor environment, was only achieved with high ACRs and not with
reduced ACR, which is not energy efficient. Therefore, it should be tested further if it is possible
to achieve uniform conditions and efficient results for an MV arrangement with a reduced ACR.
Displacement Air Distribution
Since none of the MV experiments fulfilled the desired VE, other ventilation methods were de­
ployed. The cases with DV provided enough clean air in the room, especially in the critical area
and close to the supply, to keep the tracer gas concentrations down and were perceived as ef­
fective. These results applied both for the experiment with an ACR of 20 and 10 h−1. However,
the contamination levels varied depending on the location, resulting in high concentrations in
some places and low concentrations in others. Also, since the cool air was supplied near the
floor at high velocities, local thermal discomfort due to the air speeds and draught may occur,
particularly around the feet of the workers.
Another challenge with the DV cases was when the equipment was turned on. The heat gen­
eration disturbed the airflow around the equipment and derived considerably different results.
It caused the N2O concentrations to increase on the table, making the ventilation configuration
inefficient.
The heating equipment used in the experiments was an uncertain parameter concerning the
shape and heat load. The equipment varies in laboratories and other pharmaceutical clean­
rooms depending on the specific activity. In the experiments, the heat load from the simulated
equipment was 300 W. It was assumed to be realistic for most types of equipment, but some in­
struments may have much higher heat gains, e.g. an incubator has an average heat load above
1000 W [59]. In practice, several types of equipment might be in different locations with diverse
heat gains. Therefore, the results from this study cannot be applied to every pharmaceutical
cleanroom. Also, the surface area and space used for tools can influence the findings [56], and
by adding more equipment and furniture or changing the equipment size, different results may
be found. Thus, the results are limited to the used type of heating equipment and should be
examined further for different kinds.
Local Ventilation with Box Diffuser
The LV strategy with the box diffuser provided air directly towards the table and removed the
contamination, resulting in the highest VE in any of the experiments. The ventilation layout also
removed the pollution around the room with an average VE below 1 but above 0.7 while main­
taining a good indoor environment and not causing any local discomfort. Hence, the ventilation
configuration was efficient and complied with previous findings in the literature.
Loomans et al. [39] concluded that LV resulted in a high local VE near the supplied air but also
caused large VE variations and a lower overall VE in the room. The box diffuser experiments
showed similar results with an extremely high VE beneath the diffuser and lower values in the
room. Another study by Whyte et al. [31] concluded that if using the ACE index as a measure
for VE, higher values in some parts of the room are associated with lower efficiencies in the
rest.

In their study, a high ACE was measured directly below the air inlet when using a downward flow,
but they obtained low indices in the rest of the room resulting in higher airborne concentrations
than desired.
Opposite to the studies by Whyte and Loomans, the local VE in the rest of the room when using
the box diffuser arrangement was close to 1.0, with the lowest value being 0.8 and not below
0.7 at any point, still indicating good contamination removal. This outcome was probably due to
the configuration also including a perforated supply diffuser in the middle of the room, creating
some mixing of the air in the rest of the room and diluting the contamination here.
With high cleanliness requirements, conditions with high VE variations around the room are
unacceptable. Such cases require improvements, which are currently performed by a higher
ACR and using MV. The ISO 14644 standards, as [15], demand all measuring points in the room
to meet the required particle concentration level and not only the average.
Therefore, Loomans et al. [39] concluded that with the current standard, there are no benefits
gained from ventilation configurations that result in a high VE above 1 in one place if it is below
1 at other positions. However, in their experiment, the local VE was extremely low at several
points in the room, causing the overall VE to be under 0.5. The box diffuser configuration in this
study showed an average VE in the room above 0.7, even without including the high local VE
on the table. Hence, using a combination of LV and ceiling supply diffusers seems promising
and may improve the ventilation strategy creating an efficient ventilation system with a reduced
ACR. The LV diffuser should be placed above the critical area if the setup is fixed to supply
clean air directly downward and ensure a high­efficiency contamination removal. To achieve
sufficient VEs in the rest of the room and ensure an acceptable contamination distribution, it is
recommended to combine the LV solution with a ceiling diffuser to mix the air in the remaining
part of the room.
A limitation of the box diffuser experiments was that they did not include occupant movement.
For the configuration to be optimal, it should also be efficient when there is motion in the room.
Hence, such an experiment should have been conducted. Future work with the box diffuser
could also study the setup combined with ceiling exhausts instead of the low­level wall exhausts
since the smoke showed that some of the air attached to the thermal plumes of the occupants
and rose instead of being extracted. Another relevant experiment could have been to supply an
uneven ACR distribution through the two supply diffusers. In the experiments, the airflow was
derived from a total ACR of 10 h−1, where 5 h−1 flow through the box diffuser and the other half
was through the perforated diffuser. Other distributions like 7:3 h−1 or similar could be applied.
Comparison of Airflow from Box Diffuser and Low­Impulse Diffuser
The results from the air speed and Tu measurements beneath the box diffuser showed a down­
ward airflow that was not fully laminar. However, it can be discussed how low the Tu and
air speeds should be for the flow to be considered laminar, and the findings were therefore
compared to the ones from a low­impulse diffuser installed in a cleanroom. Appendix A.11.2
presents these measurements.
The airflow from the low­impulse diffuser was around 250 l/s compared to 90 l/s through the box
diffuser, but it had a bigger area resulting in similar airflows pr cm2. Still, the low­impulse had
a slightly higher flow. Another difference was that the measuring points below the low­impulse
diffuser were vertically closer to the diffuser, with 26 cm being the furthest level away. For the
box diffuser, the closest level was 24 cm, and the most distant level was 1.14 m.

Flexibility
An important factor to consider when comparing all these different ventilation configurations and
methods is flexibility, describing how easy the ventilation system is to change if modifications
occur. Such changes could be to the room layout or activity in the room.
The ventilation solution with the box diffuser is not flexible if used in an industrial cleanroom.
The configuration depends on the placement of the critical area as it provides clean air directly
into this area, resulting in a higher VE here and lower values in the rest of the room. If the
manufacturing area needs to be moved, the risk of contaminating the product elsewhere is
high. Here, MV and DV are generally more flexible and not necessarily limited to a specific room
layout. If the working area is moved or the heat load distribution is changed, the DV method
can compensate without changing the system since the buoyant forces drive the supply system.
The supplied air is drawn towards the heat loads, so if the heat sources are close to the critical
area, the clean air will rise here [30].
Usually, MV is a flexible solution as the contamination is evenly distributed throughout the room,
but this was not the case in this study. The results of the MV cases with the swirl diffusers and
low­level wall exhaust used in Experiments 2 and 4 showed to be sensitive to the placement of
the critical area. The contamination decreased significantly when the setup was moved to one
side of the room, indicating that the configuration was not flexible.
Flexibility in terms of installations should also be considered, describing how easy they are to
implement in practice. A low­level wall exhaust is difficult to construct, and a ceiling exhaust
would be preferable. This issue was also a challenge in this study, where it was not possible
to implement a real low­level wall exhaust, so it was modelled using a displacement diffuser
instead, which could have affected the results.
Supply diffusers used for DV has similar limitations, as they can be challenging to implement
with little wall area. Also, the diffusers take up a lot of space, which should be accounted for
when designing a cleanroom. In contrast, ceiling diffusers do not interact with the furniture
layout and other obstacles in the room.
Data Processing & VE Indices
In all experiments, the performance of the ventilation system was described by the VE to nor­
malize the results based on the contamination at a certain point in relation to the concentration
in the exhaust. The VE depended on the average tracer gas concentration in the two exhausts,
but in most cases, the two exhaust diffusers did not have similar N2O levels. The exhaust clos­
est to the critical area seemed to have a higher concentration than the other, which dragged the
average down. Therefore, the results would have looked different if the highest exhaust con­
centration was used in the calculations instead of the average. Overall, it would have resulted
in higher VEs for most measuring points but not so significantly that it would have changed the
conclusions.

In general, other indices can be used to calculate the performance of a ventilation system,
such as the Contamination Removal Effectiveness (CRE) index, Performance Index (PI) and
ACE. The CRE index is similar to the VE used in this study but only accounts for the average
contamination concentration in the room and not the concentration locally for each measuring
point. The PI concerns the average contamination level to the one at the critical area, and the
ACE is the ACR in a location divided by the overall ACR [91]. The different indexes can be used
for different situations, and the results could have differed if any of these were used.
Whyte et al. [91] considered the ACE and PI the most suitable indexes for cleanrooms, while
the CRE was suggested for fixed pollution sources. Hence, a version of the CRE similar to the
performance calculations in this study is useable due to the fixed contamination, but in a real
cleanroom with several pollution sources of different strengths, other VE indexes are prioritized.
Here, the study by Whyte et al. [91] stated that the ACE should be used when the cleanroom
aims to provide large amounts of clean air in a critical location.
Pollution Sources & Tracer Gas
Since tracer gas was used in the experiments as the indicating factor for the performance of
the ventilation configurations instead of particles, the results may differ in reality and are only
applicable for particles with sizes below 5 µm. In the experiments, the pollution source was
the occupants since people have been stated to be the primary source of contamination in
cleanrooms by the literature [8]. Particle generation has different reasons, and the shedding of
hair and skin flakes is one. Therefore, the pollution was released from the right wrist of the two
occupants imitating skin flakes. However, these particles may, in practice, be larger than 5 µm.
In particular, the experiments with DV could result in different outcomes in reality since the air
may not be able to lift the larger particles if they are too heavy since they are more affected by
gravity than smaller particles.
Another limitation related to the pollution source was that different release positions were not
tested, and the tracer gas generation was steady. Realistically, the pollution concentration varies
and comes from multiple sources simultaneously. Hence, these factors could also be taken
into account in future studies. The room included two occupants, but there could be more
or fewer workers. In general, there is a need for more studies on particle generation from
people as the parameter is not generally well­acknowledged. The amount of particles generated
varies significantly from person to person, indicating that personal factors may influence one’s
contamination of the indoor environment. Here, it would be interesting to study parameters
like gender and body composition. If these factors are highly influential, the information can
help predict the pollution inside a specific cleanroom and design the ventilation system after an
accurate particle level. However, it should then be considered how to make the solution more
flexible as the system would rely on specific types of people, which changes over time.
The tracer gas gave a good indication of whether the different ventilation configurations removed
the contamination properly in the room. However, the ISO cleanliness classes depend on the
measured particle concentration of different­sized particles. Realistic particle concentrations
could not be measured since proper particle generators were unavailable. Hence, the results
cannot be compared to the cleanliness requirements, which should be validated in an actual
cleanroom under operation.

Energy Use Simulations
The energy simulations showed that the potential energy saving was a decreasing function de­
pending on the ACR. Therefore, other experiments could have included reducing the ACR even
more than 10 h−1 for the cases that proved efficient at this value to save more energy. It would
especially have been interesting to perform such investigations for the ventilation configuration
with the box diffuser since the VE in the critical area was unnecessarily high. Thus, an experi­
ment could be done to see how low the ACR could be reduced to get a VE of 1 on the equipment.
Another suggestion for future work could be to examine whether the 10 h−1 is sufficient for other
types of ventilation configurations than the ones tested in this study and whether it is feasible
to lower the ACR suggestions in the standards.
For the energy simulations, an assumption was that each ventilation system had the same SFP
independent of the ACR and supply and exhaust diffuser. This may not be the case in a real­life
situation. The pressure drop would have to be calculated for each specific system. Here, the DV
outlets often have a lower pressure drop, and thereby smaller fan components can be selected,
which can cause a reduction in fan energy [30]. A reduction in the ACR will lead to decreased
energy use, but the saving depends on several factors, such as duct dimension before and after
the ACR reduction and fan efficiency.
Many studies have been conducted with demand control filtration, DCF, utilizing VAV and proved
high energy savings, but the ventilation system in this study was CAV. Here, energy savings
would be affected by the constant airflow rate and the SFP. Further studies could examine
the HVAC system components and determine if the saving potential is the same in a small
cleanroom compared to a bigger one considering the pressure drop.
The experiments did not include any HEPA filter in the supply diffusers due to the use of tracer
gas, as it would not be affected by the filter. However, the ventilation systems would, in reality,
include HEPA (or ULPA) filters, which will have a pressure drop across the filter. This pressure
difference varies and depends on the airflow, duct, and how clean or clogged the filter is, but
generally ranges between 50­200 Pa [92]. However, this extra pressure loss was not accounted
for in the energy simulations. If considered, the energy use would be higher. It should be noted
that the energy simulations conducted are still applicable since they were used to calculate the
ratio and examine the relationship between the energy and ACR, and the exact values for the
system energy were not discussed.

Conclusion
The study aimed to investigate different ventilation configurations in an occupied ISO 8 pharma­
ceutical cleanroom to optimize the performance of the ventilation system and examine possible
energy savings by reducing the ACR. From the initial research on the topic and existing lit­
erature, several hypotheses were defined, as stated in the Objectives Section. The following
paragraphs answer these hypotheses and overall objectives.
Energy simulations concluded that reducing the ACR saved energy and that the system energy
was a linear regression that decreased with the ACR. The experiments verified the potential of
lowering the ACR to 10 h−1 without compromising the ventilation system’s performance and still
achieving the necessary air quality. Such discoveries were detected when using DV or LV.
The climate chamber results proved that different supply and exhaust types and layouts affected
the VE and contamination distribution inside the room considerably, as the outcome varied sub­
stantially depending on the ventilation configuration. The position of the critical area in relation
to the supply location also showed to have an impact and is essential to consider. Thus, choos­
ing a ventilation configuration highly depends on the aim of the cleanroom, particularly whether
it is to have an overall sufficient air quality and clean environment in the entire room or if certain
areas with higher cleanliness are prioritized.
For the MV configuration with swirl supply diffusers in the ceiling, using low­level walls exhaust
showed a more efficient contamination removal on the table than the ceiling­mounted exhaust,
agreeing with the initial hypothesis.
The investigated MV cases were inefficient and resulted in too high contamination levels. Com­
pared to MV, DV and LV air distribution methods provided much higher VEs.
DV showed a higher VE than the MV at both 10 and 20 h−1 and was generally efficient at remov­
ing the generated contamination. However, DV solutions might cause local thermal discomfort
at the ankles since high air speeds and draught rates were measured. This outcome needs to
be further investigated with human subject experiments.
The pharmaceutical equipment used to generate heat showed to influence the air distribution,
contamination level, and VE, especially when using DV. The DV solution failed to remove the
pollution from the critical area when the equipment was turned on.
The box diffuser supplying locally clean air to the critical area used in Experiments 13­16 was
the most efficient ventilation configuration in the study. It efficiently removed the contamination
on the table, and the combination with the perforated ceiling diffuser resulted in high VEs in the
room. The ventilation configuration was able to provide a VE above 1 at a reduced ACR of 10
h−1 and did not deteriorate by the heat load from the equipment or particle generation.
Due to the scope and limitations of this study, there is still a need for further investigations of
multiple aspects concerning cleanroom ventilation and energy optimization, and the conclusions
stated here are limited to the frame of the conducted experiments and simulations. Hence,
experiments with more realistic conditions need to be performed to confirm the results if the
standards should be revised.
