Abstract
Introduction:
Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death world wide. Conditions such as
left sided heart failure, can often be asymptomatic for a prolonged time, even though the physiology of
the heart is changing. Through scanning techniques such as MRI, CT and US, it is possible to investigate
the physiology of the heart and make early detection of onset of disease possible. This study proposes a
method for using MRI to acquire volumetric data of the left ventricle (LV), and building a computational
fluid dynamics (CFD) model from this, which in turn can provide hemodynamic parameters carrying
more physical insight than the MRI scan itself. The ability of CFD to provide functional insight can help
establish the correlation between hemodynamic parameters and stage of health, and in turn aid in clinical
decision making.
Methods:
A pipeline was constructed to create CFD models of the LV from 4D (3D + time) MRI data
from healthy volunteers. The data acquired contained the LV, and consisted of 30 volumes through the
cardiac cycle. Through semi-automatic segmentation, a 3D model of the LV was created for each car-
diac phase. The model of the end systolic volume (ESV) was used as the initial input for the CFD mesh.
Through registration of the ESV model to the remaining cardiac phases, the movement of the LV was pre-
scribed and implemented in the CFD model. From the motion and volume of the LV a inlet flow velocity
was calculated to match the volume expansion during the diastole. This inlet flow was used as a boundary
condition for the simulation of turbulent flow inside the moving LV model. The resulting flow of the CFD
model was analysed through qualitative and quantitative measures of the flow. The λ2-method was used
to investigate the vortex formation, and transport of concentrated fluids was used to investigate the LV
wash out.
Results:
The pipeline was successfully used to create CFD models based on three different data sets,
from two different volunteers. The observed flow patterns, vortex formation, and LV wash out, for the
two models from the same volunteer, showed great differences, which are attributed differences in the
imaging and segmentation process. Generally the models showed results comparable to those found in
the literature, regarding flow patterns and vortex formation. The LV wash out was shown to be much
more effective than in comparable studies from the literature.
It was unsuccessfully attempted use the pipeline to create CFD models based on clinical data from rou-
tine scans. The main problem was found to be patient motion during scan time, resulting in 3D volumes
of the LV deemed useless for CFD modelling.
Conclusion:
The proposed pipeline for using MRI data to build a CFD model showed promising re-
sults. Work must be done to improve imaging and segmentation methods to further improve the pipeline,
to produce consistent models across data from the same individual. Before clinical implementation can
be considered, work must be done to ensure higher patient cooperation during scanning, or finding a
method to work around the motion disturbances.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Cardiovascular disease
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of death world wide, responsible for approximately
one third of all deaths [2]. The term CVD covers, but is not limited to, heart attack, stroke, heart failure,
coronary heart disease, congenital heart disease, hypertension, and many more. Progression of many of
these diseases are in direct relation with people’s life style, regarding diet, physical activity, consumption
of alcohol, use of drugs, etc. Preventive treatment, such as educational actions, is thus a major compo-
nent in fighting these diseases [2]. Onset of disease, is not always clear, and may be asymptomatic. One
example is left sided heart failure, which can be caused by progressive systemic hypertension. The in-
creasing pressure in the systemic circulation forces the muscles of the heart to work harder, inducing
muscle hypertrophy, as well as an increased heart dilation to ensure stronger force of contraction, as
stated by Frank-Starling’s law [3]. The heart adapts progressively, as hypertension builds up, and the
patient might not experience any symptoms at first. After prolonged overload the heart diminish to keep
up, cardiac performance starts to gradually decline, and symptoms starts to appear. At this point in time,
remodelling of the heart, including build-up of fibrosis in the musculature tissue, has already put the pa-
tient at risk of multiple other pathological life-threatening conditions, such as arrhythmia or heart failure
[4, 5]. This reasons for the use of medical tools for early detection of onset of disease, such as medical
imaging for visualization of structural changes, methods for direct detection of cardiac functionality, and
computational methods for investigation of hemodynamic parameters not directly available for measure-
ment.
Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) modelling is one such method, which allows for investigation
of parameters otherwise only attainable through invasive methods, or otherwise unattainable, e.g. wall
shear stress at the inner walls of the heart, and local pressure gradients. CFD requires the input from
other medical methods, e.g. computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), or ultra-
sound (US), for the physical structure of the model and boundary conditions, but then it can provide
spatial and temporal resolution far greater than any imaging modality. This makes it a great companion
tool, which can be used in diagnosis, pre- and post-operative assessments and more. Most of the methods
used for inputs to CFD is noninvasive and thus preferable for vulnerable patients. [6, 7]
CFD as a tool in clinical practice is becoming more and more relevant, as the health care system pushes
away from treatments based on broad population-averaged data, and towards patient specific interven-
tion. It is already an established method within research and development of medical devices, such as
coronary artery stents and cardiac valve prosthesis [8, 9]. In clinical settings it has been used to under-
stand the localization of atherosclerosis and the hemodynamics of the affected arteries [6, 10]. A future
perspective of cardiac CFD is to create patient specific holistic models of the entire cardiac system, in-
cluding the physical structure of the heart, pulmonary and systemic circuit, as well as the mechanical,
electrical and hemodynamic components of the system. The increasing computational power following

1.2
State of the art
Cardiac CFD is a blooming field. It is accelerated by the technological improvements within medical
imaging techniques, ensuring higher quality and more true to life inputs to the models. Furthermore,
increasing computational power and development of mathematical methods and algorithms pushes the
field forwards [7]. This chapter aims to point out a few of the many studies within cardiac CFD and
through these, show the importance of the field.
Watanabe et al. [11] used CFD to investigate the effect of myocardial infarction on the flow in the
left ventricle (LV). They used MRI to create an end-diastolic model of the LV, then artificially simulated
increasing levels of apical myocardial infarction, and showed how the flow changed in accordance. They
showed a difference in velocity and vortex distribution, with a significant stagnation of blood in the api-
cal region, resulting in increased risk of thrombosis formation. By showing this, they demonstrated how
CFD can be used to gain more insight in development of cardiac disease, by investigating the underlying
explanation.
Seo et al. [12] looked into the effect of the mitral valve on the LV flow. They used CT images to
construct the LV model, and artificially constructed a simplified two-leaflet mitral valve based on phys-
iological measurements. They then mathematically described the opening and closing of the valve, and
investigated the resulting hemodynamics. They showed how including the valve significantly increased
the wash out of blood from the apical region of the LV. The wash out reduces the risk of thrombosis forma-
tion, and the results of Seo et al. shows how a more complex model can help us gain understanding of the
intricate works of the cardiac system. Alternatively, Dahl et al. [13] produced a 2D model incorporating
the leaflets of the mitral valve in a fluid-structure interaction (FSI) model, solving for both the motion of
the blood and the leaflets. They then verified their results using ultrasound to determine the movement
of the leaflets, thus showing how even very simplified models can be used to accurately describe the phys-
iology.
Long et al. [14] investigated the effect of the inlet boundary condition on the inlet velocity profile
and the resulting flow in a 3D model of the LV. The geometry was obtained from MRI over the entire
cardiac cycle. They showed how a difference in the area of the inlet, as well as different ways of defining
the pressure at the inlet boundary, results in very different inlet velocity profiles. This in turn results in
very different flow patterns in the LV. The area of the inlet and outlet is a somewhat dynamic entity in
a human heart, why understanding the impact of the way it is modelled, is crucial for creating reliable
simulation results, useful in the real world. This study shows exactly how difficult the process can be, and
how even small changes in the model can generate entirely different outcomes.
Doost et al. [15] investigated the impact of modelling the blood as a non-Newtonian fluid instead of
the more common Newtonian simplification. They used cardiac MRI to construct a time-varying model of
the LV, and then used different constitutive equations for non-Newtonian fluids to model the flow. They
showed a significant difference in both spatial dependent viscosity, streamlines through the ventricle,

1.4
Aim of project
A lot of work is needed before a holistic CFD model of the heart and circulatory system can be completed,
why this is out of the scope of this project. The road to reaching this is however paved with a lot of minor
contributions regarding modelling of different parts and mechanisms of the heart. The aim of this project
is to use cardiac MRI to obtain the necessary inputs to create a CFD model of the LV of the heart. The
necessary building blocks for a working CFD model includes the LV structure through the entire heart
cycle, description of the motion between individual cardiac phases, and a description of the inlet flow.
The concrete tasks in the project are as follows:
• Explore different MRI techniques and sequences for cardiac imaging, and their usefulness as inputs
for a CFD model.
• Use MRI to obtain volumetric images of the LV through the entire cardiac cycle.
• Obtain 3D structures of the LV through segmentation of the MRI images, to use in the CFD model.
• Describe the movement of the ventricle through the cardiac cycle by the change in the 3D structures,
and apply this movement to the CFD model, to correctly model the motion of the LV.
• Use the volumetric information from the 3D structures to reconstruct an inlet velocity profile for
the CFD model.
• Use MRI to measure the flow through the mitral valve, to use as a method for verification of the
reconstructed inlet flow profile.
All in all, this project is an exploratory feasibility study, which seeks to explore the possibilities of using
MRI to obtain input data for a CFD model, as well as exploring the possibilities within CFD modelling of
the LV. The final outcome will contribute towards the greater goal of one day being able to create patient
specific, holistic cardiac models, which can aid in clinical decision making, such as diagnosis of diseases,
choice of intervention, and pre-operative planning.

2.1
Physiology of the human heart
The heart is responsible for the circulation of the blood through the body, ensuring proper oxygenation
of all tissue. The anatomy of the heart and the route for blood through it, is shown in figure 2.1, and
explained in the following. The blood reaches the heart via the inferior and superior vena cava and fills
the right atrium. From here the blood is forced through the tricuspid valve, into the right ventricle, and
then through the pulmonary valve, into the pulmonary circuit. From the pulmonary circulation, the
oxygenated blood enters the left atrium via the pulmonary trunk. It is then forced through the mitral
valve (also known as the bicuspid valve) and into the LV, then further through the aortic valve and into
the systemic circulation. [21]
==PAGE:22==
8
2 Background and theory on data acquisition
Figure 2.1: Anatomy of the human heart. Arrows indicate direction of blood flow. Colors, red and blue,
indicates whether the blood is oxygenated or not, respectively. Figure adapted from [21].
The physiology of the mitral and tricuspid valves, ensures that when the intra-ventricular pressure
rises during contraction, the valves are closed, to avoid any backflow. Furthermore, as electrical poten-
tial stimulates the contraction of the ventricles, contraction of the papillary muscles is also stimulated,
ensuring the valve leaflets are not pushed backwards into the atria when intra-ventricular pressure rises.
This leaves the blood nowhere else to go than through the pulmonary or aortic valve. These will open
when the intra-ventricular pressure exceeds the pressure in the proceeding vessel. As the ventricular
contraction comes to an end and the intra-ventricular pressure drops below the pressure in the vessel,
the blood is pushed back up against the valves. The semilunar shape of the valve leaflets ensures that this
pressure serves to close the valves, again avoiding any backflow into the ventricles. [21]
Defects in any of the valves can cause unwanted backflow or leaking, which is called regurgitation.
Regurgitation can influence the dynamics of the blood flow, by introducing unwanted turbulence [21].
Several different measurements are used to quantify the different volumes of blood. The end-systolic
volume (ESV) is the smallest volume the LV will comprise, after all the blood has been pumped out. Just
before the aortic valve opens, the end-diastolic volume (EDV) is found, which is where the LV volume is
at its largest. The stroke volume (SV) is defined as the difference between the two, SV = EDV - ESV. This
calculation assumes that no regurgitation is present. The SV is not comparable across individuals, as the
size of the entire heart varies. Instead, the ejection fraction (EF) can be used, given as the fraction; EF
= SV / EDV. The EF of the LV is sometimes referred to as the LVEF to specify, but as only the LV is of
concern in this project EF will be used for simplicity. [21, 22]

2.1.1
The mitral valve
The mitral valve is composed of a ring structure, called the mitral annulus, and two leaflets, which are
further divided into three regions or segments each (see figure 2.2). The leaflets are attached to the
mitral annulus, which is composed of muscular, fibrous, and adipose tissue. The annulus prevents major
structural changes to the valve during motion of the heart, ensuring proper functionality. It is however
non-continuous at the posterior leaflet, allowing for small structural changes. [23]
Figure 2.2: Mitral valve anatomy. The three segments of each leaflet is labelled A1-3 and P1-3. Figure
adapted from [24].
The flow of blood through the mitral valve is governed by two processes, the passive filling mediated
by a low pressure in the ventricle, and the active filling mediated by the contraction of the atrium. The
passive filling starts as the LV musculature relaxes, causing the intra-ventricular pressure to drop to the
point where it is below the atrial pressure. The pressure gradient drives the flow from atrium into the
LV, thus opening the mitral valve. This flow is initially quite fast, with peak velocities in the order of
0.6-1.0 m/s or even higher [25, 26]. As the pressure gradient becomes smaller, the velocity decreases.
Looking at the velocity profile in figure 2.3, the velocities of the passive filling is what is characterized by
the E-wave. The contraction of the atrium increases the atrial pressure, and thus reestablish a pressure
gradient, causing an increase in flow into the ventricle. This flow persists until the contraction of the
ventricle, where the gradient is flipped, the mitral valve closes, and flow will come to an abrupt halt. The
active filling is characterized by the A-wave, with velocities in the range of 0.2-0.35 m/s [25–27].

2.2
The Flow of Blood
To describe the hemodynamics of blood, it is necessary to look into its physical properties. Factors such
as viscosity, pressure, and velocity determines how blood as a fluid behaves within a given domain. Being
able to describe the physics behind the hemodynamics will allow for more in-depth investigations.
2.2.1
The viscosity of blood
Viscosity is commonly known to describe the thickness of a fluid, however a more scientific way of de-
scribing it is how well a fluid resists motion when a shear stress is applied to the fluid. In turn, this comes
down to the molecular structure of the fluid. [29]
If the viscosity of a fluid is independent on the deformation of the fluid, i.e. the fluid velocity and
shear rate, it is called an ideal or Newtonian fluid (such as water). On the other hand, non-Newtonian
fluids, such as blood, change behavior dependent on the physical conditions. Blood is made up of plasma
containing a variety of cells, proteins, and molecules, resulting in a complex fluid, which physical charac-
teristics are dependent on the composition and setting, e.g. in the smallest vessels, blood can no longer
be characterized as a homogeneous fluid. Furthermore, blood is a shear thinning fluid, meaning that as
velocity increases, viscosity decreases. Figure 2.4 shows a comparison between the viscosity curve for

2.2.2
Reynolds number
When considering flow in tubes (or vessels), it is important to investigate how the particles of the fluid
moves. The overall flow pattern will be either laminar, turbulent, or somewhere in between. The two
states, at each end of the spectrum, can best be described by considering the streamlines of a flow, i.e.
the lines particles will follow over time. Following figure 2.5, a laminar flow is when the streamlines are
ordered and straight, whereas for laminar flow they are inconsistent and mixed up, creating eddies or
turbulence in the flow. [29, 32]
Figure 2.5: Illustration of streamlines in laminar and turbulent flow. Figure adapted from [33].
A good indication for whether the flow in a vessel is laminar or turbulent is by looking at the Reynolds
number. It is given as

2.3
Magnetic Resonance Imaging
For data acquisition two different MRI sequences were used, a 2D balanced steady state free precession
(bSSFP) cine sequence, and a 2D PC flow cine sequence. The theory behind each are described in the
following.
2.3.1
bSSFP
One of the most commonly used MRI sequences for functional cardiac imaging is the bSSFP. The bSSFP
is a gradient echo sequence which consists of a series of equally spaced radio frequency (RF) pulses with
flip angle α < 90◦. Between each set of RF pulses there is a set of phase encoding gradients, as well as a set
of frequency encoding gradients. This is the general setup for any given SSFP sequence. What separates
them from each other is the pattern of the applied gradients. The bSSFP sequence diagram is shown in
figure 2.6. The main principle in the sequence design is that the gradients are balanced in between each
set of RF pulses, resulting in a net gradient-induced dephasing of zero. In SSFP sequences with three
or more pulses it is possible to read out signal from the free induction decay, spin echos, or stimulated
echoes. With a constant repetition time (TR), and echo time (TE) equal to half the TR, as in bSSFP, all
three will contribute to the read out signal. [34, 35]
Figure 2.6: Sequence diagram for a bSSFP sequence with illustration of the development of the main
magnetization vector through the sequence. A single arrow indicates in-phase magnetization, in contrast
to a ring indicating out-of-phase magnetization. S: Slice, P: Phase, and R: Readout direction. TR: Repe-
tition time. TE: Echo time. Figure adapted from [35].
During each TR interval, part of the transverse magnetization will disappear due to T2 relaxation, as
well as new longitudinal magnetization will be created due to T1 relaxation. This newly formed longitu-

dinal magnetization will then experience excitation from the subsequent RF pulse, and thus some new
transversal magnetization is formed. After a certain amount of TR intervals a steady state is reached
where the transversal magnetization is somewhat steady. A characteristic of any SSFP sequence is that
TR << T2. What separates the bSSFP from other SSFP sequences, is the balance of the gradients. Lastly,
in order to reach a steady state with maximal signal strength on resonance, the sign of the flip angle α,
must switch between each repetition. Steady state can be reached without this addition, but the signal
will be much weaker [34, 35].
The bSSFP sequence is susceptible to so-called banding artefacts, which is an effect of field inhomo-
geneities. Field inhomogeneities causes off-resonance precession, which results in a phase offset between
RF pulses, and in turn loss of signal. The transverse magnetization at steady state is influenced by this
phase offset, as shown in figure 2.7. It is clear that if the offset gets close to 180◦the signal diminishes,

longer TR makes the sequence more susceptible to these artefacts. [34, 35]

The contrast of bSSFP is determined by the relation between T2 and T1, but also on the tip angle. The
relation between T2 and T1 in both fat and blood makes for high signal intensity. The short TR makes the
signal robust to flow and motion artefacts. All in all, this makes bSSFP well suited for imaging the heart,
giving good contrast between the wall and the blood. [34, 35]
For functional cardiac imaging, a single image is not sufficient, but instead a series of images must be
acquired over the cardiac cycle. This can be done in different ways, but one of the more commonly used
is by synchronizing the image acquisition with the cardiac cycle through ECG gating or PPG (finger pulse
oximeter) gating. From the gating signal, the MR scanner is capable of identifying separate heart beats
from another. Image data is then acquired during the interval of one beat at a time and retrospectively
divided into a predefined number of images. A single image within the cardiac cycle is refereed to as a
cardiac phase. The heart beat is too fast to be able to acquire complete images with satisfying spatial reso-
lution within a single beat, why data must be acquired over multiple heart beats and stitched together. A
sequence which acquires a series of images of the same location over time is referred to as a cine sequence.
[36] To be able to get a 3D image, a stack of 2D images can be acquired, layered on top of each other.
2.3.2
Phase contrast MRI
MRI is not only used for imaging physiological structures, but can also be used for imaging physiological
properties, including the flow of blood and other fluids. This most common method for this is PC MRI,
which is capable of producing a phase image where the phase of the transverse magnetization is propor-
tional to the velocity. [37]
Phase contrast utilizes bipolar gradients to encode and decode a phase shift in a given spatial direction.
A bipolar gradient is composed of two opposite acting gradients of equal magnitude. The static tissue will
experience equal, but opposite fields from the two gradients and have no net phase shift, whereas tissue
moving along the direction of the bipolar gradients will experience different fields from the opposite act-
ing gradients and thus gain a net phase encoding. The main principle is illustrated in figure 2.8. [37, 38]
==PAGE:30==
16

Figure 2.8: Principle of moving protons during a PC MRI sequence. Initially (1) there is no phase
difference between blood and static tissue. Application of the first gradient, G1 (2), enforces the same
phase shift in both blood and tissue. As time goes by the protons of the blood move (3), and after applying
the second gradient, G2 (4), a phase difference have accumulated. Figure adapted from [37].
The bipolar gradient orientation is chosen by the operator and is independent of the image slice orien-
tation, why it is possible to encode flow in any given direction in relation to the image slice. This is known
as the velocity encoding direction. The most useful for quantification of blood flow through a vessel is to
choose the encoding direction orthogonal to the image slice, so that the phase encoding is dependent on
flow through the image slice. [37, 38]
To create the actual PC image the process is repeated with an inverted bipolar gradient. This will give
rise to an image where all the static tissue has the same phase encoding as previously, while the moving
tissue has the opposite phase encoding than previously. These two images are subtracted from each other,
giving a value of zero for static tissue, and a value relating to the phase shift of the moving tissue. This
process removes any background phase change in static tissue caused by magnetic susceptibility caused
by field inhomogeneity, as it will be equal for both bipolar gradients. [37]
The output of any MRI method contains both the magnitude and phase information, which can be
presented as two individual images. For the PC method, the velocity information is found in the phase
image only, but the magnitude image can be useful for physiological orientation. From the phase data
a voxel wise calculation of flow velocity is made, most often depicting static tissue as light gray, while
brighter/darker intensities depicts higher velocities in the positive/negative velocity encoding direction.
[37]

2.3.2.1
Phase correction
For optimal imaging conditions the B0 field should be homogeneous, to avoid any phase variation. Un-
fortunately, the application of gradients introduce spatial variance in the field. The presence of the gra-
dients causes an inhomogeneity described as the concomitant field, which is proportional to the gradient
strength and inversely proportional to the B0 field strength [38, 43, 44]. The continuous switching on
and off, of the gradients induces a current in the scanner coils, causing another form of inhomogeneity,
known as eddy currents. These are especially prevalent in sequences with high slew rates, such as the PC
sequence, where it is desirable to quickly accumulate a phase change to be able to measure flow at a high
temporal resolution [43, 45].
The phase variation arising from the field inhomogeneity, can be corrected in image post-processing.
Usually the concomitant field is corrected for during the post-processing on the scanner. By analysis of
tissue in the image known to be stationary, the field inhomogeneity can be described, which allows for
eddy current compensation [43]. This can be done automatically using external software such as Medviso
Segment (Medviso AB, Lund, Sweden) [46], which is freely available for research purposes.

Theory on simulation
This chapter describes the theory and methods used for simulation of the flow of blood through the left
ventricle of the heart. Different aspects of computational fluid dynamics are presented as well as their
importance in this specific project.
4.1
Computational Fluid Dynamics
When working with the human body, it can often be impossible to set up physical experiments to gain
the needed insight. An example could be how different forms of prosthetic heart valves produce differ-
ent flow patterns in the heart. A lot of work can be done working with phantoms or animal models, but
a true to life model is hard to build, because of the intricate organic nature of how the human body works.
CFD is the field within simulation mathematics, which deals with exploiting the computational powers
of modern processors to simulate complex flow models. It is widely used within many different areas of
science, biology and medicine, where it for example can be used to simulate how the flow will be through
a prosthetic heart valve, placed inside a heart [9]. This can substantiate and add to the knowledge gained
from simple physical experiments, and increase the scientific understanding of the subject.
The overall method builds on the principle laws of conservation of mass, momentum and energy in a
system. These principles are used to build the governing (or constitutive) equations for a specific model.
The result is a set of partial differential equations (PDEs) describing the model and how it evolves over
time. As these PDEs are too complex to solve analytically, the model is discretized into small cells, where
it is assumed that they behave linearly. Furthermore, to find specific stable solutions, a set of boundary
conditions are required, e.g. the velocity or pressure at the inlet and outlet of a fluid domain. The solu-
tions to the PDEs are then approximated using an iterative numerical method. [52]
4.1.1

of Newton’s second law to a Newtonian fluid. The equation thus represents the conservation of momen-

4.1.1.1
Solution convergence
Navier-Stokes equation describes flow in all three spatial directions, and together with the continuity
equation, forms four coupled nonlinear PDEs. The end goal for the numerical iterative method is to find
a solution for the PDEs within the domain of the model. The variables to solve for are thus the velocity
field in all three spatial directions, and the pressure, at every point in the model.
Using a finite set of model points, this can be formulated as a matrix equation, containing all the
individual equations for all points. In a very simple form this can be formulated as
[A][x] = [B]
(4.3)
where [x] contains all the unknown variables. For any given solution [x] the residual error can be deter-
mined by insertion as
[A][x] −[B] = [r]
(4.4)
with [r] being the residual. The iterative solver uses a mathematical method to reduce the residual until it
reaches an acceptable low value. This mathematical method can vary depending on the simulation setup,
and will not be considered further in this project. If the iterative solver succeeds in finding a solution
with a acceptable low residual, the solution is said to be converged.
4.1.2
Boundary conditions
In order to constrain the PDEs to the specific model of choice, a set of boundary conditions must be ap-
plied. These are related to the geometry of the model, on which the PDEs are imposed, and are absolutely
necessary for the numerical approximation to converge towards any specific solution. The boundary con-
ditions specifies how the PDEs must behave at the boundaries of the domain. Within CFD, the boundary
conditions can be stated in terms of pressure, flow velocity, or mass flow, typically at the inlet and outlet
of the domain. As the boundary conditions imposes constraints on the PDEs, it is necessary that they are
well defined, i.e. they cannot be contradictory to the model. A common method in fluid dynamics is to
impose the inlet flow as a certain velocity or velocity field, while imposing the pressure at the outlet set to
zero. This restricts the model to find a solution to the PDEs where the flow velocities and pressure field,

carries the flow from the inlet to the outlet. [53, 54]
Another important boundary condition is how the flow should behave at the wall of the domain. The
wall composes every surface boundary in the model, which is not inlet or outlet. There are two main se-
tups for how the fluid interacts with the wall, slip or no-slip. With the slip condition the fluid experiences
no friction from the wall, whereas for the no-slip condition friction is present, effectively setting the veloc-
ity of the fluid at the wall, equal to the velocity of the wall (zero if the wall is stationary). This implies that
the adhesive force between the fluid and the wall is greater than the cohesive force within the fluid itself.
Within physiological CFD, the walls are not completely smooth, leading to increased adhesion, why the
no-slip condition is the most accurate. Whether friction is present or not influences the development of
the flow along the wall. [53, 54]
The last boundary condition discussed here will be movement of the boundary. Boundary movement
affects the behavior of the PDEs in several different ways, as it alters the geometric restrictions, and pos-
sibly induces volume changes to the domain. When talking about boundary movement it is essential to
discuss the two frames of references arising from two different methods of fluid analysis, the Eulerian
and the Lagrangian method. [54]
The Eulerian method of analysis builds on looking at a fixed point or volume in space. Analyzing the
particles which move within this space. This method gives rise to the spatial coordinate system, which is
fixed in place and used as reference for geometric placement in the ”real world”. The Lagrangian method
of analysis follows the individual particles as they flow through the entire domain, and is thus not con-
strained to any fixed position in space. This method gives rise to the material coordinate system, which
is fixed to the geometry of the model, as if it was painted on. The two methods are illustrated in figure
4.1. [53] Following the convention in Comsol, the spatial coordinates are referred to in lower case letters,
x, while the material coordinates are referred to in upper case letters, X.

geometry. In opposition, the material coordinate system follows the movement of the geometry allowing
the same coordinates to refer to the same internal point before and after movement. This is illustrated in

For simulations of the heart it is necessary to describe both the movement of the geometry, and the
flow of blood through the changing geometry. When describing the motion of the fluid it is most conve-

in terms of each individual particle over time. The motion of the boundaries are however best described
using the Lagrangian method, where the movement of each point on the surface can be described over
time. In order to work with both, the Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) method is introduced. The
ALE method introduces a transformation between the two domains, which allows mapping of spatially
fixed Eulerian nodes to be linked to moving nodes in the Lagrangian domain. Computationally, this is
solved by introducing a new frame of reference known as the mesh coordinate system or the ALE domain.
This coordinate system works as an intermediate step between mapping from one domain to the other,
and allows for mapping in both directions. This is illustrated in figure 4.3, where x, X, and χ denotes the

thus be described as φ = Φ(Ψ−1), with both ϕ and Ψ denoting the transformation from the ALE domain
to the other. Using the ALE method the boundaries are described using the Lagrangian method, while
the fluid is described using the Eulerian method. [53, 55, 57]

4.1.3
Turbulent flow modelling
It is difficult to imagine the flow inside the heart to be laminar. The atrium have several inlets, the vol-
ume of the chambers are small, irregular and non-smooth, the flow is pulsatile, and the peak velocities
are high. This all speaks in favor of refining the model to describe turbulent flow.
Figure 4.4 illustrates the development of a flow from laminar to turbulent, as the flow profile reaches
an edge of a plate. First the laminar flow will be maintained, but eventually it will transition into a turbu-
lent flow. Reynolds number can be used to estimate which state the flow is in. [53]

The challenge with turbulent flow is that it starts to oscillate locally and thus become time dependent,
even if the inlet flow is constant. The small eddies arising from turbulence makes it difficult to describe

patterns. Additional steps are taken to refine the formulation and more precisely describe the transport
of energy through the small oscillations. In Comsol a variety of models exists for describing turbulent
flow. Some of them introduces variables for turbulent kinetic energy, or functions to better describe the
flow in the viscous layer near the walls of the model. [53]

The discretization of the model into smaller cells, and solving the PDEs by numerical approximation is
known as the finite element method (FEM), while the actual splitting of the geometry is referred to as
meshing. The process of analyzing a problem using FEM is known as finite element analysis.
Discretization of a non-linear function, u(x), is illustrated in figure 4.7, using a linear combination
(weighted sum) of linear basis functions, gi. The basis functions are here defined as being 1 at a specific
node, and 0 elsewhere. As seen on figure 4.7, the basis functions only approximate the non-linear func-
tion, and it is easy to imagine how adding more basis functions would result in a better approximation.
[53]

Discretization is needed as the PDEs are too complex to solve analytically. Following figure 4.7, the
discretization creates a model, which is approximately equal to the original model. The discrete model is
however easier to find a solution to, as it is more relaxed in terms of its derivatives. The continuous model
requires a solution which is continuous in its second derivative, whereas the discrete model does not have
this requirement. It is evident that discretization, which assumes linearity in each of the segments, in-
troduces an error in the solution, in relation to the continuous solution. The better the discretization
approximates the continuous model (e.g. by including more nodes) the smaller the error [53, 54].
Meshing is the 3D equivalent of discretization used in CFD. The 3D geometric structure is split into
small polygons, each with a set of nodes, vertices, and faces. The nodes are what is connecting the individ-
ual polygons. During the iterative process of solving the PDEs the FEM model assumes linearity within
each individual cell. This implies that the meshing resolution needs to be fine enough to be able to ap-
proximate the non-linear nature of the PDEs. The behavior of the PDEs will be different in relation to the
geometric placement in the model, why some parts of the model might need finer meshing than others.
This is illustrated in figure 4.7, where a higher resolution of nodes are used locally in (B) to describe the
function with a steeper gradient. As an example, when analyzing flow over a blood vessel stenosis, the
flow will be more complex over the stenosis, thus requiring finer mesh resolution to accurately describe
the flow [53].
In Comsol it is possible to import a geometric structure in various file formats, and create a mesh from
this. The software offers multiple tools for automatic meshing, as well as manual mesh refinement. One
such tool used in this project is the manual control of the boundary layer of the mesh, as this is of great
importance for the fluid-boundary interaction.

4.2.1
Mesh Convergence Study
To ensure reliable results from the finite element analysis, a mesh convergence study must be carried out.
The accuracy of the analysis is directly dependent on the quality of the mesh used in the finite element
model. The quality of the mesh is dependent on how well the linearity results hold true within each of
the meshing elements, and thus how well the solution to the PDEs describe the true non-linear solution.
The quality of the mesh can be improved by different methods, where reducing the element size, effec-
tively increasing the amount of elements in the model, is the most commonly used. This method is easily
effectuated, as it allows the use of the automatic meshing tool in the software, while only changing the
coarseness parameter. Another method of improving mesh quality is by increasing the order of the ele-
ments, i.e. increasing the number of points on the lines between the nodes, allowing for more freedom
in the shape of the element. Figure 4.8 shows the difference between first and second order elements.
Furthermore it is also possible to do refinement on a global scale or only alter the mesh locally where
minimizing the error is of greater relevance, e.g. around corner structures, pressure points, etc. [53]
Figure 4.8: Illustration of a 2D and 3D triangular mesh element, both as a first- and second-order
element. Figure adapted from [53]
Increasing the number of elements in the model, increases the total number of degrees of freedom
(DOF) by increasing the number of nodes. The accuracy, but also the computational cost is directly re-
lated to the model’s DOF, so refining the mesh is a trade-off between computational time and tolerable
size of the error. [53]
A mesh convergence study investigates the error associated with a change in mesh quality. This is
done by assessing the solution for a given parameter at a specific point or surface in the model, for dif-
ferent levels of mesh quality. As the mesh quality is improved the error decreases and the specified pa-
rameter should converge towards the true value. Plotting the solution values for the chosen points in the
model, allows for visual interpretation of this process. A satisfactory solution is typically chosen to be
within a ±1% range of the asymptotic solution. [53]

The reasoning for doing the mesh convergence study is to ensure that once the model has been es-
tablished, it can be used with different inputs, i.e. different boundary conditions. For example if an
experiment is to test the effect of inlet velocity in a heart model, it is necessary to ensure that the output
is an effect of the input itself and not of uncertainties in the solution.

4.3
Analysis of simulation
Once the model is implemented and the simulation has converged to a solution, the solution needs to
be analyzed to extract relevant information. The different methods used to extract useful data from the
simulation solution will be presented in this chapter.
4.3.1
Velocity
Velocity is one of the main driving forces of the flow. One great way to visualize velocity is by extraction
of a 2D plane from the 3D model. This can show precise local velocities, however it lacks the information
of the 3D flow pattern. Streamlines are a great tool for this. Streamlines are widely used within flow
analysis to show the lines along which the fluid will flow. Theoretically a streamline is a curve, which
at every spatial point along the curve, is tangent to some instantaneous vector field. Mostly, this vector
field is the velocity field, as this will cause the streamlines to show the instantaneous velocity direction.
Plotting streamlines over time will show how the flow pattern evolves. [53]
4.3.2
Pressure
The other main driving component of the flow is the pressure. The pressure field can be visualized by 2D
contour plots for a plane of the 3D volume. This method is easily interpretable and can help show the
influence of the pressure on the flow pattern.
The intra-ventricular pressure gradient (IVPG) is a measure for the change in pressure between the
basal (close to inlet and outlet) and the apical region. Studies have shown that IVPG can be used as
an index for both systolic and diastolic function of the LV. Furthermore, IVPG has been shown to be a
predictor of progression of chronic heart failure following myocardial infarct [59–61]. By defining a set of
2D planes at different depths in the LV and calculating the mean pressure for these planes, it is possible
to quantify the difference in pressure levels over time.
4.3.3
Vorticity and vortex formation
Vorticity is the measure of local spinning motion within a fluid. It can be defined as twice the rotation of
the velocity field:
ζ = 2ω = ∇× u
(4.7)
Due to the nature of the vorticity, it cannot take arbitrary shapes. Instead it is constrained to develop
in tubes, rings, or other surface structures. Development of any vorticity structures arises from a fluids in-
teraction with the surface of its container. Considering the geometry of the LV, the vorticity is most likely
to form a torus-like structure (commonly called vortex ring or vortex) as the inlet jet moves through the
mitral valve. This is a result of the round shape of the valve, against which the blood will be dragged and
vorticity will be created. [62]
The formation of a vortex ring inside the LV is commonly accepted in the literature. Studies have
shown a correlation between different diseases, such as heart failure or mitral valve failure, and optimal
formation of the LV vortex. Optimal vortex formation has also been shown to play a positive role in the

4.3.3.1
The λ2-method
The most established method is known as the λ2-method, first introduced by Jeong and Hussain [64].
This method looks at the symmetric tensor created by S2 + Ω2, where S and Ωare the symmetric and
anti-symmetric part of the gradient velocity tensor, ∇u, respectively. The λ2-method defines a vortex
core structure as a region where the symmetric tensor S2 + Ω2 has two negative eigenvalues. This is
equivalent to ordering the three eigenvalues of the tensor and requiring that λ2 < 0. [64]
The λ2-method is commonly used to visualize vortex structures as an isosurface for a given λ2-value,
i.e. a surface where the evaluation of λ2 is equal. [66] Setting this value is however somewhat arbitrary
and mostly done empirically. Setting the value close to zero can include too many details, occluding the
visualization, while setting it too negative can result in a visualization which does not represent the actual
vortex structure. [67]

to the model itself, i.e. it tells how long time particles on average spends inside different parts of the

4.3.5
Ventricular wash out
Even though the residence time is a good indicator for stagnation, it does not tell where the blood is
moving, if it is not standing still. Ventricular wash out is a term for how well the blood left inside the
LV after systole is being ejected during the following systole, i.e. how well new blood entering the LV
from the atrium replaces old blood already residing in the LV. Generally, the flow can be split into four
different components, as illustrated in figure 4.10:
1. Direct flow: Blood which enters the LV from the atrium, and is being pumped directly out of the LV
within one cardiac cycle.
2. Retained flow: Blood which enters the LV, but is not pumped out again during one cardiac cycle.
3. Delayed ejection: Blood which is already residing inside the LV when the cardiac cycle begins, and
is then ejected from the LV.
4. Residual volume: Blood which resides inside the LV for multiple cardiac cycles.
LV wash out can be used as a marker for disease, as studies have found a worse wash out in patients
with LV aneurysm, mainly in the apical region. Furthermore, increased wash out is linked to decreased
risk of thrombosis. This is crucial when considering the insertion of a prosthetic heart valve in a patient,
considering the post-operative rate of success. The angulation of such a valve plays a critical role in the
LV flow dynamics, and thus influence the wash out. By keeping track of the different flow components it
is possible to determine the volumes of each, and in turn determine the residual blood through a series
of cardiac cycles. [12, 71–73]

5.3
Mesh motion
To describe the movement of the mesh created in Comsol, and thus simulate the beating of the heart,
a displacement field was created. To simulate the movement and transformation of the LV during the
cardiac cycle it is necessary to describe this movement to Comsol in the correct manner. It is not possible
to replace the mesh during a simulation, why it is not possible to just feed the software with all segmen-
tations throughout the cycle. Instead, what can be done, is to initialize the simulation with the mesh
from the initial segmentation. The movement can then be described as how Comsol should displace this
mesh to transform it to the mesh resulting from the segmentation of the following cardiac phases. This
displacement is described by what is referred to as the displacement field.
To create the displacement field a Matlab procedure was created, which uses the built-in function
imregdemons to calculate the displacement between two image volumes. The function uses diffeomorphic
demons, and is described in greater details in the papers by Thirion [75] and Vercauteren et al. [76]. It
takes as input the nifti files containing the segmentations of the LV for all cardiac phases, and outputs
a structure containing three 4D matrices (three spatial coordinates + time) describing the displacement
in each of the three individual spatial components, for every point in the spatial space. Describing the
displacement in the three spatial components individually is necessary for Comsol to handle it correctly.
The displacement is calculated between the first segmentation volume, as the moving part, and each of
the segmentation volumes from the cardiac phases, as the fixed part. The displacement is thus always
described in relation to the structure of the initial segmentation. This principle is illustrated in figure 5.8.
==PAGE:60==
46

Figure 5.8: Illustration of the process used to create the displacement field function.
To ensure the displacement can be repeated in a cycle the first and last time instance of the displace-
ment field relates the initial image volume to itself. This ensures no movement in the beginning and the
end, why the starting and ending points of the movement cycle are identical.
Physiologically, the LV expands in all directions, including the direction of the left atrium. This poses
a challenge, as the created inlet should not be distorted or moved during the simulation, in order to
correctly apply the inlet flow as a function of spatial coordinates. To accommodate for this three actions
are taken. Firstly, the segmentations are all aligned, so that the top of the segmentation is at the same
spatial position. This ensures that the displacement is in the direction of the apex. This will have an
effect on the flow, which is not quantified, as the pulling forces from the wall acting on the fluid will be
different. The correction is however very small compared to the overall movement, and it allows for much
easier convergence of simulation, as it minimizes displacement at the transition between inlet/outlet and
LV. This taken into account it is considered a reasonable action. Secondly, the rigid inlet and outlet are
added to the segmentations before displacement is calculated, to minimize displacement at these places
in the volume. If the inlet and outlet are not added, the empty space in their place will be moved around to
ensure a more correct displacement of the boundary of the LV. Lastly, a smoothed step function is applied
to the displacement field in the Z-direction, using the material coordinates. The step function is going
from unity to zero as a function of the Z-coordinate. For model 1 this transition happens between Z=0.09
and Z=0.10, as illustrated in figure 5.9. The step function is applied to all three spatial components of
the displacement. Effectively it sets the displacement to zero at the inlet and a large part of the inlet tract,
while no scaling is present for the LV volume. Using a smoothed step function allows for a continuous
displacement function.

5.3.1
Interpolation function
The displacement field is created as a time discrete variable. It contains a displacement field for each im-
age in the cardiac phase. However, to create a continuous time dependent simulation of the entire cardiac
cycle, it is necessary for Comsol to get a displacement field for any arbitrary time instance, at any given
spatial point. This is achieved by creating an interpolation function, which interpolates the displacement
field both spatially and temporally.
The function is written in Matlab, based on previous work by R. Hvid [19]. When the displacement
field is created, the time axis is concatenated, to be used in the interpolation function. Cubic interpolation
is used to achieve first order continuity of the movement.

seven instances of the displacement field, with a fixed time step between each. Weights and time steps
are given in table 5.2. The weights were determined empirically, and based on previous work by R. Hvid.

A continuation restriction between the beginning and the end, is needed to simulate multiple cycles
in succession. This is necessary to simulate enough cycles for the hemodynamics to reach homogeneity
between cycles. In other words, the simulation must go through enough cycles to get rid of any states aris-
ing from the initial conditions where no fluid movement is present. Continuity is satisfied if all spatial
points in the model are at the exact same location in the first and last step of the simulation, and if the
velocity of all spatial points are the same (note; not the fluid velocity, but velocity of moving mesh nodes).
The latter ensure continuation of the first derivative of the spatial position. By enforcing no movement
in the beginning and the end, these criteria are met.
For further temporal stability and cyclic homogeneity, a step function is applied to the displacement
field in the beginning and the end. It is a smooth step function which starts at t=0 s with zero value, and
ends at t=0.01 s with unit value. The step function at the end, has the opposite values and is applied to
the last 0.01 s of the cycle. This ensures that regardless of any interpolation, the displacement is zero at
the start and end. It also ensures a smooth transition away from zero displacement, which is desirable
for Comsol to be able to calculate a solution.

Turbulent flow

the interaction between the model wall and the fluid.

the better choice, as it will be able to correctly model the turbulence at least present during the E-wave.

5.5
Cyclic homogeneity
The starting point for the simulation model is a completely static situation where the pressure is defined
as p=10700 Pa, velocity as u=0 m/s everywhere, and mesh displacement as dx=0 m everywhere. This
situation is obviously not a physiologically correct starting point for any given cycle, why the simulation
must be run for several cycles to reach a state of cyclic homogeneity, where the final state of one cycle
is used as the initial state of the next cycle. Cyclic homogeneity is reached when no change in the fluid
dynamics can be observed between cycles.
5.5.1
Cycle convergence study
To ensure the simulation has reached a state of cyclic homogeneity, i.e. there is no significant difference
between consecutive cycles, a cycle convergence study is performed. The principle of this study is to mea-
sure a chosen parameter during each cycle, and then see how many cycles it takes for these measurements
to converge. There exists no standard for what measurements should be done, but generally the complete
volume should be considered. For this study, a volume integral of the flow velocities, and a volume inte-
gral of the TKE are used. These values are not meant to be meaningful, but only serve as a measure of
similarity between cycles. Using the volume integral does not ensure that the values have converged for
each individual spatial point, but it is a good measurement for overall convergence. Figure 5.19 shows
the results of the cycle convergence study.

5.6
Transport of concentrated species
To simulate and quantify LV wash out in Comsol, a multi physics module called ”transport of concentrated
species” was used. This module allows for specifying different species of fluids, and their individual fluid
properties. For this project, four different species were defined, all with the exact same physical proper-
ties, to mimic blood. For this the molar mass was specified as 35 kg/mol and the diffusion coefficient as
1.5 · 10−9 m2/s [79].
To investigate the wash out, the initial volume was specified as being filled with one fluid, and then
the inlet fluid was specified as another. For cycle 1 and 2 the inlet fluid was set to the same as the initial
fluid, as these cycles are meant for getting rid of any initial states. From cycle 3 to cycle 5, a new fluid is
used for every cycle to distinguish between the new blood from each cycle. For every time point in those
three cycles it is then possible to map out and quantify where and how much of each of the blood fluids
are present.

Flow vorticity and pressure fields
The vorticity of the flow field can aid in understanding the flow pattern. Vortex formation is important
for swirling the blood around and thus aid in replacing the blood residing in the LV.
The vorticity of the flow is illustrated using the λ2 method as explained in chapter 4.3.3.1. Figure
6.6 shows the isosurface of the vorticity plotted for λ2 = -1000 for a series of time instances during the E-
peak of model 1. The λ2 value is arbitrary and chosen empirically to achieve an interpretable visualization.
Here it can be seen how there is a vortex-like ring structure starting to form at the point where the inlet
is coupled to the ventricle, as the inlet jet is moving through. From figure 6.3 the inlet jet was seen to
reach the LV around t=0.14 s, coinciding with the start of vortex formation. This behavior of the flow is
as expected, when the domain changes from a narrow cylinder into an open volume. The ring structure
formed in the simulation is not perfectly torus shaped, but between t=0.16 s and t=0.18 s it is a complete
ring which is mostly free of the wall. The vortex fails to keep its ring structure, as it moves down towards
the apex. No vortex formation was observed during the A-peak.

CHAPTER8
Discussion
The content of this chapter is a thorough walk through of the outcomes, limitations and future aspects of
the work done and results found during the work of this project.
8.1
Data acquisition
Data acquired from healthy individuals is the ideal starting point for a proof of concept study. The quality
of data achieved when using an MR scanner is however dependent on different variables, such as body
composition, ability to control breath holding, resting heart rate, etc. For this project the image quality
obtained using the 2D FIESTA Cine sequence showed sufficient results, but this might not be the case for
every possible patient. Many patients with a cardiac disease suffer from breathing problems or obesity,
which might lower image quality. Working with the clinical data, showed how data available through
currently used routine was not of sufficient quality for the pipeline applied here, mainly due to motion.
Thus, to gain useful results higher demands on the imaging procedure must be met. Another strategy
would be to investigate the usefulness of motion correction during post-processing.
One main challenge with the images acquired using the 2D FIESTA Cine sequence, was the obtainable
resolution in the slice direction. The finest possible slice thickness was 4 mm, which is much coarser than
the frequency and phase resolution of around 0.74 mm. The slice thickness sets the limitation for how
accurate a segmentation can be made in 3D using the presented pipeline. Several attempts were made,
using different sequences, to acquire a 3D image with a finer resolution. However, none other had both
the desired resolution, contrast, and signal-to-noise ratio. Studies have proven other imaging strategies
to be successful, using the same sequences as used in this project. Nguyen et al. [84] combined short axis
and long axis images of the LV to gain higher resolution in all spatial directions. This allowed them to
get sufficient segmentations from 1.5T scan images, the same MR scanner strength as used in the clinical
data in this study. Benatti et al. [63] successfully used a series of long axis images rotated around the
apical-basal axis, also from a 1.5T scanner. This suggests that optimizing the imaging part of the pipeline
could improve the overall results and applicability. In turn this could make clinical application more fea-
sible.
Segmentation of the resulting 3D image volume were done semi-automatically. In a clinical setting a
full-automatic approach would be favoured, to be consistent across cases, and to rid the approach of any
human bias. Lots of different techniques exist for automatic LV segmentation. Many of them, more tech-
nically advanced than the methods used in this work, such as the deep learning models presented by Hu
et al. [86] and Shaaf et al. [87]. Implementation of such were unfortunately out of the scope of the project.
Measuring the velocity over the mitral valve using 2D PC MRI was found to be a technique difficult
to perform accurate and consistent. This is mainly because of the movement of the mitral annulus, and
==PAGE:100==
86
8 Discussion
because of the difficulties regarding anatomically consistent image slice placement. Because of the mov-
ing annulus, and the inlet jet formed as the blood rushes through the mitral valve, the resulting velocity
profile will depend highly on the slice location. Comparing the measured velocity to the velocity calcu-
lated from volume expansion should thus be used as a guiding tool, as the PC MRI measurements are not
a gold standard.
During the project, tests were made where the velocity measured with PC MRI was used as the applied
inlet flow. However, this resulted in unwanted complications, as for all models there exist a discrepancy
between the volume expansion and the flow measurement. Using the volume for flow determination lim-
its the source of error to one imaging sequence, rather than two. It might be possible, with the correct
expertise, to make flow measurements which are more precise, but using only the volume makes for a
more congruent model. Furthermore, for implementation into a clinical setting, it might be favourable
to derive the inlet velocity from the volume, as this simplifies the data acquisition process.
8.2
Model implementation
The implemented model is in general a simplification of the physiological reality. Some of these simplifi-
cation and their effect will be considered in the following.
The most significant simplification is most likely the missing valves, both the mitral and the aortic.
These are important for the flow in multiple ways, and the result of omitting them is difficult to quan-
tify. One function of both valves is to control the flow, such that no backflow is present. Backflow can
be observed at the outlet of the models, even though only in small volumes. The inlet flow is controlled
through the entire cycle, not allowing any backflow. However, as the inlet flow profile is made to match
the volume expansion, this backflow is minimized.
The mitral valve has been shown to have significant impact on the hemodynamics, both dependent
on the presence, the angulation of the annulus, and how its structure is modelled [72, 82]. How the mi-
tral valve is modelled, has also been shown to have an impact on the vortex formation, and thus in turn
the apex wash out. Furthermore, mitral regurgitation has been shown to have an impact on atrial blood
stagnation [63]. In summation, the importance of the mitral valve must not be neglected, and all results
should be seen in the light of this simplification.
Omitting the mitral valve, also includes omitting the papillary muscles, resulting in a more smooth in-
ner surface. The model is also implemented without any trabecular tissue, but instead with a completely
smooth inner wall. Studies have shown how this simplification results in an increased wall shear stress,
and a simplification of the turbulent flow pattern, as the smooth surface does not disturb the flow [50,
88]. The increased wall shear stress will in turn affect the RRT calculation. Smoothing the surface is
however a simplification which can be necessary for the CFD model to converge towards a solution.
One of the greatest challenges is that even with high resolution MRI it is difficult to observe the small
anatomical structures of the heart and their properties at a great enough level of detail, to correctly model
them through segmentation. Gao et al. [89] build a FSI model of the LV containing the mitral valve and
the papillary muscles from MRI data of a healthy volunteer. They were however, forced to manually
model the chordae structures connecting the leaflets to the papillary muscles as the image quality did
==PAGE:101==
8.2 Model implementation
87
not allow for segmentation of such small details. In the work by Sacco et al. [88] they acquired MRI
images of high enough quality to segment the trabecular tissue at the LV walls. This study was however
performed in vitro on organ donor hearts not suitable for transplantation, why the imaging techniques
and results are not comparable to what can be achieved on live persons. Lantz et al. [50] showed that it
was possible to create images from live persons of high enough quality to segment the trabecular tissue
and the papillary muscles using CT. This ignites the recurring debate of whether or not it can be justified
to use CT imaging to achieve higher resolution images at the cost of radiation exposure.
The inlet flow is implemented as a circular parabolic pulsatile flow. Considering that blood does not
flow through a long cylinder between the atrium and the ventricle, this is highly unlikely physiologically
correct. The alternative implementation in Comsol is a plug flow, with a flat profile over the entire inlet.
Considering the shape of the mitral valve, and the presence of valve leaflets, a plug flow might even be
a worse assumption as the parabolic profile has higher velocities in the middle, which is more alike the
physiology [90].
Including an aortic valve, which should be closed during the diastolic phase, would require absolute
congruence between flow volume and volume expansion. Another approach would be to create a FSI
model, where the wall of the heart is modelled as a mechanic part which can be affected by the forces of
the fluid. This allows for a flow dependent expansion of the LV, but rises several other challenges, such
as how the wall musculature mechanics should be modelled. Krittian et al. [91] and Gao et al. [89] have
both shown FSI modelling of the LV including soft tissue modelling of the wall. Some of the challenges
include muscle fiber orientation, determination of strain energy, and again the acquisition of patient spe-
cific properties through imaging.
Regarding the inlet and outlet, it is obvious that modelling them as two rigid cylinders is not anatomi-
cally correct. They are part of the wall structure, are not completely circular, and are not completely rigid.
Modelling these correctly will definitely have an impact on the hemodynamics, however the extent is dif-
ficult to determine. In this project the implementation of a correctly sized inlet was prioritized over the
outlet. This was done as the inlet was considered more relevant for the hemodynamics of the LV, as the
size determines the flow velocities into the LV, which in turn affects the flow pattern, vortex formation,
and wash out.
The displacement field method was shown to produce a dynamic LV which moved anatomically cor-
rect, with consistency between segmentation and model volume as shown in figure 5.10. The implementa-
tion of the Z-axis step function, to remove any displacement at the inlet was a necessary step for the CFD
model to converge. Considering the volume consistency, it can be argued that the Z-axis step function
had little to no influence on the LV movement. However, finding a way to correctly apply the inlet veloc-
ity on a moving surface, would cease the need for the step function and also the applied top alignment
(described in chapter 5.3). This would be a great improvement towards creating a model of the complete
heart, as this requires dealing with the multiple inlets of the atrium simultaneous.

8.3
Results
The results presented in this project show how it is possible to create a CFD model of the LV based on data
acquired using MRI. Results show how a physiologically accurate movement of the ventricle wall was im-
plemented, using the displacement field method. The resulting models were shown to have normal phys-
iologic values in regards of ESV, EDV, SV, and EF. The resulting hemodynamics of the intra-ventricular
blood flow, shown through visualization and quantification of velocity, streamlines, pressure, vorticity,
and wash out, are similar to what is seen in the literature, both from CFD and 4D MRI studies[12, 62,
92–94].
Outlet peak flow velocities were found to be above normal, as shown in figure 6.1, in the range of mild
stenotic patients. The outlet velocity is directly related to the radius of the outlet tract in the model. As
mentioned, the outlet tract in all models was implemented smaller than physiologically correct, compar-
ing to the PC MRI images, causing velocities higher than actual. As this is a known inaccuracy of the
model, the velocities are not of any concern.
Comparing the velocity components from model 1 and 2 (figure 6.3 and 6.4), it is clear that the flow
patterns are very different even though they are models of the same person. These differences arise from
the different parts of the modelling, especially the image acquisition and the segmentation. These are
crucial for how well the resulting model geometry corresponds with the actual LV, which in turn affects
both the movement and the flow.
The direction of the swirl in the middle of the ventricle was shown to differ between models. Nakashima
et al. [81] demonstrated how a counter-clockwise rotation leads to a greater loss of flow energy during
the systolic phase. The general impression of the flow pattern in model 1 (figure 6.3) is more chaotic and
turbulent than model 2 (figure 6.4), which in turn would cause a larger dissipation of energy through
eddies, consistent with the results of Nakashima et al. As mentioned, the direction of rotation is partially
dependent on the angulation of the mitral valve into the LV [82]. Looking at the geometry of model 1 and
2 (see figure 6.3 and 6.4, or appendix B.1), it can be seen that the angle between the inlet cylinder and the
apical-basal axis of the LV is different. The inlet of model 1 is aligned with the axis, and points directly
towards the apex. The inlet of model 2 is directed much more towards the LV wall. The two models show
different direction of swirl rotation, further substantiating the importance of inlet angulation. Further-
more, the angle between the mitral and aortic valve is also important for the flow pattern. In the model
the two are implemented as being parallel to each other, which is not anatomically correct. Implementa-
tion of a more true to life inlet and outlet, will affect the hemodynamics, but it is uncertain exactly how it
will affect the swirl rotation direction. In the current workflow only the short axis view MRI images are
used for creating the 3D LV model. From these it is very difficult to determine exactly where and how the
valves are placed. This is a direct result of the great slice thickness. Implementation of a method able to
utilize imaging from different views, as discussed in chapter 8.1, would allow for more accurate modelling
of the valves, as their angulation is better visualized through imaging with a long axis view.
The creation of a vortex ring from the inlet, as the E-peak pushes blood through the mitral valve,
shown in figures 6.6, 6.8, and 6.9, is similar to what is described in the literature [12, 62, 95]. Common
for all three models were the creation of a vortex ring structure around the inlet, where it meets the LV.
This ring then moved through the LV, lost its ring structure, and finally dissipated before reaching the
apex. It was shown how the difference in vortex structure behaviour for the three models were directly
related to the flow pattern and the pressure field. Vortex formation is highly dependent on geometry and

89
velocity, why any inaccuracies in the displacement method would have direct consequences. Taken into
account the work done to ensure the accuracy of the LV motion, the displacement method is considered
sufficiently accurate to not be a source of great error.
From the pressure fields and calculated IVPG it was shown how the front of the inlet jet coincides
with a change in pressure, from relatively low in the basal region, to high in the apical region. However,
the pressure difference through the LV was found to be very small, compared to the overall changes in
pressure through the cardiac cycle. Averaging the pressure of a slice across the LV was a simple way of
approximating the pressure at a certain LV depth. Based on the differences in pressure seen around the
inlet jet in figures 6.6, 6.8, and 6.9, the average pressure is a very rough way of describing the pressure.
For the apical slice it is a fine approximation as the pressure does not seem to differ much across the slice,
but for the mid LV planes with the inlet jet going through the center, the pressure will differ greatly.
Part of the model setup was to apply a constant pressure of 10700 Pa at the outlet. The normal aortic
pressure reaches around 16000 Pa during the systole [77]. This is again a necessary simplification to
make the simulation run, and the implications hereof was not determined. It might be possible to create
a time dependent pressure function, and apply to the outlet for more precise pressure investigations.
The results from the ”transport of concentrated species” module, show how the model can be used to
examine the ventricle wash out, as shown in figure 6.12. Results indicate an almost complete wash out
after 3 cardiac cycles, with only around 10% of old blood left in the LV for all three models. Neidlin et
al. [71] and Goubergrits et al. [72] showed around 67% and 50% wash out after 3 cycles, respectively,
also using CFD models. This suggest that the implemented models are much more efficient at replacing
the blood in the LV. Benito et al. [96] used US to quantify LV wash out in a population of 149 healthy
volunteers from the age of 26 days to 80 years. They showed that the average time for blood to reside
within the LV was <3 cycles, which is congruent with the results shown in this study. Furthermore, they
showed that the maximum time before all blood within the LV was replaced was 7 cycles, and the regions
with high residence time were located in the apical region.
Wash out of the apical region, as shown in figure 6.14 and table 6.3, showed similar results between
model 2 and 3, but much less wash out for model 1. The amount of old blood left in the apical region of
model 1 was about five to six times higher than that of model 2 and 3. Looking at the flow patterns the
new blood follows as it is pushed into the LV (figure 6.12), it can be seen how the geometry of the LV is
responsible for how far down towards the apex the blood will go before swirling around, and moving up-
wards again. Model 1 has a much sharper narrowing at the apical region, where the resulting corner-like
part of the geometry starts the swirling motion before the new blood reaches the apical region. This can
best be seen in figure 6.12, where it seems that the new blood passes just over the very narrow part of
the apical region. These result further substantiate just how important imaging and segmentation is for
accurate simulation output.
Normal practice for investigating ventricle wash out is either through 2D US, 4D PC MRI, or MRI
imaging with injection of contrast agent [71, 96, 97]. These could all be used as a method of verification,
to investigate the accuracy of the models. Being able to derive the same results using only non-invasive
MRI to create a CFD model, would be preferable for patients.
The results of the RRT study were somewhat in disagreement with the results of the apical wash out.
Higher RRT values were found at the apical region in model 3 than in model 1 and 2, even though the

results in table 6.3 shows that model 3 had the most efficient apical wash out. It should be kept in mind
that even though these two are both concerned with the apical blood flow, they are not directly dependent.
The wash out was determined based on the concentration of multiple fluid species in the apical control
volume, while the RRT was determined based on shear stress values at the wall. Taking into account that
RRT is calculated based on the shear stress, the actual values are dependent on the velocity component.
Johnston et al. [98] did CFD modeling of the aorta, and used a normalized RRT for comparison between
models. This argues that the RRT values are not directly comparable between models, as the magnitude
of the velocities varies greatly, but should instead be used as a tool for qualitative interpretation of where
in the individual models there is a higher risk of blood stagnation.
8.4
Verification
For the model to be useful in a clinical setting, the output must be trustworthy. The results are computed
purely mathematical, by solving the PDEs describing the physics, based on the inputs to the CFD model.
This argues that as long as the inputs are true to nature, so will the derived results be, within the tolerated
margin of error. This implies that the model is setup to simulate the correct physics, such as the state of
flow, and that the mesh is fine enough to capture the smallest details.
When it comes to LV hemodynamics, no golden standard exists, but in order to verify the results of
this project, several different techniques could be used. General clinical practice for measuring the mitral
flow velocities is by US. In this project it was done by 2D PC MRI to contain data acquisition to a single
imaging modality, however clinically it is deemed too imprecise for most applications [99]. US can fur-
thermore be used to verify some of the model parameters and derived results, such as model dimensions,
velocity components, or flow patterns. The greatest challenge with US is the sparse dimensionality, as
no techniques exists yet to measure flow in 4D (3D + time). MRI can itself be used to measure flow in
4D using a 3D PC cine sequence, however this option was not available at the DTU scanner at the time
of this project. Measuring the flow directly with MRI would be a great way to qualitatively compare flow
patterns. Furthermore, as all 3 velocity components are measured with 4D PC MRI, it is possible to de-
termine features such as TKE and RRT for quantitative comparison. One challenge with 4D MRI is that it
uses averaging over multiple cardiac cycles, which excludes patients with arrhythmia. Furthermore, the
sequence is reasonably time demanding, which is unfavourable for the patient. If comparable results is
feasible through CFD modelling, using an automated pipeline, it would be beneficial.
The time-frame of this project did not allow for any in-depth verification of the results through an-
other imaging modality. It does however add to the library of methods at the CFU, allowing other studies
to use CFD as a form of verification.
8.5
Limitations and future aspects
This project is focused on the LV, as this is where the first signs of the majority of CVDs are seen, and thus
the majority of the literature to rely on and compare with, is about this part of the heart. It is obvious, that
a model of the entire heart would be more interesting to look into, but modelling the entire heart, with
all the structures and mechanical functions, without any simplifications, was unfortunately not feasible
==PAGE:105==
8.5 Limitations and future aspects
91
within the scope of this project. As a trade-off the LV was deemed the most interesting part to investigate,
as results could still be clinically relevant.
A patient specific holistic model of the entire heart, including FSI modelling and electrochemical mod-
elling, is one of the end goals of cardiac CFD. The work presented here is a minor step in that direction,
which shows how it is possible to utilize MRI to acquire the input data for a model. This could be a poten-
tial stepping stone for a workflow with complete segmentation of all cardiac structures, as well as velocity
determination across all valves of the heart. In turn this could be used to create a much more complex
model, giving more insight into the hemodynamics of the heart. One of the greatest challenges with this
method is still modelling the valve leaflets, as their geometry and mechanical function is difficult to image
and accurately reconstruct in simulation.
The continuous challenge with acquisition of image data of high enough quality raises the question
whether or not it is necessary to have patient specific details on the tiniest structures of the heart for a
CFD model to accurately depict the flow. A different approach could be to create a model based on the
knowledge already available about the physiology of the heart, and then adapt this model to patient spe-
cific data. This does however not take into account any patient specific abnormalities in trabecular tissue
or the like. Verification of the workflow must be in place whether or not the model is based purely on pa-
tient specific data, or just adapted to it. If an adaptive model can achieve the same level of accuracy with
the currently available imaging techniques, as a purely specific model needs currently unattainable image
quality to achieve, it is arguably a shorter path towards clinical implementation to go with the adaptive
one.
On the other hand, the continuous development within MRI can possibly open up for even better
imaging options, either by improving imaging sequences, reconstruction methods, post-processing possi-
bilities, or by introducing new scanners with a higher field strength to the clinic. Considering the amount
of work needed on the CFD part to create a holistic heart model, it is inevitable that within the same time
frame, improvements will happen to the imaging technology too.
In a clinical setting, a CFD model can be relevant for various different aspects, but to be useful for de-
cision making, the output needs to be somewhat quantitative. As an example, consider implantation of a
prosthetic mitral valve. It has been shown how the angulation matters for vortex formation and wash out,
so by pre-operational CFD analysis it can be determined what the optimal angulation should be for the
specific patient. A quantitative measure such as the depth of the vortex in the LV, or the wash out of the
apical region, could aid in making this decision. Simple qualitative assessments, such as the rotational
direction of the LV swirl, could possible also be used, even though currently, not much information is
gained from it.
Many clinical tools and methods are based on deterministic studies, answering the question; what is
the normal state for a healthy and a sick person? A similar approach can be taken when utilizing CFD
analysis. To be clinically applicable studies would need to be carried out to determine quantitative stan-
dards for sick and healthy groups of patients. This could help determine which hemodynamics features
of the heart correlates with disease.
Finally, for CFD modelling of the heart to become clinically relevant, the work flow must be automated
to such a degree that it can be used by physicians, who are not specialists in CFD. It must be simple to
use and give out clear parameters, for the physicians to interpret. Part of the reason for automation is
==PAGE:106==
92
8 Discussion
also to keep cost down, as the cost-benefit of implementation often determines whether or not clinical
implementation is a reality.

CHAPTER9
Conclusion
It has been successfully demonstrated how MRI data of the LV can be acquired and used as basis for cre-
ating a CFD model of the LV, using the MR scanner facility at DTU.
A pipeline was constructed in which the MRI data were used for constructing both the 3D model
mesh, prescribing the mesh movement, and reconstructing an inlet velocity profile. The pipeline was
successfully applied to three different data sets from two volunteers, creating three different LV models.
Simulation results showed great differences between the two models from the same volunteer. These are
mainly attributed to the differences found in the modelling, as a direct consequence of the imaging and
segmentation process. This argues that more work should be done to optimize this part of the pipeline to
make it more accurate, consistent and trustworthy.
Attempts to use the presented pipeline to create CFD models based on clinical data was not successful.
Segmentation of the data resulted in 3D models not useful for simulation, due to patient motion during
scanning. This emphasizes the importance of the imaging method, and shows that further work needs to
be done before considering clinical implementation.
It was demonstrated how several important features of the LV hemodynamics, such as flow patterns,
vortex formation, and LV wash out, can be determined through flow simulation. The use case for CFD
in a clinical setting is still at a novel stage, and how these features should be clinically appraised remains
a question. The work done in this project demonstrates some of the potential uses of CFD in the field of
cardiovascular diseases.
The end of the road to a holistic CFD model of the entire heart and circulatory system is still a long
way ahead.
