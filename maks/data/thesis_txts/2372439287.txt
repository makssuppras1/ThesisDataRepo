Abstract
Electrodynamic loudspeakers can be characterized by the use of a linear lumped parameter model. The
physical parameters are assumed to be constant, which is sufﬁcient for small excitation levels. At large
excitation levels, however, this linear approximation becomes insufﬁcient. In that case, the model can be
extended including parameters varying with, for example, frequency and the displacement of the diaphragm.
This thesis addresses two of the three main nonlinear parameters of a loudspeaker, namely the force factor
and the suspension compliance. The force factor, Bl, characterizes the magneto-electrical performance of a
loudspeaker which, at large excitation levels, is dependent on the voice coil displacement. The compliance,
Cms, determines the force exerted by the mechanical suspension of the driver, which can be dependent on
frequency and the voice coil displacement. The loudspeaker nonlinearities are important for understanding
and describing the behavior of a loudspeaker more comprehensively.
The focus is to develop a system measuring the nonlinear force factor and compliance of the loudspeaker.
The system is based on the test driver being driven acoustically, without current ﬂow through the speaker,
allowing the measurement of the suspension parts alone, preventing the inﬂuence from electro-magnetics
part.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Study Motivation
The nonlinear suspension compliance and the nonlinear force factor of a loudspeaker are two out of the
three the main sources to intermodulation and harmonic distortion at high excursion. The Klippel Analyzer
measures the nonlinear force factor and compliance, however, these results are based on a model. PointSouce
Acoustics seeks a more direct measurement method for ﬁnding these nonlinear parameters of the loudspeaker,
based on excitation of the test driver using an external source.
1.2
Scope of the study
This Master thesis is based on the development of a measurement system where the test object is driven
acoustically via another loudspeaker. The test driver together with the ‘motor’ driver are placed inside a box
setup.
The box setup is to be designed and it should be able to measure nonlinear force factor,Bl(x), and
nonlinear compliance, Cms(x), of loudspeaker drivers with different sizes and parameters. A requirement
from PointSource is that the measurements should be easy and quick to use and the dimensions of the box
shall allow the use of the bafﬂes used in PointSource Acoustics anechoic chamber. Furthermore, PointSource
requires that the system should be able to measure loudspeakers with sizes up to 15 inch in diameter.
The design of the box includes investigation using COMSOL Multiphysics for ﬁnding the optimal box
dimensions, assuring that the box can be used for the nonlinear compliance measurements of loudspeaker
drivers with different sizes up to 15 inch. The complete system should be able to measure the required
parameters for calculation of the nonlinear compliance and the nonlinear force factor of the different drivers
under test. The measurement data will be processed using MATLAB in order to acquire the Cms(x)- and
Bl(x)-curves.

denoted as the small signal model (Fig.2.1). The physical parameters are assumed to be constant, which is

2.1.2
Loudspeaker Nonlinearities
Driven at high amplitudes (high voltage), the loudspeaker behaves nonlinearly, generating components,
which did not exist in the input signal.
Two of the main nonlinear parameters of a loudspeaker are the force factor and the suspension compliance.
The force factor, Bl, characterizes the magneto-electrical performance of a loudspeaker which, at large
excitation levels, is dependent on the voice coil displacement.
The compliance, Cms, is the force exerted by the mechanical suspension of the driver, which can be
dependent on frequency and the voice coil displacement.
Both the compliance and the force factor
Figure 2.2 shows the electrical equivalent circuit of the motor and suspension system of a loudspeaker.
The nonlinear force factor, Bl(x) and the nonlinear compliance, Cms(x) = 1/Kms(x) are illustrated in red.
Figure 2.2: Equivalent electrical circuit of coupled motor and suspension parts of a loudspeaker. 1
Nonlinear Force Factor, Bl(x)
The force factor, Bl(x), represents the coupling between the electrical and mechanical sides of the lumped
parameter model of the electro-dynamic loudspeaker. As mentioned earlier, Bl depends on the voice-coil
displacement. Bl decreases if the coil windings leave the coil gap.
The force factor is a coupling factor between the electrical and mechanical parts, which causes any
variation of Bl(x) to affect the electro-dynamic driving force F = Bl(x)i. The displacement dependent back

Nonlinear Compliance, Cms(x)
The suspension of a loudspeaker consists usually of a surround and a spider, which contribute to the
total compliance and determine the rest position of the coil. A symmetrical compliance characteristic versus
displacement is an important goal in driver design to ensure stability and robustness of the driver, lower
distortion and to avoid dynamical generation of DC-displacement that moves the coil from the optimal
position. This DC offset applies only for asymmetrical Cms(x) and only at low frequencies. Asymmetrical
surround produces excessive stress in the material that may damage the speaker eventually.
The suspension is centering the coil in the gap by generating restoring force returning the coil back to
its rest position. The restoring force is the product of the nonlinear stiffness as a function of displacement,
Kms(x), and the displacement,x: F = Kms(x) · x. The stiffness is inversely proportional to the compliance:
Cms = 1/Kms. The suspension acts almost linearly at low displacement and can be modeled by a linear
spring, but at high displacement it responds with more force than predicted by the linear spring. An example
of the suspension system, as well as the nonlinear force deﬂection curve can be seen on Figure 2.4. The
nonlinear force deﬂection curve shows a force-displacement for a linear and for a nonlinear compliance. The
linear graph is not realistic. It is only used to show that a realistic nonlinear suspension has a range where it
is almost linear. When the displacement increases, the deviation from the linear curve increases and that is
where the compliance becomes nonlinear.
The suspension compliance depends also on the temperature of the voice coil. The heat of the voice coil
is spreading via the spider to the suspension where it changes the physical properties of the material.

3.1
The Idea Behind The Measurement Setup
The system is based on the DUT being driven acoustically. This means that there is no current ﬂow
through the test driver, allowing the measurement of the suspension parts alone. This allows a more direct
method for ﬁnding the nonlinear force factor and compliance.
From theory (2.1.2), the necessary parameters for ﬁnding the force factor are the induced voltage and the
diaphragm velocity of the test driver. When the device under test is driven acoustically, the induced voltage
can be measured through its terminals. The diaphragm displacement can be measured using a displacement
laser pointed at it. The velocity is then the ﬁrst derivative of the displacement.
Theory 2.1.2 suggests that the suspension compliance can be found using Equation 2.9. This indicates that
the following parameters are required: The diaphragm displacement, x, sound pressure difference between
front and rear of the diaphragm, pD, diaphragm area, SD, mechanical mass, Mms and the mechanical
resistance of the total driver losses, Rms. Once again, the displacement of the diaphragm can be measured
using a displacement laser. When the displacement is measured, the velocity, u, and the acceleration, a, can
then be calculated, considering they are the ﬁrst and second derivatives of the displacement, respectively.
Furthermore, the sound pressure at the rear of the diaphragm of the DUT can be measured inside the box of
the acoustic excitation system. This can be used to ﬁnd the pressure difference between the front and the
rear, pD.
Therefore, the system includes a closed box with two perpendicular openings- one for the DUT and one
for another driver used as a ’motor’ to drive the DUT acoustically. When the ’motor’ speaker is driven by
high amplitude (voltage) signal, high sound pressure builds up inside the box, inducing voltage in the test
driver’s voice coil, causing its suspension parts to move.
The driver used as a ’motor’ is a 15" woofer by Tymphany-Peerless. The box used for the measurements
was with the following dimensions: 45,3 x 47,7 x 10,1 cm.
PointSource requires that the measurements should be easy and quick to use, and allow the use of the
bafﬂes used in the anechoic chamber at PointSources. However, it was established that this bafﬂe was too
thin and unstable when the ’motor’ driver was operated with very low frequencies, which resulted in a lot of
vibration and leakage, making it impossible to acquire adequate measurements. Therefore, another, 3 mm
thick, bafﬂe was used. The box setup used for the tests is illustrated by Figure 3.1.

A closed box scenario was considered in order to avoid the inﬂuence of the internal impedance of the

From the previous section and Figure 4.2(b), it could be seen that the sound pressure level from the
COMSOL simulations vs. the sound pressure level from the measurements using noise signal were not
in good agreement. A pseudo random noise signal is chaotic and has no steady state. The measurement
results using this pseudo random noise signal showed highly varying SPL at different positions inside the
box. Furthermore, the measurements did not show any indication of test driver present. As shown by the
simulation, the measurements should also have had a dip in sound pressure around the resonance frequency
of the test driver. Therefore, here the measurements were performed using a logarithmic chirp signal. A
chirp signal is a sinusoidal signal whose frequency varies with time. The frequency of the logarithmic chirp
signal varies exponentially as a function of time. The chirp signal is phase-continuous, which allows for
smooth broadband excitation, which can can minimize disturbances and abrupt signal changes to the DUT.
A measurement using a chirp signal (continuous swept-sine measurement) can produce time waveforms
of fundamental, harmonic, or residue components. It allows the simultaneous measurement of the linear
response and harmonic distortion. The log chirp signal was created using a MATLAB code, created by Puriﬁ,
which is conﬁdential.
The sound pressure inside the closed box was simulated at 5 distinct positions to gain broader knowledge
of the sound pressure around the box. The positions chosen are: in the middle of the box, at the left
lower corner, in the middle next to the bafﬂe, next to the bafﬂe but to the left, and next to the bafﬂe to the
right. The sound pressure from the simulations next to the bafﬂe to the left and to the right were identical.

4.4
Discussion
COMSOL Multiphysics is used for creating a 3-dimensional model of this acoustic excitation measure-
ment system and simulating the behavior of the sound pressure inside its box.
COMSOL does not allow the assignment of more than one boundary conditions, therefore, the ’motor’
driver is assigned as a source and its impedance is not taken into account in the simulation. The test driver
was simulated as a passive radiator with some internal mechanical-acoustical impedance.
The ’motor’ driver can either be a pressure source or a volume velocity source. Simulations vs. real
life measurements were performed in order to determine the type of the source. Furthermore, the type of
the signal used had to be determined. A logarithmic chirp signal was found to be the ﬁnal choice. The
simulations using volume velocity source were found to be closer to the results from the real measurements.
The sound pressure inside the box was simulated at few distinct positions to gain broader knowledge of

CHAPTER 5
Nonlinear Force Factor
The ﬁndings of the force factor, Bl(x), are based on Theory Section 2.1.2. Equation 2.7 provides
information regarding the necessary measurements for ﬁnding the force factor, Bl(x): the diaphragm velocity
and the voltage induced on the test driver. Therefore, the device under test (DUT) is driven acoustically via
the ’motor’ driver, inducing voltage (or electromotive force), which is, at the same time, measured through
the DUT’s terminals. Simultaneously, the displacement of the diaphragm of the test driver is measured using
a displacement laser. The velocity of the diaphragm can be found from its displacement.
A single frequency signal is enough for calculating the force factor because Bl is independent of
frequency. The results below are based on measurements where the ’motor’ speaker was driven with a 43 Hz
sinusoidal signal. The frequency was chosen based on the resonance frequency of the DUT. At resonance,
the diaphragm of the DUT moves with highest excursion, giving most information about the force factor as a
function of displacement, namely, Bl(x).

amplitude (velocity) until reaching certain velocity. When the desired velocity is reached, the sinusoidal
signal keeps playing for some time, followed by a decrease until zero is reached. The signal ends with some
zero samples. The use of this gradually decreasing amplitude of the signal allows the gradual decrease of
diaphragm displacement of the DUT. This places the diaphragm at its true zero position.

The following Figure 5.9 illustrates the diaphragm displacement and the force factor over time. This
is the same force factor as on Figure 5.8a. It is shown that the force factor becomes ’inﬁnite’ at minimum
and maximum displacement. This is due to the diaphragm movement. The force factor depends on the
induced voltage and the velocity of the diaphragm. When the diaphragm reaches minimum or maximum
displacement, it stops for a moment and its excursion changes direction. At this exact moment, the velocity
becomes very small, almost zero, which the induced voltage is being divided by when calculating the Bl(x).
When a number is being divided by a positive or negative value which is very close to zero, the resulting
value goes to ± inﬁnity, respectively. This is what is occurring in the calculated Bl(x)-curve.

5.2.2
Discussion of Possible Issues when Averaging the Bl(x) curves
One period of displacement and volume velocity data gives two Bl(x) curves. One is from -x to +x, the
other one is from +x to -x. The way the data is saved, if the two curves are averaged, they would give the
average between the correct curve and its mirror. One should be aware of this and make sure that the Bl(x)
curves are correctly averaged.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The focus of this thesis was to develop a system which uses direct measurement method for ﬁnding the
nonlinear force factor and the nonlinear suspension compliance parameters of a loudspeaker. The system
was based on the test driver being driven acoustically, allowing the measurement of the suspension parts
alone.
The necessary parameters for ﬁnding the force factor of the test driver are, the induced voltage, which is
being measured through the terminals, and the diaphragm velocity, which is derived from the diaphragm
displacement measured using a displacement laser. The suspension compliance can be found by the measured
diaphragm displacement using a laser and the sound pressure at the back of the diaphragm using a microphone
inside the box. From that, the system was found to consist of a closed box with two perpendicular openings-
one for the DUT and one for another driver used as a ’motor’.
COMSOL Multiphysics was used for creating a 3-dimensional model of this acoustic excitation mea-
surement system and simulating the behavior of the sound pressure inside its box. Simulations vs. real
life measurements were performed in order to determine the type of the source used in the simulations.
The sound pressure inside the box was simulated and measured at few distinct positions to gain broader
knowledge of the sound pressure around the box volume. Furthermore, the type of the signal used had to
be determined. A logarithmic chirp signal was found to be the ﬁnal choice. The simulations using volume
velocity source were found to be closer to the results from the real measurements.
Simulations of boxes with different shapes and sizes were also investigated in COMSOL Multiphysics.
It was established that, at low frequencies, the box dimensions are nearly insigniﬁcant in the presence of a
test driver.
The Bl(x)-curve was successfully obtained using measurements from this acoustic excitation system.
Acquiring correct Bl(x) curve, however, requires high precision. Any issue, such as DC offset or wrong
phase due to laser delay, can compromise the Bl(x) results. Furthermore, one needs to be aware of the way
the data is being saved. Wrong interpretation of the data could easily give inaccurate results. Example
for that is when averaging several Bl(x)-curves to get more optimal result. It was established that, from
the measurement data, the force factor becomes ’inﬁnite’ at minimum and maximum displacement, which
cannot be avoided. This is because the force factor is the induced voltage divided by the velocity, and when
the velocity is very close to zero, the resulting Bl goes to ± inﬁnity. The obtained force factor curve from
this acoustic excitation measurement system was compared to the Bl(x)-curves from two other measurement
systems: the Klippel Analyzer and PointSource’s constant velocity measurement system. The force factor
from this system was found to be in better agreement with PointSource’s measurement system. The results
from the Klippel Analyzer had some signiﬁcant deviations. It has to be kept in mind that the shape of the
Bl(x)-curve depends on the geometry of the coil-gap conﬁguration and the B-ﬁeld generated by the magnet.

7.1
Future Work
The work presented in this report gives insight on the topics of nonlinear force factor and nonlinear
suspension compliance of a loudspeaker, as well as information about an acoustic excitation measurement
system to acquire their curves.
However, there is much more to investigate, especially when it comes to nonlinear suspension compliance.
This section provides with ideas for future work on the developed measurement setup.
7.1.1
Box Optimization
With the current box, it is difﬁcult to switch between test drivers. Now, there are bafﬂes for 4 inch and
6.5 inch drivers. If a driver with another size needs to be tested, a new bafﬂe has to be created. Furthermore,
the current bafﬂes do not ﬁt with PointSource’s anechoic chamber. As a future improvement, a new box has
to be built. New box and dimensions are suggested in Section 4.3. Furthermore, a stiffer material should be
used for the box and the bafﬂes. For example, high density polyethylene (PEHD). Polyethylene is a material
with high toughness, shear strength and insulation ability.
7.1.2
Laser Delay
It was found out that there is an issue with the phase difference between the velocity and the induced
voltage signals. The two signals should be in phase. The core of the issue was not found,therefore, it was
manually accounted for the ’laser delay’ by adjusting it to get the best results. Therefore, the laser delay
should be further investigated in the future.
7.1.3
Filtering
A moving average ﬁlter was used to ﬁlter out the noise from the velocity signal. However, there may be
another ﬁlter, which can ﬁlter the signal better, without eliminating valuable information. A Butterworth
ﬁlter, for example, with the ’ﬁltﬁlt’ MATLAB function can perhaps be considered. The ’ﬁltﬁlt’ function
performs zero-phase digital ﬁltering by processing the input data in both the forward and reverse directions,
eliminating unwanted shift in the signal due to ﬁltering.
7.1.4
User Friendly System
In order to get enough diaphragm excursion, one needs to know the resonance frequency of the device
under test, also the amplitude/voltage of the driving signal. Right now, the user has to check the driver
datasheet in order to ﬁnd the resonance frequency. Furthermore, the user has to do test trials to ﬁnd out
==PAGE:51==
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
40
what amplitude does the driving signal need to have, so that it can supply enough power to the test driver to
achieve maximum excursion.
As a future improvement, some signal processing can be performed on the go, while measuring. First,
multiple frequency signal, with the help of some signal processing, can be used to determine the loud-
speaker’s resonance frequency. A sinusoidal signal can then be created with the found resonance frequency.
The amplitude of that signal can be chosen so that the diaphragm displacement (data from the laser) is
between, f.ex. ±8 mm. This can be done by playing short periods of the sinusoidal signal, slowly increasing
the signal’s amplitude, and checking the displacement range until it reaches the desired value. Once the
driving signal’s frequency and amplitude are found, the actual measurement can begin, then the data can be
processed, and the Bl(x) curve can be obtained.
7.1.5
Finding the Suspension Compliance
In the future, the pressure inside the box has to be further investigated. A model has to be implemented,
which takes into account any frequency dependent losses in the mechanical part, such as the creep factor and
frequency dependent damping, and can estimate the values of Mms and Rms. This model can then be used
to obtain Cms(x).
