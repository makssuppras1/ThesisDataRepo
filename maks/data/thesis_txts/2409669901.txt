Abstract
Power pylons have traditionally been constructed with steel where the conductor lines
hang from the pylon in an insulator string. Bystrup Architects has proposed a new de-
sign where the pylons will be made of ﬁberglass which is a non-conducting material. This
enables a rigid attachment of the transmission lines to the cross arms of each pylon result-
ing in an increased dynamic interaction. The rigid attachment transfers any wind induced
vibration and motion directly to the slender cross arm.
This thesis is dealing with the aerodynamic instability caused by the severe phe-
nomenon known as galloping. The dynamic instability caused by galloping is simulated
in the commercial software called ABAQUS. This has been achieved by deﬁning an aero-
dynamic load element by the user-deﬁned subroutine called "UEL".
The aerodynamic load element has been used for a parameter study to investigate the
inﬂuence of the galloping vibration, due to the direct attachment on the pylon. The study
showed that the ﬂexibility of the cross arm in the horizontal direction has a huge impact
on the vibration amplitudes caused by galloping. An increased ﬂexibility will decrease
the vibration amplitudes.
A damping was introduced in the pylon in order to increase the energy dissipation.
The result showed that the added damping did not have any inﬂuence on the galloping
vibration and the amplitude stayed the same.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Steel truss towers have traditionally been constructed to carry transmission lines where the
transmission lines are mounted with insulation strings because of steel’s electric conduc-
tivity. These insulation strings provide very ﬂexible support conditions for the conductor
lines and are very susceptible to dynamic loads such as wind load. Bystrup Architects
has proposed a new design of a power pylon, which is shown in Figure 1.1. It is a com-
posite power pylon fabricated in ﬁberglass. Fiberglass is well suited for high voltage
applications, such as power pylons, due to the non-conductivity of the raw materials.
This enables a rigid attachment of the transmission lines to the cross arms of each py-
lon resulting in an increased dynamic interaction. This dynamic interaction results in an
increased dissipation of energy compared to insulator strings [1].
Figure 1.1: Proposal by Bystrup Architects [2].
The rigid attachment transfers any wind induced vibration and motion, such as the se-
vere phenomenon known as "Galloping", directly to the slender structure and may lead to
catastrophic failure of the entire structure. Galloping is a low-frequency, high amplitude
wind induced vibration at frequencies between 0.5 and 2 Hz depending on the cable’s
vibration mode, the cable’s tension force and the span length. The dynamic instability

caused by galloping is a very complex phenomenon due to the non-linear behavior, cou-

hard to predict. All these parameters play an important role in the galloping motion.
The goal of this thesis is to introduce passive damping treatments such as constrained

beam corresponding to the cross arm of a pylon. Then the load element is applied on
the cable in the mid span. The investigation of added damping in the pylon has been
performed on a cable that is supported by a full pylon tower. The damping is added in the
cross arms and four different damping ratios has been applied to investigate the effect.

Vibrations in Transmission Lines
Large vibration in transmission lines is often due to a phenomenon called galloping. Gal-
loping is a low-frequency, high-amplitude wind-induced vibration that can cause serious
problems to the conductor line’s pylon. Galloping can occur on conductor lines located
in cold climates where the wind and rain form ice on the line and thereby create a non-
symmetrical cross section. The accreted ice adds extra mass to the system and thereby
lowers the eigenfrequency, so a strong moderate cross-wind can cause aerodynamic in-
stability. The instability results in an elliptical in-plane motion known as the galloping
ellipse with large vertical amplitudes and small horizontal amplitudes and the motion can
last up to 24 hours with amplitudes of 0.03 times the span length [3]. These vibrations can
be very critical for the construction as it can lead to fatigue failure of connections and, in
worst case, structural failure such as total collapse of the pylons as shown in Figure 2.1.
The consequences are quite severe and are quite expensive due to the cost of replacing
the pylons and in revenue loss. Thus, it is of great interest to optimize the pylon in order
to avoid the damages caused by the phenomenon.

how the stability of the conductor line can be calculated based on the results from aero-

Mechanism of Galloping
Den Hartog sets up a dynamic instability in terms of drag and lift forces, but when there
is an ice accretion on the conductor line, the displacement of the center of mass causes
the conductor to roll. This action will oscillate at the torsional natural frequency. The
torsional frequency is reduced when the ice adds up the mass to the system and when that
frequency approaches one of the lower vertical frequencies, galloping can ensue [6]. This
applies mostly to bundle conductors, whereas for single conductors, the ratio is between
0.1-0.3 [7]. The mode of the motion varies and can be obtained as single and double loops
as illustrated in Figure 2.4. Sometimes even a triple loop is observed. Moreover, galloping
of more than three loops has generally small amplitudes [8] and is not of interest.

and is not demonstrated as well as Den Hartog galloping. Galloping initiated by torsional
mechanism is proposed by some authors but the theory has not had its big breakthrough
yet in the literature. However, it seems like there is an agreement that torsional motion is
an important aspect of galloping.

Cable Element with Torsional Degree of Freedom
The major characteristic of cables is their slenderness that makes them very ﬂexible in
terms of bending and vulnerable to pressure. It is therefore of great importance to take
into account the geometrical non-linearity of the cable when making the analysis. The

non-linearity of the cable can cause convergence difﬁculties, and for that reason, a method
to simulate the static equilibrium of the cable has been developed.
A cable element in space usually consists of three translation displacement degrees
of freedom at each node but due to the eccentric weight of the accreted ice and the aero-
dynamic moment, an additional torsional degree of freedom is needed. Such kind of
element is not available in ABAQUS, but in the [12] such an element was implemented

decreases the likelihood of non-convergence.
In this thesis, the hybrid spatial beam element has been used which has a bending stiff-
ness and is allowed to be exposed to pressure. A small study in Appendix C showed that
the release of bending degrees only had a very small impact on the deﬂection and setting
the material property to be in-compressible increases the likelihood of non-convergence.

It is a relatively large deviation, but many factors may be responsible for the difference
in the result, due to lack of information in the article. Parameters like the damping ratio
and geometrical parameters used to determine the force, are parameters that are not listed.
A damping of 10%, which was recommended from [19], was tested in this simulation but
it results in almost non-existent vibrations. The main reason for the difference may lie
in the spectral analysis. The simulated conductor line vibrates with one loop per span
in the vertical direction with a frequency of 0.5 Hz which is close to the ﬁrst natural
frequency. The vibration from the article has a frequency of 2 Hz which is close to their
ﬁrst natural frequency. This mismatch makes it really complicated to reach the same
vibration amplitudes and that might be the reason for the large deviations.
4.3
Concluding Remarks
It was not possible to get the exact same result as the article, as it could be seen in Figure
4.3. There may be many parameters that cause this deviation due to lack of information
in the article. These simulations are time consuming and it was not possible to try out
all different constellations. The method still seems useful since the result showed large
vertical and small horizontal amplitudes where the trace of the motion has the shape
of the classical galloping ellipse. This indicates that the method is still applicable for
investigation of which parameters inﬂuence the galloping motion.

CHAPTER 5
Three Span Conductor Line
The numerical example from Chapter 4 was a single ﬁxed supported conductor line with
one span. In practice, the cables run through several spans so in this section the simulation
will be performed on a more practical case. The simulations will be performed with
conductor lines that run through three spans where the end of the outer spans are ﬁxed
supported, and where the middle span will be supported by a ﬁxed supported cantilevered
arm that corresponds to the pylon’s cross arm. This will give a more ﬂexible system where
the contribution to the mid span’s supports consist of the pylon’s and cable’s stiffness.
Four different scenarios are then investigated in order to get an idea of which parameters
inﬂuence the motion of galloping when the cables are mounted directly on the cross arm.

5.1
Finite-Element Models of the Transmission Lines
The ﬁnite-element model is set up and the numerical simulation for galloping on the
iced conductor line is performed with the method proposed in Chapter 3. The static
equilibrium of the cables is made individually for each cable with the method proposed
in Section 3.3. This is achieved by making a static step where the end of each span are
ﬁxed supported. Then, when the static equilibrium of the cables are obtained, the ﬁxed
supports are removed at the pylons’ tip, so that the deﬂection of the pylons’ cross arms
tip can be obtained due to the weight from the cables and the pylons. There have been
employed 200 elements to discretize the cables at each span with the element mentioned
in Section 3.2. The pylons’ cross arms have been discretized with 20 regular spatial beam
elements. It is assumed that the shape of the accretion ice is D-shaped along the conductor
line with same cross section parameters as in Section 4.1, which leads to the aerodynamic
coefﬁcients shown in Figure 4.2. The physical parameters of the iced conductor and
parameters for the numerical model is the same as the ones from Chapter 4 and are listed
in Table 4.1 and 4.3 respectively. A damping ratio of 2% is applied on the cables and
the damping is depicted by the Rayleigh damping model described in Section 3.7 and is
determined based on the two ﬁrst natural frequencies as speciﬁed in Appendix F.2. The
aerodynamic loads are generated by a wind speed of 6 m/s for all the simulations and is
only applied on the middle span.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The aerodynamic forces of a single D-shaped iced conductor line was implemented in
the numerical simulation by the means of ABAQUS software. The aerodynamic load
was implemented through an user-deﬁned element based on the method presented in this
thesis. The method was tested by comparing the results from an example in an article.
The results from the two methods differed from each, due to lack of information in the
article.
The load element was used to make a parameter study of an iced conductor line sup-
ported by a cantilevered beam corresponding to the cross arm of a pylon. Based of the
parameter study on a single D-shaped iced conductor line, it is concluded that the ﬂex-
ibility of the support in the horizontal direction is a major factor for the galloping am-
plitudes. The galloping amplitudes was increased when the ﬂexibility in the horizontal
direction was decreased. It also resulted in a slight increase of the vibration frequencies
of the cable, and a signiﬁcant increase in the frequency of the vertical vibrations in the
pylons’ cross arms. It was possible to reduce the amplitudes of the galloping vibrations
signiﬁcantly by increasing the ﬂexibility of the cross arms in the horizontal direction. The
ﬂexibility of the support in the vertical direction did not seem to have any inﬂuence on
the galloping motion when the ﬂexibility was decreased.
The introduction of added damping in the pylons did not have the desired effect. By
adding four different damping ratios in steps of 0.5%, the result showed no inﬂuence on
the galloping amplitudes. Only a small decrease was in the beginning of the time history
but as time goes the amplitudes ended at the same level for the four different damping
ratios.

CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
8.1
Further work
The work done in this thesis showed how sensitive the galloping motions are to the sup-
port conditions. This indicates that each cable attached to the cross arm is an unique
situation and each cable needs to be analyzed. The model used for the simulations in
thesis only consists of one cable, where the remaining cables contribute to the inertia of
the system. This simpliﬁcation does not take into account the contribution that the re-
maining cables have on the horizontal ﬂexibility of the pylons’ cross arms. Therefore it
is of interest to investigate whether the stiffness from the remaining cables contribute to
the galloping motion.
An increase in the vibration frequency of the cross arms was observed for the model
with the highest galloping amplitudes. This indicates that the galloping motion might can
be reduced by increasing the ﬂexibility of the cross arms in the vertical direction for some
situations. It would be of interest to see whether an increase of ﬂexibility could affect the
galloping motion.
Since the horizontal ﬂexibility of the pylons’ cross arms had a major effect on the
galloping motion, it would be of interest to investigate whether the torsional rigidity of
the power pylons’ body can inﬂuence the vibrations of the galloping cable. It is of interest
to see whether an increased ﬂexibility can decrease the galloping motions.

APPENDIX B
Abaqus Input File
Every time Abaqus submits a job it generates an input-ﬁle to the Abaqus solver. This
ﬁle contains all the information to set up the model such as geometry, load, boundary
conditions and material parameters. This section will show how to implement an user-
deﬁned element in the input ﬁle.
By the statement "*USER ELEMENT" it allows you to implement the user-deﬁned
element, where some element parameters needs to be set. The ﬁrst parameter is "TYPE",
which deﬁnes the name of the element. For Abaqus/Standard and Abaqus/Explicit the
element should be named Un and VUn respectively, where n is an integer in the range
0 < n < 10000. Numbers of nodes and active degrees of freedom per node is the next
parameters that needs to be set and the number of nodes is deﬁned by "NODES" and
the degree of freedoms is deﬁned by "COORDINATES". The amount of properties used
in the user-deﬁned element is set by "PROPERTIES", which allows you to implement
parameters to the Fortran code. The last parameter is "VARIABLES" that is the amount
of variables that can be stored in "SVARS".
The user element is called U1, and it has six active degree of freedom (ux, uy, uz, φx, φy, φz)
which corresponds to the spatial beam element used for the cable. It is of great impor-
tance that the two elements has the same amount of active degrees of freedom when you
are adding the two elements. The input ﬁle is going to look as follows
*USER ELEMENT, TYPE=U1, NODES=2, COORDINATES=6, PROPERTIES=3,
VARIABLES=3
1,2,3,4,5,6
The numbering deﬁnes the active degrees of freedom that the user element has. The next
step is to deﬁne the element by the nodes by the use of the following statement.
59
==PAGE:82==
60
APPENDIX B. ABAQUS INPUT FILE
*ELEMENT, TYPE=U1, ELSET=USER
where ELSET=USER deﬁne the name of the element set. The user element are added on
an existing element so it is not possible to number the user element with the same num-
ber as the cable elements, which means that the user element needs its own independent
element number. The elements are deﬁned by the following order
ELEMENT NUMBER, FIRST NODE NUMBER, SECOND NODE NUMBER
The ELEMENT NUMBER tells what number element it is, and the two last terms just tells
which nodes the element are connected to. The properties of the user element are deﬁned
by the following statement
*UEL PROPERTY, ELSET=USER
PROP1, PROP2, ..., PROPn
The statement *UEL PROPERTY and ELSET=USER tells what element set you assign
properties to and PROPn is a real number. It is possible to assign as many properties as
you want.
The release of bending degrees also takes place in the input ﬁle. It is done by the
following statement
*RELEASE
ELEMENT NUMBER, ELEMENT END ID, RELEASE COMBINATION CODE
The element end ID is deﬁned by S1 or S2, where number one is the beginning of the
element and number two is the end of the element. The release combination code deﬁnes
which degree of freedom that needs to be released. The bending degrees are deﬁned by
M1 and M2 which refers to the rotation about the n1-axis and n2-axis respectively. The
n1- and n2-axis depends on the beam orientation you choose for your model.

APPENDIX C

The electronic appendix is submitted with all the ﬁles, that makes it possible to resubmit
all the models made in this thesis. Figure I.1 illustrates a road map of to the digital annex
