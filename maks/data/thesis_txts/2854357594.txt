Abstract
Introducing converter-interfaced renewable generation to achieve the global decarboniza-
tion targets makes the grid more susceptible to external disturbances, ultimately leading
to large frequency deviations. Due to this, the Transmission System Operators (TSO)
require more demand-side resources to provide ancillary services while ensuring grid relia-
bility at times of disturbance. Among the available demand-side resources, electric trains
are considered significant controllable loads with predictable consumption patterns, mak-
ing them well-suited to provide these ancillary services. This thesis examines the viability
of delivering frequency reserves in the Static Frequency Containment Reserve for Distur-
bances (FCR-D) market by utilizing the traction power of S-trains in Denmark. Accord-
ingly, it first analyses the historical data of the locomotive’s tractive power and quantifies
the capacity reserves that can be extracted to provide the Static FCR-D services. It then
discusses various ways of formulating an optimization algorithm to maximize the hourly
provision of the capacity reserves of each day of the week based on the operational limits
of the trains.
Keywords: FCR-D market, traction power, electric trains, demand response, green en-
ergy

1.1
Background and Motivation
1.1.1
Transitioning to renewable generation and its challenges
Undoubtedly, the Industrial Revolution shifted the world towards a new paradigm, pri-
marily by creating substantial economic growth. However, on the other hand, it could
also be considered the root cause of all environmental problems that the world experiences
today. Human activities such as deforestation, and burning of fossil fuels have led to the
emission of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. Accu-
mulating enormous amounts of these greenhouse gases has caused temperatures to spike
because of the greenhouse effect, ultimately causing climate change and global warming
[2].
Therefore, as the first universal and legally binding agreement on climate change, ever
since the implementation of the Paris Agreement, many countries have been setting carbon
neutrality targets in order to fight against climate change [2]. Further, leaders within
the European Union (EU) also proposed the 2030 climate and energy framework as an
intermediate step in achieving carbon neutrality by 2050 [3]. The 2030 framework proposes
the actions mentioned below:
1. Reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 40% relative to the 1990 levels [3].
2. Increasing the share of renewables to 27% of energy consumption (with flexibility
left to member states regarding their national targets) [3].
3. Improving the energy efficiency via possible alterations [3].
Accordingly, incorporating renewables into a country’s national grid is considered one
of the most crucial factors in making European energy more sustainable and secure [3].
However, the integration of renewable generation into electric power systems has its own
set of challenges. One such challenge is maintaining the frequency stability of the power
system [4].
The frequency of a power system indicates the balance between power generation and
demand. Therefore, an imbalance between these two is reflected through deviations in
the system frequency [5]. Further, to ensure the operational security of a country’s power
system, its frequency must be maintained within a pre-defined range and should not
deviate far from its nominal value at times of disturbances [6].
Power system inertia is the energy stored in large rotating generators and large industrial
motors, which gives them the tendency to remain rotating for a short period of time,
usually seconds, following a disturbance [7]. Frequency stability is inversely related to this
system inertia. Accordingly, synchronous generators within conventional power systems
help mitigate frequency changes during disturbances by utilizing their sizeable inherent
system inertia, thereby enhancing frequency stability.
However, as mentioned above,
modern power systems are shifting towards generation via converter-connected sources

such as wind and solar. This converter-interfaced renewable generation has no rotational
energy, and hence, replacing conventional generation with renewables adds zero inertia to
the power system, lowering the equivalent system inertia of the overall grid. Diminishing
this equivalent inertia makes the grid more susceptible to external disturbances due to
delays in balancing power supply with demand, leading to large frequency deviations [5].
Due to the abovementioned challenges associated with frequency stabilization, the Trans-
mission System Operators (TSOs) require a comprehensive reserve of ancillary services
to ensure grid reliability during disturbances. Ancillary services are the support services
purchased to restore the balance between electricity supply and demand at all times of
the day [1]. Due to the uncertainty associated with high penetrations of intermittent
renewable generation, the interest has been shifted towards incorporating demand-side
resources to provide ancillary services [8]. Based on factors like cost and response time,
demand-side resources such as electric boilers, batteries, and electric vehicles (EVs) [1]
are becoming more prominent in providing ancillary services when compared to grid-side
ancillary services such as thermal and hydropower plants [9]. Among EVs, electric trains
are identified to have the potential to supply the necessary inertia support and frequency
support [10] due to their reliable consumption profiles while also being a significant con-
sumer of electricity. Further, as the stations on electrified railways are far apart and there
is some flexibility in arrival times, the speed of an electric train can be changed quickly
without affecting the train’s schedule.
Additionally, in modern communication-based
train control systems, bidirectional communication between the train and the ground en-
ables the implementation of frequency control through coordination with electric trains
[4]. The consumption profile of a train consists of a traction system, which is responsible
for acceleration, deceleration, cruising, and coasting, along with a braking system, which
determines whether the braking energy is regenerated for reuse or dissipated as heat [11].
Moreover, there is also an auxiliary unit that is responsible for providing heating, lighting,
ventilation, communication, etc [12].

1.1.2
Overview of the Danish ancillary services
Depending on the purpose, that is, either maintaining or restoring the balance between
electricity supply and demand, Energinet purchases the following products as ancillary
services [1]:
• Fast Frequency Reserve (FFR)
• Frequency Containment Reserve for Normal Operation (FCR-N)
• Frequency Containment Reserve for Disturbances (FCR-D)
• Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR)
• Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR)
The system frequency rises when there is a surplus in supply compared to the demand.
In such a situation, ancillary services are needed to bring the frequency down to its
predefined range. Therefore, Energinet activates the services that would either decrease
production or increase consumption. Similarly, when there is a higher demand than the
supply, ancillary services are needed to bring the dropped frequency up to its standard
range. In this case, the ancillary services are utilized to either increase generation or
decrease consumption [1].
Furthermore, these ancillary services are categorized based on two main parameters [1]:
1. Response time - how fast a production or consumption unit must react when re-
quired.
2. Activation time - how long the production or consumption unit must stay active
during its activation.
Accordingly, FFR, FCR-N, and FCR-D are the fastest ancillary services because of their
shortest activation times. These are also called frequency containment reserves because
they react to restore the system frequency without letting it deviate too far from its
defined deadband. However, if these frequency containment reserves cannot restore the
frequency within a specific time limit, aFRR and mFRR get activated so that they can
release the frequency containment reserves to make those services ready to handle new
frequency deviations within the power system. The services aFRR and mFRR are consid-
ered the slowest ancillary services because they have a significantly longer activation time
compared to the frequency containment reserves. Also, these are called the frequency
restoration reserves because they replace frequency containment reserves if the frequency
deviation persists for a defined period, thereby contributing to restoring the frequency
and ensuring that the system balance is re-established [1]. Figure 1 shows a frequency
deviation caused by a sudden disturbance in the system and how different reserves get
activated to restore the frequency to 50 Hz depending on the time the frequency deviation
persists. Accordingly, aFRR gets activated 30 seconds after the activation of frequency
containment reserves.

1.1.4
In-depth review on FCR-D service markets
Among the ancillary services mentioned under the section 1.1.2, the sole attention in this
project is paid to the FCR-D service market in the DK2 zone of Denmark.
As briefly stated above, the frequency rises when there is a surplus in generation com-
pared to the demand. At times like this, when the grid frequency rises within the band
of 50.1 Hz – 50.5 Hz, FCR-D down-regulation gets activated to bring down the frequency
to 50 Hz, its nominal value. The activation is proportional, starting with 0% delivery
at frequencies above 50.1 Hz and uniformly increasing to full delivery at 50.5 Hz. Simi-
larly, at times, due to having a demand greater than the supply, when the frequency falls
within the limits of 49.9 Hz – 49.5 Hz, FCR-D up-regulation gets activated to restore the
frequency to 50 Hz. In this case, the activation starts with 0% delivery for frequencies
below 49.9 Hz and gradually increases to reach full delivery at 49.5 Hz [1].
As large frequency deviations do not happen often in the system, full activation of FCR-D
services rarely occurs. Further, this FCR-D service is considered asynchronous, meaning
that FCR-D up-regulation and FCR-D down-regulation are procured separately [1]. Also,
FCR-D provision is categorized into two groups: static and dynamic. Dynamic FCR-D is
comprised of products that can adjust their reserves continuously. They can swiftly deac-
tivate their reserves when the frequency deviation begins to decrease [14]. Meanwhile, the
entities that do not have these dynamic properties are called Static FCR-D [15]. These
units cannot be regulated continuously and need a longer recovery time following their
activation [14]. The primary difference between these two groups is that Static FCR-D
is given a grace period of 15 minutes, and those units are not required to deactivate
and perform a second activation. Currently, purchasing a limited share of static FCR-D
services is possible because the TSOs do not predict a need to procure dynamic FCR-D
services at all times for an entire day [15].

What this project is about?
As briefly discussed in section 1.1.1, electric trains are increasingly recognized as a reliable
source of frequency support during power grid disturbances. This makes them significant
in the development of demand-side response strategies [10].
In Denmark, Danske Statsbaner(DSB) is known to be the largest passenger train op-

more. Among these, S-trains are operated within the DK2 zone, connecting different com-
muter towns surrounding the Greater Copenhagen area [16]. These S-trains are powered
through an electrified DC network of 1.5 kV [17]. Therefore, these trains can provide

Accordingly, this project will analyze the feasibility of providing Static FCR-D services
via the traction power of S-trains from a techno-economic point of view. Further, an al-
gorithm will be built to ensure optimal train fleet participation in the frequency market,
which could be utilized in formulating bidding strategies in the future.
Thus, the project was carried out in two main steps:
1. Quantifying the capacity of reserves that can be extracted from train traction ser-
vices (acceleration, cruising, coasting) for Static FCR-D provision using historical
data.
2. Given the operational limitations, building an algorithm to optimize the hourly
provision of capacity reserves for Static FCR-D from a fleet of trains.

2.1

In this project, the traction power of an electric train is referred to as the electrical power
used by the train’s traction motors to generate the necessary force to move the train [18].
This power is drawn from external electrical sources, such as overhead catenary lines or
electrified third rails, or from onboard energy storage systems like batteries in the case
of battery-electric trains [19]. This power will determine the locomotive’s acceleration,
ability to maintain the speed, and ability to overcome gradients and friction [20].
As previously mentioned, the traction power of an electric train is the key force that drives
the train’s movement while making the train operate efficiently and effectively across dif-
ferent operational conditions [20]. Therefore, addressing how this traction power is mod-
eled mathematically is vital.

4.1
Prequalification requirements specified by Energinet for bid-
ding capacity reserves in the Static FCR-D market
For a particular entity to become prequalified to participate in the Static FCR-D market,
it is mandatory to satisfy the requirements while passing the technical tests specified by
the Energinet. Therefore, before diving into how the quantification and optimization of
Static FCR-D capacity reserves were done, the requirements considered in this analysis
are documented as follows.
These are common for both Static and Dynamic FCR-D
service units.
1. Participant Bids
• The bids must be submitted to Energinet by no later than 00:30 A.M., the day
before the day of operation (D-1 early) [23].
• The bids must mention an hour-by-hour volume and a price for the day of oper-
ation. Both volume and price must always be stated with a positive sign. The
volume stated is the amount of MWs the bidder is offering to make available
[23].
• Each bid must be entered for a minimum of 0.1 MW. Each bid must always
be stated in MW to one decimal point, while the price must be mentioned in
DKK/MW or EUR/MW to two decimal points [23].
2. Requirement for prognosis at the time of bidding for reserves (Ex-ante)
• The maximum that must be bid in capacity corresponds to the 10% percentile
with delivery of capacity reserves from fluctuating renewables and flexible con-
sumption. This means that the participant’s prognosis, which must be ap-
proved by Energinet, evaluates that the probability is 10% that the sold ca-
pacity is not available. This entails that there is a 90% chance where the sold
capacity or more is available [24].
• If a participant is unable to deliver the sold capacity due to it being lower
than the 10% percentile, the participant will be excluded instantly for an un-
determined time. This will happen as a part of Energinet’s regular monitoring
[24].
In addition to the above requirements, the following technical conditions for Static FCR-
D reserves must be delivered upon performing two tests: the ramp static test and the
linearity test [24].
1. Supply inverse power at activation at frequencies between 49.9 and 49.5 Hz for
upward-regulation, and between 50.1 and 50.5 Hz for downward-regulation [23].
2. Deliver a response within 2.5 seconds [23].

5.2
Optimization of the capacity reserves through a scenario-
based approach
As discussed previously, the non-scenario-based optimization approach optimized the ca-
pacity reserves to their maximum. As a result, the FCRD-Up and FCRD-Down bidding
capacities of each hour were ascertained to be the same as the corresponding reserves
allocated at that specific hour. Therefore, scenarios were introduced in this approach
to incorporate robustness into the optimization model by better modeling the system’s
load profile. In other words, when a certain day of the week is filtered from each week of
the year 2022, for example, when all 52 Mondays filtered from 2022 are considered, the
24-hour consumption profiles from that set of Mondays that had similar characteristics
were grouped together. One such group was referred to as a scenario. Accordingly, a
technique called cluster analysis was utilized to obtain different consumption scenarios
for a certain day of the week.
Clustering is an unsupervised machine learning technique that classifies data in a dataset
into different groups based on their similarities, and these groups are referred to as clusters.
There are a lot of different clustering algorithms based on the variety of ways through
which the cluster is defined [26]. Among them, K-Means clustering is considered one of
the widely used clustering methods where the main objective is to partition the data into
a ‘K’ number of clusters in such a way that the Euclidean distance between each data
point and its assigned cluster center is minimized. A brief overview of how the K-Means
algorithm carries out its clustering can be summed up as follows [27].
1. Initialization: Randomly selects K number of points from the dataset to act as the
initial cluster centroids, or in other terms, the cluster centers.
2. Assignment: For each point in the dataset, it then calculates the distance between
that point and each of the K centroids. This is done to assign that data point to
the cluster that has the centroid with the least distance to that respective point.
3. Update centroids: Once all the data points are assigned to the clusters, the centroid
of each cluster is recalculated by computing the mean of all data points assigned to
that cluster.
4. Repetition: Steps 2 and 3 are repeated until the centroids do not change significantly,
or until the number of iterations specified by the user is reached. This is called
‘convergence’.
5. Final results: Once the data is converged, the final clusters with their assigned
data points along with the respective cluster centroids are outputted through the
algorithm.
As mentioned previously, these clusters provide a better representation of the system’s
load profile. Accordingly, the more clusters/scenarios there are, the less averaging of the
available consumption data will take place, resulting in better modeling of the system’s

load profile. However, the higher the number of clusters there are, the higher the compu-
tational time the program would need to complete its execution. Therefore, through trial
and error, it was decided to include 5 clusters for this optimization model as it provided
the best average with the lowest number of clusters.
Accordingly, the linear optimization algorithm formulated in this section computes the

Concluding Remarks
Decommissioning traditional power plants and the growing integration of intermittent,
converter-based renewable energy sources create significant challenges for maintaining
grid stability and power-balancing services. This converter-interfaced renewable genera-
tion with no rotational energy adds zero inertia to the power system. Hence, it causes large
frequency deviations due to delays in balancing power supply with demand. Therefore,
the TSOs require a diverse range of demand-side ancillary service providers to restore
the power balance and maintain grid stability during disruptions. Among the various
demand-side resources, electric trains are considered loads with reliable consumption pro-
files, making them ideal candidates for providing ancillary services. This thesis explores
the potential of using the traction power of S-trains in Denmark to offer frequency re-
serves in the Static FCR-D market. In addition, it also provides the optimal capacities
that could be extracted from these reserves to be made available for bidding in the FCR-D
market on an hourly basis for each day of the week.
Accordingly, this project analysis was conducted using the historical consumption data
of the train fleet provided by DSB. As the initial step of the analysis, the FCRD-Up
and FCRD-Down capacity reserves were quantified based on the prequalification require-
ments imposed by the Energinet for entering the Static FCR-D bidding market. After
quantifying the capacity reserves, the hourly allocation of those reserves for each day of
the week was optimized to maximize the income generated from the capacity sales on
each respective day. The optimization was carried out using three different approaches.
Among them, the scenario-based optimization of hourly capacity reserves using weighted
averages yielded the most optimistic results, while the approach without weighted aver-
ages resulted in the lowest hourly incomes. This is because the scenario-based approach
without weighted averages effectively resembled a robust optimization that focused on
the overlapping area of all scenarios, outputting the minimum capacity reserve for each
hour. As opposed to this, due to incorporating the weighting factors of each scenario,
the approach with weighted averages accounted for the contribution of all scenarios of
each hour, leading toward optimal results. Moreover, the results from the non-scenario-
based approach lie in the middle of those obtained from the aforementioned optimization
approaches. Accordingly, based on the results from the three optimization approaches,
leveraging S-trains’ traction power to provide ancillary services in the Static FCR-D mar-
ket presents a promising opportunity worth pursuing.
However, to obtain more realistic results, the uncertainty of instantaneous consumption
must be integrated into the constraints of the optimization model. As a result, the con-
straints can be restructured as chance constraints. In this analysis, K-Means clustering
was used to generate the consumption scenarios. As the outcome of the K-Means algo-
rithm is contingent on the initial positions of the centroids selected at the beginning of the
clustering process, the algorithm may produce different results at different runs. There-
fore, to enhance the consistency of the analysis, a bottom-up clustering method, such as
Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering, could be used to derive the consumption scenarios
of each day. Lastly, for future studies, an algorithm could be developed to forecast the

to accurately quantify the capacity reserves on a day-ahead or hour-ahead basis.

Limitations and Future Work
1. Uncertainty
Though the bids are mentioned as an hour-by-hour volume, the technical limit of
providing the FCR-D reserves is instantaneous. This implies that, at any given
second, the trains must be able to either increase or decrease their consumption to
provide the necessary FCR-D services. Therefore, to capture the unpredictability of
the trains’ instantaneous consumption, it is crucial to reformulate the constraints in
the optimization model by incorporating an uncertainty factor. Thus, constraints
3 and 4, representing the 10% percentile rule set by Energinet, can be modified as
chance constraints to account for the uncertainty of the FCRD-Up and FCRD-Down
capacity reserves at each hour, respectively. Moreover, constraints 5 and 6, which
define the operational limits of the trains, can be revised by incorporating scenarios
into the baseline. This way, the uncertainty in the hourly averages of the baseline
can be represented.

2. Clustering algorithm
In the second and third optimization approaches, the K-Means clustering algorithm
was applied to derive different consumption scenarios for each day of the week.
However, as mentioned previously, the K-means algorithm starts by randomly se-
lecting cluster centers and thereby assigning the data points to those centers so
that the variance of the points within the same cluster is minimized. Randomly
selecting the initial cluster centroids like this sometimes results in minimal local
convergence instead of settling into a global optimum. As a result, different results
are produced at different runs [29]. Therefore, the quality of the scenario analysis
in this project can be improved by utilizing a bottom-up clustering algorithm like
agglomerative hierarchical clustering. With no random initialization of data points,
this deterministic algorithm produces consistent results across different runs [30].
3. Forecasting algorithm
In future research, an algorithm can be built to forecast train traction power as a
function of the train schedule and other operational factors, enabling the quantifi-
cation of available reserves on a day-ahead/hour-ahead frame.
4. Prequalification tests
In addition to the prequalification requirements set by Energinet, two mandatory
prequalification tests must be conducted: the ramp static test and the linearity test.
These tests must meet the control response criteria specified by Energinet in order
to be approved for providing ancillary services. These tests aim to assess whether
a unit or system is technically capable of providing frequency reserves based on its
design [24]. This thesis demonstrates that using the traction power of S-trains to
provide FCR-D services is a feasible approach. Therefore, as a next step, future
work could involve running simulations on the rolling stock units in order to verify

if the system meets the required specifications and adjusting the control system to
enable market deployment.
5. Regenerative power
Since S-trains are DC trains, the regenerative power they generate is either dis-
sipated or utilized by another train if it is near a substation. Figures 14 and 15
show that the reserves obtained from this regenerative power during some periods
exceed the minimum bidding capacity of 0.1 MW that is required to participate in
the FCR-D bidding market. Therefore, as further research, more efficient utilization
of this regenerative power could be explored. This is by either using it as capac-
ity reserves for bidding in the FCR-D market or installing the necessary DC-AC
converters to convert the energy and supply it to nearby trains.
