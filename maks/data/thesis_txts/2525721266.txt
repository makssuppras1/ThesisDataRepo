Abstract 
 
High quality micro-/nano-mechanical resonators play an important role both in the research 
and industry of MEMS and sensors. In the field of photonics, nano-structured films, such gratings 
or photonic crystals are typically fabricated for many purposes such as light coupling, filtering, 
detection, lasing, sensing, etc. In this project, 200 nm-thick, 0.5 mm-square Si3N4 membranes 
suspended on a silicon frame were patterned with subwavelength grating structures using a 
combination of electron beam lithography and etching. The physical and optical properties of 
the patterned membranes were measured and compared with numerical simulations. Large 
area (400 mm-square) and highly reflective (~98% at specific wavelengths and polarizations) 
patterned structures were achieved, substantially improving on previous results.

1 Introduction 
Optical components of high reflectivity have been widely used in Nanophotonics during the 
last decades in various applications. A very high reflectivity has been already demonstrated 
and even commercialized in some cases, but it remains a challenge when it is required for the 
component responsible for it to have an overall dimension of several μm or nm and the price 
to remain affordable. This challenge is even harder when the component needs to be 
embedded in a Photonic IC, where fabrication or function originated reasons exclude the use 
of specific materials and geometric shapes or dimensions. In the specific case of an all-
dielectric high reflectivity mirror, there are two main approaches according to the main 
physical phenomena responsible for the reflectivity, apart from other effects present that may 
also enhance the reflectivity and improve the device’s performance (1,2). They both employ 
the concept of periodicity in the sense of geometric translational equivalence for the refractive 
index. The classification is based on the angle between the light’s k vector and the direction or 
plane of the structure with the periodic modulation of the refractive index, or equivalently, its 
unit vector(s). In the case of 0o angle of incidence, the material behaves as a photonic crystal 
and has a photonic band structure, similar to the electronic crystals (the crystalline solids) from 
Solid State Physics. The technology associated with this approach is wavelength and 
polarization specific as it implies that the component is structured in a way that the targeted 
wavelengths and polarizations cannot be guided into the material, due to the photonic band-
gap effect, similar to the electronic band-gap but the polarization may also be of relevance, 
and get reflected. The other approach is again wavelength and polarization specific and 
requires a normal incidence, a 90o angle between the k vector and the lattice’s unit vectors. In 
this case, the structure is a grating and the reflection is based on diffraction, when the 
wavelength is higher than the lattice constant(s). This approach of subwavelength gratings is 
the one that is followed for this project. 
Subwavelength gratings have gained popularity due to their high performance and 
compatibility with MEMS allowing them to integrate Photonic ICs functioning as various passive 
nanophotonic components or even high reflection mirrors for lasers, especially in the case of 
VCSELs. The coupling efficiency is enhanced by the elimination of nonzero diffraction orders. 
An example of exceptionally high reflection subwavelength gratings is the HCG (High Contrast 
Gratings) with reflectivity higher than 99% over a broad range of wavelengths (3). The high 
index contrast and comparable magnitude of the wavelength and lattice constant increase the 
complexity of an overall analytic description with exact formalisms for these systems, but an 
analytic solution has been demonstrated, by emphasizing on the high reflectivity phenomenon. 
For this case, the high index contrast contributes to the mechanical stability of the structure and 
the bandwidth. In addition, the exact reason for high reflectivity (higher than 99%) is the zero 
diffraction order suppression in transmission by the cancellation of the first and second modes’ 
lateral averages on the HCG plane. This was called destructive interference and it only works 
for the subwavelength gratings, where the zero order of diffraction is the only one responsible 
for power transfer in the transverse to the grating direction.  
Another popular use of gratings in the field of Optics and Photonics is the one for coupling 
light into and out of various waveguide components with one of the highest, if not the highest, 
efficiency, yet the lower losses reported so far both for metals (4) and dielectrics (5). The 
theoretical platform used to explain the phenomenon of light coupling is the perturbation 
==PAGE:6==
6 
 
theory and their leakage parameter for both light polarizations (TE and TM), with respect to 
the gratings grooves, has been determined in the case of rectangular gratings and deduced 
for other shapes as well (6). Detailed studies on the grating’s height reveal its importance in 
the refractive index modulation and the modification of the medium’s effective refractive index 
and finally the part of the propagating field that is perturbed and coupled into or out of the 
structure (7). In order to maximize the coupling efficiency, the direction of the impinging field 
needs to be adjusted, so that it compensates for the correction to the wave-number (8). High 
coupling efficiencies are achieved, even if more than one radiation mode is supported by the 
coupler, for an appropriate grating profile.  
As mentioned before, highly reflective subwavelength gratings are offered for extensive use in 
optomechanics, because of their very high performance and low thickness, yet small mass (9). 
Small mass, high mechanical quality factor, simple fabrication process and high reflectivity are 
properties of paramount importance in optomechanics, i.e. for the realization of a component 
that is simultaneously a cavity mirror and a mechanical resonator. In particular, SiN membranes 
fulfill the eligibility criteria to be nano-structured so as to form gratings used for Fabry-Perot 
interferometers with the notable finesse of 2000-3000 at the Telecommunication wavelength. 
The difference from the theoretical modeling was associated with fabrication and 
experimental problems and difficulties responsible for losses and scattering (10). 
Recently, the mechanical characterization of suspended SiN membranes (11) has been 
achieved and serious progress is noted in introducing them into a high finesse optical cavity, a 
necessity for cavity optomechanics experiments. A detailed study on the dependence of the 
collective optomechanical coupling strength, in the case of periodic arrays for the transmissive 
regime, on the membrane thickness is based on full transfer matrix calculations. The studies on 
the effects of pressure on suspended SiN membrane arrays (12) and electromechanics in 
coupled membranes (13) have opened the way for optomechanics with membrane arrays. 
In this project SWGs are fabricated on top of commercial Si3N4 suspended membranes and 
then occurring samples are characterized optically and structurally. For this purpose, the 
suspended membranes are processed in the cleanroom environment of AU iNano so that 
SWGs were fabricated. Optical measurements on the gratings were carried out in the Optics 
lab in order for the high reflectivity to be observed. These gratings are designed so that they 
are in the interesting and less explored intermediate regime between shallow gratings and 
HCGs and their design was based on numerical simulations. The initial goals to optimize the 
fabrication recipe and approach the set record of performance in the experimental results 
were met with success and overcome. A more efficient recipe was achieved and both lower 
transmittance and higher reflectance were measured. The polarization dependence of the 
GMR, the origin of the gratings’ optical response, was exhibited. Furthermore, the grating’s 
and incident beam’s size related effects were experimentally explored, beyond the 
simulations’ hinders and difficulties respectively. All of the results are studied for TM and TE 
polarizations. 
In chapter 2 the fundamental theory describing the shallow grating and HCG regimes is 
presented together with the MIST simulation software, the closest realistic approach for the 
SWGs in the intermediate regime between the two. In chapter 3 the fabrication protocols are 
described in detail after a brief introduction to the relevant concepts of the Nanofabrication 
field, Etching and Lithography. Chapter 4 is devoted to the optical and structural 
characterization of the samples, both in terms of method description and data processing. 
Finally, in chapter 5 the experimental results are presented and explained based on actual 
experimental data.

2 Theory 
2.1 Subwavelength gratings 
A dielectric slab waveguide can be considered as a planar surface of finite thickness, also 
known as the core, that lies on top of a semi-infinite dielectric area, the substrate, and below 
another semi-infinite area, the superstrate. Those two semi-infinite areas that consist the 
cladding may be in general of different refractive indices with each other and their refractive 
indices are lower that the core’s, so that there is at least one waveguiding mode supported. 
This system is well studied and the results of the analysis (14) are that the index contrast 
between the core and the cladding areas needs to be high enough so that waveguidance 
takes place and any guided modes supported by the system are classified in two main 
categories according to their fields’ directions with respect to the slab’s geometric boundaries. 
Modes with the electric or magnetic field always parallel to the slab’s boundaries are called 
TE or TM modes respectively. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Subwavelength diffraction gratings may be considered as slabs with a periodic structural 
modulation on their surface. This means that their structural modulation scatters the incident 
light, both the reflected and the transmitted parts, while the periodic property of the structure 
is responsible for the interference of the scattered parts in each direction. This phenomenon is 
called diffraction and the result is transmission and direction in many different directions, also 
known as orders of diffraction, each one associated with an angle (measured from the normal 
to the impinged surface) and an energy part of the incident beam. More specifically, the 
angle θi of the i-order in Transmission is given as

For the case of subwavelength gratings, it is again only the zero diffraction orders for 
Transmission and Reflection that propagate, whereas the higher diffraction orders remain 
quasi-guided within the grating. In more detail, normal incidence results in opposite 
propagation constants for the +1 and -1 orders, which are otherwise identical and couple to 
the zero propagating orders generating a standing wave pattern in the waveguide at 
resonance (20). Furthermore, the subwavelength grating performs a sharp resonant reflection 
with narrow linewidths, which theoretically can be as high as 100% at resonance. The intensity 
variation only within some nm for the propagating diffraction orders is characterized as a 
guided-mode resonance (21), exactly due to the key role of the propagating modes’ 
interactions with the non propagating guided ones. This coupling interaction reveals a 
degenerate state. A possible way to cancel the degeneracy is to break the symmetry by 
changing the incidence angle. This would cause a split in the reflection peak. Additionally, the 
linewidth of the resonance increases monotonously as the grating modulation increases, and 
vanishes as the dielectric function modulation tends to zero. Last, it has been demonstrated that 
the resonance’s asymmetry originates from the asymmetry in claddings’ dielectric functions and 
the thickness of the core layer (21). This resonant reflection can also take place in grating 
waveguides that support even higher orders of diffraction to propagate. Of course, the zero 
order, or equivalently the subwavelength gratings are the ones with the lowest energy 
dissipation (20). As the grating period becomes very small, compared to the incident 
wavelength, the grating behaves as a homogeneous medium with an effective refractive index, 
which is in principle different for TE and TM polarizations. 
2.2 High index contrast gratings 
The previous description of subwavelength gratings is valid for the cases of small perturbations 
in the refractive index or structure. As the grating’s grooves become deeper or the refractive 
index variation along one grating period grows larger, the small perturbation condition does 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
not hold anymore. The high index contrast gratings (HCG) constitute a category of near-
subwavelength gratings, where the grating period remains very close to the incident light’s 
wavelength and the refractive index contrast between its two grating materials is large. 
Gratings like these (HCG) perform the unique properties of the regular subwavelength ones, 
but also exhibit very high reflectivity over a broad bandwidth and high quality factor 
resonances (22). The high index contrast between the grating itself and the surrounding areas 
is of paramount importance for these extraordinary properties.  
The typical theoretical approach to study these systems is very similar to the one for the usual 
subwavelength gratings. Again, a guided mode resonance occurs in this complex system that 
combines the grating with the slab waveguide. As mentioned before, the slab waveguide 
supports several modes depending on its width and the wavelength, each one with its own 
propagation constant. As about the grating, it is responsible for the diffraction of the incident 
beam and the generation of the diffraction order modes, each one with a wavevector’s 
component parallel to the grating’s direction. As far as the grating and the slab are merged, 
equality between the slab’s propagation constant and a wavevector’s component in the 
grating’s direction will result in a coupling between the two modes and energy transfer 
between them. 
Another approach is to consider the grating as a slab consisting of parallel slab tapes of 
different refractive indices placed in a periodic manner. For large wavelengths, it is only the 
fundamental mode (TE or TM) propagating and higher order modes are instead cut off. As a 
result, the grating functions as a homogeneous medium of an effective refractive index for TE 
and another for TM. For lower wavelengths, more propagating modes exist. When two 
waveguide modes propagate inside the grating, the so-called dual-mode (or two-mode) 
properties emerge. In normal incidence, waveguide modes cannot be excited. 
The broadband reflection results from the co-propagation of the two waveguide modes inside 
the grating layer and the reflection occurring at each interface. Away from the grating, only 
the zero diffraction order propagates (3). Normal incidence on the grating excites two 
waveguide modes. They propagate until they are reflected back to themselves and 
unavoidably to each other and couple to the zero order diffracted mode in the area only 
accessible to transmitted waves. Absolute reflection occurs when this coupling is of opposite 
phases and destructive interference for the waveguide modes allows no power to be 
transmitted. In addition, when two points of the spectrum, which fulfill this high reflection 
condition, are close to each other, the broadband high reflectivity mentioned before takes 
place. Guided mode resonance (GMR) is offered to explain this phenomenon. When a phase 
matching condition is fulfilled between the diffracted beam and a slab’s guided mode GMR 
will be present in the reflectivity spectrum. A GMR is actually a Fano resonance between the 
discrete modes of the grating and the continuum of modes in the surrounding medium (23). 
Many GMRs closely spaced in the spectrum with the appropriate coupling among them yield 
high reflectivity over a broad wavelength range. The wavelengths where GMR takes place 
are identified from the dips in transmission spectrum. In practice, there are two dips observed 
in the transmission spectrum corresponding to GMRs. These two GMRs provide two Fano 
resonances of opposite parity holding a broadband reflection spectrum, the spectrum oriented 
by the position of these two Fano resonances (23). What is even more exciting is that the 
bandwidth can be further increased by introducing more GMRs, simply by lifting the symmetry 
of the grating’s unit cell, yet the reflection symmetry (24,25).

2.3 MIST simulations  
2.3.1 Basic principles 
As mentioned before, the complexity of the system implies the use of numerical methods and 
simulations in order to model or predict the system’s optical response. The method used is the 
Rigorous coupled wave analysis (RCWA) (26), a semi-analytical method used to solve 
Maxwell’s equations in Fourier space with the fields expressed as infinite sums of harmonic with 
respect to the space fields. The use of RCWA is very popular in light scattering problems from 
periodic dielectric structures. 
The key assumption, apart from using plane waves, is that the grating is considered an array 
of slab waveguides. A plane wave incident on the grating can excite one or more array-slab 
modes, always according to the refractive indices of the materials and the geometric 
parameters of the structure compared to the wavelength used. It is the index contrast and 
subwavelength characteristics that were discussed the ones that allow in a wavelength range 
only two modes to be with real propagation constants along the grating’s direction and 
therefore carry energy. They travel from the incident spot to the grating’s other surface, where 
they are reflected, always with a phase shift. At this exiting plane the waveguide modes are 
reflected back and coupled to each other, because of the mismatch with the exiting plane 
wave, the one to be transmitted. There is also another mode coupling to the entering plane. 
The grating’s reflectivity solution is the outcome of the modes’ full back and forth trips. In 
general, waveguide modes transmit through the grating at both planes, but in the special case 
of SWGs the zero order of diffraction is the only one to carry energy. High reflection requires 
a destructive interference at the exit plane in order to depose transmission, so the grating’s 
thickness is crucial. A high quality resonator requires constructive or destructive interference at 
both grating’s planes. The destructive interference is the result of the zero spatial mode 
overlap with the transmitted mode. The zero transmission coefficient prevents the transmission 
of optical power and the ideal unity reflection is logically deduced, as far as scattering, 
absorption and other excitations or phenomena are excluded. 
The simulations presented in this project were carried out with MIST (Model Integrated 
Scattering Tool), a successful simulation tool for optical modeling, which was developed by 
Thomas Germer from NIST (National Institute of Science and Technology). It is designed to 
execute the integration of the Bidirectionl Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) over solid 
angles introduced by the user. The effects in Reflectance by alternating the grating model 
parameters is presented. The used inbuilt models are provided by the SCATMECH library. 
2.3.2 Simulation results from MIST 
2.3.2.1 The polarization dependence 
Simulations using MIST have been performed in order to predict the GMR wavelengths and to 
have at least a rough estimation of the tendency and more importantly the sensitivity of the 
system’s optical response when varying only one parameter. This is very important from a 
designing perspective, because the wavelength range that is actually scanned and measured 
on is a finite one, from 915nm up to 985nm, and the samples’ geometric characteristics must be 
such that procure it exhibit a GMR within this range, yet a detectable polarization 
discrimination and difference in the measured transmittance. A study like this may also reveal 
the most critical parameter for the geometry of the system in terms of GMR positioning in the 
spectrum. All this information will be extremely useful for designing samples and selecting the 
appropriate parameters for the necessary processes in order to fabricate them in the 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
cleanroom, where the highest precision possible is always required even prior to the booking 
of the equipment. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In Figure  3 the reflection spectra of the same SWG are presented for both polarizations, TE 
and TM. In both cases the membrane consists of Si3N4 with refractive index 2.01, thickness 200 
nm, mean width 530 nm and 110 nm depth and period 815 nm. It is clear that GMRs are 
strongly polarization dependent, since for the SWG the TE mode is highly reflected at  nm, but 
the TM mode is highly reflected at  nm. In general, both modes are reflected but their 
reflection profiles are similar to the reflection from a regular dielectric slab with an effective 
refractive index determined by the grating’s constituent materials’ ones as well as the 
geometry and filling factor of the grating, unless the wavelength range where GMR takes 
place. The fact that those two GMRs are possible for the same grating reveals that each SWG 
can be highly reflective for both TE and TM modes. However, the associated GMRs occur in 
different wavelengths for these modes. In order to have TM-GMR reflection, the SWG’s period 
needs to be closer to the used wavelengths, than the one for TE-GMR. For example, high TE 
reflection from the SWG with 815 nm period is expected in a lot larger wavelength than 985 
nm in the same manner as TM reflection from the SWG with 645 nm period is expected in  
smaller wavelength than 915 nm. In both cases, it is not possible to detect both GMRs for the 
wavelength range scanned by the laser used in our setup. In order to achieve GMRs for both 
TE and TM modes within the above mentioned range, or even a coincidence of the GMRs, one 
could possibly use gratings with 2D periodicity instead, but this is not studied in this project.

3 Nanofabrication 
In this chapter the main techniques of nanofabrication which were used will be introduced in a 
balanced way between detail of relevance and method-specified interest. The fabrication 
process and the exact recipes will be presented for the nano-structured gratings on the Si3N4 
membranes starting from the NORCADA products as well as the most possible recipe, 
according to strong arguments, such as modern IC technology and an industry’s needs, that the 
company must have used. 
3.1 Nanofabrication theory 
3.1.1 Chemical vapor deposition of Si3N4  
First, the LPCVD (Low Pressure Chemical Vapor Deposition) of Si3N4 refers to a deposition that 
takes place in some dedicated chambers – reactors with either hot or cold walls and it is 
usually a many wafer process, especially in industry. Hot wall systems are reliable for uniform 
temperature distributions and reduced natural convection effects (27). However, cold wall 
systems have lower depositions on the walls. This is essential, because wall deposits may lead 
not only to depletion of the deposition species but also to the formation of particles, which 
usually flake off the walls and reach the wafers. This is why deposits on the walls always lead 
to memory effects. Of course, in the case of industry it would be durable to maintain hot wall 
reactors only dedicated to the growth of Si3N4 film. As a result, it is hard to argue for the use 
of the one over the other system for NORCADA. In general, all polycrystalline Si and a 
considerable amount of dielectrics’ depositions are carried out in hot wall systems. Wafers are 
close packed, in the same manner as the wafers in the thermal oxidation systems. In order to 
achieve high uniformity of deposition in such a system, the process is designed so as to keep 
the reaction strictly controlled by the deposition kinetics (28). Instead of using a diluent gas, the 
low pressures (0.1 to 1.0 torr) allow for a reduced gas phase nucleation. The dedicated 
equipment consists of tubes, similar to the thermal oxidation setups (furnaces). The gases are 
guided to the front of the furnace and controlled by mass flow controllers. The gases may 
either be injected through a ring at the front of the tube, or plumbed to a tube, which injects 
gases uniformly across the whole length of the furnace. An O-ring seals the door after and 
before the wafer loading. Of course, the tube remains flushed with N2 (or another inert gas) 
and pumped down to medium vacuum. After the deposition temperature is reached, the 
deposition gases are released for a predetermined duration, until the furnace will be flushed 
again with N2, the pressure will be raised and the wafers unloaded. An alternative design of 
the LPCVD furnaces consists of vertical chambers, so that the gravity will maintain the wafer-
to-wafer spacing uniform. The result is that natural convection effects will be uniformly 
distributed across the wafer, which is why uniformities of better than 2% in the deposition of 
undoped poly and silicon nitride are reported (27,29). The cost of the vertical LPCVD 
equipment remains higher than one of its conventional counterparts, but the quality of the 
samples received from NORCADA indicates that equipment of at least the same efficiency was 
used. 
Si3N4 can be deposited from SiH4 (silane) and a nitrogen-containing precursor, LPCVD Si3N4 is 
usually deposited from mixtures of H2SiCl2 (DCS) and NH3 (ammonia). Regular deposition 
temperatures are 700-900oC (30), while the activation energy is 1.8 eV. As about the 
deposition rate, it increases with the H2SiCl2 flow rate to the values of 10-20 A/min. For the 
stoichiometric films that were used, they have a refractive index between 2.0-2.1 and high 
tensile stress (12-18 GPa), the reason why films thicker than about 2000 A tend to crack. This 
is an important limit for designs of subwavelength gratings with deep trenches and thick slab 
waveguides. The most common and non-destructive technique to check the composition of these 
films is the measurement of the refractive index with Ellipsometry. Alternatively, the 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
measurement of the etch rate in buffered HF may be used to extract the composition details. 
To be more specific, high refractive indices indicate a Si-rich film, while low ones, the presence 
of O2 (vacuum leak), contaminated gases, or incomplete pumpdowns. The presence of oxygen 
can be also assumed when the etch rate of the film exceeds 1 nm/min in 49% HF. Other 
common impurities in Si3N4 include but are not limited to hydrogen and 0.4% Cl [34]. It is also 
found in literature (27) that these films have a 10-20 A layer of SiO2, but it may associated 
with a typical O2-plasma clean that usually takes place after the whole fabrication process. 
Increasing the concentration of H2SiCl2 in the growth ambient can decrease the stress but 
results in Si-rich films. The high concentration of silicon-containing species (H2SiCl2, SiCl2) is an 
important factor for gas phase nucleation to take place during the LPCVD of Si3N4 and 
leading to a high particle concentration in the gas. A trustworthy method to identify the Si, N 
and any other impurity concentration in the Si3N4 layer is XPS, especially when the setup of the 
equipment includes an ion gun in order to etch a specified area of the specimen. Of course, it 
is a destructive method, but in such a large amount batch process that takes place for Si3N4 
deposition, there are always some dummy wafers to monitor the production. 
Another well-established within CMOS industry approach for the CVD of Si3N4 is PECVD 
(Plasma Enhanced Chemical Vapor Deposition). PECVD of Si3N4 is very common when the last 
is deposited on GaAs, where the substrate temperature has to remain very low (27). As a 
result, RF plasma is used as an alternative non-thermal energy source for the reactant 
molecules. The ad-species are also diffused even further along the surface due to this energy, 
without the high temperature requirement. This is what qualifies the process as very efficient 
for filling small features. However, the last decades the wafer diameter has increased 
(expected to increase even further) and it is the low throughput and marginal uniformity of 
PECVD systems which is limiting their role in modern Si-based IC production. On the other hand, 
LPCVD (especially for Si3N4) is fit for GaAs-based processes, where the wafers and the 
batches remain smaller and the low temperature requirement is a necessity. As about Si-based 
IC nanofabrication with large diameter wafers, parallel-plate hot wall systems are used, 
similar to LPCVD tubes where the wafers are mounted vertically on conductive graphite 
electrodes of alternating polarity. Even if the substrate temperature is always controlled as in 
any furnace, it is a lot cooler than the LPCVD systems. The throughput of this reactor version is 
higher than the parallel-plate plasma reactor, but it is much less than the one of a regular 
LPCVD system and this is a major reason for modern industries to select the LPCVD systems 
when they are consistent with the relevant fabrication requirements. 
3.1.2 Lithography 
3.1.2.1 UV Lithography for suspended membranes’ fabrication 
 
The modern lithographic techniques are classified in two categories, the optical and the non-
optical techniques. In optical lithography, highly energetic photons are guided on a mask 
under which the resist coated specimen is located and exposed mostly on the desired areas. 
This resist is photosensitive and will change permanently its chemical and/or physical 
properties, among which its solubility in a solvent, so that there will be a large difference 
between the exposed and the unexposed areas. Afterwards, a development phase follows, 
which is practically a wet etching of high selectivity for the one of the two areas over the 
other, and results in the patterning of the specimen. The precision of this method depends on 
the energy of the photons used, usually UV, DUV or XUV, and for the last case of extreme UV 
lithography it is reported that it may reach 40nm. Among the non-optical lithographic methods, 
the most common and most studied one is the EBL (Electron Beam Lithography) and is the one 
with the highest precision so far, as it holds the record of 4nm so far. In this case, electrons 
instead of photons are directed on the resist coated specimen, but in EBL there is usually no 
mask used (except for some variations). The concept of EBL will be discussed in more detail 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
later. The other non-optical lithography methods are mostly variations and combinations of the 
above mentioned lithographic methods, combined maybe with some other processing steps 
(etch or deposition steps). Some variations of them are based on equipment, resist or mask 
related optimizations of the efficiency of each method with respect to the targeted application 
to which they were initially dedicated (31–33). An important example of a well established 
lithography method in the industry of nanofabrication is NIL (Nano-Imprint Lithography) 
(34,35). The main concept of NIL is the use of polymers that have a transition temperature 
above which they behave as fluids of some finite viscosity, while they become solid below that. 
A nano-structured stamp is used as a mold to provide the desired patterning before the 
polymer is again cooled down, below the transition temperature. The precision of this 
lithography method depends mostly on the pattern morphology on the stamp, because this is 
the one governing the fluidic behavior of the polymers, but in general it can be assumed equal 
to the precision of the lithography method used to fabricate the stamp (EBL). The main factor 
to take into account in order to argue for the use of the one method over the other apart from 
the required precision, in an industrial environment, is the profit maximization which leads to 
the cost reduction, including the required resources and working hours as well as the 
reproducibility. As far as the EBL is a single sample, not even wafer, process and for this 
particular large area of 500 square μm the total energy and process time would be too large 
to afford. As about NIL, it is fast and reproducible, but remains one wafer process. Again, the 
area to be molded is very large and the polymer in the middle of the stamp feature would be 
trapped and very hard to remove completely. On the other hand, UV lithography could be 
directly applied for this purpose and the mask would be rather easy to get. Many wafers at a 
time could be loaded or even exposed in the appropriate system.

3.1.3 Etching 
3.1.3.1 Anisotropic wet etching of Si with KOH 
 
Last, the KOH wet etch will be discussed. In general, etching can be accomplished by physical 
damage, chemical attack or the combination of them (27). An example of physical etching is 
the ion milling in which an energetic beam of inert atoms at very low pressure arrives to the 
wafer’s surface and due to collision removes atoms or molecules. Such a process yields a high 
degree of anisotropy, which practically means that the process is very directional with respect 
to the initial beam’s route. However, the etch rate is nearly independent of the substrate 
material. The representative figure of merit to describe this is the selectivity, the etch rate of 
the one material over the one of the other. In this case, the selectivity is close to unity, while in 
general it is desired to be higher than on, so that there is high control of the etching area 
distribution. At the other extreme, wet etching is purely chemical and is characterized by high 
selectivity, but low anisotropy. Of course, it is also a common practice to have a bath of the 
etchant in a liquid phase, but heated up to its boiling temperature, while the wafers are 
suspended above this boiling bath facedown, so that the vapors arriving at the wafers’ surface 
will etch with the same selectivity but higher anisotropy than its counterpart wet etching. 
However, this process is sometimes hard to control and have reproducible results due to the 
vapor fluidic effects, especially for large wafers, tends to concentrate etchant and byproduct 
droplets on the wafer’s surface and typically has low etch rates, an important drawback for 
large scale manufacturing. High selectivity and anisotropy are both accomplished in a low 
pressure chamber where RIE (Reactive Ion Etching) takes place, where the plasma chemistry has 
a leading role in the etching process, but this concept will be discussed in more detail later, 
when it will be more relevant. 
Wet etching is a purely chemical process and according to the wafer’s morphology, overall 
size and process step may present unbearable drawbacks, namely lack of anisotropy, very 
low process control or even excessive particle contamination. However, wet etching is highly 
selective and causes no damage to the substrate. The etchant’s solution is agitated to 
contribute to the transfer of etchant to the surface and the removal of the byproducts in order 
to achieve a faster and more uniform etch rate. When the etching produces gases, they form 
bubbles which prevent the supply of fresh etchant to the surface and the occurrence of the 
bubbles cannot be predicted. This problem is mostly pronounced near pattern edges. The 
agitation in the chemical bath reduces the adsorption of bubbles to the wafer. Small geometry 
features are etched slower because of the microscopic bubbles of accumulated gases (37). 
Si3N4 is etched by hot H3PO4 at 160-180oC (acids in general), while Si is etched by alcalic 
solutions (bases). Wet etching behaves directionally for crystalline materials, meaning that 
some solutions have faster etch rates along certain crystallographic directions. The result is 
sharp facets with well-controlled angles for single-crystal substrates (38). In our case, Si3N4 is 
amorphous, but Si is of zincblende single-crystal structure. KOH dissolved in water (even with 
IPA) at 80oC is a typical Si etchant with low rates for SiO2 and Si3N4. TMAH (Tetra-ammonium 
hydroxide) (CH3)4NOH at 115oC is another anisotropic etchant for Si with very high 
selectivities over SiO2 and Si3N4. EDP at 90oC or hydrazine N2H4 in water and IPA could also 
have been used. However, KOH (even with IPA) is the less toxic and hazardous among the 
above mentioned ones (most of them have been forbidden in many cleanrooms for such uses) 
and holds the record of crystal-based anisotropic etches. At 50-90oC the etch rate is 0,3-
2μm/min with crystal plane selectivity of (111):(110):(100) around 1:600:400 without IPA 
(Iso-Propyl Alcohol) or 1:400:600 with IPA addition. Surface roughness remains low, 
depending on temperature, KOH concentration, additives and agitation, but increases with IPA 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
addition. This is what justifies the use of KOH over the rest of etchants, especially in this case, 
that no metals are present. 
3.1.3.1 Plasma (dry) etching of Si3N4 (DRIE) 
The EBL process described in 3.2.2 is the first one that defines the pattern of the grating on the 
samples according to this recipe. However, this is not yet enough to end up with any structures. 
The outcome of the EBL is a structure on the PMMA layer above the Si3N4 layer which is 
required to be patterned. The patterned PMMA is actually going to be used as an etch mask 
for the etching step that follows, the essential step that results in the patterning of the Si3N4 
layer. Apart from the etch process especially designed for Si3N4 there is also another etch step 
applied, the so-called O2 plasma cleaning step in order to remove any residual PMMA before 
etching the Si3N4. In both cases, a plasma etching is used instead of the wet etching, discussed 
in section 3.1 for the Si3N4 suspended membrane fabrication. 
Plasma etching offers major advantages in comparison with wet etching. Plasma chemistry is 
significantly more controllable and repeatable in the sense that it is way easier to initiate and 
abort than an immersion wet etching plus that it is practically wafer’s temperature 
independent. In addition, high anisotropies can be achieved even for small features, while 
plasma etching is an overall fewer-particle process than any liquid media. Another important 
aspect is that less chemical waste is produced. A broad range of dry etch processes has been 
realized with various combinations of physical and chemical attack. Of course, the variety of 
etch chemistries used in each etch system is again not unique. In general, plasma etch processes 
are described with six steps. The feed gas is introduced into the chamber and broken down to 
the chemically reactive species by the plasma. These exact species are diffused into and 
adsorbed to the surface of the wafer. Afterwards, they further diffuse until they react with the 
material to be etched. Last, the reaction product is desorbed, driven away from the wafer, 
and transported by the gas stream out of the etch chamber. Similar to wet etching, the etch 
rate is limited and determined by the slowest of these steps. In the regular plasma etch 
processes the etched area is collided by the incident flux of ions, radicals, electrons and 
neutrals. Even if the neutral flux remains the largest, the main source of physical damage is 
related to the ion flux. On the other hand, chemical attack depends on both the ion flux and 
the radical flux. As the process evolves, the etched area is modified down to the depth of 
many atomic layers. 
The first plasma etch setup in IC industry was based on high pressure and low power plasmas 
(27), with the mean free paths of the plasma species being much less than the chamber’s size. 
The plasma is used to start and stop the chemical reaction(s) of etching by forming the reactive 
species from an inert precursor, the feed gas. The low energy ions in the plasma leave the 
etching process depending mostly on the plasma’s chemistry, which is extremely complex. On 
the contrary, ion milling requires low pressure and high power. In principle, there exist no 
chemical reactions as far as only noble gases (Ar) are used. It is an entirely mechanical process 
directly comparable to sputtering. The main advantages in comparison with high pressure 
plasmas are directionality and applicability. Directionality is the outcome of the high 
acceleration voltage and the low chamber pressure, which implies low probability for atomic 
collisions. High anisotropy is achieved due to chemistry independence. This is exactly why it can 
be used for an endless variety of materials, even compounds and alloys, since no suitable 
volatile etch byproducts are required. Of course, selectivity remains poor unless a chemical 
component is used. Furthermore, throughput remains low, because ion sources are restricted to 
200 mm in diameter. 
 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
Reactive ion etching (RIE) was developed in order to combine high anisotropy with selectivity 
much higher than in ion milling. In general, there is a parallel plate reactor with the specimen 
resting on the powered electrode, while the ground electrode remains always attached to the 
chamber wall in order to enlarge its effective area. In fluorinated RIE, as in our case, etch 
anisotropy may originates from sidewall passivation and/or physical damage of the 
horizontal surfaces. The physical damage mechanism is usually described by the chemical 
sputtering model (39), where ion bombardment is the energy source, increasing both the 
mobility and reactivity of the surface species, so that etching is energetically allowed. In RIE 
there is a substantial ion bombardment that allows for the use of fluorinated chemistries, but is 
expected to damage the substrate. This is an important limitation of using RIE (40) together 
with chemical contamination, especially in polymerization etches, which unavoidably leave 
behind residual films. In addition to this, gas phase particle deposition is another significant 
issue, but an even more serious issue, depending on the application, are the metallic impurities, 
such as Fe, Ni, Na, Cr, K, and Zn, mostly localized on the surface of the wafer after the etch 
because of the accidental sputtering of the electrodes and chamber’s components that get in 
contact with the plasma. Common methods to remove these impurities include O2 plasma 
treatments usually followed by wet acid cleaning as well as H2 plasma treatments. However, 
they all increase the complexity of the process. Etching with carbon-containing species leaves 
the top atomic layers (30 A for Si) heavily damaged with several Si–C bonds present, while 
total damage extends even further (300 A for Si) (41). Similarly, RIE with hydrogen-containing 
species induce Si–H defects (400 A in Si) which are hard to remove (27).  
HDP systems were introduced to offer even more benefits. HDP sources use crossed magnetic 
and electric fields so that the mean free electron path is dramatically increased. As a result, 
the rate of dissociation and ionization is very high. It is exactly this high density of ions and 
radicals which is used to increase the etch rate or to allow for other benefits, namely adequate 
ion and radical densities at very low pressures, so that the bias on the wafer-holding electrode 
may be decoupled from the ion density. This may be accomplished by attaching the wafer-
holding electrode to a second RF source, while the dc bias is set by the RF power of the second 
source. The long mean free path at low pressure is the source of anisotropic etching. 
Furthermore, the low energy results in large selectivity and low residual damage, an 
outstanding property in order to etch down to very thin layers. Etching at lower pressure thus, 
with a more vertical incidence of ions offers also less reduction of the etch rate for high aspect 
ratio features, directly relevant to some of the TE-polarization reflecting gratings to be 
fabricated. This effect is usually called microloading (42). However, a disadvantage of HDP 
etching is that the high ion flux ends up in charging floating structures leading to excess 
leakage in the pattern’s edges because of residual etch damage. Sidewall passivation for 
anisotropic etch fails because nonvolatile species’ production is limited by the low pressure and 
the feed gas flow ratios may require a modification. This comes with the price of material 
build up on the chamber walls, which flakes off contaminating the wafers. This is why O2 
plasma cleans and wall heating are widely used, so that polymeric buildup in HDP systems is 
prevented. Of course, wall temperature may be used to optimize the etch selectivity and/or 
the etch rate uniformity.  
All above considered, the most appropriate regime for our case is between RIE and HDP, so 
that there is balance between selectivity and anisotropy together with a durable etch rate. The 
major factor that discriminates one category from another is the pressure in the reaction 
chamber, but there exist no strict lines between RIE and HDP, but the classification is deduced 
instead indirectly from the benefits and defects associated with the etching, after it is 
accomplished and the resulting patterns are studied in detail. In general, plasma etching 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
requires high pressure, above 10-1 torr, RIE is between 10-2 and 10-1 torr, HDP 10-3 to 10-2 
torr, while ion milling requires pressure below 10-3 torr (27).

Figure 12 and Figure  13 show the detailed recipe step by step. The products received from 
NORCADA are encapsulated in transparent plastic vessels that statically attach to gloves or 
tweezers and require careful handling. The Si substrates should be already cut and cleaned 
both with DI water and Acetone using the sonicator for at least 3. Even if these Si substrates 
are not a part of the final sample itself, it is important to keep them clean because particles 
can easily get transferred, during handling and processing, both to the membrane and the 
vacuum chambers of the equipment. Heating the Si substrates is also important to remove any 
humidity, this 5-15 nm layer of water that adsorbs on thin film surfaces, so that the water 
soluble CB glue adheres strongest to the substrate and the NORCADA product. It is crucial that 
CB glue is restricted between the two Si substrates, the one of the product itself and the other 
added, because it can contaminate the reactor chambers or the electron microscope’s vacuum 
chamber. It has also been observed that CB glue below the suspended membrane tends to 
accumulate in the walls and come in contact with the face-down surface of the membrane. This 
type of contamination is sometimes hard to remove and remains a source of absorption or 
scattering in the optical characterization, limiting the performance of the nanostructures. Of 
course, during the sample fabrication, the membranes enter various temperature and pressure 
regimes that may risk their precious mechanical properties. This is exactly why it is important to 
use CB glue only on one corner of the sample, so that not any isolated cavity is formed 
underneath the membrane and even higher stresses or temperature gradients are induced. 
For the EBL, a 590 nm layer of 950k PMMA is used as a resist. The membrane is spin coated 
with PMMA by transferring only a small amount, a droplet, of 7% 950k PMMA and spinning 
at 5000 rpm, 2000 rpm/s for 60s and baking right afterwards at 180oC for 2min. For the 
PMMA solution used, the solvent is Anisole and it is produced by mixing 10mL of 11% 950k 
PMMA, purchased ready, and 5,7mL of Anisole. In order to avoid charging effects, a 
conductive polymer (CP) layer of 50nm is spin coated afterwards with spinning parameters 
5000 rpm, 2000 rpm/s for 60s and baking right afterwards at 90oC for 2min. This conductive 
polymer is SX-AR-PC 5000. The E-beam used for exposure is conducted at 30keV and 0.2nA, 
while the dose required is 315μC/cm2. The chamber’s pressure is 10-5 to 10-6 Torr. The CP is 
removed when the exposure is complete by dipping into water for 2min. The development 
itself is accomplished by dipping into a mixture of 7:3 IPA:DI water for 1min and stopped by 
direct dipping into pure IPA for 30s. After each dipping is completed N2 drying for 20s is 
recommended. Investigation with the optical microscope takes place at every step to monitor 
the sample fabrication. A post-bake step is used to evaporate from PMMA any absorbed 
water or IPA. 
The equipment used for dry etching requires the samples to be whole 6 inch wafers and this is 
why the samples where mounted on these with CB glue. An O2 plasma cleaning process is 
useful to remove any residuals of PMMA. The important parameters are the O2 flow rate of 
100sccm, the rf power of 50W and the duration of 30s. The etch rate for PMMA for these 
conditions is 0.5 nm/s at 1.5×10-6 mbar. The Si3N4 etch parameters are the C4F8 flow rate of 
59sccm, SF6 flow rate of 36sccm, 13.56MHz on coil with rf power of 800W, 13.56MHz on 
platen with 40W and 20oC on substrate for 95s. The etch rates of PMMA and Si3N4 with these 
parameters and a process pressure of 6 mTorr are 2.35 nm/s and 1.22nm/s respectively.  
When the etching step is completed, the sample is in principle ready. What remains is that it is 
un-mounted from the Si wafer and stripped off the PMMA. Once the sample is clean but still 
mounted of the Si substrate, AFM and SEM investigations take place for an in depth qualitative 
and quantitative study of their geometric profiles, beyond the optical microscope limits. Last, 
the sample is un-mounted from the Si substrate and cleaned with hot DI water (90oC) and 
Acetone with an emphasis on CB glue removal and the sample is prepared for optical 
characterization in the laser lab.

4.1 Structural characterization 
4.1.1 SEM 
4.1.1.1 Theory 
Regarding the SEM setup, everything remains the same with the EBL setup described in chapter 
3, especially in this case that exactly the same equipment was used (Magellan SEM FEI). What 
is different is the voltage and current used, which results in different probabilities for all the 
various phenomena of an electron beam incident on the sample to occur. In this case, it is the 
secondary electrons (SE) that can only be detected, as far as this is the only detector attached. 
SEs occur when the electron beam interacts with the electric field of the specimen’s atomic 
electrons.  The result of this inelastic scattering is a transfer of energy to the specimen’s atom 
with the possibility of expulsion of an electron from that atom as a secondary electron (SE). SEs 
have energy less than 50 eV. If the resulting electronic vacancy, because of the creation of a 
secondary electron, is filled from a higher level orbital, a characteristic X-ray of that energy 
transition is produced. Of course, elastic scattering events occur when beam’s electrons interact 
with the electric field of the nucleus of the specimen’s atoms, resulting in changing the direction 
of the beam’s electrons without a significant change in the energy of the beam’s electrons. The 
elastically scattered electrons deflected back out of the specimen are the backscattered 
electrons (BSE). BSEs have high energy, at least 50% of the incident beam’s ones. The 
combined effect of the elastic and inelastic interactions is to distribute the incident electrons 
over the 3D so-called interaction volume. The interaction volume has linear dimensions several 
orders of magnitude greater than the specimen’s surface. The escape depth of the SEs is 5-
50nm, but for the BSEs it can be even a hundred times greater. For tilted samples, the incident 
beam’s electrons travel greater distances in the region close to the surface and more SEs 
escape the sample than the ones generated from normal incidence. In addition, any edges or 
ridges of the sample emit more SEs and this is why they have brighter images. This is very 
useful for our gratings, because they are one material structures and any contrast will be 
straightforward associated with topographic rather than compositional contrast, unless any 
unexpected structures appear. However, the main challenge in using SEM for these gratings is 
the charging effect, as far as Si3N4 is an insulator. Furthermore, coating the samples with a thin 
film of conductors, such as Au or Cu is not an option, since the suspended membrane gets 
damaged severely. Apart from lowering the voltage and the current, which is a classic 
approach for insulating samples, so that the electron-charge balance over the sample remains 
close to 1 during the experiment, very high voltage was tried out with the hope of meeting the 
other charge equilibrium point of the material or simply having enough of the electrons 
transmitted through the sample using the sample’s geometry. However, this was not proven 
more fruitful, so the low acceleration voltage regime was preferred.

4.1.1.2 Measurements 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
From the whole grating surface, there are three areas of interest fabrication-wise that may not 
exhibit homogeneities with each other due to fabrication procedures. These parts represent 
other equivalent parts of the grating so it is wise to start the measurements from these areas 
and as soon as any differences are verified, further measurements can take place in order to 
study this specific case. These expected to be non-equivalent with each other areas are the 
center, the side and the corner of the grating (Figure  15). The reason lies in the two main 
procedures required for the fabrication of these gratings, EBL and DRIE. During the EBL 
exposure, the electron beam exits its path with electron optics components on top of the center 
area of the grating with the distance, known as the working distance, of 5 mm. However, there 
is a slight difference in the actual working distance between the electrons travelling to the 
central region of the grating and the ones to the side or corner. During this component-free 
longer electron path more scattering phenomena may take place and reduce the energy or 
the amount of electrons incident on the sample. Additionally, any proximity effects, in case the 
dose used was by far larger than the clearing dose, may differentiate these areas from each 
other due to the difference in the existing nearby neighboring features. In other words, the 
actual dose of these areas could be different, especially for large gratings. As about the 
development and DRIE, as the grooves are etched and the grating is formed, the fingers are 
formed which also act as micro-channels and the diluted species and reaction products in these 
three areas may have different escape options in number, resulting in different escape rates 
and maybe different dilution or reaction rates, which result in different pattern depth during a 
given time interval.

4.1.2 AFM 
4.1.2.1 Method description 
In a common AFM (Atomic Force Microscope) apparatus there is a probe mounted on top of a 
flexible Si or Si3N4 cantilever. This cantilever is mounted on a piezo-electric crystalline material 
such that the position of the probe is able to move with respect to the surface. Laser light 
incident on the opposite side of the cantilever is scattered and detected by a photodetector. 
When the tip engages the sample, this laser light is deflected and its deflection is recorded 
(42). Attractive forces curve the cantilever downwards to the sample, while the repulsive 
upwards away from it. Of course, the stiffness of the sample has an important role in the 
overall calibration, as well as the settings for the tip’s response, but the Si3N4, which was used, 
is typically very hard with stiffness similar to the cantilever. The piezo-electric crystal moves so 
that the tip scans the selected sample’s surface with an adjustable frequency, while the 
deflection is recorded in order to extract the total interaction force on the tip and the height 
profile of each sampling spot. As far as the tip’s characteristics are provided and the main 
forces responsible are modeled, many useful data can be provided. In this case, the surface 
roughness, the depth of the SWGs and the cross-sectional profile are of main interest. In 
general, various interactions are developed between the different tips and different samples, 
so specific probes and modeling systems are used resulting in a broad variety of AFM 
versions. One very important example is the MFM (Magnetic Force Microscope) which is 
typically used for the discovery and determination of magnetic domains and their boundaries 
on magnetic samples’ surfaces. In our case, the emphasis is on the total electrostatic force, 
mainly of van der Waals interactions between the cantilever and the sample, since no 
magnetic materials or liquid samples are scanned. The setup is located inside a canopy that 
consists of a very hard plastic carapace on the outside and a spongy soft layer inside it, so 
that sound waves of various frequencies from outside the AFM’s measurement chamber cannot 
reach the sample or tip during the measurement. The equipment itself stands on an optical 
bench for mechanical isolation from waves that may possibly get transferred through the 
ground.  
==PAGE:36==

It is of great meaning that for the cantilever the Hooke’s law is valid. The total force is 
extracted linearly from the cantilever’s deflection. The corresponding spring constant depends 
not only on the constituent material of the cantilever, but also on the fabrication process and 
the shape of the tip (pyramid, cone, rectangular, V-shaped).

4.2 Optical characterization 
4.2.1 Spectroscopic ellipsometry 
Ellipsometry is a well established method for providing information about a multi-layer of thin 
films, such as layers’ thicknesses and optical properties. In this project Spectroscopic 
Ellipsometry is used to determine precisely the refractive index and the thickness of Si3N4 
membranes. The ellipsometer used in this project has a white light source, a movable sample 
stage, a detector and other optical components. All of these components serve the 
ellipsometer’s function in detecting changes in the light’s polarization after its reflection on the 
sample’s surface. Elliptically or linearly polarized white light is sent onto the sample’s surface. 
The reflected light will have then embedded information about the material’s properties in the 
Ψ and Δ components, where tan(Ψ) is the ratio amplitude change after reflection and Δ is the 
change in phase defined as rp/rs=tan(Ψ)exp(iΔ). Here, rp and rs are the normalized p-
polarized and s-polarized components of the reflected light respectively. This ratio is 
measured for a wide range of wavelengths. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Apart from its many benefits, Ellipsometry remains an indirect method requiring a model to 
relate the variation of Ψ and Δ with the wavelength in order to determine the thin film 
properties. The model may be built by choosing the correct material layer sequence and 
defining the material properties with predefined data from the ellipsometer’s database. As 
about the refractive index and thickness of the Si3N4 membrane, the exact area of interest to 
be measured is the window with the suspended membrane. On the contrary, Ellipsometry is 
designed only for thin films on top of a substrate, so that enough intensity is reflected, while 
the beam is in the order of mm in size. This is why the measurement is restricted on the Si3N4 
films on the Si substrate, the frames, and the assumption of uniformity between these two areas 
is inevitable. In principle, it is a reasonable assumption because the layer’s composition, density 
and thickness are subject to the deposition process and are uniform for the whole wafer. 
However, the anisotropic KOH wet etch process to remove the Si may have removed a few nm 
of Si3N4 as well. The measurements were received in the UV-visible range (300-820 nm) and 
the Cauchy model was used for a thin Si3N4 film on a thick Si layer. Fitting the measured Ψ 
and Δ values along the wavelength to this model provided the thickness and refractive index 
of the Si3N4 membranes.

4.2.3 Laser measurements on the gratings 
A detailed description on the data collection and analysis is discussed in this section starting 
with the description of the experimental setup. A picture of the actual optical bench, where the 
measurements were carried out, and its schematic representation with the most relevant optical 
components are presented in Figure  33. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
This setup was initially used to measure the transmission of gratings, but as soon as the 
transmission of the gratings reched very low values, an additional photo-diode detector was 
added in order to measure the reflection too. The available wavelength range with this laser 
source is 915-985 nm. The laser beam is coupled into a polarization maintaining optical fiber, 
which is an optical fiber with a small geometric asymmetry and available lossy modes for one 
polarization. Afterwards, the beam is coupled out of the fiber and guided with mirrors to a set 
of lenses that form a telescope according to their focal distance and the displacement between 
them. Their role is to control the beam’s size. A 50-50 beam splitter (BS) is also used to split the 
incident beam into two. The BS has an essential role as these two beams are correlated, having 
been generated simultaneously from the same source. One part of the main beam is directed 
to the detector 2. Even though this beam is not affected by the grating, it is necessary exactly 
for this reason, because it provides the reference beam for the data analysis. The other part 
of the incident beam is focused with a doublet lens to get the desired spot size on the 
membrane. The incident’s beam size is measured with a pinhole and power measurement 
taking into account that the smaller pinhole allows a less amount of the Gaussian beam to be 
transmitted. Below the BS a half wave plate is located in order to change and control the 
beam’s polarization. This part of the beam reaches afterwards the sample with the grating on 
top of the holder, which has an 1 mm hole located at the center. As a result, any transmitted 
part of this beam reaches the detector 1. As about the reflected part of this beam, it is 
directed again through the half wave plate and the BS, where one part of it reaches the laser 
source, but the other reaches the detector 3. The detectors are Thorlabs 36 DET A/M. A CMOS 
camera (DCC 1545M) is placed close to the sample holder in order to capture the scattered 
light from the membrane’s surface and help for the precise alignment of the beam with respect 
to the grating. The positions of the grating and the CMOS camera are changed with the knobs 
controlling their Cartesian coordinates in the micro-scale. 
 
The detectors measure intensities for the duration of 0.1 ms. In order to carry out the 
measurements, the laser source is connected with a PC and functioned with MatLab. The 
selected wavelength range is scanned several times for the desired combination of sample’s

An important notice for the polarization discrimination is that the SWGs used have structural 
anisotropy, directionality in particular, in the sense that each polarization has one of its 
respective fields oscillating along either across the gratings fingers facing either a slab or a 
periodic structure with periodicity in the fields oscillating direction. This way it is well 
understood that the medium’s effective refractive index is expected to be different for each 
polarization. Additionally, the grating structurally consists of alternating air and Si3N4 slabs, so 
it is an all dielectric medium with its permittivity being a periodic function, which is expected to 
affect the electric field nonetheless. On the other hand, both of these materials are non-
magnetic ones, which is usual in Photonics. In other words, the permeability is supposed to be 
constant and common within the two materials, which is expected not to induce any difference 
for the magnetic field, at a first approach. 
5.2 The size dependence 
The effect of finite beam size was investigated experimentally as described in chapter 4. 
Beams of different sizes were directed on the same sample and the results were plotted in the 
same graph. For these experiments, SWGs supporting GMRs for TE and TM polarizations were 
fabricated and measured on. As discussed in chapter 2, the MIST simulation software makes 
the assumption of planar waves’ incidence on the designed gratings. Of course, there is an 
inventive way to overcome this barrier by performing a series of angled incidences and some 
painful data processing that include the beam’s size. However, the whole process is time 
consuming and maybe not realistic for a study that includes many samples, each one with a 
slightly different finger profile, which is relevant, and characterized with various beam sizes 
defining different angles of incidence to be used for the necessary simulations and the data 
processing that follows.

grating area of 815 nm period, 540 nm mean finger width, 50nm offset, and 93nm height. 
The measurements were conducted for three different beam sizes, namely 19, 35 and 44 μm. 
As the beam size increases the transmittance spectrum approaches the one expected from 
simulations with MIST. More specifically, the characteristic resonance shape becomes thinner 
and the transmittance decreases, meaning that the reflectance increases. In comparison with 
the simulation result, the GMR takes place 21 nm shorter wavelength than the one simulated 
with MIST, similar to other studied samples. Of course, the observed GMRs are strictly related 
to the TM polarization for the scanned wavelength range over this sample. The fact that the 
grating’s TE and membrane’s transmittance levels coincide for every beam size further supports 
the polarization dependence discussed in 5.1section. The very low transmittance (5,9% for the 
35 μm and 0,8% for the 44 μm beam size) measured with this setup was not in strict 
agreement with the power’s measurement at the corresponding wavelength. The same was the 
case for this other large highly reflecting gratings, a fact that became the motivation for 
measuring the reflectance simultaneously with the transmittance measurement, so that the high 
reflection phenomenon is inspected in detail. The reflectance study results are summarized in 
5.3 section where the measurements on a TE reflecting grating with various beam sizes are 
presented. Again, the results are the same for the reflected TE mode. As the beam size grows 
larger, the sample’s reflectance slightly decreases until the point that the current setup cannot 
be used for measurements precise enough for the sensitivity required. 
5.3 The Reflectance study 
GMR is the responsible prerequisite for the studied SWGs to be highly reflective over the 
appropriate wavelength range for each polarization. However, the reflectance itself is not 
always the quantity one easily measures, especially for experiments requiring normal 
incidence on the reflecting medium, at least with a setup similar to the one described in 
Chapter 4. This is because the incident and the reflected beam coincide and it is challenging to 
introduce an optical component in their common path so that the reflected beam is detected 
without hindering the incident one. Also, the optical components may also exhibit a reflection 
themselves and ruin a high precision measurement. The following results are summarized in 
plots that include both reflectance and transmittance spectra.

6 Conclusions 
6.1 Summary 
The key pedagogical and research goals of this project were accomplished. Nanofabrication 
methods were introduced, used and mastered in a productive way. The concept of GMR and 
SWG was addressed and the typical effects of wavelength and polarization dependence 
were observed for the intermediate regime between shallow gratings and HCGs. The incident 
beam’s size effect was experimentally explored and the grating area was achieved to be 
increased from 50 μm2 to 400 μm2. In addition, TE reflective gratings were realized and 
measured on rather than only TM ones. The group’s former experimental results were verified 
with a similar 50 μm2 TM reflective grating (Figure  43) and new records were achieved with 
larger TM and TE reflective gratings (Figure  44), which were the goals of this project.

6.2 Outlook 
The results of this project have molded a very promising and broad agenda of the group’s 
future plans. As far as the fabrication process has been optimized and the reflective behavior 
well understood from a first approach, new standards can be set. A more precise measurement 
of the reflectance will be attempted with an optical cavity setup consisting of a very high 
reflection mirror, at its one end, and the inspected grating, at the other side. The optical 
quality factor will be deduced assuming the grating the only side responsible for losses. 
Afterwards, loss mechanisms limiting the reflection will be investigated and hopefully 
overcome. Of course, the optical response of the gratings is already satisfying for some 
applications but for Optomechanics, which is the group’s research field, it is also a necessity for 
the samples to maintain their initial high mechanical quality factor, which also needs to be high 
enough for sensing applications. Other research directions to be followed by the group are the 
grating fabrication on even thinner membranes (100nm) with lower mass and different 
mechanical properties. Polarization independency combined with high reflectance and high 
mechanical quality factor may also prove interesting for sensing applications.
