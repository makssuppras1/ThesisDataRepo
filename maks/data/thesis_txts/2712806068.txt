Abstract  
The building sector globally accounts for 39 % of global energy related GHG emissions and 11 % 
relates to the energy used to produce construction materials. The most used LCA method for 
assessing the potential environmental impacts of a product system is the attributional modelling 
method, but when it comes to the building sector, we often want to assess the environmental 
consequences of building something in the future – and therefor, the consequential modelling 
framework should be used more widely.  
This study primarily seeks to investigate the consequential modelling method, and in relation to this, 
compare it to the attributional modelling method. More specific, it aims to investigate how the 
choice of marginal suppliers affect the outcome of a consequential LCA and how these results are 
compared to results of an attributional LCA.  
In order to do this, scenario modelling is used to compare different scenarios of defining marginal 
suppliers. Cross laminated timber is used as a case study for modelling the different scenarios.  
The main finding of the study is that both the choice of modelling framework in general, as well as 
the choice of marginal suppliers, does have a significant effect on the outcome of the LCA, and 
therefore it is not possible to unambiguously answer the question: ‘’what are the environmental 
impacts of product x?’’ – it all depends on the choices made when executing the LCA.  
Based on the findings, the study at last discusses the strengths and weaknesses of the consequential 
modelling method compared to the attributional modelling method.

1. Introduction and goal of the study 
1.1 Introduction and intended application  
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is the quantification of potential impacts and the resource use 
throughout a product’s life cycle: from raw material, production, use and waste management. How 
to do this has been discussed widely for the past 30 years, and it has been clear from the start, that 
results from different LCA’s can contradict from each other despite many attempts to harmonize, 
regulate and standardize LCA (Ekvall, 2019).  
This study aims to investigate the different LCA approaches, with primary focus on consequential 
LCA. The study seeks to investigate consequential LCA as a method; the role of marginal suppliers, 
their significance for the final result of the LCA, and how the consequential LCA approach compares 
to the attributional LCA approach.  
In order to investigate this, a case study is used for making different scenarios of the definition of 
the marginal suppliers. The selection of the case study is made on the basis that the outcome of the 
study should be relevant for the building sector in Denmark. The building sector (globally) account 
for 39 % of global energy related GHG emissions and 11 % relates to the energy used to produce 
construction materials (2019 Global Status Report for Buildings and Construction, 2019). Increasing 
the use of wood in construction is a proposed upcoming strategy to reduce the embodied GHG 
emissions of buildings (Andersen et al., 2021). Following this strategy will result in an increase in the 
use of wood for construction in the future compared to how it is today. In continuation of this, the 
case study used in this project is cross laminated timber (CLT).  
Different scenarios for defining marginal suppliers in the CLCA will be made, and the intention of 
the study is to investigate what impact this will have on the final result and conclusions of the 
analysis. At last, these results will be compared to results of an ALCA, highlighting the difference in, 
and importance of, choice of modelling framework when doing LCAs.

1.2 Questions of the analysis  
Understanding the different aspects of CLCA (compared to ALCA) is one of the main aspects of this 
study, as well as (most importantly) investigating the importance of modelling choices for the final 
result and conclusion of the LCA. This leads to the problem statement of the study:  
 
How does the choice of marginal suppliers affect the outcome of a consequential LCA and how are 
these results compared to results of an attributional LCA? 
 
Within this problem statement are different questions of interest, which this study aims to 
investigate:  
• What are the characteristics of CLCA and what methods are used in CLCA?  
• How are the marginal suppliers defined in CLCA?  
• How does the choice of marginal suppliers affect the outcome of a CLCA?  
• What is the difference of a CLCA and an ALCA and how much does the choice of LCA 
modelling framework affect the final outcome/decision?  
 
The following section briefly describes the theory of both ALCA and CLCA. The theory presented is 
primarily based on literature of Weidema et al. (2009), Ekvall (2019) and Hauschild, Rosenbaum and 
Olsen (2017).  For further readings, refer to the aforementioned literature. The section also includes 
a brief description of indirect land use change (iLUC). 
1.3 Attributional and consequential LCA 
When performing an LCA, two main modelling frameworks exists: attributional life cycle assessment 
(ALCA) and consequential life cycle assessment (CLCA). The object of study and overall goal with the 
LCA may be the same in both ALCA and CLCA; to assess the potential environmental impacts of a 
specific object of choice – but the way in which the study is approached is different. What mainly 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
differentiate ALCA and CLCA is the choice of processes to be included in the system and how to 
identify these processes.  
In ALCA it is estimated what share of the global environmental burdens belongs to a product, 
whereas a CLCA gives an estimate of how the global environmental burdens are affected by the 
production and use of the product. What input data to use and how to deal with multifunctional 
processes are different in ALCA and CLCA; where an ALCA is based on the use of average input data 
and handles multifunctional processes preferable by allocation, CLCA ideally uses marginal data and 
handles multifunctional processes by system expansion (Ekvall, 2019). This means that the 
background system is modelled differently in ALCA and CLCA, but that the foreground system is 
overall modelled in the same way, except for the handling of multifunctional processes (Hauschild, 
Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017).  
There are several definitions to both ALCA and CLCA. In the following sections, some of the most 
used definitions will be presented, as well as a briefly overview of the methods.  
1.3.1 Attributional LCA 
As stated above, there are several definitions of ALCA. A widely used definition is of Finnverden et 
al.,:  
‘’Attributional LCA: LCA aiming to describe the environmental relevant physical flow to and from a 
life cycle and its subsystem’’ (Heijungs et al., 2009).  
In the literature Life Cycle Assessment – Theory and Practice (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 
2017), the aim of an attributional LCA is described as:  
‘’The overall aim of attributional modelling is to represent a product system in isolation from the rest 
of the technosphere or economy’’ and that the question to be addressed by ALCA is “what 
environmental impact can be attributed to product X?” or “what environmental impact is product X 
responsible for?” (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017) 
Overall, it can be said that ALCA gives an estimate of how much of the global environmental impact 
belongs to the product studied (Ekvall, 2019).  
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
An ALCA is based on average input data in the product life cycle. It is preferred to use as specific 
(average) data as possible, e.g., average environmental performance of a supplier. Though in many 
cases the supplier is unknown (e.g., the product is not yet being produced or data is unavailable), 
and in this case, average data for the relevant geographical area is to be used (Ekvall, 2019).  
When it comes to the handling of multifunctional processes, ALCA traditionally uses an allocation 
principle; estimating what share of the burdens of the multifunctional process that belongs to the 
product investigated (and also what share of input materials, energy, etc.), based on an allocation 
key – e.g. the price (Ekvall, 2019). Though it should be mentioned, that in some cases, ILCD 
recommends solving multifunctionality in ALCA by system expansion – as also used in CLCA 
(Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017).  
1.3.2 Consequential LCA 
As well as for the ALCA, there are several definitions of CLCA. Finnverden et al., defines CLCA as:  
‘’Consequential LCA: LCA aiming to describe how environmentally relevant flows will change in 
response to possible decisions’’ (Heijungs et al., 2009).  
Curran et al. (2005) defines CLCA as ‘’the consequential approach to life cycle inventory attempts to 
estimate how flows to and from the environment will change as a result of different potential 
decisions’’. On the same line, Weidema and Ekvall (2004) describes CLCA as ‘’describing how the 
environmental relevant physical flows to and from the technological system will change in response 
to possible changes in life cycle’’ (Zamagni et al., 2012).  
Overall, it can be said that CLCA gives an estimate of how the global environmental impact is 
affected by the product being produced and used. This means that a CLCA can be used to estimate 
the consequences of a given decision (Ekvall, 2019). 
As for the input data in CLCA, marginal data (marginal processes) are used. Here the input data 
should reflect how the environmental burdens of the process change as a result of a change in the 
total functional output (Ekvall, 2019). This means that the marginal processes are the processes that 
are employed or taken out of use as a response to an increase or decrease in the demand for a 
product (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017).  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
The effects on the marginal processes can either be short-term or long-term. Short-term marginal 
effects are effects on the utilization of existing production capacity (before production capacity has 
been able to adapt to a change in demand), meaning that it does not affect the determining 
parameters of the overall market situation. Long-term marginal effects on the other hand are 
affecting the overall market situation, and here the production capacity is assumed to completely 
adapt to the change in demand, e.g. by bringing new technology into play (Weidema, Ekvall and 
Heijungs, 2009; Ekvall, 2019).  
Likewise, the scale of the studied decision can be small or large. A decision is defined as small or 
marginal when it does not affect the determining parameters of the overall market situation (e.g., 
trend in market volume), and defined as large, when it does affect the overall market situation, 
again by bringing e.g., new suppliers, markets, or technologies into play (Weidema, Ekvall and 
Heijungs, 2009).  
Another important aspect of CLCA is how to quantify the total functional output of the 
multifunctional processes and how to allocate the environmental burdens. In terms of estimating 
how the different flows of the processes are affected, when the output of products and functions 
for use in other life cycles are affected, system expansion should be used in CLCA. This means that 
the system boundary should be expanded to include the processes that are affected by this change 
in flows (Ekvall, 2019).    
1.3.2.1 Guidelines on CLCA 
There are many different ways to approach a CLCA, especially regarding how to define and which 
marginal data to use as well as how to handle multifunctional processes.  
One of the main objectives of CLCA is to identify which production technology that is affected by a 
marginal change in demand – in other words, identify the unit processes that change as a 
consequence of a decision. Weidema et al. has developed a five-step procedure to do this 
(Weidema, 2003). The procedure consists of five steps as seen in Figure 1. The procedure is being 
further elaborated in Section 2, when it is applied to this specific study.

1. Identifying the scale and time horizon of the studied change 
2. Market delimitation  
3. Identifying the market trend  
4. Identifying production constrains  
5. Identifying the suppliers/technologies most sensitive to change

Weidema (2003) five-step procedure has later on been developed and expanded. This study will 
mainly follow the guidelines and procedures as described in ‘’Guidelines for application of deepened 
and broadened LCA’’ (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009), which provides step-by-step procedures 
based on Weidema (2003) five-step procedure, slightly modified and expanded.  
The procedures for identify the unit processes that change as a consequence of a decision (the 
marginal suppliers/affected technology) are very similar to the above described five-step procedure 
and has four steps: (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009) 
1. Identifying the scale and time horizon of the potential change studied 
2. Identifying the limits of a market  
3. Identifying trends in the volume of a market  
4. Identifying changes in supply and demand  
In the guideline, default assumptions for each step are also presented for those cases where 
accurate information is not available.  
Besides a step-by-step procedure of how to identify the affected unit processes, the guideline also 
provides information on how to deal with unit processes (or product systems) with multiple 
products and how the functional unit and reference flows should be defined. These guidelines will 
be used throughout the CLCA in this study and will be further elaborated in Section 2.  
1.3.3  iLUC effects (indirect Land Use Changes) 
An LCA can be seen as a consumption-based assessment which includes all emissions as a result of 
consumption - including land use (Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021). Approximately 9% of global 
carbon emissions in 2011 originated from land use changes, but often they are not included in LCA, 
as they are not well understood, and the existing models of how to calculate them are associated 
with uncertainty (Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 2015).  
There are two types of land use changes: direct land use change (dLUC) and indirect land use change 
(iLUC), which are both caused by the use or occupation of land (Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 
2015).  
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
Direct land use changes include the emissions that are directly linked to the changes that occur e.g., 
when a forest is cleared for agricultural land. Indirect land use changes include the emissions that 
are linked to the land change that follows indirectly from the utilization of an area – e.g. when an 
agricultural area is converted from the cultivation of food crops to forest area, the food crops will 
have to be produced somewhere else, because the global demands for food is assumed to be 
unchanged (Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021).  
In practice, dLUC emissions are more often included in consumption-based assessments (LCAs) than 
iLUC emissions. According to European PEF method, assessments of products' environmental 
footprint must include dLUC effects, but not iLUC effects. Despite this, more experts still prefer to 
include the iLUC effects, despite uncertainty, in order to avoid an underestimation of the overall 
climate effects (Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021).  
There exists no common guidelines or frameworks of how to calculate iLUC effects (which is why 
they are often excluded from LCAs), but several models have been developed on how to calculate 
them.  
This study uses a method developed by 2.-0 LCA consultants for modeling iLUC effects in life cycle 
assessments. In this method, all land-intensive activities are assumed to draw on a global market 
for land, which is associated with greenhouse gas emissions. The method follows a consequential 
approach, with the aim of assessing the size of iLUC emissions if the Danish consumption changes 
(Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021). The method can be broken down into three steps: 
(Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021) 
1. Assessment of the areas (in hectares * years) which are utilized in production in all countries 
to be able to meet the Danish consumption. (This is typically done by the use of IO-tables, 
but in this study, openLCA will be used to estimate this area).  
2. Conversion of areas to productivity-weighted areas (in hectares * year-equivalents) 
3. Multiplication of productivity-weighted areas with given emission factors  
The calculation of iLUC effects is typically related to a consequential modelling approach, as it 
focuses on illuminating effects as a result of a decision or change (Energistyrrelsen and Niras, 2021). 
In this study, iLUC effects will be calculated in both attributional and consequential scenarios. This 
aspect will be discussed further in Section 5 of the report.

1.4 When to use which modelling framework 
The methodology within LCA is diverse, and the interpretation of key concepts, e.g. when to use 
which modelling framework, varies between researchers (Ekvall, 2019).  
In both ALCA and CLCA the overall goal is to assess the potential environmental impacts for a 
product/system throughout its lifecycle, but ALCA and CLCA respond to different questions. CLCAs 
consider future impacts that may happen due to a change in demand, whereas ALCAs consider 
impacts that has happened and can be attributed to the product under study (Andersen et al., 2021).  
International guidelines on when to use which method has been developed, e.g. the ILCD guidelines, 
but despite this, aspects of the terminology and when to use which method are inconsistent with 
the views within the LCA community (Ekvall et al., 2016; Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017). 
In the ILCD handbook, ALCA and CLCA are matched to four ‘’decision contexts1’’ (i.e., A, B, C1 and 
C2), to make it easier to navigate on which modelling framework to use in which situation. The 
guidelines recommend ALCA for decision contexts A, C1 and C2 and only recommends a mix 
between ALCA and CLCA for decision context B, meaning that, according to the guidelines, ALCA 
should be the preferred modelling framework in almost every situation.  
This has been discussed widely among LCA community (e.g. Ekvall, Finnverden, Weidema, Zamagni, 
Rydberg, Azapagic), arguing that ‘’the ILCD guidelines are internally inconsistent when it comes to 
recommendations on how to choose between attributional and consequential modelling’’ (Ekvall et 
al., 2016). They argue that consequential modelling should be used for both decision context A and 
B, because these decision contexts are used for decision-support and thereby the LCI model should 
reflect the consequences of a decision (Ekvall et al., 2016).  
As it can be stated from the above, understanding when to use which modelling framework has 
always been, and still is, one of the most difficult aspects of LCA, and there is still no consensus on 
when to use which method within the LCA community (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017).   .  
This topic is being further elaborated in the discussion Section 5.

2. Scope definition  
2.1 The different scenarios 
As the aim of the study is to identify how the choice of marginal suppliers in a CLCA affect the 
outcome of the analysis, and how these results compare to an ALCA, 16 different scenarios of 
defining the marginal suppliers in CLCA have been made as well as two different ALCA scenarios.  
As mentioned in Section 1.3, one of the key issues of CLCA is identifying marginal suppliers. In order 
to do this, Weidema’s five-step procedure (later developed to a four-step procedure) has been used. 
Even though following this method, the identified marginal supplier(s) at the end can be very 
different, depending on the choices made by the LCA practitioner along the way. As the aim of this 
study is to identify how the choice of marginal suppliers in CLCA affect the outcome of the analysis, 
the procedure has been used several times, each time making different (realistic) choices along the 
way.  
The first step of the procedure is to identify if the scale of the change is small- or large scale, and if 
the time horizon is short- or long term. The scenarios in this study include both small- and large 
scale changes as well as short- and long term time horizons, in order to cover the potential choices 
made later in the process as broadly as possible.  
The choice of scale and time horizon then affects the choices made in step 2 of the procedure; the 
market delimitation, where the potential suppliers/technologies must be identified in terms of 
those who (can be expected to) deliver to this market (Weidema, 2003). 
In the process of identifying possible affected suppliers (when looking at CLT used for construction 
in Denmark within the system boundary of the study - see section 2.2), this study mainly focusses 
on two different aspects: where the CLT is produced and what species of wood is used for 
production. The production location then affects the technologies used for production of CLT, in this 
case mainly electricity uses for production and transport distances.  
The third step is to identify the market trend, and in all seven scenarios, the market trend is 
identified as increasing. This because the objective of the LCA is to analyse the effects of an 
increased demand for CLT used for construction in Denmark.  
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
The fourth step of the procedure is to identify production constrains. In this study, the assumption 
is that there are no production constrains. This assumption is being further discussed in Section 5.  
The fifth and last step of the procedure is then to identify the suppliers/technologies most sensitive 
to change (the marginal suppliers). This again depends on the time horizon, scale of change as well 
as the market trend. Weidema concludes that for long-term changes in an increasing market, the 
most sensitive supplier/technology is identical to the most competitive, and if data cannot be 
obtained, it may be assumed that modern technology is the most competitive (Weidema, 2003).  
2.1.2 The identification of marginal suppliers  
As mentioned in step 3, the identified possible affected/marginal suppliers in this study is first and 
foremost dependent on the production location, affecting mainly the source of electricity used for 
production of CLT and transport distances, as well as what species of wood that is used for the 
production of CLT.  
The production location of CLT in this study is assumed to be either Germany or Sweden. This choice 
is made on the basis that these two countries are among the biggest CLT producing countries in 
Europe (CBI, 2017), and therefore two realistic potential suppliers of CLT used for construction in 
Denmark. Transport distances is then calculated from the biggest producers of CLT in Germany and 
Sweden to Copenhagen (see Section 3.2).  
From market information provided by CBI Ministry of Foreign Affairs, CLT is usually constructed from 
softwood species such as spruce, although it can be manufactured from larch, fir, douglas fir, pine 
or other timbers (CBI, 2017). On the basis of this, as well as data available in the ecoinvent database, 
the different scenarios in this study includes CLT made from spruce and pine. The rationale is, that 
if the scale is small, only spruce is used for CLT production, but if the scale is large, both spruce and 
pine is necessary to use in order to keep up with production.  
The production location of the CLT also affects the source of the electricity used for production. 
Electricity production/consumption is an important part of LCA, as this often has an important 
influence on the end result. Modelling marginal electricity in CLCA has been discussed widely, as this 
aspect is difficult to forecast into the future. This requires knowledge about local production 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
capacity as well as an understanding of energy regulation and politics affecting the current and 
future electricity production, both regional and locally (Muñoz and Weidema, 2021).   
As CLCA studies the consequences of a change in demand for a product or service, only marginal 
suppliers are considered in the system (Muñoz and Weidema, 2021). In CLCA, the electricity data 
included represent the technology which is most likely to respond to a change in demand, typically 
coal or natural gas (Lund, Mathiesen and Christensen, 2010). Even though this has been the general 
perception when modelling marginal electricity supply, the last few years rapid development of 
renewable energy sources is also taken into account in this study.  
In order to identify marginal suppliers for electricity production in this study, the information used 
is based on previous studies by Weidema (Weidema, 2003) and Lund et al. (Lund, Mathiesen and 
Christensen, 2010), as well as more recent information from Energistyrrelsen (Energistyrelsen, 
2014) (Energistyrelsen, 2021), an agreement between governing parties in Sweden 
(Regeringskansliet, 2016) and a recent example of marginal electricity in Denmark by Muñoz and 
Weidema and (Muñoz and Weidema, 2021) which also includes information on marginal electricity 
production in Sweden and Germany.  
In his study from 2003, Weidema concludes that the short term marginal supplier of electricity in 
both Central Europe (including Germany) and in Scandinavia (including Sweden) is coal, and that the 
long term marginal supplier of electricity is coal in Central Europe and natural gas in Scandinavia 
(Weidema, 2003). Lund agrees in his study from 2010 that the long term marginal supplier of 
electricity is natural gas, but he also advocates that the marginal supplier could be from wind or 
coal, and that sensitivity analysis are recommended  (Lund, Mathiesen and Christensen, 2010). In 
the more recent information used there is a general consensus that in the future, the main sources 
of electricity production in Sweden will be from nuclear power, hydro power, and wind, whereas 
natural gas and wind will be the main electricity sources in Germany. These sources of electricity 
will therefore be included in those scenarios with a long term time horizon.

For small scale scenarios, the amount of CLT in the functional unit is 1 m3, and for short time 
horizons, the period is 1 year. Both the scale and time horizon in this case is very small/short, in 
order to emphasize that short-term marginal effects are effects only on the utilization of existing 
capacity, meaning that it does not affect the determining parameters of the overall market situation 
(Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009; Ekvall, 2019). 
For large scale changes, the amount of CLT produced is 120.000 m3. The purpose is that this amount 
should represent the total amount of used CLT per year in Denmark. There is currently no data on 
CLT use in Denmark (Dagens Byggeri, 2021). Instead the amount is set based on the total import of 
wood for construction in Denmark (Rambøll, 2021), where it is assumed that CLT’s share is 10 % of 
this amount, based on a case study where CLT accounted for approximately 10 % of the used wood 
for construction of a one-family house (Rasmussen et al., 2020). For long term time horizons, the 
time period is 28 years corresponding a time period until 2050.  
For the large scale, short term scenarios, it is still important according to CLCA theory, that the 
changes does not affect the determining parameters of the overall market situation, e.g. no new 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
technology is necessary to keep up with this demand. In this case, even though the demand for CLT 
is big (120.000 m3), it is only for a period of 1 year, assuming that existing production capacity can 
keep up with this demand.  
On the other hand, for large scale, long term scenarios, the overall market situation is affected, 
meaning that new technology is brought into play (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009; Ekvall, 
2019). In this case, it is the technology for production of the CLT that is affected, more specifically 
the source of electricity used for production.  
For small scale, long term scenarios, it is not the scale of change that affects the overall market 
situation, but the time horizon being long term. In this scenario, 1 m3 CLT is produced each year for 
a time period of 28 years, meaning that the long term time horizon determines that new technology 
will be brought into play, again affecting the source of electricity used for production.  
Whereas the time horizon affects the source of electricity used for production of CLT, the scale of 
the change affects the type of wood used for production of CLT. For small scale scenarios, it is 
assumed that only spruce is used for production of CLT. For large scale scenarios, both spruce and 
pine are used for production of CLT, in order to keep up with the demand.  
2.3 LCIA method 
To assess the potential environmental impacts of the product systems, the steps included in ISO 
standard 14040/14044 (DS Dansk Standard, 2009) is followed, including selection of impact 
categories, classification and characterization. In practice, these steps are performed by the used 
software openLCA. In addition, normalization of the results will be used.  
In the selection of impact categories, it is decided which potential environmental impact categories 
that should be assessed in the LCA. In the classification step, the life cycle results are assigned the 
relevant impact categories based on their potential contribution in the relevant category. Then 
characterization factors are calculated on the basis of how much each individual emission / flow 
contributes to the relevant impact categories. This means that the characterized results states how 
much the analysed product system contributes to the selected impact categories. By using 
normalization, the results are compared with a reference in order to easier understand the size of 
the results. (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017).  
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
In this study, ReCiPe 2016 is the used LCIA method to evaluate the potential environmental impacts 
of the product systems. The ReCiPe 2016 LCIA method includes both midpoint and endpoint results. 
The midpoint characterization factors include information about the size of the potential 
environmental impacts within the midpoint impacts categories, but not what impact/effect this has 
on the surrounding environment. This is what endpoint characterization factors does, as they 
correspond to the impact within the three endpoint areas of protection: Damage to human health, 
Damage to ecosystems and Damage to resource availability (Huijbregts et al., 2016).  
In general, the midpoint characterization factors are associated with a relative low uncertainty, as 
they have a closer connection to the directly emitted emissions, whereas the endpoint 
characterization factors provide better information about their environmental significance within 
the relevant area - however associated with a greater uncertainty (Huijbregts et al., 2016). 
Figure 3 shows the relation between midpoint and endpoint impact categories in ReCiPe 2016 
(Huijbregts et al., 2017):  
 
Figure 3: Overview of the impact categories that are covered in the ReCiPe2016 methodology and their relation to the areas of

The endpoint categories are given in three different units (Huijbregts et al., 2016):  
• DALY (disability adjusted life years), related to damage to human health, representing the 
years that are lost or that a person is disabled due to disease or accident  
• species.yr (species year), related to damage to ecosystems, representing the local species 
loss integrated over time  
• USD2013 (US Dollar 2013), related to damage to resource availability, representing the costs 
involved for future mineral and fossil resource extraction 
Normalization of the results will also be used, converting each impact category to the unit person 
equivalent (PE), expressing how big an impact the relevant impact category has relative to an 
average person’s yearly impact within the category.

4. Life Cycle Impact Assessment 
4.1 The structure of the analysis   
The analysis is divided into 4 different parts, all with the aim of answering the overall problem 
statement of the study: How does the choice of marginal suppliers affect the outcome of a 
consequential LCA and how are these results compared to results of an attributional LCA?  
This means that the intention of the analysis is not to identify the actual environmental impacts of 
the 16 scenarios, but rather to explore the deviation of the environmental impacts between the 
different scenarios and why these deviations occur.  
First general endpoint results are presented, in order to identify the overall environmental impacts 
within the three areas of protection, as well as identify the overall deviation of the 16 scenarios.  
Then characterized midpoint results are presented, in order to identify the environmental impacts 
of the scenarios within the different impact categories. The results are presented in a heat map to 
easier visualise higher and lower values, and thereby make it easier to get an overview of the 
variation between the scenarios. In this section, the percental deviation of the environmental 
impacts within the different impact categories of the 16 scenarios is also presented in a table.  
In the next section, the Global Warming impact category is further analysed, as this impact category 
is both in great interest of society in general as well as being the impact category with the largest 
deviation between the 16 scenarios. Here, Global Warming impacts including iLUC effects are 
presented for each scenario, as well as the Global Warming impacts within each life cycle stage, in 
order to identify why environmental impacts within this impact category of the 16 scenarios varies 
as they do.  
Lastly, the results of the 16 scenarios are compared to an attributional LCA of the same product 
system, in order to identify the difference of a CLCA and an ALCA and how much does the choice of 
LCA modelling framework affects the final outcome/decision.

4.6 Results compared to attributional LCA 
To get an even better insight into the importance of the choice of marginal suppliers and the effect 
of this on the final result of an LCA, an attributional LCA was made in order to compare the two 
modelling frameworks (CLCA vs ALCA) to each other.  
The attributional LCA was made with the exact same product system as the CLCA, with only a few 
modifications (in relation to the attributional modelling framework): 
• Two scenarios are calculated: one located in Germany, and one located in Sweden 
• The electricity process used is an average electricity mix for each country  
• The type of wood used is 100 % spruce in both scenarios, as this is the type of wood mostly 
used for CLT production today 
• All ecoinvent processes used are now from the attributional ecoinvent database (and not 
the consequential database as used for the previous 16 scenarios) – but the chosen 
processes are the same as well as the inventory setup (see Appendix A) 
As for the consequential LCA, first the general endpoint results are presented, followed by a more 
in depth analysis of the Global Warming impact category.

4.7 Overall conclusions of the analysis 
• When looking at the overall endpoint results, the environmental impacts within each area of 
protection varies between 58 % and 114 %, meaning that the definition of marginal suppliers 
has a big influence on the final result regardless the endpoint area of protection. The trend of 
which marginal supplier that has the highest impact on the final result varies within each 
endpoint area of protection. Where in Human Health and Resource Availability the marginal 
supplier with the highest impact on the final result is the source of electricity, it is the production 
location that has the highest impact on the final result in ecosystems area of protection. Here, 
the type of wood used also has a remarkable influence of the final result, which is not the case 
in Human Health and Resource availability.  
 
• The characterized midpoint results, related to the endpoint results, also showed a large variation 
within the 16 scenarios of the final results, ranging from 41.7 % to 226.5 %, with an average 
deviation of 113.7 % within the potential environmental impacts for the 16 scenarios. The results 
indicated that, for most impact categories, the marginal supplier that has the biggest significance 
for the final potential environmental impacts is the source of energy used, though with a few 
exceptions – e.g., for the land use impact category, where the production location is of most 
significance for the final result. For the global warming impact category, it is also seen that iLUC 
effects makes up a significant part of the total environmental impacts within this impact 
category, especially in cases where renewable energy sources are used for electricity 
production.  
 
• The comparison between the attributional and consequential scenarios generally shows the 
same tendency for the different aspects of the analysis – that the source of electricity production 
is the most important factor when it comes to the endpoint area of protection Human Health 
and Resource Availability, as well as the environmental impact category Global Warming. But 
when looking at iLUC effects and the Ecosystem area of protection, it is the production location 
that is of most significance. Looking at the attributional scenarios in general to the consequential 
scenarios, there is a significant difference in the results, meaning that the choice of modelling 
framework does has a big influence on the outcome of the LCA.

5. Discussion  
In this section, the discussion is split up into two different parts: discussion of methods (CLCA and 
ALCA) and discussion of results. As the primary focus of this study is CLCA as a method, the main 
focus of the discussion will be within this topic.  
5.1 Discussion of methods  
5.1.1 Consequential LCA vs attributional LCA 
As also stated in Section 1, the overall purpose of CLCA can be described as aiming to assess the 
environmental consequences of a given decision, e.g. what will the environmental consequences be 
of deciding to increase the use of CLT for construction in Denmark. Different from the attributional 
approach, it doesn’t separate the studied system from the rest of the market or economy, and by 
this, CLCA includes lot of the physical and economic causalities that are related to the life cycle of a 
product – which are not included in the attributional approach.  
When making an LCA, the purpose is most often in some way to use the results for decisions to be 
made in the future, and if this is the case, the consequential approach should be the method of 
choice. Furthermore, the consequential approach uses specific data, and not average data as the 
attributional approach, increasing the reliability of the results specific to the studied system.  
But why is the attributional approach then still the most commonly used one? 
5.1.2 When to use which modelling framework  
There are more aspects of this question. First of all, as also mentioned in Section 1, understanding 
when to use which modelling framework has always been, and still is, one of the most difficult 
aspects of LCA, and there is still no consensus on when to use which method within the LCA 
community (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2017). More guidelines exist on when and why to 
choose between CLCA and ALCA – one of the most used ones is the ILCD handbook, recommending 
the attributional modelling framework for almost every context. Many LCA practitioners follow this 
guideline, resulting in the attributional modelling framework as being the most commonly used one.  
Overall it should be mentioned, that there is not one clear answer to this question, as no method is 
‘’better’’ than the other. First of all, ALCA and CLCA responds to two different questions: what part 
==PAGE:55==
54 
 
of the global environmental impacts is associated with the product investigated, and how does the 
product affect the global environmental impacts? (Ekvall, 2019). In the paper ‘’Attributional and 
Consequential Life Cycle Assessment’’ (2019), Ekvall discusses the choice between ALCA and CLCA 
using 5 different criteria: feasibility, accuracy, comprehensibility, inspiring and robustness. Overall 
he argues, that the CLCA method is more accurate, but that the ALCA method has advantages when 
it comes to all of the other criteria. ALCA is both more feasible and comprehensible, as it is based 
on average data, which are easier to obtain, and because of the system model often having a simpler 
structure than for CLCA. The feasibility and accuracy aspects are discussed further in the next 
section.  
Ekvall also argues that the ALCA method is more well-established than CLCA; one of the reasons 
behind this being that environmental product declarations (EPDs) are based on ALCA, with very 
specific guidelines specifying the method. He also argues that ALCA results are somewhat less 
sensitive to subjective choices than CLCA, as CLCA is often based on a lot of assumptions – all of 
these aspects resulting in the ALCA being more robust as a method. On the other hand, he points 
out, that ALCA results can easily be abused, as they are sometimes presented as a basis for a decision 
– which they cannot be used for. If one want to use LCA as a basis for a decisions, as a consequence 
of the decision on the environment, CLCA should be the used method (Ekvall, 2019).  
5.1.3 Discussion of consequential LCA as method  
Another aspect is the consequential method itself. Ekvall argues, that for a method to be used, it 
first and foremost must be possible to apply. And that for a method to benefit the environment, the 
results needs to be reasonably accurate, possible to communicate, and perceived as relevant by 
decision-makers (Ekvall, 2019). Furthermore he argues that no method is ideal from all aspects, as 
there will always be a trade-off between e.g. feasibility and accuracy (Ekvall, 2019).  
This can be directly transferred to the consequential LCA method, and give an insight to, why it is 
not as commonly used as ALCA. As stated before, the CLCA method may be more accurate than the 
ALCA, as it uses specific (and not average) data, as well as CLCA including physical and economic 
causalities that are related to the life cycle of a product. But because of this, the method may also 
be less feasible than the ALCA. It requires more time and effort to gather these information, as the 
LCA practitioner cannot just use already existing average data found in a database.  
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
More efforts have been made by CLCA experts to make the method more feasible. One of these is 
the five-step procedure developed by Weidema, as also used in this study. The overall point of the 
procedure is that is points out a single technology where the marginal effect occurs, in order to 
make the CLCA approach more feasible and comprehensible (Ekvall, 2019).  
On the other hand this, according to Ekvall, simplifies the assumptions in terms of: ‘’the relevant 
effects are either short-term or long-term rather than both, that markets and market segments can 
be clearly distinguished and do not affect each other, that the production volume of a technology is 
either completely flexible or not at all flexible, and that decisions are based solely on economic 
rationality’’ (Ekvall, 2019), which makes the results of the CLCA less accurate. Ekvall describes the 
five-step procedure as ‘’a structured way to arrive at an assumption of the marginal effects’’ and 
argues that, besides making the results less accurate, pure assumptions also results in the study 
becoming less credible, reducing the likelihood that the results will inspire to decisions and actions 
(Ekvall, 2019).  
Another important aspect of the feasibility of CLCA vs ALCA is the development of databases. As the 
ALCA modelling is often the most used one, that attributional databases are also more developed 
than the consequential databases. The databases that exist for performing LCAs today usually 
includes average data (for ALCA) but few includes marginal data (for CLCA). The Ecoinvent 3 
database, as also used in this project, does include marginal data, but the marginal data are rough 
– which was also discovered during this project (refer to Section 4). By developing the consequential 
databases, the CLCA will also become easier and more feasible to carry out in the future (Ekvall, 
2019).  
5.2 Discussion of results and limitations of the study 
As also indicated in the results section (Section 4), there was a lot of difference in the results 
between the different CLCA results (as well as the consequential vs attributional results). This 
underlines the points from the section above (5.1) that the results of CLCA is very sensitive to the 
choice of marginal suppliers.  
A way to get around this issue could be to use scenario modelling to support CLCA – as also used in 
this project. These scenarios could e.g., be with respect to technology development or potential 
==PAGE:57==
56 
 
future market shifts. This is also the conclusions of the paper ‘’Lights and Shadows in Consequential 
LCA’’ (Zamagni et al., 2012), which states that ‘’CLCA is a sophisticated modelling technique that 
provides a way to assess the environmental con sequences of an action/decision by including market 
mechanisms into the analysis’’ (Zamagni et al., 2012), but that the method still has room for 
improvements. These especially in terms of clarifying when and which market information is 
important and necessary as well as understanding the role of scenario modelling within CLCA 
(Zamagni et al., 2012).  
In this study, iLUC effects were also calculated for both consequential and attributional scenarios. 
One can argue, that iLUC effects are only relevant for consequential modelling, as iLUC effects is the 
link between demand for crops in one region and impacts in other regions (Schmidt, Weidema and 
Brand, 2015) – meaning that the studied system is not separated from the rest of the market.  
As also stated in section 1, calculating of iLUC effects are not addressed in Life Cycle Assessment 
(LCA) because the causal link between land use and deforestation is not well understood and 
because there is a lack of consensus on how to establish this link (Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 
2015). Furthermore, many different models on how to calculate iLUC effects has been made 
(Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 2015), all providing different results. Examples of this is a model 
described in the paper ‘’A framework for modelling indirect land use changes in Life Cycle 
Assessment’’ (Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 2015), which is the method also followed in this study. 
They have developed a method for both consequential and attributional modelling, where ‘’in the 
consequential model, the inventoried system represents a change in demand for a defined unit of 
land use, whereas in the attributional model the system represents the average of all global land use 
effects’’ (Schmidt, Weidema and Brand, 2015).  
In this study, simplifications were made in order to make it possible to calculate iLUC effects within 
the extend of the study. For both consequential and attributional scenarios, the same method was 
used (as described in Section 1), and the same conversion factor of areas to productivity-weighted 
areas was used in all scenarios – the conversion factor relevant for Denmark – as this was the 
available data. This means that the results may vary a bit from the ‘’correct results’’ (according to 
the followed method), but taking into account that many different methods exist, providing very 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
different results, it is estimated that the calculations used can still be considered representative of 
the extent of iLUC effects in relation to not including them at all.  
As mentioned in Section 2.1, it is assumed that there are no production constrains, meaning full 
elasticity of supply. This assumption is made on the basis of the guidelines followed in this study to 
define the marginal suppliers (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009), where it is a default to assume 
no constrains, if no information is available about constrains. By doing this, unjustified exclusions of 
suppliers are avoided (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009). Within the extend of this study, it has 
not been possible to gather further information about possible constrains of the CLT market, and 
thereby this default assumption was made.  
Another limitation of the study is that it is assumed that there are no substitutions of other products, 
in this case, that the increased use of CLT doesn’t result in a decreased use of concrete, steel or 
other similar building materials. Furthermore, the product system of the study doesn’t include all 
life cycle stages as defined in standard EN 15978:2011 (DS Dansk Standard, 2012), but only life cycle 
stages A1, A2, A3, A4 and partly D. This entails that the results cannot be used as total potential 
environmental impacts of CLT, which is neither the aim of this study to define.

6. Overall conclusion of the study 
In this study it was investigated how the choice of marginal suppliers affect the outcome of a 
consequential LCA and how these results are compared to results of an attributional LCA. To do this, 
a case study was used in the form of cross laminated timber (CLT). 16 different consequential 
scenarios were made, as well as 2 different attributional scenarios. Weidema’s five step procedure 
(Weidema, 2003), later developed as four-step procedure (Weidema, Ekvall and Heijungs, 2009), 
was used to define the marginal suppliers in the consequential scenarios. The marginal suppliers in 
the used scenarios differed from each other in regards to production location, type of wood used 
for production and source of electricity used for production of CLT.  
ReCiPe 2016 was used as assessment method, and both endpoint and midpoint results were 
compared to each other. The analysis of both endpoint and midpoint results showed a big variation 
in results, ranging from a difference of 58 – 114% for endpoint results, and 42 – 227% for 
characterised midpoint results, with an average deviation of 114% within the potential 
environmental impacts of the 16 scenarios. In most of the impact categories, it was the source of 
electricity used for production that had the highest impact on the final result, but within some 
impact categories (e.g., land use), the production location and type of wood used for production 
had the highest impact on the final results. It was also seen that iLUC effects makes up a significant 
part of the total environmental impacts, especially in cases where renewable energy sources are 
used for electricity production. Looking at the attributional scenarios in general to the consequential 
scenarios, there is also a significant difference in the results. This means that the choice of marginal 
suppliers does have a big influence on the final outcome of the consequential LCA, as well as the 
chosen modelling framework.  
When it comes to which modelling framework to use in general, no method is ‘’more correct’’ than 
the other, as both methods have advantages and disadvantages associated with the method, and 
they fundamentally respond to different questions.  
But what is clear from the study is, that it is not possible to unambiguously answer the question: 
‘’what are the environmental impacts of product x?’’ – it all depends on the choices made when 
executing the LCA – which modelling framework that is chosen in general, and how the marginal 
suppliers are defined, as both of these factors has a big influence on the final outcome of the LCA.
