Abstract 
In Denmark, waste wood is collected by municipalities for recycling. Approximately 54% is recycled into 
particleboard and the remainder used as fuel for combined heat and power plants. A fraction of WW 
consists of wood impregnated with preservatives, and may contain creosote or CCA. The main goals of 
this study were to determine the level of contamination by impregnated wood in WW, to quantify the 
creosote (PAHs and phenols) and CCA (Cr, Cu and As) concentrations in WW, to determine their fate 
during recycling and incineration and identify any issues with these processes. A total of 19 WW 
categories were identified, sampled and analyzed for PAHs, phenolic compounds (GC-MS) and metals 
(ICP-MS/OES). The analytical results were weighted based on the composition of WW to achieve a 
representative total concentration for the WW. The particleboard industry follows limit values 
requirements for As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Pb, PCP and benzo(a)pyrene (creosote), none of which were exceeded 
by Danish manufactures, based on contributions of these substances from WW. Regulated compounds 
in creosote (those with airborne OELs) that were expected to volatilize during particleboard production 
(and that had detectable concentrations in WW), were converted to exceedance production rates (EPR), 
a measure which describes a concentration value in terms of the rate of production required to exceed 
the OEL, which can then be compared with actual production rates. There were no OEL exceedances 
from WW content in particleboard identified for the PAHs naphthalene and 2-methynaphthalene, or the 
phenols: phenol, 2-methylphenol, 3-methylphenol and 4-methylphenol. During incineration, all metals 
were assumed to be transferred to the fly and bottom ash (final concentration not measured) and 
creosote related compounds were assumed to oxidize fully (not measured). Data accuracy 
(assumptions) is the main source of uncertainty in this study, further investigations should be more 
targeted and measure selected substances more directly.

 Introduction 
Wood is a versatile building material and common fuel source around the world and as such, is one of 
the most important renewable resource for humans (Rivella et al., 2006).  The regenerative capacity of 
forests, along with reparability of products, successive recyclability and energy recovery from wood 
waste makes wood the only legitimately sustainable building material available (Reynolds and Suttie, 
2007).  Modern (and historical) preservatives increase the durability and utility of wood products 
(Reynolds and Suttie, 2007), and current technology can allow for up to 16 rounds of recycling (Kim and 
Song, 2014), which extends the lifetime of wood products and reduces the need for the harvesting of 
virgin timber. From a climate change (carbon accounting) perspective, tree growth-burning cycle is 
carbon neutral, thus recycling sequesters carbon (Kim and Song, 2014) and according to Morris (2016) 
there are also significant carbon emission offsets from harvest related activities (logging & transport).  
The life cycle of wood products is very complicated due to the wide diversity of uses and variable 
lifespans of individual products. Thorough life cycle assessment (LCA) of specific items or well-defined 
product categories are challenging and they are often to specific and of limited value to waste 
management assessment as a whole. Even complex studies on wood waste fail to include all relevant 
environmental burdens (Morris, 2016), and tend to focus on specific impact categories, such as 
greenhouse gases, disposal methods, recycling options etc., although sometimes in combination, but 
never all in the same investigation. A simpler alternative to LCA is materials flow analysis (MFA), which 
tracks specified substances through a product’s lifecycle (or portion of it), and can be used as the basis 
for more inclusive LCAs.  The focus of this study is therefore not LCA, but rather a MFA, which 
characterizes waste wood and predicts the fate of certain contaminants for recycling and incineration. 
1.1 Wood Preservation 
Dry wood (<25%MC) is naturally guarded from bacteria (Kim and Song, 2014) and selecting tree species 
for specific purposes can reduce the need for preservatives, but as it is a naturally biodegradable 
material, all exposed wood will eventually be subject to decay from insects, fungi or bacteria (Reynolds 
and Suttie, 2007). Wood preservation increases the utility of wood products (fx. to resist 
microorganisms, as a fire retardant, a water repellent, and acid/alkali resistance) and has a long history, 
the earliest methods dating back to 2000 B.C. (Nicholas, 1973). This study focuses on the more modern 
preservatives, namely creosote, pentachlorohenol (PCP) and chromated copper Arsenate (CCA), which, 
according to Meuller, Chapman and Pritchard (1989), were the most used pesticides globally prior to 
1990. Wood treated with the above preservatives will hereby be referred to as “impregnated” wood in 
this report. 
According to Danish Law, the definition of impregnated wood (Moe, 2002): 
“wood that [is] completely or partially treated with wood preservatives containing biologically 
active substances for the protection of wood against wood-destroying fungi and/or pests. It 
includes both pressure and vacuum impregnated wood and wood impregnated by surface 
[treatment] such as ironing, spraying and dipping. Wood [which is] only surface treated with 
regular paint and wood oils (linseed oils) are not covered.”

 Creosote 
From the 1890’s to the 1950’s, creosote was the dominant wood preservative used worldwide (Moe, 
2002), however in Denmark its use has been limited to railway ties, power poles and harbor 
construction (Ottosen and Christensen, 2004). Creosote is a complex mixture of chemical compounds 
and consists of about 85% polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) and 10% phenolic compounds 
(phenols) and 5% N-, S- and O- heterocyclic (NSO). Of the 150-200 chemical compound present in 
creosote, only about 40 of them comprise about 95% of the total mass (Meuller, Chapman and 
Pritchard, 1989). In Denmark and the EU, creosote is classified as hazardous (Ottosen and Christensen, 
2004) and materials with more than 0,1% total weight are considered carcinogenic (Moe, 2002). This led 
to the EU banning creosote for private use within member states in 2003 (Directive 2001/90/EC), 
however some industrial applications are still permitted (Reynolds and Suttie, 2007). This is reasonable 
considering that, according to Meuller, Chapman and Pritchard (1989), when used correctly, creosote 
poses no human health concern, and it is only the mis-use, spillage and disposal than can cause 
potential harm. Therefore creosote treated wood products are categorized and treated as impregnated 
wood waste in Denmark (see below). 
 Chromated Copper Arsenate (CCA) 
After its introduction in the 1930s, pressure treating timbers with CCA preservatives gained popularity 
due to its high durability (Edo et al., 2016) and from the 1950s was the dominant wood preservative 
used globally (Moe, 2002). The general chemical formula of CCA is CrCuAs2O9. However, there are 
actually three different CCA formulations (A,B and C) which utilize different ratios of the metal oxides: 
CuO, CrO3 and As2O5 (Hingston et al., 2001). In this study the general composition of with a 1:1:1 ratio 
(ie. CrCuAs2O9) will be used to represent CCA, as the precise use of the different formulations in 
Denmark is not known.  Due to its arsenic content (hazardous), CCA was restricted to only a few 
industrial applications (fx. aquatic timbers (Hingston et al., 2001) in Europe in 2004 (Directive 2003/2(EC) 
and further limited in 2006 when the use of chromium was also banned (Reynolds and Suttie, 2007). 
Denmark however was early in recognizing the adverse effects of CCA and banned Arsenic-containing 
preservatives in 1993 (Ottosen and Christensen, 2004) in order to hinder the production of hazardous 
wastes. Discarded CCA treated wood is categorized and treated as impregnated wood waste in 
Denmark.  
 Pentachlorophenol (PCP) 
Pentachlorophenol was a widely used wood preservative in Denmark until it was banned in 1977, which 
increased the lifetime of wood to a maximum 40 years (COWI, 2004).  The amount of PCP treated wood 
to Denmark is unknown, however a 2003 study identified no PCP in tropical woods from countries 
where the preservative is still in use (COWI, 2004). Considering the decades old ban (the likelihood that 
very little PCP treated wood is still in use, and the inability for visual separation at recycling centers, PCP 
will not be considered as a stand-alone preservative in this study. However, PCP is present in creosote 
and will be therefore be indirectly addressed. 
1.2 Wood Waste in Denmark 
Wood waste is a complex mixture of materials which has a generally high level of contamination and 
variability (Edo et al., 2016). Material contaminants like plastic, metal and stone can be separated with 
sieves and magnets, while chemical contaminants such as preservatives, paints and coatings cannot be 
removed mechanically (Edo et al., 2016).  As wood with creosote, arsenic or PCP are classified as 
==PAGE:8==
3 
 
“hazardous”, Denmark passed a law in 2001 prohibiting the re-use or burning of impregnated/treated 
wood, which required all waste collection centers to collect impregnated wood separately (Ottosen and 
Christensen, 2004), and is not permitted to be recycled. Initially, impregnated wood waste was disposed 
of in landfills (Moe, 2002), however, it is now exported to Germany (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016) for treatment. 
It is expected that a total 2,7 million tons of impregnated wood will be collected as waste in Denmark 
(Ottosen and Christensen, 2004), with an additional 1,3 million tons being burned privately or in small 
boiler operations. Wood generally has good calorific value and a low moisture content when compared 
to other biomass (Edo et al., 2016) and until 2009, non-treated wood waste was burned at combined 
heat and power (CHP) facilities to recover the energy. In 2009, the wood-based panel manufacturer 
Novopan A/S, began introducing waste wood into their particleboard products which currently contain 
approximately 53% recycled wood (Novopan, 2016). Waste wood that is not utilized in particleboard is 
sent for energy recovery. Due to the sequestration of carbon, entrapment of potential contaminants 
and despite the use of energy and adhesives, particleboard production is, not surprisingly, more 
favorable from an environmental point of view than landfilling or energy production (Kim and Song, 
2014, Morris, 2016 and Rivella et al., 2006).   
1.3 Recycling – Particleboard Production 
Fiberboard refers to a group of engineered wood materials made from wood chips and adhesives. The 
three general categories of fiberboard are particleboard (low-density), MDF (medium-density) and 
hardboard (high-density) (Wikipedia, 2017). PB is used mostly as a material for household and office 
furniture, cabinets, door component and flooring (Kim and Song, 2014). This study focuses only on 
particleboard (PB) as it is the only product relevant to waste wood recycling in Denmark.   
The production process for particleboard at Novopan is as follows (Novopan A/S (2016). The 
manufacture of PB begins with virgin wood, industry waste and wood waste collected at recycling 
centers throughout Denmark. The wood is chipped, blended, dried and sieved to achieve a 
homogeneous mixture with the appropriate moisture content and particle size. Adhesive is then applied 
and the mixture is sent through a preliminary press to remove air. The board is then passed through a 
hot press at a pressure of 50 bar and a temperature of 200°C. The high pressure and temperature 
activates the glue, which gives the board its strength. After a cooling period, the boards are sanded to a 
precise thickness and sent to storage. Novopan A/S is the only PB manufacturer in Denmark to utilize 
waste wood, and they divert about 220,000 tons from Danish communities annually (Novopan, 2016). 
Although the particleboard manufacturing process requires energy and chemical inputs, several studies 
show that the incorporation of recycled wood waste makes the practice favorable to landfilling or 
incineration from an environmental perspective (Morris, 2016, Kim and Song, 2104 and Rivella et al., 
2006). However, the available literature does not address the fate of specific contaminants (ie. wood 
preservatives) in PB production/products from recycled material. As this is a material not meant for 
ingestion or internal contact, the restrictions on some of the contaminants in the above-mentioned 
preservatives are fairly loose or non-existent, but still may pose concern. Another concern that is 
unaddressed is the potential for workplace exposure (above allowable limits) to hazardous vapors that 
may be released during the “hot press” phase of production. This study investigates the potential hazard 
introduced to PB products and production staff by contaminated wood waste. It is important to note 
that exposure to contaminated dust during chipping and moving of material may also be a concern, but 
is not explored in this report.

 Objectives 
The overall objective of this study is to determine the fate of regulated wood preservatives in waste 
wood collected for recycling in Denmark. The focus is on the presence of wood impregnated with 
creosote or chromated copper arsenate (CCA) in the waste stream. The specific objectives of this study 
are as follows:  
1. To determine the composition of waste wood collected for recycling, including the contribution 
from impregnated wood. 
2. To quantify the creosote and CCA content of waste wood collected for recycling. Creosote 
content is determined by measuring the PAH and phenols in wood, while CCA is determined 
through elemental analysis of chromium, copper and arsenic, from which the potential CCA load 
is estimated.  
3. To quantify the creosote, CCA and other regulated metals in particleboard produced with 
recycled wood in Denmark, by examining the production process and properties of the 
measured compounds. 
4. To determine if there is an environmental or workplace exposure risk to regulated substances 
from recycling or incineration of waste wood. This is achieved by comparing the analytical 
results with existing limit values for relevant substances.

 Methodology 
3.1 Sampling Campaign 
The objective of the sampling campaign was to collect and characterize “clean” waste wood destined for 
recycling. An existing sampling campaign conducted by Econet A/S in March 2016 was joined. For the 
purpose of comparison, samples of virgin, consumer-ready (pre-consumer) wood were also collected.  
 Collection at Recycling Centers 
Wood samples were collected from recycling centers in the Danish municipalities of Middelfart (site a), 
Gelsted (site b) and Glamsberg (site d), which follow the same sorting procedures for wood waste. 
Waste wood is collected at the recycling centers in one of two containers:  1) clean wood and 2) treated 
wood (with preservatives). The source of wood waste is both private and industrial and sorting is 
completed by the depositor under loose supervision of the recycling center staff.  
 Sampling Procedure 
Thirty-four (34) samples of “clean” wood waste were collected by Econet and were re-grouped into 27 
categories based on their composition and application. A total of 19 samples were collected and 
prepared for chemical analysis (see Appendix A for re-categorization details). Seven (7) samples of pre-
consumer wood were provided by Johannes Fog Trælæst in Kongens Lyngby and prepared for analysis.

3.2 Laboratory Analysis 
All samples underwent a series of particle size reduction steps in order to more representative samples 
in a form readily available for chemical analyses. Processing steps were chosen according to Boldrin and 
Christensen (2010) and adapted to the specific case of this study.  
PAH’s and Phenols extracted in a Dichloromethane solvent using microwave assisted extraction and 
analyzed with GC-MS. Metals were extracted using nitric acid and Microwave assisted extraction.  
 PAHs and Phenols Extraction 
The extraction and analysis process for PAH’s and phenols was developed by analytical chemist Mikael 
Olsson (2016) of DTU Environment. Processed wood samples (2 g) and an internal standard solution 
(100µL) were added to dichloromethane (DCM) solvent (20 mL). The mixture was heated in an Anton 
Paar Multiwave 3000 microwave extractor to 120 °C for 40 minutes with a maximum pressure of 18 bar 
inside sealed capsules and let to cool for 90 minutes. The solution was then filtered through glass wool 
(rinsed with 2x10 mL DCM ) and evaporated using a Nitrogen stream to approximately 10 mL after which 
the sample was divided into two equal portions for PAHs and phenols.  
Triplicates of each sample were processed. One blank containing only the solvent and internal standard 
was included in each extraction run. A reference material (Beechwood–BCR683) for PCP and PAH was 
included an average of once every third extraction for verification of results. Detailed lab methods can 
be found in Appendix A.  
Polyromantic Hydrocarbons 
One of the evaporated portions was passed through a 1 g/6 mL florisil SPE cartridge and rinsed with 2 x 
6mL DCM. The resulting solution was then evaporated with nitrogen down to approximately 100 µL. and 
analyzed via GC-MS.  
Phenol 
The other portion was evaporated with a nitrogen down to approximately 100 µL. Phenols derivation 
was were then extracted by adding 100 µL of BSTFA:TMCS (99:1) and 3 µL of anhydrous pyridine to the 
vial, placed in a 70°C oven for 2 hours and analyzed via GS-MS.  
Gas Chromatography – Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) 
Chromatographic separation was achieved using a 60 m x 0,25 mm (inner diameter) capillary column 
with a film thickness of 0,25µm (HB-5ms) and a 10m fused guard column. GC was conducted with an 
Agilent 6890 gas chromatograph using helium as the carrier gas. Detailed GC programs for PAH and 
phenols can be found in Appendix A. 
 Metals Analysis 
Digestion and analysis methods were adapted from Edo et al. (2016), Tafur-Marinos et al. (2016) and 
EPA method 3052 (1996). Microwave assisted digestion was completed using an Anton Paar Multiwave 
3000. 
Metals  
Wood samples (0.5 g) were added to 8 mL of 65% nitric acid (HNO3), 3 mL of 37% hydrogen peroxide 
(H2O2) and 1 mL of hydrofluoric acid (HF) and left to react overnight. While stirring, the samples were 
heated in the microwave (1000 W) to 210 °C and 40 bar over 10 minutes and held (1200 W) for 15 
minutes. After cooling, 6 mL of boric acid (H3BO3) was added to consume excess flourides, and 
==PAGE:12==
7 
 
microwaved (1400 W) again to 210°C and 40 bar over 5 minutes and held for 15 minutes. After cooling, 
resulting clear solutions were diluted to 50 mL with milliQ water and analyzed via ICP-MS and ICP-OES.  
For antimony (Sb), the samples were digested in 6 ml nitric acid and 2 mL hydrochloric acid (HCL) rather 
than HNO3+H2O2, and the above process followed thereafter. 
Triplicates of each sample were processed and analyzed. One blank containing only the solvents was 
included in each digestion run. 
Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) 
Metals analyzed using an Agilent 7700 series mass spectrometer (ICP-MS) were: Li, Be, Na, Mg, Al, K, Sc, 
Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Ge, As, RB, Sr, Zr, Nb, Mo, Ag, Cd, In, Sn, Sb, Ba, La, Ce, Pr, Nd, Sm, Eu, 
Gd, Tb, Dy, Ho, Er, Tm, Yb, Lu, W, Re, Ir, Pt, Tl, Pb, Th and U.  
Metals analyzed using a PerkinElmer Avio 200 optical emission spectrometer (ICP-OES) were: Ca, P, S 
and Si.

 CCA Equivalents 
Chromated copper arsenate (CCA) was not directly measured in this analysis. As a proxy, the equivalent 
CCA was calculated based on the analytical results. Knowing the elemental ratio in CCA (CrCuAs2O9) to 
be 1:1:2 for chromium, copper and arsenic respectively, the limiting stoichiometric component was 
determined by converting the analytical results (mg/kg) into mol/kg. The entirety (100%) of the limiting 
element was assumed to be associated with CCA, which was then converted into CCA equivalents using 
the molecular mass (409,38 g CCA/mol). The maximum potential CCA equivalent was calculated using 
the limiting element concentration plus its error 
3.4 Mass Flow Analysis 
The mass flow of analyzed PAHs, phenols and metals was considered for particleboard production and 
thermal treatment.  Boiling points (bp) for all elements and compounds analyzed were collected and 
compared to process temperatures to determine the fate of the substance. The “hot press” phase in PB 
production is carried out at 250°C and the furnace temperature during thermal treatment is 
approximately 1000°C. Substances with bp’s lower than the process temperature were assumed to 
volatize or oxidize, while those with bp’s above process temperatures would remain in the product (PB 
or bottom ash).  
3.5 Exceedance Production Rate (EPR) 
Airborne contaminants (fx. PAH and phenols) at the particleboard factory were not measured directly 
and it was necessary to estimate concentration based on the analytical results. The process of concern is 
the “hot press” phase of production, where certain compounds are expected to volatize due to the 
temperature and pressure increase. The production process consists of a continual flow of material 
through a building with some form for air exchange, which required a unit of measure to represent this 
and relate it to occupational exposure limits (OEL).  
The exceedance production rate (EPR) is defined as the rate of particleboard production (t/minute) at 
which the OEL (mg/m3) of a specific substance is exceeded, when that substance has a known 
concentration (mg/t) in the raw materials.  Equation (5) describes the calculation of the EPR, where AER 
is the air exchange (ventilation) rate of the production facility in m3/minute, and cc is the concentration 
of the contaminant (mg/t) that will vaporize during heating: 
(5) 
EPR =   OEL    x  AER   
 
 
 
  cc  
The air exchange rate is used instead of the building volume, because it is the volume of air that would 
need to be contaminated in order for the entire indoor environment to maintain a specific airborne 
concentration. This value was calculated for compounds with OELs and compared to the actual 
production rate of particleboard at the factory. EPRs below the actual production rate are of concern. 
Equation (5) could be expanded to include partial releases (multiplying cc by a release coefficient) and 
multiple ingredients. Information used to determine the factory specification can be found in Appendix 
A.

4.1 Wood Waste Characterization 
 General 
Waste composition was found to be highly variable from site to site as illustrated in Figure 2. This is to 
be expected considering the indiscriminate deposition system, difference in quantity from each site and 
the fact that sampling was conducted at each site on single day from a single container.  The most 
consistent category between sites was “furniture”, which ranged from 22,7% to 26,6%. The 
“construction and demolition” (C&D) category, (35,7% to 61,1%) and the “pallets & packaging” category 
(5,1% to 26,8 %) had the most significant differences between sites due to the relative contributions to 
the total.  The “loose and misplaced” (0,4% to 9,25%) and “impregnated wood” (2,3% to 8,4%) 
categories also had relatively large variations within their respective categories, however with lower 
contribution on the overall composition of the waste

The moisture content of waste wood is relevant to the chemical analysis. Figure 3 displays average 
moisture content of the wood waste by category.  It can be seen that the “untreated wood” category 
has the highest moisture content, likely due to the fact that clean wood will absorb more moisture than 
that with surface treatment (paints, laminate coverings, lacquer etc.) found in furniture and C&D waste.

the general surface area and absorption abilities. The “loose & misplaced” category has a low MC due to 
the general contamination from composite materials (concrete, asphalt, plastic etc.) which contain little 
to no moisture. The categories that contribute most to the WW composition (pallets, packaging, C&D 
and furniture), have similar MCs which is important for determining a weighted average moisture 
content of WW, which in this  case is 14,4%.  Moisture content data can be viewed in Appendix B.

Chromated Copper Arsenate 
Chromated copper arsenate (CCA) was not directly measured in this analysis. Instead, the analytical 
results were transformed into CCA equivalents (see methods section 3.3.3). Assuming that 100% of the 
limiting component plus its maximum standard error, contribute to CCA, the resulting value is the 
“maximum potential CCA equivalent” of the wood.  
Arsenic was found to be the limiting CCA component in WW at 0,009±0,004 mg/t. The maximum 
potential CCA equivalents in waste wood was therefore determined to be 2764 mg/t corresponding to 
1465 mg/t in particleboard made from WW.  
The pre-consumer wood used for this analysis included samples of lumber, furniture, plywood, which 
are assumed to contain no CCA, but also particleboard, hardboard and MDF which may contain recycled 
wood with some CCA contamination. The average Cr, Cu and As concentrations are presented in Table 3. 
Arsenic was determined to be the limiting component in pre-consumer wood, which had concentrations 
of 0,004 ± 0,003 mol/t, corresponding to a maximum CCA equivalent of 1378 mg/t.

Phenols  
All phenol compounds analyzed except for pentachlorophen0l (PCP, BP=309°C) have boiling points 
below 250°C and are assumed to evaporate fully during the “hot press” phase of production. The 
allowable limit for PCP in user products in Denmark is 0,1% by weight (Miljøstyrelsen, 2017), that is, 
1g/t. The maximum potential PCP content in particle board contributed by recycled wood is 285,7 mg/t 
PB (ie. 199,8 + 85,9 mg/t), which is far below the allowable limit. 
According to the compound summaries in the USA based Pubchem database (Pubchem, 2017) which 
sources information globally (fx. OSHA, NIOSH), there are no established exposure limits (in air) for the 
following phenol based compounds: 2-nitrophenol, 2,4,6-trichlorophenol, 2,4-dichlorophenol, 4-chloro, 
3-methylphenol, 3,4-dimethylphenol and 2,6-dimethylphenol. The compounds 2,3,5-trimethylphenol, 
2,3-dimethylphenol, 2,4-dimethylphenol and 2,5-dimethylphenol were below detection limits. Table 6 
above summarizes the occupational exposure limits for phenols that are expected to vaporize during 
production. 
Phenol was found to have a maximum potential concentration of 111 mg/t (ie. 78,4±32,9 mg/t) of 
particle board (prior to heating). OSHA states an 8hr time-weighted average exposure limit for Phenol at 
8 mg/m3 (Pubchem, 2017). This corresponds to a production capacity of 0,072t PB/m3 of air exchange 
(”hot press” building) in order to exceed the exposure limit. This equates to an EPR of 5,05 tPB/min, 
which is less than the actual (0,8t/min).  
According to NIOSH, 2-methylphenol (o-cresol), 3-methylphenol (m-cresol) and 4-methylphenol (p-
cresol) each have a lower exposure limit (LL) in air of 10mg/m3. The individual cresols (o-, m-, and p-) 
have EPRs of 3,53 t/min, 40,4 t/min and 6,48 t/min respectively. If we say that that the LL for total cresol 
in air is 10mg/m3 (and not 30 mg/m3).  The maximum potential total cresol concentration (sum of the 3 
compounds + error) in un-glued particle board is 325 mg/t, which corresponds to an EPR of 2,16 t/min.  
 Thermal Treatment  
In 2014, Denmark produced a total of 408000 t of wood and wood packaging waste (Miljøstyrelsen, 
2016). Particleboard production utilizes 220000 t of this WW, equating to 53,9%. The remaining 46,1% 
(188000 t) is assumed to be used for energy production (ie. thermal treatment/incineration). This 
represents approximately 5,7% of the total waste (3272000 t) sent for incineration in Denmark in 214 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2016).  
Waste incineration has an optimal furnace temperate of 1050°C and after-burner (flue gas) temperature 
of 850° for complete burnout (Christensen, 2011).  The metals: sodium, potassium, zinc, arsenic, 
rubidium, cadmium, sulfur and caesium, have boiling points below 1050°C and are assumed to volatilize 
fully during the incineration process and be treated in the flue gas. The remaining metals in table 1 are 
assumed to remain in the bottom ash or fly ash.  
All PAH’s and phenols have boiling points less than 550°C and therefore oxidize during incineration, and 
are expelled as CO2 and H2O and will not be present in bottom ash or fly ash.

 Discussion 
5.1 Wood Waste 
Waste wood collected in this investigation contained 3% (wet weight) of impregnated wood, however 
due to the lack of comparable data, it is difficult to assess the representativeness of this value. For 
example, the contamination by impregnated wood in Middelfart and Glamsbjerg were 2.0 and 2.4% 
respectively, while in Gelsted that value was 5.9%. This may be because Gelsted had a small sample 
quantity compared to the other districts, and a coincidentally large deposition of impregnated wood 
that day, but this is only speculation. Regardless of 3% being satisfactory or not (ideally close to zero), it 
is a good starting point for the purpose of this study which assesses the worst-case scenarios for 
contaminants, and can be assumed reasonable (ie. stable) as Danes have been accustomed to sorting 
out impregnated wood for over 15 years. On a positive note, as we approach the end-of-lifetime of the 
impregnated wood with banned preservatives (CCA), the quantity of impregnated wood is expected to 
decrease over time (Edo et al., 2014). However, particleboard produced from contaminated wood waste 
will be much more persistent in the waste stream due to its repeated recyclability.  
The “Loose & Misplaces” fraction is also a source of contamination such as composite materials with 
concrete or asphalt and comprised 4.3% (wet weight) of the waste wood sampled. For example, one of 
the samples for this category (E10) contained asphalt and had the highest Arsenic content (4 times 
higher than the next) of all the samples analyzed. Whether this is due to the asphalt portion or 
unrecognized impregnation of the sample is unknown. It is unlikely that this contamination would be 
removed by mechanical sorting before to incineration, however the chipping and sieving process for 
particleboard production may be able to reduce this fraction.  
5.2 Particleboard Production 
 PAH 
The total PAH content is useful in determining the general level of contamination in the WW, but due to 
the complexity of creosote, and the fact that 13 of the 26 compounds analyzed comprise less than 
0,06% each of the mixture (Bestari et al., 1998), it is difficult to link directly the analytical results to 
creosote concentration. This is exemplified by examining the ratio of total PAHs to phenols, which in 
creosote is 8,5:1, but were measured at 1:1,7, obviously difficult to attribute directly to creosote. Also, 
1-methylnaphthalene, a principle component in creosote (5%) was not detected, but this does not 
necessarily indicate that no creosote was present, as this compound may have dissipated (migrated, 
evaporated, degraded etc.) during the lifetime of the treated wood. The remaining 13 of 26 compounds 
are all included in Meuller’s (1989) list of 17 principal components, however, the list includes 3 
additional compounds that were not tested. Bestarri et al. (1998) used a similar list of 15 PAHs, which 
differed only in the minor components. That being said, the PAHs with the highest contributions (8-9%) 
to creosote (in both of the above studies), namely: phenanthrene, anthracene, 2-methylnapthalene, 
naphthalene and fluorine were present. It is noteworthy to point out that Biphenyl is one of the 
medium-abundance principle components (~5%) but was not included in the methods used here. 
With all this in mind, it may be reasonable to use one or all of the above mentioned principle 
components as indicators for creosote and extrapolate. For example, 2-methylnaphthalene has the 
lowest concentration at 3,68 mg/t and comprises about 8% (by mass) of creosote, thus the creosote 
content would be:  Ccreosote = 3,68 mg/t x 100%/8% = 46 mg/t.  This however is also of limited value as it 
==PAGE:24==
19 
 
would correspond to the composition of fresh creosote and not account for loss over the lifetime (if 
any), and it has no limit value from which to make decisions. Similarly, if we use the highest 
concentration in our analysis, which was anthracene at 340 mg/t (and comprises ~8% of creosote), we 
can estimate a creosote concentration of 4250 mg/t WW. These two figure are drastically different, 
making this method unreliable without more research into degradation and loss of compounds, as well 
as detailed lifetime information about the discarded material.  
The best option is therefore to stick with established regulations. According to the European Panel 
Federation (EFP, 2016), Benzo(a)pyrene is an indicator for creosote, with a limit in wood panels (ie. 
particleboard) set at 0,0005 g/kg or 500mg/t. Although it comprise less than 1% of creosote, 
benzo(a)pyrene is used as an indicator because, it is one of the heavier components in creosote, with a 
high boiling point and low solubility. It therefore has a relatively low migration rate and concentrates on 
the surface of creosote-treated wood (Kohler et al., 2000), making it easy to sample but still difficult 
accurately relate to the initial or current concentration. The maximum potential concentration of 
benzo(a)pyrene contributed to in particleboard is 0,437 mg/t. Therefore benz(a)pyrene does not exceed 
the limit value for particleboard, and creosote is not of concern.  
This points to the source of PAHs not being solely creosote. Examining the analytical results more closely 
(Appendix C), we see that samples containing MDF have the highest PAH concentrations. Even more 
interesting is the fact that one of the MDF samples (A3) was the only sample to contain benzo(a)pyrene, 
meaning that in the instance of this study, the only source of creosote may have been MDF containing 
recycled wood. This means that either MDF contains a high amount of contaminated wood, or the other 
ingredients (ie. the adhesive resins) contain PAHs. This was not examined further in this report, but 
would be worth investigating in future studies.   
In terms of occupational exposure to airborne PAHs, only naphthalene and 2-methylnaphthalene have 
boiling points below 250°C (process temperature) and are expected to volatilize during production. 
Naphthalene and 2-methylnaphthalene have 8hr time-weighted-average (TWA-8) exposure limits of 50 
mg/m3 and 2,5 mg/m3 respectively. Using their respective maximum potential concentrations in 
equation 5 (in methods), we get exceedance production rates (EPR) of 63,8 t/m and 8,12 t/min 
respectively, thus airborne naphthalene and 2-methylnaphthalene from waste wood did not exceed 
occupational exposure limits.  
 Phenols  
Similar to PAHs, the presence of phenolic compounds may indicate the presence of creosote, but 
cannot be directly correlated the initial or current concentration of the preservative. The total phenols 
content of the waste wood was determined to be 1129 ± 333 mg/t, however of the 12 principle 
compounds identified by Mueller, Chapman and Pritchard (1989), only 6 of them were measured in this 
study. The 6 compounds excluded from this study were all xylenol species, which would be worth 
investigation in future studies of this kind.  
It was demonstrated that, at a maximum potential concentration of 286 mg/t, PCP did not exceed the 
limit value for particleboard, which is set at 5000 mg/t (EPF, 2016).  After examining the individual 
sample results, it could be seen that samples with high PCP content were those from construction & 
demolition, MDF and impregnated, which makes sense as these categories are expected to contain end-
==PAGE:25==
20 
 
of-life materials from the pre-PCP ban era.  The others phenolic compounds are not addressed 
specifically for this industry.  
Occupational exposure limits for detectable phenols expected to volatilize during the “hot press” phase 
of production, as well as the calculated EPR for each compound, are displayed in Table 6. Under the 
assumptions used to build this scenario, it was determined that the maximum potential concentration 
of phenol, 2-methylphenol (o-cresol), 3-methylphenol (m-cresol) and 4-methylphenol (p-cresol) in 
waste wood were not a levels that would result in OEL exceedances. In order to exceed the OELs, the 
above compounds would need to be between 4 and 50 times more abundant. Even summing the three 
cresol species do not result in an exceedance. It is important to consider that EPRs are estimated using 
the minimum required air exchange volumes for occupied buildings in Denmark as well as a simplified 
production schedule, which may or may not be true for the manufacturing facility. In order to verify 
these values, we would require detailed information on the production of various panel types (quantity 
and duration) and actual air exchange in the building.   
 CCA 
As CCA was not directly measured in this analysis and is presented as maximum CCA equivalents (see 
results section 4.2.3). The maximum potential CCA equivalents for pre-consumer wood and WW are 
1378 mg/t and 2765 mg/t respectively. If we consider the CCA equivalent concentration in pre-
consumer wood to be a baseline for all non-treated wood, then subtracting this value from waste wood 
concentration will yield the level of CCA contamination. The maximum potential CCA contamination in 
waste wood is therefore 1387 mg/t. Similarly, we could simply subtract the pre-consumer wood 
elemental concentrations from the waste wood and determine the potential CCA from the remainder, 
but the maximum error cannot be exploited as directly. This corresponds to an estimated concentration 
of 735 mg/t CCA in particleboard (which is assumed to contain 53% WW). Interestingly enough, applying 
this same method (using As concentration as limiting component) to the pre-consumer particleboard 
sample, we get a CCA concentration of 4070 mg/t. However copper is the limiting component in this 
instance, as it was below the method DL of 43 µg/L (ie. 430 mg/t). This means that if the copper content 
was just below the DL, then the maximum potential CCA in pre-consumer PB is 2770 mg/t. Regardless of 
the actual value, it seems that the max CCA concentration calculated in WW and particleboard, are at 
least in the same order of magnitude as that of pre-consumer PB. Measuring the chemical composition 
of particleboard directly would be more accurate and therefore preferable to the methods used here. It 
would also be advantageous to assess the different formulations of CCA, rather than assume an average 
chemical composition as in this study. Although the amount of CCA contaminated wood in WW is 
expected to decrease over time (as the CCA-era wood reaches the end-of-life), the particleboard (and 
MDF and alike) content will perpetuate the presence of this preservative for years or even decades.  
 Metals 
Considering the speculation involved in the above estimations, it is better to consult established limit 
values for the individual metal in Table 3 of the results, to assess the safety of particleboard.  The 
concentration of the EPF (2016) regulated metals (As, Cd, Cr, Cu and Pb) did not exceed limit values for 
particleboard. Mercury, chlorine and fluorine also had limit values but they were not measured in this 
study.  
Copper, was the only element with a concentration (11300 mg/t) in the same order of magnitude as the 
limit value (40000 mg/t), all other were between 1 and 3 orders of magnitude lower and therefore not 
==PAGE:26==
21 
 
of concern. Since the limit value for Cu is nearly 4 times that of the measured concentration, the 
relatively high copper content in the impregnated WW is not of concern as this category is only 3% of the 
WW stream. This is despite new alternatives to CCA containing high copper loads as is demonstrated by 
the pre-consumer impregnated wood sample which had a concentration of 961000 mg/t, over twice 
that of waste impregnated wood (444000 mg/t). There is also significant copper contribution from 
outdoor C&D wood waste, which may have contamination from impregnated wood and mineral 
residues, but, as mentioned above, this category combined with impregnated WW is still 4 times less 
than the limit value.  
The highest levels of chromium were found in the impregnated waste wood. There was also a slightly 
elevated Cr concentration in the composite sample containing asphalt, which is suspected to have 
treated wood in its structure. Although there is potential for additional chromium contamination in WW 
(paints etc), the use of chromium is limited in consumer products (Miljøstyrelsen, 2017), therefore 
Chromium does not seem to be of concern for particleboard.  
The arsenic concentration in particleboard is 536 mg/t and causes little concern when compared to the 
limit value of 25000 mg/t. Considering that the arsenic content of composite asphalt material  (17,5 
mg/kg), MDF (4,26 mg/kg), outdoor C&D (2,54 mg/kg), untreated furniture (2,43 mg/t) and MDF 
packaging (2,42 mg/kg) were all higher than that of impregnated waste wood (1,65 mg/kg), it seems 
that contamination from other sources are of greater concern than impregnated wood and require 
further investigation. All of the above listed categories are expected to be lower than impregnated 
except for the composite asphalt sample whose composition is difficult to predict. The pre-consumer 
impregnated wood had no detectable arsenic, which was to be expected.  
Lead was not the focus of this study, but as there is an existing limit value (90000 mg/t) for 
particleboard, it is worth examining. The lead in PB (6930 mg/t) is 13 times less than the limit value, so it 
not a cause for concern. The individual sample with the highest lead concentrations are those composed 
of MDF (likely due to paint in recycled wood) and once again, the composite asphalt sample.    
5.3 Thermal Treatment 
The PAH and Phenols investigated in this study are all assumed to oxidize during combustion, therefore 
creosote in its original form does not pass to fly ash or bottom ash. This being said, we know that PCP is 
well-known for promoting the formation of dioxins at high temperatures (COWI, 2004) as are other 
chlorine-containing hydrocarbons. The dioxin emission potential is an important issue and should be 
investigated further, however this was beyond the scope of study. There are also other constituents 
(and contaminants) of creosote that were not examined here that may have adverse effects or 
byproducts when burned. 
The metals content measured in this study generally agree with those found in Edo et al.’s (2016) nine 
year study in Sweden, at least having the same order of magnitude. All of the metals are expected to 
end up in fly ash or bottom ash, which in Denmark is banned for use as a fertilizer, although it is 
currently unregulated for other recycling, namely, road-base material. There are landfill criteria for solid 
waste for CD, Cr, Pb, Ni and Cu, but these values are in leachability (mg/L) and are not directly 
comparable without a more detailed analysis on leachability. Other transformations or byproducts 
formed or promoted by metals during incineration were outside the scope of this study, but would be 
valuable to a more comprehensive mass flow analysis.

5.4 Limitations and Improvements 
Although the objective of identifying issues with wood recycling associated with impregnated wood 
contamination was achieved, the data collected in this study has several limitations. Data accuracy 
should always be subject to scrutiny. Without question data accuracy, or rather inaccuracy, is the main 
limitation of this study, as there are many assumptions and estimated values.  
Regarding wood waste, the small number of sampling occasions is the main source of uncertainty. On a 
positive note, with the available data, we were able to build an accurate weighting scheme for the 
analysis, however the results remain fairly specific for this study. A more representative WW 
composition could easily be achieved with an extended sampling program that would better account for 
daily, seasonal and geographic variations. In terms of consequences for recycling, targeting the specific 
collection centers that provide wood for particleboard production would be valuable. It is expected that 
there is high variability in WW composition, which means that this study likely falls within normal 
ranges, however, increasing the sampling size would allow for accurate averaging and help define that 
variability. It would also be important to include a consistent sorting scheme from which samples could 
be taken for analysis. The analysis samples in the study were taken during an existing sampling program, 
so re-categorization was necessary to line analysis results with wood categories. Removing this step 
would simplify the data handling and improve representativeness. 
Chemical analysis for this project was completed carefully in triplicate form and is assumed to be 
accurate within the calculated errors. As with any study, the accuracy could be improved with increased 
sample numbers. The only disadvantage to the methods used for PAHs and phenols, was the poor 
extractability of several samples (fx. asphalt composite) due to binding in the filter and precipitating 
solids/films. It would also be valuable for future studies to be more targeted in the analysis, focusing on 
regulated substances only.  
The approach to determining the CCA content was unorthodox and did not add significant value to this 
study. A more specific analysis on impregnated wood, giving consideration to the three common CCA 
formulas (A,B and C) which were disregarded in this study, could potentially develop a connection 
between original CCA loads and WW concentrations. Alternatively, focusing solely on arsenic 
contamination, would produce clearer results. 
Considering the acceptability of lab results, and the likely variability of WW composition, it would be 
valuable to extend this study with a sensitivity analysis of the chemical composition. Simplifying the 
data handling and reporting by reducing the number of compounds/element evaluated down to those 
with relevant limit values, would be a good starting point. Testing the sensitivity of the weighting 
scheme (which ideally would also be simplified) would allow for the identification of significant 
contaminant sources (ie. hotspots), providing a basis for targeted solutions. This would also be useful for 
making predictions about contamination levels of WW, if composition data was available. 
Because this study focuses on particleboard production from a single factory, an alternative and more 
effective strategy would be to collect samples of mixed wood chips in lower quantities but with high 
frequency, from which the actual contaminants could be measured. It would also be valuable to sample 
the cold-glued panels (before the hot press phase) and the finished product to directly measure the 
mass transfer of choice compounds. This would increase the number of samples, but simplify the 
sampling structure and provide directly comparable results. Another advantage of this strategy would 
==PAGE:28==
23 
 
be dropping the “worst-case-scenario” approach, which is also a significant source of uncertainty and 
only valuable for the sake of discourse. 
Another significant source of uncertainty were the values used for estimating the airborne 
concentrations of compounds evaporated during the “hot press” phase of production. The company 
was forthcoming with general information, but were not willing (or able) to supply the building or 
process specifications. It was therefore necessary to estimate all relevant details for assessing 
occupational exposure potential, which may not reflect the real working environment.  
 Conclusions 
In this investigation waste wood collected at recycling centers in Denmark contained 3% (wet weight) of 
impregnated wood, which is assumed to be reasonably representative considering the long standing 
practice of separating out impregnated wood in Danish waste collection centers. Creosote and CCA are 
banned wood preservatives that were popular in the past and may contaminate the impregnated 
portion of WW. In addition to impregnated wood, arsenic contamination was also notable (highest) in 
the “loose & misplaced” fraction of WW, which contained composite materials with asphalt and 
concrete. In Denmark, approximately 54% of collected WW is used in the production of particleboard, 
while the remaining 44% is sent for thermal treatment and energy recovery (incineration). The amount 
of CCA contaminated wood in WW is expected to decrease over time however, the particleboard 
content will prolong the presence of these preservative. The pre-consumer impregnated wood had no 
detectable arsenic, as expected. 
The fate of trace metals analyzed in this study, including CCA, are expected to have a 100% transfer rate 
to particleboard during recycling. Because CCA could not be directly measured, the elemental 
concentrations of chromium, copper and arsenic were converted into CCA equivalents which equaled 
1387 mg/t in WW and 1465 mg/t in particleboard. Benzo(a)pyrene, which is used as an indicator for 
creosote and PCP (banned in 1993) is one of the principle components of the preservative were also 
measured. The concentration of the EPF (2016) regulated substances for particleboard (As, Cd, Cr, Cu, 
Pb, PCP and benzo(a)pyrene), contributed specifically by WW did not exceed existing limit values. 
Mercury, chlorine and fluorine also had limit values for particleboard but they were not measured in this 
study.  
Some components of creosote are expected to volatilize during recycling, which may cause potential 
hazards to the indoor work environment at the particleboard production facility. Occupational exposure 
limits (OEL) were addressed by converting the analytical results for PAHs and phenols into exceedance 
production rates (EPR) which estimated the volatilization of compounds based on production speed. 
Only compounds with measurable concentrations and for which OELs exist were examined. Examined 
airborne concentrations (from WW) of the PAHs: naphthalene and 2-methylnaphthalene did not exceed 
occupational exposure limits. Neither did the airborne concentrations (from WW) of the phenolic 
compounds: phenol, 2-methylphenol (o-cresol), 3-methylphenol (m-cresol) and 4-methylphenol (p-
cresol) exceed OELs. 
Creosote and its associated components (PAHs and phenols) are expected to oxidize fully during 
incineration. The potential dioxin emission potential from the combustion of PAHs and phenols is an 
important issue and should be further investigated, however this was beyond the scope of study. Trace 
==PAGE:29==
24 
 
metals are assumed to transfer fully to bottom and fly ash during incineration. The final concentrations 
in ash were not determined in this study as there are no relevant regulations for ash in Denmark.  
Data accuracy was the main limitation of this study as the calculated values are based on many 
unverified assumptions (process and factory specification, material transfer rates, etc.). An alternative 
approach to this study would be sample wood chip and particleboard directly from the production 
facility at regular and more frequent intervals and also to monitor select airborne substance in the 
factory. Additionally, a study considering the three common CCA formulas (disregarded in this study), 
could also be a valuable step in developing a connection between original CCA loads and WW 
concentrations.

A-2: Analysis of PAHs and Phenols from Wood.’ 
Mikael E. Olsson 
DTU Environment 161017 
Ver 1.1 
 
Principle 
PAHs and Phenolic compounds are extracted from wood samples by Microwave assisted extraction using 
dichloromethane as solvent. The extract is divided into two fractions where the PAH fraction undergo a clean-up 
procedure using Florisil SPE cartridges prior to GC-MS analysis. The Phenol fraction is derivatized directly using 
BSTFA:TMCS and analyzed by GC-MS. 
47 compounds, including PAHs, mono- and dimethylated naphthalenes and phenantrenes, mono- and 
dimethylated phenols, chlorophenols and nitrophenols are included in the analysis (Appendix A). 
 
Extraction 
Approx. 2 g sample is extracted using Microwave assisted extraction (MAE). 20 ml dichloromethane is added to 
each sample and extraction is performed at 120 ºC for 40 min with the maximum pressure set at 18 bar. After 
extraction the extract is filtered through glass wool and up concentrated by N2 evaporation. 
 
SPE clean-up (PAH fraction) and derivatization (Phenol fraction) 
 
1g/6 ml Florisil SPE cartridges are conditioned with dichloromethane before applying the extract. The flow-
through is collected and evaporated to approx. 100 µl before GC-MS analysis. 
 
For the Phenol fraction, the extract is directly evaporated to approx. 100 µl before derivatization using 
BSTFA:TMCS (99:1) and anhydrous pyridine at 70 ºC. The derivatized sample is then directly analyzed by GC-
MS.   
 
Chromatography 
Chromatographic separation is achieved on an Agilent 6890 gas chromatograph equipped with a 60 m x 0,25 mm 
i.d x 0,25 μm film thickness HB-5ms column with a 10 m fused Guard column (Agilent Technologies).  
 
PAHs 
1 μl sample is injected in splitlesss mode with the sample inlet held at 300 °C. The oven is programmed to 70 °C, 
then 20 °C/min to 300 °C held for 13 min, then 50 °C/min to 325 °C held for 10 min. Helium is used as carrier 
gas with a 1 ml/min constant flow.  
 
Phenols 
1 μl sample is injected in splitl mode (20:1) with the sample inlet held at 280 °C. The oven is programmed to 80 
°C, then 20 °C/min to 300 °C held for 13 min. Helium is used as carrier gas with a 1 ml/min constant flow.

Detection 
Detection is achieved using an Agilent 5975C electron impact (70 eV) triple-axis mass-selective detector. The mass-
selective detector temperatures are 230 °C for the EI source and 150 °C for the quadropole with the transfer line 
held at 250 °C. The spectra are monitored in selected ion monitoring (SIM) mode (Appendix A). 
 
Calibration standards and controls 
Calibration 
 
The calibration standards are processed through the entire method the same way as the samples. The 
concentration range in Appendix A, std1-std6, for the calibrations standards (approx. 20 µg/l to 1500 µg/l) 
represents the concentration in the extract just after MAE. If 2 g sample is used 20 µg/l equals 0.2 µg/g. 
 
Certified reference material (BCR-683) 
 
A certified reference material of contaminated Beech wood is used as control. (Appendix B). 1 – 2 g is used per 
extraction and one control should be included per extraction carousel. 
 
Standard addition 
 
For each new sample type a standard addition control experiment should be performed. 80 µl of the calibration 
mix is added to a sample and the recovery is calculated (Appendix A, recovery spike) 
 
Internal standards 
 
Deuterium and 13C labelled PAHs and phenols are used as internal standards. A mix containing all are prepared 
and added to the extraction vessel before the extraction. (Appendix A, Internal standards). 
 
Samples 
Samples needs to be freeze-dried and milled before extraction. Any residual water will greatly impair the analysis. 
 
Data treatment and calculations 
Procedure 
MAE 
1. Weigh approx. 2 g sample directly in the Teflon liner. 
2. Add 20 ml dichloromethane and 100 µl ISTD mix. 
3. Add a magnetic stirrer and a passive heating element to the Teflon liner before closing the cap. 
4. Prepare the pressure/temperature sensor last by adding 20 ml dichloromethane and a passive heating 
element. No magnetic stirrer should be added as it will break the sensor. 
5. Fill the carousel with 4,8 or 16 vessels. The MAE can only handle this numbers of vessels and if any 
other are used the effect of the microwaves can be miscalculated resulting in under- or overheating of 
the vessels. 
6. Place the carousel in the MAE and select the p/t sensor in the setup menu. A black box means selected. 
7. Open the “Wood creosote” program and type 4,8 or 16 vessels. 
8. Press “start” and “start” again after the self-check is passed. 
==PAGE:36==
Appendix A: Methods 
5 
 
9. The extraction takes approx. 75 minutes to complete where after the vessels are taken out to a fume 
hood to reach room temperature. Do not open any vessels while they are warm as the dichloromethane 
will evaporate rapidly. 
10. Print the temperature/pressure curve of the extraction. 
Filtration 
1. Pack a small piece of glass wool in a glass funnel 
2. Pass a few ml dichloromethane through the glass wool. Discard the flow-through. 
3. Gently pour the contents of the extraction vessel into the glass funnel. Try to avoid that the magnetic 
stirrer or heating element into the funnel. 
4. Wash the sample residues with 10 ml DCM and pour into the glass funnel. 
5. Wash the glass wool with 10 ml DCM. 
6. If the filtrate is not clear after filtration repeat the filtration process and pack the glass wool tighter in the 
funnel. 
7. Divide the extract into two equal parts in 40 ml glass vials (PAH fraction and Phenol fraction). 
SPE clean-up (PAH fraction) 
1. Evaporate the extract to approx. 5 ml using the evaporation device for N2. Attach new needles to the tip 
each channel.  
2. Condition the 1g/6 ml Florisil SPE tubes with 6 ml DCM. Discard the flow through. 
3. Apply the sample and collect the flow-through. 
4. Wash with 2 x 6 ml DCM. Collect the flow-through. 
5. Evaporate to approx. 100 µl and proceed to GC-MS analysis. 
Derivatization (Phenol fraction) 
1. Evaporate the filtered extract to approx. 100 µl. 
2. Add 100 µl BSTFA:TMCS and 3 µl anhydrous Pyridine. Shake vigorously. 
3. Incubate at 70 ºC for 2 hours. 
4. Proceed to GC-MS directly 
GC-MS 
1. For PAHs run method “PAH Creosote” 
2. For phenols run method “Phenols creosote” 
 
Cleaning of Teflon liners and glassware 
All dichloromethane should have evaporated away from any glass ware and Teflon liners before they are cleaned. 
1. Rinse off all residual wood pieces with deionized water. Dry at 105 ºC. 
2. If water does not remove the wood residues clean the glass ware with 32% HNO3 at 150 º C. Teflon liners 
can be cleaned using cleaning programs with HNO3 and heptane:acetone (50:50) with the MAE system. 
3. Rinse with heptane:acetone (50:50). Dry at room-temperature.

(A-2) Appendix E – Startup procedure 
 
1. Prepare fresh Mobile phase A and B and purge the pump using 50% A and B respectively. Check the 
level of the waste container and the rear seal wash solution. 
2. Run the starting conditions 95 % A and 5 % B through the column at 0.30 ml/min for at least 15 min. 
Increase the flow at 0.1 ml/min increments. Record the pressure at 0.30 ml/min in the pressure log 
book. 
3. Make sure all modules are turned on and connected before running a “blank” injection. The DAD 
should be turned on at least 15 min before analysis in order to warm up. In the “type” column choose 
“blank” and start the sequence. An analysis is performed without triggering an injection, with the 
gradient applied directly to the column bypassing the injection needle. Check the chromatogram for 
background peaks.  
4. Name the sequence according to “date” “type of analysis” “name”. Make sure the sequence is saved in 
the correct folder for the current month. 
5. Run an injection of the pigment calibration mix to confirm proper peak separation. 
6. Make sure there are enough mobile phase for the entire sequence and standby mode. If no other analysis 
is to be performed after the sequence make sure he instrument goes into standby mode when the 
sequence is finished. Under “queue” in “instruments” select “After running the queue; run Smart 
standby”.
