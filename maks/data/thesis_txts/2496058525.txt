ABSTRACT 
Bovine mastitis is a highly prevalent and costly 
infectious disease in dairy cattle worldwide. 
Corynebacterium spp. are minor pathogens 
classified as environmental mastitis causing 
pathogens. This study was undertaken to 
explore 
the 
transmission 
dynamics 
of  
Corynebacterium  spp. in two Danish dairy 
cattle herds. The included study herds were 
composed of 214 and 443 lactating cows, and 
these were tested at quarter-level once per 
month during a 12-month period. We 
demonstrated that the mean quarter-level 
prevalence for herd 1 and 2 were 24% and 
11.7%, respectively. The mean quarter-level 
incidence for herd 1 and 2 were 8% and 6.5%, 
respectively. We further estimated the daily 
mean quarter-level transmission rate to be 
0.016 and 0.018 cases/quarter-day for herd 1 
and 2, respectively. This corresponds to a mean 
cure rate on 0.012 and 0.016.  We also 
estimated the basic reproductive ratio for herd 
1 and 2 to 1.27 and 1.11, respectively. Finally, 
we investigated cow and quarter-level risk 
factors associated with Corynebacterium spp. 
infections. Our results showed that prior 
infections with minor and major pathogens 
increased the risk of Corynebacterium spp. 
IMI at quarter-level.  
 
INTRODUCTION 
Bovine mastitis is a prevalent endemic 
infectious disease in dairy herds, causing 
considerable economic losses due to culling 
and reduced milk production and quality 
(Chehabi et al., 2019). Cases with mastitis are 
classified into clinical (CM) or subclinical 
mastitis (SCM) depending on the appearance 
of symptoms (Chehabi et al., 2019). Different 
bacterial 
species, 
classified 
as 
either 
contagious or environmental, can cause 
mastitis. Among these, the gram-positive 
Corynebacterium spp.  
are a common environmental cause of mastitis. 
The environmental pathogens are mainly 
transmitted outside of the milking parlour. 
However, they can still spread between 
lactating cows during milking like contagious 
pathogens 
(Barkema 
et 
al., 
2009).  
Corynebacterium spp. are classified as minor 
mastitis pathogens, however an increased 
prevalence of intramammary infections (IMI) 
caused by Corynebacterium spp. have been 
observed in modern dairy herds (Dalen et al., 
2018; Pitkälä et al., 2004).  
For many years somatic cell count (SCC) has 
been  extensively used as an indicator of IMI 
in dairy cows (Kirkeby et al., 2019a). SCC is 
composed of different types of immune cells, 
however it does not differentiate between 
these. Recently, a novel parameter, differential 
somatic cell count (DSCC), has been 
introduced as a supplementary indicator of IMI 
(Kirkeby et al., 2019a). DSCC represents the 
proportion of polymorphonuclear neutrophils 
(PMNs) and lymphocytes relative to the 
macrophages in milk. Multiple cow and 
quarter-level 
factors 
can 
affect 
the 
susceptibility to mastitis including parity, days 
in milk (DIM), SCC, DSCC and prior infection 
history (Dalen et al., 2018). However, these 
risk factors may have different impacts on the  
individual pathogens, meaning that some risk 
factors may increase the rate of infection for 
some pathogens and not for others and vice 
versa.

 The spread of mastitis-causing pathogens 
among dairy cows can be prevented by  regular 
management strategies  including postmilking, 
teat dipping, antimicrobial, treatment and 
culling of infected cows (Dalen et al., 2018). 
Mathematical models enable prediction and 
understanding of the epidemiological and 
economical 
effects 
caused 
by 
mastitis. 
Knowledge of the transmission dynamics in 
dairy cattle herds enables simulation of the 
spread of infections. The most commonly 
applied method for estimation of disease 
transmission rates is the Poisson regression 
model. However, two novel methods by 
Kirkeby et al., (2017) have recently been 
developed and tested against the Poisson 
regression (Kirkeby et al., 2017). These new 
methods 
proved 
better 
performance 
in 
estimating transmission rates at large sampling 
intervals (Kirkeby et al., 2017). To our best 
knowledge only few papers have described the 
transmission patterns of IMI caused by 
Corynebacterium spp. which warrants further 
research into the specific properties of the 
pathogens (Dalen et al., 2018).   
The first objective of the present study was to 
explore 
the 
transmission 
dynamics 
of 
Corynebacterium spp. in two Danish dairy 
herds using data from a 12-mo longitudinal 
study. The second objective was to investigate 
cow and quarter-level risk factors that were 
associated 
with 
infection 
with 
Corynebacterium spp. 
 
MATERIALS AND METHODS 
Animals and Farms 
The 12-mo sampling period was carried out in 
two Danish dairy herds in the central region of 
Jutland (Table 1). Collection of milk samples 
occurred monthly between January 2017 and 
January 2018, down to quarter level. The 
length of the sampling period was selected to 
mimic the performance checks that take place 
approximately 11 times annually. In addition, 
it allows estimation of the transmission rate 
and the period of infection. The selected study 
herds were composed of 214 and 443 milking 
cows (Table 1). The study herds had different 
management routines but the farmers agreed to 
participate without changing their daily 
routines during the study period.   
Herd 1 
A total of 214 (~180 per sampling interval) 
lactating cows were present in herd 1 (Table 1). 
The cows were housed in freestalls and straw 
was used as bedding material. The animals 
were fed with a TMR diet consisting of soy, 
barley, concentrate, corn, and grass silage. 
However, a problem caused by mycotoxins in 
the feed occurred from March to August in 
2017. To avoid toxins, all silage was replaced 
with a new silage product in August. Lactating 
cows were milked two times a day.  The 
milking took place by use of a double 12 side-
by-side milking parlor system. To avoid 
bacteria or other contaminates, the teats were 
wiped off using clean cotton at milking.  At dry 
off, the cows were tested with bacterial culture 
to determine if their SCC was above 200,000. 
Cows passing this threshold were treated with 
intramammary antimicrobials and teat sealant. 
Lactating cows with signs of clinical mastitis 
during the lactation period were treated with 
antimicrobials 
and 
non-steroidal 
anti-
inflammatory drugs. In case the treatment did 
not work, clinically ill cows were culled. 
Herd 2 
A total of 443 (~360 per sampling interval) 
milking cows were present in herd 2 (Table 1). 
Similarly, the cows were housed in freestalls, 
however sand was used as bedding material. A 
TMR mix including soy, concentrate, and corn 
silage was used as feeding. The lactating cows 
were milked three times a day using a double

Collection of Quarter Milk Samples 
The collection of quarter milk samples were 
performed in line with NMC standards 
(http://www.nmconline.org/sampling.htm). To 
ensure that quarters were clean and to dissolve 
dirt a specific premilking teat preparation was 
applied. While, a foaming teat wipe-off 
product was used for herd 1, the teats were 
predipped using a milk wash from Trinol at 
herd 2. The teats were further cleaned using 
cotton towels soaked in water. Around four 
squirts of milk were then discarded from each 
quarter and the teats were finally sanitized with 
single- service wet wipes which were soaked 
in 90% ethanol. The teats were further cleaned 
by use of 90% ethanol spray followed by air-
drying for a minimum of 30 seconds. After 
cleaning of the teats, sampling could take 
place. For sampling, prelabeled sterile sample 
vials were applied. After filling of the vials up 
to 
15 
ml 
milk, 
they 
were 
recapped 
immediately. All samples were stored in 
thermally insulated boxes, with cooling 
elements, that were shipped to the laboratory.  
The samples were then processed 36 hours 
within sample collection. The samples were 
then preserved using 0.5% boric acid and 
further shipped to another laboratory, also in 
thermally insulated boxes with cooling 
elements, at which bacteriological analysis 
took place within 46 hours of collection. 
Statistical analysis  
The statistical analysis was conducted in R 
3.5.1 “Feather spray” (R Core Team, 2019) 
and figures were generated using the package 
ggplot2 (Wickham, 2009). We collected data 
on the dates of drying off per cow during the 
study period from the Danish cattle database. 
We estimated quarter-level prevalence and 
incidence per sampling interval for both herds. 
We corrected missing data points to increase 
the size of data by applying the following rules 
(Kirkeby et al., 2019b). A missing value 
between two positive samples was corrected to 
positive; a missing value between two negative 
samples was corrected to negative; and 
missing values between any negative and 
positive samples were corrected to negative. 
Additionally, if two positive samples were 
followed by a missing value, we corrected it to 
be positive. These rules are further explained 
in Kirkeby et al., 2019. We considered 
infections to be cured once a negative sample 
occurred after a positive sample and no 
positive sample followed after that (Kirkeby et 
al., 2019b). The cured animals were either 
spontaneously cured or treated.  However, only 
cases 
with 
CM 
were 
treated 
with 
antimicrobials for three days.  In addition, the 
proportion of transient and cured quarters were 
calculated for each sampling interval.

Confidence limits for the transmission and

Cow and quarter level risk factors 
We conducted a risk factor study to identify 
cow and quarter-level variables that increased 
the risk of infection with Corynebacterium 
spp. The cow-level variables included were 
parity and DIM. We treated parity and DIM as 
categorical variables with three and four levels, 
respectively (Figure 3, A). The quarter-level 
variables included were SCC, DSCC, and 
infection history with respect to minor, major, 
and other pathogens. We handled SCC, DSCC, 
and infection history as categorical variables 
with two levels (Figure 3, A). Quarters with 
existing infections of the pathogen were 
discarded, meaning only newly infected 
quarters were considered in the analysis.  The 
statistical analysis was performed using a two-
stage approach (Zadoks et al., 2001). First, 
univariate analyses were performed to screen 
all independent variables in a Poisson 
regression model.  Here, we used the same 
equation as [1], however we added the risk 
factors as regressors to the equation. Next, all 
independent variables with a P ≤0.20 in at 
least one risk factor level were submitted to the 
multivariate model (Zadoks et al., 2001). We 
further evaluated the estimates of the 
multivariate model by calculating the relative 
risk (RR). This was performed in R using the 
exp function of each risk factor estimate. 
 
RESULTS 
Descriptive statistics 
In herd 1, 4,665 quarter milk samples were 
collected from 214 lactating cows (Table 1). 
Among these, 1,166 samples were infected 
with Corynebacterium spp. throughout the 
study period. This corresponds to a mean 
quarter-level prevalence of 24% (range = 12.1-
38.6) (Figure 1, A). During the 12-mo study 
period, 
320 
new

with 
Corynebacterium spp. were detected. The 
mean quarter-level incidence was estimated to 
be 8% (range = 0.8-20.5) (Figure 1, B). We 
observed an increased incidence in April, May, 
and November. We corrected 230 missing data 
points according to the rules described 
previously. However, we were not able to 
correct all missing data points as many cows 
were first introduced to the herds later in the 
dataset and some cows were removed due to 
the dry period.   The mean number of transient

6

 In herd 2, 10,381 milk samples were collected

Basic Reproduction Number 
The estimated basic reproduction number for 
herd 1 and 2 for the different methods are 
presented in Table 3. In herd 1, the R0 was 
estimated to be 1.17 (95% CI: 0.66 – 1.91) 
using the Poisson regression method (Table 3). 
This indicates that each infectious quarter 
would infect 1.17 other quarters on average 
during its infectious period in a completely 
susceptible population. For method 1 and 2, the 
estimated R0 was 1.33 (95% CI:1.14 - 1.62) and

In herd 2, the R0 was 1.05 (95% CI: 0.66 – 1.69) 
using the Poisson regression method (Table 3). 
We observed similar R0 for method 1 and 2 
(Table 3). The estimated mean R0 of the 3 
methods was 1.11.  
Risk Factors 
Cow and quarter-level variables were tested to 
detect if they increased the risk of new 
infections with Corynebacterium spp. The 
results from the univariate analysis for both 
herds are presented in Figure 3, A.  In herd 1, 
we observed that prior infections with minor 
and major pathogens, and DSCC > 60 showed 
significance. These variables were further 
analysed in a multivariate model and were 
similarly significant (Figure 3, B). The 
reported estimates from the multivariate model 
are given as relative risks. We observed that 
quarters previously infected with minor and 
major pathogens were significantly associated 
with an increased risk of new infections with 
Corynebacterium spp. For prior infections 
with minor and major pathogens, the relative 
risks were 11.19 (P < 0.001) and 4.83 (P < 
0.001), respectively (Figure 3, B). For DSCC 
> 60, the relative risk was 0.60 (P < 0.001) 
(Figure 3, B).  
 In herd 2, we noted that prior infection with 
minor pathogens and DIM were significant in 
the univariate analyses (Figure 3, A). Here, 
only two risk factor levels of DIM were found 
to be significant: 1 to 60 DIM and 61 to 120 
DIM. The significant variables from the 
univariate analysis were submitted to the 
multivariate 
model 
and 
were 
similarly 
significant (Figure 3, C). However, there was a 
reduction in significance of the risk factor 
levels in DIM, meaning only the first 1 to 60 
days of lactation was significant.  For prior 
infection with major pathogens, the relative 
risk was 35.21 (P < 0.001) (Figure 3, C). For 1 
to 60 DIM, the relative risk was 1.86 (P < 
0.001) (Figure 3, C).  
 
DISCUSSION 
The transmission and cure parameters are key 
measurements to predict the cost-effectiveness 
of strategies to control and prevent mastitis-
causing pathogens. However, very few 
published papers have attempted to quantify 
the transmission profiles of these pathogens.  
The 
present 
study 
investigated 
the 
transmission dynamics of the minor mastitis 
pathogen, Corynebacterium spp., in two 
Danish dairy herds. First, we demonstrated that 
the mean quarter-level prevalence in herd 1 
and 2 was 24% and 8%, respectively (Figure 
1). Although, our results indicate substantial 
differences 
in 
the 
prevalence 
of 
Corynebacterium spp. between the study 
herds, we bear in mind that the herd sizes were 
different. The lack of comparable studies in 
European herds restricted us in comparing our 
results with other mastitis studies in the region. 
However, a Swiss study reported a quarter-
level prevalence of 14% based on 4,227 
quarters samples from 99 herds (Tschopp et al., 
2015).  
In addition, different countries have reported 
varying prevalence of Corynebacterium spp. 
including Argentina (5.2%) (Dieser et al., 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
2013), Jordan (5.3%) (Hawari and Al-Dabbas, 
2008), Canada (19.9%) (Brooks et al., 1983), 
and U.S. (9.1-23.9%) (Dalen et al., 2018). This 
reflects 
that 
the 
prevalence 
of 
Corynebacterium spp. ranges from 5.2% to 
23.9%, which is comparable with the observed 
prevalence in our study. However, these results 
cannot be applied directly for comparison due 
to the fact that milking routines, herd sizes and 
bacterial diagnosis may vary in the different 
countries and herds. In fact, previous studies 
have reported that the prevalence of minor 
mastitis pathogens are associated with the 
quality of postmilking teat disinfection in dairy 
herds (Dalen et al., 2018). We estimated the 
mean transmission rates of Corynebacterium 
spp. in herd 1 and 2 to be 0.016 and 0.018 cases 
per quarter day, respectively (Table 2). These 
estimates suggest that there was no significant 
difference in the transmission patterns between 
the two herds.  
The transmission rates achieved using Poisson 
regression for herd 1 and 2 were 0.016 and 
0.018 cases per quarter day, respectively 
(Table 2). As previously described, the Poisson 
regression model is the most common method 
for estimating transmission and cure rates in 
mastitis studies. Using Poisson regression, 
Dalen et al., (2018) estimated the transmission 
rates of Corynebacterium spp. in two 
comparable farms to 0.018 and 0.023 cases per 
quarter day, respectively. The herds studied in  
Dalen et al., (2018) were composed of 
approximately 200 lactating cows per herd and 
were located in New York and Vermont.   
In addition, Dalen et al., (2018) found that the 
cure rates for farm A and B were 0.0122 and 
0.0202, respectively. In our study, we 
estimated the cure rate for herd 1 and 2 to 0.014 
and 0.017, respectively (Table 2). Our results 
are more or less in accordance with both the 
transmission and cure rates found in Dalen et 
al., (2018). However, these results reflect that 
Corynebacterium spp. can express similar 
behaviour in dairy cattle herds from different 
countries 
despite 
different 
management 
practices. We further evaluated the accuracy of 
method 1 and 2 against the Poisson regression. 
Here, we did not detect a significant difference 
in the transmission and cure estimates between 
the Poisson regression and methods 1 and 2 in 
both herds (Table 2). In addition, Kirkeby et 
al., (2019) investigated the transmission 
dynamics of Staphylococcus aureus (S. 
aureus), 
and 
similar 
estimates 
of 
the 
transmission rates using the three methods. 
These results clearly suggest that all three 
methods yield similar accuracy in estimating 
transmission and cure rates. In Figure 2, we 
observed that the transmission and cure 
patterns were more or less similar for all three 
methods 
for 
each 
herd.  
Kirkeby et al., (2019)  described the 
transmission profiles of S. aureus which is a 
major pathogen. We observed that the 
transmission pattern of S. aureus follows a 
similar pattern as Corynebacterium spp.  
(Kirkeby et al., 2019b). However, the 
transmission rates of S. aureus in Kirkeby et 
al., (2019) were different from those we 
estimated for Corynebacterium spp. in this 
study. 
For herd 1, we observed peaks in the 
transmission rates throughout the spring and 
autumn (Figure 2).  This indicates that the 
number of transient quarter infections appears 
to be lower during the summer period, 
indicating a seasonal variation of the spread of 
mastitis pathogens. For herd 2, we observed 
the opposite pattern, meaning that the number 
of transient quarter infections were increased 
during the summer period. In addition, Dalen 
et al., (2018) observed an increase in new 
infections with Corynebacterium spp. during 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
December. Our results demonstrated an 
increased number of new quarter infections in 
November 
for 
both 
herds (Figure 
2). 
Furthermore, we noted that the number of 
cured quarter infections were linked to the 
transmission pattern, meaning that once new 
infections occur a relative proportion of 
quarters become cured (Figure 2).  
The population transmission dynamics of 
Corynebacterium spp. were assessed for each 
method by estimating the R0.  Using Poisson

for herd 1 and 2, respectively (Table 3). In 
addition, 
we 
observed 
no 
significant 
differences in the estimates of R0 between 
Poisson regression and methods 1 and 2 in both 
herds. Generally, we noted lower cure rates in 
herd 1 which explains the increased R0 for this 
herd. A previous study obtained an R0 of 1.18 
and 0.98 for two herds (Dalen et al., 2018). 
These results further support our assumption 
about that Corynebacterium spp. express 
similar behaviour in dairy herds despite 
different geographic locations and herd 
management routines. 
 Longitudinal field studies with repetitive 
measurements of IMI status allows studying 
risk factors associated with the occurrence of 
mastitis-causing pathogens. However, these 
studies are rare, expensive, and time-
consuming. Our study focused on identifying 
cow and quarter-level risk factors that 
increased the occurrence of infections with 
Corynebacteria. In herd 1, the results from the 
univariate analysis indicate that previous 
infections with minor and major mastitis 
pathogens and DSCC had a statistically 
significant impact on the number of new 
infections with Corynebacteria. These risk 
factors were similarly significant in the 
multivariate analysis, indicating that quarters 
with previous infections with minor and major 
pathogens had 11.19- and 4.83-times greater 
risk of getting infected compared to healthy 
quarters, respectively (Figure 3, B). However, 
we observed that quarters with DSCC > 60 
indicated decreased risk of infection with 
Corynebacterium spp. compared to quarters 
with DSCC < 60 (Figure 3, B). Previous 
studies suggest that quarters with DSCC > 60 
are more likely to be exposed to infections, yet 
this is not in line with our results (Damm et al., 
2017; Kirkeby et al., 2019a; Schwarz et al., 
2019). 
This discrepancy in the DSCC warrants further 
investigation into the cut-off of DSCC, 
especially due to the evolving attention of this 
parameter in the dairy industry. 
In herd 2, the univariate analysis implied that 
prior infections with minor mastitis pathogens 
and DIM had a statistically significant effect 
on 
the 
number 
of 
infections 
with 
Corynebacteria. 
The 
results 
from 
the 
multivariate analysis indicated that quarters 
with prior infections with minor pathogens had 
35.21 
greater 
risk 
of 
infection 
with 
Corynebacterium spp. compared to healthy 
quarters (Figure 3, C).  Additionally, quarters 
were more sensitive during the first 1 to 60 
days (Figure 3, C) of lactation, indicating an 
increased 
risk 
of 
infection 
with 
Corynebacteria. 
Similarly, 
(Oliver 
and 
Sordillo, 1988) reported that infections with 
Corynebacterium spp. were significantly 
increased during early lactation.  
Kirkeby et al., (2019) observed that during 
early lactation an increased number of S. 
aureus infections were detected. These results 
suggest that quarters are more susceptible to 
infections during the first 1 to 60 days of 
lactation. This information can be used to 
implement extensive monitoring in this period, 
and thereby prevent and control spread of 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
pathogens and reduce the associated economic 
loss.  
 
CONCLUSION 
This study explored the transmission dynamics 
of Corynebacterium spp.  IMI at quarter-level 
in two Danish dairy cattle herds. In addition, 
we investigated cow and quarter-level risk 
factors 
that 
were 
associated 
with 
Corynebacterium spp infections. Our results 
demonstrated a mean quarter-level prevalence 
of 24% and 11.7% for herd 1 and 2. We further 
estimated the mean transmission rates for herd 
1 and 2 to be 0.016 and 0.018 cases/quarter-
day, respectively. The corresponding mean 
cure rates for herd 1 and 2 were estimated to be 
0.012 and 0.016, respectively. We also 
estimated the basic reproductive ratio for herd 
1 and 2 to 1.27 and 1.11, respectively. The 
results from risk factor study implied that 
multiple risk factors including prior infections 
with minor and major pathogens and DIM,  
increased 
the 
risk 
of 
infection 
with 
Corynebacterium spp. Our results can be used 
to raise awareness about the impact of minor 
pathogens within dairy cattle herds. Finally, 
these results can be used to implement cost-
effective strategies to prevent and control 
mastitis causing pathogens.
