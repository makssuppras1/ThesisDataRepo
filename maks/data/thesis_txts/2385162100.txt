Abstract
Today metabolic engineering and evolutionary engineering are the conventionally used methods
for cell factory design for green production of bio-chemicals. However, construction of eﬃcient
and proﬁtable cell factories remains a challenge, a process that can take several years. Further-
more, conventional cell factory development is typically carried out using a beforehand chosen
production organism and is in most cases limited to a single well characterized strain. New
methods for cell factory construction are needed in order to shorten strain construction time,
increase throughput and to take advantage of the natural biodiversity between strains to make
the best possible cell factories. A novel example of a future high throughput method for eﬀective
strain construction is the combined CRISPR/Cas Selective Ploidy Ablation (CRI-SPA) method.
The CRI-SPA method relies on yeast mating and a Universal Donor Strain (UDS) that is marked
with 16 conditionally stable chromosomes and exhibit Cas9 activity. The UDS is engineered to
contain the pathway of interest and is subsequently mated to the chosen recipients. The diploid
CRI-SPA strains are taken through a replica-plating protocol on speciﬁc selective media, that will
ensure genomic modiﬁcation of the recipient strain and by deselection of the conditionally stable
UDS chromosomes yield a haploid strain with the pathway of interest in the recipient background.
To scale up the CRI-SPA method from a single strain output to a high throughput method, a
new CRI-SPA protocol utilizing 96-strain pin replica plating was developed. The new protocol
was thoroughly tested and results indicating successful transfer of a biochemical pathway to
a subset of a gene deletion strain library was achieved. In a second part of this study a new
CRI-SPA UDS utilizing chromosomal integration as template for pathway transfer was con-
structed. Furthermore a proof of concept experiment showed that a markerless chromosomal
gene deletion could be transferred from a genomic location in this novel CRI-SPA donor strain
to yield a strain with the gene deletion of interest in the recipient background.
The CRI-SPA method is believed to be a great addition to the conventional tools of yeast cell
factory construction, but also have great potential to be used in studies investigating novel
interactions by over expressing or deleting genes in e.g. the genome wide knock-out library.
CRI-SPA allow for simultaneous strain construction of strains with diﬀerent backgrounds, which
could be used to ﬁnd the best producer utilizing natural biodiversity in available yeast strains.
The CRI-SPA method has successfully been characterized in a 96-strain format and show great
potential to be further optimized for implementation in high throughput experiments with the
potential application of automation.

CRISPR/Cas Selektiv Ploidi Ablation (CRI-SPA) metode er en ny metode der lever op til disse
krav for eﬀektiv konstruktion af gærbaserede cellefabrikker.
CRI-SPA metoden bygger på gærparring og på en Universel Donor Stamme (UDS) der udtrykker
en Cas9 nuklease. Metoden har en særlig konstruktion der gør de 16 haploide kromosomer
ustabile under særlige omstændigheder. Denne UDS konstrueres så den bærer en ønsket biosynte-
sevej hvorefter den parres med de ønskede recipientstammer. De nu diploide CRI-SPA stammer
tages igennem en replika-pladningsprotokol på bestemte selektive media, som sikrer den ønskede
modiﬁkation af recipientstammerne og modselekterer alle 16 kromosomer fra donorstammen.
Således bliver resultatet af protokollen recipientstammer der bærer den ønskede biosyntesevej.
For at op-skalere CRI-SPA metoden til et 96-stamme format, er der udviklet en ny replika-
pladningsprotokol som udnytter et særligt 96-punkts-værktøj. Den nye protokol er blevet
grundigt testet og det er påvist at den ønskede biosyntesevej blev overført fra donorstammen til
den testede del af et gendeletionsbibliotek af recipientstammer. I den næste del af dette studie
er en ny CRI-SPA UDS stamme blevet konstrueret. I dette nye system udnyttes kromosomal
integration af den ønskede biosyntesevej i donor stammen som skabelon for den genetiske
modiﬁkation i recipientstammerne. Derudover er det bevist at en markørfri gendeletion kan
overføres fra den kromosomale placering i UDS’en til en recipient stamme ved hjælp af CRI-SPA
metoden.
CRI-SPA er en fremragende tilføjelse til de eksisterende metoder for udvikling af gærcelle-
frabrikker, og har et stort potentiale til at blive udnyttet i en række andre studier. Dette
kunne være suppressions studier hvor der ledes efter nye interaktioner mellem det undersøgte
gen og et gendeletionsbibliotek. CRI-SPA vil ligeledes kunne udnyttes i studier der udnytter
biodiversiteten i forskellige stamme baggrunde til at ﬁnde den bedst mulige producent af det
ønskede produkt efter overførsel af biosyntesevejen. CRI-SPA metoden er blevet karakteriseret i
et 96-stamme format og viser stort potentiale for at udvikle mange stammer på kort tid. Det
kunne endda blive muligt at automatisere pladningsprotokollen for et endnu højere udbytte.

Introduction
Increased demand for environmentally friendly production platforms make the development of
microbial cell factories more relevant now than ever. A wide range of products, such as biofuels,
pharmaceuticals and colorants can be produced using cell factories, but development of eﬃcient
microbial producers is time consuming and laborious. An example of this is the development
of a yeast cell factory for production of the antimalarial compound artemisinin, which is es-
timated to have taken 130 RD person years to complete (Paddon et al., 2013; Karim et al., 2017).
The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae has been comprehensively studied, is well characterized
and have been utilized by mankind for thousands of years for production of food and beverages
(Meussdoerﬀer, 2009) and in modern times in advanced cell factories for the production of both
bulk and high value products (Kavšček et al., 2015).
Currently, metabolic engineering and evolutionary engineering are the most commonly employed
methods in the development of microbial cell factories. However both methods are low through-
put and studies are usually only working with one species and even one particular strain. The
existing biodiversity between diﬀerent strains or species is an thus an under exploited resource
in cell factory construction. Methods utilizing strain diversity could prove to be a great advance
in ﬁnding better producers and new candidates for further cell factory optimization (Steensels
et al., 2014).
A study by Strucko et al. (2015) comparing two yeast strain backgrounds, a S288c laboratory
derived strain and an industrially relevant CEN.PK strain, for the production of vanillin-β-
glucoside, unexpectly found a 10-fold higher production in the S288c background compared
to the industrially relevant strain. This is one example that looking into a wider range of
strains could provide just the right strain background for production of the compound of interest.
To make development, optimization and test steps of cell factory engineering faster, high through-
put methods and automation is required. Chao et al. (2017) explains how automation will allow
for a shift in biology like it has been seen in a range of other engineering disciplines allowing a
much faster route from idea to ﬁnal product revolutionizing molecular biology. Development
and implementation of high throughput methods for strain construction is one of the ﬁrst steps
towards automation of biological systems.
1.1
Aim of This Study
In this study, a new approach to cell factory optimization is undertaken, in which the importance
of genetic background of the producing strain can be utilized for creating and ﬁnding the
best possible candidate. The novel method called CRI-SPA, utilizing Clustered Regularly
Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR), yeast mating technology and Selective Ploidy
Ablation (SPA), is investigated as a new possible strategy for eﬃcient cell factory construction,
supplementing the metabolic engineering and evolutionary engineering strategies. In a previous
proof of concept study, the CRI-SPA method was shown to enable transfer of a metabolic
pathway from a CRI-SPA donor strain to a genomic location of one recipient strain. However
the method relying alone on yeast mating and a subsequent replica-plating protocol have great
potential for being modiﬁed to a high throughput and even automatable method for yeast cell

factory construction. The work of this study aims at characterizing the CRI-SPA method and
investigating the possibilities for scaling up the CRI-SPA method to achieve a high throughput
transfer of speciﬁc genetic elements of interest to a number of recipient strains.

2
Theory
The theory section of this thesis will explain the subjects necessary to understand the principles
of the CRI-SPA method and the experiments performed in this study including homologous
recombination, CRISPR/Cas9 technology and the selective ploidy ablation (SPA) system.
2.1
Homologous Recombination in Yeast
Throughout evolution all organisms have constantly been challenged by DNA damage arising
from both internal and external sources, such as stalling replication forks and irradiation. In
order to survive both single and double-stranded DNA breaks, several repair systems have
developed over time (Lettier et al., 2006). See Figure 1 for a schematic overview of double
strand DNA repair systems.
One of the most extensively studied repair pathways is homologous recombination (HR), which
is the preferred DNA repair pathway in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. HR is divided
into diﬀerent pathways as shown in Figure 1, common to all of them is that a repair template
is necessary for repair, unlike that of non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or single strand
annealing (SSA). Both SSA and NHEJ can lead to introduction of deletions or small insertions
after a double-strand break, while HR repairs from a template and will introduce that genetic
material which can be identical to the original sequence or diﬀerent depending on the homology
between the strands. This is why the HR pathway can be utilized for integration of heterologous
DNA trough homology directed repair. Here an insert of choice is introduced by ﬂanking with
up- and downstream sequences matching the part of the genome where integration is wanted.
3
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1: DSB repair pathways
The ﬁgure shows diﬀerent pathways of Double Strand Break (DSB) repair. Figure adapted from
(McVey et al., 2016).
Chromosomal integration in S. cerevisiae has been shown possible with short ﬂanking up- and
downstream regions, but a homologous targeting sequence of at least 200 bp and recommended
>500 bp for insertion of larger constructs will give better repair frequencies (Siewers et al., 2010).
An eﬃcient way of activating HR in yeast is by transforming with a linearized fragment that
will mimick a genomic DSB which provides eﬃcient introduction of the fragment of interest
if homologous sequences are present. In recent years several methods for introducing DSB in
speciﬁc targets of the genome have been developed revolutionizing the ﬁeld of molecular biology.
One of these methods is the CRISPR/Cas9 system described below, but it should be noted that
also Zinc ﬁnger nucleases, TALENs and other systems can be used to introduce DNA breaks
and stimulate DNA repair mechanisms (Stovicek et al., 2015).
2.2
CRISPR/Cas9 Technology
Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) asociated nucleases (Cas)
have proven to be a highly valuable tool for eﬃciently creating precise genetic modiﬁcations in
a variety of organisms, including bacteria, yeast, ﬁlamentous fungi (Nødvig et al., 2015), insects
(Lei et al., 2016) and plants (Ding et al., 2016). Originally the CRISPR/Cas9 DNA manipulating
system was developed from an adaptive immune system originating from bacteria. The CRISPR
sequence encodes two types of RNA sequences. The ﬁrst, CRISPR RNA (crRNA), targets
foreign DNA from invading viruses or foreign plasmids using a complementary protospacer
4
==PAGE:16==
region approx. 20 bp long. The second RNA sequence is Trans Activating CRISPR RNA
(tracrRNA) that binds the crRNA and attaches the RNA duplex to the Cas9 nuclease complex
to mediate double-strand cuts in the invading DNA (Doudna and Charpentier, 2014). The
CRISPR/Cas9 system used for gene editing has been simpliﬁed to a two component system by
combining the crRNA sequence encoding the protospacer from the 3’ end to the 5’ end of the
tracrRNA sequence by inserting a linker loop sequence yielding a single guide RNA (sgRNA).
The resulting sgRNA is schematically depicted in Figure 38. When the sgRNA has bound to
Cas9 and the protospacer part has bound the target DNA, Cas9 will recognize a three bp long
protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) on the target sequence and create double nicks three bp
up-stream of the PAM, generating a DSB.

The ﬁgure shows the Cas9 protein (blue) complex with the sgRNA (green-black-red) bound to the
target sequence of the DNA (green) next to the PAM sequence (orange). The black arrows indicate the
cleavage sites of the two nuclease active sites of the Cas9 protein which are located three nucleotides
upstream the PAM sequence. Figure adapted from (Doudna and Charpentier, 2014)
2.3
Yeast Genome Integration Sites
Genomic integration of genes of interest will allow for increased stability compared to plasmid
borne heterologous genes and can also lead to increased or more comparable expression, because
the number of copies are known and can be compared between strains. In the study by Mikkelsen
et al. (2012) 14 novel yeast integration sites (IS) on chromosomes X, XI and XII, were investigated
for heterologous expression of a seven-step indolylglucosinolate pathway. The ISs were evaluated
based on lacZ expression and growth rates in YPD and YPgal media and eleven sites were
found to be suitable for integration and expression of heterologous genes. The integration sites
were chosen based on speciﬁc criteria: The sites had to be interspaced by essential genes to
reduce the chances of proliferation after loss of an integrated gene in a genetic rearranging event.
The ISs should be located in clusters which increases the chance of co-segregation in a crossing
and subsequent haploidization event. Moreover the sites were located in high gene expression
regions as found by the GFP expression library. Lastly each site had to be in a intergenic region
of minimum 750 bp to reduce the chance of inﬂuencing ﬁtness by aﬀecting neighboring genes.

2.4
Selective Ploidy Ablation (SPA)
The selective ploidy ablation (SPA) method utilizes yeast mating to transfer a plasmid of
interest from one donor strain to a recipient of interest. The method and protocol was developed
by (Reid et al., 2011). SPA, unlike traditional yeast crossing, bypasses meiosis which gives a
number of advantages in matters of screening for the correct strain and reduces the likelihood
of unwanted crossovers giving a clean recipient genotype in the ﬁnal strain. This is possible due
to the special constructed SPA universal donor strain (UDS). The UDS is constructed with 16
conditionally stable chromosomes due to a KlURA3 and pGal1 promoter construct inserted
next to the centromeres of all 16 UDS chromosomes, see illustration of construct in Figure 4 A.
When activated the pGal1 promoter initiates transcription across the centromeres of all UDS
chromosomes. When actively transcribed the chromatin structure of the centromere is changed
in turn destabilizing the chromosomes so they can not segregate correctly to the daughter
cells during mitosis. This will result in anaeuploid and eventually haploid cells containing only
recipient chromosomes. The URA3 counter-selectable marker can subsequently be used to
deselect for strains still harbouring any UDS chromosomes yielding strains with only recipient
chromosomes, but now habouring the plasmid transferred from the UDS. The method can be
applied to transfer plasmid DNA from the UDS to a large range of recipient strains through a
four step replica-plating protocol shown in Figure 4 B. The protocol starts with mating of the
UDS and recipient strains on solid YPD for 6-8 hours. Then the now diploid cells are transferred
to galactose containing medium (YPgal) activating the destabilization of the UDS chromosomes.
After 24h of growth the cells are transferred to 5-FOA containing medium (SCgal + 5-FOA)
only allowing growth of strains that have lost all UDS chromosomes. The ﬁnal replica-plating
step is onto YPD. After an eﬀective SPA protocol the recipient strain now harboring the plasmid
of interest should be the only strain left. In order for the protocol to work it is important that
6
==PAGE:18==
the recipient strain is ura3 minus because of the important counter-selection step of the protocol
and exhibits the opposite mating type of the UDS to allow mating. It makes ﬁnal screening
easier if the recipient has one of the auxotrophic markers that the UDS lacks such as TRP1 or
HIS3. If the use of antibiotics should be avoided the recipient should also contain an additional
auxotrophic deﬁciency to be restored by adaption of the plasmid. (REF original paper)
Figure 4: Selective Ploidy Ablation (SPA)
A) Represents all of the UDS’s chromosomes that have been marked with the counter-selectable URA3
marker and a strong galactose indusable promotor. B) Shows the SPA protocol from mating of the
UDS and recipient strain, through the diﬀerent selection steps to the ﬁnal strain with the recipient
background and the transferred plasmid. Figure adapted from (Reid et al., 2011)
2.5
The Novel CRI-SPA Method for Transfer of Genetic Information
Utilizing Yeast Mating and CRISPR Technology
The novel method CRI-SPA developed by Coumou and Mortensen (2016) provides transfer and
chromosomal integration of a metabolic pathway assisted by CRISPR/Cas9 and Selective Ploidy
Ablation (SPA). The method relies on the UDS developed for the SPA protocol containing
conditionally stable chromosomes due to a URA3 counter-selectable marker and the pGAL1
promoter placed right next to the centromere sequence of all UDS chromosomes. The CRI-
SPA UDS have been further manipulated to carry a copy of the Cas9 gene together with the
auxotrophic LEU2 marker in the IS-XII-5 site. In order to complete the system the CRI-SPA
UDS is transformed with a pathway transfer plasmid. This pathway transfer plasmid contains a
few important elements; a sgRNA sequence targeting IS-XII-5, the pathway of interest next along
with a selective marker ﬂanked by up- and downstream sequences for integration in IS-XII-5. A
plasmid map of betaxanthin pathway transfer plasmid HO_p001 (former Ant_E113) is shown
in Figure 5. The system is designed so the sgRNA/Cas9 complex will only target the recipient
IS-XII-5 and not the donor since the Cas9 gene is integrated in that speciﬁc site of the UDS
chromosome XII. Figure 6 show the principles of the UDS and CRI-SPA method for the transfer
of a pathway of interest to the recipient strain. The CRI-SPA method aims to integrate the
pathway of interest in the genome of the recipient rather than simply transferring a plasmid as in
the SPA method. The CRI-SPA plating protocol is similar to the SPA protocol described above,
7
==PAGE:19==
going through the steps; mating of UDS and recipient, destabilization of UDS chromosomes,
counter-selection against UDS chromosomes and pathway transfer plasmid and ﬁnally selection
for correct clones of the recipient strain carrying the pathway of interest. See Figure 7 for an
overview of the CRI-SPA protocol as described by (Coumou and Mortensen, 2016).

2.5.1
Betaxanthin Pathway
Betaxanthins are a yellow, highly ﬂuorescent family of pigments, produced by a spontaneous reac-
tion between free amines and betalamic acid in certain plants. Betalamic acid is produced in some
ﬂowering plants by hydroxylation of shikimate-derived L-tyrosine to L-dihydroxyphenylalanine
(L-DOPA), see Figure 8 for details of the betaxanthin synthesis. Yeast cells expressing the
cytochrome p450 CYP76AD1 and DOPA 4,5-dioxygenase (DOD) accumulate betaxanthins
in the vacuole and exhibit a yellow phenotype (DeLoache et al., 2015). The Because of their
ﬂuorescent properties, betaxanthins are an ideal candidate for high-throughput screening and
this was exploited by DeLoache et al. (2015) to screen a 200,000 mutant library of CYP76AD1
for improved L-DOPA yields in yeast. Because of the easily observable and screenable phenotype
of yeast strains producing the betaxathin pigment, it was selected as a reporter compound for
the CRI-SPA proof of concept study.
Two extra genes were inserted in the pathway transfer plasmid to increase the yields of be-
taxanthin, namely ARO4 (encoding 3-deoxy-d-arabino-heptulosonate-7-phosphate synthase)
and ARO7 (encoding chorismate mutase). The feedback insensitive alleles of the two genes
ARO4-K229L and ARO7-G141S have been shown to increase the ﬂux through the Shikimate
pathway, which connects to L-Tyrosine biosynthesis. Expression of ARO4-K229L and ARO7-
G141S has previously been exploited to increase the production of p-coumaric acid, which is a
key precursor for ﬂavonoids and stillbenoids that can be synthesized from L-phenylanaline and
10
==PAGE:22==
L-Tyrosine (Rodriguez et al., 2015).

Introduction to Experimental Work
3.1
Preliminary Work
In a study performed by (Coumou and Mortensen, 2016) in the group of Eukaryotic Molecular
Cell Biology (DTU Bioengineering) the CRI-SPA method, described in Theory section 2.5, had
been developed. A proof of concept experiment has shown the integration of the betaxanthin
pathway from a plasmid source into the genome of one recipient strain. The CEN/ARS-based
low copy number pathway transfer plasmid HO_p002 has been constructed by As.Prof. Jacob
Blæsbjerg Hoof before the beginning of this study.
3.2
Speciﬁc Aims of This Study
The overall aim of this study is to test and characterize the CRI-SPA method when used in a
scaled up setting in order to guide the development of CRI-SPA into a high-throughput method
and to widen its range of applications. The speciﬁc aims of this study are three fold.
The ﬁrst aim of this study is to scale up the existing plasmid based CRI-SPA method for transfer
of a pathway from the CRI-SPA UDS to a gene deletion library plate of recipient strains.
This ﬁrst aim should be achieved by testing and evaluating the CRI-SPA method as described
by Coumou et al. in a 96-strain replica-plating protocol to enable generation of a large number
of strains using a 96-pin-replica-tool and replica-plating on square plates. The CRI-SPA method
will be optimized with inspiration from a study using an altered SPA protocol in order to
eliminate recipient and donor background (Haarer et al., 2013). Another line of experiments
regarding the ﬁrst aim of the study is bound to investigate the functionality of a CRI-SPA
system utilizing a low copy number plasmid as opposed to the original 2 micron based system.
The second speciﬁc aim of this study is to create a new versatile CRI-SPA UDS system using an
alternative strategy, where the pathway of interest is transferred from a chromosomal location
in the donor strain rather than from a plasmid source.
The strategy to reach this goal includes constructing a new CRI-SPA UDS with the CAS9
gene-construct in a diﬀerent integration site allowing the pathway of interest to be located
in the well described IS-XII-5 site. Integration on the chromosome of the donor is belived to
be advantageous in terms of stability and possible pathway size. In this context a new yeast
integration site on chromosome XII should be designed downstream of IS-XII-5 to select against
unwanted cross-over events during pathway transfer from the UDS to recipient chromosome.
The third aim of this study is investigating whether the CRI-SPA method can be expanded to
also enable the transfer gene deletions from a chromosomal location of a UDS to a recipient strain.
To complete the third speciﬁc aim and to verify the UDS produced as part of reaching the second
aim the following experiements should be conducted. In order to test the expression of Cas9 in
the new IS a ∆ade2 deletion experiment is to be conducted. If Cas9 is actively expressed from
the new site a proof of concept deletion transfer experiment should be performed to investigate
whether CRI-SPA can also be utilized to transfer a deletion to a recipient strain. To further
investigate the eﬃciency of the CRI-SPA system a diploid CRI-SPA UDS (∆ade2) x CEN.PK
strain is to be constructed and subsequent transformed with a gRNA plasmid cutting the ADE2
12
==PAGE:24==
gene. Deletion of the ADE2 gene induces production of a red color due to an intermediate of
the adenine synthesis pathway and thus provides an easy visible screen for veriﬁcation of correct
deletion. The percentage of red colonies after transformation will reveal how well the CRI-SPA
system is cutting the the recipient allele of the ADE2 gene in the diploid.

4.4
Design of Novel Integration Sites on Saccharomyces cerevisiae
Chromosome XII
Using the sequence data available through the Saccharomyces Genome Database (SGD)
(yeastgenome.org) two possible integration sites (IS) on Saccharomyces cerevisiea chromo-
some XII were identiﬁed with inspiration from the 14 yeast integration sites described by
(Mikkelsen et al., 2012). The genome browser function ’JBrowse’ was used to locate appro-
priate integration sites. The speciﬁc criteria for the new sites included a non-coding area of
a minimum of 750 bp of size, it had to be located on the down stream side of IS-XII-5 on
chromosome XII (further away from the centromere), it should be interspaced by at least one
essential gene from the IS-XII-5 site. The location of an essential gene was determined to
be present by the mere distance between the new and the XII-5 site. The Fasta sequence
was extracted using the ’Gene/Sequence Resource’ function of the SGD web page by search-
ing for the identiﬁed regions of chromosome XII. The two integration sites have in this work
been named IS-XII-6 and IS-XII-7. Further information on location can be found in Appendix G.

5
Results
In order to improve the identiﬁcation of superior strain backgrounds for microbial cell factories,
high throughput methods for strain transformation is needed. In the previously described
study by Coumou et al. the mating based and CRISPR/Cas9-assisted CRI-SPA protocol
was developed and a proof of concept study showed that the betaxathanin pathway could be
transferred from an integrative plasmid in the CRI-SPA UDS to a chromosomal location in the
recipient strain.
The results of this study is presented in three sections corresponding to the three main aims
of the study. The structure of the following result sections will include a short motivation of
the experiments conducted, present and discuss the results obtained and ﬁnally evaluate and
conclude what ﬁndings can be used for further experiments and optimization of the method.
5.1
Plasmid-based Transfer and Genomic Integration of a Biochemi-
cal Pathway to a Yeast Strain Library Using CRI-SPA
Aiming to increase throughput of the original CRI-SPA method, the current study investigated
the transfer and genomic integration of the betaxanthin pathway from the UDS to a yeast
deletion library using a similar plating protocol as described by Coumou et al. The speciﬁc goal
was to scale up the CRI-SPA protocol to a 96-plate format. In the 96 strain set-up, schematically
shown with 12 strains in the blue box and plate 1 in Figure 10, the CRI-SPA protocol is initiated
by growing a lawn of the UDS covering the complete surface of a rectangular YPD agar plate
and subsequently printing all clones of the recipient library on top in order to facilitate mating.

5.1.1
First Investigations of Transfer of the Betaxanthin Pathway from a 2 Micron
Integrative Plasmid to a Yeast Strain Library in High Throughput
To achieve the goal of successfully transferring the betaxanthin pathway from a CRI-SPA UDS
to a full deletion strain library, the ﬁrst experiment hereafter described as EXP. 1, attempted
to utilize the UDS_1.1 harboring the 2 micron betaxanthin transfer plasmid. A lawn of the
UDS_1.1 was grown on YPD and the recipient strain library was subsequently printed on top
of the UDS_1.1 lawn after three days of growth. This YPD mating plate with the UDS_1.1
lawn and a print of all recipients on top was incubated for 18h instead of 6h as speciﬁed by
Coumou et al. due to the lack of visible growth after the ﬁrst 6h. Another important change to
the protocol was the introduction of a diploid selection step on synthetic complete (SC) medium
lacking tryptophane (trp) and uracil (ura) immediately after the mating step, to select for cells
that had successfully mated and thereby reduce transfer of unwanted background of donor and
recipient cells. Furthermore, the protocol was modiﬁed to include an additional selection step
on YPD + NTC, to improve removal incorrect recipients as this had proven to be a potential
problem in some initial tests. The modiﬁed plating protocol applied in EXP. 1 is shown in
Figure 11.

From EXP. 1 it became apparent that the UDS_1.1, carrying the HO_p001 (former Ant_E113)
2 micron betaxanthin pathway transfer plasmid, exhibited very slow growth in both liquid YPD
with and without NTC and also on solid YPD. This was found to be a limiting factor for the
protocol since a suﬃcient lawn for the initial mating step could not easily be achieved even
after three days of growth. The slow growth of the donor is most likely due to the burden
of the multi-copy plasmid when replicated in high numbers. Another explanation could be
that the betaxanthin pigments induce a toxic eﬀect in the cells which lowers the UDS growth rate.
In addition to contributing to a slow-growing donor strain, expressing the betaxanthin pathway
on high-copy plasmid may cause a natural selection for cells that have lost or altered the plasmid
and thus regained a higher growth rate. When the selective pressure of NTC is applied there
will be a selection for cells that have a functional NatMX marker, but some strains may carry
plasmids with lost or altered expression of the betaxanthin pathway as indicated by the ﬁnal
white colonies seen on plate VI.
The most obvious explanation of the lack of growth on YPgal + NTC plate III, Figure 12 C,
would be that the the betaxanthin transfer plasmid have been lost at some point before transfer
to this medium. The UDS_1.1 was grown on YPD for more than three days and the diploid
selection plate was also prepared without NTC. The same conclusion could be drawn from the
second line of the experiment where no growth was seen on the diploid selection plate with NTC
suggesting that the plasmid have already been lost at the time of transfer to this medium. In
the proof of concept experiment performed by Coumou et al. the UDS_1.1 was only grown on
YPD for 6 hours. For further experiments it should be tested if a shorter time without NTC
would indeed increase the number of correct strains, but the slow growth of the donor is very
limiting in this regard since a suﬃcient lawn can not easily be achieved and visible growth of
the mated strains took time to develop.

One way to solve both the slow growth induced by the burden of the plasmid and plasmid
instability problems, could be to use a low copy number pathway transfer plasmid such as a
centromere (CEN) and autonomously replicating sequence (ARS) based plasmid. This type
of vector should reduce the burden on the cell due to the low copy-number of the plasmid.
The CEN/ARS sequence is simultaneously providing a much more stable vector since the
sequence is maintained in a similar fashion to the yeast chromosomes because of the centromere
derived sequence. This option of optimizing the CRI-SPA system will be further investigated in
experiment 2 (EXP. 2) presented below.
Another solution to maintaining the pathway transfer plasmid in the in the UDS throughout
the initial steps of the CRI-SPA procedure would be to grow the lawn under selective pressure
of NTC and ﬁnd an alternative method for mating that does not require the recipients to be
printed on top of the donor lawn plate. This solution will be investigated further in experiment
3 (EXP. 3) presented below.
5.1.2
Reducing Growth Defects in the UDS Utilizing Low Copy Number CEN/ARS-
Based Betaxanthin Pathway Transfer Plasmid
One of the main limiting factors of EXP. 1 was the slow growth of the UDS_1.1 strain. In
an attempt to eliminate this problem, an alternative strategy was developed for the following
experiment 2 (EXP. 2) to reach the goal of successful transfer of the betaxanthin pathway from
a UDS to the recipient strain library. Utilizing the same UDS background, but now transformed
with a low copy number plasmid, the plasmid burden previously observed should be reduced
and allow for a more eﬃcient CRI-SPA replica-plating protocol.
A low copy number CEN/ARS based betaxathin pathway transfer plasmid hereafter named
HO_p002 was designed, assembled and kindly provided by Assistant Professor Jacob Blæsbjerg
Hoof. The sequence of plasmid CEN/ARS plasmid HO_p002 was veriﬁed by sequencing.
The UDS_1.0 strain was transformed with the HO_p002 plasmid yielding the donor strain
UDS_1.2 which now harbours a CEN/ARS integrative plasmid expressing the gRNA necessary
for introducing a CRISPR DSB in IS-XII-5 as well as the betaxanthin pathway repair template
in one low copy number vector. See Figure 13 for a plasmid map of HO_p002. Successful
transformation of the original UDS_1.0 with plasmid HO_p002 was veriﬁed by growth on YPD
+ NTC, the colonies exhibited yellow phenotype.

Figure 40 shows the UDS_2.1 containing the Cas9-LEU2-URA3 gene construct in IS-XII-6
and the betaxanthin pathway and NatMX marker in IS-XII-5. The advantage of having the
pathway integrated on the chromosome of the donor strain is twofold. One advantage will
aid the ﬁnal screening of correct strains. The selectable marker next to the pathway will only
be transferred to the recipient strains if the transfer of the pathway has actually taken place,
not when recipients contain the pathway transfer plasmid without successful integration of the
pathway. This will diminish the number of false positives in the ﬁnal screen. Furthermore
integration of the pathway of interest to the chromosome of the UDS will make the pathway
a lot more stable. The issues of loss of or rearrangement of the plasmid during the CRI-SPA
protocol are overcome when the transfer template is integrated in the genome. Integration in
the genome will further allow for larger pathways to be stably transferred whereas a plasmid
approach would show increased instability with increased plasmid size.

5.3
Proof of Concept Study Utilizing the CRI-SPA Method for Trans-
fer of Markerless Chromosomal Gene Deletion
The CRI-SPA systems characterized in the previous experiments all aim to integrate a pathway
of interest, that is introduce heterologous genes in a recipient strain. An important addition
to the CRI-SPA systems would be a method for also introducing gene deletions at a high
throughput level. A new CRI-SPA system for doing this could prove to be advantageous in
suppressor studies, where a speciﬁc deletion is transferred to a gene deletion strain library e.g.
48
==PAGE:60==
to search for complementary eﬀects. Transfer of deletions can also be applied in metabolic
engineering studies where eﬃcient production of a compound of interest rely on the deletion of
certain native genes of the production organism. An example of this is the production pathway
of vanillin where the alchohol dehydrogenase encoding gene ADH6 must be deleted to prevent
the reduction of vanillin to vanillyl alcohol (Hansen et al., 2009).
A third aim of this study was thus to investigate and characterize a CRI-SPA gene deletion
transfer system. An initial experiment (EXP. 4) tested the transfer eﬃciency of a gene deletion
within a diploid strain in which the recipient allele was targeted by CRISPR-Cas9 and the region
ﬂanking the deleted donor allele was used to repair the DSB. This diploid transfer experiment
presented below indicated that the gene deletion could be transferred and the second gene
deletion experiment (EXP. 5) thus investigated if a CRI-SPA mating protocol could be applied
to transfer the gene deletion of interest from a UDS to create a haploid strain of recipient
background with the acquired deletion.

6
Discussion
The ﬁndings of the CRI-SPA experiments presented above show promising progress in the
characterization and development of CRI-SPA into a versatile high throughput method for strain
engineering purposes. The experiments EXP. 1-3 investigating a plasmid based transfer system
show that the original CRI-SPA method as presented by Coumou et al. have great potential to
be optimized for high throughput transfer of genetic material when a suﬃcient number of the
correct selective plates are used. Several changes were made to the original plating protocol to
accommodate the shift from a single strain to a 96-strain format.
One of the challenges in up-scaling the protocol was the mating step, where the CRI-SPA UDS
and recipient strains were mated to form a diploid strain containing all elements necessary
for transfer of the pathway of interest. Two approaches for mating was investigated. The
preferred mating method allowed the UDS to ﬁrst grow under selective pressure in order to
prevent loss of the pathway transfer plasmid. The subsequent transfer of a full 96-print of
the UDS and the recipient library to a YPD mating plate without selective pressure then en-
sured growth of both strains and allowed eﬃcient mating as showed in EXP. 3 (Figures 25 and 26).
The diploid selection step was not present in the original CRI-SPA protocol described by
Coumou et al.. However, an earlier study, utilizing the SPA method for actin dossage lethality
screening in a gene deletion library, utilized a diploid selection step to obtain correctly mated
strains for the subsequent plating protocol (Haarer et al., 2013). In the CRI-SPA system this
step is especially important because the time on diploid selection plates also provides the right
circumstances for introducing the necessary DSB and following repair while all genetic elements
(Cas9, gRNA, repair template) of the CRI-SPA system are present in the diploid strain. In
CRI-SPA experiments conducted in this study the diploid selection media was prepared as a
double dropout synthetic media selecting for the combination of donor and recipient background.
This strategy may not be the most eﬃcient one since some growth of non-correct cells were
observed (EXP. 3 Figure 26). To work around this, two rounds of selection were applied
for this step of the protocol in the ﬁnal version of the CRI-SPA protocol used in this study.
Another solution could have been to prepare the diploid selection media with the two antibiotics
NTC and G418, corresponding to the two selective markers NatMX from the pathway transfer
plasmid of the donor and KanMX from the gene deletion cassette in the gene deletion library,
as in the SPA study byHaarer et al. (2013). Selection by antibiotic pressure would give a
stronger selection against the unwanted haploid donor and recipient background, which oth-
erwise might be shielded from the selective pressure by the correct diploids on SC dropout media.
The two pathway transfer plasmid based CRI-SPA methods investigated in EXP. 1 to 3 gave
good insight into how the CRI-SPA system behaves in a scaled up version. As also shown in the
original proof of concept study, transfer of the pathway was shown using the 2 micron based
betaxanthin pathway plasmid and an optimized plating protocol for high throughput transfer.
The CEN/ARS based transfer plasmid experiment did not show any promising results ﬁnding
mainly recipient strains carrying the plasmid on the ﬁnal plates. This is belived to be due to
insuﬃcient expression og the gRNA. Any further investigations trying to utilize a low copy
number plasmid system should look into ﬁnding the best possible gRNA for the target site of
interest and ensure high expression of the target gRNA from the plasmid.
As an alternative to the plasmid based systems, a new CRI-SPA donor strain was constructed
57
==PAGE:69==
to expand the scope of the CRI-SPA method. It may seem laborious to integrate both a lin-
earized fragment containing the genes of interest and a gRNA plasmid, compared to the original
CRI-SPA UDS system carrying just one pathway transfer plasmid harbouring both genetic
elements needed. However, the new series of UDSs are expected to be of great use for future
CRI-SPA experiments, since easy modiﬁcation of the UDS can be achieved by simultaneous
transformation of the genes of interest and the IS-XII-5 targeting gRNA plasmid. The gRNA
plasmid is even providing a DSB in the integration site chosen for the linear fragments, which
could show to improve transformation eﬃciency and ensure the possibility of a one step UDS
construction. The fact that the genes of interest is integrated in the donor strain is believed to
circumvent any rearrangement of genes that could have been the result of having the genes on a
transfer plasmid. This would be more applicable for large pathways or genes encoding toxic
compounds that are prone to undergo modiﬁcations due to the growth advantage of cells with
mutations.
The markerless gene deletion ade2∆was succesfully transferred to one recipient strain using
the new CRI-SPA UDS system. This both proved that a CRI-SPA mediated gene deletion
transfer is possible and it conﬁrmed the integration and active expression of Cas9 in the new
IS-XII-6. The fact that CRI-SPA can be utilized for introduction of gene deletions widens the
scope of the method signiﬁcantly. CRI-SPA could now be used for a range of full library studies
featuring gene deletion, over expression or expression of heterologous genes. The ability to
introduce deletions in a large selection of strains makes CRI-SPA a good option for studies
where one particular gene deletion is transferred to a full gene deletion library investigating
suppression or synergistic eﬀects. Another application of the eﬃcient introduction of gene
deletions using the CRI-SPA method would be for yeast cell factory construction. Often the
eﬀect of heterologous production pathways are dependent on certain gene deletions of native
genes in the production organsism. One could imagine a CRI-SPA system where speciﬁc gene
deletion and introduction of a pathway of interest could be performed simultaneously or in two
subsequent rounds of CRI-SPA. The test step of conﬁrming the correct gene deletion was easy in
the proof of concept experiment presented in this study due to the red color screen of the ade2∆
deletion, this allowed the test of the system to be performed with as a markerless deletion. In
the initial experiment testing the CRI-SPA repair in diploids 77 out of 78 strains eventually
gave the expected red phenotype. For this experiment an important control was lacking. A
diploid strain made from the UDS_2.0 without the ade2∆deletion and the CEN.PK102-5B
recipient strain should have also been transformed with the ADE2 targeting gRNA plasmid. In
this strain no deletion repair template would have been available, and the amount of red cells in
these transformants would have given an indication of the backgound of cells with another ade2
mutation giving a red phenotype. In order to ensure that the deletion of interest is transferred
and the observed phenotype is not due to a random mutation in the gRNA recipient target site,
a selective marker could be introduced in the gene deletion repair sequence of the donor strain.
It is believed that gene deletion utilizing a strong selectable marker will give very high transfer
eﬃciency.
The deletion introduced in ADE2 was not conﬁrmed by any other means than the color change,
reduced growth, and the comparison to the control plates. However this could easily be done by
PCR of genomic DNA of the ADE2 gene of the transformant strains. Using primers binding on
both sides of the gene, a reduced PCR fragment size compared to the UDS_2.0 would mean a
deletion had occurred and a WT length of the gene would indicate that the color shift is due to
a smaller mutation in the gene introduced by the CRISPR DSB. The fact that the control plate
with the gRNA plasmid and no repair fragment gave only white colonies is a good indication
58
==PAGE:70==
that repair without the deletion will not lead to a red phenotype. However it should be noticed
that this gRNA plasmid and no repair fragment control was grown on YPD + NTC which may
delay the expression of the red color compared to SC medium prepared with less adenin. Thus
the plate should have been SC with less adenin like the transformation plate, or incubated for
longer to make sure of an eﬃcient color screen.
All together CRI-SPA is believed to be a valuable and versatile tool promising for both inte-
gration of heterologous genes and for introduction of gene deletions. However, there are some
important limitations to the method that will be discussed in the following. The recipient
strains have to be haploid and of opposite mating type of the UDS, which in this proof of
concept study was only constructed with mating type α. In order for the counter-selection of
the URA3-marked donor chromosomes to work, the recipient strains must be ura. Furthermore,
in order to select for diploids, the recipient strains need to have an auxotrophic selectable
marker complementing the ones found in the CRI-SPA UDS e.g. TRP, MET or HIS, or carry a
strong selectable marker that is diﬀerent from the ones used in the CRI-SPA system e.g. the
KanMX marker. However, these criteria are met in several available yeast libraries such as the
genome-wide knockout library. It should also be noted that in the design of the CRI-SPA system
the LEU2 marker introduced in the UDS and the NatMX marker that is to be integrated to
the recipient strains was ﬂanked by loxP sites thus allowing for loop-out of the markers if that
should be desired at some point.
One of the challenges still to be addressed in the use of the CRI-SPA method is the veriﬁcation
and screening of ﬁnal clones. The experiments performed in this study have all had a visible
color readout allowing for easy identiﬁcation of either betaxathing producing cells or ade2∆cells.
However, not all biochemical pathways or gene deletions give a visible phenotype and furthermore
the color screen is not as straight forward as it may seem, since the donor background will also
exhibit the color produced due to the pathway genes potentially giving false positives on the
ﬁnal plates. During the diﬀerent experiments characterizing the CRI-SPA method no optimal
solution for veriﬁcation of the full 96-strain plate have been found. Diﬀerent approaches like
printing the library to selective plates, streak purifying a subset of strains to single colonies and
then streaking or spotting on selective plates have been used, but this process is time-consuming
and not high throughput. One idea for further experiments attempting to verify the construction
of correct recipients with the genetic modiﬁcation of interest is to make a dilution series from a
print of the CRI-SPA generated library plate into 96-well plates and subsequently plate from
a range of dilutions onto YPD plates. An optimal dilution should give single colonies. From
the single colony YPD plate(s) prints can be made to the diﬀerent selective plates and thereby
enable veriﬁcation of genotype. There is no guaranty that the correct clones are picked this way,
but it allows characterization from a single cell rather than the potential mix from the ﬁnal
YPD + NTC plates.
Another way of verifying the strains could be by PCR of genomic DNA. Using a scaled up version
of the fast extraction of genomic DNA method described by Mikkelsen et al. (2012) and a PCR
protocol using 96-PCR-tube plate, it should be possible to conﬁrm the integration/deletion of
interest. Primers should be designed to bind outside of the integration site and in the fragment
of interest. The two empty spots present in most library plates oﬀers positions where PCR
controls of choice could be applied. One of the empty spots in the library plates could be used
as a negative control adding the unmated recipient and the other empty spot could work as a
positive control by adding the CRI-SPA UDS (only for CRI-SPA UDS_2.0 systems transferring
59
==PAGE:71==
from a donor chromosome) .
If using the CRI-SPA system for cell factory construction, screening for the best producer of a
certain compound would be necessary. Screening for the production of a colored or ﬂourescent
compound is comparatively easy, but a large group of interesting compounds do not have an easy
readout. If growth in liquid media, chemical extraction and analysis by e.g. HPLC is necessary to
quantify production of the compound of interest, the screening process is suddenly a much more
time consuming step. Such an elaborate screening process could be aided in combination with
the use of a bio-sensor system, that can be used for screening of various bio-compounds(Rogers
et al., 2016). It is envisioned that the use of bio-sensors could be combined with the CRI-SPA
method for a one method system providing both the genetic changes wanted and an easy
screening procedure of the constructed strains.
It could be subject for discussion if the ﬁnal CRI-SPA protocol used in EXP. 3 (and EXP. 5)
represent an optimal CRI-SPA protocol. The new protocol consists of no less than nine diﬀerent
plates that takes between 18h and three days of incubation each. Compared to the original
plating protocol consisting of 4 plates, this prolongs the time from mating to ﬁnal strains by
approximately 5 days. There is still possible combinations of the selective media to try, if a
shorter plating protocol is of high importance. Using galactose containing 5-FOA media may
increase the destabilization of the UDS genome so only one round of YPgal selection is needed.
Using antibiotic selection on the diploid selection plates may reduce the donor and recipient
background suﬃciently so that only one plate is necessary in this step. However allowing time
for the Cas9 induced DSB and subsequent repair should also be taken into consideration at the
diploid selection step, and maybe two consecutive diploid plates does give the best circumstances
for this. When the selective pressure of the modiﬁcation of interest is kept throughout the
protocol, the second YPD + NTC selection step may not be necessary to obtain the ﬁnal pure
recipient strain, as this media was meant to remove recipient background with out the antibiotic
marker. Two rounds of selection on 5-FOA containing media would still be recommended, to
make sure that no plasmid or aneuploids have survived the ﬁrst selection on this media, which
does not actively kill the incorrect cells but rather inhibits growth. However, it should be
noted that the number of plates was not taken into account when designing the experiments for
this study, which primarily focused on characterizing the CRI-SPA method in a scaled up system.

Future Experiments
It is believed that the CRI-SPA technology can be of great importance in future research and
a number of ideas for future experiments for further developing and exploring the method is
presented below.
Finishing the construction of the IS-XII-5 targeting gRNA plasmid for transformation of CRI-

Characterization and comparison of diﬀerent UDS strains by growth experiments, comparing
plasmid based and chromosomal UDS systems.
Characterization and comparison of original UDS and UDS strains harboring Cas9 in diﬀerent

Veriﬁcation of a full generated CRI-SPA library by the plating or PCR approach described in

Screening a full generated CRI-SPA library by growing in microtiter plates using a Bio-Lector

Using the CRI-SPA system on a full library in a high throughput automation experiment using

in the replica-plating steps of the protocol.

Conclusion
In this study, a protocol applying the novel CRI-SPA method in a high throughput format was
characterized and developed. Meeting the ﬁrst aim of this study, results indicating successful
transfer of a biosynthetic pathway for betaxanthin production from a CRI-SPA UDS to a yeast
gene deletion library plate. The transfer of the betaxanthin pathway was shown using the
original CRI-SPA UDS carrying a 2 micron pathway transfer plasmid. The successful 96-strain
format experiment was using an optimized CRI-SPA pin-replica-plating protocol. Some of the
changes made to the original protocol was found to be of great importance for the method to
work in high throughput. Before mating of donor and recipient strains it is important that
the donor is grown under selective pressure to keep the transfer plasmid. Mating time should
also be restricted to prevent loss of the plasmid. A diploid selection step introduced to the
protocol seemed to be important for making the wanted DSB and thus induce correct transfer
of the pathway. On the same note a high expression of gRNA to induce the DSB was found
to be important for the transfer eﬃciency. Adding selective pressure against the recipient
background strains throughout the protocol after the mating step was also crucial. Transfer
of the betaxantin pathway to two recipient strains from the high throughput experiment were
veriﬁed by selective plating and yellow phenotype proved that the CRI-SPA method can be
scaled up to yield simultaneous modiﬁcations in multiple strains.
Another aim of this study was to further develop the CRI-SPA method by constructing a
new donor strain with novel features extending the range of experiments that the CRI-SPA
system can be applied in. The new constructed UDS_2.0 strain had the Cas9 gene and the
genetic markers LEU2 and URA3 integrated in a novel yeast integration site on chromosome
XII leaving the actively expressed integration site IS-XII-5 available for genomic modiﬁcations.
The UDS_2.1 strain carrying the betaxantin pathway and NatMX marker in IS-XII-5 was
succesfully constructed, but CRI-SPA experiments could not be conducted with this strain,
since the necessary gRNA plasmid was not ﬁnished within the time limit of this study. Using
the chromosomal location of the donor strain as the template sequence when transferring the
pathway of interest to the recipient strains allow for transfer of larger constructs than would be
feasible in an alternative plasmid based approach. Furthermore, pathways giving rise to toxic
compounds are better suited for genomic integration than expression from a 2 micron plasmid
in order to limit negative eﬀects on the growth of the strain and to limit rearangements in the
pathway which is much more frequent in a plsmid vector. A UDS carrying the betaxanthin
pathway and a selective marker in the IS-XII-5 site was constructed. The new CRI-SPA system
rely on a high copy number gRNA plasmid that targets the recipient chromosomes at the
wanted integration site. The construction of such a gRNA plasmid targveting IS-XII-5 was not
completed and the completed system is thus yet to be tested, but great advantages are expected
from the chromosomal transfer based system. The UDS_2.0 was instead investigated in a ADE2
gene deletion experiment showing that Cas9 was actively expressed from the new integration site.
Further more a markerless ade2∆gene deletion CRI-SPA experiment was conducted showing
eﬃcient transfer of the gene deletion to a recipient strain using an optimized CRI-SPA plating
protocol. In conclusion CRI-SPA can both be utilized for integration of genes of interest and for
introducing speciﬁc (markerless) gene deletions through an easy replica-plating protocol.
Furthermore, the CRI-SPA method show great potential for high throughput application, al-
though additional experiments should be conducted to improve ﬁnal veriﬁcation of correct strains
and screening for best candidates. CRI-SPA is envisioned to have a wide range of applications
62
==PAGE:74==
from yeast cell factory construction utilizing genetic diversity to ﬁnd the best candidate in a
large number of strains, to suppressor studies ﬁnding novel correlations between genes. It is
believed that CRI-SPA together with other high throughput methods for strain construction
will prove to be a great addition to conventional techniques for strain construction.
