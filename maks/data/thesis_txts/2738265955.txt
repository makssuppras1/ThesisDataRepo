Abstract
This thesis seeks to optimize the existing crash cell of the DTU Roadrunners solar vehicle for a lower
mass given displacement and stress constraints. The crash cell is made of a layered carbon-vinylester
sandwich composite and is modeled using the finite element method using the shell elements developed
by Fred Van Keulen. Three topology optimization algorithms are presented with each algorithm having
different finite element models simulating the crash loads. The first model is the simplest using a linear
static finite element model with clamped boundary conditions for modelling the crash loads. The second
model uses the Inertia Relief method to perform a pseudo dynamic analysis of the crash load. This model
accounts for the body accelerations of the heaviest components during the rapid decelerations of a crash
load. The third model uses an explicit non-linear material model for modeling the progressive ply failure
as the vehicle is loaded beyond the minimum ply strength. The topology optimization algorithm uses a
density-based approach with nodal design parameters. A projection method and the ”smart-man” robust
formulation is used to produce near-discrete designs. Even though the developed algorithms are used
to optimize automotive composite structures, the algorithms are generally applicable and have potential
to optimize other composite structures like airplane wings, fuselage, speed boat hulls, etc. The three
optimization algorithms were used to generate three different designs of an optimized crash cell. In the
end, the optimized crash cells were compared against each other by evaluating the displacement and stress
constraints using the other finite element models. The comparison showed that the optimized crash cell
design using the Inertia Relief method was the heaviest but fulfilled almost all displacement and stress
constraints across all three finite element models.
This is in contrast to the other lighter optimized
structures which exceed the constraints significantly when tested against the three finite element models.
Based on the comparison it was concluded that the optimized crash cell from the algorithm using the
inertia relief method was the best choice. This optimized crash cell design resulting in a weight reduction
of 25.39kg equivalent to a 37% reduction of the original mass.

Introduction
The DTU Roadrunners Solar Car is a student-built solar vehicle set to compete in the Bridgestone World
Solar Challenge, participating in the cruiser class. The DTU Roadrunner solar vehicle is a two-person
battery electric vehicle (BEV), utilizing solar panels as a range extender. The car is designed to combine
high efficiency with comfort and practicality.
Figure
1:
Route
map for the Bridge-
stone World Solar
Challenge.Picture
from [CHA24]
The Bridgestone World Solar Challenge is the most challenging student en-
durance race, covering 3000km across Australia, from Darwin in the north to Ade-
laide in the south, traveling at a minimum average speed of 78 km/h. A route
map of the competition can be seen in figure 1. The engineering challenges consist
of the high average speed, extreme heat and the only two allowed recharges, each
separated by a 1000 km. Usually, only 3 of over 20 cars have the necessary range
to complete the total distance of 3000 km. The winner is determined by the most
efficient car that has used the least amount of energy from external chargers. For
completing the full race distance every part of the vehicle should be greatly opti-
mized, far exceeding the efficiency of any commercially available car. The extreme
attention to efficiency and optimization is the reason for solar cars being the third
most sustainable form of transportation when measured by energy consumption
per kilometer, only beaten by bikes and e-bikes [con24]. This is even though the
solar car travels at three times the velocity of the bicycles.
Currently, the solar car is not efficient enough to complete the 3000km distance.
This project therefore seeks to increase the efficiency of the solar vehicle by
reducing the mass of the chassis, thereby directly reducing the rolling resistance. The current solar car
chassis is a full carbon composite monocoque design, meaning that the outer shell serves as a structural
part of the chassis.
The project is set to topology optimize the crash cell for a lower mass, given
minimum crash loads defined by the competition regulations.
The regulations concerning the crash
structure are very vague only specifying minimum loads the crash cell should withstand and the resulting
deflection will not impinge the occupants. Given defined displacement and stress constraints, the topology
optimization algorithm will optimize the structure for mass reduction. The algorithm will model the crash
cell using shell elements and a layered anisotropic material model. Due to the limited boundary condition
specifications, the finite element model will utilize both clamped boundary conditions and the inertia relief
method for constraining the model. The project will also include the effect of progressive ply failure and
the resulting mass reduction if certain plies are allowed to fail.
This project aims to develop a design sketch of an optimized chassis that reduces the mass by ap-
proximately 20%, equivalent to a 5% reduction in rolling resistance. The reduction in rolling resistance
would equate to an increased range of 35 km per charge at a constant 80 km/h, or an almost additional
80 km of total driving distance in the Bridgestone World Solar Challenge when accounting for the almost
2 additional charges.
This project will optimize the chassis using three different topology optimization models, one with
clamped boundary conditions, one using the inertia relief method, and one allowing for ply failure near the
A-pillar and roof. Each model has a different finite element model for predicting crash loading, meaning
that each algorithm should produce different structures best suited to that specific load condition. At the
end of the report, the three optimization algorithms will be compared, by giving the optimized structure
as an input to each optimization problem’s finite element model, and comparing how well the structures
perform using the different finite element models. Even though the developed optimization algorithms
have been implemented to optimize an automotive chassis, the algorithms can also be used to optimize
other composite structures, like airplanes, boats, and other thin-shelled structures.
This report is structured by first covering the regulations and chassis definition.
The report will
then cover the finite element model and material model, followed by covering the three optimization
algorithms. For each optimization algorithm, the report will describe the used theory and validate the
algorithm on simple test cases before using the algorithm to optimize the chassis. Lastly, the report will
compare the three optimized crash cells against each other using the three finite element models (clamped
boundary conditions, inertia relief, and progressive ply failure).

Figure 2:

The goal of the project is to minimize the weight of the current chassis, by
carving excess material off the chassis, while maintaining structural integrity.
The current chassis is made of carbon-vinylester sandwich structure using
a closed-celled foam as a core material.
Due to unknown loads from the
suspension, this project will focus on optimizing the crash cell. The crash cell
can be seen in figure 2. The crash cell is made by casting the top and bottom
shells separately and then “gluing” the two parts together using a band of
carbon fiber. The outer shell was then reinforced by a series of internal flat
plates, which have been cut out of a single large carbon fiber plate. The top
and bottom shell is divided into several different regions, having different
foam thicknesses and layups. The top shell is further reinforced by a series
of unidirectional carbon fiber bands, carrying the load at the most critical
regions (A and B pillars). The different strength regions can be seen in figure 3, with the layup for
each region being defined in appendix 1. All fiber directions are defined with respect to the driving axis
(denoted z-axis), using the method to be presented in section 6. Note that for aerodynamical reasons can
material initially only be removed from the internal reinforcing plates seen in figure 3c. The project will
also cover the situation where the optimization algorithm is allowed to remove material from external
surfaces.

Figure 3: Figure of the different layup regions. Each color corresponds to a different layup. The red

The carbon-vinylester composite is created from unidirectional Zoltek 50K carbon fibers and Atlac
6215 vinylester both supplied by the sponsor Monofiber. The closed-celled foam is Divinycell MC80 PVC
foam, which has cut grooves acting as a distribution medium. Note that the foam’s closed-celled structure
greatly minimizes the foam’s resin absorption compared to an open-celled foam type. The composite was
manufactured with the goal of a fiber-to-matrix volume fraction of around 50%, but the actual volume
fraction has been difficult to measure due to the varying resin absorption of the distribution grooves. The
material properties of the carbon-vinylester composite and the closed-cell foam can be seen in table 1.
The mass of the full chassis including the bonnet and tailgate is less than 140 kg, with the mass of the
crash cell accounting for 68 kg.

necessary space required for the occupants, with only the last rule 2.11.5 covering crash safety. Rule
2.11.5 specifies that the occupant cell must protect the occupants from frontal impacts, side impacts, and
rollover impacts. These impact loads are defined for a finite element analysis as:
• Frontal: Impact; a 5g load, opposing the direction of travel, applied to the front of the occupant
cell in an area less than 250 mm high and less than 600 mm wide
• Side impact: a 5 g load into the side of the occupant cell, applied adjacent to the driver’s torso in
an area less than 250 mm high and less than 600 mm wide
• Top impact: a load with components 5 g down, 1.5 g sideways, and 4 g backwards, applied at each
possible area of contact between the occupant cell and the ground when the occupant cell is upside
down; the contact area for each test load must have a diameter less than 150 mm.
with the loads being based on the fully laden mass of the solar car. For these loads, the occupant cell
structure must not fail, and any deformation must not impinge any occupants. This is interpreted as the
crash cell must not have a complete laminate failure, and must not deflect so the chassis comes in contact
with the occupants. The occupant space allows for a bit over 25 mm deflection before there may be
contact between the occupants and the crash cell, but for simplicity, the maximum deflection is set to 25
mm. This can be formulated as a topology optimization problem, with the objective of reducing the crash
cell mass given displacement and stress constraints. The full description of the topology optimization
problem is covered in chapter 2.
Note that the regulations do not specify any boundary conditions,
specific load locations, or even the type of FE analysis. The full text of regulation 2.11.5 can be found in
appendix 2. Initially, the finite element analysis will be a static linear analysis with clamped boundary
conditions but will be expanded to a pseudo-dynamic analysis using the inertia relief method, and a static
material non-linear analysis including progressive ply failure.
Figure 4: Load definitions and areas.
Instead of applying the load to all possible locations of con-
tact, the project simplifies the loading to 8 load cases; two load
cases for the frontal impact, two load cases for the side impact,
and four load cases for the rollover impact. The load areas and
numbering can be seen in figure 4. Note that the second frontal
impact is at the connection between the crash cell and the front
chassis and is included due to concerns about the load being
concentrated in the region in case of a frontal impact.
This
load case is not mandated by the regulations but is included
as an additional point of safety. Initially, the solar car was de-
signed for a 41kWh battery pack resulting in a fully loaded mass
of 750 kg, where a safety factor of 100kg has been added due
to the mass of some components being unknown. However, the
new rules presented in June 2024 only allow a battery of 15 kWh, reducing the full laden mass to be
below 650 kg, including the same 100kg safety factor. The result of the new regulations is a chassis that
is greatly over-dimensioned for the new lower mass, implying a large potential for weight savings.
Figure 5: Fail state for top impact load 1 & 2.
During the initial design phase of the chassis, it was
found that 50 kg could be saved if a number of layers were
allowed to fail during the crash. It is only load 1 and 2
that results in ply failure, with load 1 resulting in 8 layers
failing near the A-pillar and load 2 resulting in 7 layers
failing at the top of the door. The areas with ply failure
for the two loads can be seen in figure 5. Note that the
degraded structure still has the load capacity to carry the
full load and is therefore safe. Because the failed layers
will result in the stress constraint being exceeded when
using a standard linear finite element analysis, the top
impact loads for the optimization models with clamped
boundary conditions and using the inertia relief method
are scaled down to an equivalent full-laden mass of 400
kg. For this load no plies have failed and the stress constraint has not been exceeded.

Similar Projects and New Contributions
This section is a short history of automotive chassis optimization based on the review article [Mar20].
This section will further cover this project’s contributions to the automotive optimization scene. The
outlook and discussion of what’s next in the automotive optimization scene will be presented at the end
of this report.
The automotive sector is a fiercely competitive market, where companies compete to produce the most
comfortable, safest, and energy-efficient car. This is done while keeping the material and manufacturing
costs at an absolute minimum. For the chassis, this translates to having the highest torsional stiffness,
which can withstand any crash loads while weighing the least. These are simple objectives that can be
translated into optimization problems.
Figure 6: Simplified beam
model of a body structure
[Fen81]
The first example of chassis optimization is a study by Fenyes in 1981
[Fen81], using sizing optimization on a tube chassis with a fixed topology
as seen in figure 6. The study was set to optimize individual tube gauges
and materials to maximize the stiffness-to-weight and stiffness-to-cost ratios.
He found that creating a chassis of 53kg of steel and 27 kg of aluminum
would make the structure only 2kg heavier than an all-aluminum structure
while having the same stiffness. This design had both a cost and weight
advantage to the all-steel design, and a significant cost saving to the all-
aluminum design. One Important takeaway from Fenyes’s article is that the
method can only be applied to a known topology, meaning the method is
most suitable in the late design process for finetuning the final geometry.
The first true topology optimization of an automotive chassis came in
1993 with a study by Sakurada [Osh93], where he performed topology optimization on the underbody
of the chassis. This was followed up in 1995 by Yang’s and Chahande’s topology optimization of a full
chassis [Cha95].
Figure 7:
Topology op-
timisation of full chassis
[Cha95].
Up to 1993 topology optimization had been performed on ladder frame chas-
sis, but these models were not very interesting due to the ladder frame’s
ancient design and drawbacks compared to the modern unibody design. The
objective of the study by Sakurande was to improve the torsional stiffness of
the underbody structure while reducing its weight. He managed to improve
the torsional stiffness by 10%, while reducing the weight by 6%. Yang’s and
Chahande’s study of the full automotive chassis was performed using the
TOP framework developed by the Ford Motor Company. The TOP frame-
work could optimize a chassis for either compliance, eigenfrequency, weight,
or displacement, subject to either material or compliance constraints. The
initial model used by Yang and Chahande was quite complex using many
different element types for each problem, combining quad (Shell), beam, and
bar elements into a single model. The used mesh and resulting structure
can be seen in figure 7. Due to limited element resolution and the choice
of element locations, finer design details were non-existent yielding minimal
insight from the structure, with the resulting structure being very bound by
the design space mesh. Still, it showed the algorithm managed to increase
the stiffness by 165 % at a cost of 11% additional weight.
The next decades were mainly driven by increased mesh refinement from computational improvements
with the article by Reed [Ree02] being a representative example. The objective of the article was to
optimize the chassis’ torsional stiffness to weight ratio. Reed used 3D solid elements to model the chassis
as seen in figure 8. Note that design space has been greatly limited by the fixed components, like the
cabin, engine, suspension, and boot space.
This means that the algorithm had very limited design
possibilities, with the resulting optimized chassis being very similar to the design space. However, due to
the manufacturing process used in the automotive industry, the geometry had to be converted from solid,
to hollow tubes and fin shells. Even though the algorithm was greatly limited, the resulting structure
had a stiffness of 35000 Nm/deg, while weighing 100 kg. The stiffness is 2-3 times higher than similar
commercial vehicles at the time.

In 2010 the optimization scene moved away from using fixed boundary conditions, to utilizing the
inertia relief method; a method used for optimizing freely floating objects like airplanes. The change
of boundary conditions allowed for a computationally cheap method for analyzing crash-worthiness,
without resorting to a full dynamic finite element model. Among the first articles to utilize the inertia
relief method is a study by Cavazzuti et al. [Mor11], which compared the fixed clamping to the inertia
relief method, when optimizing the chassis of a Ferrari 458 Italia for a lower mass. The study combined
a series of optimization constraints concerning bending stiffness, torsional stiffness, 32 local stiffnesses,
modal response, and a single load case concerning frontal impact. The study performed 13 different
optimization sequences combining the different constraints and alternating the boundary conditions of
the 32 local stiffness constraints from clamped constraints to using the inertia relief method. A figure of
two resulting topologies using the clamped constraint and the inertia relief method can be seen in figure
9. Overall the studies found a significant weight reduction from changing the boundary conditions from
clamped to using the inertia relief method. However, the study expressed concerns about comparing the
masses of each study as the individual methods had different optimization parameters and different levels
of convergence.
Figure 9: Optimization of chassis using clamped boundaries and the inertia relief method [Mor11]
With the introduction of BEV the chassis weight, sustainability and performance have become of
increasing interest.
The main focus has been on the application of so-called ”infinite materials” like
carbon fiber composites, that could prolong the chassis lifetime for several battery generations. This
project seeks to contribute to the development by creating methods for topology optimizing a multilayered
carbon composite chassis given defined crash loads. In this project, the capabilities of the introduced
algorithm will be explored by fine-tuning an existing carbon composite chassis design, but the algorithm
and the associated methods can also be applicable in earlier design phases. The goal of this project is to
create methods for optimizing the mass of a shell structure given certain crash loads. Furthermore, the
project will also explore the result and potential mass reduction of allowing ply failure during the crash
loading. Similar to the study by Cavazzuti et al. [Mor11], the project will compare the chassis using
clamped boundaries, the inertia relief method, and allowance of ply failure. This comparison will not

This section includes a short description of the topology optimization
algorithm. Similar to [Tr¨a+24] the design variables xd are defined as

figure 2.1. The algorithm starts by filtering the nodal design variables

where η is the threshold value and β controls the smoothness of the

of the step function. Notice that a high value of β results in steep
gradients near x = η but shallower gradients near x = 0 and x = 1,
resulting in the design effectively ”locking up”. It is therefore a better
procedure to start at β close to zero and gradually increase it as the design converges.
The element design values x are used for assembling the stiffness matrix and determining the displace-
ment vector u. The element design variables and the displacement vector are then used for calculating
the objective value m, the constraints ϕ as well as their gradients (∂m/∂x and ∂ϕ/∂x). The gradients
are converted to be functions of the original nodal design variables xd by the inverse procedures of the
Heaviside projection filter and PDE filter.
Lastly, the nodal design variables xd are updated using the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA)
[Sva87]. The process is continued until the design has converged defined as the maximum change in the
nodal design variables xd.
The algorithm was initially implemented in MATLAB, because of its excellent debugging tools, and
later implemented in C++ for a significant speed-up. For the Matlab implementation of the algorithm,
the project uses the MMA implementation by Krister Svanberg (Version September 2007), and the C++
implementation uses the MMA implementation by Niels Aage (Beta version 0.99).
By timing each
implementation, it was found that the assembly of the stiffness matrix was around 5-20 times faster
using the C++ implementation compared to the Matlab implementation, depending on the problem size
and number of layers. Similarly, the C++ was 10-25 times faster at evaluating the stresses and 10-20
times faster at evaluating the sensitivities compared to the C++ implementation. However, the C++
implementation was not faster at solving the equation system Ku = f as Matlab already calls a heavily
optimized C library.

Introduction of robust formulation
Most of the optimized stress ball structures have some grey-scale areas in which the algorithm sets the
design variable between 0 and 1, resulting in an unclear picture of where to keep material and where to
remove it. To combat the grey-scale areas the ”smart-man” robust formulation is introduced. The usual
robust formulation introduces two additional structures; an eroded and a dilated version of the original
structure, with the optimization algorithm being modified to a min-max formulation set to minimize the
structure with the highest objective value.

(b) Including the robust formulation

each load had different boundary conditions. The reduction in matrix factorization results in a significant
speed up allowing a much higher mesh resolution at the same computation time.

Other optimization models
This section covers two other optimization models. The first model uses the inertia relief method as a

Inertia Relief
Inertia relief is a method for performing static finite element analysis on a freely floating body, which is
usually only determined through a dynamic analysis. The technique can also be used for bodies where the
boundary conditions are unknown. The concept of inertia relief is to approximate the rigid body inertia
that perfectly balances the applied load, effectively having a self-balancing system.
The equilibrium
equation with inertia relief is:
Ku = f −Mar

optimization problem and then test it on simple test cases.

Influence of pin-pointing location
When using the inertia relief method, the compliance and element stresses will remain independent of
the pin-point, but the displacements will not. This is because the pin-pointing sets a reference frame for
the displacement. This means that the nodal displacement can be minimized by setting the pin-pointing
between the applied force and the point masses! This makes the method unfair, as one can select an
advantageous reference frame for the optimization algorithm. One could make the comparison fair by
considering the nodal displacements relative to the mean displacement of the hole structure. However,
this project will instead select the pin constraints, so the displacements become relative to an important
object (like the driver and occupant), thereby making the displacments fair. Four different pin-pointing
locations are considered to check the influence of the pin-pointing location. The considered locations are
at the center of the stress ball, at the applied load, and at the point mass, and are referred to as ”center”,
”top” and ”bottom” respectively. The pinned boundary conditions for these three locations are chosen
close to each other being within a radius of 5mm. The last condition denoted ”wide” has the boundary
conditions still at the center of the stress ball but located at a much greater distance from each other
within a sphere with a radius of 45mm. The pin-pointing locations are seen in figure 3.3.

16
Inclusion of Ply Failure
As described in section 4, the chassis is designed to allow some laminates to fail near the A-pillars in
case of a rollover impact. This is the reason for the reduction of the top loads for the fixed boundary
condition design and the design using the inertia relief method. Now the full load is applied, resulting in
some plies failing near the A-pillars.
The challenge when optimizing with progressive ply failure is to evaluate the failed state using an
implicit method and keep a history of the layer states for calculating the constraint sensitivities as the
structure is partially failing. This method is very complex being both difficult to implement and very
computationally expensive.
Instead, this project will use a simplified approach, which evaluates the failed state using an explicit
method, and then performs the topology optimization on the failed state. The failed state is then updated
every certain number of iterations. This means that the optimization algorithm has a moving target that
updates every certain number of iterations. This can result in the algorithm oscillating between two
targets and not converging toward a design.

Comparison of models and designs
This section seeks to compare the optimization models and determine which optimized crash cell is the
best. The comparison is performed by inserting each optimized crash cell into the three finite element
models used for the three optimization algorithms. For each optimized structure the displacement and
stress constraints are evaluated using the three different finite element models. For convenience, the
three optimization algorithms presented in this project will be referred to (in order of presentation in this
thesis) as Clamped, Inertia relief, and Ply failure. The three finite element models uses the same input
parameters as specified for optimizing each crash cell. There are two exceptions, with the first being the
approximation of the max function is substituted with the actual max function and the second is the
battery masses are removed from the finite element model using the inertia relief method for the two
optimized crash cells from the Clamped and Ply failure optimization algorithms. This is because these
two optimized crash cells have no material at the interface of the battery, which results in the battery not
being fixed to the crash cell. The resulting displacement constraints and masses for the three optimized
crash cells alongside the original crash cell can be seen in table 3.8. It should be noted that the Ply failure
crash cell has the lowest weight being around 2kg lighter than the other designs, whereas the Clamped
and Inertia relief have almost the same mass. The optimized crash cell from the Clamped algorithm
fulfills the displacement constraints using the inertia relief method, however, collapses when applying
the full load and accounting for ply failure, resulting in displacements far exceeding the constraint. The
resulting crash cell from the Ply failure algorithm almost fulfills all displacement constraints using the
”clamped” finite element model, with only the side load 5 exceeding the constraint. However, this crash

twofold for the roll-over impacts. The optimized crash cell from the Inertia relief algorithm performs the
best across all three finite element models with the crash cell only exceeding the displacement constraints

Chapter 4
Conclusion & Outlook
The project set out to optimize the existing solar car chassis for a weight reduction of 20%, given regulatory
crash safety constraints. Due to unknowns of suspension loads and geometry, the project focused on only
optimizing the crash cell. As part of the project, three topology optimization algorithms with three
different finite element models have been developed, each modeling the crash load differently. The first
optimization algorithm used a static linear finite element model with clamped boundary conditions. The
second optimization algorithm used the inertia relief method to do a pseudo-dynamic analysis of the
crash, taking into account the heaviest components being the occupants and the battery pack.
The
last optimization algorithm used a non-linear material model for modeling the progressive ply failure as
the chassis partially failed during loading. In the end, the optimized crash cells were compared across
the other finite element models, evaluating the displacement and stress constraints for each combination
of the finite element model and crash cell design. It was found that the optimization algorithm using
progressive ply failure resulted in the structure with the lowest mass of less than 41 kg, a reduction of
40% compared to the original crash cell. However, it was found that the optimized crash cell from the
algorithm using the inertia relief method, was the best across all three finite element models. This crash
cell fulfilled almost all constraints across the three finite element models. This crash cell has a weight of
42.9 kg, equivalent to a weight reduction of 37.3%. The crash cell weight reduction results in a decrease
in rolling resistance of 4.5%. This is very close to the initial target of a 5% decrease, however, this target
was set using the old 2023 regulations. The result of the crash cell weight reduction is estimated to
increase the range by 23km per charge.
Note that the optimized crash cell cannot be directly implemented as the project has excluded the
interface to the suspension, which could result in significant design changes. Furthermore, the chassis
torsional stiffness has not been optimized, which is important for the driving dynamics.
Academically the project does not introduce any new topology optimization or finite element methods
not previously described in the literature. However, the academic contribution lies in the development of a
topology optimization model that incorporates several finite element and topology optimization methods,
including the Heavised projection filter, augmented PDE-filter, smart-man robust formulation, anisotropic
material properties with the Tsai-Wu-based stress constraint, and the Inertia Relief method. Furthermore,
the project does a comparison of topology optimization using a static finite element model and the pseudo-
dynamic model using the Inertia relief method on a more advanced problem than previously seen in the
literature.
It should be noted that the crash cell with the largest weight reduction was the optimized crash
cell where the algorithm was allowed to remove material from the outer surfaces. This resulted in a
weight reduction of almost 62%, far exceeding the initial target. This weight reduction would result in
an additional range of 39 km per charge. This means the biggest weight reduction is found when the
optimization algorithm has the largest design freedom.
Currently, the topology optimization models used for the automotive sector are primarily used in late
design phases, having many boundaries that limit the potential of the optimization algorithm. A goal
for this field could be to make algorithms that can be used earlier in the design phase, resulting in even
better structures. This is the same conclusion of the review article [Mar20].
Interesting algorithms that could be used at earlier design stages would include optimization of the
fiber angle and shape optimization of internal geometry alongside the thickness optimization given in
this project. It should be noted that optimizing the geometry of the outer shell is not recommended
since the aerodynamic drag has the largest influence on the energy efficiency of modern battery electric
45
==PAGE:47==
Master Thesis
July 1, 2024
vehicles, far exceeding the potential savings in the rolling resistance. Simultaneously optimization of the
aerodynamic shell and the chassis could be an option however the algorithm would be very complex,
with probably limited reduction in rolling resistance and drag compared to the shell and chassis being
optimized separately
The topology optimization algorithms using 3D-solid elements often have the most design flexibility
and the resulting structures have the best objective value. However, due to manufacturing complications
and associated costs, the 3D-solid structures must be converted into equivalent shell or beam models.
Meaning that a shell optimization algorithm is still necessary, for optimizing the final geometry.
The author strongly believes that the future for the automotive industry lies in creating the most
energy-efficient vehicles, where both drag and weight are at an absolute minimum. The potential for
weight reduction and increased energy efficiency by using composites far exceeds the upfront emissions
compared to steel alloys. However, the gains from using composites like carbon-epoxy composites are
very limited, if the chassis is not optimized for the composite’s material properties.

Note on auto-gradient using deep learning toolbox.
This section covers the time penalty of using the auto-gradient feature from the MATLAB deep learning
toolbox compared to the manually derived gradients. To evaluate the time penalty by timing the com-
putation time for calculating the sensitivities for the MBB-beam test case and the Hemisphere test case
seen in figure A.2.
(a) MBB beam test case
(b) Hemisphere test case
Figure A.2: Test cases used for comparing the performance of MATLAB’s Auto-gradient feature to
manually derived gradients.
The computation time is measured using MATLAB’s tic-toc function. Note that the measures com-
putation time only includes direct sensitivities and the right-hand side for the adjoint problems. This
means that the adjoint problem and the sensitivity terms including the adjoint vector are excluded. For
each test case, the number of elements is varied, for evaluating how the methods scale to larger problems.
Each measurement is taken as the average of the first 10 iterations with three repetitions. Figures of the
resulting computation time per element/ dof for the mass, displacement, and stress constraint can be seen
in figure A.3. For the simple mass constraint, the time penalty for using the auto-gradient function is
