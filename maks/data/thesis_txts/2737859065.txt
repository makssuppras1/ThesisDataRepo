Abstract
This project investigates the material, fracture, and mechanical properties of fly­ash based
geopolymer mortar (GPM), comparing them to those of Ordinary Portland mortar (OPM)
with a comparable mix design. The interest in using geopolymer concrete has increased
over the last decade,one of the reasons being its utilization of fly ash, a byproduct sourced
from coal­fired plants with a significantly lower CO2 footprint compared to ordinary cement.
Incorporating geopolymer concrete in the industry could globally reduce CO2 emissions.
Fly ash serves as the main binder in the mix composition of GPM. However, a major
challenge arises as current building codes and standards are based on Ordinary Portland
cement. Therefore, it is essential to investigate the material and mechanical behavior of
geopolymer mortars to determine if existing standards and codes can be applied or if new
ones need to be developed.
The project uses Class F fly ash, which contains less than 20% CaO, an alkaline activator
with a 1:1 ratio by weight of an 8M solution sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and sodium silicate
solution (Na2SiO3 ­ ’waterglass’). GPM specimens were cured at 65°C at different dura­
tions and then ambient cured at 20°C with 90% relative humidity. The OPM was water­
cured at 20°C 24 hours after demolding. Various mix designs were investigated to achieve
comparable workability between OPM and GPM. The selected mix designs and numerous
curing methods were examined to achieve desirable and comparable strengths between
the two mortars. The compressive strength development was assessed using 25x25x25
mm3 cube specimens. The mechanical properties of Ø100mm x 200 mm cylindrical spec­
imens were tested to determine ultimate strength, Young’s modulus, and Poisson’s ratio.
Larger 100x100x100 mm3 cube specimens were tested using a wedge splitting test to
determine tensile strength, Young’s modulus, and the post cracking behaviour. The ten­
sile and post­cracking properties were obtained through a semi­analytical method utilizing
inverse analysis of the test results.
The same mixing process was attempted for GPM as for OPM, but due to differing material
behavior, GPM required a longer mixing time. The workability of GPM was very viscous
compared to OPM, affecting the measurement of workability through standard methods
such as consistency by Plunger Penetration (EN 10154) and the Flow Table Test (ASTM
C 1437). The strength development of GPM after heat curing was slow compared to
OPM. Heat curing GPM without sealing the specimens resulted in high initial compres­
sive strength, which decreased over time when stored in the ambient environment. To
understand this phenomenon, the weight loss due to water evaporation from the speci­
mens was compared with their initial strengths. Correlation between the strength of cubes
and cylinders was not achieved for GPM as compared to OPM, leading to variations in
recorded maximum strengths for mechanical properties. Geopolymer mortar exhibited
approximately 100% lower stiffness compared to ordinary Portland cement when nor­
malized to the compression strength. This was also consistent with literature indicating
lower stiffness for GPM at the same compressive strength. Poisson’s ratio for GPM was
higher than values reported in the literature. The normalized tensile strengths were sim­
ilar for OPM and GPM. However, both had generally lower tensile strengths compared
to those reported in the literature and standards. Quad­linear curves effectively captured
the softening behavior of both OPM and GPM. The tensile strengths determined using the
semi­analytical approach are generally lower compared to those obtained with a simple
analytical approach.

1
Introduction
Geopolymer concrete (GPC) holds significant potential as a low­carbon emission material
for the future of the construction industry. This potential is primarily due to GPC’s use of
various aluminosilicate resources, such as clay, or waste materials like ground granulated
blast furnace slag or fly ash [1]. In this project, GPC will be investigated, with fly ash
serving as the main binder in its composition. Fly ash is a by­product collected from
coal­fired power plants. Fly­ash­based geopolymer is more widely known and easier to
work with than clay binders, which is one of the reasons it is being investigated in this
project. The carbon footprint of Portland Cement is substantial, with an estimated release
of 0.6­0.7 tons of CO2 for every ton of Portland Cement produced, contributing to 8%
of global CO2 emissions from cement production [2, 3]. Given the increasing interest in
reducing CO2 emissions, especially since the EU has stated that they need to reduce CO2
emissions by 55% by 2030 [4], new low­carbon emission materials could be key players
in achieving these goals.
One might wonder why not simply eliminate the concrete industry and use alternative
materials such as wood and fibers. However, concrete is a fundamental building mate­
rial known for its strength, durability, and versatility. It plays a crucial role in the struc­
tural integrity of buildings, bridges, roads, and other large infrastructures. Replacing con­
crete with alternative materials that provide the same level of performance and longevity
would be challenging Additionally, there is the perspective of costs. Concrete is relatively
inexpensive compared to many alternative materials. Shifting to other materials could
significantly increase construction costs, making large infrastructure projects financially
unfeasible [5].
In contrast, GPC offers numerous advantages over Ordinary Portland Concrete, including
earlier strength development, increased fire resistance, and a reduction of the carbon
footprint by around 50% compared to OPC. [6]. While OPC has been in use for centuries
with well­documented properties and is an integral part of established building codes, the
same cannot be said for GPC. Many aspects of GPC remain unknown and have yet to be
thoroughly researched [6].
In conclusion, the significance of geopolymers, especially those with 0% cement, lies in
their potential to transform the construction industry by offering a sustainable, durable, and
versatile alternative to traditional materials. As the global community increasingly empha­
sizes environmental responsibility and resource efficiency, the adoption of geopolymer
technology represents a crucial step towards a greener and more resilient future.
1.1
Objective
The objective of this project is to investigate and validate the fundamental material prop­
erties of geopolymer mortar (GPM) containing 0% cement through a series of compre­
hensive mechanical and fracture experiments. The results will be compared to those of
<Ordinary Portland mortar (OPM) to assess the performance and potential advantages of
GPM. The geopolymer mortar investigated will have the following material composition:
Fly ash (class F), 1:1 ratio of 8M sodium hydroxide and sodium silicate, and various ratios
of the sand fraction 0­2 mm.

Literature Review
Multiple sources of existing literature have been reviewed, focusing mainly on the me­
chanical properties of geopolymer concrete. The purpose of this literature review is to
understand the existing results on the mechanical behavior of geopolymer concrete, with
a primary focus on the relationship between compressive strength, tensile strength, and
Poisson’s ratio. There are numerous ways to treat geopolymer concrete, such as us­
ing different curing methods or adjusting the concentration of the NaOH solution and the
ratio of sodium hydroxide to sodium silicate. These factors can play significant roles in
determining the strength of geopolymer, leading to varied results from different sources.
In the upcoming investigations, the data have been color­coded to indicate the curing
conditions of the geopolymer concrete: ambient, heat, and mixed curing. ”Mixed” indi­
cates a combination of both curing methods. Data from S. Ridha et al. [7] are for heat­
treated geopolymers, but the mechanical properties are measured under extreme condi­
tions where the temperature has been raised to simulate the environment of an oil well.
The formulas stated in Eurocode 2 (EC2) for estimating the stiffness (Young’s modulus)
and tensile strength have been plotted as references to the geopolymer data.

3.1.1
Fly ash
Origin and Composition
Fly ash is a byproduct of the combustion of pulverized coal in electric power generating
plants. The residual ash is collected from the combustion gases using electrostatic pre­
cipitators or other particle filtration equipment before the flue gases reach the chimneys.
The chemical composition of fly ash varies considerably but all fly ashes contain substan­
tial amounts of silicon dioxide (SiO2), aluminum oxide (Al2O3), and iron oxide (Fe2O3).
Depending on the source and makeup of the coal being burned, the components of fly
ash may vary significantly, affecting its suitability as a cement replacement material in
concrete [15] or 0% cement concrete.

Physical Properties
The physical properties of fly ash include its fineness, density, and color. Fly ash par­
ticles are typically spherical and range in size from less than 1 micrometer to over 100
micrometers. The density of fly ash generally ranges between 2100 and 3000 kg/m3 The
fineness of fly ash is often measured by the amount retained on a 45­micrometer sieve.
Finer fly ashes generally contribute to improved workability and reduced water demand
in concrete mixtures [15, 16]

Chemical Analysis
Fly ash is classified into two main types, Class F and Class C, based on the chemical
composition. Class F fly ash is produced from burning anthracite or bituminous coal and
typically contains less than 20% lime (CaO). Class C fly ash is produced from burning
lignite or sub­bituminous coal and typically contains more than 20% lime. The major con­
stituents of fly ash are silica, alumina, iron oxide, and lime. Minor constituents include
magnesia, sulfur trioxide, and alkalis. The presence of these components affects the
pozzolanic activity of fly ash, which is a measure of its ability to react with calcium hydrox­
ide in the presence of water to form compounds possessing cementitious properties [15].

Solutions

8M Sodium Hydroxide Solution (NaOH)

3.2
Reaction Between Alkaline Activator and Fly Ash in
Geopolymers
An alkaline activator is necessary for geopolymers, unlike Ordinary Portland Cement
(OPC), because the activator facilitates the dissolution of silica and alumina from fly ash.
This dissolution is essential for the polycondensation reactions that form the aluminosil­
icate network, providing the material with its strength and durability. Fly ash requires
an alkaline environment to undergo these reactions, as opposed to OPC, where clinker
minerals harden through the hydration process [17, 18, 19].
3.3
Mix Designs
The mix designs used in this project have been designed to be comparable between ce­
ment and fly ash. It is acknowledged that cement mortar is a three­component concrete
consisting of cement, water, and sand, but geopolymer mortar is a four­component con­
crete consisting of fly ash, water, solid alkaline activators, and sand. Thus, a common
ground must be set before any mix design comparison can be made. In this project, the
alkaline solution, consisting of water and solids, is considered as one component, making
the geopolymer mortar a three­component concrete, now consisting of fly ash, alkaline
solution, and sand. For the decisions and dilemmas encountered during the process of
adjusting and finding the right mix design for geopolymer mortar, and achieving a compa­
rable mix between cement and fly ash mortar, please see Appendix J.6.
A constant volumetric ratio between sand and binder for each mix comparison has been
chosen because the densities of fly ash and cement are different. The same approach
has been applied to the liquid­to­binder ratio between the mixes, ensuring consistency,
see Figure 3.3.1.

Geopolymer
During the project, several curing methods for geopolymer mortar (GPM) were investi­

again. Right after the last day in the curing room, the test specimens were tested.

Analytical Approach
The data received from the actual test includes the vertical force from the test machine
and the strain from the clip gauge. This data is then transformed for further analysis.
Splitting Force
The slitting force Psp is the horizontal force that tears the specimen apart. As illustrated on
Figure 3.9.4, the force originating from the piston Pv, is transferred into a normal force N
perpendicular to the surface of the wedge. By taking the horizontal component of N the
splitting force Psp can be found. The force from the roller friction is denoted as Pf. Based
on Østergaard’s findings [20], the influence of roller friction on the results is minimal, with
µ values ranging from 0.1% to 0.5%. If this factor is ignored, an error margin of 0.4% to
1.9% may occur. By treating this information as accurate, an estimation can significantly
reduce the error. By selecting an average friction coefficient of 0.3%, the error is reduced
to within ± 0.75%. [20]

Results and Discussion
This chapter presents and discusses the results obtained during the experimental phases
of the project. First, the material properties are examined. Next, the mechanical properties
are analyzed, including the experimental challenges encountered during the execution
of the experiments. Finally, the literature review is revisited considering the mechanical

4.1
Material Properties
4.1.1
The Considerations Behind Material Choices
Fly ash
The fly ash used in the experiments might be unique due to its chemical composition.
Using fly ash from a different power plant in geopolymer mortar/concrete can significantly
impact the properties and performance of the final product. Fly ash from each power plant
can vary considerably due to differences in the type of coal used, combustion conditions,
and other operational factors. These variations can influence the chemical and physical
characteristics of the fly ash, such as its particle size distribution, mineral composition,
and reactivity [1]. For example, high silica content (SiO2) contributes to the formation
of a strong, durable geopolymer matrix. It plays a crucial role in the geopolymerization
reaction, enhancing the compressive strength and durability of the concrete. The amount
of silicon dioxide (SiO2) in the fly ash used is very high (51.75%) compared to the silicon
dioxide content in the Ordinary Portland Cement, which is 19.21%, see Table 3.1.1.
Because the fly ash from each power plant is unique, it is important to test geopolymer
concrete using the specific fly ash. Consequently, creating standards for geopolymer
concrete might be challenging if the material properties vary with different sources of fly
ash.
The project utilizes Emi Fly Ash, supplied by Emineral A/S and sourced from FYNSVÆR­
KET, a pure coal­fired power plant in Denmark. The chemical properties of this fly ash
have been thoroughly investigated, enabling comparison with fly ash from other sources.
FYNSVÆRKET will cease fly ash production in 2024, leaving NORDJYLLANDSVÆRKET
A/S as the only provider of fly ash in Denmark until 2028. Both sources produce class F fly
ash. After 2028, there will be no production of Emi Fly Ash in Denmark. However, many
coal­fired power plants are still in use today in large parts of the world. For instance, in
2022, China had the highest number of coal­fired power plants. Globally, there were 1981
coal­fired power plants in 2020, with China’s installed capacity at 1095 GW, representing
over half of the global capacity, which is approximately 2130 GW in 2024 for coal­fired
power plants [24].
Alkaline Activator
Studies show that varying the NaOH molarity affects the mechanical strength and durabil­
ity of geopolymers. For instance, one study investigated fly ash­based geopolymers with
NaOH concentrations of 8M, 10M, 12M, and 14M [25]. The results indicated that 12M
NaOH provided an optimal balance, yielding the highest compressive strength and dura­
bility properties. The reason 8M NaOH was chosen for this project was to save materials,
thereby reducing the cost of producing geopolymer. Research also indicates that a 1:1
NaOH to Na2SiO3 ratio provides good compressive strength and workability. However,
increasing the Na2SiO3 ratio (e.g., to 1:2.5) can improve compressive strength and re­
duce setting time, which can be beneficial for applications such as 3D printed GPM [26].

Comparative Analysis of Fly Ash and Cement Pollution
Because fly ash is already a byproduct of electricity production and it is not always feasible
to change the energy source, fly ash will continue to be produced regardless. This is why
fly ash can be considered a more ’green’ solution, as it is essentially ’just’ waste material.
However, looking directly at the CO2 emissions: For every ton of coal burned in a power
plant, approximately 2.4 tons of CO2 are emitted. This process also generates about 0.1
tons of fly ash as a byproduct. Therefore, while fly ash itself does not directly produce
CO2, its creation is associated with the CO2 emissions from coal combustion [27]. When
looking at cement production, approximately 0.78 tons of CO2 are emitted for every ton
of cement produced [3].
4.1.2
Exploring Material Behavior During the Casting Process for GPM
4.1.3
Change in Consistency During Mixing
The standard followed for mixing was a modified version of ”DS/EN 192­1”. For the exact
procedure, please refer to Appendix D. It was observed that when GPM was mixed, the
mixture was initially very rigid and then softened with continued mixing. This could be
due to the rapid formation of aluminosilicate gels, which contribute to the initial hardening
[28, 29]. However, as mixing continues, the system undergoes changes due to the on­
going dissolution of fly ash particles and the redistribution of reaction products. Longer
mixing leads to a decrease in viscosity and a subsequent softening of the mixture. This is
attributed to the breakdown of the initial gel structures and the formation of more stable,
less viscous phases in the mixture [28, 29].
This also meant that mixing time was a significant factor in GPM’s workability. The longer
the mixing, the more workable the substance became, and vice versa for OPM. OPM has
a very rapid setting time; thus, DS/EN 192­1 states that mixing should be done within 3­4
minutes. However, since greater consistencies for GPM were achieved with longer mixing
times, it was challenging to use the standard for mixing GPM, as it should be mixed for
longer than the standard suggests to achieve optimal workability.
4.1.4
Benefits of Slow Hardening of GPM Before Oven Curing
Unlike OPM, the GPM investigated in this project does not harden quickly after casting,
before inserting the mortar in the oven. This is a significant benefit because it is not
necessary to rush through the process. This minimizes the risk of failures during the
casting process. Additionally, the slow hardening of GPM allows for better control over the
material properties [30]. It ensures a more uniform consistency and reduces the likelihood
of cracks and other defects.
A big difference between the consistencies of GPM and OPM was also observed. Unlike
OPM, the GPM had an viscous consistency and was self­leveling. Meaning that if a lump
of GPM was put on a flat surface, it would start to level out by the gravitational forces.
This is a beneficial property if a mold with many difficult entries should be used, as the
GPM would start to self­level and reach even the most difficult parts of the molds. The
consistency and workability of the mix designs are discussed in Section 4.1.5.
The Influence of the Material of the Molds
During the project, three different materials for the molds were investigated: steel, wood,
and plastic. Steel molds were the most difficult to clean because GPM stuck very well to
the molds. However, adding a thick layer of oil made it easier to clean the molds. Wood
had the problem that after a couple of uses, it would be permanently damaged and thereby

by exposure to high relative humidity (RH) leads to microcracks, then both the compres­
sive and the tensile strength might be affected because of the potential damage to the
test specimens.
It was observed that if multiple test specimens were taken out of the curing room simul­
taneously but tested at different intervals, would those exposed to the open environment,
where the relative humidity (RH) is lower than in the curing room, exhibit higher strength
compared to the initial test. This increase in strength is likely due to shrinkage, as the
evaporation of water from the test specimens causes them to shrink and subsequently
gain strength. Conversely, when test specimens are exposed to high RH, they absorb
water, resulting in a reduction in strength.
The curing temperature in the oven is set to 65◦C based on investigations into the in­
fluence of different temperatures. These investigations found that the stagnation point
occurred between 60­75◦C [13]. The benefits of oven curing include accelerating the
hardening process, thereby allowing for faster reuse of the molds. Additionally, it de­
creases the amount of sodium hydroxide needed in the alkaline activator and reduces the
ratio between sodium hydroxide and waterglass in the activators.

4.5
Considered but Excluded Experiments
During the project, several experiments were evaluated. The feasibility and outcomes of
these experiments were discussed with experts who possess the most knowledge about
the individual sensors and test setups at DTU. Consequently, a significant amount of time
was dedicated to investigating and interviewing these experts throughout the duration of
the project.
4.5.1
2D DIC for Compression Test on Cylinders
It was discussed whether the 2D DIC could be used on the cylinders in the compression
test. The idea with this experiment was that the test specimen could be compressed to
maximum strength, be broken into pieces, and it would still be possible to get the vertical
and horizontal strain. However, there were a lot of uncertainties about whether or not the
2D DIC would give reliable results.
When the cylinder is compressed, it is expected that the surface of the side of the cylinders
moves outwards out of the plane, towards the camera. This will result in unreliable results
since the focal point of the camera would change. Another issue is that the surface of
the cylinder is round and therefore a 2D picture representing a 3­dimensional space of
the specimen, would result in DIC errors. It was also discussed if the cylinder should
be cut vertically so there was a flat surface for the 2D DIC; however, this might create
microcracks and a change in the geometry (center of gravity), which would then result in
different compression strengths.
4.5.2
3D DIC Cylinders
There are two main reasons why the 3D DIC was not used. The first reason is that there is
a significant amount of concrete dust in the lab, which could potentially damage the equip­
ment. Given that the 3D DIC setup costs around 1,000,000 DKK, a substantial amount of
time would be required to secure the equipment to prevent damage. The second reason
is that very few people know how to work with the 3D DIC system. This means that if
this system were to be used, it would take a long time to learn the software and setup
procedures. Because the project focused on many other experiments, it was concluded
that there was no time for the 3D DIC.
4.5.3
Optical Extensometer for Cylinders
The personnel in the DTU lab have never used the optical extensometer on concrete
specimens. The issue with the setup was that the mounting system for the optical ex­
tensometer was only made for specific machines used for tensile tests of small metal
specimens. In order to make the optical extensometer work on the compression machine
that was used for the cylinders, a whole new mounting system should be made ­ perhaps
a tripod. Another issue with the optical extensometer is that it takes a very long time to
mount it correctly for each test, and because there were so many cylinders that needed
to be tested, it would be ideal to focus solely on this test without any other tests. It is still
uncertain if the optical extensometer provides reliable data for this test.
4.5.4
Strain Gauges
The strain gauges are among the most precise equipment that can be used to determine
the strains in specimens. However, if they are not long enough, they might only measure
the strain in a very local area for concrete specimens. Another issue is that it takes a lot
of time to mount the strain gauges, and they can only be used once for each specimen
if the specimen should be tested to its maximum compression strength. One idea would
be to mount strain gauges on only one specimen for each OPM and GPM that need to be
CHAPTER 4. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
59
==PAGE:74==
tested, with maybe 5­10 strain gauges per specimen. The problem with this approach is
that you only get data from one specimen and cannot calculate an average or standard
deviation from multiple tests.
4.5.5
Dog Bone Tensile Test
The dog bone uniaxial tensile test is a direct method for determining tensile strength.
Previous studies at DTU have investigated fiber­reinforced concrete using this test. In the
current study, the test specimens are plain mortars, which are expected to exhibit a brit­
tle fracture. According to Associate Professor Gregor Daniel Fischer, who has extensive
experience with the dog bone test of fiber­reinforced concrete, it is necessary to conduct
numerous tests to determine an average tensile strength value for OPM and GPM with no
reinforcement. This necessity arises because even minor inaccuracies in the test setup
can lead to unreliable data and violate the uniaxial behavior and assumptions. Addition­
ally, although there are four different molds available for the dog bone specimens, the
laboratory lacks an appropriate mounting system for the test. Furthermore, except for Mr.
Fischer, no one in the DTU lab has prior experience conducting this experiment.
4.5.6

This test would be ideal and straightforward to perform for indirectly determining the tensile
strength of the specimens. However, this test only provides the maximum tensile strength
and does not capture the softening curve. Since there was also an interest in investigating
the tail of the tensile curve, this experiment was not pursued.
4.5.7
Three Point Bending Test
This test would be ideal for comparing the actual experimental results with simulations
conducted using finite element software. This would also validate the softening curve
received in the inverse analysis. However, due to time constraints, this experiment was
not performed.

4.6
Further Investigations
Many interesting aspects of geopolymer mortar were discovered during the project. How­
ever, several new parameters and questions arose during the process. To address these
questions, further investigation is necessary. The following points detail some sugges­
tions for additional research on these unanswered questions.
1. For mix design optimization and better strength comparison, keep the W/C and AL/B
ratios constant by mass, not volume. When mixing the GPM, disregard the stan­
dards regarding the mixing process used for OPM.
2. Does a resting period between heat curing have a significant impact on strength
development?
3. Investigate the initial strength of GPM by adjusting the oven curing time. At what
point does strength development stagnate?
4. Do the specimen geometry and size affect the strength and mechanical properties
of GPM while using different curing methods?
5. How does the moisture content affect the geopolymer’s mechanical properties?
6. Will an ambient­cured GPM specimen reach the same strength as a heat­cured
specimen after e.g. one year?
7. Further investigate the results from a 2D DIC versus a 3D DIC test setup on cylin­
drical specimens. Is the difference in recorded mechanical properties significant?
8. Further investigate the experimental test setup for Poisson’s ratio. For example,
rotate the test specimen to see if the same results are obtained.
9. Validate the softening curve obtained from wedge splitting in a 3­point bending test.
Adjust the results in a numerical model.

5
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study explored the mechanical and fracture properties of fly ash­based
geopolymer mortar (GPM) in comparison to Ordinary Portland mortar (OPM). Mix designs
were developed and compared, revealing distinct differences in consistency and workabil­
ity between GPM and OPM. It was discovered that longer mixing time for GPM increased
the workability significantly. Because the behavior of the workability of GPM was different
compared to OPM, it was found to be impractical to apply the standards assessed for
OPM to GPM. SEM images revealed a difference in particle shape between fly ash and
cement, which potentially could affect the workability.
Investigation of the strength development highlighted that the initial strength of GPM was
largely affected by the curing conditions and methods. Allowing water to evaporate from
the GPM specimens during heat curing led to increased initial strengths. However, when
the test specimens were ambient­cured at 20◦C and 90% RH, the development of strength
decreased. It was concluded that the moisture content had a significant impact on the
strength. Comparable strengths between OPM and GPM were determined on small cube
specimens for specific mix designs and curing methods. While investigating the mechan­
ical properties of cylindrical specimens, the compressive strength was found to be incom­
parable for GPM. This forced the comparison of the mechanical properties to be normal­
ized to the maximum strengths of OPM and GPM.
The stiffness was significantly lower for GPM compared to OPM. It was visually observed
that GPM had a higher air content than OPM, which might have affected the stiffness.
GPM exhibited larger strains before reaching the maximum strength. One experiment
determining Poisson’s ratio resulted in unrealistically high values, which might have been
due to the test setup. However, another experiment led to more realistic values but was
still high compared to the literature. GPM resulted in a larger Poisson’s ratio.
The tensile strength and fracture energy were determined by an indirect tensile test. Utiliz­
ing inverse analysis revealed that OPM and GPM had similar normalized tensile strengths.
2D DIC was used to confirm the crack development. The fracture energy for OPM was
larger compared to GPM, but when normalized, the values were similar.
It was found that the stiffness versus compressive strength could not be predicted by
Eurocode 2. However, the tensile strength versus compressive strength was consistent
with Eurocode 2. This means that some parts of Eurocode 2, along with several other
standards, cannot be directly applied to the mechanical properties of geopolymers.

A.1
Introduction
This subreport describes the method of creating and mixing the alkaline liquid used in the
composition of geopolymer concrete cast in the project. The final solution consists of 8M
sodium hydroxide and water glass in a 1:1 mass ratio; however, a solution with a 1:2.5
mass ratio is also investigated.
A.2
Theory
To create 1 L of an 8M solution of NaOH (sodium hydroxide), the solution must contain 320
g of NaOH per liter. Given that the molecular weight of NaOH is 40 g/mol, the theoretical
density of an 8M NaOH solution is 1274 g/L. However, it was measured in the laboratory
to be 1251 g/L in a solution prepared by PhD student Sahand Rahemipoor. Knowing
the density, the mass of 8M NaOH can be calculated and the amount of water can be
determined as follows:
Mwater = ρNaOH −MNaOH = 1251 g/l −320 g/l = 931 g/l
(A.1)
This means that for each liter of 8M NaOH, 931 g of water and 320 g of NaOH must be
mixed.

Workability Tests
This subreport describes the methods used in the project to determine the workability of
wet mortar. Two tests were used, namely the ”plunger penetration” (EN 1015­4 and EN
413­2) and the ”flow table” (ASTM C 1437).
H.1
Consistence by Plunger Penetration
The test method follows the standard EN 1015­4 (and EN 413­2). The consistency is
determined by dropping the plunger from a specific height into the mortar and then mea­
suring the penetration depth.
Figure H.1.1: Illustration of the test setup for penetration test.
H.1.1
Equipment
• Mold. Dimmensions, height 70mm and inner diameter 80mm
• Plunger ­ fixed to the setup
• A tamper with the weight and striking area following the standard
• Leveling square rod
H.1.2
Procedure
This simplified explanation is inspired by the Ph.D. student Rasmus Stenholt­Jacobsen,
DTU Lyngby.
* (The first mold must be filled and stroked off within 1 minute after completion of
mixing)
1. Zero the plunger depth meter, by inverting the mold and rest the plunger on the
inverted bottom. Now adjust the height of the fixtures to be ’zero’. See the first two
images in Figure H.1.1.
2. Ensure that the top position of the plunger is 100 +/­ 0,5 mm above the mortar
surface.
APPENDIX H. WORKABILITY TESTS
99
==PAGE:114==
3. Fill the mold in two layers and compact each layer with 10 ’light’ strokes with the
tamper.
4. ’Strike’ with a sawing motion at a 45­degree angle and then reverse at a flatter angle.
5. Lift and lock the plunger at 100 mm above the mortar surface (at the fixed stop)
6. Release the plunger and measure the penetration depth on the ’in­build’ ruler on the
penetration rod.
(a) Special note for geopolymers: Because the consistency is very different from
cement mortar, the plunge does not immediately stop when it hits the GP
mortar. Therefore, to compare the workability with mortar using cement, the
plunger should be manually locked in place after 1 second of hitting the mortar.
H.1.3
General Notes and Observations
• All equipment, except for the leveling square rod, was made of aluminum. This
choice was not optimal, as aluminum reacts with both acids and alkalis, leading to
the degradation of smooth surfaces in the mold. However, the leveling square rod,
crafted from brass, exhibited no issues when in contact with geopolymer or cement
mortar.
• Standard masonry cement mortar shall achieve a penetration depth of 35 +/­ 3 mm
• Report penetration as either measured depth or 35 +/­ 3 mm
• If standard consistency is desired, Redo the test with a new water content, until two
consecutive tests results in a penetration depth of 35 +/­ 3 mm

NaOH (Sodium Hydroxide): 
1. Corrosive: NaOH is highly corrosive to the skin, eyes, and respiratory system. Contact can 
cause severe burns, irritation, and tissue damage. 
2. Reactivity: NaOH is highly reactive with acids, releasing heat and potentially causing 
splattering or violent reactions. It can react exothermically with water, leading to the release of 
heat and potential splashing. 
3. Inhalation Hazard: Inhalation of NaOH dust or mist can cause respiratory irritation, 
coughing, and difficulty breathing. 
4. Environmental Hazard: NaOH can be harmful to the environment if released into 
waterways, as it can raise pH levels and disrupt aquatic ecosystems.
