Abstract
Monitoring meltwater lakes becomes increasingly important to understand ice sheet
dynamics in Greenland and Antarctica. Currently, standard methods use optical
satellite imagery, which are suitable for determining lake extend, however have lim-
itations in regard to assessing bathymetry.
This report presents an algorithm which uses laser altimetry data from the Ice,
Cloud, and land Elevation Satellite (ICESat)-2 to map meltwater lake bathymetry.
The implemented method rst uses ATL06 surface height data to detect potential
lakes. Following, bathymetry information is extracted from corresponding ATL03
photon height data. The presented algorithm minimises the amount of false positives
without compromising lake detection. Successful implementation of the algorithm is
demonstrated by evaluating its bathymetry estimation performance on various types
of lakes. This includes consulting corresponding Landsat 8 imagery. Additionally,
lake detection capabilities are veried using available reference data on lake extends
in north-eastern Greenland. The results yield, that ATL06 data can be used for
successful lake detection and that the implemented algorithm provides promising
bathymetry estimates, despite large variability between meltwater lakes.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Ice loss from Antarctica and Greenland is a signicant contributor to sea-level rise
[1] [2]. Observations show an even faster acceleration of the ice loss than predicted
[3]. Rising air and ocean temperatures provoke ice loss by increased outlet glacier
melt on contact with the ocean and by increased surface melt. Especially the melt-
water production has amplied rapidly on the Greenland ice sheet [2].
Although subglacial and supraglacial run-o leads most meltwater towards the
ocean, some will temporally be accumulated in supraglacial meltwater lakes on the
ice sheet surface [4]. These meltwater lakes have a considerable inuence on ice
sheet characteristics. With the general increase of surface melt, their number and
spatial extend grow rapidly.
Meltwater lakes primarily form during summer and tend to use the same hol-
lows in the ice sheet topography in successive years [4]. Towards the end of sum-
mer, many lakes decrease in size up until complete disappearance.
Some drain
slowly by overowing of their respective basins and running o via already existing
supraglacial streams and moulins [5]. Others experience fast drainages induced by
hydro-fractures which allows the water to reach the bed [6]. This sudden drainage
of melt-ponds to the bedrock raises the possibility for decreased basal friction and
thus increased ice velocity [7]. The created pathways may remain open, continuously
delivering surface water to the base which enhances sliding even further through the
melt season [8]. In either drainage case, meltwater lakes introduce a delay between
actual melt and run-o.
The formation of supraglacial lakes is further accompanied by a positive albedo
feedback mechanism. The decreased albedo of the darker water surface results in
increased energy absorption and thus enhances further melting [9]. Thereby, melting
is not only increased at the surrounding ice surface, but also at the lake bottom due
to the absorbed radiation [10]. As observations show, ablation from the bottom of
a lake is up to 135 % compared to surrounding bare ice [11].
Resultant, it is crucial to have knowledge about lake depths besides their horizontal
areal extends, in order to suciently characterise meltwater lakes and understand
their inuence to the ice sheet.
1.2
Objective
In visible and IR bands, water appears darker than surrounding ice. Hence, detecting
supraglacial lakes can be performed by means of passive remote sensing data as e.g.
Landsat or Sentinel-2 imagery. As water attenuates red signicantly stronger than
blue, the relation between both bands allows classication of areas as lake or ice.
The Normalized Dierence Water Index (NDWI) combines red and blue band char-
acteristics and is often used to detect lakes and their areal extend by thresholding

[12], [13], [14]. Derivation of lake depth can be obtained by exploiting the fact that
light is generally attenuated while passing through water. Empirical relationships
for approximating supraglacial lake depths have been derived by investigating the
dierences in reectance behaviour between ice, deep water and meltwater lakes [15].
However it must be considered that these empirical estimations depend on several
approximations and assumptions. For one, lake bathymetry albedo and attenua-
tion must be approximated. As both depend on the actual heterogeneity of the
supraglacial lake, correct depth estimates are limited to clear lakes with no particu-

assumed to be minimal [13] [17]. In addition, the band ratios become insignicant
with increasing lake depth and errors of 1 m or more will be introduced for lakes
deeper than 5 m [12]. Hence, in order to improve the empirical estimations, addi-
tional depth data are required to t parameters to an adequate level. This is often
done by in situ measurements [18], which however is unsuitable for general appli-
cations due to large (and increasing) amount of meltwater lakes on the ice sheet.
More accessible methods for depth retrieval are required to support the image-based
estimates.
Launched in 2018, ICESat-2 provides laser altimetry measurements which can,
among others, be used to derive bathymetry information for shallow waters [19].
In comparison to other satellite altimetry data like radar, the usage of LIght Detec-
tion And Ranging (LIDAR) data has the benet of a better spatial resolution which
results in more detailed bathymetry mapping.
Hence, in this report usage of ICESat-2 data for automatic meltwater lake detection
and depth retrieval is investigated. Existing methods focus on the processing of the
very detailed ATL03 photon cloud data [20] [21]. Due to the large amount of data
this has some computational drawbacks.
Thus, this project focusses on identifying a method to rst detect lakes from the
thinned out ATL06 surface data. Only afterwards, means for deriving bathymetry
from ATL03 photon data within the detected lake regions are developed. In that
scope, also methods for automatic detection of false positives and inconclusive data
are implemented. Further, the inuence of applied refraction corrections onto esti-
mated depths is investigated.
After implementation of the algorithm, the detection of lakes is evaluated by means
of available reference data. Additionally, dierent types of detected lakes and the
algorithm's performance on the respective bathymetry estimation is discussed.
1.3
Range of Work
As mentioned before, this report outlines the development of an algorithm for melt-
water lake detection and depth derivation. Thereby, it is focussed on processing
data from the Greenland ice-sheet, however the algorithm can potentially also be
applied for Antarctic meltwater lakes.

Afterwards, in Section 2.2 the satellite mission ICESat-2 is presented, which employs

2.1
LIDAR
LIDAR is an optical method to measure ranges and velocities. Similar to radar, a
transmitted signal is compared to a received one after its interaction (i.e. reection)
with an obstacle. Depending on duration and strength of the received signal, it
is possible to measure objects or surfaces and derive their characteristics. As the
name indicates, LIDAR thereby uses laser energy, usually in the optical domain, in
comparison to radar which transmits radio waves.
The scope of this section is to rst provide a brief overview about the basic LIDAR
principle (Section 2.1.1), and then to outline further considerations in regard to the
usage as satellite altimeter (Section 2.1.2) as well as required refraction correction
(Section 2.1.3).
2.1.1
Principle
The basic operating principle of a LIDAR is displayed in Figure 2.1. First, a col-
limated laser beam is emitted. It thereupon interacts with molecules or particles
and a fraction of the transmitted energy is scattered back towards the instrument.
There, the returning photons will be picked up by a receiver telescope [22]. After-
wards, frequency lters may be applied, before photon returns are quantied in a
detector stage.
Measuring the travel time ∆t between transmission and reception of a photon allows
deriving the range R between laser and the reecting object. The relation is
R = 1
2 · ∆t · c,
(1)
with speed of light c. As reection can occur even from smallest particles, all photon
returns from one or more laser pulses are combined in order to retrieve meaningful
information [23]. This further allows to not only consider the bare range distance,
but also to take strength and timely spread of the received signal into account. These
characteristics vary dependent on the reecting surface/object, as each particle has
its own scattering and absorption behaviour when interacting with light [22]. Hence,
besides range, LIDAR systems also measure surface characteristics or atmospheric
compositions.
Typical lasers used in LIDAR systems reach from green to NIR regions, accord-
ingly wavelengths are rather short, up to 1 µm (see Figure 2.2). In contrast, radar
systems have wavelengths in the cm to m range. These rather short wavelengths of
LIDAR systems provide them with the benet of a signicantly better resolutions.

agation eects introduced by passing through the ionosphere and troposphere.
Another factor to consider with LIDAR altimetry, especially in regard to this project,
is the interaction with water. Most photons are reected by the water surface, how-
ever some penetrate the surface and may subsequently be reected by the underlying
ground of the water body. Therewith it is possible to map bathymetry of shallow
waters in coastal areas or lakes, especially if the water is clear and calm. Rough
water surfaces or a high mud content impede the laser's capability to reach the
bottom, as more photons are reected closer to the surface.
2.1.3
Refraction Correction
When mapping bathymetry it must be taken into account, that the laser photons
have dierent travelling times in water than in the atmosphere.
However, pre-
processed photon data usually assumes a propagation in air. Hence, a refraction
correction has to be applied for all photon reections below the water surface.

4.1
Summary
Within this section, the implemented algorithm is summarised. Hence, it may be
used as short stand-alone manual to get a quick overview.
Figure 4.1 presents an overview about the algorithm. It is divided into three main
parts:
1. Pre-processing and Initialisation (blue)
2. Lake Detection from ATL06 Data (yellow)
3. Lake Depth Estimation and Refraction Correction (green)
First, this paragraph outlines the initial steps of the algorithm.
Required input data are ATL03 and ATL06 data from the same track over approx-
imately the same area.
In an initialisation step, the two datasets are matched to cover exactly the same
area. Following, all data with very low signal condence are excluded. Afterwards,
the ATL06 data are interpolated to match the ATL03 spacing and only ATL03
photon data within certain limits from the ATL06 surface data are kept for further
processing. These steps allow reducing the data by excluding ATL03 data which
most likely do not correspond to a lake or its bathymetry.
Second, a closer look is taken at the lake detection. The basic idea is to search
for at regions within the ATL06 surface height data, as water surfaces will always
be levelled.
The actual detection of at regions is implemented by computing the gradient of
the whole ATL06 surface data. Flat areas do not have any slope and hence will
show a gradient of 0. The detected regions, including a small buer, are agged as
"potential lakes". As simple at land regions show the same gradient behaviour,
they are agged as well and have to be excluded later.
Lastly, ATL03 and ATL06 data for each potential lake region are extracted and
saved.
Third, the depth estimation is regarded. It is based on the fact, that most photon
reections originate from the surface and second most reections from the underly-

Initialisation
This section describes the required initialisation and pre-processing steps before the

4.2.1
Read Input Data
As previously mentioned, ATL03 and ATL06 data are available as *.h5 data. In a
rst step, both data sets are loaded into matlab. Thereby, not all information is
required, but only the parameters presented in Table 3.2 and Table 3.4.
4.2.2
Match ATL03 and ATL06 Data
In a second step, the read ATL03 and ATL06 data are compared in regards to their
coverage and only the overlapping area is kept. As information from both datasets
is required for the processor, there is no need for keeping additional data.
The
matching of ATL03 and ATL06 data is done by using the segment ID segID, which
is available in both datasets and hence links the two.
Minimum and maximum matching segIDs are found and all data outside this area
is discarded.
Further it shall be considered, that the ATL06 algorithm only retains valid height
data. Resultant, some segIDs are not available at all. In contrast, the ATL03 data
contain all segIDs, independent on whether data are available or not. To tackle this,
missing segIDs are added to the ATL06 segIDs to match the ATL03 data. Corre-
spondingly missing latitude and longitude values are interpolated; missing height
values are set to NaN. Although no new information is introduced to the ATL06
data, this step is conducted to have a simplied correspondence between the two
datasets.

4.4

After the detection of potential lake areas and extraction of corresponding ATL03
and ATL06 data, this sections presents the extraction of bathymetry information.
The general idea is to apply a moving window to the ATL03 photon data and com-
pute the rst two most probable values for each window. As the highest amount of
photons are reected at the water surface, the rst value indicates the surface. Still
a signicant amount of reections occur at the lake ground, which is represented by
the second most probable value (see Section 4.4.1).
Further, in this section estimated bathymetry will be corrected for refraction (Sec-
tion 4.4.2) the lake extend is determined 4.4.3.
Additionally it must be considered that the previously detected areas with zero
gradient do not only include lakes, but also natural at surface regions. Distinction
of these simple at areas towards lakes with actual bathymetry is discussed in Sec-

ATL03 and ATL06 data for detected at areas have been extracted for each "po-
tential lake" (lake and at area) and here presented processing steps are applied to

4.4.1
Estimation of Frequent Heights
As mentioned before, to gain information about the lake surface, the most probable
value within a subset the ATL03 photon heights shall be extracted. The concept is
based on a depth retrieval algorithm implemented by [21], but has been modied
and adapted to comply with this project's methods and ideas.
For the extraction of the most likely values, a moving window of size 151 is applied.
Hence, 75 photons before and after the current photon of interest are included. This
window size is a good compromise between including sucient photons for valid, ro-
bust result and still having a coarse enough resolution. By choosing a larger window
size the latter would impede especially bathymetry extractions, as smaller photon
height changes are not detectable any more. It must be mentioned, that the window
size is referred to the number of valid photons, i.e. NaNs are not considered as part
of the 151 photons. A vertical resolution of 0.1 m is chosen, which means probability
of photons and hence of surface and bathymetry heights is investigated in 0.1 m bins.
To now compute the most probable value, the maximum of a histogram could be
extracted. However, this has the major drawback, that each height value is assigned
to specic bin and hence the result is rather discrete and only represents the bin
with most photons.
An alternative approach is creating a probability distribution object. Thereby, the
the photon heights are tted to a dened x-vector covering the range from mini-
mum to maximum height with the dened step size (i.e. resolution) of 0.1 m. In the
specic case, a kernel distribution is tted, which is dened through a smoothing
function and a bandwidth. Similar to the histogram, with the kernel distribution

As per design, the lake detection part of the algorithm also detects simple at surface

Figure 4.17 shows an example of a at area detected as potential lake. As the previ-

However, as no actual bathymetry is underlying, the extracted second peak (yellow)
has no clear structure. It approaches the values for the estimated rst peak (i.e.
surface), but also shows a signicant amount of clear outliers, especially above the
estimated surface. In contrast, with available bathymetry the second peak hardly
ever is estimated to be above the surface height (see e.g. Figure 4.15 for reference).
The exclusion of at areas follows a two step approach. First, remaining insignicant
data are excluded. Second, the distinction between lake and at surface is made.
Despite the exclusion of unlikely heights (see Section 4.2.3), data without useful
information remain.

This section discusses the detected lakes and respectively derived bathymetry with
respect to the corresponding Landsat-8 visual image. In this scope, additionally fu-

detected lakes are presented, but only a subset to show dierent lake types and char-

creating an algorithm to perfectly detect and map all lakes is barely feasible.

7
Conclusion
In this project, an algorithm to map meltwater lake bathymetry from ICESat-2 al-
timetry data has been developed.
The implemented method rst uses ATL06 surface data to detect lakes on the ice
sheet, before underlying bathymetry information is extracted from ATL03 photon
data. Connections between the two datasets are established by means of the segIDs.
Lake detection is performed by searching for areas with zero gradient with the ATL06
surface data. Lake surface and bathymetry are thereupon estimated by extracting
the two most likely height values in a moving window approach. Refraction correc-
tion is applied to the estimated bathymetry information to obtain the correct depth
values. False positives are minimised by comparing bathymetry probability with
background probability.
It has been shown, that the implemented algorithm successfully detects a variety of
lakes and extracts their bathymetry.
Comparisons with corresponding Landsat-8 images yield, that the quality of lake
bottom estimates is best for clear, unobstructed lakes. However, the algorithm is
also able to extract depth information in less explicit cases.
As per design, also at areas are initially agged as potential lake. But the algo-
rithm manges to discard most of these without compromising on the detection of
actual lakes. A nal false positive rate of 29% remains.
Comparisons with available lake reference data show a 60% success rate for the de-
tection of meltwater lakes. Missed lakes are caused by dierent reasons, however
most prominently by wrongly estimated ATL06 surface data. Hence, performance
of the implemented algorithm largely depends on the quality of ATL06 surface data.
Nevertheless, it can be concluded, that by means of the implemented methods the
sparser ATL06 surface data can successfully be used to detect the majority of melt-
water lakes.
As possible future improvement, the relation between between bathymetry and
background probability could be exploited further. For one thing, it can be used to
optimise the distinction between lakes and at areas; thus decreasing the amount of
false positives without compromising the detection of lakes. On the other hand it
can be used to improve the quality of estimated bathymetry.

A.3
Refraction Correction Considerations
In theory each subsurface photon experiences an individual refraction.
As it
depends on depth, the refraction for photons reected far from the surface is larger
than for photons reected close to the surface. In case of bathymetry, refraction
correction hence changes the distribution of subsurface photons and inuences the
estimated "second peak" (see Section 4.4.1). This indicates that corrections should
be applied before the bathymetry estimation. However this also means, that surface
(rst peak) and bathymetry (second peak) cannot be determined simultaneously
any more, as surface information must be known before subsurface photons can
be corrected for refraction. This requires applying the moving window for kernel
density estimation twice and hence increases computing power.
The result is presented in yellow in Figure A.1.
Red on the other hand rep-
resents the resulting bathymetry for applying the correction after estimating the
bathymetry (as generally used in this project). As it can be seen, the dierences
are rather small. Hence, the eect is deemed neglectable in regards to the savings
in computing power by correcting the bathymetry after its estimation.

function [potential_lakes] = find_lake_extend(potential_lakes)
% this functions finds the exact extend of a potential lake by assuming
% the most common surface height value to be the lake surface height.
% Corresponding required data within this extend is extracted.
% subfunction of "depth_estimation.m"
% loop over all potential lakes
for ii = 1:length(potential_lakes)
%------------------
% find most common height
[N_elements,edges] = ...
histcounts(potential_lakes(ii).surface,'BinWidth',0.1);
[~,b] = max(N_elements);
lake_height = (edges(b)+edges(b+1))/2;
%------------------
% extract lake surface & its extend

lake_surface(potential_lakes(ii).surface <= lake_height-0.25) = NaN;
lake_surface(potential_lakes(ii).surface >= lake_height+0.25) = NaN;
% compute mean surface height
potential_lakes(ii).lake_height = nanmean(lake_surface);

ind_beg = find(~isnan(lake_surface),1,'first');
ind_end = find(~isnan(lake_surface),1,'last');
