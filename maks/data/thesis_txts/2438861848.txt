CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Beamsteering is essential for the developed technologies of the current century. Satel-
lite and mobile communications industries make the use of higher frequencies and
bandwidths and are demanding shorter element sizes to incorporate a large number
of radiating elements.
Steerable directive antennas with narrower-band radiation
patterns allow an increase in the information capacity of wireless communication net-
works by improving the signal to interference ratio. In some situations mechanical
steering technology is not possible to be implemented (satellites, fast trains, cars)
thus an electrical beamsteering technology should be implemented to overcome this
limitation.
1.1
Beam forming Networks
Phase shifters [34][35] have typically been implemented more often than delay lines
for beamforming networks due to their simplicity. Optical delay lines, however, sup-
port larger bandwidths and are more suitable for the increasing bandwidth demands
of wireless communication systems. Ideally, optical delay lines should have flat group
delays across the frequencies of interest and provide continuous frequency and ampli-
tude tunability. Electronics have failed to provide those characteristics in a simple and
compact way. Although progress has been made in monolithic microwave integrated
circuits[17], their basic disadvantages are their lack of bandwidth reconfigurability be-
low to some tens of GHz using medium to low powered devices. Integrated microwave
photonics technology can reduce the size and weight of a phased array system and
offers enormous bandwidth starting from DC to tens of GHz. It also improves the
system stability and predictability, providing ultra low losses over this band with the
only limited factor being the photodiode bandwidth. Silicon photonics has emerged
as a platform to support this technology [1],[56]. Furthermore, the amorphous silicon
used here and design at DTU can sustain high optical powers and is highly nonlinear,
yielding a new path for integrated microwave photonics involving nonlinear optical
processing.

1.2
Microwave Photonics
Nowadays, Microwave Photonics (MWP) is an interdisciplinary field that involves
the interaction between microwave and optical waves.
Precisely, it uses photonic
technologies and components to deal with the processing, control, generation and
transport of microwave signals in the optical part of the spectrum. Bringing together
the advantages of RF engineering and optoelectronics and combining those two worlds,
a new palette of functionalities and features are obtained which could not be realised
in all-electric or all-optical system [19]. The use of optical fibres as a transmission
medium in MWP systems lies behind the success story in optical communications.
Optical fibre advantages include low weight (typically 1.7 kg/km as opposed to 567
kg/km for coaxial cable[18]), immunity to electromagnetic interference, high degree
of physical flexibility, low cross-sectional area and relatively low dispersion (especially
at 1310 nm for silica single-mode fibre) and very low loss (as low as 0.2 dB/km at
1550 nm in terms of optical loss, which is equivalent to just 0.4 dB/km of electrical
loss). Digital signal processing (DSP) is one of the most important parts of a number
of applications; its reconfigurability and flexibility has led to the evolution of a number
of instruments. However, electronics and more specifically digital signal processors are
bounded to operating frequencies well below several gigahertz due to the limitations of
analogue to digital converters. In addition, speed limitations, weight and cost raise the
need of a state-of-the-art high-frequency processing unit as front-end analog solutions.
As a matter of fact, employing the assets of photonics mentioned above (i.e., broad
bandwidth, high flexibility, and low weight) with the established reconfigurability of
digital electronic processors, a unique state-of-the-art wide-band microwave photonic
processor can be designed. Unique capabilities like high-frequency signal processing
and photonic filtering are composing an advanced system that can be used in the
analogue front ends such as beamforming networks.
1.2.1
Integrated Microwave Photonics
Integrated Microwave Photonics deals with the application of microwave photonics
to integration. Integrated microwave photonics[5] has became one of the most ac-
tive areas of research within the field of Microwave Photonics. Commercial wafer
scale fabrication devices have utilised several major material platforms: (i) the ele-
ments from the third and fifth column of the periodic table mostly referred as III
- V compounds (GaAs,InP), (ii) non-linear crystals LiNbO3, (iii) dielectrics (silica
and silicon nitride based waveguides) and (iv) element semiconductors (silicon on-
insulator (SOI))[19]. Thanks to the ultra-small geometrical structures of the optical
waveguides and compatibility with the silicon electronics, current silicon photonics
wire waveguides provide a decidedly integrated photonics platform for the electronic–
photonic convergence industry. The current platform face challenges like searching
ways of reducing the propagation and coupling loss to external fibre and overcoming
polarisation dependence. Silicon photonics foundries have made progress through the
years by applying state-of-the-art technology to tune the fabrication of nanometric

1.3
Thesis Outline
The objective of this project is to design, and characterise the components of an
RF beamsteering integrated microwave photonic circuit using deuterium passivated
amorphous silicon on insulator, a novel silicon-based material platform being devel-
oped by the project supervisors. For the investigated optical beamforming, an optical
carrier will be modulated by a microwave signal; optical delay lines will delay the mi-
crowave signal by delaying the optical carrier. This process will be done by using
three cascaded sub-systems performing wavelength conversion, optical filtering, and
delay. The components were designed using numerical tools (Lumerical products),
then fabricated in the Danchip cleanroom and characterised in the High Speed Opti-
cal Communications Laboratory. The fundamentals of beamforming techniques are
illustrated and conceptualised so the integrated circuit components can compose a re-
alistic device with the required system specifications. The components were designed
using amorphous silicon as the material platform to meet the predetermined specifica-
tions. A final layout was created for the final chip which includes all the components.
The components dimensions were varied in order to compensate the uncertainties
induced from the fabrication process. Finally, the components were characterised to
determine which ones would be suitable for the beamforming network.

2.1
Antennas
The IEEE Standard Definitions of Terms for Antennas (IEEE Std 145–1983) defines
the antenna or aerial as ”a means for radiating or receiving radio waves”. Particularly,
an antenna is a component which transitions between a free-space and guiding device.
The transmission line that guides the electromagnetic waves to the antenna transmit-
ting source or from the receiving end has the form of a coaxial line or a waveguide
architecture. In other words, the function of the antenna as a transmitting source
is to focus and direct the radiated energy into a specific pattern which points in the
desired direction in space. On the other hand, a reception antenna collects the energy
from free space and delivers it to the receiver. Therefore, the antenna can be consid-
ered a reciprocal structure. Antennas can be realised in various shapes, architectures,
sizes and materials. The antennas’ total size affects its operating wavelength, thus, a
number of constraints like the desired phase, radiation and electrical characteristics
need to be determined a priori according to the application[4].
2.2
Antenna Parameters
The basic antenna parameters are identified in this chapter before discussing imple-
mentation of the beam-forming network. In this chapter, the antenna is considered
as a reciprocal element operating in either transmitting or receiving state. The reci-
procity theorems of the antenna can readily applied to others[4].

2.2.2
Field Regions
The space surrounding the antenna is subdivided into three regions based on the
dominating characteristic of each field. Namely, the regions are called reactive near-
field, radiative near-field (or Fresnel) and far-field region (or Fraunhofer region) [4].
The reactive region, is the surrounded space around the antenna where the reactive
field components dominate.
In the reactive area, the electric and magnetic fields
are not necessarily in phase to each other and the angular field distribution is highly
dependent upon the distance and direction from the antenna. The radiative near field
region is the space comprised between the reactive near-field and the far-field, where
the radiative fields are already dominant, but the field patterns still depend on the
distance from the antenna. Lastly, the far field region starts where distance does not
describe the field patterns; in other words, the electromagnetic fields are independent
of the distance from the antenna and radiating and can be approximate as a spherical

2.3
Radiation Pattern
The intensity of the electric or magnetic field formed by the radiation emitted from
the antenna is a function of the amplitude and phase of the distributed currents
across the antenna aperture.
Typically, the electromagnetic fields are determined
using mathematical approximations because the contributions from the current ele-
ments accommodated and distributed in the aperture are only evaluated by adding
vectorially each current component[45]. The mathematical evaluation will involves
an integration of these components, which cannot be readily evaluated analytically.
2.4
Phased Arrays
Antenna phased arrays are composed by individual radiating antennas or elements
which can generate a radiation pattern whose shape and direction can be controlled
through the phase and amplitude of the currents flowing in each individual element[45].
The advantage of phased arrays is the inherit flexibility for steering the direction of
the radiation by properly adjusting the relative phases between the elements. To
realise the full potential of phased array technology, a processor which electronically
controls the phase and amplitude of the individual elements to steer the radiation
direction in real-time is required.

RF signal to different path delays which are connected to different antenna elements.

The Optical Beam-Forming network chip
As has been previously mentioned the antenna geometry, antenna type, element spac-

3.3.2
Balanced Photodetector and Laser source
The balanced photodetector should has a responsivity greater than 0.7 A W−1. The
common rejection ratio of the PD should be at least 30 dB. The input impedance
should be always matched at 50 Ω. A distributed feedback (DFB) laser with a radiated
power of +10 dBm and a relative intensity noise noise (RIN) of -140 dBm/Hz is
required.
The laser source should be temperature stabilised in order to keep the
emitted wavelength and RIN constant over time [41].
3.3.3
Optical Modulator
External modulation will be used for the current design. The optical modulators will
be analysed using a Mach-Zehnder modulator. The insertion loss of the MZM should
be no more than 6 dB. The half-wave voltage (Vπ) should be defined according to (3.7).
The assumptions for the modulator characteristic should define the Vπ characteristics.
It is proffered a low Vπ since a high modulation can be achieved and sometimes low
insertion losses. Typical values for high performance are less than 1 V [41].

serve as filters. The delay depends on the circumference and the coupling coefficient
of the ring. Optical ring resonators were preferred by pioneers in the field of microwave
photonics due to their small footprint and their relatively easy tunability[47],[24].

3.3.6
Phased array technology
The phased array consist a discrete number of antenna elements which are connected
to the optical beam forming network. The great number of antenna elements in the
network can offer high degree of reliability due to the extra degree of freedom given by
each independent antenna. The main advantage of this technology is that controlling
the electromagnetic excitation of each antenna in the array is can manipulate the
radiation characteristic of the array. Furthermore, in case where an antenna element
breaks in the network, if the phased array technology is well designed it will not
affect in total the radiation characteristics. Therefore, trade-off arise between the
performance of the beamforming network and its antenna element such as complexity,
footprint weight reconfigurability and cost.
3.3.7
Photonic Signal Processing techniques
Microwave photonic signal processing system employs the signal processing techniques
from electronics at the RF signal. The RF signal are converted through electro-optic
(E/O) conversion to optical domain process the electronic signals in the optical do-
main and then convert them back to electrical domain. The modulation and detection
should be taken into consideration when the signal processing techniques are applied
for the beaforming network. In addition, trade-off between the RF and optical band-
width, the amount of delays, the ripples at the spectrum arising from the gratings,
the optical filtering parameters and the complexity of the components. Proper signal
processing engineering will further reduce the modulation and detection schemes and
of course reduce the low-cost as possible. In conclusion, proper photonic signal pro-
cessing design will reduce the spurious-free dynamic range, noise figure and non-linear
distortion to the minimum[6].

4.2
Optical waveguides
A conventional optical waveguide consists of three dielectric materials with differ-
ent refractive indices. Using the a ray-optics picture, the light is guided inside the
dielectric with the higher refractive index through total internal reflection at the
core-cladding interface. Total internal reflection requires a core made out of higher
refractive index nc than the cladding ncl and substrate nsub . Hence, the refractive
index guidance condition implies that ns < nclad < nc. Moreover, the core is sur-
rounded in two dimensions x and y typically at the order of the wavelength scale of
the injected light. Therefore, the light will be confined across those two dimensions
and it will be propagating in the z-direction. The advantages of using waveguide as
a light guidance mechanism is that the confinement is conserved across the propa-
gation axis and the mode profile is conserved. The integrated optical waveguide is
considered with a rectangular amorphous Si core of nc = 3.478 and a SiO2 cladding
of nclad = 1.444 at the wavelength of λ = 1.55µm which is readily available from
a semiconductor laser media. The dimensions considered in this section are at the
same order of the x and y dimension about 0.4 −0.5 µm and 0.2 −0.3 µm. The
waveguide dimensions are directly comparable with the wavelength so the ray picture
model breaks and the electromagnetic wave analysis is dedicated.

4.2.1
Calculation of waveguides modes
A waveguide can support a finite number of optical modes which are dependent
upon the waveguide dimensions and format. The modes are the eigensolutions of the
spatially-dependent Maxwell equations which depend on the boundary conditions
and the spatially-dependent waveguide properties. In the simulations, the considered
waveguides consist of two dielectric materials.
The modal profiles are calculated
assuming that the materials are non-magnetic and absent of free charge and currents.
Expressing the Maxwell equations in terms of the E-field or H-field, the mode solutions
can be found and knowing the E-field the H-field mode can be derived or vice versa.
The boundary conditions applied of the tangential and normal components here are
continuity in the core and cladding region, the mode and the amplitude of the E-field
is invariant of the z propagation. The modal vector wave equation can be derived in
the following form,
−∇2H(r) = k2ϵ(r)H(r) + ∇ϵ(r)
ϵ(r) × ∇× H(r).
(4.2)
where k is the wavenumber of the optical field in vacuum and ϵ(r) is the dielectric
permittivity across the x and mathbfy (r = x + y) coordinates of the waveguide.
The wavelength and the wavenumber of light inside the core are λ/nc and k = 2π/λ
where λ is the vacuum wavelength. The effective refractive index of each mode can
be defined as neff = β/k. The value of the effective refractive index is determined
after the (4.2) is solved. The value of the effective index is the weighted average of
core and cladding indices with the mode field distribution as weight factor. Hence,
the effective index value lies between the cladding and the core refractive indices
ncl < neff < nc. In the case where the effective index approaches the cladding layer,
the mode is mostly confined in the cladding. On the other hand, if the effective index
approaches the core index, then the light is mostly confined in the core.
A number of analytical methods exist to calculate optical mode profiles like the
Marcati method, method of lines, and others. However, the Finite Element Method
(FEM) has been widely used during the last two decades in the analysis of waveguide
components and, more recently, so has the finite-difference time-domain (FDTD)
method. Both methods can accurately calculate waveguide properties like the effective
index, group index, mode profile, dispersion, propagation constant etc. The solvers
are numerical methods, which are adept at accurately calculating realistic waveguide
shapes having more complex refractive index distributions.
The (4.2) depends on both dimensions x and y by approximating the two-dimensional
surface of the waveguide cross section. The two dimensions consist from the core and
cladding and the FEM convert them into a set of 3D elements. Increasing the num-
ber of elements or the mesh, the accuracy and the computational time are further
increased. The mesh should be high at the areas where the light density there is
higher like the core. The appropriate number of mesh is decided from convergence
tests.

Misalignment
Here, the corrugations have been intentionally misaligned on the two sidewalls. When
there is no misalignment, the two gratings are in phase, and all the reflections interfere
constructively at the Bragg wavelength. On the other hand, when a misalignment
is introduced, the interference becomes less constructive and therefore, the grating
strength becomes weaker. In the extreme case where the two gratings are completely
out of phase, destructive interference occurs, and the whole structure behaves like a
wavy waveguide with essentially no reflection. The average waveguide width (W) and
corrugation width (∆W) are designed to be 500 nm and 50 nm, respectively. The
grating period (Λ) is 324 nm, with a duty cycle of 50%. The number of grating periods
is 284 (i.e., corresponding to a grating length of about 92 ￿m). The misalignment (∆L)
is varied from 0 to 162 nm (i.e., completely in phase to completely out of phase).[53]
The Fig. 5.40 shows the normalised transmission spectra of the gratings. When
ΛL is zero, the measured bandwidth is about 5 nm. As ∆L increases, the stop band
becomes narrower.
When ∆L is Λ2, the stop band disappears and the spectrum
becomes a straight line.

Comparison of MZI ORR and Bragg grating delay lines
It is quite important to investigate the performance between the three available delay
lines and conclude which is the most suitable to be employed in the OBFN. The
comparison will be in terms of the bandwidth, maximum delay and induced dispersion
on the signal. Mach-Zehnder interferometers have larger group delay banwdidth than
the ORR, thus the induced dispersion to the signal from the MZI is smaller compared
to ORR. For both cases, the group delay bandwidth can be controlled through the
length and the coupling coeffients of the structure. On the other hand, bragg grating
have more degrees of freedom of controling the group delay band width. Some of the
are the corrugation width, the amount of linear chirping, the apodization profile and
its parameters. The MZI maximum delay structure is way less than the ORR because
is a non resonant structure, this one of the reason that MZIs have larger group delay
bandwidths and finer tuning. Bragg grating delay lines can form an optical cavity
in the middle and behave as a resonant elements too. The physical length of the
grating offers twice group delay than the MZI because the signals will be measured
when they re reflected back. The group delay dependence of the coupling coefficient
behaves similarly in MZI and ORR; when κ = 0 the light will not coupled to the ring

Integrated Filters
The filter discussed below are all based on interference between single-mode light
paths.
For instance, ring resonator light path interferes with itself.
Moreover, a
Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI) or cascaded MZIs have two light paths that can
interfere. In a case of lattice filters additional light paths interfere. Single mode and
high index waveguides can obtained more complex filters.
Several basic filter stages may be combined in order to obtain better bandwidth.
Mach-Zehnder stages can be cascaded as a lattice or a tree structure to obtain the
desire filter stages [49]. The properties of each stage in the lattice can be tuned to
obtain good good filter characteristics. Likewise, ring resonators can also be cascaded
into ring lattice filter, the coupling strengths between the ring are optimised to ap-
proximate the desired transmission spectrum. Combining the Mach-Zehnder stages
with ring resonators a much more complex higher order filter is obtained. Attaching
ring resonators at the arms of a Mach-Zhender interferometer with multiple all-pass
rings a more specific characteristic for the filter response can obtained.

6.1
Coupling light to the chip
One of the challenging parts of the characterisation process is to couple light from and
out the fiber core facet into the chip waveguide. Special attention should be taken
here because the fiber core is larger than the fabricated waveguides, around 10 µm at
1550 nm. Here, the edge coupling technique[11] with fiber lenses has been used (Fig.
6.1), however, this techniques has limiting bandwidth as will be shown below from
the measurements. Fiber lens coupling is an easy technique to use without need to
fabricating or modifying the photonic chip itself. Fiber lens are focusing the light into
a small focal spot in order to have a better matching between the waveguide mode
and increasing the coupling efficiency. Unfortunately, the focal spot from the fiber in
some cases can be bigger than the mode of the waveguide and occur some reflections
back to the fiber that can be undesirable in some cases. Here, the lensed fibers have a
spot size of 2.5 microns, which is approximately equal to the mode size of the inverse
taper, allowing for efficient coupling
The two facets of the fiber lenses are mounted on a translation stage with three
coordinates variables X, Y and Z. The angle of fiber facets is fixed with respect to
the chip since it must be perpendicular. However, the translation stage allows the
translation of the photonic chip within the X, Y plane.

6.3
Characterising the passive micro ring resonator
The optical source is an external cavity laser that can supports wavelengths from
1520 nm to 1620 nm, providing an output power to +8 dBm and linewidth less than
200 kHz. The light-wave is sent through a fibre to a polarisation controller since
the devices are polarisation sensitive and the chip should be optimised for the TE
polarisation. Then a coupler splits the signals to the DUT and a reference Mach-
Zehnder interferometer (MZI) with an FSR of 24.9GHz. A power splitter 99/1 is
used after the DUT where the 1% of optical power is monitored by a high sensitivity
avalanche photodiode (APD) connected to an optical power meter. Two photodiodes
that are also located in each branch of the DUT and MZI are connected to two
different channels of an electrical oscilloscope where are simultaneously displayed at
each laser sweep. The experimental setup used for characterising the optical ring
resonators is illustrated in Fig. 6.6.
The transmission spectrum or the norm of the transfer function (|S21|) of each
passive micro-ring resonator has been measured as described above. The notches at
the spectrum are the resonance wavelengths of the ring resonator shown in Fig. 6.7(a-
b). The spectrum of the resonators is analysed so the coupling and the FSR can be
extracted from the measurement.
Initially, the code loads the measurement files from the two oscilloscope’ channels
that has been converted to .mat files. Therefore, the reference and estimated FSR
of the MZI and microresonator is selected. The sampling points and the frequency
are imported as well. Hence, the scan-range, frequency range and the resolution is

6.4.1
1x2 MMI coupler
The 1x2 MMI coupler has been characterised for the wavelength range of 1500 nm
to 1600 nm with a step resolution of 0.5 nm. Fig. 6.8(a-d) shows the transmission
spectrum of the 1x2 MMI coupler for coupling lengths of 31 nm, 32 nm, 33 nm and
34 nm. From Fig. 6.8(a-b) the transmission between the two ports is not totally
equal, on the other hand at Fig. 6.8(c) the coupling becomes to be identical. Note
that the resonances appear in the measurements spectrum which are typically below
−22 dB they match. In Fig. 6.8(d) the resonances are overlapping, showing that the
power is equally distributed through the two ports.
The power difference between the two ports is also investigated and plotted in
Fig. 6.9(a-d) for different coupling lengths. A majority of the points lie close to 0 dB,
which means that the power coupling is even. The power difference is not the best
way of showing the coupling here because the points are captured for a resolution of
0.5 dB and the points captured at the resonances for some wavelength may not be
the same for the other port, it can be shifted or captured at a lower or higher value.
This yields to inaccuracies so it better to observe the majority of points.
The 1x2 MMI designed with a taper width of 1.5 µm, coupling length of 34 µm
and MMI width of 6 µm exhibits the best performance in terms of power splitting.
6.4.2
2x2 MMI coupler
Likewise, the 2x2 MMI coupler has been characterised for the wavelength range of
1500 nm to 1600 nm with a step resolution of 0.5 nm. The transmission measurements
are shown in Fig. 6.10(a-c) for different combinations of coupling length and taper
width. The measurement in Fig. 6.10(a-b) based on taper width of 1.5 µm exhibit
better coupling losses than the MMI with taper width of 2 µm. This is presumably
due to coupling between the two output waveguides. The power difference plotted
in Fig. 6.10(d) indeed shows that most of the data points are located at the 0 dB
value verifying equal power splitting between the two output ports. The best coupling
design from the measurements is shown to be the one with the taper width of 1.5 µm,
coupling length of 44 µm and MMI width of 6 µm.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, the implementation of an optical beamforming network and system
integration has been discussed. The current approach is a step toward demonstrat-
ing that integrated photonics can successfully replace the electronics solutions and
comprehend all the signal processing functionalities where electronics are responsible.
To be more accurate, the components for an integrated optical beamforming network
have been designed in order to realise all the functions required for the beamforming
network to operate. The result of this was the demonstration of the design properties
set during simulations using the data from the amorphous silicon material that has
been provided from the High Speed Optical Communication group at DTU.
The thesis outline and the latest trends in beamsteering have been discussed in the
introduction. Furthermore, real world applications have been mentioned in wireless
communications to emphasise the importance of optical beamforming network for the
current century. A brief comparison between the integrated photonic and monolithic
electronic circuits has been stated and the advantages of microwave photonics have
been discussed.
In Chapter 2, the basic antenna parameters have been defined and a comparison
between phase shifter and true time delay lines (TTD) for evaluating the performance
difference has been illustrated. It was shown that the TTD is independent of the
RF frequency while the phase shifters change the array factor steering angle with
frequency tuning. Finally, a dipole model has been defined and built using numerical
tools as a proof of concept for the validity and specifications understanding of the
phase delay equations.
In Chapter 3, the system architecture is discussed, recalling realistic specifications
for the system. The key parameters of the optical beamforming network have been set
and each sub-component specification has been discussed. Finally, the system layout
is built and the final operation is decided by evaluating the analytical equations of a
general beamforming network.
In Chapter 4, the amorphous material properties are discussed and the basic
waveguide theory is presented. The waveguide dispersion and bending losses of the
current waveguide is exploited since dispersion is important for the generation of
Four-wave mixing and losses for deciding the bending radius of the waveguide at the
final layout. An attempt to simply implement the amorphous waveguide as optical
delay line with high engineered dispersion shows that the group delay produced will
not be adequate for the demands of the optical beamforming network.
==PAGE:122==
112
7 Conclusion
Chapter 5 goes through the design of the components that will comprise the
optical beamforming network.
The design components are waveguides for FWM
stage, 3-dB directional and MMI couplers for 1x2 and 2x2 ports arrangement at
1550 nm and 1530 nm. Chirped Bragg gratings have been implemented for the purpose
of delay lines and different apodisation profiles were investigated. The apodisation
profile with the dominating linear group delay is determined to be the hyperbolic
tangent. Different configurations of optical single sideband and pump filters have
been demonstrated, too. The best design with the appropriate parameters were then
sent for fabrication.
Finally, in Chapter 6, the fabricated components of the chip have been char-
acterised with the demonstrated experimental setup.
The measurements were in
agreement with the simulated components if the high concentration of measurements
point is considered correctly. However, for the coupling measurements it will be more
valid to build the coupling components to an MZI configuration in order to avoid the
sensitivity of the experimental configurations to the coupling losses and improve the
accuracy.
Overall, the work accomplished in this thesis represents a major step toward
realising a microwave photonic beamforming circuit utilising the linear and nonlinear
functionalities of the deuterated amorphous silicon on insulator platform.
