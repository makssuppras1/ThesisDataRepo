Abstract 
 
Today, wastewater is no longer viewed as a liability and waste, but instead as a valuable resource, 
both in terms of untapped energy resource and nutrient recovery. There is a strong interest in 
recovering materials and energy from wastewater streams. One valuable resource is the organic 
carbon, which can be used in biofuel production. In-order to recover Carbon, the influent and effluent 
samples were collected from Kerry and Gummerup facilities. The sampling campaign was set up based 
on the data provided by Kerry where no patterns were found for flow or COD concentrations in Kerry’s 
daily-averaged effluent samples for a 9-month period. The average COD removal during Kerry’s 
physicochemical treatment was found to be approximately 52%: from 56 ± 37 to 27 ± 20 kg COD/h. 
The COD concentration decreased from ≈ 9500 mg/L ± 39% to ≈ 3600 mg/L ± 70%.  
 
A total number of 78 influent and 72 effluent samples were collected. Consumables for 
nutrient analysis for all the samples are costly so the focus was put on the influent samples rather 
than the effluents. The characterisation of waste water was performed where influent COD value was 
7551 ± 1488 and 1269 ± 216 for the effluents of daily averaged samples. 
For C recovery, the flocculation/coagulation method was used where the propeller was set at 
150 rpm using varied Aluminium Chloride Hexahydrate dosages. The Carbon recovery was about 75% 
when 1g AlCl3/100ml of WW was introduced as compared to 2g AlCl3/100ml of WW dosage, where it 
restricted to 37%.  
The nutrient ratios C/N, C/P and N/P were calculated for Kerry’s, Gummerup’s and Municipal loads 
and it was found that Kerry offered the highest ratios between Nutrients with minimum flow rate. The 
actual C/P and N/P ratio for Gummerup and Kerry were greater than Theoretical value. Regardless of 
Kerry’s proportion of discharge, the waste water is P-limited. The more Kerry discharges into 
Municipal stream, the more P-limited it will be. Finally, If the production rate will be doubled then CNP 
ratio for Kerry will increase from 115-9-1 to 135-11-1.

1 Introduction 
 
                        With rapid growth of cities and increase in population, resources are becoming 
increasingly scarce. Population and economic growth have led to higher resource requirement, which 
has put more stress on resource supply and environment. According to an estimate, about 50 to 100% 
of waste resources lost are contained in WW (Waste water). That’s the major reason that industries 
are now focusing more on recovering those substances (Puyol et al., 2017).  
 
Waste water has many types, among which Domestic and Industrial are the ones that affect the 
human health most. The wastewater discharged from residential, office, commercial and institutes 
falls under the domestic sewage category. It is 99% primarily water mixed with organic and inorganic 
matter. The waste water effluent streaming out of the Industries is Industrial waste water and its 
composition varies, depending on the production and processing of the materials. Generally, because 
of ease of high organic concentrations present in industrial waste water, readily biodegradability and 
higher inorganic loads, it might show nutrient deficiency. When industrial effluent discharge gets 
mixed with domestic wastewater then this combination is called municipal wastewater and the quality 
of this mixture shows high variability in term of nutrients, organic and inorganic matter (Jern, 2006). 
 
Waste water has many types of constituents in it. Other than organic and inorganic materials, the 
constituents present in wastewater are micro-organisms, nutrients, biodegradable organic material 
and metals (henze et al., 2001). Today, wastewater is no longer viewed as a liability and waste, but 
instead as a valuable resource, both in terms of untapped energy resource and nutrient recovery. 
There is a strong interest in recovering materials and energy from wastewater. The composition of 
waste water generally varies from one source to another but the main parameters that actually help 
in defining the quality of waste water are solids (dissolved, suspended, colloidal), Nitrogen, 
Phosphorous and organic matter (Sperling, 2008) . 
 
The two major aims of waste water treatment are to dispose of industrial effluents without posing 
any threat or danger to human health and to recover the nutrients, energy and any other valuable 
resource from waste water. (Templeton & Butler, n.d.). The treatment is usually divided into three 
stages preliminary, primary and secondary treatments (Valentino, Morgan-Sagastume, Campanari, & 
Werker, 2017)  but if more than 95% BOD and solid removal is desired then advanced and final 
treatment are used (Kumar, 2016).

1.1 Dairy Industry and Waste Water: 
In Europe, one of the major industrial effluent producing industry is Dairy. This industry usually has 
high Chemical and Biochemical demand present in its effluent waste water due to the presence of 
high load of soluble organic matter and suspended solids. The composition of WW is also highly 
variable due to raw milk, different types of cheese (mainly whey) and some percentage of cleaning 
water. The characteristics of dairy effluents may vary significantly, depending on the final products 
and operation methods used for manufacturing purposes.(Carvalho, Prazeres, & Rivas, 2013).

1.2 The A–B Process: A Novel two Stage Wastewater Treatment System: 
In Two stage waste water treatment process, the A stage consists of the aerobic reactor and when 
influent passes through this reactor, the growth of heterotrophic and nitrifying organism take place. 
The outflow from aerobic reactor enters the B stage consisting of anoxic reactor and mixed with 
stirring. When the oxygen from air is supplied to water the bacteria start consuming the organic matter 
and as a result produce Carbon dioxide. After passing through settling tank, the WW recycle back to 
Aerobic reactor where by N2 is stripped off and Ammonia is nitrified to nitrate by the bacteria. (Henze 
& Comeau, 2008) .The concentration of solids start decreasing as bacteria feed on themselves during 
the digestion period.  So, finally Carbon dioxide is produced in aerobic digestion, while biogas 
(methane and CO2) is produced during anaerobic digestion. 
 
Figure 1: The post-denitrification single sludge biological nitrogen removal system (Henze & Comeau, 2008) 
 For higher process stability and effluent quality, a two-step A-B process is proved to be cost effective.  
A- stage is operated at high reduction rate and its stable. As, the A-stage mainly retains particulate 
and colloidal particles, only dissolved particles are passed into effluent which reduces energy 
requirement for aeration and helps in low sludge production in B-stage.  
1.2 Carbon Recovery: 
 
There are many methods to recover Carbon, one of them being use of biopolymers which can be 
produced by organic acids (Carboxylic acids) , hydrogen and methane.  Chemicals such as FeCl3, AlCl3 
and alum have been widely used as coagulants in wastewater treatment. It was noticed that when 
using in dying industry for COD reduction, AlCl3 showed high reduction in COD as compared to PAC 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
and alum. (Irfan et al., 2017). These chemicals are easy to control, have high effectiveness and are 
quite inexpensive as compared to biopolymers (Keeley et al., 2012). 
 
 Conventional sedimentation processes can remove about 30% of the Carbon from waste water as 
compared to flocculation/coagulation processes, which can recover about 97% of particulate Carbon 
and partly dissolved Carbon present in waste water. One of the conventional sedimentation process 
are Chemically Enhanced Primary Sedimentation processes (CEPS), which can perform 27% Carbon 
recovery mainly in the form of VFA’s. (Lin, Li, Li, Xu, & Li, 2017) 
1.4 Methane (CH4): 
For the waste water treatment, anaerobic digestion is considered as a vital technology. The final 
product that is obtained by anaerobic digestion of organic matter is Biogas, which is generally a 
mixture of Methane and Carbon dioxide. The composition of Methane and Carbon dioxide in Biogas 
is about 55-75 vol% for CH4 and 25-45 vol% for CO2. Furthermore, it can be used as source of energy 
for heating and electricity generation purposes (de Mes, Stams, Reith, & Zeeman, 2003).  
The production rate of Methane varies from high to low, depending on the anaerobic digester and the 
set of conditions. The anaerobic digester has mixed reactor which heats the sewage sludge with weeks 
long retention time. Firstly, fermentative bacteria breaks large organic molecules to fatty acids and 
alcohols and then when these low molecular weight compounds are break down, methane and Carbon 
dioxide are produced. (Hobson, 2000) 
1.5 Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorous (P): 
During anaerobic waste water treatment, Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorous, Sodium, Potassium and 
Sulphur are needed for the growth of bacteria (Isa, 1991). The inorganic nutrients in the ideal 
concentrations are necessary for the growth of bacteria for biological treatments to be successful. If 
the ideal concentration is not present then small nutrient loads should be provided for compensation. 
This shows how important it is to have balanced relationship between C, N and P for the 
biodegradation process to be effective. During the waste water treatment, the C:N:P ratio for aerobic 
process should be 100:10:1 and for anaerobic WWT it should be 250:5:1 (Bashaar, 2004).

2. Overall Objective and aims of thesis: 
A new wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) will be built in Assens Kommune in the coming years 
centralizing six small WWTPs around Assens. The WWTP in Gummerup was chosen as a pilot for 
testing new strategies for wastewater treatment and recovery, involving the before-mentioned A and 
B stage in a project called the HEPWAT project (see http://www.hepwat.dk/). Gummerup WWTP in 
addition to municipal wastewater receives waste from a nearby dairy industry Kerry, producing a rich 
wastewater stream that is on-site used for the carbon recovery. The Kerry’s industrial waste water is 
mainly composed of dairy (cheese) waste and thus seems a promising resource for Carbon recovery. 
Currently, the Carbon is being recovered there by treatment involving variable dosing of FeCl2and 
polymers. The Kerry industry is interested in increasing their production which will result in increased 
load of waste water and constructing a direct pipeline to the WWTP without onsite carbon removal. 
The aims of the thesis are to: 
1. Analyse and design the Sampling Campaign for an industrial Kerry’s waste water  
2. Characterise the Kerry’s waste water (C, N, P) 
3. Study the Carbon recovery potential of Kerry’s waste water and test two different methods, 
varying polymers/chemicals 
4. Assess the methane yield from the digestion of organic solids  
Finally, a case study involving the mixture of Industrial and Municipal waste water streams was 
performed and CNP loads were evaluated

3.2.1 Purpose of the statistical analysis: 
 
The purpose was to use statistical analysis on the dataset provided by Kerry to design the best possible 
sampling campaign with the aim to characterize Kerry´s wastewater. In contrast to municipal 
wastewater streams, that often show a diurnal and weekly pattern, industrial wastewater streams 
might not follow the same trends. The wastewater stream from Kerry is correlated with production at 
the factory, and therefore highly fluctuating in flow and COD even coming from the buffer tank. This 
made the statistical analysis necessary, before designing a sampling campaign 
 The focus of statistical analysis was to analyse whether any time-dependence pattern for inlet and 
outlet flow rates with COD exists or not. The co-relation between influent and effluent samples for 
COD and pH was also tried to be established. For this purpose, percentiles, mean and standard 
deviation values for distribution of flow with COD and pH were calculated to study the daily/weekly 
variations. The results of statistical analysis can be found in section (4.1.2) and (4.1.3) and based on 
those results, a sampling campaign was designed described in section (3.2.2)

pre-treatment stream. However, it is the intention that this untreated stream will be directed to the 
HEPWAT pilot plant. Therefore, in-order to get better insight of the composition, a 13-day sampling 
campaign was conducted on the pre-treatment and after-treatment stream, where the 6 daily-

3.3 Waste water Characterisation: 
 
3.3.1 Total Suspended Solids (TSS):  
 
Total suspended solids (TSS) were measured in laboratory by filtering 10 ml volume of influent sample 
and 20 ml of effluent sample, drying the filter and captured solids, then weighing the filter to 
determine the weight of the captured suspended solids in the sample. The size of the filer used was 
0.47mm. The residue left on the filter is dried to a constant weight at a temperature between 103 °C 
and 105 °C.  
 
3.3.2 Volatile Suspended Solids (VSS):  
 
To determine the operational behaviour and biological concentration, the Volatile suspended solids 
data plays critical role. The same filter on which the solids were captured for TSS is now heated to 550 
°C for 30 minutes. The weight loss determines the VSS and can be calculated as described below:  
Volatile Suspended solids = (A-B). 1000 Sample volume 
Fixed solids = (B-C). 1000 Sample volume 
Where,  
A= weight of residue + dish before ignition (mg)  
B= weight of residue + dish after ignition (mg)  
C= weight of dish or filter  
The TSS measurement was done in duplicates for both samples.  
 
3.3.3 Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) 
 
COD is a measure of oxygen equivalent of organic matter susceptible to oxidation by strong chemical 
oxidant. COD measures the organic matter which is present in the wastewater as contaminant and is 
not readily biodegradable. (Templeton & Butler, n.d.) The calorimetric method has been used in 
determining the COD in which high-range pre-prepared vials are used that change color from orange 
to green based on the amount of oxidation. The kits used are LK-514 hache Lange having range (100-
2000 mg/L O2). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
 
3.3.4 Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD):  
 
The BOD test is known as BOD5 or BOD7 since it is based on the accurate measure of DO (dissolved 
oxygen) at the beginning and end of a five/seven-day period in which the sample is held in dark, 
incubated at 20 °C. An automatic measurement system called Oxitop is used for the experimental 
determination of BOD concentration over time. The system includes a series of measuring heads which 
register oxygen depletion from a sample due to the aerobic degradation of the carbonaceous 
substances. This method analysis generates a BOD degradation curve which is used afterwards to 
determine the ultimate BOD. Since all the samples used for BOD analysis are frozen, seeding of the 
samples during the BOD test is necessary. BOD7 method was selected for measuring BOD in waste 
water with 97 ml of substrate. The samples were diluted by 1/10 and tests were performed in 
triplicates.  
 
3.3.5 Total and dissolved Phosphate:  
 
For characterisation purposes, total Phosphorous can either be determined by Converting organic and 
inorganic phosphorus to orthophosphate by ion chromatography method or using calorimetric 
method. The latter was used in our case and the kits used were LCK-350 Hache Lange having range of 
2-20 mg. In this method, Phosphate ions react with molybdate and antimony ions in an acidic solution 
to form an antimonyl phosphomolybdate complex, which is reduced by ascorbic acid to 
phosphomolybdenum blue. The effluents were diluted to 1/5 and influents were diluted to 1/10 for 
the tests purposes. The dissolved Phosphate PO43
− measurements were done by SCALAR, whereby 
the samples were filtered by 0.2 µm pore size filter paper and diluted to 1/10. 
3.3.6 pH:  
 
The more effective role of bacteria in waste water treatment is seen when the pH ranges from 6 to 8. 
For the biological activity to take place, the acids present in waste water needs to be neutralize and 
for that purpose optimum amount of alkalinity should be present. During pH measurement, it was 
carefully ensured that no heating source was nearby since pH is temperature dependent and it could 
have impact on results obtained.  
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
 
3.3.7 Total Nitrogen (TN):  
 
Total Nitrogen is the sum of all Nitrogen fractions including Ammonia, Nitrite, Nitrate and organic 
Nitrogen.For the measurement of Total Nitrogen, Hache Lange Kit 238 was used . NH4N measurements 
were done by calorimetric method in which LCK-302 Hache Lange kit having range of 47 – 130 mg/L 
NH4-N was used. The dilution factor of 1/10 and 1/20 were introduced for effluents and influents 
respectively. 
3.3.8 Size Distribution 
Particle size distribution has vital importance for the characterization process as it helps in distributing 
the total amount of particles present in WW into four categories. The size categories of conaminatns 
are dissolved (<0.001µm), colloidal (0.001-1 µm), super colloidal (1-100 µm), and settleable (> 100 
µm). By using different pore size filters 0.2, 0.45 and 0.7mm and measuring COD afterwards, we will 
try to study the effect of particles size on C recovery (Henze & Comeau, 2008) 
 
3.3.9 Carbon Recovery by Flocculation/Coagulation: 
Conventional sedimentation processes can remove about 30% of the Carbon from waste water as 
compared to flocculation/coagulation processes, which can recover about 97% of particulate Carbon 
and partly dissolved Carbon present in waste water. Before a coagulant and flocculant were selected, 
a series of jar tests was carried out to determine which coagulant and coagulant/flocculant 
combination was most suitable for pre-treatment of the wastewater used in this study. The mixing 
was done at 150 rpm and Aluminium Chloride Hexahydrate was introduced during the mixing at doses 
of 50, 100, and 200 mg/L.  The propellers were set to run for 2 mins at 150 rpm and then next 10 mins, 
the mixing was done at relatively low speed of around 30 rpm so that it should help in flocculation. 
After 30 mins of settling time, the treated water sample from top was collected and COD was 
determined for these samples again by using vial kits LK514 to evaluate the carbon recovery 
percentage.  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
 
3.3.11 Volatile Fatty Acids (VFA): 
 
In-order to perform VFA, 6ml of sample and 6ml of distilled water was added in the test glass, acidified 
with 800µl Orthophosphoric acid. 2ml of sample was taken from test glass and put in Eppendorf tube 
and centrifuged at 13400 rpm for 10 mins. 1ml of supernatant and 100µl VFA internal standard 
solution was added into GC vial and was stored at -18 degrees Celsius until SKALAR measurements. 
 
3.3.12 Biochemical Methane Potential (BMP): 
The Biochemical methane potential is the maximum volume of methane produced per g of VS 
substrate. One method of predicting the biogas production is by Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), 
which can quantify the amount of organic matter in waste water. By using strong oxidizing chemical, 
the oxygen equivalent of organic matter can be measured. For measuring BMP, mesophilic method 
was used where experiment was performed on blank, controlled and substrate samples. For WW 
samples, 32 ml inoculum and 8ml of WW in glass bottles having volume of 340 ml were placed in 
incubator at 37 °C for a month. For diluted samples, ½ diluted factor was used while for controlled 
samples, 8ml of water and 0.08g of Avicel was used. The experiments were performed in triplicates.

4. RESULTS and Discussions: 
 
4.1 Design of Sampling Campaign: 
From a visit to Kerry on 14/12/2017, six daily-average samples were collected. From the data 
presented by Kerry, the initial plan was to establish a relationship between flow rate, COD, BOD, pH 
and Temperature. The nutrient analysis of the pre-and after-treatment samples would help in 
understanding the effect of treatment on the wastewater whether if there is any observable 
treatment pattern between pre-treatment and after-treatment. Based on these results we will 
extrapolate the information of the 6-day sampling period into the 247-day period provided by Kerry, 
using COD’s variability as a marker to predict: TN, TP, BOD, COD, TSS etc. 
 
4.1.1 Statistical Analysis of Kerry’s data: 
The analysis comprises 247 points of daily-average after-treatment (effluent) samples as no 
information about the pre-treatment (influent) composition is available: COD (mg/L) and Flow rate 
(m3/d) are analysed. Overall, COD and Flow are not correlated and thus the measured flow cannot be 
used to predict COD concentration (R2 = 0.31). The composition of the wastewater discharged does 
not follow a pattern. The average COD removal during Kerry’s physicochemical treatment was found 
to be approximately 52%: from 56 ± 37 to 27 ± 20 kg COD/h. The COD concentration decreased from 
≈ 9500 mg/L ± 39% to ≈ 3600 mg/L ± 70%.

Conclusions: 
In-order to recover Carbon, the influent and effluent samples were collected from Kerry and 
Gummerup facilities. The sampling campaign was set up based on the data provided by Kerry where 
no patterns were found for flow or COD concentrations in Kerry’s daily-averaged effluent samples 
for a 9-month period. 
A total number of 78 influent and 72 effluent samples were collected. Consumables for 
nutrient analysis for all the samples are costly so the focus was put on the influent samples rather 
than the effluents. The characterisation of waste water was performed where influent COD value 
was 7551 ± 1488 and 1269 ± 216 for the effluents of daily averaged samples. The COD and BOD 
values were quite high but their ratio was about 2.3 which shows that effluent discharge is readily 
biodegradable. The COD/TP ratio was quite high, ranging from 171 to 353 for influent and effluent 
samples. Though it’s bit high, but the composition of waste water varies depending on the 
industries. High C ratio shows it has higher potential for the recovery of Carbon. 
It was observed that for 2g AlCl3/100ml of waste water, the COD reduced by 37.2% ± 15.6% and when 
1g AlCl3/100 ml of waste water was used the COD removal was about 74.8% ± 8.8%. So, 1gAlCl3/100ml 
dosage was the most optimum and effective for recovering Carbon. It was more effective than FeCl2 
used by Kerry for Carbon removal as its efficiency was only 54%. 
It was observed that the 20% of the particles were present is dissolved form, 31% in colloidal and the 
rest 49% were in particulate. 
The BMP values for the first 21 days were not so high and one of the reasons might be because it 
usually takes about 30 to 60 days to complete. So, its basically still in between initial and final stage.  
 
Kerry’s flow rate was less than Gummerup, but the nutrient load fraction was much higher in Kerry 
as compared to Gummerup, which means that the nutrient concentration is very high in Kerry’s load. 
Regardless of Kerry’s proportion of discharge, the waste water is P-limited. The more Kerry 
discharges into Municipal stream, the more P-limited it will be. Finally, f the production rate will be 
doubled by Kerry then CNP ratio for Kerry will increase from 115-9-1 to 135-11-1.
