Abstract 
The objective of this project is to examine the application of autoregressive models on the vibration 
responses themselves (rather than the correlation function) in the operational modal analysis of time-
invariant as well as time-varying structural systems. To investigate time-varying systems, the autoregressive 
techniques must be used on a short time scale. To this end, a window formulation is applied, where the 
autoregression is carried out on each time window separately. For every window the autoregressive 
frequency identification is done on the estimated modal coordinates associated with the response signal. 
Only where the modal signal has sufficient amplitude and the correlation function has the expected form, 
the frequencies identified from windows are accepted. 
The methods are applied to experimental as well as simulated responses from a T-structure where a finite 
element model of the T-structure has been used. Frequencies, damping ratios and mode shapes are 
identified. The forms of the mode shapes are discussed, and so are the use of various filters and the 
influence from other peaks on the results obtained. Moreover, the modal participation factors for the 
response signal itself are derived from the complex modal coordinates. 
When using the signal itself instead of the correlation function for frequency identification, the damping 
ratio turns out to be a good differentiator between physical and numerical modes. To this end, data from 
the Heritage Court Tower are also analyzed. 
For comparison, a simple period identification technique is applied on the modal coordinate signal, 
estimating the instantaneous frequencies. From this also the frequencies for a time window can be 
determined.  
The application of the Hilbert transform is also examined for the Heritage Court Tower data. Dividing the 
modal coordinate signal by the envelope of the analytic signal related to the Hilbert transform, the 
damping is removed. The obtained frequency variations are compared with results obtained without using 
the Hilbert transformation.

1 Introduction 
Buildings, bridges, wind turbines and other large structures are subject to vibrations, and it is central for 
human safety and for the function of the structures that the structures are stable. Every large structure is 
subjected to vibrations due to everyday use, wind, and earthquakes at some areas. At sea, large oil 
platforms are subject to large waves. 
1.1 Modal identification 
If the vibrations are large enough or reinforced because of constructive interference with the structureтАЩs 
natural frequencies, the state of the structure may change and in continuation hereof the modal 
parameters, including natural frequencies, damping ratios and mode shapes, will change. Thus, during the 
design phase and structural health monitoring (SHM) of a structure it is important to be aware of possible 
changes in the modal parameters. By tracking the structural vibrations on both long and short term, the 
modal parameters can be obtained and verify the state of the structure. A change in the modal parameters 
may indicate a change in the stiffness of the structure, and the time of this change will be known. The 
stiffness change could either be the material properties which change or a damage to a joint. SHM is 
important to make sure structures can get fixed or torn down before they collapse. 
Compared to manual inspection, monitoring the vibrations and finding the modal parameters is a safe and 
relatively easy way to do SHM on buildings, bridges, oil platforms, wind turbines and other large structures. 
Using the measured vibration data, operational modal analysis (OMA) is applicable to accurately analyze 
the monitored response signal [Ref 1]. OMA is based on ambient vibrations, and the structure can 
therefore be analyzed directly and can operate normally during testing. This contrasts with using classical 
dynamic methods or forced vibration test where only a scaled version or a closed structure respectively can 
be used [Ref 2]. When using classical experimental modal analysis (EMA) both the excitation forces and the 
vibration responses are needed. Whereas when using OMA only the vibration responses are needed, as the 
excitation forces are assumed to be of random nature. As the excitation forces are needed for EMA, the 
modal analysis is here normally carried out in laboratory conditions. Thus, OMA is more suited for civil 
structures (e.g., wind turbines, bridges, and oil platforms), whereas EMA is more suited for mechanical and 
aeronautic structures (cars, plains, spaceships, etc.). 
Structures in areas with high risk of strong winds or earthquakes needs to be health monitored more often 
as damages can be the result of these events. Especially if the frequency of the wind corresponds with the 
normal frequency of the structure, damages are prone to happen. If no constructive interference is 
encountered between the frequency of the structure and, e.g., an earthquake, the natural frequency can 
recover very close to its original vicinity, even though it may have dropped significantly during the event 
[Ref 3]. Due to this recovery effects right after the earthquake, post-earthquake analyses can 
underestimate the damage in contrary to real time identifications [Ref 4]. 
In the book by R. Brincker and C. Ventura [Ref 1] and for simulated time-varying structures by K. K. Solheim 
[Ref 5], the OMA is based on the correlation function of the response. In this project, it is examined how 
the vibration responses themselves can be used directly, and if frequencies and frequency steps can be 
verified. The purpose of this MSc Thesis is to use classical OMA on the response signal itself to estimate the 
modal properties of time-invariant as well as simulated time-varying structural systems from short time 
vibration signals using frequency domain decomposition (FDD) and the auto regressive (AR) models.

Four response signals are analyzed:  
Time invariant systems 
1. Responses measured from a T-structure in a vibration experiment. 
2. Simulated response found using a simulated finite-element model (FEM) of the T-structure and 
adding a random force. 
3. Responses measured from the Heritage Court Tower during everyday use of the building. 
 
Time varying systems 
4. Simulated response found using a state-space formulation [Ref 6] of the FEM of the T-structure and 
adding a force and a frequency step in time. 
1.2 Identification techniques 
The primary modal identification techniques used in this project are the frequency domain decomposition 
(FDD) technique and the auto regression / poly reference (AR/PR) technique [Ref 1], [Ref 7], [Ref 8]. 
The FDD is based on singular value decomposition (SVD) of the spectral density matrix. The natural 
frequencies are identified directly from the peaks in the plot of singular values. Each peak corresponds to a 
natural frequency, and the corresponding singular vectors are interpreted as the mode shape vectors. 
The time-domain AR/PR is based on AR models, in which the AR matrices are estimated through a fit to 
experimental data using the least square method. For the time-varying system, the AR/PR identification is 
done for selected window segments of modal coordinates [Ref 5]. 
Finally, for comparison, direct period identification is used on the modal coordinates.

2.3 Other time-domain techniques 
There are other time-domain techniques than AR/PR, which is the identification method used in this thesis.

3.1 Signal processing and the Hilbert transformation 
All considered signals are detrended, removing DC-offsets in the signal. This is done by segmenting the 
signal and removing the mean value of the signal in each segment. Using overlapping segments which are 
tapered by a tapering window, discontinuities between data segments are avoided. 
Moreover, filters will be used in the identification process to focus the identification on a specific frequency 
range. Either a lowpass filter or a bandpass filter is used here. Finally, the signals are down-sampled / 
decimated to a suitable sampling frequency for analysis.

5 Simulated time-varying system 
5.1 The simulated time-varying system 
The simplest way to make a time varying system once the mass and stiffness matrices are known for a 
certain structure, is to add a time varying factor to the (undamped) frequencies ЁЭЬФрм┤рпб keeping the damping 
ratios ╧Врпб at a given value. This is to avoid changing the mass and stiffness matrices at each step. 
Here the T-structure is examined again using the mass and stiffens matrix for the FE model found by ANSYS 
and a random force signal to excite the modes once every 10,000 time steps, enough to have a good 
response over 100,000 time steps of size ЁЭССЁЭСб= 0.000605 s. The time varying factor is an instant frequency 
drop on 0.5 Hz after 30,000 points (at 18.15 s), followed by an instant 0.25 Hz rise in frequency after 
another 30,000 points (at 36.30 s).

6. Conclusions 
6.1 The results achieved 
The application of AR/PR on a response signal itself rather than on its correlation function has shown to be 
successful. However, the outcome of the autoregression depends on several parameters that must be 
selected, one of them being the number of autoregressive matrices. Modes interact, and filters are 
necessary to obtain the best results for the frequency range in focus. The complex modal coordinates are 
used to determine the modal participation, which is a measure of the influence of the mode and the 
corresponding mode shape. 
One of the essential observations when using AR/PR on the signal itself is the ability to select the physical 
modes from the numerical modes based on the value of the damping ratio. Even for the Heritage Court 
Tower data, where the frequency identification from the correlation function is using several datasets to 
identify the physical modes based on the modal participation factors, the physical modes are here directly 
identified as those with the significant smallest damping ratios. 
To analyze time-varying structural systems, it is necessary to identify frequencies on a short timescale, and 
it is helpful to consider the estimated real modal coordinates of the signal and then analyze them 
separately. The window formulation, where the modal coordinate signal is divided in shorter time windows 
and AR/PR is applied on each window separately, is helpful to identify events where the frequency changes. 
This method with its modal coordinate and correlation function criteria also works when the modal 
coordinates are found from the signal itself. The identified window frequencies reveal some influence of 
other near-by modes. 
The size of the smallest eligible window changes with the size of the criterion measure ЁЭЬОрпарпЬрпб. The size of the 
window decreases rather abruptly from a large size to a small size when ЁЭЬОрпарпЬрпб passes a certain characteristic 
value. Despite the window size becomes smaller and therefore the number of windows is larger, the 
number of eligible windows also decreases with ЁЭЬОрпарпЬрпб. 
The AR/PR method has been applied directly to experimental as well as simulated response signals. For the 
T-structure studied, the method can clearly distinct between two near-by peaks, and the mode shapes are 
consistent with those found from the finite element model. The focus has here been on the four first 
modes, which all can be identified using local bandpass filters, or by using one wide lowpass filter, the 
former to establish the frequencies more correctly, the latter to understand the relative mode behavior. 
Time variations have been introduced by changing the natural frequencies in a state space model of the 
vibration response from the T-structure. Here also, the window formulation works well based on the real 
modal coordinates determined from the signal itself. 
For comparison, a period identification method is used where the тАЬinstantaneousтАЭ frequency is found as 
the inverse of a single period, and the corresponding frequency of a time window is found as the number of 
full periods within the window divided by the sum of these periods or alternatively as the inverse of the 
median of the full periods in the window. This gives a good picture of the frequency variations and 
emphasize both the influence of near-by modes and the effect of the window criteria. 
==PAGE:71==
71 / 85 
While the period identification seems to be a useful application, the use of the Hilbert transformation does 
not show better results in the analysis carried out in this project. For the window frequency identification, 
the frequencies become more scattered, and for the period identification, it makes no difference. 
6.2 Future perspectives 
The results obtained depends to some degree on the many parameters there are for the filtering and for 
the AR/PR method and further studies are needed to fully understand the use of AR/PR on the signal itself. 
For example, to what extent is the identification of the physical modes among many numerical modes, here 
based on the damping ratio, easier when using AR/PR on the signal itself. 
Another challenge encountered is the form of the filters used. While the spectrum near a given frequency 
does not change by adding a filter outside the frequency range considered, the AR/PR results are 
influenced by the frequency filters used. Even when the roll-off bands are well outside the frequency range 
studied, the frequencies in the roll-of bands are still a part of the filtered signal, and the autoregressive fit 
will be influenced by these, although maybe to a small extent. 
Regarding the window frequency identification, the best results are maybe not achieved at the minimal 
window size. The variation of window size according to the тАЬinstantaneousтАЭ period may give better results. 
To this end, the period identification technique also deserves more attention as an additional application, 
e.g. to study the influence from other modes.
