Summary (English)
The goal of this thesis is to develop a prototype of a Virtual Reality (VR) game,
which uses gamiﬁcation to "train" young children to keep their heads still in
preparation for Magnetic Resonance (MR) scans of the brain. Currently the
standard procedure is to give young children anesthesia in order to keep them
from moving during MR scans. However, research has shown that this may
have neurotoxic eﬀects on young minds. Therefore, eﬀorts are being made in
the form of preparation apps to reduce anxiety and train children in staying still
during scans in order to avoid giving anesthesia.
This project will use the ability of VR headsets to track head movements and
to use gamiﬁcation in order to encourage keeping the head still. The thesis ﬁrst
reviews previous work using VR and the eﬀorts to undertake anesthesia free MR
scans. Subsequently, after outlining the requirements for a piece of software to
register and give feedback on the user’s movements, a research prototype game
was built around it. The result is a VR racing game, where the player makes
a car drive by keeping their head still. To win the race, the player will have to
keep the head still. If they do not, then they will lose speed and possibly the race.
The project has shown that it is possible to develop an engaging game, where
the main gameplay element is to keep the head still, rather than to move. The
ﬁrst version of the prototype received negative feedback from test participants,
but after some further tests and adjustments to the gameplay mechanics, it
received very favorable feedback and positive results.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fulﬁlment of the requirements for
acquiring an M.Sc. in Digital Media Engineering.
The subject of the thesis is how to gamify keeping still in preparation for MR
scans. For this purpose the thesis develops a prototype VR game, which rewards
the player for being still. The target audience for this game is children.
The thesis consists of an overview of the state of the art for using VR in medicine
and other ﬁelds, which is used to identify the contribution of this project as well
as the core needs the the game must fulﬁll. The thesis then analyzes how these
needs can be gamiﬁed, and proposes a design. Finally, it covers the design’s
implementation, analyses the test results and discusses potential improvements.
Lyngby, 03-July-2020
Marc Christensen

Chapter 1
Problem Statement and
Background
1.1
The problem speciﬁcation
Gamiﬁcation, applying game mechanics to something that is not a game to
increase user engagement[1], has been used in the medical ﬁeld to help the
patients deal with medical procedures more eﬀectively[2]. It can for example
help reduce stress in the patients over their treatment[3], or train doctors for
minor surgeries[4].
This project aims to utilize gamiﬁcation, as a tool to help children prepare for
MR scans, which require the scanned part of the body, to be motionless during
the scan. In the case of this project, the scanned object is the head. This is
necessary, as the scan results can be rendered unusable with excess movements
of the scanned body parts. It will see the implementation of a research prototype
of a VR game, which will "train" young children to lie still in preparation for
an MR scan. In particular, the game will need to:
==PAGE:16==
2
Problem Statement and Background
• Reward players for keeping their heads still while playing
• Punish players for excessive head movements while playing.
• Be simple enough that even a young child, age 3 to 10, can play and
understand the game.
For this project a VR headsets is chosen to provide audio and visual output.
VR headsets can provide unique experiences from those of traditional monitors
or TV’s that other games use for visual and audio output. This experience is
often said by users to be more immersive, as the user feels more connected to
the world, and user satisfaction from playing games in VR can be higher as a
result of this.[5] However, more importantly for this project, given that the VR
headsets are attached to the users’ faces, they can be used to measure the head
movements of users with great accuracy.
This project was originally suggested by Melanie Ganz, in collaboration with
Kathrine Skak Madsen. In earlier research as well as an ongoing research project,
the MoCo project have investigated the use of VR technology for patient prepa-
ration before MR scanning. This would be highly beneﬁcial for the children,
as it, would reduce the need for using anesthesia during the scan to keep them
still.
Note, that the implementation featured in this project will be a research proto-
type, not a ﬁnal product. It is meant to be a prototype that can be expanded
upon in the future if the ﬁeld tests of the prototype yield positive results.
1.2
Background
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), is a type of diagnostic procedure that has
been used since the 1980’s. It uses magnetic ﬁelds and radio waves, to provide
scans of body parts surrounded by bones, most commonly the spine and brain[6].
These scans, known as Magnetic Resonance (MR) scans, can then be used to
detect abnormalities in the scanned parts of the body, which could be a stroke,
a tumor or other brain related diseases for example. The images provided by
the scans are quite detailed and are considered one of the best ways to diagnose
abnormal brain conditions[6].
==PAGE:17==
1.2 Background
3
Figure 1.1: An MR scanner.
The scanning itself sees the patient lying as motionlessly as possible in a large
cylindrical magnet, which produces an extremely powerful ﬁeld of several Tesla.
As such, patients must not bring any metallic objects in with them, and patients
with pacemakers and the like are only considered for MR scans in emergencies[7].
An electrical coil is placed around the body part that will be scanned, which will
transmit and receive the radio signals, that are decoded to scan images of the
body part in question. To ensure that these scans are as accurate as possible,
it is very important that the patient keeps the scanned part of their body as
motionless as possible, and failure to do this can result in having to redo a scan,
due to motion artefacts blurring the scan result, or even leading to misleading
results.
Figure 1.2: A poor scan result (left) vs a good one (right).

Problem Statement and Background
1.3
MR scanners and children
It can be diﬃcult to scan young children. This may be due to movement dis-
orders such as Cerebral Palsy[8], which is a permanent movement disorder that
appears in early childhood, of which the symptoms include, but are not lim-
ited to: Lack of muscle coordination, shaking (tremors) and diﬃculty in precise
movements. In 90% of cases the disorder is a result of brain damage. Around
150 children are born with this disease in Denmark every year and, according
to guidelines, a diagnosis should be made within the ﬁrst 12 to18 months of
symptoms showing. The diagnosis of this disorder can be diﬃcult to do with
the MR scanner, precisely because of symptoms such as tremors.
However, also children without movement disorders may be diﬃcult to scan due
to unrest or anxiety, which may even make them unwilling to enter the scanner
at all. For these reasons, it is standard procedure in most Danish hospitals to
give anesthesia to children under the age of 8 to 10 when they are scanned.
However, new studies have shown that there may be neurotoxic eﬀects from
anesthesia if applied to young children[9][10] and the FDA has even listed a
warning against its use on young children[11].
1.4
The Motion Correction (MoCo) Project
In light of the above, the Elsass foundation has funded The Motion Correction
Project (MoCo)[12], which is headed by Melanie Ganz, Researcher at the Neu-
robiology Research Institute (NRU) at Rigshospitalet and assistant professor at
the Department of Computer Science of the University (DIKU). The project
aims to demonstrate that most young children can undergo medical imaging
procedures, without the use of anesthesia, while retaining high quality scans.
The project aims to utilize developments in medical imaging technology to
demonstrate a novel approach of imaging. This includes, preparation for the
scan by simulating the scanning environment with a mock scanner setup to re-
duce anxiety, training the children to lie still for the scan, and a marker-less
motion tracking device that can register movements so that the doctors may
conduct motion correction in the acquired images.
==PAGE:19==
1.4 The Motion Correction (MoCo) Project
5
For the training aspect of their approach, the MoCo project contacted Michael
Rose of DTU. As part of their preparation and training aspects of their approach,
the MoCo project was attempting to develop a mobile app the children could
take home with them, to help prepare for the scans. Though not included in
the Elsass grant for the MoCo project, she also suggested a separate project, a
VR game.
The VR game Melanie suggested, was a game to help train the patients to lie still
in an MR scan, and Michael was asked if he would be interested in oﬀering this
games development as an MSc project together with the MoCo project. Hence,
Michael Rose has been the main supervisor for this project, while Melanie Ganz
and Kathrine Skak Madsen have been co-supervisors.

Chapter 2
State of the Art: VR Case
Studies and MR Scans
Without Anesthesia
Gamiﬁcation has already seen some uses in teaching children, also those with
learning disabilities, by aiding with e.g. math problems, as seen with the kinems
project[13]. This uses the Xbox Kinect to provide children with a special edu-
cation centered around movement based learning. Furthermore, VR and other
immersive interactive media has also been used for training and preparation in
other ﬁelds than the medical one in the past[14]. Interactive media has also
already been used to prepare children for anesthesia free treatment[15], even
with VR[16].
2.1
Preparing for MR scans without anesthesia
Using interactive media to help prepare children for an MR scan without anes-
thesia has been demonstrated to be possible. The Department of Radiology at
Lillebælt Hospital in Kolding conducted a study with the purpose of evaluat-
ing the need for general anesthesia for MR scans with children aged 4 to 6[17].
The study utilized a concept they developed, known as Children Centered Care
==PAGE:22==
8
State of the Art: VR Case Studies and MR Scans Without Anesthesia
(CCC). The concept involved giving the patients an interactive app, having a
trained pediatric team oversee them, having a lounge with a toy scanner to play
in and having a multimedia environment in the MR room.
The CCC concept served as part of the preparation for the scanning for one
group of patients. The results were then compared with a control group, which
was scanned using their standard procedure without having been given the
preparation.
The results were very positive.
The children treated with the
CCC concept saw the need for anesthesia reduced to only 5%, compared to the
control group in which 57% required anesthesia to complete the scan. The im-
age quality from the CCC scans did not suﬀer in and the setup required was
furthermore deemed overall cost eﬀective by the staﬀ.

2.2 Use of VR technology in medical procedures
9
The interactive application used in the study is an application available for
Android and IOS, called "Med Rumle i MR"[15], developed by MDM Region
Syd Danmark and used at Kolding Hospital. The game features a troll named
Rumle as its mascot, and serves as a demonstration of what happens during an
MR scan to prepare the patient for the scanning itself. In particular it simulates
what it is like to be inside an MR scanner via a tour of a virtual world in the
app.[18] Furthermore, it gives the children some tasks to complete in the world,
such as placing some action ﬁgures into a miniature MR scanner. The app also
blends over into the real world where the virtual world of the app has been
recreated at Kolding hospital as a playing ﬁeld, known as the Rumleverse[19].
This allows the patients to experience the virtual world in real life.
Rumle utilizes the CCC setup that was outlined in the study by Runge et al.
These are an interactive playground in the form of the Rumle app, a trained
pediatric team in the form of the staﬀat Kolding, a children’s lounge with a toy-
scanner as well as a child-friendly multimedia environment in the MR scanner
room in the form of the Rumleverse. It seems this approach has worked at the
Kolding hospital, as they saw a signiﬁcant reduction in the amount of children
who needed anesthesia during the scan[20].
2.2
Use of VR technology in medical procedures
While Rumle aims to reduce anxiety and prepare children for MR scans, other
digital media technologies, such as VR, have been used in medical procedures
before. Planning of surgeries and other medical procedures using VR, have been
shown to be able to reduce anxiety during minimally invasive procedures[4], and
even help numb pain. Furthermore, VR has been used for detecting Alzheimer’s,
as a pain control during trans-urethral microwave therapy[21], Parkinson’s to
help patients regain the ability to walk properly, and rehabilitation[4].
VR has also been used to help patients relax during treatments such as treating
burn wounds, by showing the patient a snowy landscape. "SnowWorld" yielded
positive results regarding the patients stress levels, and also helped ease the
pain of soldiers who had suﬀered burn wounds by using VR to let them shoot
snowballs. While playing the game, the patients showed half as much brain
activity related to pain, and were far less anxious due to being immersed in the
game[3].

The aforementioned projects have shown that it is possible to create immersive
VR experiences that reduce stress in the user during treatments, as well as create
applications that can reduce anxiety to prepare the user for treatments, both

VR can be used with great beneﬁts in other medical ﬁelds to train the users to

The objective of this project is similar to Rumle and My MRI in that it will
aim to prepare children for MR scans without anesthesia. Furthermore, it is
similar to the mentioned cases of utilizing VR to make the patient relax using

scan experience than they are video games with in game goals and challenges.
Compared to Rumle and My MRI this project is diﬀerent in that it is more
interactive.
In particular, the aim of this project is to have the core game goals and challenges
focused on keeping the head still, by conditioning the player to associate the
lack of head movements with rewards. At the same time, it should oﬀer an
immersive interactive experience to distract them from the scanning process
itself. Therefore, in essence, what will set this project apart, will be that it
will be more interactive than most of the other applications mentioned, making
it more of a game similar to the dentists game[2] than e.g. Rumle[15] or My
MRI[16].

3.1
Requirements
First, to reduce anxiety in the user as much as possible, and to not encourage
movement, we want the game to be relaxing, but not boring. In that vein, we
likewise do not want the game to induce motion sickness in the user, as this will
make their experience unpleasant.[27]
==PAGE:28==

A theory for why motion sickness occurs, is that of sensory conﬂict[28]. This
is when one sense of the body, in this case the eyes, senses an experience that
the other ﬁve sense do not experience. This could for example be rolling down
a hill in a barrel in VR, a situation that is likely to cause this phenomenon. If
the user only senses this experience through their eyes but not via their sense
of touch for example, this will create a disconnect in the body’s senses and the
user will feel sick.[29] To accommodate this, we can work towards a design that
will not create too great of a disconnect between what occurs in VR and what
the user is doing in real life.
As the primary audience of this game will be children under the age of 10,
its rules and concepts will have to be simple to understand. Furthermore, the
challenges presented in the game will have to appeal to both very young children
and children who are closer to their early teens, as well as gamers and non-
gamers. As such, the game ought to have parts of it that are more catered to
children who are experienced with video games, and even VR, so they do not
get bored, as well as parts that cater to children who have never seen a video
game in their life.

they are ready.

move.

3.1.2
Early game design
As most games are focused on engaging the player via player action, making a
game where that is the opposite case is quite novel. The goal now becomes to
reward the player for their non-actions rather than their actions. Essentially,
we are looking at making keeping still exciting and engaging for the player.
For this reason, the game, User Interface and general world in it, ought to be
designed in a way that does not encourage movement. Hence, the ﬁeld of regard,
i.e. the place the player must look at to eﬀectively play the game, ought to be
smaller than other games. Furthermore, it should encourage the user to keep
their head facing in one direction. It should be noted, that users of VR games
have a tendency to move their head because of visual stimuli more in VR than
in the real world[30]. As such the game ought to reduce visual stimuli as much
as possible, or at the very least keep it all in the ﬁeld of regard so that the player
is not encouraged to look elsewhere.
3.1.2.1
Initial Concepts
With the requirements outlined, brainstorming of initial ideas could begin and
early concepts could be drawn. Normally in mock scans, i.e. a simulation of
the actual scan process, the nurses have the children watch a movie while inside
the scanner to keep their attention in one place. One could base a game on
this, where the more the user moves, the more blurry and distorted the movie
becomes. However, this experience isn’t terribly interactive for the user, and
isn’t much of a game. Furthermore, should the user grow bored of the movie, or
the movie become so blurry they can’t see it, they may act up simply because
the media can’t capture their attention for long enough. At that point it isn’t
about giving up out of frustration, but rather forfeiting because of boredom or
frustration. As such, some ideas for how to make the experience a bit more
interactive to keep the patients attention for a longer time were outlined.
==PAGE:33==
3.1 Requirements
19
In the realm of video games, there are many game genres, platformers, Role
Playing Game’s (RPG), racing games, shooters, etc. Though not all of them
would be suitable for this project, with genres like RPG’s being a bit too involved
for the young audience.
Furthermore, genres such as platformers are often
focused on action, which could provoke movement in the user if not handled
properly. As such, some genres could be said to be more suited to fulﬁlling
the use case requirements than others. Those would be games that are more
relaxing to play, don’t require much input from the player, have simple controls
and don’t feature actions that could provoke head movements from the player.
With that in mind, initial ideas for game concepts that would ﬁt the style and
requirements outlined by the use cases were put forth.
The ﬁrst idea was to make the setting a rocket travelling through space, with
the player in the cockpit. Here, by keeping still, the player would travel as far
as they could go, with movements causing the rocket to burn more fuel than
when not moving. This would mean the player would get further into space, by
not moving, which would increase the number of points they would get. This
would also mean that moving would see the player lose out on potential points.
This would conceptually work similar to an autorunner, where the purpose of
the game is to get as far as possible while dodging obstacles while the player
moves forwards automatically. Only here, the challenge would be that the player
would get further by keeping still.
The second idea was to make a rail shooter esque game, where the precision
of the shots was determined by how still the player kept their head. In other
words, a shot would be lined up, and then how still the player kept until the
trigger is pulled would determine whether the shot hit its target. It would be
similar to how a hunter has to keep their riﬂe still to hit their target. This idea
was ultimately decided against, because it was felt that what would happen on
screen would be too disconnected from what would be experienced by the player.
In other words, the player would be laying still, while someone was taking shots
and essentially playing a game for them.
Another idea was to design the game as a bit of a horror game, where a monster
would prowl around outside a window, and only by keeping still would it not
notice the player. This idea was ultimately decided against because, given that
the audience is young children, they would most likely become too scared and
not have fun with the game.
Furthermore, given that VR is an immersive
experience, this could also cause panic and as a result, excess movements. In
short, this was not a very child friendly idea.

Ultimately, what was chosen as the best approach was having the setting of the
game be a race between cars, where the players car has the advantage that the
more still they keep their head, the faster they go. This was decided as the best
approach, as many young children enjoy playing with toy cars. Furthermore,
as long as the cars do not move excessively fast, there shouldn’t be too much
stimuli at once for the player, encouraging them to focus on keeping still. In
a way, this idea is similar to the rocket idea, but this idea adds a competitive
element to the game in the form of computer controlled players (AI players),
and gives the player an incentive to keep still to beat their opponents.
3.2
Game Design
The outlined design sees the player take control of a car and race against other
cars, where keeping their head still would give them an advantage in the race.
In this scene, the main camera would be tied to the player, though whether the
player would like to view the game in ﬁrst person or third person should be up
to them. This is because while ﬁrst person might be more immersive, the ﬁeld
of regard will be moved a bit ahead of the one in third person. This results
in the player as a result not being able to see another racer sneak up on them
from behind. In other words, by being able to see the whole car, the player will
also be able to see what is around them, rather than be limited to what is in
front of them. This is important, as the player cannot turn their head to look
to their side in ﬁrst person, since this would result in the game punishing them
for moving their head.
3.2.1
The Game rules
The core aspect of how a game plays are the rules of the game itself, which
govern the conditions for winning and losing. The rules of this game will be
those of a race between cars, with the ﬁrst car to ﬁnish being the winner. This
gives the player someone, in the form of the two AI players, they can try to beat,
encouraging them not only to focus on the core gameplay element of keeping
still. The only caveat here is that, usually a race has a set number of laps, while
an MR scan can take between 15 to 30 minutes for the whole process. Though,
the scan process is broken up into sequences of 4-6 minutes with breaks in
between, the time used for these sequences still vary, meaning that the standard
x amount of laps seen in many racing games may not be enough.
==PAGE:35==
3.2 Game Design

For this reason, it was decided to make the race have a variable length, a dura-
tion speciﬁed before starting in other words, where the goal would be to cross
the ﬁnish line as many times as possible within the duration. Then, once the
duration would be up, driving over the ﬁnish line would ﬁnish the race, and
whichever car ﬁnishes ﬁrst and has the most laps is declared the winner. This
solves the duration problem, and provides the administrators of the game a tool
to make the game take as long as they wish.
In game, this concept can be
justiﬁed by telling the player they are using a special fuel tank, and that once
it runs out the goal is to get to the ﬁnish line using the reserve tank of the car.
Further, by using a variable sequence length, this can also serve as a way to
alter the diﬃculty of the game, with longer sequences being harder than shorter
ones.
Of course, while the racing mechanics are one half of the game, the other half
is having the player keep their head still. This means that in the game, if the
player moves excessively, they will be punished and lose points. On the ﬂip side
however, the player should also be rewarded for keeping still, and informed they
are doing well to encourage them, rather than simply punish them if they fail.
For this reason, the game should award bonus points to the player if they manage
to not move excessively for a certain amount of time. This helps encourage them
to keep going out of want to succeed, rather than just fear of failure. As with
the fuel tank for the duration, in gameplay concept this concept of keeping
still could be explained as the fuel tank the player is using being unstable, and
needing for the driver to be still.
3.2.1.1
Game Balance and Diﬃculty
The game will be played by both young and old children, as well as children
who will have played video games and those who don’t. As such, there ought
to be a way to make the game more exciting for those who are already familiar
with games or are a little older. This is to ensure they do not grow bored of the
game even after repeat playthroughs. Therefore, some sort of diﬃculty scaling
could be what can spice the game up for these experienced audiences.

There are many ways to design diﬃculty, for example by making diﬀerent stages
with diﬀerent diﬃculty levels, as well as make the same stage more diﬃcult by
increasing the amount of challenges the player has to overcome. Though at this
stage of the design, the player only has the opponents of the race and keeping
their head still as their challenges. As such, to take some inspiration from real
life, one way to increase or decrease the diﬃculty for the player would be to
increase or lower the speed of the vehicle the player is using. A car that moves
fast is harder to control than one that moves slow, thus, the players that feel
comfortable with challenging themselves a little can increase the driving speed
of the car.
==PAGE:37==
3.2 Game Design
23
This way of using speed as diﬃculty is not a novel one in racing games, with
games such as Mario Kart[31] and F-Zero[32] being prime examples of games
that use vehicle speed as a diﬃculty selection. However, a situation could arise
where a player could pick a diﬃculty they cannot handle, which will see the
player grow frustrated with the game. However, by using speed as a diﬃculty,
an adaptive diﬃculty[33] can be added quite seamlessly: If the player makes
a signiﬁcant mistake, simply lower the speed a bit.
In fact, Kid Icarus Up-
rising does something similar with its diﬃculty level, or intensity level as it is
called.[34] In Uprising, before starting a level, the player picks an intensity level,
which ranges from 0.0 to 9.0. When the player dies in Uprising, the game will
automatically lower the diﬃculty by 1.0 intensity levels, as a way to ensure they
can beat the level. This also ensures that the player will eventually be playing
the game on a diﬃculty level they feel comfortable with. Also with higher in-
tensities, the player gets better rewards, meaning there is an incentive to play
on higher diﬃculties in Uprising via risk reward system.
Similarly to Uprising, if the player makes a signiﬁcant mistake, the game will
adapt the diﬃculty to the user and lower the speed a bit. This ensures they
can reach a level they are comfortable with, even if they pick wrong, and then
improve from there by practicing and training. Furthermore, to borrow even
more from Uprising, should the player choose to play at a lower diﬃculty level,
they will receive less points than on higher diﬃculties. This is to balance the
diﬃculties out a bit and to give the player an incentive to improve at the game.
This creates a risk vs reward system, where the higher speeds are more diﬃcult,
yet the player can obtain more points by playing on them. It should be noted
that to avoid motion sickness, the maximum speed the game allows the players to
pick shouldn’t be too high. Furthermore, since the target audience is children,
even the highest diﬃculty should not be overly challenging, as this could be
frustrating to them.
Finally, with this system, the duration of the round can work in tandem with
the speed of the vehicle, as it is more diﬃcult to drive a great amount of laps
with a low duration. This is due to the player not having the time to traverse
as many laps as they would with a higher duration. However by increasing the
speed, or diﬃculty, a player will be able to achieve high lap counts even with a
lower race duration.

3.2.2
Controls
Since the games primary focus is on the player keeping their head still, the
game must not stimulate the players senses too much, as to not break their
concentration. These stimuli that could break focus, include the sense of touch
among them, in other words, having the player focus on a complex control
scheme, while keeping their head motionless. This means avoiding asking the
player to focus on too many things at once, in the form of both the complex
controls and keeping the head motionless. As such, it’s best to have the controls
for the vehicle be as simple as possible.
==PAGE:39==
3.2 Game Design
25
To make the game simple to control, the car will drive forward by itself, rather
than have the player press forward on the controller. This means there is one
less thing for the player to focus on, as the player now only has to concentrate on
moving the car left and right and keeping motionless. Reversing, however should
be avoided, as it has been shown that moving backwards from the position the
player is facing could cause motion sickness[29]. Furthermore, not having the
player be able to turn the car would simplify things even further. This means the
car will simply follow the stage path, and with automatic turning in an on-rails
esque system, turn only when needed in a stage. With this system, the vehicle
will only move from side to side of the path when left and right is pressed, while
the car will still face forward and drive in the same direction regardless of player
input. The F-Zero[32] racing games control in a way similar to this, where if
the player moves left or right, the vehicle doesn’t turn, but rather simple moves
to the left or right while it keeps facing the same direction. In order to turn
in F-Zero, one must hold in the left or right shoulder button while moving to
the left or right, triggering a sort of drift turn. For testing alternative designs
however, the early builds will have the option for the player to turn manual
turning on.
In terms of button layout for the controllers needed, the demands are not too
high. The car drives forward by itself and, to keep the game simple, the player
is only able to move it left and right. As such the controller needed will only
need 3 main buttons: Left, Right and Conﬁrm, the last of which will only be
used in menus.
3.2.3
Challenges
However navigating circuits with nothing in them would quickly result in the
game simply being reduced to its core mechanics, i.e. keeping still to go fast and
beat the opponents. As such, in order to give the player something to navigate
around, hazards and challenges for the player to avoid were included in the
design. This injects some additional adversity beyond the other racers into the
game to not make it too boring to play. These challenges were the obstacles
and terrain hazards.

3.2.3.1
Obstacles
The obstacle hazards would be objects the player must to navigate around to
avoid. It should be noted however, that their intended function would not be
to distract the player from keeping still.
This is because that would defeat
the purpose of the game being a tool for MR scan preparation, by having the
game eﬀectively attempt to distract the player deliberately. The game should
be designed to aid the player in keeping the head still, not work against it. As
such, these obstacles will rather serve to slow the player down temporarily, as
well as subtract from the score. This ensures that the challenge the player will
face from these will be purely in the race and not in remaining motionless.
3.2.3.2
Terrain Hazards
The terrain hazards, much like the obstacle hazards, would be hazards the
player ought to navigate around. The diﬀerence is, as these are part of the
environment, they will not disappear if the player collides with them and will
still remain. Like the obstacle hazards, they are not meant to distract the player
from keeping the head still, rather they are simply there to slow the player down
in the race against the AI.
3.2.4
Points of Interest & Goals
However, as with the motion detection, we do not want the player to only focus
on avoiding failure, but also on being able to get rewards for playing well and
knowledge of the game. For this reason, scattering items and secrets around the
levels that aﬀect the player positively would likely increase player engagement.
Furthermore, items that aﬀect the game positively and hidden secrets encourage
the player to replay the levels in order to collect all the items and learn more
about the levels. Additionally, secrets found in levels can be shared with their
peers, and could help foster a community around the game.
3.2.4.1
Items
The ﬁrst of these items would be items that when picked up give the player
extra points, similar to coins in Super Mario Bros. and also the more recent
Mario Kart games[31]. These would be scattered around the level as common
pick ups, that the player is encouraged to go after while avoiding the hazards.
==PAGE:41==
3.2 Game Design
27
On the topic of the hazards, in order to help the player, items that aid the player
in dealing with these, such as brief invincibility, would help the player traverse
the levels. Likewise, upgrades for the vehicle that helps traverse environmental
hazards would no doubt also help the player navigate the level with more ease.
For this reason, two additional items were considered: An item that gives the
player a free hit against an obstacle, and an item that negates terrain eﬀects on
the vehicle. The item would give the player complete invincibility until collision
with an obstacle, rather than temporary invincibility for a set amount of time.
This approach was decided due to it creating a sort of "lives" system, where each
free hit can be stacked. This by extension means, that newer players wanting
to attempt more diﬃcult challenges, could do so, but start the game with a
"handicap" of a few extra lives.
Finally, regarding tools that assist the player, should the player lose speed from
movement, there ought to be a way for them to recover, and not be sentenced
to last place just because of a mistake. Hence, should the player lose speed due
to movement, items that will see the player regain a bit of speed will spawn
around the level. It is important to note that these should only spawn once the
players speed has already been lowered. This is because otherwise this could
result in the player picking them up and experiencing a higher diﬃculty/speed
than what they started with, which could provoke player failure if not handled
with care.
Note that with all these items to help the player get through the game, none are
designed to aid them in keeping motionless. This is because, while these tools
exist to help the player win the race, the same should not exist for the motion
detection. This is because that would defy the games purpose as a training tool,
and provide the user with a get out jail free card to simply move.
3.2.4.2
Secrets
An additional item that would serve moreso to add interest to the levels rather
than aid the player outright, would be items that reveal secrets once picked up.
This could be a key that opens a door to a secret area, or a hidden treasure chest
that the player wont know is there until they drive over where it is buried. For
this reason, locked doors, where the passages behind them can only be accessed
after a key has been obtained could go a way to make the levels more memorable.
This makes it so the user now has to explore them more to see everything the
levels have to oﬀer, possibly ﬁnding hidden shortcuts and items by doing so. It
would be similar to how Mario Karts stages often have secret routes the racers
can take that are faster than the normal path[31].

3.2.5
UI design
Though in order to communicate information, such as whether the user has
moved excessively or has done well in regards to keeping still, a User Interface
(UI) will be necessary. Preferably, this UI should also help the player focus on
keeping their gaze in the current ﬁeld of regard, and not stray too far from it.
In other words, it should encourage the player keep focusing on looking where
they are looking, rather than look all around them.
A study was conducted by researchers from Penn State University, where users
were tasked with playing a First Person Shooter game and an action adventure
game while the researchers tracked the users eye movements. It was found that
users were more likely to look to the center of the screen in the shooter than the
action adventure, likely due to the crosshair used for aiming.[35]. Furthermore,
it has been shown that a pointer of where the user is looking in VR can help
reduce motion sickness, by keeping track of where they are looking.[36]
For this reason, a pointer of where the user is looking would likely both give the
user a reference of where their gaze is currently, as well as aid in them not feeling
disoriented. This pointer will be referred to as a gaze pointer, and will serve
to help the user keep track of where they are looking. Furthermore, should the
user move excessively, the gaze pointer can change to show that the has made
a mistake.
Though, the gaze pointer alone will be a rather minimalist UI, and not tell
the player how many laps have they done or how many points they have. As
such, a Heads Up Display (HUD) to convey this information is necessary. The
information shown by the HUD ought to not be overwhelming in quantity, but
also not be too little.
As such it should be limited to the most important
information, which would be the players score, their speed, the fuel left in the
tank, the amount of lives they have left, the amount of laps done and the placing
in the race. The speed and fuel are used to communicate the players current
velocity and how much time they have left before the race is over, both of which
are essential information. Likewise the placing in the race and the amount of
laps done are equally important, as these communicate how well the player is
doing, as does their score. Finally, the amount of lives left communicates how
many times the player can drive into an obstacle without repercussions, which
will help them decide whether to be risky or cautious with their maneuvers.

Given the audience the game targets, it would most likely be best to limit the

3.2.7
Level Design
Lastly, there are the tracks, or levels, the player will be racing in. The levels
themselves ought to be designed in a way to ensure they can loop in a way
that allows the player to go through them in a lap. Additionally, they ought
to neither feel too big for the lowest vehicle speeds, but also not feel too small
for the highest vehicle speed. The ﬁrst could result in the player quickly feeling
bored traversing an endless road, and the latter that the player feels the stage
zips by too fast. Finally, and most importantly of all, the levels ought to reduce
visual stimuli to the ﬁeld of regard and not go beyond that, to not incentivize
excess head movements in the player.
Furthermore, the easiest of the stages ought to simply be a straight road, where
once the player reaches the end, they are brought back to the start of the stage,
which would count as a lap. Though some of the more advanced stages could
also feature turning. In regards to turning, though it could result in increased
stimuli to the player. Though perhaps if it is done slowly and widely enough,
it might still be viable to have turns in a stage, if they are limited to the more
advanced stages. This would greatly expand the stages beyond being a straight
road, and by extension avoid becoming too repetitive. It is for this reason that,
while having stages turn may not be ideal for the core gameplay, it should be
worth testing if it can be done in a way that does not interfere with the core
gameplay.
In terms of diﬃculty, the stages would become progressively harder, the more
the user plays the game, with the ﬁrst of them being the easiest and the latter
ones being more diﬃcult. For this reason the stages ought to be divided into
categories, preferably based on age and experience with video games. The ﬁrst
category should be for the youngest children and those inexperienced with video
games. The next should be for the young children with some gaming experience,
which could also overlap with the older children with little gaming experience.

The ﬁnal one ought to be for the older children with gaming experience, and
these levels would mostly be so that the older children can challenge themselves
a bit, and not grow bored of the game.
Additionally, the environments of the level ought to not draw too much of the
players attention, as they could provoke excessive movement. In other words,
for the environment, it would be best to examine what typically stimulates
the users visual attention, and what grabs their attention in the environment.
Then, do the opposite, or keep it in the ﬁeld of regard[35]. Furthermore, the
levels themselves ought not to be designed in a way that causes motion sickness.
It has been shown that rapid acceleration directly upwards or downwards can
cause motion sickness, as well as that darker colors tend to help alleviate motion
sickness[29]. For this reason, should some of the stages feature diﬀerent levels
of elevation, they cannot be too great, as that could cause motion sickness.
Furthermore, the game ought to utilize a darker color palette to once again
alleviate this issue as much as possible. Finally, having a visualizer of where
the player will be going gives them an idea of what to expect, and helps reduce
motion sickness. In this case, the road will serve as that visualizer, and as such
oﬀroading will not be possible.
Finally, given the audience is young children, they would most likely appreciate if
the world had a more cartoonish appearance than photo realistic. Most children
often spend a lot of time watching cartoons or reading comics, and as such a
cartoonish art style would help draw them into the game more.
3.2.8
Design summary
In summary of the proposed design, the games setting features a race between
the players vehicle and two other vehicles. The diﬃculty of the game will largely
be determined by what the player sets the vehicles maximum speed to, the faster,
the harder. The player then gets rewarded with points for keeping their head
still and punished with a loss of speed for moving their head, which will give the
AI players an advantage. Unlike with a traditional race however, the goal here
is not to beat the other two vehicles within a set amount of laps. Rather, the
player must come ﬁrst while also trying to complete as many laps as possible,
with each lap completed giving the player extra points.

3.2.9
Alternative Design Ideas
Though this design was what was ultimately decided as the one to be imple-
mented, there are many other ways a game could be made out of keeping still to
ensure a car wins a race. For example, the punishment delivered in relation to
the speed of the vehicle could work in reverse, where the car gets faster, and thus
harder to control due to failure. This means the player will have less control of
the car for a short while if they made a mistake, due to the car moving faster
than they are used to. However, this was decided against due to it most likely
provoking more errors once one has already been committed, due to having less
control of the situation. This would likely either frustrate the player or result in
the player failing on purpose because they ﬁnd it fun to drive fast, despite being
more prone to crashing. Furthermore by making the car slower as a result of
failing, you can also introduce the racing mechanic, in the sense that the other
racers gain an advantage over you when you fail. In other words this mechanic
was not chosen because it would make the game less fun, and also failing would
likely induce motion sickness due to the high speeds.
Another approach that was decided against, was a much more cinematic one.
In this version, the game would be viewed from a spectators point of view, with
the camera following the car as if it was on a drone, and everything would be
tied to how much movement the user would make.
In other words, the car
would drive completely on its own without input from the user, but how well
it would drive, i.e. whether it would crash in a turn or do it properly, would
be dependent on the players lack of movement. As such, the draw in this game
would be what happens in the game, rather than the gameplay itself. In other
words, the user would play for the visual spectacle and not for the gameplay. In
a sense it would be similar to themed pachinko slot machines, such as the Metal
Gear Solid 3 Pachislot game[38]. Pachinko is usually a rather boring kind of
slot machine game, where a ball has to fall down in a certain way for the player
to win. However in this variation of the game, where the ball falls determines
what happens to the character in the game. As such the reward for playing the
game becomes seeing the cutscenes and not experiencing the gameplay. Instead
of tying the visual spectacle to a falling ball though, it would be tied to the users
movements. E.g. if the user moved it could result in the vehicle crashing, while
if the user made minimal movements, the car would ﬁnish the race without a
hitch.

manager similarly has a script called gamemanager, which keeps track of when
the race starts, is over, the players head movements and saving and loading.

4.2.1
Player
Once the level scene loads, the user is treated to either a third or ﬁrst person

4.2.2
Motion Detection
While the racing mechanics are important to the game, the most important
aspect of it would have to be the motion detection. It should be noted that
while the game detects the players movements, it only gives feedback to its user
when they have moved excessively.
This is to not overwhelm the user with
too much information regarding how they are moving, which could provoke
them to focus their attention on too many things at once, hampering their
performance. Furthermore, as the game is meant as a way to train the user to
lie still, recording the amount of excessive movements detected by this can be
used to gauge whether the patient is ready for a scan or not in a simple manner.
At ﬁrst it was considered to utilize the accelerometer in the Oculus GO for
detecting movements, as it would be able to detect speed ups and slowdowns.
However, the amount of motion permitted before artefacts begin to occur is very
little, so little that it is measured in mili-meters. It was found after a few tests
with the accelerometer, that it simply could not detect motion this accurately.
==PAGE:56==
42
Implementation of the Design
Instead, motion detection was implemented by creating a UI slightly in front of
the player, where a blue box would serve as the ﬁeld, referred to as a motion
ﬁeld for how far the player could move, while the gaze pointer would serve as
the tracker. In short, excessive motion is detected when the player maneuvers
the gaze pointer outside the ﬁeld. Likewise, as long as the player keeps the
gaze pointer within the boundaries of the motion ﬁeld, deﬁned by how much
movement is allowed in mm, no excessive motions are detected by the game.

This approach has several advantages over the one with the accelerometer, aside
from it being more accurate. First oﬀ, simply by the nature of how it works,
it visualizes the players ﬁeld of regard, and gives the player a target for the
gaze pointer. This makes it so that the gaze pointer not only exists to keep the
player from feeling disoriented and encourage them to keep their focus on it. It
also serves as a reticle used for motion detection. Compared to the approach
with the accelerometer, the gaze pointer in this approach becomes a part of the
motion detection, rather than just something to keep the players attention on.
Above the ﬁeld of regard is a bar that slowly ﬁlls up. This is the bonus bar,
which ﬁlls up for as long as the player keeps still, and gives the player some
bonus points after an interval of time that can vary between 30 seconds to a
minute.
Once motion is detected or bonus points are obtained, feedback is given to the
user.
Both of these are communicated to the player visually in the ﬁeld of

Calibration
Though in order for this motion ﬁeld to be accurate in size, it has to be measured
and calibrated properly in game to reﬂect the movements made in the real
world. To begin with, the precise amount the user may move must be deﬁned.
According to the researchers at the MoCo project, the brain can move about
2 mm before motion artefacts begin to obscure the image, and as such that is
what will constitute as the base. Though, it is important to note, that this is
the brains movement, as in the inner brain, meaning position of the nose or
forehead for example, can shift even further than this without issue. This can
be illustrated in the graph below.
As can be seen in the graph, if the head is treated as a circular coordinate system,
a shift of 2 mm would mean the forehead would have to shift around 65 mm
for it to create signiﬁcant motion artefacts. With this in mind, measurements
to calibrate the size of the ﬁeld could be conducted.
Kathrine from MoCo
mentioned that this measurement tends to be stricter for children, around 60
mm, instead. This was the measurement they used in this paper[46], which was
co-authored by Kathrine.
Given that information on the Oculus GO’s gyroscope and its workings are
scarce, measurements were done by hand instead.
While these may be too
inaccurate for clinical use, it should suﬃce for a prototype to illustrate a proof
of concept.
The measurements were made with the forehead as a base, at
ﬁrst to establish what a 2 mm movement of the brain would translate to in
game measurement terms. For this process an experimental build of the game,

4.2.3
Game manager
Managing the rules and set up of the game is the game manager object. This
object keeps track of how much time is left of the race duration via a timed
function, as well as executes the punishments and rewards to the player for
motion detection, or lack thereof.
When race starts, the game manager receives the speciﬁed settings in the set-
tingslist, that were carried over from the main menu scene. Then, the game
manager ﬁnds the AI objects and adds the player speciﬁc settings, such as
speed and vehicle selected, to the objects. The game manager also makes sure
the player starts with the gaze pointer in the motion detection ﬁeld, and doesn’t
allow the car to start driving until it is within the the detection ﬁeld.
==PAGE:59==
4.2 Game Implementation
45
Furthermore, the game manager keeps track of what needs to happen when
motion is detected, as there are three settings for what can occur when motion
is detected. The ﬁrst is nothing, also known as practice mode, which allows
players to get a feel for the motion detection mechanic. The second is extension,
which sees the duration being extended slightly when motion is detected. This
is to work as a "second chance", allowing players to, while suﬀering from a speed
loss, receive more time to explore and collect points in the stage. This is also the
default mode, and utilizes the dynamic diﬃculty for the speed outlined in the
design section. The ﬁnal setting is end scan, where once movement is detected,
the game ends prematurely with a game over, to signify the player failed. This
mode is for the players who feel conﬁdent that they can play through the game
without any registered movements.
Once the duration of the race is up, the game manager locates the goal and sets
the goal to keep track of when the player reaches it, so that the race ends once
the player drives through the goal. Once the player drives through, the game
manager loads the games save ﬁle, checks if the players total score is among the
top 10 highest scores, and if it is, writes it to the save ﬁle.
4.2.4
AI
In every stage the player will race against two other vehicles controlled by the
computer. Both of these move a bit slower than the player which is intentional,
as this means the player is more likely to ﬁnish in ﬁrst place if they do not move
their head. Furthermore, this results in, if the player moves their head, they will
lose speed, which could see ﬁrst place going to one of the AI controlled vehicles
instead.
The AI units themselves move along a series of waypoints scattered around in
each stage. Since each stage is linear, in that the path to the goal is always
forward, the waypoints are organized as a list in the AI script. This makes it
so that once an AI reaches a waypoint, it begins moving towards the next one
in the list immediately, and once the end of the list is reached, it starts over.
This ﬁxed path approach was deemed ﬁtting in the implementation to increase
performance, as there is no need for complex AI path ﬁnding if the levels are
very linear in their design.

4.2.4.2
Random speed-up
As the opposing vehicles move slightly slower than the players vehicle, it can be
hard to get the sense that the game is really a race if the player does well with
keeping their head still. As such, a sort of handicap mechanic was introduced
for the AI players. This resulted in a system where, depending on a random
number, the AI’s would speed up to be faster than the player every so often. In
other words, giving the AI an advantage should they lag too far behind, in order
to keep the player on their toes. This concept is commonly known as rubber
banding[47], and is a subset of dynamic diﬃculty[48].
Mario Kart does something similar by granting racers that lag behind too much
a few random speed boosts every now and then, to ensure that one player doesn’t
completely dominate the race if they’re only a bit better than the AI. In short,
this mechanic was implemented to make the AI players feel more like racing
opponents than check boxes you race past.

Note, that this mechanic does not trigger if the player is behind an AI vehicle,
so as to not create situations where the player will be too far behind.
4.2.5
Hazards, items and points of interest
In regards to hazards, these are made to be square wooden walls that are erected
around the stage to obstruct the player. Once the player collides with them they
lose 5km/h of speed and points equal to (100/speed)*duration. They disappear
upon contact with the player so that the player doesn’t get stuck.
The other hazard is the environmental hazard, which in the implementation is
water that slows the player down by 50% upon entering it. Once the player
exits the water, they returns to normal speed. As such, avoiding pathways with
water becomes favorable to the player, since they don’t want to be slowed down
and lose the race.
The items that populate the levels are very similar in their properties, as they all
have colliders and a script attached to them that enacts their eﬀects. Further,
most of them work the same way, i.e. once the player collides with them, they
disappear and their eﬀects are enacted upon the player. The ﬁrst items to cover
are the coins, which are colored yellow and increase the players score when the
player collides with them. The extra lives item utilizes an asset similar to the
coin, only these coins are red, to signify they have a diﬀerent eﬀect. Though to
ensure the player doesn’t become too tanky, they have a cap of 10 extra lives.
As such, should a player have more than 10 lives and pick one up, they instead
give double the points of a normal coin.
The other two items in the game are more like upgrades to the vehicle than
common items. These are the speed boost and water raft. The speed boost
appears as a large green board, and will increase the players speed by 3. They
will only spawn if the player has already lost speed due to movements or crashing
into walls however.
This is to keep the player from reaching a speed they
didn’t specify in the menu and accidentally making the game too diﬃcult for
themselves. The water raft is quite simple, in that it simply allows the player to
traverse water without worrying about the speed loss from doing so once picked
up. Its eﬀect doesn’t expire until the stage is over, so once it has been picked
up, water is no longer an issue. This is to encourage players to explore parts of
the levels that were previously submerged.

Next, are the doors and switches. Each door, which is displayed as a large blue
box, has an associated switch, which is shown as a ﬂag that can be picked up.
These work by blocking oﬀcertain paths and shortcuts for the player, giving the
player incentive to explore the stages more to look for the switches that unlock
the secret paths a stage may contain. The switches have a hitbox as their only
property and the doors have a very bouncy collider attached to them, as well as

tion, the race ends as soon as the player reaches the goal, after which the player
is shown their score.
4.2.6
Scoring
Scoring in the game is based on several things, most importantly how many
movements have been done during the game and the placing the player ﬁnished
at, but also how many points the player got from picking up items in the stage.
The item scoring is balanced by taking the speed of the vehicle into account,
granting the player more points if they’re playing on a higher diﬃculty, via the
formula:
Points = (3 · moveSpeed)/2
(4.1)
This is to ensure players are rewarded for challenging themselves with higher dif-
ﬁculties, by multiplying the amount of base points with their speed. Originally
it was considered factoring in duration of the race as well, since this formula
would make it easy for a player with a higher speciﬁed race duration to outper-
form a lower duration player. However, given that a longer duration means the
player has to keep still longer, and that this is the core gameplay, it was decided
that this bit of favoritism for high duration players was acceptable.
==PAGE:63==
4.2 Game Implementation
49
Once the race is over, the player is given bonus points based on how well they
did in certain aspects. The placing of the player in the race is the ﬁrst of these,
and aﬀects the scoring by giving the player a bonus based on where they placed,
with the bonus being the highest in ﬁrst place. The speed of the vehicle upon
crossing the ﬁnish line aﬀects the score as well, and once again rewards the player
for playing with a higher speed by giving them more bonus points. Finally, the
amount of excess moves subtract points from a large bonus of 10000 points, by
halving the amount of points the player gets from this bonus for every movement
they made. When the bonus points and the point the player accumulates in the
stage are added together, they get their ﬁnal score for the stage.
4.2.7
Highscores, Saving and Loading
Once the game ends, the users score is saved if it makes the highscore list, which
contains the 10 best scores achieved on that stage. This is not only to add a
competitive aspect to the game amongst the user’s peers, but also to make the
user want to get better at the game, and giving them a way to quantify whether
they’ve done so. In other words, an incentive to keep playing and getting better
is added. Furthermore, highscores have been shown to have a positive eﬀect
when gamifying concepts.[50]
Saving is done by, when the player does well enough to make it on the highscore
list, taking their score and saving it and other persistent data in a json ﬁle.
Then, once the appropriate menu is selected, such as the stage select menu, the
json ﬁle is loaded, and the appropriate data, is fetched and acted upon. The
save ﬁle itself is a basic class called save and only contains a few public arrays,
which contain information such as highscores and unlockables for each stage.
Originally, it was thought that having the user enter a user name to log their
scores in each level would encourage some friendly competition amongst the
children.
Furthermore, user accounts have shown to improve the experience
when gamifying concepts[50]. However, due to data protection laws regarding
what may be stored and for how long, this had to be changed to the game simply
keeping track of the scores and nothing else.

4.2.8
Levels
There are a total of 5 levels in the game, with three of them being a straight
road for the player to drive on and the two others also featuring turns for the
player to navigate through. They are designed so that the ﬁrst level of their
design type is the easy level and the second one is more challenging, as well as
to test various concepts, such as turning. For example the ﬁrst of the straight
road levels is easier than the other, and another level is used to test the turning
mechanics. The idea would be that the easier levels can be played by the younger
audiences, while the older audiences can play the more challenging levels once
they are unlocked.
The ﬁrst of the straight road levels is called a trip to the wild west, and is
a desert setting with a single road running through the stage that the player
drives on. Once the player gets the the end of the road, there is a small cavern
that teleports them back to the start of the level. This ensures that the level
loops without featuring any twists or turns that might complicate the stage.
There is a secret shortcut in this level however, in the cave the player ﬁrst
drives through, which cuts the stages length in half by teleporting the player to
where they would be when they reached the end of the stage. This path is only
accessible from the second lap onward, as it is required to open the blue gate
before this passage can be accessed, and the switch that does so is located in
the latter half of the stage.
The second of the straight road levels is a trip to the mountains, which has a
mountainous road as its setting, and is unlocked after beating the wild west. The
way this stage works is similar to the ﬁrst one, in that it loops by teleporting the
player back to the start once they reach the end. However, an added element of
this stage is that there are branching paths in the form of alternate elevations,
similar to an old 2D side scroller. This allows the player to explore alternate
paths without turning being a factor, and as all the paths, elevated or not, lead
to the same destination, the player won’t get lost either.
Similarly to the ﬁrst stage, there is a shortcut in this stage, this time it is hidden
beneath the river bed at the second area with multiple elevations. The water
masks the blue gate that hides the path, and must be removed to gain access to
it. The shortcut works similarly to how it did in the ﬁrst stage, in that it allows
the player to skip a portion of the stage entirely.

4.2.10
Tutorial
Given that the game has some mechanics that might require an introduction,
such as the doors, switches and the extra lives, an tutorial mode was added,
where these concept could be explained. This tutorial also has the added beneﬁt
of helping people who have never played games before get introduced to concepts
which are second nature to people who play games. This could for example be
how a controller works, and that coins usually give you points in games.
It was considered simply making a small movie about how to play the game,
similar to how Super Smash Bros. introduces its players to its gameplay with
a how to play video[52]. However, after testing this approach by having some
friends play Super Smash Bros with no prior knowledge, it was found they didn’t
do too well with in the game. Furthermore, simply showing a movie of how to
play the game doesn’t allow the player to get a feel for the game in the same way
an interactive tutorial does. As such, an approach where the player is gradually
introduced to the concepts, items and hazards in the game was chosen instead.
The tutorial is a level that the player plays through as if it were a normal stage,
only in this stage they are stopped every now and then to introduce aspects
of the game via a voice over.
When starting the tutorial, the basics of the
game, keeping the gaze pointer within the ﬁeld of regard and that the car drives
forwards by itself, are covered. At the end of each voice clip, the voice prompts
the player to press the trigger to start driving, so they can access the next
tutorial.
The ﬁrst tutorial upon pressing start concerns the coins, and explains to the
player that they get points for collecting them. The second tutorial covers the
the obstruction hazards, and explains that they subtract points and lower the
users speed for a while. Next the tutorial covers the speedboosts, water and
water raft upgrades, and after that the extra lives. The doors and switches are
demonstrated next, and ﬁnally the goal is reached. Upon reaching the goal, the
player is returned to the title screen.

The voice acting for the game is mainly used in the menu and at the start and
end of a level, so as to not distract the player during the gameplay. To not sound
too intimidating, a voice actor/actress with a soft voice was sought out. As this
experience, VR games and MR scannings, are likely a new experience for most
of the audience, it would help if the voice sounds accommodating and friendly.
Initially, the headhunted voice actress was to be a 6th semester DTU student,
but as she was too busy with her bachelor thesis, it was decided that my sister,
Maria Christensen, would ﬁll the voice actress role. A script was drafted up
beforehand, and the voice actress was asked to read the lines out loud, while
being shown a screenshot of the game to give her a better understanding of
what she was talking about. The lines were then recorded using a Oneplus 6

This testing phase can be split into two parts: software tests and user tests. The
software tests serve to test if the software works as outlined by the use cases
and design. The user tests are used to evaluate its viability as an interactive
piece of software, its viability to encourage the user to keep still and whether
the audience ﬁnds it enjoyable to use.
5.1
Software Tests
The software tests were conducted, not just with the intent of testing whether
mechanics of the game worked as they should, but also whether the game mea-
sured movements correctly. The tests were conducted by testing aspects of the
game one at a time, such as whether the car would drive forward, and whether
the goal would register a lap. To aid in this, a debug mode was added, where
diﬀerences from normal gameplay were present. Changes in this mode include
the countdown at the start of the race being removed, and the vehicle not being
propelled forward automatically, instead requiring the user to press forward on
the touchpad. Tests checking whether objects behaved as they should in general
were mainly conducted on PC in the Unity Engine, and uploaded to the VR
headset to test if they worked in VR as well.

5.1.1
Calibration
The accuracy of the calibration was tested by ﬁrst setting the sensitivity to the
measurement to be tested, going from 5 mm down to 1 mm. Then once the
setting had been set, the tester would be asked to drag the gaze pointer to the
edge of the gaze ﬁeld, while their movements would be measured with a ruler.
Each of the settings matched with their speciﬁed measurements, meaning that
e.g. setting the sensitivity to 2 mm corresponded with the motion ﬁeld being
set to the correct size. For 2 mm, this meant having the user move 30 mm in
any direction. 30 mm is half of 60 mm, which is the maximum movement range,
hence moving 30 mm in any direction would exceed this range. In short, the
calibrations for the motion ﬁeld were measured to be accurate, and that if the
player exited the motion ﬁeld they would’ve moved their brain more than the
game had been set to allow them to.
5.1.2
Use case tests
Given that the calibrations had been proven to be able to detect excess move-
ment as they should, next was testing whether the game fulﬁlled the use case
tests outlined in the beginning of the design section. The ﬁrst of these was
whether the game would wait until the player speciﬁed that they were ready
to start once the game began. Upon spawning in a level, the player car stood
still until the player placed the gaze pointer within the motion ﬁeld and pressed
start, meaning this use case had been fulﬁlled.
Next was motion detection where, once the race has started, should the user
move the gaze pointer outside the motion ﬁeld, the game registers it as excessive
movement as it should, and the red cross appears in the motion ﬁeld. Likewise,
the stars of the bonus score also appear once the player has kept the pointer
within the motion ﬁeld for a certain amount of time, which the bonus bar ﬁlls up
towards as it should. This means that both use case 2 and 3 have been fulﬁlled.
The pause functionality also worked as it should, with the game halting all
activity except the gaze pointer when the player pressed the button, meaning
the fourth use case had been fulﬁlled. Once the player crossed the ﬁnish line for
the ﬁnal time, the game would not register the player moving their head outside
the motion ﬁeld as excess movement. This meant that the ﬁfth use case had
been fulﬁlled.

6.1.2
Main Tests
Though initially the main tests were unfavorable to the project, with the par-
ticipants being confused by the game and its controls, after tweaking and sim-
plifying the controls, the results proved much more favorable. This can be seen
by comparing the results of the ﬁrst test, where the build of the game featured
manually controlled turning, to the build in the second and third tests where
this had been removed. Not only were all participants able to complete the
levels put forward, but also complete them with low movement counts and with
a high placing. For this reason, it can be concluded that the manual turning
mechanic, was too complex for the audience this game would be targeting, and
therefore it was scrapped.
This simpliﬁcation didn’t end with just the controls or gameplay, but also ex-
tended to the stages. As the participants in the ﬁrst stage had great diﬃculty
completing the ﬁrst level, which was meant to be the easiest, to the point where
the youngest participant got frustrated, it had to be simpliﬁed. This resulted in
the creation of new, simpler variants of the stages, the easiest of which required
no input from the controller to complete, and the second easiest featured no
obstacles. It could be said that it was a little extreme to remove all sense of
challenge in a stage such as EZ wild west, but as the participants were already
ﬁnding the core gameplay diﬃcult in the ﬁrst stage, drastic changes had to be
made. These changes seemed to have paid oﬀtoo, as illustrated in the 3rd and
4th tests, where the participants fared very well in this easy stage, both in terms
of head movements and in game. Given the massive improvement in results, this
would appear to have been the right move.
There was concern over the game becoming too boring due to reducing the
diﬃculty to the point where the user wouldn’t even have to touch the controller.
However, in the end, it would seem that these concerns were unfounded, as
most of the users in the 3rd and 4th test, which mainly consisted of the target
audience, found the game to be fun even in the stage with the greatly lowered
diﬃculty. In the same vein, this fear of the game becoming boring was what
motivated the turning mechanic. However, given the poor results those stages
produced in regards to head movements, and with many users commenting that
it felt clunky and jarring, it can be concluded that the turning mechanic does
not ﬁt this game’s aims. This is especially evident when they are compared
to the other stages that do not feature turns, and which produced far better
results, both in regards to head movements and in how well the players did in
the game. With enough practice, the user may eventually get good enough at
preparing mentally for them, but this won’t feel natural to them, and the player
should be able to use a game mechanic comfortably, not have to plan around
and prepare for it.
==PAGE:99==
6.1 Discussion and Interpretation
85
Despite its simplicity, most users said they enjoyed the gameplay, and the older
audiences liked the diﬃculty system being tied to speed. Though, while the
results do show that increased speed contributes to the users doing slightly
worse, it was mostly the stages diﬃculties that caused a drop in performance
from the users. This likely indicates that the diﬃculty of the stage in this game,
is a greater contributor to whether the user does well or not, than the speed of
the vehicle. Especially with the results for EZ wild west, where an increased
speed of the vehicle does little to lower the users overall performance in the
stage. In this case it is most likely due to the game having been reduced to its
core gameplay, with the other aspects of it having been removed.
In terms of user experience, the participants seemed to understand the game
and how it worked quite well, and it would seem that the gaze pointer helped
direct the users focus at a speciﬁc point. This was only helped further when the
tutorial was being read out loud to the users in the Wizard of Oz tests with the
voice acting script. This could be seen in the last tests, where in comparison to
the 3rd tests, there was little confusion about what the items in the game did.
This could however possibly be chalked up to the participants in the 3rd test
not having played many games, while those in the latter ones had.
Given that most of the users had very few movements registered, outside of the
ﬁrst few tests and the tests in the stages that featured turns, it can be said that
the game encourages the user to keep their gaze focused on the motion ﬁeld,
an thus to keep their heads still. This was also what many of the users felt in
regards to how the game was explained, that it made sense to keep the gaze
pointer within the motion ﬁeld.
In terms of reliability of the results, given that the calibration for all ﬁve settings
were tested to be accurately measured, it can be concluded that in the tests,
when movement had been registered in the participants, their brains had moved
more than 3 mm.
Overall, it would seem that to produce the best results in regards to the user
keeping their heads still, it would be best to pick among the early stages with a
low speed. This could, in the training program, be the ﬁnal test, while the rest
of the stages and speed settings could be reserved for practicing with, getting
used to, or simply having fun with the game.

6.2
Reﬂections on the process
Ultimately, the design of the game, making it a racing game, showed positive
results with the users, as they kept their heads still during gameplay, as they
were meant to. Furthermore, the participants in the tests all found the game to
be fun to play, despite the simplicity of its stages and challenges.
Additionally, should the player grow bored with just the base game, they still
have incentive to seek out the collectible stickers, which as shown in the fourth
test, were quite popular with the audience. As such, it was a good idea to create
a reward in the game beyond just playing the game itself, as that gave the users
extra incentive to play the game.
Since from the tests it seems that speed isn’t as big a factor in inﬂuencing the
diﬃculty of the game as the stages are, perhaps the stage selection should serve
as the main diﬃculty setting, and then speed could be used to ﬁne tune the
diﬃculty of the stage. In other words, the diﬃculty would come from the stages
themselves and navigating them, while the speed of the vehicle would serve as
a way to ﬁne tune the diﬃculty of a stage, by tailoring it to what the user ﬁnds
comfortable. With this, players who would want to make the stage harder could
increase the speed, while the ones who are going through it for the ﬁrst time
would be able to lower it.
6.3
What could have been done diﬀerently
In hindsight a number of things could have been done diﬀerently. Given the
results of the ﬁrst tests, it was a mistake to add manual turning, despite the
results from the pilot tests. This mistake was likely due to overestimating the
youngest audiences ability to understand and use the controller in the way that
build of the game demanded. Furthermore, given that the users in the pilot
test fared better in stages that did not feature turning, this should’ve been
prioritized over their feedback in regards to the stages.
In particular, had it been known auto turning would not be made to feel smooth
for the player, even with wide roads, smoother turn animations and warning
signs, this would not have been focused on. Furthermore, the stages that fea-
tured this element, despite being the more fun stages according to the users in
the pilot tests, would have been scrapped earlier than they were.
==PAGE:101==
6.4 As a training tool for MR scans
87
Concerning stages, if the results of the tests had been known beforehand, more
time could have been devoted to making more stages similar to the ﬁrst two,
i.e. stages with a straight road. In addition, time could then have been used
to explore how these types of stages could be made engaging with clever level
design. This could for example be by splitting the road up into lanes, similar to
a highway, where certain lanes would take the player into certain unique parts
of the stage. Shortcuts or doors similar to those already featured in the straight
road stages could be hidden alongside certain lanes this way, giving the player
a stage with plenty of paths to explore, adding replay ability, without having
to turn. Finally, given how the younger users struggled with the stages with
obstacles, had this been known in advance, the variants of the stages without
obstacles could have been made earlier, with only the hard variants of the stages
containing obstacles.
6.4
As a training tool for MR scans
As a tool to train children to keep still for MR scans, the results while playing
the game seem promising, with few movements registered by the game in later
tests. Furthermore, when the players replayed a stage, they tended to do better
the second time. Whether this is transferable to when the user is in the scanner
could not be tested, but the game does illustrate how the user should act while
in the scanner, which is an experience they can take with them into the scanner
once they have played the game
Unfortunately, the MoCo project’s mock scanner was not available for use dur-
ing this projects runtime. This, could have helped in testing the game under
conditions similar to those in real scan environments. In other words, ﬁrst play
the game and then enter the mock scanner, possibly with the participants trying
out the scanning with the VR headset on ﬁrst before trying the mock scanner
without it. The purpose of would have been to have them get used to being
in the scanner after having played the game, and therefore associated the game
with the scanner. Additionally, this would have allowed for obtaining a more
accurate result of whether the game works as a training tool for the scanner, as
the participants could be told to do what they just did in game, in the scanner.

Test Conclusions
Overall, while the test results with the target audience started out rather nega-
tive, they improved over time. This was due to every iteration taking previous
feedback into account, which often meant simplifying the game. Furthermore,
the tests showed that certain mechanics, such as turning, simply were not viable
for this type of game.
Importantly however, the tests showed that it is possible to create an engaging
game, where the primary focus is to keep still. The younger audiences enjoyed
playing it and, with the latter iterations, thought it was easy to control and
understand. The children tested in the latter iterations kept their heads still as
the game instructed. Therefore, as a research prototype, this game has proved
to be viable as a device to encourage the user to keep their head still.

Chapter 7
Future Work and
Improvements
This project has seen the design and implementation of a prototype of a video
game designed to engage the player, but at the same time have its primary
gameplay element be non action. The purpose of the non action gameplay, was
to encourage the user to keep still in preparation for MR scans, and the value
of the prototype was tested in regards to this. While the results of the tests
conducted were positive, after some iterations and adjustments, there is still
room for improvement.
First oﬀ, the game itself can be improved in a number of ways. One of the
biggest improvements, would be to include more levels, as after level 3 and 4
were scrapped, there are eﬀectively only two unique levels in the game. Granted,
those two levels have diﬀerent variants that serve as an easy, normal and hard
mode, but aesthetically and in terms of the overall design, they are the same
level. As such, more levels like the ﬁrst two, i.e. a straight road with no turns,
the type of levels that proved to show the best results, would improve the game.
==PAGE:104==
90
Future Work and Improvements
The additional levels could utilize the highway design mentioned at the end of
the analysis section for multiple paths, to make them more interesting to play
without having to deal with turns. Furthermore, while the stages with turns
ultimately proved to not work with the game, elements from them could likely
still be used. For example a waterfall hiding a secret path, as in the seaside
stage, could be utilized in another stage. Other interesting aspects in future
levels could be to have multiple goals posts located in alternate paths, allowing
the player to rack up laps faster, or have certain secrets locked behind certain
speeds, meaning the user wouldn’t be able to see them until they reach this
speed.
Another enhancement that could be made, would be to make the diﬀerent
playable vehicles more distinct beyond their appearance. This could be done
by, e.g. changing how they handle, or altering how fast they move by default.
However, the advantage of the current set-up is that all handle similarly, which
allows the user to pick any car they wish without putting themselves at a dis-
advantage. This may be an advantage given that the target group is young
children, but it would be worthwhile to test.
Furthermore, as the participant in the ﬁnal test mentioned, driving multiple
laps through the stages could see them grow stale.
Therefore, he suggested
implementing a system that spawned new coins and items for a new lap, would
help prevent the stages from getting stale after a few laps. This would increase
the stages longevity, and if these items and obstacles were spawned with set
templates, would make each lap conquered feel distinct to the player.
Regarding the sound design, while the vehicle’s engine will make a more idle
sounding engine noise if brought below 10 km/h due to braking, there is no
distinction in the engines noises above that. In real life an engine grows louder
the faster a vehicle drives, and something similar could be included in this game
in the future, with software such as fabric[65], which grants a developer much
greater control over the sound of the game than the basic Unity Engine.
Second, concerns the software issues mentioned in the software test section, such
as the outlined save ﬁle bug. While the core mechanics of the game works as
intended, these bugs would have to be ﬁxed in the future to make the game

B.1.6
AI
The AI players share many similar properties with the player, namely moveSpeed,
laps, started and ended, as well as multiple vehicles that comprise their main
bodies, which the game sets at the start of the game. Yet, they also contain an
array of positions known as target. These are the waypoints that specify where
they are supposed to move towards, which they do via the movetowards() func-
tion. Once they reach a waypoint, the target waypoint changes to the next one
in the array until it reaches the end, where it becomes the ﬁrst element once
more. The target waypoint is changed via a collision with the waypoint, which
covers the whole road. It increments the index of the waypoint array by 1, so
that the AI will seek out the next waypoint in the waypoint array. It works
similar for the player, in that once they collide with the waypoint, the next one
in the array becomes the waypoint from which the distance is taken into account
with regards to placing.
Furthermore, every now and then, if the AI players are not in the lead, they
will double their speed at random and have a 5% chance to do so. This is done
by rolling a random number between 0 and 100 every 10 seconds, and if it is
greater than 95, The AI vehicle will double their speed until the next roll.

Next are the technical details regarding the sound synthesis. As mentioned in

B.2.1
Engine
First and most importantly is the engine, which has two sound eﬀects: engine
idle and engine running. These two states represent whether the car is driving
or not, as engine idle is the sound that plays if the car is moving at less than 15
km/h, while engine running is the sound used for when it goes above that. The
PD ﬁles for this uses the car engine sound example found at aspress.co.uk[54].
Given the extreme amount of customization this PD ﬁle allows for for how the
sound comes out, it was decided to use this instead of synthesizing the sound
from the ground up. It works by reading the various attributes speciﬁed in the
sliders, such as speed, wavelength and cylinders, and feeds these to the rest of
the system.
==PAGE:123==
B.2 Technical details of the sound
109
The engine running sound eﬀect is recorded into a wav ﬁle when the speed at-
tribute of the ﬁle is set to high, while the engine idle is recorded when the speed
attribute is set to low. This allows for synthesis of the same engine, as all the
other modiﬁers remain unchanged, at diﬀerent velocities, essentially making it

B.2.3
Movement detection & bonus sound
The movement detection sound, was made to sound soft yet disapproving, and
was synthesized using one of aspress.co.uk’s alarm sounds as a base. This ﬁle
works by feeding 5 diﬀerent values, speciﬁed by sliders, into four identical blocs
that each contain an oscillator and a cosine function. The blocs have two in-
puts, and one of the sliders is connected to all of them, while the others are only
connected to one, making the one that is connected to them all eﬀectively the
"master" slider. The signals from the blocs are then passed on to four diﬀerent
apliﬁers, which are also connected to a timebase bloc, that governs the pace
of the sound eﬀect, through a 4 phase bloc that splits the timebase signal into
4. For the excess movement sound eﬀect, the inputs to the timebase bloc have
been altered from their original values to 500 and 3 as the two inputs. The PD
ﬁle can be seen below.

higher pitch. This is due to the player most likely already associating the coin
sound eﬀect with getting points at the point where they get their ﬁrst bonus,
and as such a similar sounding sound will make them make the association of
this sound with something good, namely getting points. The pitch for this eﬀect
was not altered in PD, but rather in Adobe Audition, however.

B.2.6
Menu sounds
The menu has two diﬀerent sounds for when pressing a button. One is for mi-
nor buttons such as adjusting settings, while the other is more pronounced, and
is indicative that something has been initiated. This sound plays for example
when you select a stage or press start in the main menu. It is similar to how
other games have a distinct press start/conﬁrm sound and a menu select sound,
to indicate the importance of the former action.
The regular menu sound was made to not sound too intrusive, and simply give
the player an audio indication that they had pressed a button. It was meant
to have a futuristic computer like sound, given the appearance of the menu.
In comparison, the conﬁrm sound was made to be more pronounced and light-
hearted, to indicate that something cool happened.
Both of these sounds share the movement detection PD ﬁle as their base, just
with the sliders adjusted to change the sound. The menu sound having the left-
most slider, the one that governs the pitch of the signal, slid all the way to zero,
which subdues the audio signiﬁcantly, while the conﬁrm button has the leftmost
slider slid to its maximum value, which makes it much more pronounced.
B.2.7
Machine sounds & voice ﬁles
Finally, in regards to the sound, there are the MR machine sounds and voice
ﬁles. These were not synthesized, and were instead recorded. In the case of the
machine sounds, these were recorded by NRU, and were provided during the
course of the project.
Similarly, the voice ﬁles were not synthesized either. While it would’ve been
possible to use a text to speech program to generate the voice ﬁles, it was de-
cided it would feel more organic to the user to have a proper human speak the
lines and record them. This would also allow for greater control of the tone
and sound of the voice, via voice direction, than many of these programs can
provide.

C.1.1
Reﬂections & changes from the pilot test
One of the greatest takeaways from this test, was that the participants thought
the speed of the higher diﬃculties were too hard to control, and too punish-
ing. From the results, the two participants began having trouble when setting
the speed to 90 km/h and above. As such, the maximum speed was lowered
from 130 km/h to 80 km/h. 80 was chosen over 90 to not make it hard for
the players to control on the highest diﬃculties, since the challenge of the game
should come from the natural parts of the game and not a ﬁght with the controls.
Furthermore, due to the players poor performance and feedback regarding hav-
ing obstacle collisions permanently lower speed, this aspect was removed entirely.
Obstacles were made to only lower the speed of the vehicle temporarily upon
collision. It was considered to have the coins in the stages mitigate the speed-
loss upon being picked up. However with a temporary speedloss based on time
rather than pickups, the player would recover from minor failures much faster
even if no coins were around, and as such a combination of the two approaches
was pursued. Coins would recover the players speed lost from object collisions,
allowing for rapid recovery from failure if some would be nearby, but the players
speed would also recover naturally even with no coins nearby.
Regarding turning, while neither participant excelled at stages where the car
would need to turn, both participants thought that the stages that featured
them were more fun to play than those without. As such, it was decided to
attempt to keep working on these stages to make turning feel more natural, and
not too provoking. As the participants had performed best with manual turn-
ing, it was decided to focus on attempting to improve manual turning rather
than auto-turning for the next test.

Appendix F
Plan for the project
F.1
Scope
This project will be performed with Michael Rose as the supervisor, as well as
Melanie Ganz and Kathrine Madsen from the University of Copenhagen and the
Neurobiology Research Unit (NRU) at rigshospitalet as co-supervisors. It will
see the implementation of a VR game designed to prepare young children, aged
4 to 10, for MR brain scannings, by having the main focus of the game being
to lie as still as possible. If possible, a version of the game to be played in the
scanner itself will also be developed. Due to the string magnetic ﬁelds in the
scanner room, it is not possible to use regular electronics during the scan. As
such, this version would have to run on diﬀerent hardware than a VR headset,
and will be referred to as a ﬂatscreen version.
==PAGE:154==
140
Plan for the project
F.1.1
Structure and Tests
The plan for the project was divided up into four main phases: research, design
of the prototype, implementation and testing, where each of these have associ-
ated milestones, which can be seen below. Each of these phases will be executed
in an agile fashion, to ensure that improvements can be made as the project
goes on. Note, that these milestones’ dates are for when their ﬁrst instance has
been accomplished, e.g the ﬁrst prototype, not the ﬁnal one. This is so that the
results can be evaluated and iterated upon if needed.
Figure F.1: The projects milestones
The tests of the game would be tested ﬁrst on healthy adults, and then, cour-
tesy of NRU, on young, healthy children at a later date. They would primarily
be focused on whether playing the game can make them keep more still and
whether they will be more still while the scanning takes place. In other words,
whether the game can potentially improve the results of these scannings.
The writing of the report will begin with the acquisition of the ﬁrst pilot test
data, scheduled to be obtained in April, and from then on be worked on while
the project is tested further.
The project was planned this way, to ﬁrst acquire the knowledge necessary
for what provokes movements in a more immersive environment and what tends
to make the user relax in this environment. Then, from this information, create
a design around not provoking user movement and helping the user relax, which
could then be implemented and then tested in the pilot tests. If the pilot tests
proved unfavorable, another design could be made, and alongside it another
implementation.

F.1.2
The plan in practice
The plan was followed closely up until mid March, which saw the shut down of
DTU, and most of Danish infrastructure due to Covid-19. As such, the planned
ﬂatscreen version of the game, which would be played while the patients are
in the actual scanner, could not begin development, because access to the MR
scanner to be used was not available. Focus was as such directed entirely to
the VR version of the game, to be used outside the scanner. Furthermore, the
testing phase of the project became much more limited as a result, with the
ﬁrst pilot tests being conducted at the end of April as planned. Though, more
weight had to placed on data from adults, since younger test subjects were in
short supply due to the pandemic. This also resulted in the ﬁrst tests with
children not taking place until mid May, rather than late April as in the plan.
