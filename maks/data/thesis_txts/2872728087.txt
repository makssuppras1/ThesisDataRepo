Abstract
This project set out to leverage the abundant resource of crystal contacts in the Protein Data Bank,
which are hypothesized to closely match non-specific interaction patterns. These crystal contacts serve as
a vast labeled dataset that was used together with large protein language models to capture interaction
patterns. Embeddings generated using ESM-IF, ESM2, and one-hot encodings were used to train a series
of models for crystal contact prediction. The effect of the data source was also tested for the ESM-IF
embeddings, by generating embeddings using both Protein Data Bank structures and AlphaFold models
as input sources.
A systematic comparison of the various embedding approaches and their merit was conducted, reveal-
ing that structural embeddings were best suited for this task. Additionally, potential sources of data
leakage from Protein Data Bank structure backbone conformations were extensively investigated, but no
evidence of leakage was found.
Furthermore, multiple different machine learning methods were applied to try and capture optimal
decision boundaries for the embeddings, where Fully Connected Neural Networks achieved the best per-
formance. Finetuning of the ESM-IF model was also attempted using both encoder-only and encoder-
decoder approaches. This provided mixed results, as most models failed to complete training due to
exploding gradients. While the models that did complete training did not exceed the best-performing
Fully Connected Neural Networks, the updated embeddings suggested this approach could potentially
enhance performance in future studies.
The model with best performance was applied to generate predictions for Affinity-Capture Self-Interacting
Nanoparticle Spectroscopy data, however, no meaningful connection between the measurements and pre-
dictions could be observed. However, the best performing model achieved respectable performance with
a Matthews Correlation Coefficient of 0.4491 when distinguishing crystal contacts, biological assembly
contacts, and non-contacts.

Introduction
1.1
Project aim
Antibody drugs require high concentrations to enable convenient self-injection. However, these high con-
centrations can increase solution viscosity beyond acceptable limits for injection. This viscosity increase
can also compromise drug stability, highlighting the importance of accurate viscosity prediction [18].
Protein solution viscosity is determined by inter-protein structures that form through protein-protein
interactions. The concentration of proteins affect how these interactions develop and how structures form
in solution [90]. Multiple studies have found, that antibody viscosity increases with concentration. This
viscosity is also affected by the physicochemical properties and aggregation behavior of the antibody’s
Fv region [48]. It has been further demonstrated that viscosity can be effectively reduced by disrupting
non-specific self-association interactions [90].
Protein language models have been shown to capture general protein rules, with the embeddings pro-
duced by the models reflecting biophysical properties of amino acids (AAs), structural features, and
functional information [25, 62]. This has led to the development of numerous large-scale protein lan-
guage models, that can be applied for a wide variety of downstream tasks [25, 53, 10]. Therefore, these
large protein models present promising potential for capturing non-specific protein interaction patterns.
However, for such models to work, large labeled task-specific datasets are often required, which can be
both expensive and difficult to obtain [60].
Fortunately, a rich source of non-specific interaction data already exists within structural databases.
The vast majority of Protein Data Bank (PDB) structures are X-ray crystal structures, held together
by low affinity non-specific protein interactions. These interactions are known as crystal contacts (CCs)
[81, 22, 9]. The CCs therefore represent a promising data source due to their abundance and potential
for training models to identify non-specific interaction patterns.
Current viscosity predictors do not leverage CC information but instead look at biophysical proper-
ties [49]. Although CCs are not currently utilized in predictive models, they can be effectively applied
to viscosity design challenges. Crystal structure analysis can be used to guide targeted mutations that
reduce self-interactions and enhance solution behavior [18]. Therefore, this study hypothesizes, that
non-specific self-association interactions, which cause aggregation and increase viscosity, closely resemble
the interaction patterns observed in CCs.
This project aims to leverage large protein language models to predict whether AA residues form CCs
or not. Four approaches will be tested: two methods using structural information, one using sequence
information, and one using one-hot embeddings to assess whether contextual embeddings are even nec-
essary. Developing a model that works effectively with AlphaFold structures is desirable, as this ensures
compatibility with future AlphaFold models and enables broader application scenarios. Therefore, the
models trained using PDB structures will also be trained using AlphaFold models. After the models have
been trained, their predictions of CC-forming residues will be used to generate predictions of Affinity-
Capture Self-Interacting Nanoparticle Spectroscopy (AC-SINS) measurements, which serves as a proxy
for viscosity predictions.
1.2
Protein structure
AAs are the basic building blocks of proteins, with polypeptide chains formed through the sequential
linkage of AAs via peptide bonds. As shown in Figure 1, each AA contains a central α-carbon (Cα), to
9
==PAGE:11==
which an amino group, a carboxyl group, and a distinctive side chain are bound. Peptide bonds link the
carboxyl group of one AA to the amino group of the next, joining them into a continuous polypeptide
chain [64]. Each AA has three dihedral backbone angles ϕ, ψ, and ω. ϕ is the torsion angle between the
nitrogen (N) and Cα, whereas ψ defines the torsion angle between Cα and the carbon in the carboxyl
group (C’). Finally, ω describes the rotation around the peptide bond in a protein’s backbone. Omega is
restricted to be either trans (180°) or cis (0°). As ω is restricted the ϕ and ψ dihedral angles determines
the conformation of the peptide backbone. [57].
Figure 1: AA backbone with the three dihedral backbone angles ϕ, ψ, and ω. ϕ is the torsion angle between the nitrogen
(N) and Cα, ψ defines the torsion angle between Cα and the carboxyl carbon (C’), and ω describes rotation around the
peptide bond. R denotes a distinctive side chain. Figure is adapted from [57] figure 4.20
The AA side chains are classified according to their distinct chemical properties. The categories include
non-polar hydrophobic side chains, hydrophilic side chains (comprising both polar uncharged and ionic
species), and structurally unique side chains such as cysteine, glycine, and proline [64]. These classifica-
tions reflect how residues interact with each other, and with the surrounding environment, influencing
protein folding and stability. The specific order of AAs in a chain defines the primary structure of a
protein [57].
Proteins exhibit four levels of structural organization: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary.
As mentioned above, the AAs constitute the primary structure. The secondary structure describes the
folding patterns of the polypeptide backbone, with the most common structures being α-helices and
β-strands. In an α-helix, the backbone coils into a spiral stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the C’
oxygen the N hydrogen, four residues ahead [54]. β-strands, on the other hand, do not coil. Instead,
they align side by side and are stabilized by hydrogen bonds between strands, forming β-sheets. Finally,
α-helices and β-strands are connected by turns and loops. Turns often contain the AA glycine, as its
small side chain allows the dihedral angles required for a tight reversal in direction. Loops are generally
longer and more structurally irregular than turns [57, 64]. The tertiary structure is the overall shape
of a polypeptide chain, determined by the arrangement of secondary structure elements. The folds that
make up the tertiary structure are stabilized largely by hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, ionic
bonds, and disulfide bridges. These same interactions also contribute to stabilizing the quaternary struc-
ture, describing how multiple polypeptide chains form a functional protein complex together [54]. An
important aspect of protein folding involves the protein’s core generally consisting of hydrophobic side
chains, while hydrophilic residues are more commonly exposed on the surface [57]. Although the primary
10
==PAGE:12==
structure contains all the information needed for proper folding, different AA sequences can fold into
the same overall structure [34]. This intricate multi-level organization from AA sequence to assembly of
protein complexes provides the essential framework that underlies protein function [89].
1.3
Crystallography
1.3.1
Protein crystals
To accurately predict CCs, it is essential to understand how protein crystals form and how their internal
symmetry gives rise to contact points between protein molecules. The following section outlines the
principles of protein crystallization and lattice formation, which provide the structural context in which
CCs emerge.
Under the correct conditions, proteins can solidify to form crystals. These crystals comprise protein
units organized in a restricted set of orientations, maintained through non-covalent interactions [61, 5].
The smallest building block of the crystal is called the asymmetric unit. Through symmetry operations
such as translations, rotations, and screw axes, one or more copies of the asymmetric unit form the unit
cell which is the smallest repeating unit that makes up a crystal (see Figure 2) [5, 89]. These symmetry
operations are shown in Figure 3, illustrating how rotations spin the asymmetric unit around specific
axes at defined angles, while translations shift the asymmetric unit spatially without rotation. Screw
axes combine both aforementioned operations to create helical arrangements [89].
Figure 2: Crystal lattice formation from asymmetric unit. The asymmetric unit forms the unit cell through symmetry
operations, and the unit cell repeats to build the entire crystal lattice. Figure is retrieved from [89]
Asymmetric units can consist of either a single or multiple copies of a protein molecule, or parts of a
multimeric protein. Multiple copies of identical proteins frequently constitute a single asymmetric unit,
and do not need to follow crystallographic symmetry rules [5, 67]. When multiple copies of identical
proteins are repeated within an asymmetric unit, they may exhibit conformational differences despite
having identical AA sequences [43].
11
==PAGE:13==
Figure 3: Symmetry operations. Three types of transformations generate crystal contacts between protein chains: trans-
lation (linear displacement), rotation (angular displacement around an axis), and screw axis (combined rotation and
translation along the same axis).
The protein crystals can be used to determine the complex structures of proteins, by examining how
X-rays diffract when passing through the crystal. The resulting diffraction pattern can subsequently be
used to create a three-dimensional model of the protein. To obtain data on how X-rays are diffracted by
a protein crystal, it is first necessary to have a suitable crystal. Protein crystallization typically requires
highly pure protein samples. However, in reality, small amounts of other macromolecules are often still
present in the samples, which affects the crystal lattice symmetry and packing [24, 5]. In some cases,
these additional molecules are not merely contaminants but play a crucial role by forming essential in-
teractions that enable or stabilize lattice formation [56].
The process of protein crystallization can be broken into four general steps: (1) supersaturating the
solution containing the desired protein, (2) nucleation where the original micro-crystals are formed,
(3) crystal growth where the lattice is repeated, and (4) harvesting where growth is stopped and the
crystal is stored [56].
Supersaturation (step 1) is the state where the protein concentration in the
solution exceeds its equilibrium solubility. When the solution is supersaturated, nucleation (step 2) can
occur, where protein aggregates are formed to create the nuclei for crystal growth [24]. One popular
crystallization model proposes, that these aggregates form due to the protein having ”sticky patches”,
meaning small regions that preferentially stick together. This causes individual molecules to clump and
form the initial nuclei. These low-affinity, but specific, interactions between sticky patches constitute
the CCs. The CCs are essential for stabilizing and defining the arrangment of molecules within the
growing crystal lattice [22, 27, 84]. To enable the growth of the nuclei, the solution’s supersaturation
is lowered to favor growth of the existing crystals rather than forming new nuclei.
During growth,
additional protein molecules preferentially associate with crystal sites that provide the most favorable
intermolecular interactions, thereby promoting crystal growth (step 3) [24]. The new protein molecules
are added according to the crystal’s symmetry, repeating the existing structure in a process driven by
carefully controlled supersaturation [56]. When the crystal is of sufficient size, it is harvested and stored
(step 4) for use in X-ray diffraction [21, 30].
1.3.2
Crystallization conditions
To obtain crystals of sufficient size and purity for diffraction analysis, it is often necessary to refine
the crystallization conditions.
Commonly adjusted parameters include the precipitant, pH, solution
12
==PAGE:14==
additives and temperature [56]. Precipitant selection and concentration control the degree of supersat-
uration, as shown in Figure 4. Appropriate precipitant conditions reduce protein solubility and modify
water activity, directing the solution into distinct supersaturation zones: high precipitant concentra-
tions cause protein precipitation; intermediate concentrations (nucleation zone) promote nucleation; and
lower concentrations (metastable zone) support crystal growth without new nucleation events [13, 56, 30].
Figure 4:
Protein crystallization phase diagram showing the relationship between protein concentration and solution
conditions. The undersaturated region maintains proteins in a stable solution. The metastable zone permits crystal growth
of existing nuclei, while inhibiting new crystal formation. The nucleation zone promotes both crystal formation and growth.
Finally, the precipitation zone results in rapid, disordered protein aggregation. The solubility and supersolubility curves
define the phase boundaries between these regions. Figure is adapted from [13].
pH is another key variable in refining protein crystallization conditions. Adjusting the pH alters the
ionization state of surface residues, influencing protein charge and the strength of intermolecular attrac-
tions. This affects both solubility, and the ability of proteins to form CCs [30]. Importantly, changes in
surface charge can impact the conformations of polar side chains on the surfaces of proteins, highlighting
how sensitive protein structure is to crystallization conditions [36]. The addition of solution additives
can provide interaction points that facilitate CC formation, and stabilize disordered regions that might
otherwise disrupt lattice assembly. These additives enhance orderly packing within the crystal lattice
through several mechanisms: promoting CCs, stabilizing flexible loops or terminal regions, and bridging
neighboring molecules [55, 50]. Temperature plays a critical role in guiding crystallization by affecting
protein solubility and the rate of solvent evaporation. Thus, adjusting the temperature can aid in control-
ling the dynamics of supersaturation and nucleation [14]. Finally, a technique known as seeding involves
the deliberate introduction of small pre-formed crystal fragments into a metastable solution, helping to
control crystal growth. This approach reduces reliance on spontaneous nucleation and improves both
crystal size and quality by minimizing unwanted nuclei [56].
1.3.3
X-ray diffraction
Once crystals have been grown under optimized conditions, they are mounted for diffraction experiments
and exposed to a monochromatic X-ray beam [24]. The crystal diffracts X-rays and projects a pattern
of spots onto a detector, with each spot corresponding to a scattered wave from a specific set of lattice
planes. To record reflections from all lattice planes, the crystal is continuously rotated (see Figure 5).
The scattered X-rays form a diffraction pattern that provides amplitude information. Together with

to fit an atomic model [61, 5].

In the diffraction pattern, the position of the spots reflects the crystal lattice geometry, whereas the
spot’s intensity indicates how strongly each set of lattice planes scatters X-rays [61]. Each spot cor-
responds to a diffracted wave, whose intensity reflects its amplitude. However, the diffraction pattern
does not provide information about the phase, which is the angle that specifies where the peaks of the
wave occur. Both amplitude and phase are required to reconstruct the electron density map via inverse
Fourier transform [5].
Phases are crucial, as changes in their values can dramatically alter the resulting electron density map
[74]. The phases can be predicted using two main methods: direct methods or molecular replacement.
Direct methods rely on mathematical relationships to solve the positions and obtain the phases of the
heavy atoms incorporated into the protein crystal. The more commonly used molecular replacement
leverages a previously solved similar structure as a model to estimate phases, making it the most prac-
tical solution when homologous models are available [74, 5].
Once phase information has been obtained and an electron density map has been generated, the next
step is to build an atomic model that fits this map. The atomic model is built to fit the electron density,
and its accuracy is evaluated by comparing the observed diffraction data with the theoretical diffraction
pattern from the calculated structure [45]. The comparison between the observed and the theoretical
diffraction pattern is quantified by the R-work value:

1.3.4
Biological assembly contacts
When studying protein interactions using atomic models, CCs greatly complicate the identification of
biologically relevant interfaces, as they do not necessarily represent functional interactions that occur
inside the cell. [12]. Identifying biological assembly contacts (BAs) is important, as it helps distinguish
interactions essential for the protein’s function from CCs, which reflect non-specific interaction patterns
[37]. One of the most commonly applied methods for identifying BAs in the PDB is Protein Interfaces,
Surfaces, and Assemblies (PISA) [83, 12]. While PISA predicts BAs with an estimated success rate of
80–90%, about 10–20% of BAs remain ambiguous due to uncertainties in interfaces, or symmetry [46, 83].
As a result, BAs annotated in the PDB may not always reflect the true functional state of the protein,
as these annotations are derived from a combination of manual curation and computational predictions,
such as those generated by PISA [89].
Predictive models for BAs often leverage the fact that biologically relevant interfaces tend to be larger,
more hydrophobic, and more conserved in both sequence and structure compared to non-biological
interfaces such as CCs [83]. Specifically, biological interfaces contain a higher proportion of hydrophobic
residues, particularly aromatic AAs. Additionally, biological interfaces secondary structure composition
that falls between that of protein cores and non-interface surfaces, whereas non-interface surfaces exhibit
the highest fraction of residues in bends and turns [85]. Overall, distinguishing biological from non-
biological interfaces remains challenging but understanding these characteristic differences helps improve
the accuracy of BA predictions.
1.4
Machine learning
1.4.1
Natural language processing for protein modeling
Recent advances in protein structure prediction increasingly rely on machine learning approaches origi-
nally developed for Natural Language Processing. NLP methods have gained traction in protein mod-
eling, as proteins on some levels are highly analogous to language. The 20 common AAs serve as a
15
==PAGE:17==
biological alphabet, providing building blocks for complex structures. Like words forming sentences,
AAs combine into functional motifs and domains that can be rearranged to generate protein diversity.
While there are clear differences between proteins and languages, NLP methods have great potential for
analyzing proteins [60].
NLP techniques require words to be represented numerically. In NLP, this involves mapping tokens
such as words, sub-words, or characters, into vector embeddings. In protein modeling, the same prin-
ciple applies: tokens, often representing individual AAs or short k-mers, are embedded into continuous
vector spaces to capture biochemical or functional properties [60]. In both natural language, and protein
modeling, low dimensional embeddings have been successfully created to capture semantic or functional
similarity, ensuring that similar tokens are located near each other in the embedding space [58, 3]. A
limitation of these embeddings is their lack of contextual information from the surrounding sequence.
This limitation has driven the adoption of transformer models, which generates meaningful embeddings
with different representations of the same token when the context changes [60].
1.4.2
Transformer architecture
One of the key drivers in the explosion of language models for sequence analysis was the introduction
of the transformer architecture which uses an encoder-decoder setup (see Figure 6). The encoder packs
the information of the input sequence into a sequence of embeddings, which the decoder uses to auto-
regressively predict an output sequence. The encoder block consists of a multi-headed attention block
and a Fully Connected Neural Network (FCNN) [52]. In contrast the decoder block contains two multi-
headed attention layers. The layer first is masked, so each position can only attend to earlier positions in
the output sequence. The second layer attends to both the current decoder sequence and the encoder’s
output embeddings, to weigh encoder representations according to their relevance for generating the
current output token. This layer is followed by an FCNN, and a learned linear transformation with
softmax applied to produce a probability distribution over the next word prediction. Each sub-layer, in
both the encoder and decoder blocks, is wrapped in a residual connection followed by layer normalization
[79].

The encoder-decoder architecture predates the transformer, and was originally introduced to address
the limitation that neural networks required fixed dimensional inputs and outputs.
In the encoder-
decoder architecture, the encoder processes the input sequence and compresses it into a fixed size vector
representation. The decoder uses this vector to auto-regressively generate the output sequence by condi-
tioning each prediction on previously generated tokens [72]. The transformer has kept the auto-regressive
setup of the decoder but has swapped the recurrence in the encoder for attention [2, 79]. Importantly,
while auto-regressive training remains essential for generative tasks, it limits the amount of contextual
information that token embeddings can capture. When the training objective permits access to the full
sequence, the resulting representations are significantly richer. This bidirectional conditioning leads to
more informative embeddings, which improves downstream performance in transfer learning settings [23].
1.4.3
Transfer learning
Deep learning models have demonstrated the ability to transfer knowledge from one task to another,
a process known as transfer learning. [88] The transformer architecture’s ability to capture complex
sequential patterns has made it particularly effective for transfer learning applications. This capability
improves generalization by allowing models trained on large, diverse datasets to adapt effectively to
specific downstream tasks with limited labeled data [52]. The effectiveness of this approach is ampli-
fied when applied to large-scale models, as their high capacity allows them to store and generalize vast
amounts of information, making the learned representations broadly applicable across a wide range of
tasks [23].
Self-supervised learning is one of the key factors underlying the success of transfer learning.
Com-
mon self-supervised methods include masked token prediction, where certain parts of the input sequence
are masked and the model learns to predict them based on the remaining context. Next-token predic-
tion represents another approach, wherein the model predicts the subsequent token given the preceding
sequence [23]. One of the main advantages of self-supervised learning lies in significantly reducing the
need for large labeled datasets [62, 25]. This allows models to be trained on vast amounts of unlabeled
data relevant to the task, after which a smaller labeled dataset can be used to fine-tune the model by
updating its weights and biases. Additionally, self-supervised learning encourages the model to capture
the underlying structural rules of the data, leading to improved performance [23].

This project applies two pretrained protein language models for down stream tasks. This section pro-

1.5.1
Inverse folding models
These large pretrained models do not necessarily need to be trained on protein sequences. Inverse folding
models predict a protein’s sequence given its structure, which is often represented using backbone coor-
dinates, resulting in structure-to-sequence models [32, 34, 35]. This approach is based on the premise
that structure is richer in information than sequences [86].
19
==PAGE:21==
One of the first successful inverse folding language models represented the protein backbone as a graph.
In this representation, each node corresponded to an AA’s dihedral angles, while edges encoded distances
and relative orientations between residues. The graph connected each residue to its 30 nearest residue
neighbors in three-dimensional space, selected through a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithm. This
graph is fed into an encoder whose self-attention masks out all non-adjacent residues, ensuring that each
residue only attends to its 30 connected neighbors (see Figure 8)[35].
Figure 8: Overview of inverse folding graph neural network architecture. Each node corresponded to an AA’s dihedral
angles, with edges encoding distances and orientations to the 30 nearest neighbors, selected through a KNN. The encoder
uses masked self-attention to restrict attention to connected neighbors only. Figure is retrieved from [35]
By using invariant scalar features such as distances and angles, this inverse folding graph neural net-
work effectively captures important local geometric relationships that remain unchanged regardless of
rotation. As a result, the model produces the same output even if the protein or one of its chains is
rotated in space. However, this scalar representation limits the model’s ability to reason about backbone
orientation [39].
To address this, the Geometric Vector Perceptron (GVP) was introduced, which rotates consistently
with the input structure. The GVP preserves the idea of assembling a graph using the 30 nearest neigh-
bors, but fundamentally changes how geometric information is represented and processed. Instead of
relying solely on rotation invariant scalar features like distances and angles, the GVP directly incorpo-
rates vector features that capture both magnitude and residue orientation. This enables the model to
consider the orientation of nearby residues when incorporating neighborhood information. These vector
features are equivariant, meaning they transform predictably when the input structure is rotated. Thus,

the GVP represents directional information with vectors while still benefiting from the rotation invariant
dihedral angle representations [39].

The core principle of the GVP architecture is restricting non-linear operations to scalars. By keeping
vectors in the linear domain while applying non-linearities to scalars, the architecture preserves equivari-
ance while enabling the expressiveness that comes from non-linear transformations. The GVP processes
scalars and vectors through interconnected pathways. The key operations are illustrated in Figure 9
and comprises: (1) vectors are linearly transformed, (2) their norms are extracted as rotation-invariant
scalars, (3) these norms are combined with input scalars and processed through linear transformations
and non-linearities to produce scalar outputs, and (4) vector outputs are computed by applying scalar
gates to transformed vectors. The linear transformations are implemented as learnable weight matrices
and bias terms. The gating operation multiplies vectors by scalar values, thereby enabling scalar features
to influence vector outputs. This design allows the GVP to maintain geometric consistency while still
learning, expressive representations from data [38].
The GVP serves as a crucial building block for Evolutionary Scale Modeling Inverse Folding (ESM-IF).
In ESM-IF, the GVP outputs both rotation invariant scalar features, and rotation-equivariant vector
features for each AA. To feed these representations into a standard transformer architecture, the vector
features undergo a transformation to change the basis, converting them to rotation-invariant scalars.
This enables a unified representation of the concatenated scalar and vector features that preserves the
directional information captured by the GVP’s representation while maintaining rotation invariance re-
quired by the transformer architecture [34].

1.5.2
The ESM-2 model
In contrast to ESM-IF’s structure-to-sequence approach, Evolutionary Scale Modeling 2 (ESM-2) op-
erates with a sequence-to-embedding paradigm. Thus, ESM-2’s encoder-only architecture produces se-
quence embeddings that can be used for downstream tasks. ESM-2’s ability to capture structure was
demonstrated through its application in the ESMFold model to generate atomic-resolution structure pre-
dictions. ESM-2 is trained using the masked language model objective on the same dataset as ESM-IF.
One key element of ESM-2 is its use of Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE), which encodes the relative
distances between AAs through rotation-based transformations [53]. This naturally creates attention
that decays with increasing distance between residues and helps the model better generalize to longer
AA sequences [71].

FCNNs are universal function approximators capable of modeling complex patterns in high-dimensional

transformed input. The resulting values are passed through a non-linear activation function to enable
the model to capture non-linear patterns [59].

FCNNs learn optimal weights and biases through backpropagation, which calculates the gradient of the
loss function with respect to each parameter using the chain rule. These gradients indicate the direction
and size of change needed to reduce the loss function, and are used to update the parameters via gradient
descent, where the learning rate controls the step size of each update. Backpropagation and gradient
descent are not unique to FCNNs, and are some of the most common optimization techniques used across
deep learning architectures [28].
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique that transforms high di-
mensional data into a lower dimensional space by capturing the most important information in the data.
By reducing dimensions, PCA enables visualization of complex high dimensional datasets [40]. This is
accomplished by finding principal components that serve as basis vectors for projecting the data into
the reduced space. The first principal component is a linear combination of the original features, which
captures the direction of maximum variance in the data. Subsequent components are found by maximiz-
ing the remaining variance while being orthogonal to all previously selected components. Importantly,
PCA is an unsupervised method that does not consider any labels in the data, relying solely on patterns
within the features and remaining unbiased by target outcomes [66].
In contrast to PCA’s unsupervised approach, Partial Least Squares Discriminant Analysis (PLS-DA)

is a supervised dimensionality reduction technique that maximizes the separation between known class
groups. PLS-DA similarly uses components to provide low-dimensional representations for visualiza-
tion, but with enhanced class separation compared to PCA. The first component captures the direction
that best separates the classes by maximizing the covariance between the data and the class labels.
Subsequent components maximize covariance but maintain orthogonality to existing components [63].
PLS-DA supports broader applications beyond visualization, such as classification and feature selection
in high-dimensional datasets [51].
1.5.4
Data partitioning for protein structure
To properly assess the quality of a model, it must be evaluated on a reserved subset of the dataset
that was not used during model training. A common strategy is to partition the data into training,
validation, and test sets. The validation set is used to optimize hyperparameters during training, while
the test set is reserved exclusively for final model assessment to ensure accurate performance estimation.
Without this separation, the model may fit to noise in the data rather than learning generalizable rules,
a phenomenon known as overfitting [16].
Data partitioning is typically performed by randomly splitting the dataset. However, random splitting
can place homologous proteins in different partitions. This results in overestimated model performance,
as models may exploit memorization rather than acquire meaningful patterns. To address this issue with-
out discarding large amounts of data, homologous proteins must be grouped together and kept within
the same partition [75].
Determining homology between proteins is a complicated task, given that proteins can have homolo-
gous local domains but different global structures [80]. One widely applied sequence-based method for
identifying homologous relationships is MMseqs2, which uses a three-stage search process to find homol-
ogous protein sequences. First, MMseqs2 identifies target sequences that have two consecutive similar
k-mer matches aligned on the same diagonal in a comparison matrix. These diagonal positions represent
where the query and target AA sequences are aligned without gaps. By using similar rather than exact
k-mer matches, MMseqs2 can use longer k-mers (seven residues) while maintaining sensitivity. Subse-
quently, a vectorized ungapped alignment finds the best position to fit one sequence against another to
maximize matches, without allowing any insertions or deletions. This approach filters out sequences with
insufficient similarity. Finally, the remaining high-scoring candidates are subjected to Smith-Waterman
alignment for precise scoring [70]. The use of Smith-Waterman alignment allows identification of homol-
ogous regions even when proteins have different overall sequences [68]. However, sequence based methods
such as MMseqs2 cannot capture structural homology, as proteins with different sequences can adopt
similar three-dimensional structures [34].
To address this limitation, Foldseek performs structural alignment by representing structures as struc-
tural alphabet sequence rather than AA sequence. MMseqs2 then be used together with the structural
alphabet, a much faster approach than traditional structural alignment. To create the structural al-
phabet, a structural token must be assigned each residue. This is done, by extracting the geometric
properties between the residue and its spatially closest neighbor. These features, describing angles and
distances between the pair’s backbone coordinates, are used to assign each residue to 1 of 20 distinct
structural alphabet characters. The characters are determined by a pretrained variational autoencoder
that identifies evolutionarily conserved structural interaction patterns [76].

similar protein complexes are kept within the same data partition, preventing data leakage, and providing
more reliable estimates of model performance on novel structures.
1.5.5
Hyperparameter optimization
Machine learning models have two types of parameters: those the model learns automatically during
training, and those that must be predetermined, such as learning rate. These predetermined parame-
ters are defined as hyperparameters, and selection of optimal hyperparameters is essential for achieving
optimal model performance [87]. While hyperparameters are often optimized through manual tuning,
automated procedures improve reproducibility and reduce the need for human intervention. The most
common automated approach is grid search, where the model exhaustively evaluates all combinations
within a specified set of hyperparameter values to identify the best configuration [87, 7].
Rather than systematically exploring all combinations, random search samples hyperparameter com-
binations randomly from specified distributions, achieving comparable performance with substantially
reduced computation [7]. However, random search does not learn from previous evaluations, leading to
redundant sampling of poor parameter regions. Leveraging prior trials to restrict the search to better-
performing regions significantly enhances hyperparameter optimization efficiency. [8, 87].
One Bayesian approach aimed at avoiding these redundant trials is Tree-structured Parzen Estima-
tors (TPE) [87]. In broad terms, TPE works by initially conducting a number of random trials. These
26
==PAGE:28==
trials are then divided into good and bad groups based on their performance using a quantile cutoff. A
Gaussian mixture model is fit to each group using kernel density estimation with Gaussian kernels. TPE
then calculates the ratio between the probability of good trials and the probability of bad trials at each
point in the hyperparameter space, as illustrated in Figure 14. The next trials are sampled from regions
where this ratio is highest, effectively targeting areas where good performance is most likely relative to
poor performance [82].

1.5.6
Model assessment
To reliably estimate a model’s generalization performance, it is important to separate the process of
hyperparameter tuning from the final evaluation. k-fold cross-validation, which splits the data into k
subsets and iteratively trains and validates the model on different folds, is a common method for per-
formance estimation [47]. Optimizing hyperparameters on validation data makes k-fold cross-validation
unreliable, as the validation data becomes part of training and inflates performance estimates. To prevent
this, one fold should be reserved as a test set, used only after hyperparameter optimization is complete,
ensuring unbiased performance evaluation [78].
It is equally important to consider how model performance is assessed. Accuracy, though widely used,
can be misleading on imbalanced datasets where a trivial classifier that always predicts the majority
class may appear to perform well [31]. The Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) provides a more
robust alternative, and can be seen in equation 5:

Materials and methods
2.1
Data collection and preparation
2.1.1
Collection of PDB structures
All X-ray diffraction mmCIF files were collected from the PDB [9]. Additionally, the AA sequence for
each protein was also collected along with a count of how many missing residues were in the file, and
what type of ligands are present. All mmCIF files with a resolution > 3˚A were filtered out of the dataset
along with all proteins where the missing residue count was 10% of the protein sequence length. No
further filtering was conducted.
2.1.2
Collection of CC and BA data
CC and BA data were collected by constructing crystal lattices for each collected PDB structure. This
was done using pymol.cmd.symexp(), with the parameter symdist set to 10. Proteins with more than 676
chains, insertion codes, or non-numeric residue identifiers, were excluded from processing. Crystal lattice
contacts were defined as residues from different chains positioned within 4˚A of one another. The BA
information was extracted from the X-ray diffraction mmCIF files. All contacts between distinct chains
not classified as BAs, were designated as CCs. The CCs were further classified into two sub-categories:
CCs within the asymmetric unit (CCIs), and CCs between asymmetric units (CCEs). The final sequence
format represents each residue as a list detailing the number of contacts of each type, categorized into
CCIs, CCEs, and BAs. This resulted in a total 164943 sequences with annotated contact information.

2.1.4
Data partitioning
The dataset was partitioned into 20 different groups by clustering all sequences with foldseek easy-
multimercluster using the following parameters: multimer-tm-threshold = 0.65, chain-tm-threshold =
0.5, and interface-lddt-threshold = 0.65. The resulting clusters were sorted by size in descending order,
and assigned to 20 partitions using a round-robin approach.
Albumin was used as a protein to assess the quality of the partitioning. The partitioning was eval-
uated by analyzing 180 albumin proteins from the PDB to determine whether the distributions of CCs
and BAs varied across the 20 partitions. Sequences were aligned using Clustal Omega with default set-
tings, with only the first chain analyzed for multi-chain structures. The contact data was subsequently
mapped to the alignments and used to generate a contact matrix, where multiple contacts for one residue
were registered as being one residue with contacts. Contact matrices were summed for each partition
and used to generate to heatmaps to compare contact distributions across partitions. The heatmaps
were generated using the seaborn.heatmap() function.
2.1.5
Collection of AlphaFold models
Pre-computed AlphaFold models were located by submitting all PDB IDs to the UniProt ID mapping ser-
vice https : //www.uniprot.org/uploadlists/ and retrieving the corresponding UniProt IDs. The mmCIF
files without a matching UniProt ID were assumed not to have an available AlphaFold model. The PDB
IDs of multimers that have multiple uniprot IDs were also not collected. The remaining Uniprot IDs were
used to find AlphaFold IDs from http : //ftp.ebi.ac.uk/pub/databases/alphafold/accession ids.csv se-
lecting all structures from the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database release version 4.0, generated using
AlphaFold2 [77]. The resulting structures were collected from https : //alphafold.ebi.ac.uk/files/af id−
model vversion.cif. The partitioning of the AlphaFold models was done by preserving the existing par-
titions made for the PDBs, used to collect the models.
2.2
Training models
2.2.1
Embedding extractions
Embeddings were extracted at the AA level for each protein, excluding entries with non-standard residues.
Four datasets were generated using different input sources and encoding strategies: (1) ESM-IF embed-
dings from PDB mmCIF structures, (2) ESM-IF embeddings from AlphaFold mmCIF structures, (3)
ESM-2 embeddings (final encoder layer) generated from the protein AA sequences, and (4) one-hot en-
coded AA sequences. The ESM-IF models extract backbone coordinates from the mmCIF files, using
the coordinates as input with each residue consisting of the atoms N, Cα and C.
2.2.2
Dimensionality reduction: PCA and PLS-DA
All the embedding sets were visualized with PCA and PLS-DA. Both methods were applied to binary
classification tasks (CC vs. not CC) as well as multiclass classification (CCs, non-contacts, and BAs). For
each case, the embeddings from partition 20 were visualized using scikit-learn’s PCA, and PLSRegression
implementations with 2 components. For both PCA and PLS-DA, explained variance ratios for the two
components was computed and used for axis labeling in the 2D projection. Class labels were numerically

==PAGE:32==
encoded and used as targets during fitting. The finetuned embeddings for partition 20 were plotted using
the same procedure as applied to embeddings that were not finetuned.
2.2.3
KNN models
All KNN models were trained on a 1% sample of the total dataset for all embeddings with the exception
of the AlphaFold models, where a 5% sample was taken to balance the size of the datasets.
For each embedding set, a KNN model was trained. Hyperparameter optimization was performed using
a fixed train-validation split: partitions 1–18 were used for training, and partition 19 was used for
validation. A grid search was conducted over k in [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] to select the value that maximized
validation performance. To assess performance variability, K-fold cross-validation was conducted, where
partitions 1 through 19 were used as validation sets in turn, with the remaining partitions used for
training. Partition 20 was held out as an independent test set to evaluate final model performance. The
MCC was computed for each fold and used to calculate the mean and standard deviation of the model’s
performance. This procedure was applied to both binary (CC vs. not CC) and multiclass (CC, not CC,
BA) classification.
2.2.4
XGBoost models
All XGBoost models were trained using the same samples as the KNN models. XGBoost models were
hyperparameter optimized and fit to each embedding set. Bayesian optimization based on TPE was
performed using Optuna, to tune hyperparameters for the XGBoost classifier [1]. The objective was
to maximize MCC on a fixed train-validation split, using partitions 1–18 for training and partition 19
for validation. For the binary class case, the XGBoost classifier was configured with the binary log-loss
evaluation metric and the binary:logistic objective. The optimization search space included decision tree
depth (3–10); learning rate (10−3–0.5); class weight scaling factor (0–2), multiplied by the class imbal-
ance ratio; number of decision trees (50–1000); minimum child weight (1–10); row sub-sampling ratio
(0.3–1.0); column sub-sampling ratio (0.3–1.0); L1 regularization strength (0.0–10.0); L2 regularization
strength (0.0–10.0); and minimum loss reduction required for splitting on a leaf node (0–1). A total of
50 trials were executed, with the first 20 being random search. The best set of hyperparameters was
selected based on the highest MCC performance on the validation partition.
For the multiclass classification task, the same Optuna-driven tuning loop and hyperparameter search
space were employed. However, modifications were implemented to address class imbalance by computing
fixed per-sample weights rather than optimizing class weights as hyperparameters. The fixed per-sample
weights were computed using: sklearn.utils.class weight.compute sample weight with class weight =
”balanced”, applied to the training labels. The sample weights were passed to the sample weight parame-
ter during model fitting (model.fit()). The XGBoost classifier was configured with the multiclass log-loss
evaluation metric with the multi:softprob objective with three classes. All other hyperparameters were
tuned over the same ranges. A total of 50 Optuna trials were again executed, with the first 20 being
random search. The best hyperparameter configuration was selected based on validation-partition MCC.
Performance variability from different training and validation splits was quantified for both binary
and multiclass XGBoost models using their optimal hyperparameters. This was done by k-fold cross-
validation, using the same procedure as for the KNN.

2.2.5
FCNN models
FCNNs were trained on 1% of the total dataset for all embeddings except AlphaFold models, which used
a 5% sample. Additionally, FCNNs were trained on 100% of available AlphaFold model proteins for both
PDB and AlphaFold embeddings.
Each set of embeddings was used to train a FCNN for binary and multiclass classification. Per-residue
embeddings were subsequently passed through a two-layer FCNN, where each linear transformation was
preceded by layer normalization, followed by dropout and ReLU activation, with a final linear layer
mapping to the output classes. All pretrained ESM-IF weights were frozen, resulting in only the FCNN
parameters being updated during training. The model used cross-entropy loss with per-class weighting,
and parameters were updated with the the AdamW optimizer. Early stopping based on validation loss
was employed, reverting the model weights to those from the epoch with the lowest validation loss when
activated.
Hyperparameter optimization was performed using Optuna’s Bayesian optimization with TPE. The
following search spaces were used: first hidden layer size (128–512); second hidden layer size (8–256);
dropout probability (0.1–0.9), which is the same for both hidden layers; learning rate (10−5–10−2, log
scale); per-class weights for non-contacts, CCs, and BAs (each 0.5–10.0); number of epochs (1–100);
and early-stopping patience (2–10). For each of the 50 trials run in parallel, the FCNN was trained on
partitions 1–18 and validated on partition 19, with the MCC on the validation set as the objective to
maximize. The best hyperparameter configuration was selected based on the highest validation MCC.
The training set was shuffled, and batches of 512 residues were sampled across proteins, resulting in
each batch not necessarily corresponding to a single protein. Performance variability for the binary and
multiclass FCNN models was assessed in the same manner as for the KNN and XGBoost models.
2.2.6
Finetuning ESM-IF encoder with FCNN
Finetuning of the pretrained ESM-IF encoder with a FCNN attached was performed in both binary and
multiclass case. The model takes the backbone coordinates as inputs, where each residue is represented
through the atoms N, Cα and C. All encoder weights were unfrozen, enabling finetuning of the ESM-IF
encoder and the GVP layers.
The input was batched to contain four protein structures each. The batches were processed directly
by the ESM-IF GVP and encoder, followed by an FCNN appended after the final encoder layer. The
architecture and hyperparameters of this FCNN matched those of the previously optimized standalone
FCNN models for both binary and multiclass tasks. However, the FCNN weights were reinitialized prior
to training in this new setup.
Training was performed using the AdamW optimizer with a base learning rate of 1e −4.
To miti-
gate instability during the initial training phase, a linear learning rate warm-up schedule was applied.
The learning rate increased linearly over the first 10% of the total training steps, whereafter it remained
constant for the rest of the training process. This warm-up schedule was implemented via a custom
LambdaLR learning rate scheduler. For both the binary and multiclass case, the model was trained on
partitions 1–18, and partition 19 was used as a validation set for early stopping. Partition 20 was applied
for final performance evaluation.
The training of the ESM-IF encoder with FCNN model attached, was conducted both with and without
noise added to the backbone coordinates. The noise was Gaussian, added independently to each 3D
coordinate with a standard deviation of 0.1 ˚A. Specifically, for each structure in the dataset, random
32
==PAGE:34==
noise sampled from a normal distribution (mean 0, standard deviation 0.1 ˚A) was added to all coordinate
arrays before being used as input for the model. This was done for both binary and multiclass cases
with the same partitions being used from training validation and test sets.
2.2.7
Finetuning ESM-IF decoder with FCNN
Finetuning was also performed using the full ESM-IF model, including both encoder and decoder com-
ponents, with an added FCNN layer for classification. As in the encoder-only setup, this model directly
processes backbone coordinates, and was applied to both binary and multiclass classification tasks. Input
batching followed the same format, with four protein structures per batch. The decoder of the ESM-IF
model was patched to remove the masking of unpredicted residues, making the decoder bidirectional
All layers in the ESM-IF architecture, including the GVP, encoder and decoder, were unfrozen to al-
low full finetuning.
The FCNN added after the final decoder layer used the same architecture and
hyperparameters as the previously optimized models, but with reinitialized weights before training. The
initial <cath> token used to signal sequence start was removed before passing the decoder output to the
FCNN. The final decoder layer was also explicitly reinitialized, using Xavier uniform initialization (via
torch.nn.init.xavier uniform ).
Training followed the same procedure as in the encoder-only model. The AdamW optimizer was used
with a base leaning rate of 1e −4 and linear learning rate warm-up over the first 10% of training steps,
implemented via a custom LambdaLR scheduler. For both binary and multiclass classification tasks,
partitions 1–18 were used for training, partition 19 for validation (used for early stopping), and partition
20 as the test set for final performance evaluation.
2.2.8
Predicting AC-SINS data
The AC-SINS predictions were generated by loading the FCNN trained on all available AlphaFold model
data for multiclass classification, and applying it to generate predictions for 150 modeled proteins. The
AC-SINS correlations were calculated by counting the number of predicted BAs and CCs for each chain
in the protein, and summing them. The counts were loaded into a dataframe, and the correlations were
computed using pandas.corr().

3.3
Embedding extraction
To predict if individual AA residues form CCs, a meaningful numerical representations for machine learn-
ing models is required. To leverage different sources of biological information, four distinct embedding
approaches were evaluated and compared for their suitability in CC prediction.
One-hot encoding served as a baseline representation, encoding only AA identity without contextual in-
formation. The second approach utilized ESM2, a sequence-to-sequence transformer model that generates
contextualized embeddings from protein sequences. The third approach employed ESM-IF, a structure-
to-sequence GVP-transformer model that represents AAs through their protein backbone coordinates,
while incorporating contextual information. For ESM-IF, only the encoder output was extracted, al-
though the model includes a decoder component.
Previous inverse folding studies suggest PDB backbone coordinates may be an additional source of data
leakage in sequence recall tasks, as backbone conformations can reveal AA identity [20, 33]. To verify
that the ESM-IF models do not simply learn to recognize CC specific backbone conformations, AlphaFold
models were collected for all suitable proteins. AlphaFold models were used to generate separate ESM-IF
embeddings, which were then compared to PDB-based embeddings to assess potential data leakage. This
served as a fourth embedding approach.

3.5
Model scalability analysis
To evaluate FCNN scalability and enable fair comparison between embedding types, identical protein
sets were used for both PDB-based and AlphaFold-based ESM-IF embeddings. All collected AlphaFold
models and their corresponding PDB versions were used to train both binary and multiclass FCNN mod-

MCC scores of 0.4491 for the multiclass model and 0.3675 for the binary model. Unfortunately, the
FCNNs trained on AlphaFold-based ESM-IF embeddings encountered memory allocation issues during
cross-validation. Consequently, these models lack standard deviations.
3.6
Finetuning
The ESM-IF model generated the highest-performing embeddings for CC prediction. However, since
ESM-IF was not trained for this specific task, it was finetuned to produce embeddings optimized for
CC classification. This was conducted to determine whether finetuning could enhance the discriminative
power of embeddings for CC prediction. Finetuning was performed with the FCNN attached, creating a
unified model where both the embedding generation and FCNN were trained jointly.
To see if model performance could be pushed even further, ESM-IF was also finetuned with the decoder
applied. Since CC prediction does not require auto-regressive generation, ESM-IF was modified to make
the decoder bidirectional by removing causal masking. This allows each residue’s structural embedding
to be refined based on information from all other residues in the same protein chain, creating globally
informed representations. The FCNN then takes the output from the final decoder layer as input for CC
prediction, enabling end-to-end training of the complete model.
It has previously been reported, that model performance could be improved by adding noise to the
backbone coordinates during training [34, 20]. Therefore, both encoder finetuning and encoder-decoder
finetuning were attempted with and with-out noise. Due to computational constraints, both approaches
used FCNN hyperparameters from the proof-of-concept models without cross-fold validation. For the
encoder-decoder configuration, the final decoder layer was reinitialized to encourage greater parameter
adjustments in the last layers. Because finetuning can un-learn important features through catastrophic
forgetting, a warm-up phase with a lower learning rate was applied to both configurations.

4
Discussion
4.1
Preliminary contact analysis
A preliminary contact analysis was conducted to examine the differences between the CCs and BAs.
Additionally, the analysis investigated the merits of potentially splitting CCs into subcategories of CCEs
and CCIs. The preliminary contact analysis suggested clear differences between CCEs and BAs. The
BAs were observed to have a prevalence of hydrophobic residues, suggesting these interfaces involve less
accessible pockets, relying on weak hydrophobic interactions such as van der Waals forces. In contrast,
the CCEs have an abundance of hydrophilic residues, suggesting these contacts are more surface exposed
interactions that would typically involve hydrogen bonds and electrostatic forces. These observations for
the CCEs and BAs were supported by the secondary structure analysis. Here, β-strands and α-helices
were shown to be enriched for BAs, suggesting the contacts occur in less accessible pockets. On the other
hand, turns were observed to be enriched in CCEs, indicating exposure on the surface of the protein.
Residues in CCIs and CCEs showed similar KL divergence patterns relative to BAs, though CCIs ex-
hibited substantially smaller magnitude differences, particularly for aromatic residues. These smaller
differences, along with the high enrichment of β-strands in CCIs, may suggest that CCIs constitute an
intermediate category between CCEs and BAs. The exact cause for the difference in CCIs secondary
structure patterns is unclear. The idea of treating CCIs and CCEs as separate categories was dismissed,
as it could unnecessarily complicate the classification task of distinguishing CCs, BAs, and non-contacts.
Given their substantial overlap and that both appear to reflect non-specific contact patterns, it was
deemed more appropriate to treat CCIs and CCEs as a single interaction type.
When analyzed as a combined CC category, they showed a strong correlation with improved resolution
when normalized by chain length This suggests CCs contribute to more ordered crystal arrangements.
This relationship suggests CC prediction could have potential applications for crystallizability prediction.
4.2
Partitioning validation
To prevent model overfitting through memorization of structural homologs, the dataset was partitioned
based on structural similarity. The partitioning was analyzed using CC distributions in albumin to vali-
date the approach. Although partitioning did not group all albumins into a single partition, the generated
heatmaps suggest systematic differences between the partitions. Distinct CC patterns were observed,
and could indicate meaningful structural variation, in which case the separate clustering is warranted.
However, some inter-partition overlap remains, indicating limitations in achieving perfect separation us-
ing Foldseek-Multimer. Since this analysis focuses solely on CCs, other structural differences unrelated
to CC formation may account for the separation of these smaller partitions. Nevertheless, the vast ma-
jority of albumins are concentrated in only 4 out of 20 partitions. If data leakage were present, it would
be confined to these few partitions, making any resulting overfitting detectable during cross-validation
through inflated performance on the corresponding folds.
The high intensities observed in the heatmaps further suggest that CCs are reasonably consistent across
different crystallization experiments. The fact that CC patterns remain consistent within partitions, yet
differ noticeably across them, provides additional support for the validity of the partitioning scheme.
One improvement to the partitioning approach would have been to treat the AlphaFold models as a
separate dataset and partition them independently using the same clustering method. In the current
setup, these models were partitioned based on their corresponding PDB structures.
47
==PAGE:49==
4.3
Embedding analysis
The different embedding schemes were assessed through dimensionality reduction. This was done to
determine if any of the embeddings appeared to be inherently better suited for the classification task
of distinguishing CCs, BAs, and non-contacts. The improved separation of classes in the PCA plots
would suggest structure is a richer source of information for CC prediction. However, caution should
be exercised in interpreting these visual differences as both principal components combined explain less
than 7% of the variance in the data for the ESM-IF plots. While the ESM-2 embeddings had a greater
explained variance of roughly 20%, the class separation was less clear. This suggests that ESM-2 captures
patterns of sequence variation that are uninformative for this task, whereas ESM-IF’s structure-based
features provide more discriminative information relevant to the specific classes being predicted.
Multiclass PCA analysis provides additional evidence that sequence based information is less effective
than structure-based features for CC prediction. The PCA plots additionally suggest, that AlphaFold
models do not produce dramatically different embeddings compared to PDB based models. This is sup-
ported by the observation, that both sources of embeddings produce similar distributions of data points,
comparable class separations, and nearly identical explained variance. This indicates that the underlying
structural representations are largely consistent.
ESM-2 embeddings showed poor class separation in PLS-DA visualization, even though PLS-DA is
specifically designed to optimize class discrimination. This indicates that the limited class separation is
inherent to the ESM-2 embeddings themselves, rather than resulting from the unsupervised dimension-
ality reduction. In contrast, ESM-IF embeddings demonstrate clear class separation in PLS-DA for both
binary and multiclass scenarios. Collectively, these dimensionality reduction visualizations consistently
demonstrate that structure-based embeddings provide clearer class separation, making it fundamentally
more effective for this classification task.
For binary PLS-DA, PDB embeddings showed marginally better class separation than AlphaFold em-
beddings.
This suggests that backbone conformation differences could be a source of data leakage.
The improved separation between CC and non-contacts was also evident in both binary and multiclass
PCA plots. However, BA embeddings were equally well separated in both multiclass PLS-DA plots. If
backbone conformational differences cause data leakage in CC interactions, comparable effects would be
expected for BA interactions. The absence of such patterns in BAs therefore argues against backbone
conformation as a source of data leakage. A more likely explanation is that the observed differences are
caused by inherent structural differences between AlphaFold models and PDB based models.
4.4
Model assessment
Three different model types were trained on all of the embedding schemes. The KNN models served as a
zero-shot baseline to benchmark whether the other models learned meaningful decision boundaries. The
performance of KNN directly reflects the inherent predictive power of the embeddings for the given task.
Training the models on all embedding schemes enabled investigation into whether contextual embeddings,
including the structural ESM-IF embeddings and the sequence-based ESM-2 embeddings, were required,
or if one-hot encoding was sufficient. All the models trained on 1% of the data serve as proof-of-concept
models used to asses the potential of the various approaches.
The KNN models achieved reasonable predictive performance above random chance for the contex-
tual embeddings. In contrast, the random predictions from one-hot encoding KNNs demonstrates the
necessity of contextual embeddings. For one-hot encodings, the FCNN and XGBoost models appear
48
==PAGE:50==
to perform substantially better than the KNN. However, inspection of the predictions reveals that the
one-hot, FCNN, and XGBoost models primarily identify residues with high CC to non-contact ratios,
predicting all such residues as CCs. This strategy can yield non-random MCCs, but it does not reflect
meaningful learning about contact formation, confirming that contextual embedding are required for this
task. In this case, one-hot embeddings were used without incorporating contextual information, limit-
ing their effectiveness. However, one-hot representations are not inherently non-contextual and could
perform better if integrated into architectures that capture local or global sequence context, such as
convolutional models.
The proof-of-concept models further demonstrated that XGBoost and FCNN models performed bet-
ter than the KNN models.
If their performance had been lower than the KNNs, it could indicate
overfitting or ineffective use of the LLM embeddings. This superior performance therefore demonstrates
that XGBoost and FCNN models learn more complex decision boundaries that leverages the embedding
information more effectively. Notably, FCNN performance improved most substantially when incorpo-
rating the BAs class, suggesting that FCNNs can better exploit this additional class information.
The low standard deviations observed for all models shows a robust performance. When coupled with
a partitioning scheme designed to reduce structural homology across partitions, these results are very
positive.
They suggest the models have learned generalizable contact prediction patterns that work
across structurally diverse proteins. This observed consistency during cross-validation indicates genuine
learning of contact patterns rather than memorization of structural similarities and reduces concerns
about overfitting.
For all models, ESM-2’s sequence-based representations substantially underperformed their ESM-IF
structural counterparts for both PDB and AlphaFold based embeddings. This consistent drop in perfor-
mance supports the notion that structural information is a richer source of information than sequence
data, and confirms that structural information is better suited for CC prediction.
Structure-based
embeddings also demonstrated superior BA contact prediction, consistently achieving subtle MCC im-
provements absent in models trained on sequence-based embeddings. However, given that models were
trained on 1% samples, caution is warranted when interpreting these subtle improvements, as they may
reflect sampling bias rather than genuine differences between embedding types. While the AlphaFold
models used 5% of the dataset, this merely balanced the dataset sizes, resulting in approximately 800,000
residues for all sets. Although a dataset of this size should be sufficient to establish general trends and
create a proof-of-concept model, the random sampling approach used may still introduce systematic
biases that could confound comparisons between embedding types.
The potential for sampling bias is particularly pertinent when considering the performance differences
between PDB-based and AlphaFold-based structural embeddings. The PDB-based models consistently
outperformed or matched their AlphaFold counterparts and showed larger performance gains from in-
corporating BAs in the multiclass task. Whether this reflects data leakage from PDB backbone confor-
mations, inherent structural differences in AlphaFold models, or sampling bias, remains unclear from the
proof-of-concept model alone.
The FCNNs for the two structure-based embeddings were re-trained using a 20-fold increase in data,
including identical proteins. This was done to evaluate how performance scales with data size and to en-
able a more direct comparison between the two data sources. Notably, AlphaFold-based FCNNs matched
PDB-based models for both the binary and multiclass FCNNs, arguing against PDB backbone data leak-
age, which should produce more pronounced effects. The scaled up analysis therefore suggests, it is far
49
==PAGE:51==
more likely that the performance difference observed in the proof-of-concept models was due to sampling
bias. An alternative explanation for the performance difference relates to dataset composition. The
proof-of-concept dataset contained more multimeric structures, which provide larger training examples
and richer inter-chain interaction information. However, multimers were practically excluded from the
large-scale training due to the selection of available AlphaFold models, thereby removing this potential
source of performance enhancement.
Scaling data 20-fold improved all models, with multiclass models showing the largest improvement,
resulting in MMCs of 0.4466 and 0.4491 for the PDB and AlphaFold embeddings, respectively. FCNNs
showed evidence of diminishing returns when scaling data, with this effect being most pronounced in
binary models and less evident in multiclass models. This suggests the need for alternative approaches
to achieve substantial performance increases. Although the FCNNs trained on AlphaFold model-based
embeddings were not evaluated using cross-validation, their MCC scores are likely still reliable. This
assessment is supported by the strong generalization and very low standard deviations demonstrated by
all previous models.
4.5
Finetuning analysis
ESM-IF was finetuned to improve model performance, by producing better embeddings for the classifi-
cation task. Both encoder-only and encoder-decoder models were created to assess whether the decoder
would bring further performance increases through globally informed representations. AlphaFold and
PDB models were used as input both with and without noise, to assess their impact on model perfor-
mance.
Most of the finetuning configurations failed due to exploding gradients, indicating finetuning was too
aggressive. Provisions were made to reduce the risk of exploding gradients, such as a warm-up phase with
a lower learning rate, and Xavier re-initialization of the last decoder layer in the encoder-decoder models.
However, these measures were insufficient. As the learning rates were selected based on proof-of-concept
models optimized solely for the FCNN, they were likely too high, and the warm-up phase likely too short.
Two model configurations were completed, however, the performance was poor.
Both these models
underperformed the proof-of-concept model FCNN trained on pre-computed PDB embeddings.
The
performance decline of the finetuned encoder with Gaussian noise was subtle and may have been caused
either by the addition of noise or by hyperparameters that were unsuitable for finetuning. A much more
pronounced performance drop was observed for the encoder-decoder model. This is likely due to the re-
setting of the last decoder layer, which may have disrupted previously learned representations. Because
none of the configurations using the AlphaFold models as input, including pairs with and without noise,
completed successfully, it was unfortunately not possible to evaluate their impact.
The finetuned encoder model with added Gaussian noise, despite not improving on the FCNN, only
performed slightly worse.
Interestingly, the embeddings shown in the PCA and PLS-DA plots were
drastically different from the earlier PDB-based embeddings. While some of this difference may be at-
tributed to the added Gaussian noise, this effect is likely minor. Since both sets of embeddings originate
from PDB based models, the observed shift indicates that the encoder’s internal representation has been
substantially altered. The combined explained variance of the first two components increased roughly
fivefold in both the PCA and PLS-DA plots, and the embeddings appear to exhibit better class separa-
tion. This suggests that finetuning reshaped the embeddings to emphasize class separation, potentially
at the expense of other information. Although this did not produce the top-performing model, it ranked
second among those trained on 1% of the data, underscoring the merits of finetuning. Considering that
50
==PAGE:52==
the hyperparameters were originally optimized for frozen embeddings, a tailored finetuning strategy is
likely to yield further performance improvements.
4.6
AC-SINS predicions
The scaled-up FCNN trained on AlphaFold based embeddings to predict CCs, BAs, and non-contacts
was applied to predict AC-SINS data. This was done by correlating CC and BA counts with AC-SINS
wavelength measurements to determine if the non-specific contacts captured by CCs could be used to
predict self-interaction.
No strong correlations between the CC predictions and AC-SINS wavelengths could be found. This
poor performance is likely caused by a combination of factors. First, the overall predictive performance
of the model remains modest, with a maximum MCC of 0.4491, suggesting substantial room for im-
provement even under ideal conditions. Second, the AC-SINS data consisted exclusively of multimeric
antibodies, while the model was trained only on monomeric structures, creating a mismatch that likely re-
duces model performance. Finally, the sequences in the AC-SINS dataset were highly similar, suggesting
the model may not be well suited to distinguish between such closely related sequences.

Future perspectives
In this project, several promising approaches were investigated and identified to enable CC prediction.
However, many approaches could not be fully explored due to time constraints. A good place for future
studies to start would be by attempting to fix the finetuning of the multiple different model configurations
in this project. Since the primary challenge encountered was exploding gradients, future projects should
start with a lower learning rate, and a longer warm-up phase. Additional implementation of gradient
clipping would further stabilize finetuning by rescaling the gradient when they exceed predefined thresh-
olds during training. If such an initial setup is insufficient, it may be necessary to gradually unfreeze
model layers during training to maintain greater control over the finetuning process.
Future work should verify that the models developed in this study are not simply making predictions
about CCs based solely on residue surface accessibility, but are instead learning more comprehensive
structural relationships. While this is unlikely, it is still important to rule out this possibility as such
a trivial prediction would not fulfill the purpose of learning non-specific protein interaction patterns.
To investigate this, it would be interesting to reassess model performances by exclusively considering
surface-exposed residues. This was not conducted in this project due to time constraints.
Model performance may also have been limited by the lack of information about experimental crystalliza-
tion conditions. Given that CCs are susceptible to variations in experimental conditions, incorporating
this contextual information may enable the model to differentiate between similar samples that exhibit
distinct CC patterns. Preliminary analysis using a heatmap-based partition validation approach could
reveal patterns in CC consistency under different crystallization conditions. These findings could then
guide the incorporation of this metadata into future models.
The preliminary analysis in this project investigated dividing CCs into the two sub-categories of CCEs
and CCIs. Based on initial results, it was decided not to move forward with this approach, and instead
treat CCs as a single unified group. However, it would be interesting for future projects to introduce
CCIs as an additional category for multiclass prediction. It is conceivable that adding CCIs as an addi-
tional category could improve model performance, similar to how including BAs improved performance.
However, this approach has uncertain benefits and potential detrimental effects.
The scaled-up FCNNs run in this project were exclusively trained on structures from the PDB that
matched structures in the AlphaFold database. This approach effectively limits the dataset to single-
chain protein structures, potentially excluding valuable inter-chain interaction data that could enhance
model learning. Future research should therefore include multi-chain protein complexes during complete
model training to enable the models to capture these inter-chain interaction patterns
Finally, the current binary representation of CCs or non-contact is unable to capture interaction strength.
However, the dataset assembled in this project includes information on the number of CCs per residue,
making it well-suited for regression. It would therefore be interesting to train a regression model instead
of a classification model to investigate if leveraging interaction strength could better capture non-specific
interactions.
Ultimately, the FCNNs trained on the scaled-up dataset achieved reasonable performance of 0.4491
and 0.4466 for the PDB and AlphaFold-based embeddings, respectively. While further iteration should
be able to improve performance further, this project provides a solid foundation from which future studies
can build upon.

6
Conclusion
This project developed a collection of models for prediction of CCs. The project leveraged CC data
from crystal structures in the PDB, to learn non-specific protein interactions. Such interactions are of
particular interest in antibody design as it may potentially have applications for viscosity prediction.
A preliminary data analysis identified distinguishing features between CCs and BAs, revealing that
CCs are enriched for surface-exposed hydrophilic residues compared to less accessible hydrophobic BAs.
To ensure robust model training, the dataset was meaningfully partitioned according to structure. This
partitioning strategy was validated through analyzing the protein albumin, which showed consistent CC
patterns within partitions and clear differences between partitions. This approach greatly minimized the
risk of data leakage, thereby improving the reliability of the model performance measurements.
Four different types of embeddings were applied to predict CCs using four different approaches: two
embeddings that incorporated structural information from PDB structures and AlphaFold models, a
sequence-based embedding, and one-hot embedding. Notably, the one-hot embedding, unlike the other
embeddings, did not include context of neighboring AAs. The embeddings were assessed using PCA and
PLS-DA. This revealed, that the two structural models provided embeddings with the clearest separation
of the target classes (CC, BA, and non-contacts). The KNN models further confirmed these findings, as
their predictions demonstrated that structure-based models outperformed sequence-based embeddings.
In contrast, the one-hot embedding predictions were limited to only the dominant class.
The XGBoost and FCNN models produced more complex decision boundaries across all embedding
types. However, the XGBoost and FCNNs trained using one-hot embeddings, merely identified residues
with high CC to non-contact ratios, predicting all such residues as CCs. This limitation emphasized the
critical need for contextual embeddings. Consistent with the KNN results, both structural embeddings
continued to outperform the sequence-based ESM-2 embeddings in the XGBoost and FCNN models.
This further reinforces the notion that structure provides a richer source of information than sequence
alone. Additional attempts were made to improve performance by finetuning ESM-IF, but this resulted
in limited success due to exploding gradients. Nevertheless, PCA and PLS-DA plots from the success-
fully trained finetuned encoder with Gaussian noise showed improved class separation. This suggests
that finetuning could be a promising avenue for future model improvement.
Initial differences in the performance of the two structural embeddings suggested that PDB backbone
structures might cause data leakage. However, further investigation indicated that this performance gap
was actually caused by either sampling bias, or the inclusion of multimeric protein structures in the PDB
embedding dataset. To confirm this hypothesis, two models were trained with a 20-times larger dataset
consisting of identical protein sets for the AlphaFold and PDB structural embeddings, respectively. This
controlled comparison confirmed that observed differences resulted from dataset factors rather than
data leakage. The increase in data size also produced further improvements in model performance. The
scaled-up AlphaFold model achieved the best performance in this project with an MCC of 0.4491, closely
matched by its PDB counterpart with an MCC of 0.4466. While not perfect, this performance level is
quite respectable and suggests that the models have successfully captured meaningful aspects of non-
specific protein interactions.
However, when attempting to apply these CC predictions to predict AC-SINS wavelengths, the model
predictions showed no correlation with the measurements. While this outcome was disappointing, it
does not necessarily mean that the models failed to learn meaningful patterns of non-specific protein
53
==PAGE:55==
interactions.
While this work was unable to predict AC-SINS measurements, it achieved reasonable performance
for predicting CCs. It was further confirmed that structural data is a better source of information for
this task, and the comparison with AlphaFold models eliminated concerns about data leakage from PDB
structures. These insights therefore provide a solid foundation for advancing the prediction of non-specific
protein interactions for future studies.
