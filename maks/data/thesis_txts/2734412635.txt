Abstract
English:
The present thesis focuses on finding new solutions for the recycling of wind tur-
bine blades, with a particular focus on mechanical recycling techniques. The core of this re-
search lies in exploring the feasibility and effectiveness of using shredded composite materi-
als coming from wind turbine blades as a reinforcing agent in rigid polyurethane foams. The
study investigates the properties of the shredded composite material, its compatibility with
polyurethane foam, and the resultant effects on the foam’s structural and mechanical char-
acteristics. Starting from the manufacturing of three reinforced foam panels with different
volume fractions of reinforcement, it was analysed the disposition of the shredded composite
through the foam with an X-Ray Computed Tomography scan. The outcome of this investi-
gation was 2D random orientation of the shredded composite, which was used to develop an
analytical model for the prediction of the stiffness of the reinforced foam. The panels manu-
factured were then subjected to a compression test and a sandwich material made using the
reinforced foam and Glass Fiber Reinforced Composite panels was tested in three-point bend-
ing to quantify the mechanical properties of a sandwich beam. The result of the investigation
proved the increase of compressive elastic modulus and bending stiffness when the shredded
composite was added to the foam. However, it was not possible to establish a simple relation
between the mechanical properties of the reinforced foam and the content of shredded com-
posite. It is believed that a non-linear elastic behavior of the foam could have influenced the
results and led to a mismatch of experimental and analytical calculations.

Introduction
Wind energy has emerged as a key component of the global transition towards sustainable and
renewable energy sources. While wind power presents an eco-friendly and abundant energy
source, it also raises critical questions about the end-of-life disposal of wind turbine blades,
their environmental impact, and the innovations in recycling practices.
Wind Turbine Blades (WTBs), like all technologies, have a finite life cycle, which introduces a
unique set of challenges and considerations for the wind energy industry. WTBs are designed
to last for about 20 or 25 years and during their operational time, they endure a wide range
of environmental stressors, including constant exposure to wind shear, turbulence and gusts
[1]. As WTBs approach the end of their operational life, a series of challenges arise such as
their disposal, and given their size, weight, and composite materials, they appear hard to be
recycled [2]. This has led to concerns about their environmental impact and the need for
sustainable end-of-life solutions.
1.1
Environmental Impact of Decommissioned Blades
The disposal of WTBs poses environmental concerns due to their composite construction. Tra-
ditional landfill disposal is impractical and not sustainable over time. Recognizing the chal-
lenges posed by WTB disposal, the wind energy industry has been actively exploring recy-
cling solutions. These initiatives aim to reduce the environmental impact of decommissioned
blades while also tapping into their potential for resource recovery and alternative applica-
tions.
1.2
Consequences of Landfilling Wind Blades
Since wind turbine blades were designed to generate clean energy from the wind, their end-
of-life disposal through landfilling does not suits the spirit of it and it is also unsustainable in
the long run. These challenges extend beyond the impracticality of disposing of large, com-
posite structures. The environmental impact of landfilling WTBs encounters several issues
such as space constraints and land use. In fact, landfilling WTBs, due to their sheer size and
bulk, necessitates significant amounts of space. This space consumption becomes a grow-
ing concern as wind energy installations proliferate. Wind farms, located in remote and open
areas to maximize wind exposure, often require extensive land use. The additional land re-
quired for landfilling blades adds to the overall footprint of wind energy projects, potentially
encroaching on natural habitats and agricultural lands.
1.3
Recycling Wind Turbine Blades
One of the most critical environmental issues tied to landfilling WTBs is the persistence of
non-biodegradable composite materials. WTBs are primarily constructed from glass and/or
carbon fiber composites. These materials are highly durable and resistant to decomposition,
which is advantageous during the operational life of the blade but problematic during disposal.

1.4
Mechanical Recycling Techniques
Mechanical recycling methods involve shredding and grinding decommissioned blades into
smaller particles, which can be used for several different applications. The process of mechan-
ically recycling composites involves a series of cutting and grinding stages to break down the
materials into small fragments, typically measuring a few millimeters. The appearance of the
resulting shredded composite (SC) is a blend of short individual fibers, longer fiber bundles
partially saturated with polyester resin, and clusters of materials, as illustrated in Figure 4,
for the material coming from a blade of the first offshore wind farm Vindeby in Denmark. A
full LM17 blade was shredded as part of the DecomBlades project [www.decomblades.dk] and
this material is used as input for this thesis. The LM17 wind turbine blade was cut into 2 m
section at the DTU Risø campus using a chain saw. The sections were loaded into a stan-
dard container and moved to a shredder at the company HJ Hansen and DTU wind and Energy
systems received approximately 700 kg shredded blade material.

A new application for the shredded material
2.1
Scope and conceptual idea
The scope of this thesis is to investigate possible new techniques for the reuse of the shred-
ded material presented in Figure 4 into material of new wind turbine blades or wind turbines
without further material processing of the shredded blade material. To do so, the flow chart
that will be followed during the thesis is shown in Figure 5.
Figure 5: Flow chart of the thesis
The ideating phase consisted in figuring out how to reuse within the Wind Energy industry
the shredded material presented in Figure 4. At the end of the phase, the student came up
with the idea of reusing the material and utilize it as reinforcement for core materials, such as
foams (see sandwich panels shown in Figure 2) used in the WTB industry for the construction
of new blades, with the possibility to extend it also to marine and aerospace applications.
Using the principle of Fiber Reinforced Composites (FRC), the shredded composite could be
added to a Rigid Polyurethane (PU) Foam still in the liquid phase to guarantee a homogeneous
distribution of the shredded composite (SC), with the expectation of obtaining a reinforced
foam with improved mechanical properties, which could be able to compete with the existing
materials for similar applications.

will be manufactured comply with these characteristics, and, as a consequence, be relevant
for further applications. Investigations regarding the density of the reinforced foam as well

2.2
Feasibility study
To support the idea presented above, a preliminary feasibility experiment was made by the
student with the help of technicians. The goal of this first experiment was to ensure that
the SC could be spread homogeneously throughout the foam and the interface between the
SC and foam be appropriate to guarantee a good performance of the reinforced foam. For
the experiment, a similar shredded material to the one considered for the thesis was used
together with two different kinds of Rigid PU foam, H200 and H50 from PU System®, common
foams easy to find in liquid state in the market. The solid state densities of the two foams used
were ρ1 = 200 kg/m3 and ρ2 = 50 kg/m3. Both of them were weighted to have a volume of 1.5
l after expansion, and mixed with SC equal to a volume fraction of Vf = 0.04. The experiment
was done in two buckets of 5 l size, in which the liquid foam and the SC were poured and
mixed together. After the expansion time of approx 210 seconds and solidification of the
foam, 12 hours according to the manufacturer specifications, the bucket was removed and
the foam cut in half. The result is shown in Figure 7.
Figure 7: Result of feasibility experiment
The sample shown in Figure 7 was cut in half to check by visual inspection if the SC was spread
throughout the whole volume. The SC appeared to be spread homogeneously and there was
no trace of visible cracks or detachment between SC and foam, with a resulting good bond
between the two components, as shown also in Figure 8.

Theoretical background and analytical models
The principle laying behind the choice of using the shredded blade material as reinforcement
can be described by a simple rule of mixtures [10], which can be explained as the calculation
of the properties of a composite material as the weighted average of the properties of its
constituents. This model does not take into account the misalignment of the fibers, however,
it is able to give a reasonable prediction of the stiffness for many cases. For a better approxi-
mation, more complete methods can be used, where the orientation of the fibers is taken into
account, and compare the properties obtained with experimental values. Among the many
different analytical models that can describe the material considered, the Halpin-Tsai Model
(H-T) and a Rule of Mixtures Model (ROM) with corrected orientation were selected accord-
ing to [11]. The assumptions employed in constructing Rule of Mixtures (ROM), Halpin-Tsai
(H-T) equations, and various other micromechanical models are as follows: both the matrix
and fibers demonstrate linear elasticity, isotropy, and homogeneity; fibers are aligned and
distributed perfectly; the matrix is voidfree; and the bonding between matrix and fibers is
perfect [12].

3.1
Rule of Mixtures
The ROM is a widely accepted micromechanical model, which follows a mathematical approach
that analyses the behavior of composite materials on a microscopic scale for the estimation
of their stiffness with a unidirectional fibre orientation and continuous fibres:
Ec,ROM = VfEf + VmEm
(1)

proposed [13]:
Ec,ROM = ηoVfEf + VmEm
(2)

calculated using a geometrical model:

XCT Analysis of the reinforced foam
X-ray computed tomography (XCT), is a scanning technique capable of non-destructively pro-
viding highly accurate 3D inspections of fiber architectures, manufacturing defects, and ev-
erything that involves a differences in density inside the volume of the sample. Various XCT
scanners can provide detailed information across different length scales, from the entire com-
ponent down to individual fibers. XCT involves computationally reconstructing radiographs
acquired from multiple angles, and image contrast depends on variations in X-ray attenuation
through the object being studied [21]. This technique has been used in the present study to
visually investigate the orientation and the effective volume fraction of SC in the foam.

Another confirmation of the good estimate of the volume fraction of SC can be obtained from
the XCT results. In fact, Figure 27 presents the binary system of foam and SC with different
thresholds applied to distinguish the two materials. Notably, the transmission threshold of
the foam is lower than the one of SC, because of the density of electrons in the material.
This scales with the element number in the periodic table, therefore, hydrogen and carbon
and nitrogen present in the PU foam show small absorption of x-rays, whereas Si in the glass
fibre show high absorption of x-rays. The result is that the lower is the threshold, the higher
is the volume fraction of the resulting SC, since the densest spots of the foam are considered
as part of SC, as noticeable in Figure 27 (a). By lowering the threshold, less foam is included
in the calculation of the volume fraction. However, since SC is itself a composite of glass
fiber and polyester resin, which have different densities, the volume fraction of SC would
be underestimated. Therefore, it is not possible to make a precise estimate of the volume
fraction, but only a range of reasonable values. Nevertheless, the predicted volume fraction
of SC lays between the range of volume fractions found, meaning that the assumption made
for the calculation was reasonable and then usable for further calculations such as the ones
made in the analytical model.

Analytical and experimental bending stiffness
After the three-point bending test, the load-displacement curves for all of the 20 samples
tested was presented in Figure 32. As illustrated, the slope of the load-displacement curve
was expected to increase when the Vf of the shredded composite increased. However, apart

significant influence on the prediction of the behavior of the reinforced foam. However, since
the Timoshenko-Ehrenfest theory did not match with the experimental data, an analysis on
the shear modulus was made. This was done by back-calculating the shear modulus of the

10
Conclusions
In the current thesis, it was found that the density calculations for RF-0 and RF-13 were
consistent, providing a reliable basis for further investigations regarding the volume fraction
of shredded composite in the foam. Moreover, a lack of expansion of the foam during man-
ufacturing was proved after the comparison of the expected density and the experimental
one, and the adjusted results helped estimating the actual volume fraction of the shredded
composite, which revealed to be accurate and supported by the XCT analysis. The XCT re-
sults presented a 2D random orientation of the shredded composite within the foam, despite
occasional misaligned sticks. This confirmed the 2D random orientation assumed in the two
models chosen to predict the elastic modulus of the material.

Compressive stress-strain plots showed that the compressive elastic modulus of PU foam can
be increased of the 70%, from 1.7 MPa to 2.9 MPa, when adding 13 wt% of shredded wind
turbine blade material. This can be explained from the properties of the the constituents
of the shredded material described as sticks, plates. However, this improvement was much
greater than expected due to the misalignment of the sticks. Bending stiffness assessments
through three-point bending tests showed inconclusive results, with analytical theories either
overestimating or underestimating the stiffness. The reason of this was investigated with
an attempt to reconcile shear modulus through its back-calculation using the Timoshenko-
Ehrenfest beam theory, and the result highlighted discrepancies between the experimental
data coming from the three-point bending test and the analytical results, possibly influenced
by the unexpected non-linear behavior of the foam during testing. The observed non-linear
behavior is believed to have led to the non reliability of the analytical formulations used, em-
phasizing the need for a reevaluation of analytical models in future analyses.
Nevertheless, the thesis highlighted a promising outcome: the augmented stiffness of the
foam by adding shredded composite obtained from wind turbine blades, setting the stage
for a new research venture focusing on manufacturing and commercializing core panels rein-
forced with this shredded composite. With some new experiments it will be possible to prove
even more the validity of the idea developed in this thesis, improving the sustainability of the
decommissioned blades.
11
Future Work
To continue with the evaluation of how the shredded composite can improve the mechanical
properties of foams, it could be considered to use a similar but denser and stiffer foam to the
one considered in this thesis. An example could be the H200 AT from PU System®, the same
manufacturer of the H50 foam investigated in this thesis, and redo the mechanical tests, by
using a similar approach to the one presented by the student. The reason behind this advice
is the prevention of possible premature failure of the rigid structure of the foam, which was
believed to happen for the H50 foam, assuring a more consistent and stiff foam to test. More-
over, the H50 foam has an expansion volume of 20 times the liquid state, compared to the
5 times of the H200. This could become an advantage in case a higher quantity of shredded
composite is added to the foam in the liquid state, making the mixing phase less complex.
In addition, the use of different foams from Rigid Polyurethane ones, could be promising to
solve some of the manufacturing related challenges encountered, such as the lack of expan-
sion during manufacturing or the possible brittleness of the cell structure of the foam.
The future studies could also focus on the dispositions of the shredded composite within the
foam, that could be oriented at ±45°instead of longitudinally during bending, to maximize the
resulting shear modulus and the compressive stiffness of the core and enhance the perfor-
mance of the sandwich material produced.
