Abstract
In this thesis we investigate a generalized model for photon transport geometries. We
develop input-output relations considering an arbitrary number of chiral waveguides cou-
pled to an arbitrary number of mutually non-interacting quantum scatterers as well as
partially transmitting elements. With the model we reproduce the scattering properties of
two well-known geometries: A cavity coupled to a two-way 1D waveguide and a two level
emitter coupled to a two-way 1D waveguide. We also consider a geometry having both
a cavity and a two-level emitter coupled to the waveguide. For this geometry we ﬁnd an
analytical expression for the single photon transport properties.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Quantum information theory has become a ﬁeld of increasing interest in the last few
decades owing to the increasing need for faster computation and data transfer [1,2]. The
quantum key distribution protocol as well as quantum computational schemes, such as
Shor’s factoring algorithm and Grovers search algorithm, are examples of how quantum
physics can be used for secure and fast information sharing far superior to that of its
classical counterpart.
For practical purposes, such quantum devices should be small,
robust and scalable. A possible realization of such is by encoding information into the
quantized excitations of the electromagnetic ﬁeld, for example by controlling the spectrum
of propagating discrete energy packets known as photons [3]. These states are then coupled
into a waveguide and subsequently measured after undergoing controlled interactions.
Ideally, such a process can be used for making an eﬀective single photon switch and pave
the way towards an on-chip all-optical quantum computer.
We shall brieﬂy discuss a
possible candidate for a scalable waveguide as well as proposed quantum impurities to
manipulate the photon state inside the waveguide in a controlled way.
Photonic crystals
One way of conﬁning light is by using 2D photonic crystals [4]. A main advantage of these
structures lies in the fact that they are scalable and relatively easy to fabricate, and they
are therefore possible candidates for on-chip quantum network. A 2D photonic crystal
is a periodic structure of normally two materials with diﬀerent electric permittivity. By
varying the periodicity and material properties, it is possible to engineer a structure that
prohibits the propagation of certain electromagnetic waves. A local alteration of the pe-
riodicity results in regions where the presence of an electric ﬁeld is now allowed. We can
for example make a cavity in the structure shown in Fig. 1.1.1 by displacing or removing

Quantum dots
Photons are bosons, and as a consequence hereof they do not interact mutually, except
at very high energies, and even then the interaction is very weak [6].
To manipulate
the state of a photon, we require that the light interacts with matter. For applications
such as switching, we need this interaction to be non-linear. A two level system (TLS)
is an example of a non-linear operator and is also conceptually easy to understand. A
well-known example of such is the single atom owing to its discrete electronic states.
However, in order to engineer a scalable integrated quantum-optics platform, a more
promising interaction element is the semiconductor quantum dot (QD) [7]. Due to the
same discreteness in the electronic states, the semiconductor quantum dot is sometimes
referred to as an ”artiﬁcial atom” although it is made up of tens of thousands of atoms.

1.2
Project description and thesis outline
The purpose of this project is to develop a photon transport formalism suitable for con-
sidering few-photon transport through a waveguide that couples to more than one system.
We validate the model by comparing our ﬁndings for well-known geometries before at-
tempting to solve a two-system problem.
Chapter 1: We very brieﬂy summarize a few important elements of quantum physics
necessary to follow the derivations made in this project.
Chapter 2: We introduce the general geometry we wish to solve and write down the
corresponding Hamiltonian as well as discussing the assumptions made.
Chapter 3: We derive a set of equations describing the input-output relations for the
system described in Chapter 2.
Chapter 4: We test our input-output relations for a cavity-waveguide geometry. We
discuss the eﬀect of having a partially transmitting element and how to incorporate dissi-
pation.
Chapter 5: We calculate the exact dynamics for a single photon through a waveguide
coupled to a TLE. We then solve the steady state case using the S-matrix approach, for
both one and two photon transport.
Chapter 6: We apply our input-output equations to a the ”quantum Fano geometry”.
Chapter 7: We discuss our ﬁndings.

2.6
Discussion and summary
A waveguide with a side-coupled cavity and a PTE has been shown to generate a Fano line-
shape [9] in classical temporally coupled-mode theory and veriﬁed experimentally [23–25].
A Fano-lineshape is asymmetric (See Fig. 4.3.1), which in turn means that the spectral
width between high transmission output and low transmission output is smaller compared
to that of the Lorentzian lineshape. It is due to the Kerr-eﬀect that a strong input pulse
can alter the cavity resonance, and for this reason, such a geometry has been suggested
for an optical switch. In Chapter 4, we show that such a structure is unsuitable for a few-
photon switch because the cavity resonance cannot be altered due to the linear properties
of a quantum cavity. In Chapter 6, we investigate a similar geometry where the PTE has
been replaced with a TLE as to introduce a non-linear element. Because of the similarities
in the geometry, we refer to this structure as the quantum Fano geometry, which, to the
best of our knowledge, has not been investigated before.
The model described here assumes a closed system.
However, we show in Chapter 4
that we can model dissipation by introducing an extra chiral-waveguide that couples to
the system with a rate κ. In the remaining part of the thesis, we ignore dissipation which
is justiﬁed when κ is small compared to the other rates.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this thesis we have described a general system Hamiltonian for an arbitrary number
of waveguides and quantum impurities such as TLE and single-mode cavities. For this
model we have written down a set of input-output equations and used these to calculate
the transport properties for one and two photons through two well-known geometries.
We have shown how to model partially reﬂecting elements as well as how to account for
dissipation. Using the S-matrix formalism for our model we have calculated the single-
photon transport properties for the ”quantum Fano geometry” which shows a lineshape
around the TLE energy similar to that of the classical Fano geometry. We were not able
to calculate the two-photon S-matrix for this geometry but we have suggested a method
worth investigating.
