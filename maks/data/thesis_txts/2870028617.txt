Abstract
The integration of fiber reinforcement in 3D concrete printing (3DCP) presents a promis-
ing solution to improve mechanical performance, yet its effectiveness remains influenced
by fiber orientation and distribution. This study investigates the role of fiber alignment in
3D-printed and cast fiber-reinforced concrete (FRC), focusing on its impact on compres-
sive and flexural behavior. A comprehensive experimental program was conducted using
mechanical testing and X-ray computed tomography (CT) to analyze the fiber orientation,
distribution, and porosity of printed and cast specimens, with the resulting data correlated
to the mechanical test results.
The results show that in printed specimens, fibers tend to follow the print layers yet remain
predominantly perpendicular to the printing direction, leading to anisotropy and less effec-
tive crack bridging. In contrast, cast specimens exhibit a more uniform fiber distribution,
with fibers optimally aligned perpendicular to crack propagation, resulting in higher flex-
ural strength. However, even without fibers, cast specimens outperformed printed ones
in both compressive and flexural strength, highlighting that differences in material con-
solidation and interlayer bonding also play a significant role. CT scans further revealed
differences in porosity distribution, with printed specimens exhibiting larger, irregularly
shaped voids compared to the more uniformly distributed pores in cast specimens.
While fiber orientation was identified as a primary factor influencing mechanical behavior,
additional contributors—such as interlayer bonding, void distribution, and inconsistencies
in fiber dispersion—also affected the performance of printed concrete. The study high-
lights the potential for optimizing fiber orientation through modifications to the printing
process, particularly nozzle design, to enhance the structural viability of 3D-printed con-
crete. These findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the material behavior of
printed FRC.

Introduction
This chapter provides the basic background needed to understand the materials, pro-
cesses, and innovations discussed in the thesis. It begins with an overview of concrete
as a widely used construction material, then explores its evolution into fiber-reinforced
concrete, focusing on the role and importance of fibers in improving performance. Fur-
ther, the emerging field of 3D printed concrete is introduced and its features highlighted,
along with its benefits and challenges. Finally, the chapter outlines the thesis problem
and presents the proposed research questions and hypothesis.
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Concrete as a construction material
Concrete is undeniably one of the most prevalent construction materials in modern times,
valued for its versatility, durability, and cost-effectiveness. Its history dates back to ancient
civilizations, such as the Egyptians and Romans, where it enabled the construction of
durable structures like aqueducts and temples, many of which still stand today [1, 2].
The primary components of concrete include crushed stone, sand, a binder (typically ce-
ment), water, and various additives. Cement plays a pivotal role in concrete’s strength
and durability. The invention of Portland cement in the 19th century marked a significant
advancement, offering improved strength and consistency compared to its predecessors.
Modern concrete formulations combine Portland cement with carefully selected aggre-
gates to create materials that withstand structural and environmental stresses [1, 3].
Over the past five decades, advancements in material science have led to substantial im-
provements in concrete’s compressive strength. While 1960s concrete exhibited strengths
of 15–20 MPa, modern ultra-high-strength concrete achieves values up to 120 MPa, mak-
ing it a key material in constructing skyscrapers and other demanding structures. Despite
these improvements, concrete remains brittle and prone to cracking, necessitating the
use of reinforcement strategies such as steel rebars [1, 3].
1.1.2
Fiber-reinforced concrete
To address concrete’s inherent brittleness and susceptibility to cracking, fiber-reinforced
concrete (FRC) has emerged as an alternative to traditional steel reinforcement. By in-
corporating fibers into the concrete matrix, FRC enhances tensile strength, ductility, and
toughness while mitigating cracking and improving durability. Fibers bridge micro-cracks
that form under stress, redistributing loads and preventing crack propagation. This re-
sults in improved flexural strength, fracture toughness, and performance under thermal
and fatigue loads [4, 5].
Fibers used in FRC are commonly metallic, polymeric, or natural, and they can also vary
in length, aspect ratio and shape, like hooked end or straight ones. Steel fibers are partic-
ularly notable due to their high tensile strength (1100–2600 MPa), resistance to environ-
mental conditions, and cost-effectiveness. Their crack-bridging capability that can transfer
the load to the matrix by chemical bonding or mechanical anchorage into stable regions
significantly enhances concrete’s strain-hardening behavior and ductility. However, in-
corporating fibers can reduce the workability of fresh concrete, which can be mitigated by
supplementary chemical additives or cementitious materials such as fly ash. These bene-
fits make FRC a practical alternative in applications requiring rapid construction, complex
geometries, or enhanced durability [5, 6].

1.1.3
3D concrete printing
The construction industry’s increasing emphasis on efficiency, sustainability, and innova-
tion initiated an interest in automation and robotics. While automation is well-established
in other sectors, its application in construction has been very limited. However, the grow-
ing adoption of 3D concrete printing (3DCP) marks a significant step toward addressing
this gap [7].
3DCP is a digital fabrication technology that uses cement-based mortars deposited layer
by layer to create three-dimensional structures without the need for traditional formwork.
First introduced by Khoshnevis through the concept of Contour Crafting (CC), 3DCP em-
ploys robotic arms or gantry systems guided by computer numerical control (CNC) tech-
nology to construct precise and complex designs. This approach enables faster construc-
tion times, reduced labor requirements, and greater design flexibility, making it a promising
solution for both off-site prefabrication and on-site construction [3, 8].
Despite its advantages, 3DCP faces challenges in meeting conventional building stan-
dards and incorporating reinforcement. Fiber-reinforced cement-based materials are a
potential solution, as studies have shown that short fibers can enhance the mechanical
properties of 3D-printed structures. Continued research into 3D printable fiber-reinforced
concrete is essential for expanding the practical applications of this technology and achiev-
ing high-performance, durable structures [3, 8].
1.2
Problem statement
The incorporation of reinforcement in 3D-printed concrete structures presents a signif-
icant challenge due to the unique nature of the layer-by-layer extrusion process. Tra-
ditional steel reinforcement is difficult to integrate into 3D-printed designs, because the
manual positioning of steel bars increases labor costs and complicates the printing pro-
cess, which is precisely the opposite of what 3D printing aims to achieve. Additionally,
while advancements in high-strength concrete improve compressive strength, they fail to
properly address the need for tensile and flexural strength in 3D-printed concrete. These
limitations highlight the need for alternative reinforcement methods that can seamlessly
integrate into the 3D printing process [3, 8].
Use of fibers has emerged as a promising solution to these challenges, unlike steel bars,
they can be directly incorporated into the concrete matrix, reducing labor-intensive pro-
cesses and enabling compatibility with automated printing. However, the role of fiber
orientation in 3D printing remains insufficiently understood, especially compared to tradi-
tional casting methods, where fibers are typically distributed randomly [3, 8].
Fiber orientation is a critical factor influencing the mechanical performance of 3D-printed
concrete, particularly its anisotropic behavior. Research shows that fibers aligned with
the printing direction can enhance bending resistance due to their crack-bridging effect.
However, most studies focus on short fibers, like 6, 8 or 10 mm, leaving the behavior of
longer around 30 mm steel fibers under-explored. The effects of fiber length, orientation,
and interaction with the concrete matrix in 3D-printed structures remain poorly understood.
To maximize the benefits from steel fibers in 3D-printed concrete, a better understanding
of their orientation and influence on mechanical properties is necessary, especially for
longer fibers, which have been scarcely studied [3, 8]. Investigating these factors is key
to optimizing fiber integration in 3D printing and improving structural performance.

To address these challenges, the following Research Questions have been formulated:
1. How does the fiber orientation differ between 3DCP and cast fiber-reinforced con-
crete, and how does this affect the mechanical properties of the material?
(a) This question will focus on comparing the fiber alignment in both printed and
cast concrete using techniques like X-ray tomography and correlating it with
mechanical performance, such as residual tensile strength.
2. Can the mechanical performance differences between 3D printed and cast fiber-
reinforced concrete be primarily attributed to fiber orientation, or are other factors
such as print quality and layer adhesion just as significant?
(a) This question aims to explore the relative importance of fiber orientation versus
other factors using mechanical testing and microstructural analysis to deter-
mine their contributions to the overall performance of 3D printed concrete.
3. What is the impact of 3D printing on the compressive strength, density, and porosity
of fiber reinforced concrete compared to cast concrete?
(a) This question will investigate the physical properties of the printed and cast
concrete, focusing on whether the printing process introduces significant differ-
ences in density and porosity, and how these factors affect mechanical prop-
erties.
Based on the research questions a following Hypothesis has been formulated:
The mechanical performance differences between 3D printed and cast fiber-reinforced
concrete are primarily influenced by fiber orientation. When optimized in 3D printing, fiber
orientation could enhance tensile strength along the print direction.

2
Literature review
This chapter provides an overview of the key concepts and previous research relevant
to fiber-reinforced concrete (FRC) and 3D concrete printing (3DCP), focusing on fiber
behavior, crack formation, mechanical performance, and reinforcement challenges. The
discussion begins with the fundamental principles of FRC, covering fiber types, distri-
bution, and orientation, and their influence on mechanical properties. The chapter then
examines the implications of fiber reinforcement in 3DCP, addressing manufacturing con-
straints, anisotropic behavior, and flow-induced fiber alignment. Additionally, the chapter
explores concrete fracture mechanics, introducing the fictitious crack model and hinge
model to describe the transition from linear to nonlinear behavior and the role of fiber
bridging in crack propagation. Finally, X-ray computed tomography (CT) is discussed as
a method for analyzing fiber orientation and porosity, providing insights into the structural
performance of both cast and 3D-printed FRC.
2.1
Fiber Reinforced Concrete
FRC is a composite material that improves concrete’s mechanical properties by integrat-
ing fibers into the cementitious matrix. The inclusion of fibers influences initiation of cracks
and their propagation by bridging cracks with the fibers and redistributing stress across
the matrix. These fibers act as micro-reinforcements that improve tensile strength, ductil-
ity, toughness, and resistance to cracking. The fundamental mechanisms governing FRC
performance revolve around fiber-matrix interactions, which influence how the material
responds under stress [9, 10, 11].
Although FRC has its advantages, it also comes with several drawbacks, such as - it
is generally more expensive than conventional concrete, and achieving the necessary
flowability can be challenging. There’s also a risk of segregation between the aggregates
and fibers during mixing. Additionally, the low yield stress of the mix means that the
formwork needs to withstand higher pressure. Lastly, steel fibers do not match the tensile
load-carrying capacity of traditional reinforcement [11].
2.1.1
Fiber Diversity and Properties
The range of fibers used in concrete is highly diverse, with varying mechanical and geo-
metrical properties that significantly influence toughness and overall performance [10].
For example, cellulose and micro-synthetic fibers (typically made from polypropylene,
polyethylene, polyester, or nylon) are often used to control plastic shrinkage cracking.
Glass fibers are used for architectural applications, while macro-synthetic fibers—such
as polypropylene blends—offer a viable alternative to steel fibers. Natural fibers (e.g., co-
conut, sisal, jute, sugarcane) are also used, particularly in non-commercial applications.
Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) fibers can improve both flexural and compressive performance,
and specialty fibers cover newly developed materials that do not fall into the other cat-
egories. Steel fibers, which are typically crimped or hooked to improve anchorage, are
widely used to enhance toughness and provide improved post-crack load-carrying capac-
ity [12]. Aramid fibers provide high strength and improved resistance to elevated temper-
atures (suitable for extreme conditions). Carbon fibers, due to their high stiffness and low
density, can significantly improve tensile properties but are often limited by high costs.
Basalt fibers, which offer good mechanical properties and superior heat resistance, can
be a potential alternative for fire-resistant applications. Hybrid fiber systems, where dif-
ferent fiber types such as steel and polypropylene or steel and PVA are combined, have

been explored to improve both toughness and durability by leveraging the strengths of
multiple materials [4].
For fibers to be effective in reinforcing concrete, they need to meet several key criteria:
• A tensile strength significantly higher than the matrix, ideally two to three orders of
magnitude;
• A bond strength with the matrix that is at least equal to, or preferably higher than,
the tensile strength of the matrix;
• An elastic modulus in tension that is at least three times higher than that of the
matrix;
• Sufficient ductility to prevent the fiber from fracturing due to abrasion or bending;
• A Poisson’s ratio and coefficient of thermal expansion that are similar to those of the
matrix, to reduce compatibility issues[10].
For steel fibers, several key factors typically control their performance: the aspect ratio,
fiber shape, volume fraction, fiber orientation, fiber-matrix bond and surface deformation

Crack Formation and Fiber Bridging
The performance of FRC under loading can be divided into three key phases: pre-cracking,
post-cracking, and ultimate failure. In the pre-cracking phase, the matrix primarily resists
the applied stress, but some of the load is transferred to the fibers due to their integra-
tion within the matrix. This load transfer slightly increases the composite’s overall elastic
modulus compared to the matrix alone, even though the fibers have not yet fully engaged
in load-bearing [4, 10].

Fiber Bridging Mechanisms
As previously mentioned, FRC exhibits a different fracture process than plain concrete.
The process is rather complex and influenced by various factors, and when a crack is
approaching the fiber the possible mechanisms are:
• Matrix fracture and possible spalling or fragmentation around the crack.
• Debonding at the fiber–matrix interface as the crack is approaching (Cook-Gordon
effect).
• Frictional pull-out of the fiber after debonding, where the fiber slides against the
matrix.
• Fiber fracture especially if the applied load exceeds the fiber’s tensile strength.
• Fiber abrasion and potential plastic deformation of the fiber during pull-out.

2.2
3D Concrete Printing
Over the last two decades, 3DCP has transformed into a noteworthy digital fabrication
technology within the concrete and construction industries. Its rapid adoption is across a
diverse range of applications, from off-site production of prefabricated panels to on-site
construction, reflecting its growing acceptance across the various sectors [15]. This rapid
evolution and integration of 3DCP not only enhances production efficiency but also opens
new opportunities in sustainable and innovative construction practice [3].
2.2.1
Mechanical Performance: 3D Printed vs. Cast Concrete
Studies indicate that 3D printed concrete typically exhibits compressive strengths that are
10–25% lower than those of cast concrete due to the absence of formwork pressure and
the presence of interlayer weaknesses [16]. However, incorporating rigid fibers—such as
steel or basalt—can bridge interlayer micro-gaps and reduce crack propagation, enabling
printed elements to approach the compressive performance of cast specimens [17].
In terms of flexural behavior, plain printed concrete tends to be more brittle because cracks
preferentially propagate along layer interfaces. The introduction of short fibers at dosages
of 0.5–1% improves flexural capacity by effectively bridging cracks across layers, thereby
mitigating brittleness and enhancing post-crack ductility [18, 19]. Similarly, tensile splitting
strength is substantially increased—up to 26%—with fiber reinforcement at around 2% by
volume, which also strengthens interlayer bonding [18, 16].
Moreover, fiber reinforcement improves durability by reducing shrinkage-induced cracking
and minimizing moisture ingress, thus enhancing resistance to environmental challenges
such as freeze–thaw cycles and permeability issues [3]. Overall, these improvements
suggest that, with appropriate fiber incorporation, 3D printed concrete can achieve me-
chanical performance and durability standards comparable to those of conventionally cast
concrete.

2.2.3

Producing fiber-reinforced structures using 3DCP presents several challenges. One ma-

on the printing direction, with elements typically exhibiting higher strength in the printing

the inherent weakness at layer interfaces and the preferential alignment of fibers with the
flow direction. Strategies such as optimizing print patterns—using cross-hatching or al-

a more quasi-isotropic internal structure, thereby enhancing out-of-plane strength [23].

a fresh layer is deposited on a partially set one, leading to weak interfaces and poten-

adjustments, surface moisture retention, and the application of bonding agents. Innova-

2.3
Fiber Orientation
Fiber orientation plays a crucial role in determining the mechanical performance of fiber-
reinforced concrete (FRC). In cast concrete, fibers are often randomly oriented, which
provides multi-directional reinforcement. However, flow-induced effects during mixing or
placement can sometimes lead to favorable or unfavorable fiber alignment, resulting in
anisotropic mechanical behavior [4].
In contrast to traditional reinforcing bars that offer predictable strength along defined axes,
fibers are dispersed throughout the concrete, creating a discontinuous reinforcement sys-
tem. The fiber orientation factor, which quantifies how well fibers align with the principal
stress direction, is a key parameter in assessing this reinforcement efficiency. The over-
all effectiveness of fiber reinforcement in FRC depends also on their distribution within
the matrix [6]. Studies have demonstrated that while aligned fibers are more effective in
mitigating crack propagation, randomly oriented fibers can lead to variable, anisotropic
behavior [25].
2.3.1
Flow Impact on Fiber Orientation
During concrete manufacturing, particularly in processes like 3D concrete printing (3DCP),
the flow dynamics of the mixture significantly influence fiber alignment. In confined shear
flows—common in narrow, controlled channels—the fibers tend to align along the direction
of the flow. This effect is pronounced in 3DCP, where the extrusion process induces a
continuous, directional flow that typically results in fibers being oriented parallel to the
print layers, and in some cases, perpendicular to the printing direction. Variations in the
flow path, such as converging or diverging channels, alter the local shear stress, thereby
modifying the degree of fiber alignment, as illustrated in Figure 2.5 [26].

2.3.3
Fiber Orientation Analysis
Traditionally, many studies have simplified fiber orientation by reporting a single mean
fiber angle. First, slices perpendicular to the specimen’s longitudinal axis are cut, ap-
proximately half a fiber’s length from the failure plane. The slices are polished, painted
to enhance contrast, and scanned at 2400 dpi. An image analysis program then detects
fiber outlines using morphological filtering and global thresholding, and applies a Hough
transform to identify elliptical cross-sections. From these, the fiber angle (Θ) is calculated
relative to the surface, with 0° (orientation factor α = cos · Θ = 1) indicating fibers per-
pendicular to the failure plane. This method achieves an accuracy of about ±2° on the
orientation [28, 25].
Laranjeira et al. [13] proposed that fiber orientations could be modeled as a Gaussian
(normal) distribution, assuming that fibers are symmetrically spread around a central align-
ment. Yet, experimental data suggests that this might not always be the best fit; in some
cases, particularly at lower fiber angles, a log-normal distribution appears to describe the
data more accurately because Gaussian models tend to overestimate the concentration
of fibers at these angles [25].
Another method to analyze fiber orientation is the use of spherical histograms. In this
approach, each fiber is first assigned an orientation vector that is converted into spherical
coordinates (with the azimuth angle, Φ, in the XY plane and the inclination angle, Θ,
relative to the Z-axis). The angles are then divided into equal bins (using increments
such as 10°, 15°, or 30°), and the number of fibers falling into each bin is counted. The
result is a visual histogram where each “cylinder” on a sphere represents the frequency
of fibers in that orientation range [29]. Although this method provides an intuitive picture
of fiber alignment and can show whether fibers are preferentially aligned or randomly
dispersed, it does depend on the chosen bin size and might lose some precision when
trying to capture a continuous distribution [29].
2.4
Concrete Fracture Mechanics (Non-linear)
Concrete exhibits a very large fracture process zone (FPZ) due to its heterogeneous
composition—characterized by distributed microcracking, aggregate bridging, and crack
branching—which results in significant nonlinear deformations both before and during
crack propagation.
A key concept in non-linear fracture mechanics is the stress–crack opening relationship,
which is governed by a cohesive law. This relationship, often depicted in normalized
form, as can be seen in Figure 2.6, shows that once a crack initiates—typically when the
maximum principal stress reaches the material’s tensile strength— the cohesive stresses
acting over the FPZ decrease with increasing crack opening. The general idea is that
cracking starts when the maximum principal stress reaches the material’s tensile strength,
and as the crack opens, a distribution of cohesive (or closing) stresses continues to trans-
fer load across the crack faces until a critical opening is reached and stress transfer is lost
[30].

2.4.1
Fictitious Crack Model
The fictitious crack model is a key concept in understanding the tensile behavior of con-
crete. Originally proposed by Hillerborg et al. (1976) for plain concrete, the model cap-

where a “fictitious” crack forms instead of a complete loss of load-bearing capacity [32,
30, 33]. The fictitious crack model is termed “fictitious” because it does not represent a
single, discrete crack but rather an idealized continuous distribution of microcracking and
bridging effects [30].

2.5
X-ray computed tomography
X-ray Computed Tomography (CT) is a non-destructive imaging technique originally de-
veloped for medical imaging in the early 1970s. The advancement of industrial X-ray CT in
the 1990s, with the development of high-resolution X-ray sources and detectors, enabled
detailed imaging of materials at the microscale [35]. Nowadays, CT is used to reconstruct
a three-dimensional representation of an object based on its differential attenuation of X-
rays. In concrete research, CT provides detailed insights into the microstructure, enabling
the segmentation of various phases (aggregates, cement matrix, fibers, and pores) and
facilitating quantitative analysis of fiber orientation and porosity [36].
2.5.1
X-Ray Generation and Fundamentals
X-rays are a form of high-energy electromagnetic radiation capable of penetrating dense
materials. In a typical X-ray tube, electrons are accelerated by a high voltage and subse-
quently collide with a metal target [37]. This collision generates two main types of radiation
mechanisms:
• Bremsstrahlung Radiation: This ”braking radiation” occurs when electrons are de-
celerated upon interacting with the target material, resulting in the emission of a
continuous spectrum of X-ray energies.
• Characteristic Radiation: This radiation arises when electrons from higher energy
levels fill vacancies in the inner electron shells of the target atoms, emitting X-rays
at discrete energies specific to the atomic structure of the target material.[37].
After production, X-rays propagate toward the specimen. During their passage through
the material, the intensity of the X-ray beam diminishes due to three principal processes:
• Absorption: Denser materials or those with higher atomic numbers absorb more
X-rays;
• Scattering: Some photons deviate from their original paths, contributing to image
noise;
• Transmission: A fraction of the beam passes through without interaction [37, 35].
2.5.2
CT Imaging and Reconstruction
A typical laboratory-based X-ray CT system consists of three main components:
• X-ray Source – Generates a cone beam of X-rays that passes through the sample;
• Rotation Stage – Holds and rotates the sample to capture projections at multiple
angles;
• Detector – Captures transmitted X-rays and converts them into digital projection
images [35] .
During scanning, the sample is positioned on the rotation stage, and a cone beam of X-
rays is projected onto it. The X-rays are attenuated (absorbed or scattered) based on the
material’s density and atomic composition, producing 2D radiographic images on the de-
tector. The sample is typically rotated through a full 360-degree cycle, with 500 to 4000
individual projections recorded at different angles. These projections are then recon-
structed into a 3D volumetric dataset using mathematical algorithms, most commonly the
Feldkamp back-projection algorithm [35, 36], see the schematic illustration of the whole
process in Figure 2.10.

2.5.3
X-ray Energy, Power, Voxel size
The energy and power settings of the X-ray source are critical in determining image quality
and penetration depth. The voltage, measured in kilovolts (kV), controls the energy of the
X-ray photons and influences the material penetration. For dense materials like concrete,
higher voltages (e.g., 150–250 kV) are required to penetrate the specimen effectively.
The beam current, measured in microamperes (µA), regulates the number of emitted X-
ray photons and is adjusted to optimize contrast and minimize noise [35, 38].
Another important parameter is beam filtration, which involves placing a metal filter (e.g.,
tin, copper, or aluminum) between the X-ray source and the sample to eliminate lower-
energy X-rays. These lower-energy X-rays contribute to a phenomenon known as beam
hardening, where the center of the sample appears darker than its edges due to increased
attenuation of softer X-rays [38].
The resolution of an X-ray CT scan is determined by the voxel size, which is a 3D repre-
sentation of a pixel and is influenced by detector pixel size, sample-to-detector distance,
and scan magnification. Typical voxel sizes in concrete imaging range from 10 µm to 200
µm, with finer resolutions required for detailed analysis of fiber orientation and porosity
[27]. The binning setting, which groups neighboring pixels to improve the signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR), can be adjusted for different scanning needs:
• 1×1 binning (high resolution, more noise) – Each detector pixel is read individually,
providing finer spatial detail at the cost of increased noise.
• 2×2 binning (lower resolution, less noise) – Four neighboring pixels are combined
into one, improving image clarity but reducing resolution [39].

2.5.4
X-ray CT Segmentation of Concrete Phases
To distinguish phases like aggregates, fibers, pores, and cement matrix from X-ray CT
images of concrete, post processing is necessary. A common approach is image seg-
mentation using gray-scale thresholding, which uses X-ray attenuation differences due
to material density. For example, voids appear as dark regions and can be segmented
by selecting a grayscale cutoff that separates pore space from solid material. Once seg-
mented, quantitative analysis can be done to obtain pore metrics such as volume, surface
area etc. Threshold-based segmentation is also used to isolate high-density inclusions
like steel fibers, which appear very bright due to strong X-ray absorption [40]. Manual or
global thresholding is the simplest segmentation method, but it can be error-prone in het-
erogeneous concrete. Minor density contrasts between phases (e.g. aggregate vs. hy-
drated paste) or imaging noise can cause overlapping grayscale ranges. Consequently,
a single threshold may incorrectly merge or split phases [40].
Researchers have developed advanced segmentation techniques to improve accuracy.
For instance, smoothing filters (Gaussian blur, anisotropic diffusion) can be applied to re-
duce noise while preserving edges, or to separate touching objects watershed segmen-
tation can be used [40]. Machine learning approaches have also been explored, training
classifiers on voxel intensity and texture to recognize phases (matrix, fiber, void) more
reliably than simple thresholds or supervised learning aim to minimize human bias [35].
2.5.5
Fiber Orientation Analysis from CT Scans
There are two practical approaches to extract fiber orientations from CT data. In the first
method, each fiber is segmented as a separate object, and its orientation is determined
by fitting a line or calculating the principal inertia axis of its 3D shape [41]. This gives
a detailed dataset of individual fiber orientations, which can be statistically summarized
(for example, as distributions of inclination angles or as an average orientation tensor).
However, this method requires high resolution and effective separation of fibers, which
can be challenging when fibers overlap [42].
The second method evaluates the collective orientation of the fiber phase. A common
stereological approach uses a structure tensor or Mean Intercept Length (MIL) analysis
on the binarized fiber phase. In this method, a virtual grid of lines is cast through the 3D
volume, and the number of intersections with fibers is measured in different directions.
These counts are then used to compute an orientation tensor that represents the dominant
fiber alignment in the sample.It is especially useful when fibers are too numerous or too
closely spaced to isolate individually [41].
Orientation results are typically visualized using spherical plots, heatmaps, or orthographic
projection histograms, where fiber orientations are mapped onto a sphere with color in-
tensity indicating frequency [43]. Although steel fibers with hooked ends may slightly
broaden the measured orientation spread, this effect is minor compared to the overall
fiber length. Modern approaches leverage the full 3D data from CT scans to compute ori-
entation distributions or tensors that describe fiber alignment in space, as further detailed
in Section 3.3.4 [43].

3.1
Experimental Setup and Testing Procedures
To investigate how printing influences fiber orientation and its following effects on the
mechanical and micro-structural properties of concrete, specimens were prepared using
two different approaches: 3D printing and casting. This approach allows for a further
comparison between the cast and printed samples and understanding the differences. To
further comprehend the role of fibers, specimens were produced both with and without
steel fibers, isolating the influence of fiber presence from the effects of the printing.
The concrete mixture that was used for both casting and printing was provided by an
external company, Holcim. Although the exact composition of the mixture is proprietary
and therefore undisclosed, it is known that a high-strength concrete mix of grade C70
was used, specifically designed to be suitable for 3D concrete printing. The concrete
mix contained 0.8% steel fibers by volume of concrete, they were hooked-end, 30 mm
long and 0.38 mm in diameter, with a round cross-section and bundled together using
water-soluble glue for ease of mixing. Detailed specifications of the fibers can be found
in Appendix A.
3.1.1
Sample Preparation: Printing and Casting procedures
The 3D printing process was carried out by COBOD using their gantry-type printer, BOD2,
and a Mini Batch plant for material preparation. Figures 3.1a and 3.1b show the printing
process and the mixing setup.
The printing process took place over a couple of days to produce a sufficient number of
specimens, both fiber-reinforced and non-reinforced. Four walls were printed in each ses-
sion - straight with turns at both ends (can be seen in Figure 3.1a), each with a thickness
of approx 23 cm and a height of 75 cm. The 3D printing nozzle, measuring 200 mm in
width and 50 mm in thickness, created approximately 50 mm high layers.
The printed walls were produced layer by layer, adjustments to the process—such as
modifying nozzle speed and material flow rate—were made based on the observed be-
havior of the mixture to achieve optimal results. The COBOD configuration software con-
trolled the printing parameters, with a COBOD technician supervising the process.

3.1.2
Testing Procedures
To prepare for testing, the printed specimens were cut into appropriate sizes. For the
flexural strength tests, samples were cut to dimensions of 150 × 150 × 600 mm. For the
compressive strength tests, specimens were drilled into cylinders measuring 200 × 100
mm. The cylinders were oriented such that the layers of the printed material were aligned
perpendicular to the loading direction, ensuring that the applied compressive load directly
tested the interlayer bonds formed during printing. This careful preparation guaranteed
consistency across all specimens—both printed and cast—and in compliance with the
standardized testing procedures.
The cutting of the specimens was performed partly by DTU and partly by an external
company, while all tests were conducted at DTU concrete lab in Building 119.

Porosity Measurement test
The porosity of the concrete specimens was measured in accordance with the Danish
standard LBM Prøvemetode 2, which provides guidelines for determining the open poros-
ity of hardened concrete. This test method assesses the volume of voids within the con-
crete matrix that are accessible to water, providing insights into the material’s density,
durability, and potential permeability.
For the test, small specimens were prepared, cut out from larger samples to ensure con-
sistency and representative results, and weighted. The specimens were dried to a con-
stant mass in a ventilated oven at a temperature of 105 ± 5 °C. After drying, the mass of
each specimen was recorded as the dry weight.
Next, the specimens were immersed in water under vacuum conditions to ensure all ac-
cessible pores were saturated. The saturated mass of each specimen was then recorded.
Finally, the specimens were weighed while fully submerged in water to determine the
buoyant weight.

3.2.2
X-ray CT Scanning Parameters and Settings
The scanner settings were optimized to balance image clarity and scanning efficiency. For
the general scans, the X-ray energy was set to 200 kV , with a beam current of 500 µA and
a power output of 100 W. The exposure time was 1.00 s (equivalent to 1.00 fps), with a gain
of 18, using 2 × 2 binning, meaning that the pixels were grouped—four neighboring pixels
were merged into one. This binning approach improves the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
at the cost of reduced spatial resolution [39]. The effective pixel size of the reconstructed
images was 169.99 µm, providing sufficient resolution to capture the fiber orientation and
general porosity within the specimens. Shading correction was performed using a tin
0.5 mm filter. Four shading images were captured for correction, and reference images
were averaged over 256 frames to enhance accuracy and reduce noise.
Whereas for the higher-resolution scans, the X-ray energy remained at 200 kV and the
beam current at 500 µA, but the power output was reduced to 40 W. The exposure time

3.3
Post-Processing of CT Data
For post processing the reconstruction of 2D CT scans into a 3D volumetric file a program
called Metris CT Pro was used. Then the reconstructed 3D dataset was imported into the
Amira-Avizo software (year 2021.2) for segmentation and further analysis. From which
the obtained data was further analyzed using MATLAB and MS Excel.
3.3.1
3D Reconstruction from CT Scans
The reconstruction into a 3D volumetric file using the Metris CT Pro involves several
key steps. First, the 2D images were aligned using dual slice selection based on the
specimen’s rotation angle, ensuring consistency and minimizing artifacts. Corrections for
imaging issues—such as beam hardening and noise— were then applied, and advanced
back-projection algorithms transformed the attenuation data from the 2D radiographs into
a 3D dataset [50].
Normalization was then performed to standardize the gray-scale values, which reflect
the material’s X-ray absorption coefficients, while calibration guarantees that the spatial
dimensions of the 3D file match those of the physical specimen. The resulting 3D volu-
metric representation allowed for detailed visualization of internal features, such as fiber
orientation and porosity distribution, and supported further quantitative analysis [50].

3.3.3
Fiber Detection and Tracing
A fiber tracing tool in Amira-Avizo was used to detect the fibers, to be then able to analyze
the orientation and distribution within the concrete matrix. The tool works by detecting
elongated features within the segmented dataset, using algorithms that follow the shape
and continuity of fibers based on their gray-scale intensity and spatial arrangement. This
method allows for the identification of individual fibers, even in densely packed regions,
and provides detailed information about their orientation, length, curvature, and spatial
distribution [51, 52].
The fiber tracing process involved two key functions in Amira-Avizo: the Cylinder Cor-
relation and Trace Correlation Lines modules. Fibers were identified either directly from
the reconstructed 3D images or from the segmented models. The Cylinder Correlation
module computed the normalized cross-correlation of an image with a cylinder, allowing
for improved detection of fiber-like structures[52]. The visual cylinder function was used
to define the initial reference fiber in a 2D image, optimizing the settings based on experi-
mental trials to achieve the best results. The key settings that ensured an optimal balance
between detecting fibers accurately while minimizing false positives were:
• Angular sampling: 5 degrees
• Outer cylinder radius: 0.38 mm (fiber diameter)
• Mask cylinder radius: 0.5 mm
• Inner cylinder radius: 0 mm (solid cylinder)
Once the fibers were detected, the Trace Correlation Lines module was used to analyze
their orientation and connectivity. The key settings for this module were:
• Minimum seed correlation: 115
• Minimum continuation quality: 85
• Direction coefficient: 0.1
• Minimum distance: 0.7 mm
• Minimum fiber length: 8 mm
• Search cone length: 5 mm
• Search cone angle: 30 degrees
• Minimum step size: 10
These parameters were refined through iterative testing to ensure fibers were detected
with sufficient accuracy, meaning they were neither too short nor too long, nor fragmented.
A fiber was only counted if more than half of its length was detected, improving the preci-
sion of the fiber count.

3.3.4
Fiber Orientation Quantification
This study assesses fiber orientation using the principal eigenvector of the second-order
orientation tensor. A method introduced by Advani and Tucker (1987), where the diagonal
components of this tensor (axx, ayy, azz) represent the proportions of fibers oriented along
the principal axes and sum to 1. From the orientation tensor, then can the preferred orien-
tation direction (eigenvectors) and degree of anisotropy (eigenvalues spread) be derived
[42]. Unlike direct angle-based methods, the tensor approach captures fiber alignment in
all spatial directions and overcomes the limitations of 2D cross-sectional analysis, provid-
ing a more comprehensive understanding of fiber orientation.
The orientation tensor components (e.g., Txx, Tyy, Tzz, Txy, Txz, and Tyz) are provided
directly by Amira-Avizo program, which can be extracted as an XML file. Since the fibers
are undirected, meaning that a fiber oriented in one direction is physically equivalent to one
oriented in the opposite direction - the sign of each component is indeterminate. To avoid
cancellation effects when averaging, the absolute values of these tensor components are
used. This approach provides a clear measure of the magnitude of fiber orientation along
each axis, regardless of the sign.

5
Discussion
The primary objective of this research was to investigate how the fiber orientation in 3D-
printed fiber-reinforced concrete (FRC) compares to traditionally cast FRC and how this
difference impacts mechanical performance, particularly in terms of compressive and flex-
ural strength. This study specifically aimed to answer the following questions:
• How does fiber orientation differ between 3D-printed and cast FRC, and what impact
does this have on mechanical performance?
• Can the mechanical performance differences be primarily attributed to fiber orienta-
tion, or do other factors like porosity and interlayer bonding play a significant role?
• How does the 3D printing process affect the porosity, density, and internal structure
compared to cast specimens?
Key findings from the study include:
• Fiber Orientation: X-ray CT scans showed that fibers in printed specimens tended
to align along the print layers but perpendicular to the printing direction, leading
to anisotropic and generally unfavorable orientations. In contrast, cast specimens
exhibited a more favorable anisotropic orientation, with fibers better aligned perpen-
dicular to potential crack planes, resulting in higher and more uniform mechanical
performance.
• Fiber Distribution: Fiber distribution in printed specimens varied along both the
width and length of the print layers, leading to inconsistent fiber orientation values.
In contrast, cast specimens showed more uniform fiber distribution within individual
samples, though greater variation was observed between different cast specimens.
• Compressive Strength: The compressive tests demonstrated that cast specimens
consistently exhibited higher compressive strength than printed ones—both with and
without fibers. Adding fibers to the mix improved the compressive strength in both
cases.
• Flexural Performance: Cast specimens demonstrated better pre-cracking flexural
strength, 16% higher for fiber-reinforced samples and 36% for non-fibered speci-
mens compared to the printed specimens. Post-cracking behavior in cast FRC was
about twice as strong as in printed FRC, correlating with the higher fiber orientation
factors (FO) observed in cast samples. Without fibers, printed specimens exhibited
better post-cracking behavior and showed a more ductile failure mode.
• Crack Propagation: Cracks in printed specimens generally propagated in straighter
lines, because fibers were primarily aligned along the crack path and did not effec-
tively bridge across it. In contrast, cast specimens exhibited more irregular, branch-
ing cracks that deviated around fibers that were aligned along the length.
• Porosity and Voids: CT scans revealed that printed specimens contained larger,
irregularly shaped voids, while cast specimens exhibited smaller, more uniformly
spherical pores. Although total porosity values were similar (21% for printed and
22% for cast), the distribution and shape of the voids had a noticeable impact on
mechanical performance.

5.1
Interpretation of Findings and Comparison with Literature
5.1.1
Compressive Strength
Printed specimens exhibit lower compressive strength than cast specimens, both with (by
11 MPa or 19%) and without (by 22 MPa or 22%) fibers, primarily due to the layer-by-layer
printing process. The inter-layer interfaces act as planes of weakness because only the
self-weight of the concrete is applied during printing, and there are no molds to provide
some compaction [55, 16]. Also uneven curing and the loss of surface moisture after
extrusion result in drier, weaker bonds and less uniform hydration, while the extrusion
process itself introduces anisotropy and entrapped air, leading to larger, irregular voids
(see Figure 4.16a and Figure 4.16b) [56, 55, 57], although the results showed the overall
porosity to be minimally lower than in the cast specimens, as also found in literature [27].
In contrast, cast specimens benefit from continuous material placement and effective vi-
bration compaction, which produce a denser and more homogeneous microstructure and,
hence, higher compressive strength [55]. Although, cast specimens sometimes show
greater variability—likely due to minor inconsistencies in casting, vibration, or localized
mix differences. X-ray scans reveal that their voids are generally more uniform and round,
nevertheless, some larger voids stay [55], and some may form due to the merging of
smaller pores during vibration (improper), contributing to the observed variability.
The incorporation of fibers in both cast and printed specimens clearly enhances compres-
sive strength. It is achieved by providing additional reinforcement through crack bridging,
improved stress redistribution, and acting as internal confinement, which aligns with the
literature [27, 10, 4]. In printed specimens, the relative improvement was slightly higher
(14%) compared to cast specimens (11%), although the absolute strength increase was
the same—7 MPa for both. It is possible that fibers mitigate some of the weaknesses as-
sociated with inter-layer bonding in printed specimens, as a small number of fibers were
observed to cross the inter-layer regions (see Section 4.2.1, Figure 4.4a, and Figure 4.7a),
likely enhanced by the corrugated print layer surface. While the concrete matrix remains
inherently stronger in cast specimens, the similar magnitude of improvement from fiber
addition is consistent with both types exhibiting an overall fiber orientation in the XY plane.
5.1.2
Flexural Strength
Cast specimens
Cast specimens withstand higher flexural stresses both pre-cracking and post-cracking.
Their higher compressive strength contributes to elevated pre-crack flexural strength [58].
Moreover, the fiber orientation in cast specimens is generally favorable, with fibers aligned
perpendicular to the crack. This alignment facilitates efficient crack bridging with minimal
fiber slip (typically less than 0.1 mm), allowing for immediate engagement in crack bridging
and resulting in a sharp load–displacement peak [10]. Although some literature reports
a random or slightly aligned fiber distribution [57, 13, 43, 4], the results show a clear
alignment along the mold’s length—likely due to using in the casting process the same
hose as for printing and also an enhanced wall effect from longer fibers.
For cast specimens, the standard deviation was lower for pre-cracking strength but higher
for post-cracking behavior, indicating greater absolute variability despite a lower CoV due
to higher overall strengths. This variability aligns with fiber orientation data, where cast
specimens exhibit favorable but less consistent alignment, likely due to minor casting
variations that influence test results.
Printed specimens
Printed specimens display a more inclined fiber orientation, requiring additional slip before

fibers fully engage in load transfer—a phenomenon known as the ”inclined bridging effect”.
This results in an initial drop in load response followed by a gradual recovery [10].
Printed specimens generally show that better fiber alignment correlates with improved
flexural performance; however, this relationship is less clear than in cast specimens. For
instance, while specimens B03 and B04 roughly follow the expected trend, specimen
B06 deviates by exhibiting the highest fiber orientation and pre-cracking strength but a
sudden drop in load capacity after cracking. Figures 4.11a and 4.11b reveal that B06 has

and well-aligned near the start of the crack, but their number drops noticeably later along
the crack path. This uneven distribution likely limits consistent crack bridging and load
transfer, explaining the abrupt post-cracking strength loss. Such observations align with
previous studies reporting the influence of fiber bundles near the hatch [59] and variability
among nominally identical samples [60].

5.1.3
Fiber Orientation: Models and Experimental Observations
FO is crucial to the cohesive behavior of FRC, with many models suggesting that a fiber’s
bridging capacity is proportional to its effective FO. For example, Martinelli et al. (2021)

==PAGE:59==
Hinge model based on Olesen’s (2001) model includes an orientation factor that treats
fibers as if they reorient optimally to a near-perpendicular position upon crack opening,
thus maximizing load transfer [33, 61]. Laranjeira et al. (2012) further provide a frame-
work showing that the number and alignment of fibers crossing a crack can be quantified,
with improved alignment directly enhancing bridging capacity [62]. Moreover, CT-based
studies (Lorente et al. (2022)) and experimental findings (Enfedaque et al. (2018)) con-
firm that regions with more favorably aligned fibers exhibit higher residual strengths, even
when local fiber distributions vary [63, 64]. The experimental results in this study mirror
these insights: cast specimens with a higher effective FO show increased pre-cracking
strength and superior post-cracking performance due to enhanced crack bridging.
The difference between the overall fiber orientation and the orientation within the crack
region for printed specimens could indicate at local non-uniformities in the printed layers
( Figures 4.7a and 4.7b). Additionally, the process of crack opening may further influ-
ence fiber orientation, causing them to rotate or shift, which results in a more horizontally
inclined appearance—or at least altering their detected cylindrical representation. This
observation is supported by Gonzalez et al. (2024), who found that fibers crossing the
crack tend to align favorably, indicating reorientation as the crack opens [65]. Conse-
quently, the overall FO of the specimen for comparisons with cast specimens was used.
An interesting observation is that fiber alignment varied across the width of the printed
specimens (refer to Figure 4.7). This variation may result from several factors. For ex-
ample, the beams might not have been cut exactly from the center of the wall as was
instructed, and samples taken closer to the sides could show different alignment patterns.
Alternatively, the printing process itself—especially the turns at the ends of the walls, as
illustrated in Figure 5.1a—might affect fiber alignment across the width. It is also possible
that nozzle congestion on one side contributed to the observed differences.
Fiber alignment in the printed specimens - in the plane of the printed layers but predom-
inantly perpendicular to the printing direction, could be attributed to the design of the
printing nozzle used in this study, shown in Figure 5.1a. The nozzle’s geometry promotes
a diverging flow pattern leading fibers to orient perpendicularly to the flow direction, as
was described in Section 2.3 Figure 2.5. In contrast, other studies using different nozzle
designs, for example in Figure 5.1b, achieved more favorable fiber orientations [57]. This
suggests that nozzle geometry plays a critical role in influencing fiber alignment during
the printing process.

5.2
Limitations
Some of the main limitations encountered in this study relates to the CT scanning process.
Due to the machine’s weight constraints and resolution limitations, the size of the scanned
samples was restricted, and limited the ability to see the bigger picture. Additionally, some
specimens exhibited variations in moisture content from being stored outdoors, which
led to inconsistent gray-scale values across different sides of the same sample, making
thresholding for pore detection more challenging. In some instances, samples had to
be resized, which reduced the overall volume and potentially affected the relative data
collected.
There were also challenges with fiber analysis. The ROI-based approach in Amira-Avizo
tended to include more fibers than those strictly crossing the crack. In contrast, alternative
masking techniques excluded fibers that partially resided within the region of interest but
still intersected the crack. Although, it might be theoretically possible to develop custom
code in Avizo.
5.3
Suggestions for Future Research
Future research could explore the influence of nozzle geometry on fiber orientation by
investigating various nozzle shapes and flow patterns to optimize alignment and enhance
the mechanical properties of 3D-printed FRC.
The reasons behind the variation in fiber alignment across the width of printed specimens
should also be examined. Factors such as sample extraction location, nozzle turn effects,
and potential congestion could be clarified through controlled experiments.
Although inverse analysis was not performed in this thesis, future research could combine
advanced post-processing of X-ray scans with custom code (e.g., in Avizo) to accurately
detect fibers crossing cracks for use in an inverse analysis based on the hinge model.

lationship, in which fiber bridging introduces small spikes in the curve. This visualization
helps demonstrate how fibers delay crack propagation and increase fracture energy. It
also aligns with observations that cracks deviate around fibers similarly to how they deflect
around aggregates [10].

6
Conclusion
This research provides valuable insights into how process-induced fiber alignment affects
the compressive and flexural behavior of FRC. Cast specimens exhibit a stronger, more
consistent concrete matrix with favorable fiber alignment, which leads to superior me-
chanical performance. In contrast, printed specimens show less favorable and uneven
fiber alignment, making their behavior more dependent on crack initiation point. Although
overall porosity is similar for both methods, differences in void distribution and shape also
contribute to the differences.
The initial hypothesis that differences in mechanical performance between 3D-printed and
cast FRC are primarily influenced by fiber orientation is largely supported. In cast spec-
imens a clear correlation between orientation and strength was observed. However, in
printed specimens, factors such as interlayer bonding, void distribution, and inconsistent
fiber alignment also influenced performance, showing that fiber orientation is a major con-
tributor, but not the sole factor influencing the mechanical behavior of 3D-printed FRC.
The outcomes of this study suggest that further optimization of the printing process, such
as refining nozzle design, could lead to improved fiber orientation and, consequently,
better structural performance in 3D-printed FRC.
