Abstract
In response to the growing need for climate action, alternative building practices are in-
creasingly being explored within the building sector. This study is similarly motivated by
the pursuit of more sustainable solutions, by carrying out investigations on the use of
softwood as a structural connector and its integration in Dowel-Laminated Timber, with
particular focus on the potential incorporation of reclaimed timber through the use of butt-
joints.
The investigations are based on an extensive experimental campaign carried out at both
component and system levels. At the component level, the yield moment and embedment
strength of softwood are examined experimentally, and findings are used to validate the
newly proposed design provisions in the draft revision of Eurocode 5, prEN 1995-1-1
[1]. Results are then applied at system level, by examining all-softwood connections and
comparing the findings to analytical predictions made using the European Yield Model.
Additionally, a comparative study of DLT panel configurations is conducted to explore the
feasibility of incorporating reclaimed timber and omitting conventional glued finger-jointing
by introducing butt-joints into the design.
The findings show partial agreement between experimental results and analytical pre-
dictions, though notable discrepancies with a general unsatisfactory precision were ob-
served. Particularly, investigations on the shear capacity exhibited an overestimation of
the structural capacity when applying the available analytical models. Findings highlight
the need for refinement of the analytical models to accommodate softwood connectors,
and proposals for this are made throughout the thesis. The comparative study on DLT
suggests that lamellas with discontinuities (butt-joints) are still able to contribute to the
structural performance. However, a significant reduction in the mechanical performance
is observed, highlighting a tradeoff between efficiency and sustainability when incorporat-
ing reclaimed timber.
The results and findings contribute to the field of all-timber connections and DLT, with
new validation data for softwood dowels and all-timber connections, and alternative DLT
configurations using reclaimed timber.

1
Introduction
Wood’s environmental advantages have been, and remain, the driving factor for its adop-
tion in the built environment, being both sustainable and renewable as the only building
material available. With climate change calling for action, a global consensus, interest,
and need for a significant reduction in carbon emissions must be achieved, with the ulti-
mate aim of leaving zero emissions in the atmosphere, reaching net zero as called for in
the Paris agreement [2]. In this context, the commitment of the building sector remains
crucial, as it is responsible for approximately 34% of the global emissions [3]. Such strate-
gies call for alternatives to common building practices in which timber aligns well with the
intended objectives. To achieve such goals, it is necessary to evaluate the entire life cycle
of the materials we use in our society and choose them carefully. In this case, timber is
superior due to its low embodied energy, renewability, and great potential for recyclability
compared to materials such as concrete and steel. The need for a closed-loop system is
evident, with the use of sustainable materials alone no longer sufficient. Instead, circular
economy principles are becoming essential, making use of the materials already present
to reduce the use of raw materials.
Timber has been used for structural purposes for centuries, but its use has declined due
to its natural variability in mechanical properties and geometric limitations, which have
caused its predominant use to be overtaken by other materials. This and rising demands
for sustainability have given rise to what is known as Engineered Wood Products (EWPs),
which utilize timber and its eminent strength-to-weight ratio in new and innovative ways,
creating products that are competitive economically and mechanically with more conven-
tional materials and providing an increased performance over normal sawn timber. EWPs
that are widely recognized and adopted in the building industry are, for instance, Cross-
Laminated Timber (CLT), Glued-Laminated Timber (GLT), Nail-Laminated Timber (NLT),
and plywood. All offering great mechanical properties, and reducing the natural variability
of timber, offering a product which is homogeneous to a larger extent [4].
Although studies in the literature confirm that timber and EWPs provide superior environ-
mental benefits compared to concrete and steel [5] [4], they are yet challenged by the
use of chemical adhesives and metal fasteners as a means of assembly. This poses a
major environmental impact and drawback in the life cycle assessment and presents dif-
ficulties in recyclability [6]. Particularly, the increased use of chemical adhesives leads to
a release of toxic gases in the form of Volatile Organic Compounds and Formaldehyde,
which is unfavorable from an environmental perspective. Additionally, it poses several
complications for the quality of the indoor environment with Formaldehyde gases emitting
during the structure’s service life in case of high temperatures or changes in the Rela-
tive Humidity (RH) [7]. Given this, increased attention is given to traditional assembly
techniques, which rely solely on all-timber connections.
One such example is Dowel-Laminated Timber (DLT), which is an all-timber product that is
unlike other EWPs, free from both chemical adhesives and metal fasteners, addressing
the aforementioned challenges. DLT typically consists of softwood lamellas connected
by hardwood dowels using a friction fit method based on moisture differentials. With
sawn timber having approximately 60% less embodied energy than GLT [8], DLT offers
significant potential for emission reductions in construction. Furthermore, its assembly
method allows for disassembly at the end of its service life, aligning with circular economy
principles, promoting the reuse of materials.

The simplicity of the DLT system presents an ideal opportunity for integrating reclaimed
or salvaged timber due to its adaptability. This is supported by studies demonstrating
promising results when incorporating short salvaged timber [9] and using dowels made
from salvaged plywood [10]. The topic of DLT, including various adhesive-free assembly
techniques and dowel types, has been studied extensively in recent years [8] [11]. How-
ever, a promising yet underexplored alternative lies in the use of softwood dowels. Soft-
wood is generally readily available due to its fast-growing nature, low cost, and widespread
presence in existing structures and, therefore, in reclaimed or salvaged sources, making
it a practical alternative to commonly used hardwood species. Additionally, reclaimed and
salvaged timber is often available in inconsistent dimensions, which can limit its use for
structural applications. Given these geometric constraints, it may be necessary to repur-
pose the material, for instance by fabricating dowels or incorporating it as short timber
members between lamellas in DLT.
While DLT offers a simple and sustainable way of construction, there is yet no estab-
lished design framework to determine its performance. Hence, the design provisions in
the timber standard DS/EN 1995-1-1 [12], or load span tables from producers, must be
adopted. However, the current Eurocode does not provide specific guidance for designing
timber connections using wooden dowels. That said, the current draft revision DSF/prEN
1995-1-1 [1] has for the first time introduced the possibility of designing using wooden
dowel-type fasteners in hardwood. The analytical models used in the draft revision are
derived from experimental work done on steel connectors, based on the European Yield
Model (EYM) [13]. However, these theoretical assumptions have not consistently aligned
with the observed behavior of wooden connectors [14], highlighting a valuable opportunity
for further investigation.
In light of this, this thesis will aim to carry out an extensive experimental campaign on
softwood dowels, all-timber connections using softwood, and DLT incorporating softwood
dowels and butt-joints to accommodate salvaged or reclaimed timber. The investigation
begins at component level, examining the mechanical performance of softwood dowels
to evaluate the applicability of the analytical framework proposed in the draft revision
of Eurocode 5. Findings from this stage will serve as input for subsequent investiga-
tions. Building on this, the study progresses at system level by testing all-softwood timber
connections to evaluate their structural performance, assess compliance with analytical
models, and identify suitable dowels for use in DLT panels. Additionally, the study inves-
tigates the mechanical implications of integrating butt-joints to accommodate salvaged or
reclaimed timber through a comparative analysis of different panel configurations.
A set of research questions was developed during the literature review and theory chap-
ters. These are partially addressed throughout the thesis and formally revisited in the
conclusion. The research questions are listed in Section 4.1.
Note that salvaged and reclaimed wood are used ambiguously and will consequently be
referred to as reclaimed timber in this project.

2.1
Introduction to Dowel-Laminated Timber
Dowel-Laminated Timber, Dowellam, or by its original German name Brettstapel
(”brett” = board and ”stapel” = stacked), is an all-timber EWP. As the name implies, the
system originates from Germany and is said to have been invented in the 1970s by the
German engineer Julius Natterer [15]. The concept of DLT is not new, however, it has
gained renewed attention in recent years due to a growing demand for alternative con-
struction methods that align with sustainable design principles. The system is typically
used as panel elements in floor, roof, and wall constructions. It is assembled by stacking
layers of structural softwood boards (referred to as lamellas) and connecting them with
hardwood dowels inserted into tight-fit holes. This assembly method eliminates the need
for adhesives and metal fasteners, enhancing both the sustainability and simplicity of the
system. The connection method, which is referred to as ”friction fitting”, will be elaborated
later on in the thesis, together with more on the manufacturing practices in section 2.2.
The system has its main advantage for one-way spanning applications due to its unidi-
rectional alignment of the wood fibers, which all run parallel to the span. This results in
a superior performance compared to likewise EWP’s, such as CLT, in terms of spanning
length for flooring elements and axial load-bearing capacity for wall elements. Addition-
ally, the system offers improved fire performance properties, as there is no risk of delam-
ination at rising temperatures [16]. A comparative study of DLT and NLT conducted by
Minari et al. [17] [17] also demonstrated better performance in terms of deflection for DLT.
However, the system also presents limitations, particularly in terms of reduced load-
bearing capacity in the transverse direction, with the full element exhibiting anisotropic
behavior, similar to that of standard sawn timber. Moreover, studies demonstrate that the
system exhibits a relatively low bending stiffness compared to likewise EWPs [8]. Wall

ements typically range from 100-240 mm. Both types of elements can be manufactured

A comparative review done on the assembly techniques of multi-layered wood panel prod-

Given the large emphasis on the experimental part of this thesis, where hands-on practi-
cal work has played a significant role, it is essential to establish an understanding of the
typical manufacturing process of DLT elements. While the production method is relatively
straightforward, it involves key steps that must be executed properly to ensure the quality
and consistency of the system. Moisture content control, particularly through proper con-
ditioning of components prior to assembly, is consistently emphasized in the literature as
a critical factor for achieving a tight fit between dowels and lamellas, and consequently an
effective structural system [11][16]. This section outlines the main stages of processing
and assembly, based on a standardized workflow provided by an established manufac-
turer [18]. These stages are supplemented with additional remarks, insights from relevant
literature, and considerations specific to the current project.

Stage 1: Initial processing
The initial processing of timber involves identifying and removing unwanted defects and
natural growth irregularities such as knots and significant cracks. Additionally, boards are
cut into desired lengths for use as lamellas. This stage ensures the required consistency
and reliability of the lamellas used in the DLT elements. Processing can be performed
manually or with automated systems equipped with scanning and optimization technolo-
gies. In this project, CE-marked structural timber is used, meaning that the timber has
already gone through a grading procedure. The CE-marked timber complies with the
harmonized European standard DS/EN 14081-1 [19], which defines the requirements for
strength grading of structural timber.
An increasing number of studies are investigating the effective use of reclaimed timber in
structural applications, including its potential implementation in EWP’s. If reclaimed tim-
ber was incorporated in DLT, a visual inspection could be conducted following the Nordic
visual strength grading standard DS/INSTA 142 [20] before cutting boards into lamellas.
However, applying visual strength grading to reclaimed timber may present challenges.
Previous studies have shown that a significant portion of reclaimed specimens may be
rejected based on visual criteria even when demonstrating satisfactory mechanical per-
formance in destructive testing [21].
Stage 2: Finger-jointing
A practice that is commonly used in timber construction is finger-jointing boards end-to-
end with adhesives to overcome geometric constraints. However, this compromises one
of DLT’s main advantages, which is assembly without the need for adhesives. By omit-
ting conventional finger-jointing, it is important to acknowledge the geometric limitations
it entails, resulting in a tradeoff between sustainability and applicability. An alternative
to conventional finger-jointing could be the use of simple butt-joints, where lamellas are
placed end-to-end and held in place solely by the dowels, or other types of carpentry joints.
Stage 3: Profile moulder
Lamellas are planed using a profile moulder to plane out any drying defects and to achieve
the desired thickness required for the specific DLT panel. This process also allows for
the integration of various surface profiles, which may serve aesthetic purposes, such as
disguising shrinkage in service, or technical purposes, such as enhancing acoustic per-
formance. Additionally, longitudinal interlocking systems can be introduced during this
stage to facilitate easy alignment during assembly and potentially improve load transfer
between adjacent lamellas.
An interlocking system could consist of a simple tongue and groove system. Dauksta
discusses this in research conducted on behalf of Woodknowledge Wales (WKW) [16]
concerning the implementation of rough homegrown timber in the UK for DLT produc-
tion. One challenge in producing tongue-and-groove profiles from rough timber is that a
considerable amount of material must be removed to shape and smooth the boards in
the profiling process. This led to the development of an alternative profile to the original
tongue and groove, proposed by WKW, which is more material efficient and specifically
designed for DLT applications using rough timber. The profile features a modified tongue
and groove geometry that reduces the need for extensive smoothing and shaping during
the planing process. The profile can be manufactured using serrated back cutters, which
can be mounted directly into a conventional moulder, and produce large quantities at a
relatively fast rate. This makes the design both practical and feasible for integration into
a production workflow, as it can be implemented using simple and low-cost equipment.

Stage 4: DLT press
Once the lamellas that comprise the panels have been prepared, the lamellas are stacked
together vertically or horizontally to form complete elements. Hydraulic pressure is ap-
plied vertically and horizontally to bring the lamellas together tightly before insertion of
dowels.
Stage 5: Drilling holes and inserting dowels

chanically to connect the lamellas. The insertion process can be done manually by hy-
draulic pressing [23], by a simple hammering procedure [9], or with automated robotic
systems for a more efficient procedure [24]. The draft revision of Eurocode 5 [1] specifies
requirements for pre-drilling holes for dowel-type fasteners. Defining that for dowels with
a diameter greater than or equal to 6 mm, predrilling should be carried out using a drill
diameter within the range given in Equation (2.1). The feasibility of applying this require-

Stage 6: Moisture equilibrium
A key stage in the manufacturing process is achieving moisture equilibrium between the
dowels and the lamellas. Dowels are intentionally dried to a low moisture content (MC) of
6-8%, while the lamellas are conditioned to a higher MC around 12-15% [8]. Due to this
moisture differential, the dowels will absorb moisture from the surrounding timber after
insertion, causing a volumetric swelling and securing the lamellas. This swelling gener-
ates a pressure against the walls of the predrilled holes, creating a friction locking effect,
securing the boards without the need for adhesives or metal fasteners, earlier referred to
as ”friction-fitting”. This phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 2.4, where the dowel transi-
tions from its dry state at insertion to a fully expanded state in moisture equilibrium with
the surrounding lamellas.

Its hygroscopic nature also affects its dimensional stability. Timber shrinks or swells due
to moisture exchange in the cell walls. As for the mechanical properties, timber is dimen-
sionally stable above the fiber saturation point, but not below it.
This highlights one of the primary concerns in relation to all-timber connections, as the
dimensional stability affects the effectiveness of the connection. As discussed in section
2.2, the effectiveness of the friction-fitting in DLT (but also for all-timber connections in
general) naturally depends on the volumetric swelling and shrinkage characteristics of
the wood species used for the dowels.
As noted in previous studies by Schroeder [27], hardwoods generally exhibit greater
swelling and shrinkage properties than softwoods do. This can support one of the reasons
why it is typically opted for in timber connections (besides its great mechanical properties).
However, it could be argued that in the case of fluctuating humidity conditions, softwood
may offer a greater dimensional stability, due to its lower sensitivity to moisture variations.
In this context, another notable point is emphasized in studies on DLT by Bell [25], which
builds on previous research suggesting that the friction coefficient varies depending on
the orientation of the dowel’s grain, where the friction coefficient perpendicular to the grain
is expected to be higher. In production, it is often impractical and too circumstantial to en-
sure a specific orientation of the dowels. This motivates the argument that species with
the lowest tangential to radial (T/R) expansion ratio result in a more uniform swelling, and
consequently, a more consistent and reliable connection. In this regard, certain softwood
species may be superior to hardwood, depending on the species.
Innovative research is carried out to mitigate these concerns, with promising results lying
in the use of Thermo Hydro Mechanical (THM) densified wood dowels. This is shown to
exhibit enhanced mechanical properties, but most importantly in this regard, an increased
swelling potential due to the phenomenon ”set-recovery”. Describing a partial return to
the original dimensions of the compressed member when exposed to moisture. This is
usually mentioned as an undesired property in many contexts, but in the context of timber
connections, this behavior may be beneficial [11].

The time-dependent deformation of timber (creep) also presents a challenge in timber
connections. Research by Brandon et. al investigating the visco-elastic creep of tim-
ber connections emphasizes how connections typically creep more than the remaining
structure due to higher local stresses [28]. In this case, softwood poses a significant chal-
lenge as greater creep effects must be expected due to a lower stiffness. The physical
parameters mentioned are particularly relevant, as they may contribute to increased slip
and deformations in the connections, consequently reducing the stiffness of the system.
Given that DLT has a relatively low stiffness compared to other EPWs, as mentioned
earlier, this is especially undesirable.
2.3.2
Geometric parameters
Focus is given to the dowels of the connections. The function of these is foremost to
prevent the members of a DLT system or an all-timber connection from sliding apart. This
is done by transferring shear forces to neighbouring members in a combination of bend-
ing, shear, and embedment. Han et al. [8] explored experimental and numerical studies
investigating the effect of the dowel configuration in DLT panels, showing that the over-
all stiffness and load-bearing capacity are strongly affected by this. The results revealed
that panels exhibit greater stiffness properties when dowel spacing is reduced, due to in-
creased composite action. However, when the spacing falls below 50 mm, the increase
in stiffness appears to stagnate, with no additional contribution observed. It is important
to note that excessive reductions in spacing compromise the load-bearing capacity due
to a decrease in the net area of the lamellas. Similarly, increasing the dowel diameter
does not appear to improve stiffness, but instead increases the risk of crack propagation
around the drilled holes due to a weakened cross-section. However, increasing the dowel
diameter results in a significant increase in the capacity of the connector. This effect is
highlighted in studies on softwood dowels by Frontini et al. [14]. The observed increase in
stiffness with decreasing dowel spacing suggests that a longitudinal interlocking system
could offer promising benefits in terms of stiffness.
Dowel insertion angle was investigated in a study by Dourado et al. [29] on timber beams
laminated with hardwood dowels, which showed a slight increase in maximum failure
load and bending stiffness. However, it was noted that the insertion angle did not have
a significant influence on the stiffness. With only a slight improvement in the mechanical
performance of angled dowels, the practical complications associated with the insertion,
as observed by Giordano [9], can raise questions about its feasibility. Another study made
on double shear tests with densified wood dowels indicates that increased shear and
bending properties can be obtained by inserting the dowels with the growth rings parallel
to the loading direction [8], but as discussed, this may not be feasible in a production
setting.
Research on NLT, a quite similar EWP to DLT, has shown that incorporating butt-joints
without end-to-end interlocking leads to a significant reduction in flexural stiffness, with
an average decrease of 34% [30]. These findings highlight the importance of continuity
in the lamellas and naturally raise questions about the feasibility of butt-joints. However,
Giordano investigated the incorporation of short-salvaged timber in DLT, with a system
that introduced prestressing effects, which showed promising results. However, as for
the connectors, this would require controlled environments, as shrinkage of the members
would compromise the prestressing effect.

2.4
All-timber connections
All-timber connections have gained increased attention due to their alignment with sus-
tainability goals. Despite their historical use in traditional carpentry, the application of
wooden dowels in contemporary construction is limited due to a lack of guidance in the
current design codes. Teodorescu et al [31] addressed this by conducting an experimen-
tal campaign on hardwood dowels made from Massaranduba, aimed at evaluating the
mechanical behavior and comparing the results to predictions from the current analyti-
cal models in Eurocode 5 [12]. Their findings showed that timber dowels exhibit great
mechanical properties, particularly a high degree of ductility, with deformation capacity
exceeding that of steel-doweled connections. However, the study also demonstrated that
the current design basis, developed for steel dowels, performs poorly in predicting the
load-bearing capacity for wooden dowels, by significantly underestimating the capacity.
Embedment strength was examined using both wood and steel dowels, with varying re-
sults between the two. Emphasizing the need for analytical expressions designed specif-
ically for timber fasteners. A study on embedment strength conducted by Glisovic et al.
[32] demonstrated that the density has a significant influence on the embedment strength.
A separate experimental campaign conducted by Frontini et al. [14] investigated the me-
chanical performance of softwood dowels. In contrast to findings for hardwood dowels,
their study showed that predictions using the EYM aligned rather well with the experimen-
tal results, with some discrepancy. However, a distinctive failure pattern deviating from
the ones defined in the EYM was observed, involving a combination of bending, com-
pression, and shear failure in the dowels, in contrast to steel dowel behavior. This failure
mode aligns with previous findings and is typically referred to as failure mode IV (this will
be elaborated in section 3.3). Dowels exhibited varying failure patterns when subjected to
bending. While some dowels failed in a brittle manner, others exhibited ductile behavior
with signs of post-yield hardening. Frontini et al also investigated the influence of vary-
ing dowel shapes, concluding that a tighter fit between dowel and connected members
increases the stiffness of the connection. The investigations also concluded that no clear
relationship could be observed between load-bearing capacity or stiffness with the density
of the dowels.
A study by Mehra et al. [33] investigated the mechanical behavior of timber connections
using slotted-in plates and dowels in compressed wood. As mentioned earlier, the con-
cept of compressed wood shows promising results in terms of long-term effects. These
findings show an additional strong potential in terms of the load-bearing capacity. The
study compared a similar timber-to-timber connection to a timber-to-steel connection, with
the compressed wood dowels exhibiting similar failure mode and comparable capacity to
steel ones. This highlights a promising and more sustainable alternative to steel.
The renewed interest in all-timber connections is reflected in the draft revision of Eurocode
5 [1], which introduces the possibility of designing timber connections using hardwood
dowels. This presents an interesting opportunity for further research of the newly devel-
oped analytical models.

2.5
Analytical models for design of DLT and timber
connections
There is currently no specific analytical framework available for the design of DLT. How-
ever, the new draft revision, prEN 1995-1-1 [1] provides the possibility of designing timber
connections using dowels in hardwood. This new development makes it possible to as-
sess the capacity of a DLT system using the principles outlined in the Eurocode 5, by
combining the new analytical models for dowels with existing beam theory already estab-
lished in the code.
Bruzzone et al. conducted an experimental investigation with, among other things, the
aim of evaluating the applicability of predicting the effective bending stiffness of DLT pan-
els, using the mechanically jointed beam theory, also referred to as ”The Gamma Method”
on an idealized cross-section comprising outer layers with stiffness characteristics as the
lamellas, and a middle layer representing the stiffness of the dowels. The hypothesis was
that the panels connected by dowels with varying slip behavior in each shear plane would
induce a varying deformation profile, in which the dowel’s centroid is shifted from the neu-
tral axis of the system, which, in theory, would lead to an additional stiffness contribution
from the ”Steiner component” in the Gamma method. However, the experimental results
conclusively demonstrated that the contribution was negligible, with the effective bending
stiffness of the panels aligning closely to that of the individual lamellas, indicating that little
to no composite action was achieved [34].
Different analytical approaches for verifying and designing DLT are discussed by Bell
[25], who uses the more established product, CLT, as a benchmark for evaluating poten-
tial methods. He discusses the composite beam theory and the shear analogy method,
both offering valuable insights into the mechanical behavior of DLT. However, methods
yield varying results in terms of stiffness and force distribution, raising concerns about
their applicability. Based on this, Bell recommends the adoption of the established an-
alytical framework in Eurocode 5 [12], based on classical beam theory, highlighting its
consistency, practicality, and low complexity for verification of DLT systems [25].
This further supports the reasoning of designing DLT using simplified analytical expres-
sions, decomposing the system into its components. Each component can then be verified
separately to ensure that the overall system has sufficient capacity to resist the applied
loads.

3
Theory
This chapter presents the theory relevant to the thesis. This includes fundamental in-
formation related to the strength, stiffness, and mechanical behavior of timber, providing
a basis for the subsequent analysis. Furthermore, the analytical framework in the draft
revision of Eurocode 5 [1] is outlined, as it is used for comparison throughout the project.
3.1
Strength and stiffness of timber
Timber is an anisotropic material, with mechanical properties varying depending on the
direction of the load relative to the grain. This dependence arises from the wood’s cellular
structure, which is aligned along the grain. As a result, timber exhibits high strength and
stiffness properties in the longitudinal direction compared to other directions. Besides
the dependence on the grain direction, its inhomogeneous nature results in significant
strength variability due to natural defects and growth irregularities, such as knots, grain
deviations, resin pockets, etc. This variability can be significantly reduced in EWPs such
as DLT due to the possibility of redistributing weak zones from natural defects among the
different layers in the given product, leading to a more homogeneous and reliable mechan-
ical performance [4]. Due to this variability, a rather large coefficient of variation (CoV)
is to be expected when working experimentally with wood, compared to other materials.
With average CoV varying from 10-34% depending on the parameter [35].
The anisotropy of timber is typically described with respect to three main directions: the
longitudinal, tangential, and radial, as illustrated in Figure 3.1. However, in engineering
practice, this is usually reduced to two primary orientations, being: parallel (∥) and per-
pendicular (⊥) to the grain, which are used to define strength and stiffness parameters for
design purposes. Design values for strength graded structural timber are standardized
and provided in DS/EN 338 [36].

In the case of DLT, the directional differences in material properties play a significant role.
The lamellas are oriented so that the primary loads act parallel to the grain, which is pre-
ferred due to higher strength parameters. However, in contrast, the dowels are inserted
perpendicular to the grain, meaning that the load transfer of the connection relies on the
resistance of compression (embedment strength) perpendicular to the grain. Previous re-
search by Walker et al. [38] on all-timber connections showed that dowels exhibit greater
stiffness when loaded in the radial direction [38]. This suggests that a radial alignment of
dowels may influence connection stiffness in DLT systems, underlining the importance of
accounting for the anisotropic nature of wood. However, as mentioned earlier, this can
be difficult to achieve in a production setting.
3.2
Failure mechanisms and defects
In preparation for the experimental work, it is necessary to outline the potential failure
mechanisms that may be observed. Three primary failure modes are assessed relevant
to the thesis, being tension and shear failure due to flexure, and compression failure
perpendicular to the grain, resulting from timber embedment.
Bending failure is typically governed by tension in the lower fibers. The stress-strain rela-
tionship, in this case, is linear elastic up to the yield point, followed by an abrupt and brittle
failure. For short and tall members, shear failure may become the governing mode. This
is characterized by sliding and separation of the fibers, also leading to a brittle failure.
When timber is subjected to compression perpendicular to the grain, it results in local-
ized crushing of the wood cells. Here, the stress-strain relationship generally behaves
in a ductile manner, exhibiting plastic deformation after yielding. In some cases, a post-
yield hardening phase may be observed, due to densification of the crushed wood cells.
Tension parallel to the fibers and compression perpendicular to the fibers are presented
together with the work curve for each in Figure 3.2.

3.3
Johansen’s yield theory
The current Eurocode for timber structures, DS/EN 1995-1-1 [12], as well as the most
recent draft revision, prEN 1995-1-1 [1], determines the dowel effect contribution to the
lateral load-bearing capacity of fasteners using a yield theory originally proposed by Jo-
hansen [13], and later further developed by Larsen [40], commonly referred to as the EYM.
The EYM predicts the load-bearing capacity based on a plastic analysis and identifies
several potential failure modes, depending on the relation between embedment strength,
yield moment, and connection geometry. The resistance of the different failure modes is
derived from moment equilibrium and consists of the following:
• Mode I: Timber failing due to embedding pressure with no yield of the dowel. This
mode usually occurs for bolted connections with larger diameters.
• Mode II: Combination of embedment failure and bending failure. If one member
has a sufficient thickness, the dowel will bend and form one plastic hinge per shear
plane.
• Mode III: Combination of embedment failure and bending failure. If both members
have a sufficient thickness, the dowel will bend and form two plastic hinges per shear
plane. This failure mode is preferred in terms of connection ductility due to a high
deformation capacity.
The theory is derived from the use of steel fasteners, and experimental and numerical
investigations on all-timber connections have demonstrated that the existing yield model
equations produce inconsistent results when using wooden dowels, highlighting the need
to account for an additional failure mode not captured by the EYM. As a result, a new
failure mode was proposed, referred to as Mode IV, with the failure mode as described
below:
• Mode IV: Combination of bending and shear perpendicular to the grain with forma-
tion of two plastic hinges per shear plane.
The described failure modes in the EYM are illustrated together with the new ’Mode IV’ in

Yield moment
When steel is subjected to tensile forces induced by bending moments, the material ex-
hibits elasto-plastic behavior, characterized by an initial linear-elastic region followed by
the onset of a well-defined plastic plateau in its stress-strain behavior. The transition from
the elastic to the plastic stage of material behavior is defined as the yielding point, and
the moment required to induce this is the yielding moment.
The yield moment of a dowel-type fastener in timber represents the same point. However,
unlike steel, timber does not exhibit ideal elasto-plastic behavior. While timber dowels
exhibit some ductility, as noted in the literature review, the failure of timber in tension is
inherently brittle. This results in a much less defined plastic plateau than that of steel and
a short period between the onset of plasticity and rupture. Despite that, the Eurocode 5
draft revision [1] has included the possibility of calculating the characteristic yield moment
for wooden dowels as a function of the characteristic bending strength, fm,k, and the dowel
diameter, d, as given in Equation (3.2).

Theoretical synthesis and experimental
approach
Until now, the current state of the art has been reviewed in Chapter 2 through a com-
prehensive literature review, and supported by relevant theory in Chapter 3 regarding the
subject. From this, gaps are identified in the existing research, and a set of research
questions is defined to motivate and form the basis for the experimental work, providing
a unique contribution to the field of all-timber connections and DLT.
Attention is given to dowel-type fasteners at component level and all-timber connections at
system level. A notable gap in the current literature lies in the limited research on softwood
used as a connector, despite its accessibility, cost-effectiveness, and reclaimed timber
sources often consisting of softwood species, which presents great potential. Moreover,
with the introduction of the new design provisions for hardwood dowels in prEN 1995-
1-1 [1], a valuable opportunity arises to validate these analytical models using softwood
dowels to assess their applicability.
Attention is likewise directed towards DLT. While considerable research has been con-
ducted on the performance of DLT systems, the integration of softwood dowels remains
unexplored. Interesting studies have explored the integration of short-salvaged timber in
DLT, showing promising results. However, concerns remain regarding the long-term per-
formance of this system. However, from a sustainability perspective, the concept remains
appealing. An alternative solution could involve designing panels with butt-jointed lamel-
las featuring longitudinal interlocking profiles, such as those proposed by WKW Wales, in
combination with softwood dowels. This strategy may enable the integration of reclaimed
timber without compromising the performance of the panel excessively. Overall, research
exploring the use of butt-joints in DLT is scarce, though this topic is covered to a larger
extent for the related EWP, NLT. This could also serve as an alternative to traditional
finger-jointing of timber members, to avoid chemical adhesives completely.
In light of this, this thesis will investigate the mechanical performance of softwood dowels
and evaluate whether the analytical models provided in prEN 1995-1-1 [1] are applicable
for predicting their behavior. This is done on a component level by examining the yield
moment and embedment strength, and subsequently evaluating on a system level by
investigating all-softwood connections. A comparison is additionally made on the capac-
ity of more conventional hardwood species to evaluate the feasibility of using softwood.
Furthermore, the thesis will incorporate softwood dowels in DLT systems and examine
alternative strategies for incorporating reclaimed or salvaged timber and omitting tradi-
tional finger jointing. This includes comparing the stiffness across different DLT panel
configurations.

The methodology defined in DS/EN 409 was originally developed for steel dowels, and
determining the yield moment from a rotation angle criterion is most likely not applicable
for dowels in softwood or wood in general. Consequently, an alternative approach is
adopted for comparison with the rotation angle criterion in DS/EN 409. This is inspired by
the method proposed by Björnfot et al. [47] in their work on the ductility of steel-to-timber
connections.
The method involves a regression analysis, in which the yield moment is determined from
the intersection of two regression lines. The first regression line represents the linear-
elastic region of the dowel work curve (in this case, for a moment rotation curve) fitted
through the data corresponding to 10-40% of the maximum moment Mmax. The second
regression line represents the plastic region fitted to the data from 80% of Mmax and
iteratively extended until a coefficient of determination as close as possible to R2 = 0.95 is
achieved. This approach offers a balanced approximation between the end of the elastic
response and the start of the plastic response, thereby defining the point of yield. The
method is illustrated in Figure 6.6.

step by step. The procedure is carried out equally for all test specimens to ensure quality
and consistency of the collected data.
Step 1 - Preparation of test specimens: All test specimens were prepared in accor-
dance with the procedure described in Chapter 6.1.1.
Step 2 - Calibration of LVDT extensometer: Deflections were measured using an LVDT
extensometer. Prior to testing, the device was calibrated using a precision calibration jig
to verify that the output in strains corresponded to known displacements, ensuring the
reliability of measurements.
38
Softwood Dowels and Dowel-Laminated Timber
==PAGE:54==
Step 3 - Mounting fixtures to test machine: Loading and support fixtures were mounted
onto the UTM, ensuring that both were aligned. The fixtures were adjusted to fit the
correct measurements between supports and loading points (l1, l2, and l3) depending on
the diameter of the test specimen.
Step 4 - Retrieval of test specimens: All specimens were collected from the climate
chamber, measured, and weighted to determine the density at test time. Specimens were
marked with markings for supports and loading points and subsequently stored in vapour-
tight bags during testing to avoid changes in MC before post-processing.
Step 5 - Mounting of Specimens: Specimens were removed from the vapour-tight bags
and positioned on the test setup. The crosshead, equipped with a load cell and loading
fixture, was then lowered until the specimens were fixed in place.
Step 6 - Experimenting: The experiment was conducted until the rupture of the specimen
occurred. At the end of the experiment, the crosshead was raised to allow for removal of
the specimen, which was then returned to the vapour-tight bags. Steps 6 and 7 are re-
peated for each specimen until completion of all tests. The data is subsequently exported
from the acquisition software in CSV format for later processing.
Step 7 - Post processing: After testing, all specimens are cut into smaller pieces for
determination of the moisture content at test time, according to the oven drying method
in DS/EN 13183 [46] as described in Chapter 5.2.

6.4
Discussion and conclusion
Based on the results and analysis, a reasonable prediction was obtained from using the
analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 [1] for calculating the yield moment of wooden dow-
els. The degree of agreement between experimental and analytical results varied across
dowel diameter groups, with smaller dowels (8 mm and 12 mm) showing greater deviation,
whereas larger dowels (15 mm, 19 mm, and 25 mm) exhibited better alignment with the
analytical predictions. It should be acknowledged that the limited sample sizes for each
dowel diameter group represent a limitation in the analysis. A larger number of specimens
could potentially reduce the CoV and improve the reliability of the results. However, in all
cases, predictions were conservative, indicating that the analytical model is applicable for
softwood dowels and offers an overall reasonable prediction. This section of the analysis
addresses the first research question:
Q1.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the yield moment of
softwood dowels?
The analytical model was examined further to evaluate the potential of refining the ana-
lytical model to predict softwood dowels more accurately. By underestimating the yield
moment capacity, the required dowel dimensions are consequently overestimated when
designing. While this may be considered to serve as a safety margin in design, it is
worth mentioning that the structural design of structures following DS/EN 1990 [48] al-
ready accounts for calculation model uncertainty. This is accounted for by the material
partial safety factor, γM, which, among other things, incorporates uncertainties in the re-
liability of the calculation model and actual structural behavior, through the partial factor,
γ1. In light of this, it is argued that the analytical model should be as precise as possible
in representing the actual behavior, however, while avoiding any overestimation of the
yield moment capacity. This was done by updating the correction factor for the analytical
model, which resulted in a reduction in the relative differences across the different dowel
diameter groups. This addresses the second research question:
Q1.2: How can the existing analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 be improved to predict the
yield moment more precisely?
Through the analysis, it became evident that the rotation-based criterion defined in EN 409
[41], originally defined for steel, is not applicable for softwood dowels. Steel is an isotropic
material and exhibits ductile behavior in bending, including a well-defined plastic region,
which can make the rotation angle a reliable indicator of yielding. In contrast, timber is
anisotropic and fails in a brittle manner under bending. Consequently, the fixed rotation-
based approach did not manage to capture the yielding of the wooden dowels. As an
alternative, a proposal is made on determining the yield moment based on a percentage
of the maximum recorded moment, Mmax. The results from the tested population suggest
that yielding occurs at approximately 78% of Mmax, with a notably low CoV of 1,2% across

Part 2: Embedment strength
This part investigates the embedment strength perpendicular to the grain of softwood tim-
ber loaded with softwood dowels, with both made from Norway Spruce. A total of 28
specimens were tested, 7 specimens with 12 mm dowels, 7 with 15 mm dowels, and 14
with 19 mm dowels. This chapter presents key findings and summary statistics derived
from the experimental work. For a detailed description of the results for each specimen,
reference is made to Appendix B, which provides a complete set of data and figures rel-
evant to this part. This can be used for further inspection if deemed necessary.
While the tests were intended to assess the embedment strength of the timber boards,
the main failure mode was governed by the dowels of the specimens, which failed in a
combination of compression and bending. Initial tests for the 12 mm and 15 mm dowel
diameter groups were carried out with timber boards in full board thickness (t = 45 mm)
for the first two specimens. This resulted in pure compression and bending failure in the
dowel. An effort was made to increase the localized stresses and encourage embedment
failure by reducing the thickness of timber boards to two times the dowel diameter (t =
2d) for the remaining specimens, which remains within the allowable range described in
DS/EN 383 [42]. While this adjustment gave rise to a small increase in compression of
the surrounding fibers, the dominant failure remained in the dowel with little effect.
Motivated by previous findings from Frontini et al. [14], an additional set of 19 mm dowel
specimens was fabricated (resulting in a total of 14 specimens for that diameter group).
In this case, timber boards were further reduced to 0,7d (t = 0,7d), deviating from the
normative test standard, in an attempt to provoke embedment failure. This correction
resulted in notable embedding failure with clear signs of deformation in surrounding fibers.
However, still conclusively governed by dowel failure. As a result of this, the majority of
the test results do not fully represent the actual embedment strength perpendicular to the
grain, but rather a lower bound, which is governed by the connection behavior and dowel
strength.

7.2.0.2
Experimental procedure
The experimental procedure is presented, outlining the process of conducting the tests
step by step. The procedure is carried out equally for all test specimens to ensure quality
and consistency of the collected data.
Step 1 - Preparation of test specimens: All test specimens were prepared in accor-
dance with the procedure described in section 7.1.1.
Step 2 - Calibration of LVDT tranducers: Displacements were measured using LVDT
transducers. Prior to testing, the transducers were calibrated using a precision calibration
jig to establish the correlation between the transducer output in Volt Direct Current (VDC)
and the corresponding displacements, ensuring reliability and accuracy of the recorded
measurements.
Step 3 - Mounting fixtures to testing machine: Loading fixtures and support fixtures
were mounted onto the UTM. Particular attention was placed on the alignment of the
support fixture, which consisted of a custom steel bracket designed specifically for the
test series, as the bracket did not fit into the machine’s centering disk.
Step 4 - Retrieval and mounting of test specimens: Specimens were collected from the
climate chamber, measured, and weighed to determine the density at test time. Transduc-
ers were subsequently screwed onto the specimens, and the specimens were mounted
onto the steel bracket.
Step 5 - Experimenting: The experiment was conducted until rupture of the specimen or
a maximum displacement of 5 mm occurred. At the end of the experiment, the crosshead
was raised to allow for removal of the specimen, which was then returned to vapour-tight
bags. Steps 4 and 5 were repeated for each specimen until completion of all tests. The
data was subsequently exported from the acquisition software in CSV format for later
processing.
Step 6 - Post processing: After testing, all specimens are cut into smaller pieces for the
determination of the moisture content at test time, according to the oven drying method
in DS/EN 13183 [46] as described in Chapter 5.

characterized by the yield of the material and the onset of plastic deformation in a non-
linear response. It is observed that specimens that comprise thicker boards display a
behavior with a predominant linear-elastic behavior, with an abrupt drop in capacity after
reaching peak load, early in the onset of plasticity. This can be explained by localized
cracking and damage to the fibers in the dowels. The behavior is recognizable from the
observed force-displacement behavior in Part 1 (Chapter 6) concerning bending tests,
giving away a behavior governed by bending. However, unlike the previous test, the
curves generally exhibit a recovery phase by continuous plastic deformation, although
maximum capacity is not reached. This plasticity can be explained by the influence of
compression in the dowel, and not by embedment in the boards.
At the same time, a clear trend is observed when reducing the board thickness. In all
cases (most notably in S19.8-14), the reduction appears to increase plasticity and ductility,
indicated by fewer abrupt drops in capacity. This is evident in the force-displacement curve
resembling that of idealized elasto-plastic behavior to a larger degree, characterized by
a dominant plastic phase and signs of post-yield hardening. This behavior suggests a
true embedment failure mechanism, likely due to densification of crushed wood cells, as
expected from the theory (Chapter 3).

7.3.3
Concluding comments on embedment behavior
The mean embedment stress-displacement curves presented in Figure 7.12 display the
effect of reducing the timber board thickness in provoking actual embedment behavior.
While the initial tests (ES12.1-7, ES15.1-7, and ES19.1-7) exhibited slight embedding,
the derived strengths significantly underestimate the actual embedment strengths, due to
a governing failure in the dowel. In contrast, the latter specimens with reduced board
thickness of 0,7d (ES19.8-14), actual embedding was observed, showing crushing of
surrounding fibers, conclusively offering a greater basis for comparison and evaluating
embedment strength.
In light of these observations, the initial tests do not provide a valid basis for comparison
with the analytical model for embedment strength as provided in prEN 1995-1-1 [1], since
the observed failure mechanism deviates from the assumptions underlying the model.
However, test results may still offer a meaningful opportunity for comparison with calcu-
lations derived from the EYM. Consequently, the results obtained from the initial tests will
be treated and interpreted as the shear capacity, Fv, of the steel-to-timber connection.
The shear capacity will be included in the summary statistics for this part, but will not be
analyzed further.

Discussion and conclusion
The investigations of this part have led to several valuable insights. One of the main find-
ings is the limitation of the test setup in DS/EN 383 [42], when using wooden dowels. The
defined requirements proved not to be applicable, resulting in consistent failure of dow-
els, preventing the assessment of the actual embedment strength. This is attributed to the
similarity in material properties between the dowel and base material, in which the dowel,
being the weaker link, failed before reaching embedment deformations. This contrasts
with steel dowels, for which the test method was originally developed, where the large
difference in strength and stiffness between steel and timber ensures embedding failure.
Addressing this, deviations were made from the test standard motivated by previous stud-
ies by Frontini et al. [14], by decreasing the board thickness to 0,7d. This led to a shift
in the failure mechanism, leading stress concentrations away from the dowel, resulting
in plastic deformation of the board’s surrounding fibers. Resulting in actual embedment
failure and observed behavior and strength values aligning with reported findings in pre-
vious studies. It could be argued that the embedment strength using wooden dowels is
better assessed using the American test standard ASTM D5764-97a [49], comprising a
half-hole test setup in which the load is applied on the whole length of the dowel, conse-
quently removing the influence from bending of the dowel.
The experimental embedment strength was compared to predictions using the analyti-
cal model in prEN 1995-1-1 [1] as given in Equation (3.3). The comparison gave away a
conservative estimate from the analytical prediction, which is preferred, but with an unsat-
isfactory precision, having a relative difference of 32,4%. To improve agreement with the
experimental data, a correction factor was proposed and applied to the existing analytical
model, resulting in a reduction of the relative difference to 12%. While this is promising,
further validation is necessary due to the limited dataset. Nonetheless, the present study
yielded the lowest embedment strength among the compared studies, which can justify
the correction. These findings directly address the research questions reserved for this
part, being:
Q2.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the embedment
strength when using softwood dowels?
Q2.2: How can the existing analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 be improved to predict the
embedment strength more precisely?

8.2
Methodology
The objective of this test is to determine the shear capacity and slip characteristics of
a double shear configuration in all softwood timber. The conducted tests are based on
the approach described in the normative test standard DS/EN 26891 [50] in which the
connection capacity is determined at the ultimate force or the force corresponding to a
displacement of 15 mm, depending on which occurs first. The loading procedure for the
tests is based on the requirements in DS/EN 26891 [50], which specifies a cyclic loading
sequence to account for settlements in the form of initial slip in connections. Each cycle
step is expressed as a fraction of the estimated max load. The loading procedure is
presented in Figure 8.2.
Figure 8.2: Loading procedure for double shear tests according to DS/EN 26891. Source:
[50].

trolled loading rate corresponding to 0, 2Fest per minute ±25 % when below 0, 7Fest. When
above 0, 7Fest the ultimate force, Fu, or slip of 15 mm should be reached within 3 to 5 min-
utes, resulting in a total testing time of 10 to 15 minutes. An attempt has been made to
comply with these requirements. Besides the embedment strength, the test also allowed
for the determination of the slip modulus, Ks, determined as given in Equation (8.1). For
this, the cyclic loading is crucial to determine the elastic stiffness of the doweled joint,

Experimental procedure
The experimental procedure is presented, outlining the process of conducting the tests
step by step. The procedure is carried out equally for all test specimens to ensure quality
and consistency of the collected data.
Step 1 - Preparation of test specimens: All test specimens were prepared in accor-

Step 2 - Mounting fixtures to test machine: Loading and support fixtures were mounted
onto the UTM, including the pressure distribution plate for the load cell and the support
fixtures.
Step 3 - Retrieval and mounting of test specimens: Specimens were collected from
the climate chamber, measured, and weighed to determine the density at test time. Spec-
imens were mounted, ensuring proper alignment, and the steel ruler was clamped to the
specimen to monitor potential curvature of the specimen.

occurred. At the end of the experiment, the crosshead was raised to allow for the removal
of the specimen, which was then returned to vapour-tight bags. Steps 3 and 4 were
repeated for each specimen until completion of all tests. The data was subsequently ex-
ported from the acquisition software in CSV format for later processing.
Step 5 - Post processing: After testing, all specimens are cut into smaller pieces for
the determination of the MC at test time, according to the oven drying method in DS/EN
13183 as described in section 5.2.

8.4
Discussion and conclusion
During the test campaign for the present part, all specimens failed in the dowels rather
than in the connected members. Both failure modes II and III of the EYM were observed,
with the latter demonstrating superior load-bearing capacity. Comparisons with analyti-
cal predictions from the EYM revealed discrepancies by overestimating failure mode II by
10,6% and underestimating failure mode III by 15,2%. These deviations can be explained
by the behavior differences from all timber connections, which deviate from the underlying
assumptions of the EYM, assuming steel fasteners. In light of this, the analytical expres-
sions for the two failure modes were corrected by replacing the original correction factors
accounting for material differences in steel-to-timber connections and introducing new
factors accounting for wooden dowels, and more specifically, softwood dowels. Resulting
in correction factors, α(d) = 0, 92 for failure mode II and γ(f) = 1, 24 for failure mode III / IV.
The limited dataset is acknowledged by having the correction factors fit the lower bound
of the experimental results.
The joint stiffness was experimentally derived and compared to analytical predictions,
revealing that the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 [1] is highly conservative. An attempt
was made to investigate the correlation between slip modulus and density. However, no
clear trend was established due to the limited dataset and the narrow range of density
among specimens, making it difficult. Predicting the slip characteristics of timber joints can
be challenging due to several factors. In case of laboratory testing, as in the present study,
the controlled environment ensures high precision in contrast to the variability that can be
experienced in real constructions. This may justify the conservatism of the analytical
model, as it introduces a safety margin to account for construction tolerances. Besides
that, the model is originally designed for steel fasteners, where slip is primarily governed
by the connected timber members due to the high stiffness of steel. However, when
using timber fasteners, both the dowel and connected members will contribute to the
deformation behavior, deviating from the assumptions underlying the analytical model.
These findings address the first two research questions reserved to this part of the thesis:
Q3.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the shear capacity of
connections in all softwood timber, considering the dowel effect contribution based on the
European Yield Model?
Q3.2: How accurately does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the slip char-
acteristics of all-timber connections?
In general, all connections demonstrated great deformation capacity and ductile behavior
during testing. The performance of softwood dowels was evaluated relative to commonly
used hardwood species in all-timber connections and DLT by applying the corrected ex-
pressions developed throughout the thesis. As expected, the high-density hardwoods,
namely Oak and Beech, demonstrated higher shear capacities. However, softwood still
represents a viable alternative for applications where moderate shear capacity is accept-
able and larger dowel diameters are feasible. The findings indicate that for softwood dow-
els in C24 to have a similar capacity to that of Beech and Oak, the dowel diameter must
be increased by approximately 33%. Addressing the third and final research question of
this part:
Q3.3: How do softwood dowels perform in terms of load-bearing capacity and to what
extent?

This part investigates the mechanical performance of DLT panels with dowels and lamel-

panel configurations with 3 specimens in each configuration to allow for a comparative
study of the mechanical performance and behavior when introducing butt-joints to accom-

the relative change compared to the previous panel. The geometry and dowel spacing
are kept similar for a fair comparison. The three panels consist of the following:
Type 1: Reference panel This panel represents a typical DLT panel and serves as a
baseline for the other types. The lamellas are kept at full length and laminated using soft-
wood dowels. This configuration is expected to exhibit similar behavior to that of the three
individual lamellas, due to a uniform distribution of the load across the lamellas.
Type 2: Introducing butt-joints This panel differs from the reference configuration by
introducing a butt-joint in the middle lamella, which creates a discontinuity in the load
path, limiting its contribution to the structural behavior of the panel. This configuration is
intended to assess whether the dowels can be activated to bridge the discontinuity and
activate the middle lamella, or whether the lamella will act as pure dead load. Emphasis is
placed on evaluating the relative effective stiffness, hence the joint is placed at mid-span
where the bending moment is at maximum, for the most unfavourable conditions.
Type 3: Introducing interlocking system This panel differs from the type 2 configuration
by introducing a longitudinal interlocking system (tongue and groove system), as proposed
by WKW Wales [16]. The purpose of this is to counteract the decrease in stiffness caused
by the butt-joint by improving the interaction between lamellas. The longitudinal joint is
intended to support the dowels by limiting slip and improving force transfer across the
discontinuity, resulting in a more effective contribution from the middle lamella to the overal
stiffness.

Experimental procedure
The experimental procedure is presented, outlining the process of conducting the tests
step by step. The procedure is carried out equally for all test specimens to ensure quality
and consistency of the collected data.
Step 1 - Preparation of test specimens: All test specimens were prepared in accor-
dance with the procedure described in section 9.1.1.
Step 2 - Calibration of LVDT extensometers: Displacements were measured using
LVDT transducers. Prior to testing, the transducers were calibrated using a precision
calibration jig to establish the correlation between the transducer output in VDC and the
corresponding displacements, ensuring reliability and accuracy of the recorded measure-
ments. This step was carried out for all 6 transducers.
Step 3 - Mounting fixtures to testing machine: Loading fixtures and support fixtures
were mounted onto the UTM, ensuring proper alignment.
Step 4 - Retrieval and mounting of test specimens: Specimens were collected from
the climate chamber, and steel brackets were mounted onto the specimens to provide at-
tachment points for the LVDT transducers. The specimens were subsequently positioned
in the test setup.
Step 5 - Experimenting: The experiment was conducted until rupture of the specimen. At
the end of the experiment, the crosshead was raised to allow for removal of the specimen,
which was then returned to the climate chamber to avoid large changes in MC before post-
processing. Steps 4 and 5 were repeated for each specimen until completion of all tests.
The data was subsequently exported from the data acquisition software in CSV format for
later processing.
Step 6 - Post processing: After testing, all specimens are cut into smaller pieces for the
determination of the moisture content at test time, according to the oven drying method
in DS/EN 13183 [46] as described in Chapter 5.

9.4
Discussion and conclusion
A comparative study of three all-softwood DLT panel configurations was conducted to
assess the effect on the structural performance when incorporating butt-joints to accom-
modate reclaimed timber. As expected, the mechanical performance was compromised
when introducing a discontinuity in the midspan of the middle lamella, resulting in notable
reductions in both load-bearing capacity and stiffness. However, reductions appear to
be lower than the theoretical expectations, suggesting a partial engagement of the mid-
dle lamella, despite the interruption in its axial continuity. This may be attributed to the
dowels bridging the joints, and thereby enabling partial transfer of the longitudinal forces
induced by bending. The engagement of the dowels near the butt-joints is evident by the
observed shear failure in one of the test specimens’ dowels.
Only three specimens were tested for each type, limiting the ability to draw a definitive con-
clusion. Despite this, the results provide a general indication of the behavior. Based on
the present results, the introduction of a longitudinal interlocking system did not improve
structural performance. On the contrary, panels exhibited a slightly lower stiffness com-
pared to Type 2 panels. Mechanically, the interlocking joints were intended to enhance
force transfer across lamellas by improving interaction between system components, miti-
gating the negative effects of the butt-joints. The lack of improvement may be attributed to
insufficient contact pressure across the profile surfaces, with the system relying solely on
friction fitting of the dowels to hold the lamellas together. Moreover, a perfect fit was not
achieved in the production, with small gaps between lamellas, allowing for slip and con-
sequently counteracting the intent of the concept. Another plausible explanation is that
shear transfer is governed by the dowels, making the interlocking system act secondarily.
This addresses the second research question of this part, being:
Q4.2: Can the negative effects from butt-joints be mitigated through enhanced lamella
interaction with longitudinal interlocking?
Nonetheless, both Type 2 and Type 3 panels demonstrated greater mechanical perfor-
mance than expected, which opens up for further discussion of the underlying reason
behind this. To explore this, a hypothesis is presented in Section 9.4.0.1 to address the
observed behavior.
An interesting observation was made as a consequence of the manufacturing process.
During the preparation of specimen BS3.2 a gap of approximately 3 mm was present at the
butt-joint due to imprecise manufacturing. At the same time, this specimen demonstrated
a stiffness value of about 15% lower than the mean of the panel type. This observation
indicates that a tighter butt-joint enhances the stiffness, likely due to frictional resistance
and end-bearing compression between members.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the performance and mechanical behavior of softwood
dowels, all-timber connections, and DLT incorporating reclaimed timber. The project was
carried out through an extensive experimental campaign consisting of four main parts,
addressing the subject on both component and system level. Particular focus was placed
on validating the new design provisions for hardwood dowels, introduced in the draft revi-
sion of Eurocode 5 [1], and assessing their applicability to softwood dowels. An additional
objective was to explore the integration of softwood dowels into DLT, with a specific fo-
cus on the potential use of reclaimed timber. Through this series of experimental studies,
valuable insights were gained, contributing to the understanding and field of all-timber
connections and DLT systems. All driven by a strong motivation for more sustainable
building practices. While each part throughout the thesis was concluded individually and
references are generally made to the respective part, this final chapter brings the findings
together by addressing the research questions briefly and directly.
Q1.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the yield moment
of softwood dowels?
A reasonable alignment between experimental and analytical values was observed, but
with some discrepancy. The analytical model yielded conservative results for all dowel
diameters by underestimating the capacity of smaller diameters (8-12 mm) by approxi-
mately 24% and larger diameters (15-25 mm) by approximately 11%, which suggests that
the analytical model applies to some degree for softwood dowels.
Q1.2: How can the existing analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 be improved to pre-
dict the yield moment more precisely?
The discrepancy between experimental and analytical values was addressed by propos-
ing a correction factor to the analytical model to account for softwood dowels. The cor-
rection factor, βy = 0, 82, ensured alignment with the lower bound of the experimental
results, reducing the underestimation of the capacity to approximately 13,5% for smaller
diameters (8-12 mm) and approximately 1,8% for larger diameters (15-25 mm).
Q1.3: To what extent does the approach in DS/EN 409, based on a rotation criterion,
represent the actual yield behavior of softwood dowels?
The rotation-based criterion displayed a poor correlation with the actual yield behavior, pri-
marily because the mechanical behavior of timber deviates from the assumptions based
on steel fasteners. Two alternatives to the current method were proposed, one defining
the yield moment as 78% of the maximum recorded moment, supported by a low CoV
of 1,2%, and the other defining a new rotation criterion based on the dowel diameter, al-
though this showed a weaker consistency with the experimental data.

Q2.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the embedment
strength when using softwood dowels?
The analytical model displayed conservative predictions of the embedment strength, with
an unsatisfactory precision having a relative difference between experimental and ana-
lytical values of 32,4%. While a conservative prediction is preferred, effort was made to
refine the discrepancy.
Q2.2: How can the existing analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 be improved to pre-
dict the embedment strength more precisely?
The discrepancy between experimental and analytical values was addressed by propos-
ing a correction factor to the analytical model. The correction factor, αe = 1, 29, ensured
alignment with the lower bound of the experimental results, reducing the underestimation
of the capacity to approximately 12%.
Q3.1: How well does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the shear capac-
ity of connections in all softwood timber, considering the dowel effect contribution
based on the European Yield Model?
The predictive values obtained by the EYM showed reasonable alignment with the ex-
perimental results, although some discrepancies were observed. Failure mode II was
overestimated by 10,6% while failure mode III / IV were underestimated by 15,2%. Sug-
gesting that corrections are necessary to account for the behavior of softwood dowels. To
address this, corrections of the analytical models were proposed by replacing the current
correction factor accounting for material differences in steel-to-timber connections and
introducing new factors accounting for softwood dowels. The resulting factors for failure
mode II and III / IV were α(d) = 0, 92 and γ(f) = 1, 24 respectively. The limited dataset
is acknowledged by having the correction factors fit the lower bound of the experimental
results.
Q3.2: How accurately does the analytical model in prEN 1995-1-1 predict the slip
characteristics of all-timber connections?
The prediction of joint stiffness (slip modulus) using the current analytical model resulted
in a highly conservative estimate. Despite that, this was deemed appropriate given the
large differences in precision between laboratory testing and real-life construction, which
justifies a significant safety margin of the analytical model.
Q3.3: How do softwood dowels perform in terms of load-bearing capacity and to
what extent?
Softwood dowels exhibited great deformation capacity and ductile behavior during test-
ing. Their strength performance was evaluated against conventional hardwood species,
namely, Beech and Oak. As expected, softwood dowels demonstrated a lower shear
capacity, however, with acceptable performance, making softwood connectors a viable
alternative in applications where moderate shear capacity and the use of larger dowel
diameters are feasible. With findings suggesting that, to have a similar capacity to the
highlighted hardwood species, the diameter must be increased by approximately 33%.

Q4.1: To what extent does the incorporation of butt-joints, as a means of using re-
claimed timber, affect the structural performance of DLT?
The test results revealed an approximate reduction of 20% in strength and stiffness prop-
erties with the introduction of butt-joints. While this is not negligible, it still highlights a
promising opportunity for incorporating reclaimed timber and omitting glued finger joints
in DLT, by displaying a contribution from the butt-jointed lamellas and not acting as pure
dead load. The trade-off between efficiency and sustainability is evident, but may be ac-
ceptable in applications prioritizing sustainability.
Q4.2: Can the negative effects from butt-joints be mitigated through enhanced
lamella interaction with longitudinal interlocking?
Based on the present results, the longitudinal interlocking system did not improve the
structural performance. On the contrary, panels exhibited a slightly lower stiffness rela-
tive to the other butt-jointed panel. This might be attributed to several factors, including
an increased slip due to imprecise manufacturing of the interlocking profiles, a lack of
contact pressure between lamellas, and the profiles acting secondarily to the dowels in
the transfer of forces.
10.1
Future Work
Some of the findings in the present study highlight interesting areas for future research.
The last three parts of the study are primarily limited by the number of test specimens.
The proposed corrections to the analytical models could be further validated and strength-
ened through a larger experimental dataset or through numerical investigations based on
the experimental results provided in the appendices of the present study. Furthermore,
all investigations in this study were limited to the short-term, immediate response of the
connections. A valuable area for future work would be to examine the long-term perfor-
mance of all-softwood timber connections, particularly focusing on the time-dependent
creep behavior.
Moreover, additional effort could be made to explore the implementation of butt-jointed
lamellas in DLT to accommodate reclaimed timber. The mechanisms proposed in this
study to explain the structural contribution of the butt-jointed lamellas could be further val-
idated by testing various dowel configurations, or, for example, by incorporating wooden
nails to increase the internal moment arm of the ”End-bearing moment bridge” mecha-
nism, to assess if it offers a meaningful contribution. The results in the present study
suggest that the longitudinal interlocking system does not provide any meaningful ben-
efits to the structural performance. However, alternative end-to-end joinery techniques,
such as traditional carpentry joints, could be explored as potential methods to bridge the
discontinuity of a butt-jointed lamella more effectively.

placement at yield, the max moment from the max recorded force, the moment of 40%
and 80% of the max moment (used for regression analysis), and the yield moment corre-
sponding to the intersection of the regression lines, as described in the respective part.

D.5
Prototyping of DLT panels
This appendix is added to document the prototyping of DLT panels, which were carried
out to validate ideas and identify potential errors in the manufacturing process before
manufacturing the final DLT panels. It was chosen to prototype three different types of
panels, in which one parameter is changed at a time to assess the effect of the change.
• Type 1: Reference model
Plane DLT panel with full boards. This is thought to act as a reference model for the
rest of the panels.
• Type 2: Introducing butt-joints
This panel introduces a butt-joint in the middle lamella creating a discontinuity in the
panel.
• Type: Introducing interlocking profiles
This panel introduces interlocking profiles to try and reduce the slip and increase
the overall stiffness.
Moreover, a quick study was mode on the size of the drilled holes. As mentioned in
the report the Eurocode 5 draft [1] states that the drilled hole should be carried out as
described in equation (2.1), it was tested whether this requirement is feasible for wooden
dowels, more specifically softwood dowels.
D.5.1
Evaluating size of drilled holes
6 dowels with a nominal diameter of 19 mm were prepared, and six holes were drilled in
a piece of timber as shown in figure D.3, ranging from 0,79 to 1.0 times the diameter of
the dowel.
Figure D.3: Overview of drilled holes testing for 19 mm dowels
Softwood Dowels and Dowel-Laminated Timber
145
==PAGE:161==
The dowels were initially inserted using a hammer. No issues occurred for the first two
holes (19 mm and 18,5 mm). However, from the third hole (18 mm), additional rounding
of the dowel was required, and the fit was excessively tight. For the remaining holes (17,
16, and 15 mm), insertion by hand was no longer possible. A hydraulic press was then
used, which ended up causing significant damage to the dowels, making them unusable.
See figure D.4 which shows the inserted dowels.
Figure D.4: Results for drilled holes testing for 19 mm dowels
The quick investigation shows that the requirements set in the Eurocode are not feasible
for wooden dowels, or at least softwood dowels. For this thesis, a drill bit diameter of 18,5
mm is chosen for the DLT panels, as it was found most suitable.
D.5.2
Cutting and assembling DLT panels
To begin with, all timber pieces were cut using a miter saw, and the required number of
holes were drilled out using a bench drill. From this, it became obvious that the small
workspace at the bench drill would make it challenging and time-consuming to drill the
holes with satisfactory precision, even when manufacturing small prototypes. With this
knowledge, it was tested whether the holes could be drilled using a CNC machine, which
proved to be highly efficient and easily programmable.
