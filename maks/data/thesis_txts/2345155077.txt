Abstract 
This M.Sc thesis introduces the concept of indoor ventilation and the related techniques used for such 
purposes. At the start, mainly two hypothesis were made regarding the use of experimental equipment and 
the Coanda effect due to the presence of suspended ceiling (baffle system). To further validate this idea, a 
lab experiment was done in DTU lab facility where a mock room was designed to study indoor air flow.  The 
equipment used in the experiment was a laser, simple software PIVlab together with a standard 
smartphone camera.  
The whole setup provides visualization effects of indoor air flow in shape of recorded videos and later some 
frames are used in PIVlab to get the velocity and streamline patterns. From the experiments it was seen 
that the idea of using laser, a smartphone camera to record the experiment and use of simple software 
PIVlab was a current one.

Chapter 1 
1.1 Introduction  
Some of the most important parameters to consider regarding building design are thermal comfort and 
indoor air quality. With constant fresh air supply, comfortable and healthy indoor climate conditions can be 
achieved.  
In relation to indoor environment quality, ventilation has been widely used and discussed in many studies. 
The performance of ventilation and air distribution system does not only influence the indoor air quality 
directly[1], [2], but also strongly affect the total energy consumption of the building [3]. 
In ventilation and air distribution systems, there are airflow patterns such as trajectory, impingement, 
separation, circulation, reattachment, buoyancy, vortices etc. and airflow field such as temperature, velocity 
and pressure. 
 
When designing a ventilation system, it is important to be aware of the ventilation effectiveness and to avoid 
elevated air velocities in occupied zones. Design and practice are often not the same and it therefore leads 
to problems like draught. 
 
Numerical simulation using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) models is the most prevalent research 
method nowadays for studies dealing with the performance of ventilation and air distribution system in 
buildings. With computer technology, it is still not possible to predict the structure of indoor airflows with 
the distinct characteristics of low speed turbulence. 
 
Therefore, experimental measurements still play an important role for indoor airflow studies, and high 
quality experimental data plays an important role in the validation and development of numerical models.  
Experimental data is one of the best ways to understand the local airflow fields around devices in ventilated 
rooms, such as heat sources and draught problem that are still largely unknown [4].  
 
The indoor airflow is not easy to measure accurately because of large-scale, three dimensional, turbulent and 
unsteady flow. Sun and Zhang [5] summarized the most common measurement methods for indoor airflow, 
where the measurement techniques are categorized into point-by-point measurements and whole-field 
measurements.  
 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
Point-by-point method usually refer to measurement techniques that gather only one-point velocity 
information. Such as thermal anemometers, which may either disturb the flow field, as it is an intrusive 
method. To identify such as draught problem, the thermal anemometers are moved randomly around the 
occupied zone, which will disturb the flow field. They therefore, are not the best choice for indoor airflow 
measurement. 
 
Whole-field techniques usually refer to optical velocimetry and regarded to be an effective alternate tool for 
measuring indoor airflow distribution. Optical velocimetry can obtain the velocities and related statistical 
information in a globe domain without disturbing the airflows. Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), Particle 
Streak Velocimetry (PSV) and Schlieren photography are whole-field measurement techniques. 
As Whole-field, PIV and PSV techniques have been applied more and more in the recent years. Sandberg [6] 
reviewed the applications of PIV, PSV and Schlieren photography techniques in ventilated rooms in detail. 
Cao et al. [7] excellently summarized the typical PIV technology and main applications for indoor airflow 
study; the whole-field technique is becoming a powerful measurement tool for airflow study [8]. 
 
Relatively low velocities with high turbulence levels and in most cases, normally characterize the room airflow 
by unsteady behavior in a relatively large enclosure [5]. 
 
A good energy efficient ventilation system is important not only to provide comfortable, healthy indoor 
environment necessary for the well-being and productive work performance of the occupants, but also to 
reduce the energy use in buildings and the associated impact of CO2 emissions to the environment.  
A ventilation system could be characterized by fresh air being supplied into an indoor space and polluted and 
humid air being exhausted. This process can either be done by mechanical or natural ventilation, which 
utilizes the pressure differences or temperature differences between the ambient environment and the 
indoor space. A proper ventilation system is one of the key elements, when designing a building. Since 
ventilation is needed to obtain a satisfactory indoor environment, it is evaluated on the basis of thermal 
comfort level and indoor air quality. 
For thermal comfort level, it is commonly based on the operative temperature that is described as the mean 
value of the air temperature and the mean radiant temperature. However, the mean velocity of air, surface 
temperature of the floor, vertical air temperature gradient and the asymmetry of radiation can also result in 
occupant’s discomfort.  
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
The occupant’s experience of indoor air quality depends on the level of pollutants in the indoor air, relative 
humidity and odor. The indoor air quality is commonly assessed by CO2 concentration, since the occupants 
emit it and it is proportional to their activity level. High levels of CO2 can lead to tiredness, nausea and fatigue, 
however, CO2 does not cause serious health issues. Relative humidity depends on the water vapor content 
of indoor air with respect to the air temperature and it is recommended to achieve a relative humidity in the 
range of 30-70 %, as the occupants experience discomfort related to static electricity and dryness at levels 
lower than 30 %. When relative humidity exceeds 70 %, it becomes difficult for the occupants to evaporate 
heat from their body. Moreover, the relative humidity also affects the durability of the building, since high 
temperatures and a high level of relative humidity level inside leads to an increased vapor pressure difference 
in comparison to the outdoor environment. This creates depressurization, which forces the humid and warm 
air through the construction, which can lead to condensation and increase the risk of mold formation. Thus, 
ventilation of indoor spaces is essential to reach an acceptable comfort level and a healthy environment for 
the occupants. 
By ventilating the building, the supplied air can create different types of air jets depending on the local 
conditions. The air jet can be described as free jet or wall jet, which sticks to a surface in the indoor space. 
The jets can occur both in two-dimensional and three-dimensional forms. Moreover, the air jets are usually 
colder than the surrounding air, thus the jets are affected by the temperature conditions. In addition, velocity 
of the airflow has an impact on the distribution of the air. Hence, it is important that the occupied zone 
obtains the optimal climate with respect to the air temperature, air velocity, velocity gradients, mean radiant 
temperature and asymmetric radiant temperature. Outside the occupied zone, it is possible to have higher 
velocity levels and higher or lower temperatures without affecting the comfort of the occupants. 
Furthermore, it is not desired to have air jets hitting the occupied zone directly to avoid the experience of 
draught. Different types of ventilations are described later to give an understanding of what kind of 
ventilation systems are mostly used.  
The purpose of this study is to develop methods that could be deployed to observe the air movements in an 
indoor space with ventilation. This paper presents study of whole-field techniques PIV, for airflow 
measurement under experimental conditions. The experiment was conducted in non-isothermal condition.

2.1 Objectives 
Measuring techniques for mean air velocity and turbulent flow can be classified as intrusive or non-intrusive 
and as point- or whole-field measurement techniques. 
 
Intrusive measurement techniques as thermal anemometers can cause disturbances to the flow, which can 
in some cases affect the measurement results, as equipment needs to be placed at the measurement 
location. For non-intrusive measurement techniques as PIV, measuring equipment needs not to be placed at 
measuring location. 
 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate with whole-field measurement and non-intrusive Particle Image 
Velocimetry (PIV), the flow patterns of various flows in an indoor environment using a full-scale mockup.  
In cases where air is blown in rooms with mechanical ventilation, there are often several complaints about 
the draught. To identify the reason of draught is difficult but with PIV, the air movement in a large scale can 
be recorded and processed by digital image software to quantify the flow patterns. 
Literature shows that whole field techniques are new and constantly under development, so that we 
understand the principle involved in the techniques. By the whole-field measuring techniques, the 
measurements are undertaken instantaneously at different locations.  
To record the velocity components, we use PIV which is based on adding tracer particles and have an impact 
on the air motions. To capture this, a digital camera can be used, so that their displacement during a specified 
interval can be recorded. Moreover, the displacement of group of particles can be recorded. Further detail 
about PIV is described in methodology, in chapter6.   
Initially, PIV provides high resolution information over small regions, which is why PIV is suitable for 
documenting details in room flow. Because of its ability to provide detailed information of small regions, PIV 
has been used to document details of the flow around the human body by [9]. 
In indoor climate, air is moving at different speed and in different directions in a room. The air in a room can 
be either self-created using ventilation system or due to the thermal load and cold surfaces. Human senses 
are intelligent enough to feel moving objects but without being able to see where they come from. Air and

moisture are transparent and their flow patterns are invisible, in order to visualize the airflows in a room, 
there is a need of special tools and methods that can show the air flow pattern.  
To investigate the performance of air movement in a room with diffuser as supply devices, whole-field 
measurement PIV are used. Measurement and numerical prediction are presented by the airflow patterns, 
air velocity, temperature distribution, air temperature, and turbulence intensity as well as the thermal

The first hypothesis is with a whole-field technique PIV to quantify airflows in rooms with laser, standard

The second hypothesis is that due to the presence of suspended celling (baffle system) and mixing ventilation, 
the throw length from the air diffuser is substantially shortened, i.e. Coanda effect disappears.

This chapter presents an overview of ventilation types and a detail about mixing ventilation. As mixing 
involves diffuser, so all related topics are presented in detail here under.  
3.1 Ventilation 
When supplying air into the indoor space, different methods can be applied that are highly dependent on 
the type of installed air supply diffuser and the injection of air. There are serval different type of devices that 
can be deployed to ventilate a room. Moreover, the distribution also depends on the ventilation type such 
as mixing, displacement, diffuse ventilation or confluent jet ventilation. The most commonly used mechanical 
ventilation is mixing ventilation (MV) and displacement ventilation (DV) [9]. These types of ventilation 
systems, have in some cases caused problems with comfort. Therefore, this requires a need to develop 
experimental studies that considers the problem related to new ventilation techniques. 
3.1.1 Mixing ventilation 
By applying mixing ventilation, air is supplied with a rather high velocity outside the occupied zone. The air 
usually supplied from the ceiling or the wall, through suitably designed diffusers to ensure mixing of the 
supplied air and the air in the room and to ensure that the air is evenly distributed in the room. The high air 
velocity will provide a significant amount of airflow. Thus, the velocity of the supplied air should be kept at a 
level which makes sure that the mixing of the air is efficient and that the velocity of the air to be reduced to 
a desired level, before the flow enters the occupied zone. This requires the diffusers to be efficient when 
concerning the supply velocity and mixing.  
Mixing is the most commonly used form of ventilation with ceiling or wall-mounted diffusers. Mixing can be 
deployed for both heating and cooling of the indoor space. However, it requires considerably great amount 
of energy to force heated air into the occupied zone when the heated air has a lower density than the ambient 
air. This means, a higher air velocity is required as the height of the room and the air supply temperature is 
increased. In a room with a high floor to ceiling height, it is usually necessary to blow the air directly and 
vertically downwards. When cooled air with a higher density than the air in the occupied zone is supplied 
from the ceiling during times with high internal heat loads, excessive velocities can be created in the zone.  
The velocity in the occupied zone depends on the supply air velocity of the diffuser, the convection of the 
internal heat sources as occupants, lighting and equipment, and the distribution and supply pattern of the 
diffuser. When supplying both hot and cold air from the same diffuser from the ceiling, it becomes difficult

in many cases to simultaneously meet the requirements of temperature gradient, ventilation efficiency and 
velocity. Furthermore, supplied cold air temperature or large flow rates will increase the risk of draught in 
the occupied zone, when high thermal loads are present in the room. [10]

3.1.2 Throw length: 
Throw length indicates the distance where air velocity of the core of air jet has reached a specific limit 
velocity. The air usually leaves the luminaires at a rate of 2-3 m/s, which reduces towards the occupied zone 
due to resistance from mixing with the stagnant air in the room. The ventilating air may have similar 
temperature as the room air (isothermal conditions) or it can be the supply air from the diffuser with lower 
temperatures (non-isothermal), 12 K below the temperature. Due to isothermal conditions under gravity, the 
air due to its higher density moves downwards and as a result, mixing lowers the air temperature in the room. 
Supply air fittings must be located and arranged properly to provide a good aeration of the room while the 
building code requirements regarding draught in the occupied zone should be followed. The throw should 
be neither too long nor too short but actually optimal in the range. [10] 
Throw length L0.2 is normally defined by the diffuser manufacturer in the laboratory setting in isothermal 
discharge conditions at speed 0.2 m/s, and it means that supply air temperature is equal to ambient room 
temperature.[10]

Non-isothermal rays have relatively high or low temperature compared to the room air. The throw distance

the room air, so that the beam moves forward, and deflects more and more downwards during the

3.1.5 Coanda effect  
When air is blown along a boundary surface for example a ceiling, there is a vacuum between the jet and the 
ceiling that makes jet to "stick" to the ceiling, which is called the Coanda effect. This effect is of great 
importance, particularly when supplying the chilled air. In order to achieve the greatest possible Coanda 
effect, air to be supplied in small unit quantities with large spread of the ceiling as possible and with the 
greatest possible speed and the effect disappears completely when the speed is below 0.35 m/s. Thus, it is 
always important to supply air from the diffuser in a fully 360 pattern without covers on either side, as is 
the case in the experiment. With orientation to a ceiling surface, the beam will try to "adhere" to the ceiling 
surface, by creating a vacuum between the jet and the ceiling as no air can be supplied to compensation for 
the amount of room air which is supplied by the jet. This phenomenon, called the Coanda effect, provide an 
increase in the throw length of √2 (1.4) with respect to the throw distance for a free jet streams, and the 
velocity rate of the jet decreases relatively slowly. For Coanda effect to occur, the air speed must be at least

The following chapter deals with the background and the literature review related to PIV.  
4.1 Historical PIV background 
 
The observation of nature and the phenomena that occurs in nature is very interesting. In response to that 
many experimental tools and measurement techniques have been developed and are still under developing.  
 
Ludwig Prandtl developed one of the first flow visualization techniques in 1904. He prepared a water tunnel 
driving the flow manually by rotating a wheel blade. This tunnel included an upper and lower section 
separated by a horizontal wall in which the water is recirculated from the upper open channel, back through 
the lower closed channel in which the flow pattern was observed.  
 
The flow is visualized by distributing a suspension of mica particles on the surface of the water. Ludwig 
Prandtl studied the shape of the flow in steady and unsteady flow (at the beginning of the flow) with this 
method. With the advantage to change a number of parameters in the experiment (model, approach, flow 
rate, stable-unstable flow) gave Prandtl understanding of many basic functions of unstable flow phenomena. 
At that time, only a qualitative description of the flow field was possible. No quantitative data on flow rate, 
etc., could be achieved. At present, it is possible to extract quantitative information about the instantaneous 
flow rate from the flow field and some similar pictures that were available to Prandtl. 
A copy of Ludwig Prandtl water tunnel with a flash lamp for lighting and a video camera were used to achieve 
the visualization of the flow using aluminum particles distributed on the water surface. The scientific and 
technical progress in the last 20 years in optics, lasers, electronics, video and computer technology was 
needed to develop a technique for qualitative flow visualization to such an extent that it can be used for 
quantitative measurement of complex instantaneous velocity fields [4]. One of these methods is the Particle 
image velocimetry (PIV), which is a non-invasive method for measuring the flow pattern.

5.1 Literature review on PIV  
 
Literature states the term particle image velocimetry (PIV) in 1980 [7]. 
After 1980s, there has been a scientific and technical improvement in lasers, image recording and evaluation 
techniques. With help of computer techniques, we have been led to the further expansion of PIV technique. 
Most importantly PIV has become a powerful tool for studying flow in indoor atmosphere.  
PIV is defined as a technique to reach quantitative measurement of immediate turbulent velocity fields as an 
extension of quality flow visualization and PIV tracks the tracer particles seeded in the air to obtain the whole 
velocity field of the given measurement area.  
Literature states that a typical PIV system consists of a multi-pulsed laser system, use of digital camera (one 
or more, “2D-2C PIV system use 1 camera and 2D-3C uses 2 cameras, tomographic PIV uses more than 3 
cameras), which has to be synchronized with the lasers and a computer to use for control the system and for 
analyzing the data, which appears.

5.1.1 Illumination systems 
Regarding illumination system, the most common used is a double-pulsed ND, defined as Yag lasers system. 
It has an articulated delivery arm, which can generate a green light sheet consisting of 532nm wavelength 
(greenlight). The light sheet optics detected at the end of the arm can be positioned at any angle to generate 
a light sheet and illuminate the tracer particles in the measured region of importance. The main element of 
light sheet optics is one or more cylinder-shaped lens to focus the light sheet to appeal depth and field angle.  
Literature state that to guarantee the measured flow field can keep in a plane, the thickness of the light sheet 
in the measurement areas has to be around 1-3 mm or less than this. While using a thin light sheet as the 
illumination the method can be challenging for measuring a strong 3-dimensional flow filed in a substantial 
indoor environment [7]. This method cause that some particles recorded by the cameras in the first frame 
may move out of the measured plane and cannot be apprehended in the next frame, this situation will limit 
the precision of PIV measurement to the regions of the thin plane flow.

the tracer particles between the two pulses, but it also needs to be short enough due to avoid particles

5.1.2 Image recording devices 
Regarding image recording devices, the most common image recording devices used in indoor airflow 
measurements are coupled charged devices also defined CCD, cameras and complementary metal oxide 
semiconductor cameras. CCD cameras are known for having been used in PIV experiments for their improved 
three-dimensional resolution, convenience data transmission and image processing, minimum exposure 
time, high light sensitivity at 532nm and low background noise.  
CCD is defined as an electronic sensor element which can convert photons into electrons, and the CCD sensor 
in the camera consist of an array of many individual CCD elements, also known as pixels. The camera’s sensor 
resolution has a range from 2M (1600 pixels x 1200 pixels) to 29M (6576 pixels x 4384 pixels) and the 
equivalent frame frequency is cited from 35 Hz to 2 Hz [7]. A dynamic arrange of 8 or 10 bits data output per 
pixel is mentioned sufficient for most PIV purposes.  
Concerning to the time-resolved measurement for acquiring accurate turbulent information, using a high-
speed CMOS camera is better choice than a CCD camera, because high speed recording is based on recently 
develop CMOS sensors allows for possession in the kHz range, which is good for turbulence study. Moreover, 
a CMOS sensor allows for the recording and handling of up to a few thousand frames per second at 
acceptable noise level, but in trade of the sensor resolve. 
Literature states that low spatial resolution has become the main obstacle for CMOS camera to completely 
replace the CCD cameras. This is a disadvantage in the applications of CMOS cameras only due to small-scale 
measurements. Though CCD cameras are still the main image recording devices for indoor PIV measurement 
because of its better image quality and wider applied range.

measures the velocities of tracer particles instead of real airflow velocities. That is why the size of tracer 
particles should not only be small enough to achieve good tracking performance of the turbulence, but it also 
has to be large enough to distribute adequate light signal for image recording devices [7].

The (St) of tracer articles for room airflows is recommended to be less than 0.1, and the scattering 
characteristics of tracer particles is also necessary to consider mainly when large-scale PIV measurements 
are being declared. The spreading features of the particles can be quantified by the scattering cross section 
Cs, which is defined as a function of the ratio of the particle diameter to the laser wavelength for round 
particles. Large particles can give exponential strong light signal due to larger measuring area and higher 
signal-to-noise ratio.  
That is why the size of the tracer particles should be enhanced so that there is a balance between the pursuing 
conduct and the scattering characteristics. For the study of turbulent or high speed airflow in a small region 
the acceptable tracking ability is more crucial. The scattering demand should be more concerned when 
studying the air distribution in a full-size room. 
Regarding tracer particles used in indoor environment it is necessary that these are harmless to human due 
to environmental protection. In regards to this, the most used airflow measurements for indoor use are 
Oil/FEHS droplets, theatrical fog and helium-filled-soap bubbles[7].  
Oil/DEHS droplets are good for small-scale turbulence measurements due to their good tracking capacity and 
high steadiness.  
Theatrical fog fluid consists of a proprietary blend of glycol/glycerine and water. The produced fog is more 
suitable for uninterrupted local seeding because of its high concentration, consistency and generation 
amount. This procedure of smoke generation is typically inexpensive but cannot be reliable to take lengthy 
measurements. 
Lastly helium-filled-soap bubbles are used in large-scale PIV measurements, because of their neutrally 
buoyant assets and high scattering efficiency than above mentioned particles.  
The main goal is that the generators for indoor airflow should produce particles with a monodisperse size 
distribution and at a constant high production rate so that it is possible to meet the three-dimensional 
resolution of PIV experiment. The most common condensation aerosol for indoor application is a fog 
machine, which is sufficient to generate large amount of droplets or theatrical fog. Such fog machine uses 
whichever an inert gas or an electric pump to push mineral oil, glycol or glycol and water mixture into a heat 
exchanger, where the solution is heated. Afterwards this vaporized fluid is dismissed through the nozzle into 
the airflow where it gets mixed with cool air, and then condensed into an aerosol consisting of loads of fine 
particles. Commercial theatrical fog machines are inexpensive and they produce a lot of particles, though the 
generation rate of this kind of generator manages to be unsteady and discontinuous because of the limit of 
==PAGE:23==
 
22 
heating capability. That is why it is not easy to conduct long time continuous measurement using fog 
machines.  
It is important that particles are seeded into airflow with adequate, steady and spatial uniform concentration, 
and a high seeding concentration is preferred for better measurement spatial resolution, though extremely 
high density seeding can lead to poor spatial resolve because of the bad scattering performance. It is 
mentioned that the ideal concentration of the tracer particles is 10 to 20 particles in an interrogation area. It 
is suggested to use a particle counter to analyze the particle size distribution during the measuring practice 
[7].  
5.1.4 Image evaluation techniques  
The technique is based on the direct determination of the two fundamental dimensions of the particle 
velocity mentioned as displacement and time [7]. 
This technique is important due to affect the displacement information from raw particle images. It is 
preferred to capture 2 images on 2 separate frames and thereafter perform multistep cross-correlation 
analysis. The cross-correlation provides the scale and direction of the velocity vector without uncertainty. 
The correlation methods are ordinarily based on digital Fast Fourier Transform also called FFT algorithms for 
calculating the correlation functions [7]. 
The adaptive correlation is the most used evaluation and it calculates velocity vectors with an initial 
interrogation area (IA) of the size N times the size of the final IA and uses the intermediary results as 
information for the next IA of smaller size until the final IA size is reached. IA is mentioned as a sub-area in 
the recorded image and its dimensional setting directly determines the spatial resolution and accuracy of the 
measurement. When there is a smaller IA size and high overlap ratio it is possible to achieve higher spatial 
resolution. On the other hand, it requires a higher quality image recordings and consume longer computing 
time. The size of IA is set to be 32 pixels x 32 pixels or 64 pixels x 64 pixels with overlaps of 50% or 25% for 
indoor PIV applications. The adaptive PIV interrogation is more common and further progressive because of 
the automatic correlation algorithm for calculation velocity vectors based on particle images. The adaptive 
PIV can achieve higher accuracy and spatial resolution results than adaptive correlation though consuming 
more computing sources [7]. 
Another declared evaluation for indoor applications is 2D or 3D Least Squares matching (LSM). This is a grey-
level tracking technique, which presents translation, deformation and rotation of the IA. The LSM algorithm 
associates gray value information of an IA in the first time step and with the grey value information in the 
subsequent time step. LSM also takes the first order terms of fluid motion into account. This is why LSM can 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 
gain a more accurate calculation of the velocity gradient tensor, the rotation tensor and the deformation 
tensor without any conventions and influence. Due to longer computation time and requirement of high 
quality particles images LSM does not receive much devotion according to correlation techniques.  
5.2 Summary of PIV techniques in indoor airflow 
PIV can be used to reveal the structure of a turbulent attached plane jet in the entrainment process of the 
ambient air downstream from an active chilled beam in a climate chamber [7]. The PIV system is cable to 
measure the attached plane jet at a low Reynolds number with high turbulence concentration quantitatively 
and rapidly. PIV can provide detailed information about the complex air jets generated by various kinds of 
ventilation devices, which would have been impossible with conventional point-wise measurements. PIV can 
also be used to measure the flow fields inside the ventilation devices and is also an appropriate technique 
for studying the dynamic and thermal conduct of the flow inside the ventilates cavities, because it is non-
intrusive. 
Other investigations demonstrate that PIV is effective when investigating in performance of ventilation 
devices, based on 2D-PIV systems [7]. 
PIV methods can also be used regarding human airflows, because the main focus is high indoor environment 
quality. Effective ventilation system is necessary, so that we focus on the flow rate of human thermal plume. 
Here PIV is suitable for measuring the human airflows, because PIV measurements can analyze downstream 
of the inlet along with a thermal manikin in order to replicate the interaction of an inlet duct with the heated 
body of an average indoor resident. PIV can also obtain the quantitative velocity information in the breathing 
zone of a thermal manikin placed within a chamber with simulated breathing [7]. 
PIV has also been used to study the complex particle flows induced by the foot motion, mainly to understand 
how foot motion affects air transport and how walking affects pollutant diffusion. Moreover, it is possible 
that human body may affect the airflow different than a thermal manikin, so for accurate boundary it is more 
meaningful to measure based on a human body.  
5.2.1 Indoor airflow fields, small-scale models in contradiction of full-scale models 
 
Regarding small-scale models, these are used to measure techniques to calculate or evaluate ventilation 
performance with a reduced scale of the buildings or rooms, which are appropriate for PIV measurements 
[7].

PIV can measure the airflow field in the dimensions of small-scale model, which is defined to be similar to 
the field of view of typical PIV systems and the enclosures can be made translucent. PIV has been used to 
study the air distribution in the model of a powerhouse, and the research showed that the supply air velocity 
and the air inlet location had a great effect on air distribution characteristics of the large space and occupant 
zone velocity [7]. Another analysis showed that PIV measurements and an analysis of transitional flow in a

Another investigation showed that airflow patterns in buildings with cross-ventilation are complex and

showed well potential with PIV data. Different applications imply that PIV is popular and applicable for small-
scale model experiments.  
The disadvantage with small-scale models are that they may bear from scaling problems correlated with 
combined modelling of inertial and buoyancy forces, which is why results from small-scale models may not 
exactly represent the real conditions and it can be difficult to be used directly to investigate ventilation

numerical simulations [7]. 
Full-scale models are also important when analyzing the ventilation system performances and for validation 
of numerical simulations. In addition to small-scale models, the full-scale models represent the real condition 
a bit better, but these are more expensive and more challenging than small-scale models due to large space, 
non-transparent enclosures and inside difficulties.  
One research group has performed many large-scale PIV measurements in a full-scale room, where they 
developed both large-scale 2D-PIV system and large-scale stereoscopic PIV system to obtain full-scale room 
airflow fields for validating RNG and LES model. They also used to helium-fill bubbles (further described by

that PIV results can give a realistic prediction of the indoor air circulation without scaling and simplification 
problems [7].  
The above mention study states that the modified PIV technique can be a useful method to quantitatively 
measure the indoor air velocity fields in entire cross sections. Other than PIV being used in regards to large-

5.3 Disadvantages of PIV 
In addition to PIV being a powerful tool for indoor airflow examination, there are mentioned limitation such 
as block of optical paths when use of full-scale models, which effect the thermal boundaries. Also the use of 
transparent materials inside the models can change the optical paths and distort the particle images. PIV is 
not always the optimum tool for indoor airflow measurements because commercial PIV often are restricted 
to the block of optical paths and the size limit of image. Because of inadequate laser intensity commercial 
PIV is limited due to low-spatial resolve and the restricted space to expand the optical light sheet, which is 
why it is necessary to develop large scale optical system and camera shooting equipment to overcome the 
limitation. Lastly PIV system is expensive and can be difficult to use, also because accurate PIV measurement 
of indoor airflow requires a significant investment in equipment, time and experience.

6.3 Methods of measuring velocity and temperature  
Measuring techniques for simple indoor climate parameters such as air velocity and temperature, etc. can in 
general be classified into two categories: whole-field measuring methods and point-measuring methods. The 
measurements can be undertaken simultaneously over quite large regions with high spatial and temporal 
resolution, such that the parameters measured may be mapped over a whole field and hence flow 
visualizations may be realized by whole-field techniques. The qualitative information about the whole flow 
pattern is from this type of measuring technique, which permits a direct comparison with a numerical 
simulation. Related to the whole-filed measuring technique, point-measurement technique only offers one-
sensor measurement at a specified location at one time. This indicates that arrays of these measurement 
probes have to be used in order to gain local velocity and temperature in the whole domain. Though, point-
measurement method is widely used by many researchers and engineers, as it is quite inexpensive and easy 
to deal with. There are many reviews which compared these two methods [14]. 
 
6.4 Points-measuring techniques  
The measurements for velocity of indoor flows are available by two types of point-measuring techniques: 
thermal anemometers and laser doppler anemometers. Thermal anemometers are a well-established 
method and are less expensive compared with laser Doppler anemometers which are quite new. These two 
velocity measuring methods are relatively different, and each has pros and cons [14]. 
6.4.1 Thermal anemometers   
A thermal anemometers measures air within a relationship between the local velocity and convective heat 
loss from an electrically heated detecting element exposed to the fluid flow. A sensor and an electronic circuit 
==PAGE:30==
 
29 
belongs to this measuring method. The thermal anemometers have very small dimensional sensor, and the 
shape could be wires as hot-wire anemometer, films as hot-film anemometer, or spheres as hot-sphere 
anemometer [14].  
6.4.2 Laser Doppler Anemometry  
A high resolution -single point technique for velocity measurements in turbulent flows. 
The Laser Doppler Anemometer, or LDA, is a widely accepted tool for fluid dynamic investigations in gases 
and liquids and has been used as such for more than three decades. It is a well-established technique that 
gives information about flow velocity[11].  
 
Because of its non-intrusive principle and directional sensitivity, it makes it very suitable for applications with 
reversing flow, whether chemically reacting or high-temperature media and rotating machinery, where 
physical sensors are difficult or impossible to use. Moreover, it requires tracer particles in the flow.

6.5 Whole-field measuring methods   
There are many whole-field measuring techniques used to record indoor air velocity and temperature 
distributions, e.g. Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), Particle Streak Velocimetry (PSV) and infrared 
thermography. PIV and PSV are non-intrusive methods and are used in several flow applications for 
qualitative (flow visualization) and quantitative (velocity components) measurements.   
 
A research article deals with whole-field methods. It explains a point-measuring techniques, only one sensor 
measurement is done at a time at a certain position. It is possible to simultaneously measure at several 
positions with access to several sensors, but the measurements can only be carried out in discrete positions. 
In the last two decades whole-field measuring techniques are developed, where measurements are 
undertaken simultaneously at different locations. A quantitative and visualization method is particle streak 
velocimetry (PSV). The individual flow indicators give rise to streaks on the image. To facilitate identification 
of the flow direction the light is modulated to give a sequence of streaks of different lengths.  (Whole- field 
Measuring methods in ventilated rooms) 
6.5.1 Experimental method CFD 
To study room air velocity, temperature and contaminant distributions as well as the related thermal comfort 
and indoor air quality indices, experimental techniques and numerical simulations by means of CFD are two 
approaches often used. The information from CFD is about room air distributions, as well as offering great 
possibilities to predict different indicators through a user-defined function (UDF) based on solved flow and 
temperature fields. The before mentioned features make CFD a useful analyzing and designing ventilation 
systems. There are many studies providing information about indoor environment with the use of CFD 
available from literature [14] 
6.5.2 Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV)  
Particle image Velocimetry is an optical method of flow visualization. It has been developed and is being used 
to measure fluids’ instantaneous velocity. Before PIV, Laser Doppler velocimetry was mainly used to measure 
the velocity of transparent and semi-transparent fluid. The development of technology like that of PIV is 
needed in the modern research fields and the old technologies cannot offer to all the requirements of the 
modern research fields. PIV since its development has provided for the first time the ability to acquire the 
whole field experimental data of fluids.  
The method of PIV is non-intrusive to a great extent. The tracers in PIV if properly chosen cause negligible 
distortion in the flow of fluid. The method also allows high speed data processing which allows large number 
==PAGE:33==
 
32 
of image pairs. A high quantity of near-continuous information can be generated. Since PIV system uses class 
IV lasers and high-resolution, high speed cameras which bring higher cost in comparison to other 
measurement techniques. PIV technology is new and has seen rapid rise of its use in research. PIV has given 
rise to many new developments which include: Stereoscopic PIV, Dual plane stereoscopic PIV, Multiplane 
stereoscopic PIV, Micro PIV, Holographic PIV, time-resolved PIV and combinations of PIV with other optical 
field measurement techniques.  
PIV can also be used to measure velocity field of free surface and basal boundaries in a granular flow such 
as those in shaken containers, tumblers and avalanches[15].  
The main difference between PIV and other techniques like laser Doppler velocimetry, hot-wire anemometry, 
and ultrasonic anemometry is that PIV is able to generate instantaneous two-dimensional and even three-
dimensional vector fields, while the others measure velocity at a point at a time. The development of PIV has 
been a milestone in measurement technique as it allowed velocity information of whole flow fields to be 
registered in very small fractions of a second [16]. 
6.6 Symposium 
In addition to other anemometers, PIV is a measuring technique whose accurateness is directly determined 
by the displacement fault and the indecision in the time delay between pulses. These time delay between 
pulses can be minimized by use of image evaluation techniques. The displacement error is a previous 
uncertainty source for indoor PIV applications and complex to estimate, because PIV measurement can be 
seen as a chain of error producing steps. This is due to the fact that the accuracy of calculated velocity data 
depends on the quality of particle image, size of interrogation area and the statistical correlation method. It 
is indicated that the relative error can be estimated to be 1-2% in a 2-dimensinal PIV system for indoor 
applications (particle image velocimetry measurement of indoor airflow field: A review of the technologies 
and applications section discussion). Other studies have shown that an accuracy of 5-10 % is possible for the 
room airflow measurements by the use of two-component PIV technique. It states that a more practical way 
to validate the PIV measurement accuracy could result in comparing the PIV measurements with other 
anemometers for an example hot-sphere anemometer, where the accuracy at low velocity is in order of 10-
20%. 
It is concluded that sufficient uncorrelated PIV sample size has to be acquired in order to obtain reliable time-
averaged turbulent quantities, such as turbulence intensity and Reynolds stress [7].

A video file of the experiment was created while carrying out the experiment. The video files were later used

Afterwards the pictures were used in a software called PIVlab. PIVlab is a post procession software where 
the velocity distribution at specific times were calculated by vector.   
PIVlab is an open-source particle image velocimetry (PIV), which calculates velocity distribution within image 
pairs and is also used to derive, display and export multiple parameters of the flow pattern. PIV analysis in 
PIVlab is fast and efficient due to its friendly user interface. In the software once the image pairs are imported 
then letters A & B are used to denote first and the second image. Mask can be applied in the image so that a 
dark object from the image can be excluded from analysis. There are a number of post-processing techniques, 
which can be used so enhance the quality of the picture. This further helps in more accurate analysis. For 
example, it has contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE) which helps in enhancing the 
contrast of the image. For analysis a setup of cross-correlation can be done of the image data. Of the two 
correlation algorithm used DCC (Single pass direct cross correlation) and FFT (direct Fourier transform 
correlation with multiple passes and deforming windows). FFT is used in most cases as it delivers more 
accurate results. The software allows to calibrate the pixels with real-world dimensions. Sometimes 
erroneous vectors show up in the poorly illuminated region of the image and they can be removed and 
interpolated by the use of inbuilt function in the software called vector validation. The u-velocity of the profile 
behind the cylinder and parameters from areas defined by the operator can also be found. The data 
generated in the software can be exported in number of formats, image types, movies etc [17] [18].

Chapter 10 
10.1 Discussion 
Enough effort should be made not to release too much smoke which actually make the whole environment 
opaque and difficult to observe. To overcome this problem, a test procedure can be done in order to 
understand the amount of smoke needed to that can be visible in the recording process. Another idea could 
be to release the smoke in steps so that the same amount of smoke flows and is visible in the video. 
As mentioned earlier, smartphone camera was used for recording purposes. At time, there was no focus on 
the desired smoke area, which gave relatively bad frames/images. Incase such a problem occurs; recording 
should be repeated to get a proper focus thus resulting in good quality images. Due to repeated problems 
and hurdles, at times there could be too much smoke in the room which caused delays and time was required 
to make sure that the smoke clears out before the experiment cane be repeated. 
The whole experiment was based on the hypothesis of using whole field technique PIV to quantify airflow in 
rooms with laser light with lens and a smartphone camera used for recording purposes and free software 
PIVlab. Despite some focus problems faced in recording by the phone camera, it generally gave promising 
results. Use of wide-angle lens for whole field measurement due to limited size of the room was not effective 
as the recording was bad.  
The most important and valuable conclusion of the experiment was to use images of similar resolution; 1080 
X 1920 pixels. This was important while calibrating pictures/frames in PIVlab for analysis, which gave wrong 
results when the frames were of lesser resolution, which was verified by comparing the results with analytical 
solutions of velocity calculations. 
As mentioned earlier in the thesis, there were two hypothesis made; firstly, the use of equipment i.e., a laser, 
simple software PIVlab and smartphone camera and secondly regarding appearance of no Coanda effect on 
suspended ceiling (baffle system). The first hypothesis proved to be correct and the results prove that it was 
a smart choice that gave good results. Regarding second hypothesis, earlier prediction was that Coanda effect 
will not occur in this case but the experiment results showed that the prediction was wrong and Coanda 
effect did occur.

Chapter 11 
11.1 Conclusion 
Energy efficiency has a significant role in building usage, as it is important to design effective and energy-
saving ventilation devices for indoor environment. Even though it is crucial to detect these ventilation 
methods, airflow generated from these ventilation devices are very complicated and challenging to 
measure, this is where PIV makes inroads and can be used as a smart measuring tool.  
PIV has developed into a smart and intelligent measuring technique and with the aid of image and data 
processing programs; PIVlab, velocity calculations can be performed quite accurately. PIVlab is a 
sophisticated tool that can be used to determine airflow patterns, effects of air inlet and outlet and air flow 
patterns of ventilation system. The method is pretty much developed and a lot of work has been done to 
further improve it to get better and better results. Furthermore, based on this technique, other smart tools 
could be developed that could help to visualize the air flow patterns during commissioning process.   
The overall results of the experiment were quite satisfactory but further improvements could have given 
better and interesting results. Width of the room was reasonable but increased depth could have given 
better recording results. As in the experiment, a fixed laser was used but a moveable laser could have 
provided better visualization effects. Due to limited coverage from the laser, presence of a diverging lens 
could have further given better visualization effects. Apart from that, perforated black sheet helped to 
absorb the laser light and no reflection problems were faced during recording process. 
In conclusion, it is worth mentioning that a simple software like PIVlab together with the use of a standard 
smartphone camera was an affordable and easy procedure to derive the flow patterns in a room. Further 
work can be done to improve the results by using latest technologies like smart phone apps.
