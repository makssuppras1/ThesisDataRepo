Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to develop a system that can accurately track and map an non­
cooperative target in real time with the system being flown on a space craft. The system is
implemented using a camera acquiring images of the target together with a laser equipped
with optical element creating a laser grid on the target. Two images are acquired almost
simultaneous where one is with the laser grid ON and the other one with the laser grid OFF.
By detecting and matching keypoints in images with the laser OFF, the relative motion
between the space craft and the target be can solved to a scale factor. Using the images
with the laser grid ON, the distance to the target is found and thus the scale. The system
is able to operate in real time and can with some accuracy track the relative motion. The
system is however unable to map the target due to inaccuracy in the relative motion ruining
any attempt at triangulation of keypoints.

Resumé
Formålet med dette kandidatspeciale er at udvikle et system, der med stor nøjagtigthed
kan lokalisere og kortlægge et pasivt objekt i realtid. Systemmet er udviklet med henblik
på brug som en del af en rummission. Systemet er opbygget omkring et kamera, der tager
billeder af målet og en laser udstyret med optiske elementer, der skaber et lasergitter
på målet. To billeder tages næsten samtidigt, hvor et er med lasergitteret tændt og det
andet med lasergitteret slukket. Ved at detektere og matche interessepunkter i billeder
hvor laseren er slukket, kan den relative bevægelse mellem rumfartøjet og det passive
objekt findes til en skaleringsfaktor. Ved at bruge billederne hvor lasergitteret er tændt
kan afstanden til det pasive objekt findes og dermed også skaleringsfaktoren. Systemet
er i stand til at operere i realtid og kan med nogen nøjagtighed bestemme den relative
bevægelse. Systemet er dog ikke i stand til at kortlægge det passive objekt grundet stor
unøjagtighed i den relative bevægelse, der umuliggører ethvert forsøg på triangulering af
interessepunkter.

Introduction
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate methods for vision­based simultaneous Local­
ization And Mapping (SLAM) utilized for mapping and tracking non­cooperative objects
in space in close proximity to a spacecraft carrying the system. The system should thus
be able to produce both a 3D model of the object of interest and determine the relative
motion between the spacecraft and the object. To do this the task can be divided into
two parts: Firstly determine the relative motion between the spacecraft and the object of
interest and secondly build the 3D model of the object. Although the two parts at first
glance seems like two separate tasks they are in fact interdependent as the 3D model
can’t be produced without observing the object from multiple positions, where the posi­
tions are known, and thus the 3D model cannot be produced without first determining the
relative motion. Meanwhile for determining the relative motion, only the object of interest
can be used as reference. The system must thus be able to determine the relative motion
based on how the 3D model is moved between acquired each image. Potential uses of
such system are navigation in orbit around a non­cooperative object, rendezvousing with
non­cooperative target, formation flight or mapping of non­cooperative targets such as
asteroids or space debris.
The phrases ”motion”, ”path” and ”relative motion” are used interchangeably throughout
the thesis. This is due to the fact that moving either the camera, the object or both is
identical from the perspective of the camera.
1.1
Initial design considerations
Before the system can be developed some key design considerations must be made. The
first thing to consider is under which conditions the system shall be able to operate.
Another key consideration is how images are going to be acquired. As the intent is to
develop a vision­based system, a camera is an obvious choice. A consideration regard­
ing using different parts of the electromagnetic spectrum could be made, but is outside
the scope of this thesis. Other considerations regarding the camera includes the choice
between a monocular camera setup and a stereo setup consisting of two (or more) cam­
eras. The advantage of using two cameras is that their relative position is always known
and that any point that can be identified in both images can easily be triangulated into a
3D position contributing to the 3D model of the object of interest. Having two cameras
does, however, little to nothing in determining the relative motion between the object of
interest and the spacecraft, as the problem of determining how the spacecraft has moved
based on an image before and after the movement, is essentially the same regardless
of the number of images before and after the movement. Having a stereo setup comes
with the advantage of additional redundancy to the system as it would still have one op­
erational camera if one camera fails. This will however require that the system functions
with only one camera making the extra camera required for a stereo setup obsolete in any
scenario where no camera malfunctions. Having two cameras would also require double
the computing power as two cameras produce double the amount of data one camera
would. As a stereo setup provides no major advantages over monocular camera setup
but would require double the amount of computing power a monocular camera setup is
chosen. Regarding the specifications of the camera, Field of View (FoV) and resolution
are the two most important parameters. FoV is important as the object of interest should
take up a significant part of each image without causing a situation where only a tiny part

The resolution of the camera indicates how many pixels are in each image. More pixels
will give more accuracy in what can be derived from the images. More pixels does also
demand more computing power as more computational operations will have to be per­
formed. An optimal resolution should thus be found as a trade­off between accuracy and
computing power.
As only one camera will be used for the system, some kind of distance sensor is required.
The reason for this is that a single camera is unable to detect the scale of the object of
interest. In other words: a single camera is unable to differentiate between an object that is
big and far and one that is small and close. Three solutions are considered: A laser ranger
pointing in a fixed direction; a laser ranger with a motorized pointing system; structured
light illuminating the object of interest. By having a fixed laser the distance between the
spacecraft and a single point on the object of interest can be found. From this distance it
is possible to find the scale of the object. This is however limited by the fact that the laser
ranger must point at the object of interest, thus enforcing a more accurate orientation
of the spacecraft than what is required to have the object of interest within the FoV of
the camera. An alternative would be to mount the laser ranger on a motorized pointing
system allowing for the laser ranger to point at anything within the FoV of the camera. This
comes with the disadvantage of increased complexity and an additional point of failure.
This leads to the third option, namely the structured light. This option does not require
any additional pointing and does not have any moving parts. It does however require
some more computing as the structured light illuminating the object of interest has to be
analysed in order to compute the distance. It is assumed that the additional computing
power required is insignificant for the system and the structured light is the method used
moving forward.
When acquiring images, imperfections in the camera affect the final image. These effects
on the final image might only distort the image slightly but does still worsen a computer’s
ability to analyse the image. To combat this issue, these imperfections shall be measured
and accounted for. One way of doing this is described in Section 2.1. It is assumed that
this calibration can be performed prior to launch of the spacecraft and that the imperfec­
tions do not change between calibration prior to launch and at the end of the mission. If
this assumption turns out to be incorrect, a method for calibrating the camera in space
must be found.

1.2
Scene Analysis
In order to understand what would be required from the system a preliminary analysis of
the scene is conducted. Since the system is based on an optical camera, some kind of
light source has to be present for the system to observe anything. Fortunately the Sun is
almost always visible in space. The only times at which the Sun is not visible is when it
is blocked by another object. The object of interest will be illuminated as long as it is not
located in the umbra of any object as illustrated in Figure 1.2.

by light from the Sun. In situations like this the only option is to move the spacecraft and
thus the system to an orientation where the Sun is not saturating the camera.

2.2.2
FAST
Features from Accelerated Segment Test (FAST) was first proposed by Edward Rosten
and Tom Drummond in [9] as a method for detecting keypoints in an image with the advan­
tage of being less computationally heavy than other methods such as SIFT. FAST, which
detects corners in the image by examining a pixel p and a circle of sixteen pixels around
p as illustrated in Figure 2.3. p is determined to be a corner if at least twelve continuous
pixels are either all brighter than p plus a threshold t or all darker than p minus t. As a
means to improve speed, only pixel 1, 5, 9 and 13 are examined at first. Only if at least
three out of the four are all either brighter than p plus t or darker than p minus t can a
corner exist and only then will the remaining pixels be examined.
FAST can be made even faster through machine learning. This is done by detecting
corners and creating a feature vector containing d (darker), s (similar) or b (brighter) for
each of the sixteen pixels. An ID3 algorithm is then used to create a decision tree that
tells whether a given point p is a corner by only looking at the pixel that gives the most
information about the state of p. An example of such decision tree is given in Figure 2.4.
The last step in FAST is to avoid detecting multiple corners right next to each other. This
is done by computing a score function V as the sum of the absolute difference between p
and the surrounding sixteen pixels for each detected corner. If two adjacent corners exist
the one with the lower V is discarded.
In cases where the object in the images is rotated between two images it is important
to not only know where a keypoint is but also the orientation of the keypoint. For FAST
such a method is suggested in [11]. The orientation is calculated as tan2 of the first order

2.3
Keypoint Descriptors
Once a keypoint is detected some distinct description of the keypoint has to be made in
order to be able to tell if two keypoints from two different images are in fact the same
keypoint. For a keypoint descriptor to be good it should be fast to compute but at the
same time be distinctively unique to the keypoint that it describes. The theory behind the
keypoint descriptors used in this project is given in the section.
2.3.1
SIFT
In SIFT each keypoint is given a 128 dimensional vector describing the keypoint [7]. The
values in the vector are found by considering the 16×16 window surrounding the key­
point. This window of pixels is further divided into sixteen 4×4 sub­windows where the
orientation of each pixel is calculated. In order to obtain a rotation invariant descriptor the
orientation of keypoint is subtracted from all the sub­orientations. In each of the sixteen
4×4 sub­windows the gradient is calculated for the sixteen pixels in the sub­window. Sim­
ilar to how the orientation was determined for the keypoint a histogram of the orientations
in each sub­window is made; this time, however, the histogram only has eight bins. The
eight­binned histogram for each of the sixteen sub­windows surrounding the keypoint are
stored in the 128 dimensional descriptor vector. The process is illustrated in Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Illustration of how the orientations of the area surrounding a keypoint is used
to create a keypoint descriptor vector using SIFT. Image from [8]
Assuming each value in the descriptor vector is stored in byte, each descriptor vector
would require 128 bytes of memory.

performed based on the priority list. Just like in the randomized kd­tree algorithm is the
search stopped early to increase computing speed at the cost of only approximating the
best match.

Methods
This chapter describes how data is generated and how the data is processed. Further­

3.1
Image processing
This section describes how the the system is implemented with a focus on how each step
of the image processing is performed and the reasons why. The general steps are outlined
in the flowchart in Figure 3.1, while more in­depth explanations and considerations/trade­
offs are given throughout the section. First the camera is calibrated as described in Sec­
tion 2.1. This is to be done prior to operations of the system. Then two images are
acquired; one with the laser grid OFF and one with the laser grid ON. This is discussed
further in Section 3.1.2. Then the images are undistorted as described in Section 2.1.
Following the undistortion keypoints are identified in the image with the laser grid OFF.
The found keypoints are described and matched to the keypoints found in the previous
image (unless the image is the first image; in that case the next image is acquired). The
keypoint detection, description and matching is discussed further in Section 3.1.1. From
the matched keypoints the position and orientation of the camera is estimated. As a part
of this step the distances to some of the keypoints are determined. This is discussed
further in Section 3.1.2 and 3.1.3. From the estimated camera positions, a model for the
path is created. This model is only created once at least five positions of the camera is
estimated. This is discussed further in Section 3.1.4. Finally a point cloud is created from
the keypoints matched in two consecutive images. This point cloud is used to update the
list of all keypoints found as discussed in Section 3.1.5. The system is created in C++
utilizing the OpenCV library version 4.6.0 [16].
3.1.1
Keypoint detection, description and matching
In order to do anything regarding tracking, mapping or 3D reconstruction with image anal­
ysis using multiple images, a number of distinct keypoints has to be found. In this project
two methods for detecting keypoints are used. The first one is SIFT, while the other one
is FAST. The reason for choosing these two specifically is that SIFT is in literature often
presented as the baseline for keypoint detection and the performance of new novel meth­
ods are usually compared to SIFT. FAST on the other hand is presented as an alternative

3.1.2
Path estimation
Only the object of interest and any prior knowledge about the relative motion between the
spacecraft and the object can be used to determine the relative motion between them.
Initially no knowledge about the relative motion is known and thus only observations of
the object can be used to estimate the relative motion. Once the motion is determined from
a number of observations, a mathematical model describing the motion can be produced
and used to predict future motion and thus both prior knowledge and the object of interest
can be used to estimate the motion.
The first step in estimating the motion is to determine how the object of interest has moved
between two images acquired with a temporal baseline. On both images, a number of key­

commonly used and also the value used in this project [17]. The quality of each match is

3.1.3
Distance detection
As already mentioned in Section 1.1 the distance to the object of interest is found using
a laser grid. The grid emitted by the laser must change based on the distance in order
for the system to determine the distance. When illuminating a surface perpendicular to
the laser, the only thing that changes based on distance is the size of the pattern. When
the surface illuminated isn’t perpendicular to the light the shape of the pattern will also
change. These pattern changes can be used to determine the shape of the object but as
the purpose of the laser grid is to determine the absolute distance between the camera
and a point on the surface of the object of interest, these areas will not be processed
further using the light. The structured light used consists of equally spaced horizontal and
vertical lines as showed in Figure 3.2.
The grid lines emitted by the laser might be detected as keypoints. This is problematic
since the grid lines will change their position on the surface of the object. To combat this,
two images are acquired; one where the laser grid is ON and one where the laser grid
is OFF. This way the keypoint can be detected in an image where the laser grid will not
intervene, while the laser grid can also be analysed. By acquiring the two images with
a temporal baseline of as close to zero as possible, the spacial baseline between the
two images will also be approximately zero. The two images can be obtained in three
ways. The first way would be one camera acquiring two images quickly after each other
with the laser grid ON for only one of the images. If one camera is unable to acquire two
images in quick enough succession, two cameras must be used. A third option would be
to have two cameras that operate at different wave lengths and the laser in a wave length
only seen by one of the cameras. This would allow for the two images to be obtained
simultaneously but only have the laser in one of the images. For the options where two
cameras are involved, the relative position of the two cameras shall be known with high
accuracy, so the spatial baseline can be compensated for. Determining which option is
the better for the system is outside the scope of this project.

Finally it should be considered what would happen if the camera and the laser are not
perfectly aligned or if one of them is slightly tilted. Shifting the camera to the right is
equivalent to shifting the laser to the left as it would result in the same relative positions.
The same can be said about moving either up or down. Moving the laser around in the x/y­
plane while maintaining the distance to the camera in the z­direction only moves the laser
grid around on the object of interest. The size of the grid will not change and thus shifting
the laser (or the camera) in the x/y­plane has no negative effect. On the other hand, tilting
the laser does have a negative effect as this would change the distance between grid lines
on the object of interest. It is thus important that any tilt introduced when mounting the
laser and the camera is either so small that the effect is negligible or the tilt is measured
with high accuracy and compensated for in the software.

position can then be updated based on the knowledge about where these keypoints are

Data Generating
In order to test the performance and capabilities of the system, some data has to be
generated. This is done by artificially creating a scenario simulating an encounter between
an unknown object and the spacecraft carrying the system. More specifically, a 3D printed
model of the asteroid Vesta in scale 1:4 million [20] is placed on a black surface with a
black background. The black background is to emulate the darkness of space. There is no
specific reason that a model of Vesta is used and any other suitable object could replace it.
Other suitable objects include but are not limited to spacecrafts, satellites, other asteroids,
meteoroid and space debris. The camera and laser is placed in a suitable distance from

In Section 1.3 the requirements for the system are listed. Here an evaluation of the degree
to which they are fulfilled is given in addition to which further steps are required to fulfill
them.
1. The system shall be able to operate in real time ­ In the current implementation of
the system, the images are loaded from a file instead of acquiring them in real time,
so the system is not running in real time. Ignoring this, the computational time of the
system is less than one second per image when running on a 2.3 GHz processor.
The computational time is however in the range 2­3 seconds if a 400 MHz processor
is used. Assuming 5◦is the upper limit for rotation between two images for which
the system can perform satisfactory, the angular velocity between the camera and
the object of interest would have to be at most approximately 2◦/s. Comparing this
to the spin rates considered in Scene analysis in Section 1.2 given in Table 1.1, it is
found that this operation speed is sufficient for orbiting asteroids. For flybys of the
target it becomes more marginal whether the system can keep up or not depending
on specifics of the flyby. This requirement is thus fulfilled assuming the loading of
the images can be made negligible.
2. The system shall be able to accurately produce a 3D model of the object of interest
­ The point clouds produced do not resemble the object of interest. For this reason,
this requirement is not fulfilled.
3. The system shall be able to accurately determine the relative motion between the
spacecraft and the object of interest ­ This requirement is impossible to evaluate
since no ground truth exists for the relative motion. Only the motion estimated by
the system exists. This motion does, however, have the shape and scale expected.
The motion found by the system is noisy making it inaccurate. It is possible that
noise would be dampened if the image series used to test the system contained
more images.
4. The system shall be able to operate without relying on other instruments ­ The sys­
tem is developed to not use any measurements other than what is obtained through
the camera. Thus in its current form, it fulfill this requirement as the only data pro­
cessed by the system is the images obtained through the camera.
5. The system shall be able to withstand the physical environment experienced during
launch and operations in space ­ Fulfilling this requirement is outside the scope of
this thesis and is thus not fulfilled as nothing has been done to fulfill it.

5
Discussion
As the system is designed right now, a laser grid is used to measure the distance to
the object of interest. This is done by first detecting the horizontal and vertical lines and
then finding their intersections, after which the horizontal and vertical lines are not used
any further. This might not be the most efficient method of using structured light for this
purpose, as a grid of laser dots potentially could achieve the same with fewer operations.
This claim is made assuming a grid of laser dots can be treated in exactly the same way
the intersections of the laser grid lines can. Whether this claim is true or not can only
be answered by actually changing the grid lines to a grid of dots, which has not been
attempted. It could however prove valuable since the distance estimation is by far the
most time consuming operation as given by Figure 4.12. It is thus likely that the efficiency
could be improved simply by changing the optical elements creating the laser grid along
with minor changes in the implementation.
Since the distance estimation is by far the most time consuming operation in the system,
as given by Figure 4.12, it should be considered if this operation is needed. In Section 1.1 it
was argued that a monocular camera setup and a laser grid would be a better solution than
a solution based on stereo­camera setup. Since the distance estimation takes more time
than all other operations combined (excluding operations such as loading and saving data)
it could be argued that a dual camera setup would perform better. It should however be
considered that other major contributors to computation time (keypoint detection, keypoint
description and keypoint matching) are all based on highly optimized algorithms, whereas
the algorithm for distance estimation is still unoptimized. It is likely that the computational
time of the distance estimation can be brought down.
One advantage not utilized in using a monocular camera setup and structured light as
opposed to a stereo­camera setup is that when calculating the distance between grid lines
as described in Section 3.1.3, the slope of the area surrounding the grid line intersection
can easily be found. This would allow for not only a point cloud representing the object of
interest but also small patches estimating the surface of the object. This could potentially
improve the representation of the object of interest; especially if the point cloud is sparse.
If, however, the point cloud is densely populated, surface patches would do little to improve
the point cloud, while slightly increasing the computational time due to extra operations.
When comparing the average computing time for each image in Figure 4.12 and 4.13, the
number of images were the same for all the investigated methods. It should, however, be
considered that the computational time of the path modeling scales with the number of
already processed images. The reason for this, is that the model is modeled after all prior
camera positions. From Figure 4.12 and 4.13 it is apparent that the path estimation is not
a major contributor to the total computing time per image. The increasing computational
time based on the number of images should however not be fully dismissed for missions
where the object of interest is observed for an extended amount of time. In these cases
one solution would be to limit the model to only use the previous n number of images,
where n has to be determined in such a way that the computational time is within what is
acceptable for the mission.
The most time consuming part of processing each image is determining the distance to
the object. This might be explained by the fact that no effort has been put into improving
the computational time of this part. It is thus highly likely that the distance determination

can be made significantly faster and thus become a smaller contributor to the total op­
erating time percent wise. If the distance determination becomes less of a factor for the
computing time, the time spent on the keypoint detection will become more important.
This would mean that using ORB or FAST+SIFT would operate significantly faster com­
pared to SIFT lowering the motion between two consecutive images. It is possible that,
even though SIFT seems to be better for estimating the motion of the camera, ORB or
FAST+SIFT might be better since they can be computed faster and thus with a smaller
motion between the images. This can only be verified experimentally and should be done
before concluding which of the three tested methods is the better to use for the system.
In some image pairs less than five points were remaining after applying RANSAC. In
these cases, the points remaining after the ratio test were used to find the essential matrix
and for triangulation for the point cloud. This caused FAST+SIFT to be the method with
most points in the point cloud even though this was not the method with most keypoints
remaining after applying RANSAC. When RANSAC removes too many matched points
it is likely due to the matches being incorrect. It is thus problematic that the points, that
would have been discarded by RANSAC, are being used to estimate the motion and
in the point cloud as well. A possible solution could involve multiple approaches. One
approach would be to lower the motion between images to ensure the keypoints are more
easily matched correctly. In the test setup this would be obtained by moving the test
object less between images. For real applications the motion between images be can
lowered by making the computation faster by either increasing the computing power or
optimizing the implementation. Another approach would be to skip cases where too few
keypoints remain after RANSAC. This would require the skipped position to be modeled
based on the estimated path. This approach should be investigated further along with
other initiatives for making the system more robust such as implementing ways of handling
cases where no grid line intersections are found near keypoints or implementing ways of
handling cases where too few keypoints remain after the ratio test.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis is to develop a camera based system that can accurately track and
map non­cooperative targets in real time. The system is developed with the purpose of
being used as an instrument on a spacecraft where it can be used for either navigation,
scientific purposes or a combination of the two.
Such a system has been developed and the performance of the system achieves the goal
of tracking and mapping a non­cooperative target to some degree. The system consists
of a camera observing the target and a laser grid illuminating the target. The system is
capable of tracking the motion of the object with some accuracy. No quantitative measure
has been made for the accuracy of the motion. The mapping of the object is made in
the form of a point cloud which by no means resembles the target object. This is likely
due to inaccuracies in the estimation of the motion. The system is capable of operating in
real­time assuming a new way of loading images into the system is implemented. On a
processor running at 400 MHz the processing of each image will take 2­3 seconds. This
allows for the angular change between two images to be less than 5◦for most potential
target objects.
The system has been implemented and tested using different methods for keypoint detec­
tion and keypoint description. The methods implemented are SIFT, ORB and a combina­
tion of FAST and SIFT. When comparing the performance of the system using the three
different methods, it appears that using SIFT results in more correctly matched keypoints.
This is supported by both the total number of keypoints not discarded, the percentage of
keypoints not discarded and the capabilities in estimating the motion. When ORB and
FAST+SIFT are used about double the amount of keypoints are detected but a larger
percentage is also discarded resulting in fewer non­discarded keypoints than when using
SIFT. In addition to the fewer non­discarded keypoints the estimated motions are also
worse for ORB and FAST+SIFT than the performance obtained when using SIFT.
The computational time for the three methods are almost identical when describing the
keypoints and the subsequent matching of the keypoints. The biggest difference in com­
putational time between the three methods is in the keypoint detection where SIFT uses
∼3 times as long as FAST/ORB. The computational time used by the system is how­
ever not affected significantly by the choice of keypoint detection and description method
since the distance determination is significantly more computationally heavy than all other
processes. It is however expected that the distance determination can be optimized in
regards to computational time. Depending on the degree to which it can be improved, it
might cause the fact that SIFT is slower at detecting keypoints to have a significant effect.
Whether a larger angular distance between two images caused by SIFT being slower will
produce worse results than ORB or FAST+SIFT that are generally performing worse but
faster can only be determined experimentally after the distance determination has been
optimized.
It can thus be concluded that a system has been developed that is capable of tracking
non­cooperative targets with limited accuracy. The system is incapable of mapping the
target, likely due to inaccuracy in the tracking of the object. Since the system is expected
to be able to work in real time with a modification to how images are loaded into the
system, the system can be integrated onto a spacecraft and approximate the relative
motion between the spacecraft and a non­cooperative target. Potential uses could be in

• Find physical parameters of the orbit of the spacecraft instead of fabricated param­

Given a linear system
d = Gm
(B.1)
can the least square solution for m be found by first squaring the error of the system
ϕ(m) = r⊺r = (d −Gm)⊺(d −Gm)
(B.2)
= (d⊺−m⊺G⊺) (d −Gm)
(B.3)
= d⊺d −d⊺Gm −m⊺G⊺d + m⊺G⊺Gm
(B.4)
Then the squared error is differentiated with respect to m⊺and put equal to zero.
∇ϕ(m) =
∂
∂m⊺(d⊺d −d⊺Gm −m⊺G⊺d + m⊺G⊺Gm) = 0
(B.5)
= G⊺Gm −G⊺d = 0
(B.6)
Finally can the least squares solution for m be found by isolating for m
mLS = (G⊺G)−1G⊺d
(B.7)
Similarly can the least squares solution be found for a system of the following form
x = RX + tX
(B.8)
Again is the squared error found
ϕ(t) = r⊺r = (x −(RX + Xt))⊺(x −(RX + Xt)
(B.9)
= (x⊺−(X⊺R⊺+ X⊺t⊺))(x −(RX + Xt))
(B.10)
= X⊺R⊺RX + X⊺R⊺tX −X⊺R⊺x
(B.11)
+ X⊺t⊺RX + X⊺t⊺tX −X⊺t⊺x −x⊺RX
(B.12)
−x⊺tX + x⊺x
(B.13)
The squared error is then differentiated with respect to t⊺and put equal to zero.
∇ϕ(t) = ∂
∂t⊺(X⊺R⊺RX + X⊺R⊺tX −X⊺R⊺x
(B.14)
+ X⊺t⊺RX + X⊺t⊺tX −X⊺t⊺x −x⊺RX
(B.15)
−x⊺tX + x⊺x
(B.16)
= X⊺RX + X⊺tX −X⊺x = 0
(B.17)
Finally can t be isolated as the least squares solution to the system
tLS = (x −RX)X⊺(XX⊺)−1
