Abstract
This thesis explores the possibility to use boron-nitride as a way to downscale lithographic
channels for use in future transistor designs which suppresses short-channel eﬀects. I
propose a short-channel transistor consisting of graphene on top of MoS2 encapsulated in
hexagonal boron-nitride (hBN). In this device design patches of ﬂuorographene acts as
insulating layer scattering the carriers to the MoS2 layer.
In this thesis I fabricate van der Waals heterostructures consisting of graphene encapsulated
in hBN and show the possibility to use a SF6 etch to pattern channels while subsequently
binding ﬂuorine atoms to the graphene creating a high-band gap ﬂuorographene layer.
The etch angle of hBN is investigated to determine if there is any relationship between the
crystal lattice and measured angle of the slope. A way to optimize the stacking procedure
when creating van der Waals heterostructures by ultra-high-vacuum annealing is also
presented.
I ﬁnd that it is possible to ﬂuorinate graphene by the SF6 etch and it terminates on the
graphene layer. The resistance of the ﬂuorographene channels are measured and plottet
as function of channel length and the relation is found to be exponential. Comparing
this result to a calculated resistance in MoS2 shows a higher resistance for the ﬂuorinated
channels, around ∼2 kΩfor ﬂuorographene and < 2 kΩfor MoS2, at sub 35 nm lengths.
This result veriﬁes the proposed short-channel transistor design.
I also ﬁnd that the etch angle of hBN most likely is independent from the crystal alignment
and the roughness of the etch is limited by lithographic resolution.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the ﬁrst measurements of the ﬁeld eﬀect in graphene in 2004[1] the ﬁeld of
2D materials has attracted great interest from scientists and the electronic device
community alike. Today thousands of diﬀerent 2D materials[2][3][4] with diﬀerent phy-
sical properties are available and they can be stacked in diﬀerent combinations giving
researches the freedom to engineer complex electronic devices one atomic layer at a
time[3]. These devices are collectively known as van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures.
Due to the fact that researchers can make speciﬁc devices tailored to their needs
the heterostructures are applicable in a wide variety of ﬁelds like regular electronics,
optoelectronics and photovoltaics. The mayor application areas in electronics are
ﬂexible electronics[5], radio-frequency electronics and transistor electronics[6].
In traditional Si based transistor electronics the continuous downscaling of gate-length
to sub 5 nm will be problematic as short channel eﬀects are introduced[7]. 2D de-
vice approaches have been investigated using graphene transistors[8][9] which can
be encapsulated by hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) for high mobility and ballistic
transport[10] or other more complex devices using a transition metal dichalcogenide
(TMD) gate material like MoS2[11] which exhibits a heavier carrier eﬀective mass
capable of suppressing short channel eﬀects[7][12]. In particular a MoS2 transistor
with a gate-length of 1 nm by using a carbon nanotube as gate has been reported[13].
Today state of the art devices are encapsulated in hBN[14] and functionalized by 1D
graphene-metal edge contacts[15] enabling the graphene to act as a tunable contact to
the underlying TMD layer[16].
At the moment heterostructures are on research basis, since the best devices uses
materials exfoliated from high quality crystals, require manual aligning, stacking and
numerous fabrication steps. Evidently this method is not scalable, thus diﬀerent appro-
aches like chemical vapor deposition (CVD) have been investigated. However, making
devices by CVD is inherently diﬃcult due to the fact that diﬀerent 2D materials have
diﬀerent growth conditions[3][17]. Over time the stacking technique has been improved
and the latest method is a hot pick up technique[18] where 2D materials are heated
during stacking as it increases the kinetic energy of contaminations trapped in the
stack enabling them to be eﬀectively squeezed out when dropping down layers slowly
by speeds of µm/min.
In this thesis I investigate a somewhat diﬀerent approach to designing a 2D transistor.

Figure 1.1 Concept sketch of proposed short channel transistor: The current ﬂows from
1D graphene-metal contact through the semi-metal graphene layer and is blocked by a patch
of insulating ﬂuorographene. The current is forced through the TMD layer, back to the
graphene and out through the other metal contact. The TMD band structure shown here is
MoS2. All band structures originates from [2] and are calculated using Density Functional
Theory (DFT).
Fluorographene is naturally created in the SF6 etch fabrication step[19] and is a high
bandgap 2D material[2][20]. In my device I use a single graphene layer where the source
and drain is separated by patches of ﬂuorographene. The etching of hBN introduces
a constant etching angle which can be utilized to pattern narrow gate-length. This
approach solves the scalability issue in part since there is no need for aligning crystal
ﬂakes with a gap between source and drain when stacking or punching holes in the
graphene damaging underlying layers in the case of CVD approaches. The origin of
the hBN etching angle is to date not well understood.
Figure 1.1 shows a concept schematic of my device. I incorporate already proven device
designs, like 1D graphene-metal edge contacts, full hBN encapsulation and shape the
device as a Hall bar. I use a host of diﬀerent methods for device characterization,
like optical microscopy, atomic force microscopy (AFM), Raman spectroscopy and
current/voltage measurements.
In order to create the device in this fashion some key properties must be addres-
sed. First of all, the graphene must be suﬃciently ﬂuorinated to stop the device from
conducting current, thereby forcing the current through the TMD layer. Secondly, the
etch angle of hBN must be understood in order to be utilized most eﬀectively.
I will investigate the etch angle of hBN depending on crystal lattice. Verify that the
SF6 etch creates a suﬃciently insulating layer and lastly, estimate if hBN in addition
to electron beam lithography can be used as a way to make ultra short channels and
ﬁne structures.

2.1
Transistors
In conventional ﬁeld eﬀect transistors (FETs) the channel material used is silicon. Si
is a semiconductor with intrinsic band gap of 1.12 eV[21] and electron mobility of
1400 cm2 V−1 s−1[22]. On ﬁgure 2.1a a schematic of a conventional Si FET is shown.
The grey blocks are contacts: Source, gate and drain. The distance between source
and drain is called the channel length of the transistor and in most cases it is the
same as the gate-length, the width of the gate contact. In the following discussion I
consider devices with channel length equal to gate-length. In Si transistors the channel
material is p-type while it is highly doped n-type around source and drain contacts.
The gate contact is separated from the substrate by a gate oxide. By changing the gate
voltage it is possible to induce carriers in the channel by the ﬁeld-eﬀect. A positive
bias promotes electron carriers (n-type) while a negative bias promotes hole carriers
(p-type)[23]. Thus, by applying a positive gate in the conventional transistor it is
possible to repel holes in the p-type silicon creating a channel between source and
drain switching on the device.
Available processor architectures today uses gate-lengths as short as 20 nm[24] and
as the gate-length decreases with every new processor generation the International
Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS) states that a signiﬁcant problem
is controlling short-channel eﬀects[25]. As the gate-length is decreased everything
must scale accordingly such that power consumption, contact resistances, gate oxide
thickness etc. is constantly in proportion to new device architectures[7] which might
also be a part of the motivation for 2D transistors where device layer spacing is in
order of atomic layers.

As hinted in the introduction it is important to be able to control short-channel eﬀects

dimensions. The short-channel limit is not rigorously deﬁned in literature, but all
agree that problems arises when gate/channel length approaches a few nm. Diﬀerent
authors deﬁne the limit as 5 nm[7], 8 nm[31] or 10 nm[32].
The depletion layer between n and p doped Si at the source and drain contacts are
constant and when the gate and channel length is decreased, the depletion layer
accounts for a larger part of the channel compared to the gate. This eﬀectively lowers
the threshold voltage increasing the diﬃculty in operating the transistor reliably.
Another problem is drain induced barrier lowering which occur when a voltage applied
to the drain enlarges the depletion layer at the drain. This again leads to lowered
threshold voltage as the drain is able to gate the channel. Finally, with decreasing
channel length the probability of tunneling increases and the decrease of gate oxide
increases the gate tunneling[23][33]. Figure 2.4 shows a schematic view of the short-

2.5
Graphene as tunable contact
After creating a device by stacking 2D materials and shaping it, it is ﬁnished by the
deposition of metal contacts. The interface between a semiconductor and metal creates
a Schottky barrier, a potential barrier, which depends on the metal work function and
semiconductor aﬃnity χ[52]. Figure 2.9 shows the formation of the Schottky barrier
when a metal and semiconductor comes into contact.
If the barrier height is in order of KBT the thermal energy excitations are suﬃcient to
pass the barrier and the contact is ohmic causing a linear I −V characteristic. For
higher Schottky barriers or lower temperature the characteristic is non-linear. A study
of metals with diﬀerent work functions shows that a low work function decreases the
Schottky barrier height[53]. It is important to have a low, ohmic contact resistance
in order to measure the intrinsic properties of the device otherwise the transport is
dominated by the external contacts. Thus, by using graphene as a contact layer to
the TMD layer it is possible to decrease the Schottky barrier by gating it.
Figure 2.10 shows a schematic of a MoS2 transistor encapsulated in hBN and functio-
nalized by graphene-metal 1D edge contacts[16]. For this device the contact is ohmic
for VG > 20 V and non-ohmic for VG < 20 V. Figure 2.11 shows the contact resistance
as a function of gate voltage for diﬀerent temperatures, this shows that the contact
resistance is tunable by changing the work function of graphene. The inset shows con-
tact resistance as a function of temperature for diﬀerent gate voltages. It is seen that
for high gate voltage the resistance decreases as the temperature decreases indicating
low resistance contacts. This implies that the contacts are metallic since the resistance
decreases with temperature.

Chapter 4
Experimental results
In this chapter the experimental results are presented. Firstly, a method to optimize
the stack quality by Ultra-high-vacuum (UHV) anneal is presented. Then a study of
the etch angle dependence on crystal lattice and lastly electrical characterization of
ﬂuorinated devices are presented.
4.1
Stack optimization
Creating vdW heterostructures using the methods described in the previous chapter is
cumbersome and takes a lot of time when adding up all the stacking and cleaning steps.
A dirty stack with many contaminations is fast to make since the ﬂake drop down speed
can be higher and some cleaning steps can be omitted. A test is carried out to see if
a contaminated stack can be cleaned by UHV annealing. Figure 4.1 shows the dirty
hBN-G-hBN stack before and after annealing with complimentary dark ﬁeld images.
Pre annealing a lot of contaminations are seen and after UHV anneal of 1000 ◦C for
1 hour at a pressure of 2 −5 × 10−5 torr, many areas that were once contaminated
looks clean. When the stack is heated the contaminations become mobile. A fold in
the hBN ﬂake has appeared and it might be due to the contaminations grouping up
along the fold. AFM images of a clean area is also presented showing a clean interface
after annealing.
Some care must be taken when choosing a substrate for this stack cleaning method.
Figure 4.2 shows the Au/Cr index marks of the wafer before and after annealing. After
the annealing process the gold has evaporated leaving sporadic traces of chromium
which are diﬃcult to use as alignment marks when doing EBL. This is easily circum-
vented by using index marks of a metal with higher melting point like platinum or
titanium.

Chapter 5
Summary
In this thesis I have fabricated van der Waals heterostructures consisting of the 2D
material graphene encapsulated in hexagonal Boron Nitride. I have seen that by
using the SF6 it is possible to create channels in the device that terminates at the
graphene interface and ﬂuorinate the graphene by binding ﬂuorine atoms to the surface
creating ﬂuorographene. I have measured the resistance of the resulting channels of
ﬂuorographene and the results, unexpectedly shows an exponential relation between
resistance and channel length. By comparing the experimental results to the resistance
of a MoS2 monolayer, displayed on ﬁgure 4.21, it looks like ﬂuorographene has a
larger resistance than both MoS2 in the on and oﬀstate for short channels. This
ﬁnding validates my proposed concept of a hBN-G-MoS2-hBN short-channel transistor.
I have investigated the hBN etch angle dependence of the crystal lattice alignment
and found it most likely independent from crystal alignment as the angles reported
seem randomly distributed around the mean. Although, one of the angles from the
crystal structure is present, previous results show constant etching angles not found in
the crystal structure.
The roughness of the etched channels seems to be limited by the lithographic resolution
of the mask used to produce the sample as shown on ﬁgure 4.8. This enables hBN to
be used to downscale lithography by using it as a mask to pattern small channels or
structures if the overall lithographic resolution can be controlled.
Lastly, I have shown a way to optimize the stacking procedure by UHV annealing a
dirty sample, saving time when fabricating devices.
5.1
Further studies and outlook
Several things can be elaborated on. For instance the exponential relation of resistance
as function of channel length in the ﬂuorinated channels must be veriﬁed. Also, the
ﬂuorination coverage of the channels needs to be investigated. This could be addressed
by scanning tunnel microscopy of ﬂuorographene on Cu. Lastly, a device featuring a
short channel in the range of a few nm still needs to be fabricated.
The contact resistance should also be looked into, since the 1D edge contacts are
terminated by ﬂuorine atoms.

Appendix B
Glass slide production
The following recipe can be used to make the glass slides used for stack fabrication.
Requirements
• PDMS
• 30% PPC in anisole
• Super glue
• Microscope slides
Utilities: Spin coater, plasma asher, hot plate.
Procedure
A chunk of PDMS is plasma etched for 5 min. Afterwards the PPC solution is quickly
dispensed on top to minimize the exposure time of the clean PDMS surface to air.
This achieves a greater adhesion between the PPC/PDMS layers and cleaner interface
between the layers.
The PPC/PDMS chunk is placed in the spin coater and spun at 1500 RPM for
∼1 min. The PPC/PDMS is baked at 160 ◦C for ∼10 min.
The PPC/PDMS chunk is cut out in small cubes and glued on top of the glass
slide. A schematic of the ﬁnished slide is shown on ﬁgure B.1.
