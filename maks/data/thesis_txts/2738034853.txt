Abstract
People spend around 69% of their time in residences[1]. This extensive time spent indoors
emphasises the importance of maintaining a healthy and comfortable indoor environmen-
tal quality.
This thesis evaluates the indoor environmental quality in the studio apartment called Sciv-
ing Student Housing. In particular, it investigates the effect of the local climate on indoor
environmental quality and the performance of the decentralised ventilation unit.
The indoor environmental parameters temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration
and sound were measured in the housing unit in Denmark for three weeks in April and
May. Additionally, the thermal comfort parameters PMV and PPD and the local discomfort
parameters draught rate and vertical air temperature difference were measured during a
warm spring day in May.
The measurements showed a clear tendency for overheating in the Sciving unit due to the
large amount of solar gains. No local discomfort was detected. The noise from the decen-
tralised ventilation was measured at 36.4 dB(A), slightly above the requirement for noise
from service equipment in a residence. Due to the decentralised ventilation switching
between supply and return, there was a disturbing discontinuous noise.
Simulations of indoor environment and energy consumption were also conducted for three
different climate zones: Temperate, subpolar and subtropical.
The simulation of the temperate climate in Denmark showed slightly better air quality
than the measurements. The large number of hours with overheating greatly affected the
thermal comfort.
In the subpolar climate, Iceland, there were nearly no overheating hours, and the heat-
ing energy consumption was 55% higher than in Denmark, and the ventilation energy
consumption was 6% lower.
In subtropical Texas, USA, the heating and cooling energy consumption was 80% of the
heating energy consumption in Denmark. Moreover, the energy consumption of the ven-
tilation was 32% higher than in Denmark because of the extremely high relative humidity
in the ambient air.
Further, considering the findings in this study, it is strongly recommended that Sciving
increase the insulation thicknesses in the construction elements to lower heat losses, im-
plement automatic actuators on at least one of the windows, and implement an automatic
venting hatch in the kitchen to reduce overheating in the Sciving Housing unit in temperate
climates. Overall, this thesis emphasises the importance of tailoring ventilation and build-
ing design strategies to local climatic conditions to optimise indoor environmental quality
and energy consumption.

1
Introduction
The quality of the indoor environment significantly impacts the health, comfort, and pro-
ductivity of its occupants. This is particularly relevant in residential buildings, where indi-
viduals spend most of their time. According to Klepeis et al. [1], people spend around 69%
of their time in residences. This extensive amount of time spent indoors emphasises the
importance of maintaining a high-quality indoor environment. Factors such as air quality,
temperature, humidity, and daylight all contribute to the overall indoor environment and,
consequently, to the well-being of the occupants.
In 2019, a debate arose in Denmark regarding the lack of student housing. Over 100 bro-
kers in Denmark collectively asserted the necessity for approximately 31,000 additional
student housing units[2]. By 2021, this demand had escalated to 33,000 units, driven
by an increasing number of available study places. Scandinavian Living(Sciving) recog-
nised the demand and saw the opportunity to start a design project called Sciving Student
Housing. The purpose of this unit was to improve the quality of student living experiences
by creating a compact unit that holds all the necessary functions, like a toilet, shower,
kitchenette, and spacious living space[3]. Moreover, the urgent housing demand called
for a flexible pre-fabricated solution.
1.1
Problem statement
This thesis aims to evaluate the performance of decentralised ventilation in a Sciving
housing unit by analysing measurements and simulations of the indoor environment and
energy use. Given the diverse climatic conditions that buildings are subjected to, it is
essential to examine how local climates affect indoor environmental quality and the per-
formance of building systems, such as ventilation units. Through a comprehensive eval-
uation of indoor environmental quality in the unit, this research will provide insights into
the effectiveness of current ventilation practices and highlight potential areas for improve-
ment. Furthermore, this study will explore and propose design solutions related to the
U-value of constructions, shading, ventilation, and window configurations to enhance in-
door environmental quality across three distinct climate zones.
The study will try to answer the following research question:
”How will temperature, CO2-concentration, and relative humidity vary
with the use situation and the local outdoor climate?”
With the following subquestions:
• Is decentralised ventilation efficient enough in maintaining a healthy indoor environ-
ment in the housing unit, or will there be a need for additional natural ventilation?
• Will there be overheating?
• How will the housing unit perform in other climates?
• How can the indoor environment be improved?

Theory
To evaluate the indoor environment quality of a building, this chapter introduces the indoor
environmental parameters and their comfort and health effects. The chapter also intro-
duces the concepts of heating, cooling, and pollution load because these are relevant
when evaluating building design and the chosen ventilation system. Different ventilation
solution are also described. At last, the indoor environmental quality requirements for
residential buildings are shown.
2.1
Indoor environmental quality
Indoor environmental quality (IEQ) is defined by thermal comfort, air quality, sound and
daylight/lighting. However, since this thesis focuses on indoor climate in relation to ven-
tilation, daylight and lighting will not be investigated.
2.1.1
Thermal comfort
The thermal balance of the human body is obtained when the internal heat production and
the heat loss to the environment are the same[4]. The heat losses to the environment can
be in the form of convection, evaporation, or radiation. If the thermal balance is not ob-
tained and the internal heat production is high, for example, the human thermo-regulatory
system will automatically try to change the skin temperature and sweat secretion to main-
tain heat balance[4]. The thermal balance of the human body can be expressed as Equa-
tion 2.1.
S = (M −W) −(R + C + K + E + Res)
(2.1)
where
S: Heat storage in the body
M: Metabolic heat production
W: Physical (mechanical) work
R: Radiant heat loss
C: Convective heat loss
K: Conductive heat loss
E: Evaporative heat loss from skin
Res: Heat loss from respiration (convection and evaporation)
This thermal balance is influenced by the human’s activity level, clothing, the air temper-
ature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity and air humidity. When these parameters
have been measured or estimated, the thermal comfort can be evaluated by applying the
PMV-, PPD indices and the local thermal discomfort.

Humidity
There are two ways to express humidity: absolute and relative humidity[4]. Absolute
humidity states the water vapour pressure in the air. In contrast, relative humidity (RH) is
the ratio between the partial pressure of water vapour in humid air and the water vapour
saturation pressure at the same temperature and total pressure[5].
The ideal relative humidity level typically ranges between 25% to 60%, see Section 2.5.
Air humidity is crucial when considering thermal comfort, as it directly impacts how hu-
mans perceive the indoor temperature. Higher humidity will make a person feel warmer,
particularly when the air temperature is high or by a high activity level [6]. High relative hu-
midity reduces the body’s ability to lose heat through perspiration and evaporation. When
this happens, people may perceive temperatures to be higher than they are.
Air humidity also affects indoor air quality. Low humidity can lead to dry skin, irritated
eyes, and respiratory issues, while high humidity can increase mould and house dust
mites growth, aggravate allergies, and cause discomfort [7]. Maintaining optimal humidity
levels through proper ventilation is essential to ensure a healthy indoor environment.

mean radiant temperature, relative air velocity, and air humidity have been measured[8].

2.1.2
Indoor air quality (IAQ)
Indoor air quality (IAQ) refers to the quality of air within buildings as it relates to the health
and comfort of the occupants. The indoor air quality is usually evaluated by the concen-
trations of CO2- and volatile organic compounds (VOC).
IAQ is influenced by temperature, humidity, ventilation, occupancy, air purity, and pollu-
tants. Poor IAQ can lead to various health problems, including building related symptoms
like headaches, fatigue, respiratory issues, and exacerbation of allergies and asthma [7].
Monitoring and controlling IAQ through proper ventilation, filtration, and removal of pollu-
tants is essential for maintaining a comfortable and healthy indoor environment.
CO2-concentration
Humans’ metabolic process produces carbon dioxide (CO2). Therefore, the indoor con-
centration of CO2 is a good indicator of the concentration of other human bio-effluents in
the air as well [7]. As people exhale CO2, poorly ventilated indoor spaces can experience
a buildup of this gas, leading to decreased air quality.
In residential buildings, high levels of CO2 often occur during the night, when people typ-
ically sleep with their windows and doors closed. High levels of CO2 can cause drowsi-
ness, headaches, and impaired cognitive function [7]. Thus, monitoring and controlling

2.3
Ventilation principles
Air can be transported into and out of buildings by either natural forces or fan power[13].
This section describes three of the most common ventilation principles.
Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation is a common ventilation principle in residential buildings. It utilises
the buoyancy of warm air and the local wind conditions. The buoyancy of warm air can
be utilised by having openings located close to the ceiling, where warm and polluted
air accumulates indoors. The cool and fresh air would then enter through lower-located
openings, and the warm and polluted air would exit through the higher-located openings.
Furthermore, wind pressure drives air movement through building openings. The wind
creates positive and negative pressure zones, allowing fresh air to flow in through an
opening and polluted air to exit through another opening[13].
Natural ventilation can be done using three different ventilation principles: Single-sided
ventilation, cross-ventilation and stack ventilation (see Figure 2.5). Single-sided ventila-
tion is less efficient than cross-ventilation since the air simultaneously has to travel in and
out of the same opening in the facade[13]. Thus, the room depth should not be more than
2-2.5 times the room height for the air to ventilate the full depth of the room[13]. Addition-
ally, the wind and buoyancy forces may work in the opposite direction and thus reduce
the airflow[13]. Cross-ventilation is the most efficient of the natural ventilation principles
because it is wind-driven. Stack ventilation is also quite efficient because it is wind- and
buoyancy-driven.
Natural ventilation can be manual or automatic, meaning occupants can manually vent
when needed, or the windows can be equipped with automatic actuators and sensors,

allowing the windows to open and close according to indoor temperature, humidity or

The main advantage of natural ventilation is the fan related energy savings because it
utilises purely natural forces[14]. It does not require any extra space for ducts, etc. More-
over, according to [14], there is evidence that occupants prefer to have control over their
indoor environment, which they have to a high degree with natural ventilation. Neverthe-
less, there are also some disadvantages to natural ventilation. By solely being driven by
natural forces, there can be a large variation in the air flows and their direction, which can
result in occupant discomfort. Natural ventilation also contributes to a great amount of
heat loss, resulting in a higher heating demand.
Mechanical ventilation
Mechanical ventilation uses electrically-driven fans to push outdoor air into the building
and to extract warm and polluted air from the building. The air is then distributed in the
building via ducts and supplied to the zones through air terminal devices (ATD).
The centralised ventilation system (CVS) is a mechanical ventilation system with a central
air handling unit (AHU) and an air distribution system, as described above. CVS is the
most popular ventilation system in all types of buildings[15]. However, this system requires
a lot of space for ducting and extra equipment for zone-based control.
A decentralised ventilation system (DVS) is a newer mechanical ventilation system and
has been around since the late 1990’s. There are many different sizes of DVS; the larger
units are often used in classrooms, meeting rooms, etc., while the small compact units
are becoming increasingly common in residential buildings. DVS is especially used when
renovating older buildings to improve indoor environmental quality. The small and com-
pact DVS is usually implemented with a plastic duct inside the external wall, see

Hybrid ventilation
Hybrid ventilation is a combination of mechanical and natural ventilation. This ventilation
principle saves energy by using mechanical ventilation when necessary and otherwise us-
ing natural ventilation. A good design of hybrid ventilation consists of a properly planned
control system, which can minimise the large amount of energy consumed from over-
ventilation and the indoor air quality problems associated with underventilation[17]. The
criteria for switching between natural and mechanical ventilation could, for example, be
defined by the outdoor temperature, indoor temperature and CO2-concentration[18]. The
criteria for when the residence is occupied or not differs because when the residence is
unoccupied, the hybrid ventilation’s first priority would be energy efficiency[18]. Further,
hybrid ventilation also allows a reduction in the amount of ducts and, therefore, a reduction
in its carbon footprint[19].

Heating and Cooling systems
Heating and cooling systems are essential for maintaining comfortable indoor environ-
ments by regulating temperature. Heating systems are designed to increase the indoor
temperature during cold weather. Common types of heating systems are: [20]
• Furnaces: Burn fuel (natural gas, oil, or propane) to produce heat.
• Heat Pumps: Transfer heat from the outside air or ground into the building.
• Boilers: Heat water or produce steam for radiators or radiant floor systems.
• Electric Heaters: Convert electrical energy directly into heat.
On the other hand, cooling systems remove heat from indoor spaces to lower the temper-
ature during warm weather. Common types of cooling systems are:
• Air Conditioners: Use a refrigeration cycle to extract heat from indoor air and expel
it outside.
• Heat Pumps: Can function as both heaters and coolers by reversing the heat trans-
fer process.
• Evaporative Coolers: Cool air through the evaporation of water.
• Chillers: Used in large buildings to cool water, which is then used to cool the air.
The Coefficient of Performance (COP) is a measure of the efficiency of heating and cooling
systems, particularly heat pumps[21]. COP indicates the ratio between the heat the heat
pump delivers and the amount of power it uses[22]. Here, it also applies that the higher
the COP, the higher the efficiency.
COP = Heating output
Energy input
(2.29)
The Energy Efficiency Ratio (EER) is another measure of the efficiency of cooling systems,
particularly air conditioners.
EER = Cooling output
Energy input
(2.30)
The terms Seasonal COP (SCOP) and Seasonal EER (SEER) have the same foundation
as COP and EER. The difference is that SCOP and SEER show the annual average
calculation based on several tests, while COP and EER are only based on a single test
at a specific temperature[21].

Requirements
The Danish Standard DS/EN 16798 [23] provides indoor environment requirements and
design ventilation rates. The requirements are divided into four indoor environmental
categories (IEQcat)[10]:
I: High expectation - Should be selected for occupants with special needs (children,
elderly, disabled)
II: Medium expectation - The normal level used for design and operation
III: Moderate expectation - Will still provide an acceptable environment, Some risk
of reduced performance of the occupants
IV: Low expectation - Should only be used for a short time of the year or in spaces
with very short time of occupancy
Residential buildings are usually required to obtain an indoor environmental quality of
category II (IEQII).
The thermal comfort requirements for residences are the same as for other types of build-
ings; however, some lower and higher operative temperatures are accepted. The general
acceptable operative temperature range during the heating season for living spaces in
residential buildings is 20°C to 25°C, and for the cooling season, it is 23°C to 26°C[23].
The Danish standard also defines a recommended criteria for acceptable deviation of a
maximum of 6% of annual hours. In addition to these requirements, the Danish Building
Regulations requires a deviation of the operative temperature of no more than 100 hours
with temperatures over 27°C and 25 hours with temperatures over 28°C [24]. The local
discomfort requirements consist of a maximum air velocity of 0.19 m/s in summer and
0.16 m/s in winter, as well as a draught rate less than 20%, and a vertical air tempera-
ture difference between ankles and neck of less than 3°C, with a percentage predicted
dissatisfied (PD) of less than 5%.

3.3.5
Optimisation of the model
This section will describe the changes that were made to the model during the optimisation
phase.
Automatic windows were tested, and their effect was evaluated. When implementing
automatic windows in the IDA ICE model, the window opening control was changed to
either PI temperature control or PI temperature control with the schedule in Figure B.8, in
Appendix B.3. This schedule allowed the windows to only open during the day to prevent
burglary at night.
Venting hatches are also a possible venting solution[29]. They usually consist of an ex-
ternal louvre panel and an insulated internal hatch. The louvre prevents birds, etc., from
entering the building while venting, and the insulated hatch secures a lower heat loss and
solar gains than a window. Venting hatches were tested in the optimisation phase. The
venting hatch was modelled like a window in IDA ICE with a frame fraction of 0.999999[29].
The U-value of the frame/venting hatch was set to be 0.7 W/(m2·K), which was estimated
according to similar products on the market (An example is shown in Appendix A.6[30]).
The maximum opening fraction for the venting hatches was also set to be 60% in both
height and width [29][26]. The openings of the venting hatches were controlled by PI tem-
perature control, without any schedule, since the louvre would eliminate the chances of
burglary.
The reference DVS has an optional function called Night Mode. When the night mode is
activated, the fan speed switches from 66% to 33% for 8 hours. The effect of the night
mode on the IEQ was investigated in the optimisation phase.
An alternative DVS with variable air volume (VAV) was also tested. Table 3.7 shows its fan
speeds and the corresponding air flows. The product specifications for the VAV ventilation
(AirMaster) are in Appendix A.3.

This chapter presents a comprehensive investigation of the indoor environment in the

typical ventilation systems because the fan changes direction every 70 seconds, from
supply to return. Therefore, even though the noise level at a fan speed of 66% only

Discussion
The objective of this project was to evaluate the indoor environmental quality in the studio
apartment called Sciving Student Housing. In particular, the effect of the local climate on
indoor environmental quality and the performance of the ventilation unit was investigated.
This was done with measurements and simulations of indoor environmental quality and
energy use. Finally, shading, ventilation, and window design solutions were explored and
suggested for three different climate zones. The following section includes a discussion
and comparison of the measurements and the simulation results, as well as a comparison
of the indoor environment in the unit for the three different climate zones. A discussion on
factors that could be sources of errors in measurements and simulation is also included.
The measurements showed a clear tendency for overheating in the Sciving unit due to
the large amount of solar gains. No local discomfort was detected; however, slightly
higher velocities were measured by the table and sofa. These are assumed to be due to
the airflow from the heat pump. Note that radiant asymmetry was not measured. The air
quality was evaluated by the CO2-concentration, which showed that when being occupied
by one person, the DVS managed to keep the CO2-concentration below the requirements
for living rooms.
The measurements were compared with the results of the indoor climate simulation. Dur-
ing the first week of measuring, the daily mean outdoor temperature was between 2-6°C.
Therefore, these measurements were compared with the spring equinox results from the
simulation. The daily mean outdoor temperature was 15.5°C during day 14 of the mea-
suring period and had 14 hours of sunshine. Therefore, these measurements were com-
pared with the results of the simulation for summer solstice. The measurements showed
temperatures around 2-2.5°C higher than the simulation, even though the outdoor air tem-
peratures were higher for the simulation than in real life. This leads to the assumption of
an even higher number of hours with overheating in the unit than the simulations showed.
The indoor air quality was evaluated by the CO2-concentration. During the measure-
ments, a steady-state CO2-concentration of approximately 1025ppm was measured with a
sleeping occupant at night, while the simulations showed a steady-state CO2-concentration
of approximately 900ppm during the nights.
The ability for the DVS and the bathroom exhaust to lower the humidity levels in the unit
could not be tested in real life since the shower drain and sink in the mock-up were not

In residential buildings, the implementation of heat exchangers with moisture transfer can
adversely affect indoor moisture levels. This is primarily because occupants in these en-
vironments generate substantial amounts of moisture daily. As a result, integrating mois-
ture transfer into the heat exchanger may increase indoor humidity issues, like increased
mould and house dust mites growth, aggravating allergies and causing discomfort. How-
ever, this depends very much on the climate.
The humidity analysis showed how the humidity ratio in the temperate climate is often low
during the winter, resulting in too dry indoor air. Here, the heat exchanger with moisture
transfer would increase the humidity ratio in the supply air, raising the indoor minimum RH
to secure fewer hours with RH below 25%. However, during the summer, the humidity in
the ambient air is higher, and with the moisture transfer in the heat exchanger increasing
the humidity ratio in the supply, the RH of the supply air would go up to 68%, causing
discomfort and potentially an increase in house dust mite growth.
Moreover, using a heat exchanger with moisture transfer in humid climates has the same
effect during the winter but the opposite effect during the summer. This means it has
a positive effect during the winter as well as in the summer. Appendix E.3 showed an
increase in humidity ratio in supply when the heat exchanger was heating the supply air
and a decrease when it was cooling the supply air.

Some sources of error have already been discussed, however, after the analysis and
evaluation of all the results, the specific fan power (SFP) in the simulations was found
to be twice as high as the SFP of the DVS used in the Sciving unit. This meant that
the ventilation energy consumption results of the simulations were twice as high as they
would have been in real life. Moreover, the simulations were only made for south-oriented
windows, which would have been the worst case in Denmark and Texas. However, due
to extremely cold temperatures in Iceland, it would have been interesting to investigate a
unit with north-oriented windows.
Further, the measurements conducted with occupancy were limited, and there was no
moisture production by showering, cooking, etc. Therefore, the measurements of RH are
not considered representative of what they would have been if people lived in the unit.

measurements, it would be necessary to have the drains in the unit connected to sewers
so proper humidity measurements could be conducted.

Conclusion
This thesis analysed and evaluated the indoor environment in the Sciving Student Hous-
ing. The performance of a decentralised ventilation unit in the Sciving unit was evaluated
based on the measurements and simulations of the indoor environment and energy use.
Furthermore, a basis for future improvements was found by simulating the unit in three
different climate zones.
The measurements and simulations conducted in this study revealed that overheating in
the Sciving unit was primarily due to substantial solar gains, though no local discomfort
was detected. Air quality, evaluated by CO2 concentration, met the requirements for living
rooms with one occupant present, demonstrating the effectiveness of the decentralised
ventilation system (DVS). However, there were notable differences between measured
and simulated temperatures. These differences were likely due to differences in real-life
and simulated material properties, weather conditions, setpoints, internal heat gains, and
control settings of DVS and window openings.
The study found that different climate zones posed unique challenges and opportunities
for optimising indoor environmental quality and energy use. For example, the subpolar
climate resulted in minimal overheating and higher heating energy consumption, whereas
the subtropical climate required significant ventilation energy due to high ambient humid-
ity. Simulation results suggested that adding insulation, modifying external doors, and
implementing shading could improve energy efficiency and comfort in these climates.
Additionally, controlling the DVS with a CO2 sensor rather than relative humidity (RH)
or constant airflow could enhance air quality and reduce ventilation energy consumption
across all climates.
The analysis also highlighted the complexity of using heat exchangers with moisture trans-
fer in residential buildings. These heat exchangers are beneficial during winter in the
temperate and subpolar climates. They can cause too high indoor RH in the summer and
vice-versa in the subtropical climates.
The research question has been analysed and answered throughout this thesis. To an-
swer the subquestions: Yes, decentralised ventilation was sufficient to secure healthy air
quality; however, extra natural ventilation was needed to reduce the annual hours of over-
heating. Further, considering the findings in this study, it is strongly recommended that
Sciving increase the insulation thicknesses in the construction elements to lower heat
losses, implement automatic actuators on at least one of the windows, and implement
an automatic venting hatch in the kitchen to reduce overheating in the Sciving Housing
unit in temperate climates. Overall, this thesis underscores the importance of tailoring
ventilation and building design strategies to local climatic conditions to optimise indoor
environmental quality and energy consumption.
