Abstract
In this project, the biomechanics of the right and left ventricle of the commercial bivenВӯ
tricular phantom (DHPВӯ01, Shelley Medical Imaging Technologies) have been investiВӯ
gated.
Starting with 2D ultrasound scanning of the biВӯventricular heart phantom (DHPВӯ01), the
project continues with the development of a proper CAD model of the heart phantom and
investigates the performance of the digital model in COMSOL Multiphysics 5.6.
In order to create the correct 3D geometries of the ventricles, the segmentation is perВӯ
formed using Matlab 2020a.
The first part of the project consists on a BВӯMode ultrasound scanning of the phantom with
the use of a robot arm for consistent imaging plane separation. Once the 3D geometry
of the two ventricles is reconstructed, it is then transformed into a proper 3D CAD model
applicable in finite element modelling. A computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation
of the model is then performed.
The final part includes a comparison between the CFD analysis and an ultrasound vector
flow imaging (VFI) measurement of the real system present in the laboratory. The hypothВӯ
esis of the project is to develop a simulation model that does not deviate more than 10%
when compared to the measurements taken with VFI US in the laboratory.
The parameters used for comparison are the velocities extracted from a scan plane taken
from VFI US measurements and the simulated velocities extracted from a plane obtained
in CFD analysis.
After the comparison has been made, the obtained accuracy results in percentage are
mainly within the 10%, except for certain values. Due to the presence of this exceeded
values, it is not possible to accept the hypothesis.
However, it is very important to consider that the comparison has been implemented beВӯ
tween two planes that have not been aligned yet.
Due to this likely mismatch between the planes, and also due to the comparison between
velocities that may not always represent the exact same streamlines, we cannot accept
the hypothesis for entire but still consider the developed simulation model as a good one
and continue on trying to validate it with further analyses.

1
Introduction
The purpose of the project is to develop a simulation model of a biВӯventricular heart
phantom. This is done firstly by scanning the phantom and creating a three dimensional
(3D) geometry and a consecutive ComputerВӯAided Design (CAD ) model through the use
of computerised processing of images. Once the CAD model is ready it can be imported
into COMSOL to develop a simulation model. The validity of this digital model has to
be then verified by comparing the Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis with the
vector flow imaging (VFI) ultrasound measurements of the real system taken in the laboВӯ
ratory.
The dynamic heart phantom used for this study is a biВӯventricular heart model, made
of two ventricles without any atria or valves. This explains why in this project the study is
limited on the biomechanics of the ventricles.
1.1
Motivation
I have decided to work on this project because I am interested in CAD modelling and
medical image analysis and because I find the area of biomechanics of the heart very
interesting.
Cardiac diseases are extremely dangerous and are considered the leading cause of death
globally according to World Health Organization (WHO) [6].
The diseases of the heart can be divided into congenital heart defects (CHD) and acВӯ
quired cardiovascular diseases (CVD) and both of them are very important for population
and society due to prevalence, morbidity, mortality and economical costs [7]. Economics
costs related to CVD are growing and this is due to the aging of the general population
in Western countries as a consequence of improved diagnostic methods and new treatВӯ
ment options [7]. The prevalence of cardiovascular diseases will increase even more as
a result of the following three factors: the aging of the population, continued declines in
mortality from cardiovascular disease, and increasing rates of obesity and diabetes [8].
In order to diagnose and treat these cardiac conditions an understanding of the system
is needed and this is one of the main reasons why it is important to focus on the study of
the heart.
The study of the cardiovascular system, and more specifically of the heart, in fact, is
an important and challenging part of the current research field at an international level
and finding an effective solution for those diseases has become one of the research top
priorities. The study of the biomechanics of the heart and especially the understanding
of the flow patterns in the physiologic and pathophysiologic heart aims to understand the
heart remodelling and diseases processes, and to help in designing medical devices and
in providing better diagnostic.
Several imaging modalities are employed daily for diagnosing of cardiac conditions by
examining the cardiac blood flow with conventional xВӯray, echocardiography, computed
tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), radioВӯnuclide imaging, and angiogВӯ
raphy [9]. Echocardiography is clinically far more used than CT and MRI as it is a safe,
fast, cheap and reliable estimator for cardiac flow [10].
This study will contribute to the ongoing project of the research team at Center for Fast
Ultrasound Imaging (CFU), which aims to develop a patientВӯspecific heart model in order
to study flow properties and validate new ultrasound imaging methods.

Once developed, the simulation model will be used at CFU for further CFD analyses and
studies. The prospect of using those imaging phantoms gives the possibility to avoid the
need for real subjects and provides a steady frame allowing more reproducible results.
The ultimate objective for this research field is to be able to achieve a complete underВӯ
standing of the dynamic heart phantom (DHPВӯ01) followed by a full and precise compreВӯ
hension of the behaviour of the ventricles in the human heart.
1.2
Hypothesis
Hypothesis 1. The developed simulation of the biventricular heart model has a precision
within 10% when compared to the estimated velocities from the VFI US measurements
taken on the Dynamic Heart Phantom from Shelley.
1.3
Scope
The scope is limited to developing a proper simulation model of the right and left ventriВӯ
cle of the DHPВӯ01. This is a dynamic phantom which allows to a variety of motions. These
motions correspond to cycles of stretching and compression. The phantom is capable of
torsion but not of rotation. It differs from the more complex physiological motion of the
heart, where e.g. a rotation is also observed. The finite element (FE) models that will be
performed in this study are fluid dynamics models.

2.1.1
Anatomy of the circulatory system and function
The human heart is a muscular pump composed of 4 chambers, visible in Figure 2.1. It
beats an average of 70 times each minute, 24 hours each day, 365 days each year for a
lifetime [2]. The heart is an organ that pumps blood throughout the body via the circulatory
system, supplying oxygen and nutrients to the tissues and removing carbon dioxide and
other wastes [11].
Figure 2.1: Internal structure of the human heart anatomy and blood circulation, from [2]
Figure 2.2 shows the layers of the heart, which is composed of the outer epicardium,
the myocardium or muscle layer and the smooth endocardium, which lines the chambers
of the heart [2] .
Figure 2.2: Layers of the heart, showing the endocardium, the myocardium, the visceral
pericardium, the pericardial cavity, the parietal pericardium and the fibrous pericardium.
[2]
The circulatory system can be divided into two parts: the right heart or pulmonary cirВӯ
culation, which moves blood through the lungs and creates a link with the gas exchange
function of the respiratory system, and the left heart or systemic circulation, which moves

electrolytes and hormones needed to regulate body function.
The four chambers of the heart consist of two atria (right atrium, which receives blood
returning to the heart from the systemic circulation, and left atrium, which receives oxyВӯ
genated blood from the lungs) and two ventricles. The walls between the two atria and
the two ventricles are called interatrial and interventricular septa.
While the myocardium found in the ventricles is thick to allow forceful contractions to
pump blood out of the heart, the myocardium of the atria is much thinner, and serves as
a reservoir for blood coming into the heart.

2.1.2
Cardiac cycle
The cardiac cycle describes the rhythmic pumping action of the heart and is divided into
two parts. Systole is the period during which the ventricles contract and blood is ejected
from the heart. Diastole, instead, occurs the ventricles relax and allow for filling to occur
[2].
The cardiac cycle is a series of pressure changes that take place within the heart. These
pressure changes result in the movement of blood through different chambers of the heart
and the body as a whole. These pressure changes originate as conductive electrochemВӯ
ical changes within the myocardium that result in the concentric contraction of cardiac
muscle. Valves within the heart direct blood movement, which leads to organized propulВӯ
sion of blood to the next chamber. This rhythmic sequence causes changes in pressure
and volume [12].
In general, the pressures in the right ventricle and in the pulmonary artery are only about
a fifth of the pressures found in the left ventricle and in the aorta respectively [13].
2.1.3
Right and left ventricles
The two lower cavities of the heart differ from each other in their shape, dimension and
functionalities. As immediately visible from figure 2.3, the right ventricle has a thinner wall
compared to the left one.
This difference in wall thickness can be explained considering the different functions of
the two ventricles. The increased thickness of the left ventricular wall compared to the
right one results from the additional work that this ventricle is required to perform [2].
The prime objective of the cardiac muscles of the left ventricle is to pump oxygenated
blood to the peripheral cells of the body and maintaining homeostasis and integrated
health. To do so, the cardiac muscles have to keep a fairly high maximum blood pressure
called systolic blood (120 mmHg) and a minimum blood pressure called diastolic blood
pressure (80 mmHg). During the left ventricular contraction (systole), the intraventricular
blood pressure can increase to 300 mmHg.
The right ventricle receives the deoxygenated blood from the right atrium and pumps it
to the pulmonary artery. The pulmonary circulation from the right ventricle to the lungs is
delicate and short. Too high blood pressure can damage the pulmonary circulation and
the lungs.

Probes
Different types of US images can be taken based on the choice of a specific ultrasound
transducer, or probe. A probe is a device that produces sound waves that bounce off body
tissues and make echoes. The transducer also receives the echoes and sends them to a
computer that uses them to create an image called sonogram. The essential element of
each probe is a piezoelectric crystal that serves to generate as well as receive ultrasound
waves.
Probe types are most easily distinguished by looking at the shape of the probe. Linear
probes, for example, have a flat appearance. They are used to scan small parts and
shallow structures. With this type of probes the piezoelectric crystal arrangement is linear,
the shape of the beam is rectangular, and the nearВӯfield resolution is good. On the other
hand, convex probes have a curvilinear piezoelectric crystal arrangement and this allows
for a wider field of view. The beam shape is convex and the transducer is good for wider
examinations.
US measurements of the entire phantom, including solid part, right ventricle and left
ventricle, were only possible through the use of the convex (6C2) probe.

scan the RV and LV separately. For this reason, this probe was used twice firstly taking
images of the left ventricle and secondly of the right one.

Image Segmentation
Image segmentation can be defined as the segregation of pixels of interest for effective
processing. The process of segmentation does associate pixel data of only the region
of interest. It defines an initial mask by dividing the image into two regions: foreground
(=1) and background (=0). Segmentation is a crucial process in Image analysis because
it paves path for future processing of images.
Image Segmentation is therefore done at a 2D level and here it is performed with the help
of a matlab tool called вҖҷImage SegmenterвҖҷ. This tool helps on creating three 2D masks,
one for each plane (XY,XZ,YZ).
The method used is the вҖҷGraph CutвҖҷ method. Using this tool the foreground and the backВӯ
ground areas can be selected manually just by clicking on the interested areas of the
image.
Once the 2D mask is created, it is possible to refine it by using additional functions such
as вҖҷFill HolesвҖҷ or вҖҷClear BordersвҖҷ. The final implementation used in every mask is the funcВӯ
tion вҖҷJ = imclose(I, SE)вҖҷ, where dilation followed by erosion are included. This allows the
removal of holes smaller than the structuring element.
This function performs morphological closing on the grey scale or binary image I, returnВӯ
ing the closed image J. SE is a single structuring element object returned by the вҖҷstrelвҖҷ
or вҖҷoffsetstrelвҖҷ functions. The morphological close operation is a dilation followed by an
erosion, using the same structuring element for both operations.
The Image Segmenter tool automatically generates the script code.
This method is specific and not general because it requires the need to recreate the 2D
masks every time a new different image in a 2D plane is selected. Despite this, this tool
seems the best option anyway because the goal of the project is not to make a general

Volume Segmentation using Active Contour Algorithm
Active contour (AC) is one of the active models in segmentation techniques, which
makes use of the energy constraints and forces in the image for separation of region of
interest. AC defines a separate boundary or curvature for the regions of target object for
segmentation. Image processing can be defined as computerized processing of images
of different types to obtain the desired output. AC technique is applied for separation of
foreground from the background and the segmented region of interest undergoes further
image analysis [25]. Contours are boundaries designed for the area of interest required
in an image. Contour is a collection of points that undergoes interpolation process. AC
can be applied either in a 2D and 3D level. Here, it is used in a 3D level with a 3D mask
for the developing of a volume segmentation.
The algorithm, in fact, requires an initial mask for starting the AC procedure and it
is responsible for the evolution of the contour in each iteration. The initializing masks
of the rest of the images belonging to the data set are automatically computed by the
computation of a center of mass of the already segmented image preceding the current
one in process. It is necessary to compute automatically the initial mask for the next US
image slice when in a given image slice the segmentation procedure has finished [26].

together the previously made 2D masks in three different planes(XY,XZ,YZ).

5.3.1
Boundary conditions
Boundary conditions (BC) are applied to the models in order to imitate the flow condiВӯ
tions in the phantom during US measurements [17]. The boundary conditions applied for
fluid assuming laminar flow include the inlet, outlet and wall of the studied ventricle.
вҖў The inlet is defined as the boundary through which there is a net flow into the fluid
domain [27]. The type of boundary condition in the inlet is a fully developed flow.
In combination with no slip condition, the flow has a parabolic velocity profile with a
peak velocity which is twice the mean inlet velocity, as described in Eqn 2.7. The
flow tends to be fully developed after a specific length, Le, described in Eqn 2.4 and
illustrated in Figure 2.5.
вҖў The outlet is defined as the boundary through which there is a net outflow from
the fluid domain [27]. The inlet boundary needs to be considered when choosing
boundary conditions for the outlet. When the inlet is specified using velocity, the
outlet should be specified using pressure. The pressure outlet boundary condition
is set to p = 0 Pa, which corresponds to just letting the fluid flow out of the outlet
without depending on the fluid dynamics. When outВӯpressure is set to 0, it assumes
there is no pressure from the other side of the outlet which means that the flow
simply empties out into an open area [5].
вҖў The wall boundary is defined as the boundary surface between the fluid domain and
the solid domain. The wall boundary is assumed to be noВӯslip [28], which implies
that the wall velocity is equal to the fluid velocity at the wall. In this condition, the
fluid velocity at all fluidвҖ“solid boundaries is equal to that of the solid boundary [29]
[30]. Thus, noВӯslip boundary condition assumes that at a solid boundary, the fluid
will have zero velocity relative to the boundary.

The laminar flow interface is used to compute the velocity and pressure fields for the flow
of a singleВӯphase fluid in the laminar flow regime. To run a laminar flow interface, default
nodes such as fluid properties, wall and initial values need to be defined in the Model
Builder [27].
The fluid dynamics analysis considers the solution of the 3D NavierВӯStokes equations for
conservation of momentum and the continuity equation for conservation of mass. The
laminar flow interface can be used both for stationary and timeВӯdependent analyses. A
stationary analysis is computed here.

In this thesis several important goals have been achieved.

7.2
US Techniques Discussion
Compared to previous studies were CT scans were used [21], in this project the phanВӯ
tom has been scanned by US images. US techniques are better in studies where it is
necessary to co register geometries and flow measurements. Moreover, even if CT scans
can provide a better geometry, in this project a robotic arm was used to provide a more
precise scanning process. In addition, US technique is important for future studies beВӯ
cause US avoid radiation.
7.3
Results Discussion
The hypothesis of this project is to develop a simulation model that does not deviate
more than 10% when compared to the measurements taken with VFI US in the laboratory.
Due to few results that exceed that 10% in the comparison, the hypothesis cannot be
accepted. However, as already said, it is important to consider that there is still the need
to align the planes taken for comparison before to completely exclude the reliability of the
model. In addition there is the need to develop a method that ensures that the selected
velocity parameters chosen for comparison represent the same streamlines. Moreover,
other factors can have influenced the comparison, such as the walls in the simulation
model that are assumed as rigid even if they are not, and the boundary conditions in
general.
In the human heart the left ventricle have pressure five times bigger than in right venВӯ
tricle. This is not happening in the study, as visible in Figure 6.7. This can be explaining
by arguing that the analysis performed here is related to a constant flow. In addition, the
project carried on here was focused on the velocity and not on the pressure analysis. The
study of pressure in the human heart is very complicated topic and it could surely be part
of extensions or future works.
The type of boundary condition in the inlet is expected to be fully developed flow. In
combination with no slip condition, the flow has a parabolic velocity profile with a peak
velocity which is twice the mean inlet velocity. As stated in Section 2.2.1 a flow is conВӯ
sidered fully developed when the entrance length defined in Eqn 2.4 is reached. Beside
this, looking at the 2D plots of a selected plane in the inlet, as in Figures 6.13 and 6.11, it
can be seen that the flow is not fully developed. In fact, it could be that the inlets created

7.4
Limitation and future works
There are several limitations to the simulation model presented in this thesis.
First, as previously stated, the solid part of the phantom has not been segmented but
it has been simplified. Since the main goal was to focus on the geometry of the two
ventricles, the creation of a simulation model with a simplified solid part still allows for
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis and for more simplified FluidВӯ structure inВӯ
teraction (FSI) studies. Moreover, the creation of a simplified solid part in SolidWorks has
saved time on the project. The segmentation of the solid part will be carried on in future
works.
Second, the method developed to perform the segmentation is specific and not general.
The image segmentation is performed manually with the use of the вҖҷImage SegmenterвҖҷ
App in Matlab. This tool creates three 2D masks manually and automatically generates
the code. It requires the need to recreate the 2D masks every time a new different image
in a 2D plane is selected. The use of this tool seems to be a good option because the
goal of the project is not to make a general script for the segmentation but a specific one
and the best accurate model possible. Anyway, this part of the segmentation performed
in matlab could be revised in future works.
Third, due to the presence of leaks on the right ventricle of the phantom, VFI US meaВӯ
surements of the right ventricle have not been taken. For this reason, it was not possible
to compare the results of the CFD analysis of the right ventricle domain to the VFI US
measurements.
Fourth, an important limitation of the model relies on the inherent fact that the modВӯ
els are based on a phantom of the heart and not on a real heart. Thereby, even if the
ventricles in the phantom resembles the real geometries of the human heart ventricles,
the motion applied at the apex of the phantom is physiologically unrealistic, and in genВӯ
eral, the motions which can be applied to the phantom are far from the ones happening
in a real heart. The phantom also lacks of valves, which are of crucial importance in the
hemodynamics of the heart [21].
Fifth, some of the CFD parameters used for the comparisons were not estimated or
calculated with precision. An alignment between the streamlines considered has not been
performed.
Finally, when comparing the CFD analysis and VFI US measurements, an alignment
between the model and the US measurements it has not been performed. For this reaВӯ
son, it is likely that there is a mismatch that does not allow for a proper validation of the
simulation model. This alignment corresponds to another of the important future works.
