Abstract 
Food supplements can contain high contamination of carcinogenic polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons (PAH), therefore, it is useful to have efficient and fast analytical methods for their 
surveillance. In the present study, a method based on detection with dopant-assisted atmospheric 
pressure photoionization (DA-APPI) combined with a liquid chromatography/high-resolution mass 
spectrometry (HPLC–HRMS) for analysis of EPA priority PAHs in food supplements was developed. 
DA-APPI is capable of ionizing nonpolar compounds through charge exchange reactions. 
 
Conventional analyses of PAHs comprise laborious and time-consuming sample preparation, but 
recently a simple method based on consecutive solid phase extraction (SPE) steps was developed 
for quantification of PAHs in different spices. Therefore, this method was optimised for analysis of 
PAHs in food supplements. Additionally, the chromatographic resolution as well as ionization 
dopant were optimised. To evaluate the suitability of the method, the aim was to conduct a method 
validation and analyse selected commercial food supplements. Because the results were inadequate 
due to several problems i.a. instrument sensitivity, validation data from a resembling optimised 
sample preparation method was used. The method validation for analysis of the four marker PAHs 
included assessment of precision, linearity, trueness, and detection- and quantification limits. The 
method 
was 
successfully 
validated 
for 
benzo(a)pyrene, 
benzo(b)fluoranthene 
and 
benz(a)anthracene, but the lowest concentration of chrysene did not meet precision requirements 
set by EU legislation. The reason for the failure was the large variation in results. The analysis of a 
proficiency test material indicates that further optimisation is needed to be able to quantify PAHs 
in more complex food supplement matrices. 
  
 Finally, a risk assessment of the intake of PAHs was conducted. The main risk identified from 
exposure is the carcinogenicity. Ingestion of food supplements adds to the already identified high 
risk from high dietary exposure. From calculations based on a high contamination of food 
supplements, the mean dietary exposure of benzo(a)pyrene approaches the margin of exposure. 
Therefore, implementations to reduce contamination as much as possible are important.

1 Introduction 
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are organic compounds consisting of two or more aromatic 
rings. They are persistent pollutants and are primarily formed from pyrolytic processes, especially 
incomplete combustion of organic matter such as industrial processes. This pollution contaminates 
the environment, for which reason PAHs are also found in a broad spectrum of foods. Contamination 
with PAHs can also occur during food processing and domestic cooking. The PAHs generally occur in 
complex mixtures containing more than 100 compounds (Duedahl-Olesen 2013; EFSA 2008c). PAHs 
have been evaluated by several safety committees. The Scientific Committee on Food concluded 
that 15 PAHs are regarded as potentially carcinogenic to humans (EFSA 2008c). Several different 
non- and adverse effects are linked with PAH exposure including carcinogenesis (IARC 2010). 
Therefore, the risk of adverse health effects following dietary intake of PAHs should be monitored. 
 
 
High levels of PAHs have been found in different food supplements, especially those containing 
botanical ingredients (European Commission 2011a, 2015; Martena et al. 2011). Levels exceeding 
the maximum established limits for PAHs in food supplements, is of concern. Food consumption has 
been shown to be the main source of PAH exposure for non-smoking individuals, therefore research 
in this field has increased over the last decades (European Commission 2002b, 2002a; Zelinkova and 
Wenzl 2015b).   
Monitoring of PAHs in foods is important to keep track of contamination levels, to enforce 
regulations to keep consumers safe. Several sample preparation methods exist for PAH analysis for 
different food matrices. Novel methods are required to increase selectivity and sensitivity for 
individual PAH analytes, reducing analysis- and sample preparation time, as well as simplifying 
sample preparation steps. The use of solid phase extraction (SPE) has proven successful when 
combined with different extraction techniques for various matrices. Recently Szternfeld et al. have 
proven that the use of two consecutive SPE clean-up steps suffice for analysis of complex spices 
(Szternfeld et al. 2019).  
 
1.1 Aim of Project 
This project aimed to examine and optimise the sample preparation method by Szternfeld et al. for 
analysis of food supplements detected by DA-APPI-LC-HRMS and thereby achieve a fast, analytical 
method for the analysis of the 16 EPA PAHs. This included the use of different solvent types and

2 Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons  
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are a group of nonpolar, highly lipophilic, organic 
compounds consisting of two or more aromatic rings. The formation of PAHs during pyrolysis arises 
from low-molecular-weight unsaturated aliphatic hydrocarbons and is dependent on heating 
source, temperature, and oxygen availability. Thermal elimination reactions of benzene derivatives 
can also lead to the formation of PAHs (Chen and Chen 2001; Velisek 2014). The compounds have 
different levels of volatility, where some of the lowest molecular-weight PAHs (M=152-178 Da) are 
classified as volatiles, e.g. naphthalene, acenaphthene, acenaphthylene and fluorine. The majority, 
with less than five aromatic rings, are classified as semi-volatiles (Velisek 2014; World Health 
Organization 2000; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015b, 2015a). The hydrophobic properties increase with 
higher molecular weight. Structures of four selected PAHs are seen in figure 1. PAHs are chemically 
stable compounds but susceptible to oxidation and photo-degradation (Scientific Committee on 
Food 2002; WHO/IPCS 1998).

The increasing focus on PAHs relates to the conclusion drawn by the Scientific Committee on Food 
(SCF) that some PAHs are carcinogenic and genotoxic with mutagenic effects. These effects are 
decreasing with lighter molecular weights. Furthermore, other PAHs may have synergistic effects 
(European Commission 2002b, 2002a; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015b). The PAH contamination of food 
mainly happens during heating and drying processes where combustion products come into direct 
contact with the food. In addition, environmental pollution may contribute to food contamination 
by atmospheric deposition or transfer from soil or water (European Commission 2002b, 2005b, 
2006; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015a, 2015b). Thermal processing can be applied either directly or 
indirectly. The direct method, where the food and the thermal agent or smoke are in contact, is 
where contamination with PAHs occur due to deposition of particles or direct pyrolysis of nutrients 
(Rey-Salgueiro et al. 2008). Therefore, the formation of PAHs in food is dependent on several factors 
including preparation method (smoking, grilling, frying etc.), temperature, time, distance from the 
heating source as well as drainage of fat (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations 
2005; Petersen et al. 2013; Scientific Committee on Food 2002).  
3 Regulations on Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons 
In the 1970s the Environmental Protection Agency of the USA (EPA) selected 16 PAHs (Appendix 1) 
as target compounds for routine analyses (United States Environmental Protection Agency 1998). 
The list is based on the frequency of occurrence, toxicity, and human exposure as well as available 
standards, technical limitations, and knowledge at the time. Eight of the EPA PAHs show clear 
evidence of carcinogenicity and are also included in a recent list (EU 15+1 PAHs) as recommended 
by Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) and the European Food Safety 
Authority (EFSA) in 2005. These 16 EU target PAHs were based on a risk evaluation of 33 PAHs 
conducted by the Scientific Committee on Food (SCF). This was to a higher degree based on their 
toxicity compared to the EPA priority list (European Commission 2002b, 2005a; Food and Agriculture 
Organization of the United Nations 2005; Lawrence 2015; Wise, Sander, and Schantz 2015; Zelinkova 
and Wenzl 2015b).  
For many years, benzo(a)pyrene (BaP) has been acknowledged as the indicator of total PAH 
contamination. Maximum levels for relevant foods and ingredients as well as analysis requirements 
were established in 2006 and 2007, respectively (European Commission 2006, 2011b, 2011a). After 
a thorough investigation in 2008 concerning PAH levels in food, it was found that BaP was only

4 Food Supplements 
An adequate and variable diet could provide all necessary nutrients for maintaining a healthy life. 
This is, though, not achieved by all. Food supplements are designed to administer an amount of 
nutrients or other substances, which should be beneficial to health. This could include vitamins, 
minerals and various extracts from plants and herbs. The definition of a food supplement given by 
the European Union Food Supplements Directive states: “A foodstuff the purpose of which is to 
supplement the normal diet, and which are concentrated sources of nutrients or other substances 
with a nutritional of physiological effect […] marketed in dose form […] designed to be taken in 
measured small unit quantities”. Supplements come in various forms such as capsules, pills, or 
liquids. Since excessive intake of some vitamins and minerals may lead to adverse effects, all

Foodstuffs at risk of PAH contamination are known to include oils, spices, grilled or smoked meats 
and fish. However, it was recently discovered that some food supplements also contains high levels 
of PAHs (European Commission 2005b, 2005a). Food supplements is a broad and diverse group of 
matrices. The levels are varying depending on the type and content, but great variations also exist 
within type (Bratinova, St., Zelinkova, Z., Karasek, L. 2013; EFSA 2008b; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015a). 
High levels of PAHs were noticeably reported in some botanical supplements before PAH 
contamination of food was regulated in 2006. Accordingly, it was already discussed whether 
maximum limits for PAHs in food supplements should be implemented as well. Thenceforth, 
maximum levels for BaP for relevant ingredients was employed for food supplements until more 
information was obtained (European Commission 2011a). A global survey made in collaboration 
with six EU countries and co-financed by the European Commission (EC) showed that 52 % of food 
supplements contain a single botanical ingredient. They identified 491 different botanical 
ingredients, whereof the most frequently consumed is Ginkgo biloba. Furthermore, they 
determined the most common dosage forms to be capsules and tablets (Garcia-Alvarez et al. 2014; 
Mennini 2014). Since 2016, maximum limits are applied for certain supplements, including those 
containing botanicals or preparations thereof. The maximum limits for BaP and ∑PAH4 are given in

higher level of contamination. Therefore, a lower maximum level is set for products containing oils

In 2008 EFSA published data on 300 food supplements, some of which contained high levels of PAHs 
(EFSA 2008b, 2008c). Natural ingredients such as Ginkgo biloba, ginseng, spirulina or propolis are 
often linked with high levels of PAHs (Danyi et al. 2009a; European Commission 2015; Food

Standards Agency 2005; Sabrina Moret, Purcaro, and Conte 2010). In agreement with this, Martena 
et al. found that food supplements with resveratrol, St. John’s Wort, Ginkgo biloba and propolis 
showed the highest mean PAH levels (Martena et al. 2011). Additionally, Zelinkova and Wenzl found 
BaP levels ranging from <0.25-87.3 µg/kg and ∑PAH4 levels in the range of <0.25-709.4 µg/kg

The formation of PAHs in foodstuffs is similar to pyrolysis as described in section 2 Polycyclic 
Aromatic Hydrocarbons. High temperature during treatment generates free radicals, which can 
initiate formation of smaller PAHs. These are primarily found in foods, but larger PAHs can also be 
formed. They are then retained in the hydrophobic parts of the food (L. Singh, Varshney, and 
Agarwal 2016a; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015b). Contamination of food supplements containing or 
derived from botanicals has been linked to poor drying practices. It is possible to minimize 
contamination by reassessing the production and applying good practices for drying e.g. decreasing 
the temperature or reducing direct contact with the combustion products  (European Commission 
2015; Food Safety Authority of Ireland 2015; Food Standards Agency 2019; Nations 2009). Hence, 
the critical steps for manufacturing food supplements include cleaning and drying. Cleaning is 
essential because the surface of plants can be contaminated with particle-bound PAHs from the 
environment. The PAH contamination of plants is dependent on the lipid content and can be 
expected to be higher in plants with parts above ground, with high wax content, and great leaf area. 
Because lower molecular-weight PAHs are the least hydrophobic, they are more readily absorbed 
by plants. Additionally, they are found in different regions of the plant, and not only in the lipid rich 
layers (Velisek 2014). The PAH content is also dependent on the drying method, which is influenced 
by the type of plant. This could have an impact on why certain plants are more associated with 
higher PAH content than others. Another important step is the concentration of the plant material, 
which may magnify the PAH levels (European Commission 2002b; Holland and Rose 2015; Restani 
2018; Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015a, 2015b). Although contamination of foods is mainly exogenous, 
endogenous PAH contamination can occur from plant material. A range of natural compounds 
present in plants may act as PAH precursors. These include plant steroids and other terpenoids 
(Velisek 2014).

5 Analysis of Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons 
The analysis of PAHs in food generally consists of 3 steps: an extraction followed by one or more 
clean-up steps. Several factors such as the complexity of the food matrix as well as most PAHs being 
present in trace amounts make determination of PAHs difficult. Furthermore, many organic 
compounds are coextracted and thus interfering with the subsequent separation, identification, and 
quantification. Consequently, PAH analysis is laborious and time-consuming. Because food material 
is very diverse with many different components, several different extraction methods have been 
developed (Chen, Wang, and Chiu 1996; Chiu, Lin, and Chen 1997; Duedahl-Olesen 2013; Wenzl 
2010). To have an efficient extraction method, good recovery and least coextractions are preferred. 
Oils, waxes, essential oils, and natural pigments such as carotenoids and chlorophylls are the most 
typical matrix components occurring in extracts obtained from samples of plant origin. This is also 
true for botanical food supplements, which will have a content of pigments, oils, fatty acids, auxins 
(plant hormones) and aromatic compounds as flavonoids derived from the plant material. These 
compounds can be structurally and chemically similar to PAHs, with the possibility of coextraction 
and interference with the analysis (Danyi et al. 2009a; Meudec et al. 2006; Zelinkova and Wenzl 
2015b).  
Because PAHs can undergo oxidation and photo-degradation, general precautions includes 
protecting samples from light (Duedahl-Olesen 2013; Scientific Committee on Food 2002; 
WHO/IPCS 1998). The more volatile PAHs such as naphthalene are inclined to have lower recoveries 
and poor relative standard deviation independent of the sample preparation method. Gentle 
treatment, e.g. when concentrating under nitrogen stream, should be implemented when 
determining these PAHs as well as avoiding concentrating to dryness (Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015b). 
Classical approaches for sample clean-up include saponification, gel permeation chromatography, 
liquid-liquid extraction and solid phase extraction (SPE) (Rose et al. 2007; Rozentale, Zacs, and 
Bartkevics 2019; Titato and Lanças 2006; Windal, Boxus, and Hanot 2008; Zelinkova and Wenzl 
2015a). Often a multi-stage sample preparation method is used to achieve clean extracts. This is 
usually very time-consuming, and depleting high volumes of organic solvents, thus driving up the 
cost and environmental impact of the analysis. Continuous research investigates extraction 
methods that are faster, more efficient and generate less solvent waste. Recently, Szternfeld et al. 
have developed a simple method for PAH extraction from herbs and spices by SPE clean-up

(Szternfeld et al. 2019). Previously, SPE is used in combination with different extraction techniques 
(Duedahl-Olesen 2013; Duedahl-Olesen et al. 2020; Kacmaz 2016; S. Moret, Conte, and Dean 1999;

Precedently, the most used analytical method for food analysis before the year 2000 was high-
performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) with fluorescence detection (FLD) using the natural 
fluorescing of compounds. With the availability of isotopically labelled internal standards, mass 
spectrometric (MS) determination coupled to gas chromatography (GC) or HPLC became more 
widely used. Nowadays, the most frequently used methods for PAHs are HPLC-UV or FLD and GC-
MS. Usually, GC-MS is preferred because it is easily adjustable, has better selectivity, sensitivity, and 
resolution. GC is limited by the low volatility of high molecular weight PAHs, and capability to 
separate the isomeric PAHs within an acceptable run time (Bratinova and Karasek 2017; Duedahl-
Olesen 2013; Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a; Wolfgang Jira 2004; Wenzl et al. 2016; Zelinkova and Wenzl 
2015a, 2015b). In contrast, HPLC-FLD suffers from low fluorescence activity of some PAHs, outdated 
methods and lack of selectivity leading to false-positive results (Sánchez et al. 2013; Stadler and 
Lineback 2009; Wise, Sander, and Schantz 2015). Therefore, it is necessary to develop new methods, 
which can overcome the shortcomings of the traditionally used methods. One promising method is 
HPLC-MS, i.a. because HPLC provides high selectivity and efficiency, shorter analysis time, lower 
cost, and simplicity (Duedahl-Olesen 2013; Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a). HPLC, though, has similar 
challenges as GC, i.e. isomeric separation difficulties. 
Since no universal standard method for PAH analysis exists, LC and GC data from several proficiency 
tests from The European Union Reference Laboratories (EURL) and The Food Analysis Performance 
Assessment Scheme (FAPAS) have been compared. Sykes et al. assessed the possible dependency 
of the results from EURL from 2007-2010 and FAPAS since 2005 on the method of analysis (Sykes et 
al. 2013). The EURL had an equal distribution between the two, whereas FAPAS participants 
preferred LC rather than GC. They found that no significant differences existed between the average 
values from GC and LC results in neither of the proficiency test data (Sykes et al. 2013). In 2019, 
EURL participating in proficiency testing of PAHs in food supplements and herbs used GC-MS rather 
than LC (Martin van der Lee 2018). Several factors influence the choice of analysis method, including 
experience and availability.

Recently, quadrupole time of flight (QTOF)-MS with GC and LC has become more prominent, since 
this detector delivers accurate mass measurement and full-scan data. It has a high mass resolving

single quadrupole analysers. HPLC with MS detection is a promising technique, but PAHs are not 
ionized by traditional ionisation-sources like electrospray ionisation (ESI). On the other hand, 
atmospheric-pressure chemical ionisation (APCI) and -photo-ionisation (APPI) permit the analysis of 
nonpolar and low molecular weight compounds and are both able to ionize PAHs (Sánchez et al.

matrices with APPI/MS as listed by (Rozentale, Zacs, and Bartkevics 2019). 
6 Analytical Methods 
6.1 Solid-Phase Extraction 
Solid phase extraction (SPE) is a selective sample preparation method, which has the potential to 
produce very clean samples. The sorbent usually resembles reverse-phase chromatography in its 
separation characteristics. By using SPE, it is possible to reduce or eliminate interferences before 
introducing the sample in the chromatographic system and mass spectrometer. The SPE cartridges 
differ in packing amount and volume. Gravity can be used as a driving force for the solvents, but if 
the flow is too slow a vacuum can be applied. The important measure is the flow rate, which should 
allow sufficient time for the interactions between the sample and the sorbent to occur.  
 
  Initially, conditioning of the SPE cartridge removes any impurities and activates the surface to 
promote analyte retention. To best equilibrate the sorbent, the conditioning solvent should be the 
same as the sample solvent. Subsequently, the sample is loaded. The linear velocity is important for 
sample loading because the residence time should be long enough for interactions to occur between 
sorbent and analytes. The linear velocity is a function of flow rate and column size. Also, the sample 
and cartridge sizes must be matched to not overload the column. Column capacity is also a term 
used in HPLC, describing maximum uptake of analyte by the sorbent, proportional to accessible 
surface area. The pore diameter, making up most of the surface area, is a balance between not 
having too large pores, decreasing column capacity, and larger molecules being able to enter the 
pores so that effective column capacity is obtained (Snyder, Kirkland, and Dolan 2010).

 After sample loading, a washing step may be used to remove undesired contaminants. To ensure 
that analytes are not eluted, the washing solvent should have a higher elution strength than the 
sample solvent but weaker than the elution solvent. Ideally, the washing will leave the analytes just 
near the end of the column so that all weaker retained interferences are washed out. For method 
development, conditioning, loading, washing and recovery of analyte should be considered (Snyder, 
Kirkland, and Dolan 2010). 
An example of a modern SPE sorbent is the molecularly imprinted polymers (MIP), which consists 
of highly cross-linked polymers that are specifically designed for a group of structurally related 
compounds. The selectivity is achieved by synthesis of the sorbent using a template molecule to 
form specific cavities that are sterically and chemically complementary to the analytes. This results 
in better interaction between the analytes and sorbent resulting in cleaner extracts, and thus lower 
detection limits and reduction of ion suppression in LC-MS (Snyder, Kirkland, and Dolan 2010; Tosoh 
Bioscience 2014).  
6.2 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography 
High-performance liquid chromatography is based on the principle of interactions between physico-
chemical properties of compounds and molecular interactions with the stationary phase. Two 
characteristic behaviours occur during separation: differential migration and molecular spreading. 
Differential migration is the different average speeds at which the molecules migrate through the 
column. The resulting separation increases the molecular spreading. The column volume occupied 
by a given molecule defines the band width, detected as a peak in the chromatogram (Snyder, 
Kirkland, and Dolan 2010). The use of an ultra-HPLC (UHPLC) system has an optimized eluent flow, 
reducing analysis time whilst increasing throughput, resolution and sensitivity compared to 
traditional HPLC  (Castro-Perez et al. 2005; Lung and Liu 2015; Tei 2019).

6.3 Atmospheric Pressure Photoionization 
Atmospheric pressure photoionization (APPI) is a soft ionization method which ionizes compounds 
by emitting photons from a vacuum UV discharge lamp. Initially, the liquid from the LC is vaporised 
by a heated nebulizer at atmospheric pressure. This gas passes the beam of emitted photons, 
initiating ionization. Compounds with lower ionization energies (IE) than the energy of the photons, 
are ionized. Since solvents often have higher IE compared to dopants and analytes, e.g. water (12.1 
eV), ACN (12.2 eV) or MeOH (10.84 eV), this ionization minimises undesirable solvent ionization 
(Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a; Robb and Blades 2008). HPLC-MS with APPI ionization is promising, 
==PAGE:25==

Master Thesis

because APPI enables the ionization of polar and unpolar, stable compounds such as PAHs (Hollosi 
and Wenzl 2011a). The ionization efficiency is dependent on lamp current, eluent- and dopant flow 
rate as well as analyte, solvent, and dopant properties (Hollosi and Wenzl). 
 
  Several APPI parameters can be optimised to increase ionization efficiency: Drying gas flow rate 
and temperature, vaporizer temperature, nebulizer gas pressure, and capillary and collision induced 
dissociation (CID) voltages. Because APPI is a gas-phase ionization technique, the vaporizer 
temperature is mostly dependent on the analytes. The capillary and CID voltages are important for 
ion declustering, preventing noise on the baseline, thus influences sensitivity and transmission 
(Agilent Technologies 2014; Harris 2016; Robb and Blades 2008; Snyder, Kirkland, and Dolan 2010; 
Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015b).  
To enhance the ionization of analytes, dopant-assisted APPI (DA-APPI) can be used, where dopant 
is infused post-column into the LC eluent. In positive photoionization mode, formation of dopant 
radical cations is initiated, which can react with the analytes, generating either [M]+ or [M+H]+ with 
little fragmentation (figure 3). The type of ionization is dependent on IE and proton affinity of the 
analyte, dopant, and solvent. The dominant ionization mechanism with ACN as mobile phase is 
direct photoionization resulting in [M]+, i.a. because the proton affinity of the ACN dimer is greater 
than of a given analyte e.g. BaP. On the other hand, for a MeOH dimer, chemical ionization is the 
primary force because it has a lower proton affinity than BaP resulting in [M+H]+. Possible ionization 
mechanisms include: 1. charge exchange, which will occur if the IE of the analyte is lower than of 
the dopant, 2. proton transfer between dopant radical and the analyte if the proton affinity of the 
analyte is highest, or 3. proton transfer between protonated solvent clusters and the analyte, if the 
analyte has higher proton affinity (Duedahl-Olesen 2013; Harris 2016; Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a; 
Short, Cai, and Syage 2007).

Because the effect of a dopant depends on IE and proton affinities of the analytes, as well as eluents, 
different dopants and compositions thereof have been studied (Itoh, Aoyagi, and Yarita 2006). The 
main ionization mechanism for PAHs is charge exchange, thus the IE must be lower for the analyte 
(Hollosi and Wenzl 2011b; Robb and Blades 2008; Smith, Robb, and Blades 2009). Hence, for a 
dopant to be efficient, its radical cations must not react with the solvent, its own neutrals, or 
impurities. This will deplete the population of charge exchange reagents for the target analytes 
(Robb and Blades 2005; Smith, Robb, and Blades 2009). Itoh et al. found that a mixture of anisole 
and toluene (0.5: 99.5 (v/v)) gave the highest peak areas for most EPA-PAHs (Itoh, Aoyagi, and Yarita 
2006). Hollisi and Wenzl found that anisole was the best dopant when ionising EU+1 PAHs, probably 
due to its high proton affinity but lower IE (Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a). Thereby, anisole produces 
radical cations which can undergo charge exchange reactions with target PAHs, and be neutralized 
by proton transfer, being available for a longer time in the ion source compared to dopants as 
acetone and toluene (Hollosi and Wenzl 2011a). Smith et al. found that difluoroanisole added to 
bromobenzene or chlorobenzene increases sensitivity by creating radical cations that are less 
reactive with the solvent and thus increasing charge exchange ionization (Smith, Robb, and Blades 
2009). Additionally, a neat fluoro-substituted anisole introduces impurities, thus by adding a dopant 
with lower IE, it generates photo-ions without a depletion of photo-ions from reaction with the

Advantages of APPI for determination of PAHs has recently been discussed (Cai et al. 2009; Hollosi 
and Wenzl 2011a). Three major advantages were discovered especially for nonpolar compounds: 1. 
Very good sensitivity at a low mobile phase flow rate, comparable to that of APCI with a higher flow 
rate; 2. Less susceptibility to matrix effect and ion suppression compared to APCI and ESI; 3. Having 
a broad linear dynamic range, comparable to APCI but greater than ESI (Cai, Hanold, and Syage 2007; 
Ghislain, Faure, and Michels 2012; Hsieh et al. 2002; Robb and Blades 2008; Shi et al. 2015; Takino, 
Daishima, and Nakahara 2003; Theron et al. 2007). However, signal abundances in DA-APPI-MS are 
sensitive to increased eluent flow rate because a higher flow rate leads to a faster depletion of 
dopant radical ions. The optimal eluent flow rate was found to be around 200 µL/min for test 
analytes, whereas for the EU priority PAHs 300-700 µL/min gave acceptable sensitivity. The signal 
for M+ analyte radicals and dopant radicals were observed to decrease at higher flow rates (Hollosi 
and Wenzl 2011a; Tiina J. Kauppila, Bruins, and Kostiainen 2005). Solvent clusters formed around 
the photo-ions are reagents for proton transfer ionisation. The size of solvent clusters formed is 
proportionally dependent on eluent flow rates. The formation of solvent cluster should be 
minimized for efficient charge exchange to occur between dopant and PAHs because of less 
available charge exchange reagents. Anisole photo-ions do not react with MeOH or ACN, thus it can 
be used as a dopant to promote the ionization (Robb and Blades 2005).  
6.4 Quadrupole Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometry  
As the compounds are ionized, they enter the QTOF-MS (figure 4). The dual ion funnel removes gas 
and neutral noise whilst capturing the ions: An oscillating alternating electric current works to focus 
the ions, and a direct current accelerates the ions to the exit. Subsequently, an octopole ion guide 
focuses the ion beam entering the quadrupole mass filter, where electric fields can select for specific 
ions based on their mass to charge (m/z) ratio. The selected ions enter a collision cell, which can 
produce fragments of the ions. The ions are then focused to entering the TOF part of the MS. When 
the ions enter the QTOF, it separates the ions based on mass by applying kinetic energy. At the top 
of the flight tube, the ions are reversed by a reflectron to the detector. The advantages of the QTOF

7 Validation Theory 
Method validation is a confirmation of the method meeting certain requirements. If so, the obtained 
results can be trusted, and the method is suited for its purpose (B. Magnusson and U. Örnemark 
(eds.) Eurachem Guide 2014). Setting up an appropriate method validation series is important to 
obtain data required for estimation of validation parameters. An example of a validation test design 
is shown in table 4, for estimation of precision, trueness, limit of- detection (LOD) and quantification 
(LOQ). At least three concentration levels should be included, each with a minimum of 4 samples to 
obtain 6 degrees of freedom on two consecutive series. A calibration curve should be run before 
and after the validation series to obtain the best fit for the analysis results. Before validating the 
method, any outlying samples are identified. This can be accomplished with inter quartile range 
(IQR). The IQR is the middle 50 % range of data defined as quartile 3 subtracted with quartile 1. The 
concentration that lies within the inner fences (1.5 ∙𝐼𝑄𝑅) are not outliers (Brockhoff et al. 2018).

7.1 Linearity 
Linearity of the calibration curves is evaluated by linear regression by method of least squares, 
residuals, and the determination coefficient (R2). A linear model is based on the formula: 
𝑦௜= 𝛽଴+ 𝑥௜𝛽ଵ+ 𝜀௜
Eq.6
 
where 𝛽଴ is the intercept, 𝛽ଵ is the slope and 𝜀௜ is the error, which follows a normal distribution

squared (R2), which denotes how much of the variance in the model is explained by x, and when 
close to 1, the relation to y is strong. Adjusting the R-squared for number of predictors in the model 
results in the adjusted R-squared. This is useful for comparison of models with multiple terms, to 
determine whether the additional term improves the model more than it would be expected by 
chance (Brockhoff 2015e; Brockhoff et al. 2018; Minitab 2013). 
To be able to do statistical tests such as t-test and hypothesis testing, deviations from the regression 
line should be normally distributed. This can be graphically illustrated in a histogram of residuals 
against frequency. It may not be easily validated with a small number of observations, but the 
central limit theorem states that the sum of more observations approaches normality (Brockhoff et

7.2 Accuracy

variations compared to the mean and is comprised of repeatability (RSDr), intra-laboratory 
reproducibility/intermediate precision (RSDR) and reproducibility. Repeatability is a measure of the 
variability in the results when carried out by the same analyst with identical parameters and

8.3.1 Sample Extraction 
According to 836/2011 a lot of ≤25 units requires sampling of at least 1 package or unit; thus, 
homogenization of a food supplement was done by homogenising the whole package in a mortar 
prior to weighing of 1g sample in 50mL Falcon tubes (European Commission 2011b). Capsules were 
opened to pour out the dried product. Then the samples were spiked with 100 µL ISTD (1000 ng/mL) 
and let for 20 min equilibration. Extraction was initiated by adding 20 mL hexane:dichloromethane 
(hex:DCM) 3:1 (v/v) followed by ultrasonication for 15 mins. at 35˚C (max 40˚C) for dissolvation. 
Agitation was carried out on the GenoGrinder at 1500 rpm for 5 min with the addition of a ceramic 
stone. The samples were centrifuged at 4000 rpm during 10 min. at 20˚C. The supernatant was 
transferred into a brown concentration tube and concentrated under a nitrogen stream at 35˚C 
(max 40˚C) to a volume of about 1 mL. Sample extracts were stored in brown glass vials at -20˚C to 
the consecutive day. 
8.3.2 Sample Purification 
The extract clean-up was composed of two consecutive SPEs. The EZ-POP NP cartridges were firstly 
conditioned with 10 mL acetone. The sample was then loaded followed by elution with 2x7.5 mL 
hex:di 9:1 (v/v). The eluate was evaporated under nitrogen stream to 1 mL at 35˚C (max 40˚C). Then 
the SupelMIPtm SPE cartridges were conditioned with 3 mL hex:di 9:1 (v/v) and the sample loaded. 
A washing step was performed with 1 mL hexane. Then the analytes were eluted with 2x3 mL ethyl 
acetate. Prior to concentrating, 200 µL ACN was added. The samples were concentrated to 200 µL 
under nitrogen stream at 35˚C (max 40˚C) to 200 µL. Before transfer to vials, an additional 200 µL 
ACN was added. All samples were stored in a freezer at -20˚C. Following SPE clean-up, if samples 
had any precipitation they were filtered with Mini-UniPrep PP filters.

8.5 Method Validation 
Two series of Gerimax were used for method validation, each series with five replicates at 
concentration level 0.5 µg/kg and four replicates at concentration levels 2.0 µg/kg and 12 µg/kg. 
The lowest limit was close to the required limit for LOD (0.3 µg/kg). The maximum limit for 
supplements containing oil is 2 µg/kg thus this was chosen as spike concentration. The highest 
concentration was chosen for comparison purposes with internal data on PAH analysis. Otherwise 
it could have been the regulated maximum content of 10 µg/kg. The samples were analysed for the 
content of the EPA PAHs. Before calculation of validation parameters, the results were corrected 
with the content found in matrix blind samples. The validation parameters included linearity, 
precision, LOD and LOQ according to ISO 17025 (ISO/IEC 17025 2005). The calculations were carried 
out as presented in 7 Validation Theory. The series were prepared and analysed on different days. 
A proficiency test material (PT-2019-02) 006 based on spirulina was used for evaluation of the 
method trueness with one sample in each series. Furthermore, procedural blanks were prepared to 
assess whether any contamination occurred during sample preparation. 
9 Results and Discussion 
The purpose of the project was to optimise a sample preparation method for PAHs in food 
supplements as well as develop an efficient analysis method on UHPLC-DA-APPI-QTOF-HRMS. For 
evaluation of the method, validation series were prepared. Additionally, some food supplements 
were selected to evaluate the PAH content in relevant on-market products. Results for PAH content 
in the supplements could demonstrate the applicability and suitability of the method, and 
concentration levels used for a risk evaluation on PAH exposure in Denmark. Unfortunately, several 
issues were encountered with the instrument. Therefore, this section will contain results on 
preliminary experiments, evaluation of LC-QTOF results from the optimised method followed by a 
method validation of two sets of data obtained with a similar sample preparation method (also 
based on Szternfeld et al.) but analysed on a GC-QTOF-MS. These data were supplied by laboratory 
technicians.   
  In GC when the sample is injected, it is rapidly evaporated in the programmed temperature 
vaporizer inlet and carried to the column by the mobile phase carrier gas. The retention is 
dependent on the physical-chemical properties of the analytes where solutes equilibrate between 
the stationary phase and the gas phase. The column is situated in the column oven to control the 
==PAGE:41==
Julie W. Reholt 
Master Thesis 
DTU, November 2020

temperature. As the compound reaches an appropriate vapour pressure at a given temperature, 
the carrier gas flow makes the compound migrate through the column tube, diffusing in and out of 
the stationary phase. Higher temperatures increase the diffusion coefficient, leading to a faster 
elution. Temperature programming is used for increasing analyte vapour pressure and decreasing 
the retention time of late-eluting compounds. The gas phase analytes are ionized by a stream of 
impacting electrons, called electron impact ionization (Harris 2016).  
9.1 Preliminary experiments 
Preliminary experiments were conducted on sample clean-up including variations of solvent and 
volume choices to achieve the most optimal recovery of the PAHs. The samples during preliminary 
experiments were analysed with GC-QTOF-MS (Agilent 7200, Appendix 3). Three different SPE 
methods were tested to find the best combination of solvents and cartridges. The extraction 
procedure was changed according to an internal method to use the available machines for sample 
preparation. All elution steps were followed by an exhaustive extraction to evaluate whether an 
increased elution volume could increase recovery. To better retain volatile PAHs, concentrating to 
dryness was avoided, and a gentle stream was applied since it has been shown that evaporation 
may cause a 20-70 % loss of smaller PAHs (Veyrand et al. 2007).  
9.1.1 Optimisation of the Solid Phase Extraction  
Three different procedures were tested to find the optimal recovery during clean-up: hexane as 
conditioning and elution solvent, the Szternfeld (Szternfeld et al. 2019) method with their 
hexane:dichloromethane (hex:DCM) mixture, and thirdly a test of whether recommendations from

volumes are a compromise between good recovery, low coelution and long concentrating times, 
which may result in lower recoveries of the volatile PAHs exists. Therefore, different solvent volume

9.1.2 Liquid Chromatography – Gradient Development and Mobile Phase Optimisation 
Development of the chromatographic method was initiated by running a scouting gradient with a 
broad range of mobile phase strength. Different mobile phases and compositions thereof were 
tested. A mixture of 0.1 % formic acid in ACN together with 0.1 % formic acid in MilliQ was tested. 
This had lower response compared to 30 % MeOH in ACN. Formic acid is a common solvent modifier 
and was otherwise shown to maintain the abundance of radical anisole photo-ions (Amad and Sioud 
2012). The optimal composition was found to be 30 % MeOH in ACN, compared to 10, 20, 40 and 
50 % (figure 6). It was also better than pure solutions of ACN and MeOH as well as isopropyl alcohol, 
which showed no signals. The pure MeOH showed comparable signal abundances for the first PAHs 
but decreased with higher molecular weight PAHs. No explanation was found as to why the 40 % 
mix gave lower signals compared to 30- and 50 % other than the fact that the sensitivity of the LC-
MS was unstable. It was tested in triplicate the following day where signal variances were 7-48 % 
(RSD). Ultimately, the 30 % MeOH in ACN was used.

9.1.3 Dopant Evaluation for Optimal Ionization 
Ionization efficiency was tested with different dopants based on work by Lung and Liu where they 
analysed 29 PAHs (Lung and Liu 2015). Their choice of dopant was 0.5 % difluoroanisole (DFA) in 
chlorobenzene, but 0.5 % DFA in toluene gave comparably low LODs. Additionally, in the presented 
work, neat anisole, toluene, and acetone were tested. Acetone only gave rise to peaks of 10E3 thus 
this is excluded from further results. This is consistent with previous work by Itoh et al. (Itoh, Aoyagi, 
and Yarita 2006), probably because acetone self-protonates thereby its photo-ions are used (Robb 
and Blades 2008).  
 
 Toluene has previously been shown to increase the area for PAHs with higher IEs (table 8) (eluting 
in the first half of the chromatogram), relative to ionization without dopant (Itoh, Aoyagi, and Yarita 
2006). In the present work, equal areas were obtained for all analytes with toluene as dopant, 
except naphthalene. Smith et al. tested three groupings of dopants (Smith, Robb, and Blades 2009). 
They found that toluene was not optimal because it reacts with solvent molecules, consistent with 
Itoh et al. (Itoh, Aoyagi, and Yarita 2006). This is also consistent with the present results, where

The photo-ions of anisole are stable in the presence of ACN and MeOH because of its lower IE and 
high proton affinity (it will not be neutralised by proton transfer). This is reflected in its effective

compound increased and was ineffective for naphthalene (figure 8). In a study by Hollosi and Wenzl, 
pure anisole was found to give the highest signals when analysing the EU PAHs, comparable to their 
signals with 50 % anisole in toluene (Hollosi and Wenzl 2011b). A mixture of 0.5% anisole in toluene 
was tested, but the results were comparable to areas obtained with neat toluene (figure 8). Itoh et

EPA PAHs, achieving higher areas than with neat toluene as dopant and to some extent anisole

sensitivity for PAHs with high IEs, better than either dopant used neat (Smith, Robb, and Blades 
2009). The increased ionization with the mixture suggests that anisole ions are created by charge 
exchange from photons but also from toluene ions, thereby ionizing PAHs both from toluene and

mixture gave similar responses as chlorobenzene with DFA, but this was no near the case here 
where it gave more than two orders of magnitude lower responses. The decreased area abundance 
of 0.5 % anisole in toluene might be an artifact of instrument sensitivity instability.

and Blades 2008). In comparison, anisole was the second best but had a lower ionization effect on 
the lower molecular-weight analytes. DFA is an effective dopant because the radicals are less 
reactive towards the solvent (Smith, Robb, and Blades 2009). Smith et al. reported that the two best

matrix effects, which can be observed for peaks throughout the chromatogram. The main cause of 
ion suppression in LC-MS is the decrease in analyte vaporization due to matrix (Antignac et al. 2005): 
Higher concentrations of coextracted compounds may lead to increased viscosity and surface 
tension of the droplets thereby reducing the number of analytes reaching the gas phase. Matrix 
effects can also be a cause to suppression of the signal, because extracted compounds may compete 
with analytes for the charges during ionization, leading to a decreased ionization efficiency

polarity has been run for a while, leading to accumulation of charges. Later it was discovered that 
this is more prominent than first anticipated. To overcome this, polarities should be switched back 
and forth between injections within a run.

To evaluate the precision, blank samples were spiked with three different concentration levels in 
quadruplicate of 0.5 µg/kg, 2 µg/kg and 12 µg/kg. The lower limit was close to the regulated limit

used for intermediate precision. Repeatability for the cranberry dataset was calculated based on 4,

9.3.6 Measurement Uncertainty 
Initially, sources of uncertainties were identified. Incorporated in the measurement standard 
deviation are uncertainties related to the analysis result, implicitly affected by extraction time, 
temperatures, and pipette volumes. Also included are instrument effects related to some variable 
parameter (e.g. temperature) that can affect the result. Computational effects are minimised by use 
of the most appropriate model fit, in which the terms for slope and intercept comes with a normally 
distributed uncertainty. Additionally, the blank sample is accompanied by an uncertainty which with 
blank correction is incorporated into the result. 
 
 When analysis is made of a subsample from a lot, uncertainties are minimised by homogenisation. 
The sample may also affect the result, e.g. the recovery or response from a complex matrix may be 
affected by the matrix composition. Also, storage conditions such as temperature and time from 
preparation to analysis are uncertainty contributors.   
  Uncertainty related to recovery was estimated. The expanded uncertainty (U) was obtained by 
multiplying the standard uncertainty by a factor of 1.96, which provides a confidence level of 95 % 
(Hogan 2015). Usually, a combined uncertainty of precision, recovery and other significant 
contributors is estimated. That is beyond the scope of this report. Otherwise, precision and recovery 
studies take into account the influence of the calibration of the different measuring devices which 
are varied during sample preparation. The relative expanded uncertainties at each concentration 
level are given in table 10. The uncertainty of CHR was higher than the other at 0.5 µg/kg because

9.4 Conclusion 
In conclusion the method is precise and true for three of the four regulated PAHs: BaP, BaA and BbF. 
The validation was not good for the analysis of CHR which was probably because of endogenous 
contamination of the validation matrices. Otherwise, the method was precise also for CHR. The 
trueness obtained from the PT test material indicated that the method is not optimised to a final 
point where it is applicable to be used for a food category as diverse as food supplements because 
of the complex matrices. 
Sample preparation can be further optimised by using one or several more complex food 
supplement as test matrices. That may result in the method being able to obtain the required 
trueness of the PT spirulina.

10 Risk Assessment of PAHs in Food Supplements 
Risk assessment is composed of four steps, namely hazard- identification and characterisation, 
exposure assessment and risk characterisation. It provides information for the estimation of health 
outcomes from dietary exposure to hazardous compounds like some PAHs and can be used for risk 
management (IPCS 2009a). The following risk assessment is based on the scientific opinion of the 
panel on contaminants in the food chain (CONTAM) on PAHs in food and focuses on assessing the 
risk of oral exposure (EFSA 2008c). A risk assessment of the exposure from PAHs is important 
because of their toxic effects and wide contamination in many different foods. Also, food 
supplements is a special food category, where the natural ingredients may mislead the consumers 
to think of this as meaning safe, and therefore excessive or wrong use may pose a health risk, if too 
high concentration levels are present (S. J. P. L. Van Den Berg et al. 2011).

10.1 Hazard Identification 
The first step of risk assessment is hazard identification where the adverse effects of PAHs are 
identified.  The connection between PAHs and the biological response should be revised through 
two aspects: Toxicokinetics and toxicodynamics. The former includes absorption, distribution, 
metabolism, and excretion of the compounds. Only limited data exist on the toxicokinetics of PAHs 
except for BaP. Toxicodynamics describes the effects of PAHs on the target leading to the final 
toxicological response (EFSA 2008c; IPCS 2009a, 2009b).  
10.1.1 Toxicokinetics 
10.1.1.1 Absorption and Distribution 
Exposure to PAHs primarily occurs through the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), from where they are 
transferred into the circulation. Absorption may be of the parent compound or as metabolites 
formed within the lumen. Several factors influence the absorption of PAHs: size, lipophilicity, dose, 
the lipid content of the diet and the presence of bile in the small intestine. Lipid content and 
presence of bile have been shown to increase PAH absorption (European Commission 2002b; IPCS 
2009b). Absorption of the lower molecular mass PAHs is generally higher in mammals. The diffusion 
across the gastrointestinal wall is usually a rapid process for lipid-soluble compounds (IPCS 2009b). 
Oral administration with radiolabelled BaP in pigs showed the presence of BaP in the bloodstream 
after 1 hour, peaking after 5-6 hours. Another study in pigs found absorption of 30.5 % for BaP and 
86 % for phenanthrene with varying absorption rates for each compound over a time period of 24 
hours (Cavret et al. 2003; EFSA 2008c; Scientific Committee on Food 2002).  
Bioavailability describes the percentage of the administered dose, which enters the circulation as 
the parent compound. The extent of absorption is important for toxicity and can be estimated by 
measuring the urinary excretion after administering radioactive compounds orally or intravenously. 
By investigating excretion after intravenous administration, compounds that may be eliminated 
through other routes can be estimated (IPCS 2009b). The bioavailability of PAHs is in the range of 
20-50 %, increasing with increasing lipid content of the food (European Commission 2002b).  
Following absorption, the PAHs are distributed around the body and partition into or out of various 
body tissues. The rate of distribution depends on the rate of perfusion and the body site, while the 
extent is determined by the affinity of the circulation and of the organs for the given compounds. 
The transfer into tissue generally occurs by passive diffusion and is more rapid for lipid-soluble 
==PAGE:62==
Julie W. Reholt 
Master Thesis 
DTU, November 2020

molecules. Thus, lipid-soluble compounds may be highly present in adipose tissue and show a high 
tissue to plasma ratio. Therefore, this is evident for PAHs, because of their lipid-soluble properties. 
Luckily, the rate of distribution is limited by the low perfusion rate of this kind of tissue (IPCS 2009b). 
In rodent studies, PAHs have been detected in almost all organs, but primarily in adipose tissues 
(Scientific Committee on Food 2002).  
10.1.1.2 Metabolism 
The metabolism of PAHs is important in evaluating their toxicological properties since they require 
metabolic activation to be chemically reactive and exert toxic effects. Additionally, they are 
extensively metabolised in mammals. PAHs can interact with DNA, potentially leading to cellular 
changes as part of the mode of action of the toxic effects. Metabolism is the ensemble of chemical 
reactions that happen within the human organism to sustain its functions. When a chemical is 
ingested or absorbed, the body transforms it into a metabolite which can be transported for further 
use or for excretion. The rate of metabolite formation depends on the tissue, cell as well as specie. 
Metabolism also introduces major inter- and intra-species variability e.g. gender differences, dose 
and time dependency and concurrent health and pathology, all influencing toxicity level (IPCS 
2009b; Scientific Committee on Food 2002). Given that PAHs are readily metabolised in humans, no 
bioaccumulation of the parent compound occurs (EFSA 2008c). 
Metabolism is divided into two phases: Phase 1 which involves oxidation, reduction, or hydrolysis 
with an ensuing conjugation of glutathione, glucuronic acid or sulphate leading to the formation of 
more than 20 different primary metabolites incl. phenols and diols (figure 11). These can be 
subjected to consecutive phase 2 transformations for detoxification. In phase 2, the compound is 
conjugated with a molecule to mask any active functional groups and generally leading to an 
increase in water solubility for subsequent excretion. For example, the glucuronidation of phenols 
and diols inhibits their secondary oxidation yielding more highly mutagenic metabolites (European 
Commission 2002b; Grove et al. 2000). Phase 1 includes oxidation to primary and secondary 
metabolites having different complex fates (figure 11). The metabolic reactions usually lead to a 
decrease in toxicity but have the potential to lead to reactive chemical compounds that are part of 
the toxicity (European Commission 2002b; IPCS 2009b; Larsen 2013). An example of a group of 
enzymes involved in the metabolism of PAHs is cytochrome P450 (CYP) in the liver. These enzymes 
metabolize thousands of endogenous and exogenous compounds. The formation of epoxides by

which can lead to formation of DNA adducts, with subsequent risk of mutation and cancer. Epoxides 
can also result in the formation of quinones for excretion, but quinones can also do oxidative DNA

Because of differences in PAH structure, a general mechanism of action does not exist. Based on 
knowledge of BaP, three metabolic pathways are currently established for the chemical activation 
of PAHs: the pathway via the bay region dihydrodiol epoxide by cytochrome P450 enzymes, the 
pathway via radical cation by one-electron oxidation and the ortho-quinone pathway by dihydrodiol 
dehydrogenase. The first to be discovered was the bay region dihydrodiol epoxide pathway (figure 
12, 1). It proceeds in three enzyme-catalysed processes which are highly stereoselective. CYP 
oxidises an aromatic carbon double bond to the corresponding epoxide. Then an epoxide hydrolase

converts it to trans-hydrodiol. Finally, this leads to a vicinal diol epoxide, by oxidation of an adjacent 
carbon double bond. The bay region of these compounds is especially electrophilic, and they are 
considered strong mutagens in the reaction with DNA. The different isomers formed are dependent 
on the species and involved CYP enzymes. This is important for toxicity since they have different 
mutagenic potencies (EFSA 2008c).  
 
  The second pathway leads to the formation of a radical cation by loss of an electron to an oxidant 
(figure 12, 2). The reaction could e.g. be catalysed by CYP peroxidases where the PAH can act as a 
co-reductant in the peroxidase cycle of P450 enzymes. It is suggested that it is highly dependent on 
the individual ionization potential of the PAH (EFSA 2008c; Zhang et al. 2012). Finally, the PAHs can 
be activated through the ortho-quinone (o-quinone) pathway (figure 12, 3). This also starts with the 
dihydrodiol epoxide like the epoxide pathway. This is then transformed to an unstable ketol which 
tautomerizes in the presence of oxygen into a catechol, which undergoes autoxidation yielding a 
dione (o-quinone). The oxidation of the catechol consumes oxygen, creating reactive oxygen species 
(ROS), which can cause oxidative DNA damage. The o-quinones are electrophilic and thus highly 
reactive. Besides being initiators of subsequent adverse effects, they can establish a redox cycle 
which generates more ROS through reduction. It has been demonstrated that the formation of 
glucuronides is protective against the quinone redox cycling (Lilienblum, W., Bock-Hennig, B. S., and 
Bock 1985). Since both the diol-epoxide and o-quinone pathways require the hydrodiol 
intermediate, only bay region PAHs are activated by these pathways (Zhang et al. 2012).

In addition to the three pathways, many PAHs can act as inducers of metabolizing enzymes, 
increasing their metabolic activity. It is thought to be through the arylhydrocarbon receptor (AhR) 
that PAHs exert their toxic effects such as tumour promotion, immunosuppression as well as 
carcinogenesis. The receptor is located in the cytoplasm of the human cell and is primarily found in 
the liver, but also the lungs, colon and placenta. It is a ligand dependant receptor which regulates 
the transcription of several genes e.g. involved cell growth and differentiation, or in metabolism, 
such as phase 1 and 2 metabolism enzymes (Scientific Committee on Food 2002; Timme-Laragy et 
al. 2007). The activation of the AhR thus leads to disturbance of cellular homeostasis. PAHs can act 
as ligands, and subsequent binding of the ligand-AhR on target genes induces expression 
consequently influencing the carcinogenic process, but the role is not entirely established. It is 
suggested that there is a threshold for activation of the AhR by PAHs since cells expressing a high 
level of AhR were shown to not get activated at low concentrations of PAHs (Alexander, D.L., Zhang, 
L., Foroozesh, M., Alworth, W.L., Jefcoate 1999; European Commission 2002b; Scientific Committee 
on Food 2002).

10.1.1.3 Excretion 
Elimination of PAHs occurs through urine, bile, and faeces. Generally, urinary excretion is the major 
route of elimination, but lipid-soluble compounds are reabsorbed from the renal tubule and re-
enter the general circulation, thus undergo metabolism before excretion. Elimination of PAHs has 
been shown to primarily occur through the bile, thus passing the lumen of the GIT and eventually 
excreted in faeces. The PAHs that are reabsorbed and enter the enterohepatic circulation may 
undergo further metabolism within the lumen. The rate of elimination is important to consider since 
it indicates the duration of exposure as well as potential accumulation. A rat experiment showed 
that 60 % of PAHs are excreted in bile after 6 hours, and 3 % by urine after intravenous 
administration, thus demonstrating that PAHs are not bioaccumulated (European Commission 
2002b; IPCS 2009b).  
10.1.2 Toxicodynamics 
Several effects are linked with PAH exposure. These include non-adverse effects like enzyme 
induction, or adverse effects like cancer, as well as reproductive- and developmental toxicity. For 
PAHs, the concern lies on those that exhibit carcinogenic potential. Several PAHs are considered 
carcinogenic and are listed as group 1 carcinogens by the International Agency for the Research on 
Cancer (IARC) (table 11) (IARC 2010). Some PAHs have shown carcinogenicity in experimental 
animals, but genotoxicity and mutagenicity have also been demonstrated in vivo and in vitro. 
Experiments in rodents have shown tumours in the GIT, liver, lungs, and mammalian glands after 
oral administration of BaP and benz(a)anthracene (European Commission 2002b).  
When PAHs have been activated into reactive metabolites e.g. epoxides, there is a risk of DNA 
damage which can interfere with cellular processes. This is not unusual; therefore, several repair 
mechanisms exist, and it may be fatal if they fail. If DNA damage is not repaired and undergo cell 
division it can lead to mutations which can initiate cancer (Anoop Kumar Sharma 2017). Some major 
types of DNA damage include DNA adducts, base pair substitutions, frameshift mutations, deletions, 
strand breakage and chromosomal alterations (European Commission 2002b).  
Animal studies have shown various toxicological effects such as haematological effects, 
reproductive and developmental toxicity and immunotoxicity, but the most important are 
carcinogenicity, genotoxicity, and mutagenicity. In a study of PAH exposure by Culp et al., it was 
found that BaP had a 5-fold increase in carcinogenicity when administered as a complex mixture of

PAHs compared to alone, thus representing an example where a mixture effect leads to increased 
effects (Culp et al. 1998; European Commission 2002b). There is a general trend for PAHs that 
carcinogenicity is linked with genotoxicity because the mechanistic link between DNA adducts, 
mutation and cancer outcomes is similar (European Commission 2002b). This connection can be 
seen in table 11. PAHs and their metabolites can also affect hormone receptors because they 
resemble steroids, and it has been shown that they have both anti- and oestrogenic effects. It should 
be noted, though, that PAHs have shown both additive and synergistic effects but also antagonistic 
effects (European Commission 2002b).  
Other adverse effects include immunotoxic and reproductive toxicity. Immunosuppression is the 
most common immunotoxic effect which is associated with a high risk of cancer and infectious 
diseases. This toxicity is related to the reactivity of the AhR. Some PAHs and metabolites thereof 
have also been found to cross the placenta in rodents, but only limited information is available on 
reproductive toxicity. Much knowledge on some of the more complex PAHs are obtained from in 
vitro studies, thus the formed metabolites may not be relevant in vivo (European Commission 
2002b). 
Some people are part of a high-risk population. This includes high-risk genotypes and children, who 
are often at risk because of their high intake of food per body weight. A high-risk genotype includes 
genetic polymorphism which is defined as a genetic difference seen in 1 % of the population. These 
genetic deviations may increase the activation or deactivation of PAHs and are linked with increased 
risk of certain cancers (European Commission 2002b).

10.2 Hazard Characterisation 
Hazard characterisation describes the relationship between the administered dose of a chemical, or 
exposure to it, and the incidence of an adverse health effect. The first adverse effect observed with 
increased exposure is the critical effect (IPCS 2009a). For PAHs, the critical effect is the carcinogenic 
potential because exposure from foods is almost exclusively a mixture containing carcinogenic 
PAHs. For carcinogenic compounds there is no safe limit below which DNA damage has no 
consequence, therefore the benchmark dose (BMD) approach is used in risk assessment to 
determine the margin of exposure (MOE) (European Commission 2002b).  
10.2.1 Study Design Evaluation for Exposure Assessment 
Only a limited number of studies are assessing the carcinogenic potential following oral 
administration of PAHs. The years between the evaluation by IPCS in 1998 until the opinion given 
by the European Commission in 2002, two studies were published: Culp et al. (1998) and Kroese et 
al. (2001) (European Commission 2002b).  
The study by Culp et al. (Culp et al. 1998), which is also used in the EFSA report, is used for the 
following benchmark dose level calculations for exposure assessment. It aimed to use data from 
two different coal tar mixtures to determine how the tumorigenic potential varied as a function of 
the content of BaP. The study uses coal tar mixtures which also contain many other chemical 
compounds other than PAHs. By adding BaP to the second coal tar mixture, they are able to assess 
==PAGE:69==

the health effects related to this particular PAH and use its content as a marker of the carcinogenic 
PAHs in the coal tar mixtures.  
A good carcinogenic experiment is defined as having 50 animals of each sex group and one or more 
alternative methods. The exposure period for mice should be at least 1.5 years (Anoop K. Sharma 
2017). Usually, it includes three dose levels and a control group and preferable the highest dose is 
associated with minimal toxicity and no effect on survival (IPCS 2009b). In the study by Culp et al. 
they had 14 dose groups of 48 female mice: 6 dose groups for coal tar mixture 1 (0.01-1.0 g coal tar 
mixture 1 per 100 g meal), 3 dose groups for coal tar mixture 2 (0.03- 0.3 g coal tar mixture 2 per 
100 g meal), and 3 dose groups for BaP (and 5 ppm, 25 ppm and 100 ppm). Furthermore, they had 
2 different control groups. The downside of this study design is that they only used female mice 
which is not representative for the results. On the other hand, they had a high amount of dose levels 
for mixture 1 and only the required number of dose levels for the other exposure groups.  
For carcinogenic studies, the survival at the end should be above 50 % for all dose groups (Anoop K. 
Sharma 2017). The mortality rate for the two highest dose levels for mixture 1 was 100 %, and the 
remaining four dose levels had mortality above 50 % for the 2 years. Similarly, the survival rate of 
the two lowest doses for mixture 2 were above 50 %, but for the high dose, it was below 50 %. All 
mice fed with 100 ppm BaP were excluded because of death before the end. The group exposed to 
25 ppm also had a high rate of mortality below 50 %, whereas the 5 ppm group was above at the 
end of the study. Therefore, there is uncertainty from the study since the rate of survival is low 
because of the high doses.  
 
  It can be discussed whether it is reasonable to use a study on oral administration with a non-food 
to a food exposure assessment, but the lack of studies leaves no other reasonable candidate. In the 
study by Kroese et al., rats were fed BaP by oral gavage. Problems. Their extrapolation of a “safe 
dose” was insufficient leading to a too high dose (Larsen 2013). The CONTAM panel concluded that 
the most appropriate study for BMD modelling was by Culp et al. (EFSA 2008c), though the study by 
Kroese et al. had 3 concentration levels of BaP dissolved in soy oil and to groups of 104 male and 
female rats for 2 years, but the survival rate was only above 50 % for the control groups (Kroese et 
al. 2001).

10.2.2 Benchmark Dose Level 
The benchmark dose (BMD) is a dose level which is estimated from the fitted dose-response curve. 
A benchmark response is defined from which the BMD is extrapolated. The BMD lower confidence 
limit (BMDL) is the corresponding lower limit of a 95 % confidence interval on the BMD curve. By 
using the lower bound, the method accounts for uncertainties in the used study and assures with 
95% confidence that the benchmark response is not exceeded. Using a calculated BMDL for a 
response with a confidence limit at 5 or 10 % change in response compared to the control group 
(BMDL10) is considered appropriate for genotoxic and carcinogenic compounds (EFSA 2008c).  
The dose-response modelling used by the CONTAM was carried out by combining the data from the 
exposure groups in the study by Culp et al. (Culp et al. 1998; EFSA 2008c). The models were chosen 
based on a p-value above 0.1 and a low chi-square value, which are set to have a good model fit. 
With a 95 % confidence interval, a value for the BMR of 10 %, the calculations were performed with 
250 iterations. The calculated BMDL10 values for BaP ranged from 0.07-0.20 mg/kg b.w. per day with 
0.12 mg/kg b.w. per day representing the best fit. The CONTAM panel used the lowest value for 
MOE calculation similar to the JECFA because it gives more conservative results. Therefore, 0.07 
mg/kg b.w. per day was used for BaP as a marker for carcinogenic PAHs in food. The calculated 
BMDL10 values for PAH4 ranged from 0.34-0.93 mg/kg b.w. per day with 0.52-0.53 mg/kg b.w. per 
day representing the best fits. Similarly, the lowest BMDL10 was chosen for MOE calculations for the 
PAH4 as markers for carcinogenic PAHs in food, which was 0.34 mg/kg b.w. per day (EFSA 2008c; 
WHO and FAO 2006). 
10.3 Exposure Assessment 
To estimate human exposure to PAHs from dietary intake, data from the scientific opinion on PAHs 
in food from 2008 was used (EFSA 2008c). In 2005, the Commission recommended monitoring PAHs 
and from 2007 a European food consumption database was developed. Data for consumers only 
was used for their risk assessment. The food categories used in the exposure calculations are 
presented in their tables 15-17 (EFSA 2008c; European Commission 2005a). Mean concentrations 
of BaP and ∑PAH4 in 4065 food samples across 11 food categories were used for the following 
exposure assessment, and the 97.5th percentile was used for the high exposure estimates. To 
estimate the exposure, the mean dietary exposure for the whole European population was used.

10.4 Risk Characterisation 
In risk characterisation, the potential risk to human health is estimated under different exposure 
scenarios (IPCS 2009a). Since it was concluded by the CONTAM panel that exposure through 
inhalation generally contributes little to overall PAH exposure, this is not included in any scenario 
(EFSA 2008c). Based on exposure estimation, the MOE is calculated. It is the ratio between a defined 
point on the dose-response curve for the adverse effect and the estimated human intake of the 
compound:

17,500 which is equal to the former. The MOE for the group with a high estimated exposure is 8,750, 
which mean that the health concern associated with this amount of exposure is higher.

10.4.1 Dietary Exposure in Denmark 
In a report on chemical contaminants from the Danish food monitoring program, the risk of PAH 
exposure was evaluated. They decided to use their higher mean exposure to calculate the MOE to 
have the worst-case scenario using the same BMDL10 as the CONTAM panel. They too found that 
there is a health concern for the high-risk groups (95th percentile), with MOEs below 10 000. 
Additionally, they found that for children (aged 4-14 years) a mean exposure is of concern because 
MOEs were close to 10,000 (Petersen et al. 2013).

10.5 Conclusion and Evaluation 
Based on the risk assessment, the main risks from PAH exposure is their linkage to long-term toxic 
effects. Since PAH exposure is long-term, the carcinogenic, mutagenic, genotoxic- and 
developmental effects are all relevant as potential risks. Their carcinogenic effects are of primary 
concern; thus, the exposure should be as low as reasonably achievable. The main PAH exposure for 
consumers of the included food groups is food compared to cigarette smoking. High dietary intake 
of PAH contaminated foods poses a health risk. Because there is a high variation in the contents 
between foods and within foods as well as differences in dietary habits of people, it is difficult to 
estimate the dietary risk. An example is smoked and grilled foods, which have a high level of PAHs 
but are not usually a part of the normal diet. These foods may contribute significantly to PAH 
exposure for certain individuals e.g. who tend to eat more smoked and grilled foods, which 
especially is during summer when grilling is more attractive (Scientific Committee on Food 2002). 
Hence, several variables have an impact on risk assessment. There are also uncertainties associated 
with a risk assessment such as food consumption recordings and measurements of PAH contents in 
foods. Furthermore, the BMD model does not account for species differences between rodents and 
humans. The use of the lower BMD and considering the aforementioned uncertainties, the risk 
assessment is more likely to overestimate than underestimate the risk (EFSA 2008c). The lack of 
relevant oral toxicity studies further complicates the trueness of a risk exposure assessment. What 
further complicates a risk assessment on food is the fact that food often contains antioxidants etc 
which can inhibit potential PAH reactivity and thus toxicity.

When combining dietary intake of PAHs with exposure from smoking, there is a risk for high-intake 
consumers. It is possible that if all routes of exposure are considered that even a mean estimated 
dietary intake will be of health concern since for instance food supplements that are highly 
contaminated may contribute significantly to PAH exposure, since the mean dietary exposure of BaP 
was identified to approach the margin of exposure. 
In conclusion, it is important to take into account each food category which is related to a high 
amount of PAH contamination and reduce the contamination as much as possible, so that the 
overall exposure can be reduced. This is carried out by good manufacturing practices, as described 
in section “4 Food Supplements”. Additionally, control of PAH emissions to the environment and 
instruction on avoiding smoking and drying processes where combustion products come into direct 
contact with the foods should continuously be conducted.

11 Conclusion 
A DA-APPI–HPLC-QTOF-MS method for the determination of EPA PAHs in food supplements was 
developed and optimised. The optimal mobile phase composition was 30 % methanol in acetonitrile, 
and resolution was achieved by gradient elution with isocratic holds to separate critical isomeric 
PAHs. The chromatographic separation was developed based on separation of all 25 priority PAHs 
(EU and EPA), hence a compromise between baseline separation of some critical PAHs compared to 
other critical PAHs lead to the final gradient. For effective ionization of the target PAHs, 0.5 % anisole 
in toluene was used as a dopant. 
The aim was to develop an efficient and simple sample preparation method to analyse PAHs in food 
supplements. Therefore, the sample preparation was based on a simple procedure with two 
consecutive SPE purification steps developed by Szternfeld et al., which they validated on various 
types of spices and herbs (Szternfeld et al. 2019). The SPE optimisation was conducted on PAH 
standards and included assessment of solvent and volumes.  
The method was not successfully validated due to insufficient results. Because of instrumental 
sensitivity challenges, it was primarily the internal standards which could be detected. Assessment 
based on the detection of internal standards indicated the presence of matrix effects and analyte 
losses during sample preparation, specifically of the most volatile PAHs. The gradual decrease of the 
sensitivity could be explained by ion suppression, ion burn and charge competition during 
ionization. These factors together had substantial impact on the detection. The accumulation of 
charges was more prominent than first anticipated, thus further on, polarities should be switched 
between injections. The validation was therefore based on method performance data acquired from 
a similar method, obtained by optimisation of the same sample preparation method with two 
consecutive SPE steps. 
The method validation was performed on two data sets prepared from two food supplements, 
cranberry and husk, analysed by GC-QTOF-MS. The validation was based on linearity, precision, 
trueness, LOD and LOQ. The method provided an accurate determination of benzo(a)pyrene, 
benz(a)anthracene and benzo(b)fluoranthene in the food supplements with good precision. The 
trueness calculated based on a proficiency test material indicated that the sample preparation is 
not yet applicable for extraction of PAHs from very complex supplements. Trueness based on

recovery was within the acceptable range of 70-120 % and was comparable to those obtained by 
Szternfeld et al. on spices. Chrysene had a bad accuracy at the lowest concentration level because 
of high variability, but the precision term, HORRAT, showed better precision than expected.  
By using the sample preparation developed by Szternfeld et al. for complex spices, it was 
hypothesized that it would be a sufficient foundation for a new, simpler sample preparation method 
for food supplements. The method validation was in close agreement with criteria described in 
legislation for the four marker PAH. It is suggested to develop the method further by optimising the 
SPE clean-up on different matrix samples, including solvent volumes for optimal recovery and 
reduced coelution. Additionally, the optimal extraction solvents for matrix- and SPE extraction can 
be investigated. 
Further optimisation of the detection method includes optimisation of the ionization efficiency 
which is dependent on the flow- of the eluent and dopant. Moreover, the source parameters were 
based on previous work with ionization by APCI and could therefore also be optimised in 
combination with flow optimisation. Altogether, it should be possible to overcome some of the 
problems which were encountered in LC-MS detection. 
Although an assessment conducted by JECFA concluded that food supplements do not influence the 
overall intake of PAHs, this was not the case in the current study nor in a recent study on exposure 
from food supplements on the European market (Zelinkova and Wenzl 2015a). Based on the 
benchmark dose (BMD) to calculate a margin of exposure for carcinogenic compounds, it was 
concluded that the high exposure estimate for BaP and the sum of PAH4 indicates a health concern 
for an average European citizen. This is based on the lower level of BMD with a confidence limit of 
10 % change in response. Additionally, the dietary exposure in Denmark was estimated. Petersen et 
al. (2013) also found that there was a health risk for high consumption groups, as opposed to EFSA. 
This may be due to the inclusion of several other food groups potentially containing high levels of 
PAHs. When considering smoking and passive smoking, the health concern is increased. When 
considering food supplements for a Danish person, using highly contaminated food supplement 
data, the exposure approaches the margin defining the limit for health risk. It is possible that if all 
routes of exposure are considered as well as all relevant food categories, that even a mean 
estimated dietary intake will be of health concern.

Sample Preparation Procedure for Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons for Subsequent Analysis on 
GC-MS 
The following procedure was developed based on the sample preparation by Szternfeld et al. (2019). 
Extraction  
- 
Homogenize sample with coffee grinder 
- 
Weigh off 1 g in a 50 mL Falcon tube 
o Add 100 µL internal standard (100 ng/mL) to spike samples 
- 
Let sample for 20 min equilibration 
- 
About 20 mL ethyl acetate:cyclohexane 50:50 (v/v) is added as well as a cheramic stone. Shake by 
hand. 
- 
Shake sample on Geno/Grinder for 5 min at 1000 rpm 
- 
Centrifuge at 4˚C for 5 min at 5000 rpm 
- 
Transfer supernatant to glass tube and concentrate with nitrogen to 2 mL 
SPE EZ-POP NP clean-up 
- 
Condition with 10 mL cyclohexane 
- 
Add the 2 mL sample. Additionally, rinse the Falcon tube with about 500 µL pentane which is also 
added to the cartridge 
- 
Elute with 15 mL cyclohexane and collect in 20 mL volumetric flask 
- 
Concentrate to 1 mL under nitrogen stream 
SPE SupelMIP clean-up 
- 
Condition with 1 mL cyclohexane 
- 
Add the 1 mL sample 
- 
Wash with 1 mL cyclohexane 
- 
Elute with 3*1 mL ethyl acetate 
Sample concentration 
- 
Concentrate eluate from 3 mL to 200µL in concentration glass 
- 
Transfer to vial and add 100 µL toluene and blow down to 100 µL 
- 
Cap the vials and run on GC with calibration curve
