Abstract
It was estimated that the energy consumption by buildings accounts for over 1/3 of the ﬁnal energy
consumption worldwide and that the building stock in Europe is responsible for around 40% of all
energy used. Despite the signiﬁcant progress in decreasing the environmental impact of the energy
systems, there is a lot of potential for improvement especially in the area of heating and cooling.
Exploiting those potentials requires a holistic analysis and understanding of the functioning of the
energy conversion processes in the system. One of the tools that allow for such a holistic analysis
is the exergy analysis.
The aim of the project was to choose the optimal heat supply method for low energy building area
planned in Fredrikssund municipality. Both economic and energy-eﬃciency factors were taken into
account in the analysis.
Based on literature studies, eight options were chosen for further investigation, including direct
electrical heating, individual air-to-water heat pumps, cluster ground-source heat pump and diﬀerent
low temperature district heating systems. Also based on literature studies and data provided by the
municipality, the energy demand of the building was estimated. Under assumed conditions, peak
heat demand for space heating and domestic hot water were determined.
Subsequently, economic analysis of the chosen cases was performed. The considered cases were
evaluated based on socio-economic analysis, as well as private economic analysis both for the heat
supplier company and for building owners. Additionally, sensitivity analysis was made to investigate
the inﬂuence of changes in the assumptions on the results. It was determined, that direct electrical
heating performs the best, both in the socio-economic and private-economic analysis. However, it
is sensitive to the changes in heat demand and electricity prices. Second and third best solutions
from the socio-economic point of view were low temperature district heating utilizing waste heat
and, ex aequo, low temperature district heating and individual air-to-water heat pump. For the
building owners, second and third best solutions were individual air-to-water heat pump and cluster
ground-source heat pump.
Based on the initial investigation, cluster ground-source heat pump was chosen for further analysis.
To do so, model of the system was built in TRNSYS software and simulations were run to investigate
system’s energy consumption in dynamic conditions.
Energy consumption by heat pump was
calculated to be greater than the one from the preliminary analysis. The simulations indicated
problems with heat pump operation caused by the assumption that heat pump is supposed to be
used in monovalent mode. Possible solutions of those problems were discussed.
As the last part of the investigation, exergy analysis of considered systems was performed. Annual
exergy demands for space heating and domestic hot water, as well as exergy factors of the demands,
were calculated, using data with monthly resolution. Out of all the systems investigated, direct
electrical heating system had signiﬁcantly the highest exergy of resources used per building. The
system that performed the best was ultra-low temperature district heating combined with booster
heat pumps for DHW preparation. Additionally, for the system with cluster geothermal-source heat

pump, exergy analysis was made also based on the dynamic simulation. There, total calculated
exergy demand for space heating was greater than in simpliﬁed analysis and for domestic hot water
- lesser. Total exergy of resources consumed was also calculated.
It was concluded that cluster ground-source heat pump is an interesting option for heat supply for
low energy building area in Vinge, as it is acceptable from economic point of view and combines
good energy and exergy eﬃciency with the possibility of integration in the centralised energy system
at the later stages of area development.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Project background
It was estimated that the energy consumption by buildings accounts for over 1/3 of the ﬁnal energy
consumption worldwide and that the building stock in Europe is responsible for around 40% of
all energy used [13, 14].
As this energy is generated mostly by burning fossil-fuels (ﬁgure 1.1)
it contributes signiﬁcantly to the emission of greenhouse gases, that are limited by national and
international agreements, such as the Kyoto protocol or the European 20-20-20 targets [13]. Even
though in the recent years signiﬁcant progress in decreasing the environmental impact of the energy
systems was made, there is a lot of potential for improvement especially in the area of heating and
cooling. Exploiting those potentials requires a holistic analysis and understanding of the functioning
of the energy conversion processes in the system [13].
Figure 1.1: Final energy consumption by sector and buildings energy mix, 2010 [1]
One of the tools that allow for such a holistic analysis is the exergy analysis. It has been applied in
various energy-related issues, such as the analysis of renewable energy sources, transport sector or
energy sector [15, 16, 17]. Recently, the low energy concept was applied also to the built environment
[18, 19, 20, 21].
The exergy analysis combines the concept of entropy and the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics to
calculate the eﬃciency of the energy conversion in the system. Therefore, it can help to improve
the match between the quality of energy demand and energy supply [20]. Additionally, reducing the
high-exergy primary exergy spending, e.g. electrical energy, is an important step towards a net-zero
exergy target [22].

2
1.2 Objective and method
1.2
Objective and method
The aim of the investigation was to determine the optimal heat supply method for a planned
low-energy building area in Vinge, near Fredrikssund by using economic investigation and exergy
analysis.
To do so, a literature study was performed and systems for further analysis were chosen. Based on
literature sources, an investigation of the possible heat demand and heat peak load in low-energy
buildings was made. Obtained values were used for simpliﬁed energy consumption calculations.
Subsequently, an economic evaluation of the chosen options was made and the investigated cases
were compared based on the resulting NPV and unit cost of heat. Then, the detailed investigation of
the performance of the chosen case was performed using TRNSYS software. As the last part of the
investigation, exergy analysis was performed - both using the results of the simpliﬁed energy analysis
and the results of the detailed TRNSYS simulation. The results of all parts of the investigation
were subsequently compared, discussed and the most optimal solution was proposed.

Energy production and conversion on the supply side and

2.1.1
Biomass
One of the easiest way of ensuring CO2-neutrality of e.g. conventional fuel-based energy system
is converting it to biomass. However, according to the study by European Environment Agency
[23], the biomass potential in Europe can account for only approximately 15 −16% of the total
primary energy demand in 2030. The study conducted by Ericsson and Nilsson [24] assumed that
the biomass in the energy sector will be used only temporarily until 2040 and then its resources
will be allocated to transportation sector. This corresponds to the recommendations and results
from other case studies [25, 26]. The climate neutrality of biomass is also debatable, as discussed by
Cherubini et al. [27]. Additionally, burning of the biomass causes the emission of other substances,
not only the carbon uptake, thus the balanced carbon release measure caused by the use of biomass
is needed.
2.1.2
Geothermal
Another often considered option is geothermal heat, used both directly and in combination with
ground source heat pumps. Mahler and Magtengaard [28] state, that the geothermal water under
Copenhagen can be tapped at the temperature reaching 73 °C at the depth of 2000 m, so no
additional temperature increase by heat pumps is needed to supply the low temperature district
heating (LTDH) network. However, as the direct use of the geothermal heat in many regions requires
boreholes of signiﬁcant depth, it is economically unfeasible in case of small residential areas. Thus,
the use of heat pump using geothermal heat as the lower heat source is the solution more suitable.
De Carli et al. [29] present a district heating and cooling system based on a ground-source heat
pumps (GSHP) and analyse it both from energetic and economic point of view, taking into account
the population density in the district, technical solutions used and diﬀerent support schemes. It
was determined that for most of the cases the costs are lower in the systems with heat pumps with
water in ground loops and the use of the heat pumps with ﬂooded evaporators is economically
beneﬁcial in all cases considered. Oktay and Dincer [30] applied an exergoeconomic model to the
Gonen geothermal district heating system (GDHS) in Balikesir, Turkey. The results of the analysis
indicate that the major part of the exergy destruction occurs as the result losses in pumps, heat
exchangers and pipes, as well as at injection of the cooled down geothermal water back into reservoir.
It was determined, that the overall energy eﬃciency of the system is 42% and the exergy eﬃciency
50%. The cost-eﬃciency of the system increases with the increase in the heating load. The paper by
Ke¸ceba¸s [31] presents the study of energy, exergy and exergoeconomy of the Afyon GDHS in Afyon,
Turkey. The study was based on actual data from the system. The energy and exergy eﬃciencies

2.1.3
Solar
Another alternative is using solar heat to produce heat for district heating systems. The biggest
central solar heating plant (CSHP) operating is located in Vojens, Denmark and has the power of
49.0 MW [35]. The second and the third largest CSHPs in Europe are also located in Denmark - in
Dronninglund and in Marstal, respectively. In Marstal, plant is then connected to the delivery pipe
for summer operation. Diﬀerent approach is used in the smaller systems in Saltum and Ry, with
preheating of the return stream of a district heating network and no heat storage [36]. It is also
stated, that such systems can be economically competitive with other district heating technologies
under special conditions - otherwise, the acceptance of public funding or higher prices is necessary.
Between 1995 and 2004 there were 8 solar central heating plants with seasonal heat storage built
in Germany as part of the governmental R&D programme.
The report by Schmidt et al.
[37]
describes the system used in those plants, as well as gives advice about their planning and costs.
The later report by Bauer et al. [38] concentrates on diﬀerent types of seasonal thermal energy
storages used in the German CSHP (hot water thermal energy storage, gravel-water thermal energy
storage, borehole thermal energy storage and aquiﬁer thermal energy storage) and presents their
heat balances.
2.1.4
Surplus heat
The investigation by Holmgren [39] showed that it is economically feasible to use a certain amount
of industrial waste heat in the DH system in G¨oteborg, Sweden. Still, another study by Kapil et al.
[40] indicates that it may not always be beneﬁcial. It is so, because due to the possible ﬂuctuations
in the amount of surplus heat available, the production capacity installed in the DH system may
still have to cover all the heat demand in case the industry shuts down.
Additionally, including the waste heat in the DH system may decrease the eﬃciency of the heat
generation in the system.
Larsen [5] investigated the eﬀect of including surplus heat from the
supermarket CO2 refrigeration system as one of the heat sources for the low temperature district
heating (LTDH) system supplying new low energy residential area. It was found that from the energy
perspective it is preferable to use LTDH if waste heat sources are available, however, investing in
district heating network turned out to be economically infeasible.

2.2
Energy distribution
Apart from the focus on supply side technologies, there is a lot of studies concentrating on the
issues related to the district heating networks. Due to the decreasing space heating (SH) demand,
questions have been raised about the feasibility of district heating systems in the years to come.
However, a study conducted few years ago in Denmark by Lund, Mathiesen and Dyrelund [41]
concluded that the increase the expansion of the DH network that would allow to supply 63 −70%
of the households in heat (compared to present 46%) still would be beneﬁcial and it is so even with
the reduction of SH demand by 75%. It has been shown [41, 42] that using LTDH to supply heat
for low energy buildings can compete with the decentralized heat supply such as individual heat
pumps. Additionally, Dalla Rosa and Christensen [42] state, that due to the occupants’ behaviour
the heating demand and heating power can be, respectively, up to 50% and 60% higher than the
one calculated according to the standards and it should be also taken into account while performing
energy simulations.
Increasing the market penetration of DH requires the engagement of the peripheral urban areas
with relatively low heat density, where currently buildings are supplied in heat by decentralized
generation units (usually natural gas boilers or individual heat pumps). The signiﬁcant network
heat loss may overbeat the energy savings achieved due to the centralized heat supply in case of high
and medium temperature district heating [20]. Because of it, there several programmes have been
launched to investigate the feasibility, both technical and economical, of extending the existing DH
network to low heat density areas [43, 44]. However, with the decreasing SH demand and SH peaks,
low temperature district heating becomes technically feasible [45]. There are also pilot projects of
supplying the low energy building areas with LTDH, e.g. as the one carried out in Lystrup, Denmark.
Li and Svendsen [20] conducted the energy and exergy analysis of the LTDH network supplying
30 low-energy houses. Pipe diameters in the proposed network were minimized by exploiting the
maximum allowable pressure drop not only along the critical route, but along all the separate routes.
This approach allows to minimize the heat loss and investment cost of the network, however, it
increases the pump energy consumption and can be utilized only if no further network extension is
planned. The study concluded that low temperature district heating performs much more eﬃciently
than medium temperature district heating due to the decreased heat loss. Additionally, its merits are
further highlighted in the exergy analysis by the better quality match between the heat demand and
supply. The system’s performance decreases, if thermal by-pass at the consumer end is utilized and
decreasing the by-pass temperature helps to improve both the energy and exergy eﬃciency. Analysis
showed also that there is signiﬁcant possibility of exergy eﬃciency improvement by supplying the
heat for SH needs at the temperature matching the required by e.g. radiant heating. Thus, triple
pipe concept could be utilized [46]. Energy and exergy performance of the DH system was analysed
also by Tor´ıo and Schmidt [47], who made a preliminary case study of the DH system in Kassel,
Germany. The energy performance of all the four systems investigated turned out to be very similar,
with the maximum diﬀerence between eﬃciencies only 1.2%. However, their exergy eﬃciencies vary
from 32% (for the system with single heat exchangers and 95 °C supply temperature) to 43% (for the
system with two cascading heat exchangers and supply temperature 65-50 °C). Both the decrease of
supply temperature, as well as the decrease of the return temperature allow to increase the exergy
eﬃciency, with the inﬂuence the supply temperatures being greater than return temperatures.
Another option investigated is the ultra-low temperature district heating combined with a heat
pump used for the domestic hot water preparation. Zvingilaite et al. [48] analysed the feasibility of
a DH consumer unit with micro heat pump for DHW preparation in a low temperature (40 °C) DH
network and compared it with direct electrical heaters. At the current price levels the considered
solutions are comparable. However, the heat pump alternative reduces electricity consumption more
than 6 times and has signiﬁcantly higher exergy eﬃciency (0.43 compared to 0.12). Additionally,

2.3
Decentralized energy generation
Caliskan [51] analyzes and compares the biomass, solar and (natural gas ﬁred) electrical based
options of building heating in terms of energy, exergy, sustainability and the environmental impact,
exergoenvironmental, enviroeconomic and exergoenviroeconomic analyses. It is concluded that the
solar energy is the most sustainable and eﬃcient, with biomass following as second. According to
the study by Rogers et al. [52], it should be possible to provide the DHW throughout the summer
period even in the northern latitudes between 50°C and 60°C and with large daily variation in solar
radiation.
2.3.1
Heat pumps
Both air- and ground-source heat pumps are established technologies with high market share e.g.
in Sweden, Switzerland and parts of Austria. Still, in most of the other countries heat pumps are
not considered the ﬁrst choice while installing or replacing the heating system [53]. Data from
European Heat Pump Association which covers Austria, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Sweden,
Norway and Switzerland, recorded that the sales of ground-source heat pumps (GSHP) amounted to
107, 022 in 2008 and the air-source heat pumps (ASHP) sales to 469, 370. Thus, the sales of ASHP
were around 4.5 times bigger than the GSHP sales. Out of the ASHP sold, 48% were reversible.
The data presented does not specify if the units are single room heaters/coolers or systems for
the whole building, though [54]. Thus, majority of the currently performed studies of heat pumps
concentrates on investigation of their performance and optimization, not on the development of new
technology.
Hepbasli [55] created a model and performed an exergy analysis of solar assisted ground-source heat
pump with integrated DHW tank for the use in residential buildings. Exergy eﬃciency values of
the whole system was determined to be 44.06% at the reference state of 19 °C and 101, 325 kPa.
The biggest exergy destructions occur (in order) in the condenser, compressor, expansion valve and
evaporator. C¸akir et al. [56] investigated the performance of a multifunctional heat pump running
in four diﬀerent modes (air to air, air to water, water to water and water to air) with heating
capacity of the heat pump staying the same. It was determined that the water to air heat pump
has the highest (3.94) and the air to water the lowest (3.40) COP. The mean exergy eﬃciencies of
the systems were as follows: 30.23% for water to air type, 30.22% for air to air type, 24.77% for air

2.3.2
Direct electricity
Leth [8] investigated the viability of supplying a low energy single family house with space heating
and warm water produced with electricity consumed only in the period of the lowest demand between
12 p.m. and 6 a.m. It was determined that for the buildings analysed, the heating power of 5.1

DHW supply, a storage tank of 300 l heated to 60 °C was deemed the optimal solution. The study
concludes, that for the very low energy houses supplying heat with direct electricity in the oﬀ-peak
periods can be a feasible alternative to other decentralized heating options, e.g. individual heat
pumps.
2.4
Integrated approach to energy systems analysis
There are also numerous studies that use more integrated approach instead of focusing on single
stage of energy supply. The study by Østergaard et al. [57] concentrates on a both energy savings
and energy generation technologies to reach a 100% renewable energy scenario for the city of
Aalborg, Denmark. The technologies considered include wind power, low temperature geothermal
heat and biomass combined with heat storage, electric vehicles and light rail. For an eco-town
in Southern England, Weber et al.
[58] proposed using the mix of centralized and distributed
technologies. The investigation was made using the DESDOP tool, that is based on mixed integer
linear optimisation techniques. The study concluded that even though it is currently impossible
to avoid electricity from the grid, the reduction of CO2 emissions up to 20% without extra costs
compared to business-as-usual scenario are easily achievable.

[59] investigated the
possibility of 100% renewable energy system for Macedonia, using the EnergyPLAN model. The

The analysis of a net-zero exergy district of ¨Ostra Sala in Uppsala Municipality, Sweden, done

the ﬁrst, pilot phase of the project. There are 20, 000 people supposed to live in the district at
the end of development of all the four phases. The solutions considered there were related to all
stages of energy production, conversion, distribution and usage and the set of solutions included the
extension of the existing district heating and cooling network based on CHP, heat pumps running on
renewable energy, district heating driven white goods (washing machines and dish washers), smart
home automation, eﬃcient lighting, A+++ electrical appliances, and bioelectricity driven public
transport. The analysis was made using a Rational Exergy Management Model (REMM) developed
by the author.

 The ﬁnal heat consumer pays VAT, thus VAT on the heat is taken into account in the
private-economic analysis for the building owners in cases from 4.a to 4.d.
The yearly cash ﬂow is the sum of the revenue, the O&M costs, investments costs and taxes, costs
and taxes being negative numbers. The cash ﬂows are discounted over the years using a discount

 7% for the private-economic analysis of heat supplier
 6% for the private-economic analysis of the building owners
 4% for the socio-economic analysis [67]
The discount rates represent alternative return that can be gained by investment on the market.

3.4.6
Sensitivity analysis
In order to check, how certain parameters and assumptions regarding input data inﬂuence results
of the analysis, sensitivity analysis was performed.
The values initially chosen for the analysis
were considered the reference case. The assumptions investigated were chosen either because of
their likely signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results or because the initial assumptions could easily be set
diﬀerently (as it is e.g. for the value of heat losses from the grid). The parameters compared in the
sensitivity analysis were the unit price of heat and the heat tariﬀat which NPV = 0 for the heat
supplier.
The parameters investigated include initial investment costs, heat demand, discount rates, electricity
prices, supply temperature for domestic hot water, heat pumps’ COPs as well as heat losses from
the network. In each case, only one factor was modiﬁed and their possible relation to other values
was neglected. Thus, while investigating the inﬂuence of initial investment costs, it was assumed
that the change does not result e.g. in better or worse COP of an installed heat pump.

3.4.7
Discussion of the results
In general, the results are in agreement with what was expected based on the literature study and
earlier investigation performed for the area in question, where no clear so indication of a clearly
preferred solution was given, with diﬀerent cases each having their own merits.
As expected, the most economically beneﬁcial is the system with direct electrical heating from Case
1, where the unit heat price for the basic assumptions in socio-economic analysis is 0.85 DKK/kWh,
compared to 1.48 DKK/kWh for the second best Case 4.b and 1.49 DKK/kWh for cases 4.a and 2.a.
However, the results for Case 1 are sensitive to the changes in electricity prices and increase in heat
demand. As expected, the system performs well in houses with extremely low heat demand, when
its very low investment costs are not outbalanced by high electricity consumption. Because of those
factors, if the current legislation changes to allow it, direct electrical heating can be a viable option
of heat supply for very low-energy buildings, where the potential risk connected to electricity price
increases is minimized. Those ﬁndings correspond to the conclusions from Leth’s work [8]. There,
investment in the heat pump was determined to pay back in the period of 20 years marginally in
case of a building in 2020 energy class and not to pay back in that period in case of the house with
improved design. However, with the current legislation, using such system even in a energy-eﬃcient
building is impossible because of the high primary energy factor for electricity.
Total investment costs for all the cases are on similar level (between 5, 204, 272 and 8, 326, 835 DKK
from the socio-economic perspective), except from Case 1, that is signiﬁcantly cheaper than the
rest (1, 075, 605 DKK, also from the socio-economic perspective). However, for the decentralised
cases the whole costs is paid by the building owners, while for the centralised solution the cost is
divided between the heat supplier and the building owners, resulting in lower initial costs for the
latter. On the other hand, centralised systems require signiﬁcant initial investment from the heat
supplier, where also the heat production capacity has to be provided. It causes a problem with
establishing the proper production capacity in the initial stage of the area development, when the
heat demand is going to be signiﬁcantly lower than after all of the planned buildings are constructed.
Based on the results of the economic analysis it can be stated, that from the economic point of
view it often is not beneﬁcial to invest in more eﬃcient technologies, that improve the system’s
performance, but increase investment costs. It can clearly be noticed while looking at the results of

Among the centralised systems, cases 4.c and d are more expensive from the socio economic

of the lower amount of heat sold cases 4.c and d are slightly more sensitive e.g. to the changes
in investment costs.
It is so due to the lower amount of heat sold, that brings revenue to the
heat supplier company. It suggests the importance of proper estimation of the required investment
at the stage of planning and designing the system. However, it also has to be noted, that in the
investigation costs of the grid were assumed to be the same for all of the cases considered. In reality,
due to the lower amount of heat distributed and thus probably also smaller pipe dimensions, grid
costs for cases 4.c and d may be lower than for cases 4.a and b. It has to be mentioned, that the
results for centralised cases are inﬂuenced by the calculation methodology, where it was assumed,
that the heat tariﬀis to be set at the level that guarantees NPV = 0 over the period of 20 years
for the heat supplier. Thus, the building owners have to pay for the investment in grid and heat
generation capacity. However, in reality, the costs would be covered not only by the customers in
Vinge, but by all the customers purchasing heat from the heat supplier.
An interesting solution is the system from Case 3, as it provides some of the beneﬁts og the
centralised systems, while at the same time is signiﬁcantly easier to establish in the initial stage of the
development of Vinge. While it results in higher unit heat price for the building owners than cases
1 and 2.a and has high investment costs, it is relatively insensitive to the changes in assumed input
data (with the exception of investment costs). Additionally, it creates a possibility of integration of
existing cluster heat pumps into common grid at the later stage of the area development.
A possibility, that decreases the unit heat prices for all the cases is decreasing the supply temperature
for domestic hot water to around 48 °C. The exact level should be determined to ensure, that the
required tapping temperatures are still provided. In the systems with heat pumps lower DHW
supply temperature results in higher COP and in all of the system in lower heat losses (from the
heat storage tank and, in case of systems 3, 4.a and 4.b, also from the distribution pipes). All those
improvements can be reached without additional investments and thus are strongly recommended.
Another factor that inﬂuences the results are the assumed discount rates. For the socio-economic
analysis, the discount rate was based on the recommendation by Energistyrelsen [67] and set at the
level of 4%. However, it has to be noted, that the recommended value was changed 7.04.2011 from
5%. The discount rate of 4% is suggested for projects with lifetime below 35 years. For projects
with the lifetime between 36 and 70 years, discount rate of 3% is proposed, and for projects with
the lifetime over 70 years - 2%. In general, the idea behind a social discount rate is that it should
reﬂect a relative valuation of society’s well being now compared well-being in the future [68]. High
social discount rate may result in socially beneﬁcial projects being turned down, while too low one
can result in economically ineﬃcient being implemented [68]. Low social-economic discount rate

3.5 Conclusion
attaches more weight to the beneﬁts or costs occurring in the more distant future, in contrast to
the costs on the initial stage of a project. Thus, in general, developing countries apply higher social
discount rates while evaluating possible investment than developed countries (8-15% compared to
3-7%) [68]. It can be argued, that diﬀerences between those values reﬂect the diﬀerences in social
opportunity costs [68]. In Germany, in 2004 discount rate of 3% was recommended, in Norway in
1998 it was 3.5%, in UK in 2003 also 3.5%, with discount rates even lower for long -term projects
over 30 years [69]. Thus, it can be stated that the investment rate of 4% is suitably low for the
analysed project, but even lower rate of 3.5 or 3.0% could be assumed.
One of the parts of the analysis that requires more detailed investigation before making a ﬁnal
decision, is the estimation of waste heat potential available locally and the percentage of demand
possible to cover using that heat. As the amount of waste heat available changes during the year [5],
the investigation has to take into account distribution of that availability over the year. Additionally,
required output capacities of both heat pumps (large central GSHP and waste heat HP) would have
to be determined and their investment costs estimated.
Apart from that, in the analysis factors that are not directly dependent on each other (as it is e.g.
with lower heat source and heat output temperatures and heat pump’s COP) were assumed not to
be connected. Thus, e.g. increased investment costs were not tied to possible higher eﬃciency of
system components. In reality those factors may be connected and it should be accounted for in a
more detailed analysis.
3.5
Conclusion
The preliminary economic analysis using simpliﬁed assumptions was made to determine, how
considered solutions perform economically.
Both socio-economic perspective, as well as private
economic perspective from the point of view of the building owners were taken into account. The
private economic perspective for the heat supplier was accounted for by assuming, that the heat
tariﬀwill be set at a level that results in NPV = 0 over the period of 20 years.
It was found, that it is Case 1 that performs the best, both from the socio-economic perspective as
well as from the point of view of building owners. However, it is sensitive to the changes in heat
demand and electricity prices and under current regulations cannot be used as a standard heating
solution for buildings in energy class 2020 due to the high primary energy factor for electricity.
Case 2.a had the second lowest unit heat price for the building owners and Case 3 - third lowest.
As Case 2.a is a standard solution with air-to-water individual heat pump, Case 3 was considered
more interesting and chosen for further investigation. Additionally, the system in Case 3 is more
energy-eﬃcient. The biggest disadvantage of this option is the need for the building owners to
cooperate with each other. A certain beneﬁt of this solution is also the fact, that in the course
of development of the whole Vinge area, cluster heat pumps could be integrated into a centralised
supply system, if such solutions proves to be the best, when more stages of the town development
are taken into account.
Thus, after taking into account both the results of preliminary analysis and additional aforementioned
factors, Case 3 was chosen for further analysis.

The analysis of the chosen heat supply system from Case 3 was done using TRNSYS software
package, developed originally at the University of Wisconsin. It is a transient systems simulation
program with modular structure and its main applications include low energy buildings and HVAC
systems, renewable energy systems, including solar systems (both thermal and photovoltaic) and
cogeneration. Models are created by setting together diﬀerent modules.

The approach to modelling the system was based on the one presented in Master’s thesis of Bakhtiari

from the TESS library. The simulation was run with a timestep of 0.1 h (6 minutes).
Figure 4.2 shows the schematics of the model of system from case 3. The detailed description of
system’s components and input data used is provided in the sections from 4.2.1 to 4.2.8.
Pipes located in the buildings were not included in the model, as it was assumed that the systems

4.4
Discussion
Obtained results for energy consumption correspond quite well to the results from the preliminary
calculations. Simulated annual energy consumption was 11.26 kWh/m2·year, while estimated heat
demand for system from Case 3 in preliminary analysis was 6.76 kWh/m2·year.
However, it has to be noticed that in the winter period there are problems with supplying the
domestic hot water at the required temperature (at times for short periods temperature of water
supplied drops to around 30 °C). It results in energy consumption by the system lower than in case
when required supply temperature was met all the time.
One of the solutions for that problem may be further increase in the size of the storage tank. This,
however, is not recommended, as it increases both the cost of the system, heat losses and space
required to install it. Another solution would be improvement in control system. The valve directing
ﬂow from the heat pump to the heat storage tank and to the space heating part would adjust cut
oﬀthe ﬂow to the SH system to provide suﬃcient heat supply for DHW. Thanks to the thermal
mass of the buildings, such step is not likely to cause the temperature drop below comfort levels.
Additionally, to prevent it, a time limit of this cut-oﬀcould be introduced. This, however, was not
done in the model due to problems with setting up the control system and time constraints. Such
solution could be combined with a study of ﬂexibility of space heating demand in the building, to
ensure optimal operation of the system.
One of the problems related to sizing of the heat pump was already evident in this study - a smaller
heat pump reached higher seasonal eﬃciency, but was not able to cover the demand at all times
despite the fact, that used water draw proﬁle did not have signiﬁcant spikes following in short

5.1
Introduction
Exergy is the quantity stemming from the second law of thermodynamics and deﬁned as the
maximum work that can be done by the composite of the system and a speciﬁed reference environment.
It is assumed, that the reference environment is inﬁnite, in equilibrium and encloses all other
systems. Thus, the exergy is the propriety not only of the system, but both of the system and
its environment. [81] The terms available energy, exergic energy, utilizable energy, available useful
work and maximum work content are synonymous or nearly synonymous with exergy.
While energy is never destroyed during a process and just changes its form, exergy is conserved only
if all the processes occurring in the system and the environment are reversible. Exergy eﬃciency is
a measure of how close to ideal a certain process is. Thus, while the energy analysis is based only
on the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics (FLT), the exergy analysis bases on both ﬁrst and second laws
of thermodynamics (SLT) and takes into account the diﬀerent thermodynamic values of diﬀerent
energy forms and provides the information about the degradation of the energy during the processes
occurring. It can help also to quantify the quality of heat in the waste stream. [81]
To express the ”work potential” of a certain form of energy, the exergy factor fex is used.

However, this assumption is a simpliﬁcation, as the required supply temperature for space heating

As expected, the least exergy-eﬃcient solution is Case 1, where electricity is used directly both for

the network even lower than in low-temperature district heating systems. The second best solution
is the system from Case 2.b. There, heat is produced separately for the space heating and domestic
hot water purpose, what allows for a better temperature match and results in higher eﬃciency of
the SH heat pump. Thus, it can be seen that the solutions that perform the best, from the point
of view of exergy analysis, are the ones, where the best match between the quality of the energy
demand and energy of the resources used occurs. It is also interesting to notice, that while systems
from cases 3, 4.a and 4.b perform worse than the one from Case 2.b in total, it is slightly more
exergy-eﬃcient in the summer months (76.6 compared to 73.1, 68.9 and 65.8 kWh, respectively).
As it was mentioned in section 5.2.2, the greatest exergy losses occur at the stage of transformation
of electrical energy into low-temperature heat. Because the electricity consumption is the highest
in Case 1, also that loss is signiﬁcantly higher than in all other investigated cases. In general, the
use of direct electricity for generating low-temperature heat such as required for space heating and
domestic hot water purposes signiﬁcantly decreases the exergy eﬃciency of the system, as it can be
seen for cases 1 and 4.d. In the systems, where heat is generated together for space heating and
domestic hot water, there is an additional exergy loss. It is caused either by mixing the hot ﬂuid
from supply stream with colder ﬂuid from return stream or heating up the ﬂuid in space heating
system only to the temperature required (30 °C or lower, while the heat is supplied at temperature
of around 55 °C).
The results for exergy of demand correspond to the ones obtained by Jansen [12].
There, for
Dutch conditions, weighted average exergy factor for space heating was determined to be 5.8%
and for domestic hot water - 8.5%. For the buildings in Vinge, these factors are 6.7% and 10.7%,

One of the factors that distorts the calculation results is using the monthly resolution both for the

caused by neglecting the fact that the highest heat demand occurs during the periods with lowest
temperatures. The second issue is that no investigation of the electricity generation technologies
was performed in the analysis. Thus, it can be argued that, if it was taken into account, Case 1

share of the electricity consumed would come from renewable sources, especially from wind turbines.
Another thing that has to be mentioned is that as the methodology used was based on the guidebook
by Jansen [12], exergy of renewable sources was not included in determining the exergy eﬃciency
of the system.

6.1
Conclusions
The aim of the project was to choose the optimal heat supply method for low energy building area
planned in Fredrikssund municipality.
Based on literature studies and data provided by the municipality, the energy demand of the building
was estimated to be 23.85 kWh/m2·year (23.33 kWh/m2·year for the case with direct electrical
heating).
21.23 kWh/m2·year out of it was needed for space heating and domestic hot water
preparation. Under assumed conditions, peak demand for SH was determined to be 1.2 kW and
for DHW 0.3 kW. The exception here is the direct electrical heating system, where heat to cover
the demand for the whole day has to be produced during the oﬀ-peak hours between 0:00 and 6:00.
Thus, the SH peak load was calculated as 5, 44 kW.
Subsequently, economic analysis of the chosen cases was performed. The considered cases were
evaluated based on socio-economic analysis, as well as private economic analysis both for the heat
supplier company and for building owners. Additionally, sensitivity analysis was made to investigate
the inﬂuence of changes in the assumptions on the results. It was determined, that direct electrical
heating performs the best, both in the socio-economic and private-economic analysis (unit price
of heat of 0.85 and 2.40 DKK/kWh, respectively). However, it is sensitive to the changes in heat
demand and electricity prices. Second and third best solutions from the socio-economic point of
view were low temperature district heating utilizing waste heat (1.48 DKK/kWh) and, ex aequo,
low temperature district heating and individual air-to-water heat pump (1.49 DKK/kWh). For
the building owners, second and third best solutions were individual air-to-water heat pump (2.79
DKK/kWh) and cluster ground-source heat pump (3.03 DKK/kWh).
Based on the initial investigation, cluster ground-source heat pump was chosen for further analysis.
Model of the system was built in TRNSYS software and simulations were run to investigate system’s
energy consumption in dynamic conditions. Energy consumption by was calculated to be 11.26
kWh/m2·year, compared to 6.76 kWh/m2·year from the preliminary analysis. Observed problems
with system operation were discussed and possible solutions proposed. Designing the heat pump to
operate in bivalent mode was suggested.
As the last part of the investigation, exergy analysis of considered systems was performed. Calculated
exergy demand for one building was 114.5 kWh/year for space heating and 286.7 kWh/year for
domestic hot water. Annual exergy factors of the demand were 6.7% for space heating and 10.4%
for domestic hot water. Out of all the systems investigated, direct electrical heating system has
signiﬁcantly the highest exergy of resources used per building (4458.3 kWh/year). The system that
performs the best is ultra-low temperature district heating combined with booster heat pumps for
DHW preparation (1005.5 kWh/year). Additionally, for the system with cluster geothermal-source

The results of the investigation point out the noticeable disagreement between the results of
economic and exergy analysis.
Because the investment costs signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the overall
performance of the system in economic analysis, systems where those costs are lower tend to result
in better unit heat prices. On the other hand, systems with higher investment costs in the study
were generally characterised by higher energy and exergy eﬃciency. It is especially true for exergy
eﬃciency, where signiﬁcant improvement can be reached by separate heat generation for space
heating and domestic hot water. In some cases the initial investment may not pay back despite the
higher system’s eﬃciency and lower O&M costs. Based on the performed study, no deﬁnite answer,
how the compromise should be made, can be given.
Observed variations in exergy factors, both seasonal and daily, suggest the opportunity of increasing
the exergy eﬃciency of the systems by optimising the operation of storage systems, as mentioned
by Jansen [83].
It is also worth noting, that the problem of proper ground heat exchanger design signiﬁes the
increasing connection between building and energy sector planning and natural sciences and the
need of more holistic approach to the design of renewable-based energy systems than in case of
traditional ones.
Exergy analysis is one of the possible tools for evaluating energy systems, however in its simpliﬁed
version, in certain cases it does not account for all the factors that should be taken into consideration
in energy system analysis.
This is the situation for direct electrical system considered in the
investigation. As a principle, it is suppose do use the electricity in the oﬀ-peak period. Additionally,
with the high level of inclusion of wind energy into the electrical grid, it is the oﬀ-peak period, when
the production can be greater than due to stronger winds at night.
Thus, it could utilize the
energy that would not be used otherwise and, in the current state of technology, is diﬃcult to
store. Diﬀerent calculation of exergy of resources used would have to be made for diﬀerent systems
investigated to account for that, what makes the procedure signiﬁcantly more complicated. On the
other hand, it can still be argued, that this electricity could be used in a more eﬃcient way if e.g.
heat pump combined with radiant ﬂoor system was used instead of direct electrical heating.

CHAPTER 6.
CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
65
6.2
Future work
Work performed as part of this thesis project is certainly not exhaustive and there are several
possible topics of further investigation.
One of the options is the investigation of implementation of solar energy systems, e.g. in the form
of solar collectors for domestic hot water heating. Solar collectors could also be connected to the
heat pump system to regenerate the ground heat exchanger and prevent the cooling of the ground
surrounding it.
Another possible topic of further investigation is the optimisation of components of the heat supply
system. In case of the system from Case 3 that was modelled as part of this thesis, those components
would be primarily the heat pump with the heat exchanger and heat storage tank. To do so, also
demand ﬂexibility of the buildings would have to be investigated to provide information about the
possibilities of load shifting in the system.
The third possibility, connected to the optimisation of component sizes, is optimisation of the
heat supply and thermal storage strategies (both in the energy system as well as in the buildings
themselves) using exergy principles, as suggested by Jansen [12]. As the reference environment
varies with time, exergy can be ”created” in time, thus using both passive (thermal mass) and
active (storage tanks) storage technologies allows to create more exergy-eﬃcient systems.
