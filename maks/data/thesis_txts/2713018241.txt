Abstract 
Conventional physically based so-called high-fidelity (HiFi) models are currently used for analyzing urban 
drainage networks and obtaining thorough understanding of the dynamics in a network. These models 
however suffer from long simulation times and are thus not suited for investigations that require many 
simulations. Faster computationally lighter urban drainage models are thus a necessary tool when e.g., 
performing uncertainty analyses, for planning purposes, and real time control (RTC). Many different 
simplified models have been proposed, but the lack of an automatic model setup hinders the use of 
these models.  
In this study, simplified models (denoted surrogate models (SM)) based on results of HiFi models are 
used to imitate the discharge through combined sewer overflow (CSO) structures. These models are 
based on the principles of conservation of mass and computes the exchange of water between lumped 
compartments in the network. The discharges from the compartments are primarily modelled via 
piecewise linear volume-discharge relations and the results are compared with results from the HiFi 
model.  
The aim of the project is to investigate, develop and test automatic delineation methods for sub-diving 
drainage networks into surrogate model compartments. These methods include algorithms known from 
graph partitioning as well as a proposed scheme based on hydraulic conditions. The case study used is a 
HiFi model of the drainage network with 6 CSO-structures that discharge CSO to Sjælsø and Kajerød Å. 
The model performance is evaluated using the Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) after applying a 20-minute 
moving average, as well as the relative difference in the accumulated discharge over weirs and through 
pumps.  
Performances for the automatically generated models are generally good with NSE scores between 0.86 
and 0.99 for 5 out of the 6 CSO-structures. Further subdivision of compartments, based on hydraulic 
conditions or graph partitioning or done manually, can further increase performance. For the best 
model, all CSO-structures had NSE scores above 0.94. The model is tested with a two-year continuous 
simulation where NSEs above 0.91 for 5 of the 6 CSO-structures can be achieved with the fully 
automated setup. The simulation time was measured to be more than 10.000 times faster than the Mike 
Urban HiFi model that the SM is based on. 
The potential usage of the SM is illustrated by testing the implementation of additional basin volume in 
a part of the network by adding storage capacity to a surrogate model compartment. Subsequently the 
same volume is implemented in the HiFi model for validating the surrogate model results. The surrogate 
model approach yields a reduction in the CSO-volume of 13% while the HiFi model yields a similar 
reduction of 14%. Thus, the model seems to be well suited for e.g., planning applications. Thanks to 
their fast computational time, the automatically generated SM can also be used for Monte Carlo 
applications, to e.g., estimate the uncertainty span in the calculation of CSO-loads, rainfall runoff 
volumes, or for RTC.

Resumé 
Konventionelle fysisk baserede såkaldte high-fidelity (HiFi) modeller bruges i dag til at analysere 
afløbssystemer og opnå en grundig forståelse af dynamikken i et netværk. Disse modeller lider dog 
under lange simuleringstider og er derfor ikke velegnede til undersøgelser, der kræver mange 
simuleringer. Beregningsmæssigt lettere modeller er således et nødvendigt værktøj, når man fx udfører 
usikkerhedsestimeringer til planlægningsformål og realtidsstyring (RTC). Mange forskellige simple 
modeller er blevet foreslået, men manglen på et automatisk model setup hindrer brugen af disse 
modeller. 
I dette projekt anvendes simplificerede modeller (betegnet surrogatmodeller (SM)) baseret på resultater 
fra HiFi-modeller til at efterligne udledningen gennem overløbsstrukturer fra fælleskloakering (CSO). 
Disse modeller er baseret på massebevarelsesprincipper og beregner udvekslingen af vand mellem 
sammenlagte dele i netværket. Udledningerne fra disse er primært modelleret via stykvis lineære 
volumen-flow relationer, og resultaterne sammenlignes med HiFi-modellen. 
Formålet med projektet er at undersøge, udvikle og teste automatiske metoder til opdeling af 
afløbssystemmodeller til generering af surrogatmodeller. Disse metoder omfatter algoritmer kendt fra 
grafteori samt en opdeling baseret på hydrauliske forhold. Det anvendte casestudy er en HiFi-model af 
et afløbssystem med 6 CSO-strukturer, der udleder CSO til Sjælsø og Kajerød Å. Modellen evalueres ved 
hjælp af Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE), der bruger et 20-minutters glidende gennemsnit, såvel som den 
relative forskel i den akkumulerede udledning for overløb og pumper. 
De automatisk genererede modeller er generelt gode med NSE-værdier mellem 0.86 og 0.99 for 5 ud af 
de 6 CSO-strukturer. Yderligere underopdeling baseret på hydrauliske forhold eller grafopdeling eller 
manuel opdeling, kan forbedre modellen yderligere. Den bedste model opnåede NSE-værdier på over 
0.94 i alle CSO-strukturer. Modellen er testet med en toårig kontinuerlig simulering, hvor NSE-værdier 
over 0.91 for 5 af de 6 CSO-strukturer kan opnås med den fuldautomatiske opsætning. Simuleringstiden 
for SM blev målt til at være mere end 10.000 gange hurtigere end Mike Urban HiFi-modellen. 
Den potentielle anvendelse af SM er illustreret ved at teste implementeringen af yderligere 
bassinvolumen i en del af netværket. Efterfølgende implementeres samme volumen i HiFi-modellen til 
at validere surrogatmodellens resultater. Surrogatmodellen giver en reduktion i CSO-volumen på 13 %, 
mens HiFi-modellen giver en tilsvarende reduktion på 14 %. Modellen ser således ud til at være velegnet 
til fx planlægningsapplikationer. Takket være deres hurtige beregningstid kan den automatisk 
genererede SM også bruges til Monte Carlo-applikationer, til for eksempel at estimere 
usikkerhedsspændet i beregningen af CSO-belastninger, nedbørsmængder eller for RTC.

1 Introduction 
Urban drainage models serve to simulate discharge in sewer systems, to get a thorough understanding 
of the dynamics in a network, to mitigate current network issues, and control discharge to natural water 
bodies. Models with high spatial and temporal resolution, so-called high-fidelity (HiFi) models, are often 
preferred because of accurate flow estimations. However, these models are computational heavy and 
suffer from long simulation time. Hence these types of models are not well-suited if many simulations or 
fast results are required for a given application (Dobson et al., 2021; Wolfs & Willems, 2017). This could 
be for e.g., uncertainty and sensitivity analysis, real time control (RTC) and for planning applications 
(Thrysøe, 2021).  
To bridge the gap, fast computationally efficient models are becoming increasingly important. Examples 
of these are e.g., data driven rainfall runoff models (Kratzert et al., 2019) or models that only simulate 
discharge through a few selected connections (Dobson et al., 2021; Thrysøe, 2021; Vanrolleghem et al., 
2009; Wolfs & Willems, 2017). Different categories are shown in Table 1.

As simplified models do not yield results that are as detailed as the HiFi dynamical models it is important 
to identify the purpose of these models to get the results that are needed. Therefore, the term “Fit-for-
purpose models” may be used to describe this kind of models. According to Jamali et al., (2018) a fit for 
purpose model can be described as “a model that predicts the required results within the desired level of 
accuracy and manageable amount of time and computational expense.” This implies that the required 
results and desired level of accuracy are known before designing the model.  
Physically based Low-fidelity models (LoFi) seek to reduce the elements of a HiFi-model to increase 
computational time while maintaining accurate flow simulations (Davidsen et al., 2017; Savage et al., 
2016). Davidsen et al., (2017) found that a decrease of 66% of the network yielded a reduction in 
computational time of only 35% while also affecting the model results substantially. It was therefore 
shown that the complexity of the HiFi model cannot be reduced sufficiently without losing important 
network dynamics (Davidsen et al., 2017). 
Conceptual grey box models are often based on linear reservoir models or variations of these such as 
piecewise linear functions (Kroll et al., 2017), that couple the volume of a system with the outflow 
through a storage-discharge function. This require some definition of compartments to define how the 
volume is to be computed.  
Data driven models for rainfall runoff modelling have been implemented in some studies where the 
results generally have been accurate (Boyd et al., 2019; Granata et al., 2016; Kratzert et al., 2019). 
Machine learning techniques are applied on one or more parameters to simulate the flow. These models 
are so called black box models and the internal processes and relations between parameters are not 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
known; thus, the physical interpretation of the drainage system is not used. This means that the results 
are difficult to extrapolate. This limits the use of these model e.g., within planning applications to e.g., 
investigate the effect of installing additional storage volumes. Additionally, these models require a large 
amount of reliable data and thus high-performance computers to efficiently calibrate and simulate flow 
(Kratzert et al., 2019). The advantage may be that they can describe complex processes that is not 
possible to describe with physical processes.  
A type of simplified model, called surrogate models, may be based solely on the results of a different 
model, typically a HiFi model, disregarding measurements or physical considerations (such as the Saint-
Venant equations). In this case it may be described as “a model of a model”, a metamodel or a surrogate 
model (Razavi et al., 2012). The term “surrogate model” is used throughout this project as the same 
model type has been used in different studies with this notation (Thrysøe, 2021).  
Several approaches can be used to create surrogate models (Razavi et al., 2012). Razavi et al. (2012) 
divides these models in two categories: Data driven response surface models and physically based 
models. In practice, models may be combinations of these two categories (shown as the grey box 
models in Table 1). E.g., Thrysøe et al., (2019) creates a lumped simplified model and uses the response 
surface from the detailed HiFi model for calibration of the parameters. And Wolfs & Willems, (2017) 
creates a modular framework that combines the learning capabilities from the data driven approach 
with the robustness and hydraulic elements of the physically based approach.  
The creation of such surrogate models is often done manually and requires special expertise that is not 
typical within the field of urban drainage modelling. This limits the actual usage of these models that 
otherwise show great potential in several disciplines such as forecast, sensitivity and uncertainty 
analysis, planning and parameter calibration (Thrysøe et al., 2019). Thus, if these models can be created 
automatically, it would benefit the use in urban drainage modelling. 
1.1 Problem statement 
This project aims at developing a fully automated method for generating surrogate models of HiFi 
models. This is done by taking both hydraulic considerations (e.g., backwater effects) and graph 
partitioning techniques into account and still maintain computational speed, flexibility, and sufficient 
accuracy. The model setup is built on concepts presented in Thrysøe et al. (2019). The model only 
considers the drainage network and discharges from CSO structures and not the flooding above ground.  
Objectives: 
• 
Develop an automatic method for creating surrogate models of Mike Urban models.  
• 
Investigate and develop suitable delineation methods. 
• 
Test the automatic model setup on a real case area. 
The report starts with presenting the current state-of-the-art and prior work by others in the 
Background theory section (1). Subsequently two methods for automatic compartment delineation are 
generated in section 3. The overall methodology for the generation of the surrogate models is explained 
in section 4. The methods are applied in a case area that is presented in section 5. There will be 
references and small examples from the case area in the earlier sections. The results are shown in 
section 6. Finally, the models are tested on a longer period and an example of an application is shown in 
section 7.

2 Background theory 
In this section it will be explained what has been done previously within this model setup and in 
applications where the model engine (Borup, 2018) has been used. The subsequent sections will cover 
what has been added to the model setup as part of this project.  
The models used in this project are a combination between some of the terms described in the section 
above. The model is referred to as a surrogate model (SM) even though the modelling framework from 
(Borup, 2018) and (Thrysøe, 2021) applies concepts from both surrogate and physically based LoFi 
models and uses data-driven approaches in the calibration of the model.  
The term surrogate is applied because both the creation and validation are based on results from a 
detailed HiFi model. Thus, no real observations nor physical components of the network are used for 
model creation. However, as the parameters are representations of physical properties (volume and 
discharge) the model cannot be described solely as a data-driven surrogate model. It also contains 
elements of a physically based lumped LoFi model based on the principles of conservation of mass. This 
coupling between parameters and real physical properties means that the model type cannot be 
described as a black-box model and the parameters can be changed manually to e.g., investigate the 
effect of added volumes in a compartment. This would not be possible if the parameters were just 
abstract functions defined from underlying complex processes.  
The HiFi model that is used as the basis of the surrogate model is computed in Mike Urban (referred to 
as MU). Data and result files from MU are used to create the SM. A more detailed description of the 
various Mike Urban files required for the process can be found in Appendix 9.1.  
2.1 State of the art on simplified models  
Previous studies have successfully implemented simplified model setups to simulate flows in drainage 
networks. Kroll et al., (2017) has developed a model framework that is based on both structural network 
system parameters as well as simulation results. This is done in a semi-automated way to make the 
method more accessible. The procedure includes a specification of compartments and connections 
which in Kroll et al., (2017) only considers the connections manually specified by the user.  
To assist the development and use of simplified models Ledergerber et al., (2019) has developed a 
generalized procedure to create conceptual models. The procedure involves a model definition, model 
development, calibration, and validation and the steps are described in detail. The conceptual models 
are calibrated and validated in specified comparison points that are defined from key hydraulic 
structures, overflows, rain gauges etc. The study finds that special structures need to be conceptualized 
specifically for each case. Thus, automation of the procedure has not been a priority in this study.  
Wolfs & Willems, (2017) showed accurate estimations of flow in the modular framework presented in 
the study. The discharge from a compartment can be simulated in 4 ways; fixed flow, piecewise linear, 
transfer function or Artificial neural network (ANN). Thus, enabling the model to overcome insufficient 
accuracy by modelling backwater without affecting simulation time and model flexibility. The model 
setup is however not able to delineate the system automatically or systematically.  
In Thrysøe et al., (2019) the flows are simulated based on volume-discharge relationship in 
compartments and parameters are based on results from a HiFi Mike Urban model. The simulation time 
is reduced with up to 6 orders of magnitude and flows are well described within the expected range of 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
uncertainty (Thrysøe et al., 2019). The study finds that the model setup is robust with respect to 
parameters except for compartment resolution that has a high influence on flow estimates. The model 
framework is ill-suited for flat areas with backwater effects due to the way the discharge is computed. 
Furthermore, the compartments are manually delineated, and the delineation process may be 
automatized to improve the workflow.  
Dobson et al., (2021) has applied different graph partitioning algorithms to automatically subdivide parts 
of a network from a HiFi model into smaller compartments that are used in the CityWat-SemiDistributed 
(CWSD) modelling framework. The framework allows the user to pre-specify points in the network with 
key hydraulic interest where connections are needed. Hereafter the remaining network parts are 
automatically lumped into smaller compartments. The aim is to allow the user to specify demands for 
computational time and accuracy and create suitable models for these purposes. The application of 
different algorithms known from graph partitioning theory makes it possible to automate the 
delineation of the network. However, as only the key hydraulic structures specified by the user are 
considered, important network characteristics may be overlooked.  
2.2 Flexible automatic reservoir surrogate models 
None of the articles mentioned above have made a fully automated method that is based on hydraulic 
considerations in the network. The mentioned studies either rely on input from the user of the model or 
as Dobson et al., (2021), mainly defines the compartment delineations on how the network is 
connected. Thrysøe et al., (2019) claims that automation of compartment delineation may improve the 
usage of these type of models:  
“The process of setting up the compartments and calibrating them is currently a manual 
process. It does require time to examine and understand the network, deciding on 
compartment shapes and sizes and reporting links crossing the compartment boundaries. 
Once a suitable compartment design has been obtained, the results of this paper show that 
the surrogate model can be determined fairly easy and quick. We see a potential for 
automatizing the workflow in the future.” (Thrysøe et al., 2019) 
The aim of the project will thus be to create surrogate models automatically based on hydraulic 
knowledge that is usually considered for manual compartment delineation. Additionally, to avoid large 
compartments, graph partitioning algorithms will also be used.

a) - Compartments 
The urban drainage network is lumped into compartments as part of the modelling setup. 
Compartments can in principle vary to cover everything from just one single node to the entire network, 
however, to obtain stable models and useful results, the best compartment definition is somewhere in 
between.  
In practice the compartment creation means that parts of the system are lumped together into 
compartments, and these form the basis of the volume and discharge computations that the model is 
based on. An illustration of the lumping process is shown in Figure 2. These models often neglect the 
conservation of energy that is considered in HiFi models and therefore only computes the water 
volumes while disregarding hydraulic head (Chow et al., 1988). As the discharge from a linear reservoir 
only consider the volume in the immediate upstream reservoir, system dynamics such as backwater 
effects are difficult to include. Examples of conceptual models that compute the water level and thus 
also consider backwater effects do exist, but the simulation speed is not reduced as much as for other 
simplified models (Kroll et al., 2017).

In Figure 2 a conceptual illustration of the lumping process is shown. Each node is assigned to a 
compartment number based on certain criteria. The nodes and links connected to them are then 
aggregated to form the basis of the volume-discharge (VQ) relationship for each compartment. Each link 
that connects two compartments is assigned a set of parameters based on the aggregated volume in the 
upstream compartment and the discharge through that specific connection.

Chow et al., (1988) describes lumped flow routing as flow calculated as a function of time at a particular 
location. In distributed flow routing the flow is a function of time and space. Hence, HiFi differ from LoFi 
models by computing flow in every element of a network forward in time. By lumping the 
compartments, part of the spatial dimension in the model is lost, so when comparing the MU results 
with the flow from the SM, it is only possible to compare connections between compartments or outlets 
in the surrogate model. 
There are several approaches that can be made to determine the delineation and compartment 
resolution. This will be covered in detail in section 3.

The length of each “step” is dependent of the size of the system as bigger networks need longer time to

formulation of the SM as these represent stable relations between the volume and the discharge 
(Thrysøe et al., 2019).  
The VQ-parameters are described through piecewise linear (PWL) functions between the sampled 
points. PWL is in general preferred as the default parameter setup and is therefore described here as a 
part of the model setup. An alternative approach is to imitate the water levels in the upstream and 
downstream nodes and compute the flow based on the gradient between the two nodes. This is 
denoted as Gradient Based (GB) flows and is discussed further in section 6.4.

2.4 Hydraulic foundation  
In this subsection some basic hydraulic concepts that are relevant for simplified lumped model of an 
urban drainage network are explained. 
The term backwater is used to describe the water that moves backwards (or upstream) in the drainage 
system. This comes from a constraining element limiting water to flow downstream which results in 
accumulation of water upstream (Lund et al., 2018). Backwater is modelled in HiFi models by using the 
Momentum equation called the full dynamic wave function (Chow et al., 1988). As this equation is 
neglected in many conceptual model setups, backwater effects cannot be described in these types of 
models.  
The relation between the volume and the discharge can either be invariable or variable as seen in Figure 
5. An invariable relation applies to a situation where the discharge for a given water level is 
unambiguous and vice versa for a variable relation. Variable VQ relations are often caused by backwater 
in the network and the flow in lumped models are here estimated by an average. If backwater effects 
are too large the assumption in relating volume to discharge is not valid. 
 
 
Figure 5: The Storage, S, can either have an invariable or variable relationship with the discharge, depending on the flow 
characteristics of the pipe. Here R is the maximum storage and P is the maximum outflow. Figure from Chow et al. (1988). 
The concept of backwater arises from flow controls that restrict flow from going downstream. These 
restrictions can be implemented because of a discharge limit to a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) 
or because there is an economically gain by implementing the flow controls. When the outflowing 
capacity is limited e.g., from a reservoir, the upstream water level will rise as shown on Figure 6 (Butler 
et al., 2018). 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
Figure 6: Example of throttle pipe that limits the outflow, which results in water level rising in the upstream basin and finally 
causes the weir/CSO structure to overflow. Illustration is from Butler et al., (2018).  
In Figure 6 water level is rising until spilled from a weir due to the throttle pipe. This can for example be 
in a Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO) structure. The spilled volume from the structure is called CSO and 
contains a mix of wastewater and rainwater. The CSO volume is usually released into a natural water 
body, such as a lake or stream.  
In this project such structures are a priority to define when building the SM. Firstly, because such 
structures cause backwater in upstream pipes, secondly CSO volumes are crucial to estimate accurately 
for environmental purposes and thirdly because these structures control the flow of the system (Butler 
et al., 2018).   
Taking the hydraulic considerations mentioned above into account, (Thrysøe, 2021) identified 6 rules as 
guidelines for how to set suitable compartment borders in SM. These are: 
a) Hydraulic structures and constraints: These form a good border as the flows are restricted 
through these and thus an unambiguous VQ relationship is often seen. Also, the flows in these 
structures often what is desired to compute from the surrogate model.  
b) Steep pipes: As backwater is seldom seen in steep pipes these form stable compartment 
borders and may be used.  
c) Bidirectional flow: If the flow in a pipe goes in both directions this is not a good choice of border 
as these dynamics cannot be computed by the surrogate model.  
d) Q-h relationships: The relationship between the flow and the hydraulic head. This is a good 
indication of whether backwater is present in a pipe stretch and thus identify links that are not 
suitable for borders.  
e) Number of links at boundaries: The number of links between compartments should be kept at a 
minimum.  
f) Compartment size: The compartment size affects the stability of the model and thus too small 
compartments should be avoided. Too large will result in less accurate results.  
The rules are identified to help users make suitable surrogate models manually. Most of them depend 
on an assessment in the specific case whether a split should be placed at a certain location or if nearby 
links are better suited. Thus, it may be difficult to use these rules in a generic way that will always yield 
good models.

2.5 Staircase rain for parameter extraction 
Thrysøe et al., (2019) used a constructed staircase rain series to calibrate the VQ-relations (see Figure 7). 
This is a rain series that has a constant intensity for some time followed by an increase and then 
constant at that intensity – like steps on a staircase. This allows the system to reach steady state 
conditions of each rain intensity step and results in unambiguous VQ relations for all the pipes.  
The drawback is that the maximum backwater capacity will be reached as the system is full. Thus, for 
smaller rain events, flows might be underestimated because backwater effects are excessive (Thrysøe et 
al., 2019). In Thrysøe et al., (2019) this underestimation was not deemed a problem. The compartments 
in the case study were large, which reduces the impact of backwater effects, and therefore it is not 
certain that this underestimation can be neglected in all types of compartments and with all sizes. 
Figure 7: Staircase rain with 1 hour interval with increasing intensity used for parameter extraction of the SM.  
When using historical rain data for parameter extraction, dynamic storages and flows are represented. 
The relation will be ambiguous and requires more training data to account for input data uncertainty 
e.g., distribution of rainfall (Thrysøe et al., 2019). In Thrysøe et al., (2019) the difference between using a 
historic rain series and a staircase rain was found to be insignificant. As this project aims towards an 
automated procedure that is as generic as possible, the synthetic staircase rain is preferred. 
2.6 Network algorithms 
Network structures are used to describe many different real-life situations such as connections and 
relations between people and companies, modelling and forecasting within financing or trading, or 
finding hidden patterns in large data sets (Needham & Hodler, 2019). Therefore, many advanced 
methods and algorithms has been developed to analyze, understand, and manipulate graphs. A graph is 
in the simplest form a collection of points (called nodes) and the connections (called edges or 
relationships) between these (Needham & Hodler, 2019). The nodes are representations of elements, 
such as people or companies, that need to be connected and the edges describe how they are 
connected e.g., if information passes one way or both ways or if only a certain amount or type of 
information can pass through the connection.  
As drainage networks are physical networks, some of these methods may be applied here and aid in a 
better understanding of the dynamics in the drainage system (Dobson et al., 2021). One branch of 
graph-theory is to lump parts of a network based on some common features. From this simplified

3.1 Graph partitioning principles  
The Louvain and modified Louvain (denoted L_L_sc) algorithms are provided by Dobson et al., (2021a) 
and are used to lump the drainage network (Dobson, 2021). The model engine used in Dobson et al., 
(2021) is different from the one used for the SM, and thus the two frameworks are not directly 
comparable. The graph partitioning algorithms do not take the hydraulic structures of the network into 
account. In Dobson et al., (2021) these structures are preserved as compartment connections for 
hydraulic purposes and to compare flows in these structures with the HiFi model as is often desired. 
Dobson et al., (2021) provides a full model setup for one kind of simplified models. This involves both 
reading the network, delineating compartments, calibration, and running the model. In this project the 
partitioning algorithms have been modified and implemented in the SM setup to fit the MU models and 
the SM model engine.  
The key hydraulic structures can be identified as described in the following section. These can either be 
used as the only compartment connections or combined with the graph partitioning algorithms. This 
means that there might be a potential of combining the two approaches which will be done in section 
6.3. 
3.2 Identification of key hydraulic structures  
One of the key limitations with the VQ-parameters is their inability to accurately imitate backwater 
effects (see section 2.4). Additionally, the rules for manual delineation developed by (Thrysøe, 2021) 
highlight backwater as an important factor.  Hydraulic structures (a) will often cause backwater in 
upstream links as these restrict the flow. This can be a throttle pipe leaving a basin or a collection of two 
or more pipes where the downstream one has insufficient capacity. Steep pipes (b) will likely have 
unambiguous VQ-relations as backwater will not travel backwards up along high slopes. As mentioned, 
the QH-curves (d) can be used as a measure for backwater in a link. Bidirectional flow (c) will also be 
captured when investigating the QH-curves as some of the flows will be negative.  
The following chapter will be an investigation on how to use QH-curves to determine the amount of 
backwater and identify suitable connections. 
3.2.1 Defining backwater  
In links where there is an unambiguous relationship between the water level (H) and discharge (Q) (see 
Figure 8 a) a specific water level only corresponds to one specific flow, because the flow only depends 
on the hydraulic head upstream. An ambiguous relationship makes it difficult to predict as certain water 
levels may result in multiple different flows (Figure 8 b). If backwater occurs the flow through the pipe 
will be higher when the receiving node is empty (typically in the beginning of a rain event), while less 
water will flow through the pipe if the downstream node is full. This will result in a bigger spreading 
between the two lines (Figure 8 b). Based on previous studies it is important to avoid backwater in links 
to obtain accurate imitations of flow.

relationship caused by backwater.  
If the spreading between the lines is quantified, the compartment delineation may be based on these 
values and connections can be placed in areas where backwater is not seen, and discharges are better 
imitated.  
3.2.2 CDS rain for backwater identification 
It was considered whether a Chicago Design Storm (CDS) rain time series could be used to simulate 
“pulses” through the system better than the staircase rain that is used to extract parameters. This will

capture different return periods to simulate the entire range of responses within the scope of the 
project. Thus, a designed rain series with 5 different CDS-rains has been used and is shown in Figure 9. 
According to “regionale regnrække regneark” by Spildevandskomiteen, (2012) the minimum reliable 
return period is 0.4 years and therefore this is chosen as the lowest return period. As drainage systems 
are dimensioned to have water occurring on terrain at 10-year return periods this is chosen as the 
maximum rain series (Butler et al., 2018). 5 rain series are chosen with return periods of 0.4, 1, 2, 5, and 
10 years respectively. The duration is set to 2 hours and the symmetry is set to 0.2 which means that the 
rain is skewed towards the right with a longer cooling down period than starting up. This symmetry is 
chosen to simulate a pulse response. 22 hours of dry weather is inserted between the rain event to 
allow the system to empty before the next event.

3.2.3 Quantification of backwater 
If an algorithm is to be based on the QH-relationship, a single value measure is required which can be 
computed in several ways. A literature search did not show any “best practices” when quantifying 
 
 
 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
backwater effects as was required in this case. Thus, two different approaches were tested to see which 
was more suitable: 
• 
Deviation from a line computed as an R2-value. 
• 
Maximum head difference at each flow point. 
In the first approach the deviation from a line is computed as a measure of how far the points are from 
an “ideal” line. The drawback of this method is that the “ideal” line is to be determined, which may not 
be trivial. Therefore, this method was not considered suitable as it did not capture the dynamics of 
backwater effects in all cases. For more details on the method and examples of where it did not identify 
the backwater effect properly see Appendix 9.4.  
The second approach for quantifying the backwater effects is based on computing the maximum head 
difference at a certain flow. If the points are located on a single line this difference will be small whereas 
a larger difference is seen for an ambiguous relationship. The designed CDS rain time series is used for 
this analysis. This is done as described below: 
• 
The flow is divided in 100 equally spaced batches.   
• 
If there are less than 10 samples in a batch, the batch will be merged with the neighboring batch 
until it contains at least 10 datapoints. 
• 
The minimum and the maximum water level is found in each batch. 
• 
For each batch the difference between the minimum and the maximum water level is found and 
multiplied with the width of the batch interval to compute the area of the rectangle covering all 
points in the batch (the orange boxes on Figure 11). 
• 
The sum of all rectangles is computed. 
• 
The sum is normalized by dividing with the total span in water level and total span in flow.  
This yields a number between 0 and 1 where 0 indicates that all points are on a perfect line whereas 1 
indicates that the variation is as big as possible covering the entire span in water level for all flows. 
Therefore, higher numbers indicate more backwater and splits are desired where the variation is as low 
as possible.

MU-files include as a minimum, results from a model simulation for training and validation periods that 
are used to extract parameters and evaluate the model respectively. Furthermore, a simulation with a 
designed CDS rain series is necessary for the analysis of backwater effects and stability check (see 
section 3.2.2).  
Compartment delineation and aggregation defines the connections and which nodes and links that are 
aggregated into compartments. The methods from section 3 will be used in this step.  
The parameters for the model define how water is “moved” between compartments based on volumes. 
This is based on the staircase rain simulation from which aggregated volumes and corresponding 
discharges are extracted.  
The actual execution of the model is based on the model engine available from Borup, (2018). The 
validation rain is shown in Figure 20 and covers 23 days from August 1st to August 23rd, 2019. The data is 
extracted from DMI (DMI, 2020). Hydrographs in selected connections and outlets are compared based

4.2 Hydraulic considerations 
Hydraulic premises and choices were outlined for the project. Among others, the actual outlets of the 
Mike Urban model are excluded, to avoid numerical instability. See Appendix 9.3 for an elaboration. 
Instead, the outlet is set at the receiving node of the outgoing weir.  
Secondly, the SM simulates dry weather flow as a constant value that is found from catchment 
properties assigned to each node in Mike Urban. Thus, an average value is estimated from each diurnal 
pattern.  
In the case area used for this project a few pumps are present. The most important are two pumps in 
the downstream section of the selected area. These pumps discharge water from the downstream 
storage basin to a WWTP outside the selected area. In the MU model the pumps are controlled by the 
water level in the WWTP with RTC.  
The WWTP is not included in the model and thus it is not possible to apply RTC even though the model 
engine is able to handle it. A model with RTC is run in MU to investigate the effects on pumps and is 
seen on Figure 21.

In the model with RTC the pumps turn on at a certain water level which could be translated into a

RTC is only implemented in these pumps in the selected case area. Since the pumps are the most 
downstream element of the selection, the impact of the simplification cannot be assessed. The amount 
of backwater downstream will however be reduced because water is not held back in the same manner 
from the WWTP. Hence, RTC is not included in the MU simulations used for this study. 
4.3 Network flow tracer 
When delineating and lumping the network a network flow tracer was implemented. This 
implementation resembles the Mike Urban backward and forward flow tracer and is an integral part in 
the network analyses presented in the project. It is used to move around the network and identify 
certain parts. Some functions in the flow tracer are modifications of functions found in NOAH Project 
Software Repository, (2020).  
From each of the starting nodes (most-downstream nodes) a backwards trace is used to “crawl” up 
through the network by identifying the upstream node or nodes for the links. This gives the possibility of 
inserting certain criteria for the algorithm to stop (e.g., based on differences in backwater effects) and 
thus create compartments based on this. When the algorithm reaches a stop criterion, the upstream 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
node can be assigned as the new starting point and the process can be repeated until the entire network 
has been investigated.   
Figure 22: Backwards flow trace from the most downstream outlet node in the system (left). Side branches with other outlets 
(right) are not selected and thus will be very small compartments, if not merged. 
If the backward trace reaches a node that was already investigated, it stops, to avoid assigning nodes to 
several compartments. This means that it is sensitive to the order of the starting points because the first 
one will define the “main stem” and the others will be “side branches” branching out from this seen on 
Figure 22. This however was not found to be an issue in practice because most splits in the network 
occur with just short side branches (e.g., at weirs from a basin). Furthermore, these small compartments 
must often be merged into the upstream bigger compartment to meet the demands for model stability.  
The method is sensitive to the assigned direction of the links in the network. In the case study this was 
however not found to be an issue but if the method is applied to other cases, it may require 
modifications.

4.4 Numerical stability of model 
Model stability is important to consider minor compartments containing a few nodes can cause 
numerical stability issues in the SM. The volumes in these are too small to proper compute the discharge 
from the compartments and these small compartments need to be merged into neighboring ones.  
The two main factors that affect this are the compartment sizes and the timesteps of the model. The 
issue occurs if too much water is discharged from a compartment compared to its stored volume. The 
stability is based on the VQ relation in equation 555555. If more water flows from a compartment 
during one time step than what is available, the model will crash, and the simulation will stop. The 
model may also show signs of numerical instability if the discharge through connections oscillates. This 
may be because the gradient of the VQ relationship is too large. The model overestimates the flow in 
one timestep and then underestimate it in the next, due to large volume changes. This does not force 
the model to crash but the resulting flows may not be accurate, and it should be considered whether 
additional compartments should be created.

4.6 Performance indicators 
To compare hydrographs suitable performance indicators should be selected. The performance 
indicators depend on the scope and application of the model and thus not one general score can be 
used for all purposes. The indicators are found in Bennett et al., (2013).  For the comparison, the results 
of the HiFi models are considered the true observed data. As the surrogate models are based on these 
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
results, they will not be able to capture the reality better than the HiFi models. This means that a 
“perfect” model that emulate the HiFi-results well may not necessarily describe the historical observed 
data. Data from flow sensors in sewers are although not flawless either and may have functional, 
technical, or operational constraints, which can cause data to be uncertain or even misleading (Alferes 
et al., 2013). 
For purposes within hydrological modelling the Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) is often used (Bennett et 
al., 2013). This compares the modelled result with the mean of the observed data and is shown in 
equation 9. A value of 1 indicates a perfect model that captures all dynamics of the observed data, while 
a value of 0 indicates a model with similar performance as using the mean as prediction. A negative 
value shows that the model is worse than using the mean. The measure both contains information on 
the mean and standard deviation of the compared time series individually as well as the correlation 
coefficient between them (Bennett et al., 2013) and is therefore widely used within hydrological 
modelling.

Similarly, the number of separate CSO-events can be computed as this is often desired to know when 
modelling CSOs. This disregards the magnitude of an event and simply computes whether a CSO occurs 
or not. This computation only counts the number of CSOs computed by MU and the SM and thus does

Critical Success Index (CSI) (Thrysøe et al., 2019) to consider both false negatives (missed) and false

used on the long test period (see section 7.1).  A “cool-down” period is needed before an observation is 
counted as a new event. No national standard is available for this, but a period of 6 hours is chosen for 
this project.  
Before the data are compared the time series are interpolated as they may have different time steps. 
Additionally, due to the principle of translation in lumped flow routing the exact time of peak is difficult 
to determine (Chow et al., 1988). For some applications the exact timing of the peak is not of 
importance and thus a moving average can be used for the comparison rather than just comparing the 
individual time points. Thus, if a sharp peak is shifted just a short time it will still be considered a good fit 
disregarding the time shift. For e.g., warning purposes the user may need to know when a peak reaches 
the WWTP, or when flooding occurs and thus the window should either be smaller or not considered at 
all. For computation of CSOs the timing is of less importance and therefore a moving average is 
reasonable to include. In this project a window of 20 minutes has been used and the NSE values 
obtained with this moving average is denoted NSE20.

5 Case study area 
The case study used in this project is a MU-model of Birkerød in the north-eastern part of Zealand with 
the primary recipient being Kajerød Å to the west, Sjælsø to the north and Furesø to the south. The 
model is provided by Krüger and is created by the utility Novafos and contains 4154 nodes, 4204 links 
with a total length of 157 km, and 8608 subcatchments covering 930 ha including 354 ha of impervious 
area. A map of the model is shown on Figure 24 below.

The area of interest in the catchment is the western part of the model, where CSO-structures discharge 
to Kajerød Å at 5 locations along the stream. A 6th CSO-structure is in the north and discharges directly 
to Sjælsø. The impervious area of the area of interest is 88.7 ha and has a total pipe length of 36.7 km. 
The selected area of interest is marked with yellow on Figure 24. The model includes a separate system, 
which is not included in the SM.  
A conceptual model of the area of interest is shown in Figure 25. It is seen how water is transported 
from south to north through a main pipeline that leads towards a wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) 
in the north-eastern corner of the area of interest. The 6 CSO-structures are seen along the channel 
along with the basins in the system and corresponding volumes.

In this project the downstream basin discharging directly to Sjælsø is referred to as B07, whereas this 
basin is really known as B06. The original B07 from the MU model is not included in the selected area; 
thus, this is not an issue. 
The case study is chosen primarily due to the many CSO-structures that makes it suitable to test

6.4 Implementing gradient-based flow in SM 
An alternative to the piecewise linear VQ parameter is a gradient based flow connection (denoted GB). 
This aims at describing backwater effects by estimating hydraulic heads in both upstream and 
downstream compartments and then describe the flow as a function of the change in head. This allows 
water to flow in both directions and may therefore be preferred in connections with high backwater 
effects. 
This connection type is implemented in Borup, (2018) but the effect has not been investigated 
systematically. This connection type may be better in certain situations, however, as more sets of 
parameters are needed this connection type makes the model slower and adds uncertainties to the 
model. The latter because the hydraulic head may be difficult to describe in compartments with many 
elements and large volumes. Gradient based flow cannot be implemented in the outlet weirs because 
the weirs do not discharge to a downstream compartment.  
The same calibration data as for the Piecewise linear VQ parameters are used based on a staircase rain, 
but this requires that hydraulic heads (water levels) are saved from the MU results as well (see Appendix 
9.1).

7.2 Usage of automatic surrogate model creation.  
The methods discussed in section 3 can be used to automatically create a SM that can imitate flows in 
selected links and outlets in an urban drainage network. Because a big focus has been on the automatic 
creation it should be easy applicable for usages in other case areas. Investigating another case area 
would however require an assessment of an optimal threshold of the split factor in the QH method, 
which likely will vary from case to case.  
The hope is that the procedure done in this project can automatically produce SMs without the need of 
any interaction. However, as was also stated manual interaction may be needed to improve the model 
results. The addition of a graphical user interface will make it even more accessible as this would also 
allow people who are not familiar with programing in Python to create and use the SMs. Overall, the 
fully automatic procedure can reach to results like the ones shown in section 6.1 with only one or a few 
input parameters. If the delineation methods are to be combined (as shown in section 6.3) or e.g., some 
connections should be transformed into gradient based connections (as shown in section 6.4) the 
procedure requires manual interaction.  
7.2.1 Applications of SM 
As described the developed surrogate models have several usages within urban drainage modelling. 
These include planning, RTC and forecast, parameter calibration, uncertainty analysis, etc. (Ledergerber 
et al., 2019; Meert et al., 2018; Wolfs et al., 2013). These are all topics where the computational time is 
important either because fast results are necessary or because many simulations are required that 
would not be feasible with slower HiFi models.  
In cases where RTC are used to control orifices, pumps etc., fast models can be used to efficiently 
optimize setups because this is often an iterative process (Pabst, 2019). In optimization of online RTC 
applications it can also be beneficial because different setups can be run with the starting point in the 
current situation. This allow the RTC scheme to be optimized for each individual situation (Pabst, 2019). 
The latter may also be linked to applications within forecast, where fast models with input obtained 
from radar forecasts can be used to estimate e.g., the discharge to the WWTP in the subsequent period 
(Thrysøe, 2021). Studies have shown successfully that RTC can be used to control emissions from CSOs 
to improve performance of a system (Vezzaro et al., 2014). Vezzaro et al., (2014) implements RTC in a 
simplified conceptual model to control CSO at key locations in a network. Similar investigations could 
potentially be conducted with the SM presented in this project.  
Fast conceptual models are also applicable for sensitivity and uncertainty analysis. This can be used in 
Monte Carlo simulations to get the probabilities of various flows rather than just the flow provided by 
the HiFi model. One example is to provide small variations in the rain input that can account for spatial 
variation that is not considered when the rain time series is derived from a rain gauge.  
The models can be used for parameter calibration because some of the parameters are derived directly 
from parameters in the MU model (Meert et al., 2018). One example is the runoff parameters where the 
impervious area and time of concentration are extracted directly from MU. Thus, it can be investigated 
how changes in these parameters affect the flow through the network and these can be adjusted to fit 
flows from observations. This is only possible if the model runs sufficiently fast because many 
==PAGE:66==
58 
 
simulations are often necessary. This however can only be done with parameters that can be translated 
back to the HiFi MU model.  
The same idea as for calibration is relevant when the models are used for planning. This also requires 
that the parameters can be led back to MU and are based on physical properties. This can e.g., be to 
investigate the effect of reducing the impervious area or to add storage capacity. An example on how 
this can be used to investigate the effect of added storage capacity is shown below.  
7.2.2 Example on planning application 
The SMs show potential in several usages within the urban drainage modelling. One example is within

7.3 Future work 
The SM setup and model engine (Borup, 2018) that is used in this project was first applied in Borup et 
al., (2017) and used in other studies with Thrysøe, (2021) being the most recent. Thus, several concepts 
have been developed and tested and some of them have been used and tested as a part of this project.  
In this study it was shown that delineating a drainage network automatically using the SM framework is 
possible. The results showed that the flow from some outlets could be simulated satisfyingly while 
others may require further delineation, such as a more advanced QH delineation or a manual 
delineation in key spots of the network. The delineation algorithm that is based on backwater effects 
only compares two links on each side of a node. A more complex delineation algorithm might improve 
the results and e.g., be able to find gradual increases or decreases in backwater. It could also be aimed 
at ensuring that the entire volume below the crest level of a weir is included in a compartment. To 
generalize the generation and usage of the surrogate models, the automatic SM setup should be tested 
on more case areas and in more studies to further validate the methods.    
In this project three different rain time series have been used: A staircase rain used for calibration, a 
combination of CDS-rains with different return periods used for computing backwater effects and 
stability check. And a historical rain time series to compare with MU and evaluate the performance of 
the model. As mentioned in section 2.5, Thrysøe et al. (2019) finds that the staircase rain can be used for 
calibration and obtain results similar as a continuous rain series. The advantage of the staircase rain is 
that it is easy to sample VQ points for the model parameters because steady state is reached. Also, this 
rain series is generic and can therefore be applied in many model setups without changing anything 
(except for the length of each timestep that should match the size of the network to ensure that steady 
state is reached). The combination of CDS rains might also be useful to use for model calibration. The 
staircase rain will always underestimate flows (as seen in 2.5) because the system is unrealistically full. 
This may be avoided by using the combined CDS rain. Furthermore, this will reduce the required number 
of simulations and files and thus simplify the model setup. The simulation of a staircase rain is short and 
is only required to be done once, but if the results are equally good or even better it may be worth 
investigating.  
The stability criterion used in this model setup means that the SM model can run even though some of 
the compartments are partly unstable with oscillating flows (see section 4.4). Using a more complex 
stability criterion may reduce these oscillations by moving the split further upstream or downstream or 
just merge the compartments on each side of the connection. This may mean that the model can have 
more compartments before it becomes unstable than what is achieved in this model setup. Applying a 
more complex stability criterion may also affect the computational speed of the models. The approach 
used in this project ensures that the models are running but some small compartments may force the 
model to run with small timesteps and therefore run slower than what could otherwise be achieved. As 
shown on Figure 17 in section 3.2.7 the method based on backwater effects may find several small 
compartments in the upstream part of the network that could be merged and improve computational 
==PAGE:69==
61 
 
time without affecting the predictions of discharges through the outlets in the downstream part of the 
network.    
In the study the flow simulations from Mike Urban are considered as the reality. The flow routing 
computed in the Mike Urban program however also has uncertainties, and as the SM is built on results 
from MU it can only be as precise as that.  
This model setup should not be viewed as an alternative to the accurate flow routing of Mike Urban. 
Instead, the model setup should be used as a supplement and is optimal for e.g., multiple simulation 
analyses and long-term simulations.  
7.3.1 Future improvement on the model setup 
The implementation of Real Time Control (RTC) can be used with the model engine and may be required 
to imitate many drainage networks. In this project this was neglected as only the two downstream 
pumps are controlled by RTC, and the sensor that controls the flow is located outside the selected area 
of interest. Thus, it would not be possible to replicate this control setup with the case area selected 
here.  
Another type of connection denoted as the “splitter” was implemented in other studies. This is applied 
as a connection where the volume is not big enough to create a stable connection and the discharge can 
be described as a function of the flow into the compartment rather than the volume inside the 
compartment. One example of where this could be beneficial is in the complex network around basin 5 
(see section 6.2.2.5) where it is assumed that the flow could be better described with this type of 
connection. This however was not tested in this project.  
Other improvements may be regarding the DWF that is just implemented as an average flow in the SM 
setup. To proper imitate the MU results the diurnal patterns could be considered and may improve the 
model. However, as the models are often used to investigate CSOs and flooding situations the changes 
in the DWF may be neglectable.  
It was experienced that the sampling of the extracted VQ-parameters has significant impact on the 
resulting flow from especially weirs. To mitigate the effects of these a longer staircase rain with finer 
increments in the intensities could be used. A second suggestion could be to automatically implement a 
linear interpolation, which was done manually in this study.

8 Conclusion 
The focus of this study was to investigate how automatic compartment delineation could be used for 
the creation of simplified surrogate models to imitate the discharge through CSO structures. The models 
are based on simulations of a Mike Urban model where different results are used for the parameter 
extraction, the delineation process, and the validation respectively.  
Automatic delineation resulted in NSEs ranging between 0.86 and 0.99 for 5 out of the 6 CSO structures 
in the case area. The remaining outlet, weir B05w1, showed worse performance, due to the complex 
hydraulic conditions. For the test period the NSE scores ranged from 0.82-0.99. The SM was found to be 
more than 10000 times faster than the Mike Urban model. In general, the discharges through links are 
better imitated than weirs. Firstly, because the dynamics around weir structures may be more complex, 
secondly because VQ-parameters for links follow a steadier increase than for weirs. Of the two 
investigated delineation methods, there was not found one method that was better suited for all 
outlets. The one based on backwater effects showed to produce models with more constant 
performance whereas the models created with the modified Louvain algorithm were more dependent 
on choice of parameters.  
It was illustrated that it is possible to further delineate manually to improve model performance. It was 
shown that specific areas in the network could be improved by combining graph partitioning and the 
method presented in this project as all NSE scores obtained from manual delineation was above 0.94. 
Some connections may be changed into gradient-based connections that allow water to flow in both 
directions and thus can be used in areas where backwater effects occur. In the model setup the 
gradient-based flow didn’t equivocally improve the performance of the SM. Furthermore, the piecewise 
linear volume discharge relations were found to adequately imitate the flow of Mike Urban. 
The suggested SMs has several potential usages within urban drainage modelling. A planning application 
is shown, and it is found that the SM imitates the results achieved from MU adequately (13% CSO 
reduction compared to 14 % in MU). Hence there is a potential to use these models for these types of 
applications.  
The following key conclusions were found: 
• 
Automatic delineation methods with just one parameter input can be used to create SMs. 
• 
The delineation method stated in this project can identify links of hydraulic importance with low 
backwater effects and suggest these as suitable connections. The Louvain algorithms do not 
take backwater into considerations but also show reasonable performance.  
• 
The presented automated model without further manual delineation was tested for a longer 
test period. The NSE scores ranged from 0.82-0.99.

9.2 How surrogate model computes discharge: 
Rewriting the continuity balance from equation 2 to: 
 
𝑑𝑆
𝑑𝑡= 𝐼(𝑡) −𝑄𝑜𝑢𝑡(𝑉) 
(13) 
 
In the model the continuity equation, defined in equation 2, is solved numerically by using the Runge-
Kutta 4th order numerical equation scheme. The time interval is broken down into 4 increments, where 
successive storage in a compartment and compartment discharge is found for each increment. The 
storage of a compartment is extended forward in time by these increments of the independent variable 
(V). The volume at a timestep is then defined as a weighted average of the four increments after which 
the corresponding discharge is found from the piecewise linear relation (Chow et al., 1988a).  
The model works with adaptive time steps, which means that the time step can go from 1 minute down 
to 1 second. The model will lower the timestep the change of volume is too large between increments. 
This is usually a result of a compartment volume being too small compared to the outgoing discharge.   
The model computes a resulting storage per timestep called a state vector that can be used to explain 
the dynamics of the model. The SM works with a constant dry weather flow which means that the 
model reaches steady state in this period.

9.4 Measure based on the R2-value 
To identify suitable connections the staircase rain is compared with the constructed CDS rain, and a R-
squared deviation is computed. If the flow simulations are identical (R2 = 1) no backwater is observed 
and the link is suitable as connection. If R2 is lower, the flow may not be described solely based on the 
volume in the upstream compartment and thus it is not suitable as connection. When we compute R2 
we need to interpolate and therefore we disregard some values that are not covered in the original 
timespan.  
The deviation is computed using the sum of squared as shown in the equation below (Brockhoff et al., 
2017)

∑(𝑦𝑜𝑏𝑠−𝑦𝑒𝑥𝑝)
2
∑(𝑦𝑒𝑥𝑝−𝑦𝑒𝑥𝑝
̅̅̅̅̅̅)
2 
Where 𝑦𝑜𝑏𝑠 is the data obtained from the synthetic rain series and 𝑦𝑒𝑥𝑝 is the expected values if the 
points form a single line. This means that an R2-value of 1 is a perfect fit with all 𝑦𝑜𝑏𝑠 being on the 
expected line while 0 or negative values indicate a bad fit. This is usually applied on linear regression but 
was attempted here on an “arbitrary line” because the criteria should evaluate whether the data form a 
single line disregarding the shape of this. It was considered whether an exponential or a power function 
should be assumed but visual inspection showed that the data formed many different lines and there 
was no obvious choice. The “arbitrary line” was therefore assumed to be the staircase rain as these 
often form a single line because the network is unrealistically full and thus the discharge is always in the 
lower end of the possible range, which is illustrated well on Figure 499 d. 
The R2-value was computed for all links in the network but did not see to be a suitable measure when 
the QH relationships were inspected visually. Four examples are shown in Figure 499.  
1. Figure 499 (a) shows an example on a good fit where the flows obtained from the two rains are 
similar and thus no backwater occurs. This obtains a high R2-value.  
2. Figure 499 (b) also obtains an acceptable R2-value, but this is lower than (a) due to the variation 
in the right side of the plot, showing that backwater occurs during higher return periods.  
3. Figure 499 (c) seems good visually but obtains a negative R2-value. This is caused by the many 
points   without any flow but with varying water level. This also obtains negative value because 
all flows are very low. 
4. Figure 499 (d) also obtains a high R2-value but clearly has backwater occurring also during lower 
water levels.  
The most plausible reason that the R2-value does not seem to describe the data well was to the 
inclusion of the mean of the expected data. This works well for linear relationships but is not suitable for 
arbitrary lines. Other possibilities could be to include only the sum of squared errors (SSE) or compute 
some variation of a RMSE measure. However, as the spreading seemed to be describable by an area the 
second approach was based on this.

9.7 Technical documentation on the Python code.  
File configuration  
The necessary files are read from different file formats obtained from Mike Urban (MU) and saved in 
pickle files that are a binary file format that can easily and quickly be read in Python. This ensures 
that the different files are only opened and read once.   
The files from MU are:  
• *.mex: Contains all the necessary information about the network.  
• *. Prf: Contains the results from the MU simulation. Used to get the flow and water 
level data in time series format.  
• *volume.txt: Contains the volume for all nodes or links respectively in timeseries data 
format.   
The following figures show how the functions for the file configuration is arranged.    
  
  
  
The functions shown in figure ??? are explained in the following:   
Count_network  
Counts the number of relevant elements (nodes, links, catchments etc.) in a network given by the 
*.mex file.   
Get_network_dataframes  
Generates 7 dataframes with components from the *.mex files with nodes, links, weirs, pumps, 
orifices, catchments and ModelA parameters respectively. These can be used later in defining 
compartments etc.   
Some data are converted to numeric but not all. This might be necessary at a later stage.   
ConvertPrfToCsv  
Small conversion function that converts the *.prf file to a *.csv file that can be read in python with 
the m11extra.exe program. A small batch file is written that runs the program and creates the csv 
file automatically.

Compute flows (convert_prf_file_to_pkl)  
Converts the *.csv created from the ‘ConvertPrfToCsv’ to a pandas dataframe with discharges from

them as a *.pkl.   
Compute flows with water levels (convert_prf_file_to_pkl)

Converts the *.txt volume files created in Mike View to a pandas dataframe. Nodes and links are in

Model setup  
  
Compute_elements_in_network  
Takes all the dataframes read from the *.mex file and merges them into a single dataframe with only 
the columns that are required in future computations for the nodes and links selected in the *.mus 
file.   
Create_compartments  
Creates a dictionary with compartments with nodes, couplings, and catchments. Connections are not 
considered in this function. Input is the dataframe with the network information and a dataframe

a plot to visualize the compartment delineation.   
Check nodes overlap in compartments   
Checks if some nodes are in multiple compartments.  
Add connections   
Adds connections and the compartments if couplings are between different compartments or 
outlets if they leave compartments.  
Compute VQ parameters and add to connections  
Creates volume and flow steady-state points for each Connection from *.txt files containing nodes 
and links volume and flows in links. The points are added to connections.   
Writing *.prm - WritePRMfunc:  
Writes the *.prm file that the model engine (DtuSMmodel) uses.

Three approaches to define the compartments are available  
Define splits form QH analysis  
Based on the variations (quantification of backwater effects) links that are suitable as connections 
are defined.    
Get downstream nodes  
All downstream nodes are identified and used in the network trace algorithms.   
Use suggested splits  
Based on the defined splits a network trace algorithm moves though the network from the most 
downstream nodes and cuts the network where the suggested splits are.   
Remove non suggested splits  
As the algorithm mentioned above sometimes creates splits that are not in the suggested splits (i.e., 
due to loops in the network) the   
create_nodeMap_from_mus 
  
This creates individual compartments from different *.mus files in a directory. All files in a directory 
are considered and overlaps between compartments are not checked for in this step (but is checked 
later in the model setup).   
Apply algorithms from Dobson et al. (2021)  
The partitioning algorithms available from Dobson et al. (2021) can be used to split the network into 
compartments.  
Subdivide compartments with Dobson et al. (2021)  
The compartments created with either of the three approaches can be further subdivided using the 
partitioning algorithms from Dobson et al. (2021). This step is optional.

Stability check  
The stability check is based on the same procedure as the overall model setup (i.e., create 
compartments with connection and run the model). The “check” lies in that if the model is not able 
to run the compartments that cause the model to crash is merged to create bigger compartments. 
This way the model becomes more stable. A different rain period than the one used for validation of 
the model is used.    
  
Execute model  
  
Initialize model  
Initialize model from the *.prf file.  
AddAllLinksAsOutputs:  
Add connections and outlets to DtuSMmodels output variable. Also returns a list of the connections 
and outlets. The functions also need input from compartments structured in the Compartment 
class.   
Set additional flow per unit   
Adds any additional flow to the model. This includes dry weather flow and pollutants if these are 
considered.   
Subtract initial loss  
Reads rain data and subtracts initial loss.  
Set rain data for all catchments  
Adds rain data to the catchments in the surrogate model.

Run model.

Compute performance indicators   
Compares the flow computed from the surrogate model with the flow computed by the Mike Urban
