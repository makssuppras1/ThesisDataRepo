Abstract 
CAR T cell therapy is an emerging field within cancer therapy and several CAR T cell therapies have 
been approved by the FDA to treat haematological malignancies. However, 50% of patients with B-
cell lymphoma, who have had a complete response, will relapse, whereas 80% of those who relapse 
will be due to an insufficient persistence of the CAR T cells. 
The aim of this thesis is to examine a novel CAR antigen coated nanoparticle. The nanoparticle should 
be serum compatible and optimized for in vivo stimulation. Moreover, the nanoparticle could contain 
both antigens and cytokines, which are needed to induce strong CAR T cell activation and 
proliferation. For this, the activation ability of different nanoparticles was examined along with the 
serum compatibility. Moreover, several CAR T cells expansions were performed where the CAR T 
cells were stimulated with different nanoparticles. 
An antigen scaffold containing antigens and cytokines was optimized and used as a positive control 
to examine the NP. The effects of the CAR T cells produced by the antigen scaffold was shown to be 
more effective than CAR T cells stimulated with cytokines and irradiated Jeko-1 cells. The results 
demonstrated that some NPs are compatible in human serum and able to stimulate CAR T cell 
expansion. Furthermore, it was found that the addition of cytokines on the NP increased the activation 
and that NPs containing a linker made the antigens and cytokines more accessible for the CAR T 
cells, thus improving the stimulation. Moreover, an NP was used to activate T cells and their 
phenotypic traits were compared to T cells activated by the FDA approved activations, finding these 
to be quite similar. 
Additionally, two CAR constructs were examined, whereas both constructs behaved in the same 
manner in the form of cell expansion, transduction efficiency, and phenotypic analysis. The CAR 
construct containing the costimulatory domain CD28 were shown to be more efficient than a CAR 
construct containing the costimulatory domain 4-1BB, when exposed to high ratios of Jeko-1 cells.

Introduction 
T cells play a crucial role when eradicating cancer cells. For the immune system to activate T cells 
against cancer cells, the cancer specific antigens must be distinguishable from the self-antigens and 
be presented on the major histocompatibility complex to the T cells by the antigen presenting cells. 
However, this is not always possible, which will lead to a growth of the cancer cells.  
Immunotherapy was developed to improve the efficacy of the above stated process as immunotherapy 
trains the immune system to recognize and fight a specific target cell. Chimeric antigen receptor 
(CAR) T cells is a type of immunotherapy that has revolutionized the treatment for several types of 
haematological malignancies. There are currently several different types of CAR T cells approved for 
treatment of haematological malignancies, and the CAR T cell treatment has a high rate of complete 
eradication of the cancer cells.  
However, cancer patients always have a risk of relapsing. Therefore, it is important that the 
administered CAR T cells has the optimal phenotype to avoid exhaustion, be functional and persist 
in the patient, and thereby lower the risk of relapsing. Moreover, it would be beneficial if the CAR T 
cells could be boosted every now and then, to keep them active and persisting in the patient.  
The immune system 
The immune system is needed to protect the host, and it patrols the body to detect and eliminate a 
variety of pathogens, herein cancer cells. The immune system can be divided into the innate immune 
system and the adaptive immune system. The innate immune system is encoded in the germline, 
which means that we are born with this immune system, and it can react very fast against pathogens, 
thus it is the first to be activated. The innate immune system is very versatile and contains many 
different cell types. However, the innate immune system has a non-specific recognition and no 
memory cells, meaning that the innate immune system is unable to recognise all pathogens. Therefore, 
the patient can get reinfected multiple times during their lifespan and due to the lack of memory cells, 
the immune system will have to start from scratch to eradicate the pathogen1. The innate immune 
system can, nonetheless, activate the adaptive immune system, which evolve throughout the lifetime 
of the host. The adaptive immune system includes two cell types, which are the T cells and the B 
cells. The T cells drive the cell mediated immunity; and the B cells drive the humoral immunity by 
secreting antibodies. The adaptive immune system has a specific recognition and generates long term 
memory cells against their target. The efficiency and the specificity of the T cells makes them well 
suited to fight a wide range of diseases, herein cancer1.

T cells 
T cells is a versatile type of immune cells, and their development starts in the bone marrow, 
whereafter they will migrate to the thymus to be matured. Inside the thymus, the T cell receptor (TCR) 
will undergo a range of mutations, which leads to a broad TCR repertoire. This repertoire should not 
recognise the host’s own cells, thereby necessitating the maintenance of central tolerance to avert 
potential T cell-induced toxicity2. To ensure this, the T cells has to undergo positive and negative 
selection. The positive selection is needed to ensure that the T cells can recognise and bind to the 
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) and as such, T cells that do not recognise the MHC 
molecules are eliminated. To prevent autoimmunity, negative selection is needed to eradicate T cells 
that exhibit excessive binding to self-MHC peptides1–3. When the T cells has fully matured in the 
thymus, they are called naïve T cells and are committed into either CD4+ or CD8+ lineage, and will 
migrate to the lymphoid tissue4,5.  
To activate the naïve T cells, antigen presenting cells (APC), such as dendritic cells (DC) are needed. 
The APC will phagocytose the cancer cell, break it down and present the peptides on either MHCI or 
MHCII molecules. MHCI molecules will activate CD8+ T cells, whereas MHCII molecules will 
activate CD4+ T cells. Once the peptides are loaded onto the MHC molecules, the APC will migrate 
to the lymph node (LN) to activate the naïve T cells. For the APC to activate the T cell, APC must 
provide three different signals to the T cell1,3,6.  
The first signal is the interaction between the TCR and the peptide:MHC molecule on the APC. The 
second signal is binding between the costimulatory molecules CD28 present on the T cell, and 
CD80/CD86 present on the APC. The third signal is cytokines excreted from the APC; the cytokines 
are needed to guide the differentiation of the T cells3,6,7. An illustration of the T cell activation can be

seen in Figure 1. Once the T cells have been activated, they will migrate to the target tissue, where 
they will start searching for and eradicate their target cells1.

Phenotypes of T cells 
The T cells has different functionalities and different phenotypes. Upon activation, CD8+ T cells will 
turn into cytotoxic T cells (CTL), which are the most effective anti-cancer immune cells3,4. Upon 
antigen recognition, the CTL will form an immunological synapse from where the CTL can secrete 
perforin and serine protease granzymes. Perforin creates pores in the target cell, leading to uptake of 
the serine protease granzymes, which will induce cell death of the target cell3,8,9. Furthermore, the 
CTL express the Fas ligand (FASL) which will bind to the Fas receptor on the target cell, which also 
induces target cell death3.  
CD4+ T cells will upon activation differentiate into different cell types based on the needed immune 
response and the secreted cytokines from signal three. CD4+ T can divide into different T helper (Th) 
cells, such as Th1, Th2, Th17, and Regulatory T cells (Treg)4. 
Both CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells can differentiate into different subtypes of cells upon activation 
in the LN, which have different characteristics and different functions. This includes effector T cells, 
memory T cells and exhausted T cells10.  
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
The effector T cells (TEFF) search for their target cells and secrete cytotoxic molecules or effector 
cytokines to eradicate the target, however these cells are short lived. Therefore, to obtain resistance 
toward the target cell later in life, T memory cells are needed10.  
There are different types of memory T cells; stem memory T cells (TSCM), central memory T cells 
(TCM), effector memory T cells (TEM), and tissue residential memory cells (TRM). TSCM can self-renew, 
are able to generate other types of T cells, herein TEM. TCM contain the LN homing molecules and 
have an enhanced ability to replicate but a limited effector function. TRM are unable to recirculate the 
blood system, as their purpose is to stay at the target and monitor against local reinfection. Moreover, 
they have an enhanced cytotoxic effect which can be used to attract and activate other immune cells. 
TEM can migrate to the peripheral tissues, due to their adhesion molecules. Moreover, they contain 
chemokine receptors and are more cytolytic than the other memory T cells10.  
One last type of memory T cells are terminally differentiated effector memory (TEMRA) T cells, which 
have upregulated the Fas ligand but has a reduced expansion11,12. To prevent damage on normal tissue, 
the T cells can turn into exhausted T (Tex) cells. This phenomenon can arise when there is a chronic 
infection, herein cancer. Once a T cell has become exhausted, their effector function is decreased10,13.

Cytokines impact on T cells 
Cytokines are proteins that affect the interactions between cells and has a high impact on how T cells 
behaves. There are different kind of cytokines, where interleukins (IL) are cytokines produced by a 
single leukocyte, which will affect other leukocytes, one of these being IL734. One of the main 
functions of IL7 is to increase the T cell proliferation of CD8+ T cells. Furthermore, IL7 upregulates 
anti-apoptotic molecules, thereby improving the survival of memory T cells and naïve T cells 34,35  
Another example hereof is IL15, which improves the proliferation, effect, and survival of memory 
CD8+ T  cells, in an antigen independent manner. Both IL7 and IL15 are necessary for maintaining 
the proliferation of CD4+ T cells in normal conditions35,36. 
IL2 is known to stimulate T cell growth and it is necessary for the T cells’ survival and proliferation 
as well as the generation of memory and effector T cells. However, continuous stimulation by IL2 
promote the generation of Tex cells, which have been observed within tumours37. Moreover, studies 
have indicated that IL2 contributes to preserving Treg cells and altering TEFF into Treg cells. Thus, IL2 
can suppress the anti-tumour effect38. Studies indicate that IL21 restrain the generation of Treg cells 
and decrease the accumulation of Treg cells in the tumour, however IL21 by itself is insufficient in 
maintaining cell proliferation. Nonetheless, studies have shown that IL21 can be used in combination 
with IL2 to lower the amount of Treg cells and enhance the generation of CD8+ T cells38.  
Cancer immunotherapy 
Cancer is a major problem worldwide with more than 277 subtypes of cancer and in 2020, more than 
19 million were diagnosed with cancer and almost 10 million cancer patients died39–41. The current 
standard treatments include chemotherapy and radiotherapy, which is non-specific towards the cancer 
cells, thus leads to a range of different side effects and elimination of healthy tissue. These treatments 
are harsh for both the patient body and mentality40. As a response, an emerging treatment method 
against cancer is immunotherapy. Immunotherapy boosts and trains the immune system to be able to 
eradicate the cancer cells42. 
As mentioned, there are some requirements that needs to be fulfilled for the immune system to 
generate specific T cells against tumour cells. Genetic mutations can lead to alterations in the 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
produced proteins. The alterations in the protein peptide can be presented by MHC molecules as 
cancer neoantigens. However, mutations in the peptide must be able to bind and be presented by the 
MHC molecules on the APC. For the peptides to bind to the MHCI molecule, they need to, among 
others, be between 8-11 amino acid (aa) long, and for binding to MHCII, the peptides must be between 
13-25 aa long43,44. Thus, if the peptide alterations cannot be presented on an MHC molecule, the 
adaptive immune system cannot be activated against the cancer cells. 
To overcome this hurdle, immunotherapy can be used to activate the immune system against specific 
target cells. Adoptive T cell therapy (ACT) is a type of immunotherapy that involves medical use of 
T cells. There are three types of ACT, 1) tumour infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL), 2) T cells with 
genetically engineered TCR, and 3) Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) modified T cells. TILs are 
when T cells are harvested directly from the tumour, expanded ex vivo, activated and reinfused in the 
patient. However, one requirement of this strategy is that the immune system must be able to activate 
tumour specific T cells. The other two methods involve genetically engineered T cells, which do not 
require existing tumour specific T cells. The two methods are quite similar, and both require isolated 
T cells, which can be obtained from the patients’ blood. Subsequently, the isolated T cells are 
genetically modified, for example by viral insertion. The insertion can either be a tumour specific 
TCR or a CAR, resulting in a T cell genetically engineered TCR or a CAR T cell, respectively9.  
CAR T cells can be directed towards targets that are not presented by MHC molecules, and currently 
the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has approved six CAR T cell therapies for treating 
haematological malignancies9,45. Most of the approved CAR T cell therapy has been targeting CD19, 
however other targets for B cells arises, such as CD79b45,46.

Chimeric antigen receptor T cells 
The CAR is a hybrid of an endogenous TCR and an antibody, containing four elements: 1) the 
extracellular domain, 2) the hinge region, 3) the transmembrane domain, 4) the intracellular 
domain16,47, and it is illustrated in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2: The CAR receptor incorporated in the T cell membrane. The ScFv region recognises and binds to antigens. The 
transmembrane domain holds the CAR receptor in place. The intracellular domains are needed for the activation of the CAR T cell. 
The figure is created in biorender.com. 
The extracellular domain recognizes and binds to the antigens. The extracellular domain is a single-
chain variable fragment (ScFv) region from a monoclonal antibody, which is composed of a heavy 
and a light chain of an antibody16,47,48. This leads to a specific antigen recognition and binding in an 
MHC-independent way, compared to endogenous T cells, thus new antigen targets become 
available47. 
The hinge region is an extracellular part of the receptor that separates the binding region from the 
transmembrane domain. The hinge region provides and improves the stability, efficiency, and activity 
of the CAR as well as providing the optimal distance and flexibility, ensuring that the CAR can either 
reach the membrane-proximal antigens or membrane-distal antigens obtained by long and short hinge 
regions, respectively16. 
The transmembrane domain spans the cell membrane by hydrophobic alpha helixes. The main 
function is to attach the CAR to the cell membrane. The function of the transmembrane domain has 
not been extensively researched and is thus not fully understood; however, studies indicate that it 
could be important for the function of the CAR T cell, as they could be involved in the synapse 
formation16.  
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
 
Figure 3: An illustration of the four generations of CAR T cells. The first generation with only the CD3ζ domain, the second 
generation with one costimulatory domain, the third generation with two costimulatory domains and the fourth generation with the 
delivery of specific payloads, here illustrated as cytokine release. The figure is made in biorender.com. 
The intracellular signalling domain varies in the different generations of CAR T cells, which is 
illustrated in Figure 3. The first generation of CAR T cells had one intracellular signalling domain,  
which was the CD3ζ domain of an endogenous TCR. The CD3ζ domain contains 3 immuno-tyrosine 
activation motifs (ITAMs), which are needed for activation9,16. However, this first-generation CAR 
T cells had a limited signalling capacity, resulting in insufficient priming of the resting T cells. To 
improve the activation of the CAR T cells, the second generation of the CAR T cell was engineered, 
which had the addition of a costimulatory domain, which enhanced both the survival and the 
expansion of CAR T cells 47. The most common costimulatory domains are molecules from the CD28 
family, herein CD28 or ICOS, or the tumour necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) family, herein 4-1BB 
or CD27. The most used costimulatory domains are CD28 and 4-1BB16.  
The costimulatory domain CD28 utilises a pathway that recruit Grb2, Lck, and Itk to its intracellular 
trail, resulting in a high IL2 production. The signal pathway of the TNFR members recruits TNF 
receptor-associated factor (TRAF) proteins, which are involved in the persistence, proliferation, and 
differentiation of T cells16.  
Both CD28 and 4-1BB has shown similar responses when used in clinical trials to treat haematologic 
malignancies, but their persistence varied. After three months, the CD28 based CAR T cells could 
not be detected, but they had a rapid T cell proliferation, activation, cytolysis, and increased 
glycolysis. On the other hand, the 4-1BB based CAR T cells can persist in the patient for several 
years but has a slower response. 4-1BB will especially increase the persistence of CD8+ T cells and 
has a higher ratio of memory cells. Thus, CD28 based CAR T cells can be useful for a rapid 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
elimination of the tumour, whereas 4-1BB is particularly useful when a long persistence is needed to 
treat the disease16,49.  
The functions of the different costimulatory domains can be combined by adding two costimulatory 
domains in one CAR T cell. These CAR T cells are the third generation CAR T cells16,47. The third 
generation CAR T cells typically contains one costimulatory domain from the CD28 family and the 
TNFR family and have shown to have an increased effect, but they are often expressed at lower 
levels16.  
The fourth generation of CAR T cells is T cells redirected for universal cytokine-mediated killing 
(TRUCKs). TRUCKs can deliver specific payloads upon activation due to further genetic 
modifications of the CAR47.  
Mechanism and limitation of CAR T cells 
Once the CAR T cell recognises the target antigen, an immunological synapse will form, where the 
antigen binds to the ScFv region of the CAR. This antigen binding will initiate a signal cascade 
initiated by the costimulator and the CD3ζ domain. The signal cascade from the CD3ζ domain will 
involve a phosphorylation of ITAMs, which will lead to a potent signal one activation. The signal 
cascade from the costimulatory molecules will enable a potent signal two, which is needed for an 
effective CAR T cell activation, leading to killing of the target9.  
Once the CAR T cell is activated, it will start proliferating and secreting inflammatory factors, which 
will activate other immune cells and thus increase the immune response towards the target50,51. 
However, studies indicate that the CAR T cells needs over a hundred times more antigen recognition 
to induce activation, as compared to endogenous T cells52.  
Even though CAR T cell therapy has shown good results, leading to current approval of six CAR T 
cell-based therapies, there have been observed some severe side effects after patients are exposed to 
CAR T cell therapy. The most common side effect for the CAR T therapy is cytokine release 
syndrome (CRS), which is caused by the high amount of released cytokines initiated by the CAR T 
cell activation47,53. Patients with CRS have an increased level of different cytokines which, among 
others, can lead to fever, hypoxia, organ dysfunction, and hyper-inflammatory disorder. CRS can 
have fatal outcomes and is therefore important to treat, for instance by administering anti IL647,53.  
Another common side effect is immune effector cell–associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS). 
ICANS can lead to different neurological disorders, such as aphasia, ataxia, and delirium, meaning 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
that patients become disoriented, unable to communicate and to control coordination. ICANS can 
occur simultaneously with or independently from CRS47.  
CAR T cells will eradicate every single cell containing the target antigen. The CAR T cell may have 
on-target off-tumour activity, which results in the eradication of healthy tissue9,54. This can for 
instance be seen in the treatment of leukaemia or lymphoma patients with CD19 CAR T cells. Here, 
the CAR T cells also target the healthy B cells, thus requiring the patients’ to be administered 
immunoglobulin55,56. Another on-target off-tumour effect is when the CAR T cells recognise antigens 
that are not the target antigen. This phenomenon is called cross-reactivity and causes eradication of 
both cell types, which can be fatal54.  
Even though CAR T cells can recognise antigens in an MHC-independent manner, there are some 
limitations with the therapy. Approximately 50% of the patients with a complete response after CAR 
T cell therapy against B-cell lymphoma will relapse. There are different reasons why the patient 
relapses, as patients can either have an antigen-negative relapse or antigen-positive relapse45,57. 
Cancer cells are extremely adaptable, meaning that new mutations in the cancer cells can arise, 
leading to an antigen loss and thus making the CAR T cells useless, which induces an antigen-
negative relapse45,57. Furthermore, the CAR T cells can have a poor persistence and an unfavourable 
phonotype, such as exhausted CAR T cells, which will decrease the activation and persistence of the 
CAR T cell and can lead to an antigen-positive relapse45,57. Studies indicate that approximately 80% 
of those who relapse will have an antigen-positive relapse58. Furthermore, the CAR T cells have 
shown to have a poor infiltration, activation and persistence when used against solid tumours59.  
Production of CAR T cells 
CAR T cells are produced by the insertion of the CAR sequence into the T cells. There are different 
methods which can be used to insert the CAR sequence, such as viral transduction or the use of 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) Cas960,61. There are different 
steps required to produce the CAR T cells and is illustrated in Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4: An overview of the CAR T cell production process. Firstly, collecting immune cells and isolating T cells. Then, T cell

Firstly, a peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) is needed. This is often done from a whole 
blood sample, or by performing a leukapheresis procedure, where the patients’ blood is removed, then 
the leukocytes are separated, and the remaining blood is re-infused to the patient. If a whole blood 
sample is acquired, density gradient centrifugation is used to separate the mononuclear cells from the 
red blood cells. Afterwards, the T cells can be isolated from the PBMC60,61. 
The T cells are then activated to increase the expression of low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDL-
R), which is essential for the lentiviral transduction of the T cells60–62. The T cell activation can be 
done using immobilized CD3/CD28 antibodies or soluble anti-CD3 antibodies. The CD3/CD28 
antibodies can be immobilized on a culture flask or Paramagnetic beads, such as Dynabeads60,61. 
Dynabeads leads to a better activation of the T cells than the soluble antibodies and the culture flask 
with immobilized antibodies. However, the Dynabeads needs to be removed before the CAR T cells 
can be infused into patients60,61.  
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
Antibody coated nano beads, such as TransAct, can also be used to activate T cells. TransAct are a 
polymeric nano matrix, which is conjugated to CD3/CD28 monoclonal antibodies, and is 
biodegradable, meaning that there is no need for pre-infusion removal. Up until now, Dynabeads and 
TransAct are both approved to produce CAR T cells in the clinic, however, Dynabeads are more 
commonly used61,63. During activation, cytokines, such as IL2, is added to increase the cell 
growth60,61. In the initial stage of the activation, the CAR sequence can be inserted to the T cells, and 
thereafter the CAR T cells are grown to obtain sufficient cells for patient infusion60,61. 
T cell modification  
The clinic currently employs viral transduction methods to create CAR T cells. Typically, genetic 
modifications are introduced using lentiviral and gamma-retroviral vectors, allowing for a permanent 
insertion of the sequence, and thereby ensuring long-term gene expression. However, viral 
transduction does not offer precise control over the insertion site and quantity61. Nonetheless, 
lentiviruses tend to integrate the sequence in a semi-random pattern, avoiding insertion in the start 
regions of active genes, which mitigates the risk of genotoxicity. Consequently, lentiviral vectors are 
considered safer than gamma-retroviral61. The plasmids encoding the CAR sequence are used to 
transfect human embryonic kidney (HEK) 293 cells. The transduced HEK cells are used to grow the 
virus needed to modify the T cells61. 
Another method that can be used for T cell modification is CRISPR Cas9, which is an RNA guided 
nuclease that is derived from the microbe S. pyogenes. The nuclease Cas9 contains a 20-nucleotide 
guide RNA, which will bind to the target DNA through Watson-Crick base pairing, whereafter the 
Cas9 will induce a double-strand break (DSB) at target site61,64.  
After the DSB, the DNA attempts to self-repair in one of two ways: nonhomologous end joining 
(NHEJ) pathway or homology-directed repair (HDR) pathway. NHEJ utilizes the endogenous DNA 
repair machinery to rejoin the DNA strands, often resulting in insertions/deletions in the DNA strand 
that can lead to a gene knockout (KO). However, if there is a repair template, referred to as donor 
DNA, in proximity, the DNA can be repaired by the HDR. This donor DNA can be a double-stranded 
or single-stranded DNA template with homology arms matching the target sequence61. An illustration 
of the function of CRISPR Cas9 can be seen in Figure 5. The HDR often leads to knock in (KI) of 
genes, but it occurs in a lower frequency than NHEJ64. This technique allows controlled placement 
of the CAR sequence, ensuring the insertion of only one CAR sequence. Strategically placing the 
CAR sequence can produce off-the-shelf CAR T cells by disrupting the endogenous TCR sequence 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
and thereby replacing it with the engineered CAR construct. This effectively generates allogeneic 
CAR T cells61.

Biomaterials for specific T cell stimulation 
Different biomaterials, such as particles or scaffolds, have been studied to achieve an antigen-specific 
T cell stimulation. For both particles and scaffolds, the ligand choice and density can be customized 
to achieve an optimal stimulation. However, there are some differences; particles mimic the APC 
activation of the T cells, whereas the scaffolds enable a more accurate and easier way to design 
signalling molecules. Moreover, the biophysical properties of the scaffolds can be controlled by 
altering the porosity and stiffness. For both particles and scaffolds, their size, shape, material, and 
choice of ligand will affect the stimulation of the specific T cells65. This thesis focuses specifically 
on nanoparticles (NP) and antigen presenting scaffolds (ag-sc). 
Nanoparticles 
NP is a rising area within drug delivery and is characterized to have a size between 10-100 nm. They 
have some unique features compared to other particles, for instance a larger surface to mass ratio and 
an ability to deliver specific compounds to their targets66. Moreover, it is possible to manipulate the 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
shell of the NP, and thereby add modifications and different functionalities. This means that the NP 
have a more specific drug targeting and delivery, which reduces toxicity and improves safety66,67. 
To address toxicity concerns, considerable research is dedicated to biodegradable NPs, as they do not 
accumulate in the body66,67. One extensively studied biodegradable polymer is dextran. Dextran is a 
polysaccharide that is primarily composed of 1,6-glucosidic linkages. Due to its hydroxyl groups, the 
dextran can be manipulated with biomolecules; for instance, coating dextran NPs with antibodies 
enables precise NP targeting67,68. Click chemistry serves as an effective method for attaching 
molecules onto the dextran69. Additionally, dextran exhibits slow degradation by human enzymes70. 
NPs can be coated with polyethylene glycol (PEG), which can increase the efficiency and delivery of 
the NP and increase the circulation time of the NP. The PEGylation of NPs are widely used, as PEG 
is generally considered safe by the FDA. The size and concentration of the PEG can be controlled 
and vary based on its functional requirements71. 
Antigen presenting scaffolds 
Ag-sc is a compound that serves to activate CAR T cells in an antigen-specific manner. The 
compound contains a dextran backbone that is embedded with streptavidin molecules, and it allows 
for the attachment of biotinylated antigens and cytokines, as well as costimulatory molecules. When 
the ag-sc is used for CAR T cell expansion, examples of cytokines that can be used are IL2 and IL21. 
The combination of the antigen and the cytokines will result in a specific and strong activation of the 
CAR T cells72,73. An illustration of the ag-sc can be seen in Figure 6.

Aim 
CAR T cells have been approved for several types of haematological malignancies. However, the 
CAR T cell treatment is not flawless. The phenotype of the CAR T cell is crucial for its function and 
its persistence, where a low persistence of the CAR T cell may lead to patient relapse. Currently, the 
developed ag-sc can be used to induce a strong antigen-specific activation of the CAR T cells. 
However, due to serum inactivation of the ag-sc, it is currently not applicable for in vivo activation 
of CAR T cells. 
Therefore, this master thesis aims to examine a novel CAR antigen coated nanoparticle that is serum 
compatible and optimized for in vivo stimulation. The nanoparticle could contain both antigens and 
cytokines, which are needed to induce strong CAR T cell activation and proliferation.  
This thesis will explore how the different antigen coated nanoparticles will influence the activation 
and expansion of CAR T cells in vitro. To evaluate this, a model system based on CD19-targeted 
CAR T cells will be used. The thesis has the following objectives:  
1. Evaluating variations of CD19 nanoparticles for activation and expansion of CAR T cells in 
vitro 
2. Activate and expand 4-1BB and CD28 costimulatory CAR T cells to compare their phenotype 
and effects.

Methods 
This section contains the methods used in this master thesis. 
Cell maintenance and counting 
The cells were cultured in R10 media, which comprises 89% RPMI 1640 Medium, 10% heat-
inactivated Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. The cells were incubated at 
37°, with 80% humidity and 5% CO2. The cells were frequently counted and split, to keep them 
healthy throughout the experiments. 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
To count the cells, they were resuspended and then 18 µl of the cells were transferred to a 96 well 
plate and 2 µl Acridine Orange/Propidium Iodide Stain were added and mixed. Then, 10 µl were 
added to a counting slide and analysed on LUNA-FL™ Dual Fluorescence Cell Counter.  
Thawing of cells and freezing of cells 
Cells were thawed by removing one vial from the liquid nitrogen and placed on dry ice. Then, 10 ml 
R10 media were added to a 15 ml tube and warmed. One ml of the warm R10 cell media were added 
to the cell vial, thus the cells were thawed. The thawed cell mix were transferred from the vial to the 
15 ml tube. Then, the tube was centrifuged for 5 min at 1.500 rpm, whereafter the cells were 
resuspended in R10 media in the wanted concentration and transferred to a cell culture flask. 
To freeze down cells, a freezing media was first prepared by mixing FBS and Dimethyl sulfoxide 
(DMSO) to obtain a 10% DMSO. Then, the cells were counted and spun down for 5 min at 1.500 
rpm. The supernatant was discarded, and the cells were resuspended in cold freezing media at the 
desired concentration. Then, 1 ml of the resuspended cells was added to a Cryo tube and placed in a 
Mr. Frosty Freezing Container in the -80˚C freezer. Once frozen, they were transferred to the liquid 
nitrogen. 
Amplification and purification of DNA plasmids 
DNA plasmids were amplified to maintain a stock of the plasmids, which could be used to produce 
CAR T cells. One Shot™ Stbl3™ Chemically Competent E. coli cells were used in the amplification. 
One vial of One Shot and the desired plasmid were thawed on ice. Next, 1 µl of the plasmid were 
gently mixed with the One Shot vial and incubated for 30 min on ice. Then, the vial was exposed to 
a temperature of 42˚C for 45 seconds, with the purpose of heat-shocking the cells, and were then 
placed in an ice bath for 2 min. Next, 250 µL of pre-warmed Super Optimal broth with Catabolite 
repression (S.O.C) media were added and incubated while shaken horizontally at 37°C for one hour 
at 225 rpm. Afterwards, 100 µL of the transformation mix were spread on a warm LB agar plate 
containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin and incubated at 37°C overnight, while the plate was inverted. The 
following day, one colony were picked to be inoculated in a mixture of 250 ml pre-warmed LB 
medium and 100 µg/mL ampicillin and incubated at 225 rpm at 37°C overnight. 
The plasmids were purified using the QIAGEN® Plasmid Plus Maxi Kit. The bacteria were harvested 
by centrifugation at 3.000 g for 10 min. The supernatant was discarded, and bacteria was resuspended 
in 5 ml P1 buffer. Hereafter, 5 ml of P2 buffer were added and mixed by inverting the tube until the 
lysate reached a visibly viscous state, followed by incubation for 3 minutes at room temperature. 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
Next, 5 ml of P3 buffer was added, and the tube was inverted to mix the solution. Then, the lysate 
was moved to a QIAfilter Cartridge and incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes. Afterwards, 
the lysate was filtered into a new tube. Then, 5 ml BB buffer was mixed to the lysate by inversion of 
the tube. Next, the lysate was moved to a QIAGEN Plasmid Plus spin column in a vacuum stand, and 
vacuum was applied until the liquid was removed. In the washing process, two buffers were added 
under vacuum, first being 0,7 ml ETR buffer, the second being 0,7 ml PE buffer. The DNA were 
centrifuged for 1 minute at 10.000 g to remove any remaining buffer. The QIAGEN Plasmid Plus 
spin column was put in an Eppendorf tube and 200 µL of EB buffer were added and set to incubate 
for 1 minute. Then, the tubes were centrifuged at 10.000g for 1 minute, to transfer the DNA from the 
QIAGEN Plasmid Plus spin column to the Eppendorf tube. Lastly, a Thermo Scientific NanoDrop 
1000 Spectrophotometer was used to measure the DNA concentration, and the DNA was stored in a 
-18°C freezer. 
Production and purification of lentiviruses  
The lentivirus was produced in HEK-293T cells, which has been transfected with the transfer plasmid, 
encoding the gene of interest, and three packaging plasmids: pRSV.REV, pMDLg/p.RRE, and 
pMD2.G.  
In the production, HEK-293 T cells, with ~80% confluency in a T150 flask, was used, and two 15 ml 
tubes were marked as tube A and tube B. In tube A, 4,6mL Opti-MEM I Reduced-Serum Medium 
and 129µL Lipofectamine 3000 were mixed. In tube B, 111µL p3000 reagent and 4,6ml Opti-MEM 
were mixed. Then, a master mix containing 18 µg of pMDLg/p.RRE, 7 µg of pMD2.G, 18 µg of 
pRSV.REV, and 15 µg of the transfer plasmid were slowly added to tube B. Next, the content of tube 
B were added drop by drop to tube A and incubated for 15-20 minutes at room temperature. Then, 
the HEK-293 cell media was removed, and tube A was slowly added over the HEK-293 T cells, and 
the cells were set to incubate for 15 minutes at 37°C, followed by adding 30 ml warm R10 media. 
The flask then incubates for 24 hours, whereafter the virus was harvested and subsequently 30 ml 
R10 media was added to the flask and incubates for another 24 hours, followed by another virus 
harvest.  
The harvesting was done by centrifuging the supernatant from the HEK-293T cells at 500g for 10 
minutes. Then, Lentix Concentrator were gently mixed with the supernatant in a 1:3 ratio and 
incubated at 4 °C for 30 minutes. Next, the sample was centrifuged for 45 minutes at 1.500 g at 4 °C 
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
followed by removal of the supernatant. The virus was resuspended in 300 µl Phosphate-buffered 
saline (PBS) and aliquoted into 50 µl samples and stored at -80°C.

thereby making a 3-fold dilution of the virus. 20.000 SUP-T1 cells were seeded in 8 new wells. 50 µl 
of the diluted virus was added onto the SUP-T1 cells and topped off with R10 media, to obtain a 
volume at 200 µl and the cells were set to incubate overnight at 37°C. The cells were spun down at

To produce the ag-sc, the amount of the cytokines and CD19 needed to each scaffold were calculated 
to obtain the chosen ratios, for instance 1:24:6:6 ratio of dextran:CD19:IL2:IL21. To ensure an evenly 
distributed and coated dextran, IL2, IL21 and CD19 were pre-mixed. To remove debris, the dextran 
was centrifuged at 10.000 g for 2 minutes. The dextran was added to the cytokine/CD19 mixture and 
incubated for 20 min on ice. 1 µl of D-Biotin were added and set to incubate for 20 min on ice.  
The filters were rinsed three times with 6 ml milli Q water, then 6 ml PBS were added, and the filters 
were spun down twice at 3.000 g for 5 min. Next, 5 ml PBS were added and topped off with the 
produced ag-sc. The filters were then spun down for 15 min at 3.000 g. The volume remaining in the 
filters were transferred to a tube, where PBS and 10% freezing buffer, which contains 5% PSA and 
50% glycerol, were added to obtain the desired volume. The scaffolds were frozen down for long 
term storage. 
Tetramer production 
The needed amount of CD19, florescence, PBS, and freezing buffer were calculated. The biotinylated 
CD19 and the streptavidin florescence were mixed in an Eppendorf tube for 20 min on ice. Then, 
PBS and 10% freezing buffer were added, and the samples were frozen for long time storage.  
Luciferase-based detection assay 
Jurkats cells were added for each condition in a 96 well plate. Then, the different amounts of 
stimulations were added to their respective well, whereafter they were topped off with the R10 media, 
to obtain a final volume at 200 µl. Dynabeads and Jeko-1 cells were used as positive controls. The 
plate was incubated overnight and then spun down at 1.500 rpm for 5 minutes and washed once with 
200 µl PBS. Next, 30 µl Cell Lysis Buffer and 30 µl milli Q water were mixed and added to each 
well. The plate was set to incubate at 37° for 37 min, transferred to a -80° freezer for 30 min and 
moved back to the 37° incubator for 37 min. Then, the plate was spun down for 10 min at 2.000 rpm.

Meanwhile, a new plate was set up, where 100 µl of Luciferase Assay Reagent was added to each 
well. Just before running the new plate, 20 µl of the supernatant from the spun down plate was added 
to the new plate and analysed by a Synergy HT Multi-Mode Microplate Reader. Then, the 
luminescence was measured for 10 seconds per well. The specific activation was calculated by the 
following equation. 
Specific activation (%) = 100 × (experimental data − spontaneous cell death)
(maximum cell death − spontaneous cell death)

Phenotypic and functional cell assays – Flow cytometry  
Flow cytometry is a method where single cells in a population is passed through multiple lasers that 
analyse their appearance and expression of different molecules. The molecules expressed on the cells 
can be stained, and thus the phonotypical and functional triads of cell populations can be analysed. 
The CAR constructs for the lentiviral transduction contains the green florescence protein (GFP). The 
flow cytometry data from the LSRFortessa™ flow cytometer was obtained using FACSDiva 
software, and the flow cytometry data were analysed using the software FlowJo. 
Compensation  
To avoid colours overlapping, a compensation is needed. When analysing a multicolour flow 
cytometry panel, a compensation needs to be run prior to the experiment. The compensation is needed 
to define the range of the different florescence colours. 
Compensation beads for each antibody were produced by mixing one drop of OneComp eBeads and 
1 µl antibody followed by incubation for 10 min and then resuspension in 200 µl PBS. As the 
lentiviral transduced CAR T cells contains GFP, there were also prepared compensation beads for 
this colour.  
To prepare Near Infrared (NIR) composition beads, an Amine Reactive Compensation bead kit was 
used. 100 µl PBS, one drop of ArC reactive beads and 1 µl NIR was mixed and incubated for 30 min 
on ice in the dark. Then, they were washed twice by centrifugation at 1.800 rpm for 5 min, and the 
supernatant was removed through pipetting. Then, one drop of ArC negative beads was added and 
incubated for 10 min, followed by resuspension in 200 µl PBS. 
Cell staining  
The 96-well plate containing the cells was washed by centrifugation at 1.500 rpm for 5 min, and the 
supernatant was discarded. Then, 5 µl of 1% dasatinib were added to the cells and incubated for 5 
min on ice. Then, the cells were resuspended in 20 µl PBS, and 2,5 µl of the tetramer was added to 
the samples and incubated for 15 min at 37˚C. Next, 0,1 µl NIR were added to the wells to stain for 
live/dead cells, and the needed antibodies were added and incubated for 20 min in the fridge. 
Hereafter, the plate was washed twice by centrifugation at 1.500 rpm for 5 min to remove excess 
antibodies/dye, and the supernatant was discarded. Subsequently, the cells were resuspended in 200 
µl FACS buffer. Next, the samples were analysed on a LSRFortessa™ flow cytometer. The antibodies 
used to stain the cells can be seen in Appendix 1.1-1.3.

Ag-sc analysis 
As mentioned, the ag-sc contains both an antigen and cytokines, and it is used to stimulate CAR T 
cells in the laboratory. In order to test the ag-sc and find the optimal ratio of ag-sc, a range of 
experiments were performed.   
Binding of Ag-sc antigen titration  
Ag-sc were produced with a PE-dextran and different ratios of CD19. The ag-sc were produced in 
the following dextran:CD19 ratios: 1:0, 1:8, 1:16, 1:24, 1:32, 1:40, and 1:48. To further examine the 
binding of the ag-sc when cytokines were added, four different cytokine ratios were added to three 
chosen CD19 ratios. The ag-sc were produced in the following dextran:CD19:cytokine ratios, 
1:CD19:0, 1:CD19:3, 1:CD19:6 and 1:CD19:12.  
50.000 CD19 Jurkats were spiked in SUPT1 cells in a 50:50 ratio and stained with 1 stim of the 
produced ag-sc and analysed by the flow cytometer. Stim is a measuring unit used for the ag-sc, where 
1 stim is 4 µl ag-sc per 106 cells. Each condition was run in technical triplicates. The mean 
fluorescence index (MFI) for each sample were calculated and subtracted with the MFI of an 
unstained control. The specific MFI for each ratio is plotted in Figure 7-A+B.

Afterwards the cells were stained in order to analyse the phenotypic trademarks. To make a 
phenotypic analysis, the cells were split into two, and one half were stained for memory receptors 
and the other half were stained for exhaustion receptors. The gating strategy is as follows: 
The gating strategy initially involved sorting for lymphocytes, followed by isolating single cells, then 
identifying alive cells, and finally selecting CD3 positive cells. The CD3 positive gate include all T 
cells in the population. From this gate, the analysis is conducted. For the CD3 positive gate, we can 
analyse for memory and exhaustion trademarks. 
The memory analysis is conducted by first gating for CD4/CD8 from the CD3 positive gate. Next, 
the CD3 positive gate is used to find the effector T cells by using the CD28 and CD27 receptors. 
CD28 is upregulated early in the activation, whereas CD27 is upregulated later. Therefore, this gate 
shows the early and the late effector T cells. Then, the memory cells can be analysed.  
From the CD3 positive gate, the presence of CCR7 and CD45RA are analysed, as these receptors 
indicate their survival ability and proliferation ability. T cells with a high amount of CCR7 and 
CD45RA are naïve-like T cells, whereas a high amount of CCR7 and a low amount of CD45RA are 
TCM. The naïve-like T cells can be further analysed based on the increased expression of CD28, 
revealing the true population of naïve-like T cells. Cells with a low expression of CCR7 and CD45RA 
are TEM, and cells with a low expression of CCR7 and high CD45RA are TEMRA. The TEM cells can 
be further analysed to find their effector function. This is done by dividing the population according 
to their CD28 and CD27 receptors, where the double positive population will be effector memory T 
cells. Furthermore, the TEMRA cells can be further analysed based on their upregulation of CD95 
revealing the true population of TEMRA cells. Lastly, the expression of CD127 and CD45RA were 
examined. 
The MFI for each analysed receptor is calculated and plotted and can be seen in Figure 25-A. The 
individual MFI can be seen in Appendix 2.9.2 and the FlowJo analysis can be seen in Appendix 2.9.3.

Discussion  
This section contains a discussion of the results gathered throughout this master thesis. 
Ag-sc 
In the beginning of the master thesis, the ratio of CD19 and cytokines were examined in order for me 
to become acquainted with the use and methods. As can be seen in Figure 7-C, the activation achieved 
by the 1:0 dextran are similar to the other CD19 ratios, which indicates that the increased CD19 ratio 
does not have a positive impact on the Jurkat activation. This could indicate that the cytokines is 
needed in order to achieve a strong activation. Even though Figure 7 indicates that the optimal ag-sc 
is based on a CD19 40 ratio, it is worth noting the streptavidin has four binding sites74. One binding 
site is occupied by the binding to the dextran, meaning that there are three binding sites for the CD19 
and the cytokines. Since the optimum of binding is achieved by the CD19 32 ratio, this indicates that 
the streptavidin is fully saturated at this ratio. This leads to the belief that the CD19 24 ratio is more 
optimal, as it leaves some free streptavidin binding sites for the cytokines. Thus, the CD19 24 ratio 
were chosen. For the cytokine ratio, the cytokine 6 ratio were chosen, as it obtained the highest 
amount of activation before it starts to drop. Thus, the ag-sc used for the rest of the experiments are 
produced with the dextran:CD19:IL2:IL21 ratio 1:24:6:6. This is also motivated by its prior use for 
experiments in the research group, where it has been shown to be successful. 
It can be seen in Figure 12-B that the total cell number for the cells administered ag-sc will decrease 
in the beginning of the expansion. However, the ag-sc leads to a high CAR T cell number, which can 
be seen in Figure 12-C. The decrease in the cell number when stimulated with the ag-sc could be 
explained by the survival and expansion of CAR T cells, as opposed to the non-survival of T cells 
due to lack of stimulation.  
Throughout the master thesis, different cytokine combinations were used to stimulate CAR T cells 
and T cells as a control for the ag-sc. Overall, it is observed that the total cell number is highest when 
cells are stimulated with IL7/IL15 and IL2/IL7/IL15, and the lowest cell numbers were obtained when 
only IL2/IL21 were added. Moreover, it was observed that the specific stimulation such as ag-sc and 
irradiated Jeko-1 cells increased the CAR T cell population, whereas the cytokines only increased the 
total cell number, which is consistent with expectations. 
As can be seen in Figure 14, the CAR T cells stimulated with IL2/IL7/Il15 are the ones that loses 
their effect first as they are the ones for which Jeko-1 cells exhibits most growth, followed by the 
==PAGE:65==
65 
 
IL7/IL15 stimulation. This indicates that the antigen-dependent stimulation results in a more 
favourable CAR T cell phenotype that can preserve their effect for a longer period. Similar results 
have been observed by Valeria Ukrainskaya and colleagues (2021) that found a stronger functional 
activity when stimulating with an antigen-dependent activation compared to an antigen-independent 
activation75. Moreover, the addition of IL2 might increase the presence of Tex cells. This could be 
explained by Byungsuk Kwon (2021) as the presence of IL2 can drive the generation of Tex cells 37. 
For the serum compatibility of the ag-sc, Figure 16 illustrated that the ag-sc were not compatible in 
human serum, whereas Figure 22 gave some mixed results of the compatibility in human serum. 
However, due to the inconsistency in results  from the experiment “CRISPR expansion NP”, the ag-
sc is not believed to be compatible in human serum. 
NPs 
The NPs examined in this thesis are constructed with or without linkers. One reason to add linkers is 
to make the NP more flexible and thereby more accessible for the CAR T cells. As mentioned, the 
CAR T cells needs more stimulation in order to be activated, as compared to T cells, which could be 
achieved by the more flexible NPs. Based on this, NP1 and NP2 were not used to expand CAR T 
cells. NP3 have shown to be able to stimulate and expand CAR T cells in one donor (Figure 17). NP3 
are not coated with cytokines, which could lead to less stimulation of the CAR T cells, and thus the 
cytokines were added alongside NP3 in the expansion. However, if NP3 are administered in vivo, 
there are already cytokines inside the patient, which might be sufficient to stimulate and expand the 
CAR T cells. If, however, the NP are coated both with the antigen and cytokines, there is a risk of 
CRS, as the CAR T cells might be overactivated and thereby release a high amount of cytokines, 
which needs to be further examined.  
It was observed that NP3 were constantly bound to the CAR T cells in the expansion (Figure 20 –  
Figure 21). When the NP is constantly bound to the CAR T cells, there is a risk of overactivating the 
CAR T cells and either drive the CAR T cells to become exhausted or driven to death by activation. 
Moreover, in vitro studies need to be performed to evaluate if the CAR T cells can induce toxicities 
such as CRS and ICANS. 
Moreover, the serum compatibility of NP3 were examined, and as can be seen in Figure 16, the 
activation decreased when NP3 were incubated with 100% human serum. Nonetheless, activation 
was still obtained. However, there is a loss of activation when NP3 is incubated with 50% human 
==PAGE:66==
66 
 
serum, compared to when NP3 is incubated with 100% human serum. The loss of activation needs to 
be further examined.  
In order to examine the importance of the flexibility of NPs, NPB and NPC were examined, where 
there is either no linker or multiple linkers added on NPs, making it more or less accessible for the 
CAR T cells. In all three donors, no CAR T cells were present after stimulation of NPB, and the 
number of total cells were low. This could indicate that the antigen and cytokines were not accessible 
enough to stimulate the CAR T cells. When the CAR T cells were administered to NPC, the CAR T 
cell percentage increased for all three donors at the end of the expansion, even though the cells were 
halved on day 6, indicating that the branched linkers made the antigen and cytokines more accessible. 
As can be seen in Figure 24, NP3/28 successfully increase the T cell number and leads to a higher T 
cell number than Dynabeads and TransAct when small volumes of NP3/28 were used. The effect of 
NP3/28 in the small volumes could make it more cost effective compared to Dynabeads. Moreover, 
when activating cells with NP3/28, similar phenotypic traits are obtained, indicating that NP3/28 can 
be used in the same manner as the FDA approved products. As NP3/28 is composed of dextrans, 
which is generally known as a safe molecule76, it enables the possibility of in vivo activation of CAR 
T cells, making it more patient compliance and cost effective.  
Viral transduction and CRISPR generation of CAR T cells   
In this thesis both viral transduction and CRISPR Cas9 were used to produce CAR T cells. Both 
methods have pros and cons. CRISPR Cas9 is thought to produce the next generation of CAR T cells, 
as the placement and amount of CAR sequence can be controlled. This can be utilised to produce 
allogeneic CAR T cells. However, CRISPR Cas9 has a low yield of successful KI. Moreover, there 
is a limit of the size of the plasmids used for the gene insertion for CRISPR61,77,78. On the other hand, 
viral transduction is very effective and can be utilised for large scale DNA alterations79. However, 
the CAR production use viral transduction, and has a random insertion of the CAR receptor. The 
random insertion can lead to genotoxicity, as it may disrupt normal gene expression and thus lead to 
oncogenic insertional mutagenesis54. 
Even though CRISPR Cas9 has a low yield of KI, the antigen-dependent stimulation achieved by the 
ag-sc were able to specifically stimulate and grow the low amount of CAR T cells present in the 
beginning of the expansion (Figure 12).  
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
To evaluate the CD28-CAR and the 41BB-CAR, a viral transduction with an MOI5 were used. The 
increase in the MOI was done to ensure that a decent amount of T cells would be successfully 
transduced. This was done to ensure that there would be sufficient CAR T cells in the beginning of 
the transduction for them to be stimulated and grown in an antigen-independent manner.  
CD28-CAR 41BB-CAR comparison  
When analysing the phenotypic traits between CD28-CAR and 41BB-CAR, no differences were 
found between the two constructs. This was unexpected, since CARs containing the 4-1BB 
costimulatory domain generally has a higher persistence than the CD28 costimulatory domain16. 
Therefore, 41BB-CAR were expected to express a higher amount of memory receptors than CD28-
CAR. Moreover, 4-1BB were expected to have a higher amount of CD8+ cells16,49. However, the two 
donors did not behave in the same manner, thus it cannot be concluded if the 41BB-CAR has an 
increased amount of CD8+ cells. 
When comparing the efficiency of the two CAR constructs, they behaved in the same way except at 
the 30:1 Jeko-1:CART cell ratio. Here, the CD28-CAR was more efficient than the 41BB-CAR. This 
could be because CAR T cells containing the CD28 construct generally have a more rapid T cell 
activation compared to the 41BB constructs, and thus are able to kill the target cell faster16,49. 
Nonetheless, the experiment indicated that the new CAR construct containing the CD28 
costimulatory domain is functional.

Conclusion  
In this master thesis, the ag-sc used in this laboratory were optimized and used for a successful 
expansion, whereafter a killing assay were set up. The killing assay demonstrated that the ag-sc 
stimulation was the most effective in eradicating cancer cells, even after the CAR T cells were re-
exposed to the cancer cells multiple times.  
The ag-sc was used as a positive control to analyse a range of different NPs. It was found that the 
NPs were compatible in human serum, which was not the case for the ag-sc. However, it was found 
that NP3 were still bound to the CAR T cells after 10 days of stimulation. This must be further 
examined, as this could lead to some toxicities in the form of CRS. Moreover, the efficiency of the 
NPs was analysed depending on the presence or absence of cytokines, and from these activation tests, 
it was found that NPs coated with cytokines induced a stronger activation. However, the cytokine 
additions’ toxicities must be further examined. When evaluating the structure of NPs, it was found 
that NPs with a long and flexible linker increased the stimulation of the CAR T cells, as the antigen 
became more accessible for the CAR T cells. 
The NP3/28 was found to successfully activate T cells and produce cells with similar phenotypic 
traits as when cells are activated with the FDA approved products Dynabeads and TransAct.  
Moreover, when using small volumes of NP3/28, a high T cell growth was obtained relative to the 
FDA approved products. 
Two CAR constructs, 41BB-CAR and CD28-CAR, were examined. Both constructs behaved in the 
same manner in the form of cell expansion, transduction, and phenotypic analysis. Moreover, their 
efficiency was similar, although CD28-CAR were slightly more effective when exposed to high ratios 
of Jeko-1 cells. This indicates that the new construct containing the CD28 costimulatory domain is 
functional.

2.4 Irradiated Jeko-1 
Jeko-1 cells are CD19 positive cells and thus can be used for antigen-specific stimulation and 
expansion. However, to avoid Jeko-1 cell growth and the possibility of outgrowing the small amount 
of CAR T cells in the beginning of an expansion, the Jeko-1 cells were irradiated pre-stimulation. To 
evaluate the effect of the irradiation, two samples of the irradicated Jeko-1 cells were kept in culture 
for a week and counted three times a week. The results can be seen in Figure 43.

2.11 Ag-sc activation analysis by luciferase-based detection assay 
Previously, another batch of biotinylated CD19, 522, were used in the laboratory to produce the ag-
sc. To evaluate the new batch of biotinylated CD19, 794, and see if the two proteins behave in the 
same manner, the two proteins were used to produce ag-sc, which were used in a titration to evaluate 
the cell activation. The titration was based on different amount of stims: 0 stim, 1 stim, 5 stim, 10 
stim, 20 stim, 30 stim, 40 stim, and 50 stims were added to 100.000 Jurkats cells, and their activation 
were measured. Two positive controls were conducted, one where CD19+ Jeko-1 cells were used to 
stimulate the Jurkats cells in the ratio 1:2, and one where Dynabeads was set to activate the Jurkats

cells in a 3:1 ratio. Each condition was run in technical triplicates. The cells were set to incubate
