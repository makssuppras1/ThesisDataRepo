Abstract
The Joint European X-ray Monitor (JEM-X) aboard ESA’s INTEGRAL satellite has observed the X-
ray sky since December 2002 to March 2025, providing high-sensitivity coverage in the 3-35 keV energy
range. This thesis presents a systematic effort to extend the JEM-X source catalog by analyzing archival
observations.
The catalog presented here is based on archived JEM-X data between January 2008 and October 2010,
corresponding to 34 months and 346 revolutions. The scientific data analysis was carried out using the
OSA (Off-line Science Analysis) software - an instrument-specific data-processing pipeline for JEM-X pro-
vided by ISDC (INTEGRAL Science Data Centre), to get from raw data to scientific products. Products
include: lists of detected sources, sky images, light curves, and mosaic images. These were systematically
processed and filtered to extract valid source detections, using a detection significance threshold of 4.25.
and to measure the corresponding fluxes, done using scripts developed as part of this thesis.
The created catalog includes 119 X-ray sources, along with source information: source name, sky co-
ordinates, positional uncertainties, detection significance, off-axis angle, measured count rates and fluxes
converted to mCrab in two energy bands (3–10 keV and 10–25 keV). The search of X-ray sources was
performed using two detection methods, one based on single pointing images with typically exposure
times of 30-60 minutes, and one based on mosaics created by stacking the pointing images on a monthly
basis. Light curves were produced for sources detected in single-pointing images, confirming that these
sources were active only on short timescales, making their signal diluted in the mosaic images, making
them undetectable on the monthly timescale. The final source catalog therefore consists of (i) 110 sources
found in single pointing images and (ii) 113 sources found in mosaic images.
The catalog provides a foundation for further scientific investigation. As a case study, the candidate
UCXB (Ultra-Compact X-ray Binary) 4U 1822–000 hosting an accreting neutron star (NS), was selected
for a detailed analysis. Light curves and hardness–intensity diagrams (HIDs) were used to probe the
source’s temporal behavior and spectral state variations. A search for thermonuclear (Type I) X-ray
bursts was conducted, revealing no bursts in the considered time period, in agreement with earlier stud-
ies of 4U 1822-00.
This thesis contributes to the legacy of the JEM-X instrument by providing a broader and more re-
liable dataset for future astrophysical research. The methodology and results presented here enhance the
legacy value of the JEM-X archive, enabling future population studies, burst and outburst monitoring,
and multi-instrument analyses in the X-ray regime.

Introduction
Cataloging X-ray sources is a cornerstone of high-energy astrophysics, enabling population studies, source
classification, and the investigation of transient phenomena. Over the past decades, numerous X-ray
missions have contributed to the construction of all-sky and partial-sky catalogs that reveal the dynamic
and diverse nature of the X-ray universe. These includes: IXPE [Ursini et al., 2024], BeppoSAX in’t
Zand et al. [2004], MAXI [Hori et al., 2018], and INTErnational Gamma RAy Laboratory (INTEGRAL)
observations [Bird et al., 2016] [Alfonso-Garzón et al., 2012].
In particular, HMXBs and LMXBs have been prime targets in X-ray population studies. These systems,
composed of a Neutron Star (NS) or Black Hole (BH) accreting matter from a stellar companion, exhibit
a rich phenomenology: from persistent emission and strong flares to type I X-ray bursts and complex
spectral state transitions [Seward and Charles, 2010]. Catalogs that track these sources over long baselines
are key to understanding the lifecycles and spatial distribution of compact binaries in the galaxy Tauris
and van den Heuvel [2003].
X-ray catalogs have also enabled the classification of sources based on spectral properties and variability
patterns, Diagnostic tools such as hardness-intensity diagram (HID)s, color-color (CC) diagrams, and
burst histories aid in distinguishing source classes and accretion regimes.
Since its launch in 2002, the European Space Agency (ESA)’s INTEGRAL mission provided an unprece-
dented view of the high-energy universe. Its JEM-X instrument played a key role in observing X-ray
emission from a wide variety of astrophysical sources, contributing to our understanding of high-energy
phenomena both within and beyond our Galaxy. While JEM-X data appear in previous catalogs, its
long-term dataset remains underexploited.
The Division of Astrophysics at DTU Space is responsible for the maintenance of JEM-X, encompassing
both hardware and software support. In addition, the division contributes to the scientific legacy of
the mission through the development of archival data products in collaboration with European Space
Astronomy Centre (ESAC) and ESA. One such effort is the creation and extension of a comprehensive
catalog of X-ray sources detected by JEM-X throughout the mission’s operational lifetime.
This thesis contributes to that effort by systematically analyzing archived JEM-X data between January
2008 and October 2010. The goal is to detect both known and previously uncatalogued X-ray sources
across a broad range of astrophysical classes. Given the volume and complexity of the data, the work
relies heavily on dedicated analysis software to process raw data into scientific products, and automated
processing pipelines and custom scripting to handle images, mosaics and light curves.
The resulting
JEM-X source catalog will include key observational parameters such as sky positions with associated
uncertainties and average fluxes in two energy bands (3 −10 keV and 10 −25 keV).
Beyond catalog compilation, a deeper look into the detected source types and distribution is performed
through statistical assessment of the resulting catalogs - both to understand the capabilities of source
detection by JEM-X and to explore the differences between the detection of sources with varying effective
exposure times.
Special emphasis is placed on the characterization of source variability through light curve (LC) and
HIDs, and a case study of the LMXB 4U 1822–000 is conducted to investigate its long-term behavior and
spectral state evolution. Through this focused analysis, the broader value of the catalog for population
studies, burst monitoring, and source classification is demonstrated.
By expanding the JEM-X source catalog and providing structured observational data, this thesis enhances
the scientific utility of the INTEGRAL archive and lays the groundwork for future studies of high-energy
source populations.

2
Theory of X-ray Sources
2.1
X-ray Production and Emission
X-rays are electromagnetic radiation with energies more than 1,000 times of visible light and are thus
able to penetrate surfaces of all substances. High energy X-ray photons will either pass through without
being affected, or interact with individual atoms, and the higher the energy, the more easily the photons
can penetrate unaffected. Most astronomical X-rays, however, have much less energy and are therefore
easily absorbed, presenting one of the main issues when observing astronomical X-ray sources, as they
are absorbed by Earth’s atmosphere. The search for these sources corresponds to a search of material
at temperatures of the order of millions of degrees, as these highly energetic photons require extreme
temperatures to be produced [Seward and Charles, 2010].
X-rays are produced mainly through different radiation processes, typically classified as (i) thermal pro-
cesses, (ii) non-thermal processes, and (iii) inverse Compton scattering.
The thermal processes include both blackbody radiation and thermal bremsstrahlung; characteristic of
them is that the electron velocity distribution is determined by temperature. Thermal bremsstrahlung
is a free-free transition, occurring in hot (T > 105K), low-density, transparent gases, where particle
collisions take place between ions and electrons and thermal equilibrium is present. The radiation is
produced by deflection and deceleration of electrons as they pass by ions. This produces a continuum
emission spectrum descreasing exponentially at high energies, and may also include line emission from
ions excited by the collisions [Seward and Charles, 2010]. Blackbody radiation is emitted by hot, dense
surfaces, e.g. the surface of a NS. The surface absorbs all incoming radiation and emits solely due to its
own temperature. The Planckian continuum spectrum peaks at an energy directly determined by this
temperature[Seward and Charles, 2010].
The non-thermal processes include synchrotron radiation, which is produced as relativistic electrons spiral
along magnetic field lines. The radiation is independent of temperature and electrons are radiated over
a wide range of energy levels.
This creates a power-low spectrum and is characteristic of supernova
remnants and in NS environments with strong magnetic fields[Seward and Charles, 2010].
Another way X-rays can be generated is through Inverse Compton Scattering, which typically occurs
close to quasars or around ultra-relativistic particles moving over intergalactic distances. High-energy
electrons transfer energy to low-energy photons, boosting them to X-ray or gamma-ray energies [Seward
and Charles, 2010].

Solar and Stellar X-ray sources are generally observed at energies of 0.1-10 keV, as they are produced by
thermal bremsstrahlung and line emission from their hot coronas. A Supernova Remnant (SNR) can be
observed in X-rays generated through thermal emission and synchrotron radiation and with spectrums
showing emission lines from heavier elements [Seward and Charles, 2010].
X-ray sources like Active
Galactic Nuclei (AGN)s generate X-rays as the supermassive black hole in the galactic center accretes
matter, these include both Seyfert galaxies, e.g. NGC 4151, extremely luminous Quasars, e.g. 3C 273,
and Blazars, with jets aligned with our line of sight, e.g. BL Lacertae. Other sources include Gamma
Ray Source (GRS) with afterglows in X-rays, clusters of galaxies emitting from the hot intracluster gas,
and - not to be forgotten - X-ray binaries [Seward and Charles, 2010]. X-ray binaries are not just a single
type of source, but a wide category of sources that generate X-rays as a result of accretion from one star
onto a compact object.
2.3
X-ray Emission from Binary Systems
When X-ray binaries were originally discovered in the 1960s they were thought of as "X-ray stars". No
process capable of producing this amount of energy was known, and many theories arose about the newly
discovered Sco X-1 and Cyg X-2. It was not until 1971 that a fundamental understanding of these sources
was gained. Along with the launch of the Uhuru satellite came observations that made it possible to
recognize that "X-ray stars" were actually close binary systems consisting of an X-ray-emitting NS and
an optical companion star. The close proximity of the stars led to the theory that gravity was the source
of the X-ray energy: as the two stars interact through mass-exchange, the huge amount of gravitation
energy is released as X-rays [Seward and Charles, 2010].
Much more has been learned about the brightest X-ray sources in the sky since then. In general, X-ray
binaries consist of two stars co-rotating about their common center of mass. X-ray binaries come in a
large variety, and it can sometimes be difficult to tell them apart, but in the following sections some of
the most important features are described.
Properties of X-ray binaries depend heavily on the compact object, which can either be a White Dwarf
(WD), a NS, or a BH. The WD system is the most common, but it is detectable only when close to us as
it is significantly less dense than BHs and NSs and thus much less luminous. Typically, they are observed
within a few hundred parsecs, whereas the "rarer" systems containing NSs or BHs can be observed
even in neighboring galaxies Seward and Charles [2010]. WD systems are usually grouped separately as
cataclysmic variables (CVs).
X-ray binaries with either a BH or a NS as the compact object are divided into two main categories: (i)
HMXB, (ii) LMXB, based on the mass of the companion. The two systems will be discussed in more
detail and an overview of the characteristics can be seen in Table 2.

2.3.1
High-mass and Low-mass X-ray Binaries
HMXBs are young systems, since the companion stars are massive, early-type stars, which are short-
lived on astronomical timescales. The mass transfer occurs due to strong stellar winds from the massive
companion as illustrated in Figure 1.
The massive OB donor star produces powerful stellar winds,
driven by its extreme luminosities and high temperatures, emitting powerful UV photons, which transfers
momentum and maintains the stellar winds of typically 106M⊙yr−1, as they are absorbed and scattered
by their surroundings. The stellar wind causes substantial mass loss from the donor star in all directions,
allowing the compact object to accrete some of this material and produce X-rays as the in-falling gas is
heated while interacting with the wind. As the compact object moves through the stellar wind, material
is captured and forms an accretion disc.
Figure 1: Illustration of mass transfer in HMXB. The mass-transfer occurs trough strong stellar winds
from a young massive star. Figure adapted from Seward and Charles [2010]
HMXB are sometimes divided further into 3 main types; (i) supergiant X-ray binaries, where the stel-
lar wind is sufficient to drive the X-ray emission, (ii) supergiant X-ray binaries, where an additional
mechanism is needed (Roche Lobe overflow), and (iii) Be X-ray transients.

Be X-ray transients stand out, as long-term X-ray surveys have revealed major outbursts recurring over
extended periods. These outbursts are thought to arise from mass ejection by the Be star, which rotates
close to its breakup speed, leading to the formation of an extended equatorial accretion disc, which leads
to weaker stellar winds.
For some HMXBs, pulsations are observed; these types are called X-ray pulsar. These systems all have a
NS as the compact object, and as the accreted material reaches the magnetosphere, it is guided towards
the polar caps. When hitting the surface a shock is created, producing X-rays, which are shadowed by
the column of material above, creating a beam like X-ray emission. The pulsations are a result of the
polar caps being misaligned from the rotation axis. LMXBs can also be X-ray pulsars, but it is much
less common as they typically exhibit much weaker magnetic fields than the HMXB-NS systems.
In a LMXB, the mass-losing star is a faint, low-mass star that overflows its Roche Lobe - a mathematical
surface defining where the star’s gravity dominates. Mass transfer occurs through the inner Lagrangian
point, L1, forming a stream of material. Because the incoming material carries angular momentum, it
cannot accrete directly onto the compact object; instead, an accretion disc is formed. At the point where
the stream impacts the disc, a bulge is created, from which material is further accreted onto the compact
object, releasing X-rays. An illustration of this process can be seen in Figure 2. For the mass transfer to
remain stable, the compact object has to be more massive than the donor star, hence for a neutron star
the companion is smaller than the Sun, resulting in an optically faint system.
Figure 2: Illustration of mass transfer in . The low mass star fills its Roche Lobe and transfer mass
trough the inner Lagrangian point, L1 in a stream the compact companion star. As the stream impinges
the accretion disc a bulge is created from which material is further accreted onto the compact object
itself, generating X-rays. Figure adapted from Seward and Charles [2010].
A general issue affecting the detection of LMXBs is the disc itself, specifically the concept of disc-
shadowing. At very high inclination, X-ray eclipses are expected, but since the disc completely blocks
the X-ray source, no eclipse is visible, and detection is limited to X-rays scattered by wind or emitted by
the accretion disc corona (ADC), extending below and above the disc. The compact object in LMXBs
may be a BH, a NS, or a WD. Certain observational characteristics help distinguish between them. If the
source is faint, with a luminosity less than 1033 ergs s−1 and typically located within 100 pc, it is likely
a WD. At higher luminosities, the compact object could be either a BH or a NS; however, a soft X-ray
spectrum combined with high luminosity is characteristic of a BH, and the presence of X-ray bursts or
pulsations uniquely indicates a NS.
X-ray bursters are dramatic events, emitting extreme amounts of energy. The two main X-ray bursters
are the Normal X-ray burst, Type I and the Rapid bursters, Type II. The type I burst or "normal" burst
typically emits energy of 1039 ergs and lasts about one minute. It is a thermonuclear burst explained by

==PAGE:14==

unstable nuclear burning on the surface of the compact object. The compact object has to be a neutron
star, as the nuclear burning requires a surface to take place and since the sphere radiating X-rays in the
cooling tail corresponds to the size of a NS; 10−15 km. The X-ray burst can approximately be described
as a cooling blackbody, since the NS’ surface acts as a thermal emitter during the burst, whereas if the
distance is known, the approximate radius of the NS can be determined. The Rapid Burster differs from
the "normal" type I by recurring as frequently as every 10 s. Occasionally, larger bursts are observed, with
longer gaps until the next burst, but the recurrence time is not random, but depends on the properties
of the previous burst. Thermonuclear fusion can be ruled out as the cause, as the temperature remains
constant during these bursts. The behavior of Rapid Bursters is instead explained by the magnetospheric
gate model, which involves a NS with a magnetic field strong enough to produce a magnetosphere. The
magnetosphere acts as a gate that periodically "opens", allowing accreted material to fall onto the surface
of the NS, producing an X-ray burst.
So far, the focus has been on LMXBs with NSs as the compact object, but another type of LMXBs is
the very rare ultra-compact X-ray binary (UCXB); fewer than a dozen are known. These systems have
ultra short orbital periods, whereas the binary has to be very small in size, and thus only a degenerate
star can be the mass donor. Currently, it is assumed to be a 0.05M⊙helium WD.
As mentioned BHs can also be the compact object in both HMXB and LMXB, but they are significantly
more difficult to distinguish from the NS systems. The distinguishment is done by determining the mass
of the compact object, which can be done fairly well for LMXBs as the donor star is of low mass, whereas
the mass function represents a lower limit of the mass of the compact object. For HMXBs, the donor
star is massive, and a reliable value for the compact objects mass can only be determined if the mass of
the donor star and the orbital inclination are known Seward and Charles [2010].
Soft X-ray transients or X-ray nova (XRN) are mostly indistinguishable from other LMXBs.
Most
XRN are quiescent for years, until mass transfer starts up again, producing extreme X-ray outbursts
and dramatically increasing the brightness of the accretion disc. Most of these systems are good BH
candidates, but some also contain NSs [Seward and Charles, 2010].
In general distinguishing between different LMXB and HMXB, can be very difficult, although for some
systems this can be done by considering their spectral states and spectral transitions.

The main types are ’Z’ and ’atoll, typically classified through X-ray spectral and X-ray fast
variability characteristics [van der Klis, 1989]. The names represent the pattern they trace when plotting
their spectral evolution patterns, either as a CC diagram or a HID [van der Klis, 2004]. Through typical
observation, only parts of the pattern are visible, but by combining observations over an extended period
of time, the full pattern can become more obvious, either directly or by considering the power spectra of
the varying X-ray intensity.
Z-sources have luminosities close to the Eddington luminosity and can move through their entire pattern
within a single day. They show three distinct "branches": (i) the horizontal branch (HB), (ii) the normal
branch (NB) and (iii) the flaring branch (FB). Evidence shows that the mass accretion rate increases
from HB to NB and from NB to HB [van der Klis, 2004].
Atoll sources trace their pattern at a much longer time scale, and have luminosities that are 5 −10 times
lower than the Z-sources, along with few sources having luminosities closer to the Z-sources, e.i the "GX"
atoll sources in the galactic bulge [van der Klis, 2004]. The pattern of an atoll source is divided into the
banana-branch, with an upper and a lower part, and an island state. For atoll sources the transition
from soft to hard takes place at lower luminosities than hard to soft transitions, and the island state is a
hard, low-luminosity state typically occurring due to a low accretion rate, with the hard X-rays from the
corona dominating the X-ray emission [van der Klis, 2004].

3
JEM-X & Data
This project relies on data from the JEM-X instrument on board INTEGRAL, whereas this section
includes a short description of INTEGRAL and JEM-X, along with an overview and description of the
data analysis and data products. The data preparation and data sample are also presented in this section.
3.1
The INTEGRAL Mission
INTEGRAL enables the observation of X-rays and gamma rays, which cannot be done by ground-based
telescopes. It is a hard X-ray and gamma ray observatory, providing long, uninterrupted observations,
over a broad energy-range. All instruments on board are co-aligned and with a total field-of-view of
100deg2, a large part of the sky can be observed simultaneously [Kuulkers et al., 2021]. Together, with
the good angular, timing, and spectral resolution, this makes INTEGRAL well-suited for systematic
studies of X-ray sources [Sazonov et al., 2020a]. The telescope can also be re-pointed within only a few
hours, and thus aids in Target of Opportunity (ToO) observations. It has contributed to more than 600
new high-energy sources [Kuulkers et al., 2021].
The pay-load of INTEGRAL includes four instruments, the two main gamma-ray instruments - the
spectrometer, SPI (SPectrometer on INTEGRAL)[European Space Agency (ESA), n.d.g], and the imager,
IBIS (Imager on Board the INTEGRAL Satellite) [European Space Agency (ESA), n.d.b] - and two
monitor instruments: the X-Ray Monitor JEM-X [European Space Agency (ESA), n.d.e], and the optical
camera OMC (Optical Monitoring Camera) [European Space Agency (ESA), n.d.f]. SPI, IBIS, and JEM-
X are all coded mask telescopes, operating simultaneously at energies from 3 keV - 10 MeV [Chernyakova
et al., 2015].
INTEGRAL has been orbiting Earth in a highly elliptical orbit, since it was launched on October 17th
2002. Initially, one revolution corresponded to 3 days, but after a modification in 2015, it takes 8 days
for it to perform 3 revolutions [Kuulkers et al., 2021]. The telescope incorporates different dithering
maneuvers to minimize systematic errors caused by temporal and spatial background changes.
The
dithering is performed mainly in 3 different modes: hexagonal (HEX) dither, and no dither (staring),
rectangular (5x5) dither, with the latter being used for deep exposures, due to its efficiency in reducing
instrumental artifacts. The integration time for a single pointing is typically around 30-60 minutes and
the pointing is stable within 7.5” [Kuulkers et al., 2021].

3.2.1
Operation experience and problems encountered during the mission
Early in the mission, JEM-X experienced some technical issues. The operability was verified, the reso-
lutions were deemed consistent with the "prior-to-launch"-values, and the source localization exceeded
expectations - although a high rate of erosion of the microstrip anodes threatened the mission’s lifetime
[Budtz-Jørgensen et al., 2009]. The large sensitivity of the microstrip anodes to the space environment
resulted in a loss of approximately one anode per day. To fix the issue, the gas gain was reduced and
it was decided that only one JEM-X instrument would operate at a time. The fix reduced the loss to
only four anodes per year, but the lowered gain also resulted in a decrease of detector signal relative to
electronic noise. The efficiency of the electronics also decreased due to poorer signal-to-noise ratio, and
the low-energy response of the detectors has thus been modified [Brandt, 2012]. The detection efficiency
of JEM-X1 can be seen in Figure 7, showing that the peak efficiency is reached at ∼10 keV.
To even the "wear" of the two detectors, they were used alternately. JEM-X1 was used from rev 170-855,
during rev 862-975 JEM-X2 was used, and since rev 976 both JEM-X units have been in use [Brandt,
2012].

3.3
INTREGRAL/JEM-X Data
The storage and structure of INTEGRAL data is the same for all instruments. It is controlled/maintained
by the ISDC [University of Geneva, Department of Astronomy, n.d.]. After a revolution of approximately
three days, the telemetry is processed and stored as a FITS file. A single revolution corresponds to
multiple observations, that consist of pointings of 30 −60 minutes each.
Each of these pointings is
denoted as a ScW. Each ScW contains data structures that are collected in a Science Window Group
(SWG). At the beginning of the data analysis, the SWG contains raw data, prepared science data. and
housekeeping data [Chernyakova et al., 2015]. During data analysis, the created files are attached to
the group. The SWGs are further collected into an Observation Group (OG) corresponding to the given
observation. For a more detailed overview of the data storage see Appendix A.
3.4
JEM-X Scientific Analysis Levels
In this section, the analysis levels specific to JEM-X are described in more detail, with a focus on the
output-producing levels. An overview of all analysis levels used in this project can be seen in Table 4.
The scientific analysis is carried out using the INTEGRAL Off-line Scientific Analysis (OSA) package
by ISDC [University of Geneva, Department of Astronomy, n.d.], which relies on High Energy Astro-
physics Software (HEAsoft)[High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center (HEASARC),
NASA, 2025] from NASA’s High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center (HEASARC)
[NASA/GSFC HEASARC, 2025], among other software. The analysis is partly performed on individual
ScW and partly on groups of ScWs. The OSA software is written in a modular style with single executa-
bles being combined in pipelines. The JEM-X Scientific Analysis pipeline is introduced in this section,
illustrating how it goes from a single science window to combine results, such as mosaics or long-term

The first level of the analysis, COR, handles instrumental corrections of the science data, such as energy
and position corrections and the flagging of bad-quality events. Tables of both pre-flight corrections and
in-flight calibrations are used. The output is corrected data.

correctly are excluded by creating a list of start and stop times of Good Time Intervals (GTI). The quality
is evaluated by comparing housekeeping parameters with pre-set limits, considering satellite stability, and
checking for gaps in the telemetry flow. In addition, ’bad time intervals’ are flagged [Chernyakova et al.,
2018].

3.4.1
IMA-level, Source Detection and Sky Images
At the IMA-level, sky images are produced and a significant source search is performed. If a source is
detected, a new data structure is created that includes the catalog information of the source [Chernyakova
et al., 2018].
Iterative Removal of Sources
The coded-masks in JEM-X works similarly to a pinhole camera. As rays pass through the mask, a
series of shadows are imprinted on the detector, creating a so-called shadowgram. By combining the
information of the mask-pattern with the measured intensity and shadowgram, the location of the source
in the sky and the brightness can be determined. If multiple sources are present in the FoV, the source
detection and sky-image creation are done through an iterative process, Iterative Removal of Sources
(IROS) [Lund et al., 2010] implemented in the j_ima_iros script. This algorithm dientangles signals
from different sources and determines their positions by using backprojections. Photons from each pixel
are projected back through the coded mask and onto a "synthetic" sky by ray tracing. This is done for
all incoming photons, creating a noisy sky image. By analyzing the image statistically, the positions on
the sky, that the photons are most likely to have come from, can be determined. Regions with clusters
of backprojected photons are more likely represent actual sources than regions with only few photons.
All photons thought to originate from the strongest source are then removed from the noisy sky-image,
and the process is repeated until no possible sources remain. This process produces results for each ScW
in the OG and stores them separately. For each ScW, a sky image named jmxj_sky_ima.fits and a list
of detected sources in the ScW named jmxj_srcl_.fits are created, where j = 1, 2 refers to the JEM-X
instrument used. The source list file contains detection significance, fluxes, and errors for all detected
sources in the three standard energy bands as well as any user-defined ones [Chernyakova et al., 2018].
Source Detection
The IROS algorithm is performed independently for the three standard energy bands that JEM-X au-
tomatically measures in: 3-7 keV, 7-11 keV, and 11-20 keV. A source is detected by searching for peaks,
characterized by the detection significance, defined as the highest peak value divided by the RMS (Root
Mean Square) value of the immediate background. A source is detected if it has a detection significance
greater than three in at least two bands, or greater than 10 in one band. Detected sources are then saved
to a list of source candidates extracted by the IROS algorithm [Chernyakova et al., 2018].
As mentioned, the detection significance is measured at the highest peak value divided by the RMS-value
in the immediate background; this assesses the likelihood that the observed data represents a true signal
[Chernyakova et al., 2018]. In the final list of observed sources, the combined detection significance is
reported. It is calculated as,

Sky images
A sky image for a single ScW is not just a simple image. It is in fact a FITS file containing an intensity
image, a variance image, and a significance image for all chosen energy intervals [ESA, 2015].
• The intensity image, or the reconstructed image, shows the photons detected per second from
each source. It is reconstructed from the mask shadowgrams, resulting in the statistically most
probable image [ESA, 2015].
• The variance image shows the inverse number of photons detected from a given direction in the
sky, providing information about the accuracy of the intensity estimates in the intensity images
[ESA, 2015].
• The significance image is a signal-to-noise ratio map, providing a quantitative measure of con-
fidence in the intensity estimates. It is calculated as S = I/√v, where I is the intensity and V is
the variance [ESA, 2015].
A fourth image, the The Pixel Illumination Function (PIF) image, can also be produced if chosen by
the user. The PIF is useful for detecting weak sources, especially in the vicinity of strong sources, as
the signal from bright sources can interfere with the detection of weaker sources [Kuulkers et al., 2021].
This image type is typically used in combination with mosaicking, whereas it will be explained further in
section 3.4.3, where mosaicking is explained.

3.4.3
IMA2-level, Mosaics, and Source Detection
At the end of the analysis, mosaic sky images - combining all j_ima_iros images - and a list of all
detected sources are created at the IMA2-level. By default, “intensity” (reconstructed), “variance”, and
"exposure" mosaic images are produced and saved to a file named jmxj_mosa_ima.fits, along with a
collection of results from all detected sources in the jmxj_srcl_res.fits file, stored in jmxj_obs_res.fits
[Chernyakova et al., 2018].
Mosaics
The mosaic images are produced by combining individual images listed in the OG. Combining multiple
ScWs results in a longer exposure time, making weaker sources easier to detect.
Mosaics are used in this case to "amplify" weak sources by grouping overlapping ScWs together to create
images covering a greater part of the sky.
During a single pointing, very few photons are detected,
thus it is necessary to make multiple observations in the same direction of the sky, effectively creating
a longer exposure.
By combining a number of individual images, a mosaic can be created, enabling
fainter objects to be seen more clearly [Chernyakova et al., 2018].
Although the concept may seem

The coordinate transformation is automatically performed by JEM-X scientific analysis pipeline using

the polar regions [Calabretta and Greisen, 2002]. At the reference point, the transformation using a TAN

are within a certain distance of the reference point, the geometry will be preserved, making the TAN
projection ideal for localized analysis. However, if large-scale sky coverage is desired, the AIT projection
should be used. The AIT mosaics are mapped in galactic coordinates rather than equatorial coordinates
[Chernyakova et al., 2018].
The AIT projection is a commonly used spherical map projection as it
reduces distortion in the polar region, displays the entire sphere, and preserves the equal-area property.

Mosaics from PIF-images
Mosaics are a powerful way to detect weak sources; however, in very crowded fields with bright sources,
weak sources may still go undetected, because the signal from strong sources increase noise in their
surroundings. The PIF-imaging method addresses this by measuring the flux from each sky pixel through
fitting the observed shadowgram, helping eliminate systematic image artifacts. The fitting functions are
PIFs, which is used for each found source in the
sky-images. A PIF value is calculated for each sky
pixel. In the end, the resulting image is an image with weighted pixels, produced from the weighted
backprojection for each detector pixel [Lund et al., 2010]. Pixels corresponding to stronger sources are
down-weighted to enhance the visibility of weaker sources [Kuulkers et al., 2021]. Note that the results
cannot be used for flux measurements or other physical analysis - they are intended purely for improved
visual detection of sources, as shown in Figure 11.

4
Methods
In this section the systematic analysis carried out is explained, and the specific use of the scientific
analysis software, OSA is presented. All methods were used with the aim of extending the catalog of
JEM-X detected X-ray sources, as part of the legacy archive [European Space Agency (ESA), n.d.c].
All analyses were carried out for two user-defined energy bands, 3 −10 keV and 10 −25 keV, to ensure
consistency with previous JEM-X catalogs covering data from earlier periods [Drenck, 2024]. The energy
bands are also referred to as the "low-energy band" and the "high-energy band", respectively.
For this project, I was meant to use the newest OSA and HEAsoft software available, this include:
HEAsoft 6.35, OSA 11.2, j_ima_iros 6.2.4.0, and ICVERSION 27. However, due to issues with the
software and hardware, older versions of the software were used instead: HEAsoft 6.26.1, OSA 11.1,
j_ima_iros 6.2.0, and ICVERSION 26.
The ICVERSION determines the instrument characteristics used for OSA, including calibration and
instrument configuration files from the ISDC, and is therefore crucial for producing scientifically correct
results. OSA is the core tool for JEM-X data analysis, as thus is the mission-specific software used for
e.g. reprocessing, image creation, and light curve production. HEAsoft is mainly used for inspection of
the results through e.g. plotting, filtering, and inspection of the FITS files [Chernyakova et al., 2015].
The server that should have been used, which included the newest software, crashed at the beginning of
this project. I was therefore limited to the older software on another server. During the final part of this
project, the intended server was up an running again. The results from the different software versions
should, in theory, be the same, provided that the old software did not contain crucial errors. To check
for any significant differences in flux measurements arising from using outdated software, a comparison
was performed between mosaic flux measurements obtained with the newest software and with the one
used in the project.
From this comparison, another issue occurred, which rendered the test not useful. When running the
analysis for the data with the newest software, some data products from usable data were not returned.
For example, when running the scientific analysis for the JEM-X data for January 2008, only a percentage
of the available ScWs were analyzed, resulting in far fewer sky image - only 110. Compared to the more
than 700 sky images produced when running the analysis with the old software, it appears that a large
percentage of the useful data was not included. Part of a list showing which ScWs resulted in a sky
image in both cases can be seen in Table 7. The entire list can be seen in Appendix C. In the end, it
was decided to complete the data analysis using the older software.
For this project, two catalogs were created: one produced directly from the IMA-level of the scientific
analysis and therefore on a ScW level, referred to as the ScW source catalog (or simply the source catalog),
and one produced from mosaics using photometry for flux measurements, referred to as the mosaic source
catalog.
4.1
Weak Source Detection
The OSA data analysis software is sufficiently sensitive to detect strong source, but if weaker sources
are present, or if some sources are near the edge of the detector, they might not be detected. Creating
mosaics can help detect weaker sources, as explained, but sometimes this is not sufficient on its own.
A user catalog can be created and used as input to force detection of sources that are known to be present
- although some sources might be present in the sky, but not observable in X-ray at the time. To avoid
forcing the analysis of sources that are not actually detectable, mosaics are created from PIF images and
detected sources are then added to a user catalog. PIF is used to enhance weaker sources, and to ensure
that all sources in the mosaics are detected.

If a source is visible in the mosaic but not present in the ISDC reference catalog, it might be a "new"
source, but it might also be a "ghost source". Ghost sources are not real sources but artifacts of the
image reconstruction process in telescopes using a coded mask. These artifacts are rare for JEM-X as
the pattern of its coded mask is almost non-cyclic [Sánchez-Fernández, 2021], but they still appear in
some cases, especially if a very bright source is present in the FoV. Due to the geometry of the JEM-X
instrument, ghost sources appear exactly 8.32◦from the bright source, and their position can therefore
be predicted. Verification of whether a source is a ghost can be done by simply checking if there are
any bright sources in the FoV at a distance of 8.32◦. An example is presented in Figure 17, showing the
PIF-mosaic from the pointing "Cyg X-1" from June 2010. In this image, something resembling a source
can be seen near the bottom of the mosaic, very close to the edge. Upon checking, the ghost source
appears to be produced by Cyg X-1, and is therefore ruled out as an actual source.

to a user catalog for the given month.

Map Projection for Mosaicking
For mosaic creation the AIT projection was used, both in the "weak source detection" case and in the
"flux-measurement" case. In general, this worked well, although when having to create mosaics for the
second batch of data, some issues where encountered. The produced mosaic FITS file in some cases did
not have a working WCS (World Coordinate System). Translated, this means that the position of the
different sources in the mosaic could not be determined, and thus weak sources could not be detected,
as it could not confidently be determined which sources were present in the mosaic, and hence no fluxes
could be measured in the end. It seemed that the mosaics could be produced without issues, for the same
sky images, when using the newest software. As I was unable to determine the cause of the issue, and
as I wanted to ensure consistency in my data analysis with regards to the software used, I tried creating

When investigating the issue, it came to my attention that the AIT projection might not be ideal for
flux-measurements. According to Calabretta and Greisen [2002], the AIT projection is great for full-sky
maps, but as it is not conformal, it distorts local geometry. This might affect source fitting and flux
measurement, as sources in the center of the mosaic could get misshaped. For the creation of small-field
mosaics from JEM-X data, the TAN projection thus seems to be preferred as it preserves local angular
geometry (within ∼15◦of the reference point) and aligns images linearly on the sky. This is consistent
with the WCS conventions defined by Calabretta and Greisen [2002], who note that the TAN projection
is "the most useful projection for small fields". Furthermore, when using the TAN projection, all great
circles are projected to straight lines which simplifies the task of aligning small images.
To investigate whether the choice of projection significantly alters the measured fluxes, a comparison
was done between flux measurement in mosaics using the TAN projection and using the AIT projection.

positions is shown in Figure 23. The top panel shows the fluxes in mCrab for both energy bands. For
most sources the flux measurements are very close, although the brighter sources, e.g. GX 349+2 and
GX 340+0, shows larger variation. This is also illustrated in the bottom panel, showing the residuals:
∆Flux = FluxT AN −FluxAIT . These are mostly near zero and below 10 −20 mCrab, even for the

4.4
Single Source Investigation
In this section, the methods for further investigation of single sources are explained; this includes the
creation of LCs and HIDs, and the search for thermonuclear bursts.
4.4.1
Light curves and Hardness-Intensity Diagrams
From the scientific analysis, multiple LCs were created, and even multiple LCs for a single source, corre-
sponding to a LC per ScW where the source was detected. To create combined LCs, a script was created
to extract all ScW where a given source was detected. The source count rate for that source was then
extracted and combined from all LCs corresponding to these ScWs. The script can be found Appendix
D.5.1.
To create LCs and HIDs, a combination of python scripts, created for this thesis, and the lcurve tool was
used [HEASARC, 2014]. The python scripts can be found in Appendix E.3. These were mainly used to
consider LCs from sources detected in few ScWs. For sources detected over multiple revolutions and even
months, the lcurve tool was used. lcurve is a tool from FTOOLS in HEASoft [HEASARC, 2014], used
to inspect X-ray light curves, including their variability and temporal characteristics. lcurve uses time
binning to produce LCs, and hence is very useful when considering long-scale light curves. The bin size
is given in seconds, corresponding to the chosen time resolution: smaller values increase the resolution
but might also increase noise.
The LCs are typically used to investigate source variability, burts and dips. For this project, they are used
as a means to examine sources for long-term trends using larger bins sizes, and to search for thermonuclear
bursts, which will be considered further in the next section.
The spectral state changes of the sources are investigated through HIDs created by using lcurve. The
hardness ratio is calculated using Equation 1, by dividing the flux in the "hard" band - in this case the
flux 10 −25 keV - by the flux in the "soft" band - in this case the flux in 3 −10 keV. In the same manner,
intensity is calculated using Equation 2, where again soft = 3 −10 keV and hard = 10 −25 keV. For the
HIDs, as much data as possible is included, as it takes time for the source to move through all spectral
states, and only taking part of the data might not reveal the true spectral evolution of the source.
To test various bin sizes for the creation of HIDs and to ensure proper use of the lcurve tool, the well-
studied LMXB-NS system GX 5-1 was considered, to ensure that results from other papers could be
re-created. The HID of GX 5-1, shown in Figure 24, is constructed from JEM-X data from the first batch
(i.e., Table 5). With a bin size of 1000 s, it clearly displays a characteristic Z-shaped track. Two main
branches are evident: a lower branch, where hardness ratio decreases slightly with decreasing intensity,
and an upper branch where hardness increases with intensity. This morphology is consistent with the
normal and flaring branches of a Z-source, matching the results from other papers on the source, for
example the results from Paizis et al. [2005].

Thermonuclear Burst Search
The search for thermonuclear bursts is performed by considering the combined source LCs. An example
of a LC including a thermonuclear burst can be seen in Figure 25.
34
==PAGE:42==
Catalog of X-ray sources observed with INTEGRAL/JEM-X
Figure 25: 1s light curve of the second X-ray burst from IGR J17473- 2721 observed on March 26 (MJD
54551.972) by Swift using the XRT and BAT instruments. Figure from Chenevez et al. [2011].
Different approaches for burst detection were tested, all based on a common principle. In general, bursts
were flagged by first identifying a significant increase in signal-to-noise ratio (S/N > 3). Any data point
meeting this criterion was temporarily removed to calculate a final pre-burst baseline, after which the
burst candidate was evaluated using a standard-deviation-based significance test. Following the method
used in Drenck [2024], a burst was confirmed if S/3σ > 3; however, in this implementation, a detection
also required the peak to exceed 9σ.
To explore more sensitive detection, a variation of this method was applied in which the second significance
threshold could be lowered from 9σ to alternative values. In all cases, a thermonuclear burst was assumed
to occur when the signal exceeded the pre-burst average count rate by 3σ. The pre-burst average was
calculated iteratively: for each candidate meeting the S/N > 3 condition, the point was removed from
the data, the standard deviation was recalculated, and the process was repeated until no remaining points
exceeded the S/N threshold. The final standard deviation was then used to test the candidate against the
chosen second threshold. Depending on this threshold, some bursts could be detected with one method
but missed by another.
As the source flux might vary greatly during a combined light curve, which can cover months or even years,
this burst search is performed for segments of the total LC. If there are more than 50 seconds between
a data point and the next, a new segment is created. The signal-to-noise is calculated individually for
the different segments of the LC, and then for these individual parts, the second criteria is tested, to see
whether a burst might be present. All approaches used for the burst search, further take into account,
that the second criteria might be met by a single data point, but using a bin size of 1 −10 s multiple
data points following each other should meet this criteria, as a single point meeting the criterion might
just be an outlier.
The search is performed in the low-energy band, 3 −10 keV, as thermonuclear bursts are softer than the
persistent emission [Seward and Charles, 2010]. The high-energy band, 10 −25 keV, is then used as a
confirmation band, searching for a coincident peak and spectral softening during the decay. The script
used for this search is found in Appendix E.3, a long with a method verification, showing the detection
of previously known burst in Appendix B.

6
Discussion
The processing of JEM-X data resulted in the successful compilation of an extended source catalog. The
approach ensured a consistent methodology for filtering, validating, and organizing observations. Despite
this, a number of challenges arose due to software and hardware issues. These were mitigated by the use
of slower servers and older software for the data analysis.
6.1
Choices affecting the final results
Various choices were made for the creation of the catalogs, which affects the results of the final catalogs.
Some of them will be considered and discussed in this section.
6.1.1
Energy bands
For the catalog creation, two energy bands were used: 3 −10 keV and 10 −25 keV. These were chosen
to stay consistent with the previous catalogs [Drenck, 2024], and to ensure similar on-axis efficiency in
the two energy bands, which peaks at ∼10keV . The choice of energy bands does not directly affect the
sources detected by the standard analysis results using OSA, but it has an impact on the weak source
detection, as the PIF-mosaics produced for this purpose were created in these two energy bands. Hence,
the weak sources added to the user catalogs are affected by the choice of energy band. The use of the
energy bands of 3−10 keV and 10−25 keV, has an observational advantage when it comes to, for example,
LMXB studies. The thermal emission from the surface of NSs or from accretion disks happens around
energies of 1 −10keV [Seward and Charles, 2010], covered by the low energy band, and corona emission
happens at ∼10 −50keV [Seward and Charles, 2010], covered by the high energy band.
6.1.2
Detetcion threshold
The detection of sources in the final catalogs was done using a threshold of 4.25, meaning only sources
with a detection significance larger than this value were included in the final catalogs. The threshold
was chosen based on the detection of sources by the OSA software, where a source is detected if the
detection significance is greater than 3 in two of the standard energy bands or greater than 10 in one of
the standard energy bands, resulting in a combined detection significance of 4.25. However, the detection
significance threshold will undoubtedly change the total number of detected sources in the catalogs, and
a value of 4.25 might be a bit conservative considering our a priori information: all sources detected in
this catalog have been observed before, and all sources added to the user catalogs have been visually
confirmed to be there through searches of PIF-mosaics. Hence, lowering the detection threshold should
be considered for future work.
6.1.3
Mosaics on Monthly-Basis
The mosaics were created based on a chosen time period of 28−31 days. This might dilute the luminosity
of some sources, as their signals are averaged out over an entire month. If a shorter time period was
chosen, more sources active over shorter periods might have been detected, although this would also result
in a much greater number of mosaics that needed to be constructed and analyzed. The time period was
chosen as a compromise and to stay consistent with previous catalogs produced by Esben Drenck Drenck
[2024]. However, Changing the time-coverage of the created mosaics would also affect the final results.

6.2
Mosaic Creation
Mosaics were created mainly using AIT projection, but as mentioned earlier, it might not be the most
appropriate method. Although the performed comparison showed no significant change in the measured
fluxes, but as the comparison only was done for a single mosaic, other flux measures might not reveal
the same results. Hence, for future work it would be recommended to use theTAN projection, when
mosaics are created for ScWs within a limited area (less than 15◦from the reference point) [Calabretta
and Greisen, 2002]. The AIT projection might aid in the search for weak sources when used for PIF-
mosaicking by improving visualization of greater sky regions. However, use of it is not recommended
when creating mosaics intended for flux measurements.
6.3
Comparison between Catalog Results
The comparison between the ScW and mosaic catalogs highlights the complementary nature of the two
detection methods applied to JEM-X data. The ScW source catalog, which captures sources detectable
on individual short exposures, yields 110 sources, while the mosaic source catalog, based on stacked
observations that enhance sensitivity to persistent or faint sources, identifies 113 sources. When combined,
a total of 119 unique sources are detected, e.i. Table 19.
The results confirm that LMXBs are the dominant population in both catalogs, representing over half
of the detections (60 in ScW, 58 in Mosaic, and 60 combined). In contrast, HMXB are less prevalent,
though their presence is enhanced slightly in the Mosaic catalog due to improved sensitivity.
A noteworthy observation is that only a small subset of LMXBs and HMXBs are confirmed to contain
BHs, with just one HMXB-BH and one LMXB-BH identified in each catalog, while NS systems are more
commonly confirmed among LMXBs (22) and HMXBs (5). This supports the general consensus that NS
accretors are more frequently identified in X-ray binaries, consistent with prior population studies (e.g.,
in’t Zand et al. [2004], Sazonov et al. [2020b], Neumann et al. [2023], and Kretschmar et al. [2020]), which
concludes that observed LMXB populations is strongly dominated by LMXB-NSs and observed HMXB
populations is strongly dominated by HMXB-NSs.
Not only are NS accretors dominating these X-ray binary systems, but they are also much more easily
confirmed. There are plenty of astrophysical and observational reasons for this. First of all LMXB-NSs
are easy to be uniquely identified by their type I X-ray bursts, as only these have a surface where the
thermonuclear burst can take place Seward and Charles [2010]. The bursts are relatively common and can
be detected even at moderate sensitivities. Hence, the confirmation of LMXB-NSs are straigthforward.
Detection of X-ray pulsations also confirms the compact object to be a NS, typically, but not exclusively,
in HMXB Seward and Charles [2010]. Overall, the NS systems are much easier to identify. As mentioned
in section 2, different observational characteristics of the system might indicate the type of compact
object, but it is only the neutron star that can be confirmed uniquely through X-ray observations.
Source types other than X-ray binaries are also detected and thus present in the catalogs. The mosaic
source catalog recovers slightly more AGNs (9) than the ScW source catalog (7), likely due to their
typically faint and persistent nature, which benefits from image stacking. The number of SNRs and GRSs
remains stable across methods, suggesting these sources are bright enough to be detected regardless of
exposure time.
Overall, the findings demonstrate that combining both ScW and mosaic analysis allows for a more
complete catalog of X-ray sources, with the mosaic method contributing particularly to the detection
of persistent, faint, or low-variability sources that might be missed in individual ScW exposures.

6.3.1
“Missing” sources
Some sources were only detected in one of the catalogs. The investigation into the individual sources
that were only detected in ScW source catalog, showed that most of them were only detected in 4 ScWs
or less. Hence. with a count rate measured over a maximum of 4 ScW corresponding to ∼2 hrs, it is not
surprising that, when creating mosaics and averaging the emission over an entire month, their signals are
diluted, even if their count rate is hundreds of mCrab, as can be seen from the produced light curves.
The Seyfert galaxy NGC 1275 stands out as it was detected in as many as 22 ScWs, although this still
only corresponds to a combined lightcurve covering ∼200 000s ≈2.3 days, as seen from Figure 29. The
source has a very low count rate ∼10 −20 mCrab, assuming a countrate of 20 mCrab for 2 days, this
only corresponds to ∼1.33 mCrab, when taking a monthly average, whereas it is not detected in the
mosaic source catalog.
These results further emphasizes the way the two detection methods compliments eachother, as 8% of
the sources would not have been detected if only the ScW-level detection method was used, and as 5%
of the sources would be missed if only the mosaic-level detections method was used.
6.4
Source Type Distribution and the Exposure Map
In both the ScW source catalog and mosaic source catalog most of the detected sources are LMXB and
HMXB, e.i. Table 19 and Figure 26. For the ScW source catalog 40 HMXBs and 60 LMXBs are detected,
these systems correspond to 91% of detected sources. For the mosaic source catalog 43 HMXBs and 58
LMXBs were detected, corresponding to 89% of the total detected X-ray sources. This does not reflect
the abundance of these sources in the universe, but rather the abundance of the systems at the part of
the sky that JEM-X has observed as well as its energy sensitivity - since different types of X-ray sources
are more easily detected at even higher energies than JEM-X provides (e.g., accreting X-ray pulsars or
BH X-ray binaries in the hard spectral state) [Seward and Charles, 2010]
From the exposure map it is clear that JEM-X spend most of its time observing the galactic bulge or the
galactic plane, at least from January 2008 until October 2010, which is the exact regions where LMXBs
and HMXBs dominates. As LMXBs are older systems they are found near the galactic center, whereas
HMXBs with their young-OB type stars more often, are found in the spiral arms, as seen in Figure 27.
It is clear that the spatial concentration of sources aligns very well with the regions of highest exposure,
hence it should be noted that the observed source distribution is strongly influenced by observational
bias. These results correlates with earlier JEM-X catalogs, such as the one created by Westergaard et al.
[2006] summarizing all detected sources from public data available until November 2009. This catalog
showed that 81% of the detected sources in the ScW catalog and 71% of the detected sources in the
mosaic catalog were either LMXBs or HMXBs. Although similar to the exposure map in Figure 28, the
one produced in Westergaard et al. [2006] shows that the galactic bulge and the galactic plane are the
most observed parts of the sky.
From Figure 27 it can also be seen that AGNs and SNRs appear in higher-latitude regions with lower
exposure. Very few sources are detected away from the Galactic plane, likely due to both intrinsic scarcity
and reduced sensitivity in those regions.
Comparing the detected source types in the combined JEM-X catalog with other catalogs, such as multi-
mission catalogs [Fortin et al., 2024], LMXB-surveys [Sazonov et al., 2020b], INTEGRAL’s full-mission
IBIS results [Bird et al., 2016] and the 7 years MAXI/GSC source catalog Hori et al. [2018], it seems to
be a representative snapshot of the broader population. The distribution by type, particularly LMXB
dominance, matches expectations, as LMXBs dominates the soft 3 −20keV detections Sazonov et al.
[2020b]. The majority of these binaries with confirmed accretor is LMXB-NSs, which is consistent with
the finds in Bird et al. [2016]. The number of detected AGNs is also reasonable, compared to what is
visible to JEM-X [Westergaard et al., 2006] [Hori et al., 2018].

6.5
Case study of H 1822-000
When comparing the HID from 4U 1822-000 to the HID of GX 5-1, marked differences in the spectral-
timing behavior are seen, reflecting their distinct accretion regimes and source classes, in spite of both
sources being LMXB-NS systems.
For GX 5-1, the HID displays the characteristic Z-shaped track first described by van der Klis [1989],
with clearly identifiable segments corresponding to the normal branch (NB) and flaring branch (FB).
In constrast, the HID of 4U 1822-000 shows a compact distribution at low hardness (< 0.5), with no
evidence for the extended tracks or branch transitions typical of Z-sources or atoll sources undergoing
spectral state changes.
The soft-band light curve is steady at 4−6 cnts/s, while the hard-band shows larger fractional variability
likely due to lower signal-to-noise rather than intrinsic changes. The stability in both hardness and inten-
sity indicates a persistent, soft-dominated spectral state, consistent with a low-luminosity, persistently
accreting UCXB [in ’t Zand et al., 2007].
A targeted search for thermonuclear (Type I) bursts was performed for 4U 1822-000 by inspecting the
LC and hardness ratio for short-lived count-rate spikes coinciding with a drop in hardness, which would
indicate the spectral softening typical of such bursts. Neither the HID or the LC show features consistent
with bursts. The absence of threshold-exceeding events in either energy band further indicates that no
statistically significant burst-like episodes occurred during the observation in the JEM-X energy range.
The absence of bursts is in agreement with type I burst-catalogs covering the same period of time
[Galloway et al., 2020].

Future work
Several opportunities exist to enhance the completeness, accuracy, and scientific utility of the JEM-X
source catalogs produced in this work.
First, the measured fluxes in both the ScW and mosaic catalogs were obtained using older versions of
software packages such as and . Reprocessing a subset of the data with the most recent versions would
allow a direct comparison of flux measurements, ensuring that no systematic offsets or calibration updates
have significantly affected the results. This would be particularly important for cross-mission comparisons
and for sources close to the detection threshold.
Second, much of the post-processing following the scientific analysis - such as filtering, catalog generation,
and flux conversion - remains semi-manual. Automating these steps through integrated pipelines would
significantly reduce the time needed to transform raw science data into a final, quality-controlled catalog.
Given that approximately 15 years of observations (October 2010 – March 2025) still remain to be
systematically analyzed, such automation would make it feasible to complete the “Catalog of X-ray
Sources Observed with INTEGRAL/JEM-X” in a timely manner.
Third, the current catalogs could be extended in scope. For example:
• Cross-mission data integration: Combining JEM-X results with observations from missions such
as MAXI, Swift/BAT, NICER, and XMM-Newton could improve source classifications, confirm
transient detections, and enable broadband spectral studies.
• Improved source detection methods: Testing alternative mosaic timescales (e.g., shorter than 28–31
days) could improve sensitivity to short-lived outbursts without overwhelming the analysis with
excessive numbers of mosaics.

• Machine learning applications: Automated classification of light curves and hardness–intensity
diagrams using supervised or unsupervised learning could identify new patterns, cluster similar
sources, and flag unusual events for follow-up.
• Refined burst searches: Expanding the thermonuclear burst detection algorithm to incorporate
multi-band temporal profiles and adaptive significance thresholds could improve sensitivity to faint
or short-lived bursts missed by the current criteria.
Finally, several intriguing sources identified in this study merit dedicated follow-up. For example, 4U
1822-000 remains a strong candidate for an UCXB but requires optical or UV timing to confirm its orbital
period. Similarly, “missing” sources that appear only in one detection method could be re-investigated to
better understand their variability timescales and physical nature.
By implementing these improvements, the JEM-X catalogs could serve not only as a stand-alone resource
but also as a benchmark dataset for future population studies, transient monitoring, and multi-mission
synergies in the soft X-ray regime.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, the JEM-X source catalog was created by systematically analyzing archived data from
January 2008 to October 2010. Science data were produced from raw data by running OSA for all 346
ScWs within the two-year period.
To ensure detection of not only bright sources but also weaker ones, a weak source detection was performed
first. By producing PIF-mosaics on a monthly basis for all ScWs within a given sky region, weak sources
could be detected visually. These were then added to a user catalog for the corresponding month. The
user catalog was then defined, and OSA was run again, forcing detections in the imaging step and LC
extraction for all sources in the catalog. From the new data products, fluxes were measured on a ScW
level - optimal for short-timescale detections - and on a mosaic level, using PSF-matched photometry on
re-generated mosaics without PIFs) - for better sensitivity to faint and persistent sources.
This work has contributed to the long-term effort of compiling a comprehensive catalog of X-ray sources
observed with INTEGRAL’s JEM-X instrument. The analysis resulted in two complementary catalogs,
(i) the ScW source catalog, and (ii) the mosaic source catalog. Together, these catalogs contain 119 unique
sources spanning multiple astrophysical classes, with LMXBs and HMXBs dominating the population.
The analysis not only provided source information and flux measurements in two energy bands (3 −10
keV and 10 −25 keV) but also enabled a statistical evaluation of source type distribution and spatial
location, revealing strong observational biases toward the Galactic bulge and plane. A comparison be-
tween detection methods confirmed that each approach recovers unique sources that would otherwise be
missed, highlighting the importance of combining both techniques for a more complete inventory.
Beyond catalog creation, this project incorporated variability and spectral analyses, including LCs and
HIDs to characterize individual sources. A detailed case study of the candidate UCXB 4U 1822-000
demonstrated the potential of the dataset for probing long-term source behavior and accretion states,
though no thermonuclear bursts were detected in the examined time frame.

INTEGRAL Data Storage
A.0.1
INTEGRAL Data Storage
INTEGRAL data is organized in a tree-like hierarchy to ensure easy use of and access to data within
science windows, science window groups and observation groups. A single data object, such as a science
window or observation, has multiple data elements, which contains different parts of the data set, e.g.,
event list, housekeeping data.
Data handled at each level of the data analysis is stored within FITS files. Each FITS file has a primary
header unit (HDU) and one or more data sections (HDU) called FITS extensions.
Three forms of
extensions are available: (i) Image extension, (ii) ASCII table extension, and (iii) Binary table extension.
FITS enables the data objects to be split across data files, whereas to consider a specific data object a
Data Object Locator (DOL) is defined, and to get a certain part of the data structure the FITS extension
number is simply defined as well. In cases of multiple data elements with the same name, for example
science windows in an observation group, the science windows are indexed. The index is a type of FITS
Group which can contain columns describing the specific data element.
A.0.2
Data Repositories
As data are gathered by INTEGRAL they are stored in multiple repositories, which are used by e.g., the
data analysis software. A specific type of data repository, is the processed data repository, a structure
which contains the auxiliary data and calibration data and where resultant products from the ISDC
processing pipelines are stored.
The high level directory structure of this repository can be seen in
Figure 33. “XXX” is the name of the processed data repository, “aux” contains auxiliary data necessary
to interpret the data from the spacecraft telemetry, “cat” contains observational source catalogs used by
the data analysis pipeline, “ic” contains instrumental and operational data use for calibrations and “idx”,
is the index files.
Figure 33: Directory structure of processed data from INTEGRAL. Figure from Chernyakova et al.
[2015].
The “obs” directory is filled during the science analysis and contains the products for the entire observation
group. It has a subdirectory called “scw” which contains a subdirectory for each scw belonging to the
observation group.
Finally, the “scw” directory is the science window data, which contains multiple
subdirectories. The first level of subdirectories is named based on the orbital revolution number. Each
of these subdirectories contains science windows data for scws obtained within that revolution, data
belonging to a specific science window is stored in yet another subdirectory named after the Science
Window Identifier (ScWID), a 12 digits name of the form illustrated in ??. The first 4 digits “RRRR”
corresponds to the orbital revolution number, the next 4 “PPPP” is the ISOC assigned pointing number,
“SSS” is a sequence number used if pointings or slews are subdivided for the processing, “F” is a flag
created to tell the science window type. If a science window is from a pointing, F=0, if it is from a slew,
F=1, and if it is a non-science scw, F=2. The version number, “VVV” is only relevant if the processing
is performed multiple times, as it increases with every processing.
