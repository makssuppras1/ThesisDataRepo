Abstract  
CP Kelco is facing challenges with their current quality control (QC) method for quantification of 
alcohol residues of methanol (MeOH), ethanol (EtOH) and isopropanol (IPA) in pectin, carrageenan and 
waste water using headspace gas chromatography with flame ionization detection (HS-GC-FID). The 
aim of this master`s thesis was therefore to optimize the current method and validate its performance.  
In optimization of the method, the following approaches in optimization of the sample preparation 
were investigated: dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) addition, increasing sample volume, and salt addition. 
Furthermore, sample weight, equilibrium time and temperature were determined. A new column 
(ALC-1) was implemented, and GC-FID parameters of carrier gas flow, split ratio and oven temperature 
determined using a full factorial design at three levels. The method was validated in six matrices; 
carrageenan, waste water, low methylated (LM) pectin, locust bean gum and two high methylated 
(HM) pectins. The validation parameters were linearity, limit of detection (LOD and limits of 
quantification (LOQ), working range, precision (repeatability and intra-reproducibility), accuracy 
(recovery), selectivity and robustness (fractional factorial design at two levels). The new and current 
method were compared regarding repeatability, LOD and LOQ, recovery, selectivity and results 
compared by paired t-tests. The studies of the method optimization showed that the optimal method 
includes addition of DMSO, sample weight of 0.2 g, implementation of 1-propanol as internal standard 
(IS), and equilibration time of 10 minutes at 60 °C. The optimal GC-FID parameters were found to be 
carrier gas flow of 15 mL/min, split ratio of 20:1 and oven temperature of 50 °C.  
In validation of the new method, the linearity was found notably good (R2> 0.990), LOD and LOQ were 
found to be 0.11 and 0.36, 0.10 and 0.33, 0.06 and 0.19 mg/g for MeOH, EtOH and IPA, respectively. 
The working ranges for carrageenan, HM pectin, LM pectin and LBG were determined as 0.11-25, 0.1-
25 and 0.06-25 mg/g for MeOH, EtOH and IPA, respectively. Working range for waste water was 
determined as 0.002-0.5, 0.002-0.5 and 0.001-0.5 mg/mL for MeOH, EtOH and IPA, respectively.  
Repeatability found to be below 5 % for all matrices, and intra-reproducibility results within the 
acceptable Horwitz ratio of 1.5. Recoveries were found in the range of 95 %-108 %, and the robustness 
study showed that the method is sensitive to the amount of DMSO added. The comparison of new and 
current method showed improved precision and recovery in new method. LOD and LQO for waste 
water was unchanged, carrageenan and LM pectin were enlarged, whereas LOD and LOQ for HM 
pectin and LBG were improved in new method. The paired t-tests found the differences between 
quantification with new and current method equal to zero (α=0.05)

Background 
Aim and objectives 
CP Kelco, one of the leading hydrocolloid producers in the world, produces mainly pectin and 
carrageenan on their Danish site in Lille Skensved. The carrageenan and pectin are used as food 
stabilizers among many other purposes within the food and pharma industry. CP Kelco (Lille Skensved, 
Denmark) has its own quality control (QC) department where all raw materials, semi-finished and 
finished products are tested, ensuring safety, quality and functionality of the products. One of the 
quality control analysis is the determination of alcohol residues (methanol (MeOH), ethanol (EtOH) and 
isopropanol (IPA)) by headspace gas chromatography with flame ionization detection (HS-GC-FID) in 
the products and waste water samples. As the quality control department is facing challenges with the 
current control method, they are interested in an optimization of the current method. The main 
challenges with the current method are lack of baseline separation of IPA and acetone and too high 
standard deviations for the internal standard, tert- butanol (TBA), in carrageenan sample matrix. 
Furthermore, it is preferable to have a more uniform sample preparation as the current sample 
preparation is highly matrix dependent. 
The aim of this master`s thesis is to optimize the current control method and evaluate its performance. 
The objectives are listed below:  
1) Characterize and evaluate the matrices and methods implemented 
2) Apply theory on optimization of method 
3) Evaluate the optimized GC-FID method performance 
1 Introduction 
1.1 Sample matrices 
As the developed method must be applicable to all three sample matrices (carrageenan, pectin, and 
waste water), this thesis will provide background knowledge about the matrices. The following 
sections (1.1.1, 1.1.2 and 1.1.3) describe the production, chemical structure and properties for each of 
the sample matrix mentioned in this thesis.  
1.1.1 Carrageenan 
Carrageenan is extracted from red seaweed of the class Rhodophyceae.  The Kappaphycus alvarezii 
(cotonni), Eucheuma denticulatum (spinosum) and Chondrus crispus are mainly used as raw material in 
carrageenan production (Ruiter and Rudolph, 1997). 
==PAGE:9==
 
8 
 
The production line of carrageenan has multiple unit operations. First, the seaweed is water washed, 
ensuring removal of sand and stones. This is followed by alkali extraction with potassium or sodium 
depending on the carrageenan type produced. The carrageenan extract is filtrated multiple times and 
centrifugated followed by precipitation with IPA. Finally, the carrageenan is dried, grinded, and 
blended with other ingredients (mainly sugar) to form the specific carrageenan product (CP Kelco, 
2001). 
Carrageenan has a backbone of galactose with subgroups of 3.6-anhydrogalactose and sulphate esters. 
Carrageenan can be divided into three main types, lambda, kappa and iota, depending on the content 
of 3.6-anhydrogalactose and the amount of sulphate ester groups together with the position of these 
chemical groups. 
As the sulphate group are hydrophilic and the 3,6-anhydrogalactose are more hydrophobic, the 
amount and position of these chemical groups is the crucial factor in the chemical properties and 
applications (Rhein-Knudsen, Ale and Meyer, 2015).  
From figure 1, the chemical structure of the three main carrageenan types can be seen. As seen, kappa 
carrageenan contains the highest number of 3.6-anhydrogalactose units and the fewest sulphate 
groups, therefore being less hydrophilic and less water soluble compared to the lambda and iota 
carrageenan. The lambda carrageenan has the highest number of sulphate groups and does not have 
any 3,6-anhydrogalactose units, thus being very hydrophilic and easily dissolve in water. The iota is 
somewhere in between the kappa and lambda regarding water solubility (Thevenet, 2009).  
 
Figure 1- chemical structure of the three main carrageenan types; Kappa, iota and lambda (Zia et al., 2017) 
When a carrageenan dispersion is heated, the carrageenan will start to dissolute, and the carrageenan 
particles will hydrate resulting in an increasing viscosity. At a certain temperature, the viscosity will 
reach its maximum called the swelling peak temperature from where the viscosity will start to 
decrease as the swelling particles will start to dissolve. The swelling peak temperature highly depends 
on the dissolution medium used as the presence of especially salts will influence the swelling peak 
temperature and gelling temperature. As a rule of thumb, the swelling peak temperature increases 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
 
with increasing salt concentration (Thevenet, 2009).  
When the carrageenan dispersion is cooled below the gelling temperature, the carrageenan will form 
water gels. The 3.6-anhydrogalactose units play a central role in the gelling mechanism as they are 
required to form a double helical network, which is the primary structure of the gel. Due to the 
absence of 3.6-anhydrogalactose in lambda carrageenan, no gelling will occur in this carrageenan type, 
nevertheless the dispersion will have an increased viscosity which is useful in many applications. Kappa 
and iota carrageenan form the strongest gels with potassium and calcium ions, respectively.  
If the gel is heated above the melting temperature followed by a cooling below the gelling 
temperature, the water gel will be reproduced, hence carrageenan has thermo-reversible properties 
(CP Kelco, 2001).  
1.1.2 Pectin  
Pectin is extracted from peels of citrus fruit mainly lemon, orange, lime and grapefruit.  
The production of pectin can be described through four main unit operations; extraction, purification, 
precipitation and optionally further de-esterification and/or amidation. The pectin is extracted from 
the peels with acidified hot water.  After the extraction process, the extract is filtrated followed by 
precipitation with IPA. Subsequent the precipitation, the process might include a de-esterification 
and/or amidation depending on the pectin product. Finally, the pectin is dried, ground and blended 
with other ingredients (mainly sugar) to form the final pectin product. (CP Kelco, 2005). 
Pectin has a backbone of α (1→4) linked D-galacturonic acid of which partial of the carboxyl groups are 
esterified (figure 2). The ratio of esterified galacturonic acid groups to total galacturonic acid groups is 
called the degree of esterification (DE) and determines whether a pectin is high methylated (HM) or 
low methylated (LM). HM pectin and LM pectin have a DE above and below 50 %, respectively (Sharma 
et al., 2006). 
 
Figure 2- chemical structure of pectin (Thevenet, 2009) 
The LM and HM pectin differ in optimum gelling conditions and therefore have different applications. 
For HM pectin, the amount of soluble solids such as sugar and salts together with pH and temperature  
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
 
are the crucial factors in gelling mechanism. HM pectin gels at high levels of soluble solids and require 
low pH (pH<3) before gelling(Thevenet, 2009).  
LM pectin requires divalent cations (mostly Ca2+ ions) to gel and can gel in a broader pH range (pH 2.8-
7) compared to the HM pectin(CP Kelco, 2005). In contrast to HM pectin, LM pectin does not require 
low water activity, thus being appropiate in low sugar application (Han et al., 2017). The gelling 
mechanism for LM pectin is described throug a egg box model. The Ca2+ ions are held between two 
pectin chains as shown from figure 3.  
 
Figure 3 - egg box model describing the gelling mechanism of LM pectin with calcium ions(Lara-Espinoza et al., 2018) 
 
The solubility of pectin highly depends on soluble solids, pH, temperature and ion strenghts. Pectins 
are soluble in water but lump formation easily occur when mixing with water. These lumps can be very 
diffucult to dissolve when first formed.  Consequently, different approaches to dissolve the pectin can 
be used mainly by high speed mixing together with increasing temperature. At QC department of CP 
Kelco, the pectin is often disolved in a small amount of IPA before addition of water - avoiding lump 
formation.  The presence of high levels of solids and salts will decrease the solubility of pectin 
(Thevenet, 2009)

 The complete removal of the alcohol is not possible. Consequently, the amount of alcohol residues in the 
final product must be considered from a safety and quality perspective. From a quality perspective, the 
sensory quality of the pectin and carrageenan might decrease as the presence of IPA, MeOH and/or EtOH 
might lead to off flavors. Furthermore, the carrageenan and pectin must meet the purity specifications 
set by the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) for being salable (JECFA, 2009). 
From a safety perspective, the potential risk of IPA, MeOH and EtOH should be evaluated. For this 
purpose, the International Community of Harmonization (ICH) has performed a risk assessment of 
various residual solvents and categorized them into three classes; 
• 
Class I solvents are characterized by being a human carcinogenic or strongly suspected to be 
human carcinogenic, and these solvents should be avoided.  
• 
Class II solvents are characterized by being nongenotoxic animal carcinogens or possible 
causative agents of irreversible toxicity, and these solvents should be limited.  
• 
Class III solvents have low toxic potential 
MeOH is categorized as a class II solvent whereas EtOH and IPA are class III solvents (ICH, 2011).  
From Commission regulation no 231/2012, limits for residual solvents in food additives are listed. The 
limit in pectin is 1 % of MeOH, EtOH and/or IPA singly or in combination whereas it is 0.1 % for 
carrageenan (European commission, 2012). These limits are identical with purity limits set by JECFA. No 
limits for waste water as these results are for internal use. 
Furthermore, one has to take into consideration certain religious demands as halal products are not 
allowed to exceed 1 % ethanol or they will not be labeled as halal products (Alzeer and Abou Hadeed, 
2016). In order to meet these regulatory requirements, the method must therefore be capable of 
determine if they exceed 1 % for pectin and 0.1 % for carrageenan. Therefore, the sum of limits of 
quantifications (LOQ) for pectin and carrageenan must not exceed 1 % (10 mg/g) and 0.1 % (1 mg/g), 
respectively.

1.3 Headspace GC-FID – theory and optimization strategies 
In this thesis quantification of alcohol residues is determined by the use of static headspace (HS) 
sampling, followed by separation with gas chromatography (GC) and finally detection with flame 
ionization detector (FID). The principles of these techniques and optimization strategies will be described 
in the following sections (1.3.1, 1.3.2 and 1.3.3) 
1.3.1 Static headspace sampling 
In a static HS sampling, the sample is placed in a GC glass vial and incubated at a given temperature and 
time sufficiently to yield equilibrium between the gas and sample phase. During sample incubation, the 
alcohols will easily diffuse to the headspace due to alcohols high volatility - thus making them very 
suitable for HS sampling. The equilibrium distribution is described through a partition coefficient (K). K is 
termed as the concentration of analyte in the sample phase over concentration of analyte in gas phase, 
hence compounds with low K values are more likely to diffuse to the gas phase compared to compounds 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
with high K values (Tipler, 2013). K values for the alcohols of interest in a water/ air system at 20 °C are 
seen from table 1 in section 1.2. In general, alcohols have a very high K values in water/air systems. The K 
value decreases with increasing polarity in a water/air system thus MeOH has the lowest K value (5600) 
and TBA the highest K value (8300) (table 1). 
The main optimization strategy in headspace sampling is to increase the concentration of the analyte in 
the headspace (lowering K), thus improving the sensitivity of the method. To increase the concentration 
of the analyte in HS, multiple approaches have been studied and found effective. One approach is to 
simply raise the incubation temperature. This is a very effective and simple way of increasing the 
concentration in headspace especially for analytes with high K values such as the case with alcohols 
(Penton, 2015). However, it should be considered that an increased temperature often leads to 
catalyzation of chemical reactions among sample and solvent components, so the composition of the 
sample might change and decomposition of the analytes occur (Penton, 1991). In pectin samples, one 
must be aware of MeOH formation from the methyl esters when raising the incubation temperature. 
Furthermore, by increasing the temperature, diversity of the headspace might be increased, and 
compounds of no interest might interfere in the chromatogram causing bad separation and/or 
integration problems.  Another approach is to increase the sample volume as the headspace will 
decrease and concentration of analytes will concentrate. This approach is  found to be very useful in the 
case of analytes with very low k values and not a very effective for analytes with high K values as the 
affinity to the solvent is high (Tipler, 2013). Finally, a very used approach is to modify the sample matrix. 
A very common modification in alcohol analysis is applying “Salting-out-effect”.   When salting out, salt is 
added to the sample solvent e.g. water, thus the solubility of the alcohols in the solvent decreases and 
concentration in headspace increases (Tobitsuka, Ajiki and Nouchi, 2009).  
As mentioned in section 1.1.1 and 1.1.2, the gelling and dissolution of pectin and carrageenan highly 
depend on salt concentration, therefore this approach might cause problem. Nevertheless, this approach 
will be investigated together with, incubation temperature and time, solvent type and sample volume. 
 
1.3.2 Gas chromatography 
From the GC vial, a sample from the headspace is taken and injected through a septum into the GC 
system. In the GC system, the sample is mixed with carrier gas, which commonly is hydrogen, helium or 
nitrogen. In this thesis, the carrier gas is helium. The sample is led with the carrier gas to the column. In 
split mode, only a fraction of the sample will enter the column. This fraction is described through a split 
ratio, which describes the amount of sample leaving the system to the amount entering the system 
==PAGE:16==
 
15 
 
(Restek, 2002). It enters the column, which in this case is a capillary column where the components are 
separated due to affinity to the column together with the boiling point. As MeOH is the compound with 
highest polarity and columns for alcohol analysis in general are nonpolar, it will have the lowest affinity 
to the column and eluate as the first compound followed by EtOH, IPA and TBA. See table 1 for the 
boiling points and K values. The column and its coating composition are of major influence. Multiple 
reference methods for analysis of residual alcohol are given and from these different columns are 
recommended. In JEFCA method, a DB-1 column, which is coated with 100 % dimethylpolysiloxane, is 
recommended for the analysis(JECFA, 2006). In USP 467 method, a G43 phase column, which is coated 
with 94 % dimethylpolysiloxane and 6 % cyanopropylphenyl, is recommended (USP, 2016). In the current 
control method, a DB-5 column, which is coated with 95 % dimethylpolysiloxane and 5 % phenyl, is used 
for the analysis. In this thesis it is desired to implement a ALC-1 column. The composition of this column 
is proprietary and therefore no information on the composition is given.  The column has been 
developed for alcohol determination in blood samples thus being very specific for alcohol analysis.  
Various column parameters have an impact on the separation and effectiveness. A very important 
parameter is the polarity of the column. Among the mentioned column, the DB- 1 column has the lowest 
polarity (no phenyl groups) followed by the DB-5 and G43 columns. The composition for the ALC-1 
column is unknown, but Agilent Technologies categorize this column type in the mid-polar range, thus 
being the most polar among the columns mentioned. As alcohol are polar compounds, the affinity to the 
ALC-1 column will be higher compared to the other columns mentioned.  
 
Another important parameter is the film thickness of the column.  Small film thickness yields lower 
retention time, whereas larger film thickness yields long retention time. This fact should be considered 
when chosen the right column. As alcohols are very volatile and have low boiling points, hence having 
low retention time, a large film thickness would be appropriate as this will improve the separation 
(Zeeuw, 2014). However, decreasing the film thickness will increase the risk of column bleeding and this 
should therefore be considered in column selection (Zeeuw, 2014). DB1, DB-5, G43 and ALC-1 have a film 
thickness of 5.0, 1.5, 1.8 and 3.0 µm, respectively. Figure 5 compares the columns mentioned, their 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
composition, polarity and film thickness. All mentioned columns have identical column diameter and 
length, which are 0.32 mm and 30 m, respectively.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 5 - comparison of columns (DB-1, DB-5, G43 and ALC-1) on chemical composition, film thickness and polarity.  
 
Some GC settings are crucial for the performance of the GC method. First, the temperature of the oven is 
important. When increasing the temperature, the retention time and peak width will decrease. However, 
increasing the temperature might result in bad separation due to too fast elution of the compounds. If 
the retention time for the compounds vary a lot, a temperature gradient can be developed. As the 
alcohol of interest (see table 1) are relatively chemically similar, the development of a temperature 
gradient might not be necessary, and the method will therefore be isocratic if possible. 
Second, the flow is a crucial parameter. As in the case with temperature, increasing the flow will result in 
a decreased retention time and peak width. The optimal method gives narrow symmetry peaks without 
fronting or tailing peak effects and compounds eluate as fast as possible with base line separation.  
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
In this thesis split ratio, oven temperature and carrier gas flow will be investigated in method 
optimization together with the implementation of the newly provided ALC-1 column.  
1.3.3 FID detection 
When the compounds of interest enter the FID, the compounds are mixed with hydrogen and led to a 
flame tip, which is surrounded by high flow of air. An igniter remote lightening of the flame and the 
sample enters the flame and burns, thus producing ions, which 
are collected by the collector and recorded as a signal. Figure 6 
schematics the composition of the FID.  
 
The FID responds to all organic compounds and the signal is 
proportional to the number of carbon atoms. Consequently, 
MeOH with the fewest carbon atoms (C=1), and TBA with 
highest carbon atoms (C=4) have the lowest and highest FID 
signal, respectively. From figure 7, the response for the alcohols 
of interest is seen in the same concentration. As seen from the 
figure, the signal increases with the increasing number of carbon 
atoms. The observed signal (figure 7) is approximately equal to the number atoms, hence the signal for 
TBA is four times higher than the signal of MeOH.

proportional to number of carbon atoms in the compounds. 
==PAGE:19==

As the FID only detects carbon atoms, it can only be used for detection and quantification of organic 
compounds in the sample.  Furthermore, it should be noted, that the FID detector is not capable of 
identifying the sample compounds. For this purpose, a mass spectroscopy (MS) detector in scan mode 
with NIST database is commonly used and will be used in this thesis whenever identification of unknown 
compounds is required. As the FID only detects organic compounds, no signal from water is seen, hence 
the FID is a good detector for samples containing water - such as waste water samples. To ensure there is 
no water condensation in the FID system, the FID temperature must be above the boiling point of water 
(100 °C). Most FID are run at 250 °C or even higher temperatures (Dressler, 1994). In this thesis, the FID 
settings will not be investigated further, and the settings from current method will be applied.

2.3 HS-GC-FID  
2.3.1 Current method 
The sample is directly weight into a HS GC vial (Agilent Technologies – amber, 20 mL). Specific sample 
weights can be seen from table 2. 50 µL of 2 % IS TBA is added to the sample. While placing the vial on 
vortex mixer (approx. 8000 rpm), 10 mL of deionized water is added. The lid (UltraClean, 18 mm 
screwcap) is closed and the sample is further mixed for 10 seconds (approx. 3000 rpm). Samples are 
resided in incubator (Agitator, Gerstel, MP2) for 10 minutes at 60 °C and 1000 µL of headspace is 
sampled in split mode (20:1) and transferred to GC-FID system (Agilent Technologies, 7530A model). 
Carrier gas is helium held at constant flow of 5 mL/min. Separation on a DB-5 column (length: 30 m, I.D: 
0.53 µm, film thickness: 1.5 µm) and isocratic temperature of 4 minutes at 35 °C. Detailed GC-FID settings 
can be seen from appendix 1. The areas of alcohols are found by integration software (ChemStation, 
Agilent Technologies). 
For waste water samples, 10 mL of waste water is added to the GC vial followed by 50 µL of TBA. The 
sample is mixed and analyzed  with HS-GC-FID parameters described above. 
Samples are analyzed in duplicates. 
A standard curve prepared in deionized water spiked with concentration levels of 0.1, 0.5, 2.0 and 5.0 
mg/vial is performed once every 3th month and linear regression analysis is used for the quantification of 
alcohol in the samples.

2.3.2 New method 
A sample of 0.2 g is directly weight into a HS GC vial (Agilent Technologies – amber, 20 mL).  1 mL of

analyzed in singlets.

3.1 Preliminary studies 
A large variation in IS (tert-butanol) response for especially carrageenan samples has been observed. As 
the natural content of alcohol in the samples does not follow the IS variation, the quantified alcohol 
concentration in the products is not correct (figure 10). The variable level is in general observed in spiked 
concentrations and not for the natural content of the products.  
 
 
Figure 10 - double determination of an unspiked carrageenan sample with IS (TBA). The area for IPA does not vary among the two 
measurements whereas the spiked amount of IS TBA differs among the two measurements.  
The QC laboratory assume that the challenges with the variating levels of the IS in carrageenan samples is 
caused by high viscosity in this sample matrix. To investigate if this hypothesis is correct, the relationship 
between sample viscosity, alcohol response and CV (indicator of precision) of the triple determinations 
were conducted.  
3.1.1 Viscosity 
Viscosity of a 1 %, 2 % and 3 % solution of carrageenan, HM pectin and LM pectin were measured at 60, 
70 and 80 °C. For all matrices, the viscosity decreases with increasing temperature (figure 11). As the 
viscosity for both carrageenan and pectin is known to decrease with increasing temperature in the 
temperature range of 60-80°, these results are similar to known viscosity properties of carrageenan and 
pectin (Masuelli, 2011)(Gladkowska-Balewicz, Norton and Hamilton, 2014)). Comparing the viscosity 
among the sample matrices, the 2 % and 3 % solutions of carrageenan and HM pectin have higher 
viscosity (≈10000 cp) compared to the 2 % and 3 % solutions of LM pectin (<1000 cp).  For LM pectin, a 
drastic reduction from 60 to 70 °C is observed in the 3 % solution (figure 11c). As the viscosities of LM 
==PAGE:32==
 
31 
 
pectin in general are very low, the measurements of these sample were in the lower range of the largest 
spindle (spindle 1). Measurements in this range are uncertain and therefore this drastic reduction might 
be an imprecise measurement rather than a LM pectin viscosity characteristic. Furthermore, these results 
show that viscosity highly depends on solution %. In order to decrease the sample viscosity in HM pectin 
and carrageenan, it is more effective to reduce the sample solution with 1 % rather than raising the 
temperature with 20 °C.   
No swelling peak temperature can be observed for carrageenan(figure 11a) and therefore the swelling 
peak temperature must be below 60 °C. Studies by (Liu, Huang and Li, 2016) suggest that the swelling 
peak temperature of carrageenan is in the range of 30-40°C and therefore no peak in the viscosity curve 
for carrageenan is observed (figure 11a) as this is based on temperature interval of 60-80°C. 
The same but unheated solutions (1 %, 2 % and 3 %) were measured on the HS-GC-FID three times after 
incubation at 60, 70 and 80 °C. The alcohol responses and CV calculated from the triple determination 
are seen from table 8. When having a multivariate data set, the use of a PCA is useful in illustrating the

3.2 Method optimization 
3.2.1 Sample preparation 
3.2.1.1 DMSO addition 
It was found from previous sections (3.1.1 and 3.1.2) that high viscous samples are characterized by 
having bad precision (high CV). As it is more complicating to dissolve a sample weight of 0.3 g (3 % 
solution) compared to 0.1 g (1 % solution), the bad precision might be caused by poor homogeneity in 
high viscous samples. Therefore, the sample homogeneity was investigated.  
To improve the homogeneity of the samples, the effect of adding DMSO (10 %) was investigated. DMSO 
is a highly polar dissolution medium and known to dissolve pectin very well (Walter, Buren and Sherman, 
1978). Furthermore, DMSO is commonly used in HS-GC-FID methods because of its high solubility of 
polar compounds together with high boiling point/low vapor pressure  (189 °C, 0.42 mmHg (20 °C) 
(Sigma-Aldrich, 2019). DMSO contain carbon (C2H6OS) and will therefore yield a FID signal if entering the 
GC-FID system but due to the high boiling point/low vapor pressure together with an incubation 
temperature of 60 °C,  this is not very likely. In contrast to deionized water, DMSO often contain 
impurities from production and as it has high solubility it will easily dissolve impurities from pipettes tips, 
glass ware etc. thus contaminating the sample. It is important that these impurities do not influence the 
integration of the alcohols of interest. The DMSO applied was analyzed and small impurities were 
observed but none of these elute near the alcohols of interest.  
 Another commonly used HS-GC-FID solvent is DMF. Due to DMF being more toxic than DMSO (ICH, 
2011) , addition of DMF has not been studied in this thesis.  
A visual inspection of the sample homogeneity with and without DMSO addition is seen from figure 17. 
As mentioned in section 1.1.2, lump formation easily occurs and can be difficult to combat. When the 
samples are dissolved in water, the lump formation is easily seen especially in the top and bottom of the 
vial in pectin samples (figure 17).  For carrageenan, large evenly distributed lumps are observed. The 
homogeneity is significantly improved when adding DMSO to the samples, and the samples with DMSO 
do not have any visual lumps and coagulation.  
 
==PAGE:38==

Figure 17 - illustration of sample homogeneity with and without DMSO (10 %) as dissolution medium: from left: HM pectin with DMSO,

From figure 18, 19 and 20, CV for each of the solutions % and matrices are seen. When adding DMSO, CV 
decreases significantly compared to only using water as dissolution medium. For carrageenan (figure 18), 
the CVs for a 1, 2 and 3 % solution without DMSO are approximately 2, 5 and 10 times higher, 
respectively. For HM pectin, the improved precision is mainly observed in the 2 % and 3 % solutions, 
reducing the CV with more than 50 % (figure 19) – with the exception of MeOH in the 2 % solution and 
IPA in the 3 % solution. In LM pectin, the addition of DMSO do not seem to influence the precision of the 
method. This is as expected as LM pectin does not have high viscosity and variating levels of IS has not 
been observed in this matrix. As observed from figure 17, lump formation in LM pectin is observed when 
only applying deionized water for dissolution medium. These results indicate that these lumps are fully 
dissolve after incubation. This is in line with theory, as LM pectin is known to be easily dissolved when 
heated in comparison to HM pectin (Thevenet, 2009).

Overall, these results indicate that the large variation in IS is not caused by the high viscosity itself but 
rather by the poor homogeneity in high viscos samples. In the current method, the IS is directly spiked to 
the pectin and/or carrageenan sample followed by addition of deionized water. These results suggest 
that the spiked amount of IS is not evenly distributed in the sample after mixing and incubation and the 
IS might even be trapped in one of the lumps formed - complicating the diffusion of IS to HS. This 
suggestion explains why the natural content is not varying while the spiked content is. This is due to the 
natural content being more evenly distributed in the sample than a spiked content, which is placed in 
one little spot of the matrix during the sample preparation. 
Even with DMSO addition, the CV increases with increasing sample weight. Comparing the 1 % solution 
with a 3 % solution, the CV increases approximately from 2 % to 6 %, 1 % to 3 % and 3 % to 8 % for 
carrageenan, HM pectin and LM pectin, respectively (figure 18, 19 and 20). Using a 1 % solution would 
give the most precise method (CV≤2 %). In determination of LOD and LOQ, the instrumental LOD and 
LOQ is divided by the sample weight yielding the LOD and LOQ in the sample. Using a sample weight of 
0.1 g (1 % solution) would therefore yield a higher LOD and LOQ than using a sample weight of 0.3 g (3 % 
solution). In order to have a precise method capable of detecting in appropriate levels, it is decided to 
have a sample weight of 0.2 g (2 % solution) with addition of 1 mL DMSO followed by 9 mL of deionized 
water for all matrices - ensuring a uniform sample preparation.  
3.2.1.2 Sample volume 
From figure 21, the responses when adding 10, 12.5 and 15 mL of water are seen. Changing the sample 
volume from 10 mL to 15 mL, the responses of MeOH, EtOH, IPA and TBA decreases approximately from 
5 to 2, 10 to 7, 22 to 18 and 130 to 90, respectively. Therefore, increasing the sample volume will result 
in a lowering of the method sensitivity. Decreasing the sample volume would probably increase the 
response. As the viscosity would increase when adding less water, reducing sample volume from the 
current 10 mL has not been investigated.  
As mentioned in section 1.3.1, this strategy is not suitable for analytes with high K values which is the 
case with alcohols in water/air system. The alcohols are fully soluble in water and has high affinity to 
water thus adding more water does not catalyze diffusion to HS.  
As it is desired to develop a method with high sensitivity, adding more water is not a good optimization 
strategy for this method. The method will therefore use the same sample volume as the current method 
which is 10 mL.

3.2.1.3 Salt addition 
The effect of adding salt was investigated. When applying salting-out-effect, different salts can be added. 
Some salt will yield higher ionic strengths than others and therefore be more effective in this approach. 
As mentioned in section 1.1.1 and 1.1.2, LM pectin gels with divalent cations, and some carrageenan 
types form strong gels with potassium and calcium ions. Furthermore, salt decreases solubility of pectin. 
Therefore, one must be cautious when chosen the salt for these matrices. Salts of divalent cations and 
especially salts of potassium and calcium will therefore not be appropriate for this method even though a 
salt such as potassium carbonate is very effective and commonly used in salting out of alcohols (Xie, Yi 
and Qiu, 2013). A monovalent cation would be preferred. Studies by Mangione and coworkers have 
investigated the gelling potential of kappa carrageenan with potassium and sodium and found that no 
gelling occurs in the presence of sodium and suggest this is due to difference in ion radius among 
potassium and sodium (Mangione et al., 2005). Therefore, it was decided to add NaCl. From figure 22, 
the effect of adding 0.50 g, 1.0 g and 1.5 g of NaCl per vial is seen. As expected, the response increases 
with increasing salt addition for both MeOH, EtOH, IPA and TBA. When the NaCl weight is increased with 
0.5 g, the response is approximately increased with 20 %-point for all alcohols.

3.2.2 GC-FID program 
By the use of a full factorial design at three levels (33), the effect of changing the GC-FID factors (carrier 
gas flow, oven temperature and split ratio) has been studied by evaluating the resolutions, peak 
symmetries and time of analysis when varying these factors. The results of these studies are seen in the 
following sections.  
3.2.2.1 Column (ALC1) testing 
Before execution of the developed full factorial design, the new ALC1 column was tested by evaluating 
the retention times and separation of MeOH, EtOH, IPA and TBA together with acetone – a known 
interfering compound in waste water samples. 
In testing of the new ALC1 column, it was clear that acetone and TBA did not have baseline separation 
and they elute so close that separation was not possible (figure 27). Therefore, it was decided to 
introduce a new IS. JECFA method suggest the use of 3-methyl-2-pantanone as IS (JECFA, 2006). This IS 
has from previous optimization at QC laboratory of CP Kelco been studied and they found a rather high 
retention time of 10 minutes. As it is desired to have an IS which elutes close to the compounds of 
interest, the 3-methyl-2-pantanone will not be further studied. Moreover, 3-methyl-2-pantanone is a 
ketone and not an alcohol, and the chemical structure vary from the alcohols of interest thus not being 
an appropriate IS. USP suggest the use of tert-butanal  as applied in current method (USP, 2016).  
 The use of NPA as IS is commonly seen in residual alcohol analysis, supported by Boswell and Dorman 
suggesting that NPA is a more precise IS for alcohol analysis by GC-FID compared to TBA (Boswell and 
Dorman, 2016). When applying reference methods such as methods from USP and JECFA, one must be 
aware that these methods are developed for determination of various residual alcohols including NPA 
and therefore suggesting NPA as IS in these methods is not an opportunity. 
As NPA is not present in the matrices and is chemically similar with MeOH, EtOH and IPA, NPA was 
purchased and tested. On the ALC1 column, NPA elutes as the last component with fine separation from 
acetone. Final chromatogram will be shown in results of method comparison (figure 36).  
Not only does the replacement of TBA with NPA yield better separation, it also simplifies the sample 
preparation because TBA has a melting point of 26°C – hence being difficult to pipette at room 
temperature (Chemspider, 2018). In comparison, the melting point of NPA is -126 °C (Chemspider, 2018). 
From this point of the optimization, NPA replaced TBA as IS.

3.2.2.3 GC parameters  
The GC parameters of carrier gas flow, oven temperature and split ratio were determined by the use of a 
full factorial design at three levels (33).  
Figure 28 illustrates the effect of the different factors on resolution of EtOH and IPA and indicates 
whether the factor is significant or not. The green area shows the effect on the resolution, when the 
factor is changed from low to high level, and the other factors are held constant. The confidence interval

should be removed from the MLR model configured by the statistical software MODDE.  
 Figure 28 shows that when flow is changed from low (5.0 mL/min) to high (15 mL/min) level, the

the resolution with approximately 1. Again, this is due to faster elution when increasing the column 
temperature.  Changing the split ratio from low (10:1) to high (30:1) and interactions of the factors do 
not seem to have any significant effect on the resolution. These non-significant factors are therefore 
deleted from the MLR model. The same trend is seen from all the resolutions. From appendix 6, plots for 
all resolutions can be seen.

3.3.1 Linearity 
From figure 33, the linearity for MeOH, EtOH and IPA are seen. Linear regression line equation and 
coefficient of determinations (R2) are seen in each of the figures. The slope of the regression line 
describes the sensitivity of the method. A large slope indicates a great sensitivity because a small change 
in concentration yields large change in response. As seen from the regression line equations, IPA and 
MeOH have the highest (slope=1.06) and lowest (slope=0.24) sensitivity, respectively. As mentioned in 
section 1.3.3, the response in a FID is proportional to the number of carbon atoms in the analyte, hence 
this result is expected. 
At QC laboratory of CP Kelco, the R2 for method linearity must be above 0.990. Notably, all three R2 are 
safely above this limit.

3.3.5 Robustness 
The robustness of the new method was evaluated by a fractional factorial design at two levels (72) – low 
level (L) and high level (H). The factors variating are incubation temperature (L=55 °C, H=65 °C), 
incubation time (L=8 min., H=12 min.), the amount of DMSO added (L=0.5 mL, H=1.5 mL), mixing of 
sample (no mixing and full mixing), and closing of lid (improper closing of lid and proper closing of lid).  
From figure 35 the method robustness is seen. For MeOH (figure 35a), none of the factors in the design 
set up are significant. This indicates a very robust method and shows that the IS (NPA) is able to adjust in 
changes of these factors. For EtOH ( figure 35b), the incubation temperature and the amount of DMSO 
added are significant factors. For IPA (figure 35c), the addition of DMSO is a significant factor. Therefore, 
one must be precise when adding DMSO to the sample as the method is sensitive to this analysis step.

In these results some uncertainties must be noted. The method validation was based on one spiked 
concentration level and it would have been preferable to include various concentration levels - ensuring 
valid results in a broader concentration range. Furthermore, all matrices were spiked with MeOH, EtOH 
and IPA due to not being present in all matrices. Therefore, the validation was based on spiked samples 
and not only the natural content. Moreover, the samples were weight with a precision of 0.2 g ±0.0010. 
The precise sample weight was not noted and used in the calculations. This yields some uncertainty of 
the results and would theoretically yield a higher CV in precision study due to the natural content varying 
as a function of the sample weight. As this will result in an overestimation of CV, this uncertainty is not 
crucial for the method validation.  
In bias study, the certified reference material was only applied in deionized water and it would have been 
preferable to investigate bias with certified reference material in the matrices too.  
 Finally, the newly implemented IS, NPA, was introduced after optimization of sample preparation and 
therefore not included in the determination of equilibrium time and temperature. Due to the good 
method validation results, it is assumed that equilibrium is reached for NPA using incubation of 10 
minutes at 60 °C. Furthermore, the chemically similarity with especially IPA supports this conclusion.  
 
Overall, these results executed during this thesis period, has improved the precision of IS as no high 
variation of IS levels is observed. Baseline separation is seen for all compounds and the desire to have a 
uniform sample preparation is met. Therefore, these results suggest implementation of the new method 
in QC laboratory of CP Kelco.

4 Conclusion 
It was found that the variating level of IS in carrageenan was caused by poor homogeneity in the 
samples. In optimization of the method it was found that the addition of DMSO is effective in improving 
homogeneity and precision. Sample preparation of the optimized method therefore include addition of 1 
mL of DMSO followed by 9 mL of deionized water. It was found that using an equilibrium time of 10 
minutes at 60 °C, equilibrium for all matrices were reached. Using the ALC-1 column, the use of NPA as IS 
was a better choice than current IS TBA ensuring baseline separation among all analytes in the samples. 
For GC-FID program, the parameters of flow, split ratio and oven temperature were optimized. The 
optimal settings were found to be flow of 15 mL/min, split ratio of 20:1 and oven temperature of 50 °C.  
From method validation, a fine linearity of MeOH, EtOH and IPA was found (R2>0.990). The LOD and LOQ 
for MeOH, EtOH and IPA was found to 0.11 and 0.36, 0.10 and 0.33, 0.06 and 0.19, respectively. The sum 
of LOQs are 0.88 mg/g, which is below the regulatory limits. Working ranges for carrageenan, HM pectin, 
LM pectin and LBG were determined as 0.11-25, 0.1-25 and 0.06-25 mg/g for MeOH, EtOH and IPA, 
respectively. For waste water samples the working range was found to 0.002-0.5, 0.002-0.05 and 0.001-
0.5 mg/mL for MeOH, EtOH and IPA, respectively. The thesis found fine repeatability with CVs below 5 %, 
4 % and 3 % for MeOH, EtOH and IPA in all matrices. Intra-reproducibility study, all HorRat values were 
within the acceptable limit of 1.5, and an ANOVA showed no significant difference among the three 
operators. The recoveries were in the range of 95.0- 108 % for all matrices. From robustness study it was 
seen that the method is robust to variation in incubation time, mixing and improper closing of the lid. 
The method was found sensitive to incubation temperature on EtOH quantification. Furthermore, the 
quantification of EtOH and IPA was found sensitive to amount of DMSO added. Therefore, one must be 
careful in this sample preparation step.  
Comparing the new and current method, the new method has improved precision and accuracy. For HM 
pectin, LBG, the LOD and LOQ have been decreased whereas for carrageenan and LM pectin the LOD and 
LOQ have been increased. Paired t-tests comparing the quantification using current method found the 
mean differences equal to zero on a significant level of 5 %.  
 
Finally, it can be concluded that the work performed during this thesis has combat the challenges with  
high variation in IS levels for carrageenan samples, ensured baseline separation of the compounds  and  
yielded a uniform sample preparation.  
These results suggest implementation of the new method for quantification of alcohol residues in QC 
laboratory of CP Kelco (Lille Skensved).

5 Perspectives 
Addition of salt was found effective in increasing the sensitivity of the method but not applied in new 
method due to prolonging the sample preparation time. If the regulatory limits are reduced and/or CP 
Kelco has the desire to quantify in lower levels than the new method is capable of, this approach could 
be further studied.  
Furthermore, DMSO was only investigated in the amount of 1 mL per 0.1, 0.2 and 0.3 g of sample. Adding 
more DMSO might make it possible to increase the sample weight and therefore lowering the LOD and 
LOQ. This approach could be further studied if the levels of LOD and LOQ are not sufficient.  
Finally, the method includes double determinations of all samples. As little variation is seen in the double 
determination, it could be further studied whether a single determination is enough. This would reduce 
the sample preparation time with 50 %.

Appendix 3: Validation procedure 
Introducing a new control method: 
- 
Method validation and quality control for chemical analytical methods 
Purpose and Scope 
Purpose 
The purpose of this document is to ensure and document, that analytical methods used as control methods 
are fit for purpose. It is essential to know the performance characteristics of a control method, to 
determine if these characteristics meet the requirements of the analytical application. This work is called 
method validation. Moreover it is important to perform quality control on a routinely basis to ensure that 
the method continues to be fit for purpose.   
Scope 
Method validation should be carried out prior to introduction of new methodologies, or after modification 
of an existing methodology. Following method validation, criteria for routinely quality control work should 
be defined.  
 
This procedure is applicable when introducing new analytical control methodologies or modifying existing 
methodologies.  The procedure is not intended for functionality testing methods or microbiology methods.  
 
Description 
The work that needs to be carried out in order to ensure that the method is fit for purpose is well described 
in the guideline “The Fitness for Purpose of Analytical Methods” published by EURACHEM, 2014. Where 
nothing else is stated the guideline is used as reference. For more detailed information about a specific 
topic it is recommended to consult the guideline.    
  
Before issuing a new control method, we should define requirements of the method. Is low 
detection/quantification limit critical (and how low is “low”)? What is the intended working range? And 
how accurate should the method be? When the requirements have been defined, all the performance 
characteristics of the method should be investigated. For analytical methods the parameters of interest 
are:  
 
Linearity  
Working range 
Detection- and quantification limit 
Accuracy 
Precision  
==PAGE:77==
 
76 
 
Robustness 
 
The number of parameters which needs to be tested may vary, depending on the type of control method. It 
is normal to include all parameters for quantitative methods, but for qualitative testing less parameters 
may be sufficient. When defining the number of parameters needed for validation, it is necessary to 
consider any regulatory requirements/organizational guidelines which may specify parameters that must 
be included. Such documents may also set requirements for the levels of the parameters. If all of the above 
mentioned parameters are included in validation, the parameters defined in ICH guidelinei is covered 
(although the terms used to describe the characteristic parameters are not perfectly consistent).   
 
Working process 
An overview of the validation process is given in Figure 1. It is important to consider any requirements to 
the method before evaluation of the performance characteristics. This should be done in order to know 
what to aim for, but also to avoid developing a too sophisticated method. After appropriate method 
development, the performance characteristics should be evaluated and the results should be evaluated and 
compared to the defined requirements. If the method performance meets the requirements, the method is 
fit for purpose and can be implemented as a control method. If not, it is possible to relax the requirements 
or improve the method through further method development.

Linearity  
Linearity describes how well the calibration curve follows a straight line, where the response is proportional 
to the analyt concentration. Linearity can also be used to describe the overall method, and not only a 
calibration graph, depending on the methodology. For chemical analytical methods, linearity is normally 
related to the calibration graph. However, some analytical methods does not show linearity, and in such 
case other calibration functions may be appropriate (e.g. exponential functions) – then another relevant 
evaluation of the calibration must be considered.  
When evaluating a linear calibration graph, a number of parameters can be included. Sensitivity is related 
to the slope, and a “large” slope means that the method easily detects a small change in concentration (i.e. 
great sensitivity). The sensitivity may not be constant over a large range; consequently the linearity is also 
limited to a smaller range. Very often the sensitivity is constant at “low” concentrations, and then 
decreasing at “higher” concentrations. In such case it may be useful to apply several calibration graphs, 
which could be chosen depending on the level of the analyt in the samples to be measured.  
After visual inspection of the calibration graph, the correlation coefficient (R2) should be calculated. This 
must be close to 1, and should be given with an appropriate number of digits. It is realistic to aim for a 
minimum criteria for R2 = 0,990 for chemical analytical methods. Also the residual sum of squares must be 
evaluated (as plots of residuals), and these should be evenly distributed. Trends in the distribution of 
residuals may indicate that linear regression is unsuitable for the data, and another type of regression may 
be more useful, see Figure 2.  
 
Figure 2: Plots of residuals. A: Evenly distributed residuals (no trends). B & D: Trends in residual distribution. 
Linearity should be determined from analysis of at least 7 levels, which should be made as double 
determinations. When good linearity is confirmed, a more limited number of levels can be used for routine 
calibrations after implementing the control method. Linearity investigations can be made on either pure 
standard solutions, or on natural samples including standard addition/natural samples at different levels.                        
Working range 
The working range must be defined and evaluated. The lower limit of the range is defined by the detection 
limit, but the upper limit should be investigated and compared to the needs. This could be done by 
examination of linearity above the upper calibration point, e.g. if the curve can be extrapolated to a certain 
extent, or if dilution of the sample is necessary. The range could also be defined from the specifications, 
e.g. the range could be the specifications +/- 20%. An illustration of the working range is given in Figure 3, 
where “A” illustrates the instrument linear- and working range, and “B” illustrates the method working 
range (including sample preparation, e.g. dilution).

It is important to choose the right calibration level, in order to achieve the lowest quantification limit. A 
calibration curve at low level will normally end up with a lower quantification limit, compared to a 
calibration curve covering a large concentration range. However, it should be noted that the calibration

Bias is preferably determined by analysis of reference materials measured multiple times. A minimum of 10 
measurements must be made, e.g. 1 material measured 10 times or 5 materials measured 2 times. It is 
recommended to use more than 1 reference material if possible. If an appropriate reference material is not 
available, it is possible to use spiked samples. In this case it is necessary to keep in mind that the spiked 
analyts may be easier to extract than naturally bound analyts.  
An alternative is to compare the new method with an existing reference method, where the result from the 
reference method is set as the true value. However, this approach should only be used when it is 
reasonable to consider that the reference method delivers “true” results.    
Bias should be investigated at relevant levels, depending on the application. For methods where the level is 
in the same area for all samples, it is relevant to determine bias at that level. For methods which cover a 
large range, it can be relevant to investigate bias at low, intermediate and high level.

Precision  
The precision of a method is described by the repeatability and reproducibility, where the repeatability is 
the variation achieved when everything about the measurement is kept constant. Reproducibility (or 
intermediate precision) is the variation that is found when variations in e.g. calibration standards, analyst 
and time are varied. Both repeatability and reproducibility are expressed either as the absolute standard 
deviation (s), or the coefficient of variation:

Examination of both parameters can be made on either reference materials or natural samples. The 
samples should be as homogeneous as routine samples. The levels to investigate should be the same as 
those chosen for bias analysis.  
Repeatability is investigated from at least 10 determinations at each level, analyzed in one batch within a 
short timeframe.  
Reproducibility studies should be investigated with as many variations as possible. The study should involve 
at least two analysts and should be performed over at least 5 days. If more than one piece of equipment is 
available for the analysis, all of those should be included in the study. At each level at least 10 
measurements should be carried out (5 days x 2 analysts). The strength of the reproducibility study is 
enhanced by using more samples, different calibration standards (batches), and more analysts over a 
longer time period.              
Robustness 
Robustness (or ruggedness) expresses how well the system remains unaffected by small variations in the 
method conditions. Such variations could be (but is not limited to): 
Extraction time 
Mixing of sample and solvent 
Incubation temperature 
pH 
Sample size 
Sample matrix
