Master Thesis
Abstract
This master thesis investigates Novo Nordisk’s (NN) ability to knowledge share between In-
vestment Protfolio Group (IPG) Capital Expenditure (CapEx) projects and explains how NN
actively can change their processes of collecting useful knowledge and experiences from CapEx
projects with the ambition of improving CapEx project performance.
Research shows that project performance can be enhanced using a combination of formal, semi-
formal and informal inter-project knowledge sharing (IPKS) channels. The main contribution
to the existing literature is the identiﬁed gap questioning how an organisation can ensure that
lessons learned (LL) are not merely captured and shared but proven to be used.
To address this gap, multi-methodology, including semi-structured interviews, Future Workshop
and quantitative data analysis, has been used to map and research NNs challenges to empirically
investigate IPKS.
Findings show that most of NNs knowledge is mostly anchored in individuals happening on an
informal basis. NNs IPG CapEx projects are currently lacking a well-deﬁned process of IPKS -
how to collect, store, share and ensure the usage of LL. Therefore, this study proposes a formal
IPKS process, to initiate the use of LL. This is judged necessary to ensure frequent IPKS and
further encourage the informal implicit IPKS, shown to be preferred and most effective. Finally,
the purpose of the proposed solution is to foster an active usage of IPKS in NN IPG CapEx
projects and ultimately improve CapEx project performance.
Key stakeholders and representatives from the Investment Project Ofﬁce and Novo Nordisk
expressed high appreciation towards the ﬁnal recommendation. As a result, it was suggested
by IPO to have this solution tested and implemented in a large networking event coming up in
the fall, where all project practitioners are invited to discuss CapEx project execution matters
and knowledge share. Finally, the thesis output also paves the way for continuation in a future
study.
Key words: inter-project knowledge sharing, cross-project knowledge transfer, lessons learned
usage, lessons learned repository, CapEx projects

Our Motivation
This master thesis marks the ﬁnal milestone in the journey towards achieving a Master of Sci-
ence degree in Industrial Engineering and Management from the Technical University of Den-
mark (DTU).
The research topic of this thesis is inspired by the complexity and challenges involved in CaPex
projects in the pharmaceutical industry and how a company can use an organisational structure
to optimise project execution. The ﬁeld of interest is further narrowed down to how structural
knowledge sharing can make a company more efﬁcient in project execution of CaPex projects.
The research will be based on a case company, Novo Nordisk, who are a global leader in the
pharmaceutical industry and will be supported by existing academic literature. The authors of
the paper have collaborated over several months with the Investment Project Ofﬁce at Novo
Nordisk, the department responsible for providing efﬁcient, reliable and transparent investment
project execution. Interviews, workshops and informal meetings have been held together with
the Investment Project Ofﬁce, where focus has been on understanding the current status, chal-
lenges and identifying opportunities for further improvement.
Novo Nordisk has recently made an organisational transformation, where one part of the new
strategy was focused on optimising project execution of the organisation’s investment portfolio.
Recent market pressures and increased competition has led to a strategy with focus on becoming
more cost competitive, whereby improved CapEx projects related to knowledge sharing is one
of the initiatives. The research of this paper is therefore intended to help the Investment Project
Ofﬁce through on-ﬁeld research and academic literature.

Novo Nordisk’s IPG CapEx Projects are not Performing
Sufﬁciently
A key driver of a successful company is the knowledge that it has within its workforce and how
this is passed on between them [1]. Large investment projects are a strong driver of knowledge
generation within companies and can develop important insights from the challenges faced dur-
ing the projects and lessons learned from unexpected outcomes. The value derived from these
insights and lessons learned is dependant on whether this generated knowledge is transferred
within the workforce of the company, so that it is not anchored in a few individuals. Such large
investment projects, should therefore not be considered in isolation, but instead an organisation
should be focused on how the insights and lessons learned can be applied in the core business
and for future investment projects, emphasised by Söderlund and Tell (2011) [2]. In addition,
research shows that by implementing a structure for sharing inter-project knowledge (IPKS), a
company is able to reduce signiﬁcant project related costs via reduction of redundant work and
avoiding previously occurred errors [3].
At Novo Nordisk (NN), a global pharmaceutical leader, the large investment projects (further
referred to as Capital Expenditure (CapEx) projects) that fall under the category of Investment
Portfolio Group (IPG) have continued to fall below their key performance indicators (KPI) re-
lated to costs, timeline and beneﬁts, as illustrated in below ﬁg. 1 [4]. Since the IPG investments
involve substantial investments for the company, of approximately 37 billion DKK out of ap-
proximately 40 billion DKK from 2015-19 and a total of 93.2% of the NN total CapEx budget,
it is highly important for NN to ﬁnd a solution that can improve these KPIs and thereby project
performance [4–8].

==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis
Figure 1: Illustration of NN IPG CapEx project performance on cost,
schedule, and beneﬁt KPIs from 2015-2019 [9]
As these IPG CapEx projects are relatively large complex projects, there is an increased difﬁ-
culty to reaching the projects goals as these projects have an increased risk of failing, becoming
unexpectedly costly, time consuming or not adequately beneﬁcial [10, 11]. Knox et al (2017)
highlight the complexity of such large projects by stating that these are generally proven to de-
liver a year behind schedule and with a 30% increase of total projects costs [12]. It is worth to
mention, that the constant failing in reaching set KPI can also be a result from too ambitious
goal setting, however, this is out of scope in this study.
This inadequate project performance in NN IPG CapEx projects led to the creation of a Project
Management Ofﬁce (PMO) within Product Supply at NN, called Investment Projects Ofﬁce
(IPO). IPOs aim is to improve CapEx project performance by setting up effective, reliable and
transparent project execution [13]. The IPO senior director further elaborates:
“The purpose of IPO is precisely to ensure efﬁcient and professional project execution at NN
and the idea is that we do so through gathering experiences from other projects and other
activities in general and become better at anchoring and sharing these going forward and
making them available.” (see Appendix D5)
Effective IPKS is found to avoid unnecessary reinventions that are costly and time consuming
and thereby addressing poor performing KPIs related to costs, timeline and beneﬁts to ulti-
mately create a competitive advantage [3, 14, 15]. Similarly, Wasiyo et al (2010) emphasised

the need for structural IPKS to avoid the loss of knowledge and insights from projects after the
team dissolves upon project completion. This lack of collection and usage of such knowledge
leads to loss of knowledge and thereby diminishing the organisation’s ability to increase per-
formance of future projects [16]. Patriotta et al (2013) shows that companies are commonly
struggling with obtaining effective IPKS on a global scale and suggest a central place where
knowledge is managed and shared to overcome this issue [17]. This could be via a PMO in
charge of such a function, as currently done by NN, but how should a PMO manage the task to

IPKS is one way of improving project performance but the inﬂuential factors on IPKS are many,
further addressed in section 3. Designing a solution to accommodate all of the identiﬁed barriers

and improve the process of IPKS, should lead to a signiﬁcant reduction of project related costs
and avoidance of redundant work [3]. There include many different drivers that impact IPKS,
such as the organisational culture, trustworthiness and organisational/structural mechanisms as
explained by Wiewiora (2011) [18].

1.2
Research Questions
To follow a structured approach in understanding, identifying issues and deciding on an inter-
vention to make tangible improvements to Novo Nordisk, three research questions were deﬁned.
These are brieﬂy discussed below.
Research Question 1 (RQ1): "How is inter-project knowledge sharing (IPKS) currently hap-
pening in Investment Portfolio Group (IPG) Capital Expenditure (CapEx) projects at Novo
Nordisk (NN)?"
With this ﬁrst RQ the aim is to build the foundation of this thesis and describe the current sit-
uation. To answer this question, a mapping of the IPKS landscape at NN must be done. This
requires an analysis of current processes, document landscape, and knowledge sharing chan-
nels acquired through interviews with IPG CapEx project key stakeholders at NN, including
project practitioners (PPs), Investment Project Ofﬁce members (IPO) and Steering Group mem-
bers (StGs). Alongside the internal qualitative research a review of the literature on IPKS is
conducted to search for current practices within this area of study.
Research Question 2 (RQ2): "Why is IPKS in NN IPG CapEx projects such a problem when
everyone recognises the importance of it?"
In the second RQ the current situation is evaluated based on the foundation identiﬁed in RQ1.
How is IPKS currently functioning at NN? Is there any problem with IPKS in NN IPG CapEx
projects? This RQ aims at identifying the pain points regarding IPKS if present and further
mapping the identiﬁed issues through a thorough root cause analysis based on an analysis of the
qualitative research.
Research Question 3 (RQ3): "If IPKS in NN IPG CapEx projects is the answer to enhanced
project performance, how can such processes be improved?"
RQ3 is questioning the actual need for IPKS processes - formal, informal and semi-formal.
This also raises the question to the importance of IPKS - if IPKS is so important, why is it
failing so often? And how can IPKS be improved to such a degree to ultimately improve project
performance in NN IPG CapEx projects? Is the answer to enhanced project performance to
be found within the failed IPKS or does NN need to take a different approach to address this
challenge? To answer these questions, a detailed analysis of the identiﬁed pain points from
the ﬁndings from the previous two questions must be conducted to understand the underlying
issues. The analysis is further facilitating the decision on a pressing gap and the most suitable
intervention necessary to address this gap. Methods such as workshops and further interviews
will be utilised. Finally, a solution will be developed to close the identiﬁed gaps alongside a rec-
ommendation to further improvements - critically questioning whether the solution to enhanced
project performance is to be found within IPKS or not.

Deﬁning Inter-Project Knowledge Sharing
3.1
Deﬁning Inter-Project Knowledge Sharing
IPKS is the creation and distribution of learnings between projects [3, 14]. Effective sharing of
knowledge across projects avoids unnecessary reinventions that are costly and time consuming
[3, 14]. Similarly, Wasiyo et al (2010) argues that when a team dissolves upon project comple-
tion, the lack of knowledge collection and its usage is shown to be a challenge and leads to loss
of knowledge and thereby decreases the organisation’s ability to increase performance of future
projects [16]. Though it is commonly agreed that IPKS does effect the projects performance,
there is little recognition of how the effectiveness of IPKS can be measured [41]. As Albert
Einstein put it:
"Not everything that can be counted counts, and not everything that counts can be counted."[42]
Knowledge is generally divided into tacit and explicit knowledge, where the usage of explicit
knowledge can be measured by counting the shared documents, read documents, etc. However,
the effects in terms of saved cost, saved time or increased beneﬁts remain difﬁcult to measure.
Tacit knowledge sharing remains difﬁcult to measure as well, but is viewed as the most im-
portant way of transferring knowledge [43]. Matoskova (2016) elaborates on current methods
for measuring knowledge sharing, however he points out that the validity of these need further
investigation [44]. "What gets measured gets done" is a general saying in business, however,
this is shown to be tricky in knowledge sharing measurement methods and therefore it will not
be further elaborated in this literature review as it is outside the literature scope.
IPKS can be categorised in to three main areas of literature: formal, semi-formal, and informal,
[45]. A formalisation dimension is illustrated in ﬁg. 19 below. These areas will function as the
structure of this literature review and entire thesis.
Deﬁnitions of each category are ﬁrstly described followed by a deﬁnition of inclusion criteria
in each section. The sections covering informal, semi-formal, and formal include IPKS chan-
nels covering both tacit and explicit knowledge, thereby focusing on the channels of how the
knowledge is to be shared, rather than solely categorisation of the knowledge. This is chosen as
it is viewed to be important to investigate how the knowledge is activated in the best way and
thereby transferred to the needed location [45]. Throughout each section, main theories, meth-
ods and guidelines are elaborated and discussed - why or why not use formal, semi-formal and
informal - leading to a conclusion and recommendation of best practices and considerations,
which should be made on this matter. Finally, identiﬁed literature gaps are listed in regards to
covering the topic of IPKS.
Alavi and Leidner (2001) has a slightly different viewpoint, where the knowledge channels are
categorised in formal, informal, personal and impersonal [46]. Wiewiora (2011) also categories
IPKS into "Relational mechanisms" and "Technology mechanisms" [18]. The categorisations
by Alavi and Liedner (2001) and Wiewiora (2011) have been deselected, since most of the

3.1.1
What is a Lesson Learned?
When investigating IPKS you will quickly stumble upon the term ’lessons learned’ as project
management organisations such as PMI, PRINCE2 and numerous others books and articles state
their research and guidance on how to best practice the "art" of collecting and sharing lessons
learned (LL) from projects to obtain continuous improvement and increased quality of future
projects [21, 48–50]. Before elaborating on current literature in regards to IPKS it is important
to ﬁrstly deﬁne a LL, as it is closely related to the former matter. LL include elements from
both organisational learning and knowledge management [3]. LL are more speciﬁcally deﬁned
by the Project Management Institute (2017) as:
[LL] "The knowledge gained during a project which shows how project events
were addressed or should be addressed in the future for the purpose of improving
future performance." [21]
Similarly PRINCE2 (2017) speak of both learning and lessons but do not use the speciﬁc term
LL. However, the deﬁnitions of the former by PRINCE2 are highly similar to the deﬁnition of
LL by PMI:
[Learning] "Previous or similar projects should be reviewed to see if lessons
could be applied." [48]
[Lessons] "The goal is to seek opportunities to implement improvements during
the life of the project." [48]
In this thesis the deﬁnition by the Project Management Institute (2017) will be used when refer-
ring to LL. Moreover, it should be noted that LL can both be positive and negative knowledge
gained during a project [3, 21]. From this deﬁnition it can be concluded that to make use of LL,
it is required that an experience to learn from is present, a transferable place to exploit the LL
and ﬁnally, an intention to improve [36]. These requirements should include a well functioning
IPKS channel to ensure sharing of the LL.

general can be limited by ﬁve factors [40]:

[58]. It is therefore important to foster a culture where trust is central, as this ultimately results
in more IPKS.

By overcoming the barriers of IPKS or exploiting these to an advantage this would
increase IPKS and thereby lead to enhanced project performance.

knowledge via databases managed by a PMO [55]. These viewpoints are further endorsed by

people from previous similar projects or externals with relevant experience to create a "Lessons
Log". The "Lessons Log" is then to be used as input for almost every upcoming phase of the
project described in PRINCE2 (2017) [48]. PRINCE2 (2017) further lists a few examples of
what such a "Lessons Log" should include: "results of audits and project reviews". Apart from
this, PRINCE2 is very thrifty with the information of how to implement lessons learned from
other projects, hereby excluding the environmental factors needed to obtain effective handover
of lessons learned. As PRINCE2 (2017) mainly cover the area of LL via the recommendation
of creating Lessons Reports, PRINCE2 (2017) is categorised as dealing with LL in a formal
matter [48].

though the only channels mentioned directly in relation to IPKS are "codiﬁcation of explicit

this [60, 65]. Employees must be motivated to use the technology used to codify and share
explicit knowledge [66]. It is therefore important when choosing to implement an IPKS solution
based on a formal Information and Communication Technology (ITC) (e.g a LL database) where
knowledge is codiﬁed, that the interaction and communication needed to bring the stored LL
into use is included in the solution.
Apart from codiﬁed knowledge, which is typically related to the usage of information and
communication technology, formalisation of knowledge sharing can also be in the format of
structured and frequently planned meetings, seminars, conferences, training, etc. which all are
a formal facilitation of tacit knowledge [67]. Formal channels such as planned monthly LL
meetings have shown to improve IPKS and conducted case studies also conclude that this is a
preferred method of IPKS [3, 60]. The effect of formalising meetings is further investigated
and has proven its importance to IPKS [51]. Formal IPKS channels have the advantage that
these are particularly effective to share tacit knowledge and might be the only way [18, 40]. By
creating a process for sharing tacit knowledge, organisations can ensure a frequent handover of
LL and this can further lead to socialisation, networking and ﬁnally create a "snowball effect"
to increase IPKS. On the other hand, formal knowledge channels such as databases might be
an effective way to share knowledge but can also become a place where knowledge is hidden if
not put into use.

databases cannot stand alone as data relevant to a project is not always easily found and accessed

3.3
Picking the right Bandage
From this literature review it is clear there is no one way of performing IPKS as it is highly
complex matter including multiple stakeholders and processes. The complexity further makes
it difﬁcult to measure the direct effect of actions made to improve this process. Therefore, the
process of IPKS must be individually tailored to the case or company who are facing challenges
within this area. The tailoring of such a solution must endorse the accessibility and usage of
the LL, which is still to be researched thoroughly [16, 39]. Such a solution need not only to
be focusing on a formal, semi-formal, or informal channel but ideally also include the aspects
of trust and culture. Though a formal channel is a good starting point as it ensures some IPKS
and further increase the focus on IPKS, which can lead to the creation of other semi-formal and
informal channels. The channels where the IPKS are to be conducted further need to be both
connected to parallel projects and LL from ﬁnalised projects, as both may contain valuable LL
for an upcoming project or a project which is being executed. Adding to this, barriers of IPKS
must also be addressed to ensure increased project performance by the effects of IPKS. This
also implies that management should trust their employees and vica versa to share LL without
the fear of being punished [56].
It can thereby be concluded that the set hypothesis:
H1: "Enhancing project performance via formal, semi-formal and informal IPKS
channels must all be addressed to obtain optimal effects."
is partially true, as to enhance project performance can be done via a combination of formal,
semi-formal, informal IPKS channels. It is, however, also important to consider the organisa-
tional culture and trust aspects to obtain optimal effects.

4.4.3
Product Supply Project Management Network
The PSPM network is a platform for sharing knowledge of best practices within project man-
agement. This network is maintained by IPO who is responsible for organising various events
year round facilitating the networking between project management professionals in NN by
sharing experiences on project management and related disciplines. The main goal with the
PSPM network is to support excellence in the execution of projects by drawing links across
the wider project portfolio by facilitating the opportunity for PMs in PS to network and share
knowledge with other key project roles. The networks is also meant to act as a bulletin board
for the rules and tools of project execution in PS [13]. Amongst the various events organised
in the PSPM network is the PM-Summit, a full day event, where everyone in the network is
invited to the NN headquarters.

The good story
Some of the interviewed stakeholders expressed a positive experience towards IPKS in the qual-

therefore important to emphasise the fact that this is mainly happening on a local scale, where
multiple CapEx projects are executed simultaneously and thereby do not include the knowledge
existing at other sites.
On the other side, are all the other CapEx projects, whom do not share site with any other
projects. As this study only included local projects, it is only possible to make a concluding
observation that IPKS in IPG CapEx projects is missing on a local level, however, going beyond
local level to global level, does not make the situation less complex.
Even though the good story does exist, all of the interviewees at NN agree that IPKS is highly
dependant on informal processes such as individuals network, personal experiences, and indi-
viduals knowledge of where to ﬁnd the needed knowledge.

In relation to informal IPKS, there is no general tendency on how well it functions as both state-

The Chosen Pain Points
NN CapEx projects face multiple challenges as illustrated in ﬁg. 39. Many of these are depen-
dant on each other and to gain focus of which are the most essential challenges for NN CapEx
projects the pain points are clustered into main pain points based on the conducted interviews
and the most essential, present in each interviewed group, are further investigated. In total four
main pain points have been selected to further investigate by application of the so-called ’ﬁsh
bone diagrams’. The ﬁsh bone diagrams (originally referred to as Ishikawa diagrams) are causal
diagrams developed by Kaoru Ishikawa to create an overview of the potential causes of a spe-
ciﬁc event/problem [71]. Using the ﬁsh bone diagrams causes are grouped into main categories
to classify the identiﬁed causes to create a better overview. The main categories chosen in this
study are people, management, processes, measurements, culture and tools. In the following
each of the selected pain points are explained using the ﬁsh bone diagrams to identify the root
causes to each identiﬁed pain point.

Paint Point no. 4 - Missing process for using lessons learned
Missing process for using lessons learned is the fourth and last pain point chosen in this study.
In ﬁg. 39 it is seen that this pain point covers the underlying pain points no. 4.1-4.3. These pain
points are judged to be underlying issues to the overarching pain point and therefore included

The word ’using’ is highlighted, as it is wishful to emphasise that there is no process for actually
using LL. As already mentioned there is a lack of process for collecting LL, as this is only
done at the G4 presentation as a ’tick-in-the-box’ activity (see section 4.4.1). Further, there
is a lack of activating the collected LL, which speaks for the missing process for sharing LL
and ensuring that they are actually put to used when shared, since there is no process in place
addressing this. The LL are collected at G4, then stored (or hidden away) in Clarity and the
IPG secretary and basically never seen again. Some IPO members do have the access to see
them, but PPs do not necessarily have access, even though they are actually the employees who
need the LL the most - to learn from past experience to improve their own project execution
by either preventing potential risks already happened in other projects or use the opportunity to
bring in the good learnings as well. Furthermore, there is no process nor guidance in the start-up
phase in a CapEx projects, which can be one factor in the overall missing process for using LL.
As for the current process, LL are collected at G4, which is the second last gate in a CapEx
project and this is the ﬁrst and only time the PPs have to address the topic of LL throughout
the entire project execution - at least formally. It is therefore questionable, whether having the
ﬁrst and only LL collection process at such a late stage is value adding or not. Currently, no
resources are allocated to manage a LL process, potentially causing the lack of focus on this
area. Furthermore, the PEM does not comply with modern standards of the PMBOK in regards
to a LL process, further emphasising the lack of focus on the knowledge sharing area through
LL, as this has been addressed in the newer versions of the PMBOK (see section 4.4.1) [21].

The third pain point chosen to focus on is pain point no. 6 depicted in 39. The root cause
analysis to this pain point is illustrated in ﬁg. 43. Through observations and the qualitative

One of the key reasons to the high turnover rate is the issue that some PPs are basically jobless
upon project completion as their might not be another CapEx project to join when their own

PPs, however, there is no such function within NN nor are there any future plans to establish one.
The difﬁculty establishing this function has led the company to move away from this thought
and stick to current methods. The current method is that whenever a PP is done with his or her

are happening on a global level, which means that a PP should be ﬂexible in terms of location, if
the desire to continue in a new project is present. If ﬂexibility is out of discussion, the respective
PP is often demoted within NN. This said, being demoted is never a milestone anyone is aiming
for, and therefore a high number of PPs leave NN as they are in high demand in other companies

4.6
What does the Future Hold?
As stated by Züst and Troxler (2006) ’It is the business of the future to be dangerous’ [25]. You
never know, what the future brings, but one thing that is certain is the constant change. When
considering changes, it is highly likely that these changes will affect the future in some way or
another [25]. Therefore, this sections focuses on analysing the future to carefully evaluate the
impact it may cause to the system.
To assess future developments and trends in the environment, it is relevant to return to the initial
and underlying argument to this thesis addressed in section 1. Too many projects have failed
to meet their KPIs, which is one of the main reasons IPO was created - to facilitate for better
project execution through the establishing of an internal and central Project Management Ofﬁce
(PMO). If the same mistakes are repeated and CapEx projects KPIs are still not met, it might
raise the question to the function of IPO. Allocated resources can be questioned and worst case
scenario might be a relocation of these resources to be prioritised in another function within
NN.
An expected higher turnover rate is also subject to change in the near future as studied by
Alkhatib (2016). In his research it is shown that Millennials (those born between early 1980s
and mid 1990s) are noted for their high job turnover. Given the fact that this generation is be-
coming the largest group in the workforce it can become highly complicated for organisation to
withhold Millenials and therefore important to ﬁnd measures to avoid losing too much knowl-
edge when an individual leaves the organisation, which is shown to be costly [72]. This is a
challenge to the entire CapEx project execution in NN, since most of the knowledge is currently
anchored in individuals (see section 4.5). If turnover rate is high it is even more important to
make sure that the acquired knowledge is anchored within the company to avoid the risk of
repeating mistakes and spending unnecessary resources to replace the lost knowledge, which
ultimately affects the project execution and its performance.

whether it will be used and create any beneﬁt and impact.

5.2.1
Evaluating & Criticising the Future Workshop Method
The overall idea was to establish a common understanding of the problem via co-creation and
thereby ﬁnd a solution everyone understood and agreed upon. The FW was good for estab-
lishing a common understanding and great for initiating co-creation. However, this workshop
method was highly time consuming and the ﬁnal output is questionable. In general almost all
FW phases were more time consuming than planned and expected, which led to a more ’rushed’
execution than anticipated. One of the reasons to this were the thorough discussions led by the
participants. It seemed that the topic of lessons learned had not been discussed much in plenum
on beforehand, which may be one of the underlying reasons to the long time spent on discussing
the topic and their attitude towards LL and what actions could and should be taken.
Throughout the fantasy phase, it was expected to encounter a higher level of creativity and see
some more "crazy" utopian ideas in the fantasy phase such as for example conducting virtual
meeting with VR goggles, however, the participants were relatively realistic from the beginning.
The reason to this might be the facilitators "fault" or that the participants started to write things
down too fast and thereby reducing their creativity level. This may have had a negative effect
on the development of solution ideas.
Moreover, the facilitator role should not be underestimated. It is complicated and the FW is
highly dependent on the maturity of the facilitator. Since both facilitators were relatively new
in the role, this may have had a big impact on the quality of the FW output.

The suggested solutions are a subset of what is needed to obtain an ideal state of IPKS in the NN
CapEx project environment. The solutions are mainly limited to improve the IPKS channels and
not the cultural and trust aspects, which also are essential to obtaining an ideal state regarding
IPKS [18, 38, 53].
The identiﬁed ’Green Culture’ is a noteworthy challenge the NN CapEx projects are facing,
where the issue of learnings from failures are only partially represented in the LL shared across
projects. The ’bad’ learnings may in many cases be of great value by inspiring others to ﬁnd
ways to avoid similar mistakes. Changing the ’Green Culture’ to a culture, where sharing mis-
takes are accepted is therefore important to address to make full use of all LL and ensure trans-
parency throughout the entire organisation. Numerous other barriers to obtain a good learning
environment are essential to be addressed in a solution to improve IPKS. These include lack of
time, lack of learning resources, lack of an award for learning, limited options to make deci-
sions and many more as described in section 3 also identiﬁed as issues for NN CapEx projects
in ﬁg. 44.
The suggested solutions in section 6.2 have certain prerequisites, which must be in place for
these solutions to reach optimal function apart from the aspect of culture and trust within the
organisation. As identiﬁed in section 4 IPO is currently lacking strategic clarity and thereby
lacking the ’how’ to reach their vision and mission. This results in the observed lack of knowl-
edge of IPOs purpose among interviewees and a a clear direction towarsds their ambition among
IPO employees. The lack of a strategic clarity further leads to a non existent communication
strategy describing how the users are to be informed of the IPOs services and how to best make
use of these.

(and thereby relevance) and simply to gather these before they are forgotten or the project
team dissolves [21, 50]. To incorporate LL as a natural part of the project execution certain

Gate 3, and Gate 4. G5 is not viewed as relevant as the project team dissolves after G4. Upon

Lessons Learned Central
It is recommended that LL are captured in a format, which is accessible and understandable
for both senders and receivers. This could be done via a LL database where LL are collected,
prioritised and documented [3, 36]. The prioritisation can be an issue as the question emerges
of how do you select the most relevant learning which will provide value? To do this, it is
recommended to identify the employee roles whom are to add LL to the database and scoring
the learnings in some deﬁned way. Though scoring is recommended, it creates the issue of how
to score LL. Suggestions could be via impact on the project in terms of cost, time and beneﬁt,
but such scoring might not be transferable to other projects that are not sufﬁciently similar or
identical. Therefore, scoring of LL is not included in the following.
It is recommended to store LL in a central place, as the risk of solely anchoring the LL in
individuals is judged to be risky, as the turnover rate of key PPs in NN CapEx projects is high.
Furthermore, this is supported by the previous literature as it allows easy access to LL, see The
Project Management Institute (2017), PRINCE2 (2017), Enken et al (2015), and Sanz (2020).
[21, 48, 55, 76].

Discussions & Reﬂections
This section is aimed at discussing the process of this master thesis to critically reﬂect upon the
process and how it could have been designed for the better. This will further include a descrip-
tion of the main implications of the suggested solutions and a reﬂection of the thesis process.
Lastly, an elaboration of what future work is suggested to further investigate the literature area
of IPKS and to ﬁnally implementing a solution.
8.1
Reﬂecting the Proposed Solution
This master thesis investigates how NNs CapEx projects can become better at using individual
knowledge to obtain a better project performance and thereby contribute to closing the identi-
ﬁed literature gap - the insufﬁcient focus on how to ensure that LL are used in other projects
(see section 3). It was found that a formal process of collecting, sharing and activating LL is
necessary to ensure a frequent use of LL. This does most likely include a central PMO, (in par-
ticularly in NNs case) responsible for implementing and executing such a process. This would
result in knowledge being anchored at a central place and an assigned responsible able to high-
light and share the most important LL to relevant CapEx projects. The overall structure of such
a formal process of activating LL in NN CapEx projects is illustrated in section 6.2.
Though a central PMO owns the main responsibility of IPKS, the PPs must also be able to
easily access and contact other relevant PPs within the CapEx project environment to enable
the usage of LL by sharing and guiding each other. This should be encouraged by cultivating
communities of practice, as already recommended in in the solution proposal (see section 6.2.3).
The recommended community, the IPC, needs to be formalised with structured and frequently
scheduled activities to ensure that user activity is kept at a high level. The PMOs responsibility
is to ensure that the proposed process, the "Agile Lessons Learned" is well established via the
frequent LL gathering and LL guidance meetings to establish a connection to the projects and
facilitate IPKS.
Furthermore, the Agile Lessons Learned solution can be generalised and used in other compa-
nies since NNs PEM can be replaced with any other companies’ project model. Furthermore,
the points in time where a PMO should gather and guide the projects with use of LL can be
adjusted to ﬁt any new project model. Lastly, other companies are also recommended to anchor
LL in a central storage place and have a formalised cultivating community of practice to activate
the LL to ensure LL usage.
With a formal process in place ensuring activation of LL the general IPKS would be improved
and pave the way to enhanced CapEx projects performance.

8.2
Reﬂections on the Thesis Process
The process of this master thesis has both been inspiring, fun, and challenging. The close
collaboration with IPO and direct contact to employees working on currently running CapEx
projects gave insight into the actual challenges these stakeholders are facing on a daily basis.
Though the close collaboration leading to great insights, it has been a challenging journey. This
section is therefore dedicated to critically reﬂect on this thesis process - on what went well and
what could have been done better or differently.
This master thesis was largely effected by the COVID-19 since this led to an extended empirical
process, which ended the 13.04.2020 when the last semi-structured interview was conducted.
This extensive empirical process was due to the fact that many of the interviewees focus shifted
towards strictly focusing on managing their own CapEx projects, which where highly inﬂuenced
by the implications of the pandemic. This resulted in a difﬁculty scheduling many of the semi-
structured interviews and further, that the large majority of the interviews were held on Teams.
This compromised the trust between the interviewee and the interviewer as it became difﬁcult
to read each others true intentions/statements via a video conversation.
Furthermore, the ban of entering the premises of NN for non-business critical functions (includ-
ing this master thesis) and the ban of entering other NN buildings than the one each employee
where assigned to limited the master thesis access of daily competent feedback from IPO and
thereby made it troublesome to gain a rapid understanding of the researched NN case. The
extended empirical process affected the original idea to develop and test a prototype upon the
solution proposal. The initial idea was to make use of rapid prototyping and design thinking
methodology together with employees within the three deﬁned groups used to triangulate the
matters included in this study (IPO, PPs and StG). However, this was compromised and the
methodology of design thinking was instead used in the proposed implementation plan as de-
scribed in section 7 to design prototypes and test these before the ﬁnal designed solutions can
be successfully implemented.
Though COVID-19 had implications on the process of the project, the semi-structured interview
of both IPO, StG, PPs and external PBO gave a deep insight into the issues of IPKS and the
conﬁrmation that this is indeed a reoccurring topic. The gathered qualitative data therefore gave
a clear picture of the challenges that NN IPG CapEx projects are facing in regards to IPKS.

Answering the Research Questions
This master thesis was conducted to identify and develop a solution proposal in collabora-
tion with Novo Nordisk to increase their abilities to exploit knowledge gained from projects
and bring these into action in future projects to improve project performance. This was ﬁrstly
done through an investigation of NNs IPG CapEx project’s current situation by interviewing
key stakeholders with the main objective to identify the challenges NN is facing in regards to
IPKS.The most critical paint point, missing process for using lessons learned led to a solution
proposal, which incorporates a deﬁned process for LL gathering, storage of LL in a a central
repository and guidance processes describing how these LL should be put in to use.
Furthermore, this case study paves the way to increase the focus of the identiﬁed literature gaps
concerning how an organisation should ensure that lessons learned are not merely captured and
shared but proven to be used. The second gap concerns the lack of research on how IPKS
functions on a global scale and what in particular should be focused on to improve IPKS. This
case study further contributes to the literature by suggesting that a formal IPKS process could
initiate the frequent use of LL. For this suggestion to be proven valid further investigation and
testing of such solutions is needed.
This master thesis was guided by three research questions listed and answered in the following.
Research Question 1: "How is inter-project knowledge sharing (IPKS) currently happening in
Investment Portfolio Group (IPG) CapEx projects at Novo Nordisk (NN)?"
IPKS has for years been an issue in NN IPG CapEx projects and the increased focus on this
matter resulted in the creation of IPO in 2018.
Though IPO has the responsibility to act
upon this matter, this study found that Novo Nordisk’s ability to share knowledge between
IPG CapEx projects is highly dependant on each project participant’s personal network within
Novo Nordisk and the informal knowledge sharing conducted via these networks.
Research Question 2: "Why is IPKS in NN IPG CapEx projects such a problem when everyone
recognises the importance of it?"
Currently, project practitioners in NN do not know what to do about lessons learned. There is
a lack of enlightenment and requirements of where to share and access lessons learned. This
leads to occasional and informal knowledge sharing dependant on each individual’s network.
Findings from the current state analysis revealed that it is commonly agreed by the interviewees
that current IPKS is mostly non-existent. A few ﬁeld observations fall out of this category as
research showed that two local sites, where multiple major CapEx projects are being executed,
expressed good experience of IPKS. However, IPKS is found to be too dependant on network-
ing and informal processes creating a high risk for NN, as knowledge is mainly anchored in
Page 109 of 116
==PAGE:125==
Master Thesis
individuals, whom might leave the organisation. NN CapEx projects are therefore in need of
an increased formalisation of processes supporting IPKS to avoid losing knowledge when em-
ployees leave the organisation.
Research Question 3: "If IPKS in NN IPG CapEx projects is the answer to enhanced project
performance, how can such processes be improved?"
This study shows that IPKS can improve project performance, and therefore, it is found relevant
to investigate how IPKS processes can be improved in Novo Nordisk. However, it worth to men-
tion the complexity in measuring the actual effect of IPKS let alone as the project performance
depends on several inseparable factors.
Novo Nordisk’s CapEx projects are currently lacking a well-deﬁned process of IPKS - how LL
should be collected, how they should be stored, how they should be shared and ﬁnally how to
ensure their usage. Therefore, this study proposes a formal IPKS process, to initiate the use of
lessons learned. This is judged necessary to ensure frequent IPKS and further encourage the
informal implicit IPKS, shown to be preferred and most effective.
Finally, the proposed solution needs to be tested to conﬁrm whether it is the best way to improve
IPKS and thereby project performance.
