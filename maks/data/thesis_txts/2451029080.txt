Abstract 
The marine environment is a potent source of novel chemical compounds due to its vastness as well 
as the many diverse ecological niches found there. During a global expedition in 2006, 
Photobacterium galatheae S2753 was isolated from a mussel in the Solomon Sea. The strain 
produces numerous secondary metabolites with bioactive potential. Among these are the 
cyclodepsipeptides solonamide A and B, and the ngercheumicins. These depsipeptides interfere 
with the quorum sensing (QS) system of Staphylococcus aureus, hereby inhibiting virulence gene 
expression. Whilst this activity potentially makes them pharmaceutically valuable, it seems illogical 
that a marine bacterium targets the QS system of a bacterium not commonly found in the marine 
environment. This therefore prompted the question what the natural function of the solonamides 
is.  
To investigate their natural role, a series of mutants deficient in solonamide biosynthesis were 
generated during the project. The first step was to identify the gene responsible for solonamide 
biosynthesis. A putative sol gene was identified through antiSMASH analysis and deletion 
experiments confirmed that the identified gene is responsible for solonamide biosynthesis. Double-
deletion mutants, deficient in both holomycin, a potent antibiotic, and solonamide biosynthesis 
were also constructed.  
The sol deletion mutant culture extracts showed a reduction of the structurally similar 
ngercheumicins, indicating a biosynthetic link to the solonamides. To determine the possible role 
of the solonamides, several phenotypes of wild type and the single and double mutants were 
compared. The growth rates of WT and mutants were similar, indicating that the solonamides are 
not involved in growth nor do their production hamper growth. Similarly, no antibiotic effect of 
S2753 was induced by the absence of solonamides. Compared to WT, the Δsol and ΔsolM4 mutants 
showed no change in biofilm formation. However, the ΔhlmE mutants were slightly attenuated in 
biofilm formation. Further experiments are necessary to confirm a link between holomycin and 
biofilm formation.  
Genes related to DNA repair are located near the sol gene. An attempt was made to test the UV 
susceptibility of WT and mutants, but due to lack consistency in the experiments and time 
constraints, this was abandoned. It was also speculated whether the solonamides may be involved 
in defense or signaling, potentially between species or even kingdoms due to their structural

Resumé 
Det marine miljø er en potent ressource af nye kemiske strukturer pga. dets størrelse og de mange 
økologiske nicher fundet der. Under en global ekspedition i 2006 blev Photobacterium galatheae 
isoleret fra en musling i Solomon havet. Denne bakterie producer flere sekundære metabolitter med 
bioaktivt potentiale. Heriblandt er cyclodepsipeptiderne solonamide A og B, der forstyrrer quorum 
sensing systemet i Staphylococcus aureus og derigennem inhiberer udtrykkelsen af virulens-
associerede gener. De har derfor interessant farmakologisk potentiale, men fra et økologisk 
perspektiv er det mærkværdigt, at en marinebakterie interagerer med et system fra en bakterie, 
der ikke almindeligvis findes i marine miljøer. Dette rejste spørgsmålet om hvad den naturlige 
funktion af solonamiderne er.   
Det første trin i at undersøge deres naturlige rolle er at identificere genet der koder for solonamid-
biosyntese. Et potentielt sol gen blev identificeret via antiSMASH analyse, hvorefter dette blev 
bekræftet gennem knock-out eksperimenter. Dobbelt-knock-outs, hvor både holomycin- og 
solonamid-biosyntese var inaktiveret, blev også konstrueret.  
Et muligt link mellem solonamiderne, ngercheumicinerne (der strukturelt ligner solonamiderne) 
og holomycin blev observeret. Fremtidige eksperimenter bør foretages for at undersøge dette link 
nærmere. Flere karakteriserings-eksperimenter blev udført med både single- og dobbelt-knock-out 
mutanter. Ingen ændring i vækst blev observeret for nogen af mutanterne. Ligeledes var der ingen 
ændringer i antibiotisk effekt. Dette indikerer at solonamiderne ikke er involveret i vækst, samt at 
holomycin alene er ansvarlig for S2753’s antibiotiske effekt. Der var indikationer på at holomycin 
spiller en rolle i biofilm formation, men flere eksperimenter er nødvendige for at bekræfte dette.  
Flere stress-relaterede gener er lokaliseret tæt på sol genet. Det blev forsøgt at teste hvor resistente 
mutanterne var overfor UV stråler. Pga. store variationer i eksperimenterne og mangel på tid, kunne 
der ikke konkluderes på dette forsøg. Eftersom solonamiderne strukturelt minder om signal-
molekylerne fra S. aureus’ quorum sensing system, er det muligt at deres naturlige rolle også er som 
signal-molekyler. Begge disse hypoteser er interessante hypoteser for videre studier.

1 Introduction 
The tradition of natural product discovery is rooted deep in scientific history, originating all the 
way back from 1928 when Alexander Fleming first discovered the antibiotic properties of penicillin 
(1). This strategy dominated from 1940-1960 in the so-called ‘golden era’ of antibiotic discovery 
(2). Later, synthetic drug discovery experienced increasing popularity due to technological 
advances with a focus on improving existing antibiotics (3). However, the rising issue of bacterial 
antibiotic resistance, along with the realization that upwards of 99% of the microbiological 
metagenome remained unexplored, prompted novel interest in natural drug discovery (2,4). 
Natural products gained their attention due to a number of reasons. First and foremost, they are 
sources of novel compounds that can be directly used within many industries such as medicine, 
agriculture, and food (5–7). Furthermore, they serve as an important source of inspiration; both for 
development of drugs through combinatorial chemistry but also for novel materials and devices 
(8,9). From an ecological point of view, they also provide insights into the complex mechanisms at 
play in nature.  
Another reason for the increasing interest in these compounds is the realization of how many that 
remain to be discovered. Advances in sequencing techniques and genome mining approaches 
played a huge role in uncovering biosynthetic potential (Reviewed in (10)). Natural product 
discovery also benefitted from increased interest into novel ecological niches.  Most drug discovery 
efforts were originally centered around soil bacteria and fungi. However, the marine environment 
is a promising source of novel bioactive compounds. This is the case for numerous reasons; first, 
and perhaps most intuitively, bodies of water cover ~70% of earth’s surface leaving room for an 
unintelligible number of inhabitants on both micro- and macro-scale. Microbial abundance in the 
oceans is estimated to reach 106 per mL in sea-water and 109 per mL in ocean-bottom sediments  
(11). Furthermore, extreme conditions such as low oxygen levels, high pressure, salinity and 
absence of light poses the need for highly adapted microorganisms resulting in an array of 
biodiversity, increasing the likelihood of discovering chemically unique natural products (12–14). 
Despite these promising features, marine microorganisms had received little interest until just a 
few decades ago due to difficulties in sampling and cultivation (11,15).  
 Bioactive potential of Photobacterium galatheae S2753 
An especially promising group for bioprospecting, is the bacterial family Vibrionaceae, including 
both the Vibrio and Photobacterium genus (16). Classically, the Vibrionaceae have been considered

either pathogenic or symbiotic bacteria. However, during a global research expedition in 2006, 
Galathea 3, hundreds of bacterial samples, hereunder ~300 Vibrionaceae, were collected and found 
to have antagonistic activity against the Gram-negative fish pathogen Vibrio anguillarum and the 
Gram-positive Staphylococcus aureus (15,17). One of the strains exhibiting antibacterial properties 
was Photobacterium galatheae, isolated from a mussel in the Solomon Sea (18). 
The antibiotic properties of S2753 can be attributed to the production of the known potent 
antibiotic holomycin (19).  Aside from antibiotic activity, the isolated strains were also tested for 
their ability to modulate the virulence gene expression of S. aureus (20). Antivirulence drugs have 
gained increasing interest as an alternative to traditional treatments with antibiotics. Since they do 
not target the viability of the pathogen, this alleviates the high selective pressure seen in antibiotic 
therapy (Reviewed in (21)). Consequently, this is thought to slow down the development of 
resistance which according to the World Health Organization is currently one of the three biggest 
global health threats (22). Two cyclodepsipeptides with antivirulence properties were identified, 
solonamide A and B (Fig. 1) (20). Collectively, these will be referred to as the solonamides 
throughout this report.

The cyclodepsipeptides are a diverse chemical group, characterized by a ring of amino acids and 
non-amino acid moieties, cyclized through an ester-bond. They have been isolated from both fungi, 
sponges, plants, and (marine) bacteria (23–26). Cyclodepsipeptides are a promising group of 
potential drug candidates for several reasons. First of all, they exhibit a range of bioactivity 
==PAGE:13==
Master thesis (30 ECTS) 
Spring 2019

11 
 
 
including, but not limited to, antivirulence, anticancer, antibiotic, and anti-inflammatory properties 
(Reviewed by (26)). Furthermore, the replacement of an amide-bond with an ester-bond renders 
them less sensitive to inactivation by host proteases, prolonging their half-life compared to classic 
peptide-drugs (27). The structural constraints, caused by the cyclic structure, can also improve 
receptor binding (28).  
The solonamides are mainly expressed during exponential growth and inhibit virulence by 
targeting the quorum sensing (QS) system of S. aureus (20). QS is a term that was first coined by 
Fuqua et al. in 1994 (29). QS is a form of communication commonly used by gram-negative bacteria, 
although some gram-positive bacteria also possess QS systems. The systems allow the bacteria to 
detect the population density through the production of species-specific signal molecules 
collectively referred to as autoinducers. These autoinducers can freely diffuse over the cell 
membrane and will therefore only accumulate when population density is high. This allows the 
bacterium to decide when to initiate gene expression and phenotypes of certain mechanisms to 
avoid wasting valuable resources. Virulence in S. aureus is controlled through QS (Reviewed in 
(30)); S. aureus produces an autoinducing peptide (AIP) which binds to a histidine kinase, AgrC, 
located in the cytoplasmic membrane. AgrC is part of a two-component system and, in the presence 
of ATP, AgrC autophosporylates. AgrC-P then phosphorylates the response regulator, AgrA, which 
in turn binds to the promoter region and promotes the expression of virulence proteins (31). The 
solonamides inhibit quorum sensing in S. aureus by binding to the histidine kinase ArgC, preventing 
the binding of AIP and, ultimately, the expression of the virulence proteins (Fig. 2) (32).

Aside from the solonamides, four other cyclodepsipeptides belonging to a known family of 
cyclodepsipeptides, ngercheumicin F, G, H, and I, are produced by S2753 (34). It is interesting that 
S2753, a marine bacterium, produces compounds that interferes with the QS system of a 
Staphylococcus, which is not very abundant in the marine environment (20). This gave rise to the 
question of what the ecological role of the solonamides is.  
 (Not so) Secondary metabolites and their ecological roles 
Despite the large interest in secondary metabolites there is still a lot to learn about their natural 
function. Secondary, as opposed to primary, metabolites are compounds that are not considered 
essential for growth, and as such has been regarded as (mostly) dispensable for cell survival (35). 
This may be the case when the cells are grown in a laboratory setting. However, this may simply be 
because secondary metabolites are developed as part of an adaption to different environmental 
factors, conferring selective advantage for the producer (Reviewed in (36)). One could argue that 
these compounds are in this case wrongly named, as they are by no means secondary. Instead, they 
might be key factors for successful survival in changing environments.  
Originally, it was assumed that compounds exhibiting antibiotic properties were part of a defense 
mechanism (Reviewed in (37)). However, many compounds were isolated from bacteria which 
==PAGE:15==

would not normally be in contact with the pathogen which they are used to treat, questioning this 
line of thought (Reviewed in (38)). Of course, many antibiotics target are broad-spectrum and could 
hence also have developed to target competitors present in an ecological setting. It is therefore not 
wrong to assume that defense is one of the natural roles of secondary metabolites. However, many 
other functions have since been discovered and investigated. Secondary metabolites have also been 
implicated in nutrient acquisition, predator deterrence, and stress response among others (39–42). 
Interestingly, some antibiotics show dual functions as a result of varying concentrations. 
Seyedsayamdost, 2014, demonstrated that secondary metabolism in Burkholderia thailandensis 
could be induced by sub-MIC concentrations of numerous antibiotics that kill B. thailandensis at 
higher concentrations (43).  This observation indicated that these antibiotics may also serve as 
signaling molecules, a view that has also been presented in several other reviews and articles 
(42,44). This also points to the fact that the same compound may serve a number of functions.  
It should now be clear that the roles of secondary metabolites are plentiful, and it is likely that even 
more roles will surface as research within this field progresses. Understanding the functions of 
secondary metabolites plays an important role in discovering novel antibiotics. Through advances 
in technology, we now realize that a large fraction of biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs, discussed in 
Section 1.3) are still not linked to their corresponding compound and vice versa – so-called orphan 
BGCs or compounds (Reviewed in (10)). This can be attributed to several things; the compounds 
may be hard to extract and purify, or they might only be produced in very low amounts, if at all. As 
mentioned above, sub-MIC concentrations of antibiotics can be used to unlock the biosynthetic 
potential of an organism (43). Another approach is mimicking the natural environment, exemplified 
by Giubergia et al. 2016 who demonstrated that growing marine Vibrionaceae on mannose or chitin 
significantly influenced their production of compounds with antibiotic activity (45). Understanding 
the various roles that secondary metabolites play in their ecological niche may also provide new 
ideas on how to induce the production of these compounds. 
Ultimately, understanding the role of a compound within its niche is not only intriguing from an 
ecological viewpoint, it can also shine a light on its potential applications. However, to elucidate the 
natural role of a secondary metabolite, it is useful to first identify the biosynthetic pathway and the 
gene(s) involved.

 Biosynthetic gene clusters and genome mining  
The genes involved in biosynthesis of microbial secondary metabolites reside BGCs which are 
clustered physically close to each other in the genome (46).  These BGCs consist of the core 
biosynthetic enzymes as well as numerous accessory genes, such as genes mediating host 
resistance, transporters, regulatory elements, and enzymes producing specialized monomers, to 
name a few (Reviewed in (47)). 
The biosynthetic logic behind the synthesis of some of the most common classes of secondary 
metabolites, such as polyketides (PKs) and non-ribosomal polypeptides (NRPs), is well understood 
(Reviewed in (48)). These types of compounds are synthesized by polyketide synthases (PKSs) and 
non-ribosomal polypeptide synthetases (NRPSs), both of which consist of domains with unique 
functions. This, combined with the development of efficient and inexpensive whole-genome-
sequencing-techniques, has made the way for the era of genome-mining, an in silico approach where 
natural products are predicted based on the genome sequence (Reviewed in (49)). Numerous tools 
for predicting BGCs have been developed (46,50–52). For some BGCs, it is even possible to infer a 
possible chemical compound. In 1998, Stachelhaus et al. proposed a way of inferring the substrate 
amino acid of a specific adenylation(A)-domain of a NRPS on the basis of the amino acid sequence 
of the domain, referred to as the Stachelhaus code (53). Based on this code, it is possible to predict 
the substrate of a given A-domain, further aiding in the prediction of a natural product based on the 
genetic code. 
 Non-ribosomal peptides and their synthesis 
The solonamides are non-ribosomal polypeptides. As suggested by their name, NRPs are molecules 
consisting of proteinogenic and nonproteinogenic amino acids connected by peptide bonds to form 
a polypeptide chain (Reviewed in (54)). High structural diversity is observed within the NRPs. This 
structural diversity partially arises due to the wide range of substrate monomers observed, ranging 
from the canonical amino acids to sugars (e.g. vancomycin) and fatty acids (e.g. surfactin) (55,56). 
In accordance with their diversity, NRPs also has a wide range of industrial applications, exhibiting 
antimicrobial, biosurfactant, siderophore, and anticancer properties among others (55–58). 
NRPs are synthetized by NRPSs. NRPSs are multimodular enzymes where NRPs are synthesized in 
an ‘assembly-line’-manner. As opposed to ribosomal polypeptides which are synthesized by the 
ribosome based on an RNA template, the chemical structure of NRPs is determined by the 
arrangement of the NRPS. An NRPS consist of several modules and each of these modules can then 
be further divided into a series of domains with unique functions. The minimal core domains of a 
==PAGE:17==

module consists of an A-domain, a condensation (C)-domain, and a peptide carrier protein carrier 
protein (PCP)-domain, arranged in this order (Reviewed in (59)).  
The A-domain is responsible for recognizing the substrate amino acid and subsequently activating 
it by forming an amino-acyl adenylate at the expense of ATP (Fig 3.A). The activated substrate is 
then transferred to the PCP-domain where it is covalently attached to the thiol moiety of the 
cofactor 4’-phosphopantetheine (4’-PP, Fig. 3.B). The 4’-PP is post-translationally attached to a 
highly conserved serine of the PCP-domain and acts as a swinging arm, transferring the substrate 
between the modules (60). The C-domain is responsible for peptide-bond formation between 
substrates bound to the PCP-domains of adjacent modules (Fig. 3.C). The peptide bond is formed 
through a nucleophilic attack of the α-amino group of the acceptor amino on the donor substrate 
and the growing chain is hereby transferred to the next module of the assembly line (53). In this 
manner, the polypeptide chain is transferred between the modules, growing with one monomer 
each round until extension is terminated at the thioesterase (TE)-domain. The TE-domain transfers 
the full-length peptide from the last PCP-domain to a serine in its active site. The TE-domain then 
releases the finished peptide. This can happen in a number of ways depending on the final product; 
linear products may be released through hydrolysis whilst some cyclic peptides are cyclized by the 
TE-domain with the help of an intramolecular nucleophile (61). These are merely two of the 
mechanisms by which the TE-domain releases the peptide product (54). 
Whilst these four domains form the basis of NRP synthesis, numerous tailoring reactions contribute 
to the high structural diversity of NRPs. Tailoring can be carried out both in cis by domains included 
in the NRPS or by temporarily associated independent enzymes acting in trans (Reviewed in (59)).

 Predicted biosynthetic pathway of the solonamides 
When attempting to identify a putative BGC for solonamide biosynthesis, the chemical structure 
provides valuable information for the bioinformatic analysis.  As previously stated, the solonamides 
are NRPs which are synthesized by NRPSs. They both consist of four different amino acids; L-
phenylalanine, D-leucine, D-alanine, and L-leucine, as well as a fatty acid precursor and are cyclized 
through an ester-bond, a key feature of cyclodepsipeptides. 
An antiSMASH analysis (Appendix A) of the genome of S2753 resulted in eight predicted BGCs on 
chromosome 1, three predicted BGCs on chromosome 2, and no predicted clusters on the plasmid. 
On chromosome 1, four BGCs were classified as NRPSs or NRPS-like. All three BGC’s on chromosome 
2 were classified as NRPS or NRPS-like.  
BGC 2 on chromosome 1 was identified as a putative sol BGC based on the antiSMASH annotations. 
The predicted NRPS consists of four modules, consistent with the number of amino acids in the 
solonamides. Phenylalanine is the first amino acid in the solonamides and this was also predicted 
as the substrate for the first A-domain. Two epimerization(E)-domains (in module 2 and 3) 
correspond to the two D-amino acids found in the solonamides.

From this, a biosynthetic pathway for solonamide biosynthesis was proposed (Fig. 4). The starter 
molecule is either a 3-hydroxyhexanoic acid (solonamide A) or a 3-hydroxyoctanoic acid 
(solonamide B), assumed to arise from fatty acid synthesis. In the first module, an L-phenylalanine 
is added, followed by a leucine changed to D-configuration by the epimerization-domain. In module 
3, a D-alanine is added, followed by the final addition of an L-leucine. Finally, the molecule is cyclized 
by the TE-domain through an ester-bond between a hydroxy-group on the fatty acid starter and the 
carbonyl-group of the L-leucine.

After identifying a candidate core biosynthetic gene, the next step is to experimentally validate this 
candidate gene. Whilst genome mining is a powerful tool for identifying candidate BGCs,

 Aim of study 
The aim of this study was to genetically confirm the candidate gene, sol, predicted to encode an 
NRPS responsible for solonamide biosynthesis, by constructing a solM4 or a sol deletion mutant in 
both a S2753 WT and a S2753ΔhlmE (holomycin-deficient mutant) parent strains.  Secondly, the 
aim was to elucidate the natural function of the solonamides through a series of characterization 
experiments. By including a double-deletion (ΔhlmEΔsolM4) mutant in the characterization 
experiments, the aim was to investigate whether abolishing of solonamides could induce the 
production of antimicrobial compounds in S2753.

A detailed protocol for conjugation of S2753 can be found in my previous special project, “Molecular

and incubated at 25°C, 700 for 30 min. 100 μL conjugation mix was then spread onto APY+Cm15

 Bioinformatic analysis 
In silico approaches are useful tools for deciding upon a starting point for further experiments. The 
region up- and downstream of the sol gene was analyzed to investigate whether the nearby genes 
could hint at the natural function of the solonamides.  
Genes associated with stress-response are located nearby the sol gene (Fig. 5), hereunder a two-
component system responsible for osmoregulation encoded by the envZ (histidine kinase) and 
ompR (response regulator) gene, as well as several genes associated with DNA damage and repair 
(dinF, a DNA-damage inducible protein, spoT, ppGpp synthase and hydrolase, and greB, a 
transcription elongator factor). It was therefore hypothesized that the solonamides may be 
involved in stress response. This hypothesis was explored in later experiments (Section 3.8).

 Construction of a suicide plasmid 
To confirm that the sol gene encodes the NRPS responsible for solonamide biosynthesis, a suicide 
plasmid was constructed for knocking out the sol gene. One possibility is to delete the entire gene. 
However, the predicted sol gene is 16 kb long which would complicate subsequent 
complementation experiments. To avoid this, it was decided to delete only a part of the cluster. 
NRPSs are modular in nature and it has previously been demonstrated that deleting or altering 
modules or domains may lead to the formation of other compounds (73). To avoid synthesis of 
unwanted compounds, it was decided to target the 4th module, including the TE-domain which 
terminates the NRP synthesis.  
Primers were designed to amplify three different fragments; a pDM4 fragment, the vector 
backbone, as well as two regions surrounding the region targeted for deletion, the homology arms 
referred to as L(eft)-arm and R(ight)-arm. A ~20 bp sequence with homology to each of the two

other fragments was introduced via the primer-tail to both the 3’ and 5’ end of each fragments 
during PCR amplification. This resulted in a ~40 bp overlap between the fragments which can then 
be used to construct the pDM4-d-solM4 plasmid through direct cloning (Fig. 6.A).  
Direct cloning relies on a native system from a Rec prophage. The mechanism relies on the two 
proteins, RecE and RecT. These proteins are functionally similar to the lambda phage Red operon 
(Redα, Redβ, and Redγ) used for conventional recombineering (74). In conventional 
recombineering, a circular DNA recombines with a linear DNA fragment – so called linear+circular 
homologous recombination (LCHR). Direct cloning differs by allowing the homologous 
recombination of two linear DNA fragments, linear+linear homologous recombination (LLHR) (74).  
gDNA from S2753 was used as a template for a two-step PCR amplification of the homology arms 
with the following primers; L-arm Fw + L-arm Rv (L-arm, 938 bp), and R-arm Fw + R-arm Rv (R-
arm, 910 bp). For amplification of the backbone, circular pDM4 was used as a template with the 
primers pDM4-CmR Fw + pDM4-Rep Rv (pDM4, 7108 bp). After amplification, the reactions were 
separated on a gel. For all reactions, bands appeared at the expected sizes, confirming the presence 
of the fragments. The fragments were purified from gel, stained with 6x Purple Loading Dye and 
separated on a gel to confirm the presence and check the quality of the purified fragments.  
As can be seen from Fig. 6.B, pDM4 produces a clear band just below at ~7 kb, whilst both homology 
arms produce bands just below 1 kb, as expected. The purified fragments were subsequently used 
for direct cloning.

3.2.1 Restriction analysis of pDM4-d-solM4 with PstI   
Initially, the strategy was to construct the plasmid from four fragments; the two homology arms, 
and two partial pDM4 fragments, one half harboring the replicon origin and the other harboring the 
selective marker. However, this was unsuccessful. Instead, pDM4 was amplified as one fragment, 
and the three fragments were assembled into a circular plasmid, named pDM4-d-solM4. To confirm 
the presence of the plasmid and eliminate false positives (re-circularized pDM4), a restriction 
analysis was carried out using the restriction enzyme PstI (5’-CTGCA|G-3’) in NEB 3.1 (x10) buffer 
(New England Biolabs, R0140). As controls, pDM4 cut with PstI and pDM4 incubated in buffer 
without PstI were also included. The digests were incubated for 1 hr at 37°C following staining with 
6x Purple Loading Dye and gel electrophoresis. pDM4-d-solM4 was expected to result in two 
fragments of 6217 bp and 2622 bp, whilst an empty pDM4 should result in bands at 4482 bp and 
2622 bp (Fig. 7.A).  
Cut pDM4 produces the expected bands at ~4.5 kb and ~2.6 kb, confirming that the reaction 
worked. One reaction resulted in three bands at ~2.6 kb, ~4.5 kb, and ~6.2, indicating a mosaic 
sample consisting of both empty pDM4 and pDM4-d-solM4 (Fig. 7.B). Out of 24 samples, three were 
identified as positive based on their band patterns (one at ~6.2 kb and another at 2.6 kb), 
confirming that construction of the pDM4-d-solM4 plasmid was successful.

The solonamides are antivirulence compounds (20) but do not affect viability and hence are not 
expected to establish a clearing zone. Consequently, no change in antibiotic activity was expected 
as a result of an elimination of the solonamides. Holomycin, on the other hand, is a potent antibiotic 
(75) and antibiotic activity is not observed for S2753ΔhlmE (unpublished data, Shengda Zhang). 
Antibiotic effect was therefore expected to cease for strains with a hlmE deletion. 
Included in the well diffusion assay were extracts from two different media conditions; MMM+0.2% 
colloidal chitin and MMM+0.2% NAG. From each media S2753 WT, S2753ΔhlmE, the four mutants 
and a negative control (extract from blank media) was included. This was done in biological 
triplicates.

V. anguillarum and S. aureus after 24 hrs. After 48 hrs, the clearing zones on the S. aureus plate 
seems to decrease. Unexpectedly, the negative control from the colloidal chitin samples show a clear 
inhibition zone in all triplicates for both V. anguillarum and S. aureus plates. The remaining samples 
show the same pattern of inhibition as for the NAG media, where an inhibition zone is observed for 
WT, ΔsolM4, and Δsol strains against both V. anguillarum and S. aureus after 24 hrs, with a decrease 
of clearing zones on S. aureus plates after 48 hrs. However, the clearing zone is not similarly 
decreased for the negative control (Fig. 12, Table 4).

4 Discussion 
 The sol gene cluster is responsible for solonamide biosynthesis in S2753 
Following the construction of the four mutant strains, the chemical analysis showed that the 
solonamides were no longer detectable, confirming that the sol gene is responsible for solonamide 
biosynthesis. To further confirm this a complementation experiment should be carried out, but this 
was not done due to the time constraints.  
 Difficulties in partial deletion of a gene with high degree of internal homology 
Constructing deletion mutants is a common approach for confirming and investigating the function 
of a gene. However, it is also necessary to carry out a complementation experiment after deletion. 
This can pose a problem when deleting large genes.  
As demonstrated, partial deletion of a terminal module of an NRPS is sufficient for eliminating the 
solonamides. A downfall of this strategy is that derivatives from solonamide biosynthesis could still 
be present. Gallagher et al., 2017 argued that from an evolutionary perspective it would be logical 
that intermediates of a biosynthetic pathway would also be of ecological importance (or at least 
were at some point) (77). In this case, this strategy could potentially link a compound with the 
function of a biosynthetic intermediate. However, to my knowledge an example of this has yet to be 
confirmed.  
Another potential issue of this strategy arises because there is a high degree of internal homology 
within these BGCs. After constructing the pDM4-d-solM4 plasmid, the issue of whether internal 
homology in the sol gene could pose a problem during the homologous recombination was brought 
up. This was not considered during the planning of the experiments since the homology arms are 
~1 kb in length and, consequently, the likelihood of a second site of homology was not considered. 
However, using NCBI BLASTn a region with a high level of homology to the L-arm was identified 
(Appendix C for BLASTn result). The identified region is within the sol gene and integration at this 
site would ultimately result in a deletion starting in the C-domain of module 3. These issues pose as 
a valuable note for future knock-out experiments where a module deletion strategy is employed. 
However, this could likely be avoided by using homology arms outside of the conserved domains.  
Aside from simplifying subsequent complementation experiments, this strategy could also be 
applicable if the gene to be complemented encodes the biosynthetic machinery for an antibiotic 
compound. When using a heterologous host to construct or transfer the plasmid, it is normally 
necessary to ensure that the gene is under control of a strict promoter.

 Growth is not affected by the absence of the solonamides 
The solonamides are expressed during exponential growth. It was therefore speculated whether 
they may be involved in growth. To investigate this, a growth curve was constructed.  
Based on the results, no difference is observed between any of the four strains. The slight decrease 
after 915 min could be attributed to the formation of cell aggregates and biofilm which makes the 
culture less homogenous. Interestingly, a small break in the exponential phase is observed for all 
cultures. The strains were grown in MMM with NAG as carbon-source. However, the MMM also 
contains casamino acids which could also be utilized as a carbon-source. It is a common strategy for 
bacteria to have a ‘hierarchy’ of preferred carbon-sources, where the expression of metabolic 
enzymes is carefully governed by carbon catabolite repression (78,79). This metabolic optimization 
prevents the bacteria from spending energy degrading less-favored carbon-sources if more easily 
accessible carbon is present.  It is therefore possible that the break at 285 min could be attributed 
to a change from one metabolism to another, the lag being the result of a ‘transition period’. 
 Numerous genes related to stress response are encoded close to the sol gene 
A bioinformatic analysis of the genes up- and down-stream of the sol gene was carried out to 
investigate whether this might provide a hint towards the role of the solonamides. Numerous genes 
associated with stress response were located close to the sol gene. It is possible that the sol gene 
was randomly inserted into this region. However, no mobility elements are found around the sol 
gene. In addition, codon usage does not seem to differ between the sol gene and the surrounding 
genome. Therefore, it does not seem that the sol gene was inserted into this region recently.  
Numerous examples of secondary metabolites involved in different types of stress response exists. 
For example, some microorganisms produce compounds, often pigmented, that can protect them 
from UV light and it has also been demonstrated that UV irradiation can enhance the biosynthesis 
of such pigments (80,81). As previously mentioned, using stressors to induce secondary 
metabolism is also a common approach for bioprospecting. From an ecological perspective, the 
success of this method hints that these compounds play a role in stress-response.  
An OmpR/EnvZ-like two component system is predicted downstream of the sol gene. In this system, 
EnvZ is a transmembrane histidine kinase that senses changes in the osmolarity of the surrounding 
environment, whilst OmpR is the response regulator (82).  In E. coli, OmpR directly interacts with 
DNA, hereby regulating gene expression (83). To investigate whether the OmpR-like protein found 
in S2753 also possessed a DNA-binding domain, a BLASTp analysis was carried out, and a putative

DNA-binding domain was detected, indicating that the OmpR-like protein found in S2753 can also 
bind DNA (Fig. 15).

As previously mentioned, AgrC, through which the solonamides exert their effect on virulence in S. 
aureus, is a histidine kinase like EnvZ (32). Based on these observations, it is speculated whether 
the solonamides may interact with this system and potentially regulate gene expression through 
this system, potentially in response to changes in osmolarity. Due to time constraints, this 
hypothesis was not tried but this would be interesting for future experiments.  
A spoT gene is located nearby sol. SpoT is related to the bacterial alarmone, ppGpp. ppGpp is a 
collective name for the alarmones guanosine pentaphosphate and guanosine tetraphosphate. SpoT 
mainly hydrolyses ppGpp but can also act as a ppGpp synthase under certain conditions (Reviewed 
in (84)). In E. coli, ppGpp is a key factor in the stringent response. The stringent response is 
triggered by various stress conditions such as amino acid starvation, heat shock, and fatty acid 
limitation. As a result, resources are re-directed from growth processes to stress response 
mechanisms by transcriptional regulation through ppGpp. ppGpp alters the transcriptional profile 
by interacting directly with RNA polymerase in cooperation with DksA or indirectly through σ-
factor competition (85). During stress response, SpoT synthesizes ppGpp which then accumulates 
(Reviewed in (84)). This in turn directs transcription towards expression of stress resistance genes. 
This is in part mediated by promoting the interaction of alternative σ-factors with RNA polymerase. 
When stress ceases, SpoT acts as a hydrolase, lowering ppGpp levels, directing resources back to 
growth and cell division (Fig. 16).

McGlynn and Lloyd, 2000 found that the ability of E. coli to survive UV irradiation was strongly 
correlated with ppGpp synthesis (86). Following this, Kamarthapu et al., 2016, demonstrated that 
ppGpp was involved in transcription coupled DNA repair (TCR) by rendering RNA polymerase 
prone to backtracking (87). The transcription factor encoded immediately downstream of sol, GreB, 
is also implicated in TCR where it, oppositely of ppGpp, counteracts backtracking (88). A homologue 
of the E. coli gene encoding DNA-inducible protein F (dinF), which is induced by UV irradiation and 
DNA-damaging agents, was also located close to the sol gene (89). The presence of these DNA-
damage associated genes gave rise to the hypothesis that the solonamides may be involved in DNA 
repair. 
 Lack of replicability in UV experiments prevented the collection of reliant data 
Following the bioinformatic analysis, an attempt was made to test whether the solonamides are 
linked to UV susceptibility. Prior to testing the mutant strains for UV susceptibility, an attempt to 
construct a mortality curve for S2753 WT was carried out to determine the appropriate exposure 
time. However, a log transformed curve did not show a linear correlation between exposure time 
and mortality.

Despite this, the mutant strains were still tested but no colonies were observed for either.  One 
option is that none of the plated strains were viable. This explanation would account for the fact 
that no surviving colonies were recorded at all. However, S2753 and mutants are usually fast-
growing and easily cultured and it therefore seems unlikely that all six strains did not grow 
independent of one another. Another explanation for why the controls did not grow could be that 
the control half of the plate was not efficiently protected from UV exposure. This still does not 
account for the fact that no surviving colonies were observed, since the pre-experiment indicated 
that S2753 WT should be able to cope with this UV exposure.  
In the end, the results highlight the biggest issue for the experiment; a lack of consistency. First, 
some variance in exposure time must be expected due to human error. This was especially 
problematic due to the rapid increase in mortality over a short time span. Hence, a larger relative 
variance is to be expected when exposure time is set to 1 s than if exposure time had been e.g. 10 s. 
Still, one would expect that a number of surviving cells should, at least for WT, be present since 
~20% survival was observed at 3 s exposure in the pre-experiment. This also questions the validity 
of the mortality curve, and, consequently, whether any data would be comparable when this degree 
of variance is observed. These issues could potentially be relieved by using equipment where the 
UV dosage can be controlled more precisely, but, due to time limitations, the experiment was 
abandoned.  
For future studies, it would be interesting to investigate whether the absence of solonamides affect 
the UV resistance of the strain, and whether solonamide production is altered in response to UV 
exposure.  
 The absence of solonamides does not impact the antibiotic activity of S2753 
It was speculated whether the removal of the solonamides alone or together with removal of 
holomycin could stimulate the production of previously undetected antibiotic compounds. To test 
this, a well diffusion assay was carried out to test for inhibitory effects against the gram-negative 
fish pathogen V. anguillarum and the gram-positive human pathogen S. aureus.  
The results correspond with the hypothesis that holomycin is responsible for the antibiotic activity 
of S2753, since no inhibition is observed for ΔhlmE strains, regardless of whether the solonamides 
are present. However, an unexpected zone of inhibition was observed for the blank control for the 
MMM+0.2% colloidal chitin media.

It is possible that the unexpected antibiotic activity of the blank is a result of contamination or that 
the samples have been mixed up. However, the remaining samples showed the clearing zone 
pattern as expected and as observed for the NAG samples, decreasing the likelihood of a mix up 
being the answer. Furthermore, two separate blank extracts were tested, both showing a clearing 
zone. If the media was contaminated prior to the experiment, a clearing zone would be expected for 
all samples. Since this is not the case, contamination of the two blanks would have had to happen 
independently of one another.  
Bioactivity of chitin, chitosan, and derivatives thereof is a well-known phenomenon (90,91). It was 
therefore speculated whether this may be the cause of the unexpected clearing zones, since the 
chitin was not metabolized in the absence of S2753. This is contradicted by the repeated assay, 
wherein the new MMM+0.2% colloidal chitin extract does not show inhibition of V. anguillarum, 
indicating that the media is not responsible for the clearing zone.  
Based on the results, it is not possible to conclude what resulted in the inhibition exhibited by the 
blank samples. However, the remaining results are still relevant and indicate that it is holomycin 
alone that is responsible for the antibiotic effect of P. galatheae and that the elimination of the 
solonamides, holomycin, or both does not stimulate the expression of other antibiotic compounds 
under these conditions.  
 Biofilm formation may be affected in hlmE-deletion mutants 
The solonamides interfere with QS in S. aureus, and it was therefore hypothesized that their natural 
role may also involve QS. One of the common roles of QS systems is within biofilm formation and 
the agr QS system has been linked to biofilm formation (Reviewed in (92)). The constructed 
mutants were therefore used to test whether the solonamides impact biofilm formation.  
The results of a one-way ANOVA comparing the mutants (ΔhlmE, ΔsolM4, ΔhlmEΔsolM4, Δsol, and 
ΔhlmEΔsolM4) to S2753 WT indicate that no statistically significant difference is observed for any 
of the mutants. However, if ignoring the outlier-values, a significant reduction in biofilm formation 
was observed for hlmE deletion mutants. This could therefore imply a role of holomycin in biofilm 
formation.  
Biofilm does not appear to be affected by the solonamides. The solonamides are expressed during 
expontential phase, whereas biofilm formation is initiated during stationary phase (93). 
Furthermore, biosynthesis of the solonamides is not altered when grown with chitin as the carbon-
==PAGE:50==
Master thesis (30 ECTS) 
Spring 2019 
 
Laura Louise Lindqvist, 172963 
 
48 
 
 
source (93) and chitin is known to induce biofilm formation (94).  This supports the hypothesis that 
the solonamides are not involved in biofilm formation.  
No previous studies have linked holomycin to biofilm formation. Contrary to solonamides, 
Giubergia et al. 2017, observed an increase in holomycin production when using chitin as the 
carbon-source (93). This could indicate that holomycin is involved in regulation of biofilm 
formation. Most studies have centered around the antibiotic effect of this compound. A study by 
Chan et al., 2017, suggested a mechanism of action where reduced holomycin acts as an intracellular 
zinc-chelator, disrupting bacterial metal homeostasis (95). Metal ions, including Zn2+, Mg2+, and 
Fe3+, have been recorded to play a role in bacterial biofilm formation  (41,96,97) It is therefore 
possible that the chelating properties of holomycin may affect biofilm formation.  
 The solonamides may share functions with other known cyclodepsipeptides   
As mentioned previously, the cyclodepsipeptides are a diverse group of compounds exhibiting 
numerous bioactive properties (Reviewed in (98)). Many of these compounds were isolated from 
the marine environment, and it was speculated whether the solonamides may share an ecological 
role with some of these compounds.  
Oku et al., 2008 isolated two cyclotetradepsipeptides from another Photobacterium sp., unnarmicin 
A and C (99). Structurally, the unnarmicins are very similar to the solonamides (Fig. 17). Like the 
solonamides, they consist of four amino acids cyclized through an ester-bond, with an exocyclic 
fatty acid tail. These compounds were found to inhibit the growth of two Pseudovibrio sp (99). This 
is an Alphaproteobacterial genera frequently isolated from marine environments. Furthermore, it 
commonly dominates host-associated microbial communities and produce numerous potent, 
broad-spectred antimicrobial compounds (Reviewed in (100)). Based on this, it is speculated 
whether the solonamides are also able to inhibit this genus. This would confer a significant 
competitive advantage over a likely ecological neighbor.

 Crosstalk between the secondary metabolites of S2753 
The solonamides are not the only cyclodepsipeptides produced by S2753. Four novel compounds 
belonging to the known class of ngercheumicins were also identified, ngercheumicin F, G, H, and I 
(34). Like the solonamides, the ngercheumicins also interfere with the agr QS-system of S. aureus 
(34). Interestingly, a high degree of structural similarity is observed between the solonamides, the 
ngercheumicins, and the AIPs of S. aureus (Fig. 19). All molecules are cyclic structures built from 
amino acids, although number of amino acids and ring closure differs. For the solonamides and 
ngercheumicins ring closure happens through an ester-bond whilst for the AIP’s, cyclization 
happens through thiolacton linkage (103). Furthermore, the exocyclic chain differs.

Based on the chemical analysis it seems that, contrary to the initial hypothesis, a decrease in the 
ngercheumicins is observed. This decrease is assumingly not a direct result of the absence of 
solonamides. Because of the structural similarity between the ngercheumicins and the 
solonamides, one might speculate whether they share some of the core biosynthetic machinery. 
However, ngercheumicin biosynthesis is not fully dependent on the solonamides as a total absence 
of the ngercheumicins would be expected in this case. It is however still possible that they may 
share some accessory proteins.  
Interestingly, holomycin seem to have an inhibitory effect on the ngercheumicins as well as on 
solonamide A. To investigate whether the reduction of ngercheumicins observed for the Δsol and 
ΔsolM4 mutants was mediated through holomycin, holomycin levels were compared between these 
mutants and S2753 WT. If the reduction of ngercheumicin in the absence of solonamides was 
mediated through holomycin, one would expect that the solonamides decreased the availability of 
holomycin. However, the opposite is observed; holomycin is decreased in the absence of the 
solonamides, indicating that this is not the case. The observed effects are summarized in Fig. 20.

It is important to note that these effects are likely not due to direct interactions between the 
compounds. It should also be noted that no quantitative or statistical analysis was carried out for 
the chemical analysis. For the results to be conclusive, a standard-curve should first be established 
for the analyzed compounds. Furthermore, cell density was not accounted for prior to chemical 
extraction of the cultures due to the presence of cell aggregates. However, since the growth of the 
mutant strains did not differ from the WT strain (section 3.6), this would only be expected to 
account for neglectable differences. Further experiments are necessary to investigate and confirm 
the potential link between the biosynthesis of the ngercheumicins, solonamides, and holomycin in 
S2753. 
 Solonamides as potential signaling molecules  
As previously mentioned, it is hypothesized that the solonamides could be involved in QS since it 
interferes with the QS system of S. aureus. Interestingly, AIPs from one strain of S. aureus may inhibit 
the virulence expression of another strain of S. aureus, indicating that subtle structural changes may 
switch the mode of action from agonistic to antagonistic (104). Furthermore, Lyon et al. 2000 
demonstrated that the AgrC component, which is also the target for the solonamides, mediates 
these group-specific activations and inhibitions (105). This demonstrates a road of evolution where 
QS systems have evolved to ‘compete’ against other QS systems. It therefore seems plausible that 
the solonamides may belong to a native QS system of S2753.  
Interestingly, the QS-interfering properties of the solonamides are not unique in the marine 
environment. A study by Nielsen et al., 2012 showed that ~80% of a sample set of bacteria 
(belonging to the genera Vibrio, Ruegeria, and Pseudomonas, isolated based on their antibiotic 
activity against V. anguillarum and S. aureus) collected from various marine habitats worldwide

could influence virulence gene expression in S. aureus (106). Similarly, Skindersoe et al., 2008 found 
that 23% of extracts collected from a range of corals (hard and soft), algae, sponges, and seaweed 
collected from the Great Barrier Reef showed QS inhibitory activity (107). These results indicate 
that QS as well as QS inhibition are of significant importance in marine environments (Reviewed in 
(108)). Although it seems unlikely that marine bacteria have developed a defense system 
specifically for targeting Staphylococci, which are not generally found in marine environments, it is 
still possible that QS interference is a defense strategy against other types of bacteria. 
It should come as no surprise that signaling such as QS is not limited to intraspecies communication. 
Examples of interspecies, and even interkingdom, signaling are widely observed (Reviewed in 
(109)). This is underlined by the striking similarity between many eukaryotic and prokaryotic 
signaling molecules (Reviewed in (110)). It is therefore entirely possible that the solonamides are 
the result of co-evolution with the surrounding fauna and hence serve a purpose not readily 
observed in sterile lab-settings. Furthermore, it is likely that some secondary metabolites possess 
numerous functions (111,112). Advances in the way we study microorganisms and microbial 
communities will hopefully help shed a light on the role of secondary metabolites in the future. 
Ultimately, it is not possible to conclude on the role of the solonamides based on the experimental 
data obtained in this study. Still, the work done in this study serve as a valuable starting point for 
future studies.

5 Conclusion 
During this project, four new deletion mutants, S2753ΔsolM4, S2753ΔhlmEΔsolM4, S2753Δsol, and 
S2753ΔhlmEΔsol, were successfully constructed. Chemical analysis of mutants lacking either a 
partial or full sol gene confirmed that this is the gene responsible for biosynthesis of solonamide A 
and B. Furthermore, it was demonstrated that partial deletion of an NRPS was sufficient for 
elimination of the final compounds, although the approach has some drawbacks.  
The deletion mutants were subsequently used for several characterization experiments. No 
changes in growth or antibiotic production were observed. For mutants deficient in holomycin 
biosynthesis, there were indications of reduced biofilm formation, although further experiments 
are necessary to confirm this. A bioinformatic analysis revealed the presence of numerous stress-
related genes nearby the sol gene. Among these genes were several genes linked to DNA repair. 
Attempts to test the UV sensitivity of the strains were unsuccessful and abandoned due to time 
constraints.  
Based on comparisons with other known cyclodepsipeptides, it was speculated whether the 
solonamides may be involved in defense against ecological neighbors or in metal chelation. Further 
studies are necessary to investigate this. Interestingly, S2753 also produces the cyclodepsipeptides 
ngercheumicin F, G, H, and I, which are structurally similar to the solonamides. These compounds 
seem to be reduced in the absence of solonamides but increased in the absence of holomycin. 
Furthermore, holomycin seem to reduce solonamide A, whilst holomycin is reduced in the absence 
of the solonamides. A potential link between these compounds have yet to be investigated. 
Another possible role of the solonamides was inferred based on their antivirulence mechanism. 
Since they mimic the QS AIPs of S. aureus, it was speculated whether their natural role may also be 
within QS, although experimental evidence of this was not demonstrated.  
Ultimately, this project resulted in numerous ideas for future studies of the solonamides, although 
their ecological role was not determined. Furthermore, the constructed deletion mutants are a 
powerful tool for studying their function. Studying the ecological role of secondary metabolites is a 
difficult task since studying bacteria in a laboratory is far from their natural environment where 
they live in distinct niches and co-exist with other organisms. As techniques to study secondary 
metabolites in a more complex setting develops, we will hopefully gain more insight into the 
functions they serve.

6 Perspectives 
Due to the time-constraints of this thesis, a complementation experiment was not carried out. This 
should, however, be carried out before final confirmation of the sol gene. One approach for 
complementing a phenotype is by supplying the deleted gene on a plasmid. However, for the partial 
deletion mutants it might be necessary to generate an in-frame knock-in to ensure that the resulting 
protein is functional.  
Numerous potential involvements of the solonamides were suggested. The potential involvement 
of the solonamides in DNA repair would be interesting to explore in future studies. As attempted in 
this report, the susceptibility of the mutants to UV irradiation or mutagenic compounds could be 
tested. Another approach could be to investigate whether solonamide biosynthesis is affected by 
exposure to mutagens. Similarly, it would be interesting to investigate whether solonamide 
biosynthesis is affected by changes in osmolarity, as this could indicate that the solonamides act as 
a signaling molecule by interacting with the EnvZ/OmpR-like two-component system found 
downstream of sol.  
The identification of the sol gene also opens the door for constructing a heterologous expression 
system. This could be used for producing larger quantities of the solonamides which could be useful 
both for further research on its natural role as well as for industrial purposes. Antivirulence 
compounds are a relatively novel approach to fighting pathogenic bacteria and the solonamides 
therefore have the potential to be a valuable therapeutic agent.
