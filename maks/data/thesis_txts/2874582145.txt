Abstract 
Tropical bivalves play a vital role in coastal food systems, particularly in regions facing nutritional 
vulnerability and limited livelihood options. This study focused on Pinna muricata (locally known 
as “matewo”), a sessile bivalve harvested in the intertidal areas of Mozambique to assess its 
contribution to sustainable aquatic food systems, with emphasis on nutritional value and health 
risk. 
Fieldwork was conducted in February (rainy season) and May (dry season) of 2025, in Ngangale 
and Bumo-Bumo. A total of 80 Pinna muricata specimens were collected using transects, and 
edible tissues were analyzed for protein (Kjeldahl), moisture, and elemental composition via ICP-
MS/OES. Dietary exposure was estimated using the Estimated Daily Intake (EDI), based on 
reported consumption (167 g/day). Semi-structured interviews with active shellfish collectors 
provided complementary data on harvesting practices and knowledge. 
Results showed that 97% of the harvesters were women. The edible tissues of P. muricata 
showed high protein content (mean = 81.7% dry weight), with values exceeding 90% during the 
dry season. Essential elements such as iron (Fe), manganese (Mn), zinc (Zn), copper (Cu) and 
Selenium (Se) were present in nutritionally relevant amounts. Cadmium (Cd) and total Arsenic 
(As) raised concern with mean concentration of 3.9 mg/kg dw and 33.7 mg/kg dw respectively. 
The EDI estimates were 1.65 µg/kg bw/day and 18.62 µg/kg bw/day for Cd and As respectively, 
exceeded international safety thresholds: Cd and As EDIs surpassed tolerable intake levels by 2-
fold and over 600-fold, respectively. Although 63.9% of respondents reported using boiling 
followed by sun-drying as a preservation method, 86.1% declared having no awareness of food 
safety risks. 
Pinna muricata offers substantial nutritional benefits and supports women-driven livelihoods but 
also poses concern health risks due to potential toxic element accumulation and lack of safety 
awareness. Promoting the safe and sustainable use of Pinna muricata will require integrated 
strategies that combine nutritional monitoring, community-based education, and inclusive 
environmental governance. 
Keywords: Pinna muricata, Aquatic food systems, Protein content, Estimated Daily 
Intake, Mozambique.

1. Introduction 
Pinna muricata (P. muricata) is a marine sessile bivalve of ecological, cultural and subsistence 
relevance in various tropical coastal systems. On Guiduane Island, in the Inhambane Province 
(Southern of Mozambique), its harvesting occurs predominantly through informal and 
underreported small-scale activity, mainly led by women (Chitará-Nhandimo et al., 2022).  
Despite the absence of official records, this species is part of local food systems, providing 
food and income, yet it remains largely invisible to official fisheries policies and management 
frameworks. 
Like other bivalves with well-documented nutritional benefits, P. muricata, is likely to contain 
essential micronutrients important for human health (Surm et al., 2015a; Ab Lah et al., 2017a; 
Wright, Fan, & Baker, 2018; Smaal et al., 2019; FAO, 2020b). 
In addition, P. muricata play a critical ecological role as a filter-feeder, contributing to clarity 
and nutrient cycling while offering structural habitat to juvenile organisms (Smaal et al., 2019). 
However, this filter-feeding ability makes this species susceptible to accumulate potential toxic 
elements (PTEs) (Puccinelli et al., 2022, Marinaro et al., 2025), which may pose health risks 
to consumers when in higher concentrations. 
Although the ecological and economic relevance of P. muricata in Southern Mozambique has 
been described (Chitará-Nhandimo et al., 2022), little is known about its nutritional composition 
and food safety. Documenting this evidence is relevant in the context of climate change, 
declining fisheries, and efforts to promote sustainable aquatic food systems aligned with the 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDG’s), particularly SDG 2 (Zero Hunger) and SDG 14 (Life 
Below Water). 
Accordingly, this study addresses the main research question: 
• 
How can P. muricata contribute to a sustainable aquatic food system in Guiduane 
Island, considering its socio-ecological significance, nutritional value and potential 
health risks? 
 
To complement this central question, following research questions are addressed:

2. Background  
2.1. 
Sustainable Food Systems and the Role of Aquatic Resources in Guiduane 
Island 
Over the past decade, there has been growing consensus that the global food system is failing to 
provide safe, nutritious, equitable and ecologically sustainable diets (HLPE, 2020). This 
recognition has catalyzed a global call for food system transformation, particularly at local levels 
where food security intersects with ecological degradation and social vulnerabilities (Short et al., 
2021). Although the agendas for change emphasize production efficiency and innovation, they 
tend to overlook the contribution of underutilized aquatic resources that support livelihoods and 
biodiversity (March & Failler, 2022a). 
In developing countries, small-scale fisheries dominate seafood access for coastal populations, 
yet remain largely excluded from national nutrition and food security strategies (FAO, 2020a). In 
sub-Saharan Africa these fisheries account of over 90% of the fish consumed in low-income 
coastal regions, highlighting their critical, but underrecognized role in local food systems (Béné et 
al., 2015) 
Sustainable food systems aim to ensure food and nutrition security while conserving ecosystems 
and supporting livelihoods equitably across generations (HLPE, 2020). Within this framework, 
aquatic food systems, including seafood such as fish, mollusks, crustaceans, and aquatic plants 
are increasingly recognized for their role to deliver high quality protein, essential micronutrients 
and and omega-3 fatty acids (Golden et al., 2021; Ross & Baldwin, 2022). They supply 17 % of 
global animal protein and support millions of livelihoods, particularly in coastal and low-income 
regions (FAO, 2022a),  
By addressing persistent malnutrition, biodiversity decline, and social-ecological vulnerabilities, 
aquatic food systems are positioned as vital components of resilient and equitable food system 
transitions (Myers et al., 2017). Yet their contribution remains insufficiently reflected in policy 
frameworks and investment agendas, especially in regions where informal harvesting and 
gendered labor practices dominate. 
In southern Mozambique, P. muricata is harvested informally by coastal households, particularly 
women, through intertidal gleaning practices and plays a significant role enhancing dietary 
diversity and sustaining coastal local livelihoods (Chitará-Nhandimo et al., 2022). The species is 
both consumed locally and sold in markets, making it a pillar of subsistence and income (Chitará-
==PAGE:15==
Cíntia da Conceição Cassimo Timba – DTU Food / Universidade Eduardo Mondlane – July 2025

Nhandimo et al., 2022). 
However, its contribution has not yet been formally recognized in national food frameworks or 
community level strategies. This exclusion reflects a broader pattern, as small-scale fisheries 
account for the majority of seafood accessed by rural households, but remain invisible in national 
food security and nutrition agendas (FAO, 2020a). 
Looking forward, P. muricata may represents a valuable case to explore a local sustainable food 
system, in alignment with SDG 2 (Zero Hunger), SDG 5 (Gender Equality) and SDG 14 (Life 
Below Water) (FAO, 2022a). Although various SDGs relate to aquatic foods, this study focuses 
on SDG14, especially targets concerning the sustainable use of marine resources, equitable 
benefits for small-scale fisheries, and strengthening of scientific knowledge to stewardship 
(Valentini et al., 2019) 
The informal use, gendered access patterns and ecological functions position P. muricata as a 
key species that can help on developing a sustainable and community-based aquatic food 
systems. This study argues that its underutilized status reflects a structural deficiency in how local 
food systems recognize and integrate marine biodiversity into nutrition and sustainability planning. 
According to Chitará-Nhandimo et al., (2022), intertidal harvesting on Guiduane Island involves 
an average of 40 collectors per day, predominantly women, targeting a variety of mollusks. During 
spring low tides, up to 7.6 tons of invertebrates can be harvested weekly (Chitará-Nhandimo et 
al., 2022), underscoring both the ecological and nutritional significance of P. muricata. Their 
popularity is linked to their availability during low tide and the minimal equipment needed for their 
collection. 
While monitoring mechanisms do exist through local governance structures such as the 
Comunitary Fishery Council (CCP), their operational capacity remains limited, and species like P. 
muricata are not explicitly integrated into local fisheries management or food security strategies. 
 
Excluding P. muricata from institutional agendas limits the capacity to design food system 
strategies that reflect local realities, gendered and intergenerational knowledge practices in 
Guiduane island. Addressing these interlinked challenges requires producing locally grounded 
evidence that integrates socio-ecological, nutritional, and potential health risk dimensions of 
neglected aquatic species. As community-based food systems gain recognition for their role in 
resilience and sustainability (FAO, 2020a; Crona et al., 2023), the case of P. muricata offers a

valuable lens through which to reimagine the contribution of marine biodiversity to equitable food 
systems. 
 
2.2. 
Ecological and Nutritional Relevance of Pinna muricata in Guiduane Island 
P. muricata Linnaeus, 1758, is a large sessile marine bivalve belonging to the family Pinnidae, 
commonly known as prickly pen shell (Rosewater, 1961). It inhabits shallow subtidal zones with 
muddy sandy substrates at a depth of 1.5 - 4 m, anchoring itself partially to the substrate using 
byssal threads (Susetya et al., 2022). 
 
Figure 2-1. Pinna muricata Linnaeus, 1758. Source: Author, 2025. 
Ecologically, bivalves supports coastal ecosystems by filter feeding, improving water quality, and 
promotes nutrient cycling and primary productivity (Vaughn & Hoellein, 2018; Vaughn, 2018). 
Individual bivalves host diverse microbial communities and protists, while their shell surfaces are 
colonized by biofilms, algae, and sessile invertebrates. At the aggregation level, these organisms 
can alter hydrodynamics, light availability, and nutrient fluxes, thereby influencing the broader 
structure and function of surrounding coastal habitats (Vaughn & Hoellein, 2018). 
Although studies on P. muricata are scarce, evidence from related species such as Pinna nobilis 
suggests that pinnids support essential ecosystem functions, particularly when associated with 
seagrass meadows, which are essential for carbon sequestration, sediment stabilization, and 
juvenile habitat (Basso et al., 2015). These habitats, are increasingly degraded by coastal 
development, overharvesting, climate change, threatening both ecological integrity and the 
sustainability of dependent species (Orth et al., 2006; Waycott et al., 2009; Herrera et al., 2022) 
like P. muricata. 
==PAGE:17==
Cíntia da Conceição Cassimo Timba – DTU Food / Universidade Eduardo Mondlane – July 2025 
6 
In addition to their ecological roles, bivalves are valued for their nutritional composition, which is 
influenced by intrinsic (e.g., age, sex, reproductive stage), and extrinsic factors (e.g., seasonality 
temperature, salinity, food availability) (FAO, 2020a). Their biochemical components are 
commonly described in terms of moisture, protein, lipids, and ash (Mekawy et al., 2021). Moisture, 
which can represent the largest fraction, directly influencing texture, freshness, and shelf-life; 
proteins usually compose the main fraction of the dry matter, often exceeding 50% of dry weight, 
and are crucial both structurally and metabolically; lipids, although variable among species and 
seasons, include important bioactive fatty acids such as omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids; 
and ash content represents the total inorganic residue in the tissue, that includes both macro and 
trace elements. Among these, essential trace elements such as zinc (Zn), selenium (Se), 
manganese (Mn), and iron (Fe) contribute to important physiological functions and nutritional 
value (Mekawy et al., 2021). 
These nutrients are critical for human physiological function, including immune, cognitive, and 
metabolic health (Ab Lah et al., 2017b; FAO, 2020a). Because of their nutrient content and coastal 
availability, bivalves are considered an important component of strategies aimed to improve food 
and nutrition security in coastal communities with limited resources (Golden et al., 2021).  
 
2.3. 
Potential Toxic Elements and Health Risks 
Like other filter-feeding bivalves, P. muricata is biologically predisposed to accumulate suspended 
particles from the surrounding environment, a process that maintain ecosystem functioning, but 
also increases exposure to contaminants, including potential toxic elements (PTEs) (Surm et al., 
2015b; Wright, Fan, & Iv, 2018). These contaminants, once absorbed, are often retained in 
bivalves tissues for extended periods due to limited metabolic elimination and may eventually 
transfer to humans through consumption (Otchere, 2019). 
PTEs such as cadmium (Cd), lead (Pb), mercury (Hg) and arsenic (As), occur naturally in marine 
ecosystems through weathering, atmospheric deposition, and runoff, but their concentrations and 
bioavailability can increase significantly during storm events, sediment resuspension, and 
anthropogenic inputs (Tokatlı et al., 2024; Zhuzzhassarova et al., 2024). Even in areas considered 
environmentally pristine, such as remote tropical islands, natural geochemical processes can 
mobilize trace elements into the food web. This is particularly relevant in Mozambique, where 
anthropogenic contamination such as direct discharge of untreated domestic sewage, industrial 
waste (including heavy metals), urban wastewater, agricultural runoff containing pesticides and 
==PAGE:18==
Cíntia da Conceição Cassimo Timba – DTU Food / Universidade Eduardo Mondlane – July 2025 
7 
fertilizers, and port activities, in addition to the disposal of solid waste and urban plastics, is 
concentrated in estuarine zones like Maputo Bay (Scarlet & Bandeira, 2014), while remote areas 
such as Guiduane Island remain largely unmonitored. 
The degree of accumulation in bivalves depends on multiple factors, including seasonality, 
hydrodynamics, and biological traits such as size, age, feeding behavior, and reproductive stage 
(Otchere, 2019; Mehouel & Fowler, 2022). In a recent study on P. muricata from Indonesia, PTEs 
concentrations varied across tissues: gills exhibited the highest accumulation (Pb 0.45 mg/kg; Cd 
0.12 mg/kg; Hg 0.08 mg/kg; As 0.20 mg/kg), followed by digestive organs, while muscle tissues, 
those most relevant for human consumption, consistently showed the lowest levels (Pb 0.10 
mg/kg; Cd 0.03 mg/kg; Hg 0.02 mg/kg; As 0.05 mg/kg) (Suratno & Purnadayanti, 2024). 
Comparable seasonal patterns have been documented in other bivalves, such as Mytilus 
galloprovincialis, in which Cd and Pb levels peaked during winter months, likely due to increased 
PTEs availability in resuspended sediments (Peycheva et al., 2023). 
While some elements like manganese (Mn) and zinc (Zn) are essential micronutrients at low 
concentrations, chronic exposure to PTEs such as Cd, Pb, Hg and As (inorganic arsenic) has 
been associated with adverse health outcomes, including neurotoxicity, kidney damage, 
developmental disorders and increased cancer risk (Azizi et al., 2018; Tolkou et al., 2023). In 
subsistence contexts, even low concentrations can become significant over time due to 
cumulative exposure, compounded by low dietary diversity and heavy reliance on bivalves. These 
PTEs tend to accumulate in filter feeders and may remain in edible tissues for long periods, 
entering in humans diets.  
Moreover, no prior exposure assessments have been conducted for P. muricata in Mozambique, 
and there is no available data on its PTEs concentrations in edible tissues, highlighting an urgent 
need for evidence-based evaluation of dietary exposure risks in vulnerable coastal communities 
 
3. Material and Methods 
3.1. 
Study Area  
The study was conducted on Guiduane Island (also known as Inhambane Island), located in the 
north of Inhambane City, at Inhambane Bay (IB) southern Mozambique (Figure 3-1), 
approximately 300km from Eduardo Mondlane University, in Maputo. IB lies near the Tropic of 
==PAGE:19==

Capricorn and is characterized by a tropical climate with two main seasons: a rainy (September-
March) and a dry season (April-August). The bay has shallow intertidal flats (average depth <5 m) 
and reaches 10 m in channel areas. It is influenced by semi-diurnal tides and water temperatures 
range from 21–27 °C (Chitará-Nhandimo et al., 2022).  
Fieldwork took place during low-tide in February (rainy season) and May (dry season) of 2025, in 
two intertidal harvesting sites: Bumo-Bumo and Ngangale. The total number of registered fishers 
on the island was 375 (CCP, personal communication, 2025).

3.3. 
Socio-ecological Survey 
A semi-structured questionnaire was applied to shellfish harvesters, CCP representatives, and

3.4. 
Laboratory Analyses 
3.4.1. 
Condition Index 
The Condition Index (CI) was calculated to assess the physiological state of each specimen,

3.4.3. 
Elemental Composition 
Microwave-Assisted Digestion 
Dried, homogenized tissues (0.20 g) were digested in pre-cleaned quartz vessels using the Anton 
Paar Multiwave 7000, following protocol FM073-1 (DTU FOOD, 2024). The digestion reagent was 
Plasma Pure® HNO₃. Digests were diluted to achieve appropriate acid concentrations (2–5% 
HNO₃), depending on the instrument. 
 
Essential Elements  
Quantification of iron (Fe), zinc (Zn) and manganese (Mn) was performed via Inductively Coupled 
Plasma Optical Emission Spectrometry (ICP-OES). Samples were diluted in 5% HNO₃ and spiked 
with Yttrium internal standard (100 ppb). Triplicate measurements were run with multielement 
calibration curves and matrix-matched standards. 
Selenium (Se) and copper (Cu) concentrations were determined by ICP-MS due to lower 
expected concentrations and higher analytical sensitivity requirements. 
 
Potentially Toxic Elements 
PTEs were determined using Thermo iCAP TQ ICP-MS, equipped with collision/reaction cell. 
Samples were diluted in 2% HNO₃ + 1% HCl. Internal standards (Bi, In, Rh at 100 ppb) were 
added. Mercury was quantified with a low-range calibration (0–5 ng/mL). After digestion, each 
sample was diluted with ultrapure water to a final volume of 25 mL. From this, an aliquot of 1000 µL 
was transferred to acid-cleaned tubes and further diluted 1:10 with nitric acid (final concentration: 
10%) prior to instrumental analysis by ICP-OES/MS. Instrumental precision and accuracy were 
validated with certified reference materials (CRMs: oyster tissue, dogfish), procedural blanks, and 
spike recoveries.

5. Discussion 
5.1. 
Socio-ecological role of P. muricata in Guiduane Island 
The results showed that P. muricata is predominantly harvested by women, 97.2%, serving as 
both a subsistence food and source of informal income. This gendered pattern is well documented 
in small-scale fisheries across Africa and Southeast Asian, where women dominate intertidal 
gleaning and shellfish collection, yet often remain invisible in formal governance processes 
(Harper et al., 2013; Fortnam et al., 2019). In Guiduane Island, most female collectors had only 
primary education or none, and their access to markets is mediated by mainland intermediaries. 
This condition reflect the broader marginalization of women in aquatic food systems (Short et al., 
2021). 
Field observation revealed that trade in P. muricata follows informal but structured channels, 
typically involving sales to female vendors from the mainland. While this enables economic 
participation with minimal capital, it reinforces market asymmetries by concentrating power in the 
hands of intermediaries. Similar dynamics have been reported in small-scale fisheries throughout 
the Global South, where women role limited their ability to negotiate better prices, participate in 
management and also access decision making spaces due to the lack of institutional recognition 
(Bennett et al., 2021; March & Failler, 2022b) 
From an ecological perspective, P. muricata helps to sustain coastal biodiversity by clustering in 
seagrass beds, where its shells offer attachment surfaces for juvenile organisms. The absence of 
P. muricata and seagrass beds in Bumo-Bumo during the rainy season highlights the disruption 
of the habitat, pointing to a possible harvesting pressure. This aligns with studies showing that 
overexploitation of benthic bivalves can lead to cascading effects on sediment stability and 
biodiversity (Hasselberg et al., 2024). In contrast, in Ngangale, the higher number of shellfish 
collectors could reflect the reduced availability of target species in Bumo-Bumo. 
Data from harvesting effort corroborate with this. In February, only 18 collectors operated in 
Bumo-Bumo compared to 57 in Ngangale, and no P. muricata was found in Bumo-Bumo. 
Although the number of collectors in Bumo-Bumo had increased (n=15), in May, they were 
targeting other species, highlighting a probable ecological threshold. Such spatial shifts in effort 
are common in coastal communities to respond to declines in natural resources (Béné et al., 
2015) 
==PAGE:38==
Cíntia da Conceição Cassimo Timba – DTU Food / Universidade Eduardo Mondlane – July 2025 
27 
The local governance structures, Community Fishery Council (CCP), seems to lack in strengthen 
and resources needed to effectively regulate harvesting. According to interviewees, controls over 
shellfish collection are very limited, with no clear rules guiding when, where P. muricata should 
be collected. This situation mirrors a wider pattern of institutional neglect, where small-scale, 
subsistence-oriented fisheries are often overlooked in favor of export-driven or industrial sectors 
(Jouffray et al., 2020; Bennett et al., 2021) 
Moreover, this neglect is compounded by systemic exclusion of women from decision-making 
spaces. When their inclusion in fisheries management fail, the social-ecological dynamics results 
in an incomplete picture, which undermines efforts to achieve sustainability; female actors often 
bring inclusive, collaborative approaches that are essential for effective marine governance (Gissi 
et al., 2018). 
This limited inclusion is further reflected in preservation and food safety practices. P. muricata is 
typically boiled and then sun-dried, a method that extends shelf-life and enables transport without 
refrigeration (Adeyeye, 2016). While boiling can reduce certain microbial risks, it does not 
eliminate PTEs which remain stable in the tissue regardless of thermal processing, and most 
harvesters remain unaware of related health risks. Moreover, sun-drying exposes the product to 
potential recontamination if not handled correctlly. 
Although P. muricata is not currently included in food strategies, its ecological and social 
significance needs attention. There are a disconention between policy frameworks and global 
ambitions, such as the SDG (14.2 and 14.b) ante the realities of coastal communities. While SDG 
emphasizes sustainable use and access for small-scale fishers, many locally significant species 
remain unrecognized in formal marine resource planning (HLPE, 2020; Hasselberg et al., 2024). 
Recognizing the ecological and social importance of P. muricata could support integrated 
strategies that address food security, biodiversity conservation, and gender-responsive 
livelihoods. Evidence from participatory marine management areas in East Africa suggests that 
inclusive co-management, especially when it acknowledges women’s traditional ecological 
knowledge, can enhance both conservation outcomes and equity (Fortnam et al., 2019; March & 
Failler, 2022b).

5.2. 
Physiological Condition and Nutritional Value of P. muricata: Seasonal and 
Spatial Patterns in Guiduane Island 
In this study, the protein content level varied significantly, ranged from 63.8% to 92.7% (dry 
weight), with higher values observed during the dry season in Ngangale (92.7 ± 3.8%).  
Although most studies of tropical bivalves usually report protein contents between 34% and 65% 
of dry mass (Shetty et al., 2013), the elevated protein levels observed in this study can be 
explained by favorable ecological conditions during the sampling period. Reduced environmental 
stress, increased food availability, and the post-spawning recovery phase promote greater 
assimilation and accumulation of protein reserves in the tissues of bivalves, as documented in 
studies on tropical bivalves (Sivaperumal et al., 2007; Gosling, 2008) 
Moreover, the proximity of mangrove habitats enhances local environmental conditions by 
providing abundant and diverse food resources, such as plankton and detritus, which support 
improved physiological status and higher protein accumulation in benthic filter feeders like P. 
muricata. Mangrove ecosystems are well documented as nutrient-rich environments sustaining 
high primary productivity and complex trophic interactions, positively influencing the biochemical 
composition of associated fauna (A & K, 2017). It is also well established that protein content in 
shellfish varies according to species, nutritional condition, physiological state, and life cycle 
productivity (A & K, 2017) 
The highest protein values coinciding with higher condition index (CI) values, highlighting the 
better physiological condition during this period. This trend is consistent with findings in other 
tropical bivalve species, where environmental conditions in the dry season promote increased 
feeding and energy reserve accumulation (Lagade et al., 2014). Although shell length ranged 
from 94 to 154 mm, it did not correlate significantly with CI or protein content, an observation 
consistent with studies showing that morphometrics parameters alone often fail to predict tissue 
condition in bivalves. This is well documented in bivalve studies and highlights the importance of 
physiological metrics for stock assessment (Chowdhury et al., 2019; Mekawy et al., 2021) 
The concentrations of iron (Fe), manganese (Mn), zinc (Zn), copper (Cu), and selenium (Se) 
detected in P. muricata showed variation within the ranges typically reported for tropical bivalves 
(A & K, 2017). Studies in tropical ecosystems, such as those conducted in Seychelles, indicate 
that the bioaccumulation of these elements is strongly influenced by organism physiology, dietary

habits, and local environmental conditions, including proximity to nutrient-rich habitats like 
mangroves (Bodin et al., 2013) 
 
5.3. 
Potential Toxic Elements (PTEs) and Human Health Risk in P. muricata 
Among the potentially toxic elements (PTEs) assessed, total arsenic (As) presented the highest 
concentrations, ranging from 30.9 mg/kg dw in the rainy season to 35.8 mg/kg dw in the dry 
season. Although Codex Alimentarius does not establish a maximum limit for total As in mollusks, 
these values raise concern due to the potential presence of inorganic arsenic (iAs), the 
toxicologically relevant fraction responsible formcarcinogenic, neurotoxic, and developmental 
effects. The total As may overestimate the risk of exposure. 
When considering dietary exposure, Estimated Daily Intake (EDI) values for As were elevated,for 
the two scenarios, exceeding the BMDL01 values set by EFSA (2009), indicating a potentially 
critical exposure scenario for habitual consumers. 
Cadmium (Cd) levels were below the Codex maximum level (ML) of 6.7 mg/kg dw, but some 
samples collected during the dry season, from Ngangale, reached 11.6 mg/kg dw, exceeding 
regulatory thresholds. The corresponding EDI estimates were exceeded the PTMI-based daily 
equivalent (0.83 µg/kg bw/day). Given Cd nephrotoxic effects, such exceedance implies a 
concern on risk of exposure, especially under regular consumption scenarios. 
Mercury (Hg) concentrations remained low across all samples (< 0.3 mg/kg dw), below the Codex 
ML of 1.7 mg/kg dw. EDI values were also negligible (0.021–0.022 µg/kg bw/day), staying within 
the tolerable weekly intake. This indicates a low risk of exposure from Hg by consuming via P. 
muricata. 
Lead (Pb) levels, remained under the Codex ML of 5.0 mg/kg dw, with the highest variability noted 
in dry season samples from Bumo-Bumo (mean = 0.88 ± 0.93 mg/kg dw). The corresponding EDI 
estimates (0.33 and 0.56 µg/kg bw/day) do not exceed regulatory guidance values.

6. Conclusion 
P. muricata has the potential to contribute to a sustainable aquatic food system in Guiduane Island 
by providing an accessible source of high-quality protein and essential micronutrients, particularly 
in contexts of limited access to animal-sourced foods. However, this contribution is conditioned 
by the presence of potentially toxic elements such as Cd and As, which pose a chronic health risk 
under high-exposure scenarios. 
From a socio-ecological perspective, the species supports benthic biodiversity and plays a cultural 
and subsistence role for coastal communities, especially women, who are central to the intertidal 
shellfish value chain. Yet, their marginalization in decision-making and lack of food safety 
awareness reveal governance gaps that undermine the sustainability of P. muricata. 
Ensuring that P. muricata contributes effectively to a sustainable food system will require an 
integrated approach that combines environmental monitoring, nutritional valorization, food safety 
education, and inclusive fisheries governance. 
 
7. Future Perspectives and Recommendations 
Future studies and initiatives could consider the following directions to strengthen the role of P. 
muricata in sustainable aquatic food systems: 
• 
Increase sample size to allow the evaluation of inorganic arsenic (iAs). 
• 
Use P. muricata as a locally relevant species to facilitate community-based risk 
communication on environmental contamination and food safety. 
• 
Explore the integration of P. muricata into local nutrition and food diversification 
programs, especially in vulnerable coastal communities. 
• 
Support community-based participatory monitoring of P. muricata populations and their 
habitats, incorporating both scientific and traditional knowledge. 
• 
Facilitate the inclusion of female shellfish harvesters in decision-making structures, such 
as Community Fisheries Councils (CCPs), to ensure more equitable and effective 
governance.

The objective of this study is to understand the nutritional composition of Pinna muricata and 
estimate the risks to human health associated with exposure to heavy metals through its 
consumption. This research seeks to contribute to food security and the conservation of marine 
resources, providing information that can help in the sustainable management of fisheries and 
the safe consumption of Pinna muricata. To do this, we will need to ask you some questions and 
collect some of the fish you catch. Your participation is voluntary. Please read the form carefully

participate. If you agree to participate in this study, you may interrupt the interview at any time if 
you feel uncomfortable. If you have any questions, you may ask for clarification at any time before 
or during the interview. 
 
General objective: To evaluate the nutritional value of Pinna muricata collected on Guiduane 
Island and estimate the risks to human health associated with exposure to heavy metals through 
its consumption on Guiduane Island, Inhambane Province.

Research rationale: This study is expected to reveal the nutritional value of Pinna muricata in 
terms of protein, essential minerals and beneficial fatty acids, contributing to food security and 
the fight against malnutrition in the province of Inhambane. Furthermore, by assessing levels of 
heavy metal contamination, the research may support risk reduction strategies, ensuring safe 
consumption through the strengthening of policies for the sustainable management of marine 
resources, and guide interventions aimed at nutrition and public health in favor of food security 
for communities in Guiduane. 
 
Participant selection: You were selected as a potential participant in the study because you live 
on this island and have characteristics that we believe are important for your inclusion in the study 
as a collector.  
 
Voluntary participation: You may choose not to take part in the study as your participation is 
voluntary. If you do not agree to take part in the study, this will not affect your access to any 
services or benefits provided by the government or other organizations in your community. 
 
Procedures: Participants will be recruited on the days of the visit, during low tide. Participants 
will be approached at the Pinna muricata fishing sites. The purpose of the study will be explained 
verbally and, if they agree to participate, they will be able to answer a questionnaire about their 
fishing habits, consumption and marketing of these bivalves. In addition, samples of Pinna 
muricata will be purchased from collectors for laboratory analysis, to assess their nutritional value 
and possible contamination by heavy metals. The interview will last approximately 45 minutes.  
 
Expected Duration of the Study: Collections will be carried out during the dry and rainy seasons. 
First collection: Seven days in February. Second collection: Five days in May. 
 
Risks and Benefits: There are no known risks associated with your participation in this study. 
The study will not have any direct benefits for you; however, it will contribute to assessing the 
nutrients present in Pinna muricata and identifying the heavy metals that may be associated with 
this bivalve, causing potential health risks due to its consumption. The study may therefore 
provide data to the government to help improve public policies and implement strategies for food 
and environmental security in coastal communities.
