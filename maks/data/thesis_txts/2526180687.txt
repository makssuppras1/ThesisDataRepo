Abstract
The human gut microbiome is a complex system with more than 1014 microorganism inhabi-
tants. In order to ensure their own survival in an environment with a strong selection pressure,
it is a necessity that the gut microbes adapt to their competitive environment. This leads to
mutations and potentially genetic variation in the microbes that in time can aﬀect evolutionary
changes. Mutations are prescribed to occur randomly, however, in the theory the molecular
clock, mutations occur linear over time, which is referred to as clock rates. Factors such as
diet, climate, socio-cultural habits, and hygiene are all known to pose an inﬂuence on the gut
microbiome, which essentially can impact the clock rates.
The main analysis of this project turned out to be threefold; hence, three analyses were con-
ducted with the aim of deﬁning the factors that inﬂuence the clock rate for signature genes
for the human gut microbes: Bacteriodes uniformis, Eubacterium rectale, Alistipes putredinis,
and Prevotella copri. Bayesian phylogenetic analyses, developed with the program BEAST2,
were used to measure the clock rates, providing an insight into the factors aﬀecting the gut
microbiome. In order to deﬁne the factors, which had an inﬂuence on the clock rates for the
gut microbes, analyses were conducted with the main objective to investigate the age and geo-
graphical factors.
The project initiated with a detection of the diﬀerence between infants and adults. The in-
vestigation of clock rate diﬀerences relating to infants and adults, proved that adults have the
fastest mutation rate in 3 out of 4 microbes. The analysis’ sample sizes were too high for the
Bayesian phylogenetic analysis, hence, it did not provide the most accurate result, from which
an investigation on the variations of the clock rates were conducted. The favorable sample
sizes were 50 and 100 paired gene samples, which were in total 4.700 and 9.500 paired gene
samples. Lastly, a clock rate measurement for detecting the geographical inﬂuence factor lead
to the result that Israelis people had the fastest rate, and the Nordic countries had the slowest
rate, respectively. The results indicate that factors such as age and geographical distribution,
inﬂuence the mutations rate in human gut microbes.

Introduction
3.1
The gut microbiome and the evolutionary forces
The gastrointestinal tract in humans is a well-developed ecosystem, consisting of more than
a 1000 species and further, it makes up approximately 1014 microorganisms. These microor-
ganisms include bacteria, viruses, fungi and archaea and are collectively referred to as the gut
microbiome [43]. Our gut microbiome is the foundation of our metabolism and digestion, as it
ensures its functioning [19]. Studies have shown a correlation between our gut microbiome com-
position and our immune system, and furthermore, how it has a signiﬁcant impact on how the
immune system manages to maintain itself and how it develops [41]. Moreover, an imbalance in
the gut microbiome composition has been associated with many diseases, such as inﬂammatory
bowel diseases, colon cancer and obesity [52]. The gut microbiome is very complex, as a large
part of the microbes are anaerobic and for this reason, a challenge to culture in the lab [43, 53].
Additionally, it often varies due to environmental and human diﬀerences, as these are shown to
have an inﬂuence on the composition in the gut microbiome [43]. It is well-known that the gut
microbiome composition is aﬀected by diet as well as antibiotic usage [43]. The composition
is also host speciﬁc, whereas it is less understood how this in correlation with environmental
factors, aﬀect the composition of the gut microbiome [27, 53]. The gut composition is also
aﬀected by a strong selection pressure [27]. Many studies have investigated the factors that
inﬂuence the gut microbiome composition, but less on the evidence of the evolutionary forces
that operate in the gut microbiome [27, 43].
Exploring the evolutionary forces that occur in the human gut microbes make it possible to
understand how the microbes develop through time and the eﬀect it has. As an example, it has
the potential to inﬂuence and beneﬁt the medicine development in the future, and further, give
a better understanding of how diet, age, antibiotic usage and hygiene can eﬀect inﬂuence the
gut microbiome.
3.1.1
The selection pressure in the human gut microbiome
The evolution and survival of the microbes in the gut microbiome are dependent on the competi-
tive advantage of each species and how they adapt to the selective pressure in the gut microbiome
[48]. This process, referred to as natural selection, where organisms adapt to its environment
by selective changes [58]. As an example, a competitive advantage could be producing toxins
or other consequences that can turn out beneﬁcial for the microbes survival. Another positive
outcome is bioﬁlm, which is a familiar characteristic for gut microbes, as makes it harder for
other organism to overthrow their territory in the gut [52]. Bioﬁlms are controlled by quorum
sensing which enables expression of speciﬁc genes at high cell densities, resulting in the most
advantageous phenotypes [38]. These competitive advantages are developed through time and
evolution, and they are known to be prevalent by mutations [32, 52].

Mutations can be crucial when considering the strong selection pressure and also for the survival
of the organism in the gut microbiome. A mutation can aﬀect the gut microbes characteristics,
which can have a competitive advantage [46]. Mutations may occur due to replication errors,
radiation, chemicals, and spontaneous degradation of DNA. In particular, the replication errors
generate the most mutations, which mainly results in deleterious [4, 29]. A mutation is deﬁned
as nucleotide change, also deﬁned as substitution, and it has the potential to result in genotypic
variations. These genetic variations can eﬀect evolutionary changes, and they are the underlying
reason why evolutionary diversity occurs and why it continuously happens [53]. In general,
evolutionary diversity occurs in two combined steps: mutation and selection. Mutations occur
randomly and can aﬀect the variation in phenotypic characteristics in the population, which can
impact its ﬁtness [46]. The selection is an adaption process and the better ﬁt the gut microbes
characteristics are in, the better survival rate and probability of developing a higher rate of oﬀ-
springs. A mutation can both increase and decrease the value of adaption to the environment.
Depending on the class of mutations it will pose diﬀerent consequences on the gut microbes
[26]. An increasing adaption value will decrease the gut microbes ﬁtness by developing a higher
number of oﬀ-spring, which will inherit the mutations and increase the frequency of the organism
characteristics. A decreasing adaption value will make the organisms’ probability of survival
smaller, and further, lower the frequency of ﬁtness [3]. Through generations a mutation, which
has increased in ﬁtness level, can be transferred to the whole population of species which will
deﬁne the mutation as ﬁxed [47]. Continuously, some mutations do not have a beneﬁcial nor a
detrimental inﬂuence on the microbes, and they do not impact transmission of the mutation in
the microbe species. These mutations are called neutral mutations [59].
3.2
The molecular clock the relation between mutations over time
Many mutations appear neutral in the microbes, and studies comparing pairs of organism
genomes and proteins have discovered a strikingly linear rate between mutations and time [18].
This phenomena and technique is referred to as the molecular clock. The molecular clock de-
scribes the rates of molecular evolution to be constantly increasing over time [18]. Moreover,
it explains the fundamental chronological context of evolutionary diversity and it includes esti-
mated times of species divergence [23, 56]. The molecular clock rates measure the frequencies
of mutations occurring over time, and they will be referred to as clock rates.
The clock rates are inﬂuenced by selection, and furthermore, studies have reported that popula-
tion sizes inﬂuence the clock rate [17]. Gene regions do also have an aﬀect on the rates [51]. Gene
regions that are not involved in protein production and function, appear to have a higher proba-
bility to evolve nucleotide substitution, than genes that encode functional part of the protein [6].
For most gut microbes the mutation rate is approximately 10−3 per generation in the lab,
although diﬀerent studies have stated that the scale of evolution seen in microbes in the gut is
present on a daily basis [3, 29].

The molecular clock is a useful tool to apply in order to explore clock rates for organism under
diﬀerent conditions. Other studies have used the technique to investigate fossils or applied the
technique to develop control strategies [2, 22]. For this reason, it could be useful to use the
molecular clock with the aim of investigating the clock rates for microbes in the human gut
microbiome, as it makes it possible to understand the evolutionary diversity and forces that
appear under the selection pressure. To measure these rates it is critical to know how we detect
the gut microbiome composition and diversity, and what method should be used.
3.3
Detection diversity in the gut microbiome
In an environment such as the gut microbiome it can be fairly hard to detect the diversity of
gut microbes. A solution to this, is to apply metagenomics which is a favorable method to
apply dealing with the detection of gut microbes [34]. The method uses the genetic material
sampled directly from the environment. Usually, for the gut microbiome, it includes sample
selections from feces/stool. This method isolates DNA directly from the samples and afterwards,
it fragments the DNA into reads that form a DNA library, and essentially, it will be sequenced.
The sequencing process determines the genomics DNA nucleotide acid sequence composition,
further, the process also translates DNA samples into computational data. The sequenced reads
are then assembled to align and merge the reads to reconstruct the original DNA sequences.
These DNA sequences of the gut microbes samples can be used to ﬁgure out the gut microbiome
composition and diversity, and the diversity is measured by the abundance [39].
3.3.1
Canopy clustering and metagenomics species detection
A method which can be applied to detect the gut microbiome diversity is based on binning
co-abundance genes and is called canopy clustering, where metagenomics species (MGS) are
determined. Initially, canopy clustering starts as metagenomics by extracting samples from the
environment, from this it sequences the reads and afterwards it is assembled by de novo. The
reconstructed genes are used as a non-redundant gene catalog with an abundance proﬁle for
each gene. The abundance is determined by counting matching gene sequences. The MGS’ are
determined by picking one gene called the ”seed” and the genes that have a co-abundance with
a Pearson correlation (PCC>0.9) will be clustered in a so-called ”canopy” proﬁling. A median
gene abundance proﬁle for the genes in the canopy will extend the range of genes and can result
in more co-abundance genes in the canopy. The process will continue until the clustering of
gene with co-abundance is stable. The ﬁnal clustering will be deﬁned as a MGS if it includes
>700 genes [44]. The workﬂow for canopy clustering is presented in Figure 1.
The human gut microbiome mainly consists of bacteria, and it is prescribed that the bacteria
with the highest abundance on phylum level is Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, and further, on
species level it is Bacteroides vulgatus, Bacteriodes uniformis and Alistipes putredinis [19, 27].
A beneﬁcial method to use in order to proﬁle the species in the gut microbiome, is the signature
genes, which is highly conserved, and moreover, it gives an uniquely characteristic pattern for
the diﬀerent species [14].

3.3.2
Signature genes as a phylogenetic tool
Signature genes can provide useful information in taxonomic classiﬁcation and phylogenetic
analysis of gut microbes. Signature genes are a group of unique genes that appear in the core
genome and are present within the majority of the species strains [24]. The signature genes are
one of most applied methods when investigating the whole biodiversity in the gut microbiome,
and it can detect additional phylogenetic relations [14].
Signature genes are very conserved, and therefore, they rarely mutate due to the fact that they
pose an important role for the functioning of the gut microbes species. The clock rate for gut
microbes signature genes is for this reason interesting, due to the fact that they are part of the
core genome, hence, a mutation would therefore have a high impact on the evolution for these
genes. As described in the section 3.2 mutations occur in diﬀerent clock rates for this reason, it
is interesting to explore factors that are known to impact the rates for gut microbes signature
genes.
3.4
Gut microbiome inﬂuence factors
Studies have shown that the gut microbiome composition is varying from adults to infants
and that some factors can aﬀect the composition [43].

To investigate the clock rates for these diﬀerent factors, which inﬂuence the gut microbiome, it is
essential to observe how other studies have used the molecular clock with the aim of measuring
the rates.
3.4.1
Analyses using the molecular clock for gut microbes
Investigation of the clock rates for human gut microbiome is complex and for this reason, it
is fairly hard to ﬁnd literature measuring the clock rates on this subject. Therefore, studies,
using the molecular clock to detect the factors inﬂuencing the gut microbiome for animals, were
analysed.
One study investigated the relation between gut microbiome of eight sequences from Glires
species living in Qinghai-Tibet [37]. A Bayesian phylogenetic approach was used, and the se-
quences were initiated by a multiple alignment method. A selection of a prior distribution,
substitution model and a molecular clock were made. Analyses were implemented in the pro-
gram BEAST (Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis by Sampling Trees) [11], and the results proved
that the gut microbiome composition of the Glires is host speciﬁc, which may be a reason for
the divergence time of the Glires.
Another study investigated the gut microbiome diversity of 59 Neotropical wild birds. This
study tested the taxonomic associations between bird hosts and the gut microbiome [30]. Fur-
thermore, they tested the geographical diﬀerences between the bird from Rica and Peru. For
this analysis, a Bayesian phylogenetic approach was established using BEAST. The sequences
were aligned with a multiple alignment tool. The study detected that the major microbes in
the wild birds were Proteobacteria, Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, and Actinobacteria. Further it

3.4.1.1
Bayesian phylogenetic analysis
The Bayesian method is a statistical inference methodologies which use probability distributions
in order to describe uncertainties for the unknown parameters of the model. The Bayesian
analysis measures the posterior distribution of the clock rates, which is a joint distribution,
measured by a likelihood function and a prior distribution [42].
The parameters for the molecular clock dating for signature genes would be the gene’s divergence
time deﬁned as t and the evolutionary rates as r. Given the data sequences (D), the posterior
distribution for times and rates are given as a function by Bayes theorem [22]:
f(t, r | D)
=
1
z ∗f(t) ∗f(r | t) ∗L(D | t, r)
(1)
Where f(t) is the prior on divergence time, f(r | t) is the prior on the rates of branches on
the tree, which is speciﬁed using a model of evolutionary rate drift of our own knowledge. The
L(D | t, r) is the likelihood of the probability of the data [42]. The proportionality constant z,
which makes the posterior distribution integrates to 1, as a statistical distribution [42]. Clock
rates are continues parameters and the constant makes the posterior probability distribution
impossible to compute, and for this reason, a simulation, using Markov chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC), is generated by doing random sampling to ﬁt the posterior distribution [22].
3.4.1.2
Markov chain Monte Carlo
Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a method which is applied when it is impossible to
measure the posterior distribution, as mentioned above. Instead, it generates random samples
from the posterior distribution [7, 22]. It uses the parameter space which includes all parameters
in the model, and it measures the likelihood for every parameter in the model [8]. The result of
a MCMC analysis is a histogram made by the random sampling for the posterior distribution
for the given data [7]. The histogram will have a distribution shaped bell-curve, where the
clock rate will be measured by the distributions mean. The Bayesian phylogenetic analysis with
MCMC is a process which is challenging to compute and for this reason, only speciﬁc programs
are used to measure this. A program called The Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis by Sampling
Trees (BEAST) [12], establishes Bayesian phylogenetic analyses, which can measure the clock
rates by MCMC. BEAST is a powerful tool for analyses, which consist of series of sampled DNA
sequences [22]. For the investigation of the clock rates for the gut microbiome, it is a necessity
to have several sampling points in order to detect the genetic variation that occurs over time.

3.4.1.3
Information about the BEAST2 package
The Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis by Sampling Trees version 2 (BEAST2)[11] is a Bayesian
phylogenetic analysis package which consists of several programs [12]. The package includes
the program with the short name: BEAUti2, BEAST2, Tracer.
Bayesian Evolutionary Analysis Utility 2 (BEAUti2) is a manual graphic interface tool, with the
purpose of deﬁning the setting for the Bayesian phylogenetic analysis in order to align the data
set. The program generates XML conﬁguration ﬁles that can be applied in BEAST2. BEAST2
manages the settings from BEAUti2 in order to operate the Bayesian phylogenetic analysis,
by using MCMC. BEAUti2 deﬁnes suitable models for the available alignment data set, which
include the site model, the clock model, the prior distribution, and chain length for MCMC.
Furthermore, it is possible to organize partitions and tip dates [13].
The site model describes the process of evolutionary change in aligned sequences [20]. It consits
of a substitutions model which expresses the relative rates for substitutions changes and a dis-
tribution model for the rate heterogeneity across sites, respectively [13]. The site model focuses
on the diﬀerences between the data sequences and will present the genetic diﬀerences [10].
The clock model is the parameter for the evolutionary rate across every branch of the phylo-
genetic tree, which form will depend on the model chosen for the speciﬁc data. Finally, the
Bayesian phylogenetic approach analysis converts the branch lengths of the trees, to evolution-
ary time, in order to compute transition probabilities [15, 17].
The prior distribution needs to be speciﬁed for each parameter: the site model, clock model and
tree. As aforementioned, in section 3.4.1.1, the prior distribution is our own knowledge of the
clock rate for the given data in the model, and hence, it is an important aspect when measuring
the posterior distribution for the clock rates [42].
If the data has been sampled at diﬀerent dates, it is possible to set tip dates for the dated data.
The tip dates can be speciﬁed in days, months or years. This is very essential for the data which
is sampled at diﬀerent time points and further, for detecting the clock rate.
Collection of data sets, also referred to as data partitions, are sets sharing evolutionary processes.
The data partitions can share models. The models includes tree, site model, or clock model [42].
The BEAST program uses the mentioned MCMC, which is a well-known algorithm used when
measuring the posterior distribution with random sampling. In such case, it is possible to select
the chain length of the MCMC analysis. The chain length is the number of steps collecting the
posterior distribution for the given data by the random sampling. The number of steps in the
MCMC depends on the data set sample size [42].

By using the BEAST2 program package it is possible to detect the clock rates for the gut

3.4.1.4
Aim of this study
The aim of this study is to develop a tool consisting of a Bayesian phylogenetic analysis with
the program package BEAST2 v.2.5.0 by using MCMC, in order to measure the clock rates
for human gut microbes signature genes. The objective of this is to developed a deeper under-
standing of factors such as age and geographical distribution which inﬂuence the clock rates
occurring in signature genes for diﬀerent species in the gut microbiome.

Data and Methods
This section will cover the workﬂow, methods and data used in this study to demonstrate the
investigation of clock rates for the human gut microbes, and how the workﬂow was established.
4.1
The data set
The multiple data sets applied in this project were metagenomic species (MGS) ﬁles for signature
genes for gut microbe species. The total data sets present 2.354 MGS ﬁles published by 17 public
studies on microbiome in stool. 1.177 of MGS ﬁles were on DNA level, while 1.177 were on RNA
level. Each MGS presents a species and contains gene samples in FASTA format. The FASTA
format consists of a header lead with ”>”, followed by sample ID and gene initials. Lastly, the
belonging gene sample sequence is listed below the header.
Out of the 2.354 MGS’ this study focused on 4 MGS ﬁles. The 4 applied MGS’ were: hg0002
Bacteriodes uniformis, hg0003 Eubacterium rectale, hg0008 Alistipes putredinis, and hg0054 Pre-
votella copri. All 4 selected MGS’ were on DNA level. A meta data set (meta longitudinel.RData)
was applied, which contained information about the gene samples in the FASTA ﬁles: sample
ID, person ID, time of sampling, age categories, and country. In the age category the adults and
infants were applied. The geographically range for this analysis included the following countries:
three Nordic countries (Denmark, Sweden and Finland), Kazakhstan and Israel.
4.2
Data processing

4.3
Evaluation of convergent BEAST2 run
The clock rate was measured in a mean value for substitutions per site per year (s/s/y). Besides
calculating the posterior for the model parameters, the BEAST2 calculates a Eﬀective Sam-
ple Size (ESS). The ESS is the number of eﬀectively independent samples from the posterior
distribution, for every parameters which indicates if the MCMC analysis has run through a
convergent number of steps. If ESS in >200 it will be veriﬁed as a convergent run, whereas
<200 provides an inadequate analysis and the estimate for the posterior distribution for the
clock rate is poor [14]. If the clock rate parameters had a low ESS, it were rerun in BEAST2
with a longer chain length as shown in Figure 3. All clock rate included a burn-in at 10%, which
means the clock rate presents the 90% conﬁdence level for the posterior distribution.

Discussion
6.1
The diﬀerences for clock rates for adults and infants
The molecular clock results for adults and infants for the three gut microbes (B. uniformis, A.
putredinis, and P. copri) revealed that adults have a faster clock rate than infants. This reveals
that mutations will happen more frequently in the adults gut microbes than in infants, which
could indicates the age as a factor inﬂuence the selection pressure in the adults gut microbiome.
The adults gut microbiome consists of a large diversity of microbes compared to infants, and
based on this fact the results make sense, since more microbes will make the selection pressure
stronger. Moreover, adults’ diets vary quite a lot, which is known to have a high impact on the
gut microbiome. On the other hand, the infants gut microbiome is under development, and for
this reason it will be under a strong selection pressure, however, at the same time the infants
will have a fairly stable diet [43].
A study which investigated the clock rate for E.coli strain ED1a which was sampled from a
fecal sample, measured the clock rate to be 6.90 ∗10−7. This means that one substitution per
1.449.275 sites per year occurs [28]. The E. coli has a slower clock rate compared to the once
measured for adults and infants. It is the same case with Shigella sonnei, as t had a clock rate
at 6, 0 ∗10−7 substitutions per site per year and is one substitution per 1.666.666 site per year
genome-wide [31]. Both have a slower mutation which can indicate some inaccuracy, due to
the fact that a whole genome should have a higher clock rate than signature genes. The whole
genome consists of genes which are not functional important for E. coli and S. sonnei. Many
mutations must occur in these genes, despite the fact that the clock rate is a mean estimate
and that it can not tell how much the E. colis and S. sonneis signature genes mutate.
Nonetheless, it must be noted that the ESS number for all 8 subsets were around 3-4,2 which
concludes that the results are not convergent. This means that the independent number of
samples is immensely low (>200) and further, that the estimations for the posterior distribu-
tions were poor. The clock rates are measured by the histograms frequent intensity. A ESS
number above 200 means that the analysis has enough estimates for the posterior distribution,
and deﬁnes it as being trustworthy. A low ESS and a mean clock rate can be unreliable, as the
highest frequency could potentially be the tail of the posterior distribution. On that topic, not
reaching the convergent ESS for the clock rate results, will consider the posterior distribution as
poor. More random sampling by the MCMC is needed, which means an increasement of steps
in order to establish the posterior distribution. A longer run of the analyses could possibly have
resulted in a higher number of ESS and a more valid mean for the clock rates, which could have
provided results where the clock rates for infants were faster than adults, respectively. However,
this also depends on the MCMC start point in the parameter space. If the MCMC starts in the
periphery of the clock rate distribution it results in a long tail, and it will take more steps to
establish a convergent result. The starting point for the MCMC is random, meaning that two
identical analyses will eventually give the same measurements for the clock rate if the amount
of steps in the chain is high enough.

The four MGS’s chosen for the analyses were hg0002 (Bacteriodes uniformis), hg0003 (Eu-
bacterium rectale), hg0008 (Alistipes putredinis), and hg0054 (Prevotella copri). The number
indicates the abundance level, where hg0002 Bacteriodes uniformis has the highest abundance,
whereas the lowest one is the hg0054 (Prevotella copri). The MGS’ were picked based upon two
factors; the ﬁrst reason was that the MGS had a high abundance in the gut microbiome, while
the second reason was that it is known that the MGS mutates [27]. A high abundance equals a
high number of gene samples. In the project, the results showed that a roughly estimated high
number of gene samples (>10.000) takes several months, even years, to establish a Bayesian
analysis using the BEAST2 package. For this reason, a solution could have been to decrease the
number of genes samples. An argument could be to only select the gene samples that had a time
diﬀerence more than, for instance, a 100 days between the sampling times. Many of the genes
samples only had two samples points with a time diﬀerence of one day between them. It is very
unlikely to detect many mutations between these samples. Having many of these, increase the
total gene sample size, and the BEAST2 analysis takes longer to develop. A threshold would
therefore be a solution.
The 8 subsets were generated by selecting adults and infants from the metadata set. As men-
tioned in the theory section of this report, there is a diﬀerence between the adult and infants gut
microbiome composition. Especially the fact that the infants microbiome is under development
in the ﬁrst two years of its life is a critical diﬀerence [43]. The metadata set did not include an
age deﬁnition for infants, child, kids or adults, which is most crucial for the infants. Deﬁnition of
the age category depends on people participating in the samples and their deﬁnition of an infant,
which means that the clock rate could be highly inﬂuenced by a incorrect and subjective opinion.
6.2
The clock rate variation for dataset sample size
The results showed a variation in clock rates for the total paired sample size. Despite the fact
that the variation was at its lowest for the 50 and 100 subsets, it is up for discussion if the 70
subsets would actually possess the smallest variation diﬀerence. This argument is based on the
2 subsets for 705 clock rates, which in Figure 5 proves to provide a bigger variation. Further,
the results did not cluster with the other subsets for 70 paired gene samples.
While running the subsets, the 501, 505, 705 and 1003 subsets noticed to have a diﬀerent behav-
ior than the other subsets. The running time for the analyses to complete were several weeks,
which is more than for the other subsets with the same subset size. In conclusion, due to the
fact that samples sizes were identical, there must be other factors in addition to the sample size,
which impact the Bayesian phylogenetic analyses in BEAST2. The only visible factor which
can be detected from Table 6 is that the clock rate results for the 8 subsets were diﬀerent from
the others. Consequently, a diﬀerent rate means that the genes contained a diﬀerent frequency
of genotypic variations. However, this does not conclude a logical explanation as this should
not inﬂuence the computational time. Another reason for this behavior could be the length of

the ESS number is low for such results, however, when two identical runs turn out with a very
similar clock rate (a single decimal), it can be questioned if it is even possible to determine that
the result is convergent or not.
Casting a ray of light on the results in Table 6 for the clock rates, it can be stated that the
clock rate measurements are sensitive in relation to the decimal numbers. The clock rate is the
amount of sites that one substitution occur per year (1/(s/s/y). A diﬀerence in one decimal in
relation for two identical analyses can result in a site diﬀerences between 100-7.500 sites. The
length of the signature genes is unknown, however, during the project it was noticed that a
length of a gene, roughly estimated, varied from 50-200 base pair, and each MGS having 92-99
genes which can be estimated to 4.500-19.800 base pair. Therefore, 7.500 sites can have a high
impact, hence, provide a diﬀerent perspective and conclusion of the evolutionary diversity for
the signature genes.
6.3
The clock variation for geographical diﬀerences
The results demonstrated that in 2 out of the 3 gut microbes (B. uniformis and Ali putredinis),
the Nordic countries had a slower mutation rate than Israel and Kazakhstan. These results
conclude that mutation occurs more frequently in the gut microbiome for people living in
Kazakstan, and especially in Israel, in relation to the Nordic countries. However, the hypothesis
could have been that Kazakhstan and Israel clock rates were slightly more similar, nonetheless,
it is hard to tell, because there are, to my knowledge, no studies which can be compared to. On
the other hand, Figures 6, 7, and 8 revealed that the Israeli clock rate distributions did not have
a high depth in relation to the Nordic countries and Kazakhstan. This means that Israel clock
rates possessed a higher standard deviation. The reason for this is that the Israelis clock rate
estimates are more scattered. A mean clock rate, with a high standard deviation, does make
the result uncertain, compared to ﬁnding a clock rate estimate with a low standard deviation.
The Nordic countries and Kazakhstan posterior distributions showed, as the standard deviation
is low, the clock rates are more convincing. However, this could possibly be caused by the low
ESS, and if more random sampling had passed, it could have resulted in a higher standard

Studies have shown signiﬁcant diﬀerences between populations in the gut microbiome, which

The Israelis diet is very diﬀerent and can be a result of the distinct clock rates. However, it

East and Central Asia the balance between rich and poor is bigger than in the Nordic countries,
which as a consequence also aﬀect diet habits [35]. Also hygienic habits and antibiotic use would
have been useful data, as these are known to have inﬂuence the gut microbiome [43]. Again,
the results showed that 6 out of a total of 9 results were not convergent, whereas all 3 from
the Kazakhstan and the Nordic countries were not, which again possibly have inﬂuenced the
results. Additionally, none of the analyses were completed in BEAST2 with all MCMC steps,
which would have provided a diﬀerent result.
6.3.1
Other factors that inﬂuenced the clock rates during the Bayesian phylo-
genitic analysis
6.3.1.1
Inﬂuence of gap sequences
In the creation of the gene ﬁles, there was a variation of prevalence of the genes samples.
In these situations as mentioned, the gene samples that were not prevalent in all genes were
inserted as ”gap” gene samples. The ”gap” gene sample was inserted in the length as long as the
aligned gene samples in the FASTA ﬁle. This resulted in a FASTA ﬁle for each gene containing
the same amount of gene samples.
These gap sequences impact the Bayesian phylogenetic
analysis in BEAST2. A nucleotide has a probability and a gap present for all four nucleotides
probabilities, which means a gap is present and has an equal marginal probability for the site
[25]. The amount of gaps sequences in each align gene ﬁle will inﬂuence each site probability.

The analysis which investigated the clock rates variation was the only one where the amount of
gaps where known, as some gene samples were discarded in the other analyses.
6.3.1.2
Solution of increase gene samples
Another factor which can inﬂuence the clock rate is the prior distribution, as it is our own
deﬁnition of how many substitutions occur in the signature genes. In this project, the prior
distribution was measured by two sequences from the same gene, and the person with the
highest time diﬀerence between the two sequences. This was a fairly easy way to normalize
the mutation rates for the subsets. A more accurate prior distribution would be to calculate
the substitution rate for all samples in the genes ﬁles between the samples dissimilar sampling
times for the subsets and from here, calculate the average. The prior would then represent all
the samples in the subsets instead of only two samples. Moreover, it would not depend on the
genes which were picked.
6.3.1.3
Substitution model and codon eﬀects
The substitution model used was the HKY, which has an unequal transition and transversion
rate and an unequal base frequency. This means that the probability of a nucleotide changing
to a diﬀerent given nucleotide depends on the target nucleotides frequency and if the nucleotide
is a transition or a transversion [16]. The model is not too complex, nor too easy to represent,
as it is quite hard to represent the actual evolutionary diversity. The third position in a codon
is known to be more likely to have a higher mutation rate than the ﬁrst and second positions [9].
Despite this, it is known that the third position does not have the same evolutionary inﬂuence
as position one and two in the codons. The reason for this is that the third position has a higher
impact on the translation into amino acids and the functionality of the protein [9]. Therefore, a
more complex substitution would be a better ﬁt to investigate the clock rate, nonetheless, this
process is already a computational challenge.
6.4
Other Bayesian phylogenetic analysis programs
In this project, the aim was to investigate the clock rates, and for this, the BEAST2 package
was used.
The results indicated that many analyses with BEAST2 would take months to
complete. Fortunately, there are other programs such as MrBayes [33] and MCMCTree [50]
that runs Bayesian phylogenetic analyses. Mrbayes and MCMCTree are executed by commands
which would have been a huge advantage in this project. The settings deﬁned in BEAUti2 are
done manually, and therefore, it would have been less time consuming if it had been done by
commands instead. However, it is unknown whether or not it would aﬀect the time frame of
the analyses.

Conclusion
The Bayesian phylogenetic analysis approach using BEAST2 was established to measure the
clock rates for signature genes for the human gut microbes: Bacteriodes uniformis, Eubacterium
rectale, Alistipes putredinis, and Prevotella copri. Furthermore, the aim was to investigate the
inﬂuence of factors such as age and geographical distribution, and how it aﬀected the clock rates.
The investigation of age diﬀerences showed that age does have an impact on the clock rate,
adults had a faster clock rate than infants, respectively. Consequently, mutations happen more
frequently in adults’ gut microbes. Despite this, it is diﬃcult to provide a general and complete
accurate description of how much age eﬀects the gut microbes; the reason being that in this
project, a high amount of gene samples (>10.000) in BEAST2 made it impossible to ﬁnish the
analyses. In order to specify a more accurate result, more time would be a necessity.
In continuation of this, a detection of the clock rate variation for diﬀerent sample sizes resulted
in a minimum variation of sample sizes at 4.700 and 9.500. Nonetheless, it can be discussed if
6.650 was in fact the sample size that obtained the minimum clock variation.
The measurements of the clock rate on the geographical diﬀerences indicates that there are
geographical factors impacting the gut microbes. It showed that the Israeli people had a faster
clock rate, which results in mutations occurring more frequently in people from Israel rather
than people living in the Nordic countries or Kazakhstan, respectively. Israelis’ clock rates were
in general distinct compared to the Nordic countries’ and Kazakhstans. When considering B.
uniformis and A. putredinis the Nordic countries had the slowest clock rate, whereas Kazakhstan
had the slowest in E. rectale. Generally, the Nordic countries and Kazakhstan appeared to have
clock rates similar to each other. The reason could be a result of dietary habits, which has
already been shown to have a high inﬂuence on the gut microbiome.
Consequently, the results showed that there are factors, such as age and geographical aspects,
which inﬂuence the signature genes’ mutation rates. However, it is not possible to precisely
state ”why”, as more detailed investigations are necessary for this.
