Abstract
Grave’s disease (GD) is an autoimmune thyroid disease (AITD) that causes hyperthy-
roidism. The disease aﬀects about 1 in 200 people. The underlying factor and epidemi-
ology of the disease are not yet fully understood. Diagnostic tools can help reveal more
information about the disease. As autoantibodies are speciﬁc for GD, they are an appeal-
ing target for diagnosing and understanding GD.
This thesis presents a strategy to synthesise novel epitopes to target autoreactive antibod-
ies in Grave’s disease. The project aims to synthesise a complex biantennary glycan with
a clickable linker. The clickable linker is introduced to the glycan as a propargyl. The
synthesis of propargyl D-mannose was successful, but optimisations for propargylation of
D-glucosamine is still needed.
A commercially available trisaccharide was used to test click reactions with peptides
sequences from the extracellular thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR) domain.
The reactions yielded two glycopeptides (10c and 4c) and need optimisation to produce
more synthetic epitopes.
The glycopeptide 10c was tested towards autoreactive antibodies with sera from Grave’s
patients by ELISA. The ELISA was performed with the unglycosylated peptide 10 and
a scrambled sequence peptide 13 as a negative control.
The ELISA initially showed
unspeciﬁc reactivity towards the healthy samples, there for the ELISA was repeated for
the healthy samples.
The results of the experiment with the use of IgG as a secondary antibody shows that
the glycosylated peptide (10c) and the unglycosylated peptide (10) both had an elevated
response towards the Grave’s samples with a signiﬁcance level of p < 0.05 compared with
the healthy samples, analysed by ANOVA.

Resumé
Graves sygdommen er en autoimmun skjoldbruskkirtel sygdom, der forsager hyperthyre-
ose. Sygdommen påvirker en ud af 200 i verden. De underliggende faktorer og epidemi-
ologien er ikke fuldt ud forstået. Diagnostiske værktøjer kan hjælpe med at afsløre mere
information omkring sygdommen. Da antistoﬀer er speciﬁk for Graves sygdommen, er de
et tiltrækkende targets for at diagnosticere and forstå mere af sygdommen.
Denne afhandling præsenterer en strategi for at syntetisere nye epitoper mod autoreaktive
antistoﬀer i Graves sygdommen. Projektets mål er at syntetisere et kompleks biantennary
sukker med en klikbar linker. Denne linker er introduceret til sukkeret via en propargyl
gruppe. Syntesen af D-mannose var succesfuld, men en optimering for propargyleringen
af D-glukoseamin mangler.
En kommercielt tilgængelig trisakkarid var brugt til klik reaktioner med peptid sekvenser
fra det extracellulære TSHR domæne. Reaktionerne producerede to glycopeptider (10c
og 4c) og mangler optimering for at lave ﬂere epitoper.
Glycopeptidet 10c blev testet mod autoreaktive antistoﬀer med serum fra Graves patien-
ter, med ELISA. ELISAen blev udført med peptid 10 og et blandet sekvens peptid 13,
som en negativ kontrol. ELISA vidste først at der var en uspeciﬁk reaktivitet mod de
raske prøver, derfor blev ELISAen resten igen for de raske prøver.
Resultaterne for eksperimentet vidste at med brug af IgG som et sekundært antistof, at
glykopeptidet (10c) og peptid 10 begge havde forhøjet værdier med Graves prøverne med
et signiﬁkans niveau på p < 0.05 sammenlignet med de raske prøver, testet med ANOVA.

Introduction
Grave’s disease (GD) is developed by approximately 3% of women and 0.5% of men during
their lifetime, with 20-30 cases per year per 100.000 persons. The treatment options for
Grave’s are radioactive iodine (RAI), antithyroid drugs (ATD) and thyroidectomy. The
treatments have a risk of causing hypothyroidism and recurrence of hyperthyroidism1.
The current diagnosis of the disease is based on extrathyroidal ﬁndings as well as labo-
ratory ﬁndings. The detection of serum T3 and T4 and antibodies against the thyroid-
stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR) are used for the diagnosis of GD. However, assays
for distinguishing between thyroid-stimulating and thyroid inhibiting antibodies.2,3
The main goal of this project is to synthesise multiple novel epitopes for targeting autore-
active antibodies in Grave’s disease.
This thesis presents a strategy for a synthetic glycan with a click alkyne handle. The
glycans found on the TSHR has been identiﬁed to be complex N-glycans4. Therefore, the
designed glycan is a complex biantennary glycan, which should mimic the glycans on the
TSHR. The bioconjugation of glycans and peptides using click chemistry and the reac-
tivity of the glycopeptide towards autoreactive antibodies are presented in the following
thesis.
2.1
Grave’s Disease
Grave’s disease is the most common autoimmune thyroid disease (AITD) in the West-
ern World5. Autoreactive thyrotropin receptor antibodies (TRAb) or thyroid-stimulating
immunoglobulins (TSI) interact with the thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR),
mimicking the thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH). The interaction activates the sig-
nalling pathway of the TSHR, causing an overproduction of thyroid hormones leading to
thyrotoxicosis2,6. The exact underlying factors, genetic and environmental, that trigger
Grave’s disease is not fully understood, but it is suspected that immune modulators like
CD25, CD40, CD40, CTLA-4, FOXP3, and HLA are linked to the disease. Furthermore,
thyroglobulin (TG) has also been linked to the disease, and the TSHR α subunit is con-
sidered the main autoantigen in GD.7,8. Since 70% of the known genes correlated with
GD is involved in T cell function, T lymphocytes are also considered signiﬁcant in the
3
==PAGE:11==
AITD pathogenesis5. In GD, the intrathyroidal autoreactive T-cells escape the immune
checkpoints and interacts with MHC class II molecules and thyroid antigens, which will
interact with thyroidal B-cells, which then produces thyroid-stimulating antibodies. As
autoantibodies that are directed against the TSHR is central and speciﬁc against GD, it
is also believed that that the TSHR is one of the main factors for the pathogenesis of
GD. There are three classes of autoantibodies in GD; Activating, blocking and neutral.
Activating antibodies mimic the TSH, causing hypothyroidism, blocking antibodies block
the TSH action and signalling by binding to the TSHR α subunit while neutral antibodies
bind to the same part of the receptor with no impact on the receptor5,9.
2.2
Thyroid stimulating hormone
Thyroid-stimulating hormone, is produced in the pituitary gland and contains three diﬀer-
ent glycosylation sites, which is N-glycosylated with complex N-glycan chains. as seen in
ﬁgure 1. The TSH glycosylation is heterogeneous, with diﬀerences shown in the subunits.
The α subunit is prone to mono-sulphated and sialylated, while the β subunit is more
prone to di-sulphated structures and core fucosylation. These diﬀerences lead to varying
bioactivity, e.g. de-glycosylation in the β subunit leads to a ﬁve-fold less immunoreac-
tivity to antibodies. The TSHR is a G-protein coupled transmembrane receptor with
two subunits, an extracellular α unit and a transmembrane and cytosolic β polypeptide4.
The extracellular chain has three distinct binding regions for TSH, which form a com-
plex binding pocket for the stimulating protein. Furthermore, the TSHR α chain has six
N-glycosylation sites (Asn77, Asn99, Asn113, Asn177, Asn198, Asn302, seen in ﬁgure 1),
where Asn1113 is speciﬁc for human TSHR, and Asn177 is speciﬁc for mammals. The
glycosylation of the TSHR is considered detrimental to the function of the receptor. Na-
gayama et al. showed that at least four glycosylation sites were necessary for a functional
TSHR, and when three glycosylations on the α unit weren’t present, neither TSH binding
nor cAMP response was detected. It has also been postulated that the N-glycosylations
play a vital role in the correct folding of the TSHR protein in the ER10. The high degree
of glycosylations in both TSH and TSHR is believed to be a cause of the immunogenicity
in AITD. The TRAbs and TSH both bind on the extracellular α unit of the TSHR. This
is believed to be one of the explanations for Grave’s disease.
4
==PAGE:12==
Mice have one less glycosylation site than humans. Research shows that mice immunised
with a higher glycosylated α subunit of the TSHR had a signiﬁcantly higher immune
response compared to mice with their own less glycosylated mouse TSHR α subunit4. The
same ﬁndings were also shown by Gattadahalli et al., three TSHR ectodomain proteins
expressed by two cDNA’s and one from baculovirus envelope protein, was tested for
their ability to interact with autoantibodies from patients with either hyperthyroidism
or hypothyroidism. The study showed that only the glycosylated TSHR proteins would
interact with the autoantibodies. These ﬁndings prove that glycosylation of the THSR
α subunit is important for the interaction of autoantibodies in GD. Glycosylation sites
could be a possible explanation of Grave’s disease11.

2.3
Solid phase peptide synthesis
Peptides play an important role in biology and medical research ﬁelds, biochemical pro-
cesses and physiological functions and have been a big focus since the ﬁrst coupling of 2
amino acids in 1903. Solid-phase peptide synthesis SPPS was ﬁrst published in 1963 by
5
==PAGE:13==
Merriﬁeld. The procedure is based on attaching the ﬁrst amino acid to the solid phase, a
resin, and then elongating the peptide sequentially, attaching one amino acid at a time.
As the growing chain is attached to an insoluble resin, an excess of reagents can be used
and easily ﬁltered and washed away with appropriate solvents. The strategy developed
by Merriﬁeld needed improvement to both milder reaction conditions and better resins.
SPPS procedure has been improved on with the introduction of better protecting groups,
coupling agents and resins, like 2-chlorotrityl and rink amide shown in ﬁgure 2. With the
introduction of new resins, the Fmoc/tBu strategy made SPPS easier as it uses milder
deprotection conditions, is orthogonal to all other protection groups used in peptides and
doesn’t require special equipment for the ﬁnal cleavage from the resin, as the Boc strategy
uses hydroﬂuoric acid (HF)12,13.
Figure 2: 2-chlorotrityl chloride and rink amide resin, normally used for Fmoc SPPS
After loading the ﬁrst amino acid on the resin, the ﬁrst step in SPPS is deprotection of
the nitrogen, which is usually protected by Fmoc. The amino acid is usually deprotected
using piperidine, after which the activated carboxylic acid of the next amino acid in the
sequence is attached. The activation of the carboxylic acid makes the hydroxyl a good
leaving group by introducing an electron-withdrawing group and can happen by multiple
reagents including HATU, PyBOP and DCC. After the coupling of the amino acid to
the solid supported growing chain, the cycle is repeated until the sequence is complete,
as shown in ﬁgure 3. After the sequence is complete and the last deprotection of Fmoc
has been completed, cleavage from the resin is performed, which should be performed
with scavengers to ensure full deprotection of the side chains and also trap unwanted
carbocations that might react with the peptide and form by-products.
6
==PAGE:14==
Figure 3: General SPPS Fmoc strategy
After the improvements of the solid phase synthesis and the increasing demand for fast
and simple chemical peptides synthesis, automated solid-phase peptide synthesis was de-
veloped. Peptide assembly can be performed automated and simultaneously in multiple
reaction vessels. The washing and deprotection are carried out by the same solutions,
but the couplings are individual for each vessel but are carried out at the same time.
The development of automated peptide synthesis has made it possible for the fast and
simple generation of chemically synthesised peptides. Furthermore, the automation has
also been improved by real-time monitoring via fx. chromophores to improve yields and
the reaction time.14,15
2.4
Glycoproteins
Glycosylation is the most common post-translational modiﬁcation of proteins.
There
are three post-translational glycosylations of proteins; O-GlcNAcylation, O-glycosylation
and N-glycosylation4. Glycosylation in humans takes place in the vesicles in the secretory
pathway, the endoplasmatic reticulum (ER) and the Golgi apparatus. In vivo O-linked
glycosylation starts with xylose addition or with N-acetylgalactose (GalNAc) addition to
7
==PAGE:15==
serine or threonine, with no consensus amino acid sequence, but glycosylation sites can be
predicted by computational tools. After the attachment of O-linked glycosides, the galac-
tose is extended to one of the four core structures shown in ﬁgure 4, but can also be ex-
tended with poly-N-acetyllactosamine chains that are terminated with sialic acid. Further-
more, O-glycosylation can also consist of a single O-GlcNAcylation, which is mostly for cel-
lular proteins, while extracellular and surface proteins are N- and O-glycosylated. N-linked
glycosylation is the addition of an oligosaccharide, the asparagine residue of the consensus
amino acid sequence Asn-xaa-Ser/Thr, where xaa can be any amino acid except proline.
The glycosylation happens in the ER as the protein is translated. As the protein is trans-
ferred from the ER to the Golgi apparatus, multiple modiﬁcations of the oligosaccharide
occur with various glycosylhydrolases and glycosyltransferases.16 In eukaryotes, oligosac-
charides share the core structure of Manα1,3(Manα1,6)Manβ1,4GlcNAcβ1,4GlcNAcβ1-
Asn, these undergo modiﬁcations into three diﬀerent types; a high mannose, a complex
and a hybrid shown in ﬁgure 4.
These Oligosaccharide glycosylations strongly aﬀect
the function of the proteins, the folding, cell signalling, stability, adhesion regulation of
cell-cell communication and immunological recognition4,17.
The transfer of glycans to the proteins require an activated nucleoside triphosphate donor
and sugar acceptor in both O-linked and N-linked glycosylations. The transfer is mediated
by glycosyltransferases (GT).

and TSH receptor recognition. Changes in glycosylation in the thyroid gland contribute
to thyroid disorders4.
2.5
Chemical synthesis of glycans
Synthesis of glycans is important as carbohydrates play an important role in life sciences,
and the chemical route can solve some of the problems in the synthesis of glycans with
reliable mechanisms and strategies. The synthesis has always been challenging with stere-
ospeciﬁc glycosylations and modifying one hydroxyl group in the presence of many18–20.
The synthesis of glycans requires a glycosyl donor and a glycosyl acceptor. The donor
is an electrophilic species that reacts with the acceptors’ free hydroxyl group. The syn-
thetic route and choices of protection groups are very important to acquire a single free
hydroxyl group in the glycosyl acceptor and to protect the glycosyl donor so that the stere-
ochemistry of the linkage can be controlled. The glycosidic bond can either be 1,2-cis or
1,2-trans between the substituents on C-1 and C-2. The stereochemistry can be con-
9
==PAGE:17==
trolled using features of the glycosyl donor and protecting groups (PG). A factor that can
control stereoselectivity is the glycosyl acceptor, where the choice of leaving group (LG),
promoter, order of addition, reaction temperature, pressure and concentration can aﬀect
the stereochemistry of the reaction. Another factor is the sterical hindrance around the
anomeric position that can be used to control the conformation of glycosylation. Further-
more, remote PGs can also be used by sterically imposing conformational constraints and
thereby control the stereochemical outcome. Some strategies to control the stereochemical
outcome is shown in ﬁgure 5. The eﬀect of neighbouring group participation can either
promote trans or cis conformation depending on the eﬀect of the PG at the C-2 position.
An assisting PG like 2-O-acetyl will stabilise the oxocarbenium ion and promote trans
selectivity. Contrary to a non-participating group like the (S)-(phenylthiomethyl)benzyl
PG will lock the molecule in a trans-decalin form which will block the trans attack of the
glycosyl acceptor leading to a cis conformation, shown in ﬁgure 5A21. The remote con-
trol can, e.g. be 4,6-O-Benzylidine acetal in β-mannose glycosides and 4,6-O-silyl acetal
groups in α-galactose or α-N-acetylgalactosamine promote cis conformer22. Controlling
the stereochemistry in aminoglycosides, the oxazolidinone ring locks C-2 and C-3 sub-
stituents in an oxazolidinone conformation which, after an acid-catalysed anomerization,
gives the cis-conformation seen in ﬁgure 5C. Another way of achieving 1,2 transglyco-
sylation is activator-mediated concerted Sn2-type reactions (5D), the glycoside donor
leaving group is activated, allowing the acceptor to attack trans to the C-2 position23.
α-sialylation is the most diﬃcult transformation due to the tertiary anomeric carbon and
no neighbouring groups to participate. A solution is to use carbamate protections to-
gether with an α-phosphate leaving group. As sialic acids are present in many diﬀerent
glycoproteins, it is an important solution and has been used to synthesise oligosaccharides
for vaccine development24.

2.6
Bioconjugation
The chemical modiﬁcation of biomolecules are sought after as modiﬁed biomolecules play
an important role in drug development, biological research to reveal new information
about biological systems, diagnostic purposes as well as creating biological probes. Bio-
conjugation makes it possible to join together biomolecules and small molecules such as
ﬂuorophores, toxins or therapeutics.25–27 Chemical modiﬁcations are a heavily researched
11
==PAGE:19==
ﬁeld, and multiple reactions have been developed utilising cysteine residues, which are
rare in natural proteins, making them excellent for chemical modiﬁcations. Furthermore,
bioorthogonal reactions like click reactions and disulﬁde reactions are also used for biocon-
jugation. Protein modiﬁcations are highly valuable for studying biochemical mechanisms
and proteins under post-translational modiﬁcations in the body. Site-speciﬁc reactions
and control over the stability of the bioconjugation are important control. Using the nu-
cleophilic cysteine residue with fx. Maleimide, which is highly reactive under thio-Michael
conditions, as a site-speciﬁc bioconjugation, is very popular, even though at higher pH,
maleimide can cross-react with lysine residues, thereby lowering the site-speciﬁcity28.
Maleimide conjugates are also known to undergo retro-Michael addition as well as being
hydrolysed under physiological conditions as shown in scheme 1. The retro-Michael ad-
ditions lead to an early release of the conjugate as well as a free thiol which is free to
react with electrophiles in the body. A study conducted by Tumey et al. showed that the
quicker hydrolysis of the maleimide conjugate had increased eﬃcacy and safety compared
to the non-hydrolysed bioconjugate29.

pathway of thiosuccinimide to stable thioethers
Another utilisation of cysteines is disulﬁdes bridges. These bonds are not very stable in
biological environments, as molecules like glutathione reduce the disulﬁdes to free thiols.
This can, however, be an advantage in the pro-drug strategy. Deng et al. developed a
disulfate self-immolating linker, which after disulﬁde cleavage inside the cell will self trigger
a cascade rearrangement releasing the drug30. Disulﬁdes can also be taken advantage of
in chemical ligation in nucleosides where modiﬁed nucleosides are joined together, often
by a template, which is the complementary strand of the modiﬁed strands, shown in ﬁgure
628.

As stated earlier, central to bioconjugation is selectivity.
Click chemistry provides a
bioorthogonal reaction that can combine biomolecules and non-biomolecules that aﬀect a
biological system. The reaction is mutually reactive and does not cross-react with biologi-
cal functionalities, and the product is stable in physiological conditions, is highly speciﬁc,
has nearly quantitative conversion and limited or no residual by-product. One of the only
drawbacks of click chemistry is the Copper assisted azide-alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC)
is challenging when used in living cells as the free copper ions at high concentrations are
toxic to the cells. Otherwise, CuAAC is highly versatile as labelling, peptide-conjugate
linking or capture of nucleic acids. This restriction can be mediated using strain promoted
azide-alkyne cycloaddition (SPAAC), which uses a strain in cyclooctyne to promote the
reaction, which can be modiﬁed by an electron-withdrawing group to tune the reactivity
of the octyne.26,31

2.7
Analytical methods
2.7.1
MALDI-ToF-MS
Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionisation-time of ﬂight mass spectroscopy (MALDI-ToF-
MS) is a very sensitive qualitative method. The method uses a soft ionisation technique,
which preserves the molecules or minimises the change of fragmentation of the analyte32.
A sample is mixed with a matrix compound like the 2,5-Dihydroxybenzoic acid (DHB),
which is used for peptides and oligosaccharides or 3-hydroxypicolinic acid (HPA), which
is used for oligonucleotides. The sample solvent evaporates, and the sample co-crystallises
with the matrix. A short pulse UV laser is irradiated to the targeted spots, which then
desorbs from the surface and ionises the analyte and produces a charged high mass ion33.
The time of ﬂight (ToF) part of the machine consists of a ﬂight tube and an ion detector
to measure the ﬂight time, and intensity of the analytes and calculates mass to charge
ratio (m/z) based on equation 1.
m/z = 2e × E(t/d)2
(1)
Where E is the accelerating voltage, e is the elementary charge, t is the time of ﬂight
and d is the length of the drift zone inside the instrument. Heavier molecules will ﬂy
slower than the smaller molecules, and ideally, the same molecule should have the same
ﬂight time, but the kinetic energy from the laser is not experienced equally by each of
the molecules in the matrix, which aﬀects the resolution of the method which is one of
the drawbacks. To overcome this, an electrostatic ion mirror can be used, which increases
the resolution by lowering the diﬀerence in kinetic energy as well as adding ﬂight time.
MALDI is relative stable towards inference from salts, buﬀers and detergents. However,
high concentrations of buﬀers and detergents may interfere with the analysis, and therefore
further puriﬁcation may be necessary before improving the quality of the MALDI-MS34.
2.7.2
HPLC
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) uses pressure to force a solvent (mobile
phase) through a column (stationary phase), which gives separation depending on the 2
14
==PAGE:22==
phases35. There are four types of (HPLC). Three of them are based on the molecular
interaction employed in the technique, normal-phase (NP), reverse-phase (RP) and ion
exchange (IE). The last one is size exclusion (SEC), which is based on the size of the
molecules and is mostly used for polymers36. The normal phase uses a polar stationary
phase and a less polar solvent, while the reverse phase is much more common and uses
a non-polar stationary phase and a more polar mobile phase, where water-based solvent
systems are mainly used. RP-HPLC is a great method for separating and purifying reac-
tions mixtures and polar compounds, like peptides, in solutions, which is the application
in this project.
2.7.3
CD
Circular dichroism (CD) is a tool that is used for rapid detection of the secondary structure
of a protein or nucleic acids. The method uses the optical activity of a molecule, which is
measured by a circular polarised light. When the light is shined through the sample, an
optically active (chiral) molecule will absorb the light, and the result can be shown as the
∆E, which is the diﬀerence in absorbance of either the left or right polarised light. For
proteins and peptides, the peptide bond can act as a chromophore and can be measured
from 190-250 nm to get the secondary structure of the protein or from 250-320 nm to
measure the tertiary structure.
The method is great for rapid detection but doesn’t give residue-speciﬁc information.
2.7.4
PEP-FOLD
PEP-FOLD is a de novo structure prediction from amino acid sequences. The method
is based on structural alphabet letters and the lowest coarse-grain energy of the peptide.
The method simulated the sequence up to 200 times, after which it gives an overview
with the coarse-grain score, predicted QMean and TMscore. It gives the 3D structure of
the peptide, which can be used to investigate speciﬁc residues of a peptide. Furthermore,
the method also predicts the local structure seen in ﬁgure 7, where red is helical, green is
extended, and blue is random coils.37,38

Enzyme linked immunosorbent assay
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) can be performed in several formats such
as direct, indirect, sandwich and competitive ELISA, illustrated in ﬁgure 8. It’s a sensi-
tive, versatile method that is used to detect and quantify a broad range of biomolecules,
including antibodies, antigens, proteins, glycoproteins, and hormones39. The detection is
based on complexing antibodies and antigens to produce measurable results, usually by
colour development. The ELISA consists of four steps:
1. Coating
2. Blocking
3. Detection
4. Final read-out
Between each step, washing is required in which phosphate-buﬀered saline (PBS) is usually
used. The washing is to remove any unbound substrates. The detection is, as stated ear-
lier, by colour development. The enzyme most commonly used is horseradish peroxidase
(HRP) for a colourimetric signal generation due to the aﬀordability and the ease of conju-
16
==PAGE:24==
gating HRP to immunoglobulin (IgG). Together with HRP 3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine
(TMB) is the preferred substrate for colour development.
The indirect ELISA begins with a coating of the antigen to the plates, which is bound by
non-covalent hydrophobic interactions between the protein and the polystyrene surface40.
Following incubation of the antigens, the plates are washed with the buﬀer. After the
washing step, the plates are blocked to block any unbound sites on the plate to minimise
the potential for false-positive results.
The blocking is done by animal proteins like
bovine serum albumin (BSA), which is followed by another washing step. Next, a primary
detection antibody is added, typically in sera or plasma samples, followed by the addition
of an enzyme-linked secondary antibody.
Figure 8: Overview of the four diﬀerent ELISA settings
At the ﬁnal step, a substrate is added (TMB/H2O2 for HRP enzyme).
The enzyme
attached to the secondary antibody converts the colourless TMB into TMBox, which
absorbs light at 450 nm. Enzymatic oxidation of TMB is stopped by adding 1M H2SO4.
The absorbance values for the produced TMBox are measured using a plate reader and
analysed. Using a calibrator with a known amount of the antibody, A450 values can be
converted into concentrations of antibodies in the test sample.

2.7.6
Statistical methods
Following the bioanalysis of a large number of samples, which is a requirement to reach
the needed study power, statistical methods are typically applied. Statistical methods
allow for comparison of study and control groups, comparison of multiple test outcomes
etc.
In statistics it is often tested whether the means of the data are diﬀerent, also called a
null-hypothesis:
H0 = µ1 = µ2 = µ3... = µn
(2)
Where H0 is the null-hypothesis and µx is the mean for the group x. The statistical
analysis used is called a one-way Analysis of variance (ANOVA) and is used to compare
the means of a population. The ANOVA shows how diﬀerent the means of the population
are by measuring how much of the variance is explained by the groupings, and if enough
is explained by the variance, the signiﬁcant diﬀerence of the means of the population can
be concluded. For the ANOVA analysis two assumptions have been made:
1. The data comes from a normal distribution in each group
2. The variance from each group are the same
The variance can be checked by looking at the group-wise box plot.
For the normal
distribution, the residuals of the data can be checked as they should follow the zero-mean
normal distribution, which can be seen by a Q-Q-plot.
The data is often shown by boxplots, where groupings, the median and the within-group
variations can be visualised. The boxplot is usually plotted with the median, the two
quantiles Q1 and Q3, which is the upper and lower limits of 25% and 75% as well as the
maximum and minimum observation.41

5
Discussion
Antibody diagnostics are used to detect autoantibodies in a variety of diseases.
The
binding of antibodies can expose information about the autoimmune disease processes.
Synthetic epitopes can play an important role to develop diagnostics against autoimmune
diseases, i.e. citrullinated protein antibodies in rheumatoid arthritis and mimotopes in
systemic lupus erythematosus58,59.
In Grave’s disease, thyroid-stimulating antibodies (TSAbs) and thyrotropin binding in-
hibiting antibodies (TBIAbs) are found. It has been suggested that the antibodies inter-
act with diﬀerent receptor epitopes. The appearance of TSHR autoantibodies (TRAbs)
is particular for Grave’s. Therefore, the detection of elevated TRAbs is essential for di-
agnosing GD. In a study by Kosugi et al. mutated rat TSHRs was created and analysed
for interactions against TSH, TSAbs and TBIAbs. The mutations of potential glycosy-
lation sites showed that the absence of the carbohydrate changed the binding activity
of TSH and the activity of TSAbs, although the change in activity and binding did not
distinguish between direct and indirect ligand eﬀect (e.g. the change in conformation of
the receptor), Kosugi showed a change in activity for multiple diﬀerent mutated residues
and also that glycosylation of the TSHR was critical for the activity of TSAbs.60 More-
over, it was reported that the N-linked oligosaccharide chains are critical for bioactivity
of the glycoprotein hormones61. This project presents a synthetic chemical strategy of a
complex biantennary clickable glycan used to modify azide-functionalised TSHR sequence
peptides. The clickable glycan should mimic the N-glycosylation of the peptides sequence
of the TSHR, as the post-translational modiﬁcation of glycosylation is essential for the
activity of autoantibodies from GD.
Mohan et al. demonstrated synthetic epitopes from oligonucleotide-encoded peptidomes.
The epitopes were used in evaluation with phage immunoprecipitation sequencing (PhIP-
Seq). The method demonstrated by Mohan is a great way of analysing antibody repertoire
binding speciﬁcities with high throughput, but the method has some limitations like post-
translational modiﬁcations, which will most likely be absent from the library and thereby
PhIP-Seq might fail to identify monoclonal antibodies.62
Click chemistry can be used to make post-translational modiﬁcations and to form antibody-
42
==PAGE:50==
drug conjugates (ADCs). Classic conjugation methods use maleimide, disulﬁdes or lysine
amine coupling, which can have a shorter circulating half-life, lower eﬃcacy and oﬀ-target
eﬀects. Introduction of unnatural amino acids and N-glycan remodelling has been used to
address these issues63. Toftevall et al. describes a two-step enzymatic modiﬁcation and
N-glycans on antibodies followed by copper-free azide-alkyne click-chemistry. The discov-
ery of IgG speciﬁc endoglycosidases made it possible to expose the GlcNAc on the IgG
Fc-domain. Using galactosyl transferases and UDP-GalNAz (and azide modiﬁed GalNAc)
made an azide activated IgG. Using cyclooctyne modiﬁed molecules, ADCs can be syn-
thesised using this strategy, which has shown to have intact immunoreactivity. Complex
N-glycans are also crucial for human erythropoietin (EPO). The biological activity is de-
pendent on these glycosylations. Streichert et al. has used copper-assisted azide-alkyne
cycloaddition to create biologically active biantennary EPO glycoconjugates. Three EPO
variants with alkynes at the natural glycosylation sites were recombinantly expressed.
Two azide glycans were tested with diﬀerent conditions for the click reaction. The expe-
rienced Fenton-like degradation of the glycan was minimised by the change of CuSO4 to
(CH3CN)4Cu(1)–PF6 and a tris triazole as a complex ligand. With these conditions, the
EPO-glycan conjugates could be made. The conjugated EPOs showed increased biological
activity with a single glycan conjugation.64 This project focuses on creating THSR epi-
topes from chemically synthesised glycans and TSHR peptide sequences. Click chemistry
is used for glycosylations, as it has been shown that the triazole does not aﬀect biological
activity64,65.
The commercially available alkyne functionalised trisaccharide was used to click conju-
gate the glycan to an alkyne functionalised TSHR peptide. The use of modiﬁed amino
acids shows a site-speciﬁc method of glycosylation with click-chemistry. The results of the
reactions show that the reactions need optimisation. Furthermore, the mass spectrum of
the recovered glycan shows that it was not intact after the reaction, which was consistent
for all reactions, appendix D.1.3.
The potential of the synthesised glycopeptide was evaluated by ELISA. The response be-
tween the Grave’s samples and the healthy samples was not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent initially,
with a much more elevated response for the healthy samples in some instances than the
Grave’s, which was not expected.
43
==PAGE:51==
The repeated experiment gave acceptable results, showing the increased response against
autoantibodies from Grave’s patient sera. As autoantibodies are one of the key serologi-
cal characteristics of GD and a positive result can conﬁrm that autoimmunity is present,
and a negative result can usually conﬁrm that the thyrotoxicosis is due to other causes,
synthetic epitopes towards Grave’s is desired66. The use of peptide epitopes to respond
to antibodies from autoimmune diseases has also been shown by Dieker et al. In systemic
lupus erythematosus (SLE), autoantibodies are also present in patients sera, and the dis-
ease is related to Grave’s.
Dieker created peptides that mimic conformational epitopes (mimotopes) and identiﬁed a
mimotope that was bound to a nucleosome speciﬁc autoantibody. The results from ELISA
depended on the blocking agent, as Dieker experienced poly-reactivity depending on the
blocking agents used in the ELISA. As seen in the ﬁgures 23 and 24, the response for
both the glycosylated peptide 10c and the unglycosylated peptide had elevated responses
towards the Grave’s samples. Which is correlated with Kosugi’s ﬁndings that show, with
point mutations to the glycosylation sites of the TSHR, that TSAbs still respond towards
the unglycosylated regions of the TSHR60. The following steps will therefore focus on
testing and comparing the potential of other synthetic epitopes from diﬀerent regions of
the extracellular domain of the TSHR.
Current treatments of Grave’s hyperthyroidism involve radioactive iodine (RAI) 131I and
thyroidectomy, which then leads to hypothyroidism. There is also a shift away from RAI,
which is due to a risk of developing or worsening thyroid eye disease. The third option is
ADTs, which block the synthesis of TSH. Whether or not to start treatment with ATDs are
evaluated by a Graves’ Recurrent Events After Therapy score, which uses multiple factors
among other Thyrotropin binding inhibiting immunoglobulins (TBII) assays, which could
be improved by assays that speciﬁcally measures TSAbs.1 Moreover, assays to distinguish
TRAb types are also needed to not only determine the treatment process but also used
for diagnosis of Grave’s disease3.

6
Conclusion
Synthesis of propargylated D-Mannose was unsuccessful with the direct approach, but the
change to use acetylated D-mannose proved successful with an overall decent yield ( 50%).
The change to D-glucosamine using the same strategy showed multiple side products, and
the reaction needs optimisation. The protection using anisaldehyde dimethyl acetal were
tested using diﬀerent Lewis and Brønsted acids and proved unsuccessful.
Click chemistry with a commercially available alkyne trisaccharide and an alkyne func-
tionalised nucleotide was attempted with an azide functionalised TSHR peptide sequences.
The reaction with peptide 10 was successful for both the initial reaction and the scaled-
up reaction. The reaction conditions did not yield any product for the other attempts.
The change in reaction conditions yielded the glycopeptide and the nucleotide-peptide
conjugate for peptide 4 but failed for peptide 7.
ELISA towards Grave’s antibodies tested the synthesised glycopeptide 10c. The initial
ELISA showed unspeciﬁc reactivity for the healthy samples. The repeated ELISA, evalu-
ated by ANOVA, showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the reactivity towards Grave’s patient
sera and healthy patient sera for the IgG study. The IgM study results were consistent
with the initial ELISA, with elevated values for the healthy samples.
The ELISA results for the unglycosylated peptide 10 showed the same reactivity towards
the Grave’s and the healthy patient sera. These ﬁndings show potential for synthetic
epitopes of the extracellular domain of TSHR for targeting autoreactive antibodies.

7.5
Indirect enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)
Buﬀers used for the ELISA are as follows:
1. Washing buﬀer/Phosphate buﬀered saline (10xPBS). Mix 8g NaCl, 200mg KCL,
1.44g Na2HPO4 and 240 mg KH2HPO4 in 1 L of MQ water.
2. Blocking solution/PTB buﬀer.
Mix 20g of commercially available bovine serum
albumin (BSA) in 1L of 1xPBS.
3. Diluent. Mix 2g BSA in 1L of 1xPBS.
4. Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) substrate. Dissolve 3mg of TMB in 5 mL of DMSO
and adjust the volume to 50 mL with 0.1M acetate buﬀer. Add 3 µL hydrogen
peroxide (H2O2).
5. Quenching solution. Dilute concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4) to a 1M solution.
The ELISA was carried out on Nunclon 96 ﬂat bottom transparent polystyrol maxisorb
96-well plates from Thermo Fischer Scientiﬁc.
Serum boxes were prepared by diluting plasma 1:80, 1:160 and 1:320 in ELISA diluent.
The boxes were stored at 4 °C overnight.
7.6
Indirect ELISA
Coating: Antigen solution (4 µG/mL in 5% DMSO in 1x-PBS, 100µL/well) was applied
for coating of the plates. Well 11h and 12h was left blank without antigen. The plates
were incubated at 4 °C overnight.
Blocking: The remaining antigen solutions were discarded, and the plates were washed
50
==PAGE:58==
with 10x PBS solution twice. Plate blocking solution (100 µL/well) was added to the
coated plates as well as two empty plates. The plates were incubated at 37 °C for three
h.
Incubation with serum: The remaining blocking solution was discarded, and the plates
were washed with 10x PBS solution twice. The prepared diluted serum was added to the
plates (100 µL/well), and the plates were incubated at 37 °C for 1.5h.
Incubation with secondary HRP-linked antibodies: The remaining sera were dis-
carded, and the plates were washed with 10x PBS three times. The secondary HRP-linked
antibodies (100 µL/well, IgG 1:60000 dilution, IgM 1:20000 dilution), were added to the
plates. The plates were incubated at 37 °C for 1.5h.
Incubation with substrate: The remaining secondary antibody solution was discarded,
and the plates were washed with 10x PBS three times. The freshly prepared TMB so-
lution was added to the plates (100 µL/well), and the plates were incubated at r.t. for
25-30 min.
Stopping the reaction: The reaction was quenched by the addition of 1M H2SO4 (50
µL/well), and the plates were analysed in an ELISA plate reader: ELISA plate readings
were conducted on a TECAN Inﬁnite® 200 PRO multimode plate reader with i-control™
software. The absorbance was measured at 450nm, with a bandwidth of 9nm, 25 ﬂashes
and a settle time of 0 ms.
