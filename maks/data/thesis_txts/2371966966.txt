Abstract
Satellite altimetry waveforms, once deviating from the well-known Brown shape, are dif-
ﬁcult to retrack and, often, one must rely on classiﬁers that can advocate the use an
adequate retracker according to the observed echo. We propose to apply a deep gen-
erative modeling and clustering framework so that sensible clusters depicting a natural
typology of waveforms can naturally emerge, be soft-classiﬁed and sampled from. This
framework is partially applied to L1b and extensively to L2 Cryosat-2 SAR data. In this
work, we discover meaningful structured complex compressed hidden manifolds of the
echoes. Also, we design a convolutional variational auto-encoder with a one-dimensional
latent space which can consistently and solely reconstruct L2 Brown waveforms. The
distribution of the model reconstruction errors over ocean waveforms is used as a mean
to separate Brown from ’outlier’ signals. Finally, Gaussian Mixture Variational Auto-
Encoders with 5 and 10 components are trained on the remaining non-Brown waveforms.
Their insightful clustering and generative capabilities are further investigated, along with
their components spatial distribution.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Water altimetry is crucial in numerous domains among which maritime and rivers navi-
gation, ﬂooding and natural disasters, fresh water monitoring and so forth.
Today, satellite altimetry is probably the most convenient way to gain knowledge
regarding these diﬀerent ﬁelds.
However, while providing globally satisfactory results
regarding sea surface heights and sea currents estimations, the technology is still struggling
when it comes to coastal, inland water and polar areas. These diﬃculties arise for diﬀerent
reasons which are mainly related to pollution of the emitted (and returned) satellite
signals by bright inland objects or diﬀerent sea ice types. This leads to very disparate
shapes of the returned power echoes which cannot be adequately ﬁtted in order to retrieve
model parameters that can in turn be used to estimate water altimetry variables such
as height. Albeit in-situ water level measurements exist over certain rivers, those are
marginal at world scale and tend to dramatically diminish over the years. In addition, in
some regions, water resources fall within geopolitic issues among neighboring countries.
Hence, in-situ water levels, even when available, are often hidden. Therefore, even with its
current limitations for inland, coastal and polar waters, satellites remain by far the most
convenient tool to fulﬁll water altimetry and improving both technology and methodology
to solve its current draw-backs appears sound to us.
In order to develop speciﬁc algorithms (called retrackers in the satellite altimetry ﬁeld)
that are suitable for power echoes substantially diﬀerent from the classical Brown-like (or
ocean-like) echoes typically retrieved over open ocean, it is necessary to fully understand
the diﬀerent patterns (i.e. the diﬀerent types of returned power echoes) that can arise.
Hence, this project is motivated to play one’s part in solving the mentioned issues for
the Cryosat-2 satellite mission in SAR mode by :
• ﬁrst, understanding the diﬀerent types of waveforms and how they can manifest in
compressed manifolds ;
• second, producing a classiﬁer enabling recognition of the diﬀerent waveform types
to help choose a suitable retracker for heights corrections (and other parameters
retrieval in the case of Brown waveforms for instance) which is generative, i.e. which
can generate synthetic waveforms of diﬀerent classes that can be used to ease the
development of retrackers for speciﬁc power echoes types.
To the best of our knowledge, the following elements of the project can be considered
as novelties :

2.1.2
Radar types
The following paragraphs give a picture of three of the main types of radar altimeters,
namely the Low Resolution Mode (LRM) and Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and
Synthetic Aperture Radar Interferometer (SARIn) modes, all in board of the Cryosat-2
mission.
Low Resolution Mode (LRM)
LRM radar are pulse-limited across and along the
satellite track. This leads the radar pulses to have circular footprints of great extent. For
example, the Cryosat-2 mission, which embarks the three radar modes depicted here, has
a footprint diameter of about 15 km [10].
Therefore, echoes from both along (hooking eﬀect) and across (snagging eﬀect) track
oﬀ-nadir ground points can be retrieved by the altimeter. In cases where some of the
oﬀ-nadir ground points within the footprint happen to be stronger reﬂectors, they will
dominate the signal obtained from the returned power echo and incur biased range esti-
mates.
12
==PAGE:17==
Such stronger reﬂectors happen to be more present close to the coast because of calm
inland waters for example, but can also be found over sea ice for instance. Hence, LRM
radars are only suited to retrieve heights over open oceans and lacs of great extent.
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR)
SAR are Doppler-Limited in the along-track di-
rection and pulse-limited in the across-track direction [100]. This leads to a rectangular
like shape of the footprint. Hence, hooking (i.e. the retracking of along-track oﬀ-nadir
positions) issues are drastically reduced. However, snagging (i.e. the retracking of across-
track oﬀ-nadir positions) remains a serious issue. Taking again the Cryosat-2 mission
example, the footprint shape in SAR mode has an extent of about 300 m and 14 000 m
respectively along and across the satellite track [10].
Synthetic Aperture Radar Interferometric mode (SARIn)
SARIn technology
seeks to solve the remaining snagging issue by basically combining two SAR altimeters.
Adding a second antenna to the satellite enables one to measure the phase diﬀerence
between the returned echoes and eventually the range at nadir. Further details regarding
this technology are left for the interested reader in [2] for instance.
In spite of its obvious advantages, the technology is for now only embarked on the
Cryosat-2 mission and available for small polar areas (see Fig. 2.1 showing the coverage
of the diﬀerent radar modes embarked on Cryosat-2). In addition, the SWOT (Surface
Water Ocean Topography) satellite, which will be the ﬁrst to be equipped with world-wide
cover SARIn technology, will not be launched before 2019. Hence, SAR mode remains for
now the best trade-of between accuracy and earth coverage, especially for the Cryosat-2
mission.

the leading edge of the pulse, i.e. the surface at nadir when dealing with ﬂat surfaces. The

2.2
Clustering and classiﬁcation of satellite altimetry
echo power waveforms
Several approaches have been attempted to make typologies of the diﬀerent types of
waveforms to subsequently classify them. We try to give the reader an overview of these
diﬀerent approaches in Sec. 2.2.1, 2.2.2, 2.2.3, 2.2.4 and 2.2.5.
2.2.1
Visual inspection for typology and manual indicators de-
velopment and thresholding for classiﬁcation
Probably the roughest strategy, it resides in visually inspecting a series of waveforms in
order to come up with a typology.
For instance, [18] come up with complex, specular, Brown, double spikes, multi-spiked
in leading or trailing edge and very low power echoes based on the analysis of waveforms of
the Brahmaputra river measured with the Altika altimeter embarked on SARAL (Satellite
with Argos and Altika).
17
==PAGE:22==
In addition to the deﬁnition of a typology, other studies further develop indicators
and thresholds enabling a later automatic classiﬁcation of the waveforms based on their
previously deﬁned typology.
This is for example the case in [109] and [110] who try to separate waveforms (respec-
tively from the GM altimeter and the GM and ERM altimeters of the ERS-1, i.e. the
European Remote-Sensing Satellite) returned from ice and open ocean in the Pyrz Bay
in Antarctica based on the thresholding of the pulse peakiness indicator.
This type of approach is also adopted by [108] where waveforms from the Jason-
1 satellite are classiﬁed into ocean-like, coastal-like and land-like classes based on the
segmentation of three indicators, namely the number of leading edges, the peak power
and the OCOG retracker parameters.
Finally, the separation between ice and water waveforms (from ENVISAT, i.e. the
ENVIronment SATellite) in the Quinghai lake in the Quinghai-Tibetan plateau is under-
taken by [98]. They do so by computing the Normalized Diﬀerence Snow Index (NDSI)
with remote sensing imagery (inspired by [34] and [23]) and threshold it afterwards.
2.2.2
Advanced Visual inspection for typology and manual in-
dicators development and thresholding for classiﬁcation
The idea of manually designing indicators and thresholds was pushed further by [3] who
elaborated a typology of 12 types of waveforms (from the ERS-1) and enabled their
classiﬁcation by developing a rule based system expert. Such computer system, which
became very popular in the ’80s and progressively vanished during the ’90s, is among
the ﬁrst successful form of artiﬁcial intelligence. Such system can be decomposed into
the knowledge base, i.e. the set of human designed rules (here thresholds of diﬀerent
indicators regarding the waveforms), and the inference engine that can lead to practical
diagnostics, such as the waveform type inference in this case.
The rule based system expert of [3] was later reused by several studies such as [5] which
applied the system to analyze the spatial distribution of the diﬀerent types of waveforms.
[26] later sharpened the system and applied it on the entire waveforms datasets of
the ERS-1 and ENVISAT geodetic missions and came up with 10 classes of waveforms,
namely ocean-like, wide with anomalies, pre-peaked wide, ﬂat-patch, simple multi-target,
complex complex multi-target and quasi-specular.
The system was then again used by [4] for analyses and spatial distribution purposes
of the ERS-1 waveforms and by [1] to select appropriate waveform retrackers based on
the inferred type.
The philosophy of rule based system experts has been further developed by [46] through
the development of a fuzzy expert system approach to classify waveforms of the Great
Barrier Reef in Australia from the OSTM (Ocean Surface Topography Mission) on board
of the Jason-2 satellite. The resulting classes could then be used to select appropriate
retrackers to retrieve more accurate SSH (Sea Surface Height).
2.2.3
Surface based typology and machine learning classiﬁcation
Still another group of approaches consists in, ﬁrstly, establishing a typology of waveforms
based on the surface type they most likely arose from and, secondly, applying a machine
18
==PAGE:23==
learning classiﬁer based on the labels of the waveforms and a set of carefully chosen
variables summarizing the latter.
[96] developed the idea with waveforms (from the Geophysical Data Record, i.e. GDR,
of the Jason-1 satellite) arising from very general surface types such as ocean, desert, for-
est, sea ice and continental ice. The labeling was likely achieved based on the location of
the diﬀerent observations. Diﬀerent classiﬁers (i.e. a nearest mean, a k-nearest neighbors
and an MLP2 with 2 hidden layers of 5 units each with sigmoid activation functions) were
applied and compared. All the diﬀerent models were trained on the variables resulting
from the use of an LDA (i.e. Linear Discriminant Analysis) applied to reduce the di-
mensionality of the original number of variables which consisted, among others, of the
SWH, backscatter coeﬃcients, the waveform peakiness and the brightness temperature
(measured by radiometers).
[116], and later [115], reused this idea and focused on the classiﬁcation of Arctic
waveforms into FYI (First Year Ice) and MYI (Multi-Year Ice) classes, and FYI, MYI
and leads classes, respectively. Because such sea-ice types are much more intricate to
separate in terms of their location, the authors respectively used satellite and airborne
remote sensing images [116] and binary masks from the EUMETSAT (Ocean and Sea
Ice Satellite Application Facility) agency3 [115] to label the diﬀerent waveforms. The
classiﬁcations of the datasets were achieved with Bayesian classiﬁers using a series of
waveform indicators such as max power, leading edge width, trailing edge width, pulse
peakiness, stack standard deviation and backscatter coeﬃcient.
2.2.4
Clusters based typology and machine learning classiﬁca-
tion
Yet another approach commonly found in the literature is to base the typology and label-
ing of the waveforms on the resulting clusters of unsupervised machine learning, followed
once again by the training of machine learning classiﬁers. This type of approach mainly
arose because, in many cases, waveforms shapes cannot be fully apprehended by the sur-
face they were reﬂected from. For instance, a typical Brown waveform representative of
what is typically observed over oceans can also be measured over lakes of suﬃcient areas
when windy conditions are present. On the other hand, a specular waveform, typical of
calm lakes, can be observed in seas and oceans that are particularly quiet and waveless.
These examples apply to many other types of echoes and, hence, waveform shape based
typology making obtained through clustering is yet another methodology commonly used
in scientiﬁc papers.

mentum of the gradient [93]. The idea is that keeping track of the changing rate of the
gradient during a previous chosen set of iterations can enable one to adapt the step length
(otherwise controlled by the Hessian) appropriately. Hence, momentum-based stochas-
tic gradient-descent methods avoids the main draw-back of the vanilla steepest descent
algorithm which usually struggles once close to the solution because of inadequate step
lengths.
Many diﬀerent cost functions objectives can be used to train NNs. In general, the
mean-squared-error (MSE), also called quadratic loss, is the preferred choice for regression
problems while cross-entropy (with sigmoid or softmax activation function in the last layer
for binary and multi-class problems, respectively) yields better results for classiﬁcation
tasks.
One should be aware of that cross-entropy can also be used in the case of regressions
as long as the output is mapped to the ]0, 1] interval. This can be advantageous as several
terms in the computation of the derivatives cancel out, yielding sparser derivatives that
can potentially increase training speed during the backpropagation phase. In addition,
badly initializing the weights of a NN is likely to drive less severe consequences in terms of
speed and convergence of the weights towards a good minimum compared to the quadratic
loss. This can be seen on Fig. 2.8 taken from [28] comparing the structure of the weight
spaces yielded by the two objectives, which clearly showcases the healthier cross-entropy
space which will much more quickly account for and correct a bad weights initialization
scheme during the optimization process.

2.3.2
Regularizers
Because NNs can approximate any continuous function given a suﬃcient amount of hidden
units and hidden layers, they are naturally prone to overﬁtting issues. Several techniques
have been developed to mitigate this concern such as the addition of a L2 and/or L1
penalty to the cost function [31].
Other approaches focus on the injection of noise in the network, be it in the input
and or output features, or even in the weights of the hidden layers [31]. One of the most
successful method is probably the dropout technique [89]. It is achieved by, in each layer
of the network, randomly dropping a part of the units, together with their connections
during the training phase. The goal is to avoid co-adaptation of the units during training.
During the test phase, all the units and connections of the network are used and the
parameters (i.e. the weights) of the network are weighted by the probability with which
the units were dropped during the training phase, yielding results close to bootstrap
techniques without the additional computation time incurred by the latter [89].
2.3.3
Optimization
The power of NNs can be increased by adding hidden units and/or layers. It has been
shown that increasing the number of hidden layers is often more eﬀective to generate
complex functions, in addition to yielding sparser models (i.e. with less parameters) [6].
One typically refers to such deep NNs as deep networks, hence the use of the term deep
learning often encountered in the literature.
Unfortunately, deep networks are generally more diﬃcult to train, backpropagating the
gradients becoming more challenging as they tend to vanish (or explode depending on the
types of activation functions) after backpropagating through several layers. Numerous

researches have focused on developing methods seeking to alleviate such issue and are
succinctly discussed in the following paragraphs.
Layer-wise pre-training
Layer-wise pre-training [85, 42] basically consists in training
adjacent layers two-by-two before training the entire network together. The weights pre-
trained in this way allow a better propagation of the gradient during the backpropagation
phase on the entire network. With the raise of other eﬃcient and much less cumbersome
techniques, such method became disused.

be applied, be it feature or example-wise.

Optimizers
Several optimizers have been developed over the years [24, 111, 69, 93,
17, 52], often building on the vanilla stochastic gradient descent with diﬀerent ﬂavors of
adaptive updates of the learning rate.
Nowadays, Adam optimizer [52], based on the computation of the moment and expo-
nential decaying updates, is generally regarded as the gold standard in deep learning.
Activation functions
Activation functions, in addition to control the transformations
applied to the signal during the forward propagation, also play a central part in the
backpropagation eﬃciency.
In the early days of NNs, the most common activations used to be the sigmoid and
tangent hyperbolic (i.e. tanh) functions. Nowadays, they are rather obsolete with the rise
of other more eﬃcient activations. However, they can still be utilized in certain situations.
This is for instance the case of the sigmoid function which remains in use in the last layer
of binary classiﬁcation problems where its property of mapping the incoming signal to the
[0, 1] interval is opportune. This also holds for regression problems (normally applying
the identity activation function in the last layer) seeking an output in this interval. It
then becomes possible to use the cross-entropy loss function rather the quadratic one for
instance.
For multi-class problems, the soft-max activation function will be preferred with a
one-of-k encoding scheme of the outputs as, in addition to mapping the signal to a [0, 1]
interval, the scores along the output units will sum to one, making it valid to interpret
the results as probabilities.
The gold standard for most layers is the rectiﬁed linear unit (ReLU) [112] which is
equivalent to a linear activation function in the interval [0, ∞[ and maps the input to zero
elsewhere. Its simplicity makes it very cheap to compute during the backpropagation
phase. By allowing to map the signal to non bounded outputs in the positive part of
the interval, this prevents the signal from decreasing too much when backpropagating.
On the other hand, it is sometimes necessary to clip the outputs to some threshold to
prevent exploding gradients during training. ReLUs, by setting negative outputs to zero,
can provide sparser solutions. This can be seen as an advantage but also as a constraint
as once set to zero, weights cannot be reactivated during backpropagation. Such issue,
known as ’dying ReLU problem’ [51], can prevent the use of weights (that could have come
handy to model a given problem) by setting them to zero too early in the optimization
process when many of them are still typically assigned randomly and did not have the
time, nor the chance to try to learn something meaningful yet.
27
==PAGE:32==
Since ReLU ﬁrst appeared, and because of the remaining above-mentioned problems,
several new techniques inspired by that activation function arose. Among them, we can
quote the Leaky ReLU [65] which apply a slightly negative slope to the function in the
]−∞, 0[ interval instead of mapping such inputs to zero. Such modiﬁcation of ReLUs
obviously seeks to prevent ’dying ReLU’ problem but inquires the ﬁne-tuning of a new
hyper-parameter. An alternative activation function is the parametric ReLU (PReLU) [40]
which makes the slope of leaky ReLUs an additional trainable parameter of each neuron.
Yet another ReLU modiﬁcation came from [107] who developed randomized leaky ReLU
(RReLU), who randomly (within a certain range) sets the slope of the negative part of the
activation function during training, and then keeps it ﬁxed during testing. The authors
reported improvements compared to other types of ReLUs and attribute it to its stochastic
nature which could prevent overﬁtting.
Finally, one last ReLU modiﬁcation that seem promising regarding empirical results
is the exponential linear unit (ELU) [15] which computes α(exp(zm) −1) if zm < 0 where
zm is the m’th unit of a layer and α is once again a hyper-parameter to ﬁne-tune.
Despite those encouraging results, one should keep in mind that it is still unclear
whether any of the above modiﬁcations can consistently outperform vanilla ReLUs.

Despite the outbreak of batch normalization which signiﬁcantly
alleviated training issues and the need for careful initialization of NNs weights, a good

optimization problems in non convex model parameters space in general, the initial weights
in a NN optimization problem can still have a tremendous impact on the performance of
the network. For instance, setting weights to zero prevents the optimization procedure
from updating them, hence yielding extremely poor results.

symmetries in the weights space and improve the training7. However, the question of the
amplitude and the form of the random initialization schemes still needs to be answered.
In deed, too small weights will lead the gradient to shrink when passing through the

2.3.4
Convolutional Neural Networks
NNs can quickly become overly heavy and slow to train because of the amount of weights
they incur. This can become an issue for high dimensional datasets such as images and
videos for which they become impractical. In addition, conventional NNs do not explicitly
make use of the features, or generally speaking of the spatial (and temporal for videos)
autocorrelation naturally present in images.
With the raise of convolutional neural networks (CNN) [56], the use of NNs in the
imagery ﬁeld was made possible and has since yielded state of the art performances in
various domains.

2.3.5
Auto-encoders
Auto-encoders (AE) [57, 9, 113] are NNs which seek to reconstruct in the output layer the
input features [31]. Usually, in such architectures, bias terms are set to zero as this often
induces better results [55]. Note that the encoding part (i.e. the weights and activation
functions that map the input signal to the most hidden layer) and decoding (i.e. the
weights and activation functions that map the most hidden layer to the output layer)
parts of the AE can sometimes be distinguished for handiness.
There exists many ﬂavors of AEs and we will solely focus on under-complete ones,
i.e. AEs which have at least one of their hidden layers that is composed of less units
than there are input features [31]. Assuming the network is able to decently reconstruct
the input signal, it is likely hidden layers with less dimensions than the input space have
learned useful compressed representations of the data. Hence, one of the applications of
AEs is dimensionality reduction which we focus on in this project.
It is interesting to underline that a simple AE with on hidden layer, a linear activation
function in the decoder and a quadratic objective will actually learn the same mapping
as PCA [31].
Because activation functions can be chosen to be non linear and because the power
of those non-linearities can (in the manner of regular NNs as portrayed in Sec. 2.3.3)
be enhanced by deepening the network [43], AEs are potentially a much more powerful
dimensionality reduction technique than PCA [43] which is constrained to only ﬁnd linear
mappings.
30
==PAGE:35==
Finally, it is worth mentioning that AEs can be applied for outliers detection [14].
Albeit being relatively recent, a very similar idea already arose in the form of replicator
NNs [39, 104] in the beginning of the 2 000s. The idea was to identify outliers based on
the reconstruction errors of those replicator NNs. The main diﬀerence with AEs networks
lied in the choice of a staircase-like activation function in the most hidden layer to map
the observations into diﬀerent clusters.
[14] recently proposed AE ensembles that use adaptive sampling and randomized
model construction to prevent overﬁtting of the AE. Their method achieved competi-
tive results with respect to state-of-the art outliers methods.
2.3.6
Denoising Auto-Encoders
DAE ([101] and [102]) are a derivate of AEs which are meant to better generalize (and
prevent the risk of learning the identity in the case of overcomplete AEs [31]) and produce
hidden manifolds which are more robust to noise in the input space. In their most simple
form, such regularized9 AEs randomly set to zero a part (typically 50 %) of the input
features of each observation in a batch of data before feeding it to the Encoder. Despite
feeding corrupted examples to the network, the DAEs are requested to compute the loss
function by comparing the reconstructions to the uncorrupted inputs. Hence, DAEs are
forced to learn useful representations of the data and disregard the noise introduced in
the inputs. Therefore, as a bi-product, they also develop denoising capabilities.
It is interesting to note that denoising AEs, despite sharing some properties with
dropout (both setting units to zero, be it in the input features for DAEs or in the hidden
layers with dropout), are rarely combined with the latter or its companion methods such
as max-out [33].

2.3.7.2
Mixture of Gaussians latent prior
The strong assumption on the form of the prior (i.e. a unit Gaussian assumption) can
prevent VAEs from learning useful representations that do not ﬁt the prior distribution
but could still be properly decoded by the generative graph [13]. Quite often, datasets
are multi-modal in nature and it is obvious a single Gaussian is not suited for modeling
distributions. Because multi-modal distributions such as the Gaussian Mixture Model
(GMM) have a summation term in their log-likelihood function, closed-form solutions can
usually not be found and hence, not optimized on. Hence, research is being conducted to
ﬁnd ways of optimizing with respect to more complex posterior distributions.
GMM divergence in general
The best approximations of the divergence between
GMMs (and between any distribution in general) is obtained using Monte Carlo sampling
given a suﬃciently large number of samples. Unfortunately, using Monte Carlo sampling
to yield a better approximation of the variational posterior is not applied in the VAE ﬁeld
because it is excessively costly to generate accurate results, and otherwise exhibits very
high variance [76], which makes it unsuitable [54].
[29], and then [41] developed closed form approximated solutions based on the vari-
ational KL-divergence. However, in the context of mini-batch training, such approaches
are still unlikely to yield accurate enough lower bounds.
33
==PAGE:38==
ITL methods in general
More recently, to overcome the non existence of closed-form
for the KL divergence between GMM and other mixtures of the exponential family, other
forms of information theoretic divergences (ITL) have been studied [73].
Especially, closed form solutions for GMMs were derived based on the Cauchy-Schwarz
[49], Jensen-Rényi [103] and (total) Square Loss [62] divergences.
In an eﬀort of generalization, [73] developed a generic closed-form for mixtures of the
same distribution of the Exponential family ’based on Cauchy-Schwarz, Jensen-Rényi and
(total)Square Loss statistical divergences’. The generic closed-form solution can therefore
be applied to GMMs for instance.
They estimate that such closed-form can lead to
increased computational speed of 6 to 9 order of magnitudes compared to Monte-Carlo
simulations for a similar accuracy.
ITL methods and VAEs
[61] replaced the well-known KL divergence between two
single Gaussian by Jensen-Rényi divergence in the VAE framework. With their modiﬁed
VAE, they obtained equally or slightly better results (measured by the test log-likelihood
in the latent space) compared to the original VAE on several diﬀerent datasets such as
MNIST.
Shortly afterwards, [84] experimented VAEs with Cauchy-Schwarz and euclidean di-
vergences. In their experiments, Cauchy-Schwarz divergence leads to better results for
high dimensional latent spaces compared to the euclidean one and vice-versa for low di-
mensional latent manifolds. They noted increasing the batch-size can also favor a better
estimate of the divergence between the two distributions and could even map the MNIST
dataset to a 2d swiss-roll latent space.
The ITL divergences used by the above-mentioned authors require to estimate the
likelihood of the chosen distribution of the latent layer with Parzen windows. The problem
that arises then is to ﬁnd a suitable value for the smoothing coeﬃcient of the Gaussian
kernel. In deed, optimal likelihood estimation depends on the number of samples (i.e.
the batch size in our case), the number of dimensions of the distribution and the form
of the probability density function itself. For instance, the optimal smoothing for a 2-
dimensional GMM with 10 components may diﬀer from a 2-dimensional GMM with 2
components.
Hence, this can be an issue for quick experimentation when a suﬃcient
number of dimensions in the latent space to properly reconstruct the inputs is yet to be
discovered, and even more cumbersome for clustering tasks where the number of clusters
(corresponding the number of components in the multi-modal distribution) is not know a
priori.
Generative moments matching networks
Another strategy to measure the diver-
gence between the two distributions is the proposed by [60] who developed the generative
moments matching networks (GMMN). Their idea is to compute a new objective, called
maximum mean discrepancy objective, which seeks to minimize the distance between all
the statistical moments of the imposed prior and posterior distributions of the AE in each
mini-batch.
Inpainting and normalizing ﬂows
Yet another approach seeking the modeling of
more complex posterior distributions is to apply a combination of non-linear invertible10
transformations to the latent variables, be it using inpainting [22] or normalizing ﬂows [80].
10The invertibility condition ensures the gradients can be computed.

==PAGE:39==
Eventually, such transformations enable one to convert the typical factorized Gaussian
of VAEs into richer and more complex posterior distributions among which multi-modal
ones.
Adversarial AE
Adversarial AE [67] is the combination of generative adversarial net-
works (GAN) [32] and AEs.
GANs, as well as VAEs, seek to generate realistic new
examples of a given dataset by modeling its distribution. The diﬀerence between the two
methods is the strategy they use to achieve that goal. GANs are typically composed of
two networks, a generative and a discriminative one. The task of the generative network
is to produce realistic examples of the dataset from noise vectors inputs. On the other
hand, the goal of the discriminative network is to distinguish between fake samples (i.e.
the samples generated by the generative network) and the true samples from the dataset.
The generative network will be rewarded as it manages to mislead the discriminative net-
work, while the latter will be granted for the properly discriminated examples. The goals
of the two networks being antagonist, the optimization problem of GANs is essentially a
min-max game for which the optimal converged solution holds when both networks are
in equilibrium [32].
Adversarial AEs combine both GAN and AE frameworks as follows. As for classical
AEs, the inference network encodes the input features into the latent space z, the gener-
ative network reconstructs the signal in the latent space and the loss function penalizes
poor reconstructions. The novelty is that [67] introduces a network that must discrimi-
nate between the z encoded the inference graph (i.e. qθ(z|x)) and the ones sampled from
the prior distribution p(z). The model must therefore ﬁrst optimize the parameters (φ
and θ) of the encoder and decoder networks based on the reconstruction error. Then, it
must optimize the parameters of the discriminative model based on the errors that arose
when the latter was tricked by the generative model. And ﬁnally the parameters θ of
the generative network are to be updated once again, this time based on the generated
examples that failed to fool the discriminative network.
The great advantage of adversarial AEs is that any kind of prior for the latent space
can be used as long as one can sample from it.
Unfortunately, the other side of the
coin is that such models are very tricky to train as the balance in a min-max problem
is typically unstable and irreversible wins of one network over the other is a common
issue. Nevertheless, [67], with a carefully designed training set-up, managed to produce a
GMM, as well as a 2-dimensional Swiss-roll, latent spaces for the MNIST dataset. More
recently, [38] drew on this approach, again on the MNIST dataset, using a GMM prior and
further clustered the resulting latent space, yielding competitive clustering unsupervised
classiﬁcation results.
Variational Deep Embedding
Recently, [114], inspired by the deep embedding clus-
tering method from [106], built a variational framework on the latter and call it variational
deep embedding. Roughly, their idea lays in the combination of a GMM and a VAE which
modiﬁed lower bound takes into account the responsibilities of the GMM to update the
statistical moments of the latter accordingly together with the VAE parameters.

2.3.8
Ladder VAE
A new VAE architecture has recently been developed by [88] inspired by the recent Ladder
network architecture enabling connections among the encoding and decoding hidden layers
[99] and [78].
Such structure eases the training of much deeper networks, and made
possible the use of VAEs with several stochastic layers. Such VAEs, introduced by [53]
and [81], were meant to increase the expressiveness of the latent space. Unfortunately,
these models ﬁrst could not be successfully trained end-to-end [53] and the authors had
to rely on layer-wise pre-training11. Thanks to ladder VAE, such cumbersome training
can now be avoided [88].
Ladder networks were also successfully used in new semi-supervised VAEs12 such as
the auxiliary deep generative models [64]. The latter method consists in increasing the
expressiveness of the latent space by conditioning the latent variables z on additional
auxiliary variables, which enables one to optimize intractable distributions [64]. Such
auxiliary variables actually help the model converging more eﬃciently by improving the
variational approximation and hence the quality of the backpropagated gradient.
As
for standard VAEs, auxiliary generative models can be designed with several stochastic
layers of z and auxiliary variables. [64], who successfully trained such model using ladder
networks, named it skip deep generative.

Chapter 4
Methodology
This chapter seeks to give the reader a broad overview of the methodology in use in this
project and which yielded the results presented in Chap. 5.
Preprocessing
To begin with, having the data dispatched in hundreds of directories
and thousands of ﬁles is obviously not handy to experiment and later train and test
models. Our data storage solution relies on transferring the data into a single HDF5
(Hierarchical File Format) ﬁle. Such ﬁle format allows for the safe storage of big data in a
structured manner [95, 16] sharing some properties with the netCDF4 format. In addition
to being the gold standard for ﬁles storage in the big data world, it is often chosen for
deep learning applications.
We start by looping through the netCDF-4 ﬁles in the diﬀerent directories (downloaded
as depicted in 3) to gather several summary statistics of the waveforms which will serve to
better understand the structure of the data and most importantly to set aside potential
outliers.
Also, whilst the size of L2 waveforms is consistent given ﬁxed processing parameters
as selected in Sec. 3.1 (yielding vectors of 256 elements in our set-up), this is not the case
of L1b stack of waveforms which size can typically vary in a range going from [240 × 256]
to [256 × 256] (once again in our set-up). This can be an issue both for modeling (tough
masking processes exist to mitigate the problem) and data storage, datasets in HDF5 ﬁles
being typically requested to be given a ﬁxed size prior to their creation1. To overcome this
issue, we compare several interpolation techniques provided by the python binding to the
OpenCV library to ﬁx the size of the images to [256 × 256]. Note that such interpolation
could introduce some bias, however, we assume this should be negligible regarding the
slight width increase (at worse, a 6.25 % width enlargement is incurred).
Once outliers thresholds and a suitable interpolation scheme are set up, the netCDF4
ﬁles are looped through, waveforms considered as outliers are set aside, L1b data are re-
sized if needed and, ﬁnally, satisfactory waveforms (and their related important metadata)
are stored2 in a HDF5 ﬁle with the data-structure we previously designed.
With that uniﬁed dataset at hand, we then turn to the investigation of a suitable
preprocessing method to ease later learning (see Sec. 2.3.3 for more information regarding
those preprocessing techniques).

Dimensionality reduction and manifold exploration
As a preliminary mean to
explore compressed manifolds, we build diﬀerent AEs3 and DAEs of diﬀerent sizes (both
in terms of hidden units and hidden layers), some fully connected and others making use
of CNN framework. We then try (separately on the L1b and L2 datasets with diﬀerent
normalization schemes applied) to keep yielding satisfactory reconstruction costs while
progressively reducing the number of dimensions of the most hidden layer of these diﬀerent
models.
The manifolds of the models combining good compression ratios and low reconstruction
errors, as well as the original data space, are then explored and compared on their ﬁrst
principal components and t-SNE [66] axes.
Note that those experiments on the L1b data were conducted on training and test
datasets of only 5 000 images each to keep reasonable computational costs on our laptop.
Also, the remaining experiments (and the methodology presented in the following sections)
are solely conducted on the L2 dataset to keep the training times under sensible limits.
Generative modeling and binary classiﬁcation
Despite trying to compile a dataset
as heterogeneous as possible, Brown echoes still largely dominate as later underlined on
the explored manifolds in Chap.
5.
Because highly unbalanced clustering tasks are
essentially harder, we choose to adopt [97] strategy which consisted in ﬁrst applying a
binary classiﬁcation under the form of an anomaly detection framework to separate Brown
waveforms from the others. While [97] applied binary SVM, we here seek to remain within
a deep generative framework. Therefore, inspired by recursive NNs [39] and ensemble AEs
[14], we make use of a Convolutional VAE with univariate latent space (which we call
CNNVAE-1d-z) that we train exclusively on ocean waveforms. As stated by [97], at least
95 % of ocean waveforms can be considered as Brown. Therefore, we study two means of
thresholding the waveforms based respectively on their position in the one dimensional
latent space, and on their reconstruction errors. When [14] made use of ensemble of AEs
to prevent overﬁtting, we here count on the regularization power of VAEs to do the job.
Once a properly trained CNNVAE-1d-z which yields both good reconstruction costs
and a latent space that can be considered Gaussian is found, we analyze its generative
capabilities to produce new Brown waveforms and the structure of outliers using diﬀerent
thresholds (e.g. thresholds corresponding to exclude the remaining 5 % of observations
based on the distribution of the latent space or based on the reconstruction errors of the
dataset solely trained on ocean waveforms).
Once a satisfactory threshold is found, it is applied on the full dataset to separate it
into Brown and non-Brown HDF5 datasets.
Generative modeling and clustering
Focusing now on the non-Brown dataset con-
taining more balanced types of waveforms, we attempt to set up a deep generative clus-
tering framework.
We ﬁrst considered adversarial AEs from [67]. However, for our dataset, we could not
ﬁnd good tuning parameters that would prevent the the min-max optimization game from
yielding irreversible wins of one network over the other.
Instead, we focused on the GMVAE developed by [20, 86] to conduct our generative
clustering experiments on our dataset. More speciﬁcally, we built both shallow and deep

networks for models with 5 and 10 clusters. As one iteration of the GMVAE requires
to run the encoder and decoder networks K times (see the cost function in (2.21) which
exhibits a summation on y, i.e. the one of k encoding variable describing the probabilities
of belonging to each cluster) where K is the number of chosen clusters, we restrained
ourselves from experimenting with higher number of clusters on our laptop.
The generative and classiﬁcation capabilities of the best models with K = 5 and
K = 10 clusters are then presented and analyzed.
Note that we do not make use of a CNN framework with the GMVAE as it requires
trickier set up to enable conditioning on several variables.
In a fully connected set up, the graph qφ(z|x(i), y) for instance could simply be designed
by concatenating the x(i) and y vectors and then build fully connected layers on the
stacked vector. In a CNN set-up, it does not make sens to concatenate x(i), which is now
a matrix, with for instance a y vector. By reshaping x(i) into a vector to concatenate it,
one could then not reshape the vector back to its initial matrix shape.
In other ﬁelds than AEs, the bias term in each layer is often not ﬁxed to zero. It can
then be an option to use such vector to convey the labels information (or other type of
variables we are conditioning on). Such a procedure is used by [75] who, for each layer,
ﬁrst compute a matrix of weights Wb of shape [K × D] (were D is the depth of the
output CNN layer), then apply matrix multiplication b = y × Wb, producing a bias term
b of shape [1 × D] which contains the information of the labels (or any other variable)
contained in the vector y. After reshaping that bias term to [1×1×1×D], one can sum it
to the result of the convolution of the layer. We tried to adapt this procedure to produce
a convolutional GMVAE (CNNGMVAE). Unfortunately, with such model, we were only
able to reconstruct the mean signal of the dataset regardless of the examples at hand.
Another remaining solution consists in applying several CNN layers to x(i) before re-
shaping and concatenating it with y, possibly construct additional fully connected layers,
and ﬁnally compute the output (here z) of the graph (here qφ(z|x(i), y)). We do not
attempt to use this option and let its investigation for future work.

the reconstructions using the RMSE of a sub-test set of 5 000 observations.
To obtain comparable RMSE, we deprocess the reconstructed examples afterwards
and compare them with the original ones. As deprocessing requires to keep the indicators
(i.e. the mean, minimum and so forth) in memory, we only focus on the feature-wise
methods for which indicators are ﬁxed for the dataset4.

one can see the reconstructions yielded by the standardization procedure depict a better
signal within the borders of the stack of waveforms, but suﬀer from the remaining slight

For the L1b dataset, using the original space leads to unconverged solutions.
From
Sec. 2.3.3, which underlined the importance of preprocessing and adequate weight ini-
tialization, it is quite understandable that L2 signals, which range spans the interval
[0, 3.25 × 10−10], will hardly converge. Also, the fact that we are working with ﬂoat32
formated numbers can explain why TensorFlow gradients, after ﬁrst decreasing, become
nans. Rather than working with ﬂoat64 (which would result in RAM issues and exces-
sively heavy models to be stored on the hard-disk for a modest laptop conﬁguration) we
decide to normalize example-wise the L2 signals. We justify this choice because it maps
each reconstructed example on a range from 0 to 1, which allows to make use of the
cross-entropy loss function. As described in Sec. 2.3.1 and more speciﬁcally on Fig. 2.8,
such loss is quite forgiving regarding poor initialization schemes, converges generally with
fewer iterations and gradients are easier to compute.
As for the L1b data, we can make use of the spatial auto-correlation of the variables
of L2 waveforms. However, this autocorrelation is here 1-dimensional and hence, kernels
sizes and strides always have their width equal to one in a mini-batch set-up with shape
[B ×H ×W ×D] where B is the batch size, H the height, W the width and D the depth.
Therefore, ww design a non symmetrical CNNDAE7 (trained and tested on 151 000 and
190 000 observations, respectively) yielding a most compressed manifold a little smaller
than 12 % of the original space.
Fig. 5.11 delineates randomly picked training and test examples and corresponding
reconstructions, together with the general RMSEtrain and RMSEtest8. Both indicators are
very similar, showing no sign of overﬁtting as can be expected from a denoising CNNAE.
It is interesting to note the CNNDAE seems to model decently many types of wave-
forms, apart for some particularly noisy and oscillating signals for which the mean of these
oscillations seem to be the model safest choice.
Finally, it is worth mentioning that the bottom left example, which is narrowly peaked
around bin 120, cannot be predicted by the model. This is strange since on the other
hand, the top right example, which underlines similar shape and bin location, can be

The analysis of the hidden space of L1b waveforms tend to show images are mainly grouped
based on the shape of the borders separating the signal from zero padded (previously
masked) background, rather than based on the internal structure of the signals.

of the masked pixels as is the case for the L1b product (see Sec. 5.2.1). This, in addition
to the eased training process, leads us to solely focus on the L1b dataset in the remaining
generative and clustering parts of this chapter.

In order to separate Brown from non-Brown waveforms, we investigate two indicators
that thresholding could potentially be based on, namely the distributions of the latent
space and of the reconstruction errors. As the distribution of the latent space was already
discussed in Sec. 5.3.2, we now turn to the analysis of the reconstruction error distribution.
Fig. 5.19a portrays the mean reconstruction error. As can be seen, errors are centered
around zero, indicating the predictions are not biased. The best approximation of the
mean error is given by a Student-t distribution (among the 95 continuous distributions of
the scipy statistical library). As often with natural data, distributions with bigger tails
than the ones of a normal distribution better represent the error (also, the Laplace latent
space itself has bigger tails than a Gaussian).
We then turn to the analysis of the RMSEtest as the mean error is not suited for
anomaly detection (for instance, a poor reconstruction error that ﬁrst over-estimate the
signal and then underestimate it would yield a fairly low mean error). Often, the RMSE
is modeled using a χ2 distribution [25]. However, for this application, such choice leads
to a sub-optimal approximation of our RMSE (see Fig. 5.19b). In deed, the error is much

peakier close to zero than what the best χ2 can approximate. Instead, we make use of a
Burr III distribution which, as can be seen on Fig. 5.19b, is a much better approximation

Clustering capabilities
For each cluster, the corresponding 5 test examples with highest posterior probability

As can be seen, increasing the number of clusters from

and 6. This behavior, which reminds Bayesian GMMs, is particularly appreciable as it
enables automatic selection of a sensible number of components for a given problem. This
behavior is actually rather intriguing since the prior on the labels p(y) is ﬁxed to even
probabilities.
Also, the remaining clusters are more unbalanced than for the GMVAE with K = 5.
In deed, more than 50 % of the test examples are categorized as cluster 9, i.e. seemingly
single narrowly peaked shapes occurring around one third of the signal’s length. This is
to the detriment of cluster 8, i.e. the component which often yield the second highest
posterior probability for examples clustered in group 9, which hence only represent a
bit less than 4 % of the soft assignments. Cluster 2, also rarely picked, often seems to
suﬀer from the competition with cluster 9. Such behaviors were to be expected as these
components signiﬁcantly overlap.
Another example of clusters rivalry is the one of components 3 and 4, with an obvious
win of the number 3 as cluster 4 eventually vanished during the training.
Therefore, considering Fig.
5.29, it seems that, in addition to pure Brown wave-
forms extracted earlier with the binary classiﬁer, Cryosat-2 L2 waveforms can sensibly be
represented by 6 classes. However, this statement is only valid in the scope of the recon-
structions yielded by our model. In deed, as one can see also see on Fig. 5.29 for instance,
the quality of the reconstructed waveforms greatly vary as some shapes can be too hard
to reconstruct for the model. This means that the latent space representation of the data
is a rather crude one, not able to represent more subtle diﬀerences between waveforms
which could have otherwise yielded additional classes. Therefore, a more powerful model
may lead to an increased number of sensible classes.

Spatial distribution analysis
The spatial distribution of the examples based on the clusters they most likely belong to

CNNVAE with the shallow GMVAE with K = 5 and the Brown CNNVAE with the deep
GMVAE with K = 10. The ﬁrst combination yields six diﬀerent clusters numbered from

with the numbers as presented in Sec. 5.4.1. The second combination yields 11 diﬀerent
clusters numbered from 0 to 10 with Brown waveforms commit to cluster 10 and non-
Brown components assigned with the numbers as presented in Sec. 5.4.2.
Fig. 5.32 illustrates the spatial analysis over the Lake Tana in central Africa. One can
see Brown waveforms are observed over the center of the lake. As we get closer to land,
diﬀerent types of waveforms start to arise.

coast. Over land, cluster 0 largely dominates the retrieved signals, followed components
3 and 2. Hence, land is here mainly characterized by corrupted signals and, in a lesser

present in comparison.

of component 9, namely clusters 5 and 8. On land, echoes are mainly noisy, as pictured

Chapter 6
Conclusions and perspectives
Both L1b and L2 Cryosat-2 products were investigated in their original feature space,
as well as in their compressed manifolds produced by convolutional auto-encoders and
denoising auto-encoders, along the ﬁrst principal components and t-SNE axes. We observe
that both products yield very structured and complex manifolds.
Also, we note the
structure of L1b data mainly conveys information regarding the overall contour delimiting
the border between actual signals and masked-pixels. This makes the product unsuitable
for our clustering task seeking to generate a typology depicting the patterns emanating
from the non-masked pixels. On the other hand, the L2 product does not suﬀer from
that issue and is hence chosen to be further analyzed and experimented on with deep
generative modeling and clustering tools.
We designed a convolutional variational auto-encoder with a one-dimensional latent
space which can consistently and solely reconstruct L2 Brown waveforms. The manifold
that yielded the optimal model arose early in the optimization process.
Such latent
space, which would later become (through training iterations) unit Gaussian distributed
as progressively stimulated to be by the prior penalty, is best approximated by a Laplace
distribution. As a mean to threshold Brown from non-Brown waveforms, we use the Burr
III distributed reconstruction root mean square error yielded by the application of the
model on ocean waveforms. This model can therefore be used as a binary classiﬁer, in
addition to serve as a Laplace distributed generator of simulated Brown echoes.
To soft-classify L2 waveforms within a deep generative clustering framework, we uti-
lized the Gaussian mixture variational auto-encoder developed by [86]. Models with 5
and 10 components were subsequently investigated, whether it be regarding their latent
spaces, spatial distributions, clustering or generative capabilities. The lessons were four-
folds. First, the sensible number of components of the model is actually smaller than
speciﬁed (about 4 instead of 5 and approximately 6 against 10, respectively) as some
components completely overlap one another with only one dominating the others by,
in practice, always being soft-assigned to. Second, despite using a non-adaptive prior
p(y) ﬁxed to even probabilities, converged models surprisingly still evidence unbalanced
classes among the softly-assigned examples. Third, very few latent variables were actu-
ally activated (3 and 4 for 64 and 100-dimensional manifolds, respectively). This behavior
is surprising as the reconstructions are far from neatly reproducing all waveforms and,
hence, it would have appeared consistent to expect the latent manifold to make use of the
additional variables at hand to mitigate that eﬀect. Fourth, the analysis of the spatial
distribution of the clusters revealed very structured, with measurements soft-assigned to
the same components always found in sequences. This behavior is reassuring as it induces
99
==PAGE:104==
that measurements are rarely to be randomly clustered, even for observations which two
highest posterior probabilities are alike.
Investigating the capabilities of the notoriously powerful new ladder network archi-
tecture is a sound general perspective of improvement. This could make it possible to
train deeper architectures, with possibly several stochastic layers in the case of variational
models, yielding richer and more eﬃcacious models.
Regarding the t-SNE representations of the data in original and compressed spaces, one
could potentially bring out more relevant and readable examples aggregates (i.e. denser
and less numerous) by reducing the costs uncured to dissimilar observations by feeding
the t-SNE algorithm with diﬀerent distance matrices.
As far as clustering capabilities of the L1b data product are concerned, one could
apply a non-zero padding closer to (without reaching) the actual other pixels intensities.
This may bring forth compressed manifolds, as well as the original data space, that do not
mainly convey information regarding masked-pixels contour anymore. Instead, this could
lead to put more emphasis on the actual signal carried in non-masked pixels. Succeeding
to do so would bring about a resurgence of interest for the clustering of L1b data product.
Instead of using the highest posterior probabilities to exhibit the distinctive charac-
teristics of examples belonging to a given cluster while remaining easily distinguishable
from the other components, one could make use of diﬀerent indicators highlighting for
instance observations with highest ratios of the largest posterior probability over one or
several other posteriors.
To avoid falling within the Brown binary classiﬁer’s remit before utilizing the genera-
tive clustering model, one could parametrize p(y) into pθ3(y). In deed, although converged
models making use of p(y) can still yield unbalanced soft-assignments, explicitly allowing
the model to adapt its prior probabilities to the given nature of the data should, in the-
ory, allow to model the dominating Brown component while still being able to mold the
remaining ’outlier’ classes.
Finally, the optimal number of actual model’s components is restricted by its limited
reconstructions capabilities. Alleviating the latter issue could potentially lead to a latent
manifold accounting for more diverse sub-types of waveforms. Hence, more powerful net-
works should increase the number of sensible clusters naturally selected by the model.
Several trails to improve the model capacities could potentially be investigated in future
works. First, one may increase the number of activated variables in the latent spaces of our
models which, as already mentioned, are too sparse. Attempting to mitigate this problem
by increasing the number of latent variables did not show successful in our case. Instead,
one could employ the ladder network framework which is known to improve training by
propagating strong gradients deeper in the hidden layers. This could help activating the
latent variables. Second, utilizing additional stochastic layers may enable richer latent
spaces assuming such models can be trained thanks to ladder networks. Finally, inte-
grating convolutional capabilities to the framework of the models is yet another obvious
manner to enhance their power. This would require to ﬁnd a method that can condition
labels (i.e. a categorical one-hot encoded variable) on convolutional layers. Whilst often
conveyed with the bias terms in other deep learning ﬁelds, such terms are usually set to
zero in auto-encoders as this typically induces better results. Therefore, in the best case
scenario, such a conditioning method should follow another path.
