ABSTRACT 
 
Malaria has been a major global health problem of humans throughout history and is a leading 
cause of death and disease across many tropical and subtropical countries. Over the past decades, 
renewed efforts to control malaria have reduced its prevalence over half, raising the prospect that 
elimination and eradication could be a long-term possibility. To achieve this goal, the increased 
understanding of the biology of the parasite, as well as, the development of new tools, such as 
more efficacious vaccine, are crucial. 
There is a strong rationale for the development of vaccines based on antigens of blood-stage 
parasites since symptomatic malaria is caused by blood stage parasitaemia and the majority of the 
parasite´s life inside humans is spent in this stage. At present, all leading candidate blood-stage 
antigens are merozoite proteins, either on the merozoite surface or within the apical organelles. 
However, identifying protective epitopes is  a major challenge mainly caused by the complex 
structure and life cycle of the Plasmodium falciparum parasites.  
This master’s thesis intended to identify an epitope of a malaria neutralising monoclonal antibody 
(mAb) designed in our group (RAM1.25), targeting the malarial rhoptry associated membrane 
antigen, PfRAMA. PfRAMA is involved in rhoptry biogenesis and host cell invasion, thereby 
facilitating merozoite invasion of the erythrocytes, a crucial moment in the parasite´s life cycle. 
By identifying and vaccinating with the epitope of RAM1.25, we would aim to elicit an antibody 
response with similar attributes as previously observed for RAM1.25. Further, this could be 
combined with other protective epitopes from different merozoite proteins, such as PfCyRPA and 
PfRH5, thereby creating a multiepitope vaccine. In this way, we could generate an immune 
response focused against the protective epitopes of several parasite antigens.  
Epitope identification in PfRAMA was done by generating a random gene fragment library based 
on the gene encoding PfRAMA and using phage display technology to select those peptides that 
showed high binding to PfRAMA mAb RAM1.25. Using this method, an epitope from PfRAMA 
was seamlessly mapped when panned against RAM1.25. Moreover, when panning two non-
inhibitory mAbs (RAM1.89 and RAM1.94) with the gene fragment library, their epitopes were 
precisely located in different regions of PfRAMA genome. Showing that RAM1.25 inhibitory 
capacity is dependent on its binding to a specific C-term critical zone in PfRAMA. Thereof, 
RAM1.25 epitope has shown to be a short immunogenic region which could be considered as a 
promising target for future vaccine development.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
This master’s thesis aimed to identify a protective epitope in the rhoptry associated membrane 
antigen – pfRAMA, a rhoptry protein found in Plasmodium falciparum species parasites – which 
give rise to malaria. Malaria caused by Plasmodium falciparum parasites remains one of the most 
significant global public health challenges, causing millions of cases and thousands of deaths 
yearly1. There has been a significant progress in reducing malaria mortality but the spread of 
parasites resistant to frontline antimalarial drugs threaten this balance, therefore emphasizing the 
need for control and eradication through a vaccine. Two significant challenges encounter 
Plasmodium falciparum antigen identification: (i) the complexity of the parasite life cycle, which 
presents a large number of potential targets and (ii) the depth of genomic diversity across global 
parasite populations2.   Given these challenges, it has been theorised that an effective vaccine will 
have to target multiple components simultaneously, namely a multiepitope (or multivalent) 
vaccine3.  
PfRAMA plays a key role in rhoptry biogenesis and host cell invasion, therefore facilitating 
merozoite invasion in the erythrocytes.  This is a crucial moment in the parasite life cycle, since 
after invasion parasites change red blood cell’s structure to facilitate their replication and survival 
within the host cell. Cycles of blood-stage replication for Plasmodium falciparum take 
approximately 48h and it is during this stage that malaria diseases occurs4. Understanding the 
molecular and cellular events involved in merozoite invasion and the human immune response to 
merozoite antigens is crucial for the development of future malaria vaccines. Besides PfRAMA, 
multiple proteins are involved in this process, like the highly characterised PfCyRPA, PfRH5 and 
PfRipr, proteins with important roles during the infection process, which have been theorised as 
vaccine candidates since inhibitory mAbs anti those antigens have been identified5.   
Our group had previously developed monoclonal mAbs targeting PfRAMA. These mAbs had 
shown different neutralising  potencies, with some interesting candidates that exhibited the 
capacity to inhibit slightly the invasion of the erythrocytes, but even more interestingly, enhancing 
the effect of mAbs targeting PfCyRPA and PfRH5. Therefore, it was proposed as the main aim 
of this thesis, to try to map the epitope of RAM1.25. If accomplishing this, other epitopes derived 
from non-inhibitory mAbs RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 would also be searched to demonstrate their 
different locations in the PfRAMA structure.  
Several approaches can be taken in order to find an epitope, but in this cases since the starting 
point was a neutralising mAb targeting a linear epitope, phage display of a PfRAMA gene 
fragment library with mAb RAM1.25 was meant to reveal those peptides binding to the mAb, 
thereby indication the location of its epitope. Consequently, enabling the identification of an 
immunogenic epitope and emphasizing the need of directing antibody responses toward 
protective epitopes capable to inhibit to merozoite invasion of host cells. Ultimately, vaccinating 
with a protective epitope might give rise to antibody responses  with similar neutralising 
properties observed in vitro.

2 BACKGROUND 
 
Introduction to malaria 
Malaria is a disease caused by Plasmodium parasites and represents one of the transmittable 
diseases with the highest prevalence around the world, with an estimated 241 million cases and 
627.000 deaths on 20201. More than 40% of the world’s population is exposed to contracting the 
disease, a percentage that has increased due to the parasite’s resistance to anti-malarial drugs and 
the Anopheles mosquito’s resistance to existing pesticides6. Most of the cases (82%) and deaths 
(95%) are located in African regions, followed by South-East Asia and South America.  
Five species of malaria parasite from the Plasmodium genera infect humans, though most deaths 
are caused by Plasmodium falciparum7. Therefore, all work done in this thesis focuses on this 
strain. Malaria can be targeted via vector control, antimalarial drugs, and vaccines8. Each of them 
with associated flaws, that had led us to years of investigation in order to achieve better 
understanding of Plasmodium pathway and molecular studies of the parasite’s life cycle which 
are newly promising immunoprophylactic strategies aiming to identify proteins involved in the 
invasion thus seeking to eradicate this disease.  
 
 The infection process  
The Plasmodium life cycle involves two different hosts: the vector, an infected female Anopheles 
mosquito; and its target, such as a human9. The size and genetic complexity of the parasite mean 
that each infection presents thousands of antigens to the human immune system which vary 
throughout the different life stages in the human host. Understanding which of the antigens are 
most useful as a target for vaccine development has been complicated, stressing that a deep 
comprehension of the infection process after a mosquito bite is key for future progress10.  
Malaria infection caused by Plasmodium falciparum begins when an infected female Anopheles 
mosquito has a blood meal (bites) a human, injecting Plasmodium parasites in form of sporozoites 
into the bloodstream. These sporozoites rapidly migrate to the liver by traversing invading the 
hepatocytes. The intracellular parasite proliferates over a number of days to form many thousands 
of merozoites that are released into the bloodstream to invade erythrocytes and begin the asexual 
blood-stage cycle. Once inside the erythrocyte, the parasite re-models the host cell to provide a 
niche in which it can obtain nutrients required for development and division, as well as to provide 
means to evade the host immune system9,11. Intracellular forms develop through trophozoite and 
schizont stages to form merozoites that are released during egress and are primed for invasion of 
new erythrocytes to continue the asexual blood cycle, thus exponentially amplifying, and causing 
the clinical symptoms of malaria.   
Some parasites will undergo gametocyte development in the erythrocytes, and upon a new blood 
meal, the gametocytes will be taken up by the mosquito and undergo sexual reproduction in the 
midgut. Male and female gametes fuse forming a zygote, which traverses the gut epithelium of 
the mosquito and forms and oocyst. Sporozoites are released from the oocyst, moving to the 
salivary glands of the mosquito therefore closing its life cycle12, seen in Figure I.

 Mechanical steps of erythrocyte invasion  
The blood stage merozoites are relatively small and have a unique goal: to invade the next 
erythrocyte available. Egress and invasion involve the regulated discharge of at least four classes 
of secretory organelles – rhoptries, micronemes, exonemes and dense granules – positioned in the 
apical end of the merozoite13.  
While apical organelles play an essential role in invasion, it is the surface of the merozoites that 
first contacts the erythrocytes, activating a three-phase process: (i) initial interaction and 
deformation of the erythrocyte membrane; (ii) apical interaction and invasion, and (iii) 
echinocytosis and recovery of the invaded host cell.  
This initial attachment (i) is supported by a family of Plasmodium falciparum merozoite surface 
proteins (PfMSPs) which coat the entire parasite. PfMSP1, the most abundant and characterized 
surface protein, is believed to create a complex together with other large proteins to start parasite- 
erythrocyte interaction. The parasite invasion proteins (ii) used in the next steps come from the 
micronemes and rhoptries, which facilitate erythrocyte invasion upon contact. Micronemes 
contain adhesins involved in erythrocyte binding while rhoptries are released after initial host cell 
engagement. Rhoptries are pear-shaped structures that are formed de novo during each blood stage 
life cycle that have high affinity for the receptors in the erythrocytes14. Some organelle 
components are discharged on the merozoite surface prior to invasion, while others are discharged 
once the merozoite recognises and forms a tight junction (TJ) between its apical pole and the 
erythrocyte membrane, suggesting a transient role during merozoite invasion15,16,17. The post-
invasion phase (iii) takes place when the merozoites have fully entered the erythrocytes during 
echinocytosis18.  
Rhoptries are characterized by a relatively wide bulb region which narrows to a neck or duct 
towards the apical prominence of the merozoite. When the merozoites are internalized, the 
rhoptries disappear. Little is known about this process due to lack of biomarkers. Thorough the 
years, several important rhoptry proteins have been identified, such as the PfRAP1/2 protein 
complex that is implicated in the parasitophorous vacuole membrane19 and essential rhoptry neck 
proteins like PfRON2, PfRON4 and PfRON5 forming a TJ complex with PfAMA120. 
Nonetheless, all the experimental procedures performed in this thesis focus on another important 
rhoptry protein, the rhoptry associated membrane antigen, PfRAMA.

 Plasmodium falciparum rhoptry associated membrane antigen – PfRAMA  
PfRAMA is expressed relatively early in the blood stage cycle, prior to rhoptry formation. It has 
been shown to have roles in both rhoptry biogenesis and host cell invasion21,22. The protein 
sequence of PfRAMA is 861aa and it is synthesized as a 170kDa protein (full-length PfRAMA) 
in early trophozoites, hours before rhoptry formation. Hereafter, it undergoes proteolytic cleavage 
in the rhoptries, which removes the N-term, yielding a 60kDa (named p60) mature form, anchored 
in the inner face of the rhoptry membrane via glycosylphosphatidylinositol anchor (GPI). p60 
PfRAMA is discharged from the rhoptries of free merozoites and binds to the erythrocyte 
membrane by its most C-term region, thus helping the invasion process23.  Its crystal structure has 
never been determined before, but some 3D computational models suggest its tertiary structure 
mostly consists of alpha helices with many loops as connectors24, shown in Figure II.  
To elucidate PfRAMA function, different studies created conditional disruption versions of 
PfRAMA, showing that RAMA-null parasites display selective mislocation of rhoptry bulb and 
neck proteins (RONs) and produce dysmorphic rhoptries without neck region. This implies a fatal 
defect in the parasites that cannot induce echinocytosis in the targeted erythrocytes3,13.  Others 
have shown that RhopH3 and RAP1 show protein-protein interaction with PfRAMA, and that 
certain proteins like sortilin are involved in escorting PfRAMA from the Golgi apparatus to the 
rhoptries25,26.  
While research groups around the world had been documenting all the information that nowadays 
is available from PfRAMA, in our group this knowledge was used in a study that generated a 
novel PfRAMA mAb with neutralising activity27. Moreover, we demonstrated that this 
neutralising mAb in combination with PfCyRPA and PfRH5-specific mAbs potentiated the 
neutralising effect against Plasmodium falciparum28. Therefore, confirming a synergy between 
PfRAMA, PfCyRPA and PfRH5 – specific antibodies. Suggesting that combinations of them 
could elicit synergistic polyclonal antibody responses if used in a vaccine. Interestingly, it was 
also suggested that this growth inhibitory anti-PfRAMA mAb targeted a unique protective epitope 
in the C-term of the protein, but the exact location of this epitope was not precisely identified at 
that moment.  
Compilation of all these findings show that PfRAMA is indispensable for blood stage parasite 
survival and places this rhoptry in the spotlight as a promising target for a future malaria vaccine.

How are we currently treating Malaria  
Once a malaria diagnosis has been made, the drug or combination of drugs recommended for each 
specific situation may slightly vary, being the major drugs give chloroquine, quinine, artemisinin 
combinations and primaquine29. Regardless having a treatment, every year hundreds of thousands 
of deaths are accounted due to this disease. One of the biggest arising problems is antimalarial 
drug resistance. Antimalarial drug resistance is the ability of a parasite strain to survive and even 
multiply despite the administration of a specific medicine given in doses equal to or higher than 
those usually recommended. Among the factors that can ease the emergence of resistance to 
existing antimalarial drugs are: parasite mutation rate, treatment guidelines, improper dosing and 
poor quality antimalarial aid30. Resistant parasites will continue emerging, therefore requiring 
newer and better antimalarial drugs, a complicated and expensive task which nowadays cannot 
ensure success against future multidrug resistant strains31. Thus, remarking the need for 
development of new treatment options, like vaccines, that can target and eliminate the disease.  
 
 
Mosquirix, the first ever approved malaria vaccine 
WHO 2021 malaria report describes its R&D funding been led by investment in drugs (37%), 
basic research (28%) and vaccines research (19%)1 – the rest is divided between vector control 
and diagnostics. A change of paradigm is expected in the upcoming years since as of October 
2021, the RTS,S/AS0132 vaccine was approved for frontline treatment of malaria in children in 
areas where Plasmodium falciparum is prevalent32. RTS,S/AS01 (or Mosquirix) vaccine is made 
up of proteins found on the surface of Plasmodium falciparum sporozoites, eliciting an immune 
response when recognised as foreign for our bodies. It has shown to have roughly 30% efficacy 
in children aged 6 to 18 weeks32. This milestone will set precedent for future vaccines to come, 
with improved efficacy and a wider coverage.  
 
The future malaria vaccine  
The development of a highly effective and durable vaccine against Plasmodium falciparum 
malaria remains a key priority. Malaria vaccines can be categorized into three: pre-erythrocytic, 
blood-stage and transmission blocking vaccines33, seen in Figure III. One of the most important 
imperatives for future malaria vaccine improvements is to extend the period of protection and 
broaden its coverage, two traits that could be circumventing using structure-based vaccine design.  
Malaria vaccine development moved to a new era  with the RTS,S/AS01 vaccine approved for 
children vaccination for pre-erythrocytic Plasmodium falciparum. This vaccine directs immune 
responses against the major circumsporozoite protein (CSP) covering the surface of the infecting 
sporozoite32,34. This vaccine was designed as a virus-like particle (VLP) comprised of the central 
repeat and C-term of CSP fused to hepatitis B surface antigen. Recent molecular understanding 
of the immune response towards malaria is leading to new strategies to induce more effective 
immune responses35. These new approaches can increase immunogenicity, influence the type of 
response, avoid inhibitory immune mechanisms, among others. 
Reverse vaccinology is the process of antigen discovery starting from genome information36. 
Traditional vaccine development is based on injection of the disease-causing agent inactivated or 
live-attenuated version which aims to trigger an immune response towards it. On the contrary, 
reverse vaccinology aims designing vaccines from the desired immune response instead, starting 
the process where traditional vaccines end37. For example, via the isolation of a mAb from an 
individual exposed to Plasmodium falciparum infection that shows potent neutralising function. 
==PAGE:18==
 
Page 13 
 
Once a mAb with that characteristic has been identified, its protective epitope could be determined 
thus having found the smallest immunogenic part of a given key protein for infection38. Further 
than this, the high throughput discovery of protective human antibodies, sequencing of B cell 
epitopes, and the increasing structural characterization of protective antigens and epitopes, prove 
the molecular understanding to drive the development of new vaccines in the so-called “Reverse 
vaccinology 2.0” era37.  
 
 
Master’s thesis rationale  
Our group has created mAbs with neutralising capacity for PfRAMA. Therefore, in practice, 
starting with a neutralising mAb, its epitope could be mapped creating a gene fragment library 
from the PfRAMA genome and then making use of phage display to generate and isolate high 
affinity peptides.  
Phage display is a method of presenting numerous different peptides on phages to see which ones 
bind to a given target. To do this, an M13KO7 phage is used. It consists of several proteins, but 
two of them are worth mentioning: G3, which is a coat protein and G8 which provides the majority 
of the backbone of the phage39. The G3 protein can be genetically engineering to have a peptide 
at its N-term. Upon incorporation into the phage, the phage will then present a peptide on its G3 
protein. The plasmid encoding G3 is named pHEN6, which encodes an ampicillin resistance gene 
that can be used for selection. To generate fully functional phages, helper phages with genetic 
material encoding all other proteins but G3 protein must be added. This leading to the approach 
used in this thesis, which was to incorporate gene fragments from PfRAMA ligated into the 
pHEN6 plasmid into the G3 encoding DNA sequence40. Therefore, making the phages express 
random sections of PfRAMA as its G3 protein N-term. Ultimately, panning the phages against 
the neutralising mAb RAM1.25 thereby peptides recognised by this mAb could be identified. The 
extracted peptides would have high affinity for the mAb, and it could be expected that if used as 
part of a vaccine, they would induce antibodies with comparable specificity as the original mAb,  
and ultimately a protective effect would be achieved.  
Nonetheless, a highly effective vaccine should not be limited to display a single epitope. A 
multiepitope vaccine targeting different antigens involved in erythrocyte invasion, such as 
PfCyRPA, PfRH5 or MSRP5, with synergistic effects, would trigger an enhanced response since 
this is an essential development stage for the malaria parasites2.

3 VALUE CREATION  
The output of this master thesis will contribute to the design of a second-generation multivalent 
vaccine against malaria, which in the end term aims to change the paradigm that this disease 
creates nowadays. But just because a vaccine is proven to be effective at preventing a disease, 
such malaria, that does not meant it will be widely accepted or used. There needs to be enough

(ThermoFisher) in a buffer of 80 µL MgSO2, 20 µL dNTP, 8 µL ultra-purified water (UPW), 20 
µL Thermal buffer (ThermoFisher) mixed with 80 µL of the gene fragments and incubated at 
72ºC for 40minutes.  The polished gene fragments were purified using the QIAX II purification 
kit and eluted in 50 µL of UPW.

stock. Plasmids were purified from an E.coli culture using the QIAprep maxiprep kit (QIAgen) 
and eluted in UPW. Linearized plasmids were obtained after a double restriction was performed 
using 5 µg of the vector and 2.5 µL of PstI and NotI enzymes (both ThermoFisher), together with 
5 µL of Fast digest buffer (ThermoFisher) and 2 µL of UPW, at 37ºC for 1h.  The product was 
run on two 1% agarose gels, an analytical gel to compare the cut and uncut vectors, and a 
purification gel, where the QIAquick Gel (QIAgen) extraction kit was used to purify the product.

followed by dephosphorylation treatment using rSAP phosphatase (NEB) and CutSmart buffer 
(NEB) to prevent religation. To finish, the plasmids were  incubated at 37ºC 30 minutes and heat 
inactivated at 65ºC for 5 minutes.  
The linearized expression  vector pHEN6 was then ligated to the gene fragments. 0.5µg of plasmid 
was used in a molar ration of 1:20 for  plasmid:insert.  The vector and the gene fragments were 
ligated with Quick Ligase (NEB) for 1 hour at room temperature and then purified using the 
QIAquick PCR purification kit.

The pHEN6 plasmids containing the gene fragment library inserts from the gene fragment library 
were transformed into TG1 cells using electroporation. 50µL of TG1 cells were mixed either with

50% glycerol in a 7:3 ratio and frozen at -80ºC. Moreover, 96 colonies were PCR screened and a 
1% agarose gel was run, everything to evaluate the insertion rate into the TG1 cells. 
 
4.3 PANNING THE GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY  
 
Three rounds of panning were performed for all relevant mAbs. The general protocol on how to 
do a panning with M13KO7 helper phages (NEB) and gene fragment library will be explained in 
the section below.  
Generation of R0 phages 
TG1 cells, from a glycerol stock, were spread into LB no-ampicillin plates and incubated O/N at 
37ºC to obtain isolated single cell colonies. Three glycerol stocks (1.5mL each) of the transformed 
gene fragment library were thawed and mixed with 10mL of LB medium. 50µL of  M13KO7 
helper phages were added to the TG1 culture and were left at 37ºC without shaking for 30 minutes, 
thereby producing fully functioning phages displaying PfRAMA peptide fragments. The mixture 
was transferred to 200mL of LB+ampicillin medium and incubated at 37ºC 180rpm 1h. finally, 
kanamycin was added to a final concentration of 70µg/mL and incubated O/N at 37ºC, 180 rpm. 
50mL of PEG solution (14,5% NaCl, 20% PEG10,000 solution, Sigma Aldrich) were used to 
precipitate the phages in ice for 2h. The phages were spin down for 30 minutes at 10.000g. The 
pellet, containing now the phages, was resuspended in 1.5mL of PBS 1X and transferred into 
Eppendorf tubes. Following 5 minutes centrifugation at 10.000xg 4ºC, the supernatant is aliquoted 
(R0 phages) and stored at -80ºC for further use.

3x TG1 cultures were set up in 10mL of 2xYT medium in 50mL tubes, 37ºC 200rpm until OD 
reaches 0,5.  
2x 50µL magnetic protein G beads (DynabeadsTM Protein G, Invitrogen), were washed twice 
with 1mL PBS + 0.1% Tween 20, and magnetically pulled down to remove the solution. 300µL 
of phages were mixed with 500µL PBS +10% skimmed milk powder and added to 50µL of

4.6.4 
Protein expression protocol 
 
The protein expression protocol was divided into three days, moving from pre-cultures (started 
from either glycerol stocks or recently plated cell cultures) to protein harvesting.  
A clone with a plasmid encoding insert was inoculated into 20mL 2xYT medium in a 50mL flask. 
20µL of ampicillin were added and the culture was incubated O/N 37ºC 125rpm.

with 400µL of ampicillin and incubated at 37ºC 125rpm until OD reached 0.5-0.8. The 
temperature was then lowered to 20ºC the culture incubated for an extra 20 minutes. A 1mL 
sample was taken and spin down 21000xg 2 minutes 30 seconds. The supernatant was discarded, 
and the pellet stored at -20ºC for further analysis (dubbed the “before induction” sample). 40µL 
of  1M IPTG were added to the 400mL culture and incubated O/N at 20ºC 125rpm.  
The third day, the OD of the culture was measured and noted. A 1mL sample was taken and spun 
down at 21000g 2 minutes 30 seconds, the supernatant was discarded, and the pellet stored at -
20ºC for further analysis (dubbed the “after induction” sample). The rest of the culture was pooled 
and centrifuged at 10000xg 10 minutes. The supernatant was discarded, and the remaining pellets 
were dissolved in Binding Buffer (25mM imidazole, 1MNaCl and 10mM NaPO4, pH 8) - 5mL/g 
of pellet. Moreover, 1 protease inhibiting tablet was added for each 10mL of Binding Buffer. The 
cell suspension was sonicated x2 at 80% potency for 5 minutes on ice. 100µL were spun down 
for 2 minutes at 16000xg and the supernatant was transferred to a new tube. The supernatant was 
centrifuged at 40000xg for 30 minutes at 4ºC. The final supernatant was filtered through a 0.2µm 
Topfilter and further purified (see below).  
 
 
4.6.5 
Äkta purification – protein purification  
 
The obtained proteins were purified through the Äktaexpress system (Cytiva) using 5mL HisTrap 
columns (Merck). The samples were loaded onto the column and subsequently washed with 
Binding Buffer containing 25mM imidazole, 1MNaCl and 10mM NaPO4, pH 8.  
The proteins contained a negatively charged HisTag (x6 histidine residues) that binds to the 
positive nickel ions in the columns. To elute the proteins, the Elution Buffer is run through the 
system using increased gradient, the negatively charged residues from the imidazole are  
exchanged with the negative residues of the HisTag thus eluting at low concentrations of Elution 
Buffer weak binding proteins (those of non-interest) and at high concentrations, the proteins of 
interest. The eluate obtained from the purification is kept in 2mL fractions in a 96 deep well plate. 
The flowthrough of the process was also collected. Visualization through the software associated 
with the Äktaexpress system showed the eluted peak containing the protein of interest therefore 
the specific wells containing it can be chosen and used for further SDS-Page, Western Blot 
analysis (both Section 4.6.7) and Dot blot (Section 4.6.6).  
The purified proteins underwent buffer exchange to remove the traces of imidazole. 3kDa MWCO 
membranes were used for the dialysis together with 5L of 1xPBS O/N at 4ºC with magnetic 
rotation . Finally, the proteins were aliquoted, snapfrozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at -80ºC.

5 RESULTS  
5.1 CREATION OF A GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY  
 
This section describes the creation of a short and a large random gene fragment library from 
PfRAMA. Random fragments from PfRAMA were generated by DNaseI digestion, ligated into 
the pHEN6 plasmid, and transformed into TG1 cells. By these means two libraries encoding 
different regions from PfRAMA genetic material were obtained. 
 
 
5.1.1 
Small and large gene fragment libraries encoding random fragments of PfRAMA  
 
In order to generate the PfRAMA gene fragment library, the 
first step was to make fragments of its gene of the desired size 
to be fused with the pHEN6 plasmid. 
Therefore, a PCR was set up to amplify the ectodomain of 
PfRAMA gene from a PfRAMA containing plasmid. 
PfRAMA had an expected length of 2400bp and was detected 
in a 1% agarose gel, as show in Figure 1. Furthermore, a 
control without template DNA was added.  
Once PfRAMA was amplified and purified, two size ranges 
of fragments were created using DNaseI: (i) 50-250bp, small 
gene fragment library; (ii) 200-500bp, large gene fragment 
library.  
 
 
 
 
A time titration was performed to identify the optimal digestion time for creating gene fragments 
of the desired size. The PCR product was digested with DNaseI amounts ranging from 0.1-2U 
and time points from 30 seconds to 15 minutes. For visualization, it was run on an agarose gel, as 
shown in Figure 2. For the small fragment library, 1U of DNaseI and 1 minutes and 45 seconds 
was determined to be the optimal digestion time and DNaseI units. Whereas, for the large 
fragment library, 0.6U of DNaseI and 1 minute. Both shown in Figure 2. Only the small gene 
fragment library was used for further experimentation. Next, the digested fragments were 
extracted and purified, Figure 3. Finally, blunt-end polishing treatment was applied to avoid 
overhanging bases in their termini.  
The expression vector for the gene fragment library, pHEN6 plasmid, was obtained and purified 
as explained in Section 4.1. A double restriction with PstI and NotI enzymes was performed to 
linearize the plasmid. The product was run on two 1% agarose gels, Figure 4, to ensure different

5.2 PANNING THE GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY WITH NEUTRALISING MABS  
 
Once the gene fragment library was constructed, it was used to identify possible targets, or 
epitopes, with high affinity to neutralising mAbs previously engineered in our department. An in 
vitro selection process through affinity enrichment was used, involving three round of: 
immobilization of neutralising mAbs in magnetic beads, gene fragment library presentation, 
elimination of unbound phages, elution of phages and finally addition of helper phages for 
amplification.  
Three mAbs were chosen, RAM1.25, RAM1.89, and RAM1.94, being RAM1.25 the only one 
that had shown neutralising activity in previous studies thereby finding its epitope was of most 
interest. Moreover, as explained in the Background section, data from our group suggested that 
these three mAbs had very different epitopes in PfRAMA. Sections 5.2.1 and 5.2.2 will show 
detailed data about the panning performed with each mAb.  
After each round of panning, the eluted phages were used to reinfect TG1 cells to further amplify 
phages displaying high affinity fragments for the next round. For each round of the panning, 
eluates were collected (R1, R2, R3) to check for increased affinity for the panned mAb, employing 
phage ELISA.   
Colony screening PCR was performed to check the size of the gene fragment inserts. From the 
second and the third rounds of panning, cultures were plated, and selected colonies were grown 
O/N and PCR amplified, the product was run on an agarose gel. Those exact colonies were newly 
grown O/N with LB + ampicillin + kanamycin, centrifugated and phages from the supernatant 
underwent phage ELISA, to assess the affinity towards the panned mAbs. 
Phage ELISA was performed on the supernatant of the O/N grown colonies. ELISA plates were 
coated with either the mAb of interest, RAM1.25, RAM1.89, RAM1.94, or a negative controls 
TC80.1 or CyP2.38. Phages obtained from the supernatant of the selected colonies and the three 
rounds of eluates (R1,R2,R3) were tested with the coated mAbs.   
To determine which phage displaying colonies were of interest, two simultaneous characteristics 
were considered: (i) colonies showing different sizes of the insert, therefore being able to show 
from the smallest binding region to its extended neighbouring sequences; (ii) high reactivity in 
the ELISA assay towards mAb of interest. Following these criteria, colonies were sent for 
sequencing and further aligned to the PfRAMA encoding gene in order to map the extracted 
epitopes.  
 
5.2.1 
Epitope identification through mAb RAM1.25 panning  
 
RAM1.25 is an inhouse designed neutralising mAb that had shown growth inhibition capacity 
and was believed to bind an unknown region in the C-term of PfRAMA. The aim was to identify 
an epitope of RAM1.25 by panning the gene fragment library on this neutralising mAb. 
As introduced in the section above, 40 colonies were picked and used for colony screening PCR, 
Figure 7. The primers used in this PCR screening yielded a product of 338bp when the vector 
was empty. The gene fragment library was expected to be found ranging from 388-588bp. The 
estimated insert ratio after panning was 35%.

mapped perfectly to the 621 to 669 amino acid (aa) region of PfRAMA (48aa). The smallest 
overlapping sequence was as small as 14aa (647 to 660 aa region).

with mAb RAM1.25, the next step was to show that other non-neutralising mAbs mapped 
different regions from PfRAMA.

5.2.2 
Other mAbs align in different regions of the PfRAMA genome  
 
RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 are inhouse designed non-neutralising mAbs that had not shown growth 
inhibition capacity. These mAbs were believed to recognise different regions of PfRAMA than 
RAM1.25, located respectively closer to the C-term or N-term compared to RAM1.25.  By 
panning the gene fragment library against these mAbs the specific epitopes or binding regions 
were expected to be found in different areas than the RAM1.25 epitope. Moreover, TC80.1 was 
also panned with the gene fragment library as a negative control, since it targets an antigen from 
another organism, therefore enabling to demonstrate TC80.1 phages did not map at all PfRAMA.  
The process described in Section 4.1.1 was repeated with the aforementioned mAbs. Figure 11 
shows agarose gels for 40 colonies from TG1 cells reinfected with phages eluted through panning 
against TC80.1, RAM1.89, and RAM1.94, after PCR amplification. The PCR was set up to yield 
product sizes bigger than 338bp if plasmids had an insert. The overall insert rate was approx. 10-
20%. Unexpectedly, multiple bands in the running lanes for RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 on the 
agarose gels were observed. Seen this, it was decided to test for experimental errands in the third 
round of panning. Therefore, phages from the second round of panning were also PCR amplified 
and run on an agarose gel (data not shown).  Up to 160 colonies of high affinity phages for each 
mAb were screened showing the same results, therefore the process resumed with phages from 
the third round of panning.  
Same as with RAM1.25, eluates from the three rounds of panning confirmed an increased 
reactivity towards RAM1.89 and RAM1.94, that was not the case with TC80.1, which showed 
little to no reactivity for all eluates. 
16, 13 and 14 colonies (TC80.1, RAM1.89, and RAM1.94), showed in Figure 11, were subjected 
to phage ELISA. ELISA plates were coated with either mAbs RAM1.89, RAM1.94 and mAb 
TC80.1 as negative control; or coated with mAb TC80.1 and CyP2.38, as negative control. As 
previously done, colonies showing high reactivity in the ELISA were picked for further analysis. 
RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 showed lower reactivity towards the phages compared to RAM1.25. 
TC80.1, as mAbs or negative control, showed no reactivity and CyP2.38, as negative control, did 
not either. Data shown in Figures 12 and 13.  
The selected colonies were sent for sequencing and treated like the sequences obtained after 
panning with mAb RAM1.25. When trying to align the sequences obtained from panning with 
mAb TC80.1 to the PfRAMA sequence, no alignment could be found, as it was expected. Two 
unique sequences were found for both RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 that aligned to PfRAMA genome. 
The two unique sequences for RAM1.89 aligned to 722 to 761 aa region, localized closer to the 
C-term of PfRAMA. Meanwhile, the 2 unique sequences for RAM1.94 to 265 to 292 aa region, 
placing them in the N-term of PfRAMA. Overall representation can be seen in Figure 14.  
To conclude, epitopes for mAbs RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 were found locating in the C-term and 
N-term, respectively, of PfRAMA genome. The negative control, mAb TC80.1 did not map to 
PfRAMA. High affinity phages have been developed towards these mAbs nonetheless, being 
RAM1.25 the one yielding the highest reactivity colonies amongst them all.

5.3 PROTEIN EXPRESSION AND PURIFICATION  
 
After having identified 5 unique sequences aligning to the same region of PfRAMA, 4 of them 
were selected for protein expression: s3, m1, m9 and L4 (L6 was excluded due to high similarity 
with L4).  Protein expression was performed to see if these sequences, and not the rest of the G3 
protein or the whole phages, showed binding for PfRAMA mAbs.  
The 4 selected constructs were either transformed into ss320 or BL21(DE3), both non-amber 
suppressor E.coli strains. All four constructs contained a HisTag in their C-term, followed by an 
amber stop codon (TAG), therefore the rationale behind using E.coli non-amber suppressor strains 
was that it would stop the translation of the constructs as soon as the stop codon was reached 
therefore the expected proteins would be expressed, together with only the N-term of the G3 
protein and a HisTag.  
The constructs were transformed into the expression systems, harvested after 3 days, and purified 
in 5mL HisTrap columns. From the harvesting and the purification different samples were taken 
(before induction, after induction, after sonication, eluted sample, and flowthrough) and analysed 
in SDS-PAGE, WB, and Dot blot.  
 
5.3.1 
Protein expression in ss320 cells 
 
Constructs s3, m1,m9 and L4 were expressed in ss320 E.coli cells. Before protein expression, the 
constructs underwent Miniprep preparation, to isolate plasmid DNA, and were electroporated into 
ss320 cells, as explained in Section 3.6.1. Protein expression was done in 400mL 2xYT medium 
+ ampicillin, harvested and purified in 5mL HisTrap columns.  
 
5.3.1.2 
Protein purification and visualization  
 
The constructs were purified using the Äkta system. All four constructs showed single elution 
peaks, shown in Figure 15. The elution was achieved through constant flow of imidazole which 
exchanged its negative charged residues with the HisTag-proteins trapped in the column. The 
eluted fractions were collected in a 96 deep well plate for further testing.  
Therefore, it was possible to observe: (i) an initial peak that covered the first 2 eluted fractions, 
which corresponded to escaping residues from charging the column, (ii) as soon as the imidazole 
ran through the column, a clear single peak covering around 10-12 fractions was seen being where 
the protein of interest is suspected to elute, it is to remark that a small secondary peak could be 
observed for one of the constructs, and (iii) a solid steady line without any other protein eluted 
after the main peak.  
The eluted proteins were buffer exchanged O/N 4ºC with shaking, using dialysis membranes with 
MWCO 3kDa and PBS solution to remove the remaining imidazole.  
The expected sizes for the 4 proteins ranged from 6-11kDa, therefore SDS-PAGE gels suited for 
separation of smaller proteins were used to visualise the purified product. From the harvesting 
and the purification, different samples were taken (before induction, after induction, after 
sonication, eluted sample, and flowthrough); moreover, for m9, a small secondary peak was 
collected and tested (shoulder).  
==PAGE:40==
 
Page 35 
 
All samples taken were run on SDS-PAGE gels. The outcome showed different proteins being 
expressed in our system, but it was not possible to observe any of the expected constructs. Shown 
in Figure 16.  
In order to clarify if there had been a mistake during the protein purification, a Dot blot assay was 
performed in the eluted samples. The intention when performing a Dot blot was to test the intrinsic 
HisTag of the constructs to show its presence after purification. A nitrocellulose membrane was 
coated with s3, m1,m9 and L4, and identified with anti-HisTag-HRP. By these means, if the 
protein was actually eluted but in low concentrations, this mAb could detect the HisTag located 
in their C-term. Positive and negative controls were added to ensure the veracity of it. When 
revealing the Dot blot it was only possible to observe signal coming from construct s3 and the 
positive control, as shown in Figure 17. Knowing that at least s3 still conserved its HisTag a 
10kDA MWCO centrifugation was performed, aiming to discover where was the HisTag placed, 
if in the correct s3 construct (6kDa) or if in any other of the big proteins expressed. The Dot blot 
was repeated in both <10kDa and >10kDa eluates from the centrifugation and it confirmed that 
the HisTag was only present in the big proteins and not in our interest s3 construct. Regardless of 
this result, a WB was performed to finally confirm or deny s3 expression. After developing the 
WB, it could be observed that construct s3 had not been expressed or correctly purified (Data not 
shown) since no band was observed in its expected size.  
Since ss320 strain had showed expression problems in the past, and after some troubleshooting 
with this strain, it was decided to change the expression system to BL21(DE3) E.coli cells.

5.3.2.1 
Transformation and test of expression in BL21 – s3 construct  
 
The test of expression was conducted as explained in Section 3.6.3. The 10 selected colonies were 
harvested and prior to purification, were tested in WB to test for s3 construct protein production, 
seen in Figure 18. It was observed that out of the 10 colonies tested, 4 of them expressed s3 
construct, two of them, colony 1 (col1) and colony 10 (col10), were chosen for protein expression 
scale up and purification.

Protein expression, purification, and visualization

1 and 10, were purified using the Äkta system following the same elution protocol as Section 
4.3.1.2. Both constructs showed single elution peaks, shown in Figure 19.  
The eluted proteins were buffer exchanged O/N 4ºC with shaking, using dialysis membranes with

WB gels.

production, WB was performed to confirm the presence or absence of s3 construct. WB was

Figure 21. Same as it had happened before with ss320 strain, no s3 HisTag protein was observed.

the phage G3 protein + insert. Therefore, it was decided to test all constructs: s3, m1, m9 and L4 
(ss320 expression), and colonies 1 and 10 (BL21(DE3) expression) in ELISA to check for their 
affinity binding with all mAbs used through this thesis.

5.3.2.3 
ELISA on the purified constructs  
 
Even though not having been able to purify the constructs as expected, it was decided to perform 
an ELISA assay to check for possible affinity binding with the afore used mAbs. The ELISA was 
performed as described in Section 3.6.8. The purified proteins were used to coat the ELISA 
(2µg/mL). Full length PfRAMA was used as positive control and TC80.1 as negative. Either a 
rabbit anti-mouse IgG-HRP mAb or an anti-HisTag IgG-HRP (both 10µg/mL) were used as 
secondary mAb. We presumed that the constructs would have functioning HisTag, therefore being 
the anti-HisTag mAb a positive control for all samples tested.  
The results are shown in Figure 22. PfRAMA showed the highest reactivity for RAM1.25, 
followed by RAM1.89 and RAM1.94, as expected. No reactivity for TC80.1 was detected. 
Moreover, PfRAMA showed having a functional HisTag in its C-term detected by the anti-HisTag 
secondary mAb.  
Constructs m1, m9, L4 and col1 (from construct s3) showed no reactivity towards any of the used 
mAbs nor the anti-HisTag.  
Construct s3, the smallest of all of them, was recognised by the anti-HisTag mAb, as also was 
observed in the Dot blot, but confirmed to be due to the G3 protein which is of non-interest. 
The reactivity observed for col10 (from construct s3) showed that mAb RAM1.25 binds to the s3 
coated protein, nonetheless no HisTag signal was detected.  
The results obtained from all constructs were unexpected since all of them had shown previously 
to have high reactivities in phage ELISA (Figures 8 and 12) assay towards mAbs, especially 
when mAb RAM1.25 was used. Several reasons could be explain the results behind this ELISA 
and will be detailed in the discussion section.

was used as positive control, and TC80.1 mAb as negative control.

6 DISCUSSION 
6.1 CREATION OF THE GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY  
 
For a genomic library to be reliable, it is crucial that the library has a high probability of containing 
a complete copy of the insert of interest, in this case, any of the small genetic fragments digested 
from PfRAMA full-length.  
Some of the performed steps can diminish the probability of having a complete insertion, both 
during gene fragment preparation and while performing the transformation into TG1 cells: 
(i) 
Digestion time. To achieve the gene fragment library different concentrations of 
DNaseI and a concrete reaction time was set for the small and large libraries. 
These considerations worked out and the libraries were achieved but it was 
observed that part of the PCR product used was either non-digested or created 
fragments from sizes that were not interesting for the ongoing experimentation. 
Therefore, it could be suggested that this initial loss of digested product may 
lower the upcoming chances for a fragment of interest to be included in its 
corresponding library.  
(ii) 
Blunt end polishing treatment. When blunt end cloning was performed the aim 
was to obtain DNA fragments with no unpaired bases in their 5’ and 3’ ends to 
clone into linearized plasmids with the same trait. Even being an important step 
to ensure insert:plasmid compatibility, it could lower efficiency. Unlike sticky 
end cloning, here there were no hydrogen bonding between the complementary 
nucleotide overhang to stabilise the formation of insert:plasmid structure. To 
cope with this problem and avoid loss of product of interest, higher volumes of 
ligase, linearized plasmid and insert could be used to certain extent, being careful 
to avoid insert concatenation due to input excess.  
(iii) 
Variability in insertion ratio after transformation. Of the transformed TG1, 
approximately 45% contained plasmids with an insert. A ratio 1:20 
plasmid:insert was used for the transformation. To achieve a higher insertion 
ratio higher percentages could be used, while also considering transformation 
efficacy. A higher ratio could yield higher number of plasmids containing an 
insert, but to a certain extent there would be a risk of plasmids containing multiple 
inserts.  
 
6.2 PANNING THE GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY  
 
The panning against RAM1.25 indicated the enrichment of high affinity phages with increased 
signal throughout the three rounds of panning when tested in a phage ELISA assay (Figures 8 
and 9). Since half of the phages analysed were actually displaying the same peptide fragment 
from PfRAMA, it could be suggested that using the second round of panning, instead of the third 
one, might give a higher variability in the pulled down sequences nonetheless, high affinity 
phages might be missed. It could be a good idea to sequence more gene fragments coming from 
round two and three phages to demonstrate more hits in the same regions where the epitope has 
been identified.  
==PAGE:47==
 
Page 42 
 
The panning against RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 indicated, too, the enrichment of high reactivity 
phages with increased signal throughout the three rounds (Figures 12 and 13). Even though the 
pulled down phages had lower reactivities rather than with RAM1.25. Both of them showed a 
prominent increase in signal for round three eluates, emphasizing using the last round of panning. 
Despite this, the obtained gene fragment sequences after sequencing showed really low insert 
variability (see Section 5.2.2 and 6.3). This could be due to high affinity phages overtaking growth 
during reinfection. To overcome this problem round two phages were sequenced for their inserts 
and the same results were obtained. Probably to obtain more hits in the same regions where the 
epitopes were identified, a different gene fragment library could have been tested to see whether 
longer fragments or higher transformation yields would have a direct impact in the achieved 
results. Moreover, changes in the growth time during reinfection could be done, to minimise the 
possibility of having one only high prevalence phage being produced quicker during the phage 
amplification process.  
The panning against TC80.1 showed no high affinity phages and no increased signal throughout 
panning. Furthermore, when sequencing the inserts obtained after phage display, it was not 
possible to map them against PfRAMA. This was the exact expected outcome since TC80.1 is an 
anti-Trypanosoma cruzi parasite mAb which should have no overlapping sequences with 
PfRAMA. The same was expected when using CyP2.38 as negative control, since this mAb 
targets CyRPA so, the results obtained in the ELISA assays match this premise.  
As mentioned previously, both a small and a large gene fragment library were created but due to 
time constraints, all experimental work was done only with the small library. It would be 
interesting to use the large library in a similar set up to see if there might be a chance of finding 
larger mapping regions with PfRAMA, at the same time showing higher affinity binding towards 
the neutralising mAbs used.  
Concluding this section regarding phage display, we have seen that after the third round of 
panning, high affinity phages are pulled down but as the same time less unique sequences are 
integrated in them. To overcome this issue, the concentration of Tween20 used in the third round 
panning process could be lowered, for example from 0.1% to 0.08% since at that moment non-
specific binders would have been already ruled out, thereby enabling lower affinity interaction 
phage-displayed peptides to be collected thus potentially integrating different gene fragments46.  
 
6.3 SEQUENCING AND ALIGNMENT OF THE GENE FRAGMENT LIBRARY  
 
Sequencing provided the intrinsic information required to map the enriched sequences towards 
PfRAMA, for this reason, before alignment all sequences were checked to have high quality reads. 
Once done, each sequence was trimmed in order to eliminate pHEN6 backbone from the 
alignment, as explained previously. Finally, these trimmed sequences were aligned to PfRAMA 
and the sequences that showed to contain its genetic sequence were selected for further work. 
This process allowed to obtain sequences from all the gene fragment libraries that aligned to 
different regions of PfRAMA with high confidence.  
Alignment of the sequences pulled after down panning with mAb RAM1.25 showed 5 unique 
aligning sequences (and 5 more which were duplicates of them), all in the same 621-669aa area 
of the PfRAMA protein sequence (Figure 10). This fits thoroughly with the hypothesis that this 
neutralising mAb binds to a specific epitope on PfRAMA and that this epitope could be found by 
panning a gene fragment library. If closely observed, the minimal overlapping region is as small 
==PAGE:48==
 
Page 43 
 
as 14bp, showing that by means of this technique a potentially functional small epitope can be 
pulled out and used for further affinity studies.  
Alignment of the sequences retrieved by panning with mAbs RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 showed 2 
unique sequences for RAM1.89 aligned to 722 to 761 aa region, localized closer to the C-term of 
PfRAMA. Meanwhile, the two unique sequences for RAM1.94 to 265 to 292 aa region, placing 
them towards the N-term of PfRAMA (Figure 14). The fact of only having found 2 unique 
sequences aligning to PfRAMA after having screened up to 160 colonies for each mAb, indicates 
that the epitopes found for these regions could be located in a more intricate position to pull out 
and therefore a larger gene fragment library could be beneficial to add reproducibility to these 
results. Hypothetically, if the epitopes cover a broader area, a larger gene fragment library might 
improve the approach; alternatively, if these epitopes are more conformation dependent, 
PfRAMA should then be truncated following its tertiary structure in order to identify the whole 
epitope for each mAb. Nonetheless, the areas where these epitopes were found correlate perfectly 
with previous studies done in our group27 (see Background),  thereby proper functioning of the 
phage display technology for PfRAMA and the mAbs tested was be confirmed.  
6.4 EXPRESSION AND PURIFICATION OF THE CONSTRUCTS  
 
Two different expression systems – both non-amber suppressor E.coli strains, which should not 
allow translation to read through the amber codon (TGA) in order to obtain the proteins of interest 
– were used to try express and purify the constructs expressed by the phages enriched by the 
panning rounds.  
The first protein expression, using ss30 E.coli strain, was performed for 4 constructs (s3, m1,m9 
and L4), with expected sizes of 6-11kDa. SDS-PAGE showed that none of the proteins of interest 
were present in any of the elution lanes, but other bigger proteins had been expressed. The lack 
of expression of the proteins of interest might be caused due to protein malfunction of the amber 
codon, allowing translation through it, thus producing the full G3 protein together with each 
construct fused in its N-term (Figure 16).  Dot blot showed anti-HisTag signal for s3 construct, 
later ruled out as recognition of the s3 construct when a MWCO centrifugation was performed. 
Finally confirmed using WB, which showed that the 4 expected constructs had not been 
expressed. Moreover, it showed the presence of other non-interest bigger proteins with the HisTag 
in its C-term, which could be by-products of the purification process or aforementioned G3 
protein complex.  
Since the first protein expression did not work as expected, a second protein expression was 
performed, this time using BL21(DE3) E.coli strain, and only using the s3 construct due to time 
constraints. The test expression for s3 construct was evaluated in 10 colonies, 4 of them showing 
expression of a correct 6kDa band in WB assay (Figure 18). Colony 1 (col1) and colony 10 
(col10) protein expressions were scaled up to larger volumes and purified as previously done. 
Nevertheless, neither SDS-PAGE, WB or Dot blot were able to detect the presence of this 
construct after protein expression and purification. This was an unexpected result, since 
BL21(DE3) had shown to be able to express s3 construct in the test of expression. Therefore, 
pointing the expression problem to the scale up or the purification.   
Several reasons might be behind the uneven protein expression and purification achieved with 
both expression systems:  
(i) 
It has been speculated that the amber stop codon encoded in the protein’s genetic 
sequence was not activated when using the ss320 strain, therefore translating the 
whole phage G3 protein. For BL21(DE3) strain, the stop codon was active during the 
==PAGE:49==
 
Page 44 
 
test of expression but lacked translation in the scaled-up process, as seen in Figures 
18 and 21. Others have studied the possible suppression of the amber stop codon47, 
which would enable the G3 protein with the constructs to be translated instead of only 
the constructs of interest. 
(ii) 
The purification process yielded a single elution peak, guiding to select which 
fractions of the eluate contained the protein of interest. This could have implied an 
assumption error, assuming that the proteins of interest were located in the elution 
peak and not that they might have been eluted previously. What should not be ruled 
out either is an improper binding of the protein in the nickel column, since 
BL21(DE3) test of expression showed construct s3 expression.  
(iii) 
The protein purification yielded quite impure samples (Figures 16 and 20) thereby 
making a proper quantification impossible. Focusing on Figure 20, this fact led to run 
a WB gel (Figure 21) without knowing each protein concentration in the purified 
sample, thereby loading a volume of sample which might have had underestimated 
the concentration of the protein of interest thus, not enabling us to see it.  
Before moving to the discussion about the ELISA on the constructs, it will be noted that constructs 
s3, m1, m9 and L4 underwent protein expression with the ss320 expression system; and col1 and 
col10, which are two different colonies (or clones) from the s3 construct were expressed using 
the BL21(DE3) expression system.  
The results from the ELISA on the protein constructs confirm that full length PfRAMA has high 
reactivity for RAM1.25 and decreased for RAM1.89 and RAM1.94. Also, it integrates a 
functional HisTag.  
Constructs m1, m9, L4 and col1 show no reactivity at all against any mAb. This is the opposite 
result obtained previously with phage ELISA, therefore confirming the purification problems 
mentioned. 
Anti-HisTag antibody showed high reactivity towards construct s3, also observed in the Dot blot, 
but confirmed to be part of a bigger non-interest protein, as explained in Section 5.3.1.2.  
Interestingly, col10 showed high reactivity for RAM1.25 but no HisTag signal (Figure 22). This 
is an intriguing feature from the protein produced by this specific clone, since RAM1.25 showed 
reactivity towards the protein but not the anti-HisTag antibody towards HisTag. Even though the 
HisTag was being used as positive control for this assay, this could be a situation where the 
HisTag is placed hidden among the protein and scrutinized by performing ELISA before 
purification. 
Further work can be done to improve the protein expression and purification. The expressed and 
purified constructs could be sent for genomic or mass spectrometry sequencing to check for the 
presence of the HisTag and the amber stop codon thereby revealing which step changes the 
constructs’ configuration. One would expect construct 3 - 6kDa protein (Figure 18) to be 
produced due to the amber stop codon, and the 55kDa protein to be the G3 protein + a PfRAMA 
derived construct. Further, a size exclusion chromatography could be performed on the purified 
protein col10 since it had shown s3 construct expression (Figure 18). This would help to separate 
supposedly full-length G3 protein from the smaller protein produced due to the amber stop codon. 
This could in effect be a more precise way of performing size exclusion compared to use a MWCO 
spin column.   
Overall, the results obtained from the ELISA on the purified constructs might be due to protein 
expression and purification issues. Regardless of this fact, phage ELISA provided evidence that 
PfRAMA derived epitopes have been identified therefore leaving the purification step as the only 
challenge to overcome in this thesis.

6.5 OVERALL ASSESSMENT  
 
Thanks to previous work done in our group, different mAbs towards PfRAMA were used in this 
approach to identify its specific epitope regions. RAM1.25 was known to have the highest 
neutralising capacity over RAM1.89 and RAM1.94, and its protective epitope was theorised to 
be located in the C-term. The idea of looking for the epitopes for RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 came 
out of the fact that they were predicted to bind to different epitopes, based on WB data of 
merozoite extracts collected in our group. Knowing this, the epitopes of these mAbs were aimed 
to be found.  
As discussed, using a gene fragment library panned against the PfRAMA mAbs, three different 
epitopes derived from three different mAbs were identified. After sequencing and alignment, it 
was demonstrated that the theorised locations of the epitopes were actually correct, and that all 
three mAbs had different binding regions in the PfRAMA genome. For mAb RAM1.25, a 14bp 
overlapping sequence between all sequences retrieved has been show, being a really small epitope 
that might elicit an immunogenic response triggering the formation of  neutralising antibodies in 
the human body when displayed as part of a multiepitope vaccine. PfRAMA plays a key role in 
rhoptry biogenesis and host cell invasion, therefore a combination of this epitope together with 
other epitopes coming from proteins that target different steps of the merozoite invasion could 
elicit the immune response required to stop the spread of malaria.  
Before arriving to this situation, even having a promising epitope candidate from PfRAMA, its 
expression and purification need to be done into a different expression vector. The amber stop 
codon promised to be an easy way to check for proper protein expression but, even after 
troubleshooting, the final results show inefficacy to comply with the premise. Another way to 
overcome the problems encountered would be to design primers for each construct, make a PCR 
amplification, digestion, and ligation into another vector. Knowing the small sizes of the 
constructs, they could be expressed together with another protein, such as the SpyCatcher, to ease 
the purification and further visualisation in SDS-PAGE gels. All this, to confirm that the 
constructs are able to recognise RAM1.25 only by themselves and not because the whole g3 
protein.  
The approaches aforementioned during the discussion and in this section, can be used to improve 
all constructs performance with the end goal to create fully functional epitopes cloned into a 
SpyCatcher/SpyTag system48 that enables the peptides to be expressed on a and eventually be 
used in the future multiepitope vaccine.  
 
This thesis has shown the identification of an epitope from the neutralising mAb RAM1.25 on the 
malaria antigen PfRAMA. Similar prior work had been done in our group identifying epitopes 
from CyRPA, and ongoing work will render further results with other proteins, such as RH5. The 
unifying idea behind this is to include protective epitopes from these proteins into a multivalent 
vaccine, hoping to achieve a focused immune response only producing neutralising antibodies 
that act in a synergistic manner upon vaccination28,27.

7 CONCLUSION 
 
The initial hypothesis of this thesis was to identify the epitope of the malaria neutralising mAb 
RAM1.25 on the malaria antigen PfRAMA. This was meant to be done by creating a gene 
fragment library, using phage display and further sequencing, expressing, and purifying the 
peptide encompassing the epitope. It can be concluded that an epitope for the neutralising mAb 
RAM1.25 has been identified in the 621-669aa region of PfRAMA using the mentioned 
techniques.  
Small and large gene fragment libraries were successfully created, even though all 
experimentation was done only with the small library. The small gene fragment library was 
transformed into TG1 E.coli strain, yielding a 35% insert ratio, being then ready to be panned 
against neutralising mAbs of interest. 
The neutralising mAb, RAM1.25 and non-neutralising mAbs RAM1.89 and RAM1.94 were used 
for panning against the gene fragment library. Epitopes for the three mAbs were found in different 
regions of the PfRAMA gene, accurately placing them in 621-669aa, 722-761aa, and 262-292aa 
regions of PfRAMA, respectively. These finding were supported by previous data generated in 
our group when creating the mAbs towards PfRAMA. Moreover, phage ELISA data confirmed 
these epitopes to have high reactivity for its specific mAb, being RAM1.25 the one showing the 
highest reactivity.  
Four of the clones retrieved after panning on RAM1.25 with inserts mapping to PfRAMA were 
used for protein expression and purification, which were unfortunately not successfully achieved. 
Two different protein expression systems were tested, and promising results were obtained with 
the second one used, BL21(DE3), but further improvements need to be done in order to obtain 
purified constructs after the panning.  
Overall, it can be concluded that an epitope has been mapped in the C-term of PfRAMA and that 
it showed high reactivity towards the neutralising mAb RAM1.25. Moreover, two other epitopes 
from the non-neutralising mAbs RAM1.89 and RAM1.94, have been also mapped in different 
parts of PfRAMA. To obtain a fully functional construct several improvements have been 
discussed. With the ultimate goal of using a combination of a PfRAMA epitope with others from 
proteins such CyRPA and RH5 in a multivalent vaccine.
