Abstract 
The core of this project is to set up a Matlab program for designing reinforced concrete structures by using 
topology optimization. The resulting program is easy to use for anyone with a general understanding of 
Matlab coding. The program is able to produce reinforcement layouts, which after a reasonable amount of 
post-processing could be useful in real-life design of reinforced concrete. 
The program is based on the 99-line topology optimization Matlab code developed by Sigmund (2001). The 
biggest change lies in the finite element analysis, where modified versions of constitutive matrices from 
Bendsøe (1989), Darwin and Pecknold (1977), and Cai (2011) are used in order to model the material 
behaviour of the concrete and steel, both individually and when combined. These material models are 
tested and validated in the validation section and finally used in the program to get reinforcement layout 
for a beam, a column with a cantilevered part, and a wall with a window hole. 
The results from these structures show that the program runs mostly as expected but it is obvious, that 
some modifications will be needed upon further development.

1 Introduction 
Concrete has been around for millennia, and we constantly learn new 
things about it and try to improve it by adding new and innovative 
components to it, like waste products from other processes. Usually 
concrete is very brittle, and breaks easily when subjected to tension. 
In the earliest days of concrete structures, this was prevented by 
building in arches and domes, which was already known from brick 
structures. In the nineteenth century, a new invention was made by 
incorporating steel into the concrete in order to strengthen the 
tensile properties of the concrete, and reinforced concrete was 
discovered. The main objective of the steel is to take over when the 
concrete cracks in order to prevent instantaneous failure. Instead the 
steel provides a warning period when the concrete shows visible 
cracks by holding it together, providing a semi-ductile, warned 
failure. 
Modern reinforced concrete is usually created by suspending a steel reinforcement grid inside a mould, 
pouring the concrete on top of it and allowing it to set. This allows for a strong bond between the concrete 
and steel reinforcement, but this also makes one of the 
main problems with reinforced concrete obvious. When 
the steel reinforcements are subjected to water, it 
corrodes; the steel that was previously bonded to the 
concrete sheds off the reinforcement bar as rust and the 
connection is lost. This is even worse for road bridges 
where there is plenty of salt and other materials to speed 
up this process. Thus, reinforced concrete becomes a 
matter of placing the steel in the outer edges of the 
concrete where the high tension zones usually are, but not so far that outside water can easily permeate 
the concrete and reach the reinforcement steel. 
Usually the reinforcement used is a continuous grid in the entire concrete as seen in figure 1.2. This brings 
up the question of whether this is actually the most optimal way of doing it and if a lot of material could be 
saved by having a method for estimating where reinforcement is needed and where it is not. 
Topology Optimization (TopOpt) replaces the conventional trial and error approach to structural design. 
Although TopOpt in itself produces results that can be difficult to directly apply in reality, the recent 
research into post-processing the results, and the growth of additive manufacturing makes it easier than 
ever to use the results in real products. 
1.1 Literature study 
Research into topology optimization of structural elements and even entire structures has been performed 
for decades, and the idea of making an optimal structure has been researched for even longer. However, 
the methods available for doing so are at the moment only available to specialists with a significant amount 
of background knowledge, while the rest are forced to use hand calculation to even verify an un-optimized 
structure (Andersen, 2017).

One of the earliest approaches to structural topology optimization is found in Bendsøe (1989), which 
describes how to establish an orthotropic material law and implement voids in the material. Later studies 
have shown promising results for implementing different properties in tension and compression (Cai, 2011) 
and self-weight (Bruyneel and Duysinx, 2005), the last of which was forced to use alternative optimization 
methods in order to account for the lack of monotony of the convergence. Real-life applications of topology 
optimization has been investigated by Brishgella et al. (2013), which uses it to optimize the bottom plate of 
a steel box-girder bridge. 
For many years, it has been attempted to create a topology optimization method for reinforced concrete. 
Especially Gaynor, Guest, and Moen, (2013) captures this very well by optimizing a structure consisting of a 
concrete continuum overlaid with a steel truss layer. This method however is limited in the fact that the 
truss system has a limited amount of connectivity as defined by the user. 
Reinforced concrete continuum topology optimization models have also been a crowded topic, with 
Bogomolny and Amir (2012) introducing elasto-plastic materials and Bogomolny and Amir (2011) 
introducing a Drucker-Pager yield criterion and stress constraints in the optimization. 
When going a bit outside the confines of this report, Luo, Wang and Deng (2013) incorporates prestressing 
into the steel, which could easily be an interesting direction for the future of this project. 
1.2 Scope 
The scope of this report is to investigate whether it is possible to develop a continuum model for reinforced 
concrete and create a program that uses topology optimization to determine the optimal reinforcement 
layout. The program is developed in Matlab and is based on finite element fundamentals and the topology 
optimization is an expansion on the 99-line topology optimization code developed by Sigmund (2001). The 
materials are modelled as close to reality as possible, including isotropy and orthotropy and strength bi-
linearity, but excluding elasto-plastic behaviour and self-weight. 
1.3 Project formulation 
The objective of the project is to develop a Matlab program that can optimize the placement of 
reinforcement material in concrete structures. The program has to comply with the following in order to be 
considered successful: 
It should: 
 
follow the laws  of structural mechanics 
 
be able to model any ordinary structural element in 2D 
 
be possible to use different material parameters in the calculations 
 
be able to generate results which can be useful in designing reinforced concrete structures 
 
be easy to use 
 
be applicable in an eventual design process

2 Background knowledge 
This section presents the materials used in the simulations, the prerequisites for the laminate topology 
optimization program and introduces two different material models used to model the laminate structure. 
2.1 Materials 
Concrete is a composite material consisting of cement, water and various aggregates. Upon mixing, the 
concrete acts as a viscous slurry, making it possible to shape and mould it. Upon setting, the water and 
cement react chemically forming a very strong bond, which locks the aggregates in position. After 28 days, 
it is generally agreed upon that the concrete has reached a sufficient, reliable strength, but it continues to 
harden for as long as small pockets of cement are present inside the concrete and water is applied. 
Due to the hardened matrix of stone-like material of cured concrete, it has a very high compressive 
strength; typically, in the range of 30-40 MPa after 28 days, but it is very brittle and cracks at around 1/10 
of the compressive strength when put in tension. 
Despite the uneven distribution of aggregates, concrete is generally considered an isotropic material until 
cracking. When cracks appear, the cracked concrete can be considered orthotropic, since it has no further 
stiffness transverse to the crack direction, but still has its full strength in the parallel direction. Usually the 
shear strength is also kept intact due to interlocking of the aggregates. 
In order to prevent complete failure upon cracking, a grid of reinforcement steel is inserted into the 
concrete upon casting. This creates reinforced concrete (RC), which utilizes the strengths of both materials. 
The concrete is easily moulded to a multitude of shapes for different structural purposes and can protect 
the steel from water, while the steel keeps the concrete from breaking instantly when cracks appear and 
allows for a warning period when the concrete has cracked, making it possible to repair or evacuate in time. 
2.2 Finite element method 
The finite element method (FEM) or finite element analysis (FEA) is a numerical method, which is used to 
solve complex problems consisting of partial differential equations (PDE) and boundary conditions (BC). 
FEM is usually used in structural mechanics to determine the deflection field and internal stresses of a 
structure subjected to a load. The domain is discretized into a finite amount of elements, hence the name 
finite element method, in order to solve a simplified version of the problem for each element and then link 
them together into a mesh. The structure is defined by nodes, connected in ways that define the elements. 
The displacements are only calculated for the nodes and loading can only be applied to the nodes, while 
stiffness, stresses and strains are interpolated to approximate the state inside each element. 
There are many different types of elements, each with advantages and disadvantages. In this report the 
arguably simplest type of element is used, a 2D quadrilateral 4-node element (Q4) as seen in figure 2.1. This 
element consists of four nodes connected in a square with two degrees of freedom each and allows for 
very fast and easy meshing and calculation of square structures and structures that can be subdivided into 
squares with approximately equal side lengths. The stress of each element can be calculated most 
accurately in the centroid due to the simplicity of the elements.

2.3 Topology optimization 
TopOpt is the process of updating a set of design variables inside a design domain with associated 
boundary conditions in order to reach the optimal design for a given criteria. TopOpt in structural 
mechanics usually seeks to minimize the deflection due to a constant load by changing design variables 
linked to the density of elements inside the design domain. In theory it should be possible to achieve a fully 
optimized structure with any boundary conditions with a given volume fraction of the design domain filled. 
The minimization of the displacement is achieved through the minimization of the compliance, c, which is 
calculated from the displacements, u, stiffness matrix, k, and design variable, x, of each element. 
In the optimization scheme, a penalization factor, p, is introduced to the stiffness as suggested by Bendsøe 
(1989); it is included in all stiffnesses that are dependent on the design variable in the calculation of 
stiffness matrices and stresses.

strength. Though the TopOpt method used in this project does not consider material strengths, this can

3 The program setup 
The program is based on the 99-line topology optimization Matlab code developed by Ole Sigmund (2001). 
It has been modified in various ways in order to suit the current problem. The filter has been changed from 
a sensitivity filter to a density filter (27) and (28), and the convolution operator has been set up as a sparse 
matrix to save time. The optimization method has been changed from the original Optimality Criteria (OC) 
to MMA (Svanberg 1987) by using the two functions mmasub.m and subsolve.m developed by Krister 
Svanberg. Voids are parts of the circumscribed rectangle of the design domain that is not a part of the 
actual design domain, thus they should have low stiffness and not be included in the design update. Voids 
in the structure are introduced in the model by setting the design variable to zero, excluding it from the 
optimization scheme and reducing the concrete stiffness tensor in all void elements to a very low value. 
This leaves a small stiffness in all elements and no problems should occur. The structural mechanics 
problem is solved by using the expanded FEM method as described in the theory section including stress 
calculations and reduced stiffnesses. 
The program has three inputs: loads, supports, and void, along with a global variable structure, var, 
containing the variables shown in table 3.1. The input, loads is a vector containing the load value for each 
degree of freedom (dof), including zero-loadings, supports is a vector containing the supported dof 
numbers and void is a vector containing the void element numbers. Not all inputs and variables are used in 
the test cases.

6 Discussion 
The program generally worked as expected, but some parts of it need some extra work and other parts 
some changes in order to get more clear-cut results. 
The two material models for the concrete showed great promise in the single element test cases, where 
the physical properties of the material were clearly validated. By adding a steel layer, the element was 
strengthened, and the strengthening coincided with the orthotropy and bi-linearity of the steel. An angle 
optimization scheme was added, which oriented the steel towards the principal stress angle of the entire 
composite. It was discovered that firstly, the steel would not always be oriented in the optimal direction 
due to the steel having more strength in the parallel direction than in the transverse, even in tension, and 
secondly that in certain cases it would not converge to a single angle. It is difficult to tell why the angle 
would not converge and since most of the test cases were very similar to each other, the most likely cause 
is numerical inaccuracies. 
Discretization had a very limited effect on the results as long as the physical situation could be captured. 
This is very good for the usability of the program, since every time the discretization is increased the 
problem will take longer to analyse. The low necessity for discretization thus allows for a very quick 
program for analysing RC structures. 
The compliance developed in a very smooth monotonic way during the optimization procedure, which 
signifies a working optimization scheme. The lack of erratic behaviour of the compliance means that the 
FEM loop was working as intended in terms of not oscillating angles as it was seen in the angle optimization 
validation step. After many iterations the compliance would occasionally increase for a period before the 
program finished. This is partly because of the very simple tolerance statement for the TopOpt scheme, 
which should be looked into and improved if a truly optimal structure is desired. 
The steel was mainly concentrated in big lumps in tension zones, which was not expected, as generally 
reinforcement steel is applied in layers of bars or grids covering a thin, but long part of the concrete. The 
fact that it put steel only in tension zones fits well both with the expectations of the model and the general 
assumption of concrete in compression and steel in tension. 
The program was allowed to use slightly more material than predefined if it was positioned near a void 
zone. This will certainly have to be corrected if the program is used in the future, although it seemed to 
only affect the result in a miniscule way. 
Most of the steel was positioned at the edges of the structure, as previously mentioned this is very 
unfavourable, since the steel rusts upon contact with water, of which there is usually a lot on the surface of 
concrete structures. This means that the results are not directly applicable in a real design situation, but it 
could be corrected by adding an “edge zone” where the concrete is present, but the steel acts as if it was a 
void upon updating the design variables. 
Due to the formulation of the problem, the resulting design has the limitation that it does not tell whether 
it is structurally admissible, only that it is the best structure with the given volume of steel. This is a 
problem if no previous calculations has been made for the structure. The program can still work to 
redistribute the reinforcement after an initial design has been made by hand.

7 Conclusion 
As a background for the Finite Element Method used in this project were three models of material stiffness; 
an orthotropic model for the steel based on a layered model, an orthotropic model for the concrete, and an 
isotropic model for the concrete. 
Nine single-element test cases with uni- and biaxial push and pull and shear was set up. These was used to 
validate the concrete models and the interaction between concrete and steel. The tests of the concrete 
models showed compliance with the constitutive law and the only difference between the two models 
were seen when the principal stresses had non-zero values with different signs, where the orthotropic 
model showed more deflection in the tensile direction, but lower deflection in shear.  
The effect of the steel layer at different angles was tested by overlaying it on the element with concrete. 
The results were generally as expected; the steel had no effect when oriented transverse to the loading in 
the uniaxial cases, and had only a very little effect in the compression cases and a large effect in the tension 
cases. For the orthotropic concrete model, discontinuities were observed in the uniaxial cases which was 
explained by the modularity of this model, which can change from high to low stiffness with only a minor 
change in the principal stresses. 
A method for determining the optimal steel orientation based on the principal angle of the combined 
composite was tested. The test showed an instant convergence to the expected angle in most cases. For 
some cases though the angle would begin to oscillate uncontrollably after a certain number of iterations, 
which could best be described as numerical errors since similar cases did not show similar behaviour. 
From this point on it was determined that only the isotropic concrete model was suitable, and this model 
was used in a Topology Optimization scheme. Three problem settings, an MBB beam, a cantilevered 
structure and a wall with a window, were used in order to test whether the model worked as expected in 
full-scale. The results showed a generally monotonous downwards development of the compliance 
throughout the updating and the material was distributed in clear compression and tension zones, where 
the tension zones were larger because of the bi-linearity of the concrete. Furthermore, a large dependency 
on the discretization was observed. This was because a too rough structure would use corner-connected 
elements because it could not generate continuous angled element with the given mesh, for a finer mesh 
this was clearly not a problem. It was discovered that the optimization was somehow allowed to use more 
material than specified if it positioned it along the voids in the design domain, although this violation was 
observed to be very small and getting even smaller with a finer discretization. 
Finally, the program was used to calculate the optimal position of reinforcement in the three structures. 
This, as with the pure concrete test, showed a downwards trending monotonous development of the 
compliance. The steel reinforcement was positioned in clearly defined tension zones in the structure as 
expected and, unlike the full-scale concrete test, showed no dependency on the discretization. A lot of the 
steel reinforcement was positioned at the surface of the structure, which is not very good, considering the 
corrosion in the steel when subjected to outside water. 
In general it was verified, that the models abide by the constitutive law. The program can be used to model 
any 2D structure that can be subdivided into a square mesh, and it is very simple to use any Young’s 
modulus and Poisson’s ratio for both concrete and steel. The program does produce results, which can be 
used as inspiration for innovative reinforced concrete design, but the fact that the steel is placed at the 
surface layer means that it cannot be directly applied at the current stage. The program is very easy to use 
for anyone with just a little experience in Matlab coding and could be used in a real-life design process, 
==PAGE:38==
33 
 
though the actual strength of the optimized structure would have to be checked afterwards since no stress 
constraints have been included. 
7.1 Future work 
Using the previous literature described in this report as a basis, it should be possible to incorporate 
prestressing (Luo, Wang, and Deng, 2013) and self-weight (Bruyneel and Duysinx 2005). Additionally 
introducing stress restraints could be interesting to determine a structurally admissible design rather than 
the “best with what we’ve got” approach. Of course, it would also be very interesting to expand the 
problem into 3D.

function [c,loop]=ortho_c(load,supports) 
global var 
  
% FEEDBACK 
disp('Running program...') 
if isempty(load); error('No loads defined'); end 
if isempty(supports); error('No supports defined'); end 
  
% INITIALIZE 
x(1:var.nel,1) = 0; 
the_s=x*0; 
the_c=the_s; 
loop = 0; 
c=0; 
  
% START ITERATION 
loop = loop + 1; 
  
% FE-ANALYSIS 
Udif=1; 
FE=0; 
U=zeros(var.ndof,1); 
sig1=zeros(var.nel,1); 
sig2=sig1; 
sig_s=sig1; 
% FE ITERATION 
while Udif>1e-3 && FE<100 
    FE=FE+1; 
    U_old=U; 
    [the_c,sig1,sig2]=principal(U,sig1,sig2,the_c); 
    [U]=FEM(load,supports,x,the_s,the_c,sig_s,sig1,sig2); 
    Udif=(sum(abs(U_old(:)-U(:)))/var.ndof)/max(abs(U(:))); 
end 
end

    end

function [U]=FEM(load,supports,x,the_s,sig_s,R)

        [Cs,~]=Cs_mat(x(el),the_s(el),sig_s(el));

%% --------------- objective ---------------------------------------------- 
function [c,dcdgam]=objective(x,the_s,sig_s,U,R) 
global var

    for ely = 1:var.nely 
        el=el+1; 
        Cc=Cc_mat; 
        Kc=Kmat(Cc); 
        Kc=R(el)*Kc;

        Ks=Kmat(Cs); 
        dK=Kmat(dC);

        n2 = var.nnoy* elx   +ely; 
        dcdgam(ely,elx)=0;
