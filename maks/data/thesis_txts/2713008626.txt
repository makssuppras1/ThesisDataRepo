M.SC. THESIS
Abstract
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most applied yeast in industrial settings, however the non-Saccharo-
myces yeast, Wickerhamomyces anomalus, has shown promising results to be used in the industry
due to its characteristics. W. anomalus can survive and flourish in unfavourable environments by
producing various of products such as enzymes, aromas and killer toxins. These products and
the yeast itself can be used in several applications in food and beverages, as biocontrol and in
immum therapy but overall this require a deeper knowledge about the physiology of W.anomalus.
In order to characterise the fermentation physiology of W. anomalus, different parameters have
been investigated in the current work in batch fermentations under aerobic conditions in classic
yeast extract peptone dextrose (1xYPD) medium as basic medium. The highest maximum spe-
cific growth rate µmax of 0.590 ± 0.0186 h−1 with a biomass yield YSX of 0.289 ± 0.0034 gDW/gglc
is observed in 2xYPD medium at 30 °C, pH=6, stirring at 500 rpm (round per minutes) , and
aeration at 1 vvm (volume air/ volume broth / minutes) . Under these conditions, W. anomalus
produce 9-fold more ethanol and 2-fold more acetic acid and glycerol compared to the fermenta-
tion in 1xYPD medium under same conditions. This is also reflected in the biomass yields since
W. anomalus in 1xYPD reach a biomass yield YSX at 0.3296 ± 0.170 gDW/gglc with a similar maxi-
mum specific growth rate µmax at 0.5606 ± 0.0089 h−1.
Fermentations in 1xYPD with dissolved oxygen(DO)-tensions at 20, 50, 80 and 100 ± 5 % were
conducted to investigate the crabtree effect of W. anomalus. These fermentations determine that W.
anomalus is crabtree negative since no production of ethanol is observed in the fermentations with
DO at 100 ± 5 %. Furthermore, it is observed that the production of ethanol increases when the
DO-tension decreases and this indicates that W.anomalus shifts from the respiratory metabolism
to fermentation when the availability of DO decreases. This study also illustrates that W. anomalus
is quite tolerant against ethanol since the organism has demonstrated to grow up to an ethanol
concentration of 65 g/L. However under stress from ethanol, the maximum specific growth rates
µmax are inhibited by 2.2-4.6 times compared to the baseline, the fermentation in 1xYPD. Fur-
thermore, this investigation demonstrates that W. anomalus can tolerate even higher ethanol titers
than 65 g/L since the cells did not perish when they were affected by higher concentrations than
65 g/L.
Furthermore, W. anomalus can grow at pH=3 and pH=9 but the maximum specific growth rates
µmax were inhibited by 2-fold compared to the baseline, the fermentation at pH=6 in 1xYPD. The
cells at pH=9 are potentially more stressed compared to the cells in the fermentation at pH=3
due to the lower observed biomass yield and production of ethanol and acetic acid in the fer-
mentations at pH=9. Finally, two unidentified compounds are observed in the fermentations,
which potentially could be interesting metabolites such as aromas or killer toxins. But overall,
these results demonstrate that W. anomalus is a crabtree negative yeast which has the ability to
grow in elevated ethanol concentrations and at extreme pH. However, further investigation of
the fermentation physiology with the focus on the particular application is needed.

Resum´e
Saccharomyces cerevisiae er den mest anvendte gær i industrien dog har non-saccharomyces gæren,
W. anomalus, vist lovende resultater i forhold til at blive anvendt i industrien grundet dens egensk-
aber. W. anomalus kan overleve og trives i ugunstige miljøer ved at producere forskellige produk-
ter s˚asom enzymer, aroma og dræbende toksiner. Disse produkter og gæren selv kan anvendes i
fødevarer og drikkevarer, som biokonservering og i immunterapi, men dette vil kræve en større
viden omkring fermenteringsfysiologien af W. anomalus.
Forskellige parametre blev undersøgt i batchfermenteringer under aerobe forhold i et klassisk
yeast extract peptone dextrose(1xYPD) medie som basismedie for at karakterisere fermenterings-
fysiologien af W. anomalus yderligere i dette projekt. Den højeste maksimale specifikke vækstrate
µmax p˚a 0.590 ± 0.0186 h−1 med et biomasseudbytte YSX p˚a 0.289 ± 0.0034 gDW/gglc er observeret
i 2xYPD medie ved 30 °C, pH=6, omrøring p˚a 500 rpm og luftstrømning p˚a 1 vvm. Under disse
betingelser producer W. anomalus 9 gange mere ethanol og 2 gange mere eddikesyre og glycerol
sammenlignet med fermenteringerne udført i 1xYPD medie under samme betingelser. Dette af-
spejles ogs˚a i biomasseudbyttet, da W. anomalus i 1xYPD medie n˚aede en YSX p˚a 0.3296 ± 0.170
gDW/gglc med en tilsvarende µ p˚a 0.5606 ± 0.0089 h−1.
Fermenteringer i 1xYPD medie med følgende konstante disolved oxygen(DO)-værdier: 20, 50, 80
og 100 ± 5 % blev udført for at undersøge crabtree-effekten af W.anomalus. Her kan det konklud-
eres, at W. anomalus er crabtree negativ, da cellerne ikke producer ethanol i fermenteringerne med
DO p˚a 100 %. Ydermere er der observeret, at produktionen af ethanol stiger, n˚ar tilgængeligheden
af DO falder, hvilket antyder at W. anomalus skifter fra respiration til fermentering, n˚ar tilgænge-
ligheden af DO mindskes. Dette projekt viser ogs˚a, at W. anomalus er tolerant overfor høj koncen-
tration af ethanol siden organismen vokser i medier med en ethanol koncentration p˚a op til 65
g/L. Dog bliver the maksimale specifikke vækst rate hæmmet 2.2-4.6 gange under ethanol stress
sammenlignet med fermenteringen med 1xYPD-mediet. Desuden viste denne undersøgelse, at
W. anomalus kan tolerere endnu højere ethanolkoncentrationer end 65 g/L, da cellerne ikke døde
da de blev p˚avirket af højere koncentrationer end 65 g/L.
Derudover viser dette projekt ogs˚a, at W. anomalus kan vokse ved pH=3 og pH=9, dog blev
den maksimale specifikke vækst rate hæmmet dobbelte sammenlignet med fermenteringen ved
pH=6. Cellerne ved pH=9 er højst sandsynligt mere stresset end cellerne ved pH=3 p˚a grund
af det lavere biomasseudbytte, og produktionen af ethanol og eddikesyrer i fermenteringerne
ved pH=9. Sidst men ikke mindst blev der observeret to uopdagede produkter i de kørte fer-
menteringer som kunne være aroma eller dræbende toksiner. Men samlet set viser resultaterne
fra dette projekt, at W.anomalus er en crabtree negativ gær, som har evnen til at vokse i omgivelser
med forhøjede ethanol koncentration og i medie med lav og høj pH-værdi. Der er dog behov for
yderligere undersøgelse af fermenteringsfysiologien med fokus p˚a den specifikke anvendelse.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most well-studied yeast and used most frequently for industrial
settings, particularly in the production of alcoholic beverages (Parapouli et al., 2020). But the
non-Saccharomyces yeast Wickerhamomyces anomalus has demonstrated promising results in rela-
tion to being used in the industry due to its characteristics where W. anomalus has the ability
to survive unfavourable conditions and to flourish in stressful environments. W. anomalus can be
used in food and beverages, especially in wine production since it has the ability to produce fruity
aromas and acetate esters such as ethyl acetate Padilla et al. (2018). Furthermore, W. anomalus can
produce several killer toxins against both eukaryotic and prokaryotic organisms by competing
against unwanted organisms in wine production. The toxins also benefit the use of W. anomalus
as a biocontrol yeast Walker (2011). Even though W. anomalus has a qualified presumption of
safety (QPS) status from European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and classified as biosafety level
1 organism, further characterisation of W. anomalus is needed to understand the organism and
predict the growth for the use in different applications (Melin et al., 2007).
The fermentation physiology of W. anomalus has been investigated in different media such as com-
plex medium, defined medium and sidestreams medium such as apple pulp. From these studies,
the specific growth rates differ in range from 0.22 to 0.47 h−1 in aerobic conditions where the spe-
cific growth rates differ in range from 0.056 to 0.44 h−1 under anaerobic conditions but overall the
literature regarding the organism is limited (Fredlund et al., 2002; Lind, 2022; Mortensen, 2022).
The literature report different statements regarding the ethanol tolerance and the crabtree effect.
In the study (Fredlund et al., 2004), it is stated that W. anomalus is a crabtree negative yeast where
the former Master’s theses by Lind (2022) and Mortensen (2022) state that W. anomalus is a crabtree
positive yeast but it is also suggested that the yeast could be a crabtree negative yeast since the
airflow used was low and therefore the conditions was not properly aerobic (Fredlund et al., 2002;
Lind, 2022; Mortensen, 2022). The ethanol tolerance by W. anomalus differ from 3-4 %(v/v), 10-12
%(v/v) to 70-80 g/L in the literature (Lind, 2022; Walker, 2011). Due to conflicts in the reporting
of different parameters and characteristics for W. anomalus, the fermentation physiology of W.
anomalus will be investigated in this study in relation to the mentioned parameters and knowledge
regarding W. anomalus ability to grow in unfavourable conditions.

3.1
Yeast
Yeasts are unicellular eukaryotic organisms which are widespread in nature e.g. in aerial and
aquatic environments, plants, food and beverages (Rodrigues et al., 2006). Yeasts belong to the
fungus kingdom together with molds and mushrooms but yeast differ from molds and mush-
rooms since molds and mushrooms are multicellular organims. Yeasts reproduce by the sexual
cycle through meiosis or by the asexual cycle by budding cell division. The most common yeast
S. cerevisiae is a budding yeast which reproduced by budding depending on the conditions oth-
erwise meiosis occur and S. cerevisiae will produce ascospores and start to germinate (Madigan
et al., 2015). This is the same case for W. anomalus (Satora et al., 2014). The reproduction depends
on the environment and based on the cycle of reproduction, the morphology of yeast change.
Yeasts are chemoorganotrophs and heterotrophs which means they use organic chemicals to build
up biomass and to generate energy, ATP (Madigan et al., 2015). Yeasts can be classified in rela-
tion to the way they generate energy. Yeasts can either be characterised as a non-, obligate or
facultative-fermentative yeast. The non-fermentative yeasts are only able to generate energy by
respiration and not able to do fermentation, where the obligate-fermentative yeasts are only able
to metabolise the glucose through fermentation. The facultative-fermentative yeasts are able to
metabolise glucose through respiration and fermentation. The choice of which metabolism to
follow for the facultative-yeasts depend on the growth conditions, the type and concentration
of sugars and especially the availability of oxygen. Most yeasts are classified as an facultative-
fermentative yeast, and thereby has the opportunity to grow respiratory or by fermentation. This
is the case for S. serevisiae and W. anomalus (Rodrigues et al., 2006).
The carbon and energy metabolisms are closely interconnected for the yeasts which means that
the anabolism is coupled with the catabolism (Rodrigues et al., 2006). The metabolic pathway
for the central carbon metabolism is fundamentally identical among different yeast species, how-
ever the mechanisms for nutrient uptake and regulation of fermentation and respiration differ
appreciably. The central carbon metabolism will be presented below with the focus on glucose as
carbon source and S. cerevisaie is used as reference strain for the explanation since this yeast is the
most known according to metabolisms (Rodrigues et al., 2006). Furthermore, the flux distribution
in the central metabolisms for S. cerevisae and W. anoamlus is similar, it can be assumed that W.
anomalus follow the same metabolism (Fredlund et al., 2004). The central carbon metabolism for
respiration and fermentation starts with the glycolytic pathway where the glucose is broken down
(Madigan et al., 2015). When yeasts start to break down the glucose in the glycolytic pathway, ATP
is needed. ATP can be synthesised by substrate-level-phosphorylation in the fermentation or by
substrate-level-phosphorylation and oxidative-phosphorylation in respiration. A larger amount
of ATP is synthesised in the respiration than by fermentation, and therefore the yeasts will prefer
to do respiration if oxygen is available. But if the yeasts get limited by oxygen, the yeasts will do
fermentation since this process can supply enough ATP to thrive the growth of the yeasts (Berry

3.1.2
Pentose phosphate pathway
The PPP converts pentoses from hexoses, such as glucose, by removing one carbon. Glucose-6-P is
oxidised to CO2, NADPH and ribulose-5-P which can be converted to Ribose-5-P. This compound
can be synthesised to ribonucleotides used in RNA and deoxyribonucleotides used in DNA. Fur-
thermore, PPP is also responsible for producing nonpentose sugar in the cells. Finally, the PPP
generates a major amount of NADH which is a coenzyme used in many reductive reactions in the
cells. The PPP can both occur under anaerobic and aerobic conditions (Madigan et al., 2015).
3.1.3
Fermentation
After the glycolysis, the fermentation occurs if there are anaerobic conditions. The fermentation
occurs in the cytoplasm where pyruvate is decarboxylated to acetaldehyde and CO2 by pyruvate
decarboxylase using a coenzyme. Acetaldehyde is then reduced to ethanol by NADH which is
catalysed by alcohol dehydrogenase. This reaction regenerate NAD+ which can be used in the
glycolysis in the conversion of glyceraldehyde-3-P to 1,3-biphosphoglycerate or it can be used in
other pathways such as PPP (Madigan et al., 2015; Berg et al., 2015). The pathway from pyruvate
to ethanol can be seen in figure 3.1 in the left part of the figure. The overall net result from glucose
to ethanol can be seen in equation (3.2) where it can be seen that only two molecules of ATP are
generated during alcoholic fermentation (Berg et al., 2015). After being produced, ethanol either
diffuses out of the cells or uses as a carbon source for the cells.

3.1.5
Crabtree effect
As mentioned previously, the availability of sugar and oxygen has a huge impact on the yeast
metabolic physiology. The crabtree effect is a phenomenon which can explain the generation of
energy related to oxygen and sugar availability. The phenomenon is explained as a occurrence
of alcoholic fermentation under aerobic conditions with increased sugar concentrations (Snoek
and Steensma, 2007; Rodrigues et al., 2006).The phenomenon is characterised as an overflow
metabolism by high fluxes through the glycolysis, increased carbon flux towards the fermenta-
tion by-products and decreased flux through respiration under aerobic conditions (Malina et al.,
2021). The phenomenon is not well-studied in W. anomalus but it is studied deeply in S. cerevisiae
which is crabtree positive. In S. cerevisiae, the phenomenon is explained by repression of the res-
piration due to increased production of NADH which cannot be oxidised through the respiration.
Therefore fermentation occurs to remove the extra NADH by producing ethanol. The increased
production of NADH is a result of increased flux of sugar through the glycolysis. Furthermore, it
is observed that oxygen has an impact in the regulation of the activity of pyruvate decarboxylase,
which converts pyruvate into acetaldehyde. In S, cerevisiae, high levels of pyruvate decarboxylase
activity is observed under aerobic conditions which provide increased conversion of pyruvate
into acetaldehyde. In crabtree negative yeasts such as Candida utilis, the levels of activity of pyru-
vate decarboxylase increase only when the oxygen is a limiting factor (Snoek and Steensma, 2007).
The study (Malina et al., 2021) investigated the crabtree positive yeasts S. cerevisiae and Schizosac-
charomyces pombe and the crabtree negative yeasts Kluyveromyces marxianus and Schefferaomyce
stipitis to get a deeper knowledge about the crabtree effect in eukaryal cells since it was observed
that crabtree negative yeasts can grow with rates comparable to the crabtree positive (Malina
et al., 2021). Therefore, Malina et al. (2021) investigated the adaptation mechanisms in metabolism

3.1.6
Alternative carbon sources
The PPP, glycolysis, respiration and fermentation are explained with glucose as carbon source and
S. cerevisiae as organism. Most yeasts will prefer to grow on glucose but yeasts can metabolise a lot
of different carbon sources and some of these will be explained below. Yeast is able to use several
hexoses sugars such as glucose, fructose, galactose or mannose. These hexoses are metabolised
into the intermediates in the glycolysis and the fermentation or respiration can occur, e.g. galac-
tose is converted into glucose 6-P by three reactions. Yeasts are also able to use disaccharides
such as sucrose and maltose. Disaccharides are as hexoses converted into an intermediate in the
glycolysis and follow the metabolic pathway (Rodrigues et al., 2006).
Yeasts are also able to metabolise two carbons such as acetate and ethanol which can be produced
by the yeast itself typically under anaerobic conditions (Rodrigues et al., 2006). Ethanol can be
reduced into acetaldehyde and then be converted into acetate. This process can be seen in figure
3.1, even though the arrow did not show that the reactions are reversible. The acetate can then be
converted into acetyl-CoA and be used as electron donor which can be transported into the mi-
tochondria. When acetyl-CoA is used as an electron donor, the acetyl-CoA enters the glyoxylate
cycle. The glyoxylate cycle occurs in conjunction with the TCA cycle. The overall net result of the
glyoxylate cycle is icocitrate and acetate which are converted into succinate and malate. All these
reactions occur under aerobic conditions (Berry and Brown, 1987; Madigan et al., 2015).

3.1.7
External factors effect on the metabolism and growth
Several external factors can effect the growth and choice of metabolism. Some of these external
factors which are tested in this study are listed below. The different factors can affect each other
which can enhance the effect of the specific factor.
Oxygen
As mentioned previously, the availability of oxygen has a huge impact in the choice of metabolism.
If the conditions are aerobic, the yeast will do respiration while if oxygen is limited, the yeasts will
do fermentation. The oxygen can potentially inhibit the growth if the O2 is converted into toxic
oxygen by-products such as superoxide anion or hydrogen peroxide (Madigan et al., 2015).
Temperature
The temperature is essential for the growth of yeasts. Most yeasts are mesophilic and grow best in
the range between 20 and 30 °C (Deak, 2006). Overall when the temperature rises, the metabolic
rate increases and growth becomes faster. This means that the growth rate is effected by the
temperature. However, at a certain temperature, the proteins and essential cells components
may be irreversibly damaged or denatured (Madigan et al., 2015). Each species of yeasts has
a minimum, optimum and maximum temperature where yeast can grow. These temperatures
can be influenced and shifted by the physiological state of cells and environmental conditions.
Furthermore low pH and water activity, presence of antimicrobial compounds such as ethanol can
limit the range of temperature where cells can grow. If yeasts grow below or above the optimum
temperature, the resistance to other stress factors will decrease (Deak, 2006; Madigan et al., 2015).
pH
As temperature, pH has a huge impact on the yeast growth. Each species of yeasts has a opti-
mum, minimum and maximum growth pH. Overall, extreme pH can effect proteins and their
functions since the folding of them can change due to modification of the ionisation of amino acid
functional groups. The unfolding and non-folding can decreased the activity of the proteins and
they can denature. This will then impact the metabolisms since the reactions in the metabolism
are enzymatic (Madigan et al., 2015; Parker et al., 2016).
Under acidic conditions, the stability of cytoplasmic membrane is affected since the cells become
more permeable which will affect the uptake of nutrients and growth (Madigan et al., 2015). Un-
der alkaline conditions, the establishment of the electrochemical gradient across the plasma mem-
brane is difficult due to the neutralisation by OH−. The uptake of nutrients over the membrane
and the generation of ATP by ATP synthase is driven by this gradient and if this gradient is neu-
tralised, these functions will the be inhibited and thereby the growth is affected (Brown et al.,
2017; Madigan et al., 2015). When the cells is affected by the high and low pH, the growth rate
will be decreased if it is far from the optimum pH.

Ethanol
Ethanol is an antimicrobial compound produced through the alcoholic fermentation. Ethanol has
a toxic effect on several organisms as well as the producing strain. The tolerance for ethanol vary
from species to species. S. cerevisiae can tolerate 13-15 % ethanol even though some strain of S.
cerevisiae can tolerate 18 % or higher concentrations of ethanol (Deak, 2006). Ethanol can inhibit
growth in a variety of ways, e.g. by decreasing the cell volume, protein denaturation, decreasing
the glycolytic enzyme activity, and/or inhibiting transport processes such as transport proteins
(Stanley et al., 2010). The tolerance of ethanol can be reduced or increased by other environmental
factors such as pH and temperature (Deak, 2006).
3.2
Fermentation technology and parameters
There exists many different definitions of fermentation based on which setting the term is used
in. Fermentation can be used in the setting where it refers to a metabolism under anaerobic con-
ditions as explained in section 3.1.3 but it can also refers to the generation of energy by catabolism
of organic compounds into a product which can occur both aerobic and anaerobic (Stanbury et al.,
2013). The last definition refers to a bio-process. It is important to differentiate between the two
mentioned definitions. In the rest of this section 3.2, fermentation refers to a bio-process.
3.2.1
Mode of fermentation operations
Fermentations can be solid-state or submerged fermentations. The solid-state fermentation is
when the microorganisms grow on solid medium with little or no ”free” water. In this study,
the focus is the submerged fermentations, where the microorganisms grow in liquid media with
dissolved substrates where the water activity is high (Soetaert and Vandamme, 2010).
Overall, there are three modes of fermentation operations during a submerged fermentation
which are batch, fed-batch and continuous fermentation. Figure 3.4 show a simple visualisation
of the different mode of operations.

Batch
Batch fermentation is the simplest mode of operation since it is a closed system with no in- or
out-flow and thereby a constant volume of the fermentation broth. Since it is a closed system,
the possibility of contamination is less than the other two operation modes. A batch fermenta-
tion can occur in different scales such as shake flasks, single disposable system and in fermenter,
where it is possible to control process parameters such as dissolved oxygen, pH, agitation and
temperature etc. Batch fermentation is often used in the laboratory to obtain considerable quan-
tities of cells and products for further analysis. The advantages by using the batch operation are
the simplicity of use, operability and reliability due to the short time of duration. Some of the dis-
advantages for the batch operation are the possibility of building up toxic metabolites which can
restrict cell growth and product formation and batch-to-batch variability (Macauley-Patrick and
Finn, 2008; Soetaert and Vandamme, 2010). In addition, cell population is heterogeneous and con-
stantly changing during a batch fermentation which makes it difficult to make a cause and effect
relationship analysis regarding cell physiology (Fleming and Patching, 2008; Macauley-Patrick
and Finn, 2008). However in this study, the batch operation is chosen as fermentation mode due
to the simplicity of the operation and the possibility of generation of considerable amount of
experimental data.

Fed-batch
Fed-batch fermentation starts by operating as a batch fermentation before feeding of concentrated
feed medium. There exist different feeding strategies, such as variable and continuous feeding
regime, but it depends on the aim of the experiment. Overall, fed-batch allows control of the
environmental conditions, control of the growth rate, minimisation of production of unwanted
by-products and high titers of biomass and products can be achieved. These advantages make
the fed-batch interesting in the view of investigation of use of the microorganism for industrial
applications. A limitation for the fed-batch fermentation is the volume of the fermenter which
can limit the duration time and thereby effect the feeding strategy due to limited feeding volume.
Furthermore, running a fed-batch fermentation requires detailed knowledge about the organisms
when the feeding strategy needs to be made and there is a increased risk for contamination since
it is an open system (Macauley-Patrick and Finn, 2008; Soetaert and Vandamme, 2010).
Continuous
A continuous fermentation has a flow-in of concentrated feed medium and a flow-out of the
fermentation broth consisting of spend medium and cells. Continuous fermentation can be used
to prolong the exponential phase of the organisms in the batch culture and to keep the steady
state. The steady state is where the environment is maintained with less deviation in nutrients,
cell number or biomass. This is kept by using the same flowrate for flow-in and flow-out to
ensure several factors, such as volume, concentrations, biomass as well as physical parameters
remain constant during the fermentation. Although, the flowrate needs to be determined in the
proper way, where the dilution rate between the feed medium and the spent medium need to be
greater than the maximum specific growth rate, otherwise a washout will occur if the dilution rate
exceeds the maximum specific growth rate (Macauley-Patrick and Finn, 2008). The continuous
fermentation gives the opportunity to investigate the cellular physiology by changing factors,
such as feed medium or flowrate of feed after the steady state since a change will cause an effect
in the way that cells grow or in other parameters (Stephanopoulos et al., 1998). In addition,
the continuous fermentation can also be used in evolution of new cultures since it allows us to
direct the evolution of the strain under particular evolutionary pressure (Macauley-Patrick and
Finn, 2008). Although, continuous fermentation has several application possibilities in terms of
examining the physiology, it is a sensitive operation mode due to high risk of contamination
which can result in washout and loss of product. Furthermore, genetic instability can easily occur
in continuous fermentation which may lead to culture mutation that can washout the production
strain (Macauley-Patrick and Finn, 2008; Stephanopoulos et al., 1998).

3.2.2
Growth phases
A mentioned, batch fermentation is chosen as the operation mode in this study. When cells grow
in a batch fermentation, they often follow a characteristic growth curve which is divided into 6
growth phases that is illustrated in figure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: Growth curve of a microorganism in a batch fermentation. a) Lag phase; b) Transient acceleration phase;
c) Exponential phase; d) Deceleration phase; e) Stationary phase; f) Death phase. The figure is from (Mavituna and
Sinclair, 2008).
The cells start to enter the lag phase after inoculation. In the lag phase seen as phase (a) in figure
3.5, the cells start to adapt to the environment to establish the physiology chemical equilibrium
between the cells and environment. The cells activate and repress specific genes in the need of
adapting to the new environment. Due to the adaptation, there is little or no growth seen. The
lag phase can be time consuming and costly. Therefore it is highly recommended to minimise
this phase which can be done by growing the preculture in the same medium and under same
growth conditions as the fermentation to minimise the stress possibilities (Macauley-Patrick and
Finn, 2008; Mavituna and Sinclair, 2008).
After the lag phase, the cells enter the acceleration phase seen as phase (b) in figure 3.5), where
the cells begin to expand in volume by taking up water and start to divide. When the cells adapt
to the environment and expand in volume, they enter the exponential phase. In the exponential
phase seen as phase (c) in figure 3.5, the cells grow exponentially with a maximum constant rate
which is determined as the maximum specific growth rate for the microorganism. The maximum
specific growth rate is influenced by complexity of the medium, ability of major carbon and en-
ergy source, suitable physiochemical conditions such as pH, temperature but also the presence
of growth inhibitors or toxic compounds for the cells. But overall, the duration of this phase is
determined partly by the concentration of the growth-limiting substrate. At a certain point, the
cells stop to grow exponentially and enter into the deceleration phase (phase (d) in figure 3.5) due
to limited level of major substrate or accumulation of growth inhibitors or toxic compounds. In
this phase, the cells start to use the storage energy and alternative carbon sources which is less ef-
ficient as the major carbon source (Macauley-Patrick and Finn, 2008; Mavituna and Sinclair, 2008).

The cells enter the stationary phase seen as phase (e) in figure 3.5, when the rate of cell growth

is stopped before the cells enter this phase to save money and time (Macauley-Patrick and Finn,

Wickerhamomyces anomalus
In this section, a literature review of W. anomalus is made where the characteristics for the organ-
ism will be presented. The focus will especially be on the characteristics and parameters which
are investigated according to the sub-aims. Furthermore, the potential applications of W. anomalus

4.1
Taxonomy
W. anomalus is an ascomycetous heterothallic yeast of the family wickerhamomycetaceae which
is characterised by sexual reproduction by formation of hat-shaped ascospores. W. anomalus re-
produce sexually by budding Satora et al. (2014). W. anomalus has several former names such as
Saccharomyces anomalus, Endomyces anomalus, Hansenula anomala, Pichia anomala, P. anomalus and
Willia anomala (Kurtzman et al., 2008). W. anomalus was the first time characterised as S. anomalus
in 1884 and through the years up to 2008 the yeast was renamed serveral times and in 2008 the
yeast was named W. anomalus. The changes of name through the years were due to the reclassifi-
cation based on phyologenetic analysis (Kurtzman et al., 2008; Satora et al., 2014). The literature
about W. anomalus is limited due to the name changes but the literature for the former names are
also used for the review of W.anomalus.
W. anomalus and S. cerevisiae has similar taxonomy and only differ from each other in the fam-
ily order, where S. cerevisiae belongs to saccharomycetaceae and W. anomalus belongs to wicker-
hamomycetaceae as mentioned previously (Meyen and Desm, 1838; Kurtzman et al., 2008).
4.2
Environmental habitats
W. anomalus is able to grow in extreme environmental stress conditions and therefore the organ-
ism is seen in various types of environments (Satora et al., 2014; Walker, 2011). First of all, W.
anomalus is known as a spoilage yeast of foods and beverages such as dairy, fruit and bakery. The
organism is able to do spoilage since W. anomalus is acid- and salt-tolerant and produces ethyl
acetate. Although the yeast can spoilage in food and beverages, it can also have some beneficial
applications which will be explained in section 4.5. Furthermore, W. anomalus has been associ-
ated with human infections, like S. cerevisiae (Fleet, 2011). Furthermore, strains of W. anomalus are
also present in environments, such as plants, fruit skins, human tissues, wine, animals and water
(Satora et al., 2014; Walker, 2011).
4.3
Growth characteristic and crabtree effect
As mentioned, W. anomalus is able to grow under extreme environmental stress conditions which
is illustrated in the laboratory (Walker, 2011). The study (Fredlund et al., 2002) investigated the
physiology characteristics of W. anomalus isolated from airtight stored gain. The study (Fredlund
et al., 2002) figured out that W. anomalus is able to grow at temperatures from 3 °C to 37 °C, pH-
values between 2.0 and 12.4, and in water activities at 0.92 (NaCl) and 0.85(glycerol) under strictly
anaerobic conditions. Furthermore, W. anomalus is able to grow at wide range of different carbon

4.5
Applications
The applications of W. anomalus are often related to the formation of by-products, aromas and
antimicrobial compounds and the ability to grow in harsh environments also give some appli-
cation for W. anomalus. W. anomalus has a QPS status from EFSA which benefit the use of W.
anomalus for several applications but particularly application in food biotechnology (Melin et al.,
2007; Walker, 2011). Furthermore, the organism is classified as a biosafety level 1 organism which
means W. anomalus is considered safe for healthy individuals and without no restriction on han-
dling (Fredlund et al., 2002; Satora et al., 2014) which also benefit the use of the organism. Some
of the potential applications are listed below.
4.5.1
Food and beverages
As mentioned previously, W. anomalus is known as a spoilage yeast in food and beverages e.g.
due to the availability to grow in harsh environments and the production of ethyl acetate. An
example is W. anomalus is able to grow in food high-sugars products (Satora et al., 2014). But even
though the ability to spoilage, W. anomalus can have beneficial applications for food and bever-
ages. W. anomalus has been used as food-flavouring agent to enhance flavour and as a producer
of food bioemulsifiers (Passoth et al., 2006; Walker, 2011). Furthermore, W. anomalus can be used
as bio-preservation in food and feed or in enzymatic processing (Walker, 2011).
W. anomalus as a non-Saccharomyces is investigated in relation to wine and low-alcoholic bev-
erages. W. anomalus produce fruity acetate esters such as ethyl acetate, isoamyl acetate and 2-
phenylethyl acetate and other volatile aromas which can benefit the wine aroma (Padilla et al.,
2018). Additionally, ethyl acetate contributes strongly to the wine aroma but a too high concen-
tration above 200 mg/L of ethyl acetate can give a vinegary taste to the wine (Passoth et al., 2006).
Isoamyl acetate will contribute with smell like acetone and/or nail polish if occur in too high con-
centrations (Gonz´alez- ´Alvarez et al., 2012). Furthermore, W. anomalus produces several enzymes
such as glucosidases and xylosidases which are involved in the release of aroma compounds from
grape precursors (Padilla et al., 2018).

4.5.2
Antimicrobial activity
W. anomalus has shown the ability to be used as a biocontrol yeast against microbial activity in
variable environments. W. anomalus has the ability to produce a broad spectrum of killer toxins
which have an antimicrobial activity against fungi, yeast and bacterial species and viruses. An
example is the inhibition by Botrytis cinera which is a plant pathogen in e.g. vines and it has been
shown that W. anomalus protects the vines against infestation (Walker, 2011). A killer toxin which
has shown antimicrobial activity against fungi is panomycocin which has shown to exert its cy-
tocidal activity by hydrolysing the β-1,3 glucanase which are the primary polymers within the
fungal cell wall (Satora et al., 2014).
Ethyl acetate has shown to have an antifungal activity against the grain-storage mould Penillium
requeforti together with other metabolites such as ethanol and a high water activity. W. anomalus
has also shown ability to compete with the molds for the nutrients or space during storage of
grain (Fredlund et al., 2004a). The production of ethanol, oxygen consumption and CO2 have
also shown to contribute to inhibition of fungi (Passoth et al., 2006).
4.5.3
Antimicrobial therapy
Since W. anomalus is classified as a biosafety level 1 organism with no hazards for healthy individ-
uals and without no restriction on handling, W. anomalus has the potential to be used in therapy
due to the antimicrobial activity. The occurrence of yeast infections is increasing and therefore
new ways to treat these have been considered where the killer toxins are considered for therapeu-
tic use (Passoth et al., 2006). The mass of killer toxins vary from 3 to 300 kilodaltons and therefore
the toxins are too large to be used in a therapeutic treatment (Passoth et al., 2006; Walker, 2011).
But mimicking the action of killer toxins by generation of anti-idiotypic antibodies has shown to
exert a broad antimicrobial spectrum. The study (Magliani et al., 2002) demonstrated successful
the mimic of the action of killer toxins produced by W. anomalus to treat Candidiasis infections in
rats (Magliani et al., 2002; Passoth et al., 2006).

Analyses
During the fermentation, samples were taken out through the sampling port at different time

performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). The OD was measured simultaneously during the
fermentation, whereas sample broth for DW and HPLC analysis was stored in eppendorf tubes at

°C and the details about the filtration can be seen in section 8.3. The different analysis methods
give an identification of the metabolic activities of the cells.
8.1
Optical density (OD)
The OD of each sample was measured at 600 nm in the spectrophotometer (VWR, V-1200) to
determine the growth. Demineralised water was used as blank media and dilution media for
each sample. Before inoculation, the OD600 was measured in medium from each fermenter and
used as blank samples. The OD600 of the sample must be in the range from 0.1-0.6 and if the OD600
exceed the maximum of this range, the sample was diluted.
8.2
Dry-weight (DW)
The DW analysis was made to determine the concentration of biomass. The selected samples
were thawed and suspended. Afterwards, the samples were filtered through a membrane filter
(0.45 µm, MontaMil®PES, Frisenette) using a vacuum pump. The filter were washed twice with
demineralised water and dried for 20 minutes at 180 watt in the microwave and then placed in a
desiccator for 20 minutes before determination of the DW. The DW was determined by the weight
difference in the filter before and after filtering and the volume of added sample.

Conclusion
The overall conclusion for this investigation of the availability of dissolved oxygen is that an aer-
ation at 0.056 vvm is not sufficient to keep an aerobic fermentation based on the production of
ethanol and the drop in the DO level. Furthermore, 2 vvm is the best aeration compared to 1 vvm
to ensure an aerobic fermentation, even though F3-2vvm has a lower maximum specific growth
rate than F1-0.056 and F2-1vvm, but the cells use the glucose more targeted to build-up biomass
which is concluded based on the yield. However increasing the stirring for F2-1vvm could poten-
tially improve the oxygen transfer rate and thereby the availability of DO, such that the aeration
at 1 vvm could be sufficient to ensure aerobic fermentation. Furthermore, the fermentations with
2 vvm still have a drop in the level of DO and therefore another way to ensure an aerobic fermen-
tation will be by using the DO-cascade to control the level of DO.

Conclusion
The overall conclusion for the investigation of the limiting nutrients in the exponential phase, is
that the length of the exponential phase is not extended by addition of extract glucose, peptone
and yeast extract. The cells stop growing exponentially due to either limitation of glucose, too
high concentrations of ethanol, acetic acid and/or glycerol and/or the low level of dissolved
oxygen. Another reason for the inhibition could be that the cells produce an inhibitory compound
which limits the exponential growth. Based on this investigation, it is not possible to conclude if it
is only one specific factor or several factors which limit the exponential growth. But most likely it
is a combination of the cells using all the glucose and the switch between respiration to alcoholic
fermentation due to the drop in the level of DO.

Before comparing the physiological parameters, different conditions between this project and the
study (Malina et al., 2021) need to be considered. First of all, the batch fermentations in the article
are run with a DO level above 60 % which differ from the DO level used in this project where it is
assumed to be fully aerobic when the DO level is at 100 % ± 5 %. This can have an effect on the
formation of ethanol. The article do not investigate if the organism is crabtree negative or positive
but investigate the adaptation and physiology of the known crabtree negative and positive yeasts
and therefore the fermentation in the article can be run with a lower level of DO but still aerobic.
However, the organisms used in the article are well-studied in relation to the crabtree effect.
A huge difference between the article and this project is the fermentations in the article are per-
formed in a defined medium where the fermentations in this thesis are performed in a complex
medium. The defined medium is designed to a specific target so the media composition is known
where the media composition of the complex medium is unknown. Therefore it is not possible to
calculate the fermentation kinetics related to the mass balances, whereas in the defined medium
it is possible due to the known media composition. The media compositions can have an effect
in the analysis of the fermentation since the complex medium often differentiate from batch to
batch due to color and smell and can have problems in the sterilisation like precipitation where
the defined medium is colorless and defined. This can have an effect on the comparison of the
physiology parameters. When the yeast should be used for an application a defined medium is
preferred since the downstream will be more complicated with a complex medium.
Furthermore, W. anomalus is different from all the yeasts used in the article. Even though W.
anomalus only differ from S. cerevisiae in family and genus in the taxonomy, they can have differ-
ent metabolisms and different level of expression of metabolites (Kurtzman et al., 2008; Meyen
and Desm, 1838).

positive or negative. Though it can be concluded that crabtree negative yeasts can reach similar
growth rates as crabtree positive yeasts which is also concluded in the article (Malina et al., 2021).

One thing to consider with this set-up when adding the ethanol manually is the determination of
volume of ethanol to add. It is assumed that the sampling volume is 10 mL each time but since
the sampling is done manually this can cause differences in the volume taken out and thereby
variations in the expected concentrations of ethanol can happen. If the sample volume taken out
is higher than 10 mL, the ethanol concentration will be higher than expected after addition due
to the assumption of the sample volume. If the sample volume taken out is less than 10 mL, the
ethanol concentration will be lower than expected. Furthermore, the volume of ethanol to add
is quite low to take up by a syringe and therefore deviation can occur as seen in the determi-

human handling enhances the chances of more deviation. The addition of ethanol also affects the
determination of the concentrations of the biomass, glucose, glycerol and acetic acid, since the
extra volume to add over time will dilute these compounds. Thereby the concentration of these
compounds would potentially be slightly higher than shown in the figures. So overall, this set-up
with manual addition of is extremely sensitive.

makes it possible to make a cause and effect relationship analysis which could be used to investi-
gate the exact ethanol concentration tolerance of W. anomalus.
Conclusion
Overall, it can be concluded that W. anomalus can grow below ethanol concentrations of 65 g/L
but the organism can tolerate to be affected by higher ethanol concentrations up to 85 g/L. The
maximum specific growth rates are inhibited up to 4.5 times compared to fermentation without
ethanol. Furthermore, there are several bottlenecks in this set-up such as change in the aeration
when the volume decreases due to sampling and the human handling of sampling and addition
of ethanol. For further investigation of the ethanol tolerance a continuous fermentation could be
considered to find a specific concentration of ethanol.

From table 9.19, it can be observed that the biomass yield is 2-fold lower for F18-pH=9 than for
F17-pH=3 which is due to the formation of by-products by the cells in F18-pH=9. The biomass
yield indicates that cells in F18-pH=9 are doing alcoholic fermentation. The biomass yield for F3-

9.1, the reason for the production of glycerol and acetic acid in F3-2vvm is due to the drop in the
concentration of DO in the fermentation. This could potentially be the same case for F17-pH=3
where the cells produced glycerol in low concentrations. By comparing the concentrations of DO
for F18-pH=9 in appendix E and the production of by-products, it is observed that the level of
DO does not drop for F18-pH=9. This indicates that the production of by-products is not caused
by the drop in the level of DO but by the high pH which caused the switch from production of
biomass to production of by-products.
As mentioned in section 3.1.4, the ATP is generated by ATP synthase driven by the proton mo-
tive force which is the gradient across the membrane. The high concentration of H+ in the in-
termembrane space generates ATP which is used in the respiration. When the pH is high, the
concentrations of H+ are neutralised by OH−and thereby a lower amount of ATP is generated.
If the amount of ATP is lower, the cells potentially switch from respiration to alcoholic fermen-
tation and thereby the cells produce by-products as a stress signal instead of producing biomass
(Madigan et al., 2015; Parker et al., 2016). This is the case for F18-pH=9 where the biomass yield
is lower than observed for F17-pH=3 and F3-2vvm. Furthermore a high extracellular pH affect
the establishment of the electrochemical gradient across the plasma membrane which makes the
nutrient transport over the membrane more difficult (Brown et al., 2017).
Conclusion
The overall conclusion of this investigation is that the maximum specific growth rates of W.
anomalus are inhibited by 2-fold under extreme pH conditions compared to the maximum spe-
cific growth rates determined in the fermentations at the optimum pH of 6. This is potentially
due to decrease of the activity of the proteins. In addition, the cells in the fermentation at pH=9
are affected in the formation of biomass which is reflected in the lower biomass yield and higher
production of ethanol, glycerol and acetic acid compared to the cells in the fermentations at pH=3
and pH=6. This is a result of the challenges in the establishing of electrochemical gradients across
the plasma membrane, which are necessary for ATP generation and nutrient transport across the
membrane (Brown et al., 2017).

Challenges with sampling and the analysis methods
There are some challenges with the used analysis method and the procedure for sampling in this
study which can cause deviation in the calculated physiology parameters.
71 of 92
==PAGE:84==
M.SC. THESIS
9

During sampling, the sample port was disinfected with 70 % ethanol before samlping which
potentially has caused noise in the concentration of ethanol quantified by HPLC. Furthermore
during the sampling, a large amount of waste was generated since 4-5 ml broth was taken out
as waste to get a representative sample. The generation of the large volume of waste, will affect
the fermentation which was performed with constant airflow to get a constant aeration since the
aeration will increase over time when the volume inside the fermenter decrease due to sampling.
A way to avoid the large variation in aeration is by using the DO-cascade to have a constant DO-
tension instead. Furthermore, to minimise the generation of waste, to avoid the noise from the
disinfection with ethanol and to get a representative sample, a T-Luer connector could be fitted
to the sampling port. The T-Luer connector will have a ”waste” syringe connected to one of the
ports all the time where the sample syringe will be connected and disconnected to the second
port. During the sampling, a sample will be taken by the ”waste” syringe without disconnection
and afterwards the sample syringe will be connected and a sample will be taken out and the sy-
ringe will be disconnected. As the last step the broth in the ”waste” syringe will be transfered
back to the fermenter and thereby no waste will be generated.
The samples were analysed by spectrophotometry at OD600, DW and HPLC. In the presentation
and discussion of the results for the investigations, it was observed several times that the max-
imum specific growth rates based on DW were lower than the specific growth rates based on
OD600 and higher deviations for DW were observed. As mentioned in section 9.1, OD600 deter-
mines the growth based on the scattering of light and thereby this analysis method does not take
the difference between the dead and living cells into account. The DW as analysis method takes
the dead cells into account to some extent since the sample is filtered through a 0.45 µm filter
which means that the cell components and other components below this size will get through the
filter. DW as analysis method will thereby give a clearer picture of the growth compared to OD600
but there are some challenges. During the DW analysis, only 1.5 mL volume of broth was filtered
through the filter which thereby gives a low weight difference between the filter before and after
filtering. Since the difference in weight is low, it can be difficult to measure and thereby high de-
viation can occur which also is seen in the maximum specific growth rates based on DW. To avoid
the deviation, a higher volume could be taken out for DW analysis due to the knowledge about
the exponential phase. Another way to determine the growth is by colony forming units (CFU)
on agar plates but this is quite time consuming. Therefore OD600 is the easiest way to follow the
growth due to the simplicity.
Finally, some uncertainties can occur during the HPLC analysis. First of all, it took 30 min from
sampling until the sample was filtered and in the freezer. During this time period, the cells have
the ability to consume substrates and by-products which thereby will give a time shifted picture
of the fermentation. Furthermore, the samples need to be thawed, re-suspended and transfered
into the HPLC vials before the HPLC analysis. If the suspension is not done properly the sample is
not representative. The separation of peaks is done manually which can also give some variation
from sample to sample.

CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to study the fermentation physiology of W. anomalus where the focus
was to investigate the following parameters: availability of DO, limit of essential nutrients, crab-
tree effect, ethanol tolerance and pH effect on growth.
W. anomalus grows with the highest maximum specific growth rate of 0.590 ± 0.0186 h−1 and
biomass yield YSX of 0.289 ± 0.0034 in 2xYPD medium with an aeration at 1 vvm. Under these
conditions, the cells produce ethanol, acetic acid and glycerol in a higher concentration than the
cells in 1xYPD where the maximum specific growth rate is 0.5606 ± 0.0089 h−1 with a biomass
yield of 0.3296 ± 0.170 gDW/gglc. The cells in both of these fermentations start to grow exponen-
tially after 1.5 hours of cultivation and goes into the deceleration phase after 8 hours of cultivation
due to limitation of limiting amount of glucose, oxygen and/or high concentration by-products.
From the investigation of the availability of DO, it can be concluded that the aeration at 0.056 vvm
and 1 vvm is insufficient to ensure an aerobic fermentation since a trough occurs in the DO con-
centration which decreases to close to zero at these aeration rates and the cells produce ethanol,
acetic acid and glycerol. This shows that the cells shift to alcoholic fermentation when the level of
available DO decreases. An aeration at 2 vvm is sufficient to ensure an aerobic fermentation but
running with DO-cascade the level of DO can be kept constant.
From this study, it can also be concluded that W. anomalus is a crabtree negative yeast since the
cells do not produce ethanol under DO-tension at 100% ± 5%. Furthermore it can be concluded
that W. anomalus can grow below an ethanol concentration at 65 g/L but the maximum specific
growth rate is up to 4-fold lower compared to the baseline fermentation. Furthermore the cells
were able to endure higher ethanol concentrations since the cells do not perish when exposed to
ethanol titers up to 85 g/L. However the batch fermentation does not allow the tolerance to be
investigated properly.
Furthermore, it can be concluded that W. anomalus is able to grow at extreme pH-values of pH=3
and pH=9. At pH=9, the cells are stressed by the high pH which is seen by the production of
ethanol, acetic acid and glycerol and the lower biomass yield compared to the cells at pH=3. This
shows that the cells in pH=9 are potentially stress due to the disruption of the electrochemical
gradient which drives the generation of ATP. However, the maximum specific growth rates at
both pH values are 2-fold lower compared to the baseline at pH=6 which is due to the decrease
in protein activity the extreme pH.

Finally, it can be concluded that the cells produce some unknown compounds which cannot be
identified by the external standards for HPLC used in this study. A compound at retention time
7.2-7.3 min is produced in all fermentations when all the glucose is used and the cells enter the

acetate since the off-gas start to smell like acetone and/or nail-polish remover.
Overall it can be concluded, that W. anomalus is a crabtree negative yeast which has the ability
to grow in elevated ethanol concentrations and at extreme pH. However, further investigation of
the fermentation physiology with the focus on the particular application is needed.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
11
Future perspectives
It is needed to conduct additional research on fermentation physiology of W. anomalus in general
as well as with the particular application in the mind. Two parameters from this study, which can
be investigated further, are the ethanol tolerance of W. anomalus and the unidentified compounds
observed in this study.
As concluded previously, W. anomalus is relatively tolerant against ethanol and can grow in ethanol
concentrations up to 65 g/L. But overall, the batch fermentations do not allow the ethanol toler-
ance to be investigated properly due to the evaporation of ethanol over time. An alternative way
to investigate the ethanol tolerance of W. anomalus is by using chemostat which allows to specify
the concentration due to the possibility to increase the ethanol concentration over time in the feed
medium. The chemostat could be conducted by growing W. anomalus into an exponential phase
in a batch fermentation in 1xYPD medium with 65 g/L ethanol since it is shown that the cells can
grow at this ethanol concentration. When the cells are in the exponential phase, the chemostat
will be started to prolong the exponential phase and thereby the cells are growing in steady state.
The feed medium will contain an excess of all the nutrients for growth but with an increasing
amount of ethanol over time. The dilution rate used for the flows need to be determined prop-
erly because if the dilution exceeds the maximum specific growth rate, a washout can occur. The
determination of the dilution rate therefore needs to take into account that the maximum specific
growth rate will be effected by the increase of ethanol. The growth rates determined in this study
in the investigation of the ethanol tolerance can be used as guidance for determining the dilution
rate.
Further investigation of the unidentified compounds is needed. First of all, an exhaustive search
in the literature could be done to get an identification of which compounds there could be pro-
duced by W. anomalus under similar conditions as used in this study. Furthermore, the combi-
nation of the column, mobile phase and detector used for HPLC will eliminate a huge part of
the compounds produced by W. anomalus found in the literature since the conditions determine
which compounds there will be detected. This will give a range of possible compounds which
could be the unidentified compounds. HPLC standards for these possible compounds could be
run and compared with the spectrum for the unidentified compounds. Otherwise the samples
could be analysed by LC-MS which will give an identification of ionic mass-to-charge ratio of the
compound which could narrow down the range of possible compounds.
