Abstract 
 
The fast evolution of mobile networks has led to new challenges, to improve the quality of 
service experienced by the users. New applications and services impose new requirements to 
the transmission, which are harder to deal with for older wireless technologies. New techniques 
of transmission are discovered, both on the hardware (radio) and software (network 
protocols). Although some problems might be solved by technologies of the future, the usage 
of resources needs to still be optimized and this requires the network to be renewed on every 
level. The aim of this thesis is to study possible ways of improving the QoS of wireless networks 
through the modification of transport layer protocols, to fulfill the requirements of 5G 
networks. Those are a small delay, coexistence with existing protocols, low retransmission, 
good throughput usage, adaptability to the application. For this matter, the QUIC protocol, 
new protocol introduced by Google, has been studied and put to the test in wireless networks 
through simulation tools using the OPNET modeler simulator. The results have been 
compared to currently most used protocol, TCP. At the end of the day, QUIC’s performance is 
similar to TCP’s in terms of latency and throughput for two popular services: file transfer and 
HTTP streaming. QUIC, however, manages to have a slightly better resource usage, a wanted 
behavior in 5G networks. The replacement of TCP by today’s QUIC version wouldn’t bring huge 
improvements, but wouldn’t also deteriorate the performance. Google’s measurements show, 
that thanks to the QUIC protocol, they were able to achieve faster load time for Google queries 
and Youtube videos and for other services and websites the performance was similar. QUIC’s 
structure is more compliant with 5G’s requirements as it is more modular and customizable 
and some of its features haven’t still been explored and implemented yet like the additional 
bandwidth discovery mechanisms.  This leaves some space for further work on the protocol, 
which could give it a chance to become the next transport protocol for 5G networks.

PART 1  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Introduction 
 
The introduction of smartphones and tablets and their growing number from that moment has 
created a new market for software developers and operators. Apps are now not only created for 
PCs and laptops but a mobile version can and even has to be done to stay competitive on the 
market. Web services can now be accessed from anywhere and have to be able to cope with the 
increasing number of connections from multiple devices. Thanks to there bigger and better 
displays compared to previous phones, video services like Youtube or video conferences using 
Skype or Facetime are now more interesting for mobile users than before. Higher video quality 
requires more data to be sent. In addition to these, the Internet of Things starts to spread even 
more and every device in every home, street, and every car will soon need to be connected to 
the network to be able to work. In this regard, wired solutions are not really an option. To deal 
with this growth of data traffic, wireless networks had to be improved to reach higher 
throughputs and enable more reliable connections. EDGE was only able to support the traffic 
from Web services and Web browsing; UMTS and HSPA brought higher throughputs, enabling 
multimedia services [1] and LTE with even higher throughputs improved the quality of 
experience of mobile broadband applications [2]. However, the mobile data traffic is increasing 
at a quick pace and Cisco estimates that the 4G traffic has already exceeded 3G’s and will 
increase eightfold between 2015-2020 [3]. Ericsson forecasts a growth in mobile subscriptions 
for WCDMA/HSPA by 10% until 2020 and by 40% for LTE [4]. Therefore, LTE-Advanced has 
been introduced to support this traffic, increasing LTE’s throughput 10 times.  
In the near future, more service will be added to the range as new multimedia technologies 
begin to appear, such as 4k video. Those will require both, a higher throughput and a lower 
latency for the user to really have a good experience. For instance, to stream a 4k video, a 
bandwidth of 15-20Mbps is necessary [5], which is 20% of a LTE bandwidth, meaning that only 
5 user could use such a service. To be able to support all of these applications in a wireless 
network, new ways of communication need to be applied and new requirements have to be 
created for future networks. The concept of 5G networks has been introduced and its main goal 
is to drastically increase the parameters of wireless networks to deal with the traffic of 
tomorrow. Nokia Networks has written a few papers in this matter [6] and stated a few 
requirements backed up with real  use cases of the future. They mainly consider the reduction 
of the delay, the increase of the throughput, high support for M2M, huge reliability, low 
maintenance costs and a better QoS management. The use cases described are the same as 
those mentioned previously, with the addition of more sensitive ones like the tactile Internet. 
To compare with current technologies, LTE is able to reach a latency of 20ms and a throughput 
of 150Mbps and its newest version and LTE-Advanced could theoretically go up to 1Gbps. 
However, due to other technologies interfering and the poor air channel, those numbers are 
usually lower.  
Network-wise, the core of the network will also be optimized through the usage of SDN and 
NFV, two key technologies enabling a better control of the flows, an easier QoS management 
and a higher resource usage [7]. All protocols at different layers will play an important role in 
the transmission of data, to guarantee the delivery with low latency and no losses, but the 
implementation of newer ones requires different levels of modifications in networks and the 
higher the protocol on the stack, the less changes are needed. Transport protocols play an

essential role in the transmission and require only changes at the end nodes. TCP, which is 
used in 90% of the traffic, is a traffic regulating protocol, making sure that the user does not 
send more than the network can handle and that the data is sent reliably. However, it was 
designed for wired networks and its performance is much worse in wireless networks due to a 
misinterpretation of the numbers. As an example, because of the retransmissions in the 
wireless part, some delay is added to the delivery time, which in wired networks happens only, 
when buffers are overflowed in the network nodes. TCP interprets it as a loss of the packet, 
starts a retransmission and halves the amount of data that can be sent. Although, the timeouts 
are adjusted based on the RTT, it still not good enough because of the dynamic changes of the 
wireless channels, especially in urban areas. Thus, TCP needs to go through a refreshing stage.  
In terms of 5G, some researchers propose to use an open model of the implementation of 
protocols, where the protocol can be adapted based on the application running on top of it and 
the quality of the channels over which the data is transported [8]. The UDP protocol could be 
a good base for that, since the latency sensitive applications already make use of it and its 
simple structure leaves space for customization. 
Recently, a newer protocol has seen the light, QUIC, which gathers all of the best mechanisms 
from TCP studies in terms of packet loss detection and dealing with high latency. It is running 
on top of UDP and is managed in the browser, making it dependent only on the browser 
version. Its structure corresponds to the requirements of 5G networks, so it will be interesting 
to see how well it guarantees QoS. Through an event driven simulation, we would like to 
evaluate its performance in a wireless environment, compare it to TCP and eventually propose 
a few modifications.  
1.1. Report structure 
The report introduces the concept of 5G network through the study of existing mobile networks 
and the mechanisms they use for a better transmission. Then, the network is presented 
protocol-wise, with an introduction to TCP and a presentation of its features, which are then 
discussed when used in a mobile environment. Follows a study of existing solutions for the 
problem and an introduction of the QUIC protocol, which in a later chapter is seen in more 
details. In the next step, the event driven simulation model is presented with all the 
implementation details that go with it. Finally, the model is put to the test and compared to the 
TCP model and the results are discussed. Below is a detailed description of each chapter. 
Part 2 – Presentation of LTE and LTE-Advanced with all the mechanisms of the physical layer 
that enable high data-rates. Introduction to 5G networks and comparison to its predecessors. 
Part 3 – Insight on the QoS mechanisms currently implemented in mobile and transport 
networks, introduction to TCP with a discussion of its effectiveness in mobile networks. 
Part 4 – An investigation of attempts to improve the TCP performance in wireless 
environments, viewed from different network layers. Introduction to the QUIC protocol 
Part 5 – A detailed presentation of the QUIC protocol, with the packet structures and 
mechanisms implemented to establish a connection quickly, transmit the data safely and close 
without to much overhead. 
Part 6 – Discussion of the implementation of the QUIC protocol in a simulation software. 
Part 7 – Presentation of the testing strategy and the actual results of the various runs. 
Part 8 – Conclusion of the report.

2.1. LTE and LTE-A 
LTE is the latest mobile technology that has been created in the view of the growing usage of 
mobile applications. Its simple structure makes it still highly customizable to achieve a higher 
performance while already providing some great improvements compared to its predecessors. 
As an example, LTE-Advanced is the evolved version of LTE and has the capacity to provide 
up to 6 times the throughput of LTE [9], ideal to handle the growing traffic and closer to the 
requirements of 5G networks. 
a. LTE features 
LTE Release 8, latest before LTE-Advanced, has been introduced in 2008 with lots of new 
features at the time. LTE is a packet switched network with 5 main components holding all of

The PDN-Gateway (PDN-GW) is an interface to the external networks and is responsible for

Thanks to the flat and simple architecture, LTE enables a quick and easy installation of new 
base stations [10], making it a good solution for mobile operators. 
LTE users reach peak data rates of 150 Mbps for the downlink traffic and use a bandwidth of 
20MHz [2]. The latency of user applications over LTE measured as the round trip time (RTT) 
is of 10-20ms [2]. Those numbers can vary depending on the number of users and radio 
conditions. A poor radio condition, due to weather, user mobility (train, car) or other devices 
can significantly hit the performance and total bit rate [1], thus usually the users have a data 
rate of less than 10 Mbps [11]. 
To reach these high bitrates, LTE employs several methods on the PHY layer to improve the 
transmission. First of all, both Time Division Duplex (TDD) and Frequency Division Duplex 
(FDD) are used. Secondly, the data is sent using an adaptive 64-QAM modulation (6 bits per 
symbol) with an adaptive modulation and coding algorithm. If the channel condition is poor, 
then the modulation can be switched to 16-QAM (or another lower than 64-QAM). Thirdly, the 
data is transmitted using MIMO, which is a multi-stream data transmission method using 
multiple antennas in the devices to transmit the data. The data is sent in several independent 
streams over the same air interface - currently, two antennas are used for it. But MIMO is only 
used when the channel conditions are good. Otherwise, the noise and signal fading prevent this 
technique from working properly. 
 
For downlink transmission, a technique called OFDMA is used to increase the throughput even 
more. Instead of sending the data over one carrier quickly, the device sends the data over 
several carriers, orthogonal to each other at a slower pace. The more carriers for transmission, 
the higher the data rate. Thanks to the large bandwidth (20MHz), many carriers can be used. 
A connection’s parameters of a user can be determined by the base station, by the mean of a 
priority bit specifying special QoS settings for the connection. The user can be then assigned a 
higher bandwidth and a better modulation scheme than others. All of this is managed by the 
eNodeB, which then takes care of a proper scheduling for each of the users.

b. LTE-Advanced 
As the purpose of LTE was to improve the quality of experience of mobile broadband 
applications, LTE-Advanced goes even further into this goal by increasing the throughputs 
even more, improving the coverage through faster and smarter deployment, reuse of the 
bandwidth and reduction of the interferences. 
First of all, the increase of throughputs is achieved by two means: MIMO and carrier 
aggregation (CA). As in LTE, the maximum supported configuration for the highest throughput 
was 4x4 (4 receivers and 4 transmitters), LTE-A enables an 8x8 Single user transmission 
mode, with throughputs going up to 600Mbit/s. However, there could be a problem with the 
transceivers in the mobile device, because not only they would take much more space, but the 
battery consumption could also increase drastically. Looking into the future, in a few years, as 
technology advances, this might not be a problem anymore. 
 Carrier Aggregation is a method to increase the bandwidth used by reusing the existing 
spectrum. Instead of using one 20MHz band for the transmission (1800MHz or 2600MHz) 
the device would use two bands, from other unused frequency bands, in sum making a band of 
40MHz.

mechanism called Coordinated Multipoint Operation (CoMP). This mechanism should help 
improving the performance of a cell at the edge, where the signal strength is poor, but the 
interference from many base stations is high. CoMP is based on a beamforming method, where 
two base stations (or more) coordinate there scheduling for a mobile user and adjust there 
transmission power, reducing the noise from each of them perceived by the UE and improving 
the signal quality. 
Secondly, since a lot of traffic comes from crowded urban areas [12], LTE-Advanced enables 
the deployment of small cells to improve the coverage of an area through the usage of relays. 
Those are LTE cells, like any other, but instead of being connected to the backhaul via coper 
cables, fibre or microwave links, they use other LTE macrocells to transport the data to the 
core network. Relays have two main advantages: the installation is really fast (thanks to LTE’s 
architecture) and they can improve the signal of a user without amplifying the noise of the 
transmission, like repeaters do. However, this comes at the cost of additional delay 
(encapsulation and decapsulation of data is required). Relays come in two versions: inbound

and outbound. Inband relays use the same frequency band as other users of the macrocell, but 
are assigned a number of time slots. The outbound relay, on the other side, are assigned their 
own frequency for the transmission, but have a constant access in time. Both have a similar 
performance in general, however, they also have their drawbacks. Inband relays introduce a 
higher latency due to the time division, whereas the outbound relays need to cope with the 
interferences of other channels. 
Thirdly, due to the large amount of different wireless technologies, there is a need to coordinate 
all of these networks, hence improving the coverage and quality of wireless networks. LTE 
deals with this problem by offloading the traffic to smaller cells or other technologies like WiFi, 
through traffic steering. Based on the information from the base stations and other devices (eg. 
Femtocells, relays), the network can decide to handover the user to the best cell for him. The 
deployment of many small cells in an area is a common thing to do now that the hardware costs 
go down, but those cells might interfere with each other. To deal with it, an interference 
management mechanism called eICIC (enhanced Inter-cell interference coordination) has 
been made. 
 
LTE and LTE-Advanced introduce a lot of new functions to increase the throughput and 
minimize the interference effect on the transmission, but they require a lot of overhead and 
some of the mechanisms introduce additional delay to the transmission, which in general has 
been reduced a lot compared to previous technologies. Protocols running on top of the stack 
can still be hit by this delay like TCP, which we will see in the next part. 
2.2. The 5G concept 
LTE and LTE-Advanced bring a lot of changes to the networks, making them more efficient 
and faster at the same time, improving the QoS of the networks (counted as the reduction of 
latency and improved throughput). 5G networks go even further in this direction, creating a

==PAGE:14==

concept of ultrafast, broadband, zero latency networks, where all devices are interconnected 
and the transmission quality is great in every places. 
The concept of 5G networks appeared recently as a response to the evolution of wireless 
technologies, the increasing demand for bigger bitrates and the constant evolution of user 
devices [13]. Wireless networks will have to deal with new services specially designed to 
immerse the user even more in the multimedia world. Those are for example, augmented 
reality services, which make the internet more tactile and create a virtual reality [14]. Then, we 
have the usual video streaming services, with its gaining popularity thanks to platforms like 
Netflix. TVs are becoming even better and require a higher encoding level, and this translates 
to a lot more data to store to show the image. Next are the latency driven devices and the 
concept of IoT. Because we want more automation in everyday devices and a smarter way of 
using them, chips start to take care of this and let us know about the status thanks to web 
services. Devices for more advanced and real time based like cars or drones, require a really 
small latency, to avoid the dark scenarios. These use cases have led to a list of requirements for 
5G networks.  
 
FIGURE 5. EVOLUTION UNTIL 5G [15] 
The requirements in a nutshell are the following: zero latency, all devices are connected with 
each other through the broadband wireless network, throughputs beyond 1Gbps to support all 
kinds of multimedia services, perfect coverage, perfect availability, power efficiency [15]. 
Those requirements are of course a guideline and do not have to be taken strictly. The zero 
latency means that the smallest latency possible should be achieved, and wireless links 
performance should be similar to copper or fibre links. The same applies to the coverage, which 
should be perceived as perfect (2-3 bars on mobile phones). All depends on who is the final 
user in an area. 
The technologies put forward to realize this plan are the following: mmWaves/cmWaves, 
small-cells, WiFi, MIMO and beamforming, C-RAN [6]. 
mmWaves [16] or millimeter waves use the frequency band from 30GHz to 300GHz and are 
between the microwaves and the infrared [17] waves; their wavelengths are in the 1mm – 
10mm range. The pros of using mmWaves for communication are: the ability of achieving high 
datarates; their availability (no licensing in this range). However, the downside of mmWaves 
is their attenuation. The higher the frequency, the bigger the attenuation, therefore they can 
only be used over small distances, small cells. Secondly, interferences from other devices 
working in the same range. WiFi technologies, computer wireless devices like mouses already 
==PAGE:15==
15

use these frequencies for data transmission and can make the transmission channel even 
worse. 
MIMO and beam-forming have already been used in LTE systems to increase the bitrate by 
increasing the number of antennas in the devices and controlling the transmission power of 
base stations. Currently, some limitations stand in the way: the size of the device - limiting the 
number of antennas in it; In arrays of antennas, interference between each of them can actually 
impact the effectiveness of MIMO. The beam-forming in 5G would consists of antennas 
sending beams directly to the user, which would require them to track the user and this implies 
that each of the cells would have to support several hundreds of users. Such a technology is 
complex and therefore expensive, however, it would enable to reach the high bitrates. 
Small cells in 5G networks shall be even smaller in terms of area covered and much denser 
compared to LTE. Bringing the radio resources closer to the user may reduce the latency of the 
transmission, but will require a wireless connection to the backhaul, possibly in a multi-hop 
configuration. Those links will still be affected by the environment in terms of noise and 
attenuation. 
The management of the radio access resources and reduction of the power consumption could 
be achieved by CRANs. CRANs put the whole processing part of base stations into one box for 
several radio cells, thus leaving almost just the antenna at the end. In this configuration, less 
heat is generated, saving power of the unused coolers, and the radio resources are much better 
used. 
Other technologies, that are not strictly 5G will be used as building blocks for 5G networks, 
especially for the management of the nodes and resources, to achieve a greener outcome and 
more optimal both for users and operators. These building blocks are: SDN (Software-Defined 
Networks), NFV (Network Function Virtualization), HetNets, Low Throughput Networks.  
5G networks will increase the number of wireless points in networks to improve the quality of 
service, meaning more wireless links and more hops in a wireless network. End-to-end 
protocols running on top can have a different behavior as most of them have been designed to 
run in wired networks. 5G’s goal is to have a wireless transmission similar to a wired one and 
optimize the usage of resources in networks, therefore the protocols running on top must also 
go through a refreshing phase to adapt to the new networks, and optimize the usage of 
resources.

2.3. Summary 
The evolution of wireless networks has triggered a higher demand for faster transmission with 
low latency. LTE made a huge impact in the field and still leaves a wide area for customization, 
which will be exploited in LTE Advanced. Upcoming new technologies will have even higher 
requirements towards wireless networks and the 5G concept has been introduced and its goal 
is to cope with the even higher forecasted demand. This chapter has only discussed the key 
enablers for the evolution of wireless networks on an architectural and physical level. However, 
with the increasing number of cells and the multihop architecture of the network, the 
applications and protocols running on top will also need to be reviewed, amongst others the 
TCP protocol, currently used in 90% of the network traffic.

QoS and protocols 
 
Network traffic is carried by several protocols, each one operating at a different layer and 
processed at different points of the network. In mobile networks, the most essential protocols 
run on the MAC layer, which takes care of the proper transmission of the data to the final device 
from the base stations. To pass through the whole network, the key protocols run in the TCP/IP 
layers. This traffic can be decoupled into several classes based on the applications running on 
top. Some are completely insensitive and will not require a special routing scheme through the 
network, like browsing, but it will still be better if the data manages to get through the network 
quickly. Other applications require a special treatment like video services, since the content is 
consumed in real time. Several mechanisms have been installed to take care of the traffic and 
guarantee the appropriate QoS to every user session. 
3.1. QoS mechanisms 
Since 5G networks are about improvement of the wireless networks, the Quality of Service is 
also taken into consideration as a measure of the changes and the improvement of the service 
provisioning. Quality of service is defined in TS22.105 as the ‘collective effect of service 
performances which determine the degree of satisfaction of a user of a service’ [18]. QoS has 
no quantitative measure since it relies on the experience of the user, which is totally subjective. 
However, it can be related to some measurable parameters of the network and those are [19]: 
 
• 
latency – time necessary for a packet to go to its destination server and come back; in 
this case we measure the total performance of all the elements in the underlying 
network 
• 
throughput – total amount of bits that can be transmitted over the network by all of 
the users or one user for heavy services 
• 
delays in the network – delays introduced by the architecture of the elements in the 
network for instance the buffers in the base stations 
• 
effective throughput – total amount of bits sent in a given time, without counting 
the overhead and acknowledgments

5G aims at optimizing all of these parameters as much as possible. Current networks have 
already a few mechanisms installed at different levels for the provisioning of QoS to the users. 
Those can be separated into 4 categories: IntServ, DiffServ, MPLS, protocol level (end-
to-end) and network dimensioning. [19] Each of these mechanisms operate at a different 
layer of the network protocol stack (OSI model – Open Systems Interconnection). Depending 
on the layer, each mechanism has a different impact on the end result, but has also its 
limitations in terms of customization, usually the lower in the stack we are, the harder it is to 
apply some changes. 
3.2. Network dimensioning 
Perhaps the easiest way to improve the QoS is a good dimensioning of the network and avoid 
any kind of bottlenecks, in the fronthaul and in the backhaul [20]. A good coverage that also 
guarantees capacity can save some time and remove the need of installing additional 
mechanisms to handle the traffic overload. But as the traffic grows due to more bandwidth 
consuming services, the network will always need at some point more capacity and the 
drawback of network dimensioning is its poor elasticity. The installation of new base stations 
needs to be smart, to avoid the interferences and let the user have a better experience [21]. 
3.2.1. Intserv 
IntServ [22] (integrated services) is based on a resource reservation principle – resources of 
the network (bandwidth, buffers) are specially setup to achieve the connection parameters that 
have been requested by the user. For this purpose, IntServ uses a specific protocol called RSVP 
(Resource Reservation Protocol). RSVP is a transport layer protocol working on top of the IP 
layer and requesting resources for one way communication (simplex flows). It is a control 
protocol, therefore nothing else runs on top of it (like ICMP or IGMP). RSVP carries only data 
related to the reservation done by the user, all the QoS parameters and policy control 
parameters used by the nodes in the network. After the client has received the information 
about the intermediate routers on the path, it starts configuring them with the RSVP messages. 
Once the flow has been setup, each packet of the sender is verified by the routers and treated 
accordingly, based on the policies set (drop, forward, etc.). In this case, it is the application that 
sets the requirements for the routers [22]. IntServ is mostly implemented in core networks, 
therefore we will not go into details.

3.3. DiffServ 
DiffServ [22] (Differentiated Services) is an architecture where the traffic is differentiated into 
classes and the network provides a priority based treatment depending on the class of the 
packet. For instance, packets with audio or video traffic will have a higher priority compared 
to simple file transmission packets. The class of the packet is specified by the DSCP (DiffServ 
Code Point), specified in the headers of IP packets. There are three types of per hop behavior: 
best effort, expedited forwarding, assured forwarding. In the best effort case, no delivery and 
latency guarantees are given. Expedited forwarding assures low latency, low loss and a certain 
bandwidth. This type of traffic should not be buffered and have a similar rate at the ingrees 
and outgress. Assured Forwarding will take care of the congestion in some part and 
implements queuing mechanisms for this purpose like RED (dropping packets from buffer 
queues based on statistical probabilities) or WRED (RED extended to the use of weights for 
the drop policies). The policies are set by a network administrator, who logs into the router and 
configures it. 
 
3.4. MPLS 
MPLS [23] (Multiprotocol Label Switching) is a data transport technology, independent of the 
protocols running on top. Its located in the 2.5 OSI layer (not MAC, not IP) and is based on a 
label switching mechanism. Each incoming packet at an MPLS router is assigned a label, 
redirecting it to the next router, based on the destination of the packet. It goes through a 
predefined path, specially setup for it. MPLS marks the traffic that comes to the routers with a 
label equivalent to the level of QoS. This label is later used by the router to determine the output 
port to the next router. MPLS can be used for both Diffserv and Intserv; the edge routers make 
the lookup to determine the path of the packet (and the appropriate label) and this decision is 
based on the service level of the packet; the packet is then routed in the network based on its 
class like in IntServ. 
Beside network node mechanisms, the user equipment needs also to adjust the transmission 
based on the traffic in the network. For an end device, the only way to tell that something is 
wrong in the network is through the timing of the packets (arrival time). The arrival time 
should have quite a decent value, if all of the nodes do not send as much data as they wish. If

3.5. TCP 
The QoS can be provided on an end to end level basis, using the transport layer. The transport 
layer is a shared resource since it is accessed and used by elements from the access network, 
the core and the end nodes. Therefore, mechanisms implemented at this level would have to 
be harmonized to achieve an optimal performance. Currently, two protocols are used at the 
transport layer in networks: TCP and UDP. The UDP protocol has little overhead and sends 
the data as is into the network, without any algorithm ensuring a reliable delivery of the data. 
Therefore, this protocol is mostly used in real-time applications like audio/video streaming, 
gaming. Thanks to the lack of additional data in the packet and algorithms, the latency is quite 
low in the transmission. The UDP protocol is also used for services and short queries, which 
do not need a whole connection setup to work. Then, a simple timeout aware mechanism is 
enough for a successful query. 
3.5.1. TCP’s data transmission 
TCP [20] is more complex but more reliable for the transmission of data. TCP establishes a 
point-to-point, full duplex connection between two nodes to send the data. Due to additional 
overhead in the protocols header and the procedures to set up the connection (three-way 
handshake), the latency is quite high in the transmission – this is without counting the TLS 
procedure for secure channels. Therefore, this protocol is more suited for file transmission like 
for example HTTP traffic. The reliable transmission control is based on two things: a 
congestion control mechanism and segment retransmission. The segment retransmission is a 
simple stop and wait mechanism; if a segment is not received by the end node (no ACK sent to 
the server), the transmission is stopped and the segment is sent again until it arrives 
successfully to the receiver. 
The congestion control helps to avoid high packet drop due to the traffic overhead in the 
network. It helps in achieving an efficient resource usage and quick failure recovery, most 
valued from the user perspective. This congestion control consists of the following elements: 
the Congestion Window (cwnd), the Additive Increase and the Multiplicative Decrease. To this 
we have the following algorithms: Slow Start, Congestion Avoidance, Fast Retransmit and Fast 
Recovery. The transmission starts with the Slow Start, where the sender sends the data 
segments using the maximum possible size. The cwnd size is doubled every time a segment is 
received successfully. Hence, the size of the cwnd (and the amount of data sent) increases 
exponentially with each RTT. This continues until the cwnd reaches a threshold set at the 
beginning of the connection – the slow start threshold (ssthresh). Then, the connection moves 
into Congestion Avoidance mode and the cwnd is increased by one MSS and continues until 
packet loss is detected by the receiver. An error is detected whenever more than one ACKs are 
received for a given packet. The lost packet is quickly resent to the end node and this 
corresponds to the Fast Retransmit. Just after that, the sender moves into Fast Recovery mode. 
The receiver indicates to the sender that it can still receive packets, and the connection can still

be up. At this moment the cwnd is reduced by half and its size increases by one after each 
packet has been acknowledged.  
 
 
 
For each segment sent by the source, a timeout is associated with it – the RTO mechanisms -  
specifying the time at which an ACK is expected from the receiver. If a packet doesn’t reach the 
destination in time, the timer is reset to double the first value. For the next ACKs, the value is 
doubled until it reaches 64seconds. The expected time is computed based on the variance of 
the RTT, to avoid too early timeouts [24]. Once a timeout is triggered and the packet is 
considered lost, the connection goes into slow start mode, reducing the congestion window to 
1 and halving the slow start threshold.

3.5.2. Other TCP mechanisms 
To avoid the switching between Congestion Avoidance and Slow Start, several mechanisms 
have been studied, that enable a faster loss detection and trigger Fast Retransmit to halve the 
window before the RTO timeout. Other mechanisms, make the comeback to Slow Start more 
controlled and based on the delay of the packets. Currently, no TCP implementation 
implements all of these mechanisms – each TCP version has its own set of congestion 
avoidance tricks.

Forward RTO Recovery [25] (F-RTO) is a mechanism that helps in avoiding performance 
decrease, because of spurious RTOs. A spurious RTO happens, when an ACK arrives right after 
an RTO timer has gone out of time. Thus the connection goes into slow start and retransmits 
the least unacked fragments although there are none. In F-RTO, the window is not reset 
directly after an RTO, but we wait for the first ACK after a retransmission. If the ACK is the 
delayed one, the window is halved and we continue transmitting the data. Otherwise the 
standard RTO procedure is enabled.  
Hybrid slow start [26] is a variation of the standard slow start, which additionally considers 
the delay when it’s finding the right window size to go into congestion avoidance mode. As a 
reminder, the slow start algorithm is triggered after a loss detection due to a timeout. The 
congestion window is set to a minimal value (1 MSS), the slow start threshold (ssthresh) is set 
to half the congestion window at the moment of the timeout.  
Then, during the transmission, the congestion window is grown until the ssthresh at an 
exponential rate – for each ACK received the congestion window is doubled (the size of the 
window is increased by the size of the packet). In the hybrid slow start, the RTT is evaluated 
and checked against a threshold value, which is adjusted based on the RTT also and cannot be 
higher than 16ms and lower than 2ms. If the RTT is correct, the congestion window size is 
checked against a default minimum value. If both values are fine, the TCP connection goes 
from slow start to congestion avoidance, and the ssthresh is set to the current congestion 
window size. The purpose of this approach is to avoid switching to congestion avoidance in 
high latency networks, where timeouts occur quite often and packet losses can be sudden. 
Proportional rate reduction [27] is a technique from the packet pacing category. Some of the 
recovery mechanisms in TCP like Fast recovery are said to be to harmful for other concurrent 
transmissions in the network and the connection recovering. Because of the burstiness of the 
algorithms, the packets that are retransmitted are quite often lost because of the adverse 
conditions in the network. Proportional rate reduction controls the size of the packets being 
sent out by the regulation of the send interval. If the congestion window is bigger than the 
number of bytes in flight (bytes sent but not yet acknowledged) and we are still awaiting some 
ACKs, the sender will have to wait until the ACKs are received. Otherwise, the data can be 
transmitted as soon as possible. However, when the congestion window is not higher than the 
data sent, we calculate the possible send window and send data if it is big enough. Thus the 
window adjustments are much smoother. [28] 
Early retransmit [29] is a standard TCP algorithm used towards the end of the transmission, 
when there are packet losses but no newer data to send. In a normal situation, packet losses 
can be deduced from the RTO timers and 3 duplicate ACKs. However, duplicate ACKs cannot 
be seen when there is no more data to send and therefore everything depends on RTOs – 
really slow. Therefore, early retransmit lets us deduce losses faster by reducing the number of 
duplicate ACKs required and by setting loss timers to 1.25RTT – after this time the packet is 
considered to be lost.  
NewReno Fast Recovery [30, 31] enables the sender to slow down the transmission, when a 
loss has been detected, without going into slow start which is much more efficient and scales 
better for multiple concurrent connections. Instead of waiting for a timeout to happen, the 
detection of packet losses is done through duplicate ACKs. Once the sender receives 3 duplicate 
ACKs from the receiver, it considers the packet lost and halves the congestion window after 
retransmitting the lost packet. Then, for each incoming duplicate ACK, the window is enlarged 
by one segment. Once, the duplicate ACKs disappear, the connection goes back into congestion 
avoidance.

TCP Cubic [32] is one of the newest congestion mechanisms implemented in TCP and is said 
to have the least aggressive window update function, uses the resources the most efficiently 
and scales well. Cubic is based on a cubic function to get the values of the window size (like the 
name indicates). While the maximum window size hasn’t been reached yet, it is increased after 
the reception without losses of an ACK. The window is updated based on the time from the last 
loss event, the RTT, a factor β determining the increase rate and the window size before the 
loss event. When the window reaches its previous size (before a loss event or a default value in 
the case of a new connection), it switches to TCP mode, making it more friendly to other TCP 
connections in the network. If no loss has been detected for a while, it means that the 
conditions in the network are favorable and the window can start growing at a faster pace. This 
guarantees the scalability of the protocol. If a loss is detected, the value of the current window 
is saved, and the window is decreased by a factor β and the slow start threshold is reduced too. 
Then the whole process starts again. During every update, the size of a standard TCP window 
(using other algorithms) is estimated based on the ACK count and is used if this value is higher 
than the one obtained from the Cubic function (faster transport). 
Finally, the TLP – Tail Loss Probes [33]– a mechanism used to avoid RTOs and trigger fast 
retransmit and recovery before it. The idea is to send the last unacknowledged packets after 
2*RTTs of no ACK or a new packet. In both cases, the result will be a duplicate ACK and will 
kick in fast recovery. Hence, the connection will not move into slow start and the window size 
will only be decreased by a certain factor. To trigger TLP, when data is being transmitted a TLP 
timer is set to max(10ms, 2*SRTT), where SRTT is the computed RTT, and should go off before 
the RTO timer. Once the timer goes off, the sender checks if there are packets that need to be 
retransmitted, otherwise sends the sent data twice. However, TLP doesn’t mark any packet as 
lost – it just forces a retransmission, when nothing has been received for too long.

3.5.3. TCP’s troubles 
We have to keep in mind that TCP has been designed for wired networks, where the main cause 
of packet loss is related to congestion in the network, therefore the strategy employed makes 
sense. Thanks to the low BER in the transmission, a congestion in the network can be the only 
explanation of a packet loss or timeout. However, in wireless environments, packet losses can 
be related to bit errors happening on the air interface. The wireless channel is not perfect and 
signal fadings and the user mobility have a big impact on the transmission. Although 
mechanisms like ARQ and HARQ have been introduced to deal with it, the bit errors are 
replaced with a bigger delay in the delivery. For instance, HARQ introduces a delay of 2ms for 
each retransmission done [1]. Other factors add up to this delay in LTE like the CoMP method, 
which requires a coordination of base stations and therefore a transmission of the data between 
them. Short disconnections in the transmission have shown that they can disturb the 
transmission even for 1s, due to the RTOs, which are triggered after a while [34]. 
In addition, current mobile traffic models show, that users usually have short and small traffic 
sessions [35]. Most of the time, we use the phone to browse the Internet and this translates to 
small HTTP sessions, started every few minutes to download a few MBs of data (website 
content is usually text with a few images). As an example, Youtube videos are downloaded via 
HTTP requests, each creating a new session. In terms of TCP, this means that most of the time 
we stay in the slow start part of the connection [36]. 
 
FIGURE 14. YOUTUBE VIDEO DOWNLOAD ANATOMY [37] 
Furthermore, the design of the application protocol can also have an impact on the 
performance of the transmission. If the TCP sessions are used inefficiently, the user might also 
end up most of the time in the Slow Start phase. This is the case in HTTP 1.0, where each of 
the elements are downloaded in a separate TCP session. HTTP 1.1 has improved this state, by 
using one persistent connection to download everything. HTTP 2.0 finally goes even further 
and uses several parallel connections for this matter. 
All of these elements contribute to the decreased performance of TCP over wireless networks. 
Thus, there is a real need for a change in the design of this protocol and make it consider the 
wireless part of the networks.

3.6. Summary 
Several strategies can be employed to deal with the imperfections in the networks and improve 
the quality experienced by the user. However, most of them, especially those installed in the 
core network and base stations, require a special setup, which translates into additional fees 
for the user. Other mechanisms, like those in TCP, make sure that the network resources are 
used fairly and properly by all of the users. The downside of TCP is its design, specific and 
optimal in wired networks, but poor in the wireless part. Different solutions have been studied 
in this matter and will be presented in the next chapter.

Improving the end-
to-end transmission 
 
It has been decided that we will investigate an improvement of TCP at the transport layer, 
because of it’s end-to-end nature, requiring the least changes in the network, even if 
improvements could be done at every layer. In this chapter we represent solutions currently 
found to deal with TCP’s problems in wireless environment. At the end of the chapter, the QUIC 
protocol is introduced, which will be studied deeply in the next chapter. 
4.1. Taxonomy of solutions 
Newer solutions are necessary for wireless networks. Some approaches have already been 
done, and can be separated into the following groups: link layer solutions, split connections, 
TCP modifications and new protocols.  
The link layer solutions help in quickly recovering from a failure to guarantee the delivery of 
the packet to the end node. If the client manages to receive it with a small delay and 
acknowledge it before the RTO kicks in, the connection won't be affected. Link layer protocols 
have a direct access to failure information, therefore they can react quickly to them. 
The split connection solutions divide the connection for each part of the network – one for the 
wireless part and one for the wired part. The base station in this case works as a proxy and 
buffers and acknowledges the data received over the wired part. This idea is said to bring the 
biggest improvements, but hits the idea of end-to-end protocol and requires modifications in 
the network nodes, which is currently expensive. 
TCP modifications try to reduce the effects of existing TCP mechanisms in wireless networks, 
but not all of them. Most of them are there to optimize TCP's behavior in general. To improve 
the transmission quality, studies have shown that a good tweaking of the TCP protocol can also 
give good results [39]. 
New protocols are usually quite difficult to design, because the mechanism that seem to work 
in the wireless part, are not necessarily optimal in the wired part. Some attempts have been 
done both, at the application and transport layer. Protocols implemented in the application 
layer, usually make use of the raw UDP protocol and add the same overhead as in TCP for 
reliable data transfers. 
4.2. Examined solutions 
a. Link layer 
At the Radio Access Level, LTE employs two mechanisms that can help a lot in lossy 
environments and recover the corrupted packets. Those are HARQ and ARQ.

HARQ is implemented in the MAC layer and has been already used before LTE and HSUPA. 
Thanks to this mechanisms, when erroneous packets arrive to the UE, it doesn’t have to be 
resent straight from the server, but instead, the base station takes care of the retransmission. 
For every packet received correctly the user has to send an acknowledgment (ACK) to the base 
station. If the packet is incorrect, a NACK message is sent and other data packets are buffered 
at the base station. Meanwhile, the incorrect packet is sent again in one of the following forms: 
same packet again, packet with new redundancy bits (using the previous packet the 
information can be decoded), packet with new modulation scheme for higher chance of 
reception. The downside of this mechanism is that the RTT can add some delay to the whole 
transmission, which can then trigger congestion mechanisms of higher level protocols. 
 
 
FIGURE 16. HARQ MECHANISM 
If the HARQ procedure fails, then the ARQ kicks in and simply retransmits the whole RLC 
frame (frame used at the MAC layer). If these two mechanisms fail, then the higher level 
protocols take care of the retransmission. Although we have a major improvement of the 
reliability of a channel, the installed mechanisms can take at least 10ms before the data is 
delivered successfully, causing protocols like TCP to react to this like to a congestion. 
Therefore, an optimal value of the counters and timers needs to be set for the best performance. 
Based on these values, higher level protocols could adjust the transmission, without the need 
of drastically cutting down the amount of bits sent, like it is the case in TCP. Thus, if we go back 
to the formula of the TCP flow, we decrease the probability of loss, which was a major factor in 
the equation, but at the small cost of higher RTT and hence RTO. However, the gains from the 
loss reduction should out-weight the losses from the delay. 
b. Split connection 
One quite successful improvement proposed is TCP snooping [40], which can have a 
performance twice as good as regular TCP on wireless links. In this concept, the base station 
plays the role of a snoop module, a TCP proxy, which intercepts the TCP traffic and buffers it 
before sending to the final node and acknowledges it in its place. If three ACKs are received 
from the mobile station, a retransmission is done from the base station and in the meantime 
other TCP packets keep coming. The Wireless TCP (WTCP) implementation of this 
mechanisms measures in addition the RTT of the wireless channel to control the sending rate.

Again, applying the improvements from this method to the formula introduced in the previous 
chapter, the average throughput is now made up of two components: the wired and the wireless 
part. We can assume, that the wired part has quite a low latency and the loss probability is also 
reasonably low. Hence, the RTT and the p value go down, making the whole ratio go up. The 
wireless fragment, even if it might have a higher latency, will now have a smaller RTT, since 
the data is only grabbed from the based station. Also, this makes the loss probability drop 
significantly. The split connection can in this way minimize the main factors limiting the TCP 
flow throughput and hence have a much better performance of the connection (reports show 
an improvement of 10% to 500%). But this comes at the cost of breaking the end-to-end 
principle of the protocol and making the solution harder to install in networks compared to a 
simple TCP modification.

c. TCP implementations 
TCP CUBIC [32] is currently the most popular implementation in Linux and Android systems. 
CUBIC has been specially made for long distance networks, where the RTT is quite high and 
so is the delay. In those networks, the classic TCP could never achieve a reasonable 
performance with a reasonable cwnd size because of the timeouts in the transmission. Regular 
TCP window growth is quite aggressive, when it comes to high latency links, because it grows 
until it hits a congestion. By the time the sender gets any info about the loss, the RTO will 
timeout and the connection goes back to slow start mode. Therefore, CUBIC introduces new 
functions to compute the size of the cwnd in case of packet failure and the linear function in 
Fast Recovery is replaced with a cubic function. It uses a window reduction factor to decrease 
the size of the window on failure. This implementation has shown to be more performant 
compared to newReno, thanks to the smoothness on loss events during the window reduction. 
Thanks to the Cubic function, which grows exponentially at the end of the function, this 
implementation has great scaling capabilities since the client quickly discovers the maximum 
supported bandwidth. The image below shows an example of TCP Cubic window dynamics.

Another solution for TCP was the introduction of ECN (Explicit Congestion Notification), a 
field in the IP header to determine whether there is a congestion or not and if yes, to reduce 
the rate of the transmission. Thus, more feedback about the network conditions are given to 
the sender, who can adjust its sending rate to avoid losses, reducing the probability of packet 
loss.

Radio Network Feedback is a similar solution and consists on giving to the end user more 
information about the conditions in the wireless access part of the connection (channel quality, 
available bandwidth). Again, the sender uses this information to adjust his sending rate and 
parameters, which then should lower the loss probability. 
One example of such an implementation is Freeze TCP [41], which focuses on the improvement 
of TCP’s behavior during short disconnections. It monitors the state of the wireless channel 
and in case of really bad conditions advertises a zero receiver window size, which makes the 
transmission stop, but not lose all of it’s connection parameters and can be later resumed from 
the same point by sending a non zero window advertisement. This solution looks promising 
and it can bring important gains for longer disconnections (for 5s it can go up to 25% gain over 
standard TCP). However, for short ones (less than 1s) it is less than 5% gain in terms of transfer 
time [42]. 
TCP Westwood [43] is a rate-based implementation, measuring the inter-arrival time of the 
ACKs, hence getting some information about the network conditions. It is based on the 
assumption that in uncongested paths, the packet should take the same amount of time to get 
to the receiver and comeback to the sender. When a congestion is detected in the network, slow 
start threshold and the congestion window are set to a value computed based on the RTT 
measured. This method proves to be really good in wireless links, where the RTT can vary based 
on the air channel conditions. 
Finally, a pacing technique [44, 45] has been recently said to bring great improvements to TCP 
connections and this also applies to wired connections. The idea is pretty similar to the TCP 
Westwood implementation, where the sender evaluates the RTT of the network and then

adjusts the sending time and the RTO based on these values. This technique has the advantage 
of improving the utilization of the link and reducing the packet loss probability. 
Other than that, trials of customization of the TCP protocol parameters have been done, to 
estimate the best parameters for given scenarios like browsing or video streaming. Also, newer 
versions of TCP have been implemented with these parameters set to satisfy as many cases as 
possible. 
d. New protocols 
Finally, there were also attempts to modify the UDP protocol to make it more reliable but 
without sacrificing its low latency. A few of the approaches, like RUDP (Reliable UDP) [46], 
UDT (UDP-based Transfer protocol) [47] have tried to challenge TCP, the latter showing some 
improvements over TCP in terms of bandwidth usage thanks to its mechanisms, highly based 
on the RTT of the connection, from which it gets most of the information about the link. A 
more recent approach by Google, was to modify the session layer of the protocol and add some 
basic mechanisms there. The protocol they came up with is called QUIC [48] and according to 
there current tests, show a lot of promise in the future. Therefore, we will investigate it more 
in the next chapter. In general, all of these implementations still highly rely on TCP’s transfer 
principles like the Congestion Window, acknowledgments, timers and inter-arrival based 
bandwidth estimation. 
4.3. QUIC protocol capacity 
The QUIC a protocol is a new invention from Google, that has been released in 2013 and is still 
under development. It falls into the new protocols category and is based on the UDP protocol. 
The session and data management is done in the application layer. The main idea behind QUIC 
is to reduce the delay in TCP, while still keeping its congestion avoidance features and 
transmission reliability. Therefore, QUIC gathers all the best practices discovered in TCP and 
puts them into one protocol [49]. Google hasn't created this protocol specially for the wireless 
networks and focused more on the delay problem, so we don't expect it to excel in this field. 
However, some of the features implemented have been studied in TCP modifications for 
wireless and have been evaluated to bring some improvements, like the pacing of the packets 
or the bandwidth estimation. Of course, there might be a bit of marketing behind the 
presentation of the protocol, so we would like to test in a simulation of a wireless environment 
to determine the pros and cons of its behavior in these networks. 
From the TCP model formula, we could predict an improvement of the throughput since all of 
the mechanisms discussed above aim to reduce the packet loss probability which is the main 
limiting factor. The NACK trick, reporting more ACKs in one packet can improve the 
throughput slightly. However, the RTT and RTO might be hit by some of the mechanisms like 
the pacing of the packets. An evaluation of the average RTT additions should be taken into 
consideration, to make the model more aware of these mechanisms.

5.1. Overall structure 
a. General remarks 
The idea behind the QUIC protocol was to make use of the advantages of UDP to create a super 
fast and reliable transport protocol [50]. Thanks to its raw structure – UDP contains only the 
source and destination ports – it can be a highly customizable protocol, if the changes are 
applied in layers higher than transport. To make the release of the protocol faster, Google has 
decided that it is the best solution, since making a new transport protocol from scratch would 
require major OS updates, which on average takes at least 5 years. Whereas in this case, the 
update is only required at the software layer. 
QUIC’s structure and functionality is equivalent to TCP, TLS and HTTP/2 taken together. Its 
advantage over TCP are the following: Low connection establishment latency, flexible 
congestion control, multiplexing without head of line blocking, security through encryption 
and authentication, multiple streams and forward error correction. 
As seen in the previous chapter, the idea behind the usage of the latest and most promising 
techniques discovered in TCP to detect losses are proactively counter them, is to reduce the 
packet loss probability.

b. Packet structure and headers 
QUIC’s header consist of the following 4 fields [51]: public flags, connection ID, QUIC version 
and Packet number. The public flags give more information to the receiver about the 
transmission and what kind of information will be sent in the packet. For instance, the 
public_flag_version bit will tell whether the protocol is currently in the phase of connection 
establishment or not or the public_flag_reset will tell whether the protocol will be resending 
an incorrect frame. Other bits of the public header flags refer to the status of the connection 
ID, the structure of the frame (data or correction). The connection ID is a 64 bit random 
number computed by the client as an identifier of the connection. The advantage of such a 
design is that it is not affected by IP address changes. The user can roam and switch networks 
but the connection will still be setup, as long as the data is being transmitted. The QUIC version 
is a 32 bit tag representing the version of the protocol used for the transmission. This field is 
only present if the flag version bit is set to 1. The packet number identifies the packet itself and 
the data currently being sent. The first packet sent by the user should have a packet number 
equal to one and this value should be increased by one in each of the next packets. This number 
is also used for encryption and therefore it should have a 64 bit representation, otherwise the 
connection shall be closed.

kinds of packets. The special packets are: the version negotiation packets and the public reset 
packet. The regular packets are frame packets and FEC packets. QUIC tries to avoid IP 
fragmentation therefore the packets are of the size of the MTU. 
The version negotiation packet has the version flag set to one and contains a list of supported 
QUIC versions. This list is used by the server during connection establishment to determine 
the version used for the connection. After that, the version cannot be changed.  
The public reset frame is used to close the connection, just like the TCP RST message. This 
packet contains the following values: the reset tag and a tag value map containing a public reset 
nonce proof – an unsigned integer – and the rejected packet number – a 64 bit packet number.

5.2. QUIC transmission mechanisms 
c. Connection setup 
Before data can be sent a connection must be created between two endpoints [52]. QUIC is 
supposed to be a low latency protocol, surpassing TCP even at the connection establishment 
level to reduce the latency as much as possible. To setup a connection, the client needs two 
things from the server: a configuration file and a source address token. The configuration file 
contains all the information about the encryption of the connection (public keys, algorithms 
used), whereas the source IP token is used for authenticity check – once the server assigns one 
==PAGE:38==
38  
 
 
 
to the client based on his IP address, the client needs to use it in all subsequent CHLO 
messages. Thus, this procedure prevents other third party clients from interfering in the 
communication. Three scenarios are possible in the setup of a QUIC connection:  
• 
First ever contact with the server, the client does not hold any files are values from the 
server 
• 
N-th contact with the server after a long idle period. The client holds all the values abd 
files, but they might be outdated. 
• 
N-th contact with the server after a short idle period. The client has already contacted 
the server not so long ago, therefore the connection is still “fresh”. 
In the first scenario, the client has never seen the server before and sends a CHLO requesting 
a source-address token by omitting the source-address token (SNI) parameter in the CHLO 
message (for later authenticity check). At the same time, the client negotiates the version of the 
protocol that it wants to use by setting the version parameter (VER) to the currently used one. 
Several packets are sent at this point to deal with the eventual losses in the network. Once the 
server gets the message, it checks the values of the parameters and generates a source-address 
token for the client. At the same time, it checks the version of the QUIC protocol and if it is 
outdated or recently released, the server will reply also with a list of supported versions. This 
package of information is sent in a REJ (rejection) message to the client, which then updates 
all of the missing values in the CHLO message. Next, the client requests certificate chains for 
later server-side authentication and sends a CHLO with all the values sent in the previous step 
by the server contained both in the REJ message and the server config file. The server replies 
with the certificate chains and validates the selected version of the QUIC protocol, if it hasn’t 
been done in the first step. Finally, the client sends a normal CHLO message with parameters 
related to the created connection (initial congestion window, send mode). The CHLO is directly 
followed by the data of the client. Once, the server gets the CHLO, it replies with a SHLO, 
approving the connection that has been created. This procedure takes “2-RTT” (3 RTT’s if we 
consider all messages, but this is Google’s terminology) and is the worst case scenario but at 
the same time can be the most common during everyday browsing, when in search of data on 
several websites. However, in the case of everyday routine browsing (Facebook, Youtube, other 
info or social sites), this scenario will be less probable. 
In the second scenario, the client already has all the values necessary to establish a connection 
with the server, but some of them are outdated like the certificate chains and the source-
address token and need to be updated by the server. The client sends several normal CHLO 
messages with all the connection parameters, checked at the server-side and updated in a REJ 
message. The client can then send an updated CHLO followed by his data and approved server 
side by a SHLO message. This procedure takes “1-RTT” (1.5 RTT in fact). 
The final and fastest scenario requires only “0-RTT” (1/2 RTT at lest because we have to send 
the packet), because the client holds a recent configuration of a connection and hence does not 
need to receive anything from the server. It simply sends a CHLO message with all the 
connection parameters and the server replies with an SHLO.

d. Connection termination 
There are three ways to shutdown a QUIC connection [51]: explicit, implicit and sudden (error 
related). 
In an explicit shutdown, one of the sides sends a CONNECTION_CLOSE frame to the peer, 
directly shutting down the connection. This frame can be preceded by a GO_AWAY frame, 
telling the peer that the connection will end soon and no more streams will be created at both 
sides. Once all of the streams have sent a frame with the FIN flag on, the 
CONNECTION_CLOSE frame is sent. If the GO_AWAY frame is omitted and only a 
==PAGE:41==
41  
 
 
 
CONNECTION_CLOSE is sent, all working streams are closed (an error will be induced at the 
other side). 
An implicit shutdown happens, when nothing is sent over the connection for a given period 
(default is 30 seconds). This timeout period can be negotiated at connection setup and can be 
at most 10 minutes. After the timeout a CONNECTION_CLOSE frame is sent to the peer. The 
implicit shutdown can be used by setting a “silent mode” flag, when it is expensive to send an 
explicit shutdown (ex. Mobile networks) 
Finally, a PUBLIC_RESET frame can be used to reset the connection and thus close the current 
one. Just like with the CONNECTION_CLOSE, all streams are stopped without exceptions. 
e. Data transmission 
The data transmission in QUIC is similar to TCP, since QUIC implements all of the state-of-
the-art congestion and loss mechanisms used in TCP. However, QUIC differs from TCP at 
certain points, like different packet numbering method, usage of NACKs, corrections based on 
the delay, packet pacing and bandwidth estimation. 
QUIC uses also two terms defined in TCP: the congestion window and the receiver window. 
The congestion window is sender side and its size depends on the current traffic in the network. 
The receiver window is receiver side (like the name tells) and depends only on the physical 
possibilities of the device (buffer size, processing speed). 
Currently, QUIC implements the following congestion mechanisms: F-RTO, RTO, 
proportional rate reduction, early retransmit, hybrid slow start, new Reno fast recovery, TLP, 
FACK and TCP Cubic congestion window. 
Spurious RTO: In QUIC, once a retransmission has been triggered, for each ACK we check the 
largest observed value of the ACK frame 
Early retransmit: QUIC protocol relies mostly on the second condition; it checks whether it has 
packets to retransmit and has reached the end of the transmission (largest sent equals largest 
observed) and if yes, then the timers are set to 1.25RTT. 
NewReno Fast Recovery: In QUIC, this mechanism is slightly different, because the sender 
does not wait for duplicate ACKs, but for each packet counts the gap between the largest 
observed and the missing packet number reported in the ACK frame. If this gap is greater than 
a given threshold (default is 3), the packet is considered missing and is marked for 
retransmission. This technique is called FACK (Forward Acknowledgment) [53] and is also 
used in TCP. Other than that, QUIC has a time based loss detection – it compares the arrival 
time of the largest observed to the estimated arrival of the packet computed from the sent time 
and the average RTT. If this value is smaller (the packet should have been received earlier) 
then the packet is marked as lost. The window reduction factor is also different, since QUIC 
uses the Cubic congestion mechanism. During the recovery, the packets are paced using the 
proportional rate reduction algorithm. 
Congestion window: QUIC has been implemented with two congestion mechanisms: Reno and 
Cubic. The default one is Cubic, but it can be changed during the connection setup.

5.3. Further improvements of the protocol 
Because Google wants to have a fast release and deployment of its protocol (deployment in 2 
years), it uses higher layers to improve UDP’s behavior. However, to make use of the full 
potential of current mobile devices and their multiple network interfaces, the protocol would 
need to be implemented at the Kernel level, just like MPTCP. It would be then possible to send 
the data over other interfaces, if one of them is stuck due to other traffic in the network – 
offload from WiFi to 4G (or 5G) or the other way around. The only problem is that such an 
implementation takes more time in the deployment phase, around 5 years according to Google, 
based on the results of MPTCP deployment. This is caused by the necessity of upgrading an OS 
when changes are done at the Kernel level, which is always done reluctantly by mobile users – 
usually newer OS means newer functions meaning more CPU usage leading to performance 
decrease of the devices. 
Currently, the performance of the FEC mechanism is also evaluated and an optimal value for 
the FEC packets and the redundant information is investigated. Different schemes are also 
being looked at to have good advantage over duplicate ACKs and retransmissions. 
The only application layer protocol supported by QUIC currently is HTTP 2.0 (SPDY) for which 
it has been optimized, but works on other protocols are currently ongoing [54]. 
5.4. Summary 
The QUIC protocol gathers all the functionalities of TCP using the UDP protocol as underlying 
transport. All the congestion mechanisms are present, enabling a coexistence of both protocols 
in a network. In addition, QUIC removes any kind of delays where possible (handshake, 
retransmissions), making it faster and more suitable for current network models. The packet 
pacing mechanism makes it more stable in harsh data transmission conditions, giving it an 
upper hand over TCP on the paper. In the next chapter we will introduce the evaluation of the 
protocol through a simulation model and tool to test it against existing protocols.

6.2. Model design 
The model has been created taking into consideration its connection oriented design and its 
interaction with the application layer. Unlike UDP, where the data from the application layer 
is simply forwarded to be encapsulated in a datagram, QUIC’s model has to process data from 
both the network and the application layer. Because QUIC will need to keep track of the 
transmission for each node in the case of the server, the connection management and the 
communication with the app and the UDP module will be done separately. A major part in 
QUIC is its ability to create a secure logical channel without too much overhead, but since our 
main concern is its performance in wireless networks in terms of data transmission, the 
security features have been simplified as much as possible. Some of the frames introduced have 
also been omitted as they did not bring any valuable performance improvements. Below are 
some assumptions that have been taken to create the simplest possible model: 
• 
The opening sequence, where the certificate and IP tokens are verified have been 
replaced with a simple expiry check, where integer values are checked against the 
simulation time 
• 
The closure of the connection is done without stop waiting frames, but based on a FIN 
bit in the stream frame. 
• 
The stop waiting frame has been omitted and the data exchanged in this frame has been 
replaced with the direct access to the missing packet list of the receiver. 
• 
The entropy and FEC feature of the protocol has been omitted 
• 
The variable size of the packets has been replaced by a constant, average value of each 
frame 
• 
No more than one frame can be put inside one packet. If the frame is smaller than the 
MSS it is padded, which the equivalent of using a padding frame 
• 
All frames not yet used even in the real implementation have been omitted (ping, 
blocked, congestion). The design of the model, however, enables to add them easily 
later for further studies 
• 
The data in the ACK is put explicitly instead of using differences to the previous 
number, used mainly to decrease the size of the packet. 
• 
An ACK is sent every two data packets, right after a loss and at the end of the 
transmission 
• 
The delay of the connection setup will not be measured, hence an average scenario of 
1-RTT will be considered and reconnections to the server will be simulated through the 
usage of the previous parameters of a connection. 
These simplifications will enable a higher focus on the data transmission and congestion 
control in the network, which will in turn be used to determine the QoS improvements brought 
by the protocol.

6.3.3. Protocol implementation 
The task was to fit in a new process model into the existing flow. Since QUIC is a transport 
layer, end-to-end protocol, only the UE and the server had to have their models changed 
accordingly (see figure 31). Therefore, the QUIC protocol’s process model has been inserted 
between the UDP process model (or module) and the TPAL process model. 
a. Application layer process 
The TPAL module reflects the commands that can be issued at the application layer. Those are 
mainly opening a connection, sending data, receiving data, closing a connection and aborting 
a connection. These actions are converted into the following commands: OPEN, DATA SEND, 
DATA RECEIVE, CLOSE, ABORT, STATUS. These commands are then forwarded to the 
appropriate module, which can then switch its state depending on the command it receives. 
The OPEN command will trigger the creation of a child process and a mapping of the 
connection ID to the specific connection process. Then, in the child process, everything related 
to the connection will be initialized and the child will issue a call to the UDP process, to open 
a connection on the given port. The SEND command passes a piece of data to be transmitted 
to the QUIC manager. The RECEIVE command has an administrative purpose, because it 
validates the reception of the piece of data passed by the transport layer to the application. The  
close and abort functions are called, when no more data has to be sent/received or something 
happened at the application layer (application timeout) respectively. The STATUS command 
is here for additional feedback from the transport layer about the status of the connection. Its 
purpose is to gather statistics for the application layer.

b. QUIC’s processes 
QUIC’s process model has been based on the TCP process, since both protocols have a similar, 
connection oriented structure. Hence the process itself has also been split into 2 processes: the 
manager process and the connection process. 
All the session management and request processing is done in the manager process. It works 
just like the TPAL module and acts as an interfacing process between the TPAL and the 
connection process.  
Apart from handling the requests received from the application layer, the QUIC manager also 
takes care of events received from the outer network, received by the UDP process and gathers 
statistics about the incoming and outgoing traffic. Here is a description of each state of the 
manager: 
INIT: Initialization state. All the state values of the process are instantiated and the module is 
registered in the global registry, to make a mapping in the simulation between the name of the 
protocol and this module. Furthermore, the ID’s of the neighboring modules are discovered to 
map the input and output streams of this module. This state is called on its own at the 
beginning of the simulation.  
ACTIVE: This state works as a transition between all other states, which are all forced states. 
It grabs all the incoming events and checks their type to make the proper next transition. No 
other processing is done. 
==PAGE:49==
49  
 
Simulation and validation 
 
OPEN: Creates a new connection based on the remote address and local/remote ports. In TCP 
terms, it is the equivalent of creating a transport control block (TCB). If a previous mapping 
can be reused, the transport layer will assign it to the connection. Otherwise a new connection 
is created and all of the connection parameters are passed to the child connection process. 
 
 
FIGURE 32. QUIC MANAGER MODEL (LEFT) AND QUIC CONNECTION MODEL (RIGHT) 
RECEIVE: It is a state used to handle the RECEIVE command received from the application 
layer. Because it is an administrative call, only statistics are updated in this state and nothing 
else happens. 
SEND: Reacts to the SEND command from the application layer, grabs the data passed and 
transfers it to the child process, which takes care of inserting it into the sending buffer. Stats 
are updated and control is given to the child, which will take care of sending the received data. 
CLOSE: Closes the connection after the reception of a CLOSE command from the TPAL layer. 
Updates the stats and gives control to the child. 
ABORT: Same as the CLOSE command but reacts to the ABORT command of the application. 
SEG_RCV: Reacts to data received from the outer network. Whenever a data packet is received, 
the manager calls the connection process associated with the connection ID of the packet and 
passes the data to it. Traffic statistics are updated and the no further processing is done. 
STATUS: Called to get the status of a given connection in response to a request from the 
application layer. 
The core functionalities of the transmission like packet encapsulation, ACK sending/receiving 
and congestion window management is done by the connection process. The state machine in 
the QUIC connection process has been stripped down to the most essential states that make 
the protocol run. Those correspond to the four possible states of a connection : initialization, 
listen for a connection, handshake, connection established, connection closure. The state 
machine could be more established to truly represent the behavior of the QUIC protocol, but 
this is not the goal of this work. Below is a description of each state. 
==PAGE:50==
50  
 
Simulation and validation 
 
INIT: The initialization process takes care of creating a connection process with all the 
necessary components to communicate with other modules and initializes all the variables 
related to the connection (window size, timeout length, etc.). Once the initialization is done, 
the connection moves to its listen state or to the connection setup state (CHLO_SENT), 
depending on the type of connection that was asked to setup (passive/active). If the connection 
is passive, a socket is opened for incoming traffic and nothing else happens. Otherwise, a CHLO 
is sent to establish a connection with the remote node. 
LISTEN: State entered, when a passive connection has been established. The server side of a 
connection enters this state and waits for incoming CHLO messages. When received, 
depending on the values of the client tokens and the certificates, the server will reply with a 
REJ or a SHLO (see previous chapter). If it is a SHLO, the server moves to the established state 
of the connection. 
CHLO_SENT: The client waits for the reply of the server. If it receives anything else than a 
REJ or SHLO message, it treats it as an error in the connection setup. The connection should 
be closed if something like this happens. If the client has quite recent values of both the token 
and the server certificate, it can start sending some data to the server, if anyting has been put 
in the buffers. Currently, this has not yet been implemented due to limited time, but could 
probably slightly improve the transmission rate over a given time period. 
ESTAB: After a SHLO is received and sent, both the client and the server enter the connection 
established state and start transmitting data and receiving it. The exchange of data lasts until 
a connection closure is initiated by one of the ends ie. a CLOSE frame is sent. We have omitted 
the part with the FIN bits as this has not any meaning in terms of performance, but goes in the 
field of data transmission management. 
CLOSED: Reacts to a closure signal from any of the processes and shuts down the connection, 
and destroys the connection process, after it has been cleared. 
On the transmission level, it has been decided that only the most promising features will be 
implemented ie. the Cubic congestion window, the packet pacing, fast retransmit and the 
NACKs. 
c. Implemented transport mechanisms 
Because QUIC sends always packets of full size, equal to the MSS, the Cubic congestion window 
will have a discrete value ie. in number of packets possible to send in one burst. However, the 
computation of the window itself is done using byte values and the final value is rounded up. 
This is more convenient during the sending process, because the packet number is used for the 
computation of the amount of data inflight and don’t need to go through the same mess as TCP, 
where values are computed byte-wise. 
The authentication of the UE at the server has been simulated in a simple way, through the 
manipulation of time values. The token and the certificate has been set as variables storing 
time values, corresponding to their last update. If those values are higher than a given 
threshold they are updated in a CHLO-REJ round. Otherwise, the protocol moves to the next 
state. The threshold has been set, to be manipulated for each simulation round, therefore it 
will be an input parameter at the network layer of the simulation. 
Other implemented connection related mechanisms have been implemented as described in 
the section about TCP mechanisms.

6.3.4. Model validation 
a. Test cases and methods 
Before testing the model against the TCP protocol, a validation has to be made to ensure that 
the implemented mechanisms work properly. Therefore, a series of validating simulations will 
be carried out, both with and without packet drops.  
The non packet dropping scenario will be simulated by setting up the network with lowest 
possible latency and increasing the buffer size to a really high value. Hence, eventual delays in 
the base stations should not affect the packet delivery time completely. The channel model can 
be changed to an ideal one if necessary. 
The packet dropping in OPNET can be simulated through several ways. One of them is the use 
of an OPNET specific node called the packet discarder. However, due to failures in the 
configuration of this node, which needs to be compliant with the used topology and elements, 
it has not been used. Instead, once a packet has been received at one end, the managers will be 
configured to discard the packets every so often and it will not pass it to the connection process. 
Thus, the connection process should trigger the proper mechanisms in case of packet loss 
detection. 
The packet dropping technique will be useful to test the implementation of the congestion 
window, retransmissions and NACK processing. 
To test the pacing algorithm, a similar method will be applied ie. a received packet will have its 
timestamp value purposefully increased, to fake the RTT measurements and change the pacing 
of the packet. 
The validation itself will be done in three ways: through the usage of OPNET’s debugger, 
OPNET’s console logs and statistics. 
The OPNET debugger is a tool integrated with the modeler. It let’s the user enable or disable 
breakpoints implemented in the code, analyze the content and properties of the packets and 
follow the execution of the simulation through an animation tool. We will be mostly interested 
by the possibility of inspecting the packet to determine the size and properties of its fields. 
The console log will be very useful when putting printing statements in key places of the code. 
In this implementation we will monitor the correctness of the insertion of data in the buffers, 
the size of the congestion window after loss and without loss, the processing of the NACKs and 
their creation. All these tests have been mostly done during the development phase and 
therefore only a final confirmation round will be required in the end. 
b. Simulation output 
The output of a simulation will be statistics gathered during the execution and are a key 
element of the validation, as they will be also used during the final runs confirming the system. 
Below is a list of the statistics that we consider important and would like to reuse later, during 
the measurements. 
Congestion window size: Changes in the congestion window. Whenever we catch an NACK and 
process it’s data, the changes in the window that will done right after will be saved. This will be 
a value in bytes and a value in packets, since we plan to mainly use a packet based congestion 
window in the implementation and its value will be computed based on the byte values. 
==PAGE:52==

Pacing delay: The delay measured by the packet pacing algorithm and used to compute the 
send time of the next packet. 
Application layer statistics: Beside self implemented statistics in the model, those already 
implemented in OPNET will also be used to determine whether the scenario run as planned or 
not. In the case of the application layer, in our case HTTP, the following will be monitored: 
Number of successfully downloaded pages and page elements, number of aborted connections, 
traffic sent/received (in bytes and packets per seconds), page response time. 
Lower layer statistics: the UE throughput measured at the PHY layer and the number of MAC 
retransmissions and failures will give an insight on the proper configuration of the simulation. 
If the values will be too much out of range, we will be able to infer an improper setup causing

6.4. Summary 
 
The implementation of a protocol in a simulator requires to create a model that is as close to 
the reality as possible. Simpler choices can be made if the final behavior will not be affected by 
it. In this case, the handshake procedure has been simplified to only illustrate the information 
being exchanged and the values being updated. Once the model is ready, it goes through a 
verification and validation step, where all of the implemented features are checked in different 
scenarios. The main challenges in the implementation of the QUIC protocol is to connect it to 
neighboring modules – HTTP (through TPAL) and UDP. Furthermore, because it is a 
connection oriented protocol, the spawning of new connections and their management needed 
also a bit of work. After putting the QUIC model’s implementation to the test through a 
verification procedure, which has been passed successfully, it will be used in a set of 
simulations to validate the system analyzed ie. it’s performance over wireless links.

7.1. QUIC performance testbed 
The protocol will be tested in a C-RAN configuration of a mobile network [55]. As mentioned 
previously, the protocol is evaluated as a possible candidate for 5G networks, therefore it will 
be interesting to run it in the same conditions, where 5G Radio access does more processing to 
achieve a higher performance, but this could also add some latency, which is caught by TCP as 
a congestion event. 
In this setup, the base stations of classic mobile networks are replaced by remote radio heads 
connected to a baseband unit (BBU). All the data of the user is sampled at the radio head and 
sent to the BBUs for later processing and decision making. Protocol-wise nothing really 
changes and everything works just as before – only the configuration is a slightly different and 
enables better resource usage. The BBUs are connected to a gateway and an application server, 
from where they get part of the traffic. The application server can run any application that is 
deployed on it, from simple web browsing to streaming. Since our current version of OPNET 
doesn’t support too many traffic models, the streaming application needs to be simulated 
through a more intensive web browsing (lots of images and short interval times). However, 
newer versions of OPNET (17.6) support new traffic models like Video On Demand or 
streaming. 
The configuration of the network looks as follows: 
• 
9 RHHs 
• 
1 UE per RHH 
• 
3 BBUs supporting 3 RRHs each 
• 
1 application server 
The figure above presents the exact setup of the network, a typical urban area. Since it is quite 
cumbersome to create lots of UEs in the network to simulate the high traffic, it has been 
decided that fewer UEs will be put physically, but the traffic they generate will be much higher, 
following the recommendations given in the OPNET tutorials [56]. 
All of the nodes have been assigned a movement pattern to simulate the mobility of the user. 
A premade model has been used and it’s moving the user in random directions. Not many 
additional details can be given as the profile used cannot be visualized. 
We use a model for the air channel, thus air retransmissions will be possible to see. In addition, 
the model has been configured to let the user manipulate the delays observed in the fronthaul 
and backhaul of the network. Those parameters are used as an input to each simulation run. 
Additionally, the possibility to modify the HARQ timer has been added, to control the delay 
experienced in the fronthaul even more and see it’s impact on the protocol’s behavior.

7.4. Expected results 
Although QUIC is based on UDP, all of its congestion mechanisms come from TCP, therefore 
we expect it to be at least as good as TCP. However, thanks to the packet pacing mechanisms 
it has a higher probability of delivering packets and hence should have less packet drops and 
less aggressive window reductions, making it more efficient.  
If it turns out that QUIC is really worse than TCP in most of the scenarios, then an 
improvement of the implementation will have  to be considered, especially if the results will 
not cover at a certain level those obtained in another work on this protocol [57], since the 
experiments in this paper have been performed using real implementations of the protocols.

usage of the resources (not wasted on connection setup). From a QoS perspective, a faster 
download is more important than the resource usage, because it is a measure perceived by the 
user, and here we have a network specific improvement. Although TCP has to make twice as 
many connections, it still manages to get the data to the user faster. Again, a more advanced 
test where the losses are more distributed would be more interesting to see, since TCP can 
avoid here the loss windows.

7.6. Discussion 
Based on the obtained results, we can state that QUIC isn’t much better than TCP and performs 
almost the same in this simulation model. The advantage of QUIC in this model is its better 
usage of network resources compared to the TCP implementation due its less aggressive 
behavior. This is an expected result, although there were hopes for better results thanks to the 
pacing algorithm.  
Let us go again through the requirements of a 5G QoS-aware protocol: 
1. Works well in heterogeneous environments 
2. Works well in settings where connection speeds are asymmetric 
3. Operates on top of existing technologies – leverages their strengths, avoids their 
weaknesses 
4. Coexists with TCP connection, only takes it fair share of available resources 
5. Scales to very high bit rates 
6. Gives “soft” QoS guarantees on end-to-end application performance thanks to network 
probing and session knowledge (ability to learn the optimal usage during the 
connection)

7. Distributed operation, i.e., does not rely on central processing unit with global

Points 1. And 2. Would need to be checked in another simulation configuration, therefore we 
have no basis to judge the performance based on them. QUIC can, however, operate on top of 
existing and even future technologies (C-RAN configuration). As a confirmation of this can be 
the fact that the protocol is already being used for Google queries and Youtube videos. It does 
leverage the strength of wired connections, thanks to its low latency features. In the case of our 
model, the protocol was running smoothly in a bandwidth restricted environment and wasn’t 
using “dumbly” all of its resources. But we couldn’t see any behavior that would leverage LTE’s 
strength, apart from the bandwidth probing mechanism that probes the available bandwidth. 
Hence we could partly validate point 3. And 9. Point 4. Would require a different setup of our 
network, to be fully checked (2 application servers, each running a different transport 
protocol). Again, it can be validated for a wired environment since its already in use and if 
Google has also released it in mobile versions of its browser, then we can assume that it can 
coexist with TCP. And as seen in the results, it isn’t a greedy protocol in terms of resource 
usage. The Cubic window enables the protocol to scale to high bit rates when it is possible and 
when no losses have been detected, thanks to the exponential part of the function. In principle, 
based on the structure of the protocol, we could assume that it has the ability of probing the 
network’s resources thanks to its congestion feedback frame and ping frame, which haven’t 
been tested in this model implementation. The protocol still remains an end-to-end protocol 
and does not need any other node in the network to work properly. In terms of retransmissions, 
we have seen that thanks to the pacing algorithm, the protocol managed to get out of the loss 
periods, without so many window changes, which could imply partly that not so many 
retransmissions have been requested, but more data needs to be provided for a clear answer. 
Finally, the protocol does have a modular structure, thanks to the usage of various frames, and 
a special field during the handshake procedure (protocol version) that lets it select the proper 
version for an application.  
We are able to validate 6 out of the 10 bullets and the other bullets could be verified through a 
modification of the model and a different topology. In conclusion, the protocol has still the 
potential to be a 5G QoS-aware protocol, but would need another round of investigation, 
especially the mechanisms that can give soft guarantees of the QoS. As such, if the protocol 
would have to replace TCP the next day, it wouldn’t perform worse. These results are in 
accordance with those obtained in the lab tests using the real implementation, although the 
version used at that time wasn’t completely finished [57].  
The model of the protocol needs to go through an improvement phase now, and all of its 
mechanisms have to be changed to more elaborate ones, like for instance a better way of 
estimating the bandwidth based on additional information about the network, which could be 
carried by other, not implemented frames (congestion feedback, ping). The tests need also 
more elaboration, to be closer to reality ex. Use a distribution function for the losses. For 
further verifications and validations, a test in a lab environment could be done using Google’s 
real implementation and the results could be compared to those obtained here and could be 
used to show other directions of development.

7.7. Summary 
The results of the simulation have shown, that the QUIC protocol is able to deal with the losses 
and delays in the wireless channel like TCP and manages to achieve approximately the same 
throughput with this slight difference, that manages at a certain level to make a better use of 
resources. This is an expected result, because apart from the pacing algorithm, all other 
mechanisms are the same as in TCP. More experiments with a more elaborated setup would 
have to be run to have a clearer view of QUIC’s performance. The results confirm also that the 
protocol hasn’t been developed by the company purely for wireless networks, but aimed more 
at improving the latency of the connections in wired networks. On the other side, QUIC has a 
more modular structure than TCP and is much more customizable than TCP is. As a reminder, 
the protocol contains a congestion feedback and a ping frame, which could contain more 
information about the traffic and hence could help in dealing with losses and delays during the 
transfer. In conclusion, the protocol isn’t bringing any amazing performances based on the 
simulation results, but it has the potential to become the next 5G transport protocol, but this 
needs some more investigation. As a next step, tests in a live network using the real 
implementation could be performed to validate it. But it’s design responds much better to the 
requirement of 5G networks, which aim at using Open Transport Protocols on the transport 
layer.

Conclusion 
 
Current and future mobile technologies focus on increasing the throughput of users while 
decreasing the latency at the same time, to better deal and support next generation services. 
This requires a greater interoperability between network elements to achieve a transmission 
not affecting so much the neighboring cells and users. The networks of the future will focus 
also on making the networks more wireless, hence more small cells will appear, which will 
require a better management of the resources to maintain the QoS at a high level. 
The QoS can be managed at different layers of the network, and some technologies are better 
at achieving it than others, however all of them depend on each other and other protocols have 
to be taken into consideration when designing a new mechanism. 
In this work, we have focused on the QoS mechanisms at the transport layer as they require 
the least changes in the network (only the senders need to update their protocols) and still 
show low performance in wireless networks. Various improvements of the TCP protocol have 
been presented, with the spoofing mechanism being one of the most successful. However, the 
TCP protocol itself doesn’t correspond to the requirements of 5G networks, where Open 
Transport Protocols are seen as the future. A new protocol introduced recently by Google has 
been investigated, as it was said to bring major improvements over TCP in terms of the delay 
of a connection. It is a protocol implemented over UDP and hence can be easily changed by 
using a different version of a browser. 
The QUIC protocol implements all of TCP’s congestion mechanisms including the congestion 
window, fast retransmissions and retransmission timeouts, with the difference that it uses the 
latest versions of these mechanisms, which are said to bring improvements to the regular TCP 
implementation. 
A simulation model has been created in OPNET modeler based on the currently available 
documentation of the protocol. We have only implemented the mechanisms that could show a 
good performance of the protocol over wireless links and those where the cubic congestion 
window, the packet pacing, the nack format of the acknowledgments, sent every two packets 
and the fast loss detections and retransmissions (NewReno and FACK). The algorithms have 
been implemented in their most basic form: the pacing algorithm was sending the packets 
based on the bandwidth computed from the RTT and congestion window size, stream frames 
of the protocol did not carry information about the byte offset, hence out of order packets 
needed to be retransmitted. This model did not test the delay improvements of the protocol 
such as the shortened crypto handshake or the FEC frames used to recover individual lost 
packet without resending the whole data. After a verification phase, the protocol has been 
tested against the TCP model provided in the same simulation software. 
The simulation scenarios, where configured to simulate a lossy air channel, where packets are 
lost in groups or experience major delays due to the dynamic changes of the channels. The 
results have shown, that the protocol performs approximately the same as TCP, without 
significant improvements apart from reducing the aggressive behavior during losses. Hence 
the pacing mechanisms, cubic window and NACK acknowledgment helped in saving some 
overhead compared to TCP, while sending more data about the losses to the end user and 
slightly increased the resource usage during the transmission. This fulfills some of the 
==PAGE:67==
67  
 
Conclusion 
 
requirements that have been set for 5G protocols. Other points that could validate it need some 
additional work, like full implementation of all the frames of the protocol, modification of the 
testing topology.  
Although QUIC did not pass the test of wireless environments, it still shows some potential 
thanks to its design with custom frames, which can be easily extended. As it is currently, the 
protocol could replace TCP and this would not degrade the performance of the networks, it 
could eventually improve the resource usage. If we consider results given by Google, it already 
improves some aspects of the connections like load time or download speed, but this is only in 
specific cases. Otherwise, the protocol needs still some work, if the plan is to make it the 
successor of TCP. 
Future works 
QUIC’s implementation (the real implementation and in this model) needs now to be extended 
to give soft guarantees of QoS, which is achievable through the additional information sent by 
the protocol in other frame (congestion feedback, ping). More advanced versions of the 
mechanisms used (pacing, bandwidth estimation) could be based on data mining algorithms, 
to take out all of the information that would help in getting the most precise image of the 
network and the traffic on the links.  
The next step now is to improve the model created, considering the results obtained and rerun 
it in more advanced scenarios (better loss and delay distribution). Another test to be done, to 
validate these results, is to use the real implementation of the protocol and run it in a lab setup 
in similar scenarios, which could give a better direction for the changes.
