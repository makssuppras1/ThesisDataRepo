Summary (English)
Paper-based Digital microuidics (PBDMF) is a technology that enables actua-
tion of liquid drops with volume in the range of nano- to micro-liters on printed
on paper array of conductive electrodes. Droplets are used as chemical contain-
ers that can be moved, merged and split. Automated drop actuation together
with a procedure for quantitative readout allows autonomous sample prepara-
tion, handling, analysis and disposal, which makes DMF capable of resembling
standard bench-top lab protocols. PBDMF are characterized as low-cost dis-
posable devices with simple fabrication process which makes them suitable for
inexpensive biomedical diagnostics, environmental monitoring, education, etc.
The simple and easy fabrication process calls for an equally simple and easy
chip design and programming. Typical, design tools for DMF chips are either
addressing much more complex layout structures and functionalities, requiring
advanced compiler techniques, or are based on generic commercial vector or
bitmap graphic editors.
This makes the chip design process tedious and er-
ror prone task and the produced designs are dicult to map to an automated
control.
This master thesis project addresses the chip designs and control challenges by
proposing a modular text and graphical based chip design with embedded con-
trol information. This allows chip designs to be simulated and veried before
production as well as consequential automated chip control. Two foil based coat-
ing techniques are developed to provide aordable chip fabrication process. This
thesis also explores alternatives for experiment monitoring and results quanti-
cation.
In the course of this work we tried to develop and conceptualize a
portable DMF experimental platform and the corresponding software and hard-
ware tools.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Digital microuidics is a technology that allows actuation of discrete liquid drops
on an array of electrodes coated with hydrophobic isolation layer. Drops are used
as carriers of chemical substances and they are typically in the range of pico to
micro liters. By applying a voltage potential on a pair of electrodes, individual
drops can be addressed and forced to move which naturally allows them to be
transported, merged, mixed and split. Mapping this actions to corresponding
instructions allows structured control schemes to be developed and executed in
a manner very closed to a computer program. The ability to create and execute
protocols much like as the standard bench-top ones, makes this technology very
suitable for lab-on-a-chip and point-of-care devices.
Digital microuidics is an interdisciplinary eld involving physics, nanotechnol-
ogy, chemistry, biochemistry and not surprisingly computer science. Advance-
ments in these elds in the recent years allowed production of simple, inex-
pensive, disposable, portable active open paper-based microuidic (OPBDMF)
chips [12]. The production process of such a chip is based on a novel approach of
drawing electrodes on a paper substrate with a conductive ink followed by apply-
ing hydrophobic isolation coating. This-low tech fabrication scheme is capable of
delivering consistent results, thus paving the road to more advance designs and
experiments with real life applications. Disposable paper-based sensors are also
gaining a lot of popularity due to their excellent performance, low production
cost and use of conventional materials and technologies. Combining OPBDMF

1.1
Motivation
Active microuidic devices have been developed and used mainly in well equipped,
specialized labs typically by highly educated and trained experts. The broad
range of applications that these devices can be adapted to has been steadily gen-
erating more research interest. A particular application of an OPBDMF chip
can be as a part of an aordable and easy to operate POC device which can
have a signicant impact on the medical industry in the developing countries
by enabling medical exams to be performed in the led or in rural areas where
lack of medical service is a tremendous problem. Another alternative applica-
tion is to simply utilize a system based on an OPBDMF chip as an education
tool, where connecting cross disciplinary theory and practice meet in a real life
experiment. Therefore, the potential of utilizing OPBDMF chips as a part of a
portable electrochemical laboratory with medical, experimental or educational
application was recognized as the main driving force for this thesis.
The project is also interesting from purely academical and engineering perspec-
tive. Re-framing the OPBDMF chip's sophisticated design and operation ow
into a simple, intuitive and reliable process demands a number of challenges to
be overcome. Researching alternative variants for solving each of them, in order
to result in an end-to-end proof of concept solution, employs all the engineering
skills and knowledge gained throughout the year of study.

2.1
Introduction
Digital paper-based microuidic chips base they operation on the electrowetting-
on-dielectric principle. The wetting properties of typically hydrophobic surface
are modied by electric eld and this results in controlled manipulation of the
contact angle between a liquid drop sample and the chip's surface. The change

makes the drop moves. A typical construction of an OPBDMF chip is shown in

building blocks, functionality and the relations between them.
2.2

The simplicity of the open OPBDMF chips calls for equally simple, convenient,
and error prone chip design and control process. Specialized systems address-
ing this topic have been developed such as [9] and [20] but they provide com-
plex multi-step design process based on multilayer chip production technologies.
Considering the typical physical layout properties of an OPBDMF chip, these
techniques could be adapted to t the technology specics and constrains of
OPBDMF chips, but the complex design process still adds signicant overhead
and requires particular skills and domain knowledge.

based or bitmap graphical editors [12] or generic computer-aided design (CAD)

Analysis of already published chip designs [12] and the current technology limi-

shows that the dierent microuidic operations share common characteristics
and have similarities in their electrodes' physical layout, shape, design prop-

mixing with the only logical dierence that mixing is allows drop actuation in
both directions. Therefore, identifying and characterizing the building blocks of
an OPBDMF chip is a key component of deriving a set of design rules which can
be used to create a semi-automated chip design process. Each building block
serves only a particular microuidic functionality, which is basically dened as
electrodes' pattern and drop actuation path inside of this particular module.
The combination of these two characteristics is encapsulated in a microuidic
module which is used as a main building block of an OPBDMF chip.
This
results in a workow which simultaneously creates the electrode's geometrical

The modular design approach allows module's re-use and standardization by
building libraries of predened components. Allowing the components to be
parametrized, adds the needed application specic exibility.

Three base conductive elements are recognized as the core building blocks of
any chip's microuidic or supporting functionality: connection pads, electrodes,
and routing tracks. The physical dimensions of these elements correspondingly
depend on the: connection pin types and size; drop sample characteristics and
tolerances; routing area height, width, track width and spacing between tracks.
Additional constrains are applied from the utilized printing/plotting technology,
which mainly aects the minimal size and geometry of the printable elements.
Let us discuss the sizing of these components in more details.
Connection pads: The pitch value for the connection pads has been set to be
0.1 inch (equals to 2.54 mm) due to the fact that this spacing is a well estab-

connectors are available, therefore providing variety of conventional solutions.
Considering the selected pitch a proper connection pad size would be 1.5 mm by
4 mm - leaving enough space between the pads as well as providing big enough
land pattern to tolerate some slight misalignment of the paper-chip - mount-
ing construction conguration. This pad size matches the design guidelines of
particular spring loaded connector 1 as well as it is suitable for 0.62 mm single
spring loaded (pogo) pins.
Routing tracks: Routing tracks should be as wide as possible in order to
ensure low connection resistance and to decrease the risk of broken connections.
The width of the tracks depends on the used printing technique and it must be
adjusted correspondingly with a reasonable safety margin to prevent occurrence
of short circuits. A consistent gap in the range of 0.1 - 0.2 mm is sucient to
ensure reliable electrical isolation taking into account the chip's substrate and
the required electrode's driving voltage (20-150 volts).

2.3.2
Axis based chip design
Considering the chip's DIL layout and the limitation to route connections only
on a single layer, the chip connection area needs to be carefully managed. A
logical concept to address this issue appears to be to allow a microuidic module
to have maximum two columns of electrodes, which can be correspondingly
routed to the left and right columns of connection pads. If a module has only
one column, then the electrodes are split into two groups and routed to the left
and right columns of connection pads, thus utilizing the chip's connection area
and pads more eectively. Chip layout where modules are placed on one or two
vertical axis ensures that routing is always possible. A third axis is added to
the design in order to achieve exibility when positioning modules and better
utilization of the chip's active area. The so formed 3-axis structure is shown
in Figure 2.2a and it is referred as 3-axis based chip design. However, modules
are still allowed to occupy at most two out of the three available axis in order
to guarantee a routing on a single layer. A chip designed based on these rules
is shown on Figure 2.2b. Both left, right and left-right connection patters are
utilized in the rst few lines of electrodes in the active area demonstrating the
explained concepts. It is self-evident that if electrodes at the same horizontal
level are added to more that two axis (columns), routing on a single layer would
be impossible.

Execution code embedding
The functionality of a module is dened by the way it actuates and controls

vital role in the simplication of the chip design process by completely masking
the chip programming from the user. Additionally, it ensures the correct-by-
construction design approach by eliminating inconsistency in the electrodes to
connection pads mapping and the corresponding control signals.

Simulator
The chip simulator is utilized, to verify the experiment ow of a chip design. A

Five basic controls can be applied on a simulation - it can be started, paused,
resumed, resumed from a particular state or stopped. The need of start, pause,
resume and stop functions is obvious and it does not need further discussion.
The resume from a given state is important feature, since it allows chip failure
modes to be explored and corresponding recovery scenarios and algorithms to
be tested. To enable a simulation to resume from a given state, rst of all, the
full simulation state space has to be built. In the context of the paper-based
microuidic chip, building a list of all simulation steps is not a concern, since
the current designs are reaching a state space of few hundred or thousand states.

Feedback simulator
The Feedback simulator aims to represent a real hardware system capable of
monitoring an ongoing experiment and simply reporting back to the Simulator
the coordinates of the detected drops. This is achieved by registering the mod-

predened base-time interval which is further modied with a small amount of
random extra delay that simulates the variations in processing time. Then the
Simulatro compares the reported results with the expected drop position and
if they match, the simulation is resumed. This allows erroneous cases to be
detected. Additionally, to test the recovery capabilities of the Simulator, the
Feedback controller can be forced to introduce an oset to the reported coordi-
nates, thus exercising a case where a drop is not responding or misbehaving.
In the context of a real system, the Feedback simulator can be replace with
a module which observes the real state of a conducted experiment. This can
provide an input to the Simulator, when it is utilized as a physical experiment
controller, and ensure that the real experiment will match the virtual simulation.
3.5

As it was mentioned in the previous section, in the context of a real system,
where the Simulator is employed as an experiment controller, a situation can oc-
cur where the simulated state might not match the real state of the experiment.
This can be caused by external factors which prevent normal drop actuation.
Such a case can be detected based on the provided feedback and measures can
be taken to recover the normal operation of the experiment.
Currently the
Simulator has a list of all simulation states which also contains information on
the legal transitions between them. This represents a directional graph and a
shortest path algorithm can be run to calculate if there is a possible recovery
path. If the search yields a result, then the simulation can be re-executed from

4.3
Chip coating
The OPBDMF chips operate on the electrowetting principle and they require
hydrophobic isolation coating over the chip's active area to protect the printed
conductive patterns and the paper substrate from liquid exposure. The electrical
isolation properties of the coating are required since an electrical circuit can be
created between a "positive" electrode trough the uid drop and a "negative"
electrode. In case a drop sample is conductive, current will ow trough the
formed electrical connection, thus compromising the experiment, destroying the
conductive electrodes, and most probably changing the chemical content of the
drop sample. The hydrophobic property of the coating also ensures proper (high)
contact angle between the chip's surface and the drop sample required for a
drop actuation. The isolation and hydrophobic materials have to be chemically
neutral and resistible to acids and bases. A method for creating such a coating
is to spin coat the chip's surface with a thin layer of few percent Teon solution
as described in [12]. The resulted isolation layer is typically in the range of a
few microns and it is very fragile. It can easily get broken and start conducting
current even without mechanical interference.
The described chip coating process utilizing the spin coating techniques also
involves treating the chip with silicon oil and a few thermal cycles. As a result,
the technological time needed to produce a chip approximately half a day.
One of the goals in this part of the project work has been to explore and nd
an alternative process for chip coating production. An obvious substitution of
a thin layer of spin coated Teon is a prefabricated Teon foil. Market research
resulted in nding suppliers of Teon foil. Further research on the topic, yielded
results where kitchen wrap foil was used as coating layer in 2 and [1]. These
two materials were considered for future use and experiments were conducted
in order to develop methodology for utilizing them as a chip coating, aiming
for simplied, reliable, easy to implement process, which requires only widely
available materials and equipment.

4.6
Chip failure modes
A chip malfunction is typically caused by two main factors - fault in the con-
ductive ink elements or a coating defect.
There are three failures caused from problems with the conductive elements:
too high resistance, lack of connection and short circuit. The rst mentioned
can be easily detected by verication of the resistance of all conductive elements
after the annealing phase. It has not been observed a case, where the resistance
of a printed element has changed in an operation cycle, without any external
factors involved.
However, microscopic cracks or scratches on the surface of
the conductive ink can lead to increased resistance or even loss of connection.
Such error can occur due to structural defects on the photo-paper substrate,
chip mishandling or from mechanical interaction. Short circuit between con-
ductive elements is less likely to occur throughout chip operation. Typically
short circuits are introduced from insucient spacing between elements in the
printing process and they can be spotted by a visual inspection of the chip for
overlapping or touching conductive elements.

Chip coating can compromise an experiment execution by failing to provide
electrical isolation or a uniform hydrophilic surface. In the case where foil is
used to provide electrical isolation, failing to do so is not likely to happen, due to

chips, due to the very thin isolation layer and imperfections in the coating

with the hydrophobic surface which causes a drop sample to get "stuck" at a
particular area of the chip's surface and to become uncontrollable by the system.

lubrication oil layer, etc.

This chapter focuses on the design and implementation of a portable driving

5.1
Introduction
Actuation of a liquid drop on an OPBDMF chip requires the chip's electrodes
to be switched to a high voltage (HV) source according to a predened con-

HV multiplexing. Furthermore, the electrode's control is applied from the al-
ready introduced DMF Toolbox control system (CS) which is basically a separate
stand alone integrated device such as personal computer, laptop or smart-phone.
Therefore, the HV and multiplexing needs to be performed from another em-

deal with three main tasks - generating the HV, multiplexing it to the chip's

5.3.2
HVPSU design and implementation
The electrodes' driving voltage for an OPBDMF chip varies from volts to hun-
dreds of volts. The chip's operating voltage strongly depends on the chip elec-
trode's design, coating dielectric properties, coating thickness, actuated sample
volume, etc. Therefore, an adjustable high voltage power supply will ensure
proper driving voltage for the chip's electrodes matching the current require-
ments. Monitoring the voltage applied to the electrodes and the owing current,
can be used for chip's coating failure detection or liquid sample mishandling.
The assumed failure mode is basically closing the circuit between the high volt-
age terminals trough a liquid drop sample which will result in higher current
consumption.
Further damage can be minimized by detecting this excessive
current and switching o the HVPSU.
Following the mentioned features, an isolated y-back DC/DC power supply
was designed and built. The power supply is designed to operate from a single
5 volt power source and it is based on AN118 1 from Linear Technology but
further extended in order to provide electrical isolation between the primary
and secondary side, digitally adjustable output voltage and output voltage and
current monitoring.
The block diagram of the designed module is shown in
Figure 5.3.
The y-back transformer is driven by a LT3439 transformer driver with constant
frequency and 50% duty cycle. A voltage regulation is achieved by controlling
the potential of the transformer's primary side center tap. The control signal
is produced by an error amplier which tracks the dierence between the set
point and the real output voltage. The power supply output voltage is adjusted
from the MCP4725 digital-to-analog converter (DAC) which has build in non-
volatile memory. This memory allows the DAC to load and output the last
stored output value, thus ensuring a known start state. The set point voltage
produced from the DAC is fed as a reference to the error amplier (LTC6240)
where it is compared with a fraction of the output voltage. The power supply
output is rst scaled down with a voltage divided and then passed trough an
optically isolated voltage sensor (ACPL-C87B), thus ensuring electrical isolation
between the primary and secondary side of the module. This integrated circuit

7.1
Feedback provider/Computer Vision
The importance of monitoring an experiment in the context of ensuring correct
results is unquestionable. This section explores utilization of a computer vision
to monitor the proper actuation of drop samples. A primitive drop localization
algorithm which nds the drop center coordinates and it is used to provide them
as feedback to the experiment controller is demonstrated.
An autonomous execution of experiments require monitoring to ensure correct
results.
As it has been mentioned in Chapter 4.6, there are factors such as
imperfections on the chip's coating, broken conductive traces, malfunctioning
electrodes, dust particles, etc.
that can interrupt the normal actuation of a
sample on PBDMF chip's surface. Therefore, any drop movement outside of
the expected actuation trajectory must be detected and an error correction

mechanism should be invoked as an attempt to recover the normal execution
ow. In case of successful recovery the protocol can return to its normal execu-
tion, but if an unrecoverable state is detected, the protocol will be terminated
and the operator signalized.
Monitoring a position of a drop on a PBDMF chip can be approached from
two domains - electrical or optical. One possible solution can utilize impedance-
based sensing as proposed in [8]. Relying on a change in the measured impedance
when a drop is located on the top of monitored electrodes, this technique will
not detect a drop sample which does not overlap with any electrodes or it will
produce erroneous result if a drop sits on the top of connection tracks in the
chip's routing area. Furthermore, this technology requires development of com-
plex hardware, thus leading to an increased cost and complex operation. The
visual feedback takes benet of an already available smart-phones's camera in
the case where the experiment controller runs on a smart-phone. The drop lo-
calization can then be performed by analyzing a video stream captured from
the smart-phone's camera and processing it in order to precisely localize the
position of drop samples. The drop coordinates then can be fed back in the
simulator to verify proper execution of the currently running experiment. This
module is designed to replace the described feedback simulator by providing real
data.
7.1.1
Used technology

but there is an ocial port for Android OS as well. The processing functionality

an interfaced to the Android's application level. There are two options to use
the OpenCV library when developing for Android - the rst one is based on the
already mentioned wrapper and the second option is to use the Android's Native
Development Kit (NDK), which allows to embed C or C++ native code to an
Android app. Developing with NDK is a complex process with a questionable

7.1.2
Image processing algorithm
The Android OS native camera API is used to capture a video stream from the
smart-phone's camera and provide it one frame at a time to a handler method in
the Andoid app for further handling. The image processing takes place in three
main steps: (1) nding the chip's active area in a camera frame; (2) extracting
and rectifying this sub-part of the image from the main camera frame; and (3)
sample drop detection and coordinates calculation.
Since the smart-phone is not permanently xed to the prototype, it is almost
impossible to perfectly align the camera's eld of view with the area that needs
to be monitored. Furthermore, the current prototype-camera conguration does
not guarantee that the camera will be facing the chip at right angle.
Even
if a technique for physical alignment of the camera and the mounted chip is
constructed, dierent chips could have physical dimensions which do not t well
with the camera's aspect ratio. Furthermore, any vibration or user interaction
with the system could introduce misalignment and eventually lead to erroneous
results. To address these issues, image stabilization and rectication algorithm
have been implemented. To ensure proper operation, a requirement that needs
to be fullled is that the camera stream captures the whole chip area.
The image stabilization is a three step process as well: detecting area of interest;
extracting this subimage from the main frame; and applying image rectication
based on image homograph.
7.1.2.1
Chip active area recognition and perspective correction
To nd an object on an image is computationally intensive task. The bigger
the data set, the longer it takes to process. To actually detect an object, rst
of all it must be presented in the image and it needs to have some distinctive
characteristics. A reliable and relatively easy to implement technique utilizes
a set of color markers as object's characteristic. These markers have a color
that stands out of the background and they can be placed at the corners of the
chip's active area. Ensuring proper operation of the drop localization algorithm,
requires the full chip area to be presented on any camera frame and hence all
four markers are detected. Failing to detect precisely four markers will lead to
rejecting the frame due to lack of information.
A raw camera frame is shown in Figure 7.1(a). The four color markers clearly
dene the chip's active area and their centers are precisely aligned with the
corners of the active area.

7.1.2.2
Drop detection
Drop detection happened to be a much more challenging task than initially
considered. Looked from above, the drop has one distinctive feature which is
not present anywhere on the chip - it represents a circle. Therefore, a circle
detection algorithm based on Hough Circle Transform 2 has been tested as a
straight forward solution. The algorithm has been performing well when on
homogeneous background as shown in Figure 7.2a, but it has been generating
many false results when tested on a more complex image such as the one shown
in Figure 7.2b.
An attempt to tune the algorithm's parameters has been made with some vari-
able success, but no reliable circle detection has been achieved. A logical deci-
sion at this point has been to try to subtract the complex background from each
frame, resulting in an image containing only the drop sample/s and eventually
some noise.

7.1.2.3
Background subtraction
After image rectication is performed, the chip's active area appears as a static
picture on the screen. Two techniques have been prototyped and tested to imple-
ment background subtraction: based on the OpenCV's built in BackgroundSub-
tractorMOG2 library and a basic approach based on simple frame subtraction.
Both of these techniques have been tested and unfortunately they have shown
no result. The result of the background substantiation has been a gray frame
and it has taken seconds to process a single frame. It has been concluded that
the library is misused, therefore the lack of results. More research is required in
order to eventually overcome this issue and achieve success.
Another approach is to compute a background scene approximation based on a
median of a number of video frames where only the background is present. This
technique is supposed to lter out the noise in the images and provide a good
background estimation.
As a simple rst step, a direct subtraction between
two frames has been attempted. This experiment has resulted in discovering a
bug in the OpenCV core implementation. The bug 3 has been triggered when
a function which computes the absolute dierences between two matrices has
been called (Core.absdi(...)). This lack of a basic functionality has led to an
attempt to implement this subtraction on the Android app level. The result
has been successfully completed computation but processing a single frame has
required more than 10 seconds - this is simply not acceptable. However, this
is considered a partial success since an actual background subtraction has been
performed. The main drawback of this method has been the speed which has
been aecting the performance in a negative way. One method to improve the
performance has been to reduce the processing resolution (initially used 1280
by 720 pixels). Less data to compute on, shorter the processing time. Another
alternative is to take advantage of the fact that drops are actuated in a sequential
manner and they are expected to be on allays on the top of the active electrodes.
Therefore another approach called "informed drop localization" was considered
as an option.

7.1.4
Integration with the simulator
In order to integrate this image processing algorithm with the chip simulator
and controller, the list of active pads needs to be sent to the computer vision
module, and as a result if any drops are detected in the area of the active pads,
their coordinates need to be reported back to the simulator. Due to the image
rectication, the origin og the returned coordinates is according to the center of
the top left color marker, where th simulator operates on a coordinate system
with origin the top left corner of a chip. Therefore, the reported coordinates
needs to be scaled from the simulator, before being considered as valid feedback
data. This can be achieved by referencing them to the known color markers'
positions.

as a stand alone device is used.

7.2.2.1
Electro-analytical methods
Electrochemical measurements are typically done by a three electrode setup: a
reference electrode (RE) used to keep the solution to preset potential; a counter
electrode (CE) used to source electrons; and a working electrode (WE) on which
the measured current is proportional to the solution's concentration. The three
electrodes are immersed in the measured liquid sample. The so formed sensing
cell is then connected to an electronic system called potentiostat which controls
the potential on the RE to a given value and read the current produced by
the donated electrons to the WE. A simplied block diagram of a sensing cell,
potentiostat and a processing block is shown in Figure 7.7.
A potentiostat can be constructed from standard commercially available elec-
tronic components. At least two open source builds with detailed design descrip-
tion are known to this date - DStat [4] and CheapStat [19]. These potentiostats
are designed as stand alone embedded systems which can be integrated with
other platforms, for instance the platform discussed in this thesis.
A dierent approach on data processing and presenting is demonstrated from
µMED[16] which is a universal mobile electrochemical detector. This device

Optical methods
Colorimetry is well established technique for analyzing results from a chemi-

deceives developed for POC such as the portable, self contained colorimeter
described in [7]. Alternatively [22] and [10] report the use of a smart-phone's
camera and a processing application as an analytical device for POC applica-
tions. A picture taken with the smart-phone's camera is analyzed, and the color
of the detection pad is sampled. The precise numeric representation indicates
the result of the test.
The colorimetry appears to be a convenient, low cost alternative of the elec-
trochemical measurements. However, producing the sensing elements itself can
be complicated demanding process, which in particularity does not match the
simplicity of the production process of paper-based electrochemical sensor and
chip. Due to this fact this sensing techniques will not be evaluated further in
this work, but it will be considered for future development.

This thesis work has been focused on research, development, conceptualization
and prototyping of a portable digital microuidic experimental platform. Soft-
ware tools for OPBDMF chip design, programming, simulation and experiment
monitoring have been developed and demonstrated. The report also proposes
two new Teon and polyethylene foil based OPBDMF chip production tech-
niques.
An introduction to the main building components and the feasible microuidic
operations on an OPBDMF chip have been carried out in order to provide
common denition of the chip's active, routing and continence pad area and
the main building blocks. A modular axis based chip design has been proposed
to address the lack of consistency and structure with the currently used tool to

been developed to verify the model assumptions and to prove the feasibility

8.2
Discussion
The product of this thesis work proves that open paper-based digital microu-
idics can be utilized in the design of a low cost portable POC device capable
of conducting low to moderate complexity experiments. The novel approach
for rapid chip design together with the simplied chip production procedure
has proven that, a complex multi step lab based technology process can be
signicantly simplied by utilizing custom developed software, hardware and
production techniques. The conducted research on the experiment monitoring
has conrmed that computer vision can be utilized to ensure that an experi-
ment result will not be compromised by miss-actuation of the involved liquids.
Low-cost, disposable paper based biosensors have been identied as an excellent
candidate for measuring the experiment results and thus providing end-to-end
solution for POC device.
Comparing the summary of the achieved results in the previous paragraph with
the initial project scope and goals, clearly shows that the project has managed to
address all of the major aspects of designing, implementing and conceptualizing
a mobile system capable of caring out microuidic experiments on open active
paper-based chips. Furthermore, this thesis work proved that it is absolutely
feasible to construct a practical system with the currently available technologies.
The modular hardware and software design allows great exibility in terms of
future modications and exertions. This was proven by the fact that the de-
veloped hardware prototype can be controlled from the DMF Toolbox software,
a smart-phone application and even from third party well established software
tools such as Matlab.
At the current level of development, the demonstrated system allows automated
and user-friendly chip design, simulation and control, as well as accessible tech-
niques for OPBDMF chip production. The developed software, hardware and
workow enables students, educators or enthusiast to quickly and eciently
start designing and experimenting with paper-based microuidics. Therefore,
by masking the design and operation complexity of this cross-disciplinary work,
the focus of the users can be shifted towards what could be considered more
important - exploring new potential application area and developing solutions
which address real life problems.

8.3.1
Chip design
Both text based and graphical bases chip design approaches were demonstrated.
In order to make the text based chip design and programming process more user
friendly, a design language either needs to be developed or adopted and two
suitable candidates for adoption were proposed in Chapter3.2.2 - BioCoder[3]
and Aquacore [2].
Either way, a language, parser, checker and eventually a
compiler have to be developed.
The range of dened microuidic module needs to be revised and extended.
For instance, the sensing module and it's variations have not been implemented
or a module which can feed back an already proceed droplet and provide loop
functionality.
An interesting addition to the chip designer would be to add automated sensor
design as well. This would allow the senors to be sized, integrated and printed
together with a PBDMF chip, which can accelerate the process.
8.3.2
Computer vision experiment monitor
The developed proof of concept computer vision prototype suers from poor
performance and its operation is aected by change in the ambient light condi-
tions. Therefore the current implementation is not practically applicable as an
experiment monitor at this stage of development and it requires future work. To
some extend the lack of performance is caused from executing computationally
heavy operations from the Java's applications domain since core OpenCV API
functionality failed to function properly. These problems can be approached by
trying to develop the image processing algorithm with the Android's NDK or
switching to dierent computer vision library more suitable to run on the An-
droid OS. The drop detection algorithm requires rework as well since the current
version was developed under the constrains imposed from the discovered soft-
ware bugs. The test utilizing dark eld extra light yielded promising results and
investigating this further should be considered as a high priority task.

not be compromised due to miss-actuated droplets. Drop localization can be
