Abstract
Microalgae have been widely reported as a promising source of biofuels, mainly based on
their high biomass and lipid productivity, as well as the possibility for cultivation in wastew­
aters and on non­arable land. The selection of suitable strains is an essential prerequisite
for their successful cultivation as a bioenergy source. Despite their potential as a biofuel
resource, large­scale production of biofuels from microalgae is currently unfeasibility due
to high operational costs and energy­intensive process. Therefore, integrating microalgal
cultivation with other processes for achieving inexpensive nutrient and energy use is an
important area of research.
To that end, this study evaluates the theoretical potential of a cement plant’s wastewater
and heat waste to support microalgae growth for biofuel and bio­fertilizer production. The
study was conducted by following an experimental approach: six tropical marine strains
were grown under different conditions, and the best performing one in high­quality FAME
production and lower nutrient demand was selected. The selected strain was used in a
hypothetical microalgal biofuel production process that was integrated with a reference
cement plant. The process was evaluated regarding the modeled product outputs, and
energy and economic requirements.
This study identified Chaetoceros muelleri as suitable for a multiple­product algae crop
exposed to a nutrient­depleted environment. The integrated process model showed three
products: biodiesel, upgraded biogas, and bio­fertilizer with production capacities of 0.46­
0.52 tons/year, 0­1 m3/year, and 0.6­5 tons/year, respectively. For the production of these
products, the electricity and thermal energy demand amounted to 5.69­13.32 and 5.67­
25.38 MWh/year, respectively. A sensitivity analysis showed that the algae’s oil content
and growth speed are the main factors that affect the biodiesel production capacity of the
integrated process and that the price of nutrients prevented the hypothetical microalgal
biofuel production process for being economically feasible. When only using wastewa­
ter as the source of nutrients, the integrated process model showed that three products,
biodiesel, upgraded biogas, and bio­fertilizer, have theoretical production capacities of
1.2 tons/year, 14 m3/year, and 79 tons/year, respectively. The zero demand for exter­
nal nutrients allows the modeled project to have a less negative net income, but still an
economical unfeasible result.
This study’s approach investigated the potential of a possible bio­refinery and environ­
mental pollution reduction concept by integrating microalgae biomass production with ce­
ment plant wastes and by­products. It was found that the factory’s wastes and by­products
have a significant theoretical potential for viable biofuel production from microalgae when
using only wastewater as a nutrient source.

Introduction
Continuous and increasing consumption of petroleum­based fuels accounts for roughly
one­third of CO2 emissions globally, which is considered a significant contributor to global
climate change (Saifuddin et al. 2015). Biofuels have been considered a promising sus­
tainable alternative for energy production, potentially decreasing the emission of green­
house gasses and fighting climate change. Current liquid biofuels are mainly produced
in the forms of bioethanol and biodiesel from different agricultural feedstocks, including
oil crops such as palm, soybean, and rapeseed, and sugar and starch crops such as
sugarcane and corn (González­González et al. 2018).
First­generation biofuels are unsustainable and insufficient to meet the increasing energy
demands due to uncertain and poor energy balances, high water demands, and high
nutrient requirements, as well as competition for arable lands and thus with food crops
(González­González et al. 2018). Second­generation biofuels seem to be an exciting al­
ternative since they are produced from non­food biomass, including agricultural wastes
and lignocellulosic feedstock such as wood, grasses, and forest residues (González­
González et al. 2018). However, their potential to sustainably satisfy world energy de­
mands is a matter of debate regarding feedstock availability and potential adverse effects
on carbon balances and biodiversity (González­González et al. 2018).
These concerns have generated interest in developing third­generation biofuels, such as
microalgae, which can potentially overcome the drawbacks of first and second­generation
biofuels, being more productive and sustainable (González­González et al. 2018). Mi­
croalgae cultivation does not compete with food crops since they can be produced in
non­arable land and grown in wastewater as their culture medium, reducing the use of
freshwater and nutrients ((González­González et al. 2018)). Microalgae biomass can also
be fed into downstream carbon sequestration processes like hard C­chips (Agri­char) via
pyrolysis that can be used to improve soil fertility, mitigating climate change by reintro­
ducing durable carbon back into the soil (Lim et al. 2012).
Also, microalgae are gaining a reputation as “biofactories” due to the varied composi­
tion of their biomass. A well­managed and equipped microalgal biorefinery can produce
biodiesel and other value­added products such as protein, carbohydrates, and a range
of fatty acids (FAs)(Lim et al. 2012), the last ones being 10 to 100 times greater than
traditional crops such as palm oil, corn, and soybeans (Lim et al. 2012). Besides their
potential for biofuel production, high­value omega­3 fatty acids that are not desirable FAs
for biodiesel production are highly valued in human nutrition (González­González et al.
2018).
Microalgae can also sequester 10 to 50 times more carbon dioxide than terrestrial plants
(Pourjamshidian et al. 2019), providing another alternative for removing CO2 from the
atmosphere. This could be a helpful process for mitigating CO2 emission from heavy
industries like cement manufacturing. Cement plants account for around 8% of global
CO2 emissions (BBC n.d.), a deep international concern. Cement plants typically reduce
their specific CO2 emissions through various techniques, such as increased energy ef­
ficiency, application of alternative raw materials, reduction of the clinker to cement ratio,
and utilization of alternative fuels. However, these techniques have already been ex­
ploited significantly, and they can only partly reduce emissions (Voldsund et al. 2019a).
Around two­thirds of the CO2 emissions are process­related, originating from the calci­

nation of limestone where CaCO3 is converted to CaO and CO2 (Voldsund et al. 2019a).
In contrast, one­third of the emissions come from the combustion of fuels in the cement
plant’s calciner and rotary kiln. A measure such as a fuel switch can only remove one­third
of the CO2 emissions(Voldsund et al. 2019a). CO2 capture and storage (CCS) can signif­
icantly reduce both process­related and fuel­related emissions. CCS is identified as the
single measure with the most significant potential for further overall emission reductions
in the cement industry. The most mature CO2 capture technology at present is chemical
absorption with amine (Voldsund et al. 2019a). However, this technology may not nec­
essarily be the best option when alternative technologies have matured, like the oxyfuel
process, chilled ammonia process, membrane­assisted CO2 liquefaction, and calcium
looping (Voldsund et al. 2019a). Nevertheless, none of the aforementioned technologies
are cheap which poses a challenge for widespread implementation in the developing world
including the top three global cement producers, China, India, and Vietnam.
Large, highly efficiency microalgae cultivation systems can have a potential capture ef­
ficiency as high as 99%, effectively capturing 1,8 kg of CO2 per kg of dry biomass (Lim
et al. 2012). Among all the previously mentioned processes, microalgae cultivation could
be cultivated as a cheaper way of capturing CO2 from flue gases. However, large­scale
commercial algal production has yet to be established (Pourjamshidian et al. 2019).
Besides capturing CO2, the sequestration of the gas also has benefits for microalgae. A
study published in the Brazilian Journal of Chemical Engineering said that higher biomass
concentrations were achieved when exposing Chlorella sp. to high levels of CO2 (Vold­
sund et al. 2019a). The highest concentration (2.8 g/L) was register for a run exposed to
a 1.75% CO2 concentration (v/v%) at 70mL/min. The run exposed to 9.45% CO2 con­
centration at 50mL/min presented the lowest growth of 1.7 g/L. The experiment reveals
that culturing microalgae with different levels of CO2 concentration can lead to various
microalgae­specific growth rates (Voldsund et al. 2019a). Even if the present study, ex­
plained further within, did not expose microalgae to different CO2 concentrations, it sets
a precedent on how microalgae behave in the presence of different concentrations of
carbon in the broth.
For many years, algae for biofuels have been studied to produce bioethanol, biohydro­
gen, biogas through anaerobic digestion, vegetable oils (triglycerides, biodiesel), hydro­
carbons, and ethanol (González­González et al. 2018). Algae have the advantages of
rapid growth, high oil yield per unit area, and can be cultivated on land unsuitable for culti­
vation. In addition, some of the microalgae strains can accumulate a significant amount of
lipid within their cells, in which the lipid can be converted to biodiesel through transester­
ification reaction (19, 20). The known total lipid content of microalgae varies from 5­77%
(Saifuddin et al. 2015), and according to past studies, the lipid content can be boosted
under nutrient­depleted conditions. Various studies conducted under nitrogen depriva­
tion conditions have demonstrated that biomass productivity and lipid accumulation are
negatively related (Lim et al. 2012). Therefore, optimizing oil production would require
balancing supporting cell growth and stimulating lipid yields.
One study, conducted by the Center of Renewable Energy from the University of Tenaga
in Malaysia (Saifuddin et al. 2015), showed that Nannochloropsis sp. achieved the highest
growth (almost 0,45 g/L) when exposed to F2 media Nitrogen levels (0.882 mM). On the
other side, when the same strain was exposed to nitrogen content as NaNO3 of concen­
tration of 0.355 mM, the growth was minimal (0,10 g/L), showing that the nitrogen content
directly affects the microalgae’s growth. During this study from Saifuddin et al. 2015,
lipid productivity was also measured. This time, the strain exposed to a concentration

of only 0.355 mM of NaNO3 reached an oil concentration of 0.36 mg/L. While for higher
NaNO3 concentrations of 0.530, 0.705, and 0.882 mM, 0.28, 0.16, and 0.14 mg/L of oil

the lower the nitrogen content, the higher the lipid productivity. The study shows valuable
information when the strain Nannochloropsis sp is exposed to a nutrient­depleted environ­
ment but lacks information about other strains that could have better performances. The
experiment raises the question whether all strains are affected the same way under nutri­
ent starvation. To date, research efforts have focused on the lipid production of individual
species, usually investigating the effects different growth conditions have on lipid produc­
tion and content (Lim et al. 2012). Unfortunately, direct comparisons of results between
studies are unreliable, given each species’s different growth conditions and experimental
parameters and the different methods used for lipid extraction (Lim et al. 2012).
Another study published by the University of Queensland (Lim et al. 2012), analyzing
many different tropical marine strains, concluded that after seven days of growth plus two
days of starvation, Nannochloropsis sp was the highest lipid producer, with more than
six ug/mL per day. Duanella salina and Tetraselmis sp followed, with FAME productivity
of 4.5 ug/mL per day. Regarding FAME/dry weight ratios, Duanella salina had the high­
est yield (11.4%), followed by Nannochloropsis sp (10.6%), Chataerosis muelleri (5.9%),
Tetrasalnis sp. (2.5%), and Pavlova salina (1.2%) among others (Lim et al. 2012). This
study opens the question about what would happen when exposed from the beginning to
different strains to nutrient starvation. It would be very costly to replace the medium with
pure seawater in an open pond to expose the strains to nutrient starvation.
Many studies exist about exposing microalgae to nutrient­depleted conditions or utilizing
CO2 for microalgae cultivation, but the majority only focus on one strain, mainly Chlorella
or Nannochloropsis sp. Since few studies focus on more than one strain, results can
hardly be comparable due to different growing and harvesting technics.
The present study will investigate the effect of cultivating six different tropical marine mi­
croalgae strains and identify the most effective one for biodiesel production. Strains will
be grown under different conditions, relying on exposure to light, nitrogen availability,
wastewater incorporation into the system, and exposure to high salinity environments.
Then, total fatty acid methyl ester (FAME) contents were analyzed via GC/MS since qual­
itative differences in the TAG content will affect biodiesel quality and its ability to meet fuel
standards.
Theoretically, and based on previous results, the study will assess the capacity of these
six strains to sequester carbon dioxide concentration from flue gases. Flue gases and
wastewaters will hypothetically come from a reference cement plant in northern Vietnam.
Coupling with a cement plant will assess using heat waste from the cement plant to dry
biomass. Heat will also feed the anaerobic digestor to produce biogas from biomass.
An economic evaluation of the Project will be carried out to identify the most feasible pro­
duction alternative. Few studies explore that costs and challenges of developing such a
cultivation system as the inherent advantages of a microalgal fuel source are unfortunately
offset by current economic limitations. These limitations include the cost of obtaining dry
biomass, significant nutrients and solvent requirements, and limited solvent recycling ca­
pacity.
The thesis studies the effects of exposing algae to nutrient depletion and higher carbon
levels using synthetic wastewater. Experimental results will help evaluate the feasibility of
coupling with a cement plant to sequester part of its CO2 emission and use wastewaters

Methods
2.1
The Project
The Project consisted of a technical and economical evaluation of using green microal­
gae as a fuel source to power a cement factory in Vietnam. A total of six microalgae
strains were included in the Project as several strains have the potential to produce bio­
fuel. The Project aimed to model the utilization of the factory’s domestic wastewater and
heat wastes to replace other raw materials in the production and harvesting phase. For the
modeling, experimental data and general assumptions were used as a generic approach.
The Project is divided into three sections:
• Laboratory work
Objective: Collection of data to use as input in future sections. Identity the strain(s)
with the highest high­quality­FAMEs production
• Technical model development
Objective: Evaluation of the technical feasibility of the Project, using collected data
and general assumptions as input
• Economic model development
Objective: Evaluation of the economic feasibility of the Project, using technical data
and general market assumptions as input
2.2
Laboratory work
The experiment included a performance evaluation of six different microalgae strains for
growth speed and fatty acid content. These strains were grown under four different sce­
narios to identify the circumstances that boost their oil/dry­weight ratio. The different sce­
narios were:
• Light case
Two different strains were grown together and separately, under 12 and 24 hour­
light­periods. The nutrient source was F2 media, and the growing time was 13
days.
• Base case
Six different strains grown separately, under 12 hour­light­periods.
The nutrient
source was F2 media, and the growing time was 13 days.
• Nutrient depletion case (”ND”)
Six different strains grown in medias with different nitrogen’s concentrations, under
12 hour­light­periods. The nutrient source was F2 media, and the growing time was
13 days.
• Wastewater case (”WW”)
The best performing strain based on previous scenarios was boosted with different
concentrations of carbon coming from synthetic wastewater (“WW”). The nutrient
source was F2 media and synthetic wastewater, and the growing time was six days.
It grew under 12 hour­light­periods.

2.2.4
Lipid extraction
For lipid extraction, the single­step procedure from Axelsson and Gentili 2014 was fol­
lowed.
Dry biomass was ground in a mortar. The powder was resuspended in 2:1 parts of chlo­
roform:methanol (v/v) by manually shaking the tube vigorously for a few seconds or until
the biomass was dispersed in the solvent system. Finally, a 0.73% NaCl water solution
was added to produce a 2:1:0.8 system of chloroform:methanol:water (v/v/v).
Once completed, the entire chloroform phase (bottom phase) was transferred to a clean
glass tube using a glass Pasteur pipette. The sample was re­extracted by adding 1 mL
chloroform to the old tube (containing the methanol:water solution) and centrifuged for 5
min at 1200g. Again, the chloroform phase (bottom phase) was collected using a glass
Pasteur pipette and pooled with the first chloroform fraction. Finally, the chloroform from
the tube was evaporated in a nitrogen gas stream, and the remains in the glass tube (oil)
were weighed. Measurements were recorded, and samples were stored at ­20°C. 5 ml of
solvent were used per sample.
2.2.5
Transesterification to fatty acid methyl esters
For the transesterification step, where fatty acids (”FA”) were methylated to their fatty acid
methyl esters (”FAMEs”), the protocol of Breuer et al. 2013 was used. 3 mL methanol
containing 5% (v/v) sulfuric acid was added to the tubes containing the dried extracted
lipids. The tubes with the samples were closed, vortexed, and incubated for 3 hours at
70°C in a water bath. Every 30 min it was ensured that the samples were not boiling,
and the tubes were vortexed afterward. After the three hours, and once the samples
cooled to room temperature, 3 mL MilliQ water and 3 mL n­hexane were added for later
centrifugation at 1,200xG for 5 min. 2 mL of the hexane (top) phase were collected and
put in fresh glass tubes for each sample, where again MilliQ water (2 mL) was added to
wash the hexane phase. The samples were vortexed, then centrifuged for 5 min at 1200g,
and stored at ­20°C. In total, 3 mL of solvent were used per sample, and it was assumed
that 15% evaporated into the air.

using sunlight as an energy source. To reduce contamination, the starter cultures were
developed in photobioreactors (PBRs) by a third party. The PBRs were not part of the
factory’s facility, so the first starter cultures were bought from a third party. Then before
harvesting the biomass, a small section was recovered to continue the production of the
next batch.
A minimum optical density of 0,1A was assumed for the initial algae culture. The proposed
algal broth from the PBRs was then hypothetically added to the cultivation pond on one
side of the paddlewheel and circulated along the pond. After growth and circulation, the
algal biomass was collected at the harvest point on the other side of the paddlewheel
(Zewdie and Ali 2020). The electricity demand during cultivation was 0.30 MWh/ton of
dry algae (Zewdie and Ali 2020).
Emptying the pond and refilling it would be done during the same day. No water would
be recycled since the microalgae were assumed to consume all the nutrients of the broth.
Thanks to the actions of the microalgae, the now purified wastewater could be released
back into the ocean. Still, an environmental impact study must be conducted to ensure
the safety of the process. The environmental assessment falls out of the Project’s scope.
Nevertheless, it is crucial to operating legally and in line with local regulations.

Harvesting unit
Once the culture broth was set up and the microalgae grew to their optimum, harvesting
could be undertaken. Three harvesting units were hypothetically installed to harvest the
biomass from the pond. The algae broth (mf4) was first directed from the pond to the set­
tling unit (“SET”). It was assumed a 95% efficiency for the process (effDAF ) (Zewdie and
Ali 2020). After SET, biomass was concentrated to 20 g/L (2% concentration, bmconc,SET ).
This process occurred via auto­flocculation, where the algae precipitated due to the ac­
tion of carbonic components. No electricity demand was accounted for due to the auto­
flocculation (Patila et al. 2020).
It was assumed that the algae consumed all carbon present in the culture broth by the
end of the growth cycle. After settling, the solids were sent to the dissolved air floatation
(“DAF”) process. DAF is an alternative clarification process that uses micro air bubbles
to attach and float flocculated particles and suspended solids to the water surface for
removal (world n.d.). The DAF process would have an assumed capture efficiency of
90% (effDAF ) with 60 g/L (6% solids concentration, (bmconc,DAF ) as an output (Zewdie
and Ali 2020). Energy consumption of 9.44 kWh/ton of dry algae was assumed (Zewdie
and Ali 2020).

of 95% (effCEN) and energy consumption of 67.75 kWh/ton of dry algae were assumed

4
Discussion
Since none of the previous cases were economically feasible due to high nutrient prices,
it was logical to evaluate the hypothetical case where only wastewater was considered
as a nutrient source. The WW cases yielded a poor oil content when the levels of carbon
increased in the media, but it also showed a lower need for extra nutrients (F2 media),
which could be reflected in costs reduction. As part of this discussion, there was a need
to evaluate the case where only wastewater was used as a nutrient source.
The following results were based on the previously described methods and theoretical
data. They represent the maximum growth that the algae could theoretically achieve in
eight days. When assuming the pond was filled with 300,000 liters of wastewater, fol­
lowing the same procedure as for the other two scenarios (WW4 and ND_N2), a total
amount of 63.52 kg of carbon was present in the pond. Considering the biomass com­
position is 35.9% Carbon, the total biomass of 177.19 kg was obtained. The composition
translateed into a concentration of 0.59 g/L, 57% higher than for the WW4 case and 69%
higher than for the ND_N2 case. This theoretical scenario would produce approximately
1.2 tons of biodiesel, 9.1 kg of biogas, and 79.0 tons of biofertilizer a year, assuming the
same good oil/dry­weight ratio performance as in the other cases. Naturally, this was not
experimentally proved.
From an economic point of view, this scenario created incomes of 18.606,34 USD a year,
and because of the lack of extra nutrients added to the pond, the operational expenses
would decrease to 21,780.84 USD a year, 40% lower than for the WW4 case and 75%
lower than for the ND_N2 case. With an EBIT of ­3,174.50 USD per year, the project
is still not feasible, and the productivity of the strains would become the next item to
improve. Future calculations should assume a CAPEX of 25,000 USD for constructing
the open race pond (source) and 18,500 USD for constructing the wastewater treatment
plant (source). Nevertheless, a more in­depth economic analysis is needed in order to
consider local prices and costs.
A heat recovery system (HRS) is recommended when the Project aims to find an alterna­
tive way to fuel the cement plant. Usually, a cement plant has a total heat output of 811.3
kJ per kg of clinker (source), 54% in the kiln through the exhaust, and 46% in the cooler
through mid­air. The heat loss situation means that at least 484 kJ/kg (54%) could be
used for power generation. If we assume the production of the reference cement plant,
480,000 MJ could be used for power generation a year (1.332 MWh). A steam turbine
with a 40% efficiency could produce 532.8 MWh of electricity and cover at least 10% of
the electricity requirements.
For a 1 MW power plant, an investment of 1.32 million USD is needed (Verma, Mazumdar,
and Ghosh† 2020). So, if the reference cement plan has an electricity consumption of 15
MW, 20 million USD are needed. If the electricity is sold to the market at 0.066 USD/kWh,
then 1.32 million USD can be perceived as savings every year, and the plant would recover
the initial investment in 15 years (Verma, Mazumdar, and Ghosh† 2020).
When the project aims to sequester CO2 (CCS) from the cement plant, other options
could be proposed: like absorption with monoethanolamine (MEA), the oxyfuel process,
the chilled ammonia process, membrane­assisted CO2 liquefaction, and the calcium loop­
ing process with tail­end and integrated configurations. The literature says that MEA can
capture 64% of the CO2 emissions, while the newest technologies are between 73% and

==PAGE:60==
90% (Voldsund et al. 2019a). The primary energy consumption for CO2 avoided is be­
tween 1.63 and 4.07 MJ/kg CO2 for these newer technologies (Voldsund et al. 2019a). It
is also mentioned that the performance of the individual technologies shows strong depen­
dencies on site­specific and plant­specific factors. Therefore, rather than identifying one
single best technology, it is emphasized that CO2 capture in the cement industry should be
performed with a portfolio of capture technologies, where the preferred choice for each
specific plant is dependent on local factors (Voldsund et al. 2019a). When discussing
capital and operating costs, the cost of clinker increases from 49% to 92% compared
to the cost of clinker without capture (Voldsund et al. 2019b). The cost of CO2 avoided
is between 42 €/tCO2 (for the oxyfuel­based capture process) and 84 €/tCO2 (for the
membrane­based assisted liquefaction capture process)(Voldsund et al. 2019b). In con­
trast, the reference MEA­based absorption capture technology costs 80 €/tCO2(Voldsund
et al. 2019b). Taking the case of the reference cement plant that produces 850 kg CO2 per
ton of clinker according to table 2.19, between 30 and 60 million USD would be needed
per year to finance the investment and keep the prices low. It’s important to note that
the cost figures depend strongly on the steam source, electricity mix, electricity price, fuel
price, and the plant­specific characteristics. Nevertheless, creating synergies between in­
dustries or combining systems like a CCS and a HRS could help reduce costs and make
these solutions more attractive.

5
Conclusion
The robust characteristics of microalgae were shown in this work, illustrating the ability
of microalgae to adapt and survive under different light exposures, carbon source treat­
ments, and nitrogen levels.
The primary goal was to increase the algae biomass and lipid accumulation for biodiesel
production in tandem with sequestration of high carbon concentration. Based on the out­
comes of this research, it is first concluded that microalgae grow better in 12 hour­light­
periods instead of continuous periods of light.
Second, it was concluded that the best growth characteristics among strains occurred
when exposed to high nitrogen levels in the broth. The nitrogen concentration at 0.37 mg
per 30 mL of broth (0.012 g/L) gave the best growth and biomass production for all of the
strains examined. Nannochloropsis oceanica and Tisochrysis lutea registered a growth
of 0.515 and 0.214 g/L at day 13, respectively, but a low FAME content (154.4 and 96.5
ug/mg, respectively). This produced a high­quality FAME production of 1.5 mg FAME/mg
dry­weight per day for Tisochrysis lutea and 6.1 mg FAME/mg dry­weight per day for
Nannochloropsis oceanica. Since both strains were grown in full nitrogen environments,
their dependency on nutrients was at least three times higher than cases with N2 nutrient
levels. This fact would be reflected in a much higher OPEX, and not necessarily in higher
incomes.
Third, it was concluded that for only one strain, the lipid productivity increased when ex­
posing the culture to nitrogen starvation conditions. Chaetoceros muelleri registered the
highest oil/dry­weight ratio among all strains when exposed to the lowest nitrogen levels
(N2, 0.005 g/L) and no carbon. Although the growth was much slower (0.182 g/L at day
8), Chaetoceros muelleri registered the highest high­quality­FAME ratio (218 ug/mg) and
one of the highest high­quality­FAME production values per day (4.9 mg FAME/mg dry­
weight).
Fourth, it was concluded that the performance was not better when exposing the best
performing strain to higher carbon levels in the culture broth. The maximum amount of
high­quality FAME’s was produced without carbon in the culture. Growth under normal
nutrition and nutrient depletion gave lower lipid productivity at higher carbon concentra­
tion. Chaetoceros muelleri under nutrient depletion (N2) was the best performing strain
for biodiesel production, with one of the highest high­quality FAME production amount
per day and the lowest nutrient’s demand. After evaluating the upscaled technical model,
it can be concluded that using the same growth scenarios used in the laboratory is not
possible in order to keep the Project economically feasible. Even though the costs related
to the thermal energy requirements could be ignored thanks to the synergy with the heat
waste recovery system of the cement plant, the costs exceeded the incomes. The main
contributor to a negative net income is the cost of the nutrients related to F2 media.
After a theoretical assessment of using only wastewater to nurture the culture, it can be
concluded that this could be the best way to reduce the external need for nutrient and
their costs. Still, this is not enough to achieve a positive net income per year. Further
strain studies need to be carried out to improve the FAME yield, since the primary source
of income is not biodiesel but biofertilizer. For further analysis, the income from the sale
Microalgae and the potential for biofuel production
53
==PAGE:62==
of omega­three fatty acids could be considered, even though the amounts produced are
minimal.
Chaetoceros muelleri under nutrient depletion can be recommended to produce high qual­
ity and quantity of oil. The strain showed flexibility to adapt to changing physio­chemical
conditions, which means that it would likely adapt to local changing environmental condi­
tions and provide a more stable and productive culture.
When considering the construction of a pilot project, locally treated wastewater should be
analyzed and compared with a synthetic one. Chaetoceros muelleri should be exposed to
local wastewater, and its growth speed and oil content measured before recommending
it as a potential biofuel source.
According to the model, an integrated microalgae system for biodiesel, biogas, and biofer­
tilizer production is a promising alternative for reducing costly inputs and energy­intensive
processes. Through anaerobic digestion, nutrients and energy are recycled, resulting in
a closed­loop for biofuel production, which maximizes energy and by­products.
