Abstract 
 
Skeletal muscles make up much of the human body mass and serve important 
functions for movement metabolism and accurate in-vitro models are needed for 
drug development and toxicity studies. Studies have shown that continuous elec-
trical stimulation of muscle tissue can help with its maturation and create 2D 
and 3D structures that better mimic in-vivo muscle structures and critical physi-
ological functions needed for clinical testing. 
This report covers the design and development of modular platform and chip sys-
tem for creating electronic tissue models using rapid prototyping. Chips were 
manufactured using Direct-Ink-Writing using standard microscope chips as a ba-
sis. These were integrated in a platform created using Stereolithography 3D 
printing developed through an iterative prototyping process. 
Preliminary analysis of this system as a muscle pacing system showed promise 
with test showing the needed electrical and signal transmission needed for elec-
trical stimulation of electroactive cells. Furthermore, the modularity of the sys-
tem also open up opportunities for the device to be used in other electric tissue 
examination such as measurement of contractile forces of muscle cells.

1. 
Introduction 
 
Accurate in vitro replicas of human tissue derived e.g., from induced pluripotent 
stem cells, have the potential to fundamentally redefine biomedical and pharma-
ceutical research. The ability to interphase such tissue models with electrical 
sensors and stimulators have proven highly valuable for recording tissue-level 
physiological properties and responses, as well as for maturing tissue towards 
adult phenotypes. This is particularly true for neuronal, cardiac and skeletal 
muscle tissues that are electrically and mechanically active. 
The fabrication of electronic tissue culturing systems usually relies on a combi-
nation of complex techniques not least lithographical, clean room-derived manu-
facturing routines. As an intriguing low-cost alternative, additive manufacturing 
techniques –commonly referred as 3D printing– have emerged. In principle, 
these techniques allow for rapid design, prototyping and testing of complex de-
vices encompassing multiple functional materials and modalities. 
1.1 
Aims 
The aim of this project is to establish a modular, rapid prototyping route for gen-
erating electrical culturing platforms, based on a combination of 3D printing 
techniques. The system will encompass an electronic chip holder and a range of 
single-use electrical tissue culturing chips. As specific case, chips aimed at elec-
trical stimulation of cardiac and skeletal muscle cultures will be developed.  
• Aim 1: Design and fabricate a chip-holder which can be applied in common 
cell-culturing incubator environments, allows for tissue monitoring using 
optical microscopy, as well as no-tool, easy and robust chip replacement 
and wiring 
• Aim 2: Design and manufacture of single-use chips for electrical stimula-
tion of tissue cultures.  
• Aim 3: Proof of principle test of single-use chips for electrical stimulation 
of skeletal and/or cardiac tissue cultures. 
==PAGE:10==
6 of 55 
 
 
The chip holder manufacturing will be based on commercial SLA printers, while 
single-use chips will be made using a multi-material micro-extrusion printed 
combined with custom print materials derived from polystyrene. As basis for the 
project, a brief literature overview will be conducted.

2. 
Theory 
2.1 
Cell culturing and tissue models of skeletal muscle 
In order to successfully create drugs and medical devices that are clinically rele-
vant and capable of improving the life of patients in a safe and controlled man-
ner it is important to create tissue models that are as accurate as possible. This 
is no less so the case when working with models of skeletal muscles. These mus-
cles are the most abundant type in the human body and account for 40% of our 
body weight which makes them extremely relevant in many areas of medicine 
[1].  
When striving to create an accurate tissue model it is first and foremost im-
portant to consider the initial source of cell material that is utilized. Here, it is of 
course possible to source cells from other mammals and while these are the most 
readily available, they are, in most cases, not close enough to human in-vivo cells 
to be of relevance in late-stage drug screenings and tissue culture devices. There-
fore, human cell sources are needed in order to create the best conditions for suc-
cess, and here three primary sources are available. Firstly, immortalized cell 
lines can be obtained from rapidly growing tumours and are easily obtained and 
cultured due to their high growth potential. This rapid growth however also 
means that these cell types are often quite different from in-vivo cells and of poor 
relevance physiologically[2]. Much higher relevance can be obtained by using pri-
mary human cells obtained from explants given by human doners. These have 
shown to create very accurate laboratory replicas of native tissues but have a sig-
nificantly lower growth potential than immortalized cell lines. Primary cells 
have also shown to have short windows of relevance as they quickly undergo cell 
senescence which results in deviations from the tissue they are meant to repre-
sent[1]. Lastly, the most promising source of cells for tissue models seem to be 
stem cells which have shown capable of differentiating into all cell types of the 
body. These were initially sourced from human embryos, but this practice was 
faced by many ethical issues which made cells hard to obtain. Recently, however 
human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSC) have shown great promise in 
==PAGE:12==
8 of 55 
 
remedying these problems since they can be reprogrammed from the primary cell 
sources and grown into, for example, skeletal muscle cells [1][2]. An example of 
this can be seen in a study by Rao et al. in which an overexpression of the tran-
scribing factor pax7 was used to create functional muscle tissue from hiPSC [3]. 
A schematic of this process can be seen in Figure 1. 
  
Figure 1 Schematic of myotube generation from pluripotent stem cells. Source: Rao et al. [3] 
 With potential cell sources in mind, the quality of tissue models can also be im-
proved in several ways by implementing different strategies during growth and 
culturing of the cells. When looking specifically at skeletal muscle cells, these 
have traditionally been cultured in in 2D monolayers. This has resulted in tissue 
models that have been useful for high-throughput drug testing but have ulti-
mately yielded myotubes that were immature when compared to in-vivo scenar-
ios [1]. To further expand upon this, 3D tissue models have started to emerge as 
a way to culture skeletal muscle cells in a way that is more physiologically rele-
vant and mimics the structure found in humans. Rao et al. showed this by em-
bedding cells in a scaffold of fibrin-based hydrogel which allowed them to create 
bundles of skeletal muscles with key factors such as generation of contractile 
forces and calcite transients that were superior to the 2D cultures mentioned 
above [3].  Generally, 3D tissue cultures show promise in creating models with 
==PAGE:13==
9 of 55 
 
higher clinical relevance that previously. In this field, techniques are emerging 
for 3D tissue models created both with and without a supporting scaffold. Of 
these, scaffold-based solutions have shown the most promise so far with an abil-
ity to create complex structure and functional models such as the one shown by 
Rao et al while scaffold-free methods are still in their inception but show promise 
by producing tissues with high local cell density [2]. 
When working with skeletal muscles or other contractile cell types such as car-
diac tissue, electrical stimulation can also be a very important factor in the mat-
uration of cells. Several studies have shown that regular electrical stimulation of 
skeletal muscle cells has improved the maturation process of the tissue and its 
functional properties [1], [4], [5]. More specifically a study made by Khodabukus 
and Baar has shown that 24 hours of electrical stimulation through pacing re-
sulted in a doubling of the specific force produced by their muscle tissue. In this 
report it is also stressed however, that voltages for stimulation must be carefully 
chosen as high pulse amplitudes compared to the rheobase can result in electro-
chemical damage to the cells. 
 
2.2 
Electronic tissue culturing devices 
As mentioned in the previous section, skeletal muscles and electrosensitive tis-
sue in general can benefit greatly from receiving electrical stimulation during 
culturing but the use of electronics in the growing of cells extends far beyond 
this. Electronic tissue culturing devices (ETCD) can be found in many configura-
tions and with many purposes. In a paper by Modena et al. the researches high-
light a number of electronic testing and sensing methods that could be integrated 
into microphysiological systems [6]. Here, the sensing systems are designed to 
function on many cell types and not only in tandem with electroactive cells such 
as the ones found in cardiac or muscle tissue. Although not all of the sensor types 
will be mentioned here, an example could be some of the electrochemical sensing 
systems that are presented. One such sensor is the enzyme-based sensor in 
which the sensing electrode is functionalized using specific enzymes that 
==PAGE:14==
10 of 55 
 
interacts with a target molecule. In the case of sensors that target e.g., glucose or 
lactate the electrode is functionalized with glucose oxidase or lactose oxidase re-
spectively. Another example of a widely useful electrochemical sensor is the PH-
sensor in which metal oxides are introduced as working electrodes along with a 
reference electrode. This makes it possible to monitor, for example the acidity 
levels in the extracellular environment [6]. Modena et al. suggest that these, and 
other, types of sensors can be readily integrated into already existing culture 
platforms such as Organ-on-a-chip devices and a common advantage to all ETCD 
is that they allow for on-site testing without moving or disturbing the cells un-
necessarily which could damage them. Instead, cell substrate as well as 2D and 
3D tissues can be monitored continuously throughout the culturing process on 
the chip where they are grown.   
As mentioned above, the sensors presented by Modena et al. are meant to be 
broadly applicable and work even for cell cultures that are not electrogenic. In 
the case of electrogenic cell types such as cardiac tissue, however, multi-electrode 
arrays (MEA) and interdigitated electrodes (IDEs) can be commonly found in 
ETCD. An example of a ETCD can be seen in a paper published by Zhao et al. 
Here a device has been made to fit the standard footprint of a 96-well plate and 
cast with microwires. To achieve this a PDMS master was made onto which a 
polystyrene plate was cast with electrodes. A series of polymer wires were then 
cast onto the polystyrene plate to function as anchor points for cardiac tissue. Fi-
nally, the base plate was fused with at commercially available bottomless well 
plate and plate cap which resulted in the final device looking much like a com-
mon 96-well plate. This manufacturing process of the plate can be seen in Figure 
2. Due to the integration of electrodes and polymer beams it was possible to both 
stimulate the cardiac tissue electrically throughout the culturing time as well as 
monitor the contractive force of the tissue by the deflection of the beams. This 
method of creating an ETCD generates 2D cell cultures that, while not as ad-
vanced as other 3D cultures, allows for high throughput and offers the additional 
benefit of being scalable to any commercial plate size.

Another device with lower throughput potential but higher functionality was 
made by Qian et al. They created a chip that featured both a MEA as well as 
IDEs with a single well on top. Both the MEA and IDEs were patterned on a 
glass slide using photolithography and electron beam evaporation. The polysty-
rene well and the two connectors were then subsequently glued to the glass 
plate. An overview of this can be seen on Figure 3. After testing the chip proved 
successful in multifunctional recording of cell capabilities and was able to meas-
ure growth, electrophysiology, contraction and activation map of in-vivo cultured 
cardiac tissue [8].

In cases where a more complex tissue model is needed than the two 2D solutions 
presented above, Maoz et al. have managed to create a heart-on-a-chip model

bottom layer with MEA and TEER electrodes as well as a top layer with TEER 
electrodes were also created. The five layers of the chip were then assembled into 
the final device as seen on Figure 4. Tests of this device showed good functional-
ity of both MEA and TEER sensors and allowed for the chip to record both drug 
effects and biological processes making it a viable tool for assessments of cardio-
toxicity [9].

2.3 
Direct Ink Writing 
Direct Ink Writing (DIW) is an additive manufacture that relies on the extrusion 
of custom inks and distinguishes itself by being the AM method with perhaps the 
largest diversity in available materials and applications. In DIW inks are often 
custom made to the user’s specifications and extruded through one of two differ-
ent methods. These methods are continuous filament writing and droplet jetting 
respectively and can be seen in Figure 5 [10]. 
 
Figure 5 Methods of Direct ink writng. A) Continuos filament writing. B) Droplet jetting. Source: [10] 
==PAGE:18==
14 of 55 
 
A typical DIW machine consists of a gantry robot capable of moving along the 
XYZ axes. This robot is equipped with one or more cartridges into which desired 
inks are loaded and then subsequentially extruded. With this basic framework in 
place, the general quality and properties of printed parts or geometries is of 
course based somewhat on the level of sophistication of the robot but more heav-
ily on the inks used [11].  
DIW as an AM tool should generally not be compared to other similar rapid pro-
totyping tools. This is due to the fact that most other AM methods are specialized 
towards a single material class (typically polymers of metals) and optimized to-
wards printing these in a fast and replicable way. While DIW is certainly capable 
of these things the main appeal of the methods is more so its ability to create 3D 
structures using almost any material although printability relies heavily on the 
rheological and viscoelastic properties of the individual inks. Additionally, DIW 
offers the possibility of switching between inks whilst printing which makes it 
possible to make multi-material structures for specific applications. Because of 
these advantages DIW is often utilized in the production of specialized electronic 
components where it is used to create e.g., microelectrodes, supercapacitors and 
electric circuits. Examples of electronic circuits printed from soft electronic inks 
can be seen on Figure 6 [10]. 
 
Figure 6 Examples of flexible electronics printed using DIW. A) Sensor array wrapped around human finger. B) LED array 
wrapped around human finger. C) Strain sensor and microcontroller circuits mounted on textile. Source [10] 
While DIW has found success within production of electronics it has also shown 
much potential in biological applications where bioinks can be produced to create 
better tissue models.  
==PAGE:19==
15 of 55 
 
Utilizing the correct ways of creating bioinks it is possible to replicate many in-
vivo biological features. In a paper by Gu et al. a study has been done on the bio-
medical applications of bioinks and DIW. In this study the researchers highlight 
four categories of application for these tools being cytobiology, drug research, tu-
mour modelling and regenerative medicine. The paper also shows successful ap-
plications of DIW and bioinks in fabrications of tissue models for skin, bone, 
nerves muscles, adipose tissue, blood vessels and organoids which again empha-
sizes the versatility of this AM tool [12].    
 
2.4 
Stereolithography 
Stereolithography (SLA) is a type of AM which belongs in the category of vat 
photopolymerization. This type of AM device typically consists of a tray contain-
ing a photosensitive thermoset in resin form which is exposed to a certain wave-
length of light from a laser. This hardens that resin into a photopolymer that sits 
in a build plate. After the desired area has been hardened, the built plate is then 
Figure 7 Breakdown of the basic functionality of an SLA printer. Source: Formlabs [10] 
==PAGE:20==
16 of 55 
 
moved slightly to create the next layer until the model is completed[13]. A dia-
gram of this working principle can be seen in Figure 7.   
SLA 3D printing is a tool that, when applied correctly, can be useful in many as-
pects of biomedical product design. Like other AM tools SLA is first of all useful 
for rapid prototyping where models can quickly be created to showcase specific 
design features or mechanical functions. In this sense SLA will often be com-
pared to the extrusion based fused deposit modelling (FDM) method since these 
two ways of 3D printing are the most commonly used in the prototyping stage of 
product development.  
When making this comparison it is important to note that while SLA printing is 
often more costly than FDM it excels in applications where a smooth surface fin-
ish is desired as well as higher dimensional accuracy and tighter tolerances. This 
is due to the higher resolution that is achieved when using a laser for SLA print-
ing, rather than extrusion through a nozzle as is done for FDM. It also means 
that SLA generally is the preferable method for models with small details and 
interlocking parts. Another advantage found in SLA printing compared to FDM 
is the crosslinking that happens as part of the photopolymerization process 
which increases chemical resistance and allows devices to be cleaned with alco-
hol, making them more suitable for biomedical applications[14]. It also creates 
models that are isotropic as opposed to FDM where models will have different 
mechanical properties depending on which direction they receive stress or strain 
from[15]. 
It is worth noting that in spite of the advantages of SLA printed parts listed 
above, models created this way are relatively brittle. This is still something that 
can be mitigated through the use of different resin types but generally this fabri-
cation method is not well suited for parts that experience significant mechanical 
strain and this should be avoided if designing functional prototypes[16]. 
Although SLA printing can be useful for quickly creating and modifying proto-
types of different devices, there are also many examples of its use as a direct part 
of medical treatment or analysis as well as in organ models used for research. 
Examples of the former include detailed personal dental models for medical 
==PAGE:21==
17 of 55 
 
training and education as well as personalised hearing aid among many 
more[17]. Examples of SLA models that are personalized for the patient also in-
clude jaw and dental implants as well as prosthetics[18]. 
As mentioned, SLA printing and other vat photopolymerization methods have 
also had success in the work to create tissue and organ models. Here, cell-based 
bioinks have been used to create for example cartilage and liver models, among 
many others, which showed potential for use in drug screenings or regeneration 
of tissue in human patients[12].

3. 
Results 
3.1 
Initial design brief 
In order to create a satisfactory final design of the device it was important to 
first get a detailed idea of the scope of the project and what had previously been 
attempted. In doing so it was important to first analyse the existing solution and 
what opportunities it offered as well as its limitations. An existing ETCD made 
for the TMAT research group can be seen in Figure 8. 
 
Figure 8 Old ETCD device. Left: Assembled with nuts highlighted in red. Right: Disassembled showing the different parts 
As shown in Figure 8 the previous design consists of a base plate able to hold a 
total of four microscope slides. The design then features a top part consisting of a 
polymer part fitted with a series of pogo-pins that press down on the slides as 
well as two connecter ports for connection with electronic devices. This top part 
is held in place with a metal rod and screwed in place using two nuts which are 
highlighted in red in Figure 8. 
The design described above was considered an initial proof of principle for an 
ETCD capable of working with standard sized microscope slides but has several 
drawbacks which had to be remedied in future design. First among these was the 
general assembly method which required users to screw and unscrew the small 
nuts by hand whenever the slides needed to be replaced. This could be difficult 
and time consuming, especially when working in a lab setting while wearing rub-
ber gloves. Additionally, the device was made up of several different parts of 
==PAGE:23==
19 of 55 
 
different materials many of which required different methods of fabrication. An 
example of this would be the base plate of the device which is made up of both a 
polymer plate, produced in a laser cutter and a metal plate which had to be cut 
using a different machine. Lastly, the top part featured a custom-made circuit 
board fitted with the different connectors which was then attached to several pol-
ymer parts using screws and glue. This made the system relatively inflexible and 
would result in a fairly long production time if the desired functionality of the de-
vice was to be changed and a new top part had to be created.  
The challenges described above are also what have formed the base for the aims 
described in section 1.1. This means that the new solution was designed to be 
fabricated using AM methods to reduce prototyping and eventually production 
and assembly time. Additionally, the aim of the new device design was to create 
a platform which to some extent was modular to overcome the issue of having to 
create a new circuit board when switching from for example electrical pacing and 
stimulation of cell tissue to impedance sensing or other studies of cell functional-
ity. As part of the brief for the new device design it was also decided that it 
should only fit a single microscope slide at a time, making the model smaller, 
less bulky and quicker to change. 
 
3.2 
Design specifications and requirements  
With the base goal of the design in place as outlined in the previous section the 
next step was to establish which specification and requirements were important 
to achieve the design brief and satisfy the users of the device. These specifica-
tions were divided into sections to clarify the source of each requirement for the 
final product. 
 
3.2.1 Ergonomics 
First of all, it was important to consider the ergonomics of the product. This was 
not an area that presented a lot of strict demands for the product as it was not 
==PAGE:24==
20 of 55 
 
expected to be heavy or complicated enough to potentially harm the user and did 
not require continuous handheld operation which would result in specifications 
for grips or work positions. Even so, it was surmised that the final device should 
be as small and light as possible so as not to inconvenience the user when mov-
ing it around or take up unnecessary lab space. Additionally, any features of the 
design that the user would potentially interface with during operation should be 
large and simple enough to be operated in a lab setting so as to avoid similar is-
sues to the nut and screw assembly solution of the previous design. Lastly, the 
design of the device as a whole should be unambiguous to avoid any risk of parts 
being assembled incorrectly by the user. 
 
3.2.2 Environment 
 Next on the list of product specifications were considerations relating to the op-
erating environment of the device. Since it would be used as a tool in tissue cul-
turing the main operating conditions would be biological or chemical lab settings 
and the design should conform to these conditions. This mainly raises demands 
in relation to cleanliness and sterilization of the device. When working with cells 
it is naturally necessary to avoid cross-contamination and as such it is of critical 
importance that it is possible to sterilize the device by some means. Additionally, 
the design of the device should in general avoid features resulting in small gaps, 
crevices or other places where dirt, bacteria or similar could gather and would be 
hard to keep clean.    
 
3.2.3 Materials and technical specifications 
Not many specifications were needed in terms of mechanical stress on the final 
device as it would not be expected to be subject to any significant stress, strain or 
loads during conventional operation. It should of course be noted that the device 
should withstand being handled by users on a day-to-day basis and be able to en-
dure assembly and disassembly a significant number of times during its lifetime 
without deformation or snapping of parts.  
==PAGE:25==
21 of 55 
 
In terms of materials there were also few requirements to be met since, as men-
tioned, the device had few mechanical constraints and aesthetic considerations 
such as colour were of lesser importance compared to functionality. This also fit 
well since, at this point, it had been established that the main part of the proto-
typing work would be achieved using a Formlabs Form 3 SLA printer. While this 
printer does have a range of materials available it was desired to use a standard 
black resin. This was due to the fact that this resin is not only the most easily 
workable of the available types but also cheaper than engineering type resins

printer would be used also resulted in a general specification of print size limit 
and general volume of parts printed given by the maximum build volume of 14,5 ·

able to withstand some form of sterilization. Although the materials available 
through the Form 3 printer are not able to withstand autoclaving as is common 
with a lot of lab equipment, the cross-bound nature of the photopolymers used in 
SLA printers are able to withstand cleaning with alcohols [19]. 
 
3.2.4 Equipment and electronics 
Asides from the specifications that are directly related to the use and functional-
ity of the device it is also relevant to consider the other pieces of equipment that 
it must work in tandem with as well as the electric components that must be in-
tegrated in order to achieve the desired device capabilities. First of all, the device 
should be able to connect to an electronic pacing machine or sensing devices. 
This means the device must be able to integrate connectors such as the pogo-pins 
found on the top part of the old design that will press down on the microscope 
slides as well as a wired output from these that is able to connect to a pacing or 
sensing machine to allow interaction with the cell tissues. Aside from simply in-
tegrating these components the device must also feature a way to protect sol-
dered connections from chemicals or water, so the wiring is not damaged.  
==PAGE:26==
22 of 55 
 
It is also worth noting that the device might need to be in use while cells are 
placed in an incubator. This means that wires will likely need to go out through 
the door of the incubator and therefore must not be too bulky to ensure that the 
door can be properly closed, and cells inside are kept in optimal conditions for 
growth. The device may also need to be used while in a LAF bench. Since these 
function by blowing air from the top of the chamber to the bottom it is especially 
important to be aware of crevices and openings in the top of the device. Asides 
from this it might also be favourable to design the device towards one-hand oper-
ation since this can often be easier when working in a fume hood. 
Lastly, the microscope slide will need to be viewed through a microscope to ob-
serve the cultured cells. The microscope used so far with the old device views 
samples from below and as such the devices requires visibility of the slide from 
beneath as can also be seen in the previous design. Additionally, to ensure that 
the tissue sample is within the focal zone of the microscope lens it must be en-
sured that the microscope slide is as close to the bottom of the device as possible 
and not lifted too far from where the microscope is able to view it.

3.3 
Device design and prototyping process  
With the specifications and requirements outlined in section 3.2 a design process 
was started to figure out the optimal shape and functionality of the device. It 
was quickly decided that the device would still have a bottom part, which would 
hold the microscope slide, as well a top part which would hold the electronics 
much like in the previous design. As part of this several experiments were made 
to explore methods of joining the two parts with snap-fits, sliding connections 
and magnets being the main methods considered. Snap-fits were quickly dis-
carded though since the relatively small size of the device would result in very 
small features that were likely to break. Sliding connections, while possible, 
where also quickly discarded since the material proved to generate a lot of fric-
tion when sliding against itself and would often get stuck. Therefore, it was cho-
sen to use neodymium magnets as the primary method of connection between the 
two main parts of the device. It should be noted that it was, of course, still possi-
ble to use nuts and bolts or similar mechanical solutions to connect the two 
parts. It was however, chosen to forego this from the beginning of the design face 
since integrating these things into the 3D printed parts would require additional 
post processing. This was deemed undesirable since it was the ambition to create 
a solution that would be as easy as possible to recreate, change or modify in the 
future.

Figure 10 shows an early design iteration of the ETCD. In this version of the pro-

would hold the magnets. The bottom part had an indent in which the slide would 
be placed which was extended with a semicircle at one end, making room for the 
user to remove the slide with a finger without the need to push from underneath. 
In the top part, holes were made which would hold one pogo-pin each allowing

the future. These holes were made to function as press-fits for the pogo-pins by 
being slightly smaller than their outer measurements. 
While promising, this design had a series of flaws that meant further ideation 
was needed. First of all, if the magnets were simply fastened in the holes, they

had the potential to knock of small chips with the potential to contaminate

leave the tops of the pogo-pins and the wires connecting them exposed to outside 
forces which did not meet the design specifications. At this point it was also 
noted that the device should feature a way to fasten these wires so that, if pulled,

3.5 
SLA printing 
An important part of the design process has also been the technological capabili-
ties and limitations related to SLA printing. Firstly, the printing process is 
strongly related to the overall dimensional tolerances of the parts and their abil-
ity to fit together. One of the more general factors related to this is the overall 
==PAGE:41==
37 of 55 
 
resolution of the printer. In Preform, the printing software related to the Form-
labs printers, the options offered for resolution are 100, 50 and 25 mikrons and 
fairly early in the process it was decided to drop this from 100 microns to 50. 
This did impact overall printing speed somewhat but was chosen to ensure that 
finer features of the design would be properly expressed.  
Aside from this however, it is also very important to consider the orientation of 
features in both the design as well as when printing. An example of this would 
be features such as holes or cylinders which tend to have the highest geometrical 
accuracy when printed in the same direction as the print direction. An example 
of this can be seen on Figure 18 where the top part of the earlier hinge design 
can be seen in Preform. When focusing on the “hinge arm” of this part the upper 
orientation in the figure would be the favorable one. This is due to the fact that 
the central axis of the cylinder that makes up the hinge arm is oriented in the 
same direction as the print (shown with red arrow) If the part was instead ori-
ented like the bottom of Figure 18, round features could become slightly ellipsis 
shaped which would make the top and bottom part of the hinge harder to fit to-
gether. 
==PAGE:42==
38 of 55 
 
 
Figure 18 Example of part orientation in relation to print direction. Print direction shown with red arrow 
Another important factor to consider when using 3D printing in design is the 
need for support during the printing process. On Figure 19 the hinge part can 
again be seen in the two orientations also shown in Figure 18 but with the appro-
priate supports generated in order for the part to print successfully. When look-
ing at the Figure 19, the uppermost orientation is once again the favorable one in 
this case since most of the support generated here are attached to features that 
will not interface with other parts of the design. When looking at the bottom ori-
entation however, two potential problem areas have been highlighted in red cir-
cles. If the part was printed this way, supports would be generated on both the 
inside of the part where it would slide against the cassette as well as on the 
hinge arm that would need to fit into the bottom part. This is a problem since the 
support structures will leave small bumps on all supported surfaces which will 
either require very rough tolerances to accommodate or more likely require addi-

ble and sometimes required it is still best to avoid on this case since it adds to

With the design of the protype for holding the microscope slide in place the 
next step was to create the desired chip for later testing based on the slide ge-

could be used for pacing of skeletal muscle cells and as such inspiration was 
initially drawn from similar chips that had been constructed previously.’ 
In Figure 20 two examples can be seen of chips designs that had previously 
been made in the TMAT research team with the purpose of pacing cells elec-
trically. As can be seen from the figure, each chip has round interface points 
at the end of each electrode arranged in clusters of 4 which fit with the pogo-
pins also used for the design in this report. Both of the chips have been con-
structed using DIW and feature the same first two steps for their

manufacture. More specifically, a silver ink was used in both cases to create 
parallel lines of electrodes on the chips. These silver electrodes have then 
been coated with a carbon-based ink the well areas where tissues are to be

terface points for the pogo-pins have however, been left as just silver to en-
sure the best electrical connection. The two chip designs in Figure 20 then dif-
fer in the material choice for the wells printed around the electrodes with the 
upper of the two designs favoring a soft silicone well material while the bot-

4. 
Discussion 
 
One of the core points of interest in this project has been to investigate the suita-
bility of SLA and DIW 3D printing as a tool to rapidly create a platform for elec-
tronic tissue culturing in a way that was inexpensive and quick when compared 
to conventional fabrication methods such as lithography. Through this project a 
chip-and-holder system has been developed which shows promise in achieving 
this goal by creating a proof-of-concept model with the theoretical capability to 
function as a pacing device for electroactive cells. 
In its current state however, the device still has several areas in which it can im-
proved. These will be discussed in this section. 
4.1 
Materials and production 
Although SLA shows promise as a method of manufacture for tissue culturing 
devices it should still be considered whether the current choice of materials and 
production processes are the most optimal for this objective.  
It has been noted previously that for the purposes of this report a standard SLA 
resin has been used but there are still several choices of materials that could im-
prove upon the current model. As shown in this report the current material 
choice results in models that are somewhat fragile and have suffered form break-
age several times during the design process. This has been somewhat mitigated 
by improving part thicknesses in critical areas and by tweaking individual toler-
ances of parts to reduce friction on assembly. It is still a concern, however, 
whether the device would avoid breaking after regular use over an extended pe-
riod of time, especially for thinner features such as the snap fit on the cassette. 
While it is naturally still possible to increase the thickness of critical parts this 
will also increase print time, material use and eventually the bulkiness of the de-
sign and as so it might be worth considering other materials as well. Formlabs 
offer several different engineering resins that have the potential to increase 
properties such as toughness, durability and flexibility which all could have the 
==PAGE:53==
49 of 55 
 
potential to increase the longevity of the device and save users time in having to 
replace broken parts too often. The biggest issue with these engineering resins, 
however, is that they have a higher tendency towards warping which complicates 
fits between parts. In this case it might me a solution to create some parts from 
tougher resins while keeping the standard resin for parts more likely to warp 
Warping of parts in general is also something that should be looked into as a 
part of improving the printing process. So far, most parts of the device have been 
subject to relatively little warping but even so reducing warping makes it possi-
ble to tighten tolerances further, thereby reducing total variation in the system. 
 
Figure 26 Two possible orientations for bottom part of device

Figure 26 shows the bottom part of the final design which is the part most likely 
to experience warping due to being relatively large and flat. The top part of Fig-
ure 26 shows the printing orientation for this part that has been used through 
most of the project since it uses the least support material and results in the low-
est printing time. Shown below however is the part at a 45° angle which was 
used later in the project to reduce warping since the layers in this orientation are 
no longer parallel to the biggest face of the part. This resulted in some reduced 
warping but should be experimented with further to investigate angle and orien-
tation for all parts that will result in the lowest warpage while still retaining di-
mension precision of important features. As another way to reduce warping, the 
post curing step of the print process should also be optimized. For the purposes 
of this project, most parts were cured by UV lighting at a temperature of 60°C. 
The heat of this process might also be cause for some of the observed warping 
and therefore it might result in an improvement if parts were cured using only 
UV light at room temperature but for a longer time. 
 
4.2 
Design 
In terms of design the next step of improvement should be to focus on user test-
ing of the device. This would allow for observations of user behavior while inter-
acting with the device and should give clarifications as to which features should 
be iterated on further in order to create the best possible final product. One fea-
ture that was scrapped during the design phase of the device was the possibility 
of improved one hand operation of the device that came with the hinge design. 
Allowing user to test the product would clarify whether this is unnecessary or 
whether it should be an aim to reintroduce this feature.  
Additionally, users with no prior knowledge of the project should be encouraged 
to assemble the device from scratch to evaluate how intuitive the design is. This 
would also give further clarification on the subject of material choice presented 
in the previous section. If many features of the design were to snap during 
==PAGE:55==
51 of 55 
 
assembly by a new user, it might be prudent to consider tougher material 
choices. Alternatively, a single feature continuously snapping might be solvable 
through pure design.  
Beyond this, further possibilities for assembly should also be explored. Currently 
the top and bottom part are connected using neodymium magnets with four mag-
nets going to each of the two parts. This somewhat increases the cost of produc-
ing a single device but also means that more steps must be taken in post pro-
cessing after printing the device. With optimizations made in terms of material, 
tolerances and warping it might be possible to instead reintroduce the notion of a 
sliding connection between the two or, alternatively, use a snap fit connection 
such as the one used in the cassette. While this is not strictly necessary for the 
overall function of the product it is still desirable since the device should mainly 
be considered a research tool. This means that adaptability is key in order for re-
searches to quickly modify the design to their individual needs and that is im-
proved by being able to assemble the components directly after printing instead 
of having to acquire additional parts such as magnets from a supplier.  
  
4.3 
Future testing and experiments 
User tests are not the only experiments that should be made in order to further 
verify the functionality and viability of the device that has ben developed. Fur-
ther tests of the electrical functionality and biological relevance of the device 
should also be performed.  
First among these is a completion of the pacing experiment that was started dur-
ing this project. Here, it is first and foremost important to finish the proof-of-
principle chip with wells and carbon covered electrodes. With this completed 
skeletal muscle tissue should be grown and cultured between the electrodes and 
regularly stimulated using electrical pacing. If this experiment is successful, it 
should show muscle tissue with myotubes that are more mature than when cul-
tured in a 2D well system and are better reflect the muscles seen in-vivo.  
==PAGE:56==
52 of 55 
 
Asides from this experiment, future test should also aim to further utilize the ca-
pabilities of the device as well as the DIW printer. This would mean creating 
chip with multiple functionalities that use more of the pin connections that are 
available in the device. An example of this could be a chip that also featured and 
IDE or MEA system as well as deflectable polymer beams that would be able to 
‘measure for example the contractile forces of skeletal tissues.

5. 
Conclusion 
 
In this report a device aimed at electrical stimulation and sensing of cells has 
been developed. The main focus of the report has been to investigate the viability 
of rapid prototyping as a tool to quickly and cheaply create tissue culturing plat-
forms instead of the lengthy and costly clean-room procedures that are standard 
for the current methods such as lithography. More specifically SLA and DIW 
have been used to create a modified microscope chip and culturing platform that 
will allow for implementation of different sensing or stimulating arrangements 
to be implemented onto a standard microscope slide format. 
The culturing platform that has been developed using SLA printing consists of 
four parts. One is a bottom part which holds the microscope slide in place. On top 
of this a top part sits which holds a tow-part cassette with four pogo-pin connect-
ors. These can be connected to various electrical devices that can either stimu-
late electroactive tissue through pacing or sense important physiological tissue 
properties such as contractile forces. 
For the purposes of this report a microscope slide chip was designed for electrical 
pacing of cells, featuring two wells with carbon covered silver electrodes. In prac-
tice it has only been possible so far to print the chip with the silver electrodes, 
but this did still for some preliminary testing and design verification. Through 
these tests it was confirmed that the electrical resistance of the system that had 
been devised was marginal compared to that of cell media and would not inter-
fere with the voltage that was sent to the tissue. Furthermore, a test using an os-
cilloscope confirmed that the device and chip were indeed capable of sending the 
desired square wave signal to the cells which would be needed to stimulate them 
electrically.
