Abstract:  
This report attempts to establish, via case study methodology, the means by which a positioning 
strategy as represented by the Quadric Framework can be supported by technology roadmapping 
(TRM) methodology, with particular reference to the operational evidence which is aligned with 
that positioning strategy. The supporting literature is reviewed to establish a theoretical link. 
Danish dairy firm, Arla, is used as a case by which the theoretical link is tested. This is done by 
the use of the resource-based view of the firm to identify the impact of the TRM method on the 
resources which create the operational evidence. Its impact on general practicability of a 
positioning strategy is also described. The results are interpreted in light of the literature and 
future research is recommended. This includes a suggestion to repeat this methodology using 
the outcomes of a technology roadmap, in addition to the method by which it is produced, and 
to further contribute to the set of factors which influence positioning strategy practicability. 
 
Key words: Positioning Strategy, Technology Roadmapping, Resource-Based View, Quadric 
Framework

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background and Motivation 
Engineers are seen primarily to be involved in the technical aspects of business. However, they 
commonly find themselves in situations where they are exposed to the broader business 
environment and the necessity of forward planning and dealing with change. Engineers should 
be able to understand this environment and the motivations for strategic decisions made at all 
levels of the business (IPENZ, 2014). Managers who hope to influence strategy should be able to 
take a higher-level overview, to conceive of the broader picture rather than just its individual 
components (Johnson, et al., 2012). For these reasons, business strategy is increasingly relevant 
to engineers. 
Competitive advantage is a central tenet of many theories of business strategy. It entails the 
creation of value for a firm through the establishment of a unique position relative to competitors 
(Porter, 1996). Developing a positioning strategy means selecting activities that are different 
from the competition which allow a firm to serve its customers in such a way that a relative 
advantage is created. Doing so helps build a competitive advantage and deliver profitability. It is 
important that a positioning strategy be the focal point of alignment across business functions, 
particularly operations and marketing (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). 
Several models exist to aid in the creation of a positioning strategy and to assist in identifying 
business function misalignment, but few are standardisable or give instruction for bringing that 
position to fruition, particularly operationally (Kalafatis, et al., 2000).  
Engineers have a unique perspective on firm operations, underpinned by solid understanding of 
technological possibilities and resources. This creates a potential gap for engineers and 
technologists to contribute both theoretically and practically to the study of competitive 
advantage. The aim of this research is to investigate whether engineering knowledge can be used 
to build a bridge between the conceptualisation of a firm’s positioning strategy and the 
==PAGE:6==
2 
 
sustainable implementation thereof. This is done by investigating the technological resources of 
the firm and examining their relationship with competitive advantage.

1.3 Problem Statement and Research Question 
Many modern firms rely on technological innovation as the foundation for their competitive 
advantage and ultimate, sustained success. A positioning strategy can be a key component of a 
sustainable competitive advantage. Positioning strategies emanate from the field of marketing 
management and have a specific focus on the perception of the customer.  Research on the topic 
has commonly been concerned with the lack of operationalising instructions in most models of 
positioning strategy (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). 
The Quadric Framework is a tool for crystallising a firm’s positioning strategy and assisting in 
determining gaps in strategic alignment across business functions. It has been shown to be useful 
for identifying a positioning strategy that can create and sustain a competitive advantage (Zanin, 
==PAGE:7==
3 
 
2014). To this end, it synthesises the rational, emotional, internal and external aspects of the 
firm. A successful positioning strategy requires a strongly aligned operational function. Although 
the framework allows for the identification of misalignments in the rational (or operational) 
sphere, like other frameworks, it does little to suggest solutions, or directly assist in operational 
planning for strategic goal attainment. An additional framework which would assist in doing so, 
would greatly enhance the strategic utility of the Quadric Framework. 
The resource-based view of the firm (RBV) sees a sustained competitive advantage as a function 
of the correct management of the dynamics of how a firm’s resources and competences are 
acquired and managed (Moingeon, et al., 1998). Together, the resources and competences 
comprise a firm’s strategic capabilities. Technology roadmapping (TRM) is a practical foresight 
tool which can be used to link the push forces of operational technological and knowledge 
resources to the pull forces of the market (and hence the customer). This tool emerged from RBV 
theory, and permits strategic planners to “explore the evolution and development of markets for 
new products and technologies over time” and necessitates an understanding of a firm’s current 
resources and those they may potentially require to attain certain technological outcomes 
(Glaser, 2005).  
In summary, positioning strategy and RBV are key theoretical contributors to the creation of a 
sustainable competitive advantage. The Quadric Framework defines a positioning strategy. TRM 
is an operational and strategic planning tool rooted in RBV. This research will investigate the 
possibility that there exists a way in which TRM can assist in the implementation of a 
differentiated positioning strategy as outlined by the Quadric Framework. 
To this end, the primary research question is as follows: 
 
Primary Research Question: “How can the technology roadmapping process and the resource-
based view of the firm inform and support operational evidence in the Quadric Framework and 
thereby and otherwise aid in the successful operational implementation of a firm’s positioning 
strategy?"

2 Literature and Theory 
This section reviews the literature to establish a guiding framework for the research rooted in 
the existing theory. The Quadric Framework and the technology roadmap are placed in the 
context of the supporting theory and their contributions to broader business strategy. 
2.1 Strategy and Competitive Advantage 
A broad definition of strategy is the “direction and scope of an organisation over the long term, 
which achieves advantage in a changing environment through its configuration of resources and 
competences with the aim of fulfilling stakeholder expectations” (Johnson, et al., 2012). The 
essence of a firm’s strategy is concerned with the alignment of the activities and resources of a 
firm, in such a way that they will create a sustainable competitive advantage (Farrukh, et al., 
2003). 
In a competitive environment, strategy requires “the creation of a unique and valuable position, 
involving a different set of activities” and means “deliberately choosing a different set of activities 
to deliver a unique mix of value” (Porter, 1996). In Porter’s view, if a firm can differentiate itself 
from rivals and preserve this difference, it can outperform them - this difference is known as a 
competitive advantage (Porter, 1996). The term is rarely defined in the literature, and some 
authors feel the few extant definitions are ambiguous or based upon circular logic (Klein, 2002) 
(Flint, 2000).  In surveying a number of definitions, Klein concludes that they do little more than 
describe competitive advantage as “success”. Flint’s view is that the extended term “sustained 
competitive advantage” is little more than an extension of the ambiguity of the original term. 
Barney (1991) gives the following definition: “A firm can be said to have a competitive advantage 
when it is implementing a strategy which creates value and this strategy is not simultaneously 
being implemented by any potential or current competitors”. Klein’s criticism of this is definition 
is that it is a relative, not absolute, definition as the competitive advantage of potential 
competitors is unknowable.  Dodgson, et al. (2008) counter that although competitive advantage 
does have a relative dimension (as advantage is found in the activities of firms compared to their 
==PAGE:10==
6 
 
competitors), it also has an implicit absolute dimension. That is that there must be a market for 
what the firm does, or no advantage can exist. 
A competitive advantage is considered to be a sustained one, when other firms are unable to 
duplicate the benefits of a firm’s strategy. Sustained competitive advantage is not only or 
necessarily considered to be temporal, but also depends on the possibility of duplication (Barney, 
1991). Furthermore, a sustainable competitive advantage requires a unique competitive position 
and a distinct set of activities tailored to the chosen strategy. It is this system of activities which 
provides sustainability.  
The views espoused by Barney and Dodgson et al. are used as the basis of this research. A 
competitive advantage as a result of a value-creating strategy and competitive differentiation, 
comprising both absolute and relative dimensions allows for a multi-faceted exploration of the 
themes.  The absolute dimension will be dealt with in sections 2.4 and 2.5, after examination of 
the ways in which a firm may differentiate itself relative to competitors by defining a strategic 
position. 
2.2 Positioning Strategy 
 Definition 
Strategy and operational effectiveness are often conflated. Operational effectiveness means 
performing similar activities to rivals, but doing them better. This is necessary to achieve superior 
profitability, but not sufficient. Competitors can imitate techniques such as Total Quality 
Management or Lean Manufacturing, leading to a degree of similarity between competitors. 
Strategy implies electing to perform activities differently to rivals, or to perform different 
activities altogether (Porter, 1996).  
Since Barney’s definition of competitive advantage implies relative differentiation, the concept 
can be extrapolated to the idea of a positioning strategy. Porter draws upon the concept to define 
strategic positioning as “performing different activities from rivals’ or performing similar activities 
in different ways” (Porter, 1996). Doyle describes it as “the choice of target market segment 
==PAGE:11==
7 
 
which describes the customers (that) a business will seek to serve and the choice of differential 
advantage which defines how it will compete with rivals in the segment.” (Doyle, 1983) 
Ries and Trout originated the idea of positioning in the marketing sphere, defining it as a way in 
which to position a product (whether it be merchandise or a service) in the minds of the 
prospective customer (Ries & Trout, 1981). They see it as manipulating what already exists in the 
minds of customers rather than creating something new, recommending that a positioning 
message be as simplified as possible in order to be easily understood by prospective customers. 
These original ideas have since been extrapolated from the marketing arena and developed and 
applied in research across the spectrum of firm functions. 
Strategic positioning then, can be said to mean choosing activities that are different from those 
of rivals and which allow a firm to serve its customers in such a way that gives it an advantage 
over those rivals. Evidence has suggested a direct correlation between firm profitability and 
efficiency and focused clear activities which support a positioning strategy. (Kalafatis, et al., 
2000). 
 Application and Evaluation of a Positioning Strategy 
Customers are capable of recognising a clear positioning strategy and, for instance can recognise 
firms who clearly position themselves on quality or price (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). Given the direct 
impact on customer perceptions, it is necessary that a positioning strategy be clear and true, and 
be recognisable as such from outside the firm. Positioning does not in itself offer guidance in 
maintaining a consistent brand image over time (Park, et al., 1986). This means that a plan of 
action must be developed in order for a positioning strategy to be successful. 
There appears to be a scarcity of coherent positioning models and the few that exist lack both 
instructions for operationalising the position, as well as any guidelines for standardisation 
(Kalafatis, et al., 2000). At an operational level, there is little guidance regarding the types of 
positioning strategies available to a firm, primarily because positioning strategies are aimed at 
customer perception, which requires an external perspective in order to be successful. Park et al. 
believe that a positioning strategy should be developed after considering both the internal and 
==PAGE:12==
8 
 
external environments (Park, et al., 1986). Kalafatis et al. concur, positing that a clearly defined 
position is a function of both internal operational factors and external, experiential factors on the 
customer side. 
In defining a position, a firm must focus on its own distinctive strengths in order to satisfy 
customer needs differently and more effectively than rivals. In order to do this, an analysis of its 
strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats profile (SWOT) can be done in order to guide 
decision making. This can only be successfully done through a holistic internal, competitor and 
market analysis (Brooksbank, 1994). 
Many firms do not see beyond the sales and advertising implications of a positioning decision 
(Kalafatis, et al., 2000). However, consideration of internal factors before defining a position, 
does not mean that the surrounding marketing and operational efforts should be left unchanged. 
These should be designed only once a positioning choice has been made. Reversing this process 
can lead to a lack of focus and a confused position.   
A positioning strategy extends beyond the marketing function and relates to the behaviour of the 
entire firm. For this reason, when a firm considers implementation, it must develop a set of 
activities which are harmonious both conceptually and operationally and cover all business 
functions (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). These activities should be well-planned, deliberate and 
proactive.  Moreover, they must ensure alignment between marketing and the other 
environmental and business activities of the firm, not leaving sole responsibility with the 
marketing function. Decisions must be made at conceptual, strategic and operational levels, 
whilst reflecting the realities of the firm, its competitors and its target (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). 
Strategic positions are best employed over a horizon of at least a decade, not merely a single 
planning cycle (Porter, 1985). Due to the nature of markets and constant change in consumer 
preferences, competition and technology, no positioning strategy will remain eternally valid, 
necessitating an iterative process (Akpoyomare, et al., 2013). When significant changes to a 
positioning strategy are necessary, the difficulty of achieving this must be appreciated, especially 
in the short term. Changes need to be believable and sustainable for any positioning strategy 
==PAGE:13==
9 
 
shift to be successful (Kalafatis, et al., 2000). These two facets should be built into a position from 
initiation of the process. 
In addition to believability and sustainability, Brooksbank has proposed a six point checklist for 
evaluating a positioning strategy for potential success. It must: 
 Be based on a comprehensive situational analysis of the firm, its competitors and the 
market; 
 Be built around the firm’s particular strength; 
 Precisely define target markets with a detailed understanding of their requirements; 
 Accurately define competitors and reflect a coherent competitive strategy; 
 Precisely define a sustainable competitive advantage and 
 Be actionable. (Brooksbank, 1994) 
2.3 The Quadric Framework 
The Quadric Framework is the primary management tool employed by Quadric, a consulting firm 
based in Hellerup, Denmark (Quadric, 2014). Quadric helps clients create strategic differentiation 
and drive growth. It aims at addressing the needs of firms who face significant changes emanating 
from new management, strategy or ownership, or resulting from mergers and acquisitions. The 
framework is intended to “bridge the gap between business strategy and implementation” 
(Quadric, 2014). Its basis is that firms use positioning strategies in order to set the pace of their 
industries, build a following both internally and externally and to turn global trends into growth 
engines (Sherain, 2014). 
A firm’s main priority should be to manage their entire value chain, rather than simply their role 
in it. Many firms fail to do this in a systematic manner as the different functions of their business 
are not correctly linked. The central purpose of the framework is to focus firms on the right thing, 
independent of where in the value chain they lie (Introducing the Quadric Framework, 2012). The 
framework is built on the notion that at each interaction point in the value chain, there is an 
internal and external bias inherent, as well as an emotional and rational component. The internal, 
==PAGE:14==
10 
 
external, emotional and rational components of all interactions should be aligned with a 
positioning strategy (Figure 1). This can only be done once the positioning strategy itself is 
understood. Quadric believes that a firm should first develop a clear positioning strategy 
(consistent with Kalafatis et al. in section 2.2.2), implement that strategy thoroughly across all 
functions and finally, manage the business based on positioning (Assess your positioning strategy, 
2012). 
The Quadric Framework allows for better understanding of the way in which a firm differentiates 
itself and can be updated to follow differentiation over time. It has been shown that the 
framework is underpinned by established theoretical frameworks in each of its quarters (Zanin, 
2014) 
 
Figure 1: Quadric Framework (Sherain, 2014) 
 Positioning Strategy 
The positioning strategy at the heart of the framework is characterised as a simple way of 
describing a clear purpose of a firm, and the direction that firm is taking. These may be simple, 
generic ideas, but require correct implementation (Assess your positioning strategy, 2012). 
Quadric takes the view that the value chain is the starting point of strategic differentiation 
(Quadric, 2012).  For this reason, the positioning strategy must be true, relevant and high value 
to everyone in the value chain. It is only when this is achieved that the members of the value 
==PAGE:15==
11 
 
chain can effectively work together to achieve it (Introducing the Quadric Framework, 2012). 
Successful companies are able to dictate the pace in their own industries using positioning 
strategies, and can build a following both internally and externally (Sherain, 2014). 
 Internal Quarter – Cultural Uniqueness 
Successful firms have strong cultures, but those cultures are unique and pervade their actions. 
The cultural traits for which the firm is known should align with their positioning strategy 
(Introducing the Quadric Framework, 2012). The firm should ask itself whether it is creating 
cultural uniqueness that endorses behaviours or attitudes that support its positioning strategy. 
Mechanisms for creating a cultural uniqueness which reflects the positioning strategy may 
include values and awards programs, performance reviews and communications and recruitment 
(Sherain, 2014). 
 Emotional Quarter – Optimised Brand Portfolio 
The emotional quarter concerns whether the firm’s brand portfolio is optimised in order to best 
represent their position. This includes the firm’s name, its visual identity and logo, as well as 
corporate or unit brands, product brands and brand commitments (Introducing the Quadric 
Framework, 2012). The firm’s brand, its name and its identity need to be designed in such a way 
that people both inside and outside the firm and value chain are able to recognize the firm’s 
positioning strategy through them (Assess your positioning strategy, 2012). Mechanisms by 
which the emotional quarter may be aligned include identity guidelines and toolboxes. 
 External Quarter – Aligned Marketing 
A firm should align their market to build their positioning strategy whilst simultaneously driving 
sales. The marketing strategy should be designed in such a way that it prepares the market for 
future products and influences the trends. This is perhaps the easiest to get right, if the internal, 
external and rational quarters are in place (Introducing the Quadric Framework, 2012).The 
mechanisms by which marketing is aligned may include corporate communications, advertising, 
segment marketing, product marketing, sales training and sponsorships. 
==PAGE:16==
12 
 
 Rational Quarter – Operational Evidence 
Successful, profitable firms know what they should prioritise and put forward as the proof that 
they are who they say they are. This is the operational evidence that supports and reinforces the 
positioning strategy. It includes key metrics, cases and examples of the firm’s successes. A 
successful firm should collect and communicate the operational evidence that supports their 
positioning. In many cases, this may be a track record of innovation. A firm should be able to 
explain its innovation investment decisions in such a manner that the reasoning correlates 
directly with the positioning strategy (Assess your positioning strategy, 2012). The mechanisms 
for the operational evidence are found in such things as the firm’s operational focus and their 
R&D and innovation priorities (Sherain, 2014). This means that the firm’s resources must be well-
managed to produce adequate evidence. 
 
 Extrapolating the Quadric Framework 
The Quadric Framework represents a practical approach to crystallising a positioning strategy. It 
is concerned with the alignment of business functions with a core position, and the mechanisms 
that create this alignment (Figure 2). The literature states that few such frameworks contain 
pointers for operationalising these core positions. The rational quarter of the Quadric is the proof 
that a firm is what it claims to be and bears further examination. Mechanisms for the creation of 
this proof include R&D and innovation priorities. To understand how these originate, a survey of 
how firms generate and mobilise resources is useful.

2.4 The Theory of the Resource-Based View 
 Background 
For any given firm, a resource may generally be described as a strength or weakness of that firm 
(Wernerfelt, 1984).  More specifically, the resources of a firm at a point in time are those assets, 
tangible and intangible, which are tied to it permanently or semi-permanently (Caves, 1980). 
The resource-based view of the firm (or RBV) arose in the early 1980s in response to the sense 
that the majority of research in the arena of strategic management was focused on the external 
environment, rather than a firm’s strengths. This theory looks at the firm’s internal characteristics 
and inter-firm differences, shifting the focus of strategic analysis from the industry to the firm 
itself. (Lockett, et al., 2008). Johnson et al (2012) take the view that if a firm is to achieve a 
competitive advantage, it will do so on the basis of its strategic capabilities, that is, the 
“combination of its resources and competences of an organisation needed for it to survive and 
prosper” (Johnson, et al., 2012). In this paradigm, RBV can be defined as the idea that competitive 
advantage of a firm “is explained by the distinctiveness of its capabilities”. 
==PAGE:18==
14 
 
RBV theorises that firms are heterogeneous entities and that it is “the unique assets and resource 
endowments” of a firm which lead to “firm efficiencies, imperfect competition, and the 
attainment of super-normal profits” (Lockett, et al., 2008). The theory is based on two major 
assumptions. The first is that firms in an industry may be heterogeneous with regards to the 
strategic resources in their control, and the second is that those resources may be imperfectly 
mobile between firms, meaning that heterogeneity may be long lasting (Barney, 1991). 
Building on the basic assumptions, Barney (1991), suggests that in order to have the potential of 
sustained competitive advantage, a firm’s resources must have certain attributes. They must be 
valuable, able to exploit or neutralise threats in the external environment. They should be rare 
amongst current and potential competition. The resources must be imperfectly imitable and non-
substitutable, meaning there should not exist strategically equivalent substitutes which are 
neither rare nor imperfectly imitable.  These attributes, often referred to as VRIN attributes, may 
be used to analyse a firm’s resources with a view to assessing potential competitive advantage. 
 Strategic Implications 
Barney (1991) has stated that the implementation by a firm of any strategy inherently requires 
the application of its resources. Moingeon et al (1998) further argue that developing and 
maintaining a sustainable competitive advantage requires heavy focus on the way in which a 
firm’s resources are both acquired and managed. Strategy has been commonly viewed as mainly 
influenced by external factors, which obscures the strategic relevance of the internal 
environment and the resources of the firm. Furthermore, in many studies, the static status of 
resources is examined, with less focus on the dynamics of resource acquirement and 
management. 
A resource set, or even a single resource may be used to generate competitive advantage, the 
sustainability of which is reliant on how easily imitable the resources are, and whether there are 
substitutes. Combining and coordinating resources can result in organisational competencies and 
capabilities, which support competitive advantage (Moingeon, et al., 1998). 
==PAGE:19==
15 
 
In order for the resource-based view of the firm to be used as a framework through which the 
relationship between strategy and resources can be understood, the concepts of competencies, 
capabilities and organisational structure must be integrated (Moingeon, et al., 1998). Moingeon 
et al (1998) have proposed a methodology for linking resources to strategy using a two-tier 
analytical approach, examining first the portfolio of a firm’s activities and listing the product-
market linkages. This helps to identify the sources of competitive advantage. The focus of analysis 
at this level is to understand the firm’s knowledge and its effectiveness in employing it. The 
second tier of analysis involves researching the way in which resources, capabilities and 
competencies are developed inside the firm. 
The RBV and associated techniques can be used to analyse how a firm aligns its operations with 
its positioning strategy, as well as specifically to the way in which it uses technology and 
innovation to create a competitive advantage. The following section examines the link between 
technology and the theory previously discussed. 
2.5 Technology – A Strategic Perspective 
 Background  
Technology is specific type of knowledge, distinguishable from more general types of knowledge 
in that it focuses on the “know-how” of an organisation, and is necessarily “action-oriented”. In 
order to have the potential to create competitive advantage, that know-how must be mobilised 
(Farrukh, et al., 2003).  Although commonly associated with science and engineering, the 
processes and mechanisms by which technology is effectively deployed (including the supporting 
organisational structures and communication networks) are critical to business strategy. For this 
reason, technology strategies are an integral part of business strategy, and should not be viewed 
as separate. 
 Competitive Advantage 
Ries and Trout view the success of a firm as almost wholly reliant on its ability to tailor its tactics 
to the competition (Ries & Trout, 1989). They see the company as a highly mobile collection of 
==PAGE:20==
16 
 
assets, with no internal synergism, where technology is unimportant (Demaris, 1992). This school 
of thought suggests that significant advances in technology are rare and that those that do occur 
are easily imitable (Ries & Trout, 1989).  This view simplifies the strategic options open to firms 
when markets are opened by new technology to the binary options of in or out. 
However, technological change is one of the major drivers of competition and is capable of 
creating a competitive advantage, as well as erode that of well-entrenched rivals (Porter, 1985). 
Technology pervades a firm’s value chain, and all technologies in a value chain may potentially 
impact competitive advantage. Every value activity uses at least some technology to produce an 
output from purchased inputs and human resources. These technologies may be within the firm’s 
industry, but often are not. As technologies span a number of value activities, they are important 
linkages in the value chain. Due to its pervasiveness and its role in linking value activities, 
technology can have a profound effect on cost and differentiation. A firm which discovers a new 
or improved technology for an activity which is better than that of the competition, will gain a 
competitive advantage. Technological innovation is capable of providing both the relative and 
absolute advantages required for a competitive advantage, by elevating a firm above rivals as 
well as creating a market for a product or service (Dodgson, et al., 2008). 
Since technology is a key component of competitive advantage, the view of Ries and Trout seems 
outdated. It follows that a firm’s approach to technology development and use is crucial. This is 
known as a technology strategy, and is a vital component of an overall competitive strategy 
(Porter, 1985). A technology strategy should be reinforced by, and consistent with, other value 
activities across the firm, such as marketing and manufacturing. This is a difficult task, but it is 
the difficulty itself which makes managing technology a source of competitive advantage 
(Dodgson, et al., 2008). One of the major elements of any technology strategy should be R&D to 
develop technologies which lower cost across the firm’s value activities. 
Porter suggests a series of seven analytical steps which a firm should take when developing a 
technology strategy which harnesses technology to create competitive advantage: 
 Identify each of the distinct technologies and sub-technologies in the value chain; 
==PAGE:21==
17 
 
 Identify potentially relevant technologies in other industries or currently in the research 
stage; 
 Determine the likely path of change in key technologies; 
 Determine those technologies and possible technological changes which are most 
significant for competitive advantage and industry structure; 
 Assess a firm’s relative capabilities in important technologies and the cost of making 
improvements; 
 Choose a technology strategy covering all important and relevant technologies which may 
bolster the firm’s overall competitive strategy and 
 Reinforce business-unit level technology strategies at the corporate level (Porter, 1985). 
These criteria can be used to evaluate a technology strategy in light of the environment and 
business strategy.

 Resource-Based View 
Business strategy is primarily concerned with aligning the activities and resources of a firm in 
such a way that they generate a sustainable competitive advantage (Farrukh, et al., 2003). 
Technology itself is a resource which provides a firm with the capabilities to develop and deliver 
products or services to market, and therefore impacts on both the internal and external aspects 
of strategy. Taking this approach to technology considerations links it to other resource-based 
views of the firm. 
Abu Bakar & Ahmad (2010) have studied the firm’s resources as an ingredient in the success of 
technology development, and the economic financial and non-economic outcomes of a firm’s 
innovation efforts. They conclude that the firm’s intangible resources, especially product 
reputation, are the main predictors of the level of these innovation outcomes. These kinds of 
resources are difficult to imitate or replicate. In general established firms have an advantage over 
others in that they can gain such resources as product reputation not only through their access 
to finance, but importantly their pre-existing skill resources in the area of innovation and R&D 
(Abu Bakar & Ahmad, 2010). 
==PAGE:24==
20 
 
Due to the importance of the role of technology in a firm’s strategy, there is a need for a process 
which allows for the firm to link technology into ongoing business planning in the context of its 
strategy. The following section introduces a practical method for creating this link. 
 Technology Roadmapping 
Temporal aspects are crucial for technology planning in terms of both internal cycles (such as 
budgeting, planning and strategy) and external factors (competitor activity, technological 
development and market changes) (Farrukh, et al., 2003). A practical tool which can be used to 
link technology strategy into wider strategic planning whilst introducing a temporal facet is a 
technology roadmap (TRM), which can be defined as a “flexible technique that permits industrial 
strategic and long-range planners to explore the evolution and development of markets for new 
products and technologies over time” (Glaser, 2005). The technique was developed in the 1970s 
by American telecommunications firm, Motorola and has been applied with success at large firms 
across industries, such as British Petroleum and Philips (Farrukh, et al., 2003) (Dodgson, et al., 
2008). 
The TRM is useful as a framework and process for assessing and planning potential technological 
development over the mid- to long-term (5-20 years). It allows for the exploration of the dynamic 
links “between technological resources, organizational objectives and the changing 
environment” (Phaal, et al., 2004). As well as being used for forecasting and investment planning, 
it is important in that it can provide evidence of technological trends which can be used to assist 
the development of strategy (Dodgson, et al., 2008). While the roadmapping process includes 
planning for scientific research and engineering development, its ultimate focus is mission 
application (Dixon, 2001).  
 Application 
TRM application can take several forms, and the particular methodology depends to a large 
extent on the context of the firm in which it is applied. There is, however, a basic template upon 
which all methodologies are built, which is typically made up of a number of layers on a time-
based chart (Farrukh, et al., 2003). These layers focus on connecting the drivers in the market to

product and services which can address these drivers and fulfil customer needs. These products

A successful TRM is able to bridge the technical and commercial perspectives inside the firm. It 
lays out time frames and options, milestones and supporting technologies for developing new 
technologies, and outlines the market drivers which relate to them. It offers alternative 
technologies and aides in making decisions regarding trade-offs along different technology 
pathways and investment and co-ordination of R&D activities. The technique is most successful 
when there is a strong knowledge and understanding of the “underlying technologies, drivers

In several cases, this method has shown company benefits, primarily in creating new

2.6 Summary 
A firm’s strategy must give it direction and scope over the long term, and should deal with the 
alignment of resources to create a sustainable competitive advantage. Competitive advantage 
==PAGE:27==
23 
 
has a relative dimension, in that the firm must perform activities better than or different to its 
competitors, and an absolute dimension, in that there must exist a market for what the firm does 
in order for there to be an advantage at all. 
A positioning strategy creates competitive advantage by strategically placing a firm in the market 
in such a way that differentiates it from competitor firms, especially in the eyes of the customer. 
It is therefore relative in nature, as it is to be viewed in the context of a competitive environment, 
comparing the firm to others. The Quadric Framework helps a firm define a positioning strategy 
to create strategic differentiation and allows for alignment of business functions with the firm’s 
position. It therefore assists in creating the relative aspect of the competitive advantage. 
The resource-based view advances the idea that the tangible and intangible resources within the 
firm’s control must be correctly acquired and managed in order to achieve competitive 
advantage. Technology is a specific type of knowledge which must be mobilised in order to be 
valuable. It is also a resource which can be used to create competitive advantage, and can be 
examined through the lens of the resource-based view. A technology strategy is an integral part 
of overall business strategy and the development of competitive advantage. 
Technology roadmapping facilitates the creation of a technology strategy by linking technology 
to broader business strategy. It links technological solutions to existing and plausible future 
market needs. In doing so, it can be seen as contributing to the absolute dimension of competitive 
advantage. 
Effective technology strategies and positioning strategies require strong alignment across all 
business units and functions, and across the value chain, over the medium to long term. Both the 
Quadric and technology roadmapping are concerned with this alignment. The temporal and 
market-based aspects of roadmapping are congruent with the iterative nature of strategic 
positioning in the Quadric and its clarifying influence on innovation mechanisms. Together, 
through combining the relative and absolute dimensions, they represent a more holistic 
approach to the creation of competitive advantage than either separately. Figure 8 contains a

3 Methodology 
This section outlines the methodological approach to examining the research hypothesis 
presented in this report.  
3.1 Choice of Method 
It has been suggested that research questions requiring explanatory answers may be best 
approached using case study methodologies and strategies, due to the fact that answers to such 
questions require that operational links be traced over a period of time (Yin, 1994). Yin has 
concluded that the main instance in which a case study strategy is advantageous is when “a ‘how’ 
or ‘why’ question is being asked about a contemporary set of events over which the investigator 
has little or no control”. 
A case study is a qualitative research methodology wherein a current phenomenon is examined 
in its real-world context where the “boundaries between phenomenon and context are not 
clearly evident” (Yin, 1994). Case studies allow for the observation of the complex processes 
through which an input is transformed into an output, without being linked to a single method 
of evidence or data collection (McCorcle, 1984). These studies, however, should use detail to 
draw useful conclusions and should focus on the dynamics of interactions between actors, 
organisations and programs. 
In light of the nature of the hypothetical questions posed in this research, a case study research 
approach can be seen to be suitable. Furthermore, the extrapolation of the theories of 
competitive advantage and positioning strategy to embrace practical, operational tools such as 
technology roadmapping has not previously been examined in detail. The arguments proposed 
in this research are theoretical and should be backed up through practical examples which 
reinforce them, requiring several data sources. For these reasons, case study methodology has 
been selected as the method of investigation.

3.2 Case Selection 
In selecting a case, there are several criteria to consider. At the highest level, data for the firm

crystalised in a Quadric Framework. This limits the scope to those companies with whom Quadric

3.4 Analysis Methodology 
In order to examine the potential for a link between the Quadric Framework and technology

4 Case Description – Arla Foods 
Arla Foods is a global dairy firm headquartered in Viby, Denmark. It was founded in 2000, and is 
a cooperative owned by approximately 13,500 dairy farmers in seven European nations (Arla 
Foods, 2015). In January 2013, Arla announced a revision of their business strategy, creating a 
new five year strategy, known as Strategy 2017. A primary explanation given for this was the 
abolition EU milk quotas in 2015, which would remove limits on production levels of Arla farmers, 
producing milk which would not find buyers on the European market.  This would require an 
expansion of operations and marketing to find new markets (Cheese Market News, 2013). This 
led to the redefinition or clarification of their “identity”, or positioning strategy, using the Quadric 
Framework. Their new position is based around the concept of Good Growth. 
Nu Angle is a technology and innovation management company based in Cambridge, UK (nu 
Angle, 2015). Arla and nu Angle have collaborated on a technology roadmapping exercise for 
Arla’s R&D department in relation to Strategy 2017. Nu Angle promises that its technology 
roadmaps help to bridge the gap between strategic goals and technology “detail” and improve 
organisational alignment (Allen, 2015).  
As Arla’s positioning strategy has been outlined using the Quadric Framework, and its alliance 
with nu Angle promises to produce strategically aligned technology roadmaps, it is considered 
that this will provide a useful case for studying the relationship between these strategic tools.

Methodology 
Nu Angle follows an established process, developed in-house, but rooted in methodologies in the 
literature. The process is flexible and can be tailored to the client or situation. 
The first step nu Angle takes when approached to assist in the creation of a TRM is to familiarise 
themselves with the client and its culture and goals through a full survey of annual reports and 
publicly available information. 
An initial workshop is held with the relevant commercial departments in the firm, usually the 
marketing department (not technologists). In this workshop, a mindmap is created. The first 
branch of this mind map are the business drivers or consumer needs which must be addressed. 
This is done by consulting the participants on the challenges they feel need to be addressed. The 
second branch deals with the functions that are needed to address these drivers and needs. The 
third branch shows technologies that may deliver these functions. The initial focus is to ask what 
technologies are available within the sector, outside it or are new to the world (often in academic 
institutions). This is done in a workshop in collaboration with the client’s technology knowledge 
workers, working with them to determine what technologies are currently being applied in the 
relevant business areas, and where the gaps might be. 
The resulting TRM, although commonly very large, may be missing technologies. To identify any 
gaps, external experts are consulted on an individual basis, to avoid information overload. Once 
all experts have been consulted and the information compiled, a workshop with the experts may 
be held if required. Whilst internal experts are important, it is considered that going outside of 
the firm helps to avoid self-justification and provides an objective, non-personalised element to 
the process. 
The information provided by the experts about each of the technologies is: 
==PAGE:42==
38 
 
 A predicted timeline of events for each technology 
 A probability of success, or risk weighting for each 
 The location of the technology (sector, institution, etc.) 
 An idea of who the leaders are in this particular technology 
 A rating on a scale of 1-5 of how expensive investment in this technology is likely to be 
 The critical mass required to develop the technology, and where it lies. 
 
Typically, an Intellectual Property map is developed at this stage to avoid focusing on 
unobtainable technologies and to narrow the scope of the exercise. 
Alongside the client’s commercial participants, a set of criteria for evaluation of the technologies 
is developed, and the technologies are scored based on these criteria.  
Several models, such as key-base-pacing technologies, are used to help rank and prioritise the 
technologies based on their maturity and the impact they will have on competition. Those which 
are deemed to have a low priority are parked, and the high priority technologies are clustered 
into technological areas. 
These areas are then classified based on whether the firm should be a leader in this technological 
area, one of the best, or should simply keep up with the best. Objectives are developed for each 
of these categories, and then time-based bar charts are developed for each technology, which 
show the windows of opportunity in which to enter these areas. Based on these, the technologies 
can be ranked according to urgency and from this it can be determined how the client should 
approach the technology (should it monitor development, begin exploring the technology, begin 
development of integration, etc.). 
Given that the relevant technologies have been identified and prioritised according to urgency, 
timelines have been put forward, risk factors are known and practical information regarding costs 
and location have been suggested, a plan for mobilisation and acquisition of the required 
resources can be developed. A business case can now be developed for each technology area.

Application at Arla 
Nu Angle feels that without a foundational understanding of the firm, it is not possible to describe 
or obtain the outcomes they require. In the case of Arla, some of the key factors identified as 
foundational were the cooperative nature of the firm, their financial commitment to innovation, 
and the fact that their own literature points to a strong focus on alignment across business 
functions such as marketing and operations. It is this focus on alignment that nu Angle feels is 
crucial to a TRM process, as it is central to any initiative in which one is trying to create change 
or convey an important message. This focus became apparent at the outset of the process. 
Additionally, the vision outlined in Strategy 2017 to “create a future of dairy to give health and 
inspiration to the world” was considered highly important, especially given the nature of the 
initiative, which was focused on optimisation and control, the use of which is intended to deliver 
a solution for manufacturing excellence towards achieving the vision. 
In Arla’s case, the process was slightly modified at the mindmapping stage. Instead of marketing 
employees, supply chain workers were consulted at this stage and the focus of the mindmap 
altered to a small extent. The first level was based on the challenges which face Arla’s 
manufacturing. The second was to break this down into several sectors, for example – efficiency 
and what is required to improve it. Thereafter, the required functions were identified. At the next 
level broad technology areas were suggested to address the functions, and these areas were 
broken down into further levels, with more specific technology types identified. An intellectual 
property map was not developed in this instance. 
Approximately 100 technologies were identified and evaluated based on 12 criteria, as well as a 
set of 2x2 matrices, including identifying key, base and pacing technologies, and impact on the 
challenge (or market) versus the maturity of the technology. The technologies were prioritised 
based on this and placed into 7 technology areas. Under each area, a short objective was created, 
beginning with “Arla will become a leader in….” or “Arla will increase prod capacity by being 
ahead in….”. This allows upper management to see the exercise in strategic terms. Nu Angle were

technologies, as Arla did this themselves.

5 Analysis and Results 
5.1 Practicability Factors for a Positioning Strategy 
To understand how technology roadmapping (TRM) can strengthen implementation of Arla’s 
Good Growth position, a set of factors which describe how easily practicable it is can be 
composed. These factors can be derived from the prerequisite criteria for a successfully 
implemented positioning strategy in the literature (section 2.2.2). 
These criteria can be grouped under three main dimensions to be evaluated to give an overview 
of practicability. These dimensions are believability, actionability and sustainability, all of which 
have been identified by the literature as being critical to the success of implementation. Common 
definitions of the three dimensions are considered in the creation of additional, practical criteria 
not directly mentioned in prior research, but inherently underlying. Supporting detail can be 
found in Appendix B. 
Believability 
For a positioning strategy to be believable, it must first be realistically possible. This means that 
the firm should have the resources and distinctive strengths necessary to implement it, or be able 
to obtain them. It is important that managers and employees responsible for implementation 
believe that the firm has the capabilities to deliver on the position. 
Believability is founded in credibility. This may be evidenced by the degree to which a firm’s past 
actions and image correlate with its current position. This means an examination of a firm’s track 
record must show it to be in line with the positioning strategy. A firm whose track record is 
contrary to their proclaimed positioning strategy will not engender belief. 
For employees and shareholders or owners, a positioning strategy will not be believable if it does 
not have a firm foundation in understanding of the firm and its culture. For this reason, the 
positioning strategy needs to be the result of holistic internal analysis. Likewise, a deep 
==PAGE:46==
42 
 
understanding of the firm’s competitors, customers and the market must be ingrained in the 
position, meaning external analysis is a necessity. 
Believability requires not only belief inside the firm, but most also engender belief outside it, and 
therefore the perceptions of consumers or customers and others outside the firm must be 
considered.  A strongly positive external perception that the position is clear and true is 
necessary, and the experiences of the consumer must reflect that position. 
Actionability 
Believability is a precondition for actionability. If a strategy is not believable internally or 
externally, it has little hope of being effectively carried out. 
A positioning strategy must be designed with the intent of performing activities which will assist 
in creating alignment between the marketing, operations and other business functions. Without 
the potential for alignment, any implementation plan will be severely tested. Once again, it is the 
firm’s strengths and resources which will be decisive in driving this alignment. 
The existence of detailed planning is a strong indicator that implementation is achievable. With 
no plan of action, nothing is actionable. Any planning should be done over a lengthy strategic 
horizon.  
Changes in the environment mean a firm needs to be flexible and have freedom of action in order 
to respond. This means that the process requires iteration, constantly being reassessed so that 
internal and external environmental changes can be continually fed back into the strategic 
alignment process.  
Strong planning and iteration processes require that internal and external analyses are 
performed and kept updated. 
Sustainability 
For a positioning strategy to be sustainable, it must be true and maintainable. As positioning is 
focused strongly on the customer, the external perception of the position as true and clear must 
==PAGE:47==
43 
 
be maintained. This requires a certain amount of foresight in the planning horizon, generally, the 
more planning cycles covered, the more sustainable a positioning strategy might be. The process 
by which the positioning strategy is developed and planned for must necessarily be iterative to 
be sustainable. In constantly changing competitive environments, without constant re-
evaluation, positions may become stale or irrelevant and thus not sustainable. If a position 
incorporates a firm’s unique strengths and is considered and improved iteratively over a series 
of planning cycles, there is a higher chance that it will be sustainable and lead to a sustainable 
competitive advantage. 
A component of sustainability that is becoming compulsory in modern business is environmental 
sustainability with regard to natural resources. It is increasingly important that this type of 
sustainability be integral to any firm’s business strategy, and therefore it is considered vital that 
it be included in any positioning strategy.  
The literature states that if a position is to be successful, it must represent a clear, sustainable 
competitive advantage. To meet the criteria for creating a competitive advantage, the firm 
should be implementing a value creating strategy which is not simultaneously being implemented 
by any competitors (Barney, 1991). For this to be sustainable those competitors should be unable 
to duplicate the benefits of this strategy, and there should be a distinctive competitive position 
and a system of activities which are designed specifically for this strategy. Differentiation then, 
is a key factor in the sustainability of a position.

5.2 General Practicability of Good Growth 
An analysis of the practicability factors outlined in the previous section must be based on the 
merits of the positioning strategy itself, as well as the process by which it was developed. This 
means that the Quadric Framework itself, intrinsic to the development of the idea, must also be 
considered a contributory factor. 
 Believability 
Track record 
The four pillars of Good Growth are not new concepts at Arla, although they have not previously 
been coalesced to form a unified concept. Themes of corporate responsibility, health and 
nutrition, environmental concerns and sustainability are prominent in several strategic and 
communications documents prior to the development of the current position (Arla Foods, 2012) 
(Arla Foods, 2012) (Arla Foods, 2009). The firm’s marketing strategy has revolved around nature, 
entitled, Closer to Nature. 
Over the last three strategic cycles, corporate responsibility has featured prominently in Arla’s 
strategy, with regards to food safety (responsibility to consumers), environmental sustainability, 
animal welfare and quality. The Arlagården quality assurance programme has been a constant 
presence which promotes these fundamental responsibilities. In previous strategic cycles, a 
nutritional policy formed part of the grand strategy and a primary goal of the firm was to be 
known as the “company with the most natural products” (Arla Foods, 2009). 
Arla is proud and vocal about its history of over a century rooted in the cooperative spirit. It has 
long been involved in partnerships and joint ventures across the globe and has a long history of 
collaboration with universities and research institutes (Arla Foods, 2012). Collaboration with 
consumers and customers in creating innovative solutions has been part of the firm’s activities 
since at least the early 2000s (Arla Foods, 2004).

The firm has grown over the years in terms of ownership, turnover and market access. However, 
in the first decade after its 2000 inception, it was embroiled in a number of controversies. The 
firm was criticized for takeovers and acquisitions of small Danish dairies and linked to anti-
competitive behaviour, with alleged plans to create a monopoly and shut smaller firms out of the 
market. This led to problems, for example, with the Swedish Competition Authority (The Local 
SE, 2009) (Petersen, 2010). Other controversies have also arisen, such as the melamine milk 
scandal at an Arla joint venture in China in 2008 and allegations of worker exploitation in 
Bangladesh, which contradict some of the ideals of Good Growth (Albrechtsen, 2011). Many of 
these controversies have affected external perceptions of the firm. 
External Perception 
In terms of whether the new position is clear and true, Arla’s publicly communicated statements 
about Good Growth and the four pillars as outlined in section 4.1.1 do not lack clarity, and their 
annual reports and freely available strategic documents explain in detail how they perceive Good 
Growth and how they intend to achieve it. 
In the first decade of its existence as a unified entity, Arla battled with a poor image in Denmark 
and some other markets. Various controversies led to negative media attention and even 
boycotts in Middle Eastern markets after the Danish cartoon crisis in 2006.  
In Denmark, Arla attracted much criticism for that way it handled smaller competition, especially 
regarding acquisitions. It has been suggested that this created a backlash in Denmark as it seemed 
contradictory to the traditional values of cooperative Danish agriculture (Petersen, 2010). There 
was a feeling in Denmark that the firm’s globalization objectives and methods did not mesh with 
the idyllic concept of dairy cooperatives, and Danish public opinion has often been ambivalent. 
However, within the industry, the firm has fared better. In 2011 its food industry peers voted Arla 
as having the best image of any Danish food company (Maskinbladet, 2011). Creditors, such as 
Danske Bank sees Arla as a strong firm whose cooperative nature makes them flexible and allows 
them to consistently achieve solid growth (Danske Bank, 2014).

5.3 The Resource-Based View 
In this section, several resources deemed to be important to Arla are identified and analysed 
using the VRIN method. The resources have been identified from background reading of the 
firm’s own messaging, as well as analyses performed by objective parties (Danske Bank, 2014) 
(Marketline, 2012). They have then been grouped into descriptive categories. 
Physical Resources  
Arla, like any dairy firm, owns processing plants and related equipment. These are valuable as 
they are the basis for being able to operate in the industry. These resources are very common 
and easily obtainable on the open market, and therefore are not rare or imperfectly imitable, 
and cannot be substituted. 
Arla, as a cooperative dairy, has de facto ownership of the natural resources and farms it requires 
and the primary raw materials produced there. This gives them direct control over the standard 
of farming and makes interaction and collaboration with farmers much easier than in non-
cooperative dairies.  It also gives them pricing flexibility with regard to milk and direct control 
over quality. While some competitors are cooperatives, most are not and therefore ownership 
of the primary resources is rare. It is imperfectly imitable as it is difficult to switch to a cooperative 
business model and may not always be expedient to purchase farms. Substitutability requires the 
costly and time consuming effort of vertical integration. 
Organisational Resources 
All players in the food and dairy industries require rigorous quality control systems. A solid quality 
control system is not only a necessity, but may prevent product recalls and the like which 
negatively affect brand reputation. The Arlagården initiative has been in place for years and is 
==PAGE:59==
55 
 
detailed and effective, and although Arla has experienced product recalls, some competitors 
have struggled more with this problem, meaning the resource is somewhat rare. With some 
effort, however, a quality control system can be imitated. 
Distribution networks are a critical resource, creating value by ensuring that products reach the 
market quickly and efficiently. All large dairy firms require a strong distribution network, as it is 
simply a precondition to partake in the industry. The resource is not rare, imperfectly imitable or 
non-substitutable in the context of Arla and their competitors.  
The cooperative nature of Arla is a resource that informs the way the company behaves and acts. 
With primary responsibility to its owners rather than to external shareholders, it permits a certain 
amount of freedom of strategic action and promotes responsibility. The democratic aspect of the 
cooperative means that owners’ concerns are voiced and acted upon. This resource is rare, as 
few competitors are cooperatives. Imitating the cooperative model is extremely difficult and 
requires a huge strategic and operational shift, and vertical integration as a substitute, as 
mentioned, would be an immensely costly exercise and disruptive exercise. 
Arla has a history and complex system of alliances and joint ventures which speak to its 
cooperative nature. These are valuable as they allow the firm access to external knowledge, 
resources and perspectives.  It takes time and skill to set up such a system of alliances, and this 
makes this resource rare. Although other firms can create their own networks, none of them can 
precisely imitate the benefits and resources of Arla’s. Substitution by vertical integration would 
be extremely costly and difficult to manage. 
Organisational policies, such as Arla’s Code of Conduct are resources in that they direct the 
organisation’s behaviour and actions towards strategic goals. In this sense, they are valuable. All 
competitors have their own, similar policies, which may differ slightly in content, but which may 
easily imitate those of Arla at little or no expense. 
 
 
==PAGE:60==
56 
 
Financial Resources 
Cash flow and access to lines of credit are both valuable to the firm. They both ensure that 
operations can be funded and expanded and facilitate investments in innovation and mergers 
and acquisition. Arla is a large, profitable firm, but so are their competitors, some of whom have 
greater financial resources than they do. For this reason, cash flow and access to credit are not 
rare, imperfectly imitable and non-substitutable. 
Intangible Resources 
A patent search shows that Arla Foods holds a large amount of proprietary technology and many 
patents. These range widely in technological area and application (even including dental 
applications). By developing technologies which may lead to new or improved dairy products or 
new applications outside the industry, Arla is able to exploit current and future opportunities in 
the market. These technologies require large investments in R&D, have long time horizons and 
involve complex decision making. Being protected by patents, they are relatively rare and costly 
to imitate. They may be substitutable by different technologies, but research and development 
costs are high.  
The firm has a large, diversified product portfolio, which is valuable in that it provides it access to 
a number of consumer groups and mitigates risk. Few competitors have a larger product range, 
and it is costly in terms of R&D and production capability. It is difficult to precisely imitate a 
product portfolio, in terms of cost and quality, and in terms of providing the right products to 
target markets. 
Arla’s international experience and presence allows them access to and knowledge of different 
markets and helps to lower business risk, but this resource is common in their multinational 
competitors and is therefore not rare, imperfectly imitable nor non-substitutable. 
Brand power and a well-known brand name constitute a valuable resource, contributing to 
effective marketing efforts and easing new product introduction. It may also make it easier to 
form alliances. It can be difficult for newcomers to create strong brand images, and only a few 
==PAGE:61==
57 
 
large or established firms manage and retain their brand power. In Arla’s case, there are lingering 
negative consumer perceptions in some regions and markets which give other firms an opening 
to imitate this resource. 
Arla possesses a large, skilled workforce, much of which is highly and uniquely skilled, often in 
specialised areas. This is valuable as it allows the firm to make good decisions and action them 
effectively and efficiently. It allows for strong innovative capabilities and efficient production and 
distribution. Particularly in some technological areas, resources are rare and often spread out. 
This makes them costly and specific skills and combinations of skills can be difficult for rivals to 
obtain. They may often have to obtain them from competitors. Specific skills cannot be imitated, 
and creating an analogue to a particular mix of skills in a workforce is costly and time consuming.

 Addressing Positioning Strategy Practicability 
Track record  
TRM is by nature a forward-looking technique, and does not generally concern itself with the 
past, nor has it any power to change or reinforce a firm’s track record. 
External perspective  
While the TRM process does not directly impact current consumer perceptions, the technologies 
highlighted may have the capacity to bring to market improved products or to increase food 
safety to levels which may improve the reputation of the firm in future. It will have no effect in 
the short to medium term. 
Internal Analysis  
By consulting with internal experts in both the commercial and operational functions of the firm, 
a strong understanding of the challenges being faced across the business and the current 
strengths and weaknesses of the resource base are highlighted. Current internal solutions to 
technological challenges are thoroughly surveyed and the gaps noted.  
External Analysis 
The involvement of external experts in the process allows for an objective view of the market 
and future trends from people well-acquainted with it. It allows for a deeper understanding of 
the technological drivers in the market and the work being done both inside and outside the dairy 
sector which could be used to drive growth. 
Alignment  
The technology roadmapping process requires alignment to move forward. In this case, it meant 
aligning the goals and possibilities in two sections of the operational side of Arla, the supply chain 
==PAGE:64==
60 
 
and R&D. Nu Angle felt that a focus on alignment which already existed in Arla was critical to the 
success of the roadmapping process. More generally, the initial consultation which begins the 
process is most commonly carried out with marketing and later the operational function is 
brought in. This aligns the broader business functions. 
Planning  
The TRM adds a huge amount of detail to the level of planning done in connection with the firm’s 
position, especially with regard to the operational evidence. The TRM gives timelines and 
probabilities, thereby helping to define the planning horizon for the operational quarter which is 
lacking from Arla’s positioning (section 5.2). It facilitates detailed scenario planning and 
investment decision-making by suggesting costs and locations of technologies, as well as 
resources required and high-level objectives 
Iteration  
The TRM process offers Arla the chance to reassess their position in terms of the operational 
evidence and the contribution of technology strategy to the firm’s position. As the timelines 
extend further into the future, likelihoods predicted by the process decrease, offering the firm 
the opportunity to repeat the process at regular periods. 
Environmental  
It is entirely likely that the technology roadmapping process will highlight environmentally 
friendly, more efficient technologies than are currently being used. The fact that the exercise was 
carried out in relation to manufacturing efficiency also contributes to this. Manufacturing 
efficiency commonly means less use of energy and natural resources to complete the same task 
more effectively, or in the same or less time. 
Differentiation  
It was shown in section 5.2 that Arla’s positioning strategy is superficially similar to that of some 
of its competitors. Therefore it is the activities that are to be performed around this position that 
==PAGE:65==
61 
 
will create the required differentiation. The TRM process gives Arla the opportunity to 
differentiate itself from competitors by defining a different set of activities based on judgement 
of future trends in the market. The more accurate the process, the more likely a sustainable 
competitive advantage becomes through differentiation of activities and resource utilisation. 
 Strengthening the Resource Base 
Physical Resources  
Ownership of natural resources and farms (VRI) 
The TRM process affects this resource in that any potential technologies relevant to optimisation 
and control at source (on the farms) brought to Arla’s attention can be used to enhance the 
control they already possess due to ownership. If Arla can explore technologies which give it 
unrivalled control over its raw materials, this may be very difficult for competitors to substitute. 
Plants and machinery (V) 
The outcome of this TRM exercise is likely to present technological opportunities for the 
improvement of current manufacturing processes and machinery, and plant optimisation. If Arla 
can obtain these new technologies before rivals, or have a better grasp on them, this resource 
will become rare, in addition to valuable. 
Organisational Resources 
Quality control systems (VR) 
This specific TRM initiative will increase the potential to strengthen Arla’s capabilities with regard 
to their quality control systems through its exploration of technologies in related areas. If they 
can increase quality control and efficiency throughout the supply chain, this resource will become 
increasingly difficult and costly to imitate in terms of time, skills, alliances and money. 
Distribution network (V) 
==PAGE:66==
62 
 
Potential technologies which are identified in the area of efficiency could make the distribution 
of goods to retailers quicker and easier. In the food industry, speed to retailers means freshness 
and less chance of spoilage. If technology can be used to achieve this, this resource will become 
rare, in addition to valuable. 
Cooperative nature (VRIN) 
It is not within the scope of the TRM process to have any recommendatory effects upon the 
cooperative nature of the firm, but it is certainly something around which the process id 
customised. Much like the firm’s policies, the cooperative nature of the firm informs the TRM 
process and is born in mind when examining the outcomes. 
Alliances and joint ventures (VRI) 
The clarity given by the roadmap gives Arla greater decision making capabilities with regards to 
how to utilise their current alliances and joint ventures more effectively to bring to fruition their 
technology strategy and plans. By pointing out, as nu Angle’s methodology does, the locations of 
current technologies or their precursors, and the world leaders in those areas, Arla will have an 
advantage in decision making and knowledge regarding potential alliances. This may not be easily 
substitutable by rivals and could lead to a sustained competitive advantage if correctly managed. 
Organisational policies (V) 
The TRM process does not affect the policies in any way, but does draw on some of the basic 
ones in its planning stage, meaning that the cultures and ideals the policies hope to exact are 
present and constant. 
Financial Resources (V) 
The technology roadmapping exercise defines the urgency of certain technology-related projects 
and the expected costs, as well as an idea of the resources required. This allows Arla to plan 
capital expenditure, create business plans for projects including cash flow projections. If credit 
lines are required for larger scale projects, this can be planned in advance due to the clarification 
==PAGE:67==
63 
 
of possible future trends. The TRM process does not, however, enhance the rarity of the 
resource, which is still commonly available to competitors. 
Intangible Resources 
Proprietary technology and patents (VRIN) 
The TRM process allows the firm to take a view on the patents they currently hold and their 
potential usefulness. Further research can be done in areas identified by the TRM where patents 
exist, or those patents can be taken advantage of in a timely fashion. Plans can be made to invest 
in research will lead to new patents, and to patent technology in which research is currently 
underway or nearing completion. This control over patent planning will reinforce Arla’s strength 
in this area. 
Diversified product portfolio (VRIN) 
A better understanding of future technological trends, scenarios, probabilities and costs allows 
Arla to spot the potential for the further diversification of their product portfolio and invest in 
research and resources in the relevant areas. 
International experience and  presence (V) 
The TRM process helps Arla take advantage of their international presence, by potentially 
highlighting experts, research institutions and technologies in markets where Arla may already 
have some presence. This gives them the opportunities to partner and ally with them, and 
provides a slight advantage over rivals, particularly if this is a small or auxiliary market for the 
large European firms (increasing rarity). 
Brand power (VR) 
Arla’s well-recognised brand is a function of its history and market presence, and is not affected 
in any meaningful way by technology roadmapping. 
Skilled workforce (VRI) 
==PAGE:68==
64 
 
With the plans emanating from the TRM process, Arla has a better conception of the ways in 
which they will have to arrange their skilled workforce, and which skills will likely be redundant 
or of less value to them in the future. It also gives an idea of the skills that will likely have to be 
developed internally and those which will have to be acquired from outside the firm or industry. 
Having such foresight in the labour market with regard to expertise, and understanding of 
optimised skills utilisation, may be difficult to substitute.

intangible category.

6 Discussion 
6.1 Review of Results 
 Practicability 
The literature is used to develop nine general areas to use in the evaluation of the practicability 
of a positioning strategy. The three major dimensions identified from theory (believability, 
actionability and sustainability) show a correlation with Quadric’s own suggestions for using the 
Framework as a way to “bridge the gap between business strategy and implementation”.  
Quadric suggests that a firm should first develop a clear positioning strategy. This correlates with 
believability – the positioning strategy must be founded in reality and be clear. Then, they 
suggest, the positioning strategy should be implemented thoroughly across all functions. This 
requires actionability – no implementation will happen if it is not practical and planned. Finally, 
Quadric suggests that the business be managed based on positioning. This is where sustainability 
is applicable. To manage the business based on positioning, it must be apparent that the position 
creates a sustainable competitive advantage. 
Figure 16 shows that Arla’s position revolving around the concept of Good Growth is slightly 
deficient in the area of believability. It is stronger in terms of actionability and sustainability. 
The main failings in terms of believability do not emanate from the positioning strategy itself, but 
rather from past controversies which contradict it and have left an ambivalent external 
perception. The most noticeable area of weakness in sustainability is that superficially, the 
positioning strategy does not seem to be well differentiated from those of some of Arla’s 
competitors. In this instance the related activities have to be examined in depth to determine if 
there truly is differentiation. 
Figure 18 shows how the TRM process enhances the practicability of the positioning strategy. 
Arla’s track record and the external perception which should support believability in it may be 
alterable, but TRM is a foresight progress, and is not concerned with the past. It can, however, 
==PAGE:71==
67 
 
add to believability by bringing a new level of detail to internal and external analysis, which will 
add to the firm’s knowledge and be useful in business function alignment. 
In terms of how actionable the positioning strategy is, TRM’s practical nature has much to 
contribute. The process adds fine levels of detail to planning levels related to the positioning 
strategy and the operational evidence. It also provides an iterative consideration and analysis of 
operational alignment with Good Growth. It reinforces functional alignment by deliberately 
creating a link between the requirements as assessed by the commercial functions and the 
technical solutions as understood by operational functions. More than that, it paints a picture of 
the way in which this alignment will evolve as technology drives change in the market. 
Examining new and potential technologies has every likelihood of revealing greener, more 
environmentally sustainable ways to do things, as environmental consciousness is a common 
technological driver. A very important way in which the TRM will help Arla achieve positioning 
sustainability is with regards to differentiation. Arla must find techniques by which it can enact 
its Good Growth strategy in ways which are starkly and communicably different to those of 
competitors. Innovation is a critical component of the operational evidence of Good Growth and 
so activities in this area which are performed differently to and better than rivals will substantially 
increase the likelihood of a sustainable competitive advantage through differentiation. This is 
really the focus of a TRM exercise. Nu Angle’s TRM approach creates a detail-rich array of 
technological and market possibilities supported by practical options regarding finances, 
resources, alliances and strategic objectives. By providing information which can strongly support 
R&D decision-making, the TRM gives Arla the tools with which they can differentiate themselves 
in terms of operational activities. 
 Operational Evidence 
One area of evaluation is deemed to be important enough for separate analysis. It is argued that 
resources are central to implementing any positioning strategy and are also the key to creating 
the operational evidence which the Quadric Framework postulates supports and must be aligned 
==PAGE:72==
68 
 
with that positioning strategy (section 2.3.6). Arla’s resources are identified and examined using 
the VRIN method stemming from the resource-based view (RBV).  
RBV theory has been shown in section 2.5.4 to be particularly relevant to technology strategy 
development. Theory also suggests (section 2.4.2) that a sustainable competitive advantage is 
dependent on the way in which a firm’s resources are acquired and managed. Since TRM is 
concerned with the creation of a technology strategy, and the aim of the Quadric is to create 
sustainable competitive advantage, RBV is considered an apt theory for application in this 
instance. 
By providing the opportunity to reinforce and strengthen VRI and VRIN resources and help reduce 
weakness in V, VR resources, the TRM process and outcomes support operational evidence in the 
Quadric Framework.  
It is seen in Figure 17 that the resources belonging to Arla most likely to create sustainable 
competitive advantage are primarily organisational (for example, its cooperative nature) and 
those intangible resources which contribute most to its innovative capabilities. 
Several resources in the financial and physical areas exist which are valuable, but are not yet 
capable of producing a true competitive advantage, such as cash flow, access to credit, and plants 
and machinery. 
Figure 19 shows the areas where TRM can afford opportunities for improvement of resources 
and competences. A primary finding here is that while using TRM reinforces some of Arla’s pre-
existing VRIN resources in the intangible category, it does more to improve opportunities for 
competitive advantage in the physical resources, where weaknesses have been identified. This 
can be interpreted as meaning that the TRM process strengthens the organisational and 
intangible resources of Arla, and mobilises them in a focused way to help enhance the potential 
of its physical resources. 
The cooperative nature of the organisation is used in the process and informs its goals. Given a 
better understanding of how to utilise the skills inherent in their workforce, how to manage their 
==PAGE:73==
69 
 
patent strategy and the ways in which they can diversify their product portfolio, Arla’s innovation 
capabilities are fortified. Clarity is given regarding mobilisation of existing alliances, and joint 
ventures and the scope for new ones is broadened 
Regarding physical resources, such as plants, equipment and farms, TRM methodology improves 
Arla’s ability to use these resources to create competitive advantage by directly suggesting 
possible ways in which improvements in efficiency can be made.  
Understanding future trends and potential costs related to those trends provides Arla with a 
better understanding of how to utilise its financial resources, but has little impact on the rarity 
of these resources. Rivals may have similar or better resources and their own understandings of 
how to effectively use them. It may be that there is little impact on financial resources as these 
are in fact the conduit for translating strengthened intangible and organisational resources into 
improved physical resources. 
On the whole, the general methodology employed for TRMs by nu Angle has much in common 
with that proposed by Farrukh et al. However, there are a number of important differences to 
consider. The first, and most striking, is the movement away from basing the exercise solely on 
internal workshops. Once the initial workshops are completed, a survey of external experts is 
undertaken to broaden the knowledge available for application and to account for possible bias 
inside the firm. This use of external experts is in line with trends towards open innovation, which 
meshes well with Arla’s cooperative nature and growth goals. 
The second is that the workshops with the commercial and operations functions are held 
separately, rather than jointly. In doing so, it is possible that disagreements and side-tracking are 
avoided, as the operations function must directly address the pull factors described by the 
commercial. However, there may be an element of brainstorming and creativity that is 
suppressed in not doing so. 
There are two filtration steps in nu Angle’s methodology that are absent from the standard 
methodology. The first is the use of IP mapping to eliminate impossibilities, and the second is the 
==PAGE:74==
70 
 
use of urgency rankings once the timelines have been created. These added tactics help to focus 
the TRM process. 
The level of detail required from nu Angle’s methodology seems to be higher than that suggested 
the literature. This leads to more detailed planning, and better information with which to work 
in terms of decision making, consequently adding to the actionability of the Quadric Framework. 
 Summary 
Figure 20 overleaf depicts the findings in a graphical manner. It shows how the Quadric 
Framework and TRM methodology inform and support one another through positioning strategy 
implementation and the mobilisation of resources. It points out the dimensions of practicability 
and the frameworks used, and their roles in the process.  
6.2 Reflections on Methodology 
This research uses multiple sources of evidence, including interviews, email correspondence, 
internal documents, external analysis, media reports, annual and other public reports, and 
marketing literature. Input has been received from all three of the relevant firms.  
All the information has been documented and where necessary, directly referred to. Contributors 
have not been given the opportunity to give feedback in time for submission, constituting a 
weakness in the construct validity.  
Causal relationships have been shown to exist in the literature and a logical model for these 
relationships has been built. Relationships from the real-world case have been inferred through 
argument, which correspond to the logical model. This means that the internal validity of the 
case is relatively high.

6.3 Availability and Reliability of Data 
In general it was found to be difficult to co-ordinate the information emanating from three firms 
involved in different industries. Representatives of the firms are all subject to time pressures 
which make co-ordination difficult for meetings and interviews. Confidentiality requirements 
make it difficult to obtain certain documents (such as completed roadmaps) which might bolster 
some of the assertions made in the research. 
A short questionnaire was developed for examination of external perceptions, although it was 
not completed by enough people to be said to be representative of any market. It should be 
regarded as anecdotal evidence which peripherally reinforces the analysis.  
The initial interview with Paul Cornillon was done before the direction of the research had been 
fully determined and is therefore of more value for background information than any specifics. 
The interview with Steve Bone was more clearly structurally defined and elicited much relevant 
detail. 
In analysing the differentiation of Good Growth, only two of four major competitors surveyed 
had clear information available easily available regarding their positioning strategies. 
All three firms involved in the case have a wealth of information available on the internet relevant 
to this research. This has been used where pertinent, but bearing in mind the potential for bias 
characteristic of public documents such as these.

7 Conclusion  
7.1 Conclusions 
 
 
Arla is a multinational dairy cooperative with a strategic focus on innovation. It has developed a 
positioning strategy in conjunction with Quadric, using the Quadric Framework. This positioning 
strategy is based around the concept of Good Growth, which revolves around growing the 
business in a socially and environmentally responsible manner, considering nature and health.  
Tools such as the Quadric Framework have been said to provide little guidance as to the 
practicability of a positioning strategy such as this, particularly with reference to operational 
implementation. Using the literature, a set of factors can be developed which can be said to give 
guidance for this. One of these is integral to all aspects practicability, and that is the resources 
and distinctive strengths of the firm. This is also the basis for the operational evidence in the 
Quadric Framework.  
The technology roadmapping process can be shown to support and inform the positioning 
strategy in two ways. Firstly, and generally, it does so, by increasing the actionability and 
sustainability of the positioning strategy. It does this by adding a technological layer to internal 
and external analyses. Additionally, it further details planning in the area of technology and 
contributes an element of iterative thinking regarding the positioning strategy and its role in the 
products it brings to market and the way in which they are produced. The detailed scenario 
planning and technological and strategic objectives developed contribute to the activities which 
will create a sustainable differentiation from rivals. In addition to enhancing these factors, it 
“It’s not like an engineering model where you put it together and that’s it. Organisations are 
more difficult, with all the complexities of chemistry, A+B gives C, always. In a company, A+B 
gives C plus a bit of D, and on the arrow between the two, you’ve got to make it happen.” 
-  Steve Bone, Co-founder of nu Angle (2015) 
==PAGE:78==
74 
 
reinforces Arla’s commitment to environmental sustainability and uses business function 
alignment to develop solutions to challenges. At the same time it promotes alignment by linking 
technological push forces to market pull forces. 
It also supports and informs Good Growth through the operational evidence. The resource-based 
view of the firm can be used to show that the TRM process strengthens the organisational and 
intangible resources of Arla, and mobilises them in a focused way to help enhance the potential 
of its physical resources. At the same time, it guides the use of Arla’s financial resources to 
achieve this.  
A sustainable competitive advantage comprises a relative and an absolute component. A 
positioning strategy represents the relative component of a competitive advantage, as it is 
concerned with creating an advantage through differentiation from rivals. A technology strategy 
is concerned with meeting predicted needs which may arise in the market place, and as such, 
represents the absolute component of a competitive advantage. The Quadric Framework and 
technology roadmapping are practical tools for the distillation of positioning strategy and 
technology strategy respectively. Using the resource-based view, it can be shown that they 
complement one another and if used in a coordinated manner, create the conditions possible to 
precisely define a sustainable competitive advantage. 
7.2 Recommendations and Further Research 
In further research of this nature regarding firms for whom innovation is strategically important, 
it is suggested that the outcomes of any TRMs developed be used in addition to the method used 
to achieve them. The methodology employed here could be used for such an analysis and the 
results would reduce any element of speculation. 
The practicability factors laid out in section are loosely grouped into the larger dimensions of 
believability, actionability and sustainability. These dimensions build on one another to ensure 
practicability. Some of the factors straddle these dimensions, which makes it difficult to rate each 
of the larger dimensions individually. Further research should examine the possibility of precisely 
defining which factors fall under which dimension, and separating out the factors or creating new 
==PAGE:79==
75 
 
ones if necessary. A method could also be sought to more accurately quantify how strongly a 
positioning strategy adheres to these factors. 
An interesting auxiliary analysis may be to determine how nu Angle’s TRM methodology adds to 
the methodologies presently available in the literature, particularly with regard to how effectively 
the TRM process helps support business, positioning and technology strategy.

Telephone Interview - Paul Cornillon, Senior Vice President R&D at Arla Foods 
Monday 16 March 2015 
Roadmaps are not new at Arla, they have been using them for some time to define strategic 
objectives.  
Paul has a different broad definition of technology, which sees technology as knowledge more 
than any specific product. They are used for the short to long term. 
Paul’s whole team is involved, as well as marketing and operational teams. Paul feels that you 
can’t develop effective roadmaps without involving marketing – need to have a solid 
understanding of the markets you are looking at. 
The idea of Good Growth is not involved in developing roadmaps or defining objectives. Paul sees 
the identity as the DNA of the company, and it emanates from the firm’s history and operations. 
It is inherent, and can’t be changed, although it may evolve. For example, R&D co-operates with 
firms not because it is thinking about the identity (Good Growth), but rather because that is its 
history – it is a co-operative and always has been and this is simply an expression of the firm’s 
identity. 
In the past, planning for co-operation and roadmaps were done on a per project basis. Paul sees 
this as a failure, because it resulted in Arla only dealing with partners in Denmark and Sweden. 
Roadmaps need initially to be broad, and thereafter, narrow the funnel. Small initiatives (that 
may take three months, for example) are not considered in roadmapping exercises. They will 
naturally arise from the details of the roadmap, but the real focus of a roadmap is to consider 
game-changers. 
Paul sees partners as allowing Arla to reach new markets and find new solutions. In terms of this 
co-operation, he sees no correlation between the intended market for a product and the 
==PAGE:90==

geographical location of the partner. The new paradigm is to acquire partners based on the 
expertise, rather than location. 
Paul expressed discomfort with actually analysing any roadmaps produced, but was more 
receptive to analysing the process of development. He suggested a specific project currently 
underway and raised the possibility of talking to a partner company about the process and using 
this a case.

GoToMeeting Interview - Steve Bone, Co-Founder of nu Angle 
6 May 2015  
What is nu Angle’s experience with technology roadmaps, how do you view them? 
It’s quite interesting to note that engineers gravitate towards roadmapping. For example (edit) – 
they’re engineers. They come at it from a systems approach. They work with engineering 
companies who tend to see roadmaps as products. The roadmap, in my opinion, is more a 
technology plan. The thing you need to do to is to put the products and platforms together. 
Our view of roadmaps is more of a foresight view. We don’t think in terms of products. We 
consider where the technologies are likely to emerge, and what is the shortest time and longest 
time for this to happen. Bar chart and earliest and latest emergences. Assemble a set of 
technology windows of opportunities. As you go further out in time, more uncertainty, bar charts 
broaden. If it’s tomorrow, it’s a day, 10 years out, might be 5 years. 
Senior executives need to consider which are the most important technologies and can they be 
clustered together into areas. Academics tend to run workshops internally. They grab the results 
in a Powerpoint presentation. You end up with 200 technologies in a plan. What are you going to 
do with it? You can cut it several ways. You can think about costs, resources. If I don’t have them, 
can I delay something and do the most important first. You have to take a decision about 
prioritisation. 
These roadmaps are often presentations or A0 posters. What do you do with them? Nothing. 
Can you give a little bit of background about your methodology? 
==PAGE:92==
88 
 
We started the firm 10 years ago and we were offered the opportunity 8 years ago to do a 
roadmap. We read the academic literature, but most of it didn’t make sense to us. We pieced 
together pieces of the methodology and did a lot of work on technology strategy – what choices 
must be made in the short, medium and long term. Then we began with a mindmap. Mindmaps 
are normally considered an ideation or brainstorming tool. We use it differently. On the first 
branch, you have the business driver, consumer need. On the second branch, what function do 
you need to fulfil that needs, and on the third branch, what technologies will deliver that 
function? 
Sweet colour – one function. What technologies deliver that function? We don’t believe the 
technologies are always likely to be in one sector as the client, so we look outside the sector. 
What technologies are currently within, outside the sector and then new to the world (these are 
normally in academic institutions). This gives some focus for a roadmap. 
For example, biosensors. How does that break down into the future? This is where the roadmap 
comes in. 
How did you apply your methodology at Arla? 
With Arla, we modified our technology strategy process, not the roadmapping process. On the 
first branch of the mindmap we asked– what challenge does Arla’s manufacturing have? The 
second question is then to break this down into several sectors. For example, if efficiency is a 
challenge, how do we improve manufacturing efficiency? Underneath that, a function might be 
control. Or sensing. You need to sense what’s in milk before you can control it. So underneath 
that, let’s take sensing, you might say well actually, how do we sense bacteria in milk? The broad 
technology area might be biosensors for real time assessment of bacteria in milk. As you go out 
further and further in levels, they get more detailed. So biosensors can then break down into 
other sensors, and break down again and again.  You then look at your mindmap, which might 
be extremely large. Instead of having a workshop internally, which we do as well, we say, are we 
missing any technologies? Let’s go out to a set of experts that actually know about biosensors. 
These sensors might be in health care, for example, there might be sensors to measure specific 
==PAGE:93==
89 
 
biomarkers in blood, or to measure bacteria in effluent water. I mean, how many times do you 
see companies develop technologies that are already being developed elsewhere? 
We caught a cold on the first few projects. If you get a group of experts to create these roadmaps, 
where are you missing technologies, you get overloaded with content. We always go find the 
experts, go to them one to one, capture the information, and if necessary then have a workshop 
with them. We build a roadmap before going to the workshop. 
The mindmap starts by talking not to technologists, but to commercial people. The marketing 
guys or in Arla’s case, it was the supply chain. What challenges do you have? Start with their 
input. Get agreement and alignment – is this your picture of the business? Are these the 
challenges you really have? You then work with internal R&D, you get their input in terms of what 
technologies are you currently applying to these sorts of business areas within the mindmap and 
where are the gaps? 
We never just work with internal experts, you need external experts to avoid (perception vs 
reality). Go outside to avoid a blinkered view. (Other groups) they end up focusing on things on 
things they know, and you end up with self-justification by process. Management myopia. You 
need to broaden the scope and challenge the internal view. You apply an objective process, 
you’re not personalising it. That’s the advantage of having a process like this that you can apply. 
At Arla, we picked a number of technologies, there were about 100 of them. This was after we’d 
had the input from the experts. Each of the technologies had a comment about what it was. The 
experts also said this is when it’s going to happen, a timeframe on that, and also a probability of 
success. Sort of a risk weighting. Is this technology likely to be a commercial reality by the time 
you have suggested it? The further out in time you go, things become more uncertain, more risky.  
The other thing you try to do is say to the experts, where is this technology, where is it located,  
which industry sector and who is the world famous expert or centre of excellence in this 
technology area? That gives some practicality, it’s no longer merely theoretical. On a scale from 
==PAGE:94==
90 
 
1-5, how expensive will this be? Very approximately. What critical mass do you need to develop 
this?  And where is the critical mass? 
What do you mean by critical mass? 
Well, display technology might be important to Arla but they aren’t going to get into it, it’s not 
their business, and they don’t have the critical mass. It’s a bit of an extreme example. You need 
1000 scientists to develop it, but it’s all in the Far East. 
Sometimes we do an IP map, although we didn’t do it for Arla. Why? You might find that a 
technology looks attractive, it might impact some of the needs or challenges in the mindmap, but 
there are fences in the IP, and you can’t get in. You do this to see where the barriers are and to 
reduce the list. Or you can see the space is very open, or that it is not so attractive, but you can 
get in there and build a bridgehead into this area. 
At Arla we had 12 criteria on which to evaluate. We scored them 1-5 with the commercial guys. 
You build a set of 2x2 matrices. For example the Arthur D. Little matrix. It shows the impact on 
the challenge against the maturity of the market. Also, Key Based Pacing technologies. These are 
labels to label a technology around, base – something you need to be in the business at all. In 
Arla’s case, for  pasteurisation a base technology is filtration. Key – those technologies that 
differentiate your product or service in some way. You might not be the only one to have it, but 
it differentiates you in the market place. Comparison to order winners and qualifiers. 
Pacing technologies could replace key ones. They are potential disrupters. An example is 
something like measuring something in water with an old fashioned electrode. It is key, but 
mature. It’s likely to become obsolete and replaced by pacing technologies. Biosensors are faster, 
more specific, or cheaper, reliable, accurate. Pacing techs can come from another sector 
(healthcare), if you don’t have it, you’re out of business. It takes time to develop it and put it in 
your product.  
We plot maturity against impact on competition, key based pacing, and look for mature 
technologies that are key. If you don’t have pacing technologies in your portfolio, you have a 
==PAGE:95==
91 
 
problem, your mature key technologies could be replaced. We’re looking for the most important. 
So we use these matrices, plus the ranking, to prioritise them. Those that are not a prority we 
“park “, put them on the back burner, “park” is a less negative way to say it.  
So how do you present this to the client in a way that is understandable and actionable? 
End up with a set of technologies, recluster them into tech areas. Biosensors – control and 
sensing. Senior managers can’t cope with this. We tend to use pictures. We don’t explain them 
in words, we usepictures, boiling down technical detail to easy to understand visual concepts. 
Youtube videos also. 
We’re aligned with a Cambridge firm called special agent. They are engineers and scientists who 
have spent a lot of time in branding, consumer companies. They spend all their time creating 
pictures of embodiments of technology to show to senior management.  
At Arla, there was a presentation you give out and one you present. Text goes into the notes part 
and you put pictures in the presentation. 
At Arla, there is the concept they have of Good Growth. It’s their positioning strategy. What 
role would you say this plays in the way you plan and carry out the roadmapping process? 
Well with Arla, before hand, we tried to read up on them, read annual reports, notice the 
important things such as investment into a new innovation centre, 25000 employees, the fact 
that it’s a co-operative. The other thing is that if you look at a lot of their material they publish, 
they focus a lot on alignment. If you’re trying to create change or get a message across, that’s 
really important. Alignment across the operational and marketing factors, etcetera and it was 
apparent when we started to work for them. The more you can find out before you even see 
them, the better.  
Sometimes you get it wrong. It’s not like an engineering model where you put it together and 
that’s it. Organisations are more difficult, the complexities of chemistry a+b gives c always. In a 
company a+b+ gives c plus a bit of d and on the arrow between the two, you’ve got to make it 
happen. What we knew about Arla was also that their 2017 vision is to create a future of dairy to 
==PAGE:96==
92 
 
give health and inspiration to the world. So optimisation and control, which is where we focused 
on, to create this vision of delivering this solution for manufacturing excellence was really 
important. This is why this whole programme started.  
Without any foundation for anything, you can’t describe what outcomes the company wants. 
This is again my frustration and disagreement with the academics who come in run a workshop 
and then leave. What you end up with is a roadmap which is going nowhere. I think its 
intellectually interesting and all hangs together intellectually, but when the rubber hits the road… 
I have to say Arla where one of the better clients. They are good to work with, they are courteous 
and listen to what you say. They’re not arrogant. It’s important to be able to tell the client what 
you think, not what they think.  
What were the outcomes of your TRM process? Without going into too many specifics? 
We went through the process of identifying the technology areas. There were 7 areas. 
Underneath that we had a set of technologies. All of them were important because we had scored 
them. Those technologies had a lot of content with them from the internal and external experts. 
A mass of information. The report was 150 pages identifying technologies, where they were on 
the mindmap, and detail about expert opinion. Within each area, you create a one or two line 
objective for that area. “Arla will become a leader in….” or “Arla will increase prod capacity by 
being ahead in….”.
