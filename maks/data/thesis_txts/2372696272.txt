Abstract
A thermophotovoltaic (TPV) system is a technology which can convert thermal radiation
to electricity with a photovoltaic cell. The source of heat could be varied from hydrocarbons,
waste heat or solar energy. Due to controlling capability over direction and spectrum
of radiation, it has an added advantage over a conventional solar photovoltaic system.
And lack of moving parts presents a mechanically efficient and noise free system, which
are common problems in other power generation units like a diesel generator.
TPV is a highly multidisciplinary field technology which involves areas of heat &
mass transfer and semiconductor technology alike. Due to high complexity, the system
has not reached maturity.
In this thesis, a literature survey has been conducted to indicate the current extent
to which TPV technology has been analyzed. After this, the focus of the thesis has been
magnified on fully understanding the working principles of the semiconductor part of
the TPV system. A computational model is created for Silicon and Gallium-antimonide
photovoltaic cell. The model is based on the standard Hovel’s approach to simulate
internal quantum efficiency profile for a photovoltaic cell specifically with constant
doping profile. Under the assumptions made, the results for Gallium-antimonide were
in excellent match with published work. For Silicon, this model proved unsuccessful to
fully simulate the quantum efficiency profile. This model would also allow to better
understand the working principles of any photovoltaic cell.
A mathematical model for the Drift-Diffusion transport equation is created to understand
the effects of high intensity and non-ideal boundary conditions on a photovoltaic cell.
An approximate numerical solution represents these effects, also stating the significant
relation between intensity flux and internal series resistance endured by a cell during
operation.
A complete TPV system model is developed to specifically quantify the effects of a
flue gas recuperator and wavelength filter on overall system efficiency. Thermodynamic
irreversibilities are neglected and relevant assumptions are made for each component
for simplifications. The inclusion of the two sub-components increases system electrical
efficiency by almost 10 times from 2% to 19%.
Finally, an experimental work has been carried out in which an Indium-gallium-arsenide
based PIN quantum well cell is fabricated and electrical characterization and analysis
resulted in peak power production of 2 micro watts and fill factor of 0.54 for best cases.
Potential applications and economic evaluation of the technology are summarized
from other literature, which could be used to formulate similar analysis in present market
scenario as a significant update.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
At the forefront of renewable energy generation, photovoltaic technology is one of the
fastest growing sector [1]. Even considering such growth rate, photovoltaic technology
deals with many fundamental limitations. One such limitation is the dependence on
solar radiation for input power source. This project aims at understanding the limits to
a certain degree and proposing and analyzing a technology which can over come them.
The proposed technology is called Thermophotovoltaic system.
1.1
Context and Motivation
Generation of electricity by photovoltaic effect has long been considered as one of the
most promising methods to generate clean and efficient energy. The concept of light
dependent voltage has been existing for almost 200 years, when this effect was discovered
by Becquerel in 1839. But the forerunner to the current silicon based solar cells, as we
know, came into existence during the early 1950’s. However, the upper limit of energy
conversion was quickly found when William Shockley and Hans Queisser published their
results in 1960 for the ultimate efficiency for single junction silicon based solar cell
working under blackbody radiation.
Since then the main focus of research has been to make the technology more market
competitive and reducing the cost of photovoltaic cell.
Two main approaches can
be followed to achieve this requirement.
First is to lower the cost of cell without
compromising the efficiency. This can be done by using cheaper substrates as in the
case of multicyrstaline silicon. Cost of cell can also be reduced by using less materials
as in the case of thin film cells such as CdTe, CIGS and III-V (speak three-five) cells.
Both approaches can be classified under FIRST and SECOND generation photovoltaic
devices. The second approach includes attempts to increase conversion efficiency beyond
Shockley-Queisser limit. Technologies like multijunction cells, quantum dot photovoltaic
etc. fall under THIRD generation devices. Unfortunately, due to high manufacturing
cost and slow research progress, these devices are mostly subjected to extra-terrestrial
applications.
Current photovoltaic technologies, at least the mature ones, operate in the spectrum
of solar radiation. The limitation of such system can be considered in two folds. First
is, even though solar energy is nearly unlimited source, it is impossible to convert 100 %
of incident radiation to electricity. This fundamental limit is often termed as spectral

For a TPV system, burner design and emitter specifications are inherently related. The
energy released from the flame must be efficient transferred to the emitter surface. A
direct, yet approximate, measure of this efficiency is the ratio of power density of emitter
surface to the fuel input energy.
A good configuration between emitter and burner
translates to:
• Uniform emission of radiation
• High heat transfer from flame to emitter
• Uniform temperature distribution on the emitting surface area side
• High radiation power density i.e. radiation emitter per unit area

blackbody. A blackbody is one which allows and absorbs radiation of all wavelength

3.1
Introduction
All materials are classified on the basis of the macroscopic properties they display. One
such property is electrical conductivity. Metals are the material known to have high
electrical conductivity. Semiconductors are placed next in line. And insulators have
least to no electrical conduction. The general theory behind the existence of this and
many other properties are given by band theory in solids. There are many models leading
up to describing the existence of these bands and two prominent ones can be stated as:
• Nearly free electron theory: Here electrons are said to occupy definite energy states
and the ones at the highest energy level are considered to be freely moving in the
solid. The interaction between electrons are completely ignored. The electronic
states are represented by an expression called Bloch wavefunction for nth state.
And each index n representing one band.
• Tight binding theory: This model is based on the molecular orbital theory and
the fact that solid are closely packed structures. When large number of atoms
come close enough, their outer orbitals start overlapping and the band is formed
i.e. this is based on superposition of wavefunction of electronic state.
Both models can explain electronic conduction in metal, but not sufficiently in
semiconductors.
As a result, a quantum mechanical extension to the free electron
theory known as Kronig-Penny model provides simple and elegant solution in which
the Schrodinger’s wave equation (time independent) is solved with Bloch wavefunction
as the boundary condition.
The physical existence of band of can be understood by observing two concepts.
First is that, each electron can only occupy a distinct state and each energy level

3.3.5
Photovoltaic quantum efficiency
Quantum efficiency is the measure of conversion efficiency of photons to collectible
electrons for usable power. [76] has provided a concise statement regarding the accurate
modeling of this quantity.
The first method is, by solving the transport equations
through numerical procedure for accurate doping distribution and carrier generation.
But this process is very complicated. Second is involving an analytic expression developed
by Hovel where it is assumed that photo-generated carrier transport is governed by
equilibrium field[77]. This method is exclusively for semiconductors with uniform doping
profile and no electric field in space outside depletion zone. Unfortunately, both methods
are not efficient enough to predict quantum efficiency accurately [76]. For this analysis,
Hovel’s model is used owing to time constraint.
In a photovoltaic system, the diffusion length of minority carriers are one of the most
significant values. It is a measure of the effective length a minority charge carrier can
travel before recombining. The minority carriers photo-generated within the proximity
of the diffusion length and depletion zone can diffuse towards it and get drifted to the
other side and contribute to power. This closeness is represented by diffusion length.

The overall energy balance of the system has been divided into four cases. Although

assumptions have been mentioned. The key function of this model is to provide quantitatively
significant values on the effects of two critical sub-components for any TPV system
to perform efficiently.
A flue gas to air recuperator responsible improving thermal
efficiency and a band-pass filter which affect fuel consumption and PV performance
simultaneously.

6.1
Introduction
Quantum wells (QWs) are thin layers for low bandgap materials inside a host material
with a higher bandgap. Initially, the QWs were placed inside the neutral zone of a P-N
junction [95]. But the transport property of charge carriers turns to increase when an
electric field is present around in the quantum well. Therefore, more recently the QWs
were designed to be placed in the intrinsic layer of a P-I-N structure[96]. The general
structure of a QW placed in P-I-N structure is given in figure 6.1. The number of wells
and its thickness can influence the effective bandgap of the system. Therefore, it gives
the ability to tune the bandgap of the cell with respect to irradiance spectrum. This
feature can be complementary to thermophotovoltaic applications.

In an IC engine, the source of heat is also the working fluid in thermodynamic

cell to 10 MJ marine batteries. Most of them are dominated by nickle, lead, cadmium and
lithium as the primary material. [105] has stated that secondary batteries tend to have
higher gravimetric and volumetric power densities around 500 kW/m3 and more than
500 W/kg. Lithium ion batteries can have energy densities up to 0.5 MJ/kg. But the
main problem with batteries are, low shelf time i.e. degradation during non-operation.
A hydrocarbon based TPV system would practically have no degradation time, since
fuel itself can act as an energy storage unit. Also, energy densities of hydrocarbons are
at least two order higher than batteries. Therefore an efficient converter is imperative
to use this.
Fuel cells can be considered very efficient competitors for TPV. One key advantage is
that, by avoiding combustion, they are not subjected to Carnot efficiency limitations[106].
They have also demonstrated high efficiencies, up to 40 to 60 % based on lower heating
values for the major fuel cell types. [107] are one of major producers of hydrogen based
Proton Exchange Membrane fuel cells for vehicle applications, demonstrating power
densities up to 250 W/kg. But the critical disadvantage is compressed hydrogen being
used as fuel. Both it’s production and storage are quite inefficient. Methanol reforming
accounts for 95% percent of production which which is about 70% efficient1. Hydrogen
has to be stored in high pressure cylinders at 400 to 700 bars. For vehicle purpose, safety
always remains concern with such systems. For TPV system, the source of heat could
be any high temperature surface, practically operating at atmospheric pressure.
Thermoelectric generator is an emerging technology using the Seeback, Peltier and
Thompson effect in combination. Current heat-to-electrical efficiency stands less than
5 to 8% for established materials2. [108] has provided a concise comparison of both
technologies , stating that TPV has some definite advantages like low parasitic losses,
and avoiding inherent conduction losses by electrons in thermoelectric generators.

7.2
Application and economic viewpoint
Thermophotovoltaic technology has been investigated intensively over the past 20 years,

[4] has, for example, considered from
market potential, user need and value added and existing competing technology. For
faster maturing of technology, high value applications and high end user needs were said
to be targeted. Classifying all the applications based on the two factors, it was said
that recreational vehicles and military applications are two the main areas. Both would
require quiet and emission free electric power source.
[5] has provided an extensive application based assessment of the technology based
on five classes on electrical power generation.
These applications include non-CHP
combustion generators, CHP combustion generators, Solar based TPV, Nuclear TPV for
space application and waste heat recovery. A total of 12 generator units divided along
the five mentioned classes have been scored from 0 to 4, based on four indicators for
each unit. They are research and development efforts, benefits compared to competing
technology, market and cost and human impact. Final result indicated a micro CHP
unit, a self powered heating unit and a industrial heat recovery unit as the highest value
closely followed by a portable generator and solar powered TPV system.
[109] has perhaps been one of the most the detailed economic analysis carried for
TPV systems. The main objective of analysis has been to find the electricity production
cost for a self -powered domestic heating system based on silicon photocell. A system
of lifetime of 20 years, interest rate of 4.25% and maintenance of 1% is assumed. The
system values were based on operation in Zurich, Switzerland. Four cases are considered
where case 1 has a normal mono-crystalline Si based cell and Ytterbium emitter with

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis presented the concept of a thermophotovoltaic system which could be used
for possible application in power generation field.
A detailed research was done, to
understand the working principles of the system in which a thermal emitter is coupled
to a photovoltaic system to generate power from infrared radiation. Based on the review
of past works, it could be said that a lot of efforts have been made to understand the
technology in a better way.
Since electric power output is of high importance for any system, the main focus
was placed on the analysis of the semiconductor responsible for this. Based on the data
availability for TPV cells, a computational model was developed for two generic, and yet
specific, semiconductor material. Gallium-antimonide (Bandgap = 0.72 eV) and Silicon
(Bandgap = 1.12 eV). To make the analysis simple, the model only involved constants
doping conditions and non-degeneracy limit. Although it was pointed out that both
these conditions are surpassed in real applications, and at least for a GaSb based cell in
TPV application.
The main purpose of the model was to simulate the spectral quantum efficiency,
which is a direct measure of the conversion efficiency of radiation to electrical output.
For this purpose, majority carrier mobility, effective lifetime of carrier and diffusion
lengths were modeled from standard data. From this, standard conditions for a 5 μm
GaSb cell and 500 μm Si cell were set with both having n-type material as an emitter.
The internal quantum efficiency for both cells were presented and relevant references
were provided to support the profile. The effects of important boundary conditions like
surface recombination velocity and geometrical aspects like emitter and base thicknesses
were studied. Having a thinner emitter around 0.1 μm and lower surface recombination
velocity at maximum of 2 × 104 cm/sec proved to increase the quantum efficiency value.
This simulation model was based on Hovel’s analytic approach. This method proved a
bit unsuccessful in accurately modeling the quantum efficiency for Si cell but effectively
simulated for GaSb cell.
To better understand effects on high intensity radiation on performance of GaSb
cell, a mathematical model for the carrier transport equations was developed and an
approximate numerical solution was presented. The solution was based on the linear
version of the continuity equation where a constant carrier lifetime was provided as an
input. As a counter intuitive measure, the intensity factor of radiation and lifetime
were altered by same order in opposite direction. As a result, it was seen that there is a
saturation in concentration gradient for both base and emitter. This could be attributed
to internal resistance at high injection which could affect the fill factor. Also increase
in surface recombination appeared to decrease in the amount of carrier for conduction.
==PAGE:102==
84
8 Conclusion
This same effect was also reflective on quantum efficiency profile. Thus it could be said,
increasing the intensity with out any limit will not guarantee higher output power.
From a system perspective, an energy balance and component modeling was carried
in Engineering equation solver specifically to understand effects of two sub-components
on system’s electrical efficiency: a flue gas recuperator and a band pass filter to reduced
the emission of non-convertible radiation towards a GaSb cell. Four different cases were
considered where improvements with the recuperator and filter were made in alternating
and combined manner. Though the model was adopted from other sources, inclusion
a quantum efficiency for PV system and heat exchanger design was exclusive to this
analysis only. The inclusion of a perfect filter was found to have slightly better effect than
a recuperator, although the assumption taken while modeling this, may overemphasize
this fact than it actually is. At equal emitter and cell area, the minimum efficiency
was found to be 2.1% for case I and maximum efficiency stood at 19% for case IV. The
recuperator model was based on a counter flow shell and tube heat exchanger. This
present model could be further improved by including actual emissivity data for emitter
and also considering geometrical aspects for a realistic design of a TPV system.
A quantum well photo-diode based on InGaAs was fabricated on at DTU Danchip
facility with the help of epiwafers provided by Hauxing Opto, China, which has a
potential TPV applications. Four diodes were selected based on visual inspection under
a microscope and electrical characterization was carried under varied intensity of white
LED light source and a constant intensity green laser. Based on the data analysis of
the result, two diode produced representative values under green laser illumination. The
peak power produced was 1.78 μWatts.
Finally, a small literature survey was done to map out the potential applications for
this technology. Competing technologies were considered to be applicable on small scale
power generation. Based on the data at hand, a TPV based portable generator seems
to be the most viable point of focus to carry on.
8.1
Future work and discussion on improvements
Based on the framework and assumption taken into account, there are several ways
improve on the current analysis. The improvements can be on both theoretical and
experimental front.
1) Considering the case for a GaSb cell a constant doping assumption was made. The
inclusion of a Gaussian doping profile in the simulation model, which accounts diffusion
type manufacturing, is very important.
Since most of the analysis carried by other
authors includes this and TPV cells in market are available in this form.
2) In most cases doping concentration in a TPV cell is in high degenerate level.
This may be due to the fact that, at high temperatures intrinsic concentration levels
can exceed lower doping levels. This may increase contribution to saturation current
density. Therefore, this effect leads to revising the method of calculating the Fermi
==PAGE:103==
8.1 Future work and discussion on improvements
85
levels. This also leads to bending conduction and valence band outside depletion zone.
These effects are considered by other authors as well.
3) The above changes will lead to revising the transport equations where Poisson’s
equation will be involved in all three domains due to involvement of electric field in
the entire cell. Simultaneously, in TPV conditions, the semiconductor will operate in
high injection level. As a result lifetime will be excess carrier concentration dependent
and leading to nonlinear continuity equations. Such complicated equations are generally
solved with finite element methods. Solving these equations can give very high accurate
predictions of quantum efficiencies, thus the cell could be optimized in a much better
way.
4) On system analysis level, a more realistic combustion process for emitter can
done which includes irreversibilities due to required temperature drop between flame
and emitter. Geometrical considerations for the system will lead to consider view factor
between emitter and PV cell.
5) Simultaneously, actual optical properties of materials involved in the system like
emissivity and absorptivity of emitter, absorptivity, reflectivity and transmissivity of
filter and reflectivity of PV cells are just of few of data inputs which can enhance the
capability of the current system model vastly.
6) On a more advance scale, minority mobility models for GaSb are still not understood
fully. An experimental procedure must designed to measure these parameters as they
important in determining electrical conduction in semiconductors.
As a final word, it should be pointed out again that a TPV system is a complex
and highly interdisciplinary technology which involves different fields working together.
The optimization of the device does not only depends on the optimization of a single
component, but also on the whole system design and on the interactions between the
different processes.
This technology is very innovative and has undeniable potential to affect the power
generation industry. For reasons as such, collaborations between universities and industries
having experience in different fields are fundamental to increase the knowledge on the
technology and improve it significantly.
