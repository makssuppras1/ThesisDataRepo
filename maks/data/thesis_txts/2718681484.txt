ABSTRACT
The wind power industry has a high interest in new converter control strategies as an
increasing number of converter-based energy sources are being connected to the grid. This
results in new challenges regarding islanding operations, black start, P2X, and weak grid
conditions. Thus grid forming control is a possible solution for the new requirements and
challenges faced by converter-based systems.
This study focuses on the comparison of two grid-forming typologies for grid-connected
and islanding operations utilizing a CIGRE model. The first typology consists of simple
active and reactive power control loops and the second typology with the addition of
virtual admittance and a current controller. The two typologies consist of simple active and
reactive power control loops where the second typology also consists of virtual admittance
and a current controller. The second control typology is comparably more complex but
includes the favored characteristics of current limiting and damping of oscillations.
The study aims to evaluate the small signal and transient performances for the islanded and
grid-connected operation of the mentioned grid-forming typologies. This includes multiple
study cases defined for different system strengths and transient load events. Matlab
Simulink time-domain simulations in both an abc model and a dq0 reference frame model
are used to assess the performance of the control designs and perform two study cases.
The first study case includes the islanding operation where active and reactive droops are
shown to stabilize both voltage and frequency. Both of the typologies have the possibility of
performing grid transitions, but the importance of a synchronized grid before reconnection
is favorable for reduced transients. The second study case includes the parallel-converter
operation, where the load sharing between two wind farms of different power ratings is
tested for both grid-connected and islanded conditions.
The stability analysis shows that low inertia and high damping are favored for the power
loop during transient events and droop control is favorable for islanded operation. The
current controller shows vulnerabilities for islanding weak grid conditions during load steps
but improved responses for transient events. Hence utilizing different control realizations
depending on the system state can improve the general performance of a grid-forming
converter.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
Denmark’s political goal is to reduce CO2 emissions by 70% from 1990 to 2030 and further
to net zero emissions by 2050. The reached level in 2020 was 43 % since 1990. The largest
potential of CO2 reduction comes from the burning of fossil fuels through the international
transportation sector which covers 48 % of Denmark’s economy CO2 emission in 2020,
including danish cargo vessels, air transport, and trucks [1]. Thus reducing the fossil
fuel usage of heavy transport has a huge potential in lowering the CO2 emission when
using alternative fuels. To succeed in these goals the capacity of renewable energy sources
must increase significantly in the near future to ensure the increasing energy demand for
Power-to-X (P2X), Electric vehicles (EV), and Electric heating (EH). Thus by increasing
the capacity of Renewable Energy Sources (RES), the need for energy storage and energy
transportation becomes a critical factor. This can be solved by the implementation of
Energy islands, solving the challenges of the uncertainty of RES power production and
either storing or utilizing the energy when produced [2].

Apart from Denmark’s large investments in offshore wind turbine installations, the rest
of Europe follows the same trend in increasing offshore wind capacity. Here the main
front runners are the United Kingdom, Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium, and Denmark

1.2
Energy islands
On the 22nd of June 2022, it was politically decided that two energy islands should be
constructed in Denmark with the vision of developing new CO2 friendly/neutral technologies
like (P2X) and CO2 capture to become the front runners in lowering the extensive use of
fossil fuels.
One of the energy islands is planned to be located in the North Sea as an artificial island
with 3 GW of wind power connected and later developed up to 10 GW. The second energy
island is planned to be located at Bornholm with a total capacity of 2 GW [4]. The energy
islands will be the first of its kind and ensure the power to millions of homes in the future.
Furthermore, they will establish a solid connection to the neighboring countries, along
with a huge potential for technological development [5].

The energy islands are planned as modular elements that can expand upon increased
offshore wind farm capacity and hydrogen demand. This is not only a huge challenge for
the construction and layout of the island but also the control and stability of the electrical
system, as the installed microgrid should be robust and expandable without causing
instability of previously installed modules and risking the grid-supporting properties [6].
Such an Energy island can be seen in figure 1.2, where two wind farms are connected to a
common high voltage (HV) bus known as the point-of-connection (POC). The islanded
load is illustrated by the P2X plant and an HVAC connection from the island to the
onshore grid. Further, the energy island must be self-sufficient providing a frequency and
voltage reference when the microgrid is disconnected from the onshore grid and operates
in islanded conditions. The island will only be supported by renewable energy sources,
so a pure converter-based control structure is expected for the island. This requires the
development of new converter control typologies.

1.3
Wind turbines
The permanent magnet synchronous generator (PMSG) type IV wind turbines are
completely decoupled from the grid by a back-to-back power converter making it a
variable-speed wind turbine. Compared to other types, the controllability is increased to

1.4
Grid Following and Grid Forming converters
The Grid-following (GFL) converters work as a controlled current source and simply follow
the dynamics of the grid and support the grid with active and reactive power depending
on voltage and frequency measurements. The control scheme of such a controller is usually
simple as the reference voltage and frequency is measured from the connection point of
the grid, hence synchronized with the grid at all times. The simplified equivalent of a
GFL converter can be seen in figure 1.4. On the other hand, the Grid-forming (GFM)
converter operates as a controlled voltage source as the voltage and frequency references
are generated by the converter. Thus the system stability is at risk due to the lag of a
fixed reference and the controller must operate upon the connected micro-grid sources and
loads, which could be a combination of GFL and GFM converters. As the GFM controller
is a controlled voltage source the need of additional control loops are necessary to limit
the current output [8], [9].
Grid-forming converters should support an AC power system during normal, alerted,
emergency, blackout, and restoration states independently of connected conventional units
[10]. Hence a grid-forming converter can be represented as a controlled voltage source in
series with an impedance [7] with a controlled frequency and voltage as illustrated in figure
1.5.

In order to follow the transition towards 100 % renewable energy sources in the power
system, the current use of grid-following converters must evolve into grid-forming converters
in order to compensate for the reduction of system inertia, weak grid conditions and black
start [11]. Thus as more renewable energy sources are being commissioned and new large
conventional units are not being produced, the system must be controlled electrically
without the physical properties of the synchronous generator (SG) like synchronizing
torque, inertia, and damping. Luckily, grid-forming converters can emulate some of these
mechanical properties up to a certain extent and cause similar responses during disturbances
and contingencies [12]. This means that initially, the GFM converters can substitute the
conventional high-inertia system components and the previously installed GFL converters
can operate as intended.
The need for GFCs arises for islanding operation as a voltage and frequency reference is
needed to support the previously installed GFL converters and the imposed weak grid
conditions make the system vulnerable to transient events. The GFCs also have the
possibility of improved system response through their voltage source behavior and can
perform black start in case of complete system failure.
System stability issues are a concern as the converter response is usually much faster. Thus
several different methods of stability studies have previously been conducted [13] including
sequence impedance modeling [14], small-signal stability analysis [15], eigen-analysis [16]
and apparent impedance analysis [17]. These types of studies usually include linear models
represented by state-space models.
1.5
Motivation
Through the previously stated topics, the need for new technologies is evident to ensure the
goal of evolving Danish energy production to 100 % RES, the efficiency of the European
offshore wind farms, and the European system stability. Until now no standard procedure
for energy islands operation and the connection is concluded. Thus the need of testing and
implementing new technologies into isolated micro-grids like an island, where disconnection
from the grid causes little to no impact on the grid operation is the main interest before
planning new grid codes. Some control strategies are already in use such as momentary
cessation for over-frequency events and planned load shedding for under-frequency events.
Yet such control strategies can result in multiple islanded grids as in the case of the recent
Texas blackout due to an extreme winter storm in February 2021 [18] or the islanding
4
==PAGE:17==
1. Introduction
of San Diego California in 2011 [19]. In both cases, the WPPs failed to prevent system
blackouts due to fast disconnections, wrong weather forecasts and bad planning.
The dilemma of the new technologies is that some proof of concept and testing has to be
conducted before recognizing the possible potential. Since grid-forming technologies have
been developed through the past decade and still introduce new control typologies, the need
for system stability studies must be matured and verified through various simulation models
before being fully implemented into the power system. This includes extensive stability
analysis of control loops through different configurations and carefully structured tuning to
ensure the robustness of a flexible grid such as an energy island together with time-domain
simulations of various grid conditions [20]. As grid forming control for grid-connected
operation is widely covered in the literature the islanded operation control strategies still
need maturing.
1.6
Formulation of the problem
The main goal of the thesis is to investigate, implement and compare different Grid forming
control designs for offshore wind turbines during the islanded operation following the
assumption of using existing grid-forming typologies. The focus will be to investigate
system stability and implications of different control loops where the following questions
will be addressed during the thesis.
• Which control parameters of grid-forming control loops are important to ensure
system stability?
• How can stability margins be utilized to ensure small-signal stability?
• How can time-domain responses be used to validate the grid-forming control model?
1.7
Delimitation
Some simplifications of the generated model will be used and are listed below.
• The dynamic simulation model represents a complete aggregated wind farm but
individual wind turbine generator dynamics are not considered.
• Only the grid-side converter control is considered, where the DC-side will be
represented by a DC source.
• The wind turbines will operate at a predetermined power output and the MPPT
of the wind turbine and the dynamics of individual wind turbines including pitch
control and wind speed will not be considered.
• The switching element calculations are averages so an extensive transient analysis is
not possible from the model.

For this study, the focus is on well-established grid-forming control typologies with the
goal of both grid-connected and islanding operations. Different from similar comparative

Choosing a control topology
The power system presented in this chapter has to be controlled during both grid-connected
and islanding operations in order for an Energy Island to be connected to the existing
power system. The described micro-grid is a simplification of a real-life example as the
Energy Island will evolve into containing more than just a single wind farm and a load.
Hence the converter topology must fulfill the requirements listed below.
• Frequency control (islanding)
• Voltage control (islanding)
• Active and reactive power balancing (islanding/grid connected)
• Synchronisation with the connected grid
• Tunable inertia
• Overcurrent protection
In [33] an extended comparison of currently used typologies is compared whereas the most
important properties at the time are inertia control to emulate an SG and the overcurrent
protection to ensure hardware safety. The current should not exceed 20 % −40% above
the nominal value compared to synchronous generators (SG) normally output 5-7 times its
nominal current value during faults. For a converter to deliver such fault currents requires
a huge over-dimensioning of power components to deliver a comparable response, which is
very costly.
The most promising typologies fulfilling the above-mentioned requirements are synchronous
machine-based typologies due to the tunable virtual inertia and the possibility of overcurrent
protection on most. The droop-based controlled inverters do not directly include the tunable
virtual inertia but can be included by the addition of a low pass filter in the power loop
[34]. The droop-based control further eliminates the need for a PLL by either using the
droop directly or as an auxiliary loop to generate the power reference based on frequency
measurements [34]. A purely droop-based controller is very simple but not optimal for
longer durations of WPP control as converter-based units lack the possibility of larger
overcurrents and the power production of RES is stochastic. Implementing a pure droop-
based controller requires the wind turbines to operate with a reserve (below nominal power
values) and are very limited in their operation.
From table 2.7 various different controller structures are evaluated against each other. From
the previously mentioned requirements, it’s decided to choose a Synchronous machine-based
topology such that virtual inertia and current limiting is possible. Additionally, to operate
in islanding operation additional droop of active and reactive power is added to give a
voltage and frequency reference when the external grid is disconnected. For this analysis,
the "Other methods" of table 2.7 are not considered as most of them don’t fulfill the
requirement or are more complicated than the chosen typologies.
It’s desired to first implement a controller purely of outer loops, synchronizing the frequency
with the connected grid and controlling the voltage at a specified bus. Later the addition
of droop-based control is added to include the islanding operation and finally, the inner
loop of a virtual admittance and a current controller is added to limit the current. The
goal is to assess the advantages and disadvantages of the two converter typologies during

2.4
Active power control
One of the main control parameters of the GFM converter is the active power control loop
(PLC) which can emulate the behavior of a synchronous machine. The converter must be
able to control the frequency independently from the grid and synchronize with the grid
during scenarios such as local grid disturbances, black start, or frequency support and the
frequency must remain within ±0.1% in a steady state to follow general TSO requirements.
Beyond the steady state stability criteria, the GFM converter must be able to provide an
inertial response when experiencing disturbances. The converter systems’ virtual inertia
is directly related to the frequency drop and ROCOF during load events. This can be
controlled by the inertia constant H which is zero for no inertial response. The value of
the inertia constant is normally in the range of 2s to 10s for conventional units, but for
converter systems, the value can be chosen faster between 0.5 s and 6 s. Initially, the value
will be chosen to 1s. The controller is implemented as a PI controller, so the response can
be fitted to more strict requirements.

One of the main advantages of implementing a current controller is to limit the current
during faults and transient events, such that the converter operates within safe margins.
The current limit is set to ± 1.2 pu as a power converter’s possibility to dissipate heat
from large currents is very limited, normally 20 −40% above rated value for a few seconds.
The conservative value is chosen as the q-axis current will deviate from zero and the actual
current vector will exceed the chosen limit. The small allowable over current is one of the
power converters’ main drawbacks compared to synchronous machines as they can deliver
5-7 times the rated value for a few seconds.

Current controller
Let’s examine the tuning process of the current controller using the developed dq0 model.
As previously stated the optimal speed of the inner loop current controller is 10 times slower
than the converter switching cycle, hence the natural frequency is 1/10 of the switching
frequency, and the controller time constant is found as 10/fsw = 10/2950 Hz = 3.4 ms.
The resulting tuning parameters of the iterative tuning program show that it’s difficult to
find optimized values for such a fast controller earlier described for this system, so in order
to maintain stability the current controller speed is set to 20 ms for this example.
Instead of using the traditional simple phase and gain margin to tune the controller, a
robust tuning method is applied to gain the confidence of a stable controller even for
uncertain changes to the system. This is only done for the current controller as it’s known
to limit the GFC bandwidth and hence the stability of the system. The chosen tuning
requirements are chosen as listed in table 3.1.

synchronized to the grid between the two "machines", and thus the steady-state value for
the two cases becomes unequal as both frequency and voltage are restored. This requires
secondary control during islanded operation.

5.4
Summary
The islanding study case showed that the converter control typology is capable of stable
operation for both inner and outer loop configuration, yet the feed-forward loop of the
current controller caused voltage instabilities as the voltage cannot be measured during
grid transition. The damping of the current controller showed little importance during the
reconnection. The outer loop lacked to operate within the converter power ratings during
reconnection as expected and the inner loop controller managed to limit the current at the
cost of reduced response time. Finally, the enhanced SPC failed to stabilize the frequency
with the aid of droop for a power reference, so the SPC was substituted with a pure droop
configuration during islanded conditions. The transition between the two different power
loops was proven and the reconnection to the grid could be done by both. The SPC is not
discarded as the characteristics of the VSG showed favorable responses for grid-connected
conditions in the performance assessment chapter. For islanding load steps the inner loop
included unwanted oscillations induced by the step responses in weak grid conditions and
the voltage oscillations for low load conditions were increased.

DISCUSSION & CONCLUSION
7.1
Discussion
The study emphasizes the complexity of grid-forming control design as every control
loop comes with a price of either reduced response time, margin to stability, or a higher
level of complexity. This resulted in two control typologies that can operate in different
configurations depending on the grid-connected and islanded operation.
The modeling approach of the abc model is presented together with the considered control
loops. This gave useful insight into specific control parameters and the implementation of
different control loops. The power loop could emulate a VSG by the use of a synchronous
power controller and quickly tuned with a reduced-order model and Matlab tools. The
use of the well-known droop-based control approach added the islanding possibilities and
the voltage regulator improved transient response and voltage controllability. The virtual
admittance and current controller dynamics are presented and the complexity of tuning
such a loop is emphasized as simple calculation gave little information about the margin
to stability.
The developed dq0 model is presented which adds the possibility of optimized tuning
for the current controller and improved computation speed. The dq0 model advantages
are faster implementation, testing of multiple typologies, and the impact between control
loops can be evaluated for varied control parameters. The task of tuning the current
controller parameters is optimized by creating a reduced-order model in the dq0 frame and
then utilizing the Matlab Robust Control toolbox to tune the controller by an iterative
process. This process gave a more clear indication of the margin to stability and showcased
the vulnerabilities of the current controller. Yet setting the correct requirements of the
optimizing tool with a disc margin approach showed to be challenging to reach a satisfying
response.
From the performance assessment and the study cases, the comparison of the two typologies
showed that the inner loop is advantageous for damping and current limiting purposes but
at the cost of reduced response time and reduced bandwidth. Yet the current limiting and
the damping of oscillations were favorable during transient events when grid-connected and
reconnecting to the grid. In islanded operation, the inner loops induced voltage oscillations
for step responses as expected from the current controller tuning. Hence the current
controller is not favored during weak grid conditions and during load changes. Thus for an
extended control strategy, the inner loop could be activated only during transient events.
The power loop was initially implemented as a synchronous power controller mainly focusing
on the grid-connected operation and showed it could not easily be transitioned to islanded
operation due to the main assumption of having the SPC control the power flow depending
79
==PAGE:92==

on the voltage angle between two voltage sources. During islanding the voltage angle of
the opposing source is unavailable hence secondary controls were needed to stabilize the
frequency. The enhancing droop for the power reference of the SPC failed to be sufficient
and resulted in a slow frequency runaway. Instead, the synchronous power controller could
be interchanged with a pure droop when the islanded operation has been detected but
lacked the improved transient response of having an additional integrator in the loop. Yet,
tuning the filter time constant of the droop-based control loops showed that inertia could
be emulated without the need for the SPC. It was observed that the transition to islanded
droop control must occur within a few seconds after the breaker opening or before in order
to stabilize the frequency. Further, a secondary control should recover the frequency before
grid reconnection to limit large transients and oscillations.
The limitations of the model are the lack of seamless transitions when utilizing the
synchronous power controller, yet it is possible with pure droop control. Such a simple
control approach of a wind turbine is very limited in its operation compared to the
discussed enhanced performance, so a secondary controller must also adjust the droop
settings depending on variable active power production for a more extended model.
The possible improvements to the model include the necessary secondary control to adjust
the droop operating point and synchronize the grid’s voltage magnitude, voltage angle, and
frequency before reconnection to reduce transients during the transition between islanded
and grid-connected modes. Lastly, typologies with an extra integrator in the power loop are
available in the literature to improve the transient performance of a droop-based controller,
so this might be considered as well.
7.2
Conclusion
This thesis presented a comparative study of two grid-forming converter typologies and
the evaluation of small signal and transient responses during grid-connected and islanding
operations. The presented control typologies included outer loop controls to provide
the frequency and voltage references together with the inner loop of virtual admittance
and current control. Thus the emulation of a synchronous generator was utilized and
the combination of low inertia and high damping showed to be necessary for transient
stability. Additionally, the voltage at the POC was controlled by a simple integrator as a
proportional gain could induce unwanted oscillations. The active and reactive power loops
could be enhanced by simple droop control but the SPC showed to induce a frequency
runaway during islanding. Instead, a simple droop managed to stabilize the frequency with
a reduced operating range.
The control loops could be tuned to predefined stability margins with the aid of two
developed time-domain models in the abc and dq frames. The dq model had the advantage
of tuning with the Matlab Control toolbox. Hence the small signal stability could be
ensured by linearizing the dq model in Matlab to further tune the control parameters and
apply optimizing tools for the more complex inner loop. Thus the control loops became
robust to model uncertainties and disturbances.
The performance of the control loops was evaluated for grid-connected and islanded
conditions using time-domain simulations in Matlab Simulink for small signal and transient

responses utilizing the two developed models. This showcased advantages of the current
controller’s ability to limit current spikes and dampen oscillations induced by the converter
output filter and system non-linearities. The disadvantage of the current controller is the
slower response time and reduced bandwidth for load steps.
7.3
Future work
The work covered in this thesis included the investigation of well-established grid-forming
typologies and showed both pros and cons of using these. Yet different typologies could
be of interest, especially the concept of seamless transitions could be further investigated
as fast communication links might fail and system stability should remain if this is the
case. From the analysis of this thesis, some future work topics are listed below as possible
research directions.
• Seamless transition typology with automated secondary control. Design of secondary
control typology to adjust control loops to different grid states (i.e. faults, grid
synchronization, etc).
• Transient fault analysis for islanded operation. Investigation of transients of balanced
and unbalanced faults in an islanded microgrid.
• Cable resonance frequency damping investigation. Elimination of resonant oscillations
generated from HVAC cables.
• Oscillating modes between turbines of wind farm strings for different characteristic
impedance levels. What if the connected turbines’ power contribution or equivalent
impedance is unequal?
• Automated tuning tools and robust control structures. Can the tuning process of a
standard grid-forming typology be automated to predefined requirements?
• Micro-grid islanding operation with multiple vendors (i.e. BESS, WPP, PV, P2X,
etc). How can converters of different power ratings, types, and control typologies be
interconnected to ensure stability?
• Inter-converter oscillation study between two or more converters (i.e. mixed typologies
and power ratings). Harmonics stability in terms of X/R ratio, SCR, and tunable
controller parameters.
