ABSTRACT 
Background - Trichoderma aggressivum causes green mold disease in the edible mushroom, Agaricus 
bisporus. Its infection can hamper the formation of fruiting bodies and reduce crop yield in mushroom 
plantation. Recent reports exposed that artificial inoculation of Bacillus velezensis prevents A. bisporus 
yield reduction by T. aggressivum infection. We hypothesized that biocontrol activity of B. velezensis is 
influenced by the secondary metabolites of Bacilli. Since Bacilli are known for fungal hyphae attachment 
capability; it is possible that Bacilli can also attach on A. bisporus hyphae, and therefore bacterial biofilm 
on mycelia contributes to the microbial control agent efficacy.  
Methods - The antagonistic activity of Bacillus subtilis isolates and their secondary metabolite mutants 
against T. aggressivum is investigated with plate assays. Moreover, we co-cultivated B. subtilis and 
A. bisporus in liquid medium and visualized them under confocal laser scanning microscope to 
demonstrate B. subtilis’ hyphal attachment. Finally, we test a B. subtilis isolate and its selected secondary 
mutant (plipastatin) for their biocontrol potency against T. aggressivum using mushroom growing kits, in 
which biocontrol agent and infective agent is artificially introduced. With this mushroom kit test, we 
assess the biocontrol potency of selected B. subtilis strain, the effect of a secondary metabolite deletion 
on biocontrol potency, and we monitored, over time, the development of microbial population dynamics 
using selective plate assays 
Results - Absence of plipastatin in B. subtilis leads to hampered T. aggressivum antagonism. B. subtilis 
readily attaches to A. bisporus hyphae and the absence of biofilm structural genes (∆eps and ∆tasA) 
resulted in lack of hyphal attachment. The mushroom growing kit challenge test did not deliver conclusive 
results. 
Keywords: Trichoderma aggressivum, Bacillus subtilis, Agaricus bisporus, biocontrol, hyphal attachment.

INTRODUCTION 
Background and aim 
Agaricus bisporus, also known as white button mushroom, is one of the most cultivated edible mushrooms 
in the world. In 2013, its annual global production was projected to be around 4.38 million tons, with 
about a quarter of it being produced in Europe (1). As a dietary source, it is rich in riboflavin (vitamin B2), 
niacin (vitamin B3), and pantothenic acid (vitamin B5)(2). However, A. bisporus production is threatened 
by multiple pathogenic microorganism, including Trichoderma aggresivum, the causal agent of green mold 
disease (3).  
The symptoms of T. aggresivum infection on A. bisporus include green lesion and brown rust-colored spots 
in infected A. bisporus fruiting bodies. In severe cases, a T. aggressivum attack can prevent A. bisporus to 
develop fruiting bodies. Recently, its outbreak has been reported in Hungary. In a production facility, for 
example, a production loss of nearly 100% due to a T.  aggressivum attack has been reported (4). Notably, 
in May 2018, a closure of a Danish mushroom company due to ‘mold’ infection has been reported. The 
news outlet further described the mold as ‘impossible to remove’(5).  
Further, treating T. aggressivum infections seems to be a delicate matter since application of fungicides 
may hamper A. bisporus growth and could potentially make the mushroom unsafe for human 
consumption. As a result, few fungicides have been allowed to be used for mushroom cultivation. For 
instance, in Europe, there are prochloraz and metrafenone; and in North America are chlorothalonil, 
thiabendazole, and thiophanate-methyl approved (6). In addition, the problem of fungicide resistance also 
raises concern as mycopathogenic fungi, in theory, could develop resistance toward fungicides.  
Recently, Pandin et al. demonstrated the potential biocontrol properties of Bacillus velezensis QST 713. It 
is capable of mitigating T. aggressivum infection on A. bisporus, displayed in yield of the mushroom and 
reduction of T. aggressivum occurrence (7). Moreover, B. velezensis displayed the capability of attaching 
on A. bisporus carpophore and mycelia, which is hypothesized to influence B. velezensis biocontrol 
capacity (8, 9). B. velezensis QST 713, therefore, is proven to be potential to replace synthetic fungicides.   
However, in their paper, Pandin et al. (2019) expressed difficulties on transforming B. velezensis (9). In 
contrast, genetic engineering of Bacillus subtilis is more established. It is a gram-positive model organism 
for biofilm development, and its publication record is extensive (10). Moreover, B. subtilis might be an 
interesting candidate for biocontrol studies since: (a) it is capable in attaching to Aspergillus niger hyphae 
and forms interspecies biofilm, (b) it produces plethora of secondary metabolites which may hold

antagonistic properties toward T. aggressivum, and it is generally regarded as save (11, 12). B. subtilis 
could be pragmatically used, instead of B. velezensis, as mutants and transformation protocol are readily

In our study, we divided the study into three parts. 
1. Antagonism assay. It is aimed to screen which secondary metabolite plays prominent role on 
T. aggressivum inhibition. We employ a simple agar co-cultivation to observe antagonistic activity 
of B. subtilis and its secondary metabolite mutants.  
2. Hyphal attachment assay. It is aimed to test B. subtilis capability on attaching on A. bisporus 
hyphae and to see which cellular function is important for attachment. It is performed by liquid 
co-cultivation of A. bisporus and B. subtilis, followed by observation with Confocal Laser Scanning 
Microscope (CLSM).  
3. Mushroom kit challenge test. It is aimed to observe B. subtilis biocontrol properties by artificially 
inoculates mushroom growing kit with T. aggressivum and B. subtilis in a controlled experiment. 
 
Brief overview on A. bisporus: cultivation, microbial ecology, and 
interspecies interaction 
A. bisporus is typically grown on composted wheat, rye, paddy, or millet straw; supplemented with 
manure or synthetic fertilizer as nitrogen source, and gypsum to maintain pH balance (2).  Unlike some 
mushroom that could be grown axenically, such as Pleurotus, A. bisporus requires unsterilized composted 
substrate for growth (2, 13, 14). Without viable microbial biomass within the compost, the compost 
medium would lose selectivity towards A.  bisporus (15). Aside of its compost substrate requirement, 
cultivation of A. bisporus requires changes in temperature and aeration and addition of a so-called ‘casing 
soil’ (2). This made cultivation of A. bisporus a relatively complicated and lengthy process. The simplified 
explanation of A. bisporus cultivation could be described as follow.  
 
The first phase. The straw substrate is watered and fermented in aerobic composting. In this process, the 
compost temperature would rise to 80°C. Thermophilic microorganism dominate, and the preliminary 
degradation process begin. The whole process could take from 6 to 14 days  (2, 13).   
The second phase. In this phase, substrate is pasteurized at 60-65°C for two days, followed by conditioning 
at 48-51°C for two days within a controlled aerobic environment.  Rapid cellulose and hemicellulose 
degradation are happening due to microbial activity. Ammonia, which is disadvantageous to A. bisporus 
==PAGE:8==

5 
 
growth is assimilated by microorganism. Nematodes and insects were killed due to temperature. At the 
end of the conditioning step, only 40-50 % of cellulose and hemicellulose remain, and A. bisporus spawn 
could be introduced (2, 13, 16). Some authors marked the addition of A. bisporus as ‘the third phase’ (14, 
17). After the spawn is introduced, it will take approximately two weeks for the mycelia to fully colonize 
the substrate (7, 13). In this phase, temperature is maintained, approximately at 25°C under high humidity 
and high CO2 environment (2, 7, 13).  
Casing. When the mycelia have fully colonized the compost, a layer of peat moss called ‘the casing’ is laid 
on top of the compost. The addition of casing is meant to provide humidity and microorganism that are 
beneficial for mushroom growth (2). After a few days after the casing soil is added, the temperature is 
decreased, and aeration is increased to reduce CO2 and humidity level (2, 13). This change of cultivation 
condition would trigger primordia (pinhead) formation (13). Without casing layer and changes in culture 
condition, fruiting body would not be formed (2, 13, 18).  
Pinning and Flushes. After a few days after casing and temperature reduction, pinheads (mushroom 
primordia) would appear. These pinheads would later develop into full fruiting bodies. Each wave of 
fructification would be called ‘flush’ Normally, a round of mushroom cultivation is consisted into three 
flushes, with later flushes normally yielding less than earlier flushes and are more susceptible to pathogen 
attack (2, 19).

Due to changes in the micro-environmental condition within mushroom compost, microbial succession 
occur, and population dynamics comes into play (Figure 1). Some members of the community have been 
identified to play an important role in A. bisporus growth and development.  Microorganism an affect 
A. bisporus in several ways: they may act as nutrition source for A. bisporus, they helps A. bisporus to 
degrade compost substrate, they could facilitate development of primordia, and they can act as pathogen 
of A. bisporus (13, 14).  
 
As stated earlier, sterilization of phase II compost would lead to loss of selectivity toward A. bisporus. 
Apparently, this is because A. bisporus grows and thrive on microbial biomass, and this presence of 
microbial biomass seems to promote A. bisporus growth while discouraging the growth of competing 
Ascomycota (20, 21).  Among many microorganisms that acts as A. bisporus feed, Mycothermus 
thermophilus is arguably the most important as it is the best-studied (13). It is abundant in phase II 
compost (7, 22), and its degradative capability seems prominent as 60 different cellulases are produced 
by the fungi (23). M. thermophilus has been demonstrated to be decomposed in presence of A. bisporus 
– either in agar experiment (24) or by observing its decrease in relative abundance from amplicon 
sequencing (7). Some studies have attempted to apply M. thermophilus to mushroom compost to enhance 
A. bisporus growth with some observed success (13). However, in absence of M. thermophilus, addition 
of other fungal species resulted also in A. bisporus growth promotion (20).   
 
Moreover, microorganisms within the casing soil plays an essential role on A. bisporus primordia 
formation as application of sterile casing soil yields no primordia. Some studies have attempted to 
investigate the mechanism of primordia stimulation. For example, Pseudomonas putida has been found 
to stimulate formation of primordia in A. bisporus. However, its stimulation property is lost when ACC 
deaminase gene is knocked out (25). In an agar experiments, co-cultivation of A. bitorquis and P. putida 
resulted in primordia formation (26). Microorganisms found in casing soil have been shown to degrades 
1-octen-3-ol that is produced by A. bisporus, this volatile compound inhibits formation of fruiting bodies 
and contribute toward the flavor of the mushroom (18). Aside of having positive impact on mushroom 
development, microorganisms from the genus Pseudomonas might also be pathogenic toward A. bisporus 
(Figure 2). For example, P. tolasi which cause brown blotch disease in mushrooms (27). Interestingly, 
fungal attachment of P. putida and P. tolasi on A. bisporus have been documented (Figure 2).

mushroom disease. Their mechanism of action is thought to be influenced by their secondary metabolite 
(31).

Trichoderma aggressivum as causative agent of green mold disease 
When growing button mushrooms, there is a risk that the crop would be infected by green mold disease. 
Green mold disease has been occurring throughout North America and Europe in the 1980s and 1990s 
(19, 32). This disease is characterized by appearance of green mold in fungal bed which resulted in loss of 
mushroom production. Initially, four-strains of T. harzianum, which are associated with green mold 
disease, has been identified. They are named Th1, Th2, Th3, and Th4, respectively. However, upon closer 
examination, only two (Th2 and Th4) is particularly aggressive toward A. bisporus, the rest (Th1 and Th3) 
does not yield noticeable effect when inoculated into mushroom bed (3). Subsequently, after intensive 
morphological (Figure 3) and genetic characterization, Samuels et al. (2005) re-classify Th2 and Th4 as 
Trichoderma aggressivum f. europaeum and Trichoderma aggressivum f. aggressivum, respectively. The 
name aggressivum signifies their aggressivity toward white button mushroom. One of them is an epidemic 
in Europe, so the name europaeum; and the other one is North America. However, infections of 
mushroom bed by T. aggressivum f. aggressivum has been recently reported in Europe (Figure 4). In a 
Hungarian mushroom farm, Hatvani et al. (2017) reported an occurrence of green mold disease by 
T. aggressivum f. aggressivum. They described that the entire mushroom bed was covered by green mold, 
and the fungal attack resulted in nearly complete loss of mushroom formation. Isolation, molecular 
characterization, 
and 
introduction 
to 
mushroom 
bed 
confirmed 
the

of 
T. aggressivum f. aggressivum as the infecting fungi (33).

The mechanism of T. aggressivum pathogenicity toward A. bisporus has been broadly studied throughout 
the decades. Multiple studies adopting multiple approaches have demonstrate clues on ‘how and why 
T. aggressivum is pathogenic to A. bisporus’. However, it is hugely apparent that it is very difficult to single 
out one factor of pathogenicity. For instance, Savoie et al. (2001) compared Th2 (now T. aggressivum f. 
europaeum) and a non-Th2 strains of Trichoderma (which were non-pathogenic) in their enzymatic 
activities and (b) competition against bacterial isolates (from mushroom compost). The result showed that 
pathogenic and non-pathogenic Trichoderma strains displayed comparable profiles of enzymatic activities 
(with and without A. bisporus challenge); whereas Th2 exhibit better resistance against naturally occurring 
bacterial isolates. The prior result suggests that T. aggressivum pathogenicity is not because of specific 
ability in producing compost degrading enzymes, but more influenced by tolerance toward bacterial 
inhibitory effects (34). Additionally, Krupke et al. (2003) isolated two compounds from T. aggressivum, 
which displayed inhibitory properties against A. bisporus.  One of them is an unidentified compound 
extracted from the T. aggressivum infected compost, and the other one is isolated from T. aggressivum 
grown in Malt Extract Broth (MEB). The latter compound is identified as 3,4-dihydro-8-hydroxy-3-methyl-
isocoumarin (with lactone moiety).  Interestingly, when T. aggressivum extracts were tested for inhibitory 
properties, the non-pathogenic Th1 are more potent than of the pathogenic Th4 in inhibiting A. bisporus. 
In their conclusion, Krupke et al. (2003) expressed suspicion that lactonase from naturally occurring 
bacteria such as Bacillus might play a role in controlling adverse effects of T. aggressivum (35).  
 
Another finding by Flores et al. (2009) pointed out that co-cultivation of A. bisporus with Trichoderma 
species resulted in enhanced production of laccase in A. bisporus. Lacasse is secreted by A. bisporus to

degrade lignin, but it also plays a role in mushroom defense against environmental stress, such as toxic 
secondary metabolite  (36). Further, Sjaarda et al. (2015) demonstrated a notably comprehensive work 
on T. aggressivum toxicity on A. bisporus. They have shown that the toxic extract of T. aggressivum 
inhibits growth of A. bisporus, and this toxicity is mitigated by pre-incubation of the extract with 
A. bisporus laccase.  Lacasse activity from the resistant brown mushroom (strain SB65) is higher than the 
non-resistant white mushroom (strain U1), both at basal level and when induced with T. aggressivum 
extract. When the expression of the two laccase gene (lcc1 and lcc2) of two of A. bisporus (U1 and SB65) 
is studied (in absence and presence of T. aggressivum); they found out that: (a) lcc2 is overexpressed 
under presence of T. aggressivum extract, and (b) the more resistant strain SB65 exhibit faster induction 
and higher expression of lcc2 (37).   
 
T. aggressivum infection spread efficiently as relatively small amounts of T. aggressivum conidia are 
capable to induce decrease in mushroom production. Multiple studies has employed artificial inoculation 
of T. aggressivum into mushroom compost wherein low level of T. aggressivum inoculum still 
demonstrated reasonable loss on crop yield (7, 17). Furthermore, the period in which the spores 
contaminated the compost bed and the intensity of compost mixing contribute toward severity: the 
earlier the exposure in the production cycle, and the more thorough the mixing contributes, the higher 
the infection severity in terms of yield and symptoms (17).  
 
Also, cell wall degrading enzymes might plays a role in T. aggressivum pathogenicity. Chitinase, 
endoglucanase, and protease are overexpressed when T. aggressivum is co-cultivated with A. bisporus, 
both on transcription level (38), and protein level (34). More, it seems important to mention that, 
T. aggressivum to thrives in presence of A. bisporus, as they demonstrate weak growth in compost (35) – 
which hint their specialization as white button mushroom pathogen. Addition of A. bisporus exudate 
promote growth of T. aggressivum. f. europaeum (Th2), whereas the Trichoderma strains Th1 and Th3 are 
inhibited (39). Interestingly, the volatile metabolites produced by the non-pathogenic Trichoderma Th1 
and Th3 are more toxic toward T. aggressivum than the ones produced by Th 2, which corroborate with 
previously mentioned finding by Krupke et al. (2003) (35).  
 
Deducing from the literature, we hypothesized that T. aggressivum is more fit to grow in mushroom 
compost than non-pathogenic strain of Trichoderma. This fitness was influenced by multiple factors – and 
the environmental condition of the mushroom compost provide an ‘exact match’ that contributes toward

the thriving of T. aggressivum.  Multiple factors might come into play, such as (a) resistance of 
T. aggressivum against bacterial antagonism, (b) toxin and cell wall degrading enzyme production by 
T. aggressivum, and (c) growth induction of T. aggressivum by A. bisporus exudate. This specificity is 
demonstrated by T. aggressivum capability on hampering A. bisporus hyphal extension on axenic 
micromodel (9) – meaning that in absence of microorganism other than A. bisporus and T. aggressivum,

Bacillus subtilis as biofilm-forming biocontrol agent 
The gram-positive, spore-forming bacteria, and soil-dwelling B. subtilis has been extensively studied as a 
model organism to explore biological mechanism of spore formation. More, B. subtilis can form air-surface 
and air-liquid biofilms (10) and produces a multitude of secondary metabolites as around 5% of its genome 
is dedicated for secondary metabolite production (12). Within a biofilm, B. subtilis could undergone 
phenotypic specialization and attaches itself on plant roots or fungal hyphae, forming an interspecies 
biofilm (11, 40). This microorganism has a long history of studies (which dates back from 1870) (10), and 
still, novel discovery kept being published (41, 42). Application-wise, the bacteria is also widely 
researched, either as biocontrol agent (43, 44), enzyme producer (45), or probiotics (46).  
 
 
Figure 5. Illustration of B. subtilis biofilm composition (47) which depict that isogenic B. subtilis species may differentiate 
phenotypically. The BslA protein coats B. subtilis biofilm and the TasA amyloid fibers give structure to the biofilm.  
 
B. subtilis biofilm is composed of cells (either dead, vegetative, or spore) and extracellular matrix 
(Figure 5) (48). The extracellular matrix, which acts as a glue that binds the cell together, is mainly 
composed of two structural proteins (BslA and TasA) and extracellular polysaccharide (EPS). EPS is 
synthesized by the product of epsABCDEFGHIJLMNO operon (further abbreviated into eps operon), 
==PAGE:15==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
12 
 
whereas TasA and BslA is synthesized by tapA-sipW-tasA and bslA operon respectively. TasA protein and 
EPS provided structure toward B. subtilis biofilm, and absence of one of them would result in a defective 
biofilm (showed in flat colonies and thin pellicles) (10). BslA, which is found in biofilm surface, acts as a 
‘raincoat’ that provided hydrophobicity to the biofilm (10, 49).  
 
Regulation of B. subtilis biofilm formation is very complex as multiple pathways, and regulation strategies 
came into play. In this discussion, for the sake of simplicity, the discussion was narrowed to the cellular 
functions, which seems relevant for our study (Figure 6). Master regulator Spo0A, which regulates 
expression of more than 100 genes plays a pivotal role (10). Spo0A acts somewhat like a valve, when it is 
phosphorylated in a low level, the extracellular matrix gene expression is turned on; whereas, in high level, 
sporulation genes are turned on (50). Phosphorylation of Spo0A is influenced by external factors, such as 
respiration, glycerol, manganese, L-malic acid from plant roots, and surfactin (more below) (10, 51). 
Furthermore, Spo0A-P activates the expression of SinI. It acts as an anti-repressor of SinR. When SinI is 
bound to SinR, the later protein releases its repression of the matrix gene. SinR represses expression of 
tasA and eps directly and bslA indirectly (10, 48, 51).  
 
The expression of matrix gene is reinforced by SlrR. Akin to SinI, SlrR is also act as an anti-repressor to 
SinR. SinR, which represses the slrR gene, will begin to release its repression when it is bound to SinI. Then, 
SlrR would bind to SinR, reinforcing its expression (acting in double-negative feedback loop) as well as the 
expression of matrix genes. SlrR-SinR complex represses the expression of motility gene (hag) and 
autolysin gene, which contribute toward cell separation (lytABC and lytF) (10, 48, 51). 
 
As noted above, the secondary metabolite surfactin plays a role in regulating matrix production in 
B. subtilis. Surfactin incite phosphorylation of KinC by creating potassium leakage. KinC, in turn, relay its 
phosphorylation to Spo0A-P and thereby activating matrix gene expression. Interestingly, only a fraction 
the B. subtilis population produces surfactin within biofilms. Surfactin producing cells does not produce 
matrix and matrix producing cells which receive surfactin signals do not produce surfactin, resulting in a 
sort of division of labor (10, 48, 51).

Interestingly, B. subtilis is capable of colonizing plant roots and fungal hyphae, and this colonization ability 
is intertwined with biofilm formation. In laboratory settings, B. subtilis readily attaches on 
Arabidopsis thaliana plant roots and Aspergillus niger hyphae (11, 40). This attachment is absent when 
B. subtilis is deficient in matrix production (as a result of tasA and eps mutation) (4; Kjeldgaard, 
unpublished data). Furthermore, the root colonization capability makes B. subtilis an interesting 
candidate in plant protection. B. subtilis can induce systemic resistance in plants and make them more 
tolerant to pathogen attacks (10). Conversely, in A. bisporus, similar attachment of Bacillus velezensis has 
been documented (8), and its biocontrol capability (against T. aggressivum) has been demonstrated (7). 
This raises question if Bacillus biocontrol properties on mushrooms is influenced by its biofilm-forming 
capacity.   
 
B. subtilis and members of the B. subtilis group produces plethora of secondary metabolites with 
antimicrobial properties. Inhibitory effects of the secondary metabolites have been studied for decades. 
==PAGE:17==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
14 
 
The structure and the properties of secondary metabolites is diverse, but mostly they are antimicrobial 
peptides and polyketides (12). Aside from being antimicrobial compounds, the secondary metabolites are 
also utilized by Bacillus to communicate and to regulate their cellular functions (10). In our study, we 
tested three secondary metabolites (bacillaene, plipastatin, and surfactin) for their inhibitory effect 
against T. aggressivum. The characteristic of tested secondary metabolites and their impact on B. subtilis 
is briefly described below. 
 
Bacillaene is a chemically unstable polyketide which is produced by B. subtilis 3610. It is synthesized by 
the product of the pksX operon (≈80 kb) (52) and inhibits protein synthesis of prokaryotes but not of 
eukaryotes (53). Moreover, bacillaene has been showed to protect B. subtilis from predation by 
Myxococcus xanthus; and interestingly, Escherichia coli that is treated with bacillaene also displays 
resistance to predation by M. xanthus (54). Bacillaene also inhibits the growth of Streptomyces avertimilis 
(52). 
 
Plipastatin, which is chemically similar to fengycin, is a non-ribosomal polypeptides (NRP) with ten amino 
acids and a β-hydroxy fatty acid ‘tail’ attached to the N-terminus of the polypeptide. This compound is 
widely known for its antifungal properties (55). Alike to other NRPs, plipastatin is synthesized in an 
assembly line fashion by a modular non-ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS) (12, 55). Plipastatin is 
synthesized by NRPs encoded by the ppsABCDE operon (≈38 kb) (56). Plipastatins antifungal activity has 
been displayed against the plant pathogen Fusarium oxysporum and Botrytis cinerea (57, 58).  
 
Surfactin is arguably the most studied secondary metabolite from B. subtilis. Surfactin is a cyclical non-
ribosomal polypeptides (NRP), with seven amino acids and a 3-hydroxy fatty acid. It is synthesized by the 
product of the srfABCD operon (≈25 kb). This compound exhibit antibacterial and antifungal properties 
(59, 60).  Surfactin aids surface spreading of B. subtilis by lowering the surface tension of surrounding 
fluids (61, 62). Its role as communication compound is also prominent. Aside of influencing biofilm 
formation, surfactin mediates species-specific biofilm formation within different Bacillus species. For 
example, B. atrophaeus respond differently to surfactin A and surfactin C (63). Furthermore, surfactin 
seems to play a prominent role in plant protection by B. subtilis. In one instance, surfactin mediates 
B. subtilis attachment on melon leaves and at the same time, protects it from Pectobacterium 
carotovorum and Podosphaera fusca (64).

Microorganism and culture condition. B. subtilis and B. velezensis strains were stored in 25% glycerol 
stock. Prior application, B. subtilis was grown in 3 mL of Lysogeny-Broth (LB-Lennox, Carl Roth 10 g/L 
tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, 5 g/L NaCl) overnight (14-17 h)  37°C; 225 rpm. B. subtilis strain was 
preserved at -80°C in 1.8 mL-cryotubes containing 1,3 mL overnight B. subtilis LB-culture and 500 μL 
glycerol 86 %. T. aggressivum was maintained in PDA plate incubated in room temperature. Before spore 
harvesting, T. aggressivum was subcultured on a PDA plate and incubated for 7-14 days.  
 
Antagonism assay of B. subtilis against T. aggressivum. Six strains of B. subtilis with their corresponding 
secondary metabolite mutants (sfp, srfAC, ∆ppsC, ∆pksL) were selected for antagonism assay against 
T. aggressivum by growing them together on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA) plates. Frozen glycerol stock of 
T. aggressivum was scratched on the surface with sterile tips and then suspended in 60 µL of saline tween. 
Subsequently, 5 µL of spore suspension was pipetted into the center of a PDA 90 mm agar plate. 
Furthermore, 5 µL overnight culture of B. subtilis grown in LB medium was spotted approximately 2 cm 
away from the center. The plate was then dried and incubated at room temperature (20-25°C) for six days. 
Antagonism properties of B. subtilis against T. aggressivum were measured qualitatively by the size of the 
clear zone between the colony of B. subtilis and T. aggressivum. 
 
Hyphal colonization assay of A. bisporus. Three-weeks old A. bisporus culture, grown on PDA plates was 
wetted with 10 mL of physiological saline (0.9 % NaCl) and then scraped thoroughly with agar spreader. 
Afterward, 1 mL of the hyphal suspension is pipetted into 25 mL Potato Dextrose Broth (PDB) and are 
incubated at 25°C for 8-14 days without agitation. After eight days, pellets of A. bisporus macro-colonies 
are floating within the medium. One macro-colony is pipetted into each well in a 24-well plates, have the 
leftover PDB removed, washed once with physiological saline, and finally, each well is supplemented with 
1 mL of modified LB medium (100 ml LB medium supplemented with 1 mL of MgSO4 1 M and 100 µL of 
MnCl4 0.1 M). Afterward, within each well, 10 µL of overnight culture of B. subtilis (single or mixed culture) 
is pipetted. The plate was incubated inside a rotary shaker (25°C, 170 rpm, 22-24 h). Subsequently, 
A. bisporus macro-colonies were washed with sterile physiological saline three times and are fixed into 
microscope slides for visualization with CLSM (n = 3-5).   
 
Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy. Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (CLSM) was employed in 
hyphal attachment assay and visualization of mushroom carpophore. TCS SP8 Leica (equipped with a Plan-

Image processing and quantification for hyphal attachment assay. Image processing was performed 
using software ImageJ. For display, a single picture was chosen from the entire stack for the brightfield

with maximum intensity. For quantification, all of the images from the image stack within RFC and GFC 
are stacked into one image using ‘Z project’ function with maximum intensity. Afterward, the images have 
their contrast enhanced with find edges function before converted into binary images. Finally, using stack 
statistic function, the relative frequency is calculated by dividing the % volume value to the total sum of 
% volume of RFC and GFC.

buffer (pH 7.4) before being frozen at -20°C for one day. The next day, the spore suspension was thawed 
and washed twice and resuspended with sporulation buffer. Before inoculation, the spore concentration 
was determined with serial dilution on LB agar plates. The dose of B. subtilis spore was set to 2×106 
spores/g topsoil.

Attachment assay of B. subtilis on A. bisporus hyphae 
TasA and EPS as structural component of B. subtilis biofilm are crucial for attachment to A. bisporus. 
The aim of the hyphal attachment assay is to test if B. subtilis is capable of attaching on A. bisporus hyphae 
and to observe which cellular function of B. subtilis is essential for A. bisporus hyphal attachment. It is 
performed by growing B. subtilis together with A. bisporus in liquid medium for 24-h under shaken 
condition (see Materials and Methods). We reported that, based on CLSM visualization, ∆spo0A (Figure 
8B), ∆eps (Figure 8C), and ∆tasA (Figure 8D) mutant displays hampered colonization, whereas ∆bslA 
(Figure 8E) and ∆hag (Figure 8F) retains its colonization capability. We did not detect physiological changes 
in A. bisporus as a result of co-cultivation with B. subtilis (Figure S3B). Some of the B. subtilis cell clump is 
detected in the tip of protruding hyphae (Figure 8E and Figure 8F), whereas some are attaching in the 
neck of the A. bisporus hyphae (Figure 8A). Furthermore, we noticed that certain B. subtilis cells are 
attaching to A. bisporus hyphae in a compact formation (Figure 8A, 1E, and 1F), while others are arranged 
more loosely (Figure S3).  
 
Complementation assay of two B. subtilis strain for hyphal attachment. Next, to observe how the wild 
type (WT), ∆tasA and ∆eps would complement each other within the hyphal biofilm, we performed 
attachment assays of mixed cultures of B. subtilis. Two O/N culture of B. subtilis with GFP or RFP 
fluorophores were mixed (1:1 ratio) before being inoculated with A. bisporus as described in Figure 10. 
WT cells allow incorporation of ∆spo0A, ∆tasA, and ∆eps; whereas ∆tasA and ∆eps complement each 
other in forming functional hyphal biofilm (Figure. 10). Moreover, from the complementation assay, we 
noticed a pattern that ∆eps are less numerous relative to WT (Figure 10D and 2E) and ∆tasA (Figure 10A 
and 2B) when mixed. Hence, to get a more definite conclusion on which strain have more proportion 
within hyphal biofilm, we measured the relative frequency of GFP and RFP labeled strains using ImageJ 
(Figure 11; see Materials and Method). The image quantification displayed that in hyphal biofilm, ∆eps 
strain is less abundant than of WT and ∆eps; whereas ∆tasA is more abundant than of WT and ∆eps (Figure 
11). Thus, the result indicates that ∆eps attaches less readily to the hyphal biofilm than of their WT and 
∆tasA counterpart.

DISCUSSION 
Antagonism assay of B. subtilis against T. aggressivum 
The antagonism assay displayed that absence of Sfp would result in loss of antagonistic activity toward 
T. aggressivum (Figure S1). This is true for all of our six tested B. subtilis strains. The gene sfp encodes 
phophopantetheinyl-transferase which is required for production of surfactin (56), plipastatin/fengycin 
(66), bacillaene (54), and possibly more secondary metabolites. Moreover, further results demonstrated 
that loss of plipastatin hampers the antagonistic activity of B. subtilis (Figure 8). Whereas strains which 
lose surfactin and bacillaene retain their respective antagonistic activity toward T. aggressivum. In 
comparison with our group’s observation (Kiesewalter and Kovács; unpublished data), the result 
corroborates, as loss of plipastatin/fengycin deliver large impact on antifungal properties of B. subtilis 
(measured by antagonism assays against Botrytis cinerea, Fusarium graminearum, and Fusarium 
oxysporum). As for MB9_B4 and MB9_B6, which display weaker inhibition compared to other strains, they 
do not produce surfactin or plipastatin, respectively (Kiesewalter and Kovács; unpublished data).  
 
Plipastatin, which shares many similarities with fengycin, has been displayed in the literature to pose 
inhibitory properties toward plant pathogenic fungi. Plipastatin can halt germination of B. cinerea conidia 
and reduce disease severity in tomato leaves infected by B. cinerea (57). More, the exposure of plipastatin 
caused membrane and actin cytoskeleton disruption on F. oxysporum (56, 67). Growth inhibition of 
F. graminearum by plipastatin A has also been recorded (58). The antagonistic properties of plipastatin or 
fengycin, as well as its capability to induce systemic resistance in the plants, made B. subtilis as its 
producer an interesting candidate in plant biocontrol (68). 
 
The mechanism in which plipastatin/fengycin induce fungal death could be described as follow: the 
peptide portion inserts to the cell membrane, and if the NRP reached high enough concentration, pores 
would appear within the cell membrane prompting efflux of cellular content (56). In Fusarium 
gramineorum, exposure of fungi with plipastatin A resulted in vacuolization, creation of bubble-like 
structure in the tip of the hyphae, as well as disruption in structural integrity of the conidia (58).  
 
Attachment assay of B. subtilis on A. bisporus hyphae 
As a soil microorganism, Bacillus species would probably undergone interaction with filamentous fungi, 
either with Ascomycetes or with Basidiomycetes. One of the intriguing aspects of B. subtilis cross-species 
==PAGE:38==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
35 
 
interaction is its capacity on forming an intraspecies biofilm. Previous report has demonstrated that 
B. velezensis attaches to A. bisporus carpophore and soil entrenched mycelium (8, 9).  Furthermore, 
B. subtilis have been displayed to be able to colonize A. niger hyphae, in both liquid and solid medium, 
but the attachment is reported absent in A. oryzae and heat-treated cells of A. niger (11). Similar 
intraspecies colonization of B. subtilis is also observed in plant roots (40, 69, 70).  
 
More, it is apparent that biofilm forming capacity is crucial for B. subtilis intraspecies colonization, as 
∆tasA, ∆eps, and ∆spo0A fails to colonize A. thaliana roots (40) and A. niger hyphae (Kjeldgaard et al., 
unpublished data).  TasA (structural protein) and EPS serves as two major component of B. subtilis biofilm, 
an absence of one of the component would result in defective biofilm (6, 7). Spo0A, on the other hand, is 
a master regulator of sporulation in B. subtilis. A B. subtilis mutant defective in Spo0A could not form 
spores or biofilm (10, 40). Motility seems to be not crucial for A. bisporus hyphal colonization as ∆hag 
mutant readily attaches to A. bisporus hyphae. The shaken condition of the cultivation could be 
considered as B. subtilis could randomly come into physical contact with A. bisporus, attaches to its 
hyphae, and subsequently, form biofilm. Furthermore, BslA, a protein that coats B. subtilis biofilm and 
gives it a hydrophobic property (49), are also not crucial for hyphal colonization. Like in A. bisporus, both 
∆hag and ∆bslA mutant retain hyphal colonization in A. niger (Kjeldgaard et al.; unpublished data). 
 
In present study, we did not detect morphological changes in A. bisporus as a result of co-cultivation with 
B. subtilis (Figure S1B). In contrast, such changes are detected in A. niger, where bulbous cells are formed 
as a result of co-cultivation with B. subtilis (Kjeldgaard et al.; unpublished data). The morphological 
changes in A. niger are thought to be influenced by surfactin because such changes are absent when 
A. niger is co-cultivated with surfactin deficient B. subtilis (Kjeldgaard et al.; unpublished data). Notably, 
surfactin gene (srfA) was displayed to be downregulated within the case of co-cultivation of B. subtilis 
with A. niger (11); and are upregulated in B. velezensis when it is co-cultivated with A. bisporus or 
T. aggressivum (9).  
 
Moreover, as noted previously, B. subtilis biofilm component are composed of EPS, and TasA protein, 
B. subtilis, which are missing EPS, and TasA component will yield defective biofilm. However, when ∆tasA 
and ∆eps are mixed, functionality of biofilm is restored. This is true for the case of floating pellicle and 
root biofilm (40, 70). Our recent data hinted that ∆tasA and ∆eps complementation also works within the 
case of A. niger hyphal attachment (unpublished data). Interestingly, we discovered uniformity of ∆tasA 
==PAGE:39==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
36 
 
vs. ∆eps relative frequency in pellicle, root colonizing biofilm, and A. bisporus biofilm – all of them yielded 
of ∆tasA vs. ∆eps ratio of approximately 7:3 (70). We would speculate that the relative low abundance of 
∆eps is due to its difficulty of integrating into the biofilm since both strain (∆tasA and ∆eps) has been 
shown to have comparable relative fitness in a condition where matrix component are produced but are 
not essential (70). Alternatively, it is due to protein TasA which is easier to share than of EPS (70), further 
research is necessary.  
 
As a mushroom growing on non-sterile substrate, A. bisporus growth might be influenced by surrounding 
microorganism, among them are bacteria from the genus Bacillus (13, 14). However, knowledge regarding 
how A. bisporus and Bacilli (especially B. subtilis) interact is limited. Feeding most probably be one of the 
forms in which the two microorganisms interact, as A. bisporus has been known to be able to consume 
various microorganisms present within compost (13). Specifically, A. bisporus can mineralize dead 
B. subtilis cells and use it as carbon and nitrogen source (71). Multitude studies have documented the 
presence of bacteria from the genus Bacillus in mushroom compost (6, 13, 14, 16, 29, 31), indicating their 
presence among mushroom habitat is relatively standard. Also, some studies have demonstrated that 
addition of Bacilli into the compost could protect A. bisporus from T. aggressivum attack, without 
noticeable impact on mushroom growth and quality (6, 43).   
 
Regarding exposure of fungi, biofilm-related gene expression in Bacillus species might become altered. 
Pandin et al. (2019) has reported that the expression of tapA and eps operon in B. velenzensis (grown in 
compost) is relatively unchanged in presence of A. bisporus and T. aggressivum relative to that of grown 
in broth extract in exponential phase (without fungal co-cultivation)(9). Also, Benoit et al. (2015) did not 
detect noticeable changes in biofilm-related gene expression when B. subtilis is co-cultivated with A. niger 
in liquid medium (11). However, as a contrast, Khezri et al. (2017) displayed increased expression of tasA 
and decreased expression of sinR, when B. subtilis is co-cultivated with Fusarium culmorum (72).   
 
Application-wise, a question was raised, “Does biofilm influence B. subtilis capacity as biocontrol agent of 
A. bisporus.” Most studies about B. subtilis biocontrol seems to tackle the problem from secondary 
metabolite viewpoints, and seldom test the influence of biofilm on biocontrol potency. A Recent report 
indicates that extracellular matrix protects B. subtilis from colony invasion of Pseudomonas chloroapis. 
The matrix seems to act as a line of defense of B. subtilis by preventing cell to cell contact, reducing the 
killing facilitated by tubular protein T6SS held by P. chloroapis. Should the biofilm fail, or non-existent, 
==PAGE:40==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
37 
 
B. subtilis would sporulate (73). The report seems relevant with our study since Pseudomonas is prevalent 
on A. bisporus compost (13). Challenge tests of biocontrol efficacy about B. subtilis biofilm have also been 
performed, for instance, Chen et al. (2013) has reported that B. subtilis mutant defective in biofilm 
formation (∆tasA and ∆eps) displayed reduced efficacy in protecting tomato plant from bacterial wilt 
disease, induced by Ralstonia solanacearum (69). Also, the finding by Yang s(2018) supported the previous 
report. They have found that inhibition of biofilm formation, mediated by Cd2+ ions, resulted in decreased 
biocontrol efficacy against R. solanacearum (74). 
 
In light of our study, it seems interesting to introduce a biocontrol challenge test on white button 
mushroom pathogenesis setting (alike the one performed in our study) which examine the effect of 
extracellular matrix in biocontrol potency. We hypothesized that Bacillus biofilm, should it be existing in 
compost environment, contributes towards its overall fitness, and thereby biocontrol potency.   
 
For future work, more colonization assay experiments of B. subtilis on A. bisporus could be attractive. For 
instance, testing B. subtilis MB9_B1GFP colonization properties, to check if colonization is common in 
different B. subtilis strains. In other case, mixed culture colonization assay of ∆spo0A and ∆tasA-eps both 
with wild type would also be worthwhile to check incorporation of cheater (matrix nonproducer) into 
hyphal biofilm. Aside from colonization assay, it would be interesting to check if A. bisporus secretome 
(spent medium) hamper growth of B. subtilis, by agar assay or OD measurement for instance – and if the 
production of extracellular matrix influences B. subtilis survivorship.  Otherwise, should a high throughput 
screening system be available, checking multiple B. subtilis mutants for negative hyphal attachment could 
also be worthwhile.  
 
Mushroom growing kit challenge test 
Microbial enumeration results displayed that bacterial microflora grew up to the seventh day of the 
incubation period and stagnated afterward. This could be influenced by reduction of temperature on the 
eighth day, from 25°C to 15°C. Furthermore, the number of fungi was stagnating throughout the 
incubation period. The method of sampling could come into play, as the sample was taken from the top 
of casing soil where outgrowth of A. bisporus mycelium was absent. The sample taken might not be a good 
representation as the sample might misrepresent the casing soil microbiome. Moreover, based on the 
current selection method (heat treatment at 80°C for 25 min), the population composition of sporulating 
microorganism seemed to be dominated by B. subtilis MB9_B1GFP in T3 and T4 (Figure 1B and Figure S1). 
==PAGE:41==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
38 
 
Growth of sporulating microorganism was stagnating in T3 and T4 (Figure 1B), which raises questions on 
if B. subtilis MB9_B1GFP germinate or grow in mushroom casing soil.  
 
Further, microbial enumeration did not display outgrowth of T. aggressivum as displayed by Pandin et al. 
(2017), where outgrowth of fungi as a result of T. aggressivum introduction was displayed, especially in 
the fructification phase of A. bisporus cultivation. The ITS2 amplicon analysis supported prior results, 
displaying that T. aggressivum dominates the fungal community if inoculated into A. bisporus compost. 
This dominance of T. aggressivum is negated when B. velezensis QST713 is co-introduced into the casing 
soil.  
 
Microbial community, both in compost and casing soil, plays an important role in A. bisporus 
development, as the mushroom fails to develop in sterile environment. Casing soil host microorganism 
which are important for the development of A. bisporus fruiting body. Some member from Pseudomonas 
species found in casing soil helped degrading compounds, which inhibit primordia formation (14).  
 
The mushroom growing kit experiment is aimed to test B. subtilis MB9_B1 biocontrol potency against 
T. aggressivum. However, in order to do that, it is necessary to find the ‘correct’ growing condition where 
reliable outcome is produced. Artificial inoculation of T. aggressivum should be able to demonstrate a 
clear impact on A. bisporus – so that the effect of B. subtilis inoculation on the mushroom growing kit 
could be tested. Hence, we attempted optimization of experiment parameter in order to observe 
biocontrol potential (Table x, Figure 2, and Figure 3). In the first batch (Figure 2), it could be observed that 
some mushroom growing kit did not produce fruiting bodies, and the noninoculated control (T1) did not 
show a clear difference to the inoculated one (T2, T3, and T4). In the second batch (Figure 3), we obtained 
a somewhat more reliable growth of A. bisporus as we found more proportion of mushroom growing kit 
producing fruiting bodies. Unfortunately, the uninoculated control did not produced any mushrooms 
(Figure 3). In the third batch, a disease occurrence marked with green lesion on fruiting body and tissue 
necrosis is not found on noninoculated control (T1). We excluded one box from each treatment within 
the third batch due to overgrowth by gray mold fungus. The boxes, which displayed poor mushroom 
growth were incubated in different incubator at the early stage of its growth, which could explain poor 
growth on the previous batch.  
 
==PAGE:42==
Stevanus Aditya Listian – s171929 
 
 
39 
 
Artificial inoculation of T. aggressivum has been performed in several studies (17, 33, 43, 75, 76) involving 
their pathogenicity and their biocontrol. Many of them reported pronounced effect on A. bisporus as a 
result of T. aggressivum conidia introduction (17, 33, 43, 75, 76). However, in contrast to our study, the 
beforementioned studies (17, 33, 43, 75, 76) usually perform T. aggressivum introduction when 
A. bisporus spawn is introduced (the third phase) and not during casing phase such as the one performed 
in our study. Also, these studies (17, 33, 43, 75, 76) usually introduced T. aggressivum conidia toward 
A. bisporus spawned compost rather than in the casing soil. Hatvani et al. (2017) notably reported that 
the disease severity is bigger when T. aggressivum is introduced before the casing soil is introduced (33). 
More, it is also interesting to point out that the amount of conidia introduced within the reported (17, 33, 
43, 75, 76) studies is usually far below the ones employed in our studies – this could hint T. aggressivum 
employed in our study have inferior pathogenic effect.  
 
As for attempt to visualize B. subtilis MB9_B1GFP attachment on A. bisporus fruiting body with CLSM, we 
did not detect presence of B. subtilis MB9_B1GFP on A. bisporus fruiting body. Attachment of B. velezensis 
FZB42 on A. bisporus has been documented by Pandin et al. (2017) (fruiting body is immersed axenically 
with B. velezensis FZB42 (8). They also document attachment of Bacillus like and Pseudomonas like 
bacteria on A. bisporus carcophore as visualized with Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) (8).   
 
As a result of mushroom growing kit experiment trial, we noted several important parameters for lab-
scale mushroom cultivation, assuming similar experimental settings are adopted. First, we recommend to 
not compress the casing soil as it is very apparent that uncompressed casing soil is beneficial for mycelial 
expansion (Figure 2). Second, using identical incubator is beneficial for creating equal condition as 
mushroom growing is very sensitive to humidity and temperature difference (2). Shuffling the mushroom 
box position could be a recommended approach to mitigate the difference in humidity within incubator. 
Third, in terms of microorganism introduction, proper substrate-inoculum mixing is advised. In case of 
T. aggressivum introduction, it has been showed that the more thorough the mixing, the more 
pronounced the pathogenic effect (17). Proper mixing of substrate and inoculum could be improved by 
diluting the spore/conidia with more water as performed in the third batch (Table 3).

A working A. bisporus biocontrol platform in which B. subtilis and its mutants could be tested seems 
attractive. The platform would allow investigation on the influence of a given gene or cellular function on 
biocontrol potency. However, as noted above, our trial and error did not deliver conclusive result. There 
are room for improvement should a similar experiment is to be attempted in the future.  
Below described several suggestions for future improvement: 
• 
More replicate is necessary if one would reliably measure mushroom yield about microorganism 
introduction. From our results, it is very apparent that there are large variations in terms of 
mushroom size. If space would be the limiting factor, one could develop small boxes to grow 
A. bisporus.  
• 
In our experiment setting, mushroom growing kits are used. This is to cut incubation period since 
growing from compost would presumably take more time (due to A. bisporus spawn requiring 
time to colonize the whole compost substrate). If a more pronounced pathogenic effect is to be 
desired, T. aggressivum and B. subtilis could be applied during spawning phase. This would lead 
to (a) procuring A. bisporus spawn, compost substrate, nitrogen supplements, and casing soil; and 
(b) developing a novel mushroom growing system. 
• 
An alternative would be to develop axenic micromodel in which autoclaved compost is used as 
substrate, such as the one displayed by Pandin et al.  (9). The autoclaved compost is subsequently 
inoculated with A. bisporus colonized compost, or when required B. subtilis and/or 
T. aggressivum. In their report, Pandin et al. (2009)displayed diminished A. bisporus compost 
colonization as a result of T. aggressivum introduction (9).  
• 
Employing qPCR to monitor T. aggressivum and B. subtilis. This would be interesting to do since 
employing qPCR could, in theory, monitor added microorganism growth and survivorship within 
the mushroom substrate.  
• 
In regards to enhancing the effect of pathogenicity by T. aggressivum, an option is to acquire an 
aggressive pathogenic strain of T. aggressivum. Potentially isolated from infected mushroom bed 
or acquired from an external lab.

CONCLUSION 
 
The antagonism assay shows that absence of plipastatin in B. subtilis resulted in hampered T. aggressivum 
inhibition. Hyphal colonization assay shows that TasA and EPS, as biofilm structural component, is 
essential for B. subtilis attachment of A. bisporus hyphae under experiment condition. WT cells allow 
incorporation of biofilm deficient mutant. Whereas, ∆eps and ∆tasA (when mixed), complement each 
other and restore functionality of hyphal biofilm. During complementation assay, ∆eps are less abundant 
in the biofilm than of WT and ∆tasA. This suggest that ∆eps attach less readily on hyphal biofilm than their 
WT or ∆tasA counterpart. The result of mushroom growing kit challenge experiment is inconclusive. 
However, the trial and error yielded some directives on constructing biocontrol testing platform.
