Summary
This research explores the redesign of a self-tracking tool aimed at helping users
track and analyze symptoms over time. The focus of the tool was to assist therapists
and patients in understanding and interpreting mental-health data by providing an
intuitive, user-friendly interface. Through an iterative design process, key principles
from visualization research were applied to create a system that balances simplicity,
clarity, and eﬀiciency while ensuring ease of understanding for non-experts.
The final design integrated a custom visualization, such as a logbook for track-
ing phenomena and a calendar for tracking therapy sessions, addressing pain points
identified in user testing. The tool allowed users to compare current tracking periods
with previous averages, providing a clear and accessible overview of symptom trends.
The use of charts like line and spider graphs helped users identify patterns and track
changes over time, while the system’s aesthetic design was positively received for its
clarity and visual appeal.
Several challenges occurred, including the inability to establish meaningful corre-
lations between different tracked phenomena and the diﬀiculty of validating insights
with mock data.
The study concludes with suggestions for future work, including the introduction
of real-time data uploading, and further customization options for visualizations. Fu-
ture research should focus on exploring the role of therapists in the system, refining
data visualization strategies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
“Without data you’re just a person with an opinion.”
— W. Edwards Deming
A picture is worth a thousand words, and in the world of information, a well-
designed visualization allows data to tell its own story.
Every day, our population generates a vast portion of personal data through track-
ing applications, wearable devices or any digital monitoring health tools [1]. Since
data plays a key role in decision-making frameworks, data visualization became an
effective tool for making complex information more comprehensible, as visuals are
processed much faster by the brain than written text [2][3][4][5].
The field of data visualization has gained significant attention, particularly in the
healthcare industry. It enables more precise diagnoses, to identify patients at risk,
create personalized treatment processes, and improve the understanding of medical
outcomes [6][7][8][9][10].
By transforming raw data into actionable insights, data
visualization tools help professionals facilitate the identification of trends, outliers,
and patterns within complex datasets [11][12][13].
The importance of insights has also became particularly significant among self-
trackers, especially within the Quantified Self (QS) community [14]. It is not only
about the collection of personalized data but, more importantly, about drawing valu-
able conclusions from one’s tracking experience. Non-expert users play important
role in this process because they provide context to their own data, making it more
meaningful and enhancing their self-awareness [1][14].
However, self-trackers often create their own data visualizations, but for novices,
to design easy to interpret and insightful visuals can be challenging [14][15]. So far,
previous research on gaining insights has been explored mainly in the context of visu-
alization experts [16]. Many traditional visualizations are designed for audience with
technical experience with high level of data literacy, which makes it diﬀicult for non-
expert users to interpret. Even though, there is a growing number of visualizations
designed specifically for a non-tech saavy audience e.g. in journalism [3][17], many
people still have limited knowledge of data visualization and may struggle to accu-
rately understand the results [3][7]. In terms of the medical professionals, who are not
familiar with statistical visualizations, it can resulted in drawing wrong conclusions
from the patient’s data and create a potential harm to them or just simply delay

a treatment process [6]. There is currently lack of potential solutions for broader
audience with different professional experience or cultural background[3][18].
1.1
Problem statement
Larsen et al.
[19] introduced a one-button wearable device, that keeps the track
of the patient’s symptoms by logging the timestamped data. This data is then up-
loaded into a system where therapists can see the overview of the logged inputs in a
form of different visualizations. However, the current tool demands significant effort
from therapists, requiring extra preparation before sessions and challenging real-time
analysis during limited appointment times. As a result, it becomes more of an addi-
tional task rather than a time-saving resource. Similarly, patients face challenges in
interpreting the data and find it diﬀicult to engage with [20].
1.2
Research objectives
The project will explore visualization techniques on how to create effective, accessible,
and engaging data visualizations for the broader audience with the emphasis on the
non-expert users.
More precisely, how they can easily interpret and act on their
mental health data. By visualizing these patterns, individuals can better understand
and manage their mental well-being, aiding in self-analysis. The study aims also to
simplify the treatment process by enabling therapists to gain deeper insights into
patients’ mental states, accelerate their work and tailor treatment plans to be more
effective.
Therefore, a user-friendly prototype for therapists and patients will be designed.
Building on the existing platform, the new tool will try to address the stated prob-
lem and aim to bridge the gap between complex phenomena visualizations and user
comprehension by exploring techniques that help identify correlations and patterns in
patient data. Additionally, it will assess the cognitive impact of different visualization
types, evaluating how they influence user perception and interaction.
To achieve these objectives, this study investigates the following research ques-
tions:
• What are the key barriers to data interpretation for experts and users with low
data literacy?
• What different types of data visualizations can improve understanding for users?
• How does enhancing the visual appeal and usability of data visualizations affect
user engagement?

CHAPTER 2
Related work
“The purpose of visualization is insight, not pictures”
— Ben Shneiderman
With the growing interest in self-tracking and personalizing it to individual needs,
there has been an increased emphasis on effective interpretation of the data. This
chapter provides a comprehensive review of the existing literature on the challenges
faced by non-expert users in personalized data tracking and effective data visualiza-
tion guidelines.
2.1
Characteristic of non-expert user
Despite the growing number of tracking tools, significant gaps persist in understand-
ing user needs and challenges associated with data visualization [21].
Current vi-
sualization research often overlooks broader audiences and focus on a small group
instead, and assumes an idealized ’expert’ user who is WEIRD (Western, Educated,
Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic) [4].
Unlike the mentioned experts, these wider audiences have distinct differences in
their needs, including those related to disabilities, motivations, goals, usage contexts
and capabilities [22][23]. They frequently lacked education in numeracy and graphic
interpretation, came from varied cultural backgrounds and had limited experience
with visualizations [21].
Their understanding of the data visualizations, is highly dependent on their data
literacy [24]. Usually, they have general knowledge of data visualization types (e.g.
bar charts, line graphs, scatter plots) but have no professional experience in data re-
lated fields that allows them to construct visualizations and interpret them eﬀiciently
[25][14]. Therefore, the discovered insights might not be supported with evidence
resulting in misleading results [14]. According to Schneider [24] a poorly designed
visualization, different preferences or wrong chart selection can have negative effect
and can deter the users from using the tool further.
Moreover, some non-expert users may not be even interested in conducting in-
depth data analysis but instead just use data visualization for more casual purposes,
such as for entertainment, curiosity, or simply accessing the data quickly. Therefore,

all the mentioned aspects make it more challenging to create a generic solution for a
broader audience, that would give quick outcomes [5][23].
2.2
Data visualization among non-expert users
2.2.1
Current data visualization practices
Self-quantified users often use data visualizations to highlight meaningful data points,
such as extremes (maximum or minimum values) and references (e.g., time or loca-
tion), to convey insights from their personal data. These visualizations might also
include self-reflection, which helps users interpret their data through the incorpora-
tion of external contexts (such as e.g. vacation). Self-reflection can include confirming
existing knowledge, challenging it, or predicting future trends based on the data [26].
In personal data analysis, recognizing trends over time is a common insight, espe-
cially since much personal data is collected in a time series. They often describe trends
using terms like increase, decrease, or fluctuation, while some even note the absence
of change as a trend in itself. Comparison is another useful tool for understanding
personal data, whether by factors (e.g., using a drug vs. not), by time (e.g., weekdays
vs. weekends), or against external data (e.g., personal data vs. goals) [26][27].
Correlation is another type of insight, in which users observe clear associations
between two variables. In addition, often summaries, highlighting distributions, and
identifying outliers are provided in the data, sometimes explaining outliers with ex-
ternal contexts [26].
In terms of visualization, line charts are the most common, followed by bar charts
and scatterplots [15]. Visual annotations like labels, arrows, shapes, and trend lines
are frequently used to make insights clearer. These annotations can help highlight
trends, define ranges, and make the data more understandable. Despite the impor-
tance of these visual annotations, current tools do not always support them effectively.
Integrating basic statistical analysis into interactive systems could also help improve
understanding and comparison of data [26].
Choe et al. [28] pointed that one of the most common type of visualization non-
expert trackers used are custom visualizations. It is mostly due to the lack of the
existing tool that would support their needs.
However, there is no universal solution, as everyone has their own preferences and
experiences [24].
2.2.2
Challenges
Conducted studies of Oh and Lee [29] and Choe et al. [26] on users from the quanti-
fiedself.com website highlighted usability issues in data handling and visualization. A
key challenge is data interpretation. While most visualizations present factual data,

6
users often struggle to understand what actions to take. Expert guidance is especially
important for complex data [29].
User engagement is a significant factor in tool selection, where ”fun factors” and
motivation play key roles.
However, over-engagement can occur, so tools should
include clear guidelines for interpreting data and encourage behavioral change through
self-reflection [29].
Tufte pointed [27], that ”Allowing artist-illustrators to control the design and
content of statistical graphics is almost like allowing typographers to control the
content, style, and editing of prose”.
Unfortunately, in many cases, the emphasis
on aesthetics takes precedence over functionality and often prioritizes visual appeal
rather than clarity. As a result, the data lack a real value or it takes a great effort
to get useful insights. This is particularly noticeable across on-line platforms, where
visually stunning but generic graphs and dashboards often fail to convey meaningful
information [30].
However, software design and aesthetics, after all, also play a key role [29].
2.3
Guidelines for effective data visualization
Tufte [27], in his book, presented the key principles of data visualizations, which are:
graphical integrity, minimized data-ink ratio, minimized chartjunk, data density, data
over design variation, small multiples and context. They serve as the guidelines into
successful information design. It is often noticeable that traditional visualizations
lack the context [31].
He also explained the proper use of the color, as it can be your ally or worst
enemy [27][32]. A well-designed visualization uses color wisely, prioritizing perceptu-
ally balanced color scales and avoiding unnecessary complexity. It should minimize
visual clutter and highlight important information using contrast or distinct colors.
However, if there is no clear need for color and we cannot justify it, there is no reason
why we should use it [32].
The visual hierarchy is vital such as color, shape, or size to help users quickly
locate key information. Nonphotorealistic visuals, such as stylized images or sketches,
are sometimes more effective than detailed, photorealistic ones, as they reduce unnec-
essary distractions and make patterns clearer [32].
Shneiderman [33] proposed a design philosophy that focuses on creating compre-
hensible, predictable, and controllable interfaces. His approach included overview,
zoom, filter, and details-on-demand [34].
Huahai Yang et al. [35] examined the types of insights people gain from bar and
line graphs. They identified four basic insights: reading values, identifying extreme
points, understanding distribution, and describing correlations. Additionally, they
outlined four comparative insights: comparing values, comparing extrema, comparing
distributions, and comparing correlations.
Li et al. [36] and Rooksby et al. [37] highlighted that self-tracking tools should
support, most of all, reflection and making narrative around the data.
==PAGE:14==
2.3 Guidelines for effective data visualization
7
After all, to achieve effective data visualization, it is essential to tailor visual-
izations to the specific audience and the tasks they will be conducting.
Different
visualizations, even basic stats, perform differently depending on the context, so un-
derstanding the data, user and the message being communicated is the key [32].
Shneiderman [38] also emphasized that creating a successful interface doesn’t rely
on a single formula but on a balance between simplicity, clarity, and engagement.
Visualization, being a multidisciplinary field, has evolved from basic perception and
statistics to include areas such as color theory, visual cognition, interaction theory, and
information theory. This multi-disciplinary nature of visualization creates challenges
for defining a singular, unified solution [31].
Lastly, Healy, in his book [39], discusses Tufte’s reaction to Charles Joseph Mi-
nard’s visualization of Napoleon’s retreat from Moscow (Figure 2.1). Healy argues
that this might be one of the best visualizations ever created, as it conveys a vast
amount of information in minimal space, going beyond basic statistical representation.
Rather than displaying a single number over time, it presents a rich and multidimen-
sional narrative [39].
Figure 2.1: Charles Joseph Minard’s visualization of Napoleon’s Russian campaign
of 1812 [40] .
However, Healy also notes that Tufte acknowledges there are no strict principles
for creating a ”golden” visualization. While general guidelines, such as minimizing the
data-to-ink ratio and simplifying graphs, are often recommended, they do not always
apply universally. In some cases, visually engaging designs are more memorable than
plain, purely functional ones (chart junk). Though they may not be that readable,
they are easier recalled and more enjoyable to look at [39].
An example of this
is ”Monstrous Costs” by Nigel Holmes (Figure 2.2), which prioritizes visual appeal
alongside data representation.

In conclusion, there is no single way for crafting a meaningful visualization. While
guidelines exist, the effectiveness of a visualization ultimately depends on its ability
to convey the maximum amount of information in minimal space with minimal effort.
And there are no predefined rules for that.
2.4
Data storytelling
One of the potential emerging solution for a better understanding of the visuals is
data storytelling. The narratives with graphical representations provide richer com-
munication of results and understanding how data points or trends in a visualization
relate to real-world contexts [42][3].
Data storytelling is seen as a powerful tool to bridge the comprehension gap for
users with limited data literacy, as it focuses on presenting the data rather than the
data analysis itself [3].
Echeverria et al. [43] identified five key principles for transforming conventional
visualizations into data stories [3]:
• Understand the audience - determine, what level of data literacy intended users
have
• Removing non-essential elements - it involves minimizing axis details, omitting
less relevant data points, directly labeling data series instead of using legends,
and maintaining minimal color variation to effectively guide the viewer’s atten-
tion
• Using annotations and visual cues should be used strategically - excessive an-
notations can clutter the visualization and distract the audience [44]
• Capturing interest - this entails reducing unnecessary visual elements while
emphasizing key components
==PAGE:16==
2.4 Data storytelling
9
• Calling for action - designing a clear and concise visualization that effectively
communicates the core narrative
Research highlights several elements that contribute to those five key principles.
Edward Tufte stressed the importance of contextualizing data for better understand-
ing [27]. Techniques like using colors or bold elements help highlight key aspects,
while annotations (graphical or textual) provide contextual or explanatory details
[3][45]. A key element also is using explicit titles that guide the viewer towards the
intended action or conclusion [46][45][47]. Decluttering can be obtained by contrast-
ing colors, highlighting key data points, or making specific trend lines bolder. These
strategies help guide the viewer’s attention toward the most important insights [45].

CHAPTER 3
Analysis of existing
solution
“There is no such thing as information overload. There is only
bad design”
— Edward Tufte
This chapter discusses the existing platform designed for tracking patient data
and the challenges faced by both therapists and patients in using it effectively. The
chapter highlights these limitations and suggests areas for its usability and eﬀiciency
improvement.
3.1
Overview
One Button Tracker is a novel wearable self-tracking system designed to enhance the
accuracy of symptom monitoring in mental health contexts. It includes a wrist-worn
smart button connected to a smartphone via Bluetooth, allowing users to log obser-
vations instantly by pressing the button when they experience a defined symptom
[19][48]. This real-time tracking reduces reliance on memory recall, being often a
problem in self-reported data. Usually such data is acquired from questionnaires or
diaries [19][20].
The system was specifically developed to help patients and therapists identify
patterns in symptoms related to conditions such as PTSD, depression, anxiety, panic
attacks or intrusive thoughts etc. By facilitating frequent and precise tracking of
bodily sensations, emotions, thoughts and behaviors, it helps to distinguish symp-
toms and their causes that may be diﬀicult to obtain through traditional clinical
assessments [19][20].
3.2
Users
There are two types of users: therapists and patients, each with distinct tasks at
different stages of the user flow. The process is collaborative, particularly in deciding
==PAGE:18==
3.3 Current user workflow

which phenomena should be tracked.
However, even though the patient actively
tracks their symptoms, it is ultimately the therapist who must make the final decision.
Therapists rely heavily on understanding the specific triggers of phenomena in order
to target treatment effectively [20]. For more detailed description of the target user
segment and their pain points refer to section 5.1.1.
3.3
Current user workflow
The current user flow was described by Riisager et al.
in their study [48] as an
iterative process. After the beginning stage, where therapist and patient together
defined tracked symptom, the patient collects the data, which is later analyzed during
the sessions with the therapist. As a result, the treatment is continuously adjusted
by tailoring the interventions or tracked symptoms, ensuring that the therapy adapts
to the patient’s changing needs. The described user flow is shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: User flowchart of the treatment process with the usage of One Button
Tracker for self-tracking [48] .
However, current user workflow can be divided into five stages according to the
stage-based model of personal informatics introduced by Li et al. [49], which explains
the personal tracking framework and behavior change plan (Figure 3.2). It is used
here to describe the particular stages of the treatment process and perform analysis
to scope down a problem and point the challenging area.

3.3.1
Preparation stage
Patients referred to the treatment to work on their condition are provided with a One
Button Tracker, which allows them to track the occurrences of an agreed symptom
and intervention in between sessions. This, which symptom is about to be tracked,
is based on the defined observation protocol and is discussed during the assessment
interview with a therapist. If needed, it is altered during the next sessions [20].
The wristband is worn, almost all the time. When the button is pressed, a signal
is transmitted via Bluetooth to a connected smartphone app.
If a connection is
unavailable, the button stores the presses and transfers them once reconnected. The
app saves the data internally and allows users to export a file containing timestamps
of all recorded interactions [19][20].
3.3.2
Collection stage
User uses the device to log the observations by pressing on a button during the
tracking periods (between therapy sessions) based on the previously defined observa-
tion protocol. User can track one or two phenomena e.g. one press of the button
when the patient experienced panic attack and two presses, while doing breathing
exercises. The data is in timestamped format in milliseconds. Usually the whole
treatment takes around two to three months, however it can change depending on
the treatment progress, even up to one year [49][20].
Based on the previous studies by Larsen et al. [49][20], users were selected and
agreed to participate, which resulted in relatively consistent data records. However
the reasons for missing data are not analyzed in this project. Interpretation of these
inconsistencies is left to the therapist.
Users are unaware of the frequency of their symptom occurrences until they attend
a therapy session due to privacy measures.

3.3.3
Integration stage
During a therapy session or upon request from the therapist, the participant exports
the data from the application on the smartphone linked to the smart button and sends
a .csv file with the recorded button presses through email for analysis [20]. Next, the
therapist opens a data visualization prototype created on Observable notebook [50],
uploads the file from the drive and selects the observation protocol used for correct
interpretation of the data e.g. ”1 and 2 presses”. Phenomena can be renamed for
clarity if more descriptive names are preferred (Figure 3.3).
Figure 3.3: Data upload to One Button Tracker Data Visualization platform in
Observable notebook [50]) .
3.3.4
Reflection stage
Once the file is uploaded, Observable notebook is responsible of plotting the recorded
data. This stage involves reviewing collected personal data, exploring insights and
patterns, or interacting with data [49]. The user can select a start and end date for
the time period during which the data was recorded. They can also marked this spe-
cific period directly on the histogram. The histogram, accompanied by a trendline,
displays the number of occurrences of the symptom for each day. Additionally, the
bubble chart visualizes the occurrences of 1 and 2 presses per each hour, which corre-
spond to the graph above. Below the charts, statistics are displayed for the selected
period, including the average number of observations per weekday and hour of the

The visualization prototype has been used in clinical case studies examining the

3.3.5
Action stage
Lastly, the therapist based on the findings in the data and the conversation with the
patient about its context, decides if they are going to change the protocol. Due to
the iterative process, users can track additional symptoms or interventions, or modify
existing ones.
They can also stay with the current protocol and observe further
behavior. The understanding of the recorded data is important, as the treatment will
be tailored based on that [49].
3.4
Barriers and problem definition
According to stage-based model, each stage can include barriers, which hinder the
users from getting the right insights [49]. In this system, I identified several barriers
that affect the users’ ability to effectively engage with and interpret their data.
==PAGE:23==
3.4 Barriers and problem definition
16
3.4.1
Preparation and collection stage
In the preparation and collection stages, potential issues may arise related to the
user’s motivation and the tracking tool itself. However, these concerns will not be
discussed in detail here, as previous studies [19][20] have already explored, to some
extent, these challenges.
The novel design of this device addresses some of these
issues by eliminating the need for users to e.g. open another application to register
symptoms. This project will not focus on users’ diﬀiculties in remembering to track
symptoms or staying motivated. While the tool highlights unlogged data, it is the
therapist’s responsibility to understand the reasons behind the missed entries.
3.4.2
Integration stage
Regarding the integration stage, there is no feedback about the ease or time required
for data uploads, either from the patient and the therapist. However, a significant
issue arises from the fact that therapists still rely on handwritten notes or external
documents to modify the observation protocol, as the current system lacks built-in
tracking adjustments and a logbook [48]. At present, the system does not allow ther-
apists to see when or for how long a particular symptom and/or intervention was
being tracked in a simple and clear way, nor can they update the protocol directly
within the system. Also there is no possibility to add notes. As long as those miss-
ing features don’t directly prevent useful insights, an upgraded solution could make
therapists’ work faster and gather everything in one place [49].
3.4.3
Reflection and Action stage
Problems start to arise in the reflection stage, where barriers can cascade and affect
the subsequent action stage [49]. During sessions with patients, therapists have lim-
ited time to review everything, and cognitive load further hinders usability. There’s
also the challenge to obtain a holistic view of the data and interpreting it quickly.
Therapists require quick, insightful assessments, and additional time spent on data
handling adds to their workload rather than making them more eﬀicient. For patients,
clear and engaging visualizations are essential to help them understand their condi-
tion without feeling overwhelmed. However, patients may find tracking and reviewing
data tedious or too complex, reducing their engagement. As one participant noted:
”Well, I did see those graphs. And I did have a look at them, but I thought yeah, I’m
going to need some explanation from you here, what you guys think of this.”

and ultimately help rather than delay therapists’ work.
Some therapists recognize the potential of data-driven tools, but their effectiveness
is limited by missing features. While patients generate the data, it is ultimately the
therapists who must interpret and integrate it into treatment plans, ensuring that the
information is effectively used to guide therapeutic decisions. These barriers affect
the therapists’ ability to make quick, actionable decisions.

Methodology
“Measure what is measurable, and make measurable what is not
so.”
— Galileo Galilei
This project followed a Design Thinking approach, a user-centered methodol-
ogy that emphasizes iterative problem-solving process through empathy, defining,
ideation, prototyping, and testing [52]. Additionally, the Build-Measure-Learn con-
cept, introduced by Eric Ries [53], was applied throughout the design iterations, to
ensure the systematic experimentation and continuous refinement of the prototype.
These approaches provided a development framework, with the primary goal of cre-
ating visualizations that effectively serve users with varying levels of data literacy.
The entire process incorporated both qualitative and quantitative methods. The
qualitative approach was valuable for gathering in-depth insights through interviews
providing different participants’ perspectives [54]. Quantitative research was used
to generate data that could be generalized to larger populations and to test the
hypotheses faster.
The core design process, outside the user research, involved six iterations. A pro-
totype was created in Figma software [55], allowing for faster and easier adjustments,
enabling quicker testing and implementation of feedback. No actual tool was devel-
oped or coded. The following sections provide a more detailed explanation of the user
research and used testing methods.

4.1
User research
User research is a fundamental part of the entire UX design process, as it helps to
uncover actual user goals instead of relying on speculations [56]. The primary aim of
the research was to define the problems faced by different user segments (therapists
and patients) while interacting with the current system, particularly regarding the
data visualizations. However, at the beginning of the research, I did not limit myself
only to the issues related to data visualizations. For a better perspective, I wanted
also to gain broader understanding of the overall challenges users experience with the
existing platform.
The research was mostly based on the secondary data, with most of the findings
taken from previous studies related to the One-Button-Tracker [19][20][51][57][48].
Additionally, insights were gathered through the interview with domain expert and
feedback from the supervisor.
For the full overview of the user research refer to

4.1.1
Analysis
To analyze the findings, I used a grouping method, where I categorized similar re-
sponses into themes and identified the problematic areas. I asked myself questions

such as ”what are the problems?” and ”what values are most important to the users?”
[58]. As was mentioned, I didn’t want to limit myself just to the visualizations to
avoid introducing bias and potentially overlooking important insights. Instead, I fo-
cused on what seemed most significant, paying attention to what was mentioned most
frequently.
Based on these findings and findings from Chapter 3, I created user personas
(more detailed description in Section 5.1.3) and user journey maps. I categorized the
personas into three main groups: a younger patient with a high level of data literacy,
an older patient with minimal level of data literacy, and a therapist with low level of
data literacy as well. Even though, the focus was primarily on non-expert users, the
motivation for including both groups of different age and technical knowledge, was to
ensure the tool remains understandable for everyone. I also developed a user journey
map, highlighting the pain points at the each stage of interaction and identifying
opportunities for improvement. Creating personas and user journey maps helped to
structure initial findings (Appendix A).
4.1.2
Limitations
The limitation to the research, was the lack of possibility to access the actual users
involved in the previous studies, as the research phase of One-Button-Tracker project
had already finished. This limitation affected the reliability of the findings and in-
troduced gaps in the research. That meant, while I could evaluate usability with
recruited participants, I was unable to assess the true usefulness of the visualizations
in a real therapeutic setting. The effectiveness of the visualizations should undergo
further validation with the targeted users.
Due to this limitation, I recruited participants from diverse backgrounds and vary-
ing levels of data literacy. The reasoning behind this approach was that if individuals
with different levels of experience could understand the visualizations, then therapists
and patients would likely find them accessible as well. Next sections describe con-
ducted experiments in detail.
4.2
Usability testing
Usability testing was a crucial part of the design process, conducted in multiple
iterations to refine the system and improve the visualizations. Each round of testing
provided valuable feedback into how users interacted with the system, and the findings
from one round were used to refine the design in the next.
There were six iterations, however usability testing was conducted across four dif-
ferent iterations. In all iterations participants were recruited based on the eligibility
criteria: (1) no engineering background/not data literacy knowledge and very good
knowledge of data literacy, (2) diverse age group. Participants in the iterations in-
cluded both new and recurring users from the past rounds. This approach allowed me
==PAGE:28==
4.2 Usability testing
21
to get perspective how first-time users, as well as recurring users might experience the
design. Sessions were conducted in-person or remotely, and participants were asked
to complete specific tasks based on predefined scenarios. Before the test, user received
the introduction into the project and its goal. In all four iterations, participants were
guided through the tasks by a facilitator and asked to verbalize their thoughts using
the think-aloud method as they interacted with the visualizations.
4.2.1
Iteration 1
In the first iteration, eight participants were involved. The purpose of this round
was to test various drafts of basic visualizations, see how they perform in the stated
context [32] and what is the understanding of them for the users with different back-
ground. As usually, users should have general knowledge of basic types like bar and
line charts [25][14][15]. This test aimed to explore two of the research questions, what
different types of data visualizations can enhance user understanding and what are
the potential key barriers to data interpretation for experts and users with low data
literacy?.
According to the guidelines [26][27][26], I divided visualizations into three logical
categories: progress, comparison, and correlation. The objective was to determine
whether users could effectively interpret the data and trends shown in each visu-
alization, and whether the visualizations were intuitive, useful, and supportive of
decision-making. The usability tasks involved comparing symptom frequency, trends
and outliers [26][27] and interpreting the presented data.
The metric was a task
success rate and besides, follow-up questions were asked, which asked about the in-
tuitiveness and usefulness of the visualizations, such as ”Which visualization did you
find most intuitive?” or ”What did you like or dislike about this visualization?”. This
iteration was the longest user testing, as each session with the user took around 30
minutes. For the full description of the tasks and metrics refer to Appendix B.
4.2.1.1
Analysis
Obtained results were categorized into sections corresponding to the types of visual-
izations. Given the large number of responses due to the number of participants, I
filtered them to point the most important insights and to distinguish minor remarks
related to the interface. To categorize usability issues, I applied the severity ratings
model from NNGroup [59], which classifies issues into five severity levels:
• (4) critical - all issues that impedes users from completing their tasks
• (3) serious - issues that have to be fixed as soon as possible as they slow down
user’s experience
• (2) medium - issues that might not interfere with the user experience often, but
can still be frustrating for users to experience
==PAGE:29==
4.2 Usability testing
22
• (1) low - cosmetic issues that are necessary to fix
• (0) no issue - user reports a problem that turns out to be a non-issue or a feature
request
I first addressed the most critical and severe issues, followed by smaller problems
based on my own subjective assessment of their severity.
4.2.1.2
Limitations
Some participants felt they lacked overall context for the visualizations and initially
struggled to understand the topic. However, this did not significantly impact the
results, as the primary focus was on testing readability across individual charts.
The test should have been divided into smaller parts, as the number of visu-
alizations was overwhelming. A/B testing would have been a better approach, as
comparing multiple similar graphs sequentially could introduce bias, making users
progressively more comfortable with interpretation and skewing the results. Addi-
tionally, showing users a bar chart with added annotations and asking about feedback
right after the previous tasks with bar charts might have influenced their responses,
especially if they had a negative experience with the previous visualizations. From
the third participant onward, I decided to omit tasks 2 and 3, as their responses in
the first task often already covered those aspects, making the additional questions
redundant. I also decided to reduce the number of participants in the next itera-
tions, since I observed recurring patterns after three or four users, with only minor
additional remarks.
4.2.2
Iteration 2
Based on the conclusions from the previous test, the second iteration of usability
testing involved three participants and focused on evaluating users’ ability to filter
data and select the specific time period. The primary goal was to assess whether users
could effectively move around the dashboard timeline. Participants were given tasks
to select a specified date range and then three individual therapy sessions, instead
of using a fixed range. The metric was task success rate and the follow-up questions
explored users’ experiences, asking for their feedback on the ease of use, areas of
confusion, and suggestions for improvement. Thank to this iteration I could quickly
test the first look of the timeline needed for logbook. To see the detailed overview of
the user test refer to Appendix C.
4.2.2.1
Analysis
In this iteration, I did not need to group answers into themes because there were
only a few participants, and the tasks were short. This allowed for a quick overview
without the need for filtering or categorizing responses. A higher number of responses

pointing to the same issue indicated a problem that required attention. However, even
individual responses were carefully considered, as they could still highlight valuable
insights.
4.2.2.2
Limitations
There were no significant limitations impacting the results of this iteration. The small
sample size and lack of thematic grouping did not affect the outcomes, as the tasks
were short and manageable, allowing for a quick overview.
4.2.3
Iteration 4
The third round of usability testing involved six participants and focused on evalu-
ating the data upload process and how users interact with the system to associate
tracking data with a patient.
The objective was to assess how easily users could
upload a file, assign it to the correct patient, and add descriptions to the data for a
better context.
The study consisted of three tasks, each targeting a different aspect of the design:
data upload, updating descriptions, and navigating the timeline. The testing sce-
narios were designed specifically for the therapist role, as therapists are responsible
for handling data uploads, assigning descriptions and any actions happening on the
dashboard. Additionally, participants were asked how they would review the patient’s
symptom trends between two tracking periods. Metric was a task success rate, but
also subjective ratings were collected on a 1-5 scale to assess comprehension, ease of
use, and the appeal of the upload process. Follow-up questions were asked to gather
user feedback on whether the upload process was clear, what could be improved, and
if the overall process met their expectations.
I aimed to provide enough context for users to understand the system without
giving excessive detail that could skew testing results. To see the overview of the test

Analysis
The user testing interviews were analyzed by reviewing responses to each question

Feedback was examined individually, with a focus on
identifying usability issues, feature requests, and general impressions. Common pain
points and successes were highlighted, along with any unexpected behaviors observed.
Patterns in user responses were noted to understand overall trends, and qualitative
insights were extracted to inform future design iterations.

4.2.4.2
Issues and limitations
The survey did not yield significant new insights, mainly due to a low response rate.

However, it gave overview what users use the most and the impact the user inter-

4.2.5
Preference test
Three participants were involved in the test. The preference test focused on evaluating
different visualizations without the correlation section, asking participants to choose
which one they preferred, which one they found easier to read, and which one provided
more insights into the data. The objective was to gather feedback on visual appeal
and usability. During the test, participants were asked general questions like, ”Which
visualization is the easiest to read?” ”What makes this design easy to read?” ”Was
anything confusing or diﬀicult to understand?” and ”Which visualization helps you
interpret the data faster?”. For the complex overview of the results and test design
refer to Appendix E.
4.2.5.1
Analysis
Given the small sample size and the short tasks, I was able to quickly review the
responses without needing to categorize the data or group the answers into themes.
4.2.5.2
Limitations
One limitation of preference testing is that it doesn’t provide a comprehensive test
of a functional product. Participants may have preferences that are based solely on
aesthetics, and even with follow-up questions, their qualitative feedback can some-
times be too vague or unclear. This means the data gathered could be inaccurate or
incomplete, which could lead to design iterations based on faulty findings. But it is
fast to test and decide.
4.3
Evaluation of selected methods
Usability testing provided the most valuable insights, as it allowed for direct interac-
tion with participants. Even after conducting predefined scenarios, I was able to ask
follow-up questions, adapt the testing dynamically, and engage in a more informal
way to better understand user needs. This level of engagement was not possible with
the survey. Overall, usability testing proved to be a strong choice, despite my limited
professional experience in conducting such studies.
The preference test was quick and, while it might have introduced some bias, it
was still helpful. It confirmed many of my initial assumptions and hypotheses from
previous studies, reinforcing my design decisions.
4.4
Other limitations
All tests were conducted using Figma, which does not fully replicate an actual system,
despite being an excellent prototyping tool. Unless animations are flawlessly imple-
mented, the lack of interactivity and occasional inconsistencies in transitions may

due to the design itself, but because the prototype does not function as smoothly as
a fully developed system. This is especially relevant when testing animations, where

prototype was a mock data, which could impact the results of the test. Even if I tried

The design process utilized Design Thinking methodology [52], mentioned in Chap-

5.1
User research
Findings can be divided into two sections. The first discusses the pain points of both
user groups, and the second includes the list of potential visualization requirements.
It covers the design thinking stages: Empatize and Define.
5.1.1
User pain points
Based on the analysis, three main themes emerged as crucial for both patients and
therapists when interacting with the current tool:
• personalization and diverse user needs,
• need for support and guidance,
• time constraints and cognitive load.
One of the main challenges was the need for support and guidance in interpreting
self-tracking data and the cognitive load. Both patients and therapists struggled to
make sense of the visualized information without additional help as the interpretation
imposed much effort.
Based on the conducted user research (see section 4.1), the following aspects
emerged. Patients had different motivations for using self-tracking data. Some wanted
==PAGE:35==
5.1 User research
28
to educate themselves and gain deeper insight into their condition, while others felt
more indifferent toward data interpretation. A key observation was that self-tracking
was not always perceived negatively.
Instead, some patients reported surprise or
even insight when seeing their results, as they hadn’t realized the severity of their
symptoms before.
They found it also helpful when they didn’t have to verbalize
everything to the therapist because the data already provided context.
One of the challenges for patients was the diﬀiculty in distinguishing different
tracked phenomena within the visualizations.
When tracking multiple symptoms
simultaneously, it was sometimes unclear how to differentiate between them, partic-
ularly when colors were used inconsistently. In some cases, patients found it diﬀicult
to distinguish between weekdays and weekends or to differentiate between day and
night tracking data, which made it harder for them to recall patterns.
Addition-
ally, some older users faced usability challenges, such as text being too small to read
comfortably.
Patients also encountered issues related to time constraints and cognitive load.
Many found it diﬀicult to remember the context of their symptom tracking, such
as where they were and what they were doing at the time of a button press. Some
patients experimented with gamifying the data to make tracking more engaging.
Unlike patients, therapists did not focus on interpreting individual data points
but instead wanted a high-level overview that could quickly provide actionable in-
sights. Most therapists only glanced at the data briefly rather than conducting a
deep analysis. Some explicitly mentioned that they would prefer an algorithm that
could identify patterns for them, such as highlighting outliers, notable trends, or
significant time periods.
A major concern for therapists was time constraints and cognitive load. Many
found that using the system added extra work to their already busy schedules, making
it diﬀicult to integrate into their daily practice. It was emphasized that they needed a
quick way to extract key insights without having to analyze the raw data themselves.
While some users from both groups appreciated the potential of visualizations for
improving treatment, they lacked the time and expertise to engage deeply with the
data. For the full overview of the users’ pain points refer to Appendix A.
5.1.2
Visualization requirements
Based on the research (See 4.1), the list of the key visualization requirements emerged.
Therapists are interested, in general, in questions like: “How often does this sensa-
tion occur?” or “How much does this feeling hinder you in doing what you want?”
[20]. There is a clear need for visualizations that effectively display the temporal
occurrences of the tracked phenomenon, including when it occurs, the time of day,
specific days, and the relevant circumstances [26][27]. Users want to understand the
distribution of the phenomenon and identify correlation patterns with factors such as
location or other contextual elements [26]. Additionally, it is important to track data
logs and highlight days without data logs. Users would benefit from visualizing this

data broken down by weekdays, weekends, and hours (or potentially both), allowing
them to identify patterns in symptom occurrences more clearly [26][27].

Persona 1
Emma (24, Copenhagen) is an MSc. in Art with basic tech skills, tracking depres-

gain useful insights for treatment decisions. He prefers guidance for interpreting data
rather than analyzing it himself.

visually demanding to interpret.
For tracking trends over a month, I used a line graph to observe changes in two
phenomena over time. Additionally, I employed a bubble chart to represent symptom
severity, where the longer a user pressed the button, the larger the bubble appeared
(later in the iterations this aspect became invalid).
To enhance usability, I included simple filtering options, allowing users to select
specific time ranges (e.g., by weeks) and quick filters such as ”Today,” ”This Week,”
”This Month,” and ”This Year” (Figure 5.1).
Following Tufte’s principles of data visualization, I aimed to make the data easily
comparable [27]. To enhance readability and user engagement, I applied several design
choices. I used large, clear titles to immediately capture users’ attention [46][45][47].
I added basic annotations, such as tooltips that display the number of occurrences
when hovering over data points or bars [3][45]. In bar charts, I marked the highest
value bar [26] and displayed average occurrence number line directly on the chart to
provide quick context.
At this stage, I deliberately chose not to use color. This decision was made to
ensure that the initial focus remained on data clarity rather than aesthetics [27].
Color can be introduced later in the design process, when necessary.
For the dashboard, my intention was to provide context for the users while testing
but, also for me. While it was not the primary focus at this stage, I needed a rough
structure to understand how the system might function as a whole. I considered what
key highlights therapists would need when reviewing data, such as patients’ list and
data upload.
Throughout this phase, several key questions emerged:
• How can correlations between tracked phenomena be effectively displayed and
indicate context?
• How should weekdays and weekends be clearly distinguished?
• How can outliers or unusual patterns be indicated?
• Can color coding be used to represent frequency or severity (e.g., lighter shades
for fewer occurrences, darker shades for more frequent ones)?
• What key highlights do therapists need to see on the dashboard?
• What is the goal of each visualization, and how does it contribute to user un-
derstanding?

5.3.1.3
Results
The responses to the bar charts, were highly varied among users. In the first type
of visualization one participant found it easier to compare values when the bars were
placed next to each other. The largest difference between values was generally iden-
tified correctly, but some users needed extra time, especially when the differences
were subtle.
While most users could eventually determine the highest value, the
process took longer than expected for some, indicating that the visualization could
be made clearer. Two users suggested rounded bar design, as it would might make
interpretation easier. Three participants mentioned that they could not clearly rec-
ognize a trend. They suggested that annotations or a summary would be helpful in
highlighting key insights instead of requiring them to analyze the chart manually.
For the second visualization, users were able to identify the bar with the largest
difference, but it took them more time compared to the first visualization. Many
participants found it diﬀicult to assess the information at a glance and needed addi-
==PAGE:46==
5.3 Iterations
39
tional effort to interpret the data. Additionally, there was confusion regarding the
readability of the orange bars, with some users unsure whether they started from
zero or a different baseline. This uncertainty made it diﬀicult for them to accurately
assess the values and understand how they should be counted.
For the third visualization, users generally found it more visually appealing and
easier to divide into sections. Several participants noted that it was the clearest of
the three versions.
The first visualization was often described as readable but required more effort to
interpret due to referencing the scale. Some found it easy to spot values immediately,
while others felt it was slightly confusing. The second visualization was seen as the
least readable. The third was considered the clearest, but some users struggled with
exact value assessment.
A recurring issue was that the colors in these visualizations blended more than in
the previous versions, making it diﬀicult to distinguish between the different elements.
Orange was more noticeable than grey, sometimes leading to the perception that the
current session had more symptoms. In some cases, grey appeared more dominant
due to larger bars, which could be misleading.
However, overall, the contrast, in
general, between colors was considered effective.
Some users found the graphs diﬀicult to read at first but understood them better
with explanations. Some suggested adding a trend line or line graph for better clarity.
A table with numerical results was also suggested for improved understanding.
Some users felt that the graphs lacked clear differentiation between the current
and previous sessions or did not suﬀiciently show symptom intensity. There was also
some confusion about whether symptoms were compared together or separately.
Annotations were generally seen as helpful, especially for understanding total oc-
currences and comparing data across a longer period, such as a year. They allowed
users to grasp progress without needing to analyze the graphs in detail. Without
annotations, it was diﬀicult for users to determine if there was progress or not. How-
ever, some found the percentages confusing and suggested comparing data from the
first session rather than between individual sessions. The arrows indicating progress
were also confusing and should be larger, with clearer labels such as ”Improved” or
”Decreased.”
All users successfully completed the task using the heatmap, indicating that it
was intuitive and easy to read. The visualization was considered helpful in providing
insights and spotting patterns. Most users found the heatmap easy to understand.
However, a few were initially confused by the axis arrangement, expecting weekdays
to be at the bottom instead of vertical. One mentioned that they take more time to
analyze.
Users were able to identify that rumination events lasted longer than intrusion
events and that symptoms occurred sequentially. They could also recognize when
and how often symptoms appeared throughout the week. However, the overall clarity
of the graph was a challenge, and users struggled to extract meaningful insights
without additional explanation.

The visualization was perceived as messy and unclear, making it diﬀicult to imme-

Based on user feedback, several key questions emerged. One important consid-
eration is whether markers or annotations should be added to graphs to highlight
anomalies, making it easier for users to identify significant patterns without exten-
sive analysis. Additionally, there is a need to further explore the relationship between
tracked phenomena to ensure that the visualizations effectively convey meaningful in-
sights. This requires a deeper investigation into how different symptoms interact over
time and how best to represent these correlations in a clear and intuitive manner.
First, since the testing was conducted with random participants, it raised the
question of whether this truly reflects the needs and expectations of therapists. Sec-
ond, I considered whether adding a trendline would help users more easily identify
patterns in the data. Another question was how to effectively display correlations
between tracked phenomena while providing suﬀicient context. I also wondered what
the best method would be for clearly distinguishing between weekdays and weekends
in the visualizations. Finally, I needed to consider how to indicate outliers or unusual
patterns without overwhelming the visual space.
Furthermore, the initial questions posed at the beginning of the testing process
were not specific enough to each visualization category and require refinement to
better align with the insights needed for each type of data.
The changes were applied in the next iteration.

5.3.2.3
Results
Participants had diﬀiculty understanding what the timeline represented. They were
confused about why the dates were not sequential and why only session dates were
shown. Additionally, using color-coded dots to indicate high or low occurrences was
problematic because it only showed data for session dates, leaving gaps where other
anomalies may have occurred on non-session days. While all participants successfully
selected a date range, none of them realized that selecting an individual dates required
switching to the ”Individual” mode.
Further feedback from the supervisor highlighted additional issues. There was no
real need to view specific days selectively, as therapists analyze data over periods
rather than through traditional temporal filtering. Selecting a range worked well for
tracking the overall period, but since sessions occur irregularly, a standard time-based
filtering approach was not applicable. Another key issue was that therapists could
not see on which days specific phenomena were tracked, nor could they access the
protocol they needed.
The progress bar also did not make sense in this context, as the number of sessions
is not strictly predefined. While there may be an initial plan, the actual number of
sessions typically varies based on the patient’s needs.

The survey results indicate that most people track fitness, symptoms, and food,
primarily to improve health, identify patterns, or out of curiosity. Common visualiza-
tions used in tracking apps include bar graphs, line charts, data summaries, patterns,
trends, and calendar views, which aligns with Grammel at al. study [15] regarding

wished for more detailed running data and better color differentiation. Design ele-

Half of the respondents customize visualizations [28], and most find interactive

havior and increased engagement with the app. Almost 91% felt more aware of their
progress and challenges, and the same percentage expressed interest in visualizations
that suggest actionable insights. However, responses may be biased, as most partici-
pants reported being comfortable with technology and data. To see the results of the

5.3.4.3
Results
The user test results reveal several positive aspects regarding the interface and func-
tionality. First, the majority of users found the upload process intuitive, as it adhered
to common upload patterns. Users reported that the upload button was easily identi-
fiable, and the process—consisting of clicking the upload button, selecting a patient,
and confirming the action—was straightforward. Additionally, the option to manually
upload or drag and drop files was well understood, and the upload speed was generally
perceived as fast. Users comprehended that clicking the edit (pencil) icon allowed for
modifications, although some initial uncertainty was noted. Once users understood
the functionality, they found selecting symptoms from the dropdown menu to be an
easy task. However, the need to describe the tracking period posed some initial con-
fusion. Users eventually understood this requirement, though the concept itself was
unclear to them, leaving them uncertain about the purpose of describing the data.
Lastly, users demonstrated a clear understanding of the line graph’s meaning after
discovering how to expand it. While there was some learning curve in uncovering this
functionality, once users located the option to expand the graph, its purpose became
more apparent.
However, there was a lot of areas to improve. One key area for improvement was
assuming the users needed to go to the patient list first, select a patient, and then
upload the data, instead of selecting the patient from a dropdown after clicking the
upload button. While this results in more clicks for the therapist, it streamlines the
process by consolidating everything in one place. That also confirmed indirectly that
the name Patients on the sidebar was clear and understandable by the users. One
thing that could potentially biased the test, was that I asked the users to upload the
data and select ”Mikkel”, so they started from looking for that patient name first,
even I said to them to first upload the data.
One user expressed a desire for a visual cue, such as a loading animation or
progress indicator, to confirm that the upload was in progress. This was important for
user confidence, ensuring that they knew the system was processing their upload. To
address this, a graphical representation of the files being uploaded and the information
were added (however,self reflection, it might indicate we might apply only one file at
a time) to the upload screen, despite the upload bar.
A suggestion was made to implement real-time uploads, where data would be
automatically transferred from the patient’s app to the system. This would eliminate
the need for manual file handling by the therapist. However, it was acknowledged
that this could be intrusive for the patient, as they may not want to see their data
being uploaded automatically.
User questioned the necessity of the data export feature, with some suggesting
that it be removed if it wasn’t essential.
One user found it unexpected that tracking periods appeared at the same level as
symptoms and suggested moving them to the calendar view for a clearer connection
between time and symptoms.
Several users experienced confusion regarding the 1 press and 2 presses options,

as there was no clear indication of what each choice meant.
Some users guessed
randomly, with one user selecting 1 press for depression simply because it was the
first option in the dropdown. Additionally, users were unsure whether the presses
had been agreed upon beforehand, which created uncertainty.
Another common issue was uncertainty around how to close the edit window after
making changes.
All users expected a Save button or some form of confirmation
but were unsure how to proceed, fearing they might lose their progress. The current
method, where users must click the pencil icon again to close the window, led to
confusion. A clearer save/close functionality is needed to avoid this uncertainty. One
user suggested that the edit window should appear as a pop-up in the center of the
screen (similar to Google Calendar), to prevent cutting off important information and
to improve focus, as the user is typically engaged in data entry during that time.
The lack of clarity around the need for descriptions led some users to perceive
the pencil icon as optional, which created uncertainty about whether the description
input was mandatory. This confusion also extended to the idea of editing the date,
which users viewed as an additional, non-mandatory action.
First, many users expected to select a time range (such as day/week/month) rather
than using an arrow button to extend the timeline. The purpose of the ”expand”
button was unclear to some users, who associated it with switching between week
and month views. Additionally, one user questioned why the same line graphs were
displayed across different tracking periods when the data was already present in the
phenomena timelines. While most users understood the line graphs and occurrences,
one user struggled to interpret the meaning of peaks and variations, highlighting the
need for clearer context.
Several users found the expand button by chance rather than by understanding its

whether the values were high or low. Moreover, some users found the lines in the
graph too thin and lacking contrast, which made them diﬀicult to read, especially for
older users.
Users also expected that clicking or hovering over the line would provide more
interactive details, such as the number of occurrences, but this didn’t happen. Some
users didn’t realize there were more detailed visualizations beyond the initial view and
assumed that the current view was the complete data visualization. There was also
confusion over how to select a different month, with one user not realizing that they
needed to scroll. Lastly, a user questioned the relationship between different data
points, wondering if there was a correlation between occurrences, as they assumed
the current data was the entire visualization.

5.3.6.3
Results
The phenomena timeline should be divided by periods, such as depression or any
other tracked phenomena. This way, it would be clear to therapists what exactly
they are tracking and when specific phenomena or interventions were applied. After
filtering, it is important to display which phenomena have been selected, as well as the
corresponding timeframes for each selected period. Using numbering instead of dates
for tracking periods could improve clarity, allowing users to differentiate between
periods more easily.
Additionally, it should be clearly indicated somewhere that once a user selects one
side of the filter (such as phenomena or intervention), they cannot select the other
side at the same time.
==PAGE:67==
5.4 Final design decision
60
Before filtering, the data should display a general overview that includes both the
”before” and ”after” averages, providing a quick snapshot of changes. After filtering,
it’s essential that the same graphs are retained to maintain consistency in the analysis.
Regarding the correlation graphs, if only symptoms are selected, the visualization
should clearly show the relationship between symptom occurrences and interventions.
Color-coded periods on the overall bar charts may not always be reliable, so it’s
important to refine this method to ensure accurate representation, especially when
viewing both symptoms and interventions together.
When displaying data on the bar chart, it is crucial to make a distinction between
1-press and 2-press phenomena. Although the pattern might not always be immedi-
ately obvious, adding this distinction will help clarify the data. In line with Tufte’s
principles, basic statistics may not always be necessary if they do not aid in under-
standing the pattern; however, bar charts are often more accurate in such cases. On
the other hand, line graphs are useful for spotting trends and differences over time.
To enhance the heatmap visualization, it would be beneficial to add information
about the intervention’s occurrence alongside the symptoms. This could be done by
using color-coded circles or annotations to indicate when the intervention is taking
place relative to the symptoms. This additional information would help therapists
understand the relationship between interventions and symptom occurrences across
the timeline.
The spider chart was positively received by users, as it effectively allows compari-
son between the current tracking period and previous ones. While it may be diﬀicult
to interpret at first, users found it to be a powerful visualization once they understood
its structure.
5.4
Final design decision
In the final design, the logbook from the last iteration remained, allowing users to
track symptoms across tracking periods. After uploading data, users can update the
description of tracked phenomena by clicking on the three dots in the tracking period
(Figure 5.25).

Next, a bar chart displays the total number of observations for the tracking period,

readability when analyzing trends and comparisons. Hovering over data points high-

phenomena (Figure ??).

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
“Visualization gives you answers to questions you didn’t know
you had”
— Ben Schneiderman
The iterative design process significantly improved the usability of the system,
addressing key pain points identified in user testing. The transition from an initial
prototype to the final design was guided by user feedback, ensuring that each iteration
enhanced clarity, eﬀiciency, and accessibility.
A critical aspect of self-tracking tools is their ability to support reflection and
discovery [36]. The final design incorporated key principles from visualization research
to support users in tracking and analyzing their symptoms effectively. It serves as a
structured way for users to document and reflect on their symptoms over time.
In line with Shneiderman’s principles [38], it is crucial to strike a balance between
simplicity, clarity, and engagement when designing interfaces. However, in the con-
text of this project, while engaging the users is important, the primary focus was
on ensuring that users could quickly understand the visualizations. The goal was to
provide a tool that allowed users to freely explore the data and make quick assump-
tions without overwhelming them with unnecessary complexity or engagement-driven
features.
One of the most notable improvements was constructing the logbook for the ther-
apists.
Initially, users struggled to keep track of the phenomena across different
tracking periods by taking notes. This was tedious and was adding additional work
and tools to operate.
By integrating the logbook into the dashboard, the overall
clarity and integrity of the project improved. Similarly, visualizing therapy sessions
in the calendar improved the system’s ability to provide context for patient data.
Logbook was a custom visualization and according to Choe et al. [28], custom graphs
are one of the most common types of visualization for non-experts. Mostly, due to
the lack of the existing tools, like in this case.
Based on the conducted iteration, users didn’t stated they need a different vi-
sualization to demonstrate the data, rather than bar and line charts and custom
visualizations which might confirm their basic knowledge about statistics [25][14][28].
This addresses the research question about what different types of data vi-
sualizations can improve understanding for users? (see 1.2).
==PAGE:71==
6 Discussion
64
Inspired by Shneiderman’s framework of overview, zoom, filter, and details-on-
demand [34], the system allows users to start with a broad overview of their data
before refining their focus through filtering options.
The ability to compare the
current tracking period with previous averages provides a high-level summary before
users interact with specific details (see 5.1.1).
The introduction of new visualizations helped to differentiate two phenomena and
compare the tracking periods with each other, which was not possible before. The
final introduced visualizations containing simple charts gained positive feedback, as
they were understandable, easy to compare and spot the trends [26][27]. Incorporating
highlighting techniques in the visualizations addresses the need for emphasizing key
data points, such as extreme values or notable patterns [26].
The bar chart, for
example, marks peak occurrences, allowing users to quickly identify trends.
Trends and comparisons were another central focus, as recognizing changes over
time is crucial in self-tracking [26]. The combination of line charts for temporal trends
and spider charts for tracking period comparisons provides multiple perspectives on
symptom patterns. While the spider chart was initially complex for users to interpret,
once understood, it proves to be a powerful tool for identifying differences between
tracking periods.
From an aesthetic perspective, efforts were made to ensure clarity and customiza-
tion, as the user-friendly and clean interface is perceived more positively by users
[29]. During iterations participants were often mentioning the visual aspects and UI
of the prototype positively, which addressed the research question about visual
appeal and engagement (see 1.2). The decision to use separate scales for different
phenomena ensured that all visual elements remained visible, preventing smaller val-
ues from being overshadowed by larger ones. Furthermore, allowing users to toggle
between phenomena supports findings that non-expert users prefer or need custom
visualizations tailored to their needs [28].
Lastly, the approach to data storytelling followed key principles outlined by Echev-
erria et al. [43]. The system aimed to minimize clutter while emphasizing essential
insights through contrast and highlights.
Throughout the development process, several challenges and limitations emerged,
affecting the overall effectiveness of the system. One of the primary issues was the
diﬀiculty in establishing meaningful correlations between different tracked phenomena.
While filtering functionalities were successfully implemented and positively received,
the problem of identifying correlations remained unresolved. This might be due to the
lack of direct engagement with actual users, making it diﬀicult to validate whether
the provided insights were truly useful.
The memory recall challenge also remained unresolved. While self-tracking aims
to reduce recall bias in therapy, it is unclear whether users can truly remember specific
occurrences just by looking at the data. It was often mentioned by the users in the
related studies done by Larsen et al.
[19][20], that they still have problems with
contextualizing data (see 5.1.1). While visualizations provide a structured way to
analyze symptom patterns, they do not inherently help users recall contextual details.
==PAGE:72==
6 Discussion
65
Unfortunately, due to the time constraints and the focus on the logbook, the location
features was not designed.
Additionally, customization of visualizations emerged as an open question. While
the tracking process itself is personalized to each user, allowing them to select and ad-
just tracking phenomena, it is unclear whether visualizations should also adapt based
on the tracked data type. For instance, tracking rumination and intrusive thoughts
requires a different approach than tracking symptoms like panic attacks or exercises,
as we will be comparing different aspects between them. Further research is needed
to determine whether different types of tracked data require different visualizations.
To address the research question of what the key barriers to data inter-
pretation are for users with different levels of data literacy (see 1.2), it was
not directly determined, as conducting the studies with a single user group may not
fully reflect the specific needs of the target users (narrow user segment), which are
the patients and therapists.
One of the challenges faced during this research was that the data used for testing
was mock data, which may not reflect realistic values. This introduces a potential bias,
as the visualizations might not have conveyed the same insights or clarity when applied
to real data. Also the design was made in Figma, which does not fully replicate an
actual system. Additionally, in each iteration, there were recurring comments from
users expressing what they actually have to focus on. Many participants, seemed
unsure and kept repeating they are not therapists themselves and might not have
been able to fully understand what visualizations are needed to assess the patient’s
case.
A key diﬀiculty I encountered was the lack of clear purpose in the validation
process. I often found myself uncertain about what I was aiming to validate, which
ultimately hindered my ability to experiment with different visualization types. This
uncertainty, together with the limited user research, made it challenging to create the
design that meets the needs of the target users effectively.
These factors underline the complexity of designing effective visualization tools in
a context where users have varying levels of expertise and where data interpretation
can be subjective [21]. The next steps in the research should focus on testing with
real data and refining the visualizations based on actual users’ feedback.
Finally, it is not possible to assess the effectiveness of the introduced visualizations,
as each of them has advantages and disadvantages and it really depends on the user
past experience or external factors. Even if, some might work very well for certain
user segment, it is impossible to state if it will be applicable to other user group
placed in different context. It confirms the statement made by Shneiderman [38] and
[40], that creating a successful interface doesn’t rely on a single formula. Different
visualizations perform differently depending on the context, so understanding the
data, user and the message being communicated is the key [32].

Future work
“Data is the new oil? No, data is the new soil.”
— David McCandless
There are several areas for future work that could enhance the system’s effective-
ness and usability. First, introducing an application for patients could allow them to
engage more actively with their data and make them aware of their current status.
However, this raises concerns about potential biases, as patients might alter their
tracking behavior if they e.g. notice a rise in symptom severity. Further research is
needed to assess the impact of such access on data reliability and user engagement.
Data upload should be introduce as a real-time data flow from the patient’s device
to the therapist dashboard, removing the need of manual upload.
Another potential feature is integrating location tracking, which could provide ad-
ditional context for symptom occurrences and enhance recall during therapy sessions.
Another area of improvement is customization.
Users should have the option
to adjust the visualizations based on their tracking phenomena, ensuring the tool
remains flexible for different therapeutic needs.
Lastly, further investigation is required to refine the role of therapists in the sys-
tem. Since therapists are the primary users of the platform, future research should
explore what types of data visualizations best support their decision-making process,
by incorporating the actual users into the process. Understanding how they interact
with the dashboard could lead to enhancements that better align with their workflow.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
“Graphical excellence is that which gives to the viewer the
greatest number of ideas in the shortest time with the least ink
in the smallest space”
— Edward Tufte, The Visual Display of Quantitative
Information
This research highlights the critical role that self-tracking tools play in support-
ing users to reflect on and discover patterns in their symptoms. The final design
successfully integrated key principles from visualization research to offer a structured
approach for users to track and analyze their symptoms over time. Through an it-
erative design process, the system’s usability was significantly enhanced, with each
version improving clarity, eﬀiciency, and accessibility based on user feedback.
Shneiderman’s principles [38] were applied to ensure that the system balanced
simplicity, clarity, and engagement.
However, the primary focus was on enabling
users, particularly therapists, to quickly understand the visualizations and gain in-
sights eﬀiciently, rather than fostering prolonged engagement with the interface. The
introduction of custom visualizations, such as the logbook and therapy session cal-
endar, addressed the initial pain points and enhanced the system’s overall clarity.
Visualizations like line charts and spider charts provided multiple perspectives on
symptom patterns, although some users initially found them diﬀicult to interpret.
The aesthetic design of the system was positively received, with many users high-
lighting the user-friendly interface. Visual appeal and clear visual elements were key
to ensuring users could navigate the system without feeling overwhelmed by unneces-
sary complexity. However, the challenge of creating meaningful correlations between
tracked phenomena remained unresolved, partly due to the lack of direct engagement
with actual users. The limitations in the validation process also hindered the ability
to experiment with alternative visualization strategies, particularly when using mock
data.
Despite these challenges, the system succeeded in meeting the initial goal of pro-
viding a tool for tracking and analyzing patient data, especially by introducing a
logbook. However, the effectiveness of the introduced visualizations cannot be uni-
versally assessed, as their success depends on factors such as user experience and
context.

Looking forward, future work should focus on incorporating real-world patient
data, refining visualizations based on expert feedback, and investigating the impact
of allowing patients access to their data. Customization of visualizations, location
tracking, and further exploration of the therapist’s role are also important areas for
future research.
In conclusion, while the system demonstrated promise in supporting symptom
tracking and analysis, future development should continue to refine the user experi-
ence.

that doesn’t mean it will be user-friendly and easy to navigate. Even if you follow up with open-ended questions, the qualitative user feedback could be too vague to be of any 
use because users aren’t always able to explain their own preferences.  it’s really easy to wind up with inaccurate or incomplete data. And if  design iterations are based on faulty 
findings, they can go off the rail pretty quickly.
