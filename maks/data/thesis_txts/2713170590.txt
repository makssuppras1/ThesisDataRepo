Abstract
There is an increasing trend in the number of private cars in the city. However, the
public parking resources are limited. This study presents a shared parking app design
idea for ParkShare, a Denmark start-up established by Astrid Vinther Andersen in
2018. The thesis investigates the question: how could a shared parking application
help drivers find a parking spot in different scenarios and provide them with a
user-friendly experience with the process of the double diamond design model
(Design Council, 2005) and methods of Design Thinking (Stanford d.school.,2018).
The ParkShare app was designed following the five elements of UX (Benyon,
2019,p.84). Target groups of ParkShare users are defined as 1. Hovering birds: People
who are finding an available parking lot when driving. 2. Planners: People who are
planning a trip before departing. 3, Renters: People who need long-term access to a
parking space. Based on the needs of target groups and the different scenarios that
will be encountered, the main functions and information architecture of the app are
defined. Finally, a high-fidelity prototype that approximates the user interface and
interaction of the final product is created and tested for good usability and user
experience.
Keywords: Shared parking service, User experience, App design, User interface.

1.Introduction
1.1Background
There is an increasing trend in the number of private cars in the city. However, the
public parking resources are limited. It is unfeasible for the government or
entrepreneurs to build an equal number of parking lots for the public. Finding a
parking space is a challenge for the citizen, especially during rush hour. A study
estimated that, in every day's rush hour, around 30% of traffic congestion is caused by
cars hovering over available parking lots (Liu et al., 2017). The parking problem will
bring more air pollution and cause traffic congestion. Studies show that, in general,
people spend a maximum of 14 mins in the congested urban area to find a suitable
parking lot (Shoup et al., 2012). For the driver, longer hovering time on the road
means more driving stress, restless emotions, and traffic risks. Especially when
hosting sporting events, the parking problems are worse than during rush hour.
With the booming sharing economy, the private parking space sharing idea is
becoming popular and attracts more and more people to join. Those ideas serve for
other routine scenarios. For example, Mobypark offers a parking lot booking service
mainly for tourists. Their business now covers ten countries in Europe
(mobypark.com, 2022). Lai et al. (2020) stated an idea for reducing office workers'
parking costs and commuting time. This shared parking space service will recommend
the private parking lot in the closest community. ParkShare, an Aarhus start-up, offers
a shared parking booking service for hourly, monthly, and event parking
(ParkShare.com, 2022).
Peer-to-peer shared parking platforms like Mobypark and ParkShare provide
customers with booking services like Airbnb. The booking service requires customers
to know when and where they park. However, it was learned from the interview that,
usually, drivers would not plan where to park or limit their parking time. Finding
parking for the driver is a problem be solved ten minutes before the destination. Thus,
there is a contradiction between the solution of the reservation service and the
emotional problem. What is more, ParkShare contacts customers through email at
present. This inefficient communication also needs to be improved.
==PAGE:5==
1.2 The research problem
This thesis will be based on the existing service concept of ParkShare and investigate
the question: how could a shared parking application help drivers to find a parking
spot in different scenarios and provide them with a user-friendly experience.
1.3 The outcome
The outcome of the thesis is a Hi-fi prototype of the ParkShare application, which is
defined through the combined process of double diamond model and design thinking.
1.4 Delimitations
This thesis will focus only on the app's design for the user (drivers or people who
need a parking spot). The intelligent parking lock mentioned in the final solution
concept will not be investigated in depth. The user interface design will follow
ParkShare's brand standard and design with colors #2464D3 and #EA3583. For the
time limitation, the app will be only designed for iOS mobile devices.
1.5 The structure
The structure of the thesis presents as follows: First, in Chapter 2, a literature review
on the present and relative works will be investigated. Chapter 3 presents the
methodologies and design tools used to explore present problems, users’ needs, and
pain points. Chapter 4 presents how the ParkShare app was produced, designed, tested,
and iterated. Finally, Chapter 5 reflects on the main finding of the thesis.

Chapter 2. Literature Review
This chapter will examine the academic literature on technical feasibility and users'
behaviors relative to shared parking services.
2.1 Technical feasibility and marketing mechanism for
developing a shared parking service
Auction mechanism for Incentivized Shared Parking
Public parking spots are regarded as scarce resources, especially in urban areas. Quite
a few studies propose solutions tried to solve this problem from different aspects. Tan
et al. (2019) focused on solving the problem of pricing and allocating shared parking
spots. They combined the price-compatible top trading cycles and chains mechanism
(Xu et al., 2016) and two sequential auction mechanisms. Based on this mechanism,
they have introduced the concept of auctions into the pricing and allocation of parking
spaces. The highest bidder will have the right to use the space, which means that the
owner of the space will be able to earn more than expected. This kind of monetary
incentive would motivate idle parking space owners to rent out their parking lots.
Besides, the amount of auctioned parking spaces is proportional to the utility of
bidders, which means the more parking spaces, the more profits the platform will
make with a sequential auction mechanism.
Location privacy and malicious user
One of the significant problems of implementing private parking space sharing,
presented by Liu et al. (2017), is that users are unwilling to disclose their privacy
about location or time. For example, a pessimistic scenario could be malicious users
occupying the parking space without officially booking because they know the
temporal information of when the space is available.
Fu et al. (2020) designed a homomorphic encryption-based privacy protection
matching scheme for the problem above. Furthermore, the person-to-person shared
parking business is complex for commuters due to the administration of gated
residential units and opposition from neighbors who co-ownership of the parking lot
(Lai et al., 2020).
Anonymous transaction and Blockchain
Rozman et al. (2020) propose a privacy-protected parking space rental mechanism
based on which users and providers can transact anonymously online. Users sign
smart contracts online and use their contracts to unlock IoT parking locks when
parking. They compared implementations on three public blockchain networks and
evaluated the system through user-oriented tests. In their evaluation, they found that
each time a user wanted to get a new token, they had to wait for Witness generation
==PAGE:7==
and proof generation and that these times had the most significant impact on the user
experience.
Access for Returning Users and Uncertainty Handling
Some research focuses on the uncertainty of parking space sharing due to the busy
parking time. One problem is ensuring the owner of the parking lot has a place to park
when they return. This problem is defined as the uncertainties of the arrival and
departure of public users and owner users by Zhao et al. (2020). Thus, Zhao et
al. (2020) introduced a management framework from both temporal and spatial
dimensions. What is more, an intelligent parking management system was developed.
SParking, A win-win data-driven scheduling shared parking system was designed by
Zhu et al. (2020). The idea of Zhu et al. (2020) automatically calculates the period
that the owner does not use their private parking space and then rents it to public users.
A noteworthy point is data from Zhu et al. (2020) shows that weather condition is an
essential factor in the available period of renting, especially on the weekend.
2.2 Users’ preference for shared parking service and
behaviors
Users willing of using shared parking service
Liang et al. (2019) users’ (who demand a parking spot) intentions to use shared
parking service with the Combined Technology Acceptance Model and the Theory of
Planned Behavior (C-TAM-TPB). The results show that perceived usefulness (PU)
affects users’ intention most, thus providers of shared parking services should focus
on promoting the value and convenience that the service can bring to users.
Perceived behavioral control (PBC) is one of the main factors driving users to join
shared parking services. It means that people who can use or understand a shared
parking service are more likely to become users of that service. Improving users’ PBC
to the system requires a shared parking system that gives the user easy-to-understand
information such as the location, the time of booking, the time of leaving, and where
to pick up the car.
Users' preference for shared parking service features
Ardeshiri et al. (2021) focused on people’s preference for different features of the
shared parking service. Data shows people are most willing to pay for the shared
parking lots with CCTV and are suitable for SUV size or medium size. Features that
are less attractive to users are motorcycle-sized parking spots, outdoor parking,
monthly rented parking spots, and parking spots only rented during commuting hours.
They developed two potential classes’ behavior models based on a sample of 1,008
New South Wales, Australia residents. The average age of the first class is likely to be
37.1, they are well-educated, have an objective annual income ($89,330 per
==PAGE:8==
household), and are couples with children. The second class is elder (41.3 on average)
and earns more than class 1 ($109,685 per household).
Their research shows that both classes are more interested in shared parking lots
indoors than outdoors. Class 2 prefers a locked garage most. For the size of the shared
parking lots, both classes are not interested in motorbikes size, and class 1 prefers the
size of medium-sized cars or SUVs. Shared parking lots with CCTV attract class 1
most. Class 2 would like the car park equipped with CCTV and a toilet or car service
nearby. The study also mentioned that the increasing time cost of walking between
destination and parking and the cost per hour negatively impact decision-making for
both classes.
2.3 Key takeaways
The inspiration from the literature review is that there is a need to safeguard the user's
privacy and the security of the transaction in the first place. When providing
information about parking spaces, it is not necessary to reveal the personal
information of the owner of the parking space to the user, and the transaction is
anonymous. In order to ensure that others do not maliciously occupy the reserved
parking space, intelligent parking locks are an essential touchpoint in the entire
service system. In terms of user preferences, users also prefer parking spaces with
property security, such as indoor spaces, lockable garages, and parking spaces with
CCTV.
Secondly, there is a need to emphasize the value of using shared parking services to
users. Generally, the value could be that it helps the user save time and money in
finding a parking space. From the user interface aspect, providing the user with
easy-to-understand information or accessibility can also make the service more
convenient.
The shortcoming of these studies is that they either focus on the back end of the
system, attempt to address these issues from digital technology systems’ perspective,
or only state users’ preferences and willingness. Neither of them emphasizes the
aspects associated with interacting directly with the user.
This thesis focuses on developing the ParkShare app based on the five elements of
User experience: strategy, scope, structure, skeleton, and surface. It investigates how
to provide a good experience for the user (who needs a parking space) through a
sound functional architecture, information architecture, and interface design. The
outcome is a Hi-fi prototype of the ParkShare app, designed to maximize the
functionality and interaction of the final application.

Chapter 3. Methodology & Methods
This chapter introduces the methodologies and research tools used in the thesis.
3.1 The framework
The thesis framework is a combination of the double diamond design model (Design
Council, 2005) and Design Thinking (Stanford d.school.,2018). As shown in figure
3.1, the process will follow the sequences of the four steps of the double diamond.
During the process, specific design methods are chosen based on the five steps of
design thinking (Empathize, Define, ideate, prototype, and test).
First, two main goals of the Empathize step are understanding users and investigating
the problem of finding available parking lots. Then in the Define step, specific
functions of the shared parking space service will be defined following the first three
elements of UX design: Strategy, Scope, and Structure (Benyon, 2019,p.84). The third
step is to ideate the interactive medium and skeleton and the surface layer of the
human-computer interaction interface that the medium has. The last two steps are
iteration and verification. Design concepts will be realized as the prototype and
applied in user testing. After the double diamond process, the final solution of shared
parking space service is produced.

3.2 Discover: Empathize
This section introduces methods for investigating users, their problems, and the
existing experience of parking.
3.2.1 Interview
In total, six interviewers participated in the user interview. The rate of males and
females is 1:1. Interviewers are between 20 to 30 years old and have international
backgrounds. Most interviewers live in Denmark, some study and live in America,
and some state their parking experience in China.
The user interview is launched around the problem of finding parking spots. The
primary purpose is to learn about drivers’ behaviors, pain points, and habits. The
structure of the interview is as follows:
1.
Intro: First, welcome the interviewee with a brief introduction of the project and
create comfort.
2.
Basic information: Before asking questions related to the project, collect
interviewees’ basic information such as age, gender, career, etc. One of the
purposes is to break the ice through common topics quickly. Another is collecting
user feature information, which provides a reference for Personas’ creation.
3.
Parking problems: In this part, I will ask interviewees about their experience
finding parking spot problems during the weekdays or holidays. When
interviewees tell their stories, they might hide some information or cannot
describe it clearly due to vague memory. Therefore, asking them more “Why”
and “How” motivates them.
==PAGE:14==
4.
Desire and co-creation: This part will introduce the shared parking space service
concept. Interviewees are invited to share their opinions and imagination of how
this service helps.
5.
Outro: Last, remember to thank their participants and ask them to leave contact
information for return visits.
After the user interview, the core value that the shared parking space service could
provide drivers according to the existing problem will be defined.

3.2.2 Persona
From the user interview, the core values that ParkShare app could provide to users are
defined. Every core value could be seen as a motivation that drives target users to use
the app. Target users are divided into three types with different intentions and typical
users’ profiles of each type are presented with Persona. The profile includes the
following contents (Boeijen et al., 2020, p.115):
1.
Basic information：Describe who they are, where they are from, and what they
do. Besides, write down a quote that represents the personality most.
2.
Personality: Visualize the persona’s character from four aspects for better
understanding.
3.
Goals：What they want to achieve from the shared parking space service.
4.
Challenge: Problems that they find difficult to solve right now.
5.
The story：The story tells what users currently do when they encounter parking
problems. It reflects users’ pain points and needs in specific scenarios through
storytelling.
3.2.3 Contextmapping
Contextmapping is applied as the next step to modify the preconceptions of personas’
stories and make the user journey closer to the fact. Contextmapping is a user-center
design tool that invites users to co-design with their expert experiences (Boeijen et al.,
2014).
The key to contextmapping is making users immersive in the estimated context. It is
defined by Boeijen et al. (2014) as to where the product is used. In this section, the
term context is closer to the context where the user journey will happen. Users with
similar features to those four personas are invited to co-create and refine the users’
journey of finding parking spots.
Due to COVID-19, it was more difficult to organize a multi-person event. So, the
designer (i.e., me) used one-to-one interviews to draw the contextmapping with the
participants. the process is:
1. introduce the participants to the four user roles and ask them to choose the user role
closest to them.
==PAGE:16==
2. Based on the user role chosen by the participants, the participants were asked to
draw a contextmapping of their own life experiences, from leaving, driving, and
finding a parking space to parking.
Figure 3.2 - Interveners are discussing and drawing the user journey
3.2.4 User journey map
The user journey map (Benyon, 2019, p.87) helps sort pain points and design
opportunities at every stage in the find-parking process, guiding the function
definition and information architecture.
With the hand-draw users through contextmapping drafts, personas’ actions during
the related process of finding parking spots and parking are merged in a user journey
map. There are two-step flows on the map, one presents the existing experience of
finding parking lots, and the other assumes the improved user flow with the
application of shared parking service.
3.3 Define: Define
In this section, all scattered information such as users’ needs, pain points, and design
opportunities are synthesized into problems that wait to be solved by design.
3.3.1 Problem definition
Before designing the app from a functional perspective, problems that our service
wants to solve must be defined first. The problem definition is a structured way,
mentioned by Boeijen et al. (2014), which explicitly describes problems. Based on
that, transfer all the user needs, opportunities, and pain points found in the Empathize
step into issues with a problem definition structure:
1. Who explains which target user group will encounter the problem?
2. Explore the motivation and expectation of users to solve the problem
through Why.
3. Identifies the consequences of the problem by answering What if.
==PAGE:17==
3.4 Develop: Ideate
In this section, feasible solutions are developed for the defined problems.
3.4.1 Brainstorming
Brainstorming is used to get more and more diverging ideas of feasible solutions with
the problem list. In the first step, for each problem, there is at most 1 minute to
generate general solution ideas. If there is no idea, it can jump into another one. Then
in the second round, for each solution topic, there are 2 minutes to write explanatory
notes. Similar solutions are merged into three aspects: application function, service,
and product.
3.4.2 Storyboard
After solution brainstorming and evaluation, the various touchpoints in the service
system (application functions, service sessions, and physical products) have been
produced. However, the touchpoints are still independent. Thus the storyboard is used
to explain how these touchpoints cooperate in the service system.
In persona, storytelling is used to define the problems that users encounter in different
scenarios. Then, at this stage, the storyboard is used to demonstrate how the various

3.5.1 Information architecture - Card sorting
The solutions to app functionality produced from the brainstorming will be used as
the basis for the information architecture. However, before proceeding with the
functional architecture, card sorting is a necessary step, which enables the division of
product features closer to the user's mental model before the test, inviting test subjects
==PAGE:18==
with different characteristics such as gender, age, and driving age so that the result
would be closer to the habits of general.
Thirty-two cards are prepared with potential functions that feature on the application.
Then, invite test subjects to group the cards that they consider related. Hesitation is
permitted during grouping; test subjects are welcome to create an unknown area for
alone functions. After categorizing all the cards, ask test subjects to name each group
meanwhile telling why and how they name that.
3.5.2 Hierarchical structure - tree
After card sorting, all functions are categorized in a way close to users’ mental model,
scoping the skeleton of the app in breadth. Since putting all contents and categories on
the same page is impossible (Benyon, 2019, p.350), the content presented to users
should be different from the user flow. So, it is necessary to use a structural approach
to hierarchize each content, category, and item at the depth level of the app.
The process of creating a tree diagram is as follows:
1. First, create the function architecture with the card sorting results.
2. Then, create a detailed text-based tree diagram to clarify the functional
structure of the product.
3. The tree diagram will be put into the tree testing to ensure the name of
categories, functions, and information are understandable (Pia Klancar,
November 23, 2021). Five test subjects proficient in English are invited to the
tree testing, and their task is to find the specific function from the homepage.
The testing tasks are set as:



You are going to the city center this Sunday, book a spot for that.

You are going to an event this weekend. Book a spot for that.

Add a credit card.

Edit the car information.
After the tree testing, more detailed information architecture is created. Here, the
nature of contents is marked, such as interface information, buttons, pop-ups, and
warnings. Content that will be repeated or accessed at different functional levels will
be connected in the diagram by dotted lines to form a network (Benyon, 2019, p.351).

3.5.5 Cheatstorming
Cheatstorming is a variation of brainstorming in which the ideation phase of the
traditional brainstorming process will be skipped and directly uses the old ideas from
the idea pool of earlier brainstorming. The idea pool in this section contains the
design ideas analyzed in competitor analysis.
The purpose of cheatstorming is to redesign the app interfaces that interact with users
in seven specific scenarios based on competitor interfaces and user flows utilizing
design enhancement, integration of advantages, and innovation of interaction forms.
The detailed process is as follows:
1. Create an editable drawing board in Miro and pour it into the competitor
analysis table like the idea bank.
==PAGE:20==
2. Conduct cheatstorming in seven rounds, each based on a concrete scenario.
The designer (i.e., me) will use sticky notes to label ideas based on the

3. In the end, the designer (i.e., me) will organize the ideas and create
wireframes.

4.4.2 Information architecture
First, derived from the card sorting results, three main function categories of the app
are decided:
1. Search for a spot: This function category is on the app's homepage. It includes
functions for finding a parking spot immediately.
2. Reserve a spot: This category serves users planning a trip or needing a long-term
parking space rental.
3. My spot order: All functions users need during the parking process are included in
this category.
The "Spot exchange" function is considered a unique service and will be available as
a separate category. However, this service was a conceptual solution, not related to
ParkShare's service model, and was therefore removed. Other functions such as
account information or management are combined in "My account".
Then a preliminary version of the information architecture (Appendix X) was created
and tested. The feedback received from the testing was as follows:
1. When the scenario is set to find a parking spot immediately while driving or
hovering, it is incredibly inconvenient for drivers to use the app. In such a case,
users are more willing to zoom in on the map to find a nearby parking space or
to navigate to a parking space with only one click rather than searching with a
search bar or filter.
2. When users need a parking space, the only basis for finding it is the location of
the destination, no matter what the trip's purpose. Thus, in the interface, the
proportion of functions "Parking for the event" and function "Parking for
scenery" will be reduced.
3. The user profile is not an essential part of the app, with the primary function of
finding and reserving parking spaces. The functions related to personal
information management or system management will be unified in the same
category, and their proportion in the interface will be weakened.
The information architecture is iterated with users' feedback to the final version
(shown in Appendix 2). Changes and new contents beneath each main category are as
follows:
Find a spot now: Immediately finding a nearby parking spot is the highest priority
function category that users can access when entering the app. There are three ways
for them to find a parking spot. First, the user can view the nearby parking spots
directly on the map. Or, if the user can spend more attention on the app, they can
choose to "show the spots list" to compare or filter the parking spots they feel most
==PAGE:36==
satisfied with. Finally, if the user is mainly focused on driving, simply clicking "Click
for a spot" will navigate the user to the nearest parking spot.
Reserve a spot: No matter when or why the user needs a parking spot, the first action
when they find the spot is inserting the destination into the search bar. Thus, during
the test, subjects ignored the function "Reserve a spot" and used the search bar
directly. To tolerate this fault, an additional step was added asking the user, after
inserting the destination in the search bar, whether they want the location immediately
or to keep it.
My: The third category - My includes all functions of setting, editing, and managing.
Parking order information has moved into this category to meet users' general using
habits with applications.
Parking Order pop-up: When a user has a parking order in progress, the user's
primary goal of using the application changes from finding a parking space to
tracking the parking order. In this case, a pop-up will be provided on the home page to
view the order.

Chapter 5: Conclusion
This chapter concludes this thesis's main problems, solutions, and contributions. It
includes the answer to the research problem defined in section 1.2. Following, the
chapter will also discuss the limitation and future works.
In summary, this thesis developed a shared parking service app concept (faces to users
with parking spot demands) from the five elements of UX based on the double
diamond and design thinking methodology.
5.1 Summary of the problem
ParkShare is a Denmark start-up established by Astrid Vinther Andersen in 2018. It’s
a shared parking platform and plans to develop its own app. This study will take it as
an aim, trying to figure out how to design the app to be user-friendly and effective in
different scenarios.
Users’ problems
Problems that users are concerned about during the process of finding parking spots
and parking are concluded into three categories.
1. Users cannot ensure there is an available parking spot near the destination when
arriving.
2. Users cannot ensure the parking spot meets their needs, satisfies them, or is suitable
for their car.
3. Problems when leaving a parking space include finding the way back to the car and
payment issues.
5.2 The solution
The final solution concept includes the ParkShare app and an intelligence parking
lock. The parking process with the solution is as follows: the user looks for a parking
space through the app and makes a reservation. The intelligent parking lock ensures
that others do not occupy the reserved space. When the user arrives at the parking
space, the app unlocks the space, and parking begins.
The core value and main functions of ParkShare app
1.
Quickly find an available parking spot near the destination.
2.
Offer drivers the reservation service for special needs such as long-term rental or
planning for a trip.
==PAGE:53==
3.
Services related to the parking process, such as unlocking parking spaces,
checking orders, paying fees, etc.
5.3 Outcomes of the study
The outcome of the thesis is a Hi-fi prototype of the ParkShare application, which
received high commends on usability and user experience from the testing.
The key findings of the paper are summarized in terms of five elements of UX:
1. Strategy
This paper identifies three core values of the Park Share app based on facts and user
pain points through the interview for empathy. Three categories of target users were
defined based on three user needs for parking spaces, and a typical user profile was
created.
2. Scope
The functions, services, and content of the Shared parking app were sorted out
according to the user's needs.
3. Structure
This thesis uses a card taxonomy to create a functional and information structure for
the app that is as close as possible to the user's mental model and uses logic.
4. Skeleton
The framework of the interface is based on a concrete scenario, ensuring that the
functions, interface elements, and content are arranged logically and that the interface
meets the user's needs in different scenarios.
5. Surface
The Hi-fi prototype is as close to the final product's user interface and interaction. A
task-based user validation was carried out to provide inspiration and guidance for
iterations to improve the user experience.
5.4 Limitations
The limitations of this study stem from several aspects. Firstly, ParkShare is currently
only focused on the Danish market, which means that ParkShare users have a solid
Danish profile. However, due to the international background of COVID-19 and the
designer (i.e., me), it is difficult to interview large amounts of users or to interview
users with a Danish background. In this thesis, the selection of interviewees or
participants was based only on whether they could drive and weakened the nationality
criteria, prioritizing ensuring that the target number of interviews was reached.
Secondly, due to the time limitation, only one iteration of the final design prototype
was conducted. Worse, there was no significant difference between the user
experience data before and after the iteration to verify whether the iterations enhanced
the user experience.
==PAGE:54==
5.5 Future work
Future works of this study could launch from these directions:
1.
Further iterations of the user interface and Hi-fi prototype.
After the iteration test, participants also gave feedback on the version2 prototypes. For
example, in the iterated interfaces of ending parking guidance (figure 4.25), users
commented that the information provided throughout the page was too much and
repetitive and that they did not know whether to focus on the parking guidance
animation or the receipt information. They suggested that the parking receipt could be
a separate interface that appears after the parking process is complete.
In addition, due to time constraints, many interfaces were not designed in the
prototype, for example, how the different types of booking orders should be presented
during the process.
2.
Re-run user testing and collection of user experience data.
This paper obtained suboptimal test data before and after the iterations were carried
out, making it impossible to compare whether the iterations positively affected the
user experience. The reason for this is speculated to be that the flow of only one
feature was modified with little difference from the pre-iteration interface, and the
testers rated the user experience based on overall impressions. User experience data
could be collected for the interfaces involved in a single process in subsequent tests.
3.
Research on smart locks
This study is only concerned with the app's design, while the whole shared parking
system cannot function without the cooperation of intelligent parking locks. Further
research could explore the intelligent parking lock's product design and
implementation.
