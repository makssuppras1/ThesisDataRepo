Abstract
This thesis addresses the challenge of processing automated marine gravity surveys by
developing graphical and software tools that support the interpretation and refinement of
correction strategies. While crossing misfits between survey lines can be closed math-
ematically, such adjustments do not guarantee physically consistent results. The tools
presented here are grounded in a thorough theoretical understanding of gravity data be-
haviour elements of automated marine gravity surveys and enable a semi-automated,
human-in-the-loop workflow for identifying and correcting anomalies. By integrating di-
agnostic visualisations and standardised figure generation into a flexible graphical user
interface (GUI), the project bridges the gap between automated processing and informed
interpretation, and establishes a foundation for future cross-survey modelling improve-
ments.

Introduction
Marine gravity data is an important resource in geophysical studies, underpinning both
offshore and onshore applications in geodesy, tectonics, and crustal modelling. Accu-
rate processing of this data enables a better understanding of subsurface structures and
contributes to regional and global gravity models. However, automated marine gravity
surveys in Danish waters are rarely conducted under ideal conditions . They are often
affected by weather, patchy navigation data, and limited control over survey design — we
do not decide where the ship sails. As a result, the data returned from these surveys fre-
quently requires significant correction and post-processing before it can be reliably used.
A central challenge in processing automated marine gravity data lies in the identification
and correction of crossing misfits between survey lines, as seen in Figure 5.1. Crossings
— where survey points are close enough to measure the same gravity signal — are used
for validating internal consistency. Yet the software used in current processing pipelines
can misclassify and misinterpret these crossings. These misclassifications degrade the
quality of the final products and can lead to incorrect interpretations. Additionally, other
persistent sources of errors complicate processing, such as poor data synchronisation
between navigation and gravity data, and external noise.
Although prior student projects (Catania 2022 and Jørgensen 2021 and more), and de-
velopment efforts have brought the processing pipeline close to full automation, complete
automation remains unrealistic. In practice, software decisions are based on assumptions
that often fail in edge cases. This is particularly problematic when interpreting complex
survey geometries or integrating older datasets. Consequently, there is a critical need for
a semi-automated, human-in-the-loop processing framework that allows users to review,
validate, and correct software interpretations efficiently and systematically.
This thesis addresses that need by presenting a structured, flexible, and standardised
graphical user interface (GUI) and backend routine for inspecting and correcting auto-
mated marine gravity survey results. The focus is on improving tools for crosspoint clas-
sification, figure generation, and comparative analysis — both within individual surveys
and across multiple datasets. These tools support a gradual transition from the current
stand-alone (SA) marine processing approach to a more integrated Cross-Survey Mod-
elling (CSM) framework.
At the core of this transition is the pseudo Free-Air anomaly (pFA) transformation — a
method that ensures all surveys are translated into a shared, comparable setting. This
common quantity allows results to be directly compared across different surveys and
against external models, such as global gravity fields or free-air anomalies (FA) from
satellite altimetry. In this way, pFA becomes the common diagnostic, through which data
interoperability and correction consistency are maintained.
Crossings play a second vital role: not only do they validate internal consistency within a
survey, but they also offer control points between surveys. While intra-survey crossings
are finite and static, inter-survey crossings (extra-crossings) provide a growing network
of comparison points as more surveys are conducted. This unlocks new opportunities for
re-evaluating previously processed data in light of newly collected information — a key
feature of CSM where “nothing is set in stone”.

Another major contribution of this work is its emphasis on visual standardisation. Con-
sistent, repeatable figures are crucial for interpretation, quality control, and collaboration.
The GUI introduced here supports this by generating clear, comparable outputs that share
a common visual language. This consistency enables users to quickly understand and
assess the results, even across datasets processed at different times or by different peo-
ple.
While many modern workflows turn to AI to handle complexity, this project demonstrates
that classical graphical methods — grounded in physical theory and user understanding
of conditions at sea — can often provide more transparent and reliable insights. Errors
in interpretation, both from the software and from external models like altimetry, become
more visible and tractable when examined through well-designed visual tools. The GUI
does not just automate; it empowers the user to make informed corrections where needed.
Lastly, the work reflects the evolving nature of marine gravity processing. As new surveys
are acquired, they offer not just new data but new contexts for previously processed re-
sults. This recursive aspect of CSM is supported by the modular, extensible design of the
software tools developed in this thesis — laying a foundation for future development and
integration with more complex modelling approaches.
1.1
Thesis contributions
This work contributes to the marine gravity processing workflow in the following ways:
• Development of a flexible, GUI-based system for manual inspection and correction
of crossing errors and anomalies.
• Improved figure generation routines that support visual consistency and compara-
tive analysis across survey datasets.
• Introduction of backend logic to support cross-survey modelling scenarios.
• Establishment of a workflow that balances automation with user oversight, paving
the way for future fully-automated processing frameworks.

2.2
Pseudo Free-air gravity anomalies
Marine gravimetry is classified as relative gravimetry. The readings of the marine gravime-
ter, rCU, can be used to determine the gravity difference with respect to a specific point
Pref in a reference harbour, where the absolute gravity gref is known. CU stands for
4

counter-units, which are unique output readings for each marine gravimeter. Pref is as-
sociated with a point on the pier Ppier, in the vicinity of the vessel location in the reference
harbour. The absolute gravity gpier for Ppier is obtained from land by land gravimeters;
a harbour tie. Routinely, the level of water with regards to the pier is also recorded as
part of harbour tie. The reference gravity value gref for the marine survey is subsequently
obtained from gpier by appropriate reductions. Without going into details, and depend-
ing on the convention, the vertical reference level of Pref can be at sea-level, at marine
gravimeter sensor-level, or as it is in this case, at pier level, Pref = Ppier. Consequently
the reference gravity value for Pref, gref, is obtained from gpier after possibly correcting
for different water levels at the time of harbour tie and vessel stay. During the reference
harbour stay, a reference marine gravimeter reading rCU,ref is obtained as the average
reading in CU.
In principle, the scaling factor α, which is characteristic for each marine gravimeter, should
be determined empirically on a calibration line, but the manufacturers often aim to have
α ≈1mgal
CU . The marine gravimeter reading difference rCU −rCU,ref is multiplied with
this scaling factor, which converts readings in CU to gravity differences in m/s2 or mGal
(1 mGal = 10−5m/s2). In total, the equation for relative gravimetry reads
g(t) = gref + α(rCU(t) −rCU,ref)
(2.5)
where g(t) is the absolute gravity value determined for time t during the survey.
In practice however, measurements at sea are done in highly turbulent conditions which
affect readings of a gravimeter. According to my supervisor, experience shows that im-
plementing standard corrections (“best field practices”) as recommended by gravimeter
manufacturers are not always sufficient. In order for Equation 2.5 to be valid, the recorded
output reading, erCU(t), of marine gravimeters needs to be corrected by a total correction
budget cg(t).
g(t) = gref + α((erCU(t) −cg(t)) −rCU,ref)
(2.6)
The above equation connects what the marine gravimeter actually records, erCU(t) and
the estimate of absolute gravity g(t) along survey lines. Correction budget cg(t) includes
all standard corrections and possibly additional corrections.
2.2.1
Crossings as a verification tool
One important verification element of marine gravity processing at sea is to study the re-
sults at approximately the same point in space, but for different times; the crossing of
survey lines. If cg(t) is correct and survey point P(t1) = P(t2), where t1 ̸= t2, we should
expect from equation Equation 2.6 that g(t1) = g(t2). This statement is a simplification
because if g(t1) = g(t2), it only means that it is valid for this specific location of the cross-
point and not necessarily for the whole survey. Furthermore, the different conditions at
sea for these two different times can mean that the local sea level height is different.
Referring to Equation 2.4, one meter difference in sea level height at two different times
amounts theoretically to a free-air difference of ≈−0.3 mGal. The automatic surveys that
were used in this work have only 2D navigation (lat, lon), so the height information is not
even there. Fortunately it is possible to transform Equation 2.6 to quantities that are only
weakly dependent on such height differences at different times, pseudo free-air gravity
anomalies.

2.2.4
Strategy for modelling additional corrections
Finding additional corrections for a given survey is not straightforward because a misfit
can be minimised by either moving a current line towards a crossing line, the crossing line
towards the current line, or by moving both lines toward each other to close the misfit. So
the decision of which line to move and by how much must be justified based on detailed
interpretation of the survey conditions at the time of measurements. This is a delicate
process which cannot easily be automated. We need additional tools which help inter-
pretation, i.e the graphical tools that are explained in the next section, subsection 2.2.5.
There is one additional strategical decision which needs to be mentioned. We have cho-
sen a strategy where it is only the current line of the current project, cline of CPROJ, that is
being corrected. The consequence is that the correction is possibly not done in one step,
but also leaves place for the crossing line, xline, being corrected versus cline at a later
stage. Ideally we would like to correct all lines at once, but that is simply not possible.
According to my supervisor, in previous versions of the software, the correction of survey
lines were determined using mathematical adjustment tools. This ensures that the misfits

2.2.5
The Graphical tools
The graphical tools created for this thesis are the backbone for interpreting the results
of the correction budget. They allow us to quickly assess the corrections, to see if there
are any glaring errors that need to be addressed and also give us a contextual clues for
misfits.
There is a clear need for a standardised approach to graphically representing marine
gravity surveys — one that is both consistent and flexible enough to accommodate fu-
ture extensions. This standardisation should cover not only the visual layout, but also
naming conventions for variables and figures, which have previously been prone to in-
consistency. A unified format becomes especially important given the growing number of
surveys, the existence of multiple versions of results, and the fact that the data storage
format continues to evolve over time.
The product of these considerations result in a graphical user interface (GUI) created for
this project. The requirements for this GUI are as follows; it needs to be intuitive and easy
to use, flexible enough to allow several plotting options, and simultaneously robust enough
to handle different naming schemes in the results. How this GUI works is expanded upon
in subsection 5.2.2.

Automated Marine Gravity Surveys
3.1.1
Automated vs. Dedicated Marine Gravity Surveys
For the last 11 years, DTU Space have been using the CHEKAN Marine Gravimeter on
their surveys. Onboard the ships ‘Jens Sørensen’ (until 2019) and ‘Paul Løwenørn’ (since
2021), gravity data was collected automatically by the CHEKAN marine gravimeter. The
main objective of these surveys were hydrographic mapping, i.e. measuring the depths
to the sea floor (bathymetry). The marine gravity surveys were a secondary “opportunity
activity”.
Throughout this report I will use a generic term, “dedicated gravity surveys”, to describe a
carefully pre-planned and controlled gravity data collection at sea. I will also use a generic
term, “automated gravity survey” to describe the opposite; an uncontrolled, opportunistic
study. The difference between these two generic concepts is mainly the level of control
of the experiment and the possibility to decide where measurements will take place. For
dedicated gravity surveys, it is often possible to assess the quality of the collected data
in almost real-time. The marine gravity surveys described here are somewhere between
the two extremes, but lean mostly towards automated gravity surveys. While the data
collection of automated surveys is simple and cheap, and can be conducted by untrained
personnel onboard, the challenge lies in processing the data afterwards.
In the following, I will shortly describe the main elements of processing of the automated
surveys, that have been developed by my supervisor, assisted by student work (see chap-
ter 4).
3.1.2
Operational practicalities
The gravimeter was turned on in the first harbour, with the ship sailing off a few hours
later. The crew onboard were briefly instructed on how to turn the gravimeter on and off
and how to send the data once the survey was completed. However, due to this being
an opportunity activity, the needs of the marine gravity survey were not always taken into
consideration. The crew only stayed in the harbour as long as necessary for the purpose
of the hydrographic surveys; to collect supplies and to refuel. The short stay at the first
harbour (a few hours) meant that sometimes the gravimeter had not reached a stable
operating temperature prior to departure. Due to this, the first set of survey lines leading
out of the first harbour may be affected by “misreadings” caused by the so-called warm-
up effect. This problem can to some degree be mitigated if the ship returns to the same
harbour when the warm-up effect is not a problem any more (e.g. in the end of the survey).
The marine surveys that I discuss here were highly uncontrolled in the sense that DTU
Space had no say in where the ships sailed. In bad weather the ship could change course
and divert to its secondary objective of the yearly hydrographic survey programme.

3.2.1
Preparing gravity and navigation data
The navigation data collected from the hydrographic survey vessel consists of two di-
mensional Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) positions — latitude and longitude — sampled at
1Hz. This data is often fragmented, incomplete, and spread across multiple files. The
RTK navigation is provided to us by hydrographers on board. Similarly, the gravity data
can be patchy in places. The crew were instructed to manually change the data collec-
tion file once every 24 hours. Raw gravity measurements are recorded at 10Hz, while
factory-filtered gravimeter output is provided at 1Hz. By convention, the initial gravime-
ter readings, denoted erCU(t) (see Equation 2.6), correspond to this factory-filtered 1Hz
dataset.
3.2.2
Reconstruction of navigation history
Despite the prior agreement of collecting RTK navigation data continuously throughout
the whole survey (including harbour stays and transit navigation, see subsection 3.3.2),
this agreement was, due to human error, not always kept by the hydrographic crew. The
hydrographers onboard only need detailed navigation in the hydrographic survey areas
(see subsection 3.3.3), and the time in-between was often used for testing equipment and
data collection formats. The resulting RTK navigation data received from the vessel crew,
is therefore patchy and incomplete regarding opportunity gravity surveys, but complete
for hydrographic surveys. Also, during harbour stays the collection of RTK navigation is
sometimes forgotten due to human error. As we do not always know where or when these
navigation stops take place, we have developed a method of reconstructing the navigation
history from the available information. Luckily the CHEKAN marine gravimeter is almost
always turned on and independently measures a gravity signal along the ship’s route.
Rarely, there have been some incidents when the ships crew have turned the gravimeter
off at sea, leading to gaps in the gravity data. The timings of the navigation stops can be
determined from uninterrupted gravity measurements. Alternatively, the associated com-
pensating accelerometer activity of the gyro platform can be used to determine timings of
navigation stops, as seen in Figure 3.1. By performing this comparison of navigation and
gravity data, we can get an idea of where the ship has stopped, allowing us to distinguish
between ‘stops-at-sea’ and harbour stays (see subsection 3.3.1). The automation of this
task of reconstructing the navigation history is described in Kristmar 2022. If needed, the
proximity of possibly interrupted navigation information to known harbours in Denmark is
used.
In cases where we do not have navigation data in harbours, we can still determine which
harbour it is from the timing of harbour stays based on gravity data. In principle, we would
need to know the exact location in the harbour, but sometimes knowing the harbour is
sufficient, because in minor harbours there are specific locations where vessels of the
size of the survey ships can be moored.

3.4.2.3
Cross-coupling
For certain designs of marine gravimeters, horizontal accelerations along the survey line
translate to misreadings in the vertical accelerations, (Torge 1989). According to my su-
pervisor, the phenomenon is also valid for the CHEKAN gravimeter because the level
of readings for class 1 compared to class 2 lines are different, and group according to
the class. Certain types of gravimeters have built-in detection facilities for modelling the
cross-coupling effect. This is not the case for the CHEKAN gravimeter. The effect of
cross-coupling exists for all survey lines, but cannot easily be modelled, except in HSA.
The correction for this effect for all lines is part of the additional corrections, subsubsec-
tion 3.4.3.2.
3.4.3
Additional corrections
In addition to the standard gravity corrections (the linear drift and the Eötvös effect), further
empirical corrections are sometimes necessary to account for more complex instrument
behaviour at sea and other unmodelled corrections (including the cross-coupling effect).
These include a warm-up effect correction, and a non-linear drift correction, which models
deviations from the linear drift.
3.4.3.1
Warm-up correction
The warm-up effect was modelled in a previous student project, see Sunding 2017. Here
a piecewise exponential model was used, which she studied based on both the gravity
readings in the first harbour stay in different marine gravity surveys, and the laboratory
measurements taken in the basement of DTU Space. When the instrument has reached
its stable working temperature, the warm-up effect is by default negligible. Formally, the
warm-up correction is applied to all survey lines, but is very small at the end of the survey.
3.4.3.2
Empirical Non-linear Drift Function
As mentioned above in subsubsection 3.4.2.1, the remaining signal, after accounting for
the warm-up effect, can be modelled using the empirical non-linear drift function (ENLDF).
Figure 3.9 shows harbour gravity readings after having been corrected for standard cor-
rections and the warm-up effect. The intervals between harbour stays (vessel is at sea)
can roughly be approximated with the piecewise cubic hermite interpolating polynomial,
PCHIP, based on harbour stays. This method also takes the residual warm-up effect into
account, seen in the first harbour. To better constrain the ENLDF, it would be ideal to
have support points located at sea. These can, to some degree, be inferred from misfits
observed at survey line crossings (see subsection 3.6.1).

a crossing is formally defined as a coupling between two unique data records from two
different crossing lines. A corresponding crossing where the crossing lines are in reverse
order, refers to the same unique coupling of associated records. E.g. a crossing between
line 17 and line 24 is defined by record number 5 on line 17 coupled with record 42 on
line 24. The coupling is denoted (5, 42). The corresponding coupling between line 24
and line 17 is denoted (42, 5). Thus it is only necessary to find a crossing coupling for
one combination of survey lines, as the corresponding reverse combination is a simple
permutation of record numbers. Crossing couplings are fixed and predefined for survey
geometries.
3.6.1
Crossings and chains
Crossings can further be classified according to their crossing geometry; if the survey
lines cross in one unique point, the crossing is of type 1; if the lines run roughly parallel,
and have several matching pairs of data points, then the crossing is of type 2. A clear-cut
generic example of these types of crossings can be seen in Figure 3.11. Consequently
we end up with four distinct crossing types; intra 1, intra 2, extra 1, and extra 2. This
distinction allows for systematic correction of each line by comparing it to intersecting
lines treated as fixed, ensuring consistent corrections across the survey.
The classification of crossings is not always straightforward, especially in cases involving
general navigation as opposed to survey line geometry in HSA. While an automatic clas-
sification algorithm is used, there are situations where its output may be misleading or
require reclassification. This subjectivity is addressed in more detail in section 5.1, where
a framework for figure-based reclassification anc correction that I worked on is presented.
Some simple examples of misclassified crossings that have been reclassified are shown
in Figure 6.1.
The fact that crossing couplings are unique means also that we can define a subset of all
survey lines which are connected via crossings, known as chains. Chains indicate which
parts of the survey (i.e. which survey lines) are well constrained by crossings at sea, and
which are not. Not all survey lines are part of a chain, only the lines that are crossed are
included. An example of such a chain can be seen in Figure 3.12.
The concept of chains becomes especially powerful when extended with extra-crossings,
i.e crossings with other surveys. A new survey can introduce crossings that link previ-
ously unconnected survey lines, effectively expanding the chain network. This expanded
connectivity improves the model’s internal constraints at sea and plays a role in CSM,
(Lund 2024).

3.7
Stand-alone modelling and applied corrections
Stand Alone Modelling (SA) has been up until now the standard method when it comes to
processing gravity surveys. The SA modelling method deals with surveys independently
(XPROJ = CPROJ), i.e. no other surveys are considered when applying corrections such
as the ones mentioned in subsection 3.4.2. The main finding is that SA results can have
reasonable cross-over statistics for intra-crossings, but can have big inconsistencies and
discrepancies at crossings with other surveys.
3.8
Cross-Survey Modelling
SA modelling ensures internal consistency within a single survey, but can fail at extra-
survey crossings, showing big differences in results for the same location, as mentioned
in section 3.7. Cross-Survey Modelling (CSM) addresses this by aligning pFA across
surveys using extra crossings. When pFA misfits at crossings occur, targeted corrections
must be applied to one or both surveys, depending on the interpretation, an example can
be seen in Figure 5.1. The working assumption is that the adequate correction budget
for all surveys should simultaneously yield good intra- and extra-crossing statistics. Thus
we are working with all crossings within the same modelling universe; CSM. The CSM
correction is still sequential, meaning that we are only correcting cline of CPROJ in some
sequential order.

algorithmic sequential processing. Misfits at crossings can also be caused by misinter-
pretations of corrections. The graphical tools developed across these efforts make such
issues immediately apparent in a way that purely mathematical models cannot. These
tools reveal patterns and interpretations that would otherwise remain hidden, reinforcing
the importance of visual inspection as an integral part of CSM.
While the current version of the graphical tools is at present limited to single-survey anal-
ysis, it is designed for future CSM integration, as described in section 5.4.
3.9
Graphical tools
Graphical tools are very useful when it comes to interpreting marine gravity data and un-
derstanding the processing steps that transform marine gravity readings, er(t), into pFA
for a given correction budget. While earlier student projects have developed various fig-
ures — such as visualisations of harbour stays, HSAs, and crossings — these tools were
typically custom-built for narrow use cases (student projects or processing of individual
surveys). As noted by my supervisor, nearly every past project has ended up reinventing
its own set of plotting utilities. This project breaks with that tradition.
Rather than building yet another bespoke tool, this work introduces a generalised, yet
flexible, graphical tool designed to produce standardised, repeatable, and comparable
figures across the entire automated marine gravity survey processing pipeline. The em-
phasis is on consistency, clarity, and scalability — delivering figures that not only convey
complex interpretation and associated corrections at a glance, but do so using a common
visual language that supports both technical insight and cross-survey comparison.
In doing so, this project doesn’t just create a plotting tool — it establishes a new standard
for visual diagnostics in marine gravity survey modelling. It bridges the theoretical depth
of marine gravity theory with the practical realities of software design and instrumental
behaviour at sea, resulting in a tool that is flexible enough to accommodate diverse sur-
vey conditions, yet robust enough to support future expansion and evolving processing
strategies, see section 5.2.
Furthermore, this is not merely a tool for visualisation — but serves also as a feedback loop
within the software, enabling iterative updates through modelling of the additional correc-
tion budget, ∆c∆g(t). The primary methodological justification for the developed graphi-
cal tools lies in the physical proximity of the locations associated with coupled records at
crossings. Since these points are located very close to each other, the true free-air grav-
ity values should be nearly identical. Graphically displaying the misfits at these crossings
makes it easier to identify, prioritise, and correct the largest inconsistencies. These in-
sights can then be used to interpret the reason for misfits, and ultimately correct, the
survey lines contributing to the discrepancies, see Equation 2.15.

Software improvement 1: Correcting wrong classification
of crossings and/or record couplings
In my synthesis project, I created tools that made it easier to identify and analyse cross-
ings (Lund 2024). This was then further improved upon by my supervisor, resulting in an
automatic generation of figures for each automatically classified crossing. The current
software is capable of automatically detecting intra- and extra-crossings between survey
lines and creating the corresponding figures of the crossings. Furthermore, there is an
extensive bookkeeping of the coupled records of crossing survey lines, XINGS.mat. Nev-
ertheless, the classification algorithm is not always perfectly reliable. Misclassifications
can occur. Improvement 1 has the objective of establishing a general method that facili-
tates the reclassification and/or correction of crossing couplings upon visual inspection.
The task of identifying which crossings have been mislabelled and which coupled records
have been miscoupled has not been automated yet — and likely never will be — due to
its subjective nature. This kind of assessment requires human judgement and can not be
delegated to the software. To correct a mislabelled crossing classification, users provide
a list of crossing numbers for each survey, indicating which crossings they believe to be
mislabelled. All crossings have a figure, named in accordance to their unique number,
‘ict’, (e.g. `ict' = 192 →`ict00192.png'). When the crossing has been reclassified, it
is labelled as such in XINGS.mat. It is therefore possible to see whether a crossing has
been reclassified before when reclassifying.
To support the reclassification process, a function was created in MATLAB that copies
selected figures from a source folder to a designated destination folder, based on a user-
defined list — this function is called copy_selected_images.m. The function uses this list
to verify whether the figure already exists in the ‘from_intra1’ folder — which contains
figures that e.g. have previously been mislabelled as intra 1. A visualisation of the folder
structure can be seen in Figure 5.3. This is done to ensure that the function can run again
if the user is not happy with the results. The software then checks if the figure exists in
the inspect folder, which stores figures that have been reclassified, but not yet finalised.
After reclassification is complete, the figures are moved to their respective destination
folder.
In addition to the copy_selected_images.m function, I developed a script, loc_files.m,
that performs a series of logical checks to the location of the crossing figure. This script
is particularly helpful during the process of inspecting and reclassifying crossings as it
verifies which folders a crossing figure is present in and returns a logical array indicating
its status in the reclassification process. This effectively serves as a reclassification lookup
table, informing user whether a crossing has previously been processed, reclassified, or
finalised. One of the use cases for such a lookup table is when you want to reclassify a
crossing. In such an instance, then the lookup table will help the user check where the
image is located, and to see if it has been corrected already.

Software improvement 4: The absolute level
The next improvement that is planned would be a connection between the measured
gravity data from the marine gravimeter and the altimetric models mentioned in subsec-
tion 3.6.3. Usually the crosspoint misfits are closed on a relative level in relation to each
other. At the crossings, misfits can be closed arbitrarily as there is no true value that
we can get from the data. We have to instead use prior knowledge about the crossing
and survey to make an educated guess on how to correct the misfit. After applying this
tactic to the whole survey, and also any other coupled surveys, we end up with a relative
correction budget.
A more robust approach would be to treat survey lines that align well with external alti-
metric models as fixed reference lines—effectively “nailed down” as correct from the start.

6
Results
The outcome of this project is a modular, standardised GUI-based plotting tool designed
to support a wide range of post-survey processing tasks in marine gravity surveys. While
currently focused on the inspection and interpretation of within-survey crossings, the sys-
tem has been built with future extensions in mind — including many solutions, multi-survey
analysis, cross-survey modelling (CSM), and integration with altimetric models.
This chapter presents the results of the project, which builds on experience gained dur-
ing my earlier synthesis project and experiences from other student work, conveyed by
my supervisor, see chapter 4. The developed tools address critical limitations in previ-
ous workflows, particularly the lack of a systematic approach to correcting misclassified
crossings and interpreting line data. By combining theoretical foundations with practical
design, the resulting workflow supports consistent, reproducible, and physically meaning-
ful interpretation of gravity data — and has already been integrated into the main software
pipeline by my supervisor.
6.1
Initial foundations and prior knowledge
This project builds directly on the foundation laid during my earlier synthesis project,
which provided me with a valuable overview of the software’s internal logic and struc-
ture. Through this initial work, I gained hands-on experience with core data structures
such as CPROJ and XPROJ, and developed a practical understanding of how survey data
is organised and used.
Early exposure to these tools revealed a key limitation in the existing processing workflow:
the lack of a systematic way to visualise and assess survey results after the data had
been collected. This observation became a central motivator for the development of more
robust, standardised tools to aid in post-survey interpretation.
6.2
Improvements to figure classification and sorting
A significant part of this thesis involved considering how we classify and interpret cross-
ings between survey lines. Until now, the classification has been done automatically, with
little human input, which can be prone to errors when running into edge cases. Therefore,
I developed a method for moving and correcting the wrong classifications of crossings in a
consistent yet flexible way. This method takes into account the complex nature of gravity
survey processing and measurements, and allows the user to correct any misclassifica-
tion that might occur.
This approach acknowledges that multiple valid interpretations may exist for a single sur-
vey. The tool I created supports iterative exploration, allowing the user to converge on a
solution that is both physically meaningful and internally consistent, and allows them to fi-
nalise the solution only when they are satisfied. Importantly, the crosspoint-classification
code has already been implemented by my supervisor into the main software, a direct
validation of the method’s utility and relevance. A result of reclassification can be seen in
Figure 6.1.
The approach also reduces manual sorting effort and promotes reproducibility. It provides
a standardised way to visualise and assess corrections — not just as isolated fixes, but
as part of a theory-driven gravity modelling strategy.

6.3
Developing a modular and expandable GUI
To support this improved workflow, I developed a graphical user interface (GUI) that makes
the interpretation and comparison of survey data more accessible, especially in post-
survey phases. The GUI can act as a flexible yet standardised workspace for survey
analysts in the future.
As of now, the groundwork has been laid, and users can explore datasets and generate
consistent plots across different surveys. The GUI produces figures using a consistent
visual style, making them easier to interpret and compare across surveys. This directly
addresses the issue of non-standardised figures.
The GUI is built using a modular logic structure. This makes it straightforward to expand
in the future, for example to support multi-survey visualisation, add harbour tie correction
tools, or link directly to external altimetric models. It serves as a backbone for future
development in the post-survey correction process.
6.4
Balancing Structure and Flexibility in Post-Survey
Corrections
One of the central challenges was to find a balance between structure and flexibility. On
one hand, a rigid framework ensures that theoretical consistency is maintained — for
instance in how gravity corrections are visualised, or how clines are selected and han-
dled. On the other hand, post-survey analysis often requires expert judgment and manual
refinement.
The GUI addresses this by offering default selections based on survey data while allowing
users to override them through drop-down menus, checkboxes, and manual entry. This
makes it possible to standardise the overall structure of the figures while still accommo-
dating the nuanced decisions that users must make.
This new workflow also helps replace the previous reliance on SA results, which had good
intra-survey results, but failed when comparing crosspoints between surveys together and
thus failing to model the gravimeter’s real-world behaviour. Instead, the GUI supports a
correction strategy that accounts for changes in the data and can therefore easily change
the correction budget, based on Equation 2.15, and aligns the visual outputs more closely
with physical reality.
6.5
Integration with broader software and modelling strategy
The GUI is designed to work seamlessly with the existing processing environment. It di-
rectly reads from .mat files derived from the internal CPROJ and XPROJ structures, ensuring
compatibility with current workflows while also introducing a higher level of standardisa-
tion.
In parallel with this, the GUI supports a strategic shift away from SA-based modelling
toward the Cross Survey Modelling (CSM) approach that will be adopted for future cam-
paigns in Danish waters. The GUI’s output figures will play an important role in this tran-
sition, helping users interpret and align crossings with improved confidence.
6.6
Summary of results
Through this project, I have produced a set of tools and methods that are already being
used in the main processing software. The crosspoint classification code has been in-
tegrated by my supervisor, and the GUI is now ready to be the next tool for post-survey
visualisation and correction workflows.

7
Discussion
7.1
Future Development
While much of the thesis has already outlined potential extensions of the GUI, it is worth
briefly summarising the main directions for future development. Many of these were iden-
tified early in the project but could not be implemented due to time constraints, and their
relevance has only become more apparent during the course of the work.
A key improvement is fully integrating crossing surveys into the GUI. At present, external
surveys cannot be visualised underneath the current survey. Future updates should allow
these external datasets (e.g., XPROJ) to be shown underneath the current survey (CPROJ)
— particularly at crosspoints — enabling systematic evaluation of misfits. This would
support more robust decision-making around how to apply corrections.
Another anticipated feature is the ability to simultaneously plot an xline together with the
cline in the pseudo Free-Air anomaly (pFA) figure as seen in Figure 5.14. This would
allow users to make visual comparisons between lines, whether from the same survey or
another one, and could be particularly useful for diagnosing localised mismatches, height
offsets, or temporal drifts.
Further development is also needed for the integration of external gravity models. While
these models currently appear as static backgrounds, a more interactive approach would
allow users to query, compare, or even benchmark results against the models in a con-
trolled way. This would greatly enhance the interpretability of figures, and offer a more
transparent framework for assessing data quality. This connects directly to the discussion
in section 5.4, where the goal is to integrate interpretations of survey lines with external
free-air (FA) gravity models.
As shown in Figure 5.1, survey lines are not always the weakest data source—some,
like cline #016_019 and cline #016_021, do not follow the external altimetric models
particularly well, yet they mirror each other closely. Since these lines are parallel and part
of the same HSA, they are expected to reflect the same gravitational signal. This internal
consistency highlights the potential value of marine gravity surveys as independent and
complementary data sources for improving geoid modelling.
All of these improvements have been part of the overall design considerations of the
GUI and fit naturally into its current structure. Implementing them would take the tool
closer to supporting more complex survey scenarios, including cross-survey modelling
and standardised figure generation — ultimately aiding in the broader goal of repeatable
and high-quality post-survey processing.
7.2
Design challenges
The development of the improvements created for this thesis, and especially the GUI,
revealed several key challenges, particularly in balancing structure and flexibility within
figure standardisation, ensuring an internal consistency across the interactive tabs, man-
aging plotting logic and verifying the GUI’s robustness across different surveys and use-
cases.
7.2.1
Balancing structure and flexibility
During the creation of the GUI, one of the main problems was managing the balance
between structure and the flexibility of user inputs, without compromising the standard-
Automated Marine Gravity Surveys in Danish Waters
47
==PAGE:55==
ised figure outputs. On one hand, the user needs the freedom of being able to override
default settings, explore different surveys and set their own plotting parameters. On the
other hand, figures need to remain visually consistent to support the ability to perform a
comparison between different surveys, clines or other elements.
A balance was achieved by carefully compartmentalising user input. Controls were grouped
into tabs, as seen in Figure 5.9, and each plotting function shared the same code-skeleton
and referenced the same settings saved in plotSettings. However, enabling this flex-
ibility without sacrificing consistency required a rigid structure in how parameters were
passed from one tab to another, and how plotSettings was updated, ensuring that the
latest input was saved and used for the current figure. Without this, user input could
quickly turn figures into unusable images, possibly leading to incorrect interpretations
down the road.
7.2.2
Maintaining synchronisation across tabs
The GUI operates with multiple interconnected tabs, each of which can modify the shared
settings, ranging from which survey to plot to whether coastlines should be included or
not. As mentioned above, one of the key problems was ensuring consistency across the
different tabs, and making sure that the latest user input was also the input used when
plotting. By constantly updating the UserData property of the GUI figure, which acted as
the central reference point for both static settings and plotting variables, plotSettings
were always up to date with the latest choices made.
Even though this was the case, some care had to be taken to prevent conflicting updates.
For example, when a user selected cline in one tab and then changed the HSA in another.
These interdependencies across the GUI also made debugging a lot more complex. Small
changes in one area of the code could lead to drastic changes elsewhere and inadvertently
affect the behaviour. This led to the major focus of the development being structuring the
internal logic and “awareness” of the GUI.
7.2.3
Managing plot logic and layout consistency
One of the most technically detailed aspects of the project was the generation of stan-
dardised plots across a wide range of user-specified inputs. The user could vary the x-
axis, select different survey line subsets and toggle external model overlays. All of these
needed to be reflected in the figure output without breaking visual or analytical consis-
tency. If this was broken, the figures would be useless as nothing could be interpreted
from them.
This was managed using pre-defined axis limits, colours, and titles. All of these were
extracted from the data at the start when loading the survey and saved as figure_statics
in plotSettings. Doing this allowed interpretations to be made straight-away without
further manual editing.
7.2.4
Testing across different use cases
Finally, a substantial part of the latter development time was spent on testing different
user-inputs across different surveys and settings. In particular, literal edge-cases at the
edge of surveys revealed unexpected bugs.
As the intention of the GUI is for it to be used for post-survey interpretation, reliability
and reproducibility are essential. Testing focused not just on bugs, but on ensuring that
the same inputs would lead to consistent figures. By doing this, it was clear that some
assumptions made during early development (e.g. clines being sequential, having com-
plete data, indices always matching) would prove themselves incorrect and lead to prob-
lems later on.

Testing real-world survey configurations and comparing GUI figures to previous results
helped understand what needed to be addressed. Edge cases often appeared during
development, so the GUI had to be made as robust as possible, being able to handle

To address this problem, several fallback options were introduced, and some defensive

wider audience. Future testing and feedback from other users will be essential to ensure
usability and general applicability.

8
Conclusion
This project tackled the limitations of current automated marine gravity survey processing
by introducing a more structured and adaptable workflow. Drawing on both theoretical
principles and detailed knowledge of the software and survey data, I have created new
tools that support clearer interpretation, better error handling, and more consistent visual
outputs in a semi-automated environment.
Improvement 1 is a method of systematically restructuring and reclassifying crossings,
allowing a consistent correction of previously misclassified crossings while still making
room for future expansion and updates. The integration of improvement 1 into the software
already shows that my work is needed and applicable.
The main output of this project is the framework and beginnings of a modular GUI that
standardises figure output across different users and surveys while still allowing future
development and manual refinement. The GUI supports not only default settings, but
also overrides (e.g. drop-down menus, checkboxes, and manual entry), enabling both a
profound consistency and flexibility.
In the near future, this will allow us to move away from a reliance on stand-alone (SA) mod-
elling and go towards cross-survey modelling, which allows for more accurate handling of
cross-survey misfits. As mentioned, the GUI has left room for future improvements; both
improvements that have been considered (see section 5.3 and section 5.4), and improve-
ments that might show themselves to be important down the line. These include tools for
visualising crossing surveys, tie-point corrections, and integration with external models.
The tools developed contribute to a much more robust, reproducible, and theoretically
consistent and tied survey workflow. The project forms a foundation for future expansion
and deeper integration within Denmark’s marine gravity survey strategy.
