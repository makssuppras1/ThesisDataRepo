Abstract
Having the capability of knowing when components are on the verge of failure has many appli-
cations. Their replacement can take place before more damage is caused by the actual failure,
they can be used to their absolute limit and servicing would take a shorter time, reducing the
downtime of the components dependencies. A commercial software dedicated to this task would
be in a very high demand, since manufacturers are always trying to increase eﬃciency and proﬁts.
The general goal of the project is to build a computational platform for simulating mechan-
ical systems with diﬀerent levels of degradation based on a multi-physics modelling approach and
high ﬁdelity models, i.e. based on principals, axioms and laws. The speciﬁc goal of this project
is to simulate and investigate the dynamical behaviour of a rotating shaft supported by ball
bearings under several levels of deterioration. From the simulation model, lateral vibrations of
the rotating shaft interacting with damaged ball bearings are obtained leading to time series,
which will encompass the progression of the bearing deterioration. Using the time series, the
ﬁnal aim is to predict the remaining useful life (RUL) of the rotor-bearing system. As a sig-
niﬁcant amount of failures occurs in the rolling element bearings, they are the main focus of
this numerical experiment. In the future, following the general goal of the project new machine
elements and respective failure models will be investigated using similar procedure.
The high ﬁdelity model of the rolling bearing element involves Hertz Contact Theory
(HCT). Through the use of geometry and a few basic assumptions, this theory correlates the
topology of two contacting surfaces to the pressure distribution and the shape of the contact.
The force is then related to the geometry of the contact through
F = Kδn
where F is the force, δ is the deformation, n is a constant dependent on the shape of the object,
and K is the load deﬂection factor, which is related to the two contacting objects’ geometry
and material properties. Through this equation, the bearing stiﬀness force coeﬃcient can be
calculated. The bearing damping force coeﬃcient comes from elasto-hydrodynamic lubrication
(EHL) theory and experimental research, it is correlated with the stiﬀness coeﬃcient.
The
bearing itself is modelled as a 4 degrees of freedom (DOF) system, with vertical and horizontal
DOFs of the bearing’s inner and outer races. The outer race of the bearing is supported by a
viii
==PAGE:11==
ﬂexible pedestal, which is assumed to be rigidly mounted on a foundation. The defect of the
bearing is generated by projecting a cosine wave with a very small period onto the defective
zone on the outer or the inner races. The severity of the defect is increased by adding more
periods to the cosine function and spanning the defect over a larger area, to simulate a growing
raceway spall. Based on empirical investigation [18], a critical spall length is deﬁned, and once
multiple simulations are complete ranging from defect-free to damage at the critical point, the
RUL prediction is possible. Another possible approach is to use maximum levels of vibration for
deﬁning the threshold, as suggested by ISO [35].
Degradation is an exponentially growing process, and features of the generated vibration,
which reﬂect this, are extracted and used for further processing.
A health indicator (HI) is
deﬁned by transforming all observations with a vector calculated using principal component
analysis (PCA). The main usage of PCA is to transform a set of features of an observation
to one dimension.
Using the derived values of the HIs of the signals generated at diﬀerent
defect severities, an exponential degradation model (EDM) is used to predict the RUL, based
on incoming and past time series. It is an adaptive model and, therefore, updates itself as new
information comes in, meaning the more observations are available the stronger its predictive
ability becomes. The model is sensitive to exponential growth, and thus the trend of the HI must
reﬂect this.
Computation time of the complete model depends heavily on its complexity, and to avoid
this, the systems were kept simple. Two cases were tested, one with 4 DOFs and one with 10
DOFs.
The 4 DOF systems simulated behaved as expected, and the prediction of the RUL
was possible with good accuracy. The 1 element system was even better, possibly due to the 4
DOF system not including as many DOFs. In conclusion, a mathematical model was created to
mathematically predict RUL of a bearing. The predictions gave positive results, and the next
step is to validate the model and possibly make minor adjustments.

Introduction
Machine elements are among the most important inventions of the modern day society. From
industrial to commercial use, machine elements can be found in a wide array of objects, and each
type of machine element has a broad scope of usage. Machine elements refer to components that
are required for a mechanism to serve its function, such as components of a car, which include
motors, bearings, shafts, screws, etc.
Rolling elements are one of the main causes of machine breakdown [8], due to the fact
that when in use, bearings are subject to constant forces, which over time wear them down.
This is obviously a problem, and in some cases a breakdown of a machine requires its complete
replacement, a problem that all machine users want to avoid.
The ability to ﬁnd a defected machine element before its breakdown is of major importance
for those who want to avoid a complete mechanical failure of a machine. Therefore, a method for
diagnosing a machine’s system health could be extremely economical with multiple possibilities
for practical applications. It could also be more time-eﬃcient, and in the modern world with
its increasing concern for the environment and scarce resources, it could save raw materials
and other essential resources and reduce green house gas emission. For example, an industrial
machine could be ﬁtted with a few accelerometers, which constantly or periodically feed data to
the diagnostic software, and with an adequate software, it could notify the user of a mechanical
fault before it proves detrimental to the machine’s operation. Catching a fault before it becomes
too large would save both money and resources and, therefore, reduce a carbon footprint.
The Finite Element Method (FEM) has become a popular way of simulating real world
components [39]. It involves splitting the analysed component into multiple smaller elements
and mounting them into a matrix, which can be used to solve or analyse the entire component.
Although the theory behind FEM is relatively simple, its application with larger and more
complex systems can be computationally expensive. The most prominent dilemma in FEM is
that less intricate models give faster results but lose accuracy, whereas more complex models
take much longer time to compute but give much more accurate and realistic results.
Artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) and other machine learning (ML) techniques have become in-
creasingly popular in the modern world [41]. This is mainly due to how inexpensive, powerful
and fast computer processors have become and how fast processors are evolving. Although many
of the methods for machine learning techniques have been speculated, theorised and proven years
1
==PAGE:17==
before they could be economically and practically applied, we have seen a tremendous increase
in applicability and development in the past few years [41]. People have also been applying these
techniques in numerous ﬁelds, ranging from marketing advertisements to users [42] to bioinfor-
matics and genetics [43]. The scope of the ﬁelds, wherein machine learning is applicable, seems
to have no end, and due to their complexity the models created using these methods are often
times much more accurate than models created by people.
This thesis aims at combining machine learning methods with FEM to generate a model
that can quickly read data and diagnose any issues in the machine’s operation if they arise.
This will be done by ﬁrst creating a mathematical model based on ﬁnite element (FE) theories,
with the main focus on bearing elements. A simulation of a fault will then be introduced to the
model, based on previous papers written on the subject [8]-[12]. Once the defect is introduced,
a mathematical model of a system is generated with varying degrees of deterioration, and the
vibrations are simulated. Using the time series of the vibrations, the remaining useful life (RUL)
of the bearing is estimated by an exponential degradation model (EDM), which adaptively learns
when given data.
1.1
Applications
A machine health diagnostic system has a multitude of applications. It should be capable of di-
agnosing a defective component and be able to predict failure. These components are calculated
using only a vibration signal from an accelerometer, meaning that a simple sensor can be attached
to a speciﬁc location in a machine and send the signal to a software for processing. It can be very
diﬃcult to do this without mathematically modelling a system, due to all diﬀerent factors at
play. Data-driven techniques, which use vibration signals of components run to failure, require
that components are run until failure. This can be expensive and not economical for a manufac-
turer to investigate, but a technique that uses a mathematical model requires only computing
power. Computing power can be signiﬁcantly cheaper than doing actual experiments; therefore,
a software that mathematically computes and predicts the RUL of any general component is in
high demand.
Once a mathematical monitoring system exists that requires only computational process-
ing of a vibration signal, there is a high possibility that it would be used to monitor a wide
variety of machinery. A diagnostic system, which predicts when the bearings in one’s car wheels
2
==PAGE:18==
need to be replaced or when something is not working correctly, gives the added beneﬁt of not
having to experience the component breaking but rather replacing it before it happens. This
can be commercialised. A production line with a conveyor belt built from a series of similar
components could attach an accelerometer to each bearing or to the ones most likely to break to
track the changes and be alerted once a failure prediction is made. This could decrease downtime
of machinery and, therefore, improve eﬃciency and save money.
Another possible application is to be able to test a machine design before manufacturing
it. An accurately simulated model has the potential to notice mistakes in a design that the
designer does not. Notifying of a sub-standard design before manufacturing can improve safety
and reduce general losses.

2
Previous Work
Extensive research has been put into fault detection of machinery. Early detection of faults has
economic value, if defects are found before they cause more damage to machinery. In some cases
it can also be a safety concern; for example, in aeroplanes, an undetected fault can cause the loss
of hundreds of lives.
To gain a better understanding of the forces that erode machinery over time, it is im-
perative to have physical and mathematical understanding of the problem. Hence, producing
mathematical models will aid our understanding and lead to simplifying and increasing the ef-
fectiveness of current methods used in fatigue progression and failure prediction.
Modelling of any sort of bearing is a very heavy task, mostly due to the non-linear time-
dependent parameters, which aﬀect stiﬀness and damping of the system [8, 9]. This has put a
large constraint on studies in the past. However, in recent years, with the evolution of powerful
and cheap computing power, this computationally heavy task has become vastly more feasible,
and more eﬀort has been put into the research of bearing mechanics and its eﬀects in machinery
[39].
The basis for all physical bearing models is the Hertz Contact Theory (HCT) [36, 38].
Heinrich Hertz laid down the fundamentals of contact theory in the late 19th century, using
simple, reasonable assumptions, which are still applied today. These simple, yet powerful as-
sumptions and the precise, applicable, geometrical equations derived from them are the main
power of HCT.
A few years after Hertz, Arvid Palmgren studied bearings intensively and conducted
countless empirical experiments that formed the basis of bearing fatigue life, as well as a means
to calculate stresses associated with the contact. Along with Gustaf Lundberg, he published a
work on cyclic fatigue life of bearings (fatigue life of bearings under periodic load), in 1947, a
seminal work that opened many new possibilities for research [38].
It is a well known fact that vibration plays a large role in mechanics and engineering, and
a lot of information is stored within it, which can be extracted and processed using the correct
methodology. C. S. Sunnersjo [4] studied the eﬀects and trends of vibration in ball bearings.
These results gave insight into the meaning of certain vibrational signatures and how one should
interpret and process them.
With the rise in popularity and applicability of FEM, more studies and research papers
4
==PAGE:20==
on the mathematical model of rolling element bearings started to be published. The 1970s saw
an increased prevalence in published papers that applied the Hertz Contact Theory numerically
in a mathematical model. This marked the shift from predominantly empirical studies to include
numerical research in the ﬁeld as well.
Fatigue aﬀects all types of manufactured products, be they dynamic or static. There are
many diﬀerent ways of classifying fatigue and estimating the lifetime of a product. A commonly
used scale for calculating a lifetime of a part is the L10 criterion, which is the amount of time (in
seconds or cycles) that 10% of products are expected to fail after. Another lifetime predictor that
is becoming more and more popular is the remaining useful life (RUL) method. This method
is split into two sub-categories: physical based or data-driven. The physical based techniques
attempt to apply physical laws to estimate the RUL, whereas the data-driven techniques utilise
statistics and previous data to estimate the RUL. The RUL approach is favoured for the purposes
of this thesis, since it can predict with much more accuracy, when something is going to fail, due
to its ability to read real-time data to update the estimations, something that other techniques
have trouble doing.
Comparable to the rise in use of FEM, the present day sees the rise in use of machine
learning methods, so that the fabrication of newer, more intricate models becomes a possibility.
Often times, calculations and models become so intricate that processing results and numbers
can be a near impossible task; therefore, there is a demand for a method to solve the problem of
data storage and mining.

Rotor Modelling
Modelling of a simple rotor-bearing-disc system has long been studied and is currently well
understood [44].
It is a dynamic ﬁnite element problem that can be solved using methods
presented in previous work [39, 44].

2.2
Bearing Modelling
In 1981 on behalf of NASA, Bernard J. Hamrock and Duncan Dowson published a comprehensive,
detailed book on ball bearing mechanics [1], a basic collection of research gathered from multiple
5
==PAGE:21==
sources. It explains the conventions, methods, simpliﬁcations and assumptions used in the ﬁeld
of bearing modelling, as well as the underlying theories.
Fukata et al. [2] conducted one of the ﬁrst computer simulations of ball bearings. Their
model was a simple two degree of freedom (DOF) system that aimed at modelling the radial
vibration of a ball bearing, taking into account only the stiﬀness of the bearing.
D. W. Dareing and K. L. Johnson [3] produced a mathematical model that was very
similar to Fukata et al. but included the damping forces of the bearings by applying the theory
of elastohydrodynamic lubrication (EHL).
T. A. Stolarski and S. Tobe wrote their book Rolling Contact [37], in which, as the title
indicates, rolling contact under diﬀerent geometric mechanics was discussed, and bearings were
the main topic reviewed. K. L. Johnson’s Contact Mechanics [36] describes similar concepts
as Rolling Contact but focuses more on general contacts, as well as methods for calculating
stresses. Finally, T. A. Harris and M. N. Kotzalas wrote a book, Essential Concepts of Bearing
Technology [38], most of which is similar to the two previous books, albeit it also includes a
chapter on lifetime estimations of bearings.
There are also a number of factors that can be included when modelling bearings, although
not all of them are necessary in many cases. Wei Bian et al. [6] studied the eﬀects of temperature
within their model of a bearing, as its eﬀects can be devastating under certain circumstances.
However, a majority of papers published on bearing modelling have modelled a bearing as a
spring-damper system and neglected many of other features, such as temperature or rolling
element mass [3, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11].
Building on the theories of engineers like Hertz, Palmgren and Johnson, many more studies
have been published. As a result of this work, the mathematical modelling of bearings is currently
adequately understood, and the methods, which are generally accepted and precise to the extent
so that results are accurate, have been described [2, 3, 9]. Furthermore, the methods can be
extrapolated to include defects in the bearings that can be modelled mathematically instead of
studied empirically.

2.4
Defect Modelling
Modelling of a healthy bearing is simply the essential foundation for its actual applications. One
of the main motivations for development of bearing models is to understand how and when faults
occur. Currently, many studies diverge in their methodology, but nearly all studies are based on
ﬁndings and results of studies reviewed in Section 2.2.
Petersen et al. [8] generated a 6 DOF model of a deep groove ball bearing with horizontal
and vertical degrees of freedom for the shaft and the bearing inner race, the bearing housing
and the outer race, and the high frequency resonance. The rolling elements within the bearing
were modelled as spring-damping elements. The model calculated the non-linear motion of the
bearing, when it is subject to a line spall and an extended spall. The extended spall was generated
using a Tukey window with added noise. The numerical results reﬂected a trend similar to the
one that was measured with an actual bearing, although their model only included a single
bearing, and they did not model any other components of a general machine.
N. Sawalhi and R. B. Randall [9] worked on a bearing model that was very similar to
Petersen et al. However, they used only a single degree of freedom to imitate the high frequency
resonance. They simulated the defect to be able to include faults in the rolling elements them-
selves and also modelled a gear and a shaft to combine the components into one large model.
This approach produced a more realistic model, since vibrations of shafts and gears play a role
in the trends of the vibration signatures measured in a real system.
Jing Liu and Yimin Shao [10] studied how diﬀerent types of defects aﬀect the parameters
associated with solving the system, such as the load deﬂection factor (see Section 4.3.2). Most
studies of this topic do not take into account the eﬀects that a defect’s surface proﬁle has on
geometrically dependent components, like this one does. This paper considers the bearing as a
2 DOF system but introduces a diﬀerent way of modelling the defect and geometry.
When it comes to modelling of a defect, no single accepted method seems to exist, and
many research teams aim at developing a new and improved defect model and utilisation of the

==PAGE:23==
underlying ﬁndings of research discussed thus far [8, 11, 12].
2.5
Fatigue and Lifetime Estimation
The earliest theory on a bearing life was produced by Arvid Palmgren in 1924 [37]. It was later
expanded with the help of Gustaf Lundberg to explain the subsurface principal stresses, and
together they applied Waloddi Weibulls theoretical work to estimate a probability of survival for
a bearing [23]. K. L. Johnson wrote a book, Contact Mechanics [36], in which lifetime estimation
and methods are discussed in detail.
Multiple papers have been published on the topic of the RUL applied to bearings, most of
them being data-driven methods, as modelling the RUL requires a number of variables many of
which are chaotic in nature and diﬃcult to predict mathematically. At this level, a majority of
researchers begin to experiment with diﬀerent types of ML tactics. Kamal Medjaher er al. [19]
applied a Mixture of Gaussian-Hidden Markov Model (MoG-HMM) in an attempt to estimate
RUL. Mehdi Behazad et al. [20] explored a new feature based on high frequency vibration to
include into their model, which is based on a feed forward neural network. A. Hinchi and M.
Tkiouat [21] used a convolutional long-short-term memory (LSTM) neural network to predict the
RUL. The software company MathWorks has done considerable research on mechanical systems
and the computing it involves, they present a methodology for using an exponential degradation
model (EDM) in bearing RUL predictions [45].
Some physics-based methods include work of Wei Gui et al. [17], where they estimated
stresses occurring within bearings and made a connection between accumulated damage and
the number of cycles the bearing had completed. Nathan Bolander et al. [18] estimated the
RUL using a ﬁnite element model of a bearing and simulated spall propagation over time by
introducing kill elements that get gradually removed from the FE model.

3.1
Hertz Contact Theory
Heinrich Hertz was one the pioneers in development of a theory of contact that still holds valid
today. The main underlying assumptions in Hertz’s theory are the following [36]:
• The strains due to the contact of two bodies are within the elastic limit of the material,
meaning that no plastic deformation occurs.
• The bodies in contact are assumed to be elastic half-spaces.
• The surfaces are continuous and non-conforming, the area of contact is negligible in size
when compared to the characteristic lengths of the contact bodies.
• No frictional forces are acting between the contact surfaces.
The strain is deﬁned as the percent elongation or contraction of an object under pressure,
and according to the ﬁrst assumption
σ = Eε
holds at all times, where σ is the pressure in pascals applied to a surface; E is the Young’s
modulus or the modulus of elasticity of the material whose units are also pascals; ε is the strain,
which has no units since ε = ∆l/l, where l is a characteristic length of the object. Therefore, it
can be seen that the strain is linearly proportional to the pressure. A half-space is simply either
half of an object that has been sliced in half with a plane, so that each half is a mirror of the
other; an elastic one simply implies an elastic material (see Figure 1).
From these assumptions a few additional characteristics of this type of contact can be
stated. There is no adhesive force keeping the bodies in contact together: if the force keeping
the contact intact dissipates, then the bodies assume their original shape with no stresses at
the contact. The geometry of the contact area is strictly dependent only on the geometry of
the bodies; only the area of the geometry is aﬀected by the force pushing the bodies together.
Based on these assumptions, Hertz also managed to deduce that the pressure distribution of an

3.2
Finite Element Method
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a methodology for analysing complex systems [39]. Types
of systems include structures, heat transfer and ﬂuid mechanics, and FEM is useful in virtually
any ﬁeld of engineering [39]. The basis of the ﬁnite element methods is the establishment of
governing equations, which explain the system and simultaneously solve it.
FEM is just a
mathematical model of a real physical problem. Although the technique dates back to the mid
20th century, it has gained more momentum and popularity over the past years as computing
power has become signiﬁcantly more advanced, thus allowing for more intricate, complex and
10
==PAGE:26==
precise models. Multiple ﬁelds of engineering have already beneﬁted from FEM and continue to
do so as new models are introduced.
The problem begins with a system, be it a structure or some sort of a ﬂow. This system
may have some complex geometry that cannot be solved using conventional methods but can
be solved in parts, if the whole system is reduced into smaller sub-systems. Deﬁning multiple
points of interest in the system, so-called nodes, and solving the system gives the solutions of the
system at each node. The nodes themselves must interact with each other, and the interaction
medium of the nodes in the system is called elements. The elements connect nodes via shape
functions, which are equations dependent on the geometry of the type of element and are solved
using boundary conditions.
With regards to structural elements, as heat transfer and ﬂuid ﬂow are not a part of
this thesis, there are many diﬀerent types of elements to choose from. The choice of elements
depends on what the analyst aims to achieve. Elements can be 1, 2 and 3-dimensional, with
each dimension adding a degree of complexity to the model and also increasing computational
time. Although in reality, all structures are three-dimensional, by use of symmetry it is possible
to reduce the number of dimensions of a system [16]. An example can be a simple beam under
compression with some arbitrary length and cross-sectional area. Although it is a 3-dimensional
problem, the dimensions of the cross section can be ignored and a simple beam mechanics theory
can be applied, so the problem becomes only 1-dimensional. The types of elements that are
commonly used are line, triangular and rectangular type elements in two dimensions, which can
be expanded to 3 dimensions creating tetrahedral and brick elements. Each element has a certain
number of nodes located on its edges and/or on the mid-side between two nodes. As previously
stated, each element type serves a diﬀerent purpose and has diﬀerent governing equations, and
great care must be taken in choosing a correct type of elements for a certain task, since it could
simply give incorrect, non-applicable results [39]. Since the FEM can be used for both static
and dynamic systems, it is a suitable methodology for analysing dynamic machine elements and
systems, in this case bearing, shaft and disc mechanics. A comprehensive description of the ﬁnite
element method can be found in Klaus-Jurgen Bathe’s book Finite Element Procedures [39].
There is no single theory behind FEM, it is more of a systematic approach used to nu-
merically estimate a solution to a problem. The equations used to explain nodes and elements
are dependent on the operation and thus are varying, depending on goals to be achieved. Fig-

3.3
Lifetime Estimations
Knowing when a component will fail or reach the end of its life is equal parts important and
diﬃcult. It is important for two main reasons. Firstly, it gives the knowledge that will enable the
user to change components at the optimal time and, secondly, to change the component before a
catastrophic failure, which could have even more detrimental consequences. Both these reasons
will save material costs and time, as well as reduce the risk of accident due to catastrophic
failure.
However, component failure is extremely diﬃcult to predict, ﬁrst of all due to the
chaotic behaviour of fault progression over time in components. Manufactured components are
also diﬀerent on the molecular/structural level; slight statistical deviations in manufacturing will
ultimately have an eﬀect on where failure begins and propagates.
Since bearings are the components that fail most frequently in machinery [27], most life-
time estimation methods are aimed to be applied for bearings. Engineers, such as G. Lundberg
and A. Palmgren, paved the path for modern diagnostic systems, but older methods are in-
complete in many ways. The problem with the conventional methods of component lifetime
estimation is the fact that all calculations are independent of the actual bearing. In other words,
these methods could approximate the lifetime of the average bearing but not of the particu-
lar bearing. This is a big problem. If a component fails extremely early, it is able to cause a
16
==PAGE:32==
catastrophic failure that could not have been foreseen. On contrary, if a component lasts for an
unusually long time, then it will be replaced before the replacement is needed. Both cases will
have an overall negative eﬀect on safety and cost eﬃciency.
The solution to this problem is a model or a method that is periodically updated to give
the most accurate and relevant information. These methods are called remaining useful time
(RUL) methods, and their aim is to regularly approximate how much time a component has left
before it is no longer useful. For the purpose of this thesis, RUL methods are used due to their
dynamic and personalised capabilities when applied to an individual component.

Generally speaking, the columns of a

a

reducing tool, which applies matrix operations on a data set table in a matrix form [41]. If a data
set has n observations and m features, then each observation represents a point, which can be
plotted in an m-dimensional space. A unit vector, which extends in the direction of the highest
variance in the data set, is labelled the ﬁrst principal component; a unit vector orthogonal to the
ﬁrst principal component that spans the direction of the second highest variance is the second
principal component and so on. Since the ﬁrst principal components contain the most variance,
it is possible that projecting the data set onto these axes could cause some sort of meaningful
separation of the observations, so that trends can be observed.

threshold value of when the component will break.

does not know when it will take place. Deﬁning the values of the features projected onto a

Obviously, components degrade over time. Therefore, it is suitable to ﬁt some sort of a
degradation model with the analysed data. There are two types of degradation, non-destructive
and destructive.
Non-destructive degradation is a degradation process that can be directly
measured through physical characteristics (crack growth, battery voltage), whereas destructive
degradation requires some sort of invasive measures in order to be estimated (e.g. corrosion
in a chemical container, strength of an adhesive bond). Within the categories of degradation
there are multiple sub-categories of potential models: linear, exponential, power, logarithmic and
Lloyd-Lipow, non-destructive degradation can also apply Gompertz modelling [25]. Mechanical
components experience cumulative damage over time, which can be measured to some extent
without any invasive means. This makes it a non-destructive degradation process, which can be
modelled using an exponential degradation model (EDM), due to the cumulative damage over
time [25]. The general formula for an EDM is

time dependencies do not necessarily need to be deﬁned before the process begins, it allows the
model to update itself as time progresses. It means that the model has the capability to be
dynamic. This is important, since the path that a component takes from installation to failure
is unique, and a model that captures this aspect is preferred, as the algorithm learns about each
component. The following steps show how to combine the discussion topics of this section and
apply them to a mechanical component:
• Acquire vibration signal at as many diﬀerent time steps as possible from component instal-
lation to its failure.
• Apply operations on the signal to extract features. This could be the root mean square
(RMS) of the signal, the kurtosis or whichever factor that may help in the model’s predic-
tion.
• The extracted features should be set up in a matrix, similar to a data set.
• The features should be ranked by importance and impact, and features with little eﬀect
should be removed from the rest of the analysis.
• Apply PCA to the remaining features on the test set to obtain the principal components.
• Initialise the EDM and deﬁne the threshold as the value of the last observation (observation
at failure) projected onto the ﬁrst principal component.
• Train the EDM.
• Review its ability to accurately predict the RUL.
The only remaining issue is ﬁne-tuning of the model and possibly generation of multiple
models to create an ensemble of predictive models, which simply means having an array of slightly
diﬀerent models predicting the same variable, as it has been shown that ensemble methods tend
to have higher accuracy [41].

4
Machine Elements
Machinery is comprised of an array of diﬀerent components dedicated to complete speciﬁc tasks.
In regards to rotating machinery, it is possible to distinguish three main components.
The
ﬁrst one is the shaft, its main aim is to transfer rotation between components. Bearings act
as the supports of the system, they act as rotational and translational constraints. The ﬁnal
components are the discs, they can be very eﬃcient in storing kinetic energy whilst rotating but
are also ideal gear wheels. Gear wheels are the components, which can transfer torque from
one shaft to another, used, for example, in gear boxes. This section discusses each of the three
components and how they are modelled using the ﬁnite element method. See Figure 6 for an
example of a system set-up.
4.1

point to another. The translational axis parallel to the axis of rotation is also assumed to be
static; in other words, the sum of the forces acting translationally along the axis of rotation is

4.3
Bearing
A bearing is a machine element that constrains all degrees of freedom of an object, except for a
rotation about the axis perpendicular to its circular face. It consists of three main components.
First, there is the inner race with a circular bore, which is simply a hollow cylinder with a groove
at the centre of the outer boundary going all the way around that boundary.
It allows the
bearing to be mounted on a circular shaft or an object. Lying on the groove of the inner race are
a series of rolling elements, either spherical or cylindrical, located within a container called the
cage, which holds the rolling elements at a constant angle with regards to each other. The cage
allows a contact between the inner and the outer races, so that rotation of the rolling elements
is enabled. The last main component is the outer race, which similar to the inner race has a
groove but encompasses the inner face of the hollow cylinder. It has a circular boundary and is
25
==PAGE:41==
usually attached to a rigid support, called a pedestal or casing. Most commonly the inner race
spins, but in some cases, such as wheels of cars and bicycles, the outer race spins, while the inner
race acts as a support. To decrease the frictional coeﬃcient, rolling elements, such as spheres
or cylinders, are added in between the races, so that rolling becomes more energy-eﬃcient. If
a bearing has spheres contained in the cage, it is called a ball bearing, and with cylinders it is
called a cylindrical roller bearing. Figures 8 and 9 show a simple depiction of a ball bearing.
The main dimensions of a general bearing are:
• Number of rolling elements within the bearing, nb;
• Diameter of the rolling element, Db;
• Inner bore diameter, d;
• Inner race outer diameter, d1;
• Outermost diameter, D;
• Outer race inner diameter, D1;
• Pitch diameter (twice the distance from the centre of a rolling element to the centre of the
bearing), Dp;
• Inner race groove radius, rg,i;
• Outer race groove radius, rg,o.
There are many types of bearings, such as deep groove ball bearings, angular contact ball
bearings, cylindrical roller bearings. They constrain diﬀerent DOF depending on application, all
allow rotation about the axis perpendicular to the cross section, and some do not constrain the
other two rotational DOF. The diﬀerences in modelling diﬀerent types of rolling element bearings
(bearings with rolling elements e.g. not journal bearings) are not big; the main diﬀerences involve
the geometrical relations discussed in the following sections. It is seen as rather arbitrary to
simulate all types of bearings, as it adds too much complexity and variables to analyse; therefore,
deep groove ball bearings will be the main topic, so that the discussion can be kept concise.
The accurate modelling of a bearing can be a computationally heavy task. The behaviour
of a bearing has an angular dependency, since the location of the rolling supports within bearings
is not constrained to any one position; this makes it a time-dependent problem. Not only is the
problem time-dependent, but it is also non-linear, which adds a large degree of complication to

4.4
Global Model
Up until now, the components have been considered separately, but they need to be gathered
and coupled with each other. The shaft is the foundation of the system and acts as a liaison
between the other components. Each shaft element is comprised of two nodes, and two shaft
elements located next to each other share one node. Thus, the ﬁrst element E1 contains nodes
N1 and N2, the second element E2 contains nodes N2 and N3 and so on. Therefore, the only
nodes that are not contained within two elements are the ﬁrst node and the last one. This is

Mathematically this means that, considering only the shaft elements, all 8 degrees of
freedom of shaft element are deﬁned by the matrices in equations 15 to 18 and are added together
at the nodes. The matrix, which contains all the elements, so-called global matrix, is of size
4 × NN = 4 × (NE + 1), where NN is the number of nodes, NE is the number of elements,
and 4 comes from the number of degrees of freedom of each node. Each element has a reserved
spot in the global matrix, the ﬁrst 8x8 shaft element matrix is mounted on the ﬁrst 8 rows and
columns of the global matrix. Since element 2 shares a node with the ﬁrst one, it will be mounted
on rows and columns 5 to 12, and since they now share a node, they are mathematically coupled.
This is done for all of the elements. The word "mounting" simply refers to the addition of the
element matrices to the correct locations in the global matrix. Figure 5 shows the systematic
approach graphically. The next step is to mount the other components, bearings and discs, on
the system. Since these components are installed at a single place, then they are mounted on the
node at which they are located, and their matrices are mounted in that position of the global
matrix. This is done for the global stiﬀness, damping, gyroscopic and mass matrices.
The current model assumes that both bearings and discs have only four degrees of freedom
in the horizontal and the vertical directions and the rotations about them. For the disc, there
is no problem, but the bearing model described in Section 4.3 assumes degrees of freedom to
include the bearing casing, which is not included in the current global model.
Therefore, a
new model is proposed to incorporate slight changes. Instead of having an 8x8 matrix for each
element, a 12x12 matrix is proposed. Since each node has the potential of having a bearing, two
more degrees of freedom are added at every node. This poses a problem, as the nodes that do

5.1
Pre-processing
There are multiple steps that must be undertaken before a solution is sought out. The ﬁrst
step is to deﬁne the actual system.
Assume a system with NB bearings located on nodes
[nb,1, nb,2, ..., nb,NB], with ND discs located on nodes [nd,1, nd,2, ..., nd,ND] and a number of shaft

objects cannot intersect each other without consequences), then the number of shaft elements
must be greater than or equal to the sum of disc nodes and bearing nodes minus one. In the
case of shaft modelling, every element touches two nodes, but the nodes at the ends of the shaft
touch only one element each; therefore, there is always one more node than element.

stage the potential to mount bearings on either node of the element is given. The mass, gyroscopic

6
Defect Modelling and Fatigue
This section discusses in detail the construction of a high ﬁdelity mathematical model of a
defective bearing element and reverberation of the defect’s eﬀects through the system.
The
section also discusses the methodology used in the diagnostic part of the defect. Modelling the
defect has little meaning, if one does not know how to operate on its functions that will give
valuable information. It is very important to note that the method of modelling the defect and
the method of predicting its progression are independent methods; in other words, one method
scrambles the puzzle, and the other one pieces it together.
6.1
Fatigue and Fatigue Progression
It is well known that fatigue will occur over time in dynamic machinery, and it is unavoidable.
However, due to the chaotic behaviour of fatigue progression, predicting it can be extremely
diﬃcult and inaccurate, and it is a ﬁeld, which is not very well understood. First let’s deﬁne
the fatigue limit as the point, where defects in machinery cause catastrophic failure. The fatigue
limit of machine elements is highly dependent on the function, and a machine element will have
a diﬀerent fatigue limit depending on how it is used.
Many theories have been put forth in an attempt to roughly estimate the expected life of
bearings (see Sections 2.5 and 3.3). A common estimate of the duration of bearing components
is the life in number of cycles denoted Ln, where the subscript n is the percentage of bearings
that fail after L cycles. Identical bearings under identical loads and circumstances will fail at
diﬀerent moments, following a probabilistic distribution, this distribution is known as the Weibull
distribution [37].
The problem with the theories discussed so far is that they lack the ability of updating
themselves once a bearing begins its operation. It means that if a bearing develops a defect
sooner than the average, then these theories will not be able to adjust themselves to recalculate
the life of the bearing. In other words, these theories calculate the expected lifetime of a bearing
based on statistics of the "general" bearing, but one would like to estimate when any "individual"
bearing will fail. One such theory that can be used to predict a failure of an individual bearing
is the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) theory. The remaining useful life is deﬁned as the amount
of time (in seconds or cycles) that a component has left until failure occurs, or when it needs
to be replaced. In bearings, the RUL is calculated once a defect is detected, and using diﬀerent
45
==PAGE:61==
techniques the RUL can be updated based on life-fed vibrational data. There are two main
accepted ways of deriving the RUL, model-based prognostics and data-based prognostics. Model-
based prognostics calculate the RUL through physical means, with the application of multiple
theories and material properties. This can often times be overly complicated and vaguely deﬁned
[22], since variables describing eﬀects, such as creep, fatigue, wear and others, must be included.
Data-based prognostics seem to be the overly popular method of the RUL estimation, mainly
due to advancements and evolution of machine learning methods in recent years. It simply uses
data from real bearing time series to estimate the RUL for the next individual bearing. Given
that a bearing under a load is rotating, the basic steps for a predictive model of this kind are
following:
• Measure the vibrations at some time intervals.
• Extract features from the vibration.
• Feed features into the predictive model.
• Over time vibrations change due to fatigue and defect formation, which are picked up by
the sensors.
• Using the change, estimate the RUL. The more time series, the more accurate the prediction
will be.
• Train model with more cases.
As discussed in Section 2.5, there are many diﬀerent, equally eﬀective methods for es-
timating the RUL of a bearing; depending on the resources at hand, some methods may be
preferred to other.
A few problems arise, when one wants to implement these methods in a numerical ex-
periment. From the numerical perspective, there should be a theory that aids in predicting the
trend of the progression, e.g. how the defect will grow over time. The second problem concerns
the limit/threshold or the deﬁnition of a catastrophic failure of a bearing.
Regarding the ﬁrst problem, since this thesis is a numerical one, it is possible to generate
vibration time series with multiple diﬀerent speciﬁcations about severity, bearing properties and
so forth. Then given that one has simulated multiple cases at diﬀerent defect severities, train a
model based on the generated data and possibly deﬁne the time between the changes in the defect

severity. This can be justiﬁed, because if this technique were to be applied on actual machinery,
then the model would have to be trained on the actual machinery, and in a proof-of-concept

The second problem is more diﬃcult to address, as the fatigue limit is heavily dependent
on a great number of conditions. Wei Guo et al. [17] discuss the damage variable D ∈[0, 1] ,
where 0 deﬁnes a healthy bearing and 1 a destroyed one. They state that bearings usually fail
once the damage to the bearing reaches a critical damage Dc , which is usually on the range of
0.2 −0.8. Another way of deﬁning the fatigue limit is by using ISO [35], which deﬁne a limit of
allowable vibrations for a bearing function. Nathan Bolander et al. [18] deﬁne the fatigue limit
at the point, where a spall reaches a length equal one rolling element spacing, called the critical
spall length, and is calculated as the angle between any two consecutive rolling elements in the
cage multiplied by the radius of the contact. They do, however, state that in some circumstances,
a bearing can function even if the spall has progressed over the entire circumference of the race,
but for the purpose of this numerical experiment it will be used as a means of proof-of-concept.

Multiple models for bearing defect simulation have been researched. Due to a number of diﬀerent
defect types, no single model can be used to describe all defects. A defect is deﬁned by the race
it is located on, the angle of its centre (φd) and its angular span (∆φd). If the defect is on the
outer race, then its position does not change over time, but on the inner race, the defect rotates
at the angular velocity of the shaft, therefore

Machine Learning Implementation
Although there are many ways to implement ML, all methods require a set of features to be
analysed. Choosing these features is an extremely important step, missing a key feature could
result in a non-functioning model.
One would hope that a feature could be, for example, a
segment of a vibration or a frequency response plot, but in reality the features that must be
extracted are only single numbers. Therefore, a series of mathematical operations needs to be
performed on the generated vibration time series in order to extract the single number features
and feed them into the learning model.
Much eﬀort and discussion has been put into which features to extract from a signal in
order to obtain accurate results. One does not want to extract too many features, as this may
cause over-ﬁtting [15], which will negatively aﬀect results during the model testing phase [41].
Therefore, it is decided to focus mainly on time domain and frequency domain features. After
this point, the steps can get somewhat complicated.
Once all the features of the signal have been calculated, it is time to apply PCA to the
values. At this point, it might be a good idea to reduce the number of features used. When
selecting features or parameters for prognostics and in attempt to identify a health indicator
(HI), a variable representing the "health" of a component, the focus should be on parameters
with three key qualities: monotonicity, prognosability and trendability [29]. Monotonicity is a
measure of the general positive or negative trend of the HI: high monotonicity means a positive
trend, and a low one means a negative trend. Prognosability identiﬁes how well clustered the
HI is at failure; after multiple run-to-failure scenarios, what the variance of the HI value is.
Trendability is a similarity measure between the trajectories of condition indicators. Due to
its nature, the value of the HI will be slightly diﬀerent, when bearings in similar run-to-failure
situations ultimately fail. This is why it is imperative to deﬁne a single principal component
vector used to calculate the HI for bearings under similar enough conditions. If a bearing is
replacing another bearing, then it is feasible to use the same HI threshold and transformation
vector for the newly installed bearing. Intuitively, one should not use the data obtained from
a bearing and apply the methods on a completely diﬀerent bearing under completely diﬀerent
conditions.
It should be rather obvious that when trying to predict a bearing’s failure, one should
extract the same features from the vibration signal as extracted for a similar bearing that is
56
==PAGE:72==
used as a reference. Let’s assume that a company has a machine with an arbitrary, but large
enough number of similar bearings under similar conditions (ﬂat, straight conveyor belt) and
wants to optimise bearing replacement. Assume next that there are also accelerometers on the
pedestals of the bearings, and the vibration signal is recorded and saved. At ﬁrst, there are no
data regarding when the component will fail. As bearings start to gradually fail and become
replaced, the vibration signals are stored and processed. After a certain amount of time, the
company has multiple run-to-failure trends and has created multiple exponential degradation
models (EDMs), then the models could be used in an ensemble to more accurately predict the
RUL. An ensemble method in this case could simply be the average predicted RUL given data
obtained from all models [41].
There is one problem, that is how to pick and choose the features. The relative importance
of each feature can be described by monotonicity, which can be calculated as

the point of bearing failure.

Simulations and Model Training
The main aim of the thesis is to simulate a defective system, the design of which is a delicate
task. The simulations are developed and coded in MATLAB, and the procedure and key aspects
are discussed in this section. The simulated vibration of the system is then fed into the ML
model, the set-up of which is also discussed in this section. Some assumptions need to be made
during the modelling process, and they are:
• The damping and stiﬀness eﬀects of the bearing inner and outer races are ignored and
assumed to be a part of the shaft and the pedestal, and thus these eﬀects are outsourced
only to the rolling elements. However, mass is included.
• The bearing pedestal properties are assumed and not calculated.
• Bearing clearance is assumed to be zero and is justiﬁed for the purposes of this simulation
[8].
• The value of the health indicator threshold will be approximately the same if a new com-
ponent is installed to replace the old one.
The last point means that a new threshold value does not need to be calculated every time a
component is replaced. It stands to reason that if a bearing fails and has available data recorded
through its lifetime, then the bearing that replaces it will behave similarly enough to apply ML
methods on the failed bearing data to predict the behaviour of the replacement bearing.

Simulation Post-Processing
Post-processing is, simply put, a series of mathematical operations applied to the simulated
vibration time series; the operations can be found in Section 7. The results of the observations
should be stored in a table and saved for further processing.
Using the feature table, calculate the monotonicity of all features that have runs available
for processing. Then remove features with a monotonicity below a predeﬁned threshold and
use the remaining features to calculate the PCs. The ﬁrst PC transforms an observation to the
current value of the HI. The HI is the variable to be input into the EDM to predict the RUL.
A similar method for processing the results and predicting the RUL is provided by MathWorks
[45].
The simulated vibration signal and its processing is then compared to the actual vibration
signal, processed in the same way, and the results are compared. The real vibration signal is of
a wind turbine and is provided by MATLAB.

Results
When simulating diﬀerent vibrations and responses in a numerical way, the choice of a system is
arbitrary until it is tested and veriﬁed in a real system. Due to the desire to analyse one system
deeply and accurately instead of rough analysis of multiple systems as well as lack of time and
insuﬃcient resources, the simulations were kept simple and concise.
There are two main experiments carried out. The ﬁrst one is a 4 DOF model, which
includes a cross section of a shaft element with a bearing mounted on it. It considers only the

10.3
Frequency Domain Analysis
As stated in Section 7, a lot of information can be extracted from the peaks of FFT graphs.
Mainly, frequencies of the peaks can be informative about where a defect is located. The defect
was simulated on the outer race of the bearing, and peaks should therefore be dependent on
the ball pass frequency of the outer race fbpo and its harmonics. However, there may be peaks
due to other frequencies that are also monitored. The monitored frequencies are the ball pass
frequencies of the inner and the outer races, the ball spin frequency, the shaft rotational frequency
and the bearing cage rotational frequency as explained in Section 7.
To analyse the frequencies, a peak ﬁnding algorithm is implemented to deduce the location
and the amplitude of each peak. If a peak resonates at a harmonic of one of the monitored
frequencies, a dashed line depicting the harmonic is plotted.
The defect-free case, the sub-
simulation with a defect of the half critical length and the most severe defect are plotted in the
descending order to compare changes in peaks and resonating frequencies as the defect severity
progresses. The algorithm is not perfect, as deﬁning a peak can be mathematically complicated
and each sub-simulation has diﬀerent values for the peaks, albeit similar frequencies. Figures
31 and 32 show the resonance graphs for the 4 and 10 DOF simulations with a precision of 50.
The ﬁgures show what peaks in the FFT graph resonate with a monitored frequency, as well as
how they change with the increasing defect severity. The top ﬁgure shows a defect-free bearing,
the middle ﬁgure presents the FFT graph when the defect size is half of the critical length, and
the bottom ﬁgure shows the FFT when the spall size has reached the critical spall length. The
peaks calculated by the peak ﬁnding function are also graphed in the ﬁgures. They show that
there are some peaks that do not resonate with any of these frequencies, but those peaks are
negligible in size, and all relevant peaks are captured. Clearly all of the larger amplitude peaks
seem to resonate with a frequency of the ball pass frequency of the outer race and some of its
harmonics (BPO). This shows that all larger, more serious peaks are related to the defect, which
was deﬁned to be on the outer race.
The only noticeable diﬀerence between the two cases is the scale of the amplitudes. The
10 DOF simulation has much higher amplitudes than the 4 DOF simulation. This may be due
to the added components to the system as they can cause more severe vibrations.
The most notable factor in Figures 31 and 32 is that the model resonates with many
diﬀerent frequencies, but as the defect becomes larger, the harmonics of the BPO frequency are
74
==PAGE:90==
heavily favoured and cause much more resonance than any other frequencies. This demonstrates
that a potential indicator for locating and diagnosing a defect can be found in frequency domain
variables, such as FFT graphs. It means that at ﬁrst many frequencies resonate. If a defect
develops on the outer race, then it will be reﬂected in the FFT graphs, since the frequencies of
the harmonics would resonate more vigorously. If a defect develops on the inner race, then the
frequencies dependent on the ball pass frequency of the inner race will resonate more and so on.
However, it can be a very complicated process in reality, since noise can negatively aﬀect the
recorded data. FFT is simply an eﬀective solution for diagnosing bearing defect frequencies, but
frequencies of other larger components have the tendency to mask less prominent frequencies
[33].
Figures 33 to 34 show the spectral kurtosis trends for three defect severities, as well as
peaks that resonate at any of the monitored frequencies, the same ones as in the FFT ﬁgures. The
most notable diﬀerence is the number of peaks that do not resonate with any of the monitored
frequencies. As the defect severity increases, more peaks start to resonate, especially to the
harmonics of the BPO frequency. This trend points to the possibility of a correlation between
the defect severity and the number of peaks resonating at the frequency attributed to a defect in
the component. Unfortunately, it is not possible to compare the simulated data with the wind
turbine data (see later), since the rotational velocities and the exact size of the wind turbine
bearing are all unknown, and therefore the frequencies cannot be calculated and monitored.

Vibration of a Wind Turbine
As a simple way of comparing the simulated results to a real case, the same method as was
applied to the simulated time series is applied to recorded vibration time series of a wind turbine,
following similar steps as previously described [45].
Vibrations were recorded from a wind turbine for 6 seconds a day for 50 days to see the
changes in time. There are a few notable diﬀerences that must be considered when comparing
the wind turbine to the generated vibrations. The wind turbines bearing is signiﬁcantly larger
than the simulated bearing; the sampling rate of the accelerometer was 97656 Hz, and it is
unknown how far along the bearing’s degradation was at the recording of the ﬁrst sample.
The amplitudes of the vibrations do not seem to vary as drastically between recordings
for the wind turbine as they do in the simulated data (see Figure 35). The FFT graph does not
show any visible change as time progresses, or at least they are less noticeable when compared to
the simulated vibrations. This can be attributed to small changes in the vibrational amplitude,
a correlation between the vibrational amplitude and the amplitude of the FFT was also observed
in the 4 and 10 DOF simulations. There is, however, a very noticeable change in the SK graph
for the wind turbine, as it shows a clear progression in amplitude over time. As stated in [26], SK
has been seen as a better estimate for wind turbines, since the rotational velocity is not constant
at all times; if it were constant, such as in the case of the simulated data, the results should be
quite similar. This points to a possibility that some element is missing in the simulations that
appears in recorded vibration data. There are too many diﬀerences between the simulated cases

and the wind turbine case to make any concrete conclusions, but there is a hint from the real-life
data that suggests that the modelling could be improved.
Graphs from other studies [20, 21, 27] that recorded the progression of the amplitudes of
a bearing from start to failure also show a very slow progression of the vibrations’ amplitudes
over time. This could be due to the fact that the degradation rate was not considered in the
simulations. The simulations simply assume a linear progression in defect length in every iter-

methods were found that could accurately deﬁne the rate of defect progression over time, the
growth is assumed to be linear, given that each iteration in the simulations is evenly spaced out

the end, then a progression, which is similar to one seen in the wind turbine vibration signal,
could be observed.

Feature Extraction
The steps undertaken in this part of the experiment can be somewhat complicated. When calcu-
lating the principal components (PCs) of the extracted features, better results can be obtained
by splitting the data set into a training and a test set as commonly done in machine learning and
data analytics [41]. This can also be warranted, because when estimating the RUL of a bearing,
one does not have the future data for the bearing. Therefore, to accurately and realistically
predict the RUL, the simulated data must be split into a test and a training set. The training
set includes all simulated data, up to a certain point, e.g. from iterations 1 through 5 if there is a
precision of 10, and the test set would be iterations 6 to 10. The PCs can be thought of as trans-
formation vectors from an n dimensional space to a single dimensional space; the transformation
vectors can then be applied to any future observation, so that future observations undergo the
same transformation.

split (break point) of test and training sets aﬀects the rating produced by the monotonicity. The

RUL Prediction
The ﬁnal step is to use the training set to predict the outcome of the test set, in simpler terms:
prediction of the RUL. The RUL estimation for each case is depicted in Figures 48 to 51. The
blue line shows the true RUL, which in this case is simply the number of iterations that are left
to be compiled, with a 20% conﬁdence interval. The red dashed line shows the predicted RUL
based on the model. The predicted RUL’s path does not start at the ﬁrst iteration, because the
algorithm used to predict the RUL applies a sequential slope test to detect whether the slope
of the incoming data is signiﬁcant enough to be considered a defect based on the T-test. Once
there is a signiﬁcant deviation of the slope, then the algorithm detects a defect and immediately
predicts the RUL with conﬁdence intervals. It should be expected that the ﬁrst predictions are oﬀ
with a large conﬁdence interval, but should converge to a solution as time or iterations progress.
This happens because the weights of the diﬀerent variables are estimated at ﬁrst (guess values),
and then they are updated and improved as new data come in.
The 4 DOF systems perform relatively well when predicting the RUL. With a precision
of 10, the EDM algorithm does not detect a defect until the 6th iteration, although this is not
strange, since there are not very many observations in this simulation.
This becomes more
apparent in the 50 precision simulation, as it detects a defect at the 5th iteration, a relatively
similar number of iterations was required to detect a defect. The 10 precision case predicts the
RUL well, and after a single iteration the true RUL is within the model’s conﬁdence interval,

The 50 precision simulation is bit more rebellious: at ﬁrst the predicted RUL is 0 with a very

Discussion
Although a lot of good results were obtained, there are deﬁnitely multiple elements lacking in
the mathematical model. The ML technique used to predict the RUL of a bearing seems to be
promising, and with further research and development, it could be possible to turn the results
and modelling techniques into a very useful monitoring and prognostic software based purely on
mathematics and theories of applied mechanics.
11.1
General Discussion
The main goal of this project is to predict failure of a bearing through a mathematical model using
machine learning. The groundwork that makes this possible is Hertz Contact Theory (HCT),
which, in simple terms, applies geometrical derivations and material-speciﬁc properties (equations
32 to 49) to calculate parameters to solve force balance equations for contact between diﬀerent
types of surfaces (equations 52 and 53). HCT was applied to calculate the load deﬂection factor
(equation 54) of a bearing, which relates the elasticity of the material and the bearing geometry
to the time-dependent stiﬀness forces exerted on the bearing’s rolling elements. Estimates of
bearings’ rolling element damping (equation 70) have been previously derived through elasto-
hydrodynamic lubrication theory and empirical research and thus give the potential to model
a bearing as a spring-damper system. The damping coeﬃcient was calculated by ﬁnding the
solution to the quasi-static displacement of the bearing and estimating the quasi-static force
applied to the bearing from external components, such as a shaft or a disc. The calculated initial
displacement was also used as the initial solution when simulating the model. The bearing model
neglected damping and stiﬀness of the inner and the outer races, as they are very small when
compared to stiﬀness and damping provided by the shaft and the bearing casing. The bearing
was modelled as a 4 degree of freedom (DOF) system, with horizontal and vertical axes for
the inner and the outer races of the bearing. The DOF for the inner race of the bearing were
constrained to be equivalent to those of the shaft that it holds, a way to connect the diﬀerent
models.
The shaft was modelled as an 8 DOF system with mass, gyroscopic and stiﬀness com-
ponents (Section 4.1), a majority of damping assumed to come from the bearings. The shaft
was comprised of two nodes interacting with each other, each node had a vertical and horizontal
axis parallel to the cross section of the shaft, as well as two rotational DOF about those axes.
93
==PAGE:109==
The components were mounted onto nodes and interacted with one another through the shaft,
mathematically speaking. A disc model was also included (Section 4.2). Two of the bearing
DOFs were incompatible with the shaft model. This was solved by adding two DOFs to the
global model on the bearing containing nodes.
A spall along the races of the bearing was the simulated defect. By applying equation 55
and including a defect proﬁle Cr(φ) to simulate the surface of the spall, a reaction in the system
was stimulated. The defect proﬁle equation was simply a cosine curve with more periods as
the spall size increases; using an analytical equation for the defect proﬁle was preferred, so that
the derivative would be easily calculable and would not require computationally heavy methods
to numerically calculate them (equation 79). Spall surface proﬁles are unique in nature and
chaotic, making it diﬃcult to simulate accurately. The defect surface aﬀected the deformation
of a rolling element situated on it, which changed stiﬀness and damping force distribution,
ultimately changing the simulated vibration time series.
It should be noted that no theory
linking the statistical probability of a defect occurring at any point in time was applied, neither
was a theory about defect progression over time. What was simulated were the vibrations of the
bearing given that the defect had reached a certain size. On every iteration, the spall length
was increased by a set amount. In reality however, spall size does not necessarily grow linearly
with time; it could be possible that the third or fourth iteration in Figure 19 explain accurately
the vibration trend for a majority of the bearing life, and the last three iterations develop only
shortly before failure.
To simulate the vibrations, an ordinary diﬀerential equation solver was used. It numeri-
cally estimates the next point in time based on the previous point. So, given an initial solution
calculated from the quasi-static solution, the next point is estimated, similar to the Newton-
Raphson method; a few iterations are required before the simulated vibration signal converges
towards a more periodic vibration. Equation 85 and 88 allowed a mathematical solution for
calculating acceleration at the next time point given a displacement and velocity of the previous
point. Thus, after the steps undertaken so far, a method for simulating a vibration of a bearing
with ranging defect severities was formulated.
The next step was to attempt to predict, when a component would fail or need replace-
ment, based on the mathematical model. To do so, some sort of threshold needed to be deﬁned.
The threshold was taken to be the critical spall length [18], deﬁned as the distance between two
94
==PAGE:110==
consecutive rolling elements of a bearing. Four cases were analysed. The ﬁrst system was a single
element system containing a bearing on the ﬁrst node and a disc on the second node. It had
10 DOF in total, since two extra DOF were added to simulate the bearing. It was simulated
with a precision of 10 and 50, where precision is deﬁned as the number of sub-simulations from
defect-free to a defect of the threshold value length. The second analysed system was a 4 DOF
system, a simple simulation where only a small cross section of the shaft was used. As a control
and for comparison, an actual vibration signal of a wind turbine recorded for 6 seconds a day
50 days before failure was processed in the same way as the simulated data. There are many
diﬀerences between the wind turbine system and the simulated systems. For instance, rotational
velocity is not known for the wind turbine, neither is the size of the bearing within it, no vibra-
tion signals are available prior to the 50 day test period, making it diﬃcult to assume how far the
degradation process has come along. Therefore, it is diﬃcult to draw any concrete conclusions,
albeit similarities or diﬀerences between the cases can point to some possible conclusions.
The ﬁrst step in predicting the RUL of the component is to process the incoming signals.
This was done through principal component analysis (Section 3.3).
The PC vectors can be
thought of as transformation vectors, which reduce the dimensionality of a problem; the goal is to
ﬁnd a transformation vector that has a monotonic, preferably exponential growth with time. This
was done by rating the monotonicity (equation 111) of each feature, which were extracted from
the vibration signal (Section 7). Since one does not have future data, an adaptive model, which
"learns" from incoming data, is used. The break point was deﬁned to split the data into a training
set, which trains the model to predict the values of the testing set that includes observations
collected after the training (in time). The PC or transformation vectors were calculated based
on the training set, the vector also calculated the threshold value of the health indicator (HI),
which is a measure of degree of degradation. The threshold value used is calculated beforehand.
In reality, for obvious reasons, this is not possible, but under the assumption that there exists
a data set containing the vibration data for an identical bearing in an identical system from
installation to failure, the threshold value of the HI should be statistically similar for a newly
installed bearing. Therefore, a RUL with conﬁdence intervals was calculated to try to make up
for the true statistical variance of the HI. The HI should be regarded as a variable independent
of the simulation. In reality, it is a variable that is calculated from real data, and its threshold
depends on the system, the operation and the user. When new data come in, the threshold value
95
==PAGE:111==
is based purely on past experience and is not dependent on the data derived from the current
component. Since all modelling was done mathematically, it is diﬃcult to grasp the variables
that make similar components behave and degrade diﬀerently.
Once the PC vectors were obtained, each new observation was transformed using them
and input into the exponential degradation model (EDM), which attempted to predict the RUL,
by updating the variables it needs (equation 13).
The variables of the EDM were updated,
so that it reﬂects accurately an exponential growth trend that ﬁts the calculated HI at each
given defect severity. A sequential slope detection algorithm was applied to test whether a new
observation was statistically signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the previous observation. If the algorithm
picked up a signiﬁcant diﬀerence, then a defect or a degradation had been detected. Once the
defect was detected, the adaptiveness of the model came into play, and with increasing iterations
the model converged to a solution of the true RUL. Both the 4 DOF and 10 DOF simulations
converged towards the true RUL, the 50 precision cases did so more accurately and the 10 DOF
out preformed the 4 DOF. The 10 DOF 50 precision simulation in many regards was similar to
the wind turbine control case, which shows promise.
11.2
Useful Results
Despite multiple shortcomings and diﬃculties, there are valuable results that can be extracted.
If the process of mathematical modelling is improved and tested, then a method for predicting
the RUL mathematically will become available and could be applied. The ability to predict the
RUL based on mathematics alone would save time and money, because a run-to-failure data
set of a similar component would not be required.
The EDM’s ability to be very adaptive,
constantly updating the values of its variables based on incoming data, makes it very suitable
for monitoring machine systems, since there are always diﬀerences in performance between all
machine components.
When comparing the model developed with other models of relevant research, many sim-
ilarities can be found. Figure 53 shows a zoomed in plot of the generated vibration signal. Not
only are the peaks very well deﬁned, but this trend closely resembles Figure 21 in [9]. When the
results of the simulated vibration time series are compared with [14], Figures 4-9 of the cited text
seem to give very similar trends as the results presented here, although the comparison is diﬃcult
due to diﬀerent conditions. The similarities found with other papers imply that modelling of a
96
==PAGE:112==
bearing was done well.
Figure 53: Zoom in on simulated vibration. The plot shows that the time diﬀerence between the peaks
is equivalent to the ball pass frequency of the outer race.
The vibrational amplitude of the bearing time series grew with increasing severity, as
was expected. Not only that, but the FFT showed clear resonance with the BPO frequency.
However, the SK did not perform as well, which implies that the FFT might be a better indicator
for diagnosing a defective bearing. Clear trends were noticed though for the wind turbine data
set and the SK. It was also seen that resonating frequencies had much higher amplitudes in the
FFT graphs as the defect grew larger, pointing to the possibility that the amplitude can be an
indicator of defect severity. The higher frequencies also resonated more severely with increased
defect severity, which could imply that monitoring higher frequencies should be preferred
The high ﬁdelity model of the bearing can be modiﬁed to produce any type of rolling
element bearing. The program script allows for angular contact roller bearings, as well as cylin-
drical roller bearings. With very minor adjustments, double row bearings can also be modelled,
thus further expanding the capabilities of the work completed.
A method for processing a vibration signal and extracting features was also developed.
This methodology can be reused for data-driven RUL calculations, as well as simulated calcula-
tions. Clear steps that need to be undertaken in this sector have been well described and can be
applied. A similar progression of the HI was also noticed between the wind turbine data and 10
DOF 50 precision simulation, reinforcing the idea that the modelling has been done well.
In all of the simulated cases, the EDM managed to predict the RUL with rather good
precision. That coupled with the results of the wind turbine example implies that the method

used for predicting the RUL can be applied to real machinery. This brings us closer to develop-
ing a model that can predict failure independent of run-to-failure vibration time series of past
components.
11.3
Shortcomings and Diﬃculties
In the complete model, the FE bearing model and the ML model are interdependent via a massive
number of variables, equations and assumptions. It can, therefore, be a very intense task to ﬁnd
errors, variable correlations and draw concrete conclusions. For example, the number of rolling
elements in a simulated bearing has dramatic eﬀects on the resulting vibrations calculated.
Figure 54: Simulated vibration time series with 11 rolling elements.
Figure 54 shows the evolution of the vibration time series for a bearing with 11 rolling
elements and a defect ranging to its critical spall length that is diﬀerent from the case where 15
rolling elements are used (Figure 19). This may be due to the fact that the critical spall length is
larger for a bearing with 11 rolling elements. It may also be due to the eﬀects of the defect proﬁle
function. However, it can be concluded that the model is very sensitive to the number of rolling
elements included, which it should preferably not be. It could also be attributed to a badly
designed bearing, its dimensions cause abnormal vibrations and the model could be accurate.
The shift from the vibrations of a defect-free bearing to the vibration of a bearing with a
small defect is dramatic. This is in part due to the fact that the simulation technique used for
a defect-free bearing assumes a completely smooth surface with absolutely no physical defects.
However, the eﬃcacy of this can be questioned, as bearings are never 100% smooth and defect-
98
==PAGE:114==
free. This is why, even though the defect’s average depth is on the order of micrometers, it causes
a radical shift in the amplitudes of the vibrations.
The method for generating the defect proﬁle can also be questioned. A rational equation
with a derivative was used, so that the damping eﬀects could be included. Therefore, a defect,
such as the one generated by Petersen et al., could not be applied. MATLAB’s Simulink does
oﬀer a solution, as it can compute the numerical derivative and integral of a signal, and a defect
proﬁle generated through randomness could be used. This solution was attempted, but due to
the size of the system and lack of available computing power, it simply did not work and would
freeze before the simulation could be completed. A new Simulink model would also have to be
created for each defect severity simulated, which again adds a degree of extreme complexity and
a requirement of superb organisation to create and maintain those Simulink models. Another
diﬃculty with generating the defect proﬁle is the fact that there is no single accepted method
for defect generation. Setting up and coding the script for diﬀerent defect proﬁles is a long and
daunting task, since multiple things can go wrong (due to the array of variables). Furthermore,
each proﬁle has diﬀerent compatibility with the rest of the code. For example, if the defect
function is diﬀerentiable, then this needs to be taken into account in the entire script; if it is
diﬀerentiable by parts, then it needs be taken into account as well. There is also the problem of
expanding the defect. Multiple defect severities and their progression were considered, but not
even a single paper was found that considered this. Therefore, it is very hard to say how one
should scale a defect, whether one should add another period to the cosine function used or just
simply elongate the function, so it spans more length (see Figure 55).
The number of diﬀerent ﬁelds of knowledge also played a role in the shortcomings of
the results. FEM was applied along with Hertz theory, vibrational analysis, defect modelling,
material science (RUL prediction), ML, failure prediction and a lot of mathematics. Many of
the diﬀerent topics required very deep knowledge and understanding to be applied in a correct
manner, and a mistake at any point would ultimately have grave eﬀects on the end results.
Human error can also contribute to shortcomings. Due to the number of possibilities
during the modelling process, many diﬀerent scripts were written, and this made the organisation
process very complex. Back-tracking was also diﬃcult, since making a new script for every little
item, which needed to be changed, would have generated enormous amounts of scripts. Recording
every little change and its resulting eﬀects would also have a massive drawback, since going back

Another diﬃculty was that to the best of knowledge, no single research has been done

Conclusion
In summary, this project aimed to predict failure of a bearing by applying a mathematical model
and machine learning. First, a high ﬁdelity bearing model was developed and coupled with a
mathematical shaft model to simulate bearing vibration. A defect was introduced to the bearing
model at varying severities to simulate the progression of the vibration time series. Using the
time series, features of the signal were extracted in the time and frequency domain and through
machine learning methods, the RUL of the bearing was predicted. A 4 DOF system and a 10 DOF
system were simulated. The major ﬁndings were that the RUL was predicted by both models,
although this must ﬁrst be validated with a real recorded time series of the same system. It was
also found that frequencies of the defect and some of its harmonics resonated more severely as
the defect grew, pointing to the possibility of diagnosing a defect’s location and severity through
frequency domain analysis.

Future Objectives
Analysis of the identiﬁed shortcomings and better understanding of the problem at hand indicate
that there are numerous possibilities for improvement of the modelling and prediction techniques.
A more intricate mathematical model can be formulated. There are always assumptions
that need to be made in modelling. In the bearing model formulated, the mass of the rolling
elements was ignored, as well as their momentum and inertial eﬀects. The orientation of the
rolling elements was also ignored, since it was assumed that rolling element surface geometry
was radially constant. These factors could be included, but at the same time they complicate
the model immensely, thus slowing down the computation time and increasing the memory
requirements to run the simulation. It could also be possible to include mass, damping and
stiﬀness components of the bearing races in the global model. This could be done through a FE
model made in a software, such as ANSYS, and these parameters could be estimated. Including
a DOF for high resonance frequencies could also be considered in the new mathematical model,
as it is an accepted method and defects often excite higher frequencies [34].
The defect proﬁle and the way it is generated has room for improvement. The defect
proﬁle used in the modelling was very simple, and it was used, because it has an analytical
derivative. This is an extreme simpliﬁcation, and a defect proﬁle generated in a manner, such
as Petersen et al. [8], is a much more realistic interpretation of what the proﬁle would actually
look like. A method for numerically diﬀerentiating a randomly generated defect proﬁle would
oﬀer an adequate solution, but again would greatly increase the computation requirements.
Another future goal is to add more statistical components to the defect modelling part.
One component could be the statistical probability of a defect forming at any given location.
Realistically, a defect does not form in one location and develop from there, but over time many
defects can form in diﬀerent places at diﬀerent times, and larger defects have most likely been
developing for a longer time than the smaller ones. Another component to add could be the
statistical growth of the defect over time, or how the defect grows over time. These components
could be developed by means of very intricate FE models that simulate sub-surface cracks and
their growth and are coupled with statistics of run-to-failure trends of actual bearings to validate
the FE simulation.
Diﬀerent ML and AI methods could also be developed to predict the RUL. Many papers
[19, 20, 21] and [27]-[31] use diﬀerent AI and ML models, all of which show promising results
103
==PAGE:119==
in applicability. Using diﬀerent methods is generally preferred, since applying many diﬀerent
methods to predict the same variable will, on average, give better results [41]. Ensemble methods
can also be used in a single modelling technique. For example, having the break point of the
test and the training data at diﬀerent points results in a diﬀerent PC transformation vector.
Mathematically this imposes a diﬀerence between cases and could lead to better results.
A
MoG-HMM could be applied to predict the RUL, as well as a long-short term memory (LSTM)
model, although a lot of research and development is needed to create these models in addition
to deep understanding and experience in the ﬁeld of data analytics. It is also possible to attempt
to extract and use diﬀerent features or use only a certain number of features. In the test cases,
features needed only a monotonicity of 0.3 to be included in further analysis. For some cases, 15
or more features were extracted, whereas the extracted feature were only 5 in the wind turbine
control. Including too many features has the potential to cause over-ﬁtting, so it could be possible
to improve the model’s predictive capabilities by reducing the number of features.
Artiﬁcial intelligence, which focuses on locating the defect and estimating its severity,
could also be developed. The results showed that as a defect increases in size, frequencies related
the defect begin to resonate. The frequencies at which resonance occurs could be used as an
indicator of the defect location, and the amplitude of the resonance could be an indicator of its
severity. Using a model, such as a LSTM artiﬁcial neural network, the frequency domain can be
monitored, and changes in the frequency domain over time could be used to train the model and
help it develop.
It is also possible to develop an adaptive model to simulate the bearing vibration using
actual bearing data. This would be accomplished by setting up a simple system and attaching
accelerometers on the bearing casing. The system is then modelled with the methods formulated,
but has the added ability to adapt to incoming signals. Thus, the signals from the real system
are input into the mathematical model, and the parameters change to more closely resemble the
real signal. It is the same way as the exponential degradation model worked when predicting the
RUL: as more observations are recorded, the model updates its parameters to match incoming
data and predict future observations. Creating an adaptive model, such as this one, would be
very beneﬁcial, since over time the model becomes more accurate and potentially will not need
input data from the actual system to simulate and predict the vibration signal.
The ﬁnal goal would be to develop a software, which includes everything discussed so
104
==PAGE:120==
far. In this software, the users can design their own machine systems and simulate them. The
software could apply its modelling techniques to identify all sorts of parameters, such as the
threshold value for health indicators.
It could then be used to monitor machines and raise
alarms, if something goes wrong. This software could be developed given the right investment
and motivation, but it would take a lot of man-power to be fully completed. If it is developed,
then monitoring systems can become much more commonly available and have the potential to
be extremely economical. Knowing when a machine needs servicing or part replacement, before
failure happens, decreases the risk of catastrophic failure, which can be detrimental to human
health and company earnings. This vital information could also aid in decreasing machine down-
time, as well as protect components near the faulty one. Such software has also the added beneﬁt
of allowing a designer to test out a machine before manufacturing it, which could potentially be
very economic and could also help validate a design.

A
Preparing the Simulation
All development of scripts was done in a Linux environment (Linux Mint 19 Cinnamon) on a
computer with an Intel Core i5 quad-core processor running at 2.3 GHz and 7.6 GB of DDR4
random access memory (RAM), all dedicated towards simulations.
Linux is a recommended
operating system for this task, as it is much easier to develop and change the settings of the distro,
when compared with Microsoft Windows or Apple OS, as well as turning oﬀany unnecessary
background applications (every bit counts). Memory issues and crashes put a great hindrance on
the completion of this project. Therefore, it is reasonable to discuss ways to avoid RAM issues.
A.1
Programming Environment
At the start of the project, Python was used to run all simulations. This is not recommended
as Python, although simple and a powerful tool for data analysis, is extremely slow for purposes
of numerical calculations. This became clear, when one Python script took ten minutes to run,
while an equivalent MATLAB script took a few seconds with more accurate results. This is due to
the fact that MATLAB was designed for matrix calculations and does them at an astonishingly
faster rate than Python.
Numerical methods, such as odeXX, are also much more complex,
calculating at a variable time steps for optimum accuracy, whereas the Python equivalent does
not. Other numerical methods exist within MATLAB that tremendously help, as they would not
have to be rewritten in a new language in order to be used. MATLAB has also endless resources
of documentation and toolboxes designed for engineering, such as the predictive maintenance
toolbox, which was designed to estimate the RUL of components and machine learning toolboxes,
which do not have a Python equivalent. Therefore, it is recommended to use MATLAB instead
of Python.
A.2
Data Storage and Handling
Since the ﬁnal goal is to simulate multiple diﬀerent systems with varying components, a good
method for extracting information from across multiple simulations and comparing them is re-
quired in order to evaluate how a variable aﬀects the results. To do so, all information about a
simulation should be stored in a way that it can easily be searched for. In this project, it was
done by generating an array of identiﬁcation codes stored in text ﬁles, used to ﬁnd the informa-
111
==PAGE:127==
tion required, the searching algorithms were simply made MATLAB scripts. Two types of ﬁles
exist: the ones with the simulated data and the ﬁles that store the information. Within every
simulation folder, there is one information ﬁle and multiple run-to-failure simulations. These
ﬁles are stored within a directory labelled with a name, date, time and a unique integer, so that
no two directories storing the simulation data have identical names. Therefore, all of these ﬁles
can be stored within a single directory to avoid confusing and to keep the data organised. The
precision was deﬁned to be the number of simulations in each run-to-failure simulation, the
defect severity varies from none to maximum allowable threshold defect size, deﬁned in Section
6.2. Having more simulations on this interval should result in a better estimate of the RUL since
more information usually means better results in statistics. When one wants to be precise and
complete 100 runs with varying defect severity, then the memory required to store this is around
1-2 GB (becomes much larger for every DOF added to the system). Subsequently, comparing
diﬀerent runs with each other can quickly use up a lot of memory, since there is also a lot of RAM
required to run the script that compares the simulations. This is why saving the simulations is
always recommended, so that simulations are not lost when the software crashes and then one
would not spend more time to simulate everything again.
A.3
RAM Eﬃciency
If the computer used has 8 GB of RAM or less, then it is recommended to take measures in order
to eﬃciently use and empty memory. These tips were discovered after a long arduous hours spent
on trying to optimise the scripts, so that the computer would not crash, a common sight. There
are some routines that might help in saving RAM:
• Delete variables that are not being used or are no longer needed, especially matrices and
vectors e.g. clearvars varname in MATLAB.
• Pass on as few variables of as little size as possible into ODE functions, when multi-
threading many simultaneous simulations. Having large input size variables will add more
to the RAM requirements.
• If memory issues persist, it is possible to decrease the swap rate of the computer, as then
memory stored in the RAM is swapped over to the computer’s hard-drive, decreasing the
load on the RAM. Simply add the line vm.swappiness=X, where X is the new value of the
112
==PAGE:128==
swap rate, to the bottom of the ﬁle located in /etc/sysctl.conf.
• Save simulated data before comparing. When comparing, load only speciﬁc variables from
a ﬁle, fileEnsembleDatastore in MATLAB requires predictive maintenance toolbox and
multi-threading.
• Bearing containing nodes use more RAM, since they add extra DOF. A defective one
takes even more RAM and longer simulation time, since more calculations are involved.
Bearings should of course not be avoided, but one should keep in mind this added load on
the computer.
These steps are optional if RAM is not an issue.
A.4
Multi-Threading
Depending on the number of DOF in the system simulation, the run can take any amount of
time imaginable (at least a few seconds), and it grows very large very fast. One element is in
the order of 10 seconds to a minute (depending on the accuracy wanted from the simulation),
two elements take up to a few minutes, three elements at least ﬁve to ten minutes and so forth.
Since the run time grows so quickly, larger systems become harder to simulate, especially when
one wants to have at least 100 simulations in each run-to-failure simulations. Multi-threading
is a method of utilising more cores of a computer’s processor to run a single script. When one
does not specify the multi-threading use, then the entire script runs on a single core, leaving the
others free and not working. Multi-threading can be applied to solve diﬀerent severities of the
same system, but great care must be taken when doing so. Certain rules for multi-threading
need to be fulﬁlled to make it work; the tasks that are split up have to be mutually exclusive,
meaning they cannot aﬀect each other. MATLAB’s parfor loop was used instead of a normal
for loop when iterating through and simulating the diﬀerent defect severities. The run time was
reduced by the number of cores speciﬁed to be used during the run. However, this does also
increase the amount RAM requirements by the same amount, as the time was reduced. 8 GB of
RAM is recommended to use multi-threading on a one element system with one bearing and at
least 16 GB otherwise, although it will soon be insuﬃcient, if more elements are being added.

A.5
Ordinary Diﬀerential Solver
Sometimes when the sample rate of a sensor is lower than the frequency of a vibration, information
is lost, and the read signal is incorrect. A similar phenomenon happens, when an incorrect ODE
function is used, the settings of used functions are not correct or are at default values. MATLAB’s
odeXX functions oﬀer greatly versatile methods for solving ordinary diﬀerential equations but need
to have their settings changed. There are 8 diﬀerent ODE solvers oﬀered by MATLAB, and their
accuracy varies. The ﬁrst ODE solver to try is ode45, as it is recommended by the Mathworks
documentation for ODE solvers. However, it can happen that a system is "stiﬀ", which has
no clear deﬁnition but has to do with eigenvalues and instabilities due to them. This can cause
results to lose accuracy. MATLAB oﬀers a few diﬀerent ODE solvers to tackle this problem, such
as ode15s, ode23s, ode23t and ode23tb. The function ode15i is used for fully implicit problems
and is not applied in this project. ode23 can be used, when problems have bad tolerance issues,
and in the presence of moderate stiﬀness, the results are usually much lower than those obtained
with ode45. ode113 can be more eﬃcient than ode45, when many tolerance problems arise. Not
surprisingly, multiple issues with diﬀerent ODE solvers came up, and solutions to these problems
were not clear. The ﬁrst problem was a tolerance issue: when the tolerance is too high, the
code is faster, but results are inaccurate to a degree that it is very obvious that the results are
incorrect. It is recommended to change the tolerance of the ODE solver to the lowest allowable
amount and set the Refine option to 2. The Refine option simply adds more points around the
interpolation area making the signal peaks more smooth. After much trial, error and analysis,
the conclusion was made that ode45 should be used for defect-free bearings, since it is not highly
chaotic and non-linear, the results were good. For defective cases, ode45 resulted in a similar
vibration trend as in the simulations with tolerance problems. However, ode113 managed to
work through those simulations without any trouble. Hence, ode113 was applied to simulations
of defective bearings. To change the settings of the ODE functions:
1
o p t i o n s = odeset ( ’ RelTol ’ ,2.22045 e −14, ’ Refine ’ , 2 ) ;
2

Newton-Raphson Method
In many cases, one is required to solve a system of non-linear equations for which the Newton-
Raphson method is applied. Given a function f(x) = 0, where one wishes to solve for x, an
initial guess is made with regards to the value of x, called x0. The next guess is made using a
line that passes through (x0, f(x0)), at which point the slope of the line is equal to f′(x0). The
equation of the line is, therefore, y = f′(x0)(x −x0) + f(x0). The next guess x1 is then the
solution to 0 = f′(x0)(x1 −x0) + f(x0), which can be generally rewritten as
xn+1 = xn −f(xn)
