Summary
The objective of this project was to design, build, and test a 300 W DC/DC converter
for a wave energy harvesting buoy under development by Resen Waves. Input voltages
ranging from 25 V up to 700 V were identified as the main challenge in the application.
The output voltage was required to be constant, and a Synchronous Buck-boost topology
was selected, due to its ability to both step up and step down the input voltage. The
converter was controlled using Average Current Mode control and operating as either a
Boost, Buck-boost, or Buck converter, depending on the input voltage. The final design
of the converter proved to be able to fulfill the objective to some extend with a minimum
efficiency of 68% and a maximum of 95%. Lastly, the modifications needed in order to
fulfill the project objectives were identified and described.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis project revolves around the development of an offshore wave energy harvest-
ing buoy, designed by Resen Waves. The buoy is 170x170x100cm in size, and can deliver
a continuous output power of 300 W with surges of up to 600 W. Applications of the
buoy include powering instruments on the seabed and other data logging applications in
remote costal areas of the world where a diesel generator would normally be used. An
image of the buoy is shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Resen Waves 300W Wave Energy Harvesting Buoy
The buoy converts the kinetic energy in the waves to electrical energy through a
gearbox and generator that outputs an AC voltage. The AC voltage is rectified to DC
and controlled by a Power Converter that ensures the output voltage of the converter
is constant. All power electronic elements of the buoy are mounted inside the cylinder
in the middle of horseshoe shape of the buoy. The cylinder is hermetically sealed and
filled with a non-corrosive gas to ensure a stable and moisture-free environment for the
electronics. As the title of the thesis indicates, the power electronics of the buoy is the
topic of this project. A simplified diagram of the electrical system of the buoy and the
key components relevant to this thesis is presented in Figure 1.2.
The AC/DC converter rectifies and controls the voltage to charge the seabed battery
as well as keeping the 24 V Ancillary Battery charged. Keeping the ancillary battery
charged is critical to the functionality of the entire buoy as all electronics and controls

In this section an overview of the theoretical background to the converter is presented.
This includes a walk through of the selected topology and how it will have to be operated
in order to fulfil the specifications. Furthermore, the details of the control scheme will
be discussed.
2.1
Topology
The selected topology for this application is the Synchronous Buck-boost converter shown
in Figure 2.1. This topology is highly versatile and has the ability to be operated as
both a Buck-, Boost-, and Buck-boost converter because of the arrangement of the 4
transistors.

The three operating modes are needed in order to handle the very wide input voltage
range previously mentioned and they are further elaborated in the following subsections.

2.2
Control System
The purpose of the control system is to generate PWM-signals to drive the transistors
based on feedback from the power converter. For this application Average Current Mode
- control (ACM) has been selected to compensate the PWM signals. A Digital Signal
Processor (DSP) will be used for the practical implementation of the control system. In
Figure 2.8 a flow diagram of the control scheme is shown.
The controller consists of two loops; an outer voltage loop and an inner current loop.
The voltage loop takes the output voltage as feedback and the voltage compensator
provides the reference for the current compensator, that takes the inductor current as
feedback. Voltage and current compensation are both performed with PI-compensators.
Each PI-compensator has a proportional gain, KP, and a integral gain, Ki. Due to the
three operating modes (Boost, Buck-boost and Buck) of the converter, the compensator
gains have to be tuned independently for the three modes. In the mentioned Synthesis

CHAPTER 3
Practical Implementation
This chapter contains a detailed description of the design process gone through to achieve
the thesis goals. Included is the selection of the power/driving components, the design
of the PCB layout and the DSP implementation of the control system.
3.1
Active Components
The active components required for the converter are:
• MOSFET’s with the appropriate voltage/current ratings
• Gate drivers to drive the MOSFET’s
• Sensing components to deliver feedback to the control system
All active components (disregarding the transistors) share the need for one particular
requirement, which is the need for isolation between the input and output sides of the
component. Due to the high voltage/current levels in this application, the digital control
circuits could be severely damaged in the case of a high voltage/current transient in the
system. In the following subsections the decision making process for selecting the various
active components is presented.

3.1.3
Voltage Sensor
The proposed control system requires both the input and output voltage as feedback.
The input voltage is needed for the Mode Selection and the output voltage is feedback for
one of the PI-compensators. To avoid complete destruction of the Analogue to Digital
Converter-module on the DSP, voltage dividers are utilized to step down the voltage for
sensing purposes. The voltage divider circuit is shown in Figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: Voltage Divider Circuit
R1 has been chosen to 1 MΩto limit the current flow. R2 will be selected to obtain a
maximum Vsense = 2 V. On the input side of the converter, this will result in fairly poor
sensing resolution due to the wide input voltage range. However, limited resolution is
not a critical issue, as the input voltage is only needed for mode selection. The precision
of the voltage sensing is greater on the output side of the converter, where it is also far
more important because the output voltage is used as feedback for the ACM control
system. To provide isolation between the power circuit and the DSP, the Avago ACPL-
C87A is selected. It is an optically isolated voltage sensor that takes an input between
0 −2 V and outputs a differential voltage signal. In Figure 3.4 the functional block
diagram of the voltage is presented.
Vin in the figure comes from the voltage divider node and the two outputs Vout+ and
Vout−are passed to the ADC channels on the DSP. The input and output side of the
voltage sensor are supplied by two 5 V voltage regulators to maintain complete isolation.

3.6.2
Practical Measuring
Waveforms
Measuring VDS and VGS of the MOSFET’s has been done with two different techniques
depending on whether the MOSFET in question was a high- or low-side transistor. On
the low-side MOSFET’s (Q2 and Q3) VDS and VGS can be measured directly as source is
shorted to ground. The source of the high-side MOSFET’s (Q1 and Q4) are shorted to
the switch nodes (SWN1 and SWN2 respectively) of the converter so VDS is measured
as Drain-to-Ground subtracted with Source-to-Ground. Similarly VGS is measured as
Gate-to-Ground subtracted with Source-to-Ground. This measuring technique means
that the measurements on Q1 and Q4 will have a higher noise level compared to those of
Q2 and Q3 due to the doubled combined ground loop. As the Gate-Source voltage is only
12 V, the noise is more significant in those measurements. In the results in Chapter 4,
the Gate-Source voltages has been filtered in MatLab using a moving average filter with
a window size of 4 to remove some of the noise from the ground loops. The unfiltered
measurements are given in Appendix B. The inductor voltage has been measured using
the same differential technique to avoid shorting one of the switch nodes to ground
through the oscilloscope. Externally mounting the inductor made it possible to measure
the current with a Hall Effect current probe connected to the oscilloscope. The output
voltage ripple has been measured with the oscilloscope channel AC-coupled to remove
the large DC component.
Transients
Similarly, when measuring the output voltage under transient input step conditions, AC-
coupling of the oscilloscope channel was used. The data sets have been plotted both
filtered and unfiltered. The filtering is performed using a moving average digital filter

5.1
Discussion
Results
The Drain-Source and Gate-Source voltages of the MOSFET’s show a converter switch-
ing properly, and they are comparable with the ideal waveforms displayed in Chapter 2.
Stray inductance and of course the effects of the power inductor are visible in the form of
slopes on the Drain-Source voltages. The most noticeable waveform in the results is un-
doubtedly the inductor current, from which most of the issues can be deduced. Figures
4.3b, 4.9b and 4.13b showed that the inductor core saturated, when conducting large
currents. This corresponds with the core saturation current calculated to isat = 0.71 A in
Equation 3.20. Particularly Boost and Buck-boost mode operation yields high converter
currents, partly due to low input voltage, but Buck-boost mode has inherently higher
currents from the topology itself (ideal waveforms shown in Section 2.1.2). While the
MOSFET’s are rated for 8 A of continuous current, the high current peaks generated
by the saturated inductor core will have an effect on the life time expectancy of the
transistors. The thermal images further showed that the transistors conducting in the
positive half-period, when the current peaks were largest, were subjected to a lot of
heating. The mass and surface area of the MOSFET’s are significantly smaller than
the inductor, hence the relatively low temperature of the inductor core that was ob-
served. The output voltage ripple was also affected by the load. The maximum ripple
occurred in Buck-boost mode with ∆Vout = 2.5 V with switching noise peaks of upwards
of 10 V. As the output voltage of this converter is meant to be a DC-bus in the fully
developed Power Management System of the Buoy, the output ripple is not a critical
issue. Furthermore it can be mitigated by additional electrolytic capacitors to limit the
low-frequency ripple and additional ceramic capacitors to limit the high-frequency ripple.
The transient results revealed that the converter is capable of attenuating input volt-
age steps of 50 V in 100 ms showed in Figures 4.15 and 4.16. Ocean waves occur on a
much larger time scale, in a matter of seconds and not milliseconds, so for the applica-
tion the speed of the control system is adequate. The resulting voltage transients was
a 60% change in the output voltage, which is fairly large. The spikes can be limited by
increasing the speed of the control system and/or increasing capacitance on the input to
reduce the speed of the steps and/or on the output to reduce the spikes on the output.
The start-up tests showed that the converter control system is capable of stabilizing the

Despite the large saturation currents from the inductor core, the efficiency of the
converter remains above 80% for the largest part of the operation range. Buck-boost
mode is, as expected, the least efficient operation mode. Partly due to the doubled
switching losses and partly due to high currents. The calculated efficiency showed a
slight drop in efficiency from 88% to 87% in Buck-boost mode, but the measurements
showed a different story. Current peaks of upwards of 15 A through the transistors will
result in large amounts of heat. This is also a likely cause for the 68% minimum effi-
ciency at Vin = 114 V. The efficiency test was carried out in one sitting, so the system
had accumulated a large amount of heat by the time, the operation mode changed to
Buck-boost, and the efficiency of the converter dropped fast, when entering Buck-boost
mode. Besides Buck-boost mode the measured efficiency corresponds well with the cal-
culated efficiency. When the converter initially changes from Buck-boost to Buck mode
operation, the measured efficiency is slightly higher than the calculated efficiency. This
follows from the input current, which is slightly larger in the modelled than in the mea-
surements. The additional losses in the measurements comes from parasitic components
that was not accounted for in the calculations.
The thermal images of the converter provided an overview of the stress distribution
across the components. The MOSFET Q1 was subjected to large amounts of stress
across the operation range of the converter, while Q4 was subjected to significantly less
stress. Furthermore, the thermal tests showed that the power components placed on the

converter, having a very unequal distribution of stress will cause some components to
fail quicker than others. In an offshore applications such as this, this is not advisable as
maintenance costs are very large.
Control System
The control system of the converter has proven to perform well regarding the basic func-
tionality. It can maintain a constant output voltage with a very wide input voltage range
with testing performed from Vin = 25 V to Vin = 570 V. However, a small offset error in
the output voltage was noted and was determined to be caused by insufficient tuning of
the controller gains. Furthermore, instability with Vin =
{
80 ± 2 V
120 ± 2 V was noted. This
was due to a lack of margin in the Mode selection voltage feedback. If the input voltage
would fluctuate around either of the Mode change voltages, the operation would change.
The efficiency measurements showed that Buck-boost mode resulted in a large drop in
efficiency, due to higher currents that caused more heat and more stress on the com-
ponents. Limiting the time the converter has to operate in Buck-boost mode can lead
to a higher efficiency and longer lifetime-expectancy. However, there are some practical
limitations that makes removing Buck-boost mode entirely very difficult; the duty cycle
in Boost mode would have to be extremely small and approaching 0, and the duty cycle
in Buck mode would have to be very large and approaching 1, so having Buck-boost
mode as a buffer between Boost and Buck mode simplifies the control a great deal. Fur-
thermore, feedback inaccuracy is a natural limit to the size of the Buck-boost mode
operation interval.
The start-up tests revealed that start-up with an output power larger than 15 W will
saturate the control system. This is because the controller will output maximum duty
cycle for 0 V feedback from the output voltage. If the load is large, the initial inrush
current through the converter is very large and the current feedback compensator will
attempt to limit the current, while the output voltage feedback is still too small, so the
controller saturates quickly.
PCB Layout
Regarding the layout of the PCB, it was noted that the MOSFET Q1 in particular was
subject to large amounts of heat throughout the operation modes. This combined with
insufficient heat sinks meant that the were some reliability issues and Q1 was replaced
many times. Furthermore, Q2 and Q3 were prone to overheating during Buck-boost
mode operation due to their position on the back of the PCB. Q4 was mostly unaffected
by heat due to the relatively low current through it in Boost and Buck-boost mode. It
is difficult to estimate if the heat sinks had been sufficient had the inductor core not
saturated, but it is unlikely.

The power to the digital circuits was supplied with a single 24 V internal plane in the
PCB to emulate the real world conditions of the converter. This supply voltage meant
that a large number of voltage regulators were necessary, where each regulator resulted
in additional losses. Furthermore, the regulators took up space on an already compact
PCB. Limiting the number of regulators will thus increase the overall efficiency of the
system as well as leaving more space for other components.
Measuring points on the PCB was found to be somewhat sufficient, however, easier
access to Drain-Source and Gate-Source voltages and more ground access points would
have simplified the testing and data collection. Lastly, differential oscilloscope probes
would have improved the noise level in data for particularly the high-side MOSFET data
sets and the inductor voltage.
5.2
Future Work
In order to make this design fully functional to the application, some changes and opti-
mization are necessary. Mostly it is question of tuning and adjusting the design as the
basic functionality is present in the current design.
Control System
Starting with the control system, it requires additional tuning of the controller gains to
avoid offset errors and possibly improve the speed of the controller although this is not
critical. Furthermore, additional filtering of the controller feedback, in particular the
current feedback, would increase the stability of control system. To avoid the discussed
instabilities with the input voltage close to or equal to one of the Mode Change voltages,
there will have to be implemented some features ensuring full stability of the operation
modes regardless of the input voltage. Here follows some proposals:
• Margins on the Mode change feedback to avoid repeated change in operation mode
if Vin =
{
80 V
120 V
• A Mode Change Counter-variable that limits the speed of Mode change by forcing
the converter to stay in one mode for a specified amount of time
• A hardware (or efficient software) filter on the input voltage feedback to have more
stable feedback
The start-up procedure of the control system needs additional work as well. Start-up
with heavy loads could be achieved by implementing a soft start feature in the code, that
increases the duty cycle with a slope instead of a step. This will limit the initial inrush
current through the converter and prevent controller saturation.
==PAGE:67==
5.2 Future Work
59
The output voltage was selected to 100 V in order to limit the duty cycle in Boost
mode operation. Furthermore, no information regarding the input voltage in normal
operation in the real world application was provided, so it was not possible to optimize
the output voltage. Data sets containing the output voltage of the generator over a time
interval of some days/weeks will provide the opportunity to select an output voltage
that will ensure that Boost and Buck mode are used equally and thus distribute the
stress more equally over the components. Alternatively, the converter can be optimized
to operate either in Boost or Buck in normal operation and only go into the other mode
under abnormal condition.
Components
Regarding the components, they are adequate for the application for the most part, ex-
cept for the inductor which proved to be unable to conduct the high currents without
reaching core saturation. A physically larger inductor with a larger magnetic path will
result in a smaller flux density in the core and increase the saturation current limit. A
gapped core could be used to decrease the permeability of the core, but the large induc-
tance needed would mean that many turns would be required, so a larger core would be
the better solution. Similar approach of increasing the physical size can be applied to
the heat sinks on the MOSFET’s. The ones used in the current design were much too
small and limited the power output of the converter a great deal.
The input and output capacitance of the converter was adequate for testing purposes.
However, replacing the DC input from a lab supply with an AC input from a genera-
tor, the rectification on the input will increase the required capacitance on the input to
maintain a stable input voltage. The relatively low frequency of the AC input generated
by the ocean waves requires more capacitance than higher frequencies. Similarly on the
output, if less ripple and switching noise is desired, it can be achieved by adding more
capacitance, both electrolytic and ceramic.
The experiment that was carried out by replacing Q1 with a wire in Boost mode
indicated that some efficiency can be gained be optimizing how the current is conducted
through Q1. Further tests could be carried out to investigate the possibility of adding a
low-voltage low-RDS,on MOSFET in parallel with Q1 to conduct when the input voltage
is low and the converter is operating in Boost mode. This would add an additional
component to the system, but it might increase the overall efficiency and life-time of the
converter.
The DSP used in the tests have the advantage of having a large number of I/O ports,
so it is possible to use the same chip with more elements included in the power electronic
system such as the DC/DC transformer circuit etc.

PCB Layout
A new PCB layout is required under any circumstances in order to continue the project,
and the main design goal of the new layout should be additional space to mount large
heat sinks on the MOSFET’s if further on-shore testing of the converter is the objective.
If the layout is meant to be mounted inside the buoy, the layout should be designed to
utilize the abundance of cool metal surfaces available in the buoy. As the temperature of
seawater rarely surpasses 25 ◦C anywhere in the world, cooling of the power components
can be very efficient, if they are thermally connected to the cylinder wall. Furthermore,
if it is required to have all components mounted to the surface of the PCB, a larger board
than 10x10cm is required as the inductor will take up a large amount of space. Additional
filtering capacitance on the input and output will take up a significant amount of space
as well. In particular the input capacitance requires space as the voltage rating and thus
the physical size is large. Lastly, the addition of a DC/DC transformer to increase the
voltage to 1 kV will require space. A 10x20 or 10x30cm PCB will be adequate.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
A wide input voltage DC/DC converter has been designed, build and tested for this
Thesis project. A Synchronous Buck-boost topology was used. The converter was put
through a number of tests to assess the functionality of it as well as the converter
efficiency. With efficiency above 80% for the majority of the operation range, the power
losses in the converter was adequately low. This was despite the fact that the inductor
used in the testing was much too small for the application. Tests of the converter under
large loads showed a saturated inductor core. This caused very high peak currents to
run through the MOSFET’s causing the temperature of them to increase. Heating was
further worsened by the heat sinks mounted to the MOSFET’s, that proved to be too
small for the application.
The output voltage ripple was found to be below 3% regardless of the operation mode.
This will provide an adequately stable DC-bus from which the voltage can be increased
to 1 kV and transmitted to the seabed battery. Furthermore, it will provide a stable
voltage from where the ancillary battery can be charged through a voltage regulator.
The control system was implemented on a DSP using Average Current Mode control.
Transient tests revealed a control system able to stabilize the voltage as required from
the specifications. However, some limitations to the capabilities of the control system
were discovered; Instability with the input voltage Vin =
{
80 ± 2 V
120 ± 2 V, and only No- or
Low-load start-up was possible due to a lack of soft start feature.
The PCB layout was found to be inadequate in terms of space for mounting heat sinks to
the MOSFET’s. Furthermore, the inductor had to be mounted externally due to limited
space, so if it is an objective to mount all components to the PCB, a bigger layout is
necessary. With the changes and additions described in Section 5.2 the converter will
be able to fit the application. The necessary changes are listed below:
• Additional work on the Mode Change part of the control system
• Soft start feature
• Larger inductor
• More capacitance on input and output
• PCB layout optimized for larger heat sinks
– or for mounting inside the buoy
