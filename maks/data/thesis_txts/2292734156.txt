Abstract
In this thesis is the concept of phosphor recovery from biobased ﬂy ash by precipita-
tion of Struvite-K investigated. First of all is the solubility product of Struvite-K
in a saturated aqueous solution determined to be 4.78 × 10−10 at 25.2 ◦C. Fur-
thermore is the solubility product tendencies of Hazenit also determined as the
approximated exponential function Ksp(pH, [10 ◦C..40 ◦C]) = 3 × 1010e−7.108pH re-
garding the pH dependency for temperature under 40 ◦C.
Secondly is the solubility ratio of biobased ﬂy ash, to demineralized water, deter-
mined to be 1:5.5. or 0.18 g mL−1 at room temperature. From the biobased ﬂy ash
was about 25% of the phosphorus release, as it was possible to recover 0.62wt%
phosphor from the biobased ﬂy ash. In addition, to phosphor recovery were it also
possible to recover 39wt% potassium and a negligible amount of cadmium.
Precipitation of Struvite-K was obtained, from the soluble biobased ﬂy ash part,
with both MgO and MgCl2 · 6 H2O as the magnesium source. The best phosphor
recovery rate were obtained with MgO, as it resulted in an almost linear correlation
between the Mg/P ratio and the phosphor recovery rate. Meaning that an almost
complete phosphor recovery is possible if the magnesium to phosphor ratio is
1:1.
Furthermore, there was a good correlation between the experimental and
theoretical phosphor recovery rate. Phosphor recovery based on MgCl2 · 6 H2O
did however not show as good results. As the phosphor recovery rate were less
than 50% and impurities of K2SO4 were detected in the Struvite-K. The phosphor
recovery rate can be improved by raising pH, but it may result in precipitation of
Hazenite rather than Struvite-K.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The cadmium contamination of phosphorus-based fertilizers is an increasing prob-
lem since cadmium from the fertilizers contaminated the soil and the plants grow-
ing in the soil.
Which led to a contamination of the crops and livestock, and
eventually resulted in a buildup of cadmium in the internal organs of the human
body. Combined with an upcoming shortage of phosphorus rock in the near future.
Which would lead to a fertilizer shortage and thereby a food shortage if the issue
is not being faced [10, 11].
In an attempt to become independent of fossil fuels, some Danish power plant
has started to burn biomass, especially straw and wood chips instead of coal and
oil. Which is good for the overall carbon footprint, as straw and wood chips are
considered a renewable and carbon-neutral energy source. However, this practice
slowly depletes the phosphorus from the soil since the ash from the combustion
is highly contaminated with heavy metals and, therefore, is not allowed to be
redistributed back to the soil [5, 6].
In order to face both the upcoming shortage of phosphorus rock and the cadmium
contamination of phosphorus-based fertilizers, in addition to halting the depletes
the phosphorus from soil used for biomass production. This thesis would deal with
the problem of recovering phosphorus from the biomass ﬂy ash without the heavy
metals. So that the phosphorus can be returned to the ground as fertilizers and
thereby keep the food and biomass production on the same level as now. Without
contaminating the soil and reduce the dependency of rock phosphorus.

1.1. THESIS OBJECTIVE
1.1
Thesis Objective
The objective of this thesis is to acquiring experimental data of recovering of phos-
phorus from combusted biomass ash from Danish power plants (either from straw
or wood chips) by crystallization of Struvite-K and investigate the recovering rate
and purity of the crystals. Also, comparing the experimental data with existing
crystallization models.
The project will address the following subjects:
• A literature study of Struvite production from combusted biomass ﬂy ash
from Danish power plants, and the problem of cadmium contamination of
phosphorus-based fertilizers.
• Investigation of operation pH, temperatures, and concentration for forming
Struvite-K crystals from combusted biomass ash.
• Experiments considering the crystallization of Struvite-K compared to phos-
phorus contents and cadmium contamination.
• Develop crystallization model of Struvite-K and comparing the model with
the existing at KT.
1.2
Learning Objective
This thesis will address the general learning objectives as follows:
• Review of literature data and considerations for the experimental work.
• Executing experimental work with varying parameters of crystallization of
Struvite-K.
• Analyzing the experimental data and comparing it with existing models.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.3
Thesis Overview
The section is designed to give a chronological overview of the chapters in the
thesis with a small description of each chapter.
Chapter 2:
Background Theory
The ﬁrst pat of this chapter contain an
introduction to the expected shortage of phosphate and the concept and prediction
of peak phosphorus. The second part consist of an introduction to Struvite history,
properties, and commercial production. In addition to Struvite is Struvite-K, a
member of the Struvite crystal group, also presented in this chapter.
Chapter 3: Theory
This chapter describes the theory behind the concept of
solubility and crystallization which is used in this thesis. Furthermore does the
chapter also include an introduction to the applied sample analyzes conducted in
the thesis. In addition, to short descriptions of the mathematical model, program
used to predict the outcome of the experimental work.
Chapter 4: Problem Deﬁnition/Solution
The fourth chapter describes the
two main issue of the thesis. First of all the determination of the solubility product
of Struvite-K and secondly the possibility to produce Struvite-K from biobased ﬂy
ash. The chapter also sets up experimental objectives to obtain experimental data
required to solve the problems and the underlying thoughts behind the decisions.
Chapter 5: Methodology
The ﬁfth chapter describes the methodology behind
the experimental work of this thesis.
Chapter 6: Results
In the sixth chapter, the results are presented. Based on
the experimental work is the result of the problems described in Chapter 4. Re-
garding the solubility product determination of Struvite-K and the possibility of
Struvite-K production based biobased ﬂy ash. In addition, to a comparison be-
tween the experimental data and the data deduced from the mathematical model.
Chapter 7: Discussion
In this chapter is results and assumptions presented
and discussed to verify the validity of the results.

2.1
Phosphorus
Phosphorus is one of the most impotent chemical element on earth. Since phos-
phorus is a fundamental component of DNA, RNA, and ATP and is, therefore, an
essential building block for all life and food production. Also, to being a funda-
mental element for all living organisms is phosphorus a limited resource without
any substitute.
Even though phosphorus can be found throughout the earth’s
crust and is the 11th most abundant element. Is there only a few geographical
location where the phosphorus deposits are practically useful. Which can lead to
oligopolies on the phosphorus market [12].
2.1.1
Phosphorus cycle
Just like the carbon and nitrogen circle, does the phosphorus cycle play a huge
role in the ecosystem. The phosphorus cycle distinct itself from the two other
cycles since only a fraction of phosphorus is absorbed by the atmosphere. In fact,
most models neglected the phosphor absorbed by the atmosphere. It also diﬀers
from the carbon and nitrogen circle, as it with the introduction of modern sewage
systems and phosphate rock based fertilizers; no longer is a closed circle but an
open system [8].
The open phosphorus cycle now starts with mining of phosphate rock deposits.
The phosphor is then used in the agricultural sector or for non-agricultural ap-
plication and industrial application. The majority of the phosphor is then tied

to agricultural products or consumer goods which are consumed by humans. A
small portion of the phosphor used in the agricultural sector is not absorbed by
the plants and run oﬀthe ﬁelds and end up in natural water bodies. The phos-

phosphor is released to natural water bodies, or is deposited directly or indirectly

To sum up the phosphorus cycle. The phosphor is recovered from phosphorus rock,

natural cradle to cradle principle is no longer in use in the phosphorus circle but
have been replaced with the cradle to grave principle due to human interference

2.1.2
Peak Phosphorus
The consumption of phosphorus rocks has increased with such a rate within the
last century. That the society has, in fact, become addicted to phosphorus rocks,
if the current global food production should be maintained [14]. The demand of
phosphate is already at an all-time high level with no descent in sight. With an
estimated population growth rate of 0.44-1.12 % within the next 35 year would
result in an increase of the global food production by 70 % by 2050, in order to
fulﬁll the expected global demand [15, 14]. Combined with a worldwide growing
middle class, with a diet consisting of highly phosphorus demanding products such
as meat and dairy products. In addition to the growing food demand, have the
chemical industry emerging used of biomass, especially for biofuels increased the
demand for phosphorus rocks. The ﬁrst time an increased demand of phosphorus
rocks was attributed to the production of biofuels was in 2007, which might explain
the 800 % price increase for phosphate rock from ﬁrst quarter in 2007 to the third
quarter in 2008 [14, 16].

2.1.3
Cadmium contamination problems
Contamination of phosphorus rock with heavy metals, especially cadmium is a
rising problem for the fertilizer industry. The degree of contamination by cad-
mium in phosphorus rock varies considerably dependent on the location of the
phosphorus mine. From highly contamination phosphorus mines in Morocco with
a cadmium contamination of up to about 500 mg kg−1, to mines with almost no
cadmium contamination in Finland and Russia, where the contamination is under
1 mg kg−1 [23, 11]. During the last century, the average cadmium contamination
has increased as the least contaminated phosphorus mines have been exhausted.
This have resulted in an average European cadmium contamination of 60 mg kg−1
P2O5 which is assumed to rise in the future [11].
By using fertilizers with a cadmium contamination of 60 mg kg−1 P2O5 or more,
will the cadmium accumulation in the ground increase from 40 to 125 % over a
100 year period [24]. To avoid ground contamination with cadmium, must the
cadmium fertilizer contamination be reduced to 20 mg kg−1 P2O5, since at this
level cadmium would only slowly accumulate or even decrease [24].
The level of cadmium contamination of the soil does not seem the eﬀect the growth
or yield of the crops, but the cadmium contamination level appears to aﬀect the
cadmium uptake in the plants. It is, however, diﬃcult to say exactly how big
the eﬀect of cadmium contamination soil is since it depend on the speciﬁc crop
species, soil composition, and pH. Generally is the cadmium uptake higher in loam
soil than in sandy soil, and the uptake increase with increasing soil pH [25]. The

2.2
Alternative phosphorus sources
Mankind had been using phosphorus-rich fertilizers for millenniums, before the use
of rock phosphorus started in the late 19th century. Even before Justus von Liebig
conﬁrmed that the inorganic salts of phosphorus and nitrogen, were the essential
to humus on plant growth and not organic matter in 1840 and the discovery of
phosphorus by Henning Brandt in 1676 [22, 30, 31].
By the introduction of agriculture, occurred the need for fertile farmlands. One
of the earliest methods to improved the solid with phosphorus was the concept
of ﬁre-stick farming. Where a small area of wilderness was burned down to rely
on the phosphorus that was bound in the vegetation. The enriched phosphorus
area was then farmed until the phosphorus added to the soil, from the ash, was
exhausted. After that, the area was abandoned, and a new spot of wilderness was
burned down, and the cycle continued [22].
As the human population rises and wilderness suitable for ﬁre-stick farming di-
minished increases the need for enhancing the soil with phosphorus-rich fertilizers.
Such as phosphorus-rich matter like manure, crop and food residues, and human

excreta or ”night soil” [22]. With the Industrial Revolution and migration from
land to towns, it is not only the people who are migrated but also their phosphorus-
rich night soil. Meaning that the local phosphorus cycle is broken, as the phosphor
obtain by the crops no longer return to the same ﬁeld as earlier. Since the food
is transported from the land to the towns, but the night soil is not returned to
the original ﬁeld but ﬁelds closer to the towns. As the knowledge and awareness
of health and hygiene increased in the mid-1800s does the Sanitary Revolution
occur, with the introduction of the sewer system that fundamentally transformed
the previous land-based disposal of human wastes to a water-based disposal sys-
tem. Which, in fact, changed our civilization from a phosphorus recycling society
to a phosphorus throughout society. Since the night soil from most European and
North American cities was no longer returned to the land. This lack of recycling
resulting in a decrease of phosphor in the soil and thereby a reduction in food
production. To tackle the lowering phosphor contents in the soil, artiﬁcial fertil-
izer was required. The ﬁrst attempt to solve the lowering phosphor contents in
the soil was the use crushed bones as fertilizers since it contains a high amount of
calcium phosphate [32]. The supply of crushed bones is however limited, so it was
quickly replaced with the discovery of guano deposit on uninhabited islands in the
Atlantic and Paciﬁc. Guano is, in fact, bird and bat droppings that have accu-
mulated over centuries. Although the island provided signiﬁcant guano reserves,
was not it enough to replace the phosphor removed from the phosphorus cycle by
the sewage of the cities in the industrial world. It was ﬁrst with the introduction
of rock phosphorus that a continuous supply of phosphor that was capable to re-
placed the phosphor that ends up in the sewer were introduced. It was however
not before the mid-1900s that mineral phosphorus application as a fertilizer sky-
rocketed since phosphate rock was assumed a plentiful source of phosphorus and
it was cheap and, therefore, replaced organic sources [22].
2.2.1
Sewage
That sewage contains phosphor is not a surprise because it has been known since
1676, where Henning Brandt extracted phosphorus from urine [31]. It is however
ﬁrst recently that phosphor has been recovered from the sewage on an industrial
scale. Of course, sewage has been used to fertilize ﬁelds but with the introduction
of sewers in the mid-1800s, was the untreated sewage redirect into rivers, creeks,

streams, lakes, and harbors, rather than to local ﬁelds due to hygiene requirements.
This practice resulted in an increasing number of problems as the sewer system
grew [22, 33]. The need for dealing with rising sewage problem was recognized
as early as 1858. The early solution to the problem was however not to treat the
sewage, but to extend the sewer system and thereby move the problem out of sight.
Early experimental tests with sewage farming were performed but failed due to
poor management, location, and wet weather that lead to the abandoning of the
idea.
It was ﬁrst at the beginning of the twentieth century that the ﬁrst commercial
sewage treatment plant was constructed. The technology was rather primitive us-

was drastically improved with the introduction of activated sludge plant and sed-
imentation tanks, with subsequent separate sludge treatment in the course of the
twentieth century. It was however ﬁrst in the late eighties that the Danish Par-
liament passed the Act that included the removing of phosphorus and nitrogen,
in addition to the ongoing practice of removing organic matter from the sewage
[34, 33].
The improved puriﬁcation technique did, however, create new problems as the
sewage treatment plant produced a large amount of phosphorus-rich sludge. In
addition to the phosphor, this the sludge contains a vast amount of organic matter
and a mixture of heavy metals. The obvious solution would, of course, be to use
the sludge as fertilizers, but due to regulations, that require that the sludge may
not be distributed to recreational areas, private gardens and only to ﬁelds under
cultivation of not edible crops. In fact, no edible crops are allowed to be cultivated
in an area where sewage sludge have been applied for one year after delivery.
Furthermore, the sludge should be plowed into the ground within 6 hours after
the distribution. In addition to the complicated distribution rules, is there also
an upper limit on the heavy metals contains in the sludge, if the sludge is allowed
to be distributed on farmland, the limit can be found in Table 2.1. Which, all in
all, lead to the situation that the farmers are pay to receive the sludge from the

Solubility
Solubility describes the chemical elements or compound ability to dissolve into a
solvent. The solvent may be a gas, liquid or solid and the solubility for a solvent
is primarily depended on pressure and temperature. For gas based solvent is the
eﬀect of the solubility signiﬁcantly for even smaller pressure changes. Which is not
the case for solid and liquid solvents, were only minor change in their solubility is
caused by pressure changes[72].
In this thesis, will the focus be on liquid solvent or more speciﬁc water as the
solvent, which is also known as an aqueous solution. The temperature eﬀect of
the solubility, of an aqueous solution, is typical to increased in the solubility when
the temperature rises. This is not always the case, as some salts solubility de-
creases when the temperature rises. The prediction of solubility dependence of the
temperature is extremely severe. Even through some correlation to determine the
solubility dependence of temperature exist is the only guaranteed way to deter-
mining the dependence by experimental measurements [72].
In addition to the temperature, does the pH also aﬀect the solubility of an aqueous
solution. In fact does many compounds solubility strongly depends on the pH of
the aqueous solution. As the anion in many sparingly soluble salts is the conju-
gate base of a weak acid. By lowering the pH, may the protonation of the anion
dramatically increase the solubility of the salt. This is however not the case for
salts whose anion is the conjugate base of a stronger weak acid or a strong acid
since it have little to no eﬀect on the solubility of the salt. [72, 73]

space. The crystal is exposed to diﬀraction gradient for X-ray light with wave-
lengths on the order of 1 ˚A, and from the angles and intensities of the beams, a
three-dimensional picture of the density of electrons can be obtained. The main
positions of the atoms and chemical bonds can be derived from the electron density
and thereby determine the composition of the crystal. By comparing the image
with a database of benchmark images of pure compounds, is it possible to deter-
mine the composition of the examine crystal. [82]. An example of a benchmark
can be found in Table 6.6 which contain the benchmark of pure Hazenite.
X-ray crystallography is applying the phenomena of diﬀraction that occurs light
encounters an obstacle. The light wave will either travel through the slits or bend
around the obstacle depending on the composition of the obstacle. The encounter
with an obstacle will provide a diﬀraction pattern of constructive interference
and destructive interference where two waves either interact in phase or out of
phase[82]. The phase diﬀerence can be calculated with Equation 3.3.1.
x(cos(θo) −cos(θ)) = nλ
(3.3.1)
Where x is the distance between the points where the diﬀraction repeats, θo is the
impact angle, θ is angle of reﬂection, n is any integer and λ is the wavelength.
As the name implies does X-ray crystallography not use visible light but x-ray
beams. The diﬀraction of an X-ray beam occurs as an interaction between the

various possibilities, with three variable parameters. Where the ﬁrst parameter

the ratio between the water and ﬂy ash. At room temperature is the ratio of ﬂy

Chapter 7
Discussion
The results and the assumptions leading to the results are in this chapter presented
and discussed. The discussion is conducted for the determination of the solubility
product of Struvite-K, the possibility of Struvite-K production based on biobased
ﬂy ash, and the comparison between the experimental data and the mathematical
model. In order to clarify and validity of the results.
7.1
Solubility product of Struvite-K
The results for the determination of the solubility product of Struvite-K were
obtained by experiments with pure chemicals, in order to eliminate external inﬂu-
ences. This was achieved by synthetic precipitation of Struvite-K, by the use of
KH2PO4 as the potassium and phosphor source and either MgO or MgCl2·6 H2O as
the magnesium source. Both methods are described in the literature, as a possible
way to precipitate Struvite-K. The molarity ratio between potassium, magnesium
and phosphor were for all experiments conducted with the ratio of 1:1:1. The tem-
perature were kept constant by a heat bath and was assumed to be constant even
though minor ﬂuctuation did occur. The experiment were ended when the satu-
rated solutions reached equilibrium, or it was ended when the saturated solutions
were assumed to be in equilibrium. The reached equilibrium where determined by
pH since a constant pH indicated equilibrium. When the equilibrium stat were
reached, the precipitates were allowed to settle, and samples from the liquid phase
were collected for the ICP-OES analysis after the sedimentation had occurred.
The samples for ICP-OES analysis were collected by a pipette and diluted with
87
==PAGE:102==
7.1. SOLUBILITY PRODUCT OF STRUVITE-K
demineralized water at a known ratio. The precipitate was then recovered by vac-
uum ﬁltration.
Samples of the precipitate were redissolved in demineralized water and analysed
with an ICP-OES analysis, to determine the composition of the precipitated crys-
tals. The chosen concentration for the redissolving was however not high enough
to be able to identify the crystal composition, due to the ion contamination of
the demineralized water. Additional attempts of the determine the crystal compo-
sition by increasing the concentration were not conducted, due to time constraints.
Some of the precipitate samples were also analysed with an X-ray crystallography,
which most surprisingly showed that the MgCl2 · 6 H2O based precipitate not was
Struvite-K as expected but instead Hazenite KNaMg2(PO4)2 · 14 H2O, which is a
member of the Struvite group. The precipitation of Hazenite may be explained
by the addition of NaOH in order to increase pH to a level Struvite-K precipita-
tion is described in the literature. The MgO based precipitate were not examined,
as it was not possible to recover enough uncontaminated precipitate, due to the
use of silicone oil and the low concentration of MgO based precipitate. The low
concentration were a result of the desired of avoiding undissolved MgO in the pre-
cipitate. Which were achieved by adjusted the concentration of MgO to just under
the solubility limit of MgO, while maintaining the molarity ratio between potas-
sium, magnesium and phosphor. Even through the MgO based precipitate were
not completely determine to be Struvite-K, is it still assumed to be Struvite-K
based on other MgO based experiment and the literature.

The Struvite-K production based biobased ﬂy ash experiment were divided into

7.2.1
Solubility and composition of biobased ﬂy ash
To determine the solubility ratio of biobased ﬂy ash to water was diﬀerent amounts
of biobased ﬂy ash dissolved in water at room temperature and without adjusting
pH. After a given time, it was assumed, that equilibrium were achieved and the
liquid, and solid phases were separated by vacuum ﬁltration. The solid phase was
dried and weighed, and compared with the amount of biobased ﬂy ash dissolved.
Samples from the liquid phase were collected for the ICP-OES analysis with a

The optimal solubility ratio of biobased ﬂy ash to water was determined to be

Based on the value from the ICP-OES analysis is the potassium recovery as ex-

Possibility of Struvite-K production based biobased
ﬂy ash
The precipitation based on biobased ﬂy ash were achieved, either by adding either
MgO or MgCl2 · 6 H2O to the soluble biobased ﬂy ash phase. Which as expected
resulted in precipitation of Struvite-K. The MgO based Struvite-K precipitation
were a lot better than the MgCl2 · 6 H2O, as the MgCl2 · 6 H2O based precipitate
in addition to Struvite-K also included impurity of K2SO4.
The phosphor recovery based on MgO can almost be described as a linear corre-
lation between the Mg/P ratio and the phosphor recovery rate. Meaning that an
almost complete phosphor recovery is possible if the magnesium to phosphor ratio
is 1:1. This is however not the case for the MgCl2 ·6 H2O based phosphor recovery
rate.
Furthermore does the experimental recovery of phosphate only deviates less than
10 % from the ideal theoretical recovery for the MgO based Struvite-K. The diﬀer-
ence between the experimental and the theoretical recovery may be explained with
the loss of precipitated Struvite-K on the glassware or impurity in the precipitated
Struvite-K like K2SO4.

precipitation of Hazenite, as the precipitate based on MgCl2 · 6 H2O precipitated.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This project has had three main objectives. Determine the solubility product of
Struvite-K, determine the possibility of Struvite-K production based on biobased
ﬂy ash, and a comparison between the experimental data and the mathematical
model.
Struvite-K’s solubility product is determined to be 4.78 × 10−10 at 25.2 ◦C, based
on the experimental data obtained in this thesis. A temperature dependence for
Struvite-K’s solubility product has been observed, as the solubility product in-
creases when the temperature increases until 25 ◦C and that the solubility product
decreases when the temperature increases from 40 ◦C prospectively. The behavior
between 25 ◦C and 40 ◦C have not been possible to determine in this thesis, but a
change in solubility product in the interval is expected.
Quite surprisingly did the precipitation with KH2PO4 and MgCl2·6 H2O not result
in Struvite-K as expected but instead in Hazenite. Hazenite is a member of the
Struvite crystal group, and it is ﬁrst recently been described in the literature.
Furthermore is the solubility product of Hazenite currently not available, so it has
been determining in this thesis.
The solubility product of Hazenite is both depend on pH and temperature. Hazen-
ite’s solubility product behave with an exponential tendency, regarding pH at tem-
perature under 40 ◦C and may be described with the following approximation:
Ksp(pH, [10 ◦C..40 ◦C]) = 3 × 1010e−7.108pH. A temperature dependence for Hazen-
ite’s solubility product has been determined by pH between 9 and 10.5, as the

solubility product increases when the temperature increases until 30 ◦C. At that
point the solubility product decreases as the temperature increases from 30 ◦C
prospectively.
The optimal solubility ratio of biobased ﬂy ash to demineralized water were deter-
mined to be 1:5.5. or 0.18 g mL−1 at room temperature. From the biobased ﬂy ash
was about 25% of the phosphorus release, as it was possible to recover 0.62wt%
phosphor from the biobased ﬂy ash. In addition, to the phosphor recovery was it

8.1
Future Work
Based on the results in this thesis, proposals for future work is presented for the
further investigation and improvement of phosphor recovery, from biobased ﬂy ash
by precipitation of Struvite-K.
Further work is needed to determine Struvite-K’s solubility product dependency
of temperature. Especially in the temperature interval between 25 ◦C and 40 ◦C,
as a change in the solubility product is observed but not determined.
This thesis conﬁrms the proof of concept that it is possible to recover phosphor
from biobased ﬂy ash by precipitation of Struvite-K. Further work is needed to
optimise the process and perform an economic analysis to determine the viability
of the process.
Most urgently is to improve the recovery rate of the phosphor from the insoluble
part of the biobased ﬂy ash. Investigate the purity of the precipitated Struvite-K
when cadmium is present during the precipitation, and to determine the conversion
time of MgO to Struvite-K, when MgO is present in quantities greater than the
solubility of MgO.
