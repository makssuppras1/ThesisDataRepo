Abstract
Rubble-mound breakwaters are considered to be a conventional type of
breakwater which can absorb the attack of the waves. The main pur-
pose/scope of the present thesis, is concentrated on rubble-mound break-
waters, whose free-board magnitude has either null or negative value (i.e.
submerged breakwaters).
The goal of designing such structures, is on
the one hand to ensure coastal protection and beach stabilization and on
the other hand to minimize the cost of the construction due to the lower
amount of required stones. For such structures, a great amount of water
is allowed to over-top the structure, and thereby the breakwater is not
responsible for absorbing the total portion of the wave energy.
The present project consists of both a theoretical and an experimental
approach to investigate the stability of a submerged breakwater exposed
to irregular waves. The first part of the project, is the determination of
the design parameters based on surface elevation measurements taken at
water depth of h = 21.3 m at Samsø in Denmark. The design parameters
are estimated through an extreme wave height analysis at deep and shal-
low water conditions, including also the phenomenon of wave shoaling,
refraction and breaking occurring in shallow waters.
In contrast to conventional rubble-mound breakwater, submerged struc-
tures typically consists of one main armour layer (i.e.
homogeneous).
A preliminary analysis is presented, with the aim of determining an ap-
propriate armour stone size which can meet the requirements of stability
and low damage. Following the stability expressions related to submerged

breakwaters, a theoretical appropriate armour stone size is calculated and
afterwards an illustration of the theoretical design is given.
The last part of the present master thesis, is to examine and confirm
whether or not the stability expression found from the literature can be
used for practical real-life designs. In addition, the effect of the structure
height, slope and crest width in the stability and the level of damage of
the breakwater, are examined through various experiments.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Backround of rubble-mound breakwaters
Traditionally, emerged rubble-mound breakwaters are widely used around
the globe, to prevent coastal erosion, to ensure protection of the coastal
areas and to dissipate the wave energy.
However, significant changes
have been observed regarding the evolution of the breakwaters, departing
from conventional type of rubble-mound non-overtopped structures, to
low-crested breakwaters and more recently to submerged breakwaters.
Not to mention, the evolution and the changes observed in the different
types of breakwater, on the one hand are related to a more cost effective
design, and on the other hand to any requirements in terms of ecological
and environmentally friendly construction and design solutions.
The main difference between emerged and submerged breakwaters, is that
emerged structures have their crest above the higher (HSL), mean (MSL)
and lower (LSL) sea level, meanwhile the freeboard is considered to be
negative for submerged structures [9]. With the passage of time, the stud-
ies and the construction of submerged structures reaches high popularity

for the protection of vulnerable coastal-eroded areas.
The implementation of rubble mound breakwaters is considered to be
quite sufficient for reducing the wave action/loading and thereby to pro-
tect the coastlines from erosion. However, considering large required stone
sizes, a conventional rubble-mound breakwater can lead to ineffective-cost
designs. An alternative, which can results in a low-cost design compared
to conventional breakwaters, is the implementation and the design of a
submerged structure. For this cases, an amount of water is allowed to
overtop the structure and thereby less wave energy is concentrated on
the breakwater and less amount of rocks will be required to withstand
the wave attack (i.e. cost effective design). However, it is of major im-
portance to determine the amount of the wave transmission and thereby
examine whether or not the coast will be exposed to significant transmit-
ted waves (i.e. waves that over-topped the structure) that can result to
erosion. To determine the applicability and the stability of the submerged
structures and to keep a balance between cost-environmental effective de-
signs and acceptable amount of transmitted waves , several studies have
been made and are briefly analysed in the following section.
1.2
Previous studies
Several arguments have been raised recently, regarding the stability and
the efficiency of the implementation of submerged breakwaters. There-
fore, several experimental works are performed in order to verify the sta-
bility and the corresponding damage of submerged structures.
The experimental work from [1], is concentrated on experimental results
regarding the stability of the head of a breakwater (emerged and sub-
merged), and it is also given a stability comparison between the head and
the trunk part of the structure. The writers, separated the breakwater-
plan view into different segments ( back slope (FS), front slope (FS),
crest (C), total section (TS), back head (BH) and front head (FH)), in
order to determine which parts of the breakwaters are more exposed to a
larger damage. The following figure, can provide the reader with a better
illustration of the latter sections:
==PAGE:21==

Figure 1.1: Experimental model - Top view [1]
From the experiments, it was observed that the the back head sections of
the breakwater, have been the most stable parts, considering submerged
conditions. This was not the case for the back slope section, and this
outcome it might be related to the fact that when the free-board is neg-
ative, waves can over-top the structure and thereby activate higher wave
forces in the back side of the trunk section. In addition, both front slope
and head sections, are found to behave quite similar as their stability
decreased when the freeboard decreases. Indeed, for lower magnitude of
freeboard, the front section of a breakwater is responsible to withstand a
larger amount of the wave energy.
To ensure the stability of such structures, an appropriate armour stone
size should be selected through the design. To associate the important
freeboard - factor with the stability of a submerged breakwater, Van der
Meer et al. [5], focused on introducing a submerged stability expression.
Through their work, they have gathered experimental data set and re-
sults made for submerged structures. It has been found that the stability
of a submerged structure can also be larger compared to nonovertopped
structures as an amount of waves is allowed to overtop the structure (i.e.
less wave energy/forces are concentrated on the total structure). They
concluded, that the most important factors related to the stability of a
submerged structure, are the damage, the stability number and the free-
board. In that way, an experimental stability expression for submerged

structures has been given and is introduced is the corresponding chapter
in the present project. However, due to the limitation of the experimental

Wave transmission, is considered to be an important factor for designing

However, from further researches on this field, it has been found that also
the crest width might be a governing parameter for the wave transmission,
as also suggested in [10].
1.3
Main objectives of the present project
The main objective of the present thesis project, is to evaluate the per-
formance of submerged structures attacked by irregular waves, based on
a theoretical and an experimental approach.
Regarding the theoretical approach, the available expressions related to
the stability of submerged breakwaters are utilized and an appropriate
armour stone size is estimated. Typically, submerged structures are con-
sisted of a main armour layer, and the same rocks are used for all the
sections of the breakwater. The stone size, is approximated theoretically
based on different freeboards, lifetime of structure etc. The main goal of
the theoretical approach is the illustration of a stable design following the
literature requirements.
As the theoretical stability expressions derived from experimental tests
where bias and scatter points can occur, it is also chosen to evaluate
the performance of the submerged breakwaters experimentally. The first
experimental goal is to confirm that the stability expression used in the
theoretical part is valid and can be used for practical designs. In addition,
the effetcs of the freeboard, of the crest width and the slope of the struc-
ture are evaluated through various experiments. Through comparisons
between the theoretical and the experimental approach, suggestions are
given regarding the existing formula.

2.1
Introduction
In the present chapter, the main parameters that are utilized for the
design of the submerged breakwater are estimated. For the estimation of
such parameters, a time series of successive sea states is considered, each
represented by a significant wave height. By considering a time series of
successive sea states, a long term distribution series of significant wave
heights, spanning several years is produced.
Long term statistics, are
referred to the statistics of the extreme significant wave height in the
long term distribution. In the statistical analysis of the long term wave
height distribution, the significant wave height is considered to be the
random variable.
For such statistical analysis, it is required that the chosen data set should
meet the following conditions. The first condition is related to the inde-
pendence of the data points in the data set, which means that there is no
correlation among the extreme significant wave heights. Homogeneity is
==PAGE:24==

the second condition that should be taken into account, meaning the data
set includes measurements made for the same statistical population (e.g.
wind-generated waves). Sea state measurements are typically refereed as
local wind-generated waves and swell waves. Swell waves, are refereed
to waves that they moved beyond the area where they are generated and
they are no longer developed by the wind. The last requirement, known as
Stationary long term climatology, indicates that the statistical properties
of the data set should be time invariant (e.g. no trend in the mean), as
it would not be possible to predict future occurrences based on historical
data [11].
To determine the corresponding extreme wave height events, the peak-
over-threshold (POT) method is utilized as analyzed in the following sec-
tions. The extreme wave height distribution is found by selecting specific
candidate theoretical distributions. Afterwards, the next task is to fit the
theoretical distributions to the chosen data set by considering a fitting
method, presented in the corresponding section. To evaluate which the-
oretical distribution approximate bests the data set, the average relative
error is estimated. Finally, the last step of this procedure is to estimate
the design significant wave height based on the most appropriate theoret-
ical distribution.
Not to mention, two different procedures are analysed in the present
project, one considering an extreme wave height analysis in deep water
(i.e. Rayleigh distribution is valid) and one in shallow water (i.e. Rayleigh
distribution is not valid). A comparison between the results given by the
the two different procedures are also presented in the current chapter.

Extreme wave height analysis at deep water
The extreme wave height analysis, considering deep water conditions is
examined in the present section. An approximation of a linear behaviour
of the waves can be assumed for such conditions, and thereby a theoretical
distribution can be used to describe the wave characteristics. The base
for such theoretical distributions, is the Gaussian or normal distribution
which can lead to a Rayleigh distribution. It has been found that the
wave heights in deep water, obey the rules of the Rayleigh distribution,
and thereby the Rayleigh distribution can be used to characterize such
data set at deep water.
Considering the detailed design of the breakwaters (i.e estimation of de-
sign parameters, layers’ stability, wave transmission etc.), different char-

2.3.1
Peak-over-threshold (POT) method
The complete data set does not meet the requirements related to indepen-

about homogeneity, only wind generated waves are taken into account for
approaching a relationship between the significant wave height and the
peak wave period as shown in the latter section.
As for the independence requirement, it would be possible to proceed an
analysis including annual series data set (i.e. maximum annual values)
instead of the complete one.
However, considering a duration of only
10 years of measurements, this can lead to a data set with few extreme
events and thereby uncertain estimations. Thus, a partial series data set
is proffered to be used for such statistical analysis. To utilize a partial
series data set, the peak-over-threshold method is used by considering
an appropriate threshold value.
For the specific study, the threshold
value is chosen to be 1.5. Not to mention, it is assumed that an extreme
event is at least occur once per year. For selecting the threshold value,
it is also considered that the partial series data set should consists of
much more extreme events compared to the annual series data set. The
procedure applied for the POT method, follows the main idea behind the,
either "zero up crossing" or "zero down crossing" method, by utilizing

Typically, rubble-mound breakwaters are also consisted of under-layers,
namely a filter and a core material layer. The material used for the latter
layers are finer compared to the one used for the main armour layer. Con-

corner of the flume, a wave absorber is placed with the aim or reducing
the reflection of the waves. However, for the present experiments, the
wave absorbed has not been utilized.

4.4.1
Simulated time for each experiment
To begin with, a specific amount of time should be chosen for the duration
of each experiment. Considering regular waves (i.e. repetition of wave
characteristics), the generated forces on the stones are maintained the
same during the whole duration. Therefore, for larger regular waves and
thereby larger wave forces, the equilibrium damage orientation can be
reached in a shorter period of time [20].
However, the duration of a
storm should be considered as an important parameter for the stability
of a structure and thereby for structures’ lifetime.
For the case of random waves (i.e. irregular waves), the wave characteris-
tics are changing continuously, and thereby the wave attack/forces differs
from wave to wave. The damage of the structure, can be occurred through
the generation of larger waves resulted to higher frequency of large wave
forces. By re-analyzing experimental works for different number of waves,
Van der Meer suggested that after 500-1000 generated waves, the rate of
increase of the damage, is reduced. This outcome, is related to the fact
that during the period of 500-1000 waves, at least one group of the larger
waves is generated and thereby results to a great amount of damage. The
decrease in the increase rate of the damage, occurs due to the changes in
the slope-sides and the crest width until reaching the damage limit (i.e.
damage equilibrium). Therefore, it is chosen to simulate the experiments
for a duration of 40 min per each, to ensure that at least one group of
large waves is generated.

to the modified stability number, smaller values of the modified stability
number (i.e. it is expected that the significant wave height is smaller
after wave breaking) would have resulted for larger damages (i.e. it is
expected larger damage due to overtopping and wave breaking before the
structure). Thus, smaller values of the modified stability number and
larger values of the damage, might have shifted the blue curve (i.e. Sta-
bility expression) more on the left side in Fig.6.5, and thereby closer to
the present experimental results and even better to provide a good fitting.
However, this is just a consideration based on what is expected to oc-
cur from the literature. These processes are more complex considering
real-life conditions, and thereby in order to confirm the latter analy-
sis/explanations, more experimental tests should be conducted.
On the other hand, several other factors might resulted to larger damage
to the present experiments. To begin with, the experiments conducted at
the laboratories should represent real-life conditions as much as possible.
For that reason, it has been found that for experiments made for break-
waters, a sandy bed has also been placed inside the flume and when the
waves are generated, the sand-particles can transport around the flume
and positioned also among the stones of the breakwater. Due to the sed-

Conclusion
7.1
Overall conclusions
The main purpose of the present project, is to evaluate the performance of
submerged breakwaters following both a theoretical and an experimental
approach and the main conclusions/suggestions are presented.
For the extreme wave height analysis, the Weibull distribution provides
the best Goodness of fit to the data set. From plotting the significant
wave height over the return period, the results reveals that when the
extreme wave height analysis is conducted at shallow water, the design
significant wave height is overestimated for larger return periods.
The outcomes regarding the preliminary analysis, shown a large range of
required stone size for different damages and probability of exceedance
but not for the different lifetimes. For the present case, the lifetime might
be considered as a governing parameter more for designing the structure
at deep water rather than at a shallow regime.
==PAGE:104==
86
Conclusion
For the detailed design, the characteristic wave height H2% is used instead
of the H1/3, as wave breaking and shoaling are considered. The freeboard
is chosen to be zero, as the design wave height is quite large compared to
the existing water depth, and thereby for larger values of freeboard, larger
wave transmission and erosion of the coast would have been expected.
Based on the latter information, and for an initiation of damage S = 2 and
probability of exccedance of pf = 0.2, the sieve size of the required stone
is found to Dn = 0.745 m. The wave transmission coefficient, is directly
related to the magnitude of the design wave height, of the freeboard and
of the crest width, and is found equal to Ct = 0.32. An illustration of the
theoretical design is given in Fig.3.7.
Regarding the experiments, the design wave conditions are measured and
calculated just before the structure. A foreshore is considered, in order to
represent the effects of the shoaling and the wave breaking processes in the
design wave conditions. For the reflection, five different wave gauges are
utilized to provide more accurate results, and the damage of the structure
is estimated via a vertical and a horizontal laser.
The failure modes between conventional and submerged breakwaters dif-
fer. The failure mode and the erosion of the seaward slope, depends on
the one hand on the magnitude of the wave heights and the overtopping
similar to the conventional breakwaters and on the other hand on the mag-
nitude of the freeboard. For larger freeboards, more energy is allowed to
dissipate the structure, and thereby less wave energy is concentrated on
the seaward slope (i.e. smaller damage). The failure modes of the crest
width, are also related to the freeboard and the overtopping. Extreme
conditions of overtopping with large magnitude of freeboard can result
to severe erosion of the crest width. As for the rearside slope, its failure
mode is more related to the amount of the wave transmission and the
magnitude of the crest width. Lower amount of wave transmission with
larger crest width can lead to a reduction of the damage in the sea-ward
slope.
The experimental results of the present work, do not coincide with the
outcomes of the suggested stability expression. The main reason behind
these differences, it might be related to the fact that the present experi-
ments have been made, considering a foreshore and thereby the shoaling
==PAGE:105==
7.1 Overall conclusions
87
and the wave breaking process, meanwhile the tests used for deriving the
stability expression are conducted for a flat sea-bed. Another important
reason, is that during the addtion/removal of the water, few stones might
have removed and this could have led to higher estimation of the eroded
area and thereby of the total damage.
The freeboard might be the governing parameter, for the stability of a
submerged breakwater. It has been observed that by increasing the free-
board (i.e. lower structure height), the magnitude of the damage is much
smaller. However, selecting higher values of freeboard, on the one hand
increase the stability of the structure, but on the other hand provide less
protection to the coastal area due to larger wave transmission.
As for the effects of the crest width, it has been found that even though
larger crest widths resulted to larger eroded area, this might not always
indicates higher instability of the structure. Therefore, it might be con-
cluded that the crest width should be a governing parameter not for the
stability expression but for amount of the wave transmission that can
reach the coastal area. As for the slope, it can be might confirmed that
the slope should not be a governing parameter for the stability expression
as different slopes resulted to almost the same damage magnitude.
For several cases, a comparison between two cross sections in the middle
and in the corner is made, where the damage measured through a laser and
visually respectively, and the results indicates differences in the amout of
damage.
The overall conclusion, is that even though the effect of the freeboard, of
the crest width and of the slope are quite clear when analysing separately
each parameter, the combination of all these parameters is much more
complex. Therefore, to keep a balance among a safe stable structure with
a safe coastal area and a cost-effective design, the selection of the latter
parameters should be made very carefully.

Conclusion
7.2
Future research - suggestions
Since the academic and the industrial research in the field of submerged
breakwater is limited, several future works might be suggested.
1. The existing stability formula is found based on few tests. Therefore,
more tests should be conducted to ensure the validity of the formula.
However, any further experiments should be conducted in a sea-bed with
a slope in order to include the wave breaking, shoaling and over-topping
processes and thereby provide much similar results to real-life conditions.
In addition, a sand-seabed should be placed inside the flumes, to also
represent on-site conditions.
2. Submerged breawater designs should performed in hydrodynamic mod-
els, to expand the existed knowledge regarding the flow conditions and
the wave dynamics around submerged structures (ex: MIKE21, OPEN-
FOAM).
3. Since machine learning is the future, optimization tools should be used,
in order to predict the optimal combination of the freeboard, of the crest
and of the slope, which can ensure a stable structure, protection of the
coastal area from being eroded, and an economical-efficient design. This
could be a potential thesis project, in collaboration between the Dep. of
Civil and Mechanical engineering with the Dep. of Computer science.
4. Since the application of the submerged breakwater is increasing, studies
regarding the environmental impact of such structures should be made
(MIKE 21 can be used). This could be another potential thesis project,
in collaboration between the Dep. of Civil and Mechanical engineering
with the Dep. of Aqua.
5.
An Optical-based particle analysis, might be a much more appro-
priate technique of estimating the damage of a submerged breakwater
during experimental tests. This process, can lead to much accurate re-
sults regarding not only the amount of the total damage, but also the
determination of the most vulnerable sections.
6. One of the most important suggestions after conducting this thesis,
==PAGE:107==
7.2 Future research - suggestions
89
is that for similar future experiments at DTU, the initial shape of the
structure should be measured after adding the water to the water flume,
and the final shape before removing the water, to avoid any additional
movement of stones during these processes.
