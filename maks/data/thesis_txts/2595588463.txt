Abstract
The hippocampus is structurally divided in several subfields with different functions. There is
great interest in studying whether hippocampal subfields are differently affected by age and
their specific roles in cognitive impairment derived from healthy ageing. Structural MR scans
are often used for in vivo tissue segmentation of brain tissues. Nevertheless, the lack of tissue
contrast and signal loss in medial temporal lobe region, and the small size of hippocampal
subfields makes this task difficult. The use of ultra-high field 7T structural data and recently
developed automated tools can overcome these problems. The objective of this master thesis
is to study the relation between the hippocampal subfield volumes, cognitive performance and
hippocampal neurochemical changes across lifespan in healthy adults.
Structural and proton single-voxel-spectroscopy MR data acquired at 7T and CANTAB cognitive
testing have been used. First, a robust segmentation pipeline for 7T data has been implemented
and tested. T1-weighted MPRAGE scans have been segmented into the different hippocampal
subfields with FreeSurfer tool to quantify subfield volumes.
Then, asymmetry and volume
changes of subfields with age have been studied with parametric statistical testing. Age-related
changes have also been analyzed for performance on episodic verbal and spatial memory,
visuo-spatial working memory and sustained attention. Hippocampus metabolic measurements
have been also analyzed using the same methodology. Lastly, Spearman’s rank correlations
tests have been performed to find associations between subfield volumes and cognitive scores
and between subfield volumes and spectroscopy.
It has been found that subiculum and hippocampal fissure volumes remain stable throughout
age while the rest of subfields shrink across lifespan.
Age-related impairment of cognitive
functions in which hippocampus play a key role have been also observed. Statistical analyses
reveals that mIns, Cho, tNAA and tCr concentrations increase with age while Glu concentration
does not change. Correlation analyses show differential relevant associations between regional
volumes and cognitive tests scores. Particularly, it has been observed that most hippocampal
subfield volumes are associated with verbal free recall memory. However, when the effect of
age is taken out of the analysis, associations are no longer present. The same effect is found
when studying associations between subfield volumes and spectroscopy data.
In this project it has been shown that automated segmentation of the hippocampal subfields
is reliable at submillimeter resolutions using 7T. Advantages of using ultra-high fields have
been also observed in spectroscopy: reliable measures on absolute concentrations have been
obtained. Associations between hippocampal subfield volumes and cognitive tests suggest that
different regions are involved in different cognitive processes. Results also suggest that bigger
volumes are associated with better cognitive performance only from certain age, but further
research is needed to confirm it. Further study of the causality between structural, functional
and neurochemical events on larger data sample would be convenient to better understand
hippocampal area.

Introduction
1.1
Project motivation
The brain controls most of the activities humans perform, from body movements to integration
of sensory information and memory processes. Therefore, deeper knowledge on brain areas
anatomy, function and chemical composition as well as connections among them is needed
for earlier detection of diseases, potential treatment development and healthy aging promotion.
Specifically, the hippocampus is a complex structure located at the Medial Temporal Lobe (MTL)
of the brain and it is structurally divided in several hippocampal subfields. Its main functions
include the consolidation of declarative memory, as well as context-dependent spatial learning
and regulation of emotional behaviors [1]. It has recently been suggested that different subfields
have distinct functions. Hence, there is an increasing interest in the study of hippocampal
subfields both in presence and in absence of neurological damage to better understand
hippocampus mechanisms within the brain. This region is particularly sensitive to age, thus
study of atrophy related to neurochemical changes and cognitive decline is specially interesting.
While magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is helpful to get in vivo information of most brain
structures, hippocampus size is small and medial temporal lobe is characterized by signal loss:
often delineation of its subregions at clinical imaging resolution is not reliable. Therefore, it is
challenging to link these structures to their function, usually screened with neuropsychological
testing.
Thanks to recent technological improvements we have increased the knowledge of hippocampal
subfields boundaries. More specifically, ultra-high magnetic field scanners, i.e. ≥7 Tesla (T),
enable the acquisition of higher resolution images than clinical 1.5-3T magnetic resonance (MR)
scanners, consequently some subfield boundaries may be reliably delineated in vivo [2, 3]. Now,
subfield segmentation has potential to reveal subregion-specific aging and disease effects. In
addition, proton spectroscopy (1H-MRS) may also benefit from the use of higher fields. The
increased sensitivity to some neuronal metabolites is likely to shed light on the chemical changes
underlying cognitive decline and hippocampal atrophy across lifespan.
The Danish Research Centre for Magnetic Resonance (DRCMR) is a research group with
main focus on brain MR study, settled at Hvidovre Hospital on the south-west area of greater
Copenhagen [4].
DRCMR hosts the most powerful magnet in Denmark, a Philips Achieva
7T MR scanner for research purposes only.
It allows acquisition of high-resolution images
at submillimeter resolutions, which are not possible to achieve on most clinical scanners.
Structural imaging with different contrasts from 60 healthy individuals have been acquired on
the 7T MRI scanner. In addition, cognitive data covering several cognitive domains, including
memory and attention is available. Single-voxel spectroscopy data from hippocampal area have
also been acquired. Participants’ ages vary from young adults to elderly, hence data have been
categorized according to three different age groups for a cross-sectional lifespan study. It is
likely that age is influencing the hippocampus volume, and in particular that certain subfields are
specifically age-sensitive [5]. This project focuses on the development of robust segmentation
of the hippocampus and in particular of its subfields across the different age-groups from
the acquired structural MRI data.
Further, the volume of the different subfields is linked
with performance on cognitive tests to explore whether different subfields are associated with
different functional outcomes. Finally, volumes of the hippocampal subfields are associated
with different metabolic profiles from spectroscopy data.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.2
Objectives
The main goal of this project is to implement a pipeline for the robust segmentation of
hippocampal subfields from 7T structural data.
The secondary objective is to study the
relation between hippocampal subfield volumes, cognitive performance and hippocampal
neurochemical changes across lifespan. Data used in this project had been previously acquired
for another project at DRCMR [6]. Therefore, data acquisition and processing of metabolic data
are not part of this master thesis. In order to fulfill the objectives the following specific goals
must be achieved:
• To understand acquisition process of data and to become acquainted with spectroscopy
data processing
• To pre-process structural MRI
• To develop robust segmentation including tests for inhomogeneities and testing different
set-ups to develop a final pipeline
• To perform statistical testing on the effect of age in hippocampal subfield volumes,
cognitive performance and metabolite levels
• To perform statistical testing of the relation between hippocampal subfield volumes and
cognition
• To perform statistical testing of the relation between hippocampal subfield volumes and
metabolic profiles

Theoretical Background
2.1
Anatomy and function of the brain
In humans, the central nervous system consists of the brain and the spinal cord. The brain is
an organ encased within the skull that controls everything from motor responses to information
processing. Therefore, its structure and function have been widely studied over the history.
Knowledge of anatomy and function of brain areas as well as connections among them is
needed to understand processes like aging and neurological diseases. Over the past decades,
deeper knowledge on living brain has been gained as new tools have become available.
Technological progress on imaging techniques as well as development and refinement of
neuropsychological tests have facilitated the detailed study of living human brain. Contrary
to histology post-mortem studies and animal studies, these new tools provide knowledge on
brain structural, functional and chemical changes during human life.
Ongoing neurological
investigations try to provide explanation to processes that remain unknown. However, many
brain regions can be reliably described at this point.
The brain’s three major parts are the cerebrum, the cerebellum and the brain stem.
The
cerebrum is the largest area and it splits into left and right hemispheres. Actually, some of
the anatomical references for the brain are defined with respect to the position from one of
the hemispheres. A summary on the anatomical terminology to be used from now on in this
document is included in Figure 2.1. Figure A.1 shows a more general picture on directional
terms and anatomical planes, which also apply to the brain.
Figure 2.1: Brain anatomical references shown over brain representation [7].
The cerebrum’s surface is a highly convoluted region called cerebral cortex, a thin layer
composed of gray matter where the bumps are called gyri and the grooves are called sulci.
If the grooves are very deep, they are called fissures [1]. Structurally, these act as boundaries

for the arbitrary regions that each hemisphere is divided into: frontal, parietal, occipital and
temporal lobes [8]. Outer cerebrum’s surface, the cerebral cortex, contains six layers that differ
on the cellular composition and perform different high brain functions. Most of signals that leave
the cerebral cortex do it from layers V and VI, while sensory signals reach cortical layer number
IV. Association functions are mainly performed by layers I, II and III. [9]. In humans, most of the
cerebral cortex is composed of neocortex. It is the most recent component of the cerebral cortex
and it is only present in mammals. Interestingly, humans have more neocortex percentage than
other species. Therefore, the cerebral cortex always works together with lower centers of the
nervous system to which it is connected by input and output pathways to perform high brain
functions.
Functionally, cerebral cortex is divided in motor and sensory areas, depending on whether
controlled activities are related to movement of body parts or to sensory inputs. Main sensory
areas include the primary auditory, visual and somatosensory cortex.
Secondary areas
surround primary areas and integrate information coming from the corresponding primary areas
instead of receiving input directly from senses. Figure 2.2 shows how the main cerebral sensory
and motor areas are spatially located within the cerebral cortex: visual signals terminate in the
occipital lobe, auditory signals terminate in temporal lobe. Posterior half of the frontal lobe is
occupied by the motor cortex, where many signals that initiate movements are initiated.
Figure 2.2: Left lateral view of the motor, sensory and association areas of the cerebral cortex
[10].
Figure 2.2 also shows main association areas.
Association areas do not fit into motor or
sensory categories since they receive simultaneous input from different cortical regions. These
specialized regions integrate new and stored information into more complex functions such as
memory, emotion, perception and problem-solving skills. The most important association areas
are [10, 9, 11]:
• Parieto-occipitotemporal association area.
This area integrates and interprets signals
from the surrounding sensory areas, transforming and processing sensory input into
meaningful perceptions. It can be further divided in functional areas in charge of different
tasks including computation of spatial coordinates of the body, language comprehension,
processing of reading information and learning of naming.
• Prefrontal association area.
It is highly involved in executive function.
Executive

functions include attention, memory, working memory, planning, temporal integration,
decision-making, monitoring and inhibitory control. They are all interrelated: although
some areas of the prefrontal cortex contribute more than others to some of the executive
components, it is difficult to dissociate them by location. Prefrontal association area is also
involved in motor responses. In fact, input signals reach this region before they reach the
primary motor, which executes the response.
• Broca’s area. It has the specific role of word formation. It is a special region located in the
frontal cortex that initially plans the patterns of the words and short sentences to be said.
• Limbic association area. This region of the temporal lobe belong to the more extense
limbic system. It mainly deals with behaviour, emotions and memory. The limbic system
includes the hippocampal formation, which will be described in detail later in this Chapter.
Neurologist Korbinian Brodmann created a map of the human cerebral cortex in 1909 that
divides it into 52 regions called Brodmann’s areas. Although it was created based on histological
structural differences, it has been shown that it matches many of the functional areas of the
cerebral cortex and even today it is commonly referred to by neuroanatomists and psychologists.
For instance, primary motor cortex is more conveniently referred to as the area 4 in Brodmann´s
classification.
The second major part of the brain is the cerebellum, a movement control center. The smaller
cerebellum lies behind the cerebrum. It receives input from the spinal cord providing information
about body position in space and from the pons of the brain stem relaying information from
the cerebral cortex specifying the intended movements. It is divided into three areas, anterior,
posterior and flocculonodular lobes, separated by two fissures. Functionally, the cerebellum
splits into vermis, intermediate zone and lateral zone of the hemisphere. The vermis controls the
muscle movements of areas like the axial body, neck, hips and shoulders while the intermediate
zone controls muscle contractions in the distal parts of limbs. The lateral zone of the hemisphere
joins the cerebral cortex, therefore concerning sequencing and timing of movements [10].
Thirdly, the brain stem connects the cerebrum and cerebellum to the spinal cord through
a complex net of fibers and cells.
It consists of hypothalamus, thalamus, midbrain, pons
and medulla oblongata.
Some classifications consider that thalamus and hypothalamus
(diencephalon) are not considered part of the brain stem but of the limbic system instead.
Brain stem controls essential functions such as breathing, cardiovascular system, stereotyped
movements of the body, equilibrium, sleep cycle and eye movements thus containing motor and
sensory nuclei [10].
Other accessory structures like the basal ganglia can be found in the brain. This motor system
lies laterally to the thalamus and works in close association with the cerebral cortex and other
motor systems to control complex patterns of motor activity [10]. Anatomical, functional, and
connective pathways description of the nervous system are the topic of whole textbooks. It
should be noticed that some of them suggest different classifications than the ones described
in this section. Further details on the functions and processes of brain areas can be consulted
in such encyclopedias and textbooks, although reality is that we are not able to fully understand
some of them and many others remain unknown. Further research is yet necessary; the medial
temporal lobe is a clear example of it.
2.1.1
Medial Temporal Lobe
Medial Temporal Lobe (MTL) region belongs to the limbic system and it include a system
of anatomically related structures that play an essential role in conscious memory for facts
and events and spatial navigation.
It consists of the hippocampal region, which contains

the hippocampus (including the Ammon’s horn, dentate gyrus, and subicular complex), and
the adjacent cortical areas (perirhinal, entorhinal, and parahippocampal cortices), collectively
referred to as the parahippocampal region [12, 13].
Figure 2.3 shows the anatomical
organization of MTL in a schematic representation, where the perirhinal cortex is further divided
into Brodmann areas 35 and 36. It should be noted that there is not standard classification of
the hippocampal structures; different naming and sub-divisions can be found in the literature.

The small hippocampus lie under the cerebral cortex, medial to the lateral ventricle. The subfield
anatomy in the anterior portion (hippocampal head) differs from the middle (body) and posterior
(tail) sections. The hippocampal body consists of two sheets of neurons folded onto each other.
One sheet is named the dentate gyrus (DG) and the other is the Cornu Ammonis or Ammon’s
horn. Ammon’s horn is further divided in four parts, called CA1-CA4 fields (Cornu Ammonis from
1 to 4) [12]. The subicular complex is the most inferior component of the hippocampal region.
It lies between the entorhinal cortex and the CA1 subfield. Although there is no consensus
on it, the definition of subicular complex provided in this document includes subiculum (sub),
presubiculum (presub) and parasubiculum (parasub) according to [3].
Other structures in the MTL include the hippocampus-amygdala-transition-area (HATA), fimbria
and hippocampal fissure. HATA lies in the medial region of the hippocampus, superior to the
other subfields. The fimbria is a fiber bundle that exits posteriorly the hippocampus and plays
a key role on the connection of this structure with other parts of the brain. The hippocampal
fissure is just the space between the molecular layers of some subfields. These regions are not
considered hippocampal subfields themselves, but they are located on the hippocampal area
and support some of its functions [3].
Regarding the parahippocampal area, Figure 2.4 shows the cortical areas ventral to the
hippocampus. The entorhinal cortex (ErC) occupies the medial bank of the rhinal sulcus, while
the perirhinal cortex (PrC) occupies the lateral bank. Lastly, the posterior parahippocampal
cortex (PhC) lies lateral to the rhinal sulcus.
MTL receives inputs from other parts of the brain, more specifically from the association areas
of the cerebral cortex. PrC mainly receives sensory information from the neocortex visual areas
related to qualities of objects. On the other hand, PhC receives strong inputs from dorsal stream
areas about the spatial information. PrC redirects this information to the lateral ErC while PhC
projects mainly to the medial ErC. Then, the ErC sends it to the hippocampus, where most of
the input information converge [12, 15]. Therefore, ErC is the main way of communication way
between the hippocampal subfields and neocortex. Hippocampal subfields are involved in the
so-called trisynaptic circuit, which works as follows: first, the ErC sends excitatory information

MTL have numerous connections with other structures of the limbic system including the

Another important connection takes place between the MTL and the dorsal part of the striatum
(caudate), the nucleus of the basal ganglia. It is believed that both systems are functionally
connected through the prefrontal cortex and ventral striatum. Although basal ganglia is a motor
system, striatum also plays a role in memory, emotion and cognitive planning. There is evidence
that shows that hippocampus and caudate may interact to enable flexible navigation behavior
and that in some cases both systems cooperate to retrieve memories [16].
Regarding function, structures in the MTL are involved in the formation of declarative memory.
Larry R. Squire proposed a model for long-term memory classification in which more than one
memory systems work in parallel to support behavior. In this context, declarative memory is
the conscious memory for facts (semantic memory) and events (episodic memory).
While
declarative memory is related to the human capacity of recollecting facts, non-declarative
memory is related to skills and habits, and it is expressed through performance [17]. Figure
2.5 shows this memory division as well as the brain systems that participate in each type of
memory.
The hippocampus main functions are the consolidation of declarative memory, as well as
context-dependent spatial learning and regulation of emotional behaviors (due to its connection
to the rest of the limbic system) [18].
It has recently been proved that different subfields
have distinct functional roles [2, 19]. PrC is involved in object processing, PhC in contextual
association, CA1 in memory integration, CA3 in memory recall and DG in pattern separation.

Hippocampal structures are specially sensitive to age, including age-shrinkage effects and
cognitive decline in learning and memory. Atrophy of the hippocampus related with age has
been widely reported in the literature [5]. Nonetheless, the study of individual subfields is a
recent field of study. Mixed results for volume changes with normal ageing in the different
subfields are reported in the literature. Usually, CA1-4, DG and subiculum are examined. One
of the processes that may contribute to shrinkage is the decrease in neuronal production, i.e.
neurogenesis with age. In humans, DG has been shown to have a key role in neurogenesis,
therefore it is likely that DG volume decreases as we age. Neurogenesis reduction has also
been related to cognitive decline in hippocampal region.
CA1 atrophy has been proposed
as an Alzheimer’s marker, but other healthy ageing studies have found linear and nonlinear
volume decrease with age. Mixed results are also provided for CA2-4 and subiculum: while
some studies report non-significant atrophy, others report significantly decrease of volume with
increasing age. Disagreement may be due to differences in methodologies, sample size and
statistical analyses [20, 21].
In addition, neuronal loss and a reduction on the synaptic plasticity are also age-related.
As humans age, the number of synaptic connections decrease, dysfunction of dendritic
processes increase and neurotransmitters change. But synaptic plasticity differently affects
hippocampal subfields: studies in rats have shown that DG is specially affected by deficits
in synaptic plasticity. The reasons for changes in synaptic activity with age are under study,
but one hypothesis is that excessive glutamate neurotransmission may contribute to altered
synaptic transmission. Reduction in dendritic branching alterations, in the number of dendritic
spines and synapses in these subfields may also have impact on functional changes. Lastly,
reduced concentration of hippocampal N-acetylaspartate (NAA) and increased concentration of
myoinositol (mIns) metabolites have been reported in humans with increasing age [18].
Patients with hippocampal region damage show some degree of memory impairment.
But
several studies have proven that if the lesions also extend to adjacent medial temporal cortex
the memory impairment is more severe [12]. MTL lesions affect memory independently on
the sensory modality in which information is presented. Besides, immediate memory remains
intact. MTL regions have been also proven to be affected by diseases like depression, epilepsy
and neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) [2]. Since each subfield has
different neuronal populations and connectivity it is also expected to find different sensitivity of
the subfields to these pathologies and it is currently under study.
The most common neurodegenerative disorder is AD which accounts for 60% of the cases of
dementia. It is believed that neuropathological damage from AD firstly spreads to ErC and later

2.2
Cognitive tests
Cognitive tests aim to assess neuropsychological performance by establishing links between
brain and cognition. There is evidence of the relationship between brain structure alterations
and cognitive functions alterations, but exact mapping between the two of them is very difficult
due to the complexity of both processes [25]. Cognition is a complex process and it is usually
subdivided in several cognitive domains including learning and memory, attention, executive
function, visuo-spatial function and language. Cognitive performance of one or more of these
domains declines normally with age. It also declines in patients suffering from neurological
conditions such as Parkinson’s disease, multiple sclerosis, attention deficit disorder, Alzheimer’s
disease and many others. Consequently, cognitive tests can provide a differential diagnosis
between healthy ageing and patients, depending on the cognitive domain whose activity is
declined and on the extent.
Most tests assess performance through a series of questions or simple tasks designed to
measure processes involved in cognition. More ”traditional” tests have been used in clinical
practice for years, and they have been widely verified and validated [26]. More recently, thanks
to the new technological improvements, computerized testing methods have been developed.
They are more commonly used in neuroscience research.
2.2.1
Computerized cognitive testing
In recent decades, computerized cognitive assessment tests have gained popularity over
”traditional” test methods because of their multiple advantages. While ”paper and pencil” tests
have proven to be useful for screening, advanced computerized tests additionally provide
information on changes over time, such as normal cognitive decline with age.
Since they
have higher sensitivity they are suitable for testing within normal range. They allow to measure
reaction times in an accurate way, instant feedback is provided and they are more accessible,
since language barriers disappear [27, 28].
Nowadays, one of the most popular options for testing cognition is the CANTAB test battery.
CANTAB stands for Cambridge Neuropsychological Test Automated Battery and it consists of a
standardized battery of 13 cognition tests that cover the domains of attention, memory, executive
function, decision making and social cognition [29]. Tests can be used in assessment of both

2.2.2
”Paper-and-pencil” cognitive testing
Although most computerized tests have been validated and are they are extensively used in
research, paper-and-pencil assessments are very used in clinical practice to provide differential
diagnosis. Lack of reliability and problems from the interaction with computer monitor may
affect performance of computerized tests [26].
Furthermore, most paper-and-pencil testing
require drawings or manual constructions or spoken responses which cannot be converted to
a computer test.
Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) is a common screening tool for MCI. It helps physicians
in the detection of this clinical state that often evolves to dementia. MoCA test consists of
several tasks presented in a single paper page to be completed in about 10 minutes. Different
tasks assess short-term and delayed memory, working memory, visuospatial ability, executive

has an associated score and the outcome of the MoCA test is the sum of all tasks scores [38].
The Trail Making Test is used to measure visual attention and executive function [33]. It was

Wisconsin card sorting test was originally developed at the University of Wisconsin in 1948,

is always aimed [39]. The test also has multiple computerized versions. These simple tasks
requires planning and judgement, therefore elevated number of moves to solve the problem
may indicate clinical condition.
There are many other tests that have been validated and they are widely used in
neuropsychological evaluations.
Different tests have been optimized to evaluate different

brain diseases or brain damage. Given the large amount of tools that are available, selection
of the appropriate ones for each experiment is extremely important. The appropriate cognitive
tests, combined with medical image screening constitute the basis of neuroscience research.
2.3
Magnetic resonance
Magnetic resonance phenomenon has been widely used in medicine and research to obtain
information on human anatomy and function. MRI is one of the main medical imaging modalities
together with planar radiography, computed tomography (both making use of X-rays), nuclear
medicine and ultrasound.
Contrary to X-rays and nuclear medicine, MRI does not involve
ionizing radiation.
Besides, the high spatial resolution and high contrast resolution on soft
tissues of MRI makes it very convenient for brain imaging. MRI can provide anatomical and
functional information, depending on the acquisition set-up. Therefore, applications not only
include the study of brain structures, but also the study of brain activity. It is worthy mentioning
that multimodal imaging studies have become an popular choice as they provide combined
information from different image modalities and the amount of information that can be extracted
from a tissue is maximized.
MR is based on the magnetic properties of some of the body atoms, mainly the paramagnetic
hydrogen (protons), since there is a huge amount of them on the form of water molecules and
they generate enough signal to be detected. Nuclear spin is one of the intrinsic properties of
these nuclei, which, simplifying, means that they rotate around their own axis. In the absence
of a magnetic field B0, protons point randomly. As a consequence of spin magnetic property,
protons behave as small compass needles that align themselves in a presence of a magnetic
==PAGE:21==
12
CHAPTER 2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
field, reaching an equilibrium state. When the nuclei are placed under constant B0, usually
1.5-3T in human clinical applications, they swing around the direction of the field in a conical
motion, and this movement is called precession. The frequency at which protons precess is
determined by the product of the gyromagnetic ratio of hydrogen and the magnetic field strength,
and it is called the Larmor frequency [40].
Due to movements and nuclear interactions the alignment with the field is only partial,
and protons largely point randomly.
However, many of these nuclei form together a net
magnetization in the direction of the field, and it is actually the sum of many contributions what
is being tracked in MR technique. For instance, if an equal number of these arrows point in
all directions, the net magnetization will thus be zero, as in the absence of a field. But in the
presence of the field B0 there will be a net magnetization of the order of parts per million. Since
the net magnetization is generally the sum of many contributions that swing in synchrony, the
net magnetization itself swings as a compass needle. It is proportional to the field: larger fields
produce a higher degree of alignment and thus a large magnetization. In equilibrium state, the
net magnetization consists of longitudinal component only, which cannot be directly measured.
When a second magnetic field B1, perpendicular to B0 and much weaker, is delivered in
synchrony with the precession of the nuclei the magnetic spins are rotated away from equilibrium
around the weak magnetic field.
This process is called excitation.
Due to resonance
phenomenon, a small magnetic field B1 will have a great effect if it is delivered at resonance
frequency.
The flip angle depends on the time the field B1 is on.
B1 is produced by
radio frequency (RF) waves emitted from an alternating current from an electromagnetic coil.
Radio waves are magnetic fields that change direction (oscillate) in time and they push the
net magnetization away from equilibrium. This allows to keep track of protons signal, from
transversal component of magnetization. When the current is turned off, the spins take some
time to go back to equilibrium state after excitation.

Relaxation is due to 2 distinct
phenomena: spin-spin and spin-lattice interactions [41].
During excitation, radio waves are emitted from the body.
Only when the spins are back
to equilibrium (only longitudinal magnetization component is left) signal stops being emitted.
Radio waves are emitted at the same frequency that the nuclei rotation. The strength of the
radio waves, known as amplitude, depends on the size of the net magnetization (transversal

13
magnetization) and on the orientation, and it is weakened as the oscillations of the nuclei in
the direction of the second field gradually vanish. These waves induce a voltage in a coil and
therefore they are measured and later analyzed.
Three additional perpendicular magnets called gradients are necessary to generate a linear
variation of B0 so that image can be spatially encoded. Normally, they are referred to as Gx,
which varies the field in x-direction, Gy, which varies the field in y-direction and Gz, which varies
the field in z-direction. Although details on imaging formation are not covered in this manuscript,
gradients are essential in MR scanners: by linearly changing the field strength, the precession
frequency changes as well, and this difference in frequency is used for spatial encoding of the
image [42].
Interactions between nuclei give rise to magnetization constantly approaching the equilibrium
state.
This is called relaxation and it is the reason why spins go back to equilibrium after
excitation. The speed at which relaxation occurs depends on the proton interactions with their
neighbors. The relaxation occurs on two different time scales: the magnetization perpendicular
to B0, transversal magnetization decreases rapidly on a timescale T2.
However, it takes
considerably longer to recover the magnetization along the field (longitudinal magnetization),
on a timescale T1. Contrast in MRI comes from differences in the relaxation times (T1, T2)
and proton density [40]. Different MR settings, called sequences, are designed with the aim of
maximizing the differences on these parameters. By modifying the time the different fields are
being delivered in an smart way, the signal coming from some tissues can be enhanced while
the signal coming from some other tissues can be attenuated or suppressed. For instance, it is
possible to acquire T1-weighted images where differences in T1 are reflected and differences
in T2 and proton density are minimized. This is only possible because of prior the knowledge on
human tissues’ relaxation times. A MR sequence usually starts with a preparation step, such
as excitation with 90º flip angle or inversion pulse with 180º flip angle; and it continues with
readout, which is specified by a loop-structure to acquire the maximum information possible in
the shortest time possible [42]. Sometimes, chemical contrast agents are administered to be
used to further increase contrast and to improve visibility of some tissues.
MR phenomenon can be used to produce structural and functional images. Each of these
modalities have different applications equally interesting for brain imaging. In neuroscience,
structural data is normally used to quantitatively measure tissue volumes and shapes. MRI
is the most suitable imaging method for morphometry purposes since the obtained contrast
resolution on brain soft tissues is high. Morphometry will be introduced in next Section. In
addition, structural data provides an anatomical reference of both healthy and damaged tissue
that can complement functional imaging. By contrast, functional MRI (fMRI) gives information of
neural activity based on the BOLD (Blood Oxygenation Level Dependent) effect. BOLD contrast
appears due to inhomogenities in the magnetic field of venous capillaries following blood supply
and composition modifications due to neuronal activity.
MR also provides information on metabolic concentrations.
Proton Magnetic Resonance
Spectroscopy (1H-MRS) is used to measure neurochemistry in vivo in a non-invasive way.
MRS takes advantage of the small change in resonance frequency experienced by nuclei
with different chemical neighbors. Molecular structure and proton position within a molecule
determines a chemical shift of parts per million [40]. Different chemical shifts are represented as
line peaks at different frequencies on the spectrum of the acquired data. The area under those
peaks is proportional to the total hydrogen concentration of that substance in the body, and
therefore to the total concentration of the metabolites. Using a priori knowledge of metabolite
chemical shifts and relaxation times it is possible to decompose the spectrum and to estimate
concentrations. The most common metabolic profiling technique is single-voxel spectroscopy
==PAGE:23==
14

(SVS), which is often used in research. In this modality, a sample volume of interest (VOI)
is targeted and a frequency spectrum is obtained. Volume selection without contribution from
surrounding tissues is essential to get good spectral quality. However, voxels need to be large in
order to get sufficient signal and they generally contain more than one kind of tissue. A previous
structural MRI is needed for correct positioning of the volume of interest, which increases the
scanning time.
MRS parameter optimization is important to keep good spectral quality and therefore for
accurate metabolite quantification. These include B0 shimming, power adjustment and water
suppression.
Shimming is needed to keep magnetic field homogeneous and hence avoid
perturbations.
If remained unsuppressed, water signal causes distortions on the spectrum
baseline and overlap with surrounding metabolite signals [43].
Normal ageing process and some brain pathologies may result in changes on biochemical
concentrations of brain metabolites. MRS can also be useful for differential diagnosis of brain
tumors. Only some molecular components, called metabolites, can be measured from proton
signal at clinical field strength. Some of the chemical components present in the brain at enough
concentrations to be quantified include N-acetyl aspartate (NAA), creatine (Cr), choline (Cho),
myo-inositol (mIns) γ-aminobutyric acid (GABA), glutamate (Glu), lactate (Lac) and glutathione
(GSH). NAA is a neuronal marker since it is only present in neurons and dendrites, but is
has been also suggested to be closely related to ATP production. Cr is a marker for aerobic
metabolism, playing a role in keeping cell homeostasis.
Cho reflects glial cell membrane
turnover. Alterations in Cho levels may suggest changes in gliosis, inflammation or myelination.
mIns acts by keeping cell volume and fluid balance and it has been suggested to be involved
in brain cell signaling. GABA is the major inhibitory neurotransmitter while Glu is the major
excitatory neurotransmitter in human CNS. Lastly, lactate’s presence is an indicator of anaerobic
metabolism [42, 44].
MR scanners require complex instrumentation. A extremely powerful, superconductive magnet
able to generate an homogeneous strong field of 1-3T is needed to create B0. More recent
technological advances have made possible to build higher field scanners, but their use is
restricted to research purposes. Secondly, RF coils to emit the perpendicular B1 and to receive
signal from spins are needed. The emitted field is weak compared to B0, in the order of micro
T. High sensitivity is needed to detect the weak radio waves emitted by the nuclei, which in
turns induce a voltage over the coil. Hence, RF electronics on transmission and reception are
needed such as amplifiers, analog-to-digital converter and a computer, among others. Three
perpendicular gradients are necessary to generate a linear variation of B0 so that image can be
spatially encoded. They apply fields of the order of mT per meter (typically 40-80 mT per meter)
to encode the signal in each direction (x, y and z). Additional MR hardware includes gradient
amplifiers and sequence controllers for field synchronization; regarding software, console and
reconstruction software are also needed.
2.3.1
7T MRI
Most of the routine scanners used in hospitals produce magnetic fields ranging from 1 to 3T.
Actually, the current Danish high-field limit is 3T for clinical applications.
Nonetheless, the
technological trend is to use higher fields; there exist human scanners beyond 10T used for
ethically approved clinical research. Many of the ultra-high field MR research is based on 7T
scanners as they are already available in dozens of laboratories [45].
Ultra-high field images allow higher spatial resolution and contrast between gray and white
matter is improved with respect to 3T imaging. As the magnetic field B0 strength increases so
does the fraction of spins becoming aligned. Therefore, the signal being produced can be better

detected and differentiated from background noise. In other words, the signal-to-noise ratio
(SNR) increases as the field strength increases. Hence, the spatial resolution also increases,
up to submillimeter resolutions [45]. Recent studies on 7T imaging evidence the improved

and easier delineation among structures is one of the most promising advantages of ultra-high

Applications in neuroimaging also include fMRI and MRS improved capabilities. Regarding
metabolic information, using higher B0 increases the chemical shift dispersion leading to more
accurate metabolic information. Recent studies suggest an improvement on the number of
metabolites that can be detected and on the accuracy; specifically, the inhibitory GABA, whose
concentration is low and hence difficult to detect at lower B0 [46].
Relevance of imaging of the hippocampal structures has been already stated. The improved
image spatial resolution on 7T MR scanners has opened a new study field; technological
improvements have increased the knowledge of boundaries of MTL subregions making feasible
to delineate the main hippocampal subunits and parahippocampal regions in vivo.
This
morphological analysis is potentially useful to show how structures are differently affected by
ageing process and to differentiate it from atrophy due to diseases such as AD and mesial
temporal sclerosis.
Currently, efforts are put on creating and improving robust automatic
segmentation methods to substitute the laborious manual method [46].
1H-MRS may also
benefit from ultra-high fields. Increased sensitivity can be used to reliably assess hippocampal
neurochemical changes accross lifespan and relate them to cognitive aging.
Nevertheless, the use of these scanners also present some challenges.
Ultra-high field
scanners are bigger and technically more complex, which translates in higher costs. Another
challenge is the biological effect of increasing the field: although 7T scanners have been shown
to be safe for human use, experiencing dizziness and metal taste is more common. They are
more sensitive to patient motion and tissue overheats faster. Some periodic movements such
as breathing or heart beats can be modelled and removed, but sudden, unexpected patient
movements cannot [45].
2.4
Image segmentation and morphology
based analysis
Image segmentation consists of separating an image into different segments or classes. In
medical imaging, those segments can be body tissues, a whole organ, a sub-organ or any
other region of interest. Segmentation can be either manual, where the user delineates the
boundaries between different structures, or automatic, where image properties such as intensity
values are used to decide boundaries. Reliable automated methods are desirable when large
amount of images are to be segmented, since manual process is laborious and time-consuming
[47].
Tissue segmentation has become an essential tool for quantitative MRI, since structure volumes
can be computed and shape can be described thanks to morphometry analysis posterior to
segmentation. Therefore, MRI-based segmentation is a common practice in neuroimaging used
to relate in vivo structural changes with functional and biochemical changes [47]. As mentioned
in Section 2.3, MRI has a good soft-tissue contrast and spatial resolution, hence is suitable for
brain tissue segmentation. Specifically, ultra-high field MRI offers improved image resolution
which makes feasible to delineate smaller structures and it might add value to some procedures.

Morphometry applications include tumor quantification; by acquiring data on the same diseased

Two of the most common software used in neuroimaging are FreeSurfer [49] and Statistical

FreeSurfer is an open-source software for the analysis
and visualization of both structural and functional brain MRI developed by the Laboratory for

It is supported on Linux and macOS systems and MATLAB

SPM12, the latest version of SPM software, is a free, copyright software for statistical analysis

Actually, SPM stands for Statistical Parametric Mapping, which refers to
the set of statistical tests methods used to assess functional data in which the software
is built on.
SPM12 does not have specific system requirements but it needs MATLAB to
run and relies on some externally compiled C routines. Its most recent version is not only
suited for fMRI analysis, but also for structural MRI, PET, SPECT, electroencephalography
(EEG) and magnetoencephalography (MEG) data analysis. It additionally includes tools for
spatial and temporal processing of fMRI data [50].
CAT12 is an extension of SPM12 for
computational analysis on MRI developed by the Structural Brain Mapping Group at the
Departments of Psychiatry and Neurology, University of Jena. Similarly to FreeSurfer, CAT12
includes processing tools such as skull-stripping, spatial registration to a template, gray-white

One of its main features is
voxel-based morphometry tool, an automated tool for providing volume estimation of a region
of interest [52].
Additional software for neuroimaging are ITK-SNAP and FSL. ITK-SNAP is an open-source

for the analysis of fMRI, MRI and diffusion tensor brain imaging [54].
It includes FIRST,
a segmentation tool for subcortical structures based on a Bayesian model.
Some other
neuroimaging software including morphometry analysis tools can be found. These tools may
differ on the theoretical concept they are built on to segment structures. They usually include
a user guide interface for visualization of the data and navigation through different features, as

To summarize, although several protocols have been implemented they follow different rules
depending on the input data. Some of them only deal with AD or MCI patient data, which
can bias the results. Other methods are only implemented for 3T and they result in different
outputs; hence they cannot be translated into ultra-high field images where the resolution is
higher. Common problems include the lower accuracy estimation for smaller subfields. Another
common problem is the use of geometrical rules for delineation of some structures when the
in-vivo data is not informative enough. Because of this, most of the protocols do not segment
hippocampal head an tail, but only hippocampal body.
All this evidences the need of an
harmonized protocol for hippocampal subfields as well as further investigation on the benefits
of using ultra-high field MRI. Nevertheless, FreeSurfer and ASHS have proven to be robust if
the quality of input data is good.

Magnetic resonance data
3.2.1
Data description
Structural data was acquired with a Philips Achieva 7T whole body scanner. A dual transmit
coil and a 32-channel receive head coil were used. The relevant sequences for this study are
described below.
Magnetization Prepared RApid Gradient Echo (MPRAGE) is a sequence for acquiring
high-contrast 3D-T1-weighted images in reasonable scanning times.
The sequence starts
with a non-selective 180º inversion pulse followed by some variable time delay. Then signal
is acquired by using consecutive gradient echoes with small flip angles. MPRAGE images
have good SNR at short scanning times. [58]. Specifically, a T1-weighted MPRAGE sequence
(slices=380, slice thickness=0.5 mm, TR=8.0 ms, TE=3.2 ms, flip angle=7 degrees, field of
view=256x256x190 mm, voxel size=0.5x0.5x0.5 mm, scan duration
4 min) was acquired
for hippocampal segmentation and for placement of the 1H-MRS hippocampal voxel. It was
19

20

also used for within voxel tissue segmentation into gray matter (GM), white matter (WM) and
cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) for partial volume correction of spectroscopy data.
Secondly, T2-weighted Fluid Attenuation Inversion Recovery (FLAIR) aim is to suppress the
cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) signal by applying a non-selective 180º inversion pulse previous to
excitation. This sequence has shown potential to reveal lesions that are difficult to see with
other sequences. [58]. A FLAIR sequence (slices=168, slice thickness=1 mm, TR=6000 ms,
TE=364 ms, TI=1825 ms, field of view=232x232x168 mm, voxel size=0.686x0.686x1 mm, scan

with a semi-localised by adiabatic selective refocusing (sLASER) sequence (TR/TE=3700/32
ms, bandwidth=4 kHz, data points=2048) with voxel placed in the left hippocampus (30x15x15
mm3, 64 acquisitions, scan duration 4 min). A non-water suppressed spectrum was acquired
at the beginning of the sequence. Water suppression was performed with variable pulse power
with optimized relaxation delay (VAPOR) was applied. VAPOR scheme makes use of selective
RF pulses with different power and optimized timing with respect to T1 relaxation of water to
efficiently suppress the water signal [59].
A shim box 15 mm longer than the voxel in all directions were centered on the voxel and the
FASTMAP algorithm was applied for second order B0 shimming. Shimming is crucial to benefit
from ultra-high magnetic field. FASTMAP technique is a popular choice for rapid and automated
shimming based on measuring B0 field plots along projections [60].
Strong nonlinear field
inhomogeneities cannot be compensated by linear shims alone, but higher order shims are
needed. FASTMAP adjusts field homogeneity by using first and second order shims, resulting
in improved spectral resolution.
T1-MPRAGE structural MRI (already described before in this Section) was used for anatomical
reference and for tissue classification.
3.2.2
Hippocampal subfields segmentation
Image pre-processing
Structural MPRAGE underwent bias field correction and spatial normalization since they are the
data the segmentation is based on.
Bias field signal is a low frequency signal originated by the inhomogeneities in the magnetic
field of the scanner and patient-induced electrodynamic interactions. This smoothly varying
signal not only reduces the high frequency components of the image but also changes the
intensity values of the image pixels. Temporal lobes are particularly sensitive to this artifact,
because of the subject’s head position in the coil.
As it can be seen in Figure 3.1, the
same body tissue may have very different pixel values across the image. This might cause
intensity-based segmentation methods to misclassify tissues [61]. This problem aggravates in
ultra-high field scans: due to safety restrictions, local transmit coils are used instead of whole
body coils. The use of head coils results in large intensity variations due to transmission of
more heterogeneous B1 field. In addition, the frequency at higher fields is also higher and
the wave pattern changes. Therefore, standard bias correction methods are not optimized for
7T. Accurate tissue classification is essential for this project, hence two different correction
approaches were compared.
Subfield hippocampal segmentation is performed on FreeSurfer. As part of the classification
process, structural MRI volumes undergo a cortical reconstruction process through recon-all
main stream.
It includes a bias field correction step with function nu_correct.
It uses the
==PAGE:30==
3.2. MAGNETIC RESONANCE DATA
21
Brain Imaging Software Toolbox to perform non-parametric non-uniform intensity normalization
[49]. nu_correct can also be run independently, so this method was tested for the project’ data.
However, the process is fully automated and it is not refined for ultra-high field data, and the
results were not good enough.
SPM12 also performs bias correction as part of its segmentation module. The user can modify
the default settings since empirical exploration is the way to know what works best for each
type of data. Bias regularization and full width at half maximum (FWHM) are two of those
parameters. Higher bias regularization means that input volume has little bias so the algorithm
does not to model it. FWHM of Gaussian smoothness of bias relates to the smoothness of
the inhomogeneity. If non-uniformity on data is smooth, large FWHM is recommended. In
previous studies at DRCMR it had been shown that selecting 30 mm FWHM and medium bias
regularization (0.01) yielded optimal bias correction for 7T MPRAGE. 60 mm FWHM and light
regularization (0.001) were also tried out for comparison. 30 mm and medium regularization
results slightly outperformed so all volumes were processed in the same way.

types of tissues. Besides, the estimated bias field is shown.

The Spatial Adaptive Non Local Means Denoising Filter on CAT12 was used after bias field

The advantage of this filter is that it does not need a priori
information on the image noise level, as it adapts to the amount of noise of the image locally.
The strength of the filter is automatically determined by estimation of residual noise, although
manual input is also accepted. This step is also important to increase the robustness of the
segmentation of the hippocampal area.
Segmentation in FreeSurfer
Open-source FreeSurfer v6.0 includes a module for automated segmentation of hippocampal
subfields [3]. This publicly available tool has been used to segment the hippocampal subfields
from the structural volumes. The other automated segmentation tool available, ASHS, was
discarded because of the MRI input requirements.
By the time this project started, ASHS
required a T1-weighted scan and an oblique coronal T2-weighted scan with high in-plane
resolution (0.4x0.4 mm), while FreeSurfer offers more flexibility and it seems more adequate for
this project’s data.

This tool generates a segmentation based on a probabilistic atlas.
Brain images from 15
subjects were acquired ex vivo at 7T and hippocampal subfields were manually segmented.
Data comes from generally old subjects (mean is 79 years old), some diagnosed with Alzheimer
disease while others were healthy subjects at the moment of their death. Due to the extremely
high resolution of the atlas (0.13 mm isotropic), delineation of a large number of structures is
accurately defined. In addition, a set of 39 in vivo scans with surrounding hippocampal areas
manually delineated are available. Delineation of surrounding structures is included just to assist
to subregion delineation, thus segmentation on standard resolution (1 mm), 1.5T MPRAGE
images is enough. The ex vivo and in vivo data are combined by to conform the final probabilistic
atlas. The probabilistic atlas is represented as a compact tetrahedral mesh where each vertex
has an associated vector of probabilities for the different hippocampal subfields. It is adaptive
to each region, which means that topology and resolution in uniform regions is different than
in convoluted areas. Any in vivo MRI scan can be segmented by making use of the statistical
atlas. A generative model followed by Bayesian inference is used to find the most likely labels
for a set of input MRI intensities given the atlas [3].
The input to the segmentation algorithm is the output of the recon-all pipeline of
FreeSurfer. recon-all stream consists of several pre-processing steps to finally produce cortical
reconstruction surfaces of the brain.
The stream includes multiple steps such as motion
correction, bias field correction (although it is no effective for this project’s scans), skull-stripping,
and brain segmentation into 36 structures and it takes about 10 hours to run. Independently
on the mode of operation, the segmentation can be run together with the recon-all stream or
afterwards. In order to run the segmentation module MATLAB R2012 runtime is required, but
MATLAB licenses are not required. -conf-hires2 flag was included to avoid rescaling to standard
1 mm FreeSurfer resolution and to keep the submillimeter resolution of input images.
Due to the generative nature of the algorithm, segmentation might be based on T1 volume,
but also on an additional volume with different contrast. When only a T1 volume is available it
has to contain the hippocampus and to have high resolution (better than 1 mm is desirable).
If an additional MRI volume of the same patient is available it might be useful to include
it in the segmentation process.
Accepted contrasts include T2, proton density and even
another T1, as long as this second scan is pre-aligned to the main T1 scan.
This mode
of operation is recommended when the additional scan has similar resolution than the main
scan or when the additional scan does not cover the whole hippocampal area. Lastly, only
the additional scan might be used for segmentation. This option is recommended when the
additional scan has higher resolution than the T1 scan and it covers the whole hippocampal
region. Available data for this study includes T1-weighted (MPRAGE) and FLAIR volumes,
both covering the whole head. Two segmentation methods were used for later comparison:
MPRAGE-based segmentation and multispectral segmentation with FLAIR as an additional
input. Since resolution of FLAIR images is lower, FLAIR-based segmentation was discarded.
Output include volumes with the discrete segmentation volumes for left and right hemispheres,

the individual metabolite spectra. LCModel method analyzes acquired in vivo brain spectra
as a linear combination of in vitro model solutions spectra. It uses a model-free constrained
regularization that accounts for baseline and lineshape that best fits the input data, avoiding too
much bias due to under-parametrization but also over-fitting. Maximum-likelihood estimates of
neurometabolite concentrations are given.
Selected VOI has to be big enough so that sufficient signal is acquired to quantify
neurometabolites present at low concentrations. As a consequence, several tissues are present,
usually white matter, gray matter and cerebrospinal fluid.
In proton spectroscopy, water
concentration is the reference value for quantification. While CSF is mostly made of water, most
of the metabolites are contained in GM and WM. Therefore, partial volume effects may cause
inaccuracies when quantifying metabolites. One possibility is to report concentration ratios to
a metabolite that is assumed stable (reference) instead of absolute concentrations. The main
disadvantage is that if a change is found, it is not possible to determine whether it is caused by
the metabolite, the reference or both [70].
If absolute concentrations are aimed, partial volume correction should be performed using the
known water concentrations for different tissues. In this project, VOI tissue fractions have been
taken into account for their different water concentrations. To do so, first a 3D binary mask with
1s where the VOI is present has been created. It has with the same size than the MPRAGE scan
and it is co-registered to imaging coordinates. Secondly, automatic segmentation of MPRAGE
structural image has been performed with CAT12, the voxel-based morphometry tool of SPM12.
The 3D T1-weighted MPRAGE can be used since it has good quality and it was acquired at the
time of scanning [70]. The output includes tissue probability volumes for WM, GM and CSF
normalized to 1. Next, the binary mask and the partial volume maps are overlaid. For every
pixel in the VOI, the tissue with the highest probability is assumed to be the correct one and
then that tissue counts is increased by one unit. Finally, each tissue count is divided by the total
amount of pixels that are 1 in the mask to get the tissue fractions.

3.3.1
CANTAB cognitive tests
CANTAB tests assess four key domains: attention and psychomotor speed, executive function,
memory and emotion and social cognition.
Sustained attention, episodic memory and
visuo-spatial working memory are the key domains studied in this project, as it has been shown
that impairments in these cognitive functions are related to ageing process. It is hypothesized
that changes in MTL with age partially explain individual differences in these memory domains,
as MTL plays a key role in attention and memory.
Participants performed several cognitive tasks outside the scanner on a tablet. Relevant tests
for the present project include Paired Associates Learning (PAL), Verbal Recognition Memory
(VRM), Spatial Working Memory (SWM) and Rapid Visual Information Processing (RVP). These
tests have been shown to be sensitive to normal ageing cognitive impairment and they cover the
cognitive functions the hippocampus is involved in. Description of the task, the main cognitive
subdomain they cover and examples on when to use each test are provided below.
Paired Associated Learning (PAL) and Verbal Recognition Memory (VRM) tests aim to assess
memory performance. PAL involves visual information, while VRM deals with verbal information.
PAL is designed to assess visual episodic memory and learning. In PAL test, initially several
boxes (coloured squares) are shown on screen which are opened one by one in a random order.
Boxes may or may not contain any pattern that becomes visible only when boxes are opened.

levels of increasing difficulty are shown as participants solve the easier ones. This test is related
to visual memory and new learning and it is recommended to assess Epilepsy, Parkinson’s
disease and Multiple Sclerosis, among others. Besides, PAL is used for early detection of
Alzheimer’s disease, by discriminating those patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI) who
will develop the disease and those who will not [71].
For VRM, firstly a sequence of words is shown on screen one by one. Participants’ must recall
as many of those words as they remember. Next, two words are shown together on the screen,
one of them from the original list. Participants are asked to identify the word they have seen
before. The second task is repeated after a delay. This test evaluates verbal memory and
new learning, and it is recommended to assess cognitive function in schizophrenia. Retrieval of
verbal information recalls tapping into temporal lobe therefore assessing hippocampus function.
One specific memory type is the Working memory, which is assessed by the Spatial Working
Memory (SWM) test. It assess the ability to hold and manipulate visuospatial information in mind.
PAL test is designed to measure some aspects of spatial working memory, since participants
are required to remember the spatial distribution of the patterns. However, SWM task design
is different. Initially, several boxes are shown on screen. Using an elimination process, the
participant has to find the yellow ‘token’ that is hidden inside one of the boxes. The patient has
to use the token to fill up a column of tokens on the right side of the screen. Yellow tokens are
hidden in a different box every time but they are never hidden under the same box more than
once. Thus, participants’ spatial working memory is assessed, as well as providing a measure
of strategy. This test can be used to assess many disorders such as depression, affective
disorders and autism and diseases such as multiple sclerosis, schizophrenia, Alzheimer’s and
Parkinson disease.
Sustained attention, a subdomain of attention, can be assessed with Rapid Visual Information
Processing (RVP) test. Participants are shown digits that appear in a pseudo-random order and
they are asked to recognize target sequences on those. There are several phases wit increasing
difficulty and they must react as quickly as possible. This test may be used to assess cognitive
function in Alzheimer’s disease, depression or multiple sclerosis, among others.

3.3.2
Data description
CANTAB provides multiple outcome measures for each test that include the number of trials,
number of errors, mean and median latency time, accuracy measurements, etc. The selected
==PAGE:38==
3.4. STATISTICAL ANALYSES
29
key measures used in this project are introduced below.
For PAL, the total errors adjusted, PALTEA, has been selected for further analysis. It provides
the number of times the subject selects an incorrect box, adjusted for the estimated number of
errors that participants would have made on levels they did not reach. This way, performance
can be directly compared across all individuals. This is specially useful for the analysis of our
data sample, since older adults are expected to make more mistakes and young adults are
expected to reach more challenging levels.
Regarding VRM, 3 key outcome measures have been chosen according to previous literature
on hippocampal subfields. VRMFRDS is the free recall, that is, the total number of distinct
words that a participant correctly recall during the immediate stage. VRMIRTC is an immediate
recognition score.
It adds up the total number of target words that the subject correctly
recognizes plus the total number of distractor words that he/she correctly rejects during
immediate stage.
Finally, VRMDRTC measures delayed recognition.
It adds up the total
number of target words that the subject correctly recognizes plus the total number of distractor
words that he/she correctly rejects during delayed stage.
Both VRMIRTC and VRMDRTC
are selected to detect any possible discrepancies between immediate and delayed recognition
memory.
For SWM, SWMBE has been selected.
It accounts for the number of times the subject
incorrectly revisits a box in which a token has previously been found.
The final score is
calculated across all assessed trials.
2 outcome measures have been selected for the assessment of sustained attention (RVP
test).
RVPA is a sensitivity measure of the participant to the target digits sequence.
It is
a key measurement since it provides a single index of how good a subject is at detecting a
target sequence against distractors. RVPTH is the total number of target sequences that the
participant correctly identified within the allowed window time.
When combining brain imaging and cognition tests it is possible to make conclusions on the
performance of certain cognitive domains. Using single psychological test outcomes, however,
is often not enough to make strong conclusions, since each test measures task-specific effects.
Instead, it is desirable to create composite scores to study cognitive aging.
Selection of
outcome measures and number of constructs of a single composite must be done with care.
There exist several approaches to create composite scores.
In general, it is common to
lump together tests outcomes that are correlated they are likely measuring the same cognitive
process.
A composite score representing visuo-spatial working memory (vsWM) has been
created from SWMBE and PALTEA scores. Both are highly correlated and target for the same
cognitive process. These scores have been z-scored (standardized) and summed into a single
score. Finally, vsWM has been inverted so that a higher value represents a better performance.
3.4
Statistical analyses
Before carrying out any statistical analyses, data has to be analyzed for outliers. A boxplot
method has been used to identify values that deviate from the rest of the group and can be
considered troublesome. Values above Q3+3xIQR or below Q1-3xIQR have been considered
as outliers. Q1 and Q3 stands for the first and third quartile respectively, while IQR is the
interquantile range.
Whenever an outlier is found, there are 3 possibilities to proceed: to keep the analysis with the
outliers, to remove outlier single observations or to take out the whole subject from the analysis.
Arguments can be made for any of those. It is likely that test results do not change independently
==PAGE:39==
30
CHAPTER 3. MATERIALS AND METHODS
on the final decision. If the outlier value is caused by an error in the segmentation of structural
data or an artefact, this could potentially imply that the rest of subfield volumes could also be
incorrect. On the other hand, outlier values could indicate just a deviation from the rest of the
group for that single subfield but all the others might still be fine. It has been decided to take
the whole subject out, since it is the safest method and it is difficult to justify why to trust in
some values and not others for the same participant. The same argument applies to cognitive
and spectroscopy data. Nonetheless, different aspects have to be considered depending on
the nature of data and the type of analysis and they will be further detailed in the corresponding
section.
Multiple data analyses have been done. First, volumetry analysis have focused on investigating
the asymmetry and atrophy of hippocampal subfields for different age groups.
Secondly,
cognitive function have been assessed in relation to age. Thirdly, neurochemical changes with
age on hippocampal region have been studied. Next, cognitive data have been correlated to
volume data to evaluate whether cognitive decline is associated with volume decrease. Finally,
an exploratory analysis has been performed by studying the correlation between metabolite
levels and subfield volumes. MPRAGE-based segmentation results have been used. More
details on each test is given in next subsections.
Statistical analysis were performed in R
programming language [72]. p-value=0.05 is set as statistical significance level.
3.4.1
Asymmetry
An asymmetry index (AI%) has been calculated for each raw subfield volume as [73, 74]:
AI = |VLH −VRH|
VLH + VRH
· 100
(3.7)
where VLH and VRH stand for left and right hemispheres volume respectively. Asymmetry is
expressed as a percentage and it does not provide information on lateralization. A multivariate
ANOVA (MANOVA) using Pillai’s trace with the 12 subfield AIs as dependent variables has been
performed to test the primary hypothesis that volume overall differed across age groups.
Univariate one-way analysis of variances (ANOVA) is a parametric test that allows to compare
the means of two or more independent groups. In the current model, the response variables,
AIs, are continuous. The explanatory variable is categorical with three levels: age group splits
into young, middle and old levels. Twelve tests could have to be performed to test the effect
on every hippocampal subfield. However, conducting multiple one-way ANOVAs increases the
probability to find a significant result, even if there are no real differences between groups. This
is known as Type I error, and it is likely to increase when running multiple univariate comparisons.
In order to control for it, the multivariate extension of ANOVA, MANOVA, can be performed
instead. MANOVA handles multiple continuous dependent variables and treats them as one
multivariate response.
In order to be able to rely on MANOVA results, some data assumptions must be examined. The
presence of univariate outliers must be evaluated as explained before in this report. In total, 4
outliers have been found (1 hippocampal tail, 1 hippocampal fissure, 1 molecular layer and 1
total hippocampus) corresponding to 3 different subjects. These 3 subjects have been excluded
from the asymmetry analysis. Next, multivariate outliers have been analyzed with Mahalanobis
distance with alpha level of 0.001.
No multivariate outliers have been found.
Absence of
multicollinearity has also been tested, as dependent variables should not be too correlated to
each other. In this case, dependent variables observations refer to the same individuals, but to
different brain regions; consequently, multicollinearity has been assumed not to be problematic.
Univariate normality of the residuals has been tested for each subfield with Shapiro-Wilk test.
Results show that only CA2/3, CA4 and GC-DG residuals are normally distributed. Multivariate
==PAGE:40==

normality assumption is not met neither. Lastly, homogeneity of variance or homoscedasticity
can be statistically tested with Levene’s test. Assumption is not met for CA4 and presubiculum,
although p-values are close to significance level. In summary, some of the assumptions for
MANOVA are not met. Nevertheless, both ANOVA and MANOVA are robust to deviations from
assumptions when sample sizes are equal, as in this case. Pillai’s trace test is specially robust
to deviations. In addition, one can still compute MANOVA while accepting a lower level of
statistical significance for the result. Therefore, it has been decided to still compute MANOVA
with Pillai’s trace as test statistic to test the hypothesis.
Secondly, a different MANOVA test has been used to test if asymmetry is significant. MANOVA
has been used with subregion volumes as dependent variables, and a two-level factor indicating
the hemisphere (left or right) as independent variable. One outlier was found for presubiculum
and no multivariate outliers were found. There is univariate normality of the residuals, but not
multivariate normality. Variances are homogeneous for all subfields except for HATA. Therefore,
Pillai’s trace has been used as test statistics.
In cases where the overall multivariate result is significant, post-hoc multiple univariate ANOVA
have been performed to identify the specific subfields that contribute to the significant overall
effect.
p-values have been adjusted by Benjamini-Hochberg method to control the false
discovery rate (FDR), defined as the expected proportion of false discoveries amongst the
rejected hypothesis [75]. Besides, model diagnostics plots have been inspected in every case
to visually assess the deviations from assumptions. Model residuals versus fitted and normal
QQ plot show adequacy of ANOVA for the data, thus numerical deviations from assumptions
previously found can be ignored. Results are shown in Section 4.2.1.
3.4.2
Age group volume differences
Spectroscopy data is only available for the left hemisphere; consequently, from this point on,
analyses are performed on left hippocampal measures. It is of interest for this project to study
how subfield volumes vary across the three age groups. A multivariate analysis of covariance
(MANCOVA) has been used for this purpose.
Similarly to the asymmetry study, subfields
volumes are the dependent variables, while categorical age group is the explanatory variable.
The difference now is the inclusion of gender as a covariate. Covariates are added so that their
effect on the relationship between group variable and dependent variables is eliminated. Prior
to analysis subfield volumes have been normalized by dividing by ICV and multiplying by 1000
as a factor to avoid scaling effects. The aim of this normalization is to take the effect of head
size out.
MANCOVA assumptions have been investigated. Assumptions include those of MANOVA and
additional assumptions of ANCOVA related to covariates. Covariates must be uncorrelated with
the group variable but correlated to the dependent variables. For each subfield, a univariate
ANCOVA has been performed including an interaction term between gender covariate and
group predictor.
Since the interaction term is not statistically significant in any case, this
assumption is met. Regarding the rest of assumptions, 2 subjects have been excluded due
to univariate outliers and no multivariate outliers have been found. Univariate normality of the
residuals for is met for most of the subfields. Multivariate normality test is significant, hence
multivariate normality of residuals assumption is violated. Non-significant values of Levene’s
test indicate very similar variance between groups.
The null hypothesis in MANCOVA is that the adjusted population means of the dependent
variables are equal [76]. If the null hypothesis is rejected, as expected, separate univariate
ANCOVA are performed to study which variables contribute to the overall multivariate
significance. Since there are small violations of some assumptions, model diagnostics has to be

done. If residuals versus fitted values plots do not show any trend and normal QQ-plots do not
show big deviations from normality ANCOVA is reliable. Multiple post-hoc pairwise comparisons
are needed to determine if the adjusted mean difference between specific pairs is statistically
significant. p-values were adjusted with Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control FDR.
3.4.3
Age group cognitive function differences
Several aspects must be considered when dealing with CANTAB data. CANTAB guidelines
recommend the use of parametric statistical tests for the analysis of cognitive measures, but
sometimes data is not suitable for it.
Accurate results depend on the fulfillment of several
assumptions. CANTAB measurements are characterized by floor and ceiling effects that disturb
the normal distribution of the data. This means that many subjects have the same score. For
instance, Figure 3.5 shows the histogram of PALTEA score, where it can be seen that data has
a strong positive skew, probably because many young participants made less mistakes during
the task. Also notice the variation in tasks scores and skew orientation from one test to another.
This is because of the design of the tests itself, while the composite score, vsWM, has been
standardized. Therefore, small deviations may be statistically considered as extreme values.
These deviations do not necessarily mean that the subject misunderstood the task; it may be
due to different individual cognitive performance. It is important not to remove these extreme
values in order not to lose information. Another consequence of highly skewed data is that it
may result in non normally distributed residuals.
Figure 3.5: Histograms of CANTAB cognitive tasks data.
After taking a closer look to the data, no univariate or multivariate outliers have been detected
in spite of the data distribution.
Although raw data is not normally distributed (except for
VRMFRDS), assumption of normal distribution of errors is only violated for RVPA, VRMDRTC
and VRMIRTC, according to Shapiro-Wilk tests of normality. There is homogeneity of variances
according to Levene’s test.
MANOVA with PALTEA, RVPA, RVPTH, VRMDRTC, VRMIRTC, VRMFRDS, vsWM as
dependent variables and age group as independent variable has been performed. The main
hypothesis is that cognitive function declines with age. Gender has not been included as a
covariate in the analysis of cognitive data as there are no between-group differences in test
performance for males and females, and all groups are well matched for sex. Pillai’s trace has
been used as test statistic.

3.4.4
Age group metabolite differences
This analysis focuses on studying the age-related differences for each of the metabolite
concentrations that were reliably quantified. A multivariate ANCOVA with ratio between gray
matter and white matter as a covariate is the desirable test to use.
As mentioned in Section 3.2.3, 2 subjects have been excluded from the analysis due to poor
quality spectra. However, in order to minimize data loss, for each metabolite the group mean
has been imputed. In addition, one outlier has been detected for tCr level and no multivariate
outliers have been found.
Assumption of normality of residuals is met for the 5 studied metabolites as Shapiro-Wilk test is
not significant, although there is not multivariate normality. There is homogeneity of variances
as Levene’s test is not significant. Adding the covariate term makes sense only if there is no
significant interaction between the covariate and the grouping variable. There is homogeneity
of regression slopes as the interaction term between group and GM/WM is not statistically
significant in all univariate models for each metabolite.
Consequently, MANCOVA using Pillai’s trace including Glu, mIns, tCho, tNAA and tCr as
dependent variables has been performed. The ratio between gray matter and white matter
(GM/WM) in the voxel of interest has been included as a covariate. GM/WM can provide some
information on whether the potential metabolite changes occur in the same way in GM and WM
or if these tissues are differently affected by age.
Significant main effects have been further studied by post-hoc ANCOVA and FDR-corrected
pairwise comparisons. Results are shown in Section 4.4.
3.4.5
Subfield volumes correlations with cognitive function
After having tested age-differences it is of interest to find the relationships between the three
different types of data: volumes from segmentation, cognitive data and metabolite levels. First,
the hypothesis that some hippocampal subfield volumes are associated to some cognitive
functions outcomes will be investigated. One way to do it is to find the correlation between
paired samples of data.
The most common type of correlation is Pearson correlation, which is used for continuous,
random data where both variables are normally distributed and it detects linear relationships
between them. Since most of the cognitive outcomes on the study sample are nonnormally
distributed, Spearman’s rank correlation might be used instead to find relevant monotonic
associations. While Pearson correlation finds the strength and direction of the linear relationship
between two variables, Spearman’s rank correlation determines the strength and direction of
the monotonic relationship between two ranked variables [77]. As most cognitive test outcomes
present markedly floor or ceiling effects, it has been decided to use the nonparametric, safest
version of correlation.
Spearman correlation coefficient, ρ, is found from the ranks of the
variables instead of from their actual values. The use of ranked values also makes this test
robust to extreme values, which can be useful given the design of CANTAB tests, where some
participants might get extremely low or high scores.
Partial correlation measures the associations between two variables, as explained in lines
above, whilst controlling for the effect of one or more continuous variables. This specific case of
==PAGE:43==
34

correlation has been used to account and remove the effect of gender and age from the analysis.
The strength of the associations is represented by ρ, which varies from -1 o 1. The closer ρ is
to 0, the weaker the relationship is.
Three different analyses have been run to test the hypothesis that differential relationships
exist between cognitive functions and hippocampal subfield volumes. First, partial Spearman’s
correlations with gender as covariate have been studied. Secondly, age has been added as
second covariate. As tests scores all differed across age groups, it is interesting to take the
effect of age out and to study whether significant associations remain. Since a total of 104 tests
(13x18) have been run for 57 samples, it is convenient to correct for multiple comparisons by
FDR. A different way to take the effect of age out is to perform the tests of partial correlation
separately on the different age groups. This way it is possible to visualize the differences in
associations in the different groups. Therefore, the third analysis consists on finding different
correlations for each age group controlling for the effect of gender. Since the number of samples
is very reduced (only 18-20 in each case), corrected p-values may draw too strict conclusions.
Here, it is more interesting to focus on what the data looks like. A larger sample would be
needed to yield stronger conclusions. After looking at the scattered data it has been decided to
remove one subject as it constantly deviated from the general trend.
Correlations have been tested between all subfield volumes and total hippocampal volume and
all cognitive scores, and p-values have been corrected for multiple comparisons using FDR
method. Tests are one-tailed, testing for a negative correlation between volumes and PALTEA
and SWMBE and a positive correlation between volumes and the rest of tests (RVPA, RVPTH,
VRMDRTC, VRMIRCT, VRMFRDS and vsWM).

age has been added as covariate. Thirdly, different correlations controlling for the effect of
GM/WM have been found for each age group.
Correlations have been tested between all
subfield volumes and total hippocampal volume and all metabolites, and p-values have been

Discussion
The core of this thesis was to implement a pipeline for the segmentation of the hippocampal
region into its substructures, using 7T data acquired previous to this project.
Since scans
have high resolution (0.5mm isotropic) new automated segmentation methods can be used,
but some of their limitations must be noted. In spite of the higher contrast and spatial resolution
on 7T data, some structure edges cannot be seen in vivo. The lack of a standard protocol
results in different segmentation methods applying different rules to derive their morphological
boundaries, especially on the subiculum/CA1 and CA4/GC-DG boundaries. Moreover, most
segmentation protocols lump some structures together, making quantitative comparisons
between studies difficult [78].
Another general drawback of subfields segmentation is that
smaller regions are less reliably delineated. Although performance of the algorithm has not been
directly measured in this project, robustness test gives information on the repeatability. HATA,
parasubiculum, fimbria and HP fissure are the smallest regions included in the segmentation.
Calculation of CV shows that this value is larger for these regions. On the contrary, CV value
for total hippocampal volume is very small, as expected.
Specifically, a potential problem of FreeSurfer tool is that it is based on a probabilistic atlas built
from 7T ex vivo data and 1.5T in vivo data, acquired exclusively from elderly individuals. As
sometimes boundaries cannot be seen in in vivo data, the segmentation will rely on the atlas
instead of on the image intensity. Therefore, segmentation in young adults might be biased
and less reliable [3]. Furthermore, although the atlas is based on 7T structural scans, this
method has not been validated for ultra-high fields. Brown et al. [79] compared the test-retest
reliability of FreeSurfer hippocampal subfield segmentation within and across scanners on 3T
data, and Mueller et al. [80] compared different segmentation techniques in 3T ADNI2 dataset.
However, to the best of the author’s knowledge, there is not any study comparing the Freesurfer
segmentation outcome coming from different scanners, acquisition sequences or even compare
it with other automated methods in 7T. In addition, the whole process of pre-processing structural
images, recon-all routine, manual editing, subfields segmentation and evaluation of the results
may take up to 30 hours. Although this is a significant improvement with respect to manual
segmentation, the time it takes to segment a single volume is still high.
In spite of all these concerns about automated algorithms, test-retest reliability analysis showed
that FreeSurfer v6.0 results are reliable for this data sample: CV values are small and average of
the paired differences is non significantly different from zero. It also revealed that there is not any
advantage of using an additional FLAIR scan to perform segmentation. According to FreeSurfer
segmentation authors, multispectral segmentation can provide more reliable results, particularly
when additional scan resolution is higher than main scan resolution [3].
However, spatial
resolution of our FLAIR is lower, which is likely to be the reason for the worse performance.
To sum up, the robustness study indicates that the FreeSurfer algorithm is repeatable and that
MPRAGE-based segmentation slightly outperforms multispectral segmentation for the given
data. Addition of optimized T2-weighted scans could improve the performance even more.
FreeSurfer also estimates ICV as part of the recon-all routine and it has been used for head
size normalization. This estimate does not come from segmentation, but from the alignment
of input MR volume to the MNI305 brain atlas. This has been criticized as the alignment of
images not only includes the skull, but also other head tissues. This means that ICV estimate
might be biased by the total brain volume, which atrophies with age, and consequently it
would not be a reliable estimate of head size [81, 65]. Besides, there exist different methods
47
==PAGE:57==
48
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
to correct for the head size assuming ICV estimation is reliable. In this project, proportion
method has been selected. Other popular choices when working with regional segmentation of
brain tissue include the use of ICV as covariate or raw volumes normalization through residual
methods. After literature review, it has been seen that there is not standard choice for neither
ICV estimation or ICV correction method. All of them (and many others) are commonly used,
but there is a chance that it affects the results and it is worthy to, at least, mention the different
possibilities.
Hippocampal subfield volumes from segmentation have been used to test asymmetry.
Statistical testing showed that asymmetry does not significantly increase with age, and that there
is not overall right-left significant asymmetry for any subfield except for CA2/3. Right hemisphere
CA2/3 is significantly bigger than the left CA2/3. By looking at the asymmetry percentages by
age groups in Table A.1 it can be noted that HP fissure, fimbria, parasubiculum and HATA have
slightly higher asymmetry indices than the rest of the subfields. Since these are the smallest
regions and their segmentation may be less reliable it is expected that AI is higher, as it has been
noted previously in this section. Visual inspection of the data reveals that right volume is slightly
bigger than left volume in most of the regions (see Figure A.2), but statistical testing concludes
that these differences are not significant. Significant in vivo laterality of the hippocampus in
healthy adults have been reported in the literature, with right hemisphere hippocampus being
bigger [74, 82]. It has also been suggested that the asymmetry of the whole hippocampus and
also of its subregions increases in presence of some pathologies [73]. However, literature on
hemispheric asymmetry on hippocampal subfields is not conclusive. As in this project, some
previous studies did not find any evidence of hemispheric subfields asymmetry and age [21, 20].
The primary hypothesis when testing for age-related volume differences was that hippocampal
subfields are not uniformly affected in healthy aging. Study of hippocampal subfield volumes
changes within age groups show that all left hemisphere subfields atrophy with age except
for subiculum and HP fissure. Total hippocampus, included only in univariate analyses, is also
significantly affected by age for this study sample. Results suggest that hippocampal fissure and
subiculum volumes remains closely to constant across lifespan. These results are in line with
several previous studies on healthy volunteers [21, 83, 20]. According to this study, subiculum
volume remains stable across age, but other studies have reported age-related differences
of subiculum volume. Disagreement may be due to the definition of subiculum itself: in this
study, subiculum is analyzed separately from pre- and para-subiculum. In other publications,
the whole subicular complex is referred to as subiculum. The lack of standard definition of labels
also complicates comparison of CA regions with other studies: often, CA4 and DG are reported
as a composite region, as well as CA1 and CA2. Zheng et al. [83] did not find age differences in
parasubiculum, in spite of using the same segmentation protocol. Their study sample is larger
and the methodology is different, which can partially explain the differences in results. Moreover,
parasubiculum is a small region, and results must be carefully interpreted.
It has been found that volume differences between young and middle adults are non-significant.
This might suggest that relationships between volumes and age is non-linear and/or volume
drops only from certain age, in line with other studies [21, 20]. In fact, a potential disadvantage
of testing groups instead of continuous variables is that the type of relationship between two
variables is not known. However, the three age groups were chosen this way to sample levels
at early, middle and later points on adults life with a reasonable range of ages within each
group. Interestingly, according to ANCOVA results, left CA2/3 atrophies with age while right
CA2/3 volume is unaffected by age. Left CA2/3 reaches significance level whilst right CA2/3
does not.
Regarding cognitive tasks, there exist many different automated cognitive batteries, as
==PAGE:58==
49
explained in Section 2.2.
The choice of the specific testing set should not interfere
with the results as long as the tests target the same cognitive domain.
However, other
neuropsychological tests batteries would be better suited for healthy ageing studies and detect
higher variability in younger adults. In this project, a composite score targeting visuo-spatial
working memory and individual test outcomes targeting spatial and verbal memory and
sustained attention have been used. The choice of individual test outcomes has been motivated
by the type of assessments found in previous literature [82, 84, 83, 85].
However, other
studies lump together several cognitive tasks into a composite episodic memory score or a
fluid cognitive function score [86].
Structural equation modelling is often used to find the
latent variable model that best fits for each data sample. Even though there exist different
methodologies, comparison between different studies should be possible.
Cognitive decline across lifespan has been widely studied, and results on age group differences
for different cognitive tasks are not surprising.
Sustained attention, episodic memory and
visuo-spatial working memory comprise cognitive domains that decline with age [18]. Results
from cognitive testing in this study sample agree. Differences in performance on young and
middle adults are only significant for VRMFRDS and vsWM. As in the case of subfield volumes,
this suggests that cognitive impairments become more evident from certain age. Concerning
data distribution, floor/ceiling effects have been observed. This is a limitation of cognitive test
batteries often present when used in healthy ageing adults. These test are commonly used
to provide differential outcomes in healthy controls and diseased people or to undergo studies
in elderly people, whose cognitive performance is expected to deteriorate. Due to the nature
of the study sample and of some of the tests, less variability in young and/or middle adults
results is observed in some cases. It is intrinsic to healthy ageing studies but there is sufficient
variability in the sample so that these cognitive tests can be used for further analyses. Since
this is a cross-sectional study, individual differences are not taken into account. In addition,
there exist several mechanism that seem to promote successful cognitive aging, such as such
as physical exercise, extensive cognitive interventions and intellectually stimulating lifestyle that
could reduce the impairment of cognitive functions [87]. More specific to hippocampal subfields,
benefits include the enhancement of neurogenesis, which takes place in the DG. Hence this
region may particularly benefit from promotion of healthy aging.
Most of the hippocampal subfields are structurally affected by age and cognitive impairments
are also associated with healthy aging. The primary hypothesis when testing volumes against
cognitive tasks was that different subfields are associated with different functional outcomes.
Spearman’s correlation results controlling for the effect of gender show some significant
correlations between normalized hippocampal subfield volumes and some cognitive functions.
Positive monotonic associations are present between volumes and all cognitive tests except for
PALTEA and SWMBE. Both scores account for the number of errors hence they increase as
we age. In general, less scattered data and stronger linear tendency is observed for verbal
immediate free recall (VRMFDRS) associations.
In fact, this is the test that is significantly
correlated to volumes more often. This can be partially due to the normal distribution of the
test data, opposite to non-normal distribution of the rest of tests, and lack of floor/ceiling effects.
Subiculum volume positively correlated to verbal delayed recognition memory and to immediate
free recall for this data sample. Previous literature has reported the association to delayed
verbal memory: Zammit et al. [84] found that subiculum volume is associated with visual and
verbal delayed recall (episodic memory) in old adults using the same software for hippocampal
subfields segmentation. This project’s results show that subiculum is not strongly associated
to visual memory, but contrary to [84], the sample comprises adults of a wider age span. It
has been found that CA1 volume is only correlated to verbal immediate free recall, while CA2/3
volume is associated to visual memory and to visuo-spatial working memory. CA4 is associated
==PAGE:59==

CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
to visual and verbal episodic memory, visuo-spatial working memory and immediate verbal
memory. CA1 has often been associated with memory retrieval, as it is an output structure,
while structures like CA2-4 and DG are more related to encoding and learning, as they are
input structures [82, 84]. Therefore, the opposite findings regarding CA1 and CA2-4 regions
would have been expected. However, other studies have found associations of CA1 volume
with immediate verbal recall when using FreeSurfer software for segmentation [88], similarly
to this study. Furthermore, the same studies also found strong correlations of CA2/3 and CA4
subfield volumes with both immediate and delayed verbal memory [82, 88]. This indicates that
further study on these regions is still needed. This project results’ show that GC-DG volume
significantly correlates to all cognitive tasks that have been studied. CG-DG is strongly affected
by age, as it is involved in neurogenesis process, therefore these associations are not surprising.
They are also in agreement with previous literature where DG volume has been related to
verbal and visuo-spatial memory [85, 88]. Presubiculum volume correlates to verbal immediate
free recall and with visuo-spatial working memory, while parasubiculum volume significantly
correlates to visual episodic memory. Although there is not much literature available on these
subfields in relation to cognition, a previous study has shown that parasubiculum volume did
not correlate with neither immediate nor delayed verbal recall [83]. This group used FreeSurfer
for segmentation and a study sample with similar age to the one in this study, but they did not
find significant shrinkage of parasubiculum with age.
In order to understand the role of age in these associations, same statistical tests have been
repeated including age as covariate.
When controlling for the effect of age and correcting
p-values for multiple comparisons, no significant correlations are found. When taking the effect
of age out, smaller volumes are not strongly associated with lower tasks scores, especially
in the young group. Therefore, it is the negative correlation between volumes and age and
between cognition and age what drives the positive associations between volumes and cognitive
performance. This suggests that ”bigger is better” is an oversimplification when it comes to the
relationship between hippocampal volumes and cognitive functions. Instead, it seems that loss
of tissue may lead to a decline in function only in older adults [89]. In fact, many of the papers
published on healthy ageing and hippocampal subfields use a larger sample size consisting
of middle age and older adults [88, 84]. This is the reason different tests have been done for
different age groups. In this study, less than 20 old adults have been included. It seems that
correlations between hippocampal volumes and memory become increasingly positive as the
age of the sample increases, but due to the limited sample size it is not possible to prove it from
this study.
Several metabolites from different brain areas were reliably quantified from this MRS data and
and have been investigated in [6]. These were chosen based on a CRLB lower than 20%.
However, CRLB might not be a reliable measure of data quality, according to [90], as high levels
of CRLB are also expected in cases of low metabolite concentrations. For this project, 5 of
these metabolites corresponding to the left hippocampus area have been analyzed and related
to structural differences. Assuming that CRLB is an unbiased estimator, age-group statistical
analysis reveals that Glu metabolite levels remain constant across lifespan, while the rest of
examined metabolites presented age differences. mIns, Cho, tNAA and tCr levels increased
with age. tNAA only presents differences between middle and old adults group. According to
previous literature, tNAA concentration decreases in hippocampus over age [90, 91], contrary
to the results found for this data sample. This most likely implies a decrease in neuronal density
and neuronal dysfunction associated with tissue loss. However, Cleeland et al. [90] measured
NAA or NAA ratios instead of tNAA which could be the cause of the difference in results. mIns
concentration is strongly affected by age, with significant differences between young and middle
adults. Since mIns is exclusively found in glial cells an increase in its concentration could mean
==PAGE:60==
51
an increase in glial cell size and activity in hippocampus [91]. Cho and tCr show differences
of young and middle adults with old group. This agrees with previous literature on absolute
concentrations that suggest different patterns of metabolic changes across lifespan [91, 92].
It must be noted that comparison with results reported in previous literature is difficult, since
neurochemical changes are often reported as ratios or several metabolites are reported together
as they are not distinguishable using lower magnetic fields or short repetition time sequences.
In addition, GM/WM/CSF distribution is highly dependent on the individual’s anatomy. These
results had already been reported by Lind et al. [6], who acquired the data this project is based
on, as part of another research study. Since these data come from 7T scanner it is thought to
shed a light on the spectroscopy field, where disagreements in ageing-sensitive regions such
as the hippocampus are present.
Lastly, analysis of hippocampal subfield volumes in relation to spectroscopy data has been
done. This analysis is exploratory: there is not previous literature relating metabolite levels
from the hippocampal area with each of the structures of the hippocampus. Firstly, GM/WM
and gender have been included as covariates. As age effect is not taken into account and
Glu and tNAA are the less affected metabolites by age it is expected that none of the subfield
volumes is significantly correlated to any of these metabolite levels. Negative associations of
mIns and tCr with most of the subfield volumes are also expected, as concentration increases
as we age. When age is added as covariate none of the correlations is statistically significant
anymore. Therefore, similarly to the cognitive function case, age may seem to be mediating
these relationships.
One of the limitations in this project is the study sample size. A greater number of middle age
and older subjects would be necessary to make stronger conclusions on differential associations
of subfield volumes with other in vivo data. Secondly, this is a cross-sectional study and it
might not reflect individual characteristics accurately. Concerning correlation analyses between
hippocampal subfield volumes and cognitive tasks and metabolites, it should be noted that an
observed association does not assure that the relationship between two variables is causal.
Therefore it would be convenient to perform other kind of analyses relating these three type of
data available.

6
Conclusion and future work
The aim of this master thesis was to reliably obtain the hippocampal subfield volumes from
automated segmentation of 7T scans as well as to investigate the relations between subfields
volumes, cognitive performance and neurochemical metabolites on the hippocampus across
lifespan.
During this project, a robust segmentation pipeline has been developed for 7T structural
data, including data pre-processing, error testing and repeatability analysis. Optimized bias
field correction and denoising of structural volumes preceded automated segmentation with
FreeSurfer v6.0. Bland-Altman plots and CV values showed that MPRAGE-based segmentation
slightly outperforms multispectral-segmentation for the given data, and therefore the first
method has been used for further analyses. Hippocampal subfield and surrounding structures
(HATA, fimbria, molecular layer and hippocampal fissure) volumes have been obtained for both
left and right hemispheres on 59 healthy adults divided in 3 different age groups. No asymmetry
has been found across age groups except for CA2/3 structure. Statistical analysis of age-group
differences suggests accelerated shrinkage with advancing age, except for subiculum and
hippocampal fissure volumes. CANTAB tests targeting long-term visual memory, immediate
and delayed verbal memory, visuo-spatial working memory and sustained attention have been
studied as they are related to hippocampal function.
All of them significantly decline with
age and they have been related to hippocampal subfield volumes through non-parametric
testing.
Differential associations have been found, but these relationships are no longer
significant when controlling for the effect of age.
In addition, absolute concentrations of 5
neurometabolites coming from 7T-1H-MRS on a hippocampal region of interest data have been
analyzed. mIns, tCho, tCr and tNAA concentrations increase with age while Glu concentration
remains more or less constant.
Associations between neurochemical concentrations and
hippocampal subfield volumes have been studied in an exploratory analysis.
It reveals
significant negative correlations between some glial metabolites that change across lifespan
and some subfields that shrink across lifespan. Actually, when taking the effect of age into
account these associations do not hold anymore.
Future lines of study may include further investigation on the causality of cognitive performance.
For instance, inflammation marker measurements may be underlying structural differences
or decline in cognitive performance. Besides, segmentation of parahippocampal regions and
delineation of subfields in posterior hippocampus may provide a better insight of the functional
structure of hippocampus and how they are interconnected. Lastly, there is an increasing trend
to use longitudinal studies to take into account individual differences. It could be of interest to
design a new study with longitudinal data and compare the results with the available literature
on hippocampal subfields.
