Abstract
The Danish food-based dietary guidelines (FBDG) advise the Danish population to increase
consumption of vegetables while decreasing consumption of red meat, e.g. by substituting
red meat by pulses. However, quantitative estimates of the health impact of such a substitu-
tion is lacking. Therefore, the aim of this thesis was to investigate the overall health impact
of substituting red meat by pulses in the Danish diet by comparing the change in disease
burden attributable to nutritional risk factors and chemical exposures.
The health impact of substituting red meat by pulses was modelled including the following
health outcomes colorectal cancer (CRC), diabetes, ischaemic heart disease (IHD), mega-
loblastic anaemia (MBA), iron deﬁciency anaemia (IDA), intellectual disability (IQD), and
chronic kidney disease (CKD) in the terms of Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALY), based
on national consumption data and food monitoring. Four substitution scenarios were de-
ﬁned: 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% and compared with current dietary patterns. In addition,
the health impact on the four sub-populations men, women, small children, and children and
adolescents was estimated.
An overall beneﬁcial health impact was estimated for substituting red meat by pulses with an
increasing beneﬁt with increased substitution. Up to 118 (95% UI 85; 148) DALYs/100,000
could be averted yearly by a 100% substitution. A decreased burden of IHD due to increased
pulse consumption was estimated to be the largest contributor to the health beneﬁt with up
to 44 (95% UI 17; 69) DALYs/100,000 averted. However, the substitution would also increase
the disease burden of IQD due to increased lead exposure in children with up to 1.7 (95% UI
0.3; 3.5) DALYs/100,000. For sub-populations, men were estimated to experience the largest
beneﬁcial health impact of substituting red meat by pulses with up to 179 (95% UI: 134 ; 223)
averted DALYs/100,000. In contrast, small children were estimated to experience a negative
health impact with an increase in burden of up to 56 (95% UI: 10 ; 120) DALYs/100,000.
This study estimated an overall health gain of substituting red meat by pulses in the Danish
population. These results highlight the importance of diﬀerentiating between diﬀerent sub-
populations in FBDG for the Danish population.

Background
The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) estimated, based on the year 2017, that dietary risks
worldwide cause more than 10 million deaths [1]. Food based dietary guidelines (FBDG) is
a tool used by national authorities to guide the population towards a healthier lifestyle. In
Denmark 10 FBDG are set by the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration [2]. To follow
these guidelines, some food products have a recommended upper intake, such as red meat,
saturated fat, and sugar, while others have a recommended lower intake, such as fruits and
vegetables. Consuming less of a speciﬁc food is expected to increase consumption of another
food [3]. Therefore, substitutions of foods in the diet may be relevant to investigate. One of
the speciﬁc Danish FBDG is to choose lean meat and reduce the red meat consumption to
less than 500 grams (g) a week. Several substitutes are suggested, one of them being pulses,
the dried edible seeds of the leguminous plant, including beans, lentils, and peas [2]. The
Nordic Nutrition Recommendations (NNR) (2012) also recommends an increased consump-
tion of pulses while decreasing the consumption of red meat [4].
Diet trends may aﬀect consumption of diﬀerent foods in the diet. One of the main trends
at the moment is the sustainable diet. Animal-derived food products are known to have a
larger environmental impact than most plant-based foods [5]. Therefore, following a sus-
tainable diet often results in decreased or no consumption of animal-derived products, while
increasing the consumption of plant-based products. Among other dietary trends vegetari-
anism and veganism are increasing in popularity, where vegetarians avoid meat, poultry, and
seafood, and vegans refrain from any products derived from animals including eggs, dairy,
and gelatin [6].
Foods and diets are known to have a high level of complexity consisting of many components,
some foods may cause an adverse health eﬀect while others may cause a beneﬁcial health
eﬀect. These health eﬀects may be directly associated with the food in question or be asso-
ciated to speciﬁc food components, e.g. chemical contaminants or nutritional components.
To evaluate foods as a whole, considering both adverse and beneﬁcial health outcomes, the
risk-beneﬁt assessment (RBA) was developed [7]. RBA can be used to investigate the overall
health impact of food components, foods, and food substitutions.

Aim and Objective
The aim of this Master’s thesis is to investigate the overall health impact of substituting red
meat by pulses in the Danish diet by comparing the change in disease burden attributable to
nutritional risk factors and chemical exposures. This is done by quantifying the adverse and
beneﬁcial health eﬀects of both red meat and pulses by using the health metric Disability-
Adjusted Life Years (DALY).
The objectives are stated as the following research questions:
• What are the beneﬁcial and adverse health eﬀects, both toxicological and nutritional,
associated with consumption of red meat and pulses?
• What is the overall health impact of substituting red meat by pulses in the Danish
population?
Research study design and scenarios
A literature review of the health eﬀects of red meat and pulses was used to obtain the aim
and objective of this Master’s thesis. This was done together with model development of the
burden expressed in DALYs using the statistical software R. The variability and uncertainty
were incorporated using Monte Carlo techniques. Diﬀerent scenarios were investigated. The
following population scenarios were evaluated:
1. Red meat substituted by pulses in the Danish population
2. Red meat substituted by pulses in the Danish female population
3. Red meat substituted by pulses in the Danish male population
4. Red meat substituted by pulses in the Danish population below the age of 6
5. Red meat substituted by pulses in the Danish population between the age of 6 and 18
For each of the population scenarios (1 to 5) the following substitution scenarios was evalu-
ated:
(a) 25% of the consumed red meat substituted by pulses
(b) 50% of the consumed red meat substituted by pulses
(c) 75% of the consumed red meat substituted by pulses
(d) 100% of the consumed red meat substituted by pulses

Introduction
3.1
Risk-Beneﬁt Assessment
Foods and diets have a high level of complexity consisting of many components, both nu-
tritional, chemical, and microbiological. Due to the complexity of foods, consumption of
most foods can cause both adverse and beneﬁcial health eﬀects [7]. In previous times, the
assessment of risks and beneﬁts was separated into two disciplines, risk assessment and ben-
eﬁt assessment. These two disciplines were typically conducted based on diﬀerent methods.
However, within the past decades the two disciplines have been connected, in the discipline
of risk-beneﬁt assessment (RBA) [8]. The RBA is integrating the scientiﬁc disciplines of
toxicology, nutrition, and epidemiology. These three disciplines are applied for the diﬀerent
competencies. Toxicology is applied for investigation of the acute and chronic adverse health
eﬀects, nutrition is applied for investigation of the risk caused by incorrect nutrient intake,
and epidemiology is applied for investigation of the association between human food intake
and adverse or beneﬁcial health eﬀects [8]. By integrating diﬀerent scientiﬁc disciplines, con-
fusion of the terminology may arise [9]. The terminology used in this thesis is described in
box 1.
Box 1. RBA terminology
Food component. A nutrient, chemical, or microbiological agent present in foods [8].
Adverse health eﬀect. A change in morphology, physiology, growth, development, or life span
of an organism, which results in impairment of functional capacity or impairment of capacity to
compensate for additional stress or increase in susceptibility to the harmful eﬀects of environmental
inﬂuences [10].
Beneﬁcial health eﬀect. The opposite of an adverse health eﬀect. It may increase quality of life,
counteracting an adverse health eﬀect, or reduce the probability of loss of life [8].
Hazard. A biological, chemical, or physical agent in, or condition of, food with the potential to
cause an adverse health eﬀect [11].
Beneﬁcial component. The beneﬁcial counterpart to a hazard [11].
Risk. A function of the probability and severity of an adverse health eﬀect occurring to humans due
to exposure or lack of exposure [8].
Beneﬁt. A function of the probability and severity of a beneﬁcial health eﬀect occurring to humans
due to exposure or lack of exposure [8].
Health impact.
The combined health consequences of the risks and beneﬁts studied in intake
scenarios expressed using a health metric [12].
RBA is mainly based on the risk assessment approach, including the following four steps:
hazard identiﬁcation, hazard characterisation, exposure assessment, and risk characterisation.
In RBA, a parallel process for the beneﬁcial health eﬀect is also conducted and the risks and
beneﬁts are then either qualitatively compared or integrated at the ﬁnal step (ﬁgure 1). This
tool can be applied on both food items such as red meat and pulses, single food components

3.1.1
Variability and Uncertainty
Variability and uncertainty should be distinguished in RBA [13]. Uncertainty is relating
to the lack of knowledge and can be reduced by more or better data [14]. Variability is
relating to the natural variation in a population, thus can not be reduced [13]. The exposure
assessment is an example where variability between individuals is shown. Variability may also
be expressed as variation between sex and age groups. Uncertainty is introduced by all model
parameters, however, not always quantiﬁed. It is important to describe the uncertainties
introduced, both the quantiﬁed and unquantiﬁed [14].
One way to keep variability and uncertainty separate is by using two-dimensional Monte
Carlo simulations (ﬁgure 3). In the two-dimensional Monte Carlo simulations, the variability
and uncertainty are kept as separate vectors.
Variability is kept in column vectors and
uncertainty in row vectors. This is then integrated through the RBA model as a matrix with
the column length of the variability and the row length of the uncertainty, thereby creating
the variability dimension and the uncertainty dimension. The output of the stochastic model
incorporating both variability and uncertainty is created by taking the mean of the variability
dimension and the 95% CI of the uncertainty dimension [13].

3.1.2
Problem Formulation
The problem formulation is where the risk-beneﬁt question is deﬁned. This decision is often
based upon food policies or trends in the population [12]. It could also be based on FBDG
to investigate the overall health impact of following or not following the FBDG. The studied
population, scenarios, and level of aggregation of the RBA should be considered during the
problem formulation. The studied population could be based on country boundaries, sen-
sitive groups, or sub-populations. Sensitive groups or sub-populations are typically chosen
where the health outcome is aﬀecting a speciﬁc part of the population (e.g. lowered IQ in
children) or where a sub-population is known to have a speciﬁc eating pattern (e.g. vegetari-
ans). Alternative scenarios are used to investigate the impact of a change in intake compared
to either the reference scenario or a zero intake scenario. Alternative scenarios can be deﬁned
as substitution scenarios or population scenarios. Alternative substitution scenarios are a big
part of food substitution studies. It can be deﬁned as an intake to reach a speciﬁc amount
speciﬁed in a FBDG or a fraction of the food substituted. Also, the type of substitution
model should be speciﬁed.
This can be an iso-caloric substitution, by portion size, or a
gram to gram substitution. The level of aggregation of the RBA should also be considered,
referring to the focus of the study, i.e. whether the risk-beneﬁt question is concerning food
components, foods, whole diets, or food substitutions [8, 12].
For this thesis, the risk-beneﬁt question is based on the FBDG number 5: Choose lean meat
and lean cold meats, and the FBDG number 2: Eat fruit and many vegetables [2]. In the
FBDG number 5, it is stated that a maximum of 500 g of red meat should be consumed in
a week, including processed red meat, corresponding to two or three dinners and some cold
cuts a week. The suggested substitutes are poultry, ﬁsh, eggs, legumes, and pulses, leading
to the risk-beneﬁt question of substituting red meat by pulses, excluding processed red meat.
Red meat is deﬁned according to the World Cancer Research Fund (WCRF) as unprocessed
mammalian muscle meat. This includes beef, veal, pork, lamb, mutton, horse, and goat
[15]. For this thesis, pulses are deﬁned according to WCRF as the dried edible seeds of the
leguminous plant, including beans, lentils, and peas [16]. This is also in agreement with
the deﬁnition made by the Food and Agricultural Organization (FAO) as leguminous crops
harvested solely for the dry grain, thereby excluding crops harvested green for food, soybeans
and groundnuts used for oil extractions, and leguminous crops used for sowing purposes [17].

3.1.3
Identiﬁcation of Health Eﬀects
The identiﬁcation of adverse and beneﬁcial health eﬀects is a crucial step in the RBA. As the
adverse and beneﬁcial health eﬀects for the RBA are selected here. First of all, to conduct
an RBA at least one adverse and one beneﬁcial health eﬀect has to be selected [12]. How-
ever, only health eﬀects with substantial evidence should be included in RBAs. Such health
eﬀects can be identiﬁed through a literature review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses,
also reports conducted by governmental agencies and non-governmental organisations can be
investigated.
Substantial evidence can be deﬁned through diﬀerent schemes. One such scheme is the one
set by the World Cancer Research Fund (WCRF) (2018), grading evidence as convincing,
probable, limited - suggestive, or limited - no conclusion [18]. Health outcomes with evidence
of convincing and probable are normally the only health outcomes included in RBAs [8]. The
Nordic Nutrition Recommendation (NNR) has evaluated diﬀerent nutrients and the poten-
tial health eﬀects caused by toxic amounts or deﬁciencies [4]. The NNR is grading evidence
accordingly to the grading scheme deﬁned by the WCRF, as convincing, probable, limited -
suggestive, and limited - no conclusion [4]. As for the WCRF, convincing and probable is
needed for inclusion in RBAs. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has also evalu-
ated diﬀerent nutrients and contaminants for the potential health eﬀects based on available
evidence [19]. In addition, burden of disease (BoD) studies may be investigated, to check for
inclusion of relevant health outcomes. Good quality studies should be used for establishing
the evidence. Good quality randomised controlled trials (RCT) studies or human observa-
tional studies are typically needed for a high level of evidence [4].
3.1.4
Exposure Assessment
After the identiﬁcation of adverse and beneﬁcial health eﬀects, the characterisation of health
eﬀects and the exposure assessment are performed simultaneously [12]. These two steps are
somewhat linked. The dose-response relationship (DR) established in the characterisation
of the health eﬀects is relating exposure of a food or food component to a speciﬁc health
outcome. This relation may be aﬀected by external factors (e.g. conversion factors) or in-
ternal factors (e.g. absorbed doses and biomarkers). Therefore, the exposure data should be
translated into the same measure as the DR.
The exposure assessment is incorporating consumption data of the given food with concen-
tration data of a given food component in the food, e.g. consumption data of red meat and
pulses, and concentration data of nutrients or contaminants in these foods. Consumption
data can be obtained from dietary surveys, where concentration data is usually obtained from
food monitoring [20]. Background exposure of the food component should also be considered,
Page 7 of 96
==PAGE:19==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology
DTU
where the food component is present in other dietary sources [21].
Diﬀerent types of exposure methods are applied for acute and chronic health eﬀects. Acute
health eﬀects can be caused by microbiological hazards, natural toxins, or high amounts
(poisoning) of nutrients or contaminants. For acute health eﬀects, the exposure to the given
component in one day or one serving is estimated [12]. In contrast, for chronic health eﬀects
the long term mean intake is estimated. Chronic health eﬀects can be caused by ingestion
of foods, deﬁciencies of nutrients, or long-term consumption of contaminants, and may be
measured as an increased or decreased risk [8]. For this thesis, only chronic health eﬀects
are considered. Dietary intake can be expressed as the mean intake of a population or sub-
population, but can also be expressed per individual and thereby include variation for further
estimation. Dietary surveys are typically reported for several days, e.g. for seven days. The
individual long term intake is then estimated as the mean intake over the number of reporting
days [22].
3.1.5
Characterisation of Health Eﬀects
The step of characterising the adverse and beneﬁcial health eﬀects are, as mentioned pre-
viously, done simultaneously with the exposure assessment. This step is where the DR is
established, relating the human exposure of a food or food component to a speciﬁc health
outcome considering severity [23].
Dependent on the data available for the RBA, diﬀerent approaches can be applied. The ex-
posure of a food component can be compared with Health Based Guidance Values (HBGV)
or Dietary Reference Values (DRV). HBGVs are usually set for contaminants to a level at
which no adverse eﬀect is observed (NOAEL). Contaminants are only associated with risks,
not beneﬁts. For nutrients, however, it is not as simple. Nutrients is often considered to
exert a dual-risk, taking into account both the risk of deﬁciency and the risk of toxicity.
The exposure where no risk is observed is typically expressed as the window of beneﬁt. The
risk of deﬁciency is typically established from human requirements for this nutrient as lower
intake levels (LL), whereas the risk of toxicity and upper intake level (UL) is established from
NOAEL studies [8].
Human observational studies can describe the exposure of a given food or food component
with a health outcome by an association measure, such as a relative risk (RR). The RR
describes the probability of occurrence of the disease in the exposed population compared
to the non-exposed. The RR is usually describing an increased or decreased risk of a health
outcome per amount of the food, e.g. an average portion size. This can be extrapolated to
describe a RR per gram consumption of the food or food component, based on the standard
assumption that the relation is log-normally distributed [24].

3.1.6
Risk and Beneﬁt Characterisation
In this step, the probability of occurrence of the adverse eﬀect is integrated with the severity
of that speciﬁc health eﬀect. Beneﬁcial health eﬀects are typically shown as a reduction in
risk of an adverse health eﬀect. This is done by incorporating the exposure with the DR for
the given health eﬀect (ﬁgure 1). The aim of this step is to investigate the change in burden
for the alternative substitution scenarios. The change may be expressed in a health metric,
e.g. a common metric or a composite metric, dependent on the risk-beneﬁt question.
The common metric describes a single dimension of the health outcome, such as incidence
(new annual cases), prevalence (total annual cases), or mortality (annual deaths) [8]. The
common metric is limited to express the dimensions mentioned individually. However, it may
be diﬃcult to compare diﬀerent health outcomes in a single dimension, such as the mortality
or incidence of cancer and IQ disability.
For the composite metric, diﬀerent dimensions are integrated in one metric. By including
both incidence and mortality in one metric, diﬀerent health outcomes may be compared
with less diﬃculties. Using the composite metric more information is needed for estimation,
thereby making it more complex to conduct the RBA [8, 12]. Diﬀerent composite metrics
have been developed for diﬀerent scientiﬁc areas, such as the Willingness to pay, Quality-
Adjusted Life Years (QALY), and the Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALY). For RBA
studies, the composite metric of DALY is the most commonly used. This is also the metric
used for estimates in the GBD study [25] and by the World Health Organization (WHO) [26].
3.1.6.1
Incidence and Prevalence Approach
The number of cases of a disease may be expressed as the prevalence or the incidence of the
disease in the population. The incidence reﬂects the number of new cases per year, where
the prevalence reﬂects the number of total cases in the population at a given point of time.
For the incidence approach, an exposure is investigated for the future health loss connected
with all new cases in the given year due to this exposure, i.e. all health loss due to new
cases in the given year. The incidence approach is reﬂecting the future burden resulting from
current disease. For the prevalence approach, all cases at a given time are investigated, i.e.
the annual health loss of all cases at the given time. The prevalence approach is reﬂecting the
current burden resulting from previous acquired diseases [26, 27]. Since the two approaches
are reﬂecting a burden at diﬀerent time periods, it should be considered to use the same
approach when possible.
For this thesis, the incidence approach was used unless stated
otherwise.

3.1.6.2
Top-Down and Bottom-Up Approach
The change in incidence (or prevalence) and mortality due to a change in food consumption
can be estimated from two diﬀerent approaches: the top-down or the bottom-up approach
[9]. The top-down approach takes its starting point in current incidence or mortality of a
given health outcome in the population. This is combined with DR, typically obtained from
epidemiological studies. The change in incidence or mortality due to the change in food
consumption may be estimated as an impact fraction (e.g. the potential impact fraction
(PIF) further elaborated in section 4.3.2), and is typically expressed as the annual health
impact [9]. The bottom-up approach takes its starting point in the food or food component
and is also called the risk assessment approach. The consumption data is combined with
DR, typically obtained from animal studies, to estimate the incidence or mortality of a given
health outcome from a given exposure [9, 28]. The top-down and the bottom-up approach can
be used parallel in an RBA for diﬀerent health outcomes. However, diﬀerent uncertainties
are introduced from the two approaches and should be considered in the comparison of the
health eﬀects [9].
3.1.6.3
Disability-Adjusted Life Years
The DALY is a composite metric which compares the given state of health with the ideal
state of health. One DALY is equal to one healthy life year lost and is thereby measuring
the loss of health due to morbidity and mortality. Since it includes both morbidity and
mortality the DALY allows comparison of diﬀerent health outcomes. The DALY integrates
incidence of disease with the age of onset, duration of disease, mortality, life expectancy, and
severity as measured by disability weights (dw). Further description of the DALY is found in
Devleesschauwer et al. (2014) [29]. In addition, the technical basis for the DALY is described
by Murray (1994) [30].
The DALY is composed of two components, the Years Lived with Disability (YLD) and the
Years of Life Lost (YLL) due to premature death:
DALY = Y LD + Y LL
(1)
The YLD quantiﬁes the burden of living with a given health outcome, the morbidity, esti-
mated from the annual number of cases (AC) in a given population, the duration (D) of the
disease until remission or death, and the dw. The YLD is estimated from the following:
Y LD = AC · D · dw
(2)
The YLL quantiﬁes the burden of premature death.
This is estimated from the annual
number of deaths (AD) and the remaining life expectancy (LE) from the following:
Y LL = AD · LE
(3)

Exposure Assessment
Consumption and concentration data was required for estimation of the exposure to the food
components vitamin B12, iron, zinc, selenium, cadmium, and lead in the Danish population
for the diﬀerent scenarios. The consumption data used in this thesis was obtained from the
Danish National Survey of Diet and Physical Activity (DANSDA), 2011-2013 [39]. DANSDA
is a nation wide, cross-sectional survey including diet and physical activity in a representative
sample of the Danish population from 4 to 75 years of age. For this study, data was restricted
to individuals who reported for all seven days and where information on body weight was
available. This gave a total population sample of 3,669 individuals. Concentration data
for nutrients were obtained from Danish food monitoring of foods on the Danish market of
both Danish and non-Danish origin (FRIDA) [37]. FRIDA includes data for more than 1500
food types with more than 200 parameters. Through the year of 2004 to 2011 Petersen et
al. (2013) conducted a market analysis investigating chemical contaminants in foods on the
Danish market, including trace elements of cadmium and lead [38]. Concentration data of
cadmium and lead, used in this thesis, was derived from the study of Petersen et al. (2013)
[38].

A gram to gram substitution of the prepared weight was considered, based on the assump-
tion that individuals substituting red meat by pulses would substitute to a similar portion
size, i.e. 1:1 in prepared amounts. Furthermore, it was assumed that the substitution with
pulses would be equally distributed among the included pulse types. Consumption and con-
centration data were therefore converted into prepared amounts to estimate the individual

Characterisation of Health Eﬀects
4.3.1
Zinc and Selenium
The exposure to zinc and selenium was estimated for the current intake in the population
and for the four diﬀerent substitution scenarios. Strong evidence has not been established for
these nutrients and the association with speciﬁc health eﬀects. However, selenium has been
found to be related to protection of proteins and lipids from oxidative damage and function
of the thyroid hormone metabolism [4]. Zinc has, among other functions, been found to be
essential for normal function of the immune system and normal DNA synthesis and cell divi-
sion [4]. Both nutrients have been found to be necessary for normal body function [4]. Since
no speciﬁc health outcomes were associated with zinc and selenium with suﬃcient evidence,
the exposure to these nutrients were compared with average requirements (AR) and UL. An
UL has not been established for zinc by the NNR, therefore zinc exposure was only compared
with the AR established [4]. For selenium, both AR and UL were established by the NNR,
therefore selenium exposure for the diﬀerent scenarios were compared with the established
AR and UL [4].

Iron
Iron is typically diﬀerentiated in two types: haem iron (hFe) and non-haem iron (nhFe).
Therefore, for the exposure assessment the exposure of these two types were estimated. The
NNR (2012) estimated that half of the iron in meat products is hFe and that the other half is
nhFe. Non-animal derived products were assumed to contain only nhFe [4]. NNR assumed the

Lead
Dietary exposure to lead has been associated with learning diﬃculties measured from intel-
ligence quotient (IQ) tests of children. The DR implemented in this study was estimated by
the WHO (2011), based on a meta-analysis relating blood lead (BPb) levels below 15 µg/dL
to the IQ of children by Lanphear et al. (2005) [49, 76]. The meta-analysis by Lanphear et
al. (2005) included seven cohort studies from USA, Mexico, Australia, and Yugoslavia with
a total study population of 1,333 children [76]. For the seven studies, two diﬀerent IQ tests
were applied, WISC (intelligence scale for children) and WPPSI (for preschool and primary
school), both of them speciﬁcally used for children. The IQ test was done at diﬀerent ages
in the seven studies, the earliest IQ test was done for the Cleveland study with a mean age

study with an IQ test at age ten, BPb samples were taken at the age of ﬁve years. Since
the earliest IQ test was done at age 4.8, age of onset for IQ disability (IQD) was assumed
to be at age ﬁve. Lanphear et al. (2005) found the strongest association of BPb with IQ
decrements for testing at seven years of age [76]. However, lead accumulates in the human

Dietary exposure to cadmium has been associated with Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in
several epidemiological studies. CKD was categorised in ﬁve stages, where the early stages,

be required [78]. The ﬂowchart of the model is shown in ﬁgure 17, this ﬂow was followed for

6
Discussion
In this thesis, the health impact of substituting red meat by pulses in the Danish popu-
lation was estimated by quantifying the beneﬁcial and adverse health eﬀects of red meat
and pulses. It was found that up to 118 healthy years of life could be gained per 100,000
individuals yearly by substituting 100% of the red meat by pulses for model scenario x1.
A decreasing beneﬁcial health impact was shown by decreasing substitution of red meat by
pulses. In contrast, by including the health outcome of MBA an overall negative health
impact was estimated (model scenario x3). Furthermore, the health impact was found to
vary for diﬀerent sub-populations. Men were found to experience the highest health gain due
to substitution of red meat by pulses. Meanwhile, small children below the age of six were
estimated to experience a health loss due to the substitution. Thus, these results highlight
the importance of diﬀerentiating between diﬀerent sub-populations in FBDG for the Danish
population.
The change in the health impact estimated for men and women is expected to originate from
the diﬀerence in dietary patterns together with the diﬀerence in the background incidence of
diabetes and IHD. For most of the health outcomes, the sub-population of men is estimated
to have a higher health gain from the substitution than for women. However, for IDA the
health gain is higher for women than for men, even though more cases were estimated to
be averted for the male population. This can be explained by the reference Hb levels for
the male population which was higher than for the female population. Since the reference
Hb is used to determine the severity level of anaemia, lower reference values will result in
a higher probability of a higher severity level than higher Hb reference values, resulting in
more DALYs that potentially could be averted.
6.1
Food Substitutions
To our knowledge this is the ﬁrst RBA quantifying the health impact of substituting red
meat by pulses, investigating toxicological and nutritional risk factors of both red meat and
pulses. Temme et al. (2013) investigated the impact of substituting meat and dairy by plant-
derived foods on iron and saturated fatty acids in Dutch young adult females, comparing the
estimates of daily intake of these nutrients with threshold values [81]. An increased intake
of iron was found, however of lower bioavailability, which is in line with the results of this
thesis. Similar results were found by Temme et al. (2015) investigating the substitution of
meat and dairy by plant-derived foods among Dutch children, also comparing the estimates
of daily intake of nutrients with threshold values [82]. They also found an increase in iron
intake by this substitution but of lower bioavailability. Furthermore, they investigated the
Page 64 of 96
==PAGE:76==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology
DTU
change in vitamin B12 and zinc intake, and found that for the 100% substitution scenario
the proportion of girls in the age of 4-6 with an intake below the AR would be 10% for
vitamin B12 and 6% for zinc. The results of this thesis show a markedly higher proportion
with an intake below the AR (vitamin B12: 18%, zinc: 42% and 53% for men and women,
respectively) when substituting 100% of unprocessed red meat by pulses in the Danish diet.
This is surprising, since animal-derived products are known to be the only source of vita-
min B12. However, in the study of Temme et al. (2015) some plant-derived substitutes
as vegetarian meat substitutes and milk chocolate sprinkle were also considered sources of
vitamin B12, indicating either fortiﬁcation of these products or inclusion of animal-derived
ingredients [82]. This may explain the diﬀerence in proportions below the AR of vitamin B12.
In this thesis, the food substitution of red meat by pulses was investigated. For this inves-
tigation the substitution was speciﬁed as a gram to gram substitution of prepared food, i.e.
the amount of prepared red meat was substituted with the same amount of prepared pulses.
Temme et al. (2015) used a similar substitution approach, investigating the substitution
of red meat and dairy by plant-derived foods for similar use in Dutch children [82]. They
too speciﬁed substitution scenarios of fractional substitutions, 30% and 100%, of prepared
amounts. However, other substitutions may be more realistic, such as an iso-caloric substitu-
tion or protein to protein substitution. Springmann et al. (2018) investigated the health and
nutritional impact of sustainable diets using iso-caloric substitution, where the total energy
content of the diet is kept constant [5]. The iso-caloric substitution was deemed unrealistic
for this RBA, since the energy content of prepared red meat is up to three times the energy
content of prepared pulses (data not shown), resulting in larger portions. It was found unre-
alistic to increase the portion size by this much. However, for the substitution in this thesis,
the daily energy intake of the individual is decreasing, with the largest diﬀerence in the 100%
substitution scenario. As the long-term energy balance is known to be constant this may
not reﬂect the most realistic substitution [4]. Another substitution option is to substitute on
protein level, i.e. gram protein by gram protein. The protein content of prepared red meat is
up to four times the amount of prepared pulses (data not shown). However, this would also
result in very large portion sizes, and therefore deemed unrealistic. Thomsen et al. (2018)
investigated the impact of substituting red and processed meat by ﬁsh in the Danish diet
[83]. Speciﬁed substitution factors were applied for substituting by portion size. In that
way, cultural eating patterns of red meat, processed red meat, and ﬁsh were accounted for.
Substitution factors may be another relevant substitution model to implement in this study.
However, no substitution factors were known for substituting red meat by pulses.

6.2
Exposure Assessment
The consumption data used in this thesis reﬂects the diet of the Danes in the years of 2011-
2013 [39]. However, the diet of the Danish population may have changed within the last
decade and the consumption data may not reﬂect the current diet of the Danish population.
The Danish association for vegetarians ("Vegetarisk forening") found an increase in the num-
ber of vegetarians in Denmark from 3.8% to 8.2% from 2010 to 2017 [84]. This indicates
that the consumption of meat in the Danish population could be lower than reﬂected in the
consumption data used in this thesis. If this is the case, it would aﬀect the substitution
scenarios and result in a smaller health impact. Thereby, the beneﬁcial health impact of
substituting red meat by pulses may be overestimated.
The consumption data was based on seven days of recording consumption on the individual
level. It was assumed that these seven days were reﬂecting the individual long-term intake,
thereby that the within individual variability equals out [85]. However, this is only accounting
for a part of the within individual variability. Some foods may only be consumed once or twice
a month, and these may not be accounted for. This may cause an underestimation of rarely
eaten foods, such as pulses. Furthermore, under- and over-reporters were not excluded in the
consumption data, this may cause a skewed result. This could be investigated by considering
energy intake and basal metabolic rate. However, Thomsen et al. (2019) found the diﬀerence
of including or excluding under- and over-reporters to be negligible for substitution of red
and processed meat by ﬁsh using the DANSDA consumption data [86]. Even though, this
thesis investigates a diﬀerent substitution model similar diﬀerences were expected. There-
fore, under- and over-reporters were expected to have a negligible eﬀect on the ﬁnal estimate.
In the exposure assessment of this RBA, background exposure of other dietary sources was
accounted for. However, non-dietary sources were not accounted for. Non-dietary sources
may however be relevant for chemical contaminants such as lead and cadmium. Smoking
is known to be the main source of cadmium exposure in the smoking population, whereas
food is the main source of exposure in the non-smoking population [56]. The health outcome
caused by cadmium, CKD, was only considered for individuals with an UCd above 1.0 µg/g
creatinine. Therefore excluding the background exposure of cadmium may cause an under-
estimation of the burden of CKD, thus the contribution of cadmium for smokers would be
relevant to include in this RBA. However, this was not possible due to lack of knowledge of
the individuals, i.e. the individuals included in the dietary survey. For lead, the main sources
are known to be food and drinking water, however also air, dust, and soil may contribute [55].
Drinking water was not accounted for in this RBA, since only dietary sources were accounted
for. However, since this RBA investigates the change in burden due to substitution of red
meat by pulses, the exclusion of non-dietary background lead exposure was expected ta have
no impact on the ﬁnal estimate. The exclusion of non-dietary background cadmium exposure
Page 66 of 96
==PAGE:78==

DTU
is however likely to cause an overestimation of the beneﬁcial health impact of substituting red
meat by pulses. It could therefore be argued that the background exposure from non-dietary
cadmium sources should be further investigated in future RBAs.
The bioavailability of a given component may aﬀect the estimations largely. The bioavailabil-
ity of the two types of iron, hFe and nhFe, has shown to be important for the direction of the
results. As mentioned previously the intake of iron increases with the substitution scenarios
but the bioavailability decreases. The IDA model was found to be very sensitive with regards
to the bioavailability of nhFe (results not shown). The bioavailability of both hFe and nhFe
was estimated by the NNR (2012) [4]. They based the bioavailability of nhFe on a review
by Bothwell et al. (1989) [87]. Bothwell et al. (1989) underline the uncertainty around this
estimate and state that many factors are aﬀecting the bioavailability of nhFe, both inhibitors
and enhancers. The main enhancers are known to be the MPF-factor (meat, poultry, and
ﬁsh), vitamin C, and organic acids, such as citric acid, lactic acid, and ascorbic acid [4, 87].
Meanwhile, the main inhibitors are known to be calcium, phytates, and the dietary protein
complex (most plant-based protein) [4, 87]. As red meat is substituted with the plant-based
protein of pulses, eliminating the enhancing eﬀect of the MPF-factor and substituting it with
the inhibiting eﬀect of the dietary protein complex in the meal, the absorbed amount of iron
may be overestimated in the substitution scenarios resulting in a large overestimation of the
beneﬁcial health impact.
The absorption of all components may be dependent on the food source and nutritional
components of the whole meal [4, 55, 56]. Absorption of zinc is found to be inhibited by phytic
acids, where animal-based meals are known to be enhancing absorption [4]. Components may
also be using the same absorption routes, i.e. be competing for absorption. It has been shown
that the absorption of cadmium is dependent on a number of diﬀerent factors, such as the
nutritional status of the individual, gender, age, and smoking status, but also the presence
of zinc, iron, and calcium may aﬀect absorption [56]. Since cadmium competes with iron for
absorption, iron deﬁciency (ID) has been linked to a higher cadmium absorption [56]. This
is due to the body being able to up-regulate transporter genes of nutrients when in need [4].
However, this has not been considered in this thesis, and may cause an overestimation of the
beneﬁcial health impact.
Some health eﬀects may cause an increased risk of other health eﬀects. It has been shown that
many years of suﬀering from diabetes can be causing CKD, thereby increasing the probability
of incidence of CKD [56]. This was not accounted for in this thesis and may have caused
a small underestimation of the negative health impact of CKD. IDA is frequently reported
in individuals suﬀering from chronic diseases, such as CKD and cancer. This could indicate
that there may be an association between the mechanism of IDA and CKD and cancer [88].
This was not accounted for in this thesis.

6.3
Health Outcomes
The overall health impact was mainly attributed to the decreased disease burden due to
diabetes and IHD. Five out of seven health outcomes included in this RBA were restricted
to speciﬁc age groups based on the evidence of the DR. Four of these were restricted to the
adult population. This was clear when investigating the sub-populations of the small children
below the age of six, and children and adolescents between the age of 6 and 18 where two
and three health outcomes, respectively, were contributing to the overall health impact.
De Oliveira Mota et al. (2019) quantiﬁed the burden of IDA in the French population from
dietary iron intake expressed in DALYs [61].
Their burden estimate is equivalent to the
reference scenario of IDA in this thesis. They estimated that IDA was causing a burden of
16 (95% CI: 11 ; 20) DALYs/100,000 from the iron intake of the current French population
[61]. In contrast, in this thesis IDA was estimated to cause a burden of 65 (95% UI: 44 ;
85) DALYs/100,000 from the iron intake in the reference scenario in the Danish population
(results not shown). This diﬀerence in estimated DALYs may be due to the diﬀerence in
reference in blood Hb levels for the French and Danish population. The reference blood Hb
levels used by de Oliveira Mota et al. (2019) are slightly higher than the reference blood Hb
levels used in this thesis. As discussed previously, lower blood Hb levels may lead to higher
probability of a higher severity stage of IDA [61]. De Oliveira Mota et al. (2019) further
investigated the eﬀect of an increase in red meat consumption on the health loss caused by
IDA in the French population and estimated a decrease in the burden caused by IDA [61].
However, they did not account for substitution of other foods, as was done in this thesis.
For this RBA, the health outcomes CRC, diabetes, IHD, IDA, IQD, CKD, and MBA were
included. However, there may be other relevant health outcomes associated with red meat
and pulses. Strong evidence may not be established for all relevant health outcomes. This
can be due to multiple reasons: 1) there is no strong association, 2) lack of studies, or 3) lack
of knowledge. By only including health eﬀects which have been recognised by NNR, WCRF,
and EFSA, or used in the GBD study (2017), some relevant health eﬀects may be excluded.
This could be accounted for by investigating meta-analyses and systematic reviews.
De Oliveira Mota et al. (2019) estimated the burden attributable to red meat consumption
due to CRC and cardiovascular diseases (CVD) in France [57]. They estimated a burden of
19 (95% UI: 8 ; 33) DALYs/100,000 and 21 (95% UI: 12 ; 32) DALYs/100,000 for CRC and
CVD, respectively, for the French population. CRC was included based on the same evidence
as in this RBA, whereas CVD was included based on it being the second most important
cause of disease mortality in France [57]. CVD was included even though strong enough ev-
idence has not yet been established. Ruan et al. (2019) also included health outcomes with
limited evidence in the estimation of the burden of cancer attributable to red and processed
Page 68 of 96
==PAGE:80==

DTU
meat consumption in Canada [89]. Besides CRC, the health outcomes of stomach and pan-
creatic cancer due to red meat consumption was included. However, stomach cancer is not
considered a health outcome of red meat consumption by the WCRF, whereas for pancreatic
cancer the grade of limited - suggestive was given by the WCRF [15]. Health outcomes with
less evidence could be included in RBAs, however it should be clearly stated and it could
introduce large uncertainties. One way to deal with this is to include such health outcomes
as model scenarios.
Relevant health outcomes not included in this RBA can also be due to relevant food com-
ponents that have been left out due to other reasons, e.g. aﬂatoxin. Aﬂatoxin has been
associated with liver cancer with convincing evidence by the WCRF [16]. In this report,
foods that may be contaminated includes cereals, seeds, nuts, some vegetables and fruit, and
pulses. However, aﬂatoxin was not included in this assessment due to lack of concentration
data (appendix A table 29). Other components may also be relevant to include in an RBA
investigating the substitution of red meat by pulses but may have been left out due to lack
of knowledge. One such component may be folate. Both red meat and pulses are a source of
folate in similar amounts in the raw foods. In prepared amounts, however, the amounts may
be somewhat diﬀerent. However, large uncertainties is associated with the retention factors
of folate dependent on the food and preparation styles [4].
In this RBA toxicological and nutritional hazards were included, however, red meat and
pulses may also be associated with microbiological hazards. Li et al. (2019) investigated the
burden of microbiological hazards of animal derived foods for the year of 2010 [90]. They
estimated animal-derived foods to contribute with 26 (95% UI: 17 ; 34) DALYs/100,000 for
microbiological hazards, including Campylobacter spp. and Salmonella enterica from red
meat [91]. By excluding microbiological risks the beneﬁcial health impact of substituting
red meat by pulses may be underestimated, since red meat is known to have many potential
microbiological risks. In contrast, pulses are known to have a limited potential microbiolog-
ical risk, typically eliminated by the long preparation time. Preparation is known to have a
large eﬀect on microbiological risks. Preparation was not considered in this thesis. It may
be relevant to investigate the preparation of red meat and pulses further, for both inclusion
of microbiological hazards and for better understanding of the retention factors.

6.4
Dose-Response Relationship
In this thesis the RRs were assumed to follow a log-linear association. However, the associa-
tion may be described by a diﬀerent model shape. This could aﬀect the estimated individual
RR and thereby aﬀect the estimated health impact. Also, the level at which the association
is established, i.e. for whole foods or food components, may aﬀect the DR greatly. In this
RBA, DRs were established for the whole foods of red meat and pulse with the association
of CRC, diabetes, and IHD. For the health eﬀects anaemia (IDA and MBA), IQD, and CKD
the evidence was established for speciﬁc food components. Thomsen (2019) found that using
DR established for associations between food components and health eﬀects may lead to a
wrong estimation of the estimated eﬀect size of the health impact, as the complexity of the
food matrix may aﬀect the association [31]. Therefore, it was recommended to use DR de-
scribing the association between whole foods and health eﬀects, where evidence is suﬃcient.
This could, therefore, indicate a small underestimation or overestimation of the eﬀect size of
the health impact of IDA, IQD, CKD, and MBA. Furthermore, Thomsen (2019) stresses the
importance of speciﬁed substitution in DR studies [31]. The DRs used in this thesis for CRC,
diabetes, and IHD were established for the single foods of red meat or pulses and not for the
food substitution of these. As non-speciﬁed substitution studies are typically adjusted for
energy intake, diﬀerent food substitutions may be covered in the DRs [31].
Thomsen et al. (2019) found DR estimates to be contributing largely to the uncertainty of
RBAs [86]. They highlight the need for better studies to derive associations between health
eﬀects and whole foods or food components, since this may decrease the uncertainty.
6.5
DALY
The health impact of substituting red meat by pulses was quantiﬁed using the composite
health metric DALY. This is the more common health metric in RBA studies, making it eas-
ier to compare results across studies. However, other health metrics are available, e.g. QALY
and the Willingness to pay. The QALY is similar to the DALY. The QALY is a measure of
the quality of life in a population, where DALY is a measure of the disability in a population
[8]. Neither the DALY or the QALY integrates economic burden in the ﬁnal estimate. In
contrast, the Willingness to pay health metric integrates the cost of pain, disability, and pre-
mature death. The Willingness to pay is mostly used to investigate what individuals would
be willing to pay to obtain health improvements or avoid adverse health eﬀects [92]. As this
was not the aim of this thesis, the DALY was deemed an appropriate measure.
The composition of the DALY may, however, value some parts of the population more than
others. The younger population is valued higher than the older population since they have a
longer remaining life expectancy. Some diseases are known to aﬀect the younger population
(e.g. IQD), where others are known to have higher incidence in the older population (e.g.
Page 70 of 96
==PAGE:82==
Master Thesis, MSc. Eng. Food Technology
DTU
diabetes). The life-long health eﬀects with onset early in life will result in a higher YLD,
i.e. a higher DALY, than life-long health eﬀects with onset later in life, due to longer re-
maining life expectancy. Furthermore, the female life is valued more than the male life. This
is again due to the longer life expectancy, since women have a longer life expectancy than men.
The dw’s used in this thesis were obtained from Salomon et al.
(2015) [33] and Soerjo-
mataram et al. (2012) [43]. Haagsma et al. (2014) stressed the importance of using the same
sets of dw’s for comparability due to diﬀerent methods used to obtain the dw’s in diﬀerent
studies [32]. Using diﬀerent sets of dw’s may also open up for the possibility of a more de-
tailed description of the disease [32]. The dw’s derived by Soerjomataram et al. (2012) were
used for a more detailed model of cancer [43].
The dw’s established by Salomon et al. (2015) have been derived from population surveys
representing diﬀerent parts of the world [33]. Individuals were asked to compare two hy-
pothetical individuals with diﬀerent health outcomes and state which one they found more
healthy [33]. From this, dw’s were estimated for 235 health outcomes with the respective
95% UI. However, since dw’s are opinion based and there may not be a true dw, it could be
questioned whether the 95% UI is representing uncertainty. It could be argued that the 95%
UI may actually reﬂect the variability in a population of how diﬀerent health outcomes are
perceived to aﬀect the health status of individuals.
The dw’s are derived from opinions of 18 to 70 year-olds. However, they are commonly used
on individuals down to age zero [33]. Some health outcomes may aﬀect diﬀerently on diﬀerent
age groups, e.g. IQD. IQD may have a small impact on small children, e.g. in kindergarten,
but may have big impact on the quality of teenagers and adults life. This was not possible
to account for in this thesis. However, it may be interesting to investigate the possibility of
accounting for such an age speciﬁc dw.
6.6
Model Scenarios
The three model scenarios of x1, x2, and x3 included diﬀerent models. Of these three model
scenarios, x1 was considered to be more realistic. The IHD1 model, used for the x2, was
developed upon the estimates from the GBD results tool [45]. This tool can be useful for
estimations, however, the basis for these results are unknown. Therefore, the IHD2 model
was preferred where the inputs of the model and the quantiﬁed uncertainty were known. For
model scenario x3, the MBA model was included. This model was based on limited data
and some questionable assumptions were made. It was assumed that all individuals with
an inadequate intake of vitamin B12 were deﬁcient. Furthermore it was assumed that every
individual suﬀering from vitamin B12 deﬁciency would develop MBA. These assumptions
introduce large unquantiﬁed uncertainties and causes overestimation of the health impact.

However, such assumptions were necessary for a complete MBA model due to the lack of
needed data.
6.7
Variability and Uncertainty
A probabilistic approach was implemented in this thesis. Therefore the variability and un-
certainty were quantiﬁed when possible by using Monte Carlo techniques for separation of
the variability and uncertainty. Accounting for variability is important in order to not ne-
glect high-risk consumers in the population. For this thesis, variability was accounted for in
the consumption data as individual data. The variability was accounted for throughout the
estimations by estimating on the individual level and taking the mean over the variability
dimension for the ﬁnal estimate of the health impact (the ∆DALY) of substituting red meat
by pulses in the Danish population. Variability was also accounted for in some of the models
by input data being age or gender speciﬁc. For the CRC, diabetes, and IHD models the
current incidence and mortality rates were speciﬁed for sex and age groups. Furthermore,
for the diabetes and the IHD1 models, variability was accounted for in the DALY per case
estimates speciﬁed for sex and age group. For the IDA model, variability was accounted
for in several steps: the AR distributions, Hb reference status, threshold values per severity
level, and the translation factor of being ID stage 1 to ID stage 3 (ﬁgure 12, section 3.1.3).
For the CKD model, variability was accounted for in the reference GFR by gender. The
variability of remaining life expectancy was also accounted for by sex and age groups. The
variability of vitamin B12 requirements within diﬀerent age groups were not accounted for
in this thesis, due to lack of data. Also, the bioavailability of components may vary largely
between individuals and in sub-populations, this was not accounted for in this thesis [4].
Some uncertainties were quantiﬁed, whereas others were unquantiﬁed. For the three models

were introduced both from the DR and the dw’s. However, for all the models, unquantiﬁed
uncertainties were introduced. All model inputs are known to introduce uncertainties since
no knowledge is perfect [20]. One way to deal with unquantiﬁed uncertainties is by assessing
them qualitatively. EFSA (2006) suggests such an approach, where the unquantiﬁed uncer-
tainties are described and scored in the form of +, ++, +++, -, - -, - - -, and combinations
of these such as +/- - to show the direction and magnitude of the potential impact on the
assessment. The unquantiﬁed uncertainties of this thesis with the expected direction and
magnitude are presented in table 28.

and the IDA model. A change in the reference scenario in the substitution model would

6.8
Future Perspectives
This thesis addresses the health impact of substituting red meat by pulses in the Danish diet
quantiﬁed in the health metric DALYs. To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst study quantifying
the health impact of substituting red meat by pulses. However, some challenges still remain
to be addressed. The MBA model includes large unquantiﬁed uncertainties due to necessary
assumption caused by lack of data. Preferably, data associating the dietary exposure to vi-
tamin B12 to vitamin B12 in the blood should be available, as vitamin B12 in the blood is
used for diagnosis of MBA.
It would be relevant to investigate the possibility of quantifying some of the unquantiﬁed
uncertainty in this RBA. By reﬂecting more of the uncertainty quantitatively in the ﬁnal
estimate it may be easier to estimate the impact of the uncertainty on the ﬁnal estimate.
Bootstrapping could be a method used for quantifying some of the unquantiﬁed uncertain-
ties. Bootstrap is re-sampling repeatedly from a data set and calculating the statistics for
the re-samplings (e.g. mean and sd). This could give information about the uncertainty of
the mean for example.
It may also be interesting to investigate the composition of macro nutrients with this substi-
tution since both red meat and pulses are known to be high in protein. Also, other macro
nutrients such as ﬁbres and fatty acids could be relevant for such a substitution.
Lastly, it could be interesting to investigate a whole diet substitution. To our knowledge,
RBAs for whole diet substitutions has not yet been done. However, this may be very com-
prehensive, since more health outcomes and food components should be included. Also, the
deﬁning of the substitution may be very comprehensive. Furthermore, a level of ﬂexibility
may be addressed to make it more realistic.

Conclusion
This thesis estimated a beneﬁcial health impact of substituting 100% of red meat by pulses
with 118 (95% UI: 85 ; 148) averted DALYs/100,000 yearly in the Danish population, includ-
ing the following health outcomes: CRC, diabetes, IHD, IDA, IQD, and CKD. A decreased
burden of IHD due to increased pulse consumption was the largest contributor of the health
beneﬁt with 44 (95% UI: 17 ; 69) averted DALYs/100,000 per year. However, this substitu-
tion would also increase the burden of IQD due to increased lead exposure in children with
1.7 (95% UI: 0.3 ; 3.5) DALYs/100,000. Furthermore, the model of MBA due to vitamin B12
deﬁciency should be investigated further for a more realistic estimate on the health impact.
By investigating sub-populations in the Danish population, men were estimated to experience
the largest beneﬁcial health impact of substituting 100% of red meat by pulses with 179
(95% UI: 134 ; 223) averted DALYs/100,000. In contrast, small children below the age of
6 were estimated to experience a negative health impact with an increase in disease burden
of 56 (95% UI: 10 ; 120) DALYs/100,000. Thus, these results highlight the importance of
diﬀerentiating between diﬀerent sub-populations in FBDG for the Danish population.
