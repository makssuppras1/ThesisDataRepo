Abstract
Spray drying is the process of transforming liquid feed with a solid fraction into dried
particles by spraying the feed into a chamber with a hot drying agent. Moisture evaporates
from the droplets, and these drying kinetic mechanisms can be described by numerical
mass transfer models. This study investigated three evaporation models; the Reaction
Engineering Approach (REA), Mezhericher’s model, and Multiphase Mixture Evaporation
(MME).
A single droplet drying experiment was designed to compare the model predictions of
droplet temperature and diameter during drying to the experimental measurements. The
glass filament technique was applied, where a single droplet was hanging from a glass
filament string inside a channel with hot air. There were large discrepancies between the
predictions and experiments for both skim milk and colloidal silica. Particle breakage and
inflation were beyond the scope of the numerical models, hence they failed at predicting
the diameter increase for skim milk droplets subjected to drying temperatures greater
than 118◦C. Yet, the experiment proved useful at characterising the drying behaviour of
materials and determining the equilibrium evaporation temperature.
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations were also undertaken of a Niro pilot-
plant spray dryer using both steady-state and transient time discretisation. The REA and
MME models exhibited identical results for the outlet temperature, outlet humidity, and
pressure drop. However, they predicted different regions of occurrence for the deposits,
and the probability density curves for the particles escaping the walls of the chamber were
also dissimilar. The REA exhibited a gradual curve for the particle temperature, whereas
the MME showed two distinct peaks.
In conclusion, drying kinetics of droplets is highly intricate to model numerically, as the
particles may take one of many morphologies. Nevertheless, for future work Mezhericher’s
model had possibilities of implementing the concentration profile across the droplet radius
to evaluate crust formation, and comparing the internal pressure to the ambient for risk
of particle breakage.
Keywords: Drying Kinetics, Mass Transfer Models, Single Droplet Drying Experiment,
Mathematical Modelling, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), Spray Drying.

1
Introduction
Evaporation is the process, where liquid water transitions to water vapour. Water can
evaporate at room temperature, since the molecules at the top surface have smaller in-
termolecular attractional forces than those within the bulk. Thus, if the molecules gain
enough kinetic energy (e.g. through a beam of sunlight), they will escape into the ambient
air. However, evaporation will only occur if the partial vapour pressure is less than the
saturated vapour pressure. Condensation is the opposite of evaporation, and this occurs
when saturated air is cooled [81, 69]. There exists numerous industrial applications using
the principle of water evaporation to achieve specific properties of a product, and one of
them is spray drying.
A spray dryer is a process equipment used for drying liquids or slurries to produce powders
that are needed in a variety of products, from food to battery powder [83].
Feed is
sprayed into a large chamber through nozzles, and mixed with hot air entering above.
The liquid fraction is evaporated during the thermal process, and left is a dry particle
with a lower moisture content than before the spray drying process. It is not practical to
track the properties of the particle such as temperature, moisture content, and mass during
operation. In literature, there exist few numerical models and experiments attempting to
describe the characteristic drying curve of materials. Only a fraction of these have also
implemented their findings into a CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) model. This
project sought to identify the differences of present numerical models and compare the
results with experimental data. Finally, the last part of the project was to implement the
drying kinetics model into a CFD simulation.
1.1
Aim and objectives
The aim of this project was to analyse the differences of numerical drying kinetics models,
compare with experimental data, and implement into CFD-code. The objectives of the
project were to:
1. Research relevant literature on drying kinetics and collate the findings into a thor-
ough literature review.
2. Use numerical drying models described in literature and convert models to python
scripts.
3. Design single droplet drying experiment and investigate both milk and colloidal silica
as suspended particles.

1.2
Research justification
Spray drying equipment is becoming increasingly more popular in recent years, and the
spray drying industry is valued at around 5.57 billion USD in 2020 [23]. The spray drying
method is the simplest and most popular for drying any liquid or semi-solid feed, and
food is the most popular application of the spray drying market with 66% market share
[23]. However, the chemical market share is becoming more popular, due to the greater
need for small lithium particles in car batteries. Companies are interested in using the
spray drying technology to decrease the size of the particles as this will give a greater
surface-area-to-volume ratio and increase performance [78].
The method of spray drying is intricate to replicate in an experimental setup, and the
process is often regarded as a black box. Manufacturers can measure the inlet and outlet
temperature and moisture, and one can also see if there are any deposits on the walls
inside.
Yet, particle paths and their properties during operation of the plant are not
possible to measure nor track. As a result, CFD is the most widely used tool for analysing
spray dryers, because it allows the process engineer to study what is going on inside
the spray dryer without perturbing the flow. Nonetheless, the mathematical equations
used in CFD are derived from simplified modelling approaches, as it is not feasible nor
possible to replicate all processes in nature. Computational resources are often a limiting
factor in industry, and therefore, one must setup the simulation to balance accuracy and
computational cost. This includes choosing the right physics models and being aware of
the differences of using one over the other. To validate if these simplifications inherent in
CFD are over-constraining the flow problem and quantify the sources of error, experiments
may be used. Experiments also have their inaccuracies, but together with CFD, they are
supplementing each other to drive the knowledge about spray dryers forward.
Often
complex problems are divided into minor parts, and an example is studying how liquid
water evaporates from skim milk. By only studying a single droplet, numerical models
used in CFD to predict evaporation rate can be validated against experimental, single
droplet drying measurements. Parameters in the numerical may be tweaked to better
match the realistic process, and this knowledge is fed back to the full spray dryer CFD
solver. Resulting in better CFD simulations of spray dryers with less risk of deposits,
fewer burned particles in the final product, and a size distribution matching the needs of
the client. All in all, aiding in designing the spray dryers of tomorrow.
To the best of the author’s knowledge, no published studies in literature have utilised the
Reaction Engineering Approach (REA) drying kinetics model in the CFD software STAR-
CCM+ for both steady-state and transient spray drying simulations. Thus, the novelty
factor of this project was implementing the REA evaporation model, and compare with an
ordinary mass transfer which did not take into account the drying kinetic dissimilarities
of materials. Furthermore, single droplet drying experiments were conducted to confirm
the correctness and identify the limits of the mathematical mass transfer models.

2.1
Spray drying in practice
The following paragraphs are based on the book Spray Drying Handbook by Masters [44].
Spray drying is a process where liquid droplets are dried to particles in a moving gaseous
drying medium. It utilises liquid atomisation to speed up the removal of moisture, and
spray drying can be classified as a suspended particle processing (SPP) system. ‘Atomisa-
tion’ refers to the process of liquid bulk break-up into billions of singular droplets creating
a spray and should not be confused with atoms and nuclear physics. Other examples of
SPP systems exploiting liquid atomisation are spray agglomeration, spray granulation,
spray reaction, spray absorption, and spray cooling. Often a fluid bed or flash dryers are
used to handle the wet solid / moist powder, and especially fluid beds are regularly com-
bined with spray dryers to achieve a predefined particle moisture, structure, temperature,
and functional properties that are not possible with the convectional spray drying process
alone. Depending on the properties of the final product, post-treatment equipment may
be utilised resulting in a two or even three-stage (multiple) layout. A spray dryer setup is
shown in Figure 1, where the hot air enters and passes through the air distributor causing
a swirling motion. The drying agent meets the liquid feed injected through the atomizer,
and the gas and particles travel around the chamber before exiting at the bottom. This
goes into a cyclone, where the particles are separated from the gas, and the dry product
can be collected.
Liquid evaporates from the droplets due to the heat of the drying medium combined
with the speed leaving the atomizer. For effective atomisation, pumpable feedstocks are
required, and the droplets leave the atomizer with a diameter ranging from 5 up to 600 µm
[44]. The small droplets provide a large surface area to volume ratio, resulting in rapid
drying due to accelerated rates of mass and heat transfer. Spray dryers may be designed to
produce fine or coarse sized particles and agglomerate or granulate particulate structures
– design parameters are tweaked to meet the requirements of the client.
The purpose behind spray drying varies. Especially for perishable products, spray drying
improves shelf life by reducing water content to levels that eliminates microbial growth
and resulting in greater product stability. Examples of spray-dried household products are
instant coffee, baby foods, flavouring compounds, powdered cheese, diet supplements, and
beauty products such as face powders and lipsticks. Finally, the pharmaceutical industry
is another major sector. This market entails the most demanding spray drying conditions
as they are often working with sterile substances (antibiotics) and special aseptic layouts
are adopted.

Disadvantages of spray dryers are their high installation costs compared to other dryer

complex supporting structures. Spray dryers are of the convection type, and they have

evaporative capacity. Nevertheless, if a spray dryer has been shown to produce the de-
fined powder properties and quality, then energy consumption is rarely the reason for not
choosing spray drying as the processing route.
Experimental methods of spray dryers are diﬀicult and time consuming, thus commercially
available software computer programmes are used as the common approach to predict
droplet movement and particle drying. There are examples of anemometers in pilot-plant
drying chambers to measure air velocities, and for larger sized units, possible methods are
gas holography, shadow doppler velocimetry, and gas tracer techniques. Yet, any probing
device interferes with the flow, and this is not desirable. In summary, the standard proce-
dure is to use CFD as it can provide insightful information about droplet trajectories and
particle properties. A downside is that the calculations are complex obligating special ex-
pertise to set up and analyse the simulations along with an extensive demand for computer
capacity. The purpose of the CFD simulations is among others to provide predictions for
the thermal eﬀiciency, properties of final products, and wall deposits. Wall deposits occur
when moist droplets hit the walls of the chamber and stick. Deposits may build-up and
break off giving burned particles in the final product. Product deposit formation can be
limited by operating parameters, design of air disperser, and chamber (dimensions / cone
angle).

2.2
Multiphase flows with droplets
Their exist four categories of multiphase flows: gas-liquid, gas-solid, liquid-solid, and three-
phase flows. Spray drying is a type of gas-liquid flow, and it is also known as dispersed
phase flows since one phase is dispersed and the other is continuous. According to the
definition, in the continuous phase one can go from one point to another while staying in the
same medium. Oppositely, it is not possible to travel from one droplet to another without
going through the gas. The dispersed phase is not materially connected. The dispersed
phase flow can be dilute or dense. A dilute dispersed flow is where the fluid forces (drag
and lift) control the particle motion. Contrastingly, a dense flow is where collisions or
continuous contact primarily influence the particle motion. The particle volume fraction
is often used to provide a general indicator whether the flow is dense or dilute.
Phase coupling is an important concept when studying multiphase flows. The flow is said
to be one-way coupled, if a phase affects the other with no opposite effect.
Two-way
coupling is when there is a mutual effect between the flow of two phases. Finally, four-
way coupling is when interactions between the individual parts of the dispersed phase
is also considered. A spray dryer is an example of a four-way coupled flow. A particle
transfers moisture mass during evaporation to the continuous phase, and the eddies in the
gaseous medium affect the trajectory of the droplet. Finally, there is the particle-particle
interaction as the droplet may collide or agglomerate with other particles.
In general, coupling can occur through mass, momentum, and energy transfer between
phases. Mass transfer is evident when evaporation or condensation processes are present,
and the addition or depletion of mass may also affect momentum coupling. Otherwise, mo-
mentum coupling is caused by the drag on the two phases. Finally, energy coupling takes
place through heat transfer between phases. Coupling parameters are used to quantify
the importance of coupling.
Droplet and particle sizes are critical when working with spray dryers. Droplets are often
assumed to be spherical and characterised by their diameter. The spread of the particle
size distribution is either monodisperse (close to single size) or polydisperse (wide range).
If the standard deviation is less than 10% of the average particle diameter, the distribution
is often considered to be monodisperse.

Drying kinetics of droplets
2.3.1
Stages of evaporation
A droplet is characterised to be a subdivision of the feed created by the atomizer with
residual moisture present [83]. On the other hand, a particle refers to a droplet having
undergone evaporation and with little to no moisture left. To characterise the stages of
evaporation of a droplet being dried, the five-stage concept proposed by Dolinski and
Ivanicki (1984) [19] is the most commonly used in literature (see Figure 3). It consists of
two primary stages and a total of 5 sub-steps:
Numerical modelling of drying kinetics for spray dryers
11
==PAGE:28==
Figure 3: Stages of evaporation.
The first drying stage is known as low-temperature
period, and the second drying stage is the high temperature period. The sub-steps are:
(a) initial heating and evaporation, (b) quasi-equilibrium evaporation, (c) crust formation
and growth, (d) boiling, and (e) porous particle drying.
1. Initial heating and evaporation. Rapid increase as the droplet goes from initial to
equilibrium temperature which is usually similar to the wet bulb temperature.
2. Quasi-equilibrium evaporation.
Droplet temperature increases only gradually be-
cause more and more solid matter is formed at the surface. A surface with increasing
concentration of solid matter has a lower vapour partial pressure, which lowers the
rate of evaporation. As a result, a greater fraction of the heat energy goes to heating
the droplet instead of evaporation.
3. Crust formation and growth. A crust forms on the outer surface and thickens to-
wards the centre of the droplet, when a critical concentration of solid matter is
reached. It becomes more diﬀicult for heat and mass to escape the droplet, and rate
of evaporation drops. Again, more energy goes to heating of the droplet leading to
a steep gradient on the temperature curve. This stage relies heavily on the crust
permeability and how easy vapour can diffuse through the medium.
4. Boiling.
The droplet temperature has reached the boiling point of the water at
the given pressure. The vapour trapped inside the crust generates a large internal
pressure and depending on the strength and permeability of the crust, it may crack
or inflate. Alternatively, it may explode and completely disintegrate. All free liquid
will at the end of this stage have evaporated.
5. Porous particle drying.
There may still be some liquid bound internally in the
dry particle, and this is the stage where it slowly evaporates. Finally, the droplet
temperature will asymptotically approach the surrounding temperature.

Some classify the first two substeps as the low-temperature period, since the temperature is
usually close to the wet bulb and far off the dry bulb temperatures. The high-temperature
period is substeps three, four, and five [55].
2.3.2
Morphology
Every year, more than one thousand papers are published in the Web of Science on the
topic of spray drying. This confirms the ever-growing attention for spray drying as an
alluring process for industrial applications. Nevertheless, there is still a scarcity regarding
the characterisation of the components’ distributions inside the droplet during drying [74].
The capillary pressure is a critical parameter with growing importance as the particle
dries. The pressure is derived from the contrast in pressure inside and outside the droplet
and is linked to the surface tension force, which acts upon reducing the surface area of
the droplet). When solvent evaporates in the initial drying stage, the droplet shrinks and
the internal pressure increases. Once the crust is contoured, the outer radius and thereby
volume remains constant. This leads to an increasing pressure applied over the new-formed
crust which balances the internal pressure. Depending on the molecular size of the solutes,
molecular interactions, and material properties, the crust may be rigid or flexible. These
differences in mechanical behaviours and pressures depending on the ambient and internal
conditions influence the particle morphologies. As illustrated in Figure 4, particles may
have (a) a dense or (b) hollow core, (c) a wrinkled surface, shaped like (d) a doughnut or
(e) raspberry, (f) a porous structure, or finally (g) a hairy like surface. In the following
shapes (a), (b), and (f) will be described in more detail [74].
Figure 4: Diagram showing the possible morphologies a particle may adopt when a droplet
is dried. Source: [74].
Numerical modelling of drying kinetics for spray dryers
13
==PAGE:30==
Dense particles are formed, when the rate of solute enrichment at the surface is slower than
the evaporation flux of liquid. Thus, the crust formation occurs slowly, and droplet ends
up with a dense core. Oppositely, if the diffusion of compound towards the centre is slower
than the rate of solvent being removed, a hollow core may appear. For spray drying, where
solvent removal rates are high, the hollow cores are most easily obtained. Furthermore,
smooth spherical particles are created when the crust has enough mechanical strength to
withstand the inward capillary pressure, driven by the liquid evaporation. Some studies
[15, 57] used a Peclet number-based analysis (PNA) to predict if a particle was dense (Pe ≪
1) or hollow (Pe ≫1). The Peclet number is defined as the ratio of advective and diffusive
transport rates. The Peclet number accounts for the ratio of solute enrichment at outer
surface and evaporation of moisture. Nevertheless, it cannot account for all morphologies
pictured in Figure 4 as the number provides no information about component distribution.
Furthermore, the initial drying conditions are utilised to calculate the Peclet number, but
it may change during drying due to varying diffusion coeﬀicients. This has been confirmed
by the study of Chew et al. (2015) [14], where they looked at skim milk and found a
deviation for high Peclet numbers (hollow core) with dense particles being formed.
Porous structures are achieved, when the continuous phase (e.g. water in whole milk)
evaporates faster than the dissolved phase (milk powder). To avoid coalescence of the
dispersed phase, a stabilising agent is often required and for milk this is inherited in the
molecular structure.
2.4
Numerical droplet evaporation models
In literature there exists a few attempts at numerically predicting the drying curve of
various materials. The models may be divided up into those limited at describing only
droplets with dissolved or insoluble solids, and finally those that are able to predict both.
In literature, near all theoretical models are based on moisture content and described by
partial differential equations (PDEs). Kuts et al. [40] derived a dissolved-model linking
particle drying rate to either a linear or exponential change in moisture content with
respect to time.
However, Kuts et al.
incorporated an arguable boundary condition
stating that the crust-wet core interface temperature was equal to that of drying air wet
bulb [48]. Sano and Keey [64] did not consider the temperature distribution within the
dissolved particle, but found satisfactory agreement between model predictions and their
own experimental data for skim milk droplet drying.
Yet, other studies in literature
compared their measured drying histories to the predictions by Sano and Keey and found
an unexplained, distinct discrepancy [48].
For droplets with insoluble solids, Mezhericher did not find any studies in literature taking
the radial temperature distribution into account [48]. The most cited insoluble model is
the one by Abuaf and Staub [1], where a Stefan-type mass balance equation was used to
describe the vapor mass flow rate through a single straight crust capillary. This model
was adopted and simplified by later studies, but it is restricted to only work for relatively
low temperatures and under atmospheric pressure.
Of the mixed models, the work by Nesic and Vodnik [55] found good agreement with
experimental data for droplets with insoluble solids. For droplets with dissolved solids,
there was a noticeable discrepancy of the temperature in the second stage. Yet, this mixed
model is one of few that considered the liquid concentration profile by relevant PDE of
diffusion, but the temperature distribution was neglected.

2.5
Single droplet drying experiments
Spray drying is a process, where numerous droplets with different sizes are simultane-
ously sprayed into a chamber. Each droplet will have their own trajectory, and along this
path, there will be various ambient conditions acting on the droplet. Thus, it is nearly
impossible to take samples and precisely track what occurs inside the droplet during the
intermediate stages. The final product is affected by the temperature and moisture con-
tent histories during drying. Examples of factors are crystallinity, surface composition,
and amount of heat- and/or oxygen-sensitive materials left in the particle. These affect
solubility, dispersibility, flowability, and other process properties. In summary, the adver-
sity of sampling during spray drying makes it impractical to provide information about
the temperature and humidity history of a particle [25].
There exist experiments in literature attempting to circumvent these diﬀiculties by only
analysing one droplet, known as single droplet drying (SDD) experiments. This involves
drying a single particle in controlled ambient conditions mimicking the spray dryer en-
vironment. SDD systems may be categorised in three types; (a) droplet suspended on
tip of a glass filament, (b) free-falling droplet, and (c) droplet levitated by acoustic or
aerodynamic fields [25]. Type (a) is the most published experiment in literature, and it
was first done by Ranz and Marshall in 1952 [60]. The free-falling is the approach most
closely replicating the drying conditions inside an industrial spray dryer. Particle size
can be adjusted to mimic the properties of the industrial application. Some pharmaceuti-
cal companies use the mono-dispersed droplet technique for producing high-value medical
powders. A major drawback of the free-falling method is the diﬀiculty of monitoring the
temporal temperature and humidity histories when falling in free motion. Indirect mea-
surements such as determining the terminal velocity of the droplet [38] or similar examples
are mentioned in a few studies [65, 80].
Of the three types, the glass filament is the only intrusive approach. Here a thin glass
filament is used to suspend a droplet at the tip during the whole drying process. Even
though, it is intrusive and the glass filament will affect the heat and mass transfer, then
studies in literature found that for a Ø1.6 mm droplet, the tip of the glass filament only
takes up 0.2% of the overall volume.
As a result, the heat conduction would be less
than 1% of the total heat input. The main advantage of this SDD system is the ability

2.6
CFD
This report is concerned with describing the drying kinetics of droplets, thus, only a brief
description of the governing principles behind CFD has been included. Firstly, the fluid
domain is divided up into numerous small volumes. The conservation equations are then
transformed using Gauss’ theorem from a set of partial differential equations to a set of
coupled algebraic equations by taking the assumption that e.g., the temperature, velocity
field, and pressure are constant over the individual volumes. This approach is known as
the finite volume method. Moreover, boundary conditions are prescribed to the inlets,
outlets, and walls. Values for all the variables at each cell can then be calculated using an
iterative matrix solver that inverts this set of coupled, non-linear equations. As a result,
a discrete approximation of the flow field is found.
If particles are also present, then
iterations are carried out to couple the dispersed and continuous phases until convergence
is obtained [41].

with details about past studies of transient spray drying simulations publicly available in
literature.

2.6.1
Two-way coupling procedure
Spray dryers are in practical terms four-way coupled, but most studies in literature have
neglected particle-particle interactions, and only considered the particle-gas coupling. The
two-way coupling procedure for a generic Euler-Lagrange solution in CFD under steady-
state conditions consists of six steps. Firstly, the fluid flow is solved without considering
the source terms of the dispersed phase until convergence is reached. Secondly, the par-
ticle properties and trajectories are sampled along with the source terms for each control
volume. Source terms are then incorporated into the fluid flow and recalculated for pre-
defined number of iterations or until convergence. If convergence of the two-way coupling
has not been achieved, then the coupling iterations are repeated [73]. To ensure numerical
stability, an underrelaxation procedure is utilised, where γ is the underrelaxation factor
and it is between zero and one.
2.6.2
Steady vs unsteady
Spray dryers contain unsteady two-phase flows with time-varying temperatures and hu-
midities. The primary flow structure is often the jet in the center of the chamber, and it
may be undulating and sway from side to side caused by the swirling air coming from the
air disperser and the rotary atomizer. Due to the small mass of the particles, they tend to
follow the gas phase, underlining the importance of predicting the correct jet structure.
Flows that vary with time are classified to be unsteady, and as turbulence is varying
in time, this often requires the CFD simulations to be unsteady.
Contrastingly, for a
steady solution, the mean quantities do not change with time, and this is employed when
computational resources are limited or when only interested in the converged solution of
the instantaneous quantities. The Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS)
are used to solve steady-state simulations, and here a local optimised time step is applied
to each cell to solve the steady-state case. Oppositely, URANS (Unsteady RANS) are
used for transient simulations as the equations contain a time derivative. For URANS a
global time step is applied to capture the instantaneous time-varying quantities, resulting
in a time-accurate solution.
For CFD-modelling of spray dryers, the most common approach in literature is using
steady-state. Ullum et al. [77] concluded that there were negligible changes in the move-
ment of the jet but this conclusion was made based on a steady-state approach. On the
other hand, Keshani et al. [37] confirmed the time-dependent nature of the flow, and the
study used a transient RNG k-ϵ turbulence model. However, the simulation was single
phase as Keshani et al. did not include a discrete phase. Other examples of previous
studies are Jin et al. [35] and Woo et al. [82] that both applied a temporal discretisation.
Yet, to reduce the computational costs, they applied a simplified two-dimensional axisym-
metrical model with swirl. This is an arguable assumption as the position of the chamber
outlet greatly varies from plant to plant, and the irregularity of the jet and flow field
cannot be captured with this axisymmetrical method. To the author’s best knowledge,
there only exist one study in literature of a fully transient three-dimensional simulation
with transient particles, and it was mentioned in the paper by Fletcher et al. (2006) [24].
However, it was only briefly described, and the conclusion was that it was too expensive
to run. No mesh or time step convergence studies were performed.
Discretising the time domain of a spray dryer simulation posses its disadvantages. Firstly,
CFD simluations are often parallelised in domains to solve quicker, but this is not always
possible with particles as they rarely stay in one domain during a time step. Secondly,
there is a substantial memory requirement as new parcels are injected at each time step,

Methodology
The equations of the numerical evaporation models - REA and Mezhericher were solved
using iterative loops, and the methods were distinctly different.
3.1.1
REA
The equations of the REA were solved in series, and the model was written in python.
The steps were:
1. Droplet temperature, weight, diameter, and moisture content were given as initial
conditions.
2. Nusselt and Sherwood numbers were calculated using Eq. (13) and (14), to find the
heat and mass transfer coeﬀicients.
3. Using the droplet temperature, the saturated vapour concentration was found along
with the activation energy applying relevant equation for product investigated, e.g.
Eq. (36) for skim milk.
4. Eq. (29) was utilised to estimate the evaporation rate, dm/dt, and Eq. (38) deter-
mined the droplet temperature change dTd/dt.
5. Chen and Lin (2005) [11] found a time step of 0.5 s to be suﬀicient, and a forward
differencing scheme was used to calculate the new droplet temperature and weight.
To find the diameter, an experimental correlation was used [43].

in any of their papers, what values they used.
Considering the results by Chen and
Lin [11] as datum, the mean deviation between the REA-model predictions by Chen
and Lin and the author of this project with the thermal conductivity taken at room
temperature kg = 0.025 W/m K, was 0.8% ± 1.1% for temperature and 2.2% ± 1.2%
for mass.
Double standard deviations are given after the ±-symbol.
A more correct
value would be using a conductivity of 0.033 W/m K taken at the drying temperature
of Tb = 106.6◦. Alternatively, the film temperature could also be fitting. However as
seen, in Figure 8, the average deviation increased to 8.1% ± 11.4% for temperature and
6.7% ± 5.7% for mass, when using a higher conductivity of the gas. Therefore, to ensure
likewise implementation of the model, the lower conductivity was used.

and the definition of the heat transfer coeﬀicient using the Nusselt number definition

Finally, the MME predictions were also included for comparison. This model did not con-
sider any resistance to evaporation due to the crust formation, and therefore the drying
curve was very similar to that of a pure water droplet. At the start, the temperature
dropped for the MME, and the phenomenon can be explained by the initial droplet tem-
perature being greater than the quasi-equilibrium evaporation stage. Thus, water vapor
evaporated at first before a balance of the heat supplied and heat removed with evapo-
rated mass happened, which occurred at the equilibrium evaporation temperature [45].
Furthermore, once all moisture had evaporated, all the thermal energy went to heating up
the particle, hence a steep temperature increase was observed along with the mass curve
staying flat.
3.3
Mezhericher - Colloidal silica
Mezhericher’s two-stage model can be used to predict drying curves for both dissolved
and insoluble solids, and colloidal silica was used first to validate the implementation. In
Chapter 3.4 the comparison with whole milk is presented. Colloidal silica is a dispersion
of insoluble, amorphous silicon dioxide (SiO2) particles in water [10]. The results were
compared to the model predictions by Mezhericher [48], and to the experimental data and
model predictions by Nesic and Vodnik [55]. One of their experiments was using 30 wt%
colloidal silica drying at Tb = 178◦C, ug = 1.4 m/s, H = 0.002−0.010 kgw/kgd, the initial
weight of the droplet was 4.1 × 10−6 kg, and the insoluble SiO2 spheres were 16 nm in
diameter [55]. The droplet temperature and mass curves are plotted in Figure 9.
The markers indicate the experimental data, and the model predictions by Nesic and Vod-
nik were closest to the experimental data. For the first stage, Mezhericher predicted the
droplet to increase in temperature until it reached a constant level, where the evaporation
rate was constant too as seen by the straight line in Figure 9(b). Colloidal silica is said to
skip the boiling stage of evaporation, when subjected to temperatures far above the boiling
point. The hypothesis in previous studies was that the crust is highly porous and fragile;
thus, the crust does not provide much resistance to evaporation. As a result, the drying
curves are very similar to those of pure water droplets [55]. Predictions by the standard
evaporation model in STAR-CCM+ (MME = Multiphase Mixture Evaporation) were in-
cluded as mode of comparison. MME did not consider the presence and effects of the
crust. Hence, why the evaporation model fitted the data better than Mezhericher, which
is a mixed model able to describe both insoluble and dissolved droplets. For the mass
curve, MME was similar to Mezhericher, whereas Nesic and Vodnik predicted a steeper
gradient.
For both the first and second drying stages of Mezhericher’s model, the droplet was radi-
ally discretised, and a convergence study was performed to investigate the effects of this
parameter. Beati [4] used 51 nodes in the second stage for both the wet core and crust
regions, and the python model of this study found no differences in using more nodes.
Similarly for the time step, a time step of ∆t = 0.1 s was identified to be suﬀicient.

4
Single droplet drying experiment
This project designed and conducted a single droplet drying experiment using the glass
filament technique. Reasons being the ability to monitor temperature, diameter, and mass
change using the same setup. A single experiment was carried out, where the temperature
and diameter of the droplet were tracked. Due to limited time and financial budgets, it
was not possible to monitor changes in the particle mass. Mass changes of a single particle
from start to end of drying period were expected to be in the order of 50 to 150 µg, which
was not feasible to be measured with the equipment available nor to be bought within the
economical constraints. For reference, a human hair weighs 200 to 400 µg [50].
4.1
Methodology
4.1.1
Drying tower
Single droplets were dried in a square drying channel, and the experimental setup is shown
in Figure 12 and 13. A Wagner Furno 750 heat gun was used as heating and fan element.
This particular heat gun was bought as the temperature could be controlled by a digital
display from 50◦C to 630◦C in increments of 10◦C. Furthermore, the airflow capacity
was 800 standard L/min, resulting in a maximum air speed of 10 m/s for a 36 mm x
36 mm tube.
Experiments in literature do not describe whether their velocity ranges
tested were limited by the flow rate meter, fan, or something else. However, most studies
used a velocity spectrum of 0.5 m/s to 2 m/s, which is several magnitudes lower than
what is experienced initially in a spray dryer. Lima (2022) [74] is one of few studies that
mentioned, their maximum velocity was limited by their flow rate meter only being able to
measure velocities between 0.06 m/s to 0.5 m/s. Depending on the size of the droplet and
the shape of the filament that the droplet sits on, the velocity of experiments in literature
might have been limited by the droplet not being blown off. For this experiment, a hole
plate design was implemented as a way of measuring the flow rate. Due to the temperature
variations and low velocities, a hot wire measurement system was not applicable.
The bottom plate sat on top of the mouth piece to the heat gun using a close fit tolerance
seizure. Four Bosch Rexroth aluminium elements (30 mm x 30 mm) were positioned to
balance the structure and ensure the channel was co-linear to the flow exiting the heat
gun. A groove was milled into the bottom plate to hold the 120 mm lower channel section
in place. The 120 mm corresponded to more than 4 diameters of the heat gun nozzle
downstream with the aim to make the turbulence die out and ensure a fully developed
flow. Two holes were drilled into the lower channel section; one for the pressure tap and
the other for temperature monitoring.

For designing the hole plate, both hand calculations using the formulas of Idelchik [33] and
CFD simulations were carried out in STAR-CCM+. For conciseness, the CFD simulation
methodology and models will not be described in detail here, but a mesh of 30 million
cells was applied. CFD simulations were carried out to study the effects of the jets exiting
the hole plate. For the low speeds of 1.5 m/s, the hole plate had to have a tiny opening
percentage to generate a pressure loss greater than 10 Pa. This was a criterion as the
measurement equipment could only measure with an uncertainty of u(∆p) = 0.05 Pa.
According to the formulas of Idelchik, if using Ø1 mm holes, the opening percentage had
to be 10% to generate the required pressure difference. However, the CFD simulations
found that this generated jets on the other side that did not diminish downstream, causing
an irregular flow field, which was not of interest. To overcome this, two hole plates were
implemented; the first generating the required pressure loss, and the second was designed
to smoothen the jets. This was done by placing the holes offset compared to the first
hole plate. Opening percentages of the first and second hole plates were respectively 17%
and 50%. The first hole plate had 17x17 Dhp1 = Ø1 mm holes evenly spaced, whereas
the second plate had 16x16 Dhp2 = Ø1.8 mm holes. The second plate had larger holes
to increase the opening percentage and thereby minimising the chance of jets causing an
irregular flow distribution across the cross-section of the channel.
CFD simulations showed that for a velocity of 1.5 m/s, then 120 mm (equating to 60Dhp2)
downstream, the velocity profile for a section perpendicular to flow direction and 5 mm
away from the wall, the average was 1.7 m/s with a standard deviation of 0.2 m/s. Average
was higher due to law of mass conservation, as the inlet condition was specified to be 1.5
m/s across the entirety of the inlet surface. This was considered to be acceptable. The
two hole plates were separated by a 10mm mid channel section to allow for the jets of the
first hole plate to diffuse before interacting with the second hole plate.

After the second hole plate, a 220 mm top channel section was inserted to allow for the flow
to develop before hitting the droplet. Two holes were again drilled to allow for pressure and
temperature measurements to be taken. These were positioned 20mm above the second
plate. A bypass barrier plate was implemented to circumvent the air, thereby avoiding
evaporation before monitoring had started. Furthermore, the intent was also to preheat
the channel as the metal parts would cause a thermal delay before being heated up and
reaching a thermal equilibrium with the surrounding air. Other studies have also used the
bypass technique, for example Vora (2020) [79] using a plate being slotted into a groove.
For this experiment, the bypass plate was controlled by a lever connected via a pin glued
into both components. A tiny screw on the opposite edge controlled the position of the
plate when perpendicular to the flow.
When the bypass barrier plate was open and allowing air to transfer over the droplet, the
plate was held in place by a bolt clamping the lever and a beam together. As a security
measurement, the 30 mm x 30 mm bypass outlet was positioned on the other sides of the
glass windows, avoiding hot air from burning any measurement equipment or wires. A
final temperature measurement was taken right before the window section to measure the
temperature of the air before hitting the droplet.
Temperature resistant 40 mm x 40 mm x 4 mm glass windows were used for observing the
droplet shrinkage, and they were held in place by angled beams top and bottom secured by
3x M2 screws. The angled beams ensured that the channel was closed for leakages without
overstraining the glass and allowing for thermal expansions to occur during operation.
A 10mm groove was cut into the top of the upper channel section as a place for positioning
the brass beam where the droplet was hanging from. The top beam (part of the droplet
holder) was brass as the rest of the tower was aluminium. For elevated temperatures,
there was a chance of the thermal expansions causing two metal parts of similar material
to be bonded together.
Thus, by utilising dissimilar materials for the channel section
and top beam, this was avoided. The brass beam also had two Ø2.5mm holes spaced
10mm from the centre each side; here glass capillary tubes were each held in place by a
single M1 screw. The glass capillary tubes were holding the thermocouple measuring the
temperature of the droplet.
Droplets were hanging from glass strings mounted to a brass beam and held in place by
a single M1 screw. Glass was used, due to its low thermal conductivity, thereby limiting
the heat transfer from the rod to the droplet. The glass string was made by the glass
blowing craftsman Anders Raad, and different diameters of the strings were experimented
with.
Ø2 mm was found to give the most spherical droplets and highest diameter of
droplet to rod ratio. Other studies in literature [79, 42] constructed a knot at the end of
the string to increase the contact surface, and thereby making it easier for the droplet to
stick. However, this was not found to be working well, instead the beam was flat at the
end.
Finally, the top plate had four holes in the corners like the bottom plate, and four rods
and bolts clamped the assembly together. Bottom plate was screwed to a construction of
30x30mm Bosch Rexroth aluminium profiles. This provided the foundation of the tower,
ensuring that the channel did not turn or tilt when turning the bypass barrier plate or if
accidentally hitting the tower.

types of metals, welded at one end.
If junction is heated or cooled, a tiny voltage is
created that can be correlated to the temperature. Various combinations of metals are
available, and ”base metals” thermocouples are the most used, known as types J, K, T, E,
and N. Type-k is frequently used, due to their low cost and temperature range [56]. The
thermocouples of this experiment were all of type-k. When working with thermocouples,
their responsiveness is not instantaneous and a delay is present, often expressed as a time
constant. This is defined as the time it takes for the sensor to respond to 63.2% of its total
output signal when subjected to a step change in temperature [30]. Heat is conducted from
the outer sheath to the element wires, and the rapidness of this heat transfer process defines
the reponsiveness of the sensor. Quick time constants are achieved with smaller sheath
diameters, but these are not as durable as thicker ones.
The data acquisition system
uses a formula or table to convert the measured voltage to a correlated temperature.
Alternatively, the thermocouple can be calibrated using any of the known temperature
points. For example, a 2-point calibration is done by placing the thermocouple in ice water
(0◦C) and boiling water (100◦C) under standard atmospheric conditions. The measured
voltages can then be linearly correlated to the voltage [62].
A simple experiment was carried out to determine the time constant of the thermocouple
used inside the droplet.
A BERM REX-C100 oven was preheated to 150◦C, and the
thermocouple was connected to a DaqBoard 2000 DBK19-0 data acquisition system with a
sampling frequency of 1000 Hz. Data was collected in DASYLab 12.0, and the temperature
was measured in the ambient air for 10 s before being positioned in the oven cylinder.
See Appendix 8.4 for pictures of the experimental setup. By tracking the temperature
profile of the oven and assuming that the oven temperature was non-varying, the lumped
capacitance model was applied to find the time constant, τ [5]:

4.1.4
Procedure
The procedure for the drying experiment is described along with some illustrations of the
key steps in Figure 14:
1. Weighing materials
1.1. The measuring cup was rinsed with demineralised water. This was done to
remove impurities from the measuring cup, and thereby increased the surface
tensional forces of the droplet.
1.2. Mettler PM4800 DeltaRange weight scale with a precision of ±0.01 g was used
to measure the desired solid content of the material of interest. Afterwards,
demineralised water was added to the mixture, and a 2 ml microsyringe was
used for the last couple of grams of water to ensure the weight was within ±0.01
g of the desired value. Gloves and safety glasses were used as colloidal silica
can have a drying effect on the skin.
1.3. Solution was mixed well to a coherent liquid.
2. Setup and calibration
2.1. Thermocouples were connected to the data acquisition system, and the three
thermocouples were inserted into the holes below and above the hole plates as
well as the hole after the bypass plate. They were positioned such that the tips
were in the center of the channel.
2.2. The camera was connected to the computer using a micro-usb cable.
2.3. Camera zoom was reset to focus on the droplet, and thermocouples were tested,
making sure they worked as they should.
2.4. The droplet holder was inserted into its slot in the top plate of the drying tower.
2.5. Camera was set to start recording.
2.6. From the top of the drying tower, ruler was lowered into channel such that it
was next to the glass holder. These images were later used for calibration and
setting the scale of pixels to millimeter.

3. Single droplet drying experiment
3.1. The heat gun was turned on at the desired fan and temperature settings. This

any slight movement made it drop.

3.6. The bypass plate was opened to allow air blowing over the droplet. Simul-

3.7. During the drying process, the micromanometer was kept an eye on, and the

3.8. The temperature measurements were set to record for a predefined period, hence

3.9. The bypass plate was closed, and the droplet holder was removed attentively

3.10. Images were taken with a phone camera of the dried droplet for later analysis.
3.11. The glass filament string and droplet thermocouple were cleaned by removing
any debris or burned particles.
The equipment was then ready for another
droplet to be dried and the experiment was repeated from step 3.3.

4.2.1
Thermal boundary layer
The aluminium walls of the drying tower conducted heat to the surrounding air, and as a
result, a temperature profile was established across the cross-section and upstream from
the outlet of the heat gun. To investigate the thermal effects of the walls, temperature
measurements were taken at three vertical points and in 5 mm increments across the cross-
section. The positions of the 5 vertical probes were 20 mm before the first hole plate, 20
mm after the second hole plate, and 30 mm below the droplet. Two different flow rates
(67% and 100% on the heat gun display setting) and a single temperature (200◦C on the
heat gun display setting) were investigated. The temperature of the ambient air was 23◦C.
Measurements were taken over 2 min intervals with a sampling frequency of 3 Hz, and the
temperature profiles are shown in Figure 15.
It is unclear whether the temperature stated on the display of the heat gun was the outlet
temperature or the temperature of the heating coil. Nevertheless, heat was lost to the
surroundings through the walls as the temperatures were 65◦C to 110◦C lower than what
was stated on the display of the heat gun. Furthermore, a thermal boundary layer was
established on the walls, resulting in a temperature gradient towards the centre of the
channel, and the temperatures at the walls were 25◦C to 35◦C lower than in the centre.
The differences could be lowered if the walls were insulated as done by other studies in
literature [79, 42]. It was diﬀicult to tell where the thermocouple was actually measuring
the temperature. Even though, there was a shift in all profiles with higher temperatures on
the opposite the side of the holes, then it is likely that this was due to the thermocouples
not measuring at exactly a single point. The more of the probe that was exposed to the
hot air, the higher the temperature measured at the tip.

Thermocouples
To measure the temperature of the droplet, three different thermocouples were tested of
various diameters; Ø0.32 mm, Ø0.076 mm, and Ø0.05 mm. All from different manufactur-
ers. The first and thickest thermocouple was rigid, and due to the stiffness of the wire it
was easy to position correctly. The second was too brittle, and the welding at the tip broke
after the first experiment. Finally, the third thermocouple was too thin making it diﬀicult
to position, and the deviations on the measurements were too high. For example, when
positioned in ambient air, the temperature measurements would fluctuate ±6◦which was
unsatisfactory. Attempts were also made at manufacturing home-made thermocouples by
welding nickel wires together, however, all joints easily failed under the slightest tension.
As a result, the Ø0.32 mm thermocouple was used for all droplet measurements.
To determine the time constant, τ, of the thermocouple, 10 experiments were carried out,
and one of the curves is shown in Figure 16(a), where the logarithmic to the fraction on
the left side of Eq. (101) is plotted against time. The slope of this curve was τ. The high
sampling rate of 1000 Hz, meant that noise was captured in the signal too. Therefore,

it was found that the average time constant of the thermocouple was τ = 0.37 s ± 0.04 s,
giving a coeﬀicient of variation of 12%. These results were acceptable as the drying times of
the particles were between 150-500 seconds, thus the time constant was several magnitudes
smaller than the period of interest.

This chapter is concerned with the numerical CFD modelling of the behaviour of two

The CFD domain of the spray dryer was discretised into a finite number of small vol-

Zafiryadis [83]. Zafiryadis varied the mesh count from 1.1 × 105 to 2.2 × 106 cells, and
concluded that a mesh size of 1.2 × 106 cells yielded acceptable results [83]. Hansen simu-
lated four meshes from 7.4 × 105 to 2.2 × 107 cells, and found the results of the gas phase
(outlet temperature, outlet humidity, and pressure drop) to not converge asymptotically
towards a constant value for the base size approaching zero. Thus, Richardson extrap-
olation could not be applied. Both mesh studies of Zafiryadis and Hansen were using
a steady-state approach. Hansen concluded that the transient effects of the jet swirling
and any side-to-side motion were smeared out by the steady-state simulations, and the
fluctuations acted as noise instead. It was seen in the results, where the values were fluc-
tuating around the mean. If the noise was in the order of 2-5%, it exceeded the deviation
between the meshes in the asymptotic range. In other words, the transient phenomena
occurring inside a spray dryer were found to affect the possibility of doing a Richardson
extrapolation of the steady-state simulations.

somewhere in the flow field often takes the longest time. By activating a parcel fraction
cut-off, then these parcels are mitigated when the fraction is reached and the sub-stepping
is stopped. Often a value of 0.1% is used [70], and this was implemented only in the

DPM, it often occurs that more parcels are present in one partition than another. Thus,
the hardware is not utilised eﬀiciently. Instead, Lagrangian load balancing may be enabled,
so that the domain is partitioned using the Lagrangian load information. Here the default
value for the parcel weighting is 1, meaning that the relative weight of the parcel versus
cell favours the parcel. If set to 0, the load balancing is strictly Eulerian. Furthermore,
re-partitioning the domain is computationally expensive, and the default value for the
update frequency is rebalancing at every iteration. If the solution changes slowly, it is
insuﬀicient to rebalance at every iteration. Instead, a frequency of 10 was adopted [70].

function, it is divided into ranges of equal mass, volume, or number (depending on the
specification of the flow rate), and this defines the parcel sizes injected. The number of
parcel streams prescribes the number of aforementioned ranges [72]. Zafiryadis found that
for steady-state simulations a value of 800 was suﬀicient [83]. As illustrated in Figure 6, the
parcels are tracked from start to finish for steady-state simulations. Whereas for transient
simulations, new parcels are injected every time step, resulting in a high number of parcels
being continuously tracked as they sum up over time. As a result, the parcel stream value
can be significantly reduced for transient simulations compared to steady-state, and over
time, the total number of parcels is suﬀicient to statistically represent the particle size
distribution.

5.2.2.4
Identifying sources of error
The results of the steady-state simulations were not as expected; the REA should restrict
the evaporation rate compared to the MME, however, the results showed the opposite.
Thus, after correspondence with Volupe AB, it was decided to run a few iterations with
different values for some of the parameters to identify sources of error. The changes were;
−Reducing number of parcel streams from 800 to 10. From experience, Volupe AB
stated that a high number of parcel streams can sometimes cause problems. Further-
more, by decreasing the number of parcels, the simulation got closer to the single
particle setup, which worked as expected for some input parameters.
−Lowering the injected mass flow rate by a factor 10, i.e. from 3.38g/s to 0.338 g/s.
The hypothesis was that by reducing the mass flow rate of particle mass, the con-
tinuous phase would be able to cope better with calculating the correct evaporation
rate. If the relative humidity was close to 1 in any of the cells, this may have caused
the evaporation rates to be estimated erroneously.
−Setting the swirl to zero instead of 15,200 rpm. Advanced boundary conditions like
defining the swirl of the particles when injected may cause numerical instabilities
and errors. Hence, this was set to zero to see if this was the root of the error.
Of note, for the three iterations, it was only the parameter of interest that was altered, the
other values remained as default. Therefore, any cross effects were not identifiable. The
results of the Lagrangian mass source and humidity at the outlet for the three simulations
are stated in Table 8.
There were no differences to be found when contrasted to the
original setup, i.e. the REA kept sourcing more mass to the gas phase compared to the
MME, when in theory, it should have been the opposite.
In summary, the REA estimated a higher humidity of gas phase, but at the same time,
the REA particles hitting the wall also had a greater moisture content compared to MME.
There were no erros to be found in the Lagrangian mass and energy sources, and the
mass and energy balances were also within the computational errors expected for a CFD
simulation of this complexity. The next paragraphs compare the two evaporation models
using an unsteady time discretisation, and then Chapter 6.3 discusses further explanations
for the root cause of the REA evaporating more water.

5.2.3.2
Time step
In industry, two time scales are not often applied for spray drying simulations. The first
being a micro time scale defined using a characteristic distance near the inlet or atomizer
and a corresponding velocity scale. Hansen suggested using the radial distance between
the inner and outer diameter of the drying air inlet as the characteristic distance, which
was 25 mm. Using the flow rate of 12.2 kg/h, the corresponding velocity was 8.54 m/s. The
micro time scale was defined to be the ratio of these, and it came out to be Ts = 0.00293
s. Best practice is to use a time step smaller than the micro time scale. To be on the
safe side and based on the findings of Hansen [28], the time step used in this project was
∆t = 5 × 10−4, i.e. a factor 6 lower. As evident in Figure 47, the convective Courant
number was between 1 and 4 in the jet near the inlet and atomizer for the coarse mesh
using a base size of 16.8 mm. Outside the jet, the Courant number was well below 1. No
time step convergence studies were performed due to limited computational resources, and
this is suggested for future work.
The second time scale describes how long the simulation should run for, before convergence
is expected to be fulfilled. It is at macro scale, and it is uses the volume of the spray dryer
and volume flow rate of the inlet to determine how long it takes to change the air. Best
practice suggests the simulation to perform more than 2 filling times, and for the Niro
pilot plant spray dryer, the volume of the chamber was 1.26 m3. The volume flow rate was
0.143 m3/s, hence the macro time scale was 8.81 s. The flow properties listed in Figure 45,
showed that it took at least 7 air changes before the mean values settled to acceptable
levels.

The unsteady MME was solved using the coarsest mesh, and the results for the three

Particle mass through walls
Particles were allowed to escape through all walls, whereas the gas phase could only exit
through the outlet. The escape-condition for the particles was implemented to allow for
risk of deposits to be considered, and the total particle mass flow rate is shown in Figure 52.
Over 75% of the particles escaped through the side walls of the chamber, i.e. cone and
cylinder. The outlet was the second or third largest bin with 15-17% of the total flow
rate, and it was about a factor 10 larger than the outlet piping. This shows that most
of the particles which made it to the bottom of the chamber, at this point followed the
flow field of the gas phase. This is reasonable according to the equation for momentum
response time (Eq. (104)). The small particles near the end of the drying process had a
significantly smaller response time and followed the continuous phase. REA and MME
predicted similar flow rates, but as seen in Figure 53, which shows the liquid mass flow

particle density graphs later. Especially for the walls closest to the atomizer such as the
ceiling and cylinder had a higher water fraction, due to these particles quickly escaping
the jet. With the REA predicting a greater resistance to evaporation, it is reasonable the
REA particles had a higher moisture content.

Particle phase
Finally, the last paragraphs compares the data for the particles escaping through the walls.
Starting with the particle diameter distributions in Figure 56 for the cone and cylinder
walls. The distributions were close to identical for unsteady MME and REA, and this
was true for all walls of the domain. More particles were tracked for the steady-state, due
to the lower computational costs, and this provided more data points for the probability
curves. However, the figures showed that the bell shapes were smoother for the transient
simulations.
A hypothesis is that the transient simulations had a better accuracy for
predicting the particle trajectories, as the particles reacted upon the instantaneous motion
of the flow field. Whereas, for the steady state simulations, particles were injected to a
fixed flow field and tracked from start to end, and then the Eulerian phase was solved
with the updated Lagrangian sources. This caused the steady particles to only experience
a non-varying flow field, leading to more distinct particle trajectories and thereby a more
bumpy probability density curve for the diameter.

ulations were again similar to the steady-state results, and humidities were higher for
the REA. For the ceiling wall in (a), all models indicated a local maximum between
50% ≤XH2O ≤70%. This is important when evaluating risk of deposits. Second to last
were the particle temperatures, and the results are plotted in Figure 58 for the ceiling
and cone walls, and Figure 59 for the cylinder wall and outlet. For both unsteady simu-
lations, there was a large shift towards higher temperatures for all walls, and the spread
also lowered resulting in curves with sharper peaks and shorter tails. This meant that for
the cone wall, there were no differences between MME and REA. When comparing the
REA and MME steady-state, the temperature probability curves matched the expected
drying curves. MME had two significant peaks, due to the sharp s-profiled temperature
curve as discussed previously in Chapter 5.2.2. Fewer of the particles were present in the
domain in the middle between the droplet equilibrium evaporation temperature and bulk
temperature. Oppositely, the more gradual temperature curve of the REA model, resulted
in more particles with a temperature between the two peaks.

6
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results of the previous three sections, and a conclusion is in-
cluded at the end of the thesis to summarise the main points.
6.1
Numerical models
The numerical models were implemented, and by tweaking various parameters, it was
possible to achieve excellent correlation with the model predictions by the original authors.
Nevertheless, there was limited information available about what material properties were
used by the authors. For example, the drying curves in Figure 8 illustrated the sensitivity
to the thermal conductivity of the drying air, and best correlations were found when taking
the value at room temperature. Film temperature was also investigated, but the deviations
were too large at the start. Secondly, it is also relevant to identify if the properties are
dependent on certain flow parameters such as temperature. Computational costs increase
if more equations are added, hence it is a balance of computational affordability and
accuracy.
Adding temperature dependent properties may also improve the universality of the model,
making it more agile to various materials and drying conditions. Though, it was a strug-
gle to find reliable sources for material properties of substances like skim and whole milk
powder. These must be accurate as they are used for the predictions of the drying curves,
and if not known, a method could be to tweak these parameters in the numerical model
to match experimental values. However, one should be careful with extrapolating these, if
then used in a CFD spray drying simulation, where the particle sizes and thereby drying
kinetics may be different. Both the REA and Mezhericher relied on experimental cor-
relations (like Eq. (36)) for linking the vapour concentration density at the particle-gas
interface to the saturation water concentration through a fractionality constant. These
dependencies on experimental correlations and functions increase the accuracy, but si-
multaneously lower the agility of the models as they are only applicable to the specific
material and drying conditions. Furthermore, the REA used an experimental correlation
to describe the diameter change, and this was only valid for small drying velocities and a
narrow temperature spectrum.
There were no studies in literature that had successfully implemented the REA for colloidal
silica, and for the experiments in Chapter 4.4, Mezhericher’s model failed at computing the
second drying stage of colloidal silica. As a result, the numerical models can be applied to
both insoluble and dissolved droplets, but for some materials they are not able to predict
the drying kinetics as this may be too different from what they were originally developed
for.
Numerical modelling of drying kinetics for spray dryers
91
==PAGE:108==
When comparing the results of the simpler models like REA and MME with the more
computationally demanding Mezhericher, one may question when Mezherihcer’s model
should be applied. Results were very similar to the simpler models as Mezhericher e.g.
used the methodology of REA to find the fractionality coeﬀicient for milk. The radial
discretisation and complications of solving a fully implicit finite difference scheme with
fixed time-step and moving boundaries, led to substantially higher computational costs,
but the usefulness of Mezhericher lies in the possibilities of the radial discretisation. If
Fick’s law was implemented into the equations of Mezhericher, allowing for the moisture
concentration to be evaluated incrementally along the radius of the droplet, then one
could improve the accuracy of determining the transition from first to second stage. This
is relevant for larger particles like the ones used in the single droplet drying experiment,
as the Biot number was less than 0.1, which is the criterion for assuming a droplet lumped
heat capacity.
Yet, Mezhericher’s model did not estimate results that were much different from the REA.
All experiments for both skim milk and colloidal silica droplets showed that the radius
decreased until a crust and outer surface was formed, resulting in a fixed diameter. Here
it would be relevant to evaluate the moisture concentration at the outer surface, and the
second stage could be activated leading to better matching with the experimental data.
One may argue that this is only relevant for larger particles, and therefore not relevant for
spray dryers, where the particles sizes are much smaller than used in experimental setups.
Oppositely, harsh drying conditions can burn the outer surface of a small milk particle,
before the center moisture has evaporated. Thus, the temperature on the outer surface
may also be added as a criterion for the end of the first stage. Studies by Chew et al.
[15] and Osman et al. [57] suggested that a particle was dense if the Peclet number was
Pe ≪1 and hollow for Pe ≫1. For all particles in the experiments, the Peclet number
was 100 < Pe < 200, but it was not all particles that ended up being hollow. For example,
the colloidal silica particles were closer to a dense solid core. Furthermore, none of the
materials created smooth spherical particles, this indicated that the crust did not have
enough mechanical strength to withstand the inward capillary pressure, driven by the
liquid evaporation.
None of the mass transfer models considered radiation, as Lin and Chen estimated this
to have an influence of no more than 4% of the convective heat transfer [42]. With the
added complexity and the computational costs associated with ray-tracing, radiation was
deemed excessive hence excluded.
On the topic of stopping criterion. Figure 24(b) and 26(b) indicated that the radius of the
colloidal silica droplets stopped changing when Rd/Rd,0 ∼0.8. Other studies in literature
suggested the criterion to be when solid volume fraction was ϕs = 0.6 [3]. This would
depend on how the spheres would package themselves. For example, from material science
it is known that the packaging factor is 0.74 for a face-centered cubic and 0.68 for a body
centered cubic. This argues for the criterion to not only be dependent on the solid volume
fraction, but also the likelihood of materials organising themselves in certain patterns.
Even, if a radial concentration profile was implemented, the numerical models would fail

author’s have tried to use the pressure difference in Eq. (53) between the partial vapour
pressure at the interface, pv,i, and the pressure of the gas, pg, to identify if the internal
pressure exceeded the outer pressure and compared this to the tensile strength of the
crust. Figure 4 visualised 7 different morphologies that a dried particle may take, and

Experiments
There was a poor agreement between the experimental data and the model predictions,
and the following paragraphs covers the sources of error associated with the single droplet
drying experiment.
Initial experiments were carried out to map the performance of the fan, and results showed
the temperature differences between above the bypass plate and below the first hole plate
were 13◦C and 9◦C for the two flow rates. Moreover, temperatures were more than 75◦C
lower than what was specified on the heat gun. When running on level 6 out of 6, the
maximum speed attained in the channel was 2.5 m/s, which was 7.5 m/s less than specified
by the manufacturer. The results underline that the pressure head of the drying tower
was pushing the fan and heating element. Furthermore, the heat gun did not perform well
at the lowest temperature setting (50◦C) as there were random temperature spikes every
5 to 10 minutes. The spikes were up to 100◦C, and only lasted less than a second, but
they were observable in the temperature profiles as evident in Figure 62(a). For higher
temperatures, there were no issues found, and the heat gun performed well.
Even though, the model predictions were disparate from the experimental data, the ex-
periment was considered to be a success as it captured well the overall shape of the drying
curves for both skim milk and colloidal silica. The experiment and numerical model had
their own sources of error, and these could be improved to achieve a lower discrepancy.
Representative material properties and implementing a radial concentration profile would
enhance the accuracy of the model predictions. For the experiment, a greater droplet
diameter to glass rod ratio could be achieved with investigating different shapes and sizes
for the end of the rod. Due to the design of the experiment, the back side of the droplet
was not in contact with the drying air, and the thermocouple also perturbed the flow
around the body. Liquid was also climbing on the walls of the glass filament, and some
studies used water repellent surface coating to prevent the droplet from elevating. For
example, Vora used a Teflon coating to make the filament hydrophobic, and they made a
knob at the tip hydrophilic by coating with reconstituted whole milk before being baked
on a Bunsen burner [79].
Using a thinner thermocouple for the droplet would minimise the internal heating of the
droplet. However, with thinner thermocouples it is likely going to be harder to control
the position of the thermocouple, and they also break more easily when working with
and cleaned between experimental runs. The internal heating caused by the presence of
the thermocouple was also observed in the results for skim milk.
Blow ups happened
without the thermocouple too. In Figure 21(b), the boiling occurred 30 s later without

6.3.2
Implementing Mezhericher’s evaporation model in STAR-CCM+
It was not possible to implement the more advanced Mezhericher model in STAR-CCM+.
A discussion on why this was the case is summarised in the following paragraphs.
For Lagrangian multiphase simulations, STAR-CCM+ had three mass transfer models
available: (a) droplet / multiphase mixture evaporation, (b) REA spray drying evapora-
tion, and (c) user-defined evaporation. The first could be selected for all types of material
models (e.g. liquid, multi-component liquid, multi-component particle etc.). The second
was only available for Liquid-Solid-Gas mixture, and the third was limited to single-
component liquids. STAR-CCM+ did not allow the user to write their own evaporation
model for multi-component mixtures. A possible solution might be to use the user-defined
evaporation model for a single component liquid, and then tweak the parameters such
that the material properties would match the coherent droplet properties. If this was to
be implemented, then STAR-CCM+’s user code functionality must be applied. User code
is a framework available mainly for specifying more complex boundary conditions. The
user code is written in c++. It extracts the field functions from the solver and uses these
to calculate new values that are fed back into the solver as boundary conditions. The
work-around for implementing the user-defined evaporation model is writing a user code
that loops through all parcels and calculating the resultant evaporation rate according to
Mezhericher’s model. The corresponding diameter change is calculated by STAR-CCM+’s
Lagrangian solver and here it is not possible to specify the model used.
Nevertheless, to satisfy the numerical model, the user must be able to overwrite the di-
ameter change, which is critical during the 2nd stage where the crust is formed and outer
diameter is fixed but the mass transfer remains non-zero. According to Volupe AB, Smart

Expert and Siemens Platinum Solution Partner, and to the best of the author’s knowl-

diameter and temperature in STAR-CCM+. ANSYS Fluent has this functionality, but
Aerotak does not use this software, hence ANSYS Fluent was not considered for this

In summary, even with STAR-CCM+’s most advanced features, it is not possible to imple-

in terms of Lagrangian multiphase and drying for some time. Volupe AB said that the
option to allow for more input and more intricate models perhaps might be added in the
future, but it is not expected anytime soon.
6.3.3
Full spray dryer - steady-state
The spray dryer was first simulated steady-state using a pseudo time scale. This approach
gave results for the REA model that were not matching the theoretical expectations.
The outlet humidity was greater of the gas phase, while the REA particles had a higher
moisture content too compared to the MME. Several parameters were studied, and there
were no warnings to be found in neither the mass balance nor residuals. An alternative
approach for studying the conservation of mass, would be to study the total mass of the
system and not only the water vapor component. However, if one monitors the injected
and exiting mass, then one is likely to find that mass will accumulate over time, due to the
surplus of injected mass compared to the exiting mass. The reason being that as described
in Chapter 5.2.1.4, the active parcel fraction cut-off ratio was set to 1%, causing 1% of the
parcels and their mass to be removed from the domain. STAR-CCM+ does not currently
allow these cut-off parcels to be measured. Hence a potential mass accumulation would
vary from iteration to iteration, and it would be diﬀicult to distinguish whether any mass
conservation was due to numerical errors or the active parcel cut-off feature. For future
work, it would be beneficial to do a sensitivity study on the value of this active parcel
fraction cut-off.
A possible explanation of the bug was identified when running the single particle simulation
setup, using particle sizes closer to what is found in spray dryers. Chen and Lin’s [11]
model predictions were for an initial particle diameter of 2 mm, but the single particle
simulation showed in Figure 30 that mass kept evaporating for the steady-state REA
beyond the solid limit. When looking at the particle density curve in Figure 37(b) for
the particles escaping the outlet, there was shift towards lower diameters for the REA.
Oppositely, the humidity profiles in Figure 41 showed that the REA had more moisture.
These results are contradicting, and could only be achieved if the REA particle lost mass
other than vapour. Finally, when studying the humidity and temperature flow fields in
Figure 35 and 36, the most noticeable discrepancies were spotted near the outlet. Flow
fields were approximately equal near the top of the chamber, but dissimilarities became
more apparent closer to the outlet, as the particles here were the driest. In other words,
larger risk of the particles kept evaporating beyond the solid limit as seen in Figure 30.

6.3.4
Stochastic vs deterministic
CFD simulations were stochastic and not fully deterministic, meaning that the same mean
results were obtained no matter how many times the CFD simulations ran. However, if
studying an individual time frame, the results were likely to be different as the particles
were injected in a stochastic nature determined by the particle size distribution and number
of parcel streams. This is why the steady-state approach is relevant, as one is interested
in the mean and not the instantaneous flow fields. However, some transient phenomena
may not be accurately captured by the pseudo-transient approach such as the swaying of
the jet or particles following a fluctuating eddy, hence why there were differences in the
results between the steady and unsteady simulations for the full spray dryer. Whereas
for simpler simulations like the single-injected particle setup, the differences between the
transient and steady simulations were insignificant. Except for the REA, where a bug was
present in the code for the steady-state simulation.
6.3.5
Condensation
Both evaporation models allowed for condensation to occur, which is feasible. Dry particles
might be subjected to humid air and the particles would absorb some of the vapour.
Nonetheless, it was unclear, how the REA estimated the condensation rate, and a guess
would be that the source code of STAR-CCM+ reversed the model.
Thus, making it
equally demanding for moisture to condensate as it was to evaporate. Published research
on condensation of particles for the spray drying application is scarce to non-existing. A
hypothesis is that the condensation rate for a dry particle subjected to humid air is not
equal to the evaporation rate. An analogy can be drawn to a wet sponge, where it requires
increasing force to squeeze out more water, but once wetted again, it easily absorbs liquid.
Similarly for the skim milk droplets, the reason for the increased energy demand was due
to the moisture in the center having to travel through the porous structure providing
resistance to flow. However, at the periphery, the moisture content is low, hence it will
likely lead to rapid condensation rates, where the crust would be wetted from the outer
surface towards the center. These perspectives must be considered when studying the
results of the CFD simulations.
6.3.6
Computational costs
Replicating a spray dryer using mathematical equations and discretising these into smaller,
finite-sized volumes, requires many CPU cores with a lot of memory.
Therefore, the
computation times of the three simulations were evaluated, and the REA model was faster
than MME for both steady-state and transient simulations. Unsteady simulations took
3 times longer then steady-state to reach a converged solution, but the differences in the
flow fields were minimal. Depending on the resources available, it might be advantageous
to only perform steady-state spray drying simulations during a development project with
a client. Later, transient simulations can be implemented to check the results are similar.
Parallelisation may be improved, and one may also investigate the effects of the active
parcel fraction cut-off ratio. Thus, there exist opportunities for lowering the solving time
of the unsteady simulations.

7
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to analyse the distinctness of numerical drying kinetics models,
compare with experimental data, and implement into CFD-code. Two numerical evapora-
tion models, Reaction Engineering Approach (REA) and Mezhericher’s model, were im-
plemented in python, and results were verified by comparing to the model predictions by
the original authors and experimental measurements. The REA was found to be sensitive
to the thermal conductivity of the ambient drying gas, whereas the solver of Mezhericher
was diverging for some input parameters. The drying rate was driven by the contrast in
vapour concentrations, and the numerical models used the same experimental correlations
for describing the relations between the vapour concentration density at the particle-gas
interface and the saturation water concentration for droplets with dissolved or insoluble
solids.
A single droplet drying experiment was carried out using the glass filament technique,
where a single droplet was hanging from a glass filament string inside a drying channel
with hot air. Two materials were investigated; skim milk (20wt% and 40wt%) and colloidal
silica (10wt%). Drying temperatures of the bulk flow varied from 52◦C ≤Tb ≤152◦C,
and velocities were 1.9 m/s ≤ug ≤2.5 m/s. Experimental values did not match the
model predictions neither for skim milk nor colloidal silica, but it was proved useful at
characterising the drying behaviour of materials. Skim milk exhibited a gradual increase
in temperature, whereas for colloidal silica the jump was rapid, and this was captured
well by the glass filament experiment. Furthermore, it can be used for determining the
equilibrium evaporation temperature and the risk of particles blowing up.
Skim milk
droplets inflated for drying temperatures at Tb ≥118◦C, and the radius increased by up
to 30% greater than its original diameter.
CFD simulations were carried out for a Niro pilot-plant spray dryer using both steady-state
and unsteady modelling approaches. Two different evaporation models were compared -
Multiphase Mixture Evaporation (MME) and REA, and all simulations estimated similar
for oulet temperature, outlet humidity, and pressure drop. However, there were differences
in the particle phase and where the models predicted deposits to occur on the walls of
the chamber. This was attributed to the particle trajectories in the steady-state simula-
tions being resolved for a non-varying flow field. The steady-state CFD simulations also
identified a bug in the STAR-CCM+ software, where mass kept evaporating beyond the
solid limit of the particle when using the REA model steady-state. Finally, the computa-
tional costs for the transient simulations were three times greater than for the steady-state
simulations.

Future work
1. Implement Fick’s law into Mezhericher’s model to evaluate concentration gradient
across the droplet. Furthermore, improve the numerical stability of Mezhericher’s
solver to not diverge for some input parameters.
2. Research particle breakage models and how these may be used to predict the particle
morphology at the end of drying.
3. Find a thermocouple with a smaller diameter yet tough enough to not break during
the experimental procedure.
4. Improve experiment by using pressurised air or installing a dehumidifier to lower
the humidity of the drying air. If using a dehumidifier, one must install a humidity
sensor inside the drying channel or other ways to estimate the humidity of the bulk
flow. Furthermore, walls of the drying tower may be insulated to minimise the height
of the thermal boundary layer and the temperature difference between the outlet of
the heat gun to the top of the tower.
5. Run time step and parcel stream convergence study of transient CFD simulations.
6. Change the boundary conditions of the spray dryer, so that the particle residence
time lowers. This allows for greater discrepancies between evaporation models, hence
easier to identify the pros and cons of the two modelling approaches - MME and
REA.
7. Identify how REA calculates the condensation rate, and how this correlates to the
real world. The single droplet drying experiment may be reversed with a particle
being subjected to humid air instead.
