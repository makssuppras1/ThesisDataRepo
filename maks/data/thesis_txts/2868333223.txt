Abstract
With an ever-increasing demand for photovoltaics worldwide, fast and cost-effective
methods for properly analyzing the conditions and degradation of installed mod-
ules are needed for large-scale plants to reduce time, labor, and cost. One such
degradation is mechanically induced cracks, which can cause partial disconnects
in the module’s cells, reducing the module’s power generation or accelerating the
occurrence of other degradation types.
Models that utilize an imaging method called electroluminescence (EL) to predict
or simulate the photovoltaics module power have previously shown great accuracy.
Many papers use laboratory conditions for these images to minimize the error in the
applied models. This project aims to apply and validate these models for outdoor
field use, where the models were applied with EL images captured in non-ideal
outdoor imaging conditions.
Seven solar cell types (14 in total), with and without cracks, were manufactured and
characterized to investigate this. All investigated cells were mono-crystalline silicon
cells with varying busbars, sizes, and technologies, such as monofacial, bifacial, and
interdigitated back contact (IBC).
Photovoltaic parameters were found through current-voltage (IV) measurements for
healthy and degraded cells, where further parameters were found by curve fitting
the measured results into the single-diode model.
Using EL and the photovoltaic parameters, images of the cells were captured using
two different camera types: a high-resolution Silicon CMOS detector camera and
a low-resolution InGaAs detector camera. These cameras captured EL images for
five different imaging conditions. These included indoors with both cameras and
outdoors using the InGaAs camera in overcast, and sunny light conditions with
varying camera angles, increasing environmental noise sources.
The EL images were applied to an analytical model by Kropp et al. and a data-
driven non-linear regression model by Santamaría et al. With the Kropp model, a
calculated series resistance map could be generated from two captured EL images
for a cell, which was then used to simulate the power and power loss of the cells.
The model showed high precision results for three of the five tested imaging condi-
tions but would vary greatly between imaging conditions, with a maximum RMSE
variation of 0.048. The data-driven model could predict the power using features
extracted from the captured EL images, such as the number of busbars, histogram
standard deviation and mean, and the area of B- and C-type microcracks in the cell.
Santamaría’s model was less accurate than the Kropp model but only deviated in
the RMSE by max 0.013 between conditions, resulting in a suitable model for appli-
cation in outdoor field EL and use in photovoltaic condition analysis, as it appeared
less prone to environmental noise.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
The demand for photovoltaics (PV) in the industrial and private sectors has in-
creased in recent years, with prices going from 4.12 $/W to 0.22 $/W from 2008 to
2018, making it the cheapest source of electricity ever [1, 2]. This can be seen in the
amount of installed capacity, with an increase from 1.2 TW to 1.6 TW from 2022
to 2023 [3], and with an expected rise to 8.5 TW by 2050 [4].
While the price of photovoltaic technology is expected to decrease further, the main
contributor to this price decrease comes from the price of the modules themselves,
while other Balance of system (BOS) factors such as project costs, installation labor,
and operation and maintenance (O&M) labor decrease at a much slower rate, as
can be seen on fig. 1.1 [5]. The figure shows an expected decrease in total cost
for installation, with the modules contributing a 10 percentage point decrease from
2024 to 2032, and with only a 3 percentage point decrease for labor costs.
If the total system cost can be lowered further by streamlining the different BOS
factors, such as reducing the amount of labor needed during installation and O&M,
it could further reduce the time and money needed for new large-scale solar projects
in the future.
Photovoltaic cells and modules can have multiple defects, from defects that can occur
at the beginning of their life in the production line to defects that can occur and
worsen throughout their lifetime. These defects can affect the performance of the
solar panels and their lifetime, shortening the time before decommissioning. To find
the severity of these defects, the panel’s performance would have to be measured,
with different methods. Besides the performance, the type and amount of defects in
the panel are also relevant. There are many defect types, and not all can be detected
using the same methods.
The current methods for measuring solar panel performance can be long, time-
consuming processes that can cost both the consumer and the manufacturer in the
long run.
Current methods require that current-voltage (IV) characteristics are
either measured in-field using an IV-tracer [6] or that a panel be uninstalled and
transported into a laboratory in controlled conditions, where it can be flash-tested

1.1.2
Theory of Electroluminescence
Electroluminescence (EL) is a diagnostic tool used in photovoltaics to see defects
that may be invisible to the human eye. EL reverses how a solar panel works by
turning current into light instead of light into current, essentially turning it into an
LED. Since solar panels are made from semiconductor materials, such as polycrys-
talline or monocrystalline silicon (Si), the current generates excited electrons that
emit light through radiative recombination [17].
The wavelength of the emitted
light will peak close to the bandgap length of the given material, which for silicon

specifics of the different camera types will be discussed in section 1.2 and section 2.4.

which can have a large significance in outdoor EL imaging, where the signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR) can be low in high-irradiance scenarios.
1.1.3
Electroluminescence for defect detection
EL can be used to find various defects at the cell level as well as module level, such
as microcracks, finger failures, shunt faults, cell interconnection disconnects for the
cell level, PID, and full string disconnects or shunted bypass diodes for the module
level. [11]
As microcracks are the main defect investigated in this project, we will explore their
nature and consequences and how different severity levels may be classified.

Microcracks can appear in modules due to many different factors. They can already
be present from the module manufacturing, or develop and worsen throughout the
module lifetime during field deployment. The main reason microcracks appear in
the first place is due to the fragility of solar cells. This is because typical monocrys-
talline silicon is only around 150 µm thick [23], meaning that only a small amount of

Electroluminescence Inspection of Photovoltaic Systems

stress can crack the cell. In the manufacturing process, cracks can appear through-
out the entire process due to the many thermal and mechanical stresses that can
occur [13], such as soldering, where different thermal expansion between materials
occur, or during the lamination process, if procedures are not followed correctly
[13]. Moreover, improper panel handling during installation or transportation can
also generate cracks [7]. When a module is installed, cracking can appear throughout
its lifetime, such as through weather e.g. hail, or via thermal cycling [14].
Microcracks can vary a lot in size and severity, from small insignificant cracks to
covering large areas [24, 22]. These severities can be classified into three different
types, A-cracks, B-cracks, and C-cracks, and can be visually seen on fig. 1.5 [22].
Each mode represents the severity of the cracking. A-type cracks are visible cracks
that do not reduce the EL signal and, therefore, have little to no effect on the
cell performance but may develop into type B or C through stresses during field
deployment. B- and C-type cracks are partially and fully disconnected areas of the
cell, leading to little or no current being generated and, therefore, a measurable
decrease in the performance of the affected panel. Differentiating between B- and
C-type cracks requires an EL image taken at the cell short-circuit current (ISC) and
at a low (usually 10%) ISC, where B-type cracks will appear brighter than C-type at
low current, where C-type will appear completely dark in both images. This is due
to B-type only being partial disconnects, where some current may still flow. [22]
1.2
State-of-the-art electroluminescence
technology and methods
In this section, we go through state-of-the-art methods where electroluminescence
is used to quantify power loss and cell crack type classification and detection,
which have been studied through analytical techniques and with various data-driven
machine-learning models. These studies are useful as they turn EL images, normally
manually observed and analyzed by a human expert qualitatively, into something
quantitative and measurable. This may make EL a prime candidate for measuring

1.2.3
The Kropp series resistance and power loss model
A model created by Kropp et al. [8] has shown more promise in cases of severely
cracked cells [25]. Like the Haunschild model, the Kropp model can calculate a series
resistance map from 2 EL images at different current biases and uses the resulting
map to perform simulations to estimate the resulting power. Validation from sub-
sequent power measurements showed a large precision between measurements and
simulation of less than 4.3% [8], and has also shown great precision for severely
cracked cells [25]. A figure showing the series resistance and resulting power output
can be seen on fig. 1.7, where severely cracked areas are shown to have a high series
resistance and, therefore, result in a decrease in the module power. [8]

1.2.4
Data-driven power loss approaches
In recent years, data-driven models that run on different types of machine-learning
methods have also seen a rise in interest [30, 31, 27, 32, 13]. Much like the analytical
models, the data-driven models run on different EL images, in some cases for entire
modules [32], and sometimes just for cell level images [27]. There are many data-
driven models to choose from, such as convolutional neural network (CNN), random
forest, gradient boosting, and recurrent neural network (RNN), and while some
papers focus on which of the different methods are best [30], other papers also
choose to compare the same model type, such as CNN, but from other people’s
research, to see how the CNN models differ from paper to paper [13]. This includes
data-set size, specific customizations to the CNN, and data acquisition methods
[13].
Some of these models focus more on the severity of the microcracks themselves,
[13], which can be useful for automatically detecting severely damaged cells through
classification and segmentation [33], but does not give a direct power loss for the solar
panel. Although it requires an extra step and labor, power loss measurements of the
specific damaged cells could be performed, which could reduce the total amount of
panels that need to be measured.
Other papers have focused more directly on power loss by using polynomial regres-
sion, such as Karimi et al. [32], who created a model that through IV and EL data
on different solar module brands and Silicon cell types, with a total of 30 modules
with 60 cells each. This model is mainly focused on busbar corrosion, another type
of defect, where the model can predict the severity of the corrosion, as well as the
power loss that follows the degradation. By intentionally exposing the modules to
damp heat and thermal cycling, they could artificially degrade the cells, such that
they would contain cracks and busbar corrosion, which gave them a total of 195
different EL images, and a total of 11,700 individual cell images, giving them a
large set to train. The model was then trained with a supervised CNN model to
predict cell corrosion. A polynomial regression model was used to predict the power
and series resistances of the cells from the extracted features. The features that are
in focus for this paper are Median EL intensity (med), the fraction of dark pixels
(FDP), normalized busbar widths (NBBW), and busbar corrosion ratio (BBCR).
Their power prediction results can be seen on fig. 1.8.
In the end, their model can predict BBCR on selected cells with an accuracy of 94%,
and their power prediction model was able to predict power using the median and
FDP features with a R2 value of 0.88 and 0.87, respectively, only requiring an EL
image and IV-curve information. [32]
Because there are many types of solar modules, as well as the number of busbars
and cell types, such as monocrystalline-Si and multicrystalline-Si, creating an all-in-
one data-driven model can be a challenge, as there would be an enormous amount
of data needed to cover all cells and module types [34].
This also includes the
immense amount of hours sometimes used to artificially degrade the modules, with

1.2.5
Further advancements of EL power loss models
While the models mentioned above have shown good results depending on the ap-
plication, they all acquire their EL images with the same method: images taken
indoors using a high-resolution Si-CMOS camera and only imaging a few cells or
modules at any time.
With the global demand for photovoltaics increasing rapidly [3, 4], fast and precise
methods for imaging and diagnostics of large photovoltaic powerplants are needed
to save time and costs for the photovoltaics provider, and the customer. This means
reducing labor costs by acquiring EL images faster, such as outdoor drone imaging
during daylight [37, 15], and the ability to apply automatic and precise quantitative
modeling of the acquired images to save time on manual analysis of the images,
which can also be prone to human error. If these models that have already been
applied and validated for indoor EL images in controlled environments, can also be
applied for various outdoor conditions, then we are already a good step into reaching
these goals.

1.4
Project Objectives
The main goal of this thesis is to test previously applied analytical and data-driven
EL resistance map and power loss models and to investigate how the results from
the models vary with different EL imaging methods. To do this, we need to acquire
indoor and outdoor daylight EL images using two different camera types and 4
different lighting conditions, which we do for new and degraded cells of varying
cell types and technologies, such as varying busbars, or monofacial, bifacial and
interdigitated back contact (IBC) cells. To measure a loss in power, half of the
cells are intentionally degraded with microcracks, which have been applied through
mechanical stress, and the other half are newly manufactured cells of the same types,
which are used as reference.
The analysis of the EL images will include analytical and data-driven models to
estimate a power output through simulations, and the resulting power outputs will
be compared with the different cell types between the degraded cells and their re-
spective reference cells. The models will be applied for both the indoor and daylight
images, to see how the resulting simulations compare based on the imaging con-
ditions, as the daylight images will include lower resolutions, more environmental
noise, and different imaging angles. This includes images taken during daylight ir-
radiances, such as sunny and overcast weather, to see how varying irradiances affect
the images and the applied models. We wish to do this to validate the models for
various conditions, such as outdoor EL images captured during the day. Lastly,
critical points of the daylight imaging process will be analyzed to evaluate how they
affect the applied models.
Turned into bullet points, the project objectives can be summarized as such:
• Study different analytical and data-driven models for simulating and estimat-
ing power output.
• Collect EL images for new and degraded solar cells and panels in varying
daylight conditions to test and validate the models.
• Evaluate and compare the resulting simulations of these models using daylight

1.5
Project limitations
Due to the limited time available for this thesis, we need to constrain ourselves to
some realistic goals that we can reach within the available time frame. This leaves
us with many open options for further research within the described topic for the
future.
The first limitation is the use of solar cells. For this project, multiple different solar
cell types have been used and tested. However, only 1 of each type was tested, with
an undamaged reference cell and a corresponding degraded cell. To test consistency
and variability in the measured data, it would be ideal to have multiple samples of
the same cell types, to further investigate patterns that may emerge from certain
models/types. Furthermore, we limit ourselves by only investigating cell-level EL
instead of the more realistic module-level EL. This results in our images being of
higher resolution than may be available during field imaging. However, this will
be investigated later in the report via a down-sampling of the obtained images.
Module EL imaging could include other imaging methods, such as drone imaging,
which could also affect image quality, and is therefore an area of interest for the
future.
The weather and the seasons have also constrained the project. Due to the project
starting in late August, outdoor imaging conditions were more limited as the days
were getting shorter.
This means there is a possibility for more varied imaging
conditions when the sun will be out for longer, as well as higher in the sky.
Another limitation of the project is the degradation type. As mentioned in sec-
tion 1.1.1, many defects can occur in a PV cell, such as PID or busbar corrosion.
However, for this project, we will only focus on mechanically induced defects or
microcracks. Future studies could mean the modeling of other defects, or a combi-
nation of defects, such as PID and microcracks together.
Lastly, we are limited by the number of models studied in this project.
Many
different models, both analytical and data-driven, have been investigated using EL.
However, for the sake of this thesis, only two have been investigated: one analytical
and one data-driven. Further analysis of outdoor EL with other applied models
could be a possibility in the future.

2.4.1
Challenges of daylight EL imaging
Outdoor EL imaging is not new. However, it poses challenges that make it less
desirable than indoor imaging. There are more limitations on the equipment that
can be used and some special setups that may be needed to enhance image quality
and reduce noise, which will be discussed here. [7]
The main challenge with daylight EL inspection is the environmental noise, mainly
the light from the sun.
This is not a problem for indoor imaging, as they are
usually acquired inside a dark room where almost no light is present. Therefore,
the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for outdoor imaging is expected to be significantly
lower compared to outdoor imaging. There are many methods for increasing one’s
SNR. The first one to notice is shown on fig. 1.4, where silicon will emit light at
around 1150 nm when a current is applied to the panel. This is coincidentally at a
water vapor absorption band [15], as visualized by the AM 1.5 spectrum [16]. By
limiting the captured signal to be around the peak of the silicon emission spectrum
and within the water absorption band, which can be done using a bandpass filter at
around 1150 nm with a 50 nm width, the amount of noise can be reduced significantly
[15].
The light can also cause the camera equipment to cast shadows on the imaged solar
modules, resulting in incorrect post-processing of the final EL images [39]. When
the camera and solar module are at an angle relative to each other, the resulting
image may also become blurry if parts of the module further away are not properly
in focus or if the depth of field is narrow. An example of this can be seen on fig. 2.6
[7].

2.4.3
Outdoor EL imaging with modulation
There are generally two different methods for outdoor imaging panels, either with
a camera attached to a tripod or a drone, where different camera models can vary
[7]. Here, we focus on the InGaAs camera, the relevant camera used for daylight
imaging in this thesis.
A common method for daylight EL imaging is modulating EL imaging, a procedure
similar to lock-in thermography, which works on the principle of taking multiple
(tens to several hundred) images with a modulating current applied on the measured
panel, such that there is a constant switching of EL signal and background signal
[15, 37]. The concept of modulating EL imaging can be seen on fig. 2.9 [15, 20]. The
figure is an illustrative example of how one may acquire EL via modulation, where
the ratio between the camera framerate and the modulation is 2 to 1, meaning that
for each period in the modulation, 2 images are taken, the first being at an applied
bias, and the second at no applied bias, which acts as a background image which
can be used for noise subtraction.

Chapter 4
Model application and
characterization
Now that the desired images EL images have been captured and processed for the
different conditions, we can apply the final images to our models.
In this chapter, we go through the different applied models used to estimate the
power loss of the degraded cells. The chapter consists of two models: The Kropp
model [8] and a non-linear regression cross-validation data-driven model based by
Santamaría et al. [27]. The main goal for this chapter is to recreate these models
with our own obtained data and cell properties and to see how the different imaging
methods and imaging conditions affect the final results of the models, to see if
the different outdoor conditions perform better, worse, or similar to the indoor
conditions, where the noise from the environment, and the used equipment differ.
This way, we can validate if the applied models are suitable for application on
outdoor EL imaging.
Before the models can be applied, some properties from the earlier measurements
must be extracted, such as IV-curve parameters needed for the Kropp model or the
cracked cell area for the data-driven model. A description of how the models work is
then introduced, and finally, the results of the models are presented and discussed.
4.1
Application of Kropp’s series resistance
mapping and power simulation
In this section, we go through the Kropp model [8], how it maps a series resistance
to an EL image, and how we use this series resistance to estimate the power loss
of the cells. The paper uses an approach called ”EL power prediction of modules”
(ELMO), which can predict the power of a solar cell or module by using just two
EL images and their data sheet parameters. The ELMO method has two indepen-
dent models that can either be used for mechanically damaged modules as well as
PID-affected modules. However, for the relevance of this thesis, we will only focus
on the model relevant to mechanical damage.
The principle behind the series resistance mapping for the mechanically degraded
cells/modules is that none of the main parameters of the cell junction, such as ideal-
Evaluating Analytical and Data-Driven Module Power Loss Models for Daylight
Electroluminescence Inspection of Photovoltaic Systems
35
==PAGE:48==
ity factor n, saturation current density J0, and shunt resistance Rsh are unchanged
by the presence of cell disconnects or cracks, and that only the series resistance Rs
is changed. From this principle, it should be possible to create a series resistance
map, from just two EL images: one at low current (10% ISC in our case), and one
at high current (100% ISC), as well as the data sheet parameters for the reference
cell. [8]
Before we proceed to the Kropp model itself, we need some cell properties that we can
extract from the IV curve measurements. Afterward, we discuss the methodology for
obtaining the resistance maps from the EL images and, finally, how these resistance
maps can be used to predict the cell’s final power.

4.1.3
Kropp model power simulations for resistance maps
Now we are our resistance maps for all of our cells and their imaging conditions,
which we can apply to the power simulations by Kropp. The main principles of the
power model are shown visually on fig. 4.6.
We start with our resistance map found in section 4.1.2, which we then split into
several series resistance bins, which define our segments. The width of each bin is
simply the maximum series resistance over the number of segments: max(R′
s)/N.
Since some resistances are more present than others, we weigh each segment by their
share of the relative area of the cell. For reference cells, this would mean that there
is a large share of low resistances, while degraded cells would be more spread toward
the larger resistances:
Arelative = Asegment
Atotal
(4.5)
Since each segment is a range of resistances, the mean resistance for a segment is the
mean of all the counted resistances within the defined range. This mean resistance
is then applied to the single-diode model with the parameters found in the IV-curve
fitting from section 4.1.1, which is then weighed by the relative area. The single-
diode equation is solved using the python package pvlib [58], from which we obtain
the PMP for the simulated cell. This gives us the simulated power for that given
segment, and the process is repeated for all N segments. Finally, the sum of the
powers from all segments can be found, giving us our final simulated power for the
cell. For the Si resistance maps, the number of sections was set to N = 10, while
all of the InGaAs imaging conditions were set to N = 100, which is explained in
section 4.4. Initial results from the power simulations can be found in section 4.1.4,

Power simulation with changing segmentation
Another thing to investigate in the model is the number of segmentations done
during the power simulations. In Kropp’s original paper [8], they only performed
10 segmentations during the series resistance- and area segmentation for all of the
simulations. For this project, we investigated whether the amount of segments would
vary the model’s accuracy. This was first done on the Silicon series resistance maps,
similar to the equipment used in Kropp’s paper [8].
Here the segmentations were increased from 10 to 100 in steps of 10, and the re-
sulting mean simulation deviation for the amount of segmentations can be seen on
fig. 4.16 for the Si and indoor InGaAs simulations. Here we observe that the indoor
InGaAs simulations rapidly decrease the mean simulation deviation as we increase
the amount of segmentations, though with less change in error with each segmenta-
tion increase. On the other hand, we have the Si simulations that start low at just
10 segmentations, with a mean error of 4.82%, but slowly increase as the number of
segmentations is increased, with a mean error of 5.08% at 100 segmentations. While
the exact cause of this is unknown, we believe it stems from the large variation in
series resistance found in the InGaAs maps. Looking at fig. 4.17, we observe a sig-
nificant difference in the maximum calculated Rs, with the shown 4-busbar example
having a maximum Rs in the InGaAs map that is almost 5 times larger compared to
the Si map for the same cell. therefore, since there is a larger ’span’ in the amount
of available Rs values, the map needs to be segmented more, such that the bins that
each Rs is segmented into is more narrow, and therefore a more realistic depiction of
the Rs values for each given segment. However, we observe the inverse effect when
increasing the segments on the Si maps, where the error increases the more we nar-
row the Rs bins. It could be possible that the error of the InGaAs simulation will
also increase with an ever-increasing number of segmentations (100+), although,
this has not been investigated in this report.

Model performance comparison & evaluation
We have now investigated the different methods and model applications used for this
thesis, and what could be changed and investigated for further model improvements.
In this final section, we will do a final comparison between our applied Kropp model,
the two versions of Santamaría’s non-linear regression model, as well as compare our
results with the original papers [25, 27], before moving on to chapter 5, where we
will conclude this project.
Kropp model performance comparison
We first compare the results from our Kropp model with the one performed in
Santamaría’s thesis [25]. In the thesis, the Kropp model is tested on two types of
mini solar modules (3x3 cells) consisting of a 2-busbar mono- and multi-crystalline
silicon, where the Kropp model was applied at different degradation stages, with
an increasing amount of artificially induced cracks. In the final stage, the module
consists of a mix of cells with no damage, some with a few visible microcracks,
and some with large disconnected areas. Since we are only working with mono-
crystalline solar cells and a mix of reference and degraded cells, we will compare our
results with the ones obtained from the mono-crystalline module at the final stage
of degradation. The resulting Kropp resistance map of the module in the final stage
can be seen on fig. 4.24 [25]. For the thesis, indoor images with the same Nikon
D3500 used for this project were used.
In the thesis, the resulting Kropp power simulation gave a high accuracy for the
module, with a deviation of just 0.65%, and an RMSE of 0.11. Comparing this with
our own obtained results, we got the lowest deviation at 4.82% and the lowest RMSE
of 0.128 (not normalized), so while we get quite a high difference in the deviation, the
RMSE are similar. The difference could come from a few factors. First, we have that
our own data set contains many different solar cell types, whereas the minimodule
only contains 2-busbar cells. If the Kropp model has a higher inaccuracy for different
cell types (such as cell parameters or EL emission), this could affect the final result.
Another reason could be the inclusion of the undamaged cells in the module. As

Chapter 5
Conclusions
In this project, solar cell degradation through mechanically induced microcracks
has been studied to determine their effect on cell performance. This has been done
by manufacturing and measuring various solar cell types, differing in size, busbar,
and technology, and their measured performance with already-manufactured cells
with microcracks. Electroluminescent imaging was then performed on the cells for
five different imaging conditions, varying in equipment and experimental conditions,
such as imaging location and weather. based on information extracted from these
images, one analytical and two data-driven models have been tested to predict the
cells’ power performance. The goal was to test the performance of these models
with an increasing amount of possible sources of error stemming from the different
imaging conditions.
First, seven cell types were selected for study throughout this project, varying from
0 to 5 busbars. These cells were manufactured to be used as a reference, and seven
cells that had already been manufactured for a previous project [25] were used for
power loss, as these had been degraded with microcracks.
The selected and manufactured cells then had their IV curves measured using a solar
flasher such that key parameters of the cells, such as ISC, VOC, and PMP, could be
determined. Correction of the measured curves was required for correct parameter
determination. Further parameters from the IV curve were later extracted by fitting
the data with the single-diode equation, which was used for later model application.
Electroluminescent images of the cells were then performed, first with a high-resolution
Silicon CMOS camera on indoor conditions and afterward with an InGaAs camera
under four different conditions, one indoor and three outdoor. For the outdoor con-
ditions, one was during overcast weather, while the other two were during sunlight,
where one of these was taken with a camera angle. Modulation imaging was per-
formed to obtain optimal results for outdoor imaging. For all captured images, a
background image, an image at 10% ISC, and an image at 100% ISC was captured.
The goal was to study the effects of noise, such as light, and a camera angle on the
images, which could worsen their quality. The captured images were processed by
cropping, perspective-correcting, and background subtraction.
Before the model application was possible, more information from the measurements,

Electroluminescence Inspection of Photovoltaic Systems

such as the IV parameters and characteristics from the EL images, was needed. Two
types of IV curve fitting were tested, and one method using the single-diode equation
proved the best when applied to subsequent model applications. In the fitted IV
curve, a higher-than-expected series resistance was found, the cause of which is either
an error in the fitting method, unit conversion, or in the model application, but still
ended up with satisfying results when applied. Of the EL characteristics, three key
features were needed for the application of the data-driven model: the EL histogram
standard deviation, the EL histogram mean, and the B- and C-cracked area. Here,
slight improvements could be made for determining the B- and C-cracked areas,
such that the busbar and background are more properly subtracted from the final
area.
The model application could now be performed. First, the Kropp model was tested
[8], which could create a series resistance map of a solar cell, given a 10% and 100%
ISC image, and the found IV parameters from the fit. The obtained resistance maps
found a discrepancy between the two camera types, where the EL images captured
with the InGaAs camera could obtain series resistances over 1000 Ωcm2 in certain
disconnected areas. The direct cause of this is believed to stem from the exposure
time of the cameras, where the Silicon CMOS camera had a significantly higher
exposure time, which could lead to light dispersion in the final image. This would
lead to a smaller calculated series resistance for these areas.
Resolution down-
sampling of the images was also tested but resulted in a minimal increase in the
calculated series resistance.
After the series resistance mapping, power simulations of the maps could be per-
formed. The results were compared to the power obtained from the IV measure-
ments, where the mean deviation of each simulation for each imaging condition
could be found. The best-performing simulation was the EL images captured with
the Silicon CMOS camera, with a mean deviation of 4.82% for all tested cells and
an RMSE of 0.128. The worst performing imaging method was the InGaAs detector
images captured indoors, with a mean deviation of 8.53% and an RMSE of 0.209.
Given normalized values for the predicted and measured power, the RMSE changes
to 0.052 and 0.099 for the best and worst results, respectively.
The data set was also split into low, medium, and high cell degradation severity,
where a deviation and RMSE for each degradation were found. A lower RMSE was
found for higher degraded cells, but no significant change in the simulation deviation
was observed.
Next, we applied Santamaría’s non-linear regression model, where three features
were included: The number of busbars, histogram standard deviation, and the his-
togram mean or the area of the cracked B- and C-type cracks. The first model
that used the histogram mean was found to have the lowest RMSE of 0.087 and the
highest RMSE of 0.110, while the second one that used the B- and C-cracked area
had the lowest RMSE of 0.066 and a highest of 0.079. Therefore, the model that
used the B- and C-cracked area as a feature was deemed the best on all accounts.
Like the Kropp model, the data set was split into severity, as an increase in devi-
ation was observed for highly degraded cells. This split was performed on the B-
and C-cracked feature model and showed an RMSE for low and medium degraded
cells of 0.054 and 0.050, respectively, while the highly degraded cells were 0.168, a
66
Evaluating Analytical and Data-Driven Module Power Loss Models for Daylight
Electroluminescence Inspection of Photovoltaic Systems
==PAGE:79==
significantly higher value. Similarly, simulation deviation was shown to be 4.98%
and 5.75% for low and medium degradation, respectively, while being 27.62% for
the highly degraded cells.
Lastly, a comparison between the two models has been done. Overall, the Kropp
model has shown to be the best model for three of the five imaging conditions by
RMSE but worst for the other 2. Meanwhile, the non-linear model, which utilized
the B- and C-cracked area as a feature, had the lowest RMSE for the two remaining
conditions. While the non-linear regression model was not the best for most con-
ditions, results have shown minimal change in RMSE between imaging conditions,
suggesting that the model is less prone to environmental noise during imaging. We,
therefore, conclude that the non-linear regression model using the busbars, his-
togram standard deviation, and B- and C-cracked area as features is the best-suited
model for the problem investigated in this thesis. This is especially true for low-
to medium-degraded cells, where the model was the most accurate while showing
higher inaccuracies for high degradation.
For future studies, outdoor applications of these models could be applied to module-
level photovoltaics instead of just cell-level ones, as well as testing on a wider range
of cell types. Furthermore, EL images in more weather conditions could be acquired
for further testing. Since only two models have been tested for this project, more
models could be tested for use in field applications, including models that may focus
on another defect type, such as PID, or multiple defect types simultaneously.
