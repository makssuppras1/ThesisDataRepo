Abstract
Additive manufacturing is developing very rapidly and being used by several companies
mostly because it is particularly useful in small-quantity production runs and rapid
prototyping. At the company, where this master project was carried out SLS printing
is used to support design engineers and provide printed nylon parts for their prototypes.
Due to the long cooling time, currently, only a fraction of the maximum capacity can
be used. The present master thesis work is aiming to reduce the cooling time of SLS
printing without compromising on quality.
The project contains a brief review of all the additive manufacturing processes and
focuses more on the principles and suitable materials for SLS printing. Furthermore,
it provides an overview of Computed Tomography, its basic principles, and industrial
applications.
The thesis is investigating the feasibility of in-printed cooling channels within the pow-
der matrix. A thermal simulation was developed to predict cooling rate behavior with
diﬀerent cooling channel diameters, numbers, and locations within the print cake. Af-
ter the selection of the ideal parameters, a more advanced simulation was developed in
order to predict cooling time with diﬀerent print heights from 100 mm, currently used
on a daily basis, to 300 mm which could be the maximum print height of the printer
if the cycle time could be decreased.
Experimental investigations were carried out based on the simulation results. To iden-
tify quality deﬁciencies due to the faster cooling, a series of measurements were con-
ducted on the CT scans of the test parts. Diﬀerent approaches were used to discover
dimensional or geometrical inaccuracies compared to the normally printed parts. Due
to the small population of the test pieces, statistical tools like F-test and Normalized er-
ror calculations were necessarily applied to validate the results with a higher conﬁdence
level.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Additive manufacturing (AM) is developing very rapidly and being used by several
companies mostly because it is particularly useful in small-quantity production runs
and rapid prototyping. This chapter provides relevant information about Novo Nordisk
and the role of AM technologies in the company’s daily life, introduces the project
motivation both on the industrial and scientiﬁc level as well as it describes the research
questions of this master thesis.
1.1
Novo Nordisk
Novo Nordisk is a leading global healthcare company, founded in Denmark in 1923
with a focus on diabetes, and later it has become one of the world’s leading healthcare
companies. Today, beyond diabetes, Novo is using its capabilities to tackle needs in
serious metabolic diseases, such as obesity, and in rare blood and endocrine disorders.
Some of Novo’s great achievements in numbers:
• 50% of the world’s insulin supply is produced by Novo.
• 30 million people are using Novo diabetes care products everyday.
• 1 billion insulin pens have been produced.
• There are clinical trials conducted in more than 50 countries. [18]
Novo drugs are mostly delivered by injection devices developed by engineers in the
Device Delivery Solutions (DDS) department. The Prototype lab is part of DDS and
provides solutions to implement ideas, as their motto says "If you can dream it, we can
make it" - Walt Disney. The main focus area of the lab is to manufacture both metal
and plastic components for prototypes based on new ideas of design engineers. A well-
equipped workshop is available for that purpose, which includes several metalworking
machines and tools, DLP, SLA and SLS printers.
According to Jonas Tromborg Marquartsen, who is responsible for the additive manu-
facturing technologies at the Prototype Lab, Novo Nordisk saves around 400-600 DKK
on each in-house printed parts. This calculation is based on the average market price
of outsourced print requests. In 2019 more than 40 000 prototype parts were printed
in the toolshop, saving millions of DKK by having an internal printing shop.

1.2
Project motivation
The Prototype Lab in DDS delivers SLS printed prototypes on a day to day basis. Typ-
ically, design engineers receive ordered parts around 10am, and before 2pm engineers
must submit new part for overnight printing in order to ensure daily printing. This
leaves a four-hour window for designers to test and redesign parts for next iteration.
Currently, the SLS printer works with 1/3 of its maximum volume to enable day to day
delivery. The printing time with that capacity is approximately 8-10 hours, and the
cooling takes almost as long depending on the number of parts. The matrix is 170◦C
after printing, and it needs to cool below the glass transition point(≈60◦C) before
harvesting the parts in order to avoid deformation.
Industrial motivation
Currently the Prototype Lab is only utilizing 1/3 of printing capacity and engineers
get at best 4 hours, to assemble, test, conclude, redesign CAD before they must sub-
mit parts again, for next day iteration. In order to maintain delivery deadline, the
Lab occasionally breaks up the powder cake to decrease the cooling time, however,
this is not recommended by the supplier and in-house experience showed that it might
decrease quality to an extend that they can’t be used. This hazardous game is neces-
sary on demanding days to deliver every parts in time. Reduced cooling time in SLS
printing could eliminate the necessity of hazarding by breaking up the part cake and
it would make it possible to deliver good quality in time without taking evadable risks.
Furthermore, it provide ﬂexibility for the Prototype Lab that can be used to either:
• increase printing capacity, or
• increase the window the designers have, before they need to hand in CAD for
next day print job.
Scientiﬁc motivation
SLS is a state of the art technology with a wide range of potential. However, there
is only a handful of researches regarding the acceleration of cooling. Methodology for
reducing the cooling time, has not yet been explored.
Discovering the possibilities
of this area could enhance the utilization of SLS or even other powder bed fusion
technologies.
1.3
Scope of the project
The main goal of the project is to discover the possibility and limitations of decreasing
cooling time without compromising on quality. This could lead to an increased time-
window for design engineers between receiving ordered parts and uploading new ones.
It could also allow to increase capacity and deliver more parts if necessary. The project
scope is to investigate and identify the possibilities for achieving such reduction in
cooling time, including the applicability of diﬀerent cooling channels and orientations.

1.4
Research questions
The following section outlines a series of research questions and necessary actions to
be taken to guide the thesis during the project.
1. What kind of cooling is feasible to implement in a challenging environment
with high temperatures and powder with poor thermal properties?
In order to answer Research question 1, the following actions will be performed:
• Explore achievements and existing solutions in previous research papers.
• Predict cooling behavior and ﬁnd feasible layouts and channel parameters by
running Comsol Multhiphysics simulations and heat transfer calculations.
• Perform test with both regular print parameters and implemented cooling
system to validate simulations results.
2. Is it possible to decrease cooling time without compromising on geometrical
and dimensional quality?
In order to answer Research question 2, the following actions will be performed:
• Create a test part with frequently occurring features that - based on previous
experience of the Prototype Lab - often fail, if cooling is to rapid.
• Run a traditional print without any added cooling and perform CT scans
on parts to create a base of comparison.
• Demonstrate CT scans after test prints with new implemented cooling sys-
tem to compare the quality of the new parts to the traditionally printed test
parts.
3. On day to day delivery printing service is it possible to increase printing ca-
pacity while maintaining quality by introducing additional cooling?
In order to answer Research question 3, the following actions will be performed:
• Perform simulations and test prints with increased build height.
• Analyze the feasibility and limiting factors of increasing daily capacity.

State of the art
The following chapter is intended to give a brief introduction to the Additive Manu-
facturing techniques, subsequently elaborate the Selective Laser Sintering that is used
during this master thesis project. The second part of this chapter will introduce the
Computed Tomography scanners, principles and industrial applications which play a
signiﬁcant role in the quality inspection of the printed plastic parts at Novo Nordisk.
2.1
Additive manufacturing
In the 1980s, rapid prototyping (RP) was ﬁrst introduced to create three-dimensional
solid models. With the advancement of RP techniques, including the ability to produce
complex parts in an eﬃcient manner, the ﬁrst AM method was presented by researchers
at the University of Texas, Austin in 1986.
Several new AM processes have been
developed in the past 30 years. [23] [27]
Additive manufacturing is a computer controlled process that creates three dimensional
objects by depositing materials layer by layer. First, a digital 3D model is made using
computer-aided design (CAD) software and converted to a stereolithography (STL) ﬁle.
In this process, the drawing made in the CAD software is approximated by triangles,
then it is sliced into individual layers, which subsequently controls the hardware and
supply the toolpath G-code for a 3D printing machine(Figure 2.1). Depending on the
ﬁrmware software of the printer, the machine uses a speciﬁc algorithm to recreate the
model in the real world from the bottom slice to the top, until the object is complete.
Speciﬁc post-processing or ﬁnishing procedures can be necessary after certain AM
technologies. In the past few decades many areas, such as communication, imaging,
architecture and engineering have all undergone their own digital revolutions and now,
AM can do the same, bringing ﬂexibility and eﬃciency to manufacturing operations.

The ASTM International Technical Committee F42 on additive manufacturing tech-
nologies has issued an ISO 52900:2017 standard on process terminology. Based on that
additive manufacturing processes are classiﬁed into seven areas on the basis of: type
of materials used, deposition technique, and the way the material is fused or solidiﬁed
(Figure 2.2). [2]
Figure 2.2: Seven Additive Manufacturing processes along with classes of materials and method of
deposition. [19]
• Material extrusion: Generally, the material is deposited through an extruder onto
a printing platform. Typically, a thermoplastic ﬁlament is melted and extruded
through a hot end. However, viscous materials such as concrete, clay, organic
tissue, or food can also be used with the same process.

2.1. ADDITIVE MANUFACTURING
7
• Vat photopolymerization: The photopolymer resin is exposed to a laser beam or
digital light projector, which hardens the material. Vat photopolymerization is
mostly associated with thermoset plastics.
• Material jetting: The printheads spray a liquid material onto a substrate, which
is similar to the 2D inkjet process however, while inkjet printers work with single
layer, material jetting builds layer upon layer. Often with this technique, photo-
sensitive plastic resin (photopolymer) is the utilized material that is cured with
an ultraviolet (UV) light.
• Sheet lamination: This is a process where sheets of materials are stacked together
layer-by-layer, with the desired shape etched into each shape. The ﬁnal object is
then removed from the bounded sheets either by laser cutting or CNC machining.
This unique 3D printing process often used not only with paper, but also with
metals and plastics.
• Powder bed fusion: Within this process the energy source is either a laser or
electron beam, which is directed at the bed of powder to heat the particles until
they melt together. This technology is mostly associated with metals (mainly
titanium), or plastics (mainly nylon).
• Direct Energy Deposition: In which metal, as a powder or wire feedstock, is fed
in front of an energy source, such as an electron or laser beam, mounted on a
multiaxis robotic arm. The material is melted onto a substrate layer-by-layer.
Used with metals such as titanium and cobalt-chrome.
• Binder jetting: This process uses two materials; a powder based material and a
liquid bonding agent, which acts as an adhesive after deposited onto the powder
bed. Can be used with several materials such as gypsum, sand, glass, metal, and
a few others. [12]
2.1.1
Selective Laser Sintering
Selective Laser Sintering is a Power Bed Fusion (PBF) technique. PBF processes were
one of the ﬁrst commercialized AM processes, developed at the University of Texas.
PBF is a collective term which includes various AM processes such as SLS, selective
laser melting (SLM), electron beam melting (EBM) and multijet fusion (MJF). The
technologies can be distinguished by the type of materials they utilize and by the
type and amount of light used to sinter the particles on the powder bed. In all cases,
objects are built layer-by-layer through the use of thermal energy resulting from the
combination of increased temperature and the use of a light source. The feedstock is
powder in every cases, which provides a great beneﬁt because the loose powder particles
inside the bed act as a support, therefore, there is no need for extra support material.
[6] [8]
Basic principles of SLS
As Figure 2.3 illustrates the SLS system is comprised of six main parts such as the
building platform, upon which the 3D object is fabricated; a laser, responsible for
the sintering process; the Galvano mirrors, which project and direct the laser beam

to the correct printing locations; a powder reservoir platform or hopper, which holds
and distributes fresh powder onto the platform; a mechanical roller that spreads and

the unsintered powder material. [1]
Figure 2.3: Schematic representation of Selective Laser Sintering. [1]
Selective Laser Sintering uses a low-power laser beam as the power source to sinter
powdered material. The printer itself heats the powder, so the laser needs to provide
only a small increase in surface temperature of the powder to induce sintering. Sintering
is the process of forming a solid mass of material using heat without liquefying it. It
is very similar to the traditional photocopying, where the toner is melted to create an
image on the paper. The laser sintering process involves many diﬀerent steps such as
powder preparation, data preparation, part building, cooling, and breakout. [16]
Powder preparation includes the blend of the virgin and used powder and loading
it into the apparatus. Data preparation includes both designing the CAD ﬁles and
distributing the parts within the printing area. In SLS there can be even hundreds
of parts printing in the same cake, therefore, the location of the parts is extremely
important in order to achieve similar heat distribution in each layers of the print. This
is normally done by a support software in order to achieve better print quality.
To build the parts, a thin layer of powder is heated close to its melting point by heaters
and the laser is directed to draw a speciﬁc pattern onto the surface of the powder bed.
Once the ﬁrst layer is completed, a roller distributes a new layer of powder on top
of the previous one and the process continues until the building is completed. After
the printing is completed, the parts are buried in unsintered powder, and the part
cake comprising the sintered parts and the unsintered powder is allowed to cool as
the sintered polymer solidiﬁes. The build phase is followed by cool down of the whole
cake material, which takes at least as long as the build phase. Due to the high heat
capacity of the powder the temperature decreases slowly. Finally, in the breakout step,
the parts are harvested from the part cake. [20] [13] [24]
At Novo Nordisk the general rule is to let the cake cool as long as it was printed, which
is normally around 9-11 hours. This is the cooling time this master thesis is aimed to
improve.

Furthermore, it is the only technology for industrial quality control of parts with non-
accessible internal features or multi-material components. Dimensional CT metrology
is also useful for assembly quality control, since the geometry and dimensions of compo-
nents may diﬀer in disassembled and assembled state meaning that the assembly may

2.2.1
Types of CT scanners
A basic CT scanner consists of an X-ray emitting source, a moving system, a detector
and electronic as well as computational devices for data acquisition and elaboration.
Industrial CT scanners are fundamentally diﬀerent from medical scanners even though
they both based on the same physical and mathematical principles, the devices and
procedures are quite diﬀerent. Although both types of scanners need to take X-ray
projections from several angulations, in industrial CT scanners the object is rotated
in the beam path produced by a stationary X-ray source, while in medical CT it is
the opposite, the X- ray unit (source and detector) rotates around a stationary patient
(Figure
2.5). In addition the doses of radiation have to be limited in medical CT
in order to protect the patient, while industrial CT often calls for high penetration
power as there is a demand for measuring larger and thicker workpieces made of more
absorbing materials. Also resolution and accuracy requirements are diﬀerent, therefore,
scanning parameters usually diﬀer signiﬁcantly. [15] [3]
Figure 2.5: Examples of medical and industrial scanners. [3]
Another important distinction between diﬀerent types of CT scanners is based on the
X-ray detector. Depending on the type of detector used, CT scanners may be able to
detect only one or multiple slices per revolution (Figure 2.6). The X-ray detector can be
either a ﬂat panel detector (area detector consisting of a 2D array of pixels), or straight
or curved line detectors (consisting of a 1D array of pixels). The latter yields higher
accuracy, is more eﬃcient and resist higher X-ray energies allowing thicker objects to
be measured. However, the use of a line detector is more time consuming as only one
slice is measured during one rotation of the workpiece and in addition the object has
to be moved in the Y-direction for every new slice to be measured.

CT scans can be classiﬁed based on their X-ray source as well. For the common electron

therefor linear accelerator sources can be used for large and high absorbing parts. [4]
2.2.2
Basic principles of Computed Tomography
In principle, the generated X-rays propagate through the workpiece, and due to ma-
terial absorption or scattering the X-rays are attenuated. The amount of attenuation
depends on the length travelled in the absorbing material, the type of material and the
energy of the X-rays. The attenuation is measured from diﬀerent angular positions by
the X-ray detector, which captures the projection image from the X-rays that traversed
the workpiece.
Mathematical reconstruction of these projected images leads to a 3D voxel model. A
voxel is the 3D analogue of a pixel and the gray value of this pixel is an indicator for
the absorptivity of the material. Software plays a vital role for the reconstruction of
the volume model out of the acquired 2D projection images. Reconstruction is usually
done by ﬁltered back-projection, which is based on the mathematical Linear Integral
Transformation model. Subsequently, the voxel data can be post-processed to detect
the edges (segmentation), measure the dimensions and control quality of the workpiece.
[15] [3]

2.2.3
Industrial applications
Industrial application of CT scanning became frequent since the 1980s. At the be-
ginning it was mainly for material analysis and non-destructive testing because the
uncertainty of the measurement was around a few hundreds of millimetres. As both
hardware and software have developed, the metrological performances of industrial CT
systems have continuously improved and measurement uncertainty of few micrometres
has become possible. The ﬁrst commercial coordinate measuring system using a CT
sensor was presented in 2005. [15]
Since about 2005 the manufacturing industry started showing great interest in CT
technology for quality control purposes on many diﬀerent areas:
• The rising complexity of components and products, with more complex, often
internal, features that cannot be controlled on dimensions and tolerances in a
non-destructive way with tactile or optical CMMs. Control of complex assembled
products also call for CT: one need to look inside the assembled product to detect
mismatches like undesired gaps or collisions between individual components of the
assembly. Measuring the components of an assembly separately (even if within
speciﬁcations) does not guarantee proper functioning of the assembled system.
• Techniques like plastic injection molding with metallic inserts, two-component
injection molding or other production methods results yielding multi-material
components, which also requires CT for non-destructive quality inspections (ge-
ometry, dimensions, ﬁtting, porosity).
• New production methods, in particular additive techniques, made such internal
cavities possible, which cannot be produced with any traditional methods and
cannot be measured on tactile or optical coordinate measuring machines.
• Furthermore, CT has the unique possibility to apply for a double quality check in
one step and one measurement task: e.g. checking dimensional quality (precision)
and checking material quality (e.g. porosity, weld quality, etc.) can be done using
the same CT measuring data. [4]
Today, industrial CT scanning is used in many manufacturing areas, from quality con-
trol of large castings to inspection of micro-components. Some of the key applications
are: non-destructive testing, failure analysis, assembly analysis, reverse engineering and
dimensional metrology. The number of applications and number of installed scanners
in industry is growing rapidly. [15] [3]

quality on the upper half of the inner wall. Vertical direction is less complicated to
apply and carries less uncertainty, in addition the powder removal is easier in the

Discussion
This master thesis is a comprehensive project and touches many diﬀerent areas such
as thermal simulations, experimental tests and quality evaluations based on CT scans.
In the following chapter the major results and discoveries will be discussed in a way to
answer the Research Questions from Chapter 1.4.
5.1
Cooling layout based on simulations
Simulations predict that signiﬁcant decrease can be achieved in cooling time by imple-
menting cooling channels into the powder matrix.
According to the simulations, the channel diameter does not remarkably inﬂuence the
cooling rate. Therefore, the main motive to select the ideal size was to have it big
enough to ensure powder removability from the inside of the channel. Experiments
showed that the powder from a 10 mm channel is easily removable with a 2 mm
drill, therefore it was an adequate diameter.
The number of channels have a better impact, however the 4-channel-layout was favored
besides the 5 and 9 options. Even though 9 channel would provide more than an hour
diﬀerence in the cooling rate, it would highly limit the usability of the print area,
moreover, it would increase operation time for setting the cooling up. The diﬀerence
between 4 and 5 channels is less than half an hour (Figure 4.3), hence number 4 was
selected due to its less signiﬁcant space limitation.
The location of the cooling channels was another parameter to deﬁne and it was done
by running a number of simulations with diﬀerent channel locations in order to discover
the position with the best cooling rate.
5.2
Cooling times
After the right parameters were deﬁned, improved simulations were run to determine
the necessary cooling time with diﬀerent build heights. Figure 5.1 shows the result of
the simulation for diﬀerent heights. The cooling times with and without the channels
are illustrated and compared to each other. It clearly shows that the cooling time
can be signiﬁcantly decreased with implementing cooling channels to the powder cake
and circulate air through them. It can also be observed that the diﬀerence between
added cooling and without added cooling increases together with the build height. The
greatest ∆t can be achieved at the 300 mm, therefore, the system has the best proﬁt
there. Furthermore, the cooling time with the channels seems to follow a logarithmic
tendency and have less and less increment.

The driving force of the cooling in general is the temperature diﬀerence. The 20◦C air
is constantly circulating through the channels, therefore, the highest cooling rate can
be expected from there. The required cooling time increases with 1.3 h from 100 mm
to 150 mm high powder matrix, then 1.2 h from 150 mm to 200mm. However, after
200 mm the increment is only 0.6 h to 250 mm and from there to 300 mm it is 0.5 h.
It would decrease more if we went further. The main reason for that is the relation of
the hottest area and the channels. Speciﬁcally the relation of their position. On Figure
5.2 it is illustrated that with 100 mm build height, the hottest area would be along the
horizontal axis, while the cooling channels are vertical hence they are perpendicular
to each other.
The transition happens at 200 mm height, since the width of the
powder matrix is 200 mm as well, therefore, the hottest area is the middle. Above this
height the shape of the hot area elongates vertically and it becomes parallel with the
cooling channels. If the height was further increased, the relation would stay the same,
therefore, signiﬁcant changes in the cooling time can not be expected anymore.

The ﬁrst Research Question was: What kind of cooling is feasible to implement in a
challenging environment with high temperatures and powder with poor thermal proper-
ties?
Based on the previously presented results, it can be stated that in-printed cooling
channels with an optimized layout are feasible. That solution is able to decrease the
cooling time by 50% and even more with increased print heights. The higher the print
cake, the more beneﬁt the cooling system has.
5.3
Quality comparison of the Test parts to the Original
parts
The aim was to ﬁnd the answer for the second Research Question: Is it possible to
decrease cooling time without compromising on quality?.
Evaluating the precision of the SLS printer was not part of the project. The quality
evaluation was based on a comparison. The goal was to do the same what the printer
has been doing, but faster and without quality reduction. Chapter 4 introduced the
results of diﬀerent measured features.
To get a statistical overview of the quality measurements two tools were used to further
analyze the data: F-test and Normalized error calculation.
F-test is the common way of comparing variances between two samples and the result
is strictly based on the measurement data. This statistical tool showed around 50%
conformance between the population of the Test and the Original parts. To get more
conﬁdence about the results, the Normalized error calculation was performed on the
same data set. Since that tool takes the uncertainty into account, it is expected to
show higher conﬁdence. With that calculation the conformance level of the populations
increased from 50% to 83% leaving only the Cylinder diameter 1 and 2 behind. However,
Table 4.3 and Table 4.4 shows that the diameter of the faster cooled parts are almost
every cases in between 1.50 - 1.52 mm, only in 1 case it reaches 1.55 mm (Test 2 - Part
15, Table 4.4) while the slowly cooled parts reached 1.58 mm. The nominal value for
the diameter is 1.50 mm, therefore it can be stated that based on those measurement
are either the same or better than the original parts.
Conformance to the slowly cooled part does not necessarily mean good quality.
A
good example is the Rib thickness, which is a typically problematic feature in plastic
production in general. Conformance is achieved in between the fast and slow cooled
parts, however, if the numerical data is compared to the nominal expectations, then it
shows a 0.1 mm deviation from the 1.5 mm nominal value in general at one case even
0.16 mm. Another example is the Cylindricity 1 and 2 features from the same Table
4.3 and 4.4. It shows values from 0.16 mm to 0.20 mm.
Roundness was measured at 9 diﬀerent cross-sections and it was discovered that the
form error increases towards the top of the test parts. Besides the numerical values,
the statistical tools also highlighted that there is non-conformance in the roundness at
70 mm and 79 mm height. There are grooves around the top of the part and since
they further decrease the thickness, that complicates the printing, furthermore it makes

deformation in the top 10 mm of the test part. The parts in Test 2 performed better

5.4
Utilization of the saved time
This section partly answers Research Question 3: On day to day delivery printing ser-
vice is it possible to increase printing capacity while maintaining quality by introducing
additional cooling?. Further conclusion will be presented in Chapter 6.
In Chapter 4 the presented results clearly showed that the cooling time can be decreased
by 50% without compromising on quality. This extra time can be utilised in diﬀerent
ways.
The ﬁrst possibility is to reduce the cycle time therefore, increase the time gap from 4
to 7.5 hours between the part delivery and beginning of the printing (shown on Figure
5.3). This could give extra time for engineers to test, redesign and submit parts for
next iteration.

In Chapter 1.2 the problem was mentioned about the occasional necessity of breaking
up the printed cake in order to fasten up the cooling process. This happens because
the 10 hours print time is an average and it gets longer when there is an increased
demand for parts for the next day and the printer has to work with increased nesting
density. In order to deliver every piece within the promised hours, the Prototype Lab
needs to take the risk of quality reduction at some degree. The decreased cooling time
can solve this problem and ensure in time delivery without compromising on the part
quality.
Another utilisation of the extra time can be the extension of capacity. The printing
height can be increased by 50% and go from 100 mm to 150 mm. The test result in
Chapter 4.3 showed that the cooling time can be reduced from 13 to 6 hours. Figure

that set-up. The new cooling method allows to ﬁt within the 24 hours cycle, yet it only
gives 0.5 hour gap between part delivery and the start of the next printing job. This
is clearly not fortunate for design engineers who need daily iteration with new compo-

Furthermore, cylindrical parts with greater length than 100 mm does not need to be
tilted within the powder bed anymore and can be printed vertically, which provides a
better quality. [11]

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
This master thesis was focusing on decreasing the cooling time in SLS additive man-
ufacturing. In the process cycle the cooling time is as signiﬁcant as the print time
itself.
The initial approach was to print cooling channels into the powder matrix and circulate
cooling medium through them, by connecting to a vacuum chamber. Air was considered
as cooling medium for safety reasons and for its easy accessibility in the Prototype
Lab. Vacuuming the air through the channels was rather preferred than blowing the
ﬁne powder, because it can cause health problems in case of inhalation.
Vertical channel orientation was considered for multiple reasons. The powder had to be
removed from the inside of the channel and it is easier vertically due to the gravitational
support. There is safety layer around the powder matrix, therefore, it is not possible
to print the channels all the way to the boundary layer, therefore, the channels have to
be moved through the safety layers in order to connect them to the vacuum chamber.
In addition, the vertical movement of the channels cause less alteration in the powder
matrix than the horizontal.
Simpliﬁed simulations were designed to predict the inﬂuence of diameter, number and
location of the channels regarding the cooling time.
According to the results, the
diameter has the least impact among the others, therefore, the main motive for choosing
the size was to ensure powder removability. Even though, more channels could provide
better cooling rate, the high number of channels would highly limit the usability of the
print area, moreover, it would increase operation time for setting the cooling up. After
analyzing the results, 4 channels were selected for experimental tests, and their best
locations were identiﬁed through further simulations. During the experimental tests
setting up the cooling with 4 channels required 20-30 mins.
After the right parameters were deﬁned, improved simulations were designed to predict
the necessary cooling time with diﬀerent build heights.
Those simulations showed
promising results and the experimental tests later validated the ﬁndings. 50% reduction
in cooling time was achieved with 100 mm build height, which is the regular height for
daily printing at the company. The simulations further predicted even better relative
cooling time with the cooling channels than without them. The second experimental
test was 150 mm high and the cooling time decreased from 14.5 hours to 6 hours. The
greatest proﬁt of the cooling system would be at the maximum build height at 300 mm,
where, according to the simulations the 30 hours cooling time could be reduced to 8.4
hours.
The quality of the parts is an important factor, therefore, several measurements were
conducted to explore if any quality reduction has happened due to the faster cooling.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
A number of diﬀerent geometrical and dimensional features were measured on the
Original parts without the cooling and the Test parts with the fast cooling.
The
data were further analyzed with statistical tools such as F-test and Normalized error
measurement. Based on the observations, measurements and comparisons with the
statistical tools, the faster cooled test parts proved to be as good as - and in some
cases even better than - the slower cooled ones, therefore, it can be stated that the
cooling channels have no harmful eﬀect on the part quality.
The extra time saved on the cooling can be utilized diﬀerent ways. The cycle time
can be decreased for 100 mm build height and this could leave more time between
delivering the parts and submitting the new ones for next iteration. That would leave
more time for design engineers to test and re-design on the same day. However, this is
easier in theory than in real life, due to the extra factors that needs to be considered.
The print would require human presence when it is done, which is normally around
12-1 am currently. On the other side the print job can be started later, but that also
exceeds regular working time.
Another utilisation of the extra time can be the extension of capacity. The printing
height can be increased by 50 % and go from 100 mm to 150 mm and the cooling time
is 6.5 hours with the new cooling channels instead of 13 hours. That was proved by
the second experimental test. The down-side of this is the increased print time which
is 15 hours with that height. The process can ﬁt in the daily cycle, however, it would
reduce the gap time for design engineers between delivering the parts and submitting
the new ones to 0.5 hours.
If the delivery time could be increased from 1 day to 2 days, that would give enough time
to utilize the new cooling system with even 300 mm build height, since the estimated
print time is 30 hours, while the simulated cooling time with the channels is 8.5 hours.
That would leave 9.5 hours extra time within the 48 hours window.
The cooling
preparation takes 0.5 hours and cleaning the parts afterwards takes a few more, based
on the quantity of parts, but it would still be within the 2 days delivery. That would
mean a 50% increase in capacity with 300 mm build height in 2 days compared to
100 mm current daily delivery. In this way the new cooling system could provide the
highest beneﬁt for the company in times when increased capacity is required.
==PAGE:73==
CHAPTER7
Future work
The print times for diﬀerent heights mentioned in the thesis are only averages, and can
vary based on the nesting density. Besides that, there is some uncertainty in the print
time estimation of the machine, which - based on previous observations - can cause 1-2
hours deviation from the estimated time. The 2 factors together can occasionally result
extended print time such as 14 hours instead of 10 hours for 100 mm build height and
19 instead of 15 hours for 150 mm. In order to implement a daily cycle with 150 mm,
it is recommended to investigate the necessary print parameters and make sure to ﬁt
in a certain time limit.
Furthermore, the simulation can be improved in a manner to consider both sintered
and powder material.
During the project work the print cake was considered as a
homogeneous domain, a mix of both. However, a more accurate and reliable simulation
could be achieved by taking both of them into account separately in a proportional
way. The current simulation with the current set up predicts cooling time with around
3.9% nesting density which was the goal for the tests, but in reality there are diﬀerent
print demands each day, and it can aﬀect the cooling.
The experimental tests were conducted for 100 mm and 150 mm build height and only
once for both due to limited machine availability. Tests with 200 mm and 300 mm
are recommended to get more experimental data. Automation of taking the powder
cake out of the printer after completion and setting up the cooling without human
interactions would be a great next step for the project.
