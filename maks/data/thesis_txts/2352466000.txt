Abstract 
This project deals with heat transfer coefficient in a scanning rheometer called the VADER1000. The 
VADER1000s primary function is to deal with viscoelastic abilities of polymers. The problem for normal 
rheometers is the stretching of the filament, which generates heat in the filament. Jets containing nitrogen 
cool down this heat generated from the filament stretch. It is unknown how well the cooling jets effectiveness 
is. Therefore, in order to understand the cooling behavior, the Comsol Multiphysics 5.2A software is used for 
simulating this single-phase problem with laminar flow and heat transfer in fluids. This method is a classic 
CFD problem where the applied mesh is unstructured for 2D axis symmetry simulations as well as for 3D 
simulations. In 3D all simulations have been done using a twentieth part of the system. The parameters, 
which have been investigated most, are the flowrate, the filament radius, and inlet jet radius. The interval 
for the flow rate has been between 8

for thin radius, thereby a Coandă effect will occur, which is seen when the radius is about 2 		 and below.

With increased flow rate, the heat transfer coefficient increase linearly, while increasing inlet radius has a 
hyperbola effect on the heat transfer coefficient. However, both flowrate and inlet jet radius increase the

for the current chamber, it is not possible to derive a regression model for every flowrate, filament radius, 
and inlet jet size, unless a perfect Coandă effect cooling can replace the unstable swirls or these swirls could 
be more stable.

Literature 
2 Introduction  
Polymers are used around the globe for many purposes. Some are used in antifouling coating for the shipping 
industry, some are used for construction in products such as bottles, and new research has even suggested 
that polymers can be used as medicine. However, polymers have to be tested, and one machine that tests 
viscoelasticity for polymers is the VADER1000. VADER1000 is a newly patented machine, which heats up the 
polymer to the melting point and thus makes it possible to stretch the polymer[1]. By measuring the radius 
and the Hencky strain, it is possible to determine viscoelasticity of the specific polymer. Yet, by stretching, a 
filament heat is generated in the middle of the filament, like a stretched rubber band. This heat generation 
is very unpleasant since the viscoelasticity is highly temperature sensitive. Thus, VADER1000 varies from the 
normal FSR procedure, as this apparatus has a cooling method that is able to reduce the heat problem. By 
injecting nitrogen from jets, the slightly colder nitrogen enters the chamber and the filament is cooled. 
However, it is yet to be known how large an effect it will have on transporting the heat away from the 
stretched polymer. The main chamber is quite small and it is practically impossible to measure the effect of 
the cooling equipment. Instead, it is possible to use computational fluid dynamics to simulate the behavior 
of fluid flow and hence heat transfer coefficient. The simulating works by placing small geometries into large 
ones and thus calculating advanced flow and temperature models for every single small geometry and put it 
together to give an overall view of the fluid behavior in the system.  
The objective of this report is to define how the heat transfer coefficient is depending on different 
parameters. This report will deal with three parameters; jet inlet size, flowrate and filament radius. It is 
possible to describe the heat transfer coefficient qualitatively and quantitative. Thus, this report will go 
through some of the main subjects in the literature; afterward, the behavior of the system is tested in 2D by 
changing some of the parameters. This is followed up by testing the same parameters in a 3D analysis. The 
objective is to state the possibility of deriving a multiple variable regression that can describe the heat 
transfer coefficient to any values of the parameters within a given interval.

3.1 Conduction 
Conduction is an energy that is transported from one object to another through a medium, or from one 
medium to another without movement [2,3]. An illustration of conduction is seen in Figure 3-1, where a 
metal rod is heated up. The closer to the heating source, the higher the measured temperature will be. This 
means that energy is lost with increased distance.

3.2 Convection 
For energy convection, there are two types of convection, forced convection and natural convection (also 
called free convection) [3]. The two types of convection differ from conduction. It is only in fluids that 
convection can appear, while conduction can appear in solids as well as in fluids [2,3]. For understanding the 
two different types of convection, an illustration is shown in Figure 3-2. In the left part of the figure, an 
example of natural convection is seen. A vessel of water is heated up by fire, the water then starts to heat up 
near the bottom, letting the hotter water start to rise to the top of the batch. The cold water is then forced 
down to the bottom of the vessel, where the fire will heat it up, which forces the previous cold water to 
stream upwards. Therefore, it is said that it is internal forces that drive the circulation in the vessel [2,3].

4 Rheometry 
When polymers are formed into different products, such as bottles or medical equipment, the plastic is 
melted before casting. Thus, it is of interest to know these viscoelasticity abilities that the individual polymer 
has. Therefore, different kinds of rheometers have been developed in order to measure these viscoelastic 
abilities that different polymers have. In this section, a brief introduction to different kind of rheometers is 
described. These rheometers are not necessarily designed for polymers but also for other kinds of non-
Newtonian fluids as well.    
4.1 Münstedt tensile rheometer 
One method that has been used to measure viscoelasticity is the Münstedt tensile rheometer. An example 
of how it could be setup is shown in Figure 4-1. The Münstedt tensile rheometer works by having a polymer 
set between two pulling elements. The upper element is attached to a motor through a string which can pull 
the upper element, thus the filament can be stretched [6]. The pulling elements and polymer are placed in a 
vessel containing some kind of silicone oil. The silicone is heated up through a slit wall, where hot water flows 
in the slit and thus heats up the oil in a controlled environment. The idea is to melt all the crystal, so it 
becomes more elastic/fluid. When all the crystals are melted, the motor starts to pull the element up, 
consequently stretching the filament. The idea behind letting the polymer melt in a silicon oil based material 
is that polymer should not react nor diffuse into the oil.  A problem with this technique is that the polymer 
gets hotter the closer to the center of the polymer stretch, just like a rubber band, thus making it difficult to 
keep the temperature uniform in the vessel. The problem by having unregulated temperature is that the 
viscoelasticity is very temperature dependent [6] which then brings some uncertainty to these 
measurements, from this apparatus.

4.2 Filament stretching rheometer (FSR) 
A different approach for the Münstedt tensile rheometer is a filament stretch rheometer, FSR. An example 
of an FSR is seen in Figure 4-2.  The idea is that a polymer is dissolved in a solute so it is in the liquid phase 
during the experiment, which is performed at room temperature. Thus, the polymer is placed between two 
endplates [7,8], which can stretch the polymer, similar to the Münstedt tensile rheometer (page 10). 
 
Figure 4-2:  A schematic overview of FSR, with elements. To describe the individual elements, a quote from the creator of the figure 
is used [7] : “(a) fluid filament,(b) linear motor with one or two moving plates, (c) force transducer, (d) top endplate, (e) bottom 
endplate, (f) diameter sensor, (g) (optional) camera for observing profile near endplate, and (h) computer system for control and 
data acquisition.” This figure is not a definitive method of the scheme for an FSR since different configurations exist. 
The stretching of the polymer is controlled by an electrical system, which has a connection to a motor that 
has a predetermined velocity profile. It is not defined whether it is one or both plates that move. However, 
the time-dependent measurements are the diameter of the stretched filament and the force that is placed 
on one of the end plates, see c) in Figure 4-2[8]. The diameter can be measured with different devices such 
as laser micrometers that analyze video images of the stretched filament, or wire gauges [8].

4.3 Alternative Filament Stretching 
Another attempt for defining viscoelastic abilities is described by [9]. The procedure is similar to the FSR (page 
12), here the main change, compared to the previous section, is that the polymer is heated to a temperature, 
where all the crystals are melted, which is similar to the Münstedt tensile rheometer (10). Thus, one of the 
differences between the Münstedt tensile rheometer and this stretching rheometer is that the upper and 
lower pulling element is heating up like an oven, with a cobber chamber surrounding the sample in order to 
secure uniform temperature around the filament. One of the problems with the two previous rheometers is 
that the filament is stretched which means it has an unpleasant rubber band temperature effect. Therefore, 
a method of cooling is introduced.

cannot cool down the center filament without heating up the oil surrounding the polymer.

4.4 VADER 1000 
The VADER1000 (Versatile Accurate Deformation Extensional Rheometer) machine works a lot like the 
machine that was presented in at page 13, with the oven and nitrogen jets. The main difference between 
these two machines is that the feedback control is a closed loop, which means that the sagging problem may 
occur [1,9]. This problem is solved with a laser measurement, which has a wider scanning area than the 
machine from [9]. Thus, the VADER1000 is more productive, since there is a smaller amount of waste, and 
therefore a smaller waste of time. Figure 4-4 is an illustration of the VADER1000, where the control chamber

8.1 Structure of jets  
When a jet is injected into an infinity large environment the injected fluid, will go through some stages, which 
are illustrated in Figure 8-1. The first region after leaving the nozzle is the core region. The Core region is 
where the iconic spike is seen. Because of the injected fluid drag some of the surrounding fluid with it, the 
jet start to have a more smooth profile [17,18]. Thus, the injected fluid lose speed with larger distance which 
can be seen in Table 8-1, and by that the core region influence diminishes. After the core region comes the 
developing region, where the flow gets a bell-formed velocity profile. It starts in the mixing region where the 
velocity distribution is between the core and developing. The mixing region ends when the velocity profile is 
only containing the bell-shaped velocity profile. 
 
Figure 8-1: Idealized jet structure, through the three different stages. Edited from [18]   
When the jet is in the mixing region, the flow will start to oscillate, which will result in small vortices, which 
increases in the axial direction, which leads to a mixing of the ambient fluid and the jet fluid. This oscillation 
is called Kelvin-Helmholtz instability.  Thus, by increasing the vortex size, the Reynolds number will start to 
increase, not due to increased velocity, but to increased length size to a symbolized infinity. This will result 
in a breakdown of the core jet flow and the developing region will start. A picture of this phenomenon is seen

9 Computational Fluid Dynamics 
For this project, the investigation is done through computational fluid dynamics (CFD). CFD has the advantage 
to simulate cases that are either impossible to measure or to save time and money because the different 
kinds of experimental equipment are simulated and not build. This leads to an understanding of fluid 
behavior [22]. The idea is to get a reasonable solution in a reasonable time. In this thesis, the simulations are 
calculated in the multiphysics program named Comsol. There are other CFD programs as well such as Open 
Foam and CFX.   
The geometry of a system can often be complex. Frequently it may be because it is circular and there can be 
obstacles for fluids which complicate the calculation even for Navier-Stokes equation and equation of energy. 
Therefore, with CFD, small subdomains can be made. These subdomains set up small systems where it is 
assumed that these fluids are pure Newtonian fluids. These assumptions ensure the balances for the system 
to be more simple and easier to calculate [15,22]. Thus, with a large quantity of subdomains, a more accurate

15.6 Solution settings   
To compute the solutions that were desired for this project, different settings had to be used to solve these 
problems. The first setting was the tolerance that was set to be 107m. A fully coupled solution method was 
used, since it generates results faster, however, it takes more computational power. The method of iteration 
was changed along with the simulations. Sometimes the method with the Constant Newton scheme could 
be used, this method is a fast approach of computing compared to the other solver types that are available 
in Comsol, however, if the damping factor is too high, some solution disturbance can occur. Another used 
solver was the automatic highly nonlinear (Newton) which is a good initial value guess solver, to coarse mesh, 
and afterward, the solution method of the Constant Newton could be used.  
Two different techniques were used for these 3D simulations. The first one is through an auxiliary sweep, 
where a non-geometry variable can change like in a parametric sweep. However, in the auxiliary sweep, if 
the next step in the sample size were too high to be used for a further initial guess, then Comsol would change 
the parametric value to be between the steps and use that as an initial guess for computing the next step in 
the sample size. Thus, a method could be a low-velocity field which is easy to solve and thereby accelerates 
the flow rate slowly to the desired flow rate. Another method could be by using a high factoring scale on the 
viscosity and thereby slowly decrease it to its normal value. The problem with this method of the auxiliary 
sweep is that it has a tendency to crash when the step size or damping factor is too high. This makes the 
method useable for low jet Reynolds Number solutions and is called the “shotgun” method in this project 
since it was not available to foreseen when the simulation would crash.  
The other method of solution was the series solution, by letting a coarse mesh solution be an initial value to 
the next mesh, which was slightly finer and thereby systematically simulated the result until the desired mesh 
had generated the result.

20.2 Transient simulation 
Since many simulations have shown a strong indication that multiple steady state or unstable solutions can 
occur, an examination with time dependency simulations is made to see if there is some tendency in this 
being a plausibility. In the first transient simulation with different meshes, the setting was with low jet 
Reynolds number and filament radius is 2.5 		, shown in Figure 19-2. The outcome gives two almost 
identical velocity solutions but very different outcomes of the heat transfer coefficient. This could be an

is possible. The unstable solutions are best seen for smaller inlet jets, and even though it is only for a range 
of 1 second they still appear clearly. This unstable solution may also be a result of a bad meshing, or even

20.3 Influence of geometry 
This project has primarily only been dealing with two of the geometrical parameters. However, the 
subsequent studies have shown that either a reduction of the backward orientated fluid flow or better 
stabilization of the swirls will give a better cooling effect than the Coandă effect. This project has only dealt 
with the stabilization of the swirling around the filament by increasing the chamber radius. This has given a 
positive result; however, there is definitely some uncertainties in it. First, this has only been tested on three 
geometrics and could easily be lucky scenarios. Second, the mesh is not tested enough, even though the 
mesh settings are at a fine quality, and therefore it is possible to say that it has some stabilizing effect of  the 
jet. The indication is so good, so far, that it should be possible since it clearly tones down the swings from 
before the chamber radius was increased. Therefore, this increase may not be a total stabilization of the 
swirls, since some swings are seen for the results of those transient simulations. Nevertheless, it is very 
unclear how it affects the heat transfer coefficient, the low filament radius with high jet Reynolds number 
gave a higher heat transfer; however, this could easily be a mesh result as well. Consequently, this needs 
much further investigation, so no conclusion can be taken.    
Another geometry parameter that could be interesting to investigate is the lower filament plate arm. By 
examining short filament heights, i.e. high value of the filament radius, velocity profiles shows that the 
Coandă effect is the only present above the filament. It could be interesting to increase the height of the 
filament plate in order to avoid these instability swirls. It is possible that the swirls are still unstable, but it 
does not affect the heat transfer coefficient since it is cooled down by a direct Coandă effect. This means

21 Conclusion 
In this project, the objective was to define how each parameter affected the heat transfer coefficient for a 
stretched polymer by scanning rheometer, VADER1000, in order to make a regression model that could 
describe the heat transfer coefficient for each parameter, jet inlet radius, jet flow rate, and filament radius. 
These simulations were computed through the software Comsol Multiphysics, using unstructured mesh. The 
investigated filament size for this project was the largest type based on volume that VADER1000 can operate 
with at Danish Polymer Center. The filament was assumed stretched in a uniform cylindrical distribution. 
Which means that the radius was the same in the bottom of the stretched filament as well in the middle, 
opposite to a real case scenario where it will be thicker at the plates which are stuck to the filament in both 
ends. The computed simulations were made in 2D as well as in 3D.  
In the 2D section, the temperature difference between the stretched filament and the chamber were 
investigated, the result showed that ΔL had a neglectable influence on the heat transfer coefficient. Thus, 
the ΔL was excluded in the 3D simulations. 
In the 2D simulation, every investigated parameter was simulated to different flow rates since it was 
previously shown that increased flow rates had a linear tendency for the heat transfer coefficient, where the 
operational flow rate of 8

 to 14

[23]. In the 3D section, all three parameters were tested separately. 
Thus, the flowrate lead to a linear tendency, with the higher slopes correlating with high values of filament 
radii.   
The inlet investigation was made by using two different approaches for the 2D simulation. The first one was 
made to see if changes for the inlet slit would change the heat transfer coefficient if the Reynolds number 
were constant. Consequently, it is seen that the heat transfer coefficient exponentially decreased. Another 
approach in the 2D simulation was the method of changing the Reynolds number, by only sweeping at the 
outer radius of the slit. In this other aspect, it was by making the outer radius of the slit thinner, the heat 
transfer coefficient along with an increased jet Reynolds number. Similarly, in the 3D simulation, it was seen 
that the heat transfer coefficient was proportional to the inverse value of the inlet radius, %V.  
Thus, by examining the jet Reynolds number in 3D as well as in 2D, the jet Reynolds number was linear 
depended on of the flow rate, and reciprocal dependent of the inlet radius, which was similar to the heat 
transfer coefficient, thus indicating that the heat transfer coefficient has a direct correlation with the jet 
Reynolds Number. However, since it is possible to generate the same tendency in 2D with constant Reynolds 
number, it could mean that it instead was the average velocity of the inlet that had an influence on the 
desired cooling effect  
The most troublesome part of this project has been to investigate how the filament radius and thereby how 
the height of the filament affected the heat transfer coefficient. In the 2D simulation, resulted is almost 
smooth parabolic curve, here the heat transfer coefficient decreases with lower radius. However, in the 3D 
simulation, it is seen that the there is no logical tendency in the data set, even with small jet Reynolds 
number. Investigation of velocity profiles and streamlines indicates that formations of swirls around the 
filament when the radius is around 2.5 		 and higher. However, when the cooling is only dependent on a 
total Coandă effect, it seems as if the tendency of the heat transfer coefficient is smoother. The heat transfer
