Abstract
In this work, PVT and viscosity studies have been conducted for oil related system. As concerns
PVT studies, well-deﬁned oil-related hydrocarbon mixtures are used as relevant mixtures to
simulate the reservoir ﬂuids. The work is focused on the experimental determination of the
saturation pressure and liquid fraction through the constant mass expansion experiments in
the temperature range from (10 to 200) ◦C. The studied systems comprised a binary mixture
(methane/n-decane), a ternary mixture (methane/n-butane/n-dodecane) and also a multicompo-
nent mixture (methane/n-butane/n-octane/n-dodecane/n-hexadecane/n-eicosane). This binary,
ternary and multicomponent mixtures are used as a model to avoid the expensive costs of reservoir
samples and they can be used as a stepping stone to get a deeper insight into the more intricate
reservoir ﬂuids. The experimental results on saturation pressure and liquid fraction are compared
with calculation results obtained through three diﬀerent EoS models (Soave-Redlich-Kwong,
Peng-Robinson and PC-SAFT) in order to test their performance.
As concerns viscosity, a capillary viscometer based on the Hagen-Poiseuille principle is used.
Determination of the capillary constant is performed at temperatures from 25◦C to 200◦C and
pressures from 1 bar to 1400 bar by using n-decane and toluene, which allowed identifying the
minimum viscosity that can be measured by using this capillary. The viscosity of toluene is
measured at 25◦C and 50◦C in the pressure range from 100 bar up to 1400 bar and 600 bar up
to 1400 bar, where the viscosity of toluene is higher than 0.6 cP. A comparison of the obtained
results with existing literature data is presented.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Petroleum plays an important role in human daily life since it is a primary source of energy,
especially for human activities. The scarcity of hydrocarbons in the conventional reservoirs leads
the exploration trend to aim for deeper formations. Some of these reservoirs, for instance, the
ones located in the North Sea region, may be entombed as deep as seven kilometers from the sea
surface [1]. Due to this depth, the condition in which the hydrocarbons are stored is regarded as
high pressure and high temperature (HPHT). The characteristics and thermodynamic properties
of the hydrocarbons under these extreme conditions need to be well comprehended to be able
to generate a ﬁeld in an economically eﬃcient manner, while also maintaining the excellent
engineering practices.
In order to develop and validate models that may describe the characteristics of HPHT
reservoir ﬂuids better, the experimental measurement of the PVT properties of such systems is
required. One of the essential properties to be known is the saturation pressure. The knowledge of
the saturation pressure can signiﬁcantly increase the recovery by identifying the precise moment
to inject water to maintain reservoir pressure. Moreover, the knowledge of the liquid drop out
value below the saturation point pressure gives an idea on the reservoir pressure at which the
maximum condensation from a gas reservoir happens.
It is also important to get some insights into the viscosity to exploit the oil from a deep
reservoir as it signiﬁcantly aﬀects the ﬂow rate of the oil from the reservoir into the ground
surface and the oil recovery factor. Even small changes on the viscosity could result in a huge
impact on ﬂow properties. Due to the fact that viscosity data are not always available at HPHT
conditions, experimental measurement of the viscosity is required.

1.1
PVT
In contemplation of studying the phase equilibria of a system, the relation of the two phases
of gasses and liquids need further explanation. Phase is described as any homogeneous and
physically distinct part of a system which is isolated from other elements of the system by
deﬁnite bounding surfaces [2]. As an example, there are diﬀerent boundaries between ice and
water in which each substance is physically distinctive and homogeneous. To gain a better
understanding of the relationship between the pressure and the phase volumes at one or many
constant temperatures, the PVT is the particular experiment that principally used [3]. PVT is
an analysis to determine the phase behavior of reservoir ﬂuid and its properties through the
life of reservoir pressure and temperature [4]. From PVT study, one can acquire the value of
determining ﬂuid properties such as bubble or dew point pressure, formation volume factor, and
solution gas ratio [5]. The volume of liquid condensed from a gas reservoir is also an essential
element as it may give information in which pressure the maximum condensation can be obtained.
The information can also be applied to avoid near wellbore condensate blocking which can
considerably decrease the gas relative permeability [6].
There are several diﬀerent PVT experiments used depending on the ﬂuid type either oil
or gas. More precisely, for oil, there are constant mass expansion (CME) or ﬂash liberation,
diﬀerential liberation (DL), and separator test. While for gas, there are constant composition
expansion (CCE), constant volume depletion (CVD), and separator test [7]. Constant Mass
Expansion (CME) is a series of pressure-volume experiment in which the oil is set in a high-
pressure container under single phase condition at a certain interest temperature. The oil sample
is increased, and therefore, the volume of the ﬂuid is expanded causing the decrease of pressure
[8]. The pressure of the cell will be continuously reduced until the ﬁrst bubble appears. The
knowledge of the bubble point allows the prediction, the plan, and the handling of oil production
from reservoirs [9, 10]. It is marked as the pressure where the ﬁrst few molecules leave from the
liquid and form a small bubble of gas [2]. Further, the pressure is kept reduced stepwise where
the pressure and volume of the liquid are recorded and measured. The same procedure also
goes for the gas where the pressure is maintained depleted until the ﬁrst oil drop is formed. The
pressure at which the ﬁrst oil droplet occurs from the gas is deﬁned as dew point [11]. Both, the

The saturation pressure can be ascertained from the relation between pressure and vol-

depiction of the phase behavior. It is advantageous to determine the steps of ﬂuid production
from a reservoir to surface conditions and responsible for the decision of the production plan [13].
The behavior of multi-components system is more complex than the behavior of pure sub-
stance. Instead of a single line outlining the vapor-pressure curve, there is a broad region in
which two phases exist. This region is called the phase envelope or two-phase region where it
is conﬁned by a bubble point line and dew point line [2]. Inside the phase envelope, there is a
quality tie line which normally drawn by dashed lines. Each of the tie lines has its respective
number which informs the liquid volume percentage. The bubble point line is a line constructed
by a combination series of bubble points which separates the liquid phase and two phase region.
While the dew point line is a line that consists of a dew points combination which separates the

On of the purpose from the P-T diagram is to create reservoir ﬂuids classiﬁcation. The
classiﬁcation is essentially based on the location of initial reservoir temperature relative to the
phase envelope critical temperature. Petroleum reservoir ﬂuids are classiﬁed into ﬁve diﬀerent
types which are black oil, volatile oil, dry gas, wet gas, and retrograde gas [15]. Black oil mainly
consists of a heavy fraction of hydrocarbons and it is also called as the low shrinkage oil. The
initial reservoir condition of the low shrinkage oil is situated far from the bubble point line,
therefore, it can endure great pressure depletion before releasing the solution gas. For volatile
oil, the critical temperature is lower compared to black oil’s and close to the initial reservoir
temperature. Hence, it is also called as the high shrinkage oil which means it easily releases the
solution gas as the pressure decreases. The volatile oil is lighter than black oil and has more
intermediates and fewer heavy components.

Both the dry gas and wet gas are categorized as natural gas [16]. Dry gas mostly contains
methane and some intermediates. Dry gas has a critical point located far from the initial reservoir
condition, making it never precipitates condensate even at the surface where the pressure is low.
Similar to dry gas, the wet gas reservoir also has the initial reservoir temperature higher than the
cricondentherm. However the diﬀerence between dry gas and wet gas situated at the surface,
where wet gases precipitate liquid which is known as condensate. It happens as the wet gas has
more heavy fraction molecules thus making the phase envelope larger and has longer dew point
lines than the dry gas.
Retrograde gas is considered as a novel phenomena, as its critical point is located close to
the initial reservoir condition. Hence, many of retrograde gas reservoirs are near-critical ﬂuids.
The longer dew points line is causing the initial reservoir temperature to be placed in between
critical temperature and cricondentherm. If the reservoir pressure is reduced isothermally, it will
cross same quality lines twice. After the dew point line is passed, more liquid will be formed.
However, further reduction in the pressure will make the condensed liquid vaporized again and
eventually going into single gas phase at low pressure.

Viscosity
Viscosity is an essential property which plays a signiﬁcant role in the development of any
application involved ﬂuid ﬂow. It acts as a fundamental parameter to calculate dimensionless
groups such as Reynolds number and Prandtl number to get some insights into the transportation
of the ﬂuid. As a liquid ﬂow, it measures the quantity which describes the resistance of the ﬂuid
[17]. Viscosity is created by the collisions between the neighboring particles of the ﬂuid while
they are moving at diﬀerent velocities. Formally, viscosity describes the ratio of the shearing
stress to the velocity gradient of the ﬂuid. This is described by the following equation:
µ = τ
˙γ
(1.1)
Where µ is the viscosity of the ﬂuid, τ is the shear force and ˙γ is the shear rate. Depending
on the viscosity behavior as a function of shear stress, shear rate, and the deformation history
[18], a ﬂuid can be broadly described as Newtonian or non-Newtonian ﬂuid. The ﬁrst viscosity
experiment was conducted by Sir Issac Newton (1642–1726), who introduced the ﬂow behavior
of ﬂuid with a linear relationship between shear stress (mPa) and shear rate (1/s) as the Newton’s
Law of Viscosity in 1687. In the parallel plate viscosity experiment, the liquid is conﬁned into
two parallel plates which are located on the two sides with suﬃcient distance (h), and it is shown

Bingham plastic ﬂuid is the ﬂuid that has a linear relationship between the shear stress and

the dynamic viscosity µ to the mass density of the ﬂuid ρ. The SI physical unit of the kinematic
viscosity is m2/s while in the oil and gas industry, Stokes is used as the standard unit, where 1

2.2.1
Measurement Methods
The viscosity measurement is to measure the ﬂow resistance, which uses the strain rates in a
given instrument geometry and the corresponding stresses. In principle, there are several ways
proposed to measure the viscosity, which are described as the following [29]:
a. Dragging the ﬂuid ﬂow with the sliding surface or rotating surface.
b. Falling the body through the ﬂuid.
c. Forcing the ﬂuid ﬂow through the ﬁxed geometry, for example, capillary tube, annulus,
and a slit.
According to the three main methods for the viscosity measurement, the ﬂow resistance of
the ﬂuid is measured by the boundary force, torque or the pressure drop. Meanwhile, eﬄux time
is recorded when the body is fall through the ﬂuid. The Instruments that measure the viscosity of
the sample are so called viscometers. The viscosity is only performed when the sample is in the
single phase. There are mainly three kinds of viscometers, and each of them has its advantages
when performing the viscosity measurement, which are described as following [29]:
a.
Falling Body Viscometer
The theory of the falling body viscometer is by means of falling a body which has a
geometrical shape of either sphere or cylinder through the ﬂuid [29]. The ﬂuid is stationary in
the vertical falling tube and the measured body which has a known size and density is set in
the reference point (mark point) of the viscometer. Once the body has been released from the
reference point, it ﬂows through the liquid in the falling tube. In principle, the falling body keeps
accelerating until the viscous force combined with the buoyant force given by the ﬂuid balance
the net force of gravity. In this time, the velocity of the falling body has been reached its terminal
and maximum velocity. And the viscosity of the ﬂuid is measured by the eﬄux time of a falling
body (∆t) between the two reference points of the falling tube.

Rotational Viscometer
In general, one characteristic of the rotational viscometer is that it has the ability to operate

Apparatus Cleaning and Pressure Calibration
Prior to every experiment, the PVT cell needs to be cleaned twice by using toluene and ethanol.
The toluene was poured into the funnel (attached to V33) to let it ﬂow out through the metal tube
(attached to V41) to clean the dead volume section. While the toluene still ﬂowing out, the V4
valve was closed from the software, and the cell piston was moved slowly to expand the cell
volume. After the cell volume had reached 30 mL, the stirring was turned on to circulate the
toluene over the cell. In order to clean any substances left in the cell, therefore, every surface
area in the cell should be in contact with toluene. To help the bubble easier to be removed which
hindering the cleaning process, this procedure was done at 135◦angle, and stopping the stirring
as well as moving the piston back to push the bubble out. Finally, the piston was moved to
expand the volume to its full capacity. This procedure was done twice and left the cell soaked
with toluene for a night. For the second run, the funnel and metal tube were placed in other valve
inlets to make sure every section had been exposed with toluene. The similar procedure was also
conducted for the ethanol.
Before an experiment with a new mixture, the pressure transducer in the PVT cell needs to
be calibrated. Pressure is one of the sensitive parameters that can aﬀect the measurement result.
The pressure transducer may contribute to errors in the measurement since this device frequently
undergoes a wide range of pressure and temperature which can change its reading sensitivity. In
order to ensure that the pressure transducer can measure properly in every experiment, it must
be calibrated before an experiment is started. A SIKA gauge of 1500 bar pressure capacity is
employed in the calibration as a reference pressure gauge. The calibration should cover the

Binary and Ternary Mixture Preparation
The preparation of the binary and ternary mixtures is similar and can be performed in an external
high pressure cylinder. This is the case, since these two mixtures have saturation pressure around
391 bar and 317 bar, respectively at room temperature. The high pressure cell was preconditioned
by vacuuming it for a night to remove any contaminants. Prior to the mixing, liquid alkanes in the
binary and ternary mixture were degassed to removed the unwanted gas before mixed with other
components. For the binary mixture of methane/n-decane, the n-decane was ﬁrst poured into
the sample cell. While for the ternary mixture of methane-n-butane-n-dodecane, the n-dodecane
was ﬁrst put into the sample cell then followed by n-butane and the last component was methane.
In order to ﬁll the n-dodecane, which is in a liquid state under room condition, a burette was
connected to the sample chamber of the sample cell. The n-dodecane was poured into the burette

dodecane ﬂow out. The sample valve of the sample cell was opened and the n-dodecane in the
burette was let to ﬂow into the sample cell by the hydrostatic pressure diﬀerence. The volume
of n-dodecane introduced in the cell was observed from the burette reading scale which has an
accuracy of 0.1 mL. The outlet valve was closed and the sample cell was taken into the fume
hood for transferring the next components.

3.2.3
Measurement Procedure
Saturation Pressure Measurement
The measurement was started from high pressure where the mixture was in single phase. In
principle, the saturation point could be found by continuously decreasing the pressure (constant
pressure depletion) until the ﬁrst bubble or liquid drop is observed as the sign of phase changing
from single phase into two phases. In pursuance of saving the measurement time, the cell pres-
sure was decreased to maximum 3 bar from the saturation point which was noticed from a rapid
depletion test. Afterward, the piston movement mode was changed into constant ﬂow rate to
deplete more the cell pressure by expanding the cell volume slowly with the stirring switched on.
This constant ﬂow rate mode has more accurate measurement compared to the constant pressure
since the rate of the volume expansion can be adjusted. The slower the development goes, the
better the accuracy will be since the pressure change is not very rapid. Thus, no swabbing eﬀect
will be produced. The ﬂow rate was normally used between 0.5 mL/h to 2 mL/h, depending on
the compressibility of the mixture. This procedure was repeated three times and the image inside
the cell was taken at the saturation pressure. After 5 minutes waiting the stirrer was stopped.

3.2.5
Uncertainties
In principle, no physical quantity can be measured with a perfect certainty, and some errors
always occur during any measurement. The purpose of this section is to discuss the experiment
uncertainty analysis of a derived quantity.
• Mole Fraction
The uncertainty in the mole fraction can be resulted from the inaccurate value of liquid density.
During the preparation of binary and ternary mixture, an uncertainty occurred since the liquid
density value is obtained from NIST and PC-SAFT EoS for the pure compounds. The uncertainty
may be reduced by measuring the liquid density using the densimeter, which was completed as
in the liquid mixture (n-octane/n-dodecane/n-hexadecane/n-eicosane). While the uncertainty
in multi-component mixture lay during the mixing procedure where the mixing pressure and
temperature also were hardly maintained to be constant. This was the case since there was not
any available pump feasible to support the high saturation pressure (500 bar). Moreover, the
sample cell failed the leakage test conducted at 500 bar. There is also an uncertainty during the
transfer of methane and butane into the PVT cell. The uncertainty occurred due to the mass that
could be weighed was only the mass of the gasses stayed in the bottle. While the mass of butane
that was leaving the bottle might be diﬀerent since the environment pressure was not the same as
in the bottle at initial pressure. These uncertainties might lead to the diﬀerent mass value of the
gasses which can result in a diﬀerent mole fraction. The estimated uncertainty in mole fraction
is 0.003.
• Pressure, Volume and Temperature value
Every measuring device has an uncertainty which changes over time, thus it needs to be regularly
calibrated. The measuring devices in the PVT cell which may posses some degree of uncertainty
are the pressure transducer and the resistance thermometer. The temperature measured by the
Pt100 sensor which is taken as input may contain error. As well as the pressure transducer, which
also may contain an error due to harsh condition (high pressure and temperature) exposure. In
order to reduce the uncertainties, a pressure calibration with another gauge was conducted prior to
every experiment. There also lies uncertainty due to the undulation of ambient temperature which
may aﬀect the pressure reading. However, the latest risk can be minimized by incorporating the
circulating bath to keep the surrounding temperature constant.

Regarding the volume reading, the value is given based on the position of the piston and
thus independent of pressure. While the relation of pressure and volume of all materials mostly
depends on each other through its isothermal compressibility value. Hence, there is uncertainty in
volume reading which may be diﬀerent from the value derived from the sample’s compressibility.
• Liquid Fraction
Some uncertainties also lies at the measurement of liquid fraction. The crystal clear ﬂuid makes
the phase boundary determination diﬃcult to be deﬁned. The larger the cell volume, the higher
the error will take place since the ruler relates the volume measurement with the cell volume
given by the piston’s position. Three repetitions of measurement between diﬀerent users were
completed, in pursuance of reducing the uncertainty. In addition to that, a signiﬁcant error
may be given by diﬀerent setting or position of the camera. The liquid fraction determination
extensively depends on the photo taken during the volume steps of the CME procedure. Hence,
the correct measurement will be determined by the quality of the picture. The location of the
ruler of the photo will also give uncertainties to the measurement. The estimated uncertainty of
the liquid fraction is 5% of the measured volume.
• Saturation Pressure Determination
The sign of phase change occurence was not always clear. At some temperatures, the change
from monophasic into diphasic was giving diﬃcult establishment due to the low cloud intensity.
Hence, process and the starting point was hard to be determined. The uncertainty for the satura-
tion pressure determination is approximately around ± 0.2 bar for bubble point pressure and ± 1
bar for dew point pressure.

4.1
Experimental Set Up
In this project, a HPHT capillary viscometer made by Sanchez Technologies is used to measure
the viscosity at HPHT (high pressure high temperature) conditions. Figure 4.1 shows the
schematic of the viscosity set up in this project.
Figure 4.1: Schematic of the viscosity Set Up.
The system for measuring the viscosity contains an ISCO D-SERIES Pump Controller, a
sample cylinder, high pressure valves, a high pressure manual piston pump, a SIKA pressure
54
==PAGE:70==
Chapter 4. Viscosity
gauge, a capillary viscometer and a computer. Figure 4.2 shows a picture of the experiment set
up used during the experiment.
Figure 4.2: Picture of the viscosity set up
The ISCO pump is connected to the water side of the sample cell to control the pressure. The
valve V1 is used to open and close the path between the ISCO Pump and sample cylinder. With
the help of the ISCO Pump, pressure for the sample cell is gradually increased or decreased until
it has reached the targeted pressure value.
Sample is injected into the high pressure manual piston pump when the pressure on the ISCO
pump is stable. High pressure valves V2 and V3 are used to control the inlet and outlet of the
manual piston pump, while valve V4 controls the inlet of the viscometer. Worth mention is that
V4 is a three way valve so that the outlet path which is free of connection plays an important
role when cleaning the viscometer, as it is the path to remove the waste sample.
Valves to control the inlet and outlet paths for the capillary viscometer are EV-2 and EV-4.
To avoid the pressure leaking during the measurement, these two valves are required to be closed.
Meanwhile, valves EV22 and EV44 are used to control the inlet and outlet of the capillary tube
inside the viscometer. In this system, computer plays the most important role as it monitors the
state of the valves and the pressure condition, meanwhile it controls the temperature condition of
experiment and it allows to program the measurement.
• ISCO Pump
ISCO pump is a syringe pump which pumps the water into the cell to ﬁll the system. The
maximum pressure that the ISCO pump could support is 750 bar. It has two valves which are

piston cylinder to the water side of the sample cell. ISCO pump plays an important role in the

4.3.1
Preparing Work of the Viscosity Measurement
• Calculating the Reynolds Number
Capillary viscometer is based on the Hagen-Poiseuille equation, which involves the laminar
ﬂow through a long cylindrical pipe in a small constant cross section area. Therefore, Reynolds
number is determined to guarantee the ﬂow is laminar. During the experiment, pressure drop
should be higher than 0.5 bar, otherwise, the measurement result is not accurate. One way
to increase the measuring delta pressure is to decrease the radius or increase the length of
the capillary tube. Laminar ﬂow occurs as the Reynolds number is below a critical value of
approximately 2,040 [42]. To make sure the experiment follows the Hagen-Poiseuille equation,
the Reynolds number is calculated as:

4.3.4
Further Improvement and Suggestion
During the measurement of viscosity, many problems have been encountered. The viscometer
always leaking at the highest pressure of 1400 bar, and leaking problem becomes more serious at
high temperatures such as 200 ◦C. In this study, leaking problem is mainly caused by the break
of the O-ring, an important part of the piston inside the viscometer. The break of the O-ring is
mainly caused by the friction of the O-ring with the piston when making the injecting and ﬁlling
procedure during the measurement. Moreover, when the operation of the experiment is under
high pressure and high temperature conditions, O-ring tend to break very fast. Once O-ring has
been seriously damaged, pressure suddenly drops out and all the measurements must stop. To
solve this problem, one way is to change the O-ring material which might have better quality
under the HPHT conditions.
Moreover, it can be seen that, K factor and viscosity results of toluene by using the capillary
which has a length of 9.262 m and a diameter of 0.5 mm are not so good at high temperature
conditions. One improvement is to change the type of capillary tube, which might have a smaller
diameter and larger length, so that the pressure drop measured by the capillary viscometer could
be larger. The available diameter of the capillary tube will be 0.25 mm if still measuring the
viscosity of toluene at the maximum temperature of 200 ◦C and ﬂow rate of 5 mL/min. The

Conclusions and Recommendations
CME experiments had been performed for three diﬀerent mixtures. Through this study, saturation
pressure and liquid fraction data for the binary mixture is reported in the temperature range from
104 ◦C to 150 ◦C. Whereas in the temperature range from 10 ◦C to 200 ◦C for ternary mixture
and multi-component mixture. Both bubble and dew points were found for the binary system.
From the liquid drop out measurement, the critical temperature for this binary system should be
between 125 ◦C and 130 ◦C. For the ternary and multi-component system, only dew points were
found through the course of experiments. These ﬁndings were conﬁrmed by the liquid fraction
measurements. The maximum liquid fraction values were obtained at 10 ◦C for both ternary and
multi-component system which were measured around 30%. While for the binary mixture, the
maximum liquid condensation was occurred at 130 ◦C which the closest studied temperature
above the critical temperature. For the methane/n-decane system, a good agreement is found
between the obtained results and previously reported experimental data which yields AADs% of
0.11% from Rijkers et al. and 0.37% from Reamer et al.
As concerns the modeling of the saturation pressure, PC-SAFT model is found to have the
lowest AAD% value (5.66%) for the three systems followed by PR model (7.71%) and SRK
(14.02%). PC-SAFT and PR have a similar AAD% value for the multi-component system
which is around 4.4%. Meanwhile, SRK model has the highest AAD% value (15.01%) for the
three systems and a large discrepancy from PR and PC-SAFT. Additionally, SRK shows higher
deviations at high temperature, while PC-SAFT model tends to have negative deviations at high
temperatures.
