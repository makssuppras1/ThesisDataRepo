Analysis of charging needs in the municipality of 
Copenhagen based on parking data  
 
Kyriakos Michailidis 
Department of Technology Management and Economics, MSc in Transport and Logistics, DTU 
Denmark, July 2022 
 
Abstract 
This thesis will give the reader insights into the parking patterns and their evolution in the municipality of 
Copenhagen over a time span of 10 years (2012-2021). Connect them with relevant data and estimate the 
number of chargers at a detailed zone level. It proposes a strategic and data-driven approach to estimate the 
average charging infrastructure required. An attempt to model the results will be presented, followed by 
insights on modelling at agent level. Finally, together with the overview of the results, feedback is given on 
the method of the data-driven approach for linking parking and charging demand.  
Keywords: parking; demand; charging; Copenhagen; strategic analysis; simulation.

1 Introduction 
Parking infrastructure is one of the most important aspects of a city. Despite the transport fleet is shifting 
slowly from fossil fuel engines to electric, the number of private vehicles continues to increase [1]. This 
implies that the upcoming amount of vehicles needs to be predicted and  accommodated. Alongside with 
the new vehicles being placed strategically within a city, the charging infrastructure needs to be decided 
and planned carefully to allocate and satisfy the charging needs of the increasing fleet of electric vehicles. 
This includes deciding the type of charging infrastructure to the corresponding locations in order to 
estimate the power needs which will then define the investments that need to be made in each respective 
area of a city. It is certain on the other hand, that the type of parking(residential/overnight, service-
oriented/short-term, place of employment/medium term) will be defining the type of charging and vice 
versa.  
Creating and allocating correctly parking spots a city can avoid traffic created by “cruising for parking” 
[2]. The search for a parking spot can lead to extra noise and emissions especially from ICE (internal 
combustion engine) vehicles due to low speeds. It can be however argued that as the technology for motor 
batteries is improving, the ICE vehicles will be phased out and together with these, the local emissions will 
be eradicated, and the noise reduced. However, the benefits of creating the proper amount of parking 
spaces saves a significant amount of energy and time for the users of private cars. Finally, cities and 
investment institutions need to have tools that allow them to estimate and decide the type and location of 
the charging infrastructure within a city. 
This thesis focuses on Copenhagen. There is a plan for a carbon neutral city by 2025 [3], the electric 
vehicle fleet shows increase, thus the charging and parking needs of these need to be predicted. Currently, 
there is a significant amount of chargers scattered across the city and many houses have already installed 
private chargers. However, there is a need for the chargers and parking spots to be carefully allocated 
within the city so there is both cover for the type and amount of demand. For this reason, relevant maps 
have been produced both in the main context and the appendices to visualize the existing parking 
infrastructure and some of the calculated variables. The target of this thesis is to detect some parking 
patterns and connect them to the charging needs  
Based on data provided by Statistics Denmark the number of commuters by rail has dropped during the 
pandemic and at least in the first quarter of 2022, a period during which all of the COVID19 restrictions 
were uplifted, the number does not seem to return to pre-COVID19 levels [4]. This becomes more apparent 
with the increase in the stock of passenger cars [1] since 2019. These two patterns together with the general 
reduction in electric car prices to normal levels, show that the increase in fleet of cars in the short term is 
imminent. Moreover, physical space becomes even more important inside cities therefore, estimating the 
proper amount of parking spots can save up space that could be used as possibly “green” space. 
In Copenhagen, parking is not a major concern since it has been under a lot of regulations and is relatively 
easy to find a parking spot. However, analyzing the parking patterns and making an initial estimation of the 
charging needs can provide useful insights for future research and the municipality. Moreover, the 
methodologies that will be proposed in a later chapter can further assist researchers or interested parties in 
creating a complete predictive and area specific model.

1.1 Literature review 
Electric vehicle ownership is on the rise[1], [5]. This tendency is backed up by many different factors that 
can be separated into 3 large categories. Technology improvement, economic motives and policies and 
environmental. These factors affect each other and are backed up or dependent on each. This thesis is 
focusing on analysing the parking patterns and connecting them with the charging needs, hence it is 
affected mostly by the economic motives and policies and technology improvement categories. On the one 
hand, policy making is the one affecting parking space creation and the cost of it. It can also give different 
incentives to private companies to create charging infrastructure throughout the city. This public-private 
sector cooperation is boosting the EV penetration into the main traffic mixture since it provides a mixture 
of policies and incentives that makes EV’s and the subsequent charging attractive to customers.  On the 
other hand, this increase creates additional energy demand. However, the transition towards renewable 
energy sources renders prices to become lower but supply to become volatile [6], [7] in combination with 
the rising energy demands of electric vehicles. This makes the need for predicting charging needs 
imperative, especially in Copenhagen where the green transition is targeted to happen before 2030 [3]. 
Specifically, in the current project charging needs are estimated through data and not traditionally modelled 
on agent level. Most of the available literature follows an agent driven approach where parking generation 
is modelled individually and locally [8]–[11]. That way parking demand can be predicted in some extent 
however there are some implications. The multi-factorial nature of modelling parking demand hinders the 
attempt to estimate it for an area but rather for a specific type of land use or very specific conditions. In this 
case, such research already exists and  can cover the calculation needs [11]. It is not however yet 
interlinked with charging needs. This thesis proposes a more generalized approach to estimating on average 
the charging infrastructure in areas and not zone specifically. An attempt to model the results will be 
presented, followed by insights on modelling in agent level appropriately. 
Additionally, the increasing fleet of electric vehicles [5] is increasing the need for modelling parking and 
charging needs that consider most of the factors involved in both the estimations. These become more 
complicated because parking is also affected by non-measurable factors such as policies and tendencies that 
play a vital role in the decision of parking [11], [12]. Policies also affect the use, purchase of vehicles. If an 
attempt to model parking demand and link it to type of charging, it would require a large amount of 
resources to be completed because of the complexity of the subject.  
1.2 Contribution of the thesis 
The proposed method of a data-driven approach offers an alternative to decision makers that need a faster 
way of estimating a range of charging demand and linking it with parking demand. However, this forces 
the decision makers to accept several assumptions that are also obstructing the current method from 
becoming fully analytical. For this instance, a method of modelling such effort is written in the final 
chapter of this thesis. However, modelling parking demand and linking it with charger estimation is subject 
to many different factors and the results would be sensitive to changes. The impact of policies on parking 
demand that a model cannot handle if they are not quantifiable. Non-quantifiable factors such as preference 
or some of the policies render demand for both charging and parking estimation sensitive to changes. An 
example could be if some charging spots in the city start to apply charges for staying and occupying after

the charging period is over [13]. Including all these factors in a model is extremely challenging and would

1.3 How the thesis is organised 
This thesis is using a data-driven approach by combining and analysing results from two different sources. 
Firstly, detailed on-street parking data that provide information on the amount of parked vehicles, 
registered parking spaces and their location from 2012 until 2021. This data provides these numbers in 
three hourly snapshots within a random day in specified months, namely at 12:00,17:00 and 22:00 and 
every March and September (1st and 3rd quarter of each year). The specific day of counting was not 
provided while there were some information that were excluded for shortening or relevance reasons. The 
description, basic statistics and major findings for these data can be found in the corresponding chapter. 
Secondly, using data from the GAIA [14] model and through the use of QGIS software the charging 
infrastructure has been allocated also in the map and through the use of specific zones the charging needs 
will be analysed. Moreover, an algorithm has been developed that, using GAIA, estimates the number of 
charging events taken under consideration the uncertainty. However, estimating the amount of chargers is a 
more complex and larger project and it requires interdisciplinary cooperation. It is also acknowledged that 
this approach is used mostly to detect some initial patterns, methods and data to be used in order to estimate 
a detailed model of calculating and allocating charging infrastructure. 
2 Methodology 
The current chapter contains the definition of the project together with the calculation steps of the thesis. 
2.1 Scope 
This project has the target of providing insights for the parking patterns in Copenhagen and connect these 
with the charging needs by simulating charging events throughout the different time slots of the analysis. 
Moreover, it has the target of providing a strategic overview of the parking landscape of the city and 
connect this with the charging infrastructure that is and will be required in the future. Finally, it provides 
feedback on the outcomes of the data-driven approach and proposes a method of predicting and linking 
parking demand and charging needs (type and amount). 
2.2 Calculations 
The calculation methods used to append the results of this current project will be presented. The data 
handling and cleaning methods can be found in the next chapter. The analysis will be done on a 3-level 
basis. First, the parking patterns will be extracted by analysing the parking utilization (πi) and difference 
variables (δi). This will be followed by using the GAIA model [14] to write an algorithm that estimates   
the number of chargers based on the number of charging events locally for each OTM (On-The-Map) zone. 
Finally, these two will result in a map that can assist the decision on the type and amount of charging 
needed per zone.

δ1: Is the difference between the lunchtime and evening parking utilization. Practically it can be translated

home, this variable in combination with δ3 could help define type of charging for some areas.

δ2: Shows the shift between evening and residential type of parking. Negative prices of this variable show a

of -0.04 while the 3rd quartile price is 0. This means that there is minimum shift in occupancy between 17

These two variables are used to shape parking patterns throughout the city. The analysis for each of these 
variables is presented in a later chapter. Following the shaping of the parking patterns the chargers were 
estimated through an algorithm that used the equations from the thesis titled “Charging, steady-state SoC 
and energy storage distributions for EV fleets” [15] and is built upon the model under progress named 
GAIA [14]. 
The algorithm that is presented in the gives an estimation of charging events as a function of the number of 
EVs (electric vehicles) over a number of days. Firstly, the input parameters need to be defined. These are: 
1) Average driven distance: 37 km for Denmark [16]. This parameter stays the same for all the 
different calculations. In a fully detailed model this parameter is interlinked with many different 
parameters.  
2) Maximum EV range: this parameter was separated into three distinct categories for predictions on 
the average maximum EV range. The scenario where the batteries for mainstream EVs remain the 
same gives a value of 250 (kms), in the slight increase scenario the range is increased to 320 kms 
and in the big increase scenario the parameter was inputted as 500 kms. These parameters were 
chosen after looking the average weather conditions in Denmark with lower than 25 °C 
temperature and wind which lowers the average battery range [15], [17]. 
3) Number of days: simulation days, 90 in this case. The reason for calculating in different days is to 
produce an adequate number of simulations. 
4) Number of zones: the number of rows in the dataset that also is the number of zones (the entries to 
the database are per zone).  
5) Number of EVs per zone: This parameter required special handling as it had to be converted into a 
NumPy array to be inputted into the algorithm. In the case of the research, the columns inputted 
were different estimates of parked EV’s per zone based on the number of electric vehicles and only 
the most probable scenario was chosen to be further analyzed with its time slots (12,17 and 22 
o’clock). The rest of the prices were taken as the daily average parked vehicles. 
6) A simulation function was written that was based on the paper “Charging, steady-state SoC and 
energy storage distributions for EV fleets” [15] was used. This function is calculating for each zone 
the State-of-Charge (SoC) by calculating the survival function for a given ratio of driven 
distance/maximum EV range. The function is based on a probability distribution function (PDF) 
[15, N. eq. 1+5] and uses beta distribution as the proper type of distribution. Then the appropriate 
survival function is chosen by obtaining the cumulative distribution function (CDF) to obtain the 
SoC [15, N. eq. 2+3]. Finally the parameters of this functions are obtained through the equations 
3a, 3b, 5a and 5b [15, N. eq. 3a,3b,5a,5b]. The corresponding results graph can be found in the 
paper used to extract this simulator [15, Fig. 4].

7) The outcome of the simulation algorithm is an array that has the shape of (OTM zones(rows) x 
days of simulation(columns)) and each column shows one day of simulation and each cell is the 
number of charging events for each respective zone.  
8) The average of each row was taken (average of 90 days) and inputted into the QGIS layer.  
9) The number of chargers is calculated simply as the 30% of the charging events [15, Fig. 4] which is 
the optimal level of utilization for fast charging. This percentage will also be used for slow 
charging in this case for simplification reasons as it is calculated in a more complicated manner.  
10) The chargers are mapped according to the parking patterns and will be presented in the 
corresponding chapter. 
Due to the extensive nature of the report, the type and amount of chargers per zone had to be estimated for 
a specific scenario. A decision tree was picked as the most appropriate method to provide the decision 
support and showcase the logic behind using a scenario. In brief, the decision tree is a decision support tool 
used for quick decision making under uncertainty. The decision maker is taking the decisions under 
different factors and assigns probabilities to each one. After assigning the probabilities, these are either 
multiplied by a value or, as in this case, calculated as a probability of occurrence for each scenario. The 
probabilities should not be equal to each other.  
Specifically, the decision to be made was which of the three EV penetrations is most probable to happen 
and under which conditions this can happen. Hence, the decision variables (what is most probable to 
happen) were assigned to be the EV penetration levels. The numbers for each penetration level were 
obtained from the relevant research and the GAIA model [14], [15].These numbers were used later to 
calculate the amount of EVs from the amount of parked vehicles. This conversion will be shown later. 
Subsequently, each one of these scenarios was assigned a probability based on the relevant literature and 
research done. Then the factors affecting the initial choice are defined. 
The first factor affecting EV penetration was the battery range. The scenarios for each penetration level 
were mentioned briefly in the algorithm steps while the probabilities were assigned accordingly to each 
scenario. For example, the probability of the battery range staying the same in a scenario that the EV 
penetration will be low is higher than a high EV penetration because in case of high EV penetration the 
battery range would be further researched and become more available rather than a luxury item.  
A similar course of thought was followed for the non-quantifiable measure of state policies, which was 
chosen as the second choice factor. This would include a mixture of incentives and regulations that could 
affect the penetration levels. So in each separate scenario the aggressive policy is more likely to happen and 
with better technology improvements in battery range (and probably more economic motives to the relevant 
research) there could be higher probabilities that these would be followed by an aggressive policy mixture 
[18], [19]. 
 Finally, after multiplying each respective percentage, the conditions under which the most probable 
scenario was chosen. It should be noted that the percentages were assigned after relevant search in the 
available literature, articles and other relevant inputs. The figure of the decision tree can be found below. 
The highest probability (highlighted in red) is the most prominent scenario, that of high EV penetration 
with the battery range remaining the same while an aggressive policy mixture is being implemented. 
The calculation steps for the decision tree can be found in relevant literature [20].

3.2 Charger data and OTM zones 
Using data and equations from the ongoing research on GAIA model [14], [15], several charger locations 
have been extracted and inputted into QGIS. The chargers then were counted inside specific areas named 
OTM [23] zones which are used in the model. The OTM zone data contain besides the location also the 
geometrical information as outline length and area size. The charger data contains number of plugs, type of 
charger,  total power used, municipal data (district number). However, after evaluating that new chargers 
are being deployed constantly and the type and time of charging can depend on many different parameters 
it was decided that another method should be used to estimate the number of charging events. Thus, a 
simulation algorithm was written that provided the number of cars, simulation days, EV range and average 
distance driven could calculate the number of charging events. This algorithm is available in the appendix. 
Thus, GAIA was used to extract some required decision and steady state distribution model parameters.  
Conclusively, the following chapter will be dedicated to analyzing the two variables that will be used to 
extract the parking patterns and extracting the number of charging events per OTM zone based on 
predefined assumptions. The two variables that will be used to describe the parking patterns are the parking 
utilization πi with i=12,17,22 and the parking internal day variation δi with i=1,2,3. Moreover, the 
algorithm’s results will be presented and mapped to show the charging needs of each OTM zone for each 
respective charging category.

4 Parking pattern extraction, analysis and estimation of charging needs  
This chapter presents the outcome of the methods presented and provides feedback of the results for each 
part used in the final outcome (charging maps). 
4.1 The parking utilization variables πi  
The parking utilization variables are a direct measure of parking occupancy. Together with the number of 
parked vehicles they can be used to estimate parking demand and design policy measures through 
appropriate models. They can also be used to pinpoint areas of interest where parking occupancy is either 
too high (meaning there need to be regulated of new ones created) or too low meaning the space available 
could be utilized.  
It should be noted that the parking utilization itself is not able to define parking patterns. This is mostly due 
to the fact that car owners using their private vehicles only for leisure or long travel (more than 50kms) 
purposes have these parked constantly to a spot. This is a usual phenomenon in Copenhagen as the 
extensive bike lanes and good public transport coverage offer plenty of alternatives to the use of car. 
Despite, that this phenomenon exists, parking utilization shows the relative amount of coverage in an area 
and can be used to predict in the level of agents (locally) parking demand. Thus, the analysis and 
conclusions are drawn on the basis that the percentage of vehicles that are steady parked throughout the day 
are not taken under consideration and only the vehicles that are used for commuting/parking throughout the 
day will be analyzed.  
Comparing the utilization variables in size for the same monthly periods can provide useful insights. 
Specifically, if on the same measurement day π22 > π12, π17 this means that mostly the area is a residential 
area. If π12> π22, π17 then this area is mostly a business district. However, in Copenhagen exists also the 
phenomenon of mixed-use land where there are stores on the ground floor of a block of flats. These 
combinations will be shown more clearly by the variables presented in 4.2. Whenever 𝜋𝑖≥1 this means 
that there are more cars parked than the designated in each spot, hence the zone is considered to have more 
parked vehicles than registered spaces. This inequality (having more parked cars than registered park 
spaces) can be due to the fact that some areas are parking restricted until 22:00, thus until the morning 
parking is free and the spots not registered, which in turn allows a “looser” parking choice (e.g., not in a 
designated spot). The phenomenon can be observed in the presented appendix maps of the parking 
utilization variables. 
The parking utilization variables can offer also insights on the policy implications throughout the time.

by a small analysis on policy implications in the parking utilization or occupancy rate. It should be noted

5.1 A guide for best use of the research 
In order to fairly use this research, the reader can apprehend conclusions on the high parking occupancy 
throughout the day in most of the areas of the city. This phenomenon is not repeated in the outskirts of the 
surrounding districts in which the residential parking is either covered by private parking spaces or free on-
street parking.  
Generally, the presented maps and chargers can provide a strategic overview of the parking patterns and 
charging needs that appear in the city throughout the day. It is also assumed that areas of short-term parking 
referred in chapter 4.2 and subsequently presented in the current chapter with the number of estimated 
chargers will potentially require the creation of fast charging premises while the areas with bright yellow 
color will require slow chargers. However, the maps generated cannot be used specifically for zone analysis 
or to estimate the amount of plugs, type of charging or power needed currently. Thus, the presented charging 
event maps can provide predictions for the amount of electric vehicles indicated in table 4 for the optimal 
charging scenario [15, Fig. 4] of 30% and assuming that users remain parked until their charging cycle is

6 Summary and conclusions 
Estimating parking needs and in detailed level, is one of the most important problems that can be solved for a 
city. With the constant rise of electric vehicles in the transport market share, the charging infrastructure is 
becoming inevitably linked to parking. A data-driven approach is attempted in this thesis to approach a 
strategic or more generalized solution to the problem. Some insightful maps have been extracted that 
pinpoint areas of interest with either high daytime or residential parking. Subsequently, the number of 
chargers within these areas was estimated for each zone to give a final overview of the potential charging 
needs in each respective area.  
The extracted parking patterns can be used for defining the type of charging needed (slow or fast). The areas 
with bright pink/fuchsia color will mostly require fast chargers as they contain “daytime” parking while the 
bright yellow areas are strictly residential (figs. 8,9 and 10). These will require slow chargers. However, as 
shown in the presented maps and analyzed, many areas of the city show mixed use. Thus, a model that can 
estimate the parking demand on agent level and specifically for each zone is required. Moreover, the amount 
of charging needed has proven to be a far more complex subject and it requires extensive time and resources 
to be estimated. Finally, estimating slow charging was more complicated and thus only the estimate of fast 
chargers was used to calculate the amount of slow chargers from slow charging events.  
This thesis presents the strategic overview of the type of charging infrastructure and type of parking spots 
needed to accommodate the upcoming demand from EV users. It shows the areas that require strategic 
allocation of either dense parking occupancy or stricter regulations. Finally, the research provides a way of 
calculating and predicting the demand for the aforementioned commodities while pointing out the potential 
barriers that exist in estimating such numbers. 
6.1 Limitations and assumptions 
In this current chapter the limitations and assumptions used throughout the project are presented. 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
6.1.1  Assumptions 
a) The precise day is not available in the original dataset. So, it is assumed that the counting was on 
a random day and not a day with special events or circumstances that could potentially attract 
more vehicles into the respective areas. Linked to this, it is also assumed that the day was not a 
Saturday or Sunday, in which cars are used mostly for leisure purposes. 
b) Linked to the previous assumption. The precise hour of the counting is also not available; hence 
it is assumed that the cars parked at 12 were not the same as the cars parked at 17 or 22. This 
creates some limitations on the conclusions that can be drawn and will be discussed later. 
c) Another assumption is that the data provided count on the one hand the on-street parking levels 
however company parking lots and private parking stations are not counted. This however is not 
affecting the outcome of this project as companies or services that provide parking spaces for 
employees or customers are mostly using private space and not public. Finally, these 
establishments are calculating the demand for parking in their premises and create the required 
infrastructure. 
d) It was hard to make an accurate estimation on the amount of parked vehicles in 2030 thus the 
convention was made that the EV penetration percentages will be used to depict the most recent 
situation (September 2021). The amount of vehicles was not calibrated to 2030 because that 
would require calibration of all the calculated parking patterns to the future. This would make 
move the calculations out of the scope of the thesis. 
e) Extracting the amount of charging needs from parking demand is not 100% correct as these two 
are interlinked. There are many different factors that have not been considered and will be 
referred in the limitations below. 
f) The average travelling distance inputted in the algorithm is not affected by the presumed 
technology improvements, namely the average range of EV’s that has been inputted to the 
algorithm.  This has implications especially on the usage of budget electric vehicles with 
batteries that need frequent charging and cannot fast charge. These users would most probably 
travel the least amount of time and search for quick parking options with slow charging, rather 
than fast chargers. 
g) Calculating the average number of parked vehicles per zone tampers with the distribution of 
charging events throughout the day however the limits of this thesis do not allow such extensive 
calculations and reporting. 
6.1.2 Limitations 
A. The research does not take under consideration the possibility of home charging and the home 
chargers could not be taken under consideration. This affects the research especially in areas where 
there are homes with private parking spaces. 
B. The density of the OTM zones and the distance they have not been taken under consideration which 
would be required for more accurate predictions. 
C. A car that is used only for leisure or long distance travelling and remains parked at a spot for several 
days creates an input to the data that is presented as newly parked vehicle every hour and raises the 
parking occupancy rate. Then the estimation generated by the model takes under consideration this 
stopped vehicle and creates a pseudo-need for charging. Copenhagen has excellent public transport 
coverage, and this is a common phenomenon that does not assist the estimation of charging needs. 
D. When estimating slow charging it is assumed that the user who has finished charging will remove 
their car from the charging space and search for another. This though is unlikely especially during 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
nighttime, and if it is not regulated with economic incentives it can lead to a big problem for slow 
charging electric vehicles meaning it can create a pseudo-need for slow chargers. 
E. In order to estimate power needs, investment cost and other parameters linked to charging 
infrastructure more accurate predictions are required to be inputted and extracted from the relevant 
algorithms.  
Estimating and accurately predicting charging demand requires extensive research and collaboration. 
Moreover, the tools provided need to take under consideration as many factors as possible affecting both 
parking demand and charging needs. 
6.2 Future research 
The results of this thesis show an overview of the calculation possibilities that exist between the fields of 
charging and parking demand. By entering more relevant data into the existing databases, a regression could 
be created that predicts parking demand accurately and links it to the available vehicle projections [29], [30]. 
After acquiring the appropriate data, the 90% of estimations needs to be analysed and any outliers or extreme 
values excluded. Specifically, for the data driven of the current thesis the extreme values provided overview 
of some areas but if a value-based assumption was to be made, they would create an imbalance to the main 
population. Moreover, projecting with uncertainty the parked vehicle data would require the calibration of 
the πi and δi variables. 
These calculation steps would have to be followed by clearly defining the different zones and separating 
them into distinct categories such as Business district, Leisure areas and store centres, Mixed use areas with 
small businesses and housing, purely residential areas, Areas with private parking facilities and special 
regulations. Then the aforementioned parameters would be calibrated by the use of these distinct zones. 
Finally, buffer zones of “comfortable walking distance” (~250m) need to be created around the chargers and 
the zones and some type of cost to be calculated for each charging-parking combination.  After calibrating 
these different parameters, a zonal estimation can be done safely and the number and type of charging can be 
predicted with high accuracy. 
Additionally, for the calculation algorithm the average driving distance could be estimated as a function of 
the battery technology improvements. Special considerations need to be made in areas that show high 
occupancy (larger than 90%) and thus might be considered full by drivers.  
6.3 Discussion 
Parking is one of the most important aspects of a city. It can reduce the amount of traffic and if it is regulated 
it can make it easier for municipal authorities to control the space available. Optimizing parking spaces and 
linking the latest with charging needs allow a municipality to plan policies and save resources (monetary, 
energy and space). The charging capacity and capabilities of a city can be defined by predicting the demand 
and adopting appropriate policies. Specifically, in the example of Copenhagen, parking is under a lot of 
regulations. However, there is no specific model for predicting parking demand that would assist policy 
makers to take preventive measures and avoid high parking occupancies. If this model also is to be linked 
with charging needs, it could fill a research gap that currently exists.  
Conclusively, this thesis is making an initial attempt to link parking demand and patterns with charging 
demand. The results of such method can be used for general assumptions and give insight to specific areas in 
Copenhagen that need special consideration when it comes to planning charging infrastructure.
