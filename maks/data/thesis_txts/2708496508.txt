Abstract
Extensive university projects always include several students as contributors. Students
would typically contribute with a subproject that they develop, often as part of their
thesis. However, most of the software developed within student projects is never uti-
lized. This is because students do not integrate and deploy their code, the code is poor
in quality, and there is no documentation, making it diﬀicult for others to utilize the
software.
In the master’s thesis, a software development method for student projects has been
developed. This method aims to solve the problem that not enough software created
in student projects is integrated and deployed when the student project is a subproject
within an university project. The development method has been developed by following
the steps in design science research. Essentially, the solutions to the different challenges
were found by analyzing how the same challenges were addressed at companies. The
solutions found have been redesigned to comprehend the constraint that exists within
student- and university projects. The project’s outcome is the development method, III
Student Development Method, which is the first development method that aims explicitly
to increase the output of student projects within extensive university projects. This
project ultimately suggests that students should utilize the III Student Development
Method when carrying out student projects that are part of an extensive university
project.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There has been considerable growth in the collaboration between companies and uni-
versities within the last decade. Companies have turned to universities for early-stage
research to get access to the best scientific minds in specific domains. Last year alone,
the Technical University of Denmark had over 1200 cooperation agreements with compa-
nies [17]. Collaboration between companies and universities is an excellent opportunity
for both partners. Companies might be able to cut down their research budget, and at
the same time, they get access to the best engineering minds and the newest research-
based knowledge. The universities get the opportunity to work with industry cases and
apply their knowledge and research.
The software engineering domain has been in enormous development in recent decades,
and new technologies are still constantly emerging. Because of this, companies have an
excellent opportunity to optimize their businesses through digitization and automation.
However, since new technologies emerge, it is diﬀicult for companies to be front-runners
in scientific research.
For scientific research, universities play a crucial role because
research and innovation are crucial elements of the university’s work environment and
vision. Especially students writing a bachelor or master thesis can benefit from collabo-
ration between the industry and the university. Students receive a real-world challenge
they should try to solve, and the company gets a relatively cheap resource that might
look at the challenge from new angels. Students working on a bachelor or master thesis
that are part of a larger university project contribute to the overall project with a smaller
project. Many students and other developers might contribute to the same project for
larger projects. Having multiple contributors leads to the never-ending challenges of
software development: Which development model should be used to systematize and
organize the software development process. The success of any software project is mea-
sured upon the ”holy trinity” that includes; satisfying user requirements, carrying out
the project within the allocated budget, and planned time [14]. In order to consider
the development of a project as successful, all the aspects of the ”holy trinity” must be
satisfied. Often, it is very likely that projects are not considered completed successfully,
which is mainly due to underestimation and the fact that user requirements tend to
change many times throughout a project.
Development methods might be introduced to students at universities.
However,
companies might have the most interest and need for an optimal development process
since it impacts their performance and, ultimately, the cost to market. Thus, companies
spend many resources to organize themselves to support whatever development model

they find optimal for their business. When working in well-organized companies, there
might be a well-defined development model like Scrum or Kanban for the employees,
which means that development processes can be streamlined. Furthermore, the orga-
nization might be well structured regarding team structure, such as having full-stack
feature teams or separated frontend and backend teams or something completely differ-
ent.
Universities are entirely different institutions, and no unified software development
method that students should follow doing their theses exist.
Many students might
contribute to developing larger projects at universities throughout their bachelor’s or
master’s thesis. Students might skip tasks related to integration and deployment unless
that’s what their project is about. Students will not spend time setting up a pipeline
that integrates with the existing systems since it is a very time-consuming task that
might not benefit the students that much regarding grading compared to the time they
might need to use. The result is that the project owner ends up with several independent
projects that need to be integrated into an overall project. Furthermore, when students
are finished with their thesis, they might not be available for help afterward. Thus,
getting help regarding independent projects becomes tough, making the integration task
even more diﬀicult. This introduces a problem on integrating multiple subprojects into
an overall project when each subproject is created independently by students and has its
own setup about software architecture and continuous integration, continuous delivery,
CI/CD, pipelines.
1.1
Problem
The main problem focus of this project is that not enough software projects from students
are integrated into the main project and deployed to a production environment for
university projects. The issues relate to the nature of university projects, which are
based on several student projects, whereas scientific research has a higher priority than
software development. Furthermore, no current guidelines or methods are represented
to supervisors and students to follow when carrying out university projects. Thus, sub-
projects might be developed in several different ways, making it more challenging to
integrate.
1.2
Solution
The challenges for more extensive university projects are the same challenges compa-
nies have in many ways. Therefore, much knowledge can be obtained by analyzing how
companies structure themselves and their development model. It is exciting to see how
companies handle CI/CD and how this differs depending on the software architecture of
the project. It is also interesting to see which technologies and frameworks companies

utilize to optimize their development process.
The solution to the university problem regarding integrating multiple subprojects
into an overall project when each subproject is created independently is a method that
should be utilized when students carry out their thesis. This method represents a set

Thus, the outcome of this project will be a set of artifacts that

within the student project should be increased, resulting in more student projects going
into production.

CHAPTER 2
Research methods
In this chapter, the challenges of having multiple student projects as part of a more
extensive university project will be elaborated on and further investigated. The chapter
also introduces the scientific methods utilized within the project. Thus the chapter covers
the following topics; Motivation, whereas the motivation for the project is described.
Then the scientific method utilized to analyze and solve the problem will be explained.
Afterward, interviews of professors will be summarized to find general pain points. Then,
the problem will be explained in dept throughout a detailed problem description, wherein
the problem landscape and the root problems are defined. At last, research questions
will be defined, which will be the question that the project will address later on.
2.1
Motivation
As was explained in the introduction, university projects created in collaboration be-
tween universities and the industry are a significant and growing trend that benefits
both parties. However, like companies spend many resources optimizing their internal
process and streamlining their workflow, Universities should do the same when they in-
clude students as contributors to university projects.
The motivation of this project is based on the optimization potential that lies within
the facilitation of university projects that include students as contributors through their
bachelor’s or master’s thesis. By defining streamlined processes for university projects,
there will be an increased chance that student projects can be integrated and utilized
when the project ends. That benefits both the university projects and the students. For
the university projects, the people responsible would spend less time integrating student
projects that are handed over when a student project ends. From a student’s point of
view, they will be developing software that can be integrated into the existing project
resulting in fewer projects that are never integrated. Thus, it will motivate students to
develop code that is utilized in the end.

2.2.1
Design science
Design science research in an information system is a discipline utilized to develop inno-
vative solutions to enhance human knowledge with a scientific underpinning. Thus, the
goal of design science research is to define innovative artifacts in the form of constructs,
models, methods, and instantiations [10] that can optimize existing solutions or solve
problems hitherto. Within computer science, design science research aims to improve
knowledge on how a system should be developed or designed to achieve a set of goals.
This knowledge can be referred to as design knowledge. The use of design knowledge
includes designing and developing an application or database properly, designing and
modeling business processes, organizing and aligning digital divisions to support orga-
nizational strategies, etc. Design science research projects are centered around humans;
thus, the outcome of a design science research project will be utilized or practiced by
humans in almost every case. Therefore, it is important to design and develop artifacts
with the human in mind when performing design science research. There are several
ways that design science research can be performed; however, in this project, the design
science research process that will be utilized includes six phases defined as follow [15]:
1. Problem identification and motivation: Define a specific research problem and the
motivation behind it. It can be useful to decompose the problem to capture the
complexity of the problem. Furthermore, it is important to justify the value of
a solution because it is the motivation for the researcher. It will also make the
research audience pursue the solution and accept the result.
2. Objectives of a solution: Define the objective of the solution. The objective of the
solutions can be divided into two groups; quantitative and qualitative. A quantita-
tive solution intends to replace a current solution with a better solution. A quali-
tative solution intends to develop artifacts to solve problems not yet addressed.
3. Design and development: Design and develop artifacts such as constructs, mod-
els, methods, and instantiations. The design and development of artifacts include
determining the intended functionality, the artifact’s design, and the actual arti-
fact’s development. This is an iterative process, meaning that these actions are
performed multiple times.
4. Demonstration: Utilization of the artifacts to solve specific problems. This can
be carried out, for example, through a case study. The task requires suﬀicient
knowledge of utilizing the artifacts to solve the problem.
5. Evaluation: Evaluation of the solution by inspecting the individual objectives and
the observed results from the utilization of the artifacts.

6. Communication: Communicating the problem and the way it is crucial. Share the
result of utilizing the solution with other researchers and practicing professionals.
The steps above set the foundation for this research project going forwards. Hence
the goal of the design science research is to design and implement a solution that should
be evaluated. The evaluation will be carried out as a case study described in Section 2.2.2.
The case study aims to gain the necessary design knowledge that can be evaluated.
2.2.2
Case study
In the previous section, six phases of design science research were described. The demon-
stration phases of the design science research process are about utilizing the solution
artifacts developed. Thus, the demonstration phases can be carried out as a case study,
which will be the course of action in this project. A case study in this project aims to get
in-depth knowledge of how the solution addresses the different challenges when utilized.
The knowledge can be utilized to optimize the solution to crack the different challenges
within the domain.
In this project, The Live Road Assessment project [16] will be utilized as the uni-
versity project to perform the case study, and Software engineering 2 (SE2) will deliver
software that should be integrated with the existing project that exists within the LiRA
project. What the LiRA project is about and why it is suitable for the project will be
explained in Section 7.
2.3
Interviews
In order to identify the challenges related to student projects, interviews were performed
to get a broader understanding of the challenges on the subject. Essentially, two inter-
views were carried out with different DTU professors who have been working on other
university projects. Each professor had an independent view on the subject. The first
interviewed professor did not see that the purpose of the student project was to get
software into the production environment, but rather that student projects were utilized
to test out theoretical models on industry data and deliver the results as data. The
second professor had a different experience. He easily recognizes the problems of having
students as contributors to extensive projects. He explained that within an extensive
project named CITIES, only 2 out of 11 student projects ended up in production in
the end. The rest were newer integrated into the project. He explained that the major
reason was the lack of experience from the students: “The main reason is that students
are inexperienced in developing software. Imagine that the bachelor or master project
might be the student’s first or second large project”. From the interview with the second
professor, the following challenges were identified by the professor within the CITIES
project:

• The programming process of a student project is hard to track when the students

2.4.1
Focus of student projects
Student projects developed as subprojects within a larger university project are often
developed by students carrying out their bachelor’s or master’s theses. As for all student
theses, the primary purpose of the thesis is to meet the learning objectives, contribute
with scientific results and write a good thesis, unlike employees at a company whose main
focus is to quickly develop their current tasks and move on to the next one. Because
writing a good thesis is the main priority for students as the grade are heavily based
on the report and their scientific contribution, making software production-ready is not
the main priority. Thus, keeping a high software quality standard by following best
practice standards is not highly prioritized. The same goes for setting up integration
and deployment pipelines. As this time-consuming task does not affect the grade heavily,
students might not prioritize it.

2.4.2
Limitation of student projects
It is essential to understand that student projects are created under certain limitations.
This is especially the time frame and the operational cost and knowledge. The limitation
regarding time is a factor that would be considered when developing the solution arti-
facts since the time frame cannot be changed. The time frame can trivialize some of the
critical elements related to integration ability and deployment since students only need
a local version of the software running and, as discussed previously, might not prioritize
making it production-ready due to their focus.
Another limitation is the operational cost spent within a project. The cost limita-
tion is highly related to deployment to cloud servers, whereas free tier programs do not
always cover the needs. Although this might not be the most significant limitation, it
can still prevent some students from trying out deployment.
The last limitation is the amount of knowledge a student has. The biggest challenge
in developing quality and integration-ready software is students’ inexperience in creat-
ing large-scale, industry-ready software solutions. A bachelor or master thesis would
typically be a student’s first or second large software project. Furthermore, it is possible
that the student has not configured a whole CI/CD environment before. The combi-
nation of inexperience in developing and CI/CD makes it hard to deliver software of
a certain quality that meets production-ready standards. Obviously, the student can
study and learn, but this is a time-consuming task that might not be prioritized because
the primary purpose is not to create the software. In short, student projects need to be
focused, so lots of work on infrastructure and integration work cannot be done.

Project management of software projects is an important aspect, as it scopes the respon-

• Software developers: Developing the software. Often divided into frontend and
backend developers depending on the project and the organization structure.
The roles above list some general roles that might exist at companies. Each company

Project management in software projects is an essential part of the software develop-
ment process. The topic of project management is a well-known topic in many different
business areas. In software development, project management has been in constant de-
velopment within the last decades, and nowadays, almost every company with a digital
department utilizes some form of project management. Companies invest many resources
in project management because it is one of the keystones in software development, and it
ensures that the software developed corresponds to the project goals. Therefore, a lot of
valuable knowledge can be obtained by analyzing how companies address the challenges
that exist for university projects.
Two problems encountered in university projects were tracking work progress for
external parties and handling many contributors in extensive projects. Companies solve
this by combining project management tools, a defined project structure, and a branching
strategy. By utilizing a project management tool, companies can easily: prioritize work
items, plan agile sprints, track the progress of work items, and link code changes to
work items so that everybody can find detailed information about specific code changes,
which can be almost impossible to obtain by only reading the code.

structure and branching strategy to keep a project streamlined.

have their own project structure, and follow their own branching strategy. This is very
useful for the individual project and the student; however, this is not the best approach

project management tools that everyone utilizes, making it impossible for parties to track
work. Thus, the solution maturity of project management at universities is low.

The challenges that exist within the integration and deployment domain are strongly
correlated. Software is typically not deployed because no infrastructure guidelines are
defined within the overall project. For students to continuously deploy their code, it
would require them to implement and configure automated pipelines themselves, which
is time-consuming when multiple environments need to be configured. If students do
not implement an automated pipeline, code will typically only be tested locally on the
students’ machine. Another challenge with integration and deployment for university
projects is the many contributing students. Student contributes to the overall project
with a subproject developed throughout their thesis. When a thesis ends, the student
might become unavailable for the project owner, meaning that it becomes diﬀicult to
clarify or explain the project content afterward. Because of this, it is crucial to get the
code integrated and deployed as part of the thesis.
Companies adapting to microservices architecture need to integrate and deliver code
changes continually. Due to the lack of software engineers within the industry, com-
panies must not waste their resources; therefore, manually integrating, testing, and
deploying code is rarely an option. Instead, these processes are automated as much
as possible. As this is a crucial element in software development, many projects man-
agement tools, automated testing tools, and development frameworks already exist to
ease the configuration of automated flows. Both the solution- and application domain
maturity are high within the industry domain as companies heavily embrace CI/CD.
Both solution- and application domain maturity for university projects are low as no
tools specifically aim to ease CI/CD for university projects taking the constraints into
perspective.
The solution to the stated challenges within university projects can be found in the
way companies set up their pipeline and infrastructure. Some companies might have
dedicated divisions handling all CI/CD, while other companies might handle it on a

5.1.2
Microservice architecture
As the solutions artifacts developed in this project aim to solve the stated problems for
more extensive applications with a certain amount of complexity, the target architecture
that should be utilized is the microservice architecture. In this section, the microservice
architecture will be explained and how it addresses the problem related to the monolith

5.1.2.1
What is a microservice architecture?
The common characteristic of a microservice architecture is that it is an architectural
approach to building software applications where the application is decomposed into sev-
eral smaller units, that can be independently deployed. In a microservice architecture,
the conceptual idea is to keep services small and independent. Therefore, the business
logic layer known from the monolith architecture is decomposed into smaller units based
on the business capability in the domain. These units are known as microservices, and
each microservice is a unique application with its own code-base, data storage, and in-
frastructure. As a microservice is an independent unit, it can be developed in its own
programming language and utilize its own data storage technology independently of
what other microservices might utilize. Microservices are intended to be independently
deployable and have automated deployment infrastructure configured. Because of this,
microservices can be deployed continuously and independently. In addition, microser-
vices can be scaled both vertically and horizontally on a service level. This is because
each service is an independent unit. It is possible to either add more computational
power to the unit (vertical scaling) or deploy more instances of the same unit (horizon-
tal scaling). A basic overview of the microservice architecture is shown in Figure 5.2
==PAGE:44==
36

Figure 5.2: Microservice base architecture.
As shown in Figure 5.2 the architecture has a clear separation between frontend and
backend applications. The frontend domain contains all client applications, that is, the
applications that clients interact with. The frontend applications do not contain any
business logic at all. The business logic exists within the backend domain as microser-
vices, and each microservice exposes this logic through an API. The communication
between the frontend applications and the microservices is done via an API gateway.
The following sections will describe the content of microservices and how to scope them.
How services should communicate with each other, and the difference between a code-
first- and API-first approach when developing a microservice.
5.1.2.2
What is a microservice in a microservice architecture?
As previously described, a microservice is an independent unit in the backend domain
that carries the responsibility of a particular business capability. However, microservices
are general characteristics based on the following:
• A microservice is responsible for a single capability.
• A microservice is individually deployable.
• A microservice must run in a separate process.
• A microservice owns its own data store.
• A small team can maintain a handful of microservices.
• A microservice is replaceable.
==PAGE:45==
5.1 Micro service architecture
37
The first two and last two characteristics can only be realized if the scope of functionality
of the microservice is reasonable. Getting the scope wrong will prevent the service from
adhering to those four characteristics.
A microservice is responsible for a single capability
Each microservice should be scope based on business capabilities. A business capability
in this context is a business process performed by the company that supports a business
goal; for example, a business capability for a company that sells something online would
be handling payments. A microservice should only be scoped to a single business capabil-
ity and each business capability should only be implemented within a single microservice.
A microservice is individually deployable
An essential aspect of a microservice is that it can be deployed independently of other
services. Hence, a microservice will have its own deployment pipeline, which can be
executed independently of other deployment pipelines within a project. Deploying a
microservice should not have any impact on other services due to its independence.
A microservice must run in a separate process
This relates to microservices being independently deployable.
In order to make the
services as independent as possible, microservices should run on one or more separate
processes that are separated from other services.
A microservice owns its own data store
A microservice should own the datastore where needed data is kept. This is important as
it increases the independence of a microservice. Data lakes should generally be avoided
as they introduce tighter coupling in the project domain and increase the complexity of
the data model.
A small team can maintain a handful of microservices
A microservice should be owned by only one team. With that comes the responsibility
of updating and maintaining the service. It does not make sense to talk about the size
of a microservice in terms of lines of code. The thing to consider is the amount of work
that comes with the maintenance of the microservice, which should typically not extend
the amount of work that one team can deliver.
A microservice is replaceable
Teams should be able to replace a microservice, which can be due to several different
reasons, for example, performance issues or change of technology. Either way, replacing
an existing microservice with a new one should be possible.

5.1.2.3
Communication Patterns between Microservices
A crucial aspect of the microservice architecture is the communication pattern in which
different services communicate. A big obstacle regarding scaling and agility is the de-
pendencies between services in a system. While this is not a problem for a single team
working with a monolith system, it becomes a growing issue for services within the mi-
croservice domain. Projects following a microservice architecture would have several
smaller services within the project domain, each maintained by a single team. If these
services were to communicate directly, it would result in tight coupling between services
and increase dependence on other teams. Adding a system creates additional dependen-
cies and complexity in the overall system. This is illustrated in Figure 5.3
Figure 5.3: Imperative programming paradigm.
Figure 5.3 illustrates the increase of complexity when adding additional services into
the project landscape. As the Figure shows, there may not be a problem when having
few services, but when the number of services increases, so does the number of depen-
dencies and the system’s complexity.
The solution to the problem stated above is utilizing an indirect communication pat-
tern, which decouples the dependencies between services. A typical indirect communi-
cation pattern is an event stream. An event stream provides indirect- and asynchronous
communication in a continuous real-time flow of records. A record holds the relevant
information of an event, structured as a key-value pair. By utilizing an event stream,
services dispatch and subscribe to events. Thus, services only communicate with the
event stream, not directly with other services.

stream pipe and the different services within the project domain. Adding new services

handle the subscription, and the dispatcher does not have to do any additional work.
This is profoundly suitable for an architecture where services may be added throughout

5.1.2.4
The Code-first and API-first Approach
When a team begins the implementation of a new service, they can generally choose
between two implementation approaches; the code-first approach and the API-first ap-
proach.
In the code-first approach, the service and its API are built simultaneously. This is
a synchronous approach wherein the API is not guaranteed finalized until the service
implementation is finalized. API consumers would need to wait until both the service
and API implementation is done until they start using the API. This approach might
work well in feature- or cross-functional teams, where the same team owns both the
frontend and backend applications; hence the developers would know how to utilize the
API before it reaches its final state.
The API-first approach is, as the name insinuates when an API is designed and
implemented as the first step of the development process. Typically this is done together
with the target consumers of the API by creating an API contract. An API contract is
a contract that contains detailed information regarding the API and the endpoints that
exist within the API. First, when the API is implemented, the rest of the system is built
on top. The API-first approach is an asynchronous approach that allows backend and
frontend teams to work in parallel.
Figure 5.5 illustrates the code-first approach and the API-first approach. Each de-
velopment is illustrated with steps that represent the time of development. Thus, in the
code-first approach, a backend team will first develop a service and its API simultane-
ously. The frontend team must wait for the backend team to finish before consuming

and testing the API in the context of the frontend applications. Suppose the communi-
cation is not clear between the teams developing the frontend application and the team
developing the backend service. In that case, the API might not be entirely suitable
for the frontend team to use. This could delay the time to market since the API might
need some rework before it is usable. The API-first approach also shown in Figure 5.5
illustrates the illustrates, like for the code-first approach, the development steps in time.
Here, the frontend and backend teams define the API together as the first step. When

also reduces the time-to-market. Thus, the API-first approach is suitable for projects
where teams should work independently, as it reduces the dependency between systems
and teams.

organization’s structure. This will be further outlined for each of the patterns.
5.1.3.1
The Direct Communication pattern
As the name implies, the Direct Communication pattern utilize direct communication
between the frontend application and the backend services. This is fairly the easiest
setup and it suitable for smaller applications, because the complexity is relatively small.

5.2
Micro Frontends
In Section 5.1 the concept of microservices was explained, and how the architecture
helps address the challenges stated in Chapter 4 that relate to working independently
and developing highly integrative and deployable software. Microservices might address
many of the challenges related to independence, but mainly in the backend domain.
Thus, there are still challenges to overcome within the frontend domain when several
student projects should contribute to a single frontend application. As briefly mentioned
in Section 4.1.2, micro frontends are a possible solution to the challenges in the frontend
domain. This section will explain the concept and ideas of micro frontends.
5.2.1
What are Micro Frontends?
Micro frontends employ the same idea as microservices, which is to create an application
based on a combination of several smaller, independent parts. It is well described by
Micheal Geers [5] and by Cam Jackson [11], that both have written articles based on
their own and others’ experiences. Briefly, micro frontends involve breaking up large and
complicated systems into small, more manageable pieces and then explicitly documenting
their dependencies between each other. Essentially, micro frontends are independent
applications built into the main application, so it appears as if it were a single application
from the user’s perspective. Choices of technologies, codebases, and release processes
are independent team decisions and make the team capable of operating and evolving
independently without excessive coordination. This allows teams to work independently
on a frontend application without interfering with others. The following are some of the
benefits of using micro frontends:
• Codebases that are smaller, more cohesive, and easier to maintain.
• Increasing scalability through more decoupled, autonomous teams. Applications
can be developed faster and maintained easier, increasing the ability to develop
and scale and application.
• It is possible to upgrade, update, or even rewrite parts of the frontend with micro
frontends more smoothly than previously.
• Since micro frontends are independent, it is easier to ensure that the rest of the
app remains stable. Micro frontends eliminate the need to keep track of an entire
app.
The key benefits share many of the same headlines as microservices, which is natural
as the core ideas are the same. As for every software architecture, nothing comes without
==PAGE:54==
46

a price, and the same goes for micro frontends.
Micro frontends can result in the
duplication of dependencies resulting in an increasing amount of data that the user
must download before the application works. It would also be more challenging to test
the whole frontend application locally as it requires that all micro frontends are served
locally.
Furthermore, keeping all developers working off the same standards can be
challenging when the application is broken into smaller parts. That can especially have
a negative impact on the UI and the usability of the application if developers do not
utilize the same styling standards.

5.2.1.1
Micro Frontends and deployment
Another aspect of micro frontends is that they each have their own independent deploy-
ment pipeline. Thus, a micro frontend can be deployed independently with a minimum
associated risk of harming other application parts. Teams do not need to be concerned
with the state of other teams’ micro frontends. An individual micro frontend that is
ready for production should be able to do so, and it should be up to the team that builds
and maintains it.

Having the codebases and deployment pipelines decoupled allows teams to be fully inde-
pendent. With full ownership, teams can focus on what is important: delivering value to
their stakeholders. This enables them to move quickly and eﬀiciently. However, teams
must be structured around vertical slices of business functions rather than technical ca-
pabilities to achieve an independent state. That means that one team is fully responsible
end-to-end for a micro frontend.

Experience With Micro Frontends
In this section, the experiences with micro frontends from the company DFDS will be
explained. The benefits and drawbacks are based on the existing knowledge in the micro
frontend domain. However, as this is still a relatively new architecture that companies
are just beginning to explore, there are still many use cases to examine and things to
learn. Therefore, this next part will be based on the experience that DFDS had when
utilizing micro frontends for their new booking experience.
5.2.2.1
Why DFDS Choose to Try Out Micro Frontends
The company DFDS is within the business area of logistics and ferry transportation.
DFDS started their digital journey about two decays ago, and like many others, they
built almost all their application with a monolith architecture. They mainly developed
applications to be utilized in-house in order for them to digitize their businesses pro-
cesses.
Their passenger booking application was originally built in C# utilizing the
MVC framework. In anticipation of the company’s moving to microservices architec-
ture, it was decided to separate the booking application into frontend and backend
applications. Therefore, the frontend components were rewritten in TypeScript using
the AngularJS framework. As a result of the separation, the company slowly started de-
composing its monolithic backend application into microservices. Later down the road,
it was decided to build a shared library of components in-house to streamline the design
across company applications. However, as other teams started to utilize the ReactJS
framework (or library) and had a good experience, the shared component library was
created in React. Thus, the passenger booking application also started to contain react
components mixed with the existing angular code.
As one might imagine, the application eventually grew very complex in domain knowl-
edge. It still contained MVC code parts that were never rewritten, Angular code as the
main framework, and ReactJS components for shared components. At the time, the
organizational structure of the digital department was development teams divided into
frontend and backend teams. At the end of 2020, DFDS changed its organization into
feature teams, allowing teams to own features end-to-end. As this had many positive
benefits, it also introduced some challenges, especially for a legacy system that had no
neutral team as owner.
The journey above led to the following pain point DFDS wanted to address when
designing their future booking experience:
• The mix of technology in a single application. The mix of MVC, Angular, and
React made it very diﬀicult for new developers to understand the code, and the
on-boarding process of new developers became longer and longer.
• Very complex application. It was customary to spend up to one day getting the
application to work on a local machine if one had not worked with it for a while.
==PAGE:57==
5.2 Micro Frontends
49
• Hard to introduce a new feature. Due to the complex domain, it was hard to
introduce a new feature, and doing so became a very time-consuming task each
time.
• Time consuming to test and deploy. As the whole application needed to be tested
before changes could go to production, every test needed to pass. One test failing
would block changes from going into production.
This blocked the team from
getting things out in production because one or more tests failed even when they
had nothing to do with the current changes. As a testrun could take up to 20
minutes before failing, this became a real issue, and sometimes end-to-end test
was disabled to get changes out.
DFDS chose to experiment with micro frontend as it addresses many of the issues they
encountered. Each micro frontend could be developed in independent languages/frame-
works. They would also get less complex applications because applications are kept
small only to contain specific functionality. That also allows them to introduce new
features more smoothly. Finally,testing should also be simplified since only updated
micro frontends will require testing, which could be unit tests and end-to-end tests.
5.2.2.2
DFDS Experience with Micro Frontend
The new booking experience that DFDS aimed to develop included functionality that
spans across several teams based on team capabilities. This led to the question of how
different micro frontends should be sliced. Eventually, it was decided to slice based on
functionality. This resulted in multiple micro frontends per page, each interacting with
the other. This approach was basically chosen because that was recommended by others
that had experimented with micro frontends. However, this slicing of the micro frontends
did not suit the needs of DFDS, as one team ended up with several micro frontends that
were very small and very basic.
Furthermore, DFDS followed the BFF pattern, so
each micro frontend also had a dedicated BFF. Teams ended up with too many micro
frontends, making development and management more extensive. Therefore, the slicing
was rethought and changed. In order to avoid creating too many micro frontends, it
was decided only to create them when the complexity of a feature was over a certain
amount or if the micro frontend should be utilized in several applications. At the end
of the product’s first version, launched in October 2021, the application contained only
a few micro frontends.

Insights From DFDS’s Experiences
Micro frontends did work in the DFDS example but in an adjusted way. It was clear
that mapping each feature to a micro frontend was not suitable for this project due
to different reasons. One of the main reasons was that the frontend application was
mainly owned by one relatively small team. A second team contributed with several
microservices to the application, and a third team contributed with user authentication.

5.3.1
Infrastructure as code
The infrastructure of an information system is broadly defined as a set of information
technology (IT) components that are needed for an information system to operate. The
infrastructure components used to provide IT services and solutions include hardware,
software, networking components, an operating system (OS), and data storage. The
two primary IT infrastructure types are the Traditional infrastructure and the Cloud
infrastructure.
In a traditional IT infrastructure, the following hardware and software components
are included: data centers, servers, networking hardware, desktop computers, and enter-
prise application software.
Cloud computing IT infrastructures are almost similar to traditional ones. Through
virtualization, however, end users can access the infrastructure via the internet, which
==PAGE:60==
52
5 Technologies
allows them to use computing resources without having to install on-premises. Using
virtualization, a service provider can connect up to many physical servers at any loca-
tion. Then, it abstracts and divides resources, such as storage, in order to make them
accessible to users almost anywhere an internet connection is available.
Having a flexible, reliable, and secure IT infrastructure will help a company achieve
its goals and keep its competitive edge. On the other hand, if the IT infrastructure is
not implemented correctly, companies can face connectivity, productivity, and security,
including system disruptions and breaches.
Infrastructure as code or IaC is a way developers can manage infrastructure with
written code instead of manually configuring the infrastructure. When utilizing IaC,
configuration files are created. These files contain the infrastructure specifications of
a given application. The infrastructure specification in code format makes it easier to
edit and distribute to other applications. At the same time, it minimizes the chance
of human errors as it eliminates the need for manual configuration, ensuring that the
infrastructure configuration is the same each time.
IaC is an evolving domain, and the rise of popularity is strongly correlated with
many switching to a microservice architecture and moving their applications to the cloud.
Cloud infrastructure can be configured typically by utilizing a provided user interface.
However, if a project contains many applications, the best way to ensure correctness in
the configuration is by utilizing infrastructure as code. Imagine migrating to a cloud
service with a simple setup at the beginning. Later on, it is decided to utilize Kubernetes
as a management tool for containerized applications. The best approach to meet this
change request is to apply the necessary changes to the infrastructure configuration file,
test that everything works, and then distribute the updated file to all other available
applications. In that way, a developer does not have to manually add the changes to
each application in a user interface. It also allows developers with less experience to
deploy code without extensive knowledge of the existing infrastructure. Suppose devel-
opers are provided with the infrastructure files they need and modify them to suit their
application. In that case, developers can deploy code with the same infrastructure setup
as existing applications without too much trouble.

2. The IaC files are pushed to the code repository. The code repository should be

5.3.3
Containerization with Docker
In CI/CD, one crucial aspect of an automated flow is that developers can trust that the
code will work the same way each time deployed. In the meantime, IaC allows devel-
opers to update their infrastructure easily and change the deployed instance’s physical
location. Many developers might be familiar with the phrase ”It works on my machine!”
which refers to code that works locally on a single developer’s machine but breaks down
when deployed elsewhere. While this has become a viral meme in the developer commu-
nity, it describes a real challenge to ensure code always runs as intended independent of
the physical deployment.
Developers utilize containerization to solve the challenge stated above. Container-
ization in software development is utilized for packaging software into standardized
containers and isolating them from the runtime environment. In that way, an applica-
tion is ensured to always run in the same way independently of the given infrastructure.
These software containers comprise all the required resources for the application’s run-
time. That is libraries, configuration files, or tools, to name a few. A software container
only contains the required resources to ensure high performance and avoid unnecessary
memory usage.
A tool to apply containerization of applications is Docker. Docker might be familiar
to most developers, and many companies utilize it as part of the CI/CD setup. In short,
Docker is an open-source containerization platform that enables developers to pack their
software in containers. While developers can perform containerization without Docker,
the tool makes it safe and easy to build, deploy, and manage containers. Furthermore,
Docker provides a public registry with several pre-configured images, which is lightweight,

needs to run. That allows developers to pick images with only the necessary resources for
their application to run. In CI/CD, containerization contributes to the vast advantages
of standardizing the build, test, and deployment flow, especially with Docker images.

6.1.2
Repositories and Branching Strategies
Repositories and branching strategy are two key elements for keeping a structured and
clean project. In this context, repository refers to the git repository, wherein the source
code of the project or projects is kept and visioned. It is not how the repository itself
is structured but rather where to place it. Branching strategy refers to how branching
should be carried out when working on the project. A branching strategy should be
streamlined within a project to keep the project clean and remove unused branches. A
clean repository is vital, especially after a student project ends and handed over.
6.1.2.1
Repositories
The following artifact represents a set of guidelines on how repositories should be han-
dled to help the project owners keep track of the code repositories within a project. The
guidelines explain where repositories should be initialized, what a repository should in-
clude, and how to streamline the naming of repositories.
Where to initialize repositories
The overall repository structure depends on the size of the overall project. In some cases,
all repositories can be kept within the same project. In other cases, it is useful to divide
a project further, separate the different domains of a project, or separate backend and
frontend applications.
The repository structure to choose differs depending on the project, and it might evolve
during the implementation of the project as some domains might grow more prominent.
However, from a student’s point of view, this should not be a concern as the project
manager should decide where the student project should be placed. Thus, at the begin-
ning of a student project, the student should be granted access to the git project. Here,
students should initialize their git projects in the right domain. This must be done at
the very beginning of the project so that the student does not create another project
elsewhere and then need to move it later on.
What to include in a repository
In general, two approaches can be utilized to store and organize source code; Mono-repo
and Poly-repo. The entire project is stored under the same git repository when utilizing
a mono-repo strategy. Each backend application, frontend application, and shared li-
braries are put into separate directories under the same git repository. Poly-repo is the
==PAGE:70==
62
6 The III Student Development Method
opposite. Each application and the shared library has its own git repository.
For university projects, a poly-repo strategy should be utilized, meaning that each
application should be stored in its own repository. Poly-repo should be utilized over
mono-repo for the following reasons:
Onboarding
At a glance, Mono-repo makes it easier to onboard someone as the whole project can be
fetched at once. When the project is fetched, all the code needed to run the application
exists at the destination. Thus, a new developer can get started without complete do-
main knowledge. For poly-repo, a new developer has required a higher level of domain
knowledge because the developer would need to know which repositories are needed. Fur-
thermore, the developer would need to utilize the correct branch, which can be tricky if
the different repositories do not follow a unified branching strategy.
Running application locally
A mono-repo would be easier to run locally than a poly-repo. Because all applications
are stored in the same repository, all application paths are known, meaning that scripts
can be developed that start the whole project. Each application needs to be opened and
started separately for a poly-repo, for example, in separate terminals. However, this is
not an issue in most cases because applications are deployed to some server that can be
remotely accessed.
Independent development
Mono-repo introduces a tight coupling of projects, whereas poly-repo has no coupling
between projects. Thus, mono-repo requires a higher level of communication than a poly-
repo for the simple reason that it has multiple owners. A higher level of communication
significantly impacts the ability to work independently as changes can unintentionally
impact other parts of the system when, for example, core logic is changed.
Continuous integration / Continuous delivery pipeline
One of the key principles of microservices is that a component should be independently
deployable. For Continuous Integration, this goal is commonly achieved by setting up
a build and release pipeline. Configuring and triggering a build and release pipeline is
simpler for a poly-repo than for a mono-repo, as the pipeline will trigger whenever a code
change is merged for a specific application only. For the mono-repo, the pipeline can be
configured the same way as for poly-repo. However, if not configured, the pipeline will
trigger each time code changes are merged, meaning that changing one application will
trigger all pipelines. Thus, additional configuration is needed using mono-repo, making
it harder to utilize infrastructure as code.
Both mono-repo and poly-repo offer benefits and drawbacks. Both approaches have
been proven to work within more extensive projects [9]. However, it is recommended
to utilize a poly-repo strategy for university projects as it allows collaborates to work
==PAGE:71==
6.1 Project Management Solution Artifacts
63
independently. While mono-repo might simplify the on-boarding process and local test-
ing of the whole system, this does not necessarily provide value in student projects. It
is unlikely that students need knowledge of every application in the domain, but rather
the ones they need to integrate with. Furthermore, it is not the student’s job to test the
whole system, including other collaborator’s applications. Poly-repo removes the cou-
pling from other projects and allows the students to work more independently, which is
crucial in minimizing dependencies risk to external parties.
What to include in the poly-repo
A repository should, in general, consist of 3 things; source code, pipeline files, and a
readme file. The source code is the application and test files like unit test files and
integration test files. The pipeline files are needed to configure the pipeline. As will
be explained, the pipeline shall be defined as code. Therefore, these pipeline files need
to be included in the repository. The last file is a readme file. The readme file should
include all relevant information regarding running the code.
6.1.2.2
Branching Strategy
A branching strategy can help ensure that a repository is kept clean and meaningful.
A branching strategy ensures that everybody within a team follows the same version
control rules when contributing to a repository. This is important to avoid unhealthy
and unstructured repositories and ensure sound and streamlined version control for each
project. Furthermore, it plays an essential role in CI/CD as branches help to organize
the flows.
The recommended utilization of branches for student projects is:
• Never work on the main branch. The main branch should always be a working
version. Therefore, changes pushed to the main branch should always be reviewed
and tested before merging. Students should never push changes directly into the
main branch, as this can break down environments if automatic pipelines are con-
figured. Most tools offer only to allow changes in the main branch coming from
pull requests.
• Branches should be based on features. Students should utilize feature branching
to create a branch for each feature. When a feature is completed, it should be
merged with the main branch. This flow ensures simplicity, which is essential in a
student project as only one or few students might work in the same repository.
• Delete branches after merge.
In order to keep the repository uncluttered and
healthy, it is essential to delete the feature branches when they have been merged
into the main branch. In that way, only active branches are kept in the repository.
Most tools offer automatically deleting a branch after a merge, making it easier to
follow this guideline.
==PAGE:72==

• Link branches to work items. Pull requests can be linked to work tasks (issues,
bugs, epics, features) when utilizing project management tools. In general, one
branch should be created for each feature.
Thus, it can be beneficial for the
version control to link pull requests to work items when. Automatic flows can be
configured in most tools that automatically set a work task to ”completed” when
the pull request is completed.
• Give meaningful names to branches. Either name the branch according to the
feature’s name or the work task id if that approach is utilized. Always use lowercase
and replace space with underscore like ”this_is_a_feature_branch” or ”123456_-
feature”.

6.2.2
Microservices
A microservice should be develop only when the student project include the development
of new business logic. When that is the case, a new microservice should be introduced.
A microservice is relatively easy to integrate within the project landscape as it is a
independent unit that can be deployed almost everywhere.
6.2.2.1
Technology
A microservice can be develop in almost every programming language that supports asyn-
chronously communication. Thus a student could freely choose between Java, Python,
C#, NodeJS etc.. The same goes for the data storage, students can freely choose the
data storage technology they like, for example, SQL and NoSQL. Because the applica-
tion is isolated it does not matter which technology the student choose. That being said,
just because the architecture allow the student to pick freely, it do not means that the
student should do it. It will always be best to pick the same technologies when possible
for several reasons. First, it would be easier to seek help in the project, because other
developers might work with the same technology. Second, it reduced the domain knowl-
edge complexity as fewer technologies need to be maintained. Thus it is recommended

that the student develop their service in a programming language already utilized in the
project, however, it is not required and the architecture fully support the students to
pick freely.
6.2.2.2
Ensuring high code quality
It can be challenging for students to create software of high quality because they usually
have little experience, and the stuff they develop during their Bachelor’s or Master’s
theses may be something entirely new to them. Nevertheless, following simple rules can
ensure that the code is easier to understand for other developers.
• Always use type strong languages. It is important to use strongly typed program-
ming languages, so other developers can see how the code works, and what data
thus input in different functions.
• Always expose a user interface for the exposed API like Swagger or FastAPI. In
almost every case, a microservice should expose a API to the public world. At
least in development environment, there should be a public user interface so that
other developers can see the endpoint without reading the code.
• Do not leave unused code in the files. In order to keep files clean all unused code
should be remove also commented code. If unused code are kept in the files it
should always be stated why.
• Utilize environment variables when applicable. Environment variables should be
used for security reason and to enable deployment of multiple environment. Secret
should always be put into environment variables that is things like password, access
tokens and API keys. Other variables that should be put in environment variables
are ones that change based on the environment, for example database connection
strings, since you should have a database for each environment. It is important
not to include the environment variables files (.env file) in the git repository as
that is a potential security risk.
• Student should always include a Readme.md file with instructions on how to run
the application and other relevant information.
In addition to the above, students should always structure their applications well.
However, this really depends of the choice of programming language. A general rule is
to keep files short and divide code based on functionality For example controller classes,
model/interface classes and function classes. Functions/methods that are utilized more
than once should be made generic to avoid duplicated code.
6.2.2.3
Security
Microservices expose a public API that might be accessible from the outside world
depending on the configuration of the server where they are deployed. It is recommended

A microservice runs on a server, so its code isn’t visible to users. Nevertheless, avoid

6.2.3.2
Designing a micro frontend

micro frontend on a certain page and setting the frame for the base layout. The students
should be aware of this fact, as they should not develop a whole layout including header
navigation, content section and footer. Students should only develop a fragment that
can be loaded into a page in the main application.

6.2.3.5
Security
An essential aspect of frontend development is to be aware that frontend applications
are always insecure. Everything in a frontend application is exposed. Therefore, it is
important not to include anything sensitive in a frontend application. All communication
to the underlying system should happen through a BFF as this can be counted as a more
secure application because it is not running on the users’ devices.
6.2.4
BFF
Students should develop a Backend for Frontend when they also develop a frontend
application, and that frontend application needs to communicate with the backend sys-
tems. The functionality of this application is significant in that it facilitates the division
of responsibilities among applications and prevents frontend applications from commu-
nicating directly with backend services.
6.2.4.1
Technology
As for microservices, students can develop the BFF application in any programming
language that supports asynchronous communication. However, it is advisable to utilize
that same programming language as other applications. Therefore, the BFF should be
programmed in the same language as the frontend application, typically NodeJS, or in
the same language as the microservice.
6.2.4.2
Ensuring high code quality
In order to ensure high code quality in the BFF app, students should follow many of
the same guidelines stated for microservices and micro frontend:
• Utilize a strongly typed programming language.
• Do not leave unused code in files
• Utilize environment variables.
• Include Readme.md file
In addition to the above topics, students should follow the following recommendation:
• Divide endpoints based on the domain of functionality. It is essential to divide the
functionality based on the domain to avoid giant files with different responsibilities.
Furthermore, it is also beneficial to divide based on secure and not secure endpoints.
In that way, security can be defined only once in the BFF, and the code because
easier to read as there will be a precise segmentation of secure and non-secure
endpoints.
==PAGE:80==
72

• Meaningful HTTP responses. It is essential to deliver meaningful HTTP responses
to the frontend. Successful HTTP requests should be responded to with only the
requested data and a proper HTTP status code that corresponds to the CRUD
operation. In cases where an error occurs somewhere in the flow, it is essential to
handle this properly and deliver a meaningful HTTP response to the frontend.
• Configure CORS Rules. When an odd communication error occurs between the
frontend application and the BFF, the reason behind it is most likely CORS errors.
Students should be aware of this from the beginning and configure CORS rules for
the BFF. That includes defining allowed origins, which essential is allowed URLs
that can send requests to the BFF. Defined allowed headers, which is especially
important when customized headers are sent. Students can also define the max-
imum file size sent to BFF, which becomes handy when a BFF should transfer
files.
6.2.4.3
Security
The BFF exposes a public endpoint (API) that the world can request. It is impossible
to protect this API with an API key because the frontend applications would need to
include that key in the request to the BFF, and then the key is exposed. The best way
to protect the BFF is to configure CORS rules regarding allowed origins. Furthermore,
secure endpoints, that is, endpoints that require a valid token, should always validate
the token received because it is easy to generate and send a fake token. Hence, the
students who follow the stated guidelines regarding good code quality separate public and
protected endpoints. Thus, those students can create a generic authentication function
to secure a subset of endpoints instead of doing it for each endpoint.

example of this files will be given in Chapter 7, Section 7.5.
The main pipeline file is provider-dependent, meaning that a pipeline file created for
Azure DevOps will not work in GitLab and vice versa. As the artifacts within project
management state, a project should be utilizing the same provider for sub-projects within
the main project.
A student should be aware of the following when modifying the main pipeline tem-

• Pick the correct template file. A frontend- and a backend application might need
different configurations, therefore, different template files.

Docker is very documented, and it would be easier for students to find a configuration

Evaluation
This chapter presents an evaluation of the solution method and the associated artifacts
based on the case study’s learnings. The evaluation step is an essential step of design
science as it helps determine how effective the artifact is at solving the stated challenges.
The evaluation will be performed by evaluating each artifact in relation to the objectives
defined in Chapter 4. The evaluation of the artifacts will be summarized in the end when
answering the research questions.
8.1
Evaluation of the Project Management
Artifacts
The problems encountered in the project management domain were mainly related to
diﬀiculties tracking the student work related to software development. The objectives
primarily focused on utilizing a unified tool within a university project and guidelines
for structure repositories and branches.
The artifact related to utilizing a unified project management tool was demonstrated
in the case study. The case study illustrated how simple the workflow becomes when
the initial configuration is in place, which did not require much work. The utilization
of a project management tool also supported the objective related to enforcing an agile
workflow. As the artifact states, students should plan their work continuously by cre-
ating work items. Students are very much encouraged to work in sprints, as was also
demonstrated in the case study. This is a very important aspect of keeping students on
track of their work, as they would have clear objectives to aim for constantly. At the
same time, by defining tasks causally, it is much easier to get an overview of doing a
project. Ultimately, by utilizing a project management tool, students would be able to
optimize their work process.
Regarding repositories and branching strategy, these artifacts ensure that reposito-
ries are created in a unified way, meaning that one repository carries one service. The
artifacts also ensure that only active branches are kept in a repository and linked to
working items, making them easier to track. As demonstrated in the case study, these
artifacts should be reasonably easy to follow, and they do not introduce any additional
work.

One important thing regarding these artifacts is that some of the work that needs
to be done is not the student’s responsibility. The initial configuration of the project

Another important aspect is that students should be forced to utilize the project

8.2
Evaluation of the Software Development
Artifacts
The problem encountered in the software development domain was essential that the
software was not good enough and diﬀicult to integrate. Thus, the objectives focused
on optimizing the code quality and making it easier to integrate code. These objec-
tives should be fulfilled while still allowing students to work independently and without
extending the workload.
The artifacts state a target architecture that university projects should aim for. Es-
sentially, this is a microservice architecture extended with the micro frontend architec-
ture and BFFs. The target architecture was demonstrated within the case study. The
focus has predominantly been on showcasing the utilization of micro frontends, as that
is the application that somehow should function as one. The case study clearly demon-
strates how the micro frontend approach introduces decoupling of the frontend domain
and makes it easy for students to work independently on their frontend applications.
However, one thing that has not been discussed is when a micro frontend should be
integrated into the exiting project landscape. It is doubtful that every student project is
good enough to reach the production stage of a university project. As for now, there is
no recommendation regarding who has the responsibility for integrating students’ micro
frontends within the main application.
However, the artifacts clearly state that the
micro frontend should be developed with the API-first approach in mind, meaning that
the data that should be loaded into the micro frontend should be clearly defined. This
would make it easier to integrate the micro frontend.
Regarding BFF and microservices, these application types were not heavily demon-
strated in the case study.
This is because BFFs and microservices applications can
already be developed in any programming language, and the code quality will always
depend on the individual students’ skills. However, the case study demonstrated when
to develop either a BFF, a microservices, or both.

In what way does providing students with generic pipeline and infrastructure tem-

How well software can be integrated and how independently the same software can

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This project strives to solve the challenges discussed in Chapter 2 by introducing a de-
velopment method for student projects. This has been achieved by developing several
sets of artifacts that together form a development method that should be utilized in
student projects. The artifacts span project management, software development, and
integration and deployment. The objectives related to each domain and their associated
artifacts were explained in Chapter 4. These objectives were later utilized to design and
develop the content of the artifacts explained in Chapter 6.
The outcome of this project is the III Student Development Method, which is the first
development method that explicitly targets students’ projects within extensive univer-
sity projects as its audience. This method consists of different artifacts that have been
developed by analyzing existing industry solutions and case studies. These solutions
were further elaborated in a technology chapter that also evaluated the benefits and
drawbacks of each technology. The method has been demonstrated throughout a case
study that demonstrated the effectiveness of the different artifacts. Based on the case
study and a corresponding evaluation chapter, it can be concluded that a predefined
development method does make student projects easier to integrate and deploy. In ad-
dition, it can be concluded that utilizing template files makes it more manageable for
students to deploy their code into a predefined infrastructure. Also, it can be concluded
that by utilizing the stated target architecture, a student would be able to work inde-
pendently throughout the project and still deliver software that can be integrated.
Therefore, it can be concluded that the developed method, III Student Development
Method, addresses the stated challenges that exist within the student- and university
projects. The research method of this project has been based on the design science re-
search process. In regards to this, it can be concluded that this III Student Development
Method has been developed by following the design science research process to ensure
and evaluate the scientific contribution.

Detailed project description
Large software projects created in collaboration between the industry and universities, play an
important role when developing cutting edge technology. While companies have the industry
knowhow and capital resources, universities are ahead when it comes to research and utilizing new
technologies. For larger projects at universities, many students might contribute to the development
throughout their thesis. This introduces a problem on how to integrate multiple subprojects into an
overall project, when each subproject is created independently by students and has its own setup in
relation to software architecture and CI/CD pipelines.
The aim of this project is to define methods that make it easier to utilize software created in bachelor
or master theses. The methods should make it easier to get the software created within student theses
into production. Thus, it should focus on optimizing and streamlining the software development and
structure but also the integration of the software into the overall context. Since large software projects
1
==PAGE:132==
at universities typically are created in collaboration between the university and the industry, the
projects are in most cases too large and complex to be carried out within a single thesis. Therefore, it
is important that software is developed in a way which makes it easy to integrate with other
subprojects. Here, the goal is to define methods that ease integration of a subproject into an overall
project, and find a way for streamlining the software architecture and CI/CD pipelines.
The outcome of this project will be a method that universities should utilize when facilitating student
projects. The method should make all subprojects within a larger project more streamlined and more
integratable. Thus, the usability of the software created within the student project should be increased,
resulting in more student projects going into production.

problem landscape is shown in Figure 1:

As shown in Figure 1, several challenges have been discovered. Together, these challenges form a
problem landscape which exists within the context of this project. The problem can be divided into
three subgroups; project management, development and integration.
The project management have the following challenges:
-
Main focus is on creating a good thesis
-

As for all student theses, the main purpose of the thesis is to meet the learning objectives and write a
good thesis. Naturally, this affects the focus on creating quality software. Another factor is that the
student thesis is written in a fixed timeframe. This can trivialize some of the key elements that relate
to integration ability and deployment, since students only need a local version of the software running.
For both the project facilitator and the students, it can be challenging to keep track of progress. The
challenges are that the projects are carried out independently and also developed in non parallel time.
The software development have the following challenge:
-
Students are inexperienced in developing software
The biggest challenge in regards to developing quality and integration ready software is the
inexperience students have in creating large scale, industry ready software solutions. A bachelor or
master thesis would typically be a student's first or second large software project. Furthermore, it is
possible that a student has not configured a whole CI/CD environment before. The combination of the
inexperienced in developing and CI/CD makes it hard to deliver software in a certain quality that
meets production ready. Obviously the student can study and learn, but this is a time consuming task
that might not be prioritised, because the main purpose is not on creating the software. Furthermore,
the effect of the learning will be coming to take effect during the project itself.
The integration challenges is the following:
-
Project do not follow a unified CI/CD setup for e.g. pipelines
-
Student use different technologies and programming languages
-
Software is not documented
There are several challenges that make student projects difficult to integrate in an overall project.
First, the difference in use of technology and programming languages makes it more difficult to
combine two or more projects. Two codebases developed in different technologies e.g. C# and
Python, that should be merged into one codebase would require a lot of code rewriting, which is a
very time consuming task to do.
Another challenge is the inconsistency in the CI/CD setup. When the overall project does not have
guidelines for a CI/CD setup, students might set up their own CI/CD environment or even skip it,
since it is not needed for them in order to make the code run locally. Because of this inconsistency it is
a challenging and time consuming task to integrate and  deploy the software afterwards.
The last challenge, which also relates to the timeframe and the inexperience, is that the software is not
documented. For the inexperienced developer, it can be challenging to document parts of the code that
they might not fully understand. Furthermore, the time constraint can also trivialize this task.
Depending on the code quality, it would be hard for a person to integrate the code afterwards,
especially if the code does not follow best practise guidelines.

The milestones plan define fixed due dates of when different parts of the project should be
done. These milestones are created in order to overcome the work areas with highest risk and
at the same time follow the natural flow of a software development process.
The milestones represent overall due dates for the project and when several parts should be
done on a high level. A milestone is defined with a due date, a goal and the part of the
report which should be done. Each milestone will contain a fixed number of sprints
depending on the due date. The sprint length scoped to a 2 week interval with 4 working days
each week.
