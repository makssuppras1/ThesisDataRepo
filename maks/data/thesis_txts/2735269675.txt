Abstract
Marine biodiversity decline and degrading ecosystems due to anthropogenic activities
constitute a global crisis with dire impacts on ocean health and consequently human
well-being. The need for targeted policies and results on preservation efforts has led
to the emergence of startups with innovative solutions that aim to fill the knowledge
gap with automated and consistent monitoring. These newly founded companies are
highly focused on product development and do not allocate resources to efficient project
management due to constraints that are inherent to this venture setting. This fact
can become problematic, especially so in the marine monitoring sector, where projects
are an indivisible part of the process. The purpose of this thesis is to address this
challenge by developing a project management method implementing lean and agile
methodologies to accommodate the particular needs of startups. This was achieved by
exploring existing literature, identifying gaps, compiling relevant tools, and combining
them in a framework. The framework was adjusted to Anemo Robotics, a company
operating in this sector by gathering data on their operations, conducting interviews,
and implementing feedback loops to ensure its suitability. The model was tested in one
of the company’s projects to ensure robustness and received approval. Furthermore,
it was presented to an external experienced project manager who validated it and
provided recommendations. In conclusion, the development of a project management
framework tailored to startup challenges in this sector offered a pathway for addressing
the pressing subject of marine biodiversity loss and contributed to bridging the general
knowledge gap in project management in startups.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement
Currently, humanity is in the midst of a biodiversity crisis; in the last 50 years, the
wildlife population has shrunk by 68%. Scientists refer to this phenomenon as the
6th mass extinction, fully driven by human activity due to unsustainable land, water,
and energy use. Legislative and public pushes have started to drive various stakehold-
ers into action to mitigate these effects and restore biodiversity with MRV methods.
However, sparse and inadequate data collection methods render this endeavor hard
and inefficient, especially in underwater environments.
Anemo Robotics, is a DTU startup aiming to automate marine monitoring with the
use of underwater cameras and AI software based on computer vision. The company,
recently founded by four engineers, has just started acquiring clients and projects.
They have identified a knowledge gap when it comes to efficient planning and executing
these projects since so far their focus has been on product development. Recently they
secured a large-scale project in collaboration with other stakeholders which led to the
realization of the gap as the company did not know how to commence with planning
and the resources required.
How can Anemo Robotics adequately plan and execute projects with
clients, while operating in a highly dynamic setting in a niche sector?
To address the problem statement, the following research questions will be answered:
1. What are the main challenges the company is facing when it comes to planning
and execution as a startup in this sector?
2. How can the company enhance resource allocation and strategic planning while
executing projects?
3. How can the company adopt and improve these processes to achieve success?
To answer these questions, existing literature on the topics of biodiversity preservation
and project management in startups will be explored to identify the main challenges
and provide an overview of the potential tools available for enhancing resource allo-
cation and strategic planning. These tools will be adapted to the company’s needs
and compiled in a framework that can be used in current or future projects. Finally,
the framework will be tested on the company’s current project and the results will be
discussed to offer insights on its adoption and future improvement potential.
1.2
Thesis Outline
This section provides a brief overview of the thesis to make the contents of it clearer
to the reader.
Chapter 1 introduces the topic and provides the problem statement and the
necessary research questions that must be answered in the scope of this work.

1.3
Learning Objectives
The learning objectives of this thesis are designed to cultivate a deep comprehension
of the research problem. More specifically, the topics of biodiversity, innovation, and
project management in startups will be addressed to achieve this and enhance critical
thinking.
Upon completion, the expected outcomes are:
• Concrete understanding of the biodiversity crisis and the significance of preser-
vation and the inherent challenges of startups
• Identification and analysis of the company’s challenges in project planning and
execution in their sector.
• Comprehensive literature review of the topics.
• Application of advanced project management principles to plan, execute, and
evaluate projects in a framework
• Testing and evaluation of framework in the company’s current project.
• Critical reflection and discussion of findings and recommendations and insights
into the process.

Anemo Robotics
2.1
Brief company introduction
Anemo Robotics was established in December of 2022 and incorporated in May of
2023. The company is a DTU start-up that provides tools to monitor marine biodi-
versity around man-made structures amid the urgent global biodiversity crisis. The
company’s mission is to empower organizations to make sustainable choices by pro-
viding cutting-edge monitoring technology and combining it with proprietary AI fish
detection software.
In simple terms, the product consists of a hardware device and software. The hardware
is an underwater camera equipped with sensors that acquire data on marine life and
capture short videos. The data is then fed to the AI fish detection software, which
is used to quantify species abundance. The premise is that companies that have off-
shore structures (e.g.wind farms), would use the product to assess and monitor marine
biodiversity around said structures. The assessment will serve as a tool to form an
action plan to mitigate potentially adverse effects on nature caused by these activities,
eventually leading to more sustainable practices.
As of March 2024, the company consists of four co-founders and five student workers.
Each employee has a different focus area and expertise.
2.2
Hardware
As aforementioned, the technology the company provides consists of two modules. In
this section, the hardware will be presented as it is of essence in the overall applica-
tion. The company sees it as the means to gather the appropriate data to perform the
assessments.
Anemo Robotics is developing underwater cameras designed to operate autonomously
for over a month with minimal intervention throughout the duration of the deploy-
ment. Their impact on ecosystems is minimal, as they are stationary and the materials
used are specifically designed for marine environments. The deployment of cameras
reduces the need for costly and time-consuming manned surveys. This allows for more
frequent and detailed impact assessments of marine biodiversity for clients.
When the company commenced the development of the cameras, the design was very
simplistic. The initial iteration served the purpose of testing the viability of cameras
as an option for marine assessments. In figures 1 and 2, the first prototype iteration
can be seen before and during tank testing.

2.3
Software
The product’s other module is the proprietary software developed by the company.
Essentially, the footage obtained from the cameras in the deployments is processed
using the software.
Traditionally, in marine biodiversity assessments, video analysis has been done manu-
ally by scientists. They assess each frame individually and measure the abundance of
species by manually counting fish.
The company has automated this process, reducing the need for manual counting and
minimizing the time needed for processing by developing a multi-class model based on
the YOLO-v8 framework. Presently, the model is capable to count fish in the videos
and to classify them in species.
The development of the model required a great amount of data. The footage used for
training the model was obtained by deployments with clients and DTU Aqua. The
company also received already manually processed data with labeled species and count,
from DTU to further enhance the algorithm and test the model’s functionality. The
software can now predict species with an 80-90% accuracy. A snapshot can be seen
below in figure 6.

2.4
Market Overview
The company has almost completed the product development on the MVP, but what
is the market for their solution?
To answer this question, a market overview will be provided to showcase this need and
its application in real-life scenarios. The individual markets that have been identified
can be classified as those that have a direct impact on biodiversity and those that
affect marine biodiversity indirectly. The market segments that have been identified
are:
• Biodiversity positive initiatives
• Offshore construction projects/companies
• General Health of Marine Environments
2.4.1
Biodiversity positive initiatives
In recent years, the focus has shifted to mitigating the negative effects human activity
has brought upon marine biodiversity. Various companies have emerged that are de-
Page 7 of 67
==PAGE:19==
veloping solutions that contribute to the improvement of ocean habitats through the
creation of artificial reefs, fish gardens and use of ecological concrete (Vivier et al.,
2021). These initiatives utilize technological advancements in sustainable materials
that have a lesser impact on marine life to help restore ecosystems that have been
degraded by construction and pollution.
This segment constitutes the first market that Anemo Robotics has targeted, as ex-
tensive monitoring is needed (Vivier et al., 2021). The potential of this market is
good whereas the challenges and difficulties are deemed low, making it the perfect
beachhead market for the company. Most companies are small and easily reachable as
they are highly receptive to innovation and collaboration with startups. Furthermore,
Anemo Robotics’ technology is very well suited for the mission these companies have
adopted. Demonstrating the effectiveness of their solutions by monitoring is of utmost
importance to them.
Challenges in this market segment are comparatively lower as the structures and tests
are typically conducted closer to shore, allowing for easier accessibility. Furthermore,
the regulations are less strict, and projects are carried out on a smaller scale requir-
ing 3-5 cameras per project.
The company has reached out immensely to various
companies that belong in this segment and has received positive responses, with two
commitments to purchase MVP units once they become available.
Looking closely to the size of these companies, the growth potential might appear
relatively low if compared to bigger players in other market segments. However, it
is important to note that this market has helped the company solidify its role as a
provider of marine biodiversity monitoring solutions. Testing the product with these
smaller companies has led to a more robust design of the solution, as the company
involved the customers in the product’s design process and gathered valuable feedback.
Lastly, as these companies apply for funding they are often required to partner up
with someone to monitor their results. Therefore, it is concluded that there is a big
opportunity in partnering up with companies for such projects. Examples of companies
that belong in this market are ARC Marine, Econcrete, CircularReef and Reefy.
2.4.2
Offshore construction projects/companies
Construction or energy companies that have offshore projects such as Ørsted, Vatten-
fall, Boskalis and Total Energies, can have adverse effects on marine life when a project
is commenced. Bergström et al., 2014 on their study on effects of offshore wind farms
on marine wildlife, concluded that in the areas they carried out their assessments, the
impact varied. In the construction phase, the impact to marine biodiversity was neg-
ative, but throughout the operations both a positive and negative impact was observed.
The recent growth of offshore wind energy promises great potential for marine biodi-
versity monitoring and assessment. The incentive for monitoring is highly motivated
by their corporate culture, as many of these companies are striving to be biodiversity
net positive. Accurate monitoring is crucial for them to reach that goal. The size and
expenditures of these projects, signify that a portion of their budget is allocated to
Page 8 of 67
==PAGE:20==
monitoring. Current methods are very costly, and ineffective as they consist of yearly
expeditions that only capture a small time snippet of the situation. Furthermore, they
are heavily CO2 intensive.
Biodiversity monitoring for these companies has been part of their strategic goals al-
ready. Whether this is mandated by regulatory requirements that become stricter or
it is done for PR purposes, they are eager to demonstrate their true commitment to
biodiversity protection to their shareholders and the public.
For example, according to Ørsted, 2023 website they have set the ambition to deliver
a net-positive biodiversity impact from all new renewable energy projects it commis-
sions from 2030 at the latest. Ørsted has led initiatives such as the development of
artificial reefs to support Atlantic cod at Borssele 1 & 2 in the Netherlands; monitoring
of crustacean habitats at Westermost Rough in the UK; and a program to protect and
conserve the North Atlantic right whale.
Even though the potential of this market segment is good, the barrier to enter is
greater, when compared to biodiversity enhancing companies, as these corporations
are big, well-established and risk-averse. Furthermore, projects are typically located
far from shore and in deeper parts of the ocean, which poses logistical challenges.
Therefore, the company desires to gain experience through other projects in the first
market and thereafter transition in this sector.
2.4.3
General Health of Marine Environments
The title of this section is quite broad, as every market segment has the potential
of ensuring the health of marine environments. However, the differentiating factor is
the motive behind it as explained above. Initiatives led by non-profit organizations
or research activities are done for the advancement of science and with the ultimate
goal to preserve biodiversity. Therefore, this market segment refers to initiatives led
by these parties.
Anemo Robotics has already worked in this sector through their collaboration with
DTU Aqua. This collaboration has led to the validation of the technology through pilot
tests and to the improvement of certain product components. Based on the company’s
experience working with DTU Aqua and assisting in deployments, it is apparent that
this sector would need a technology such as this one. For example, the processing time
for one hour of footage captured in an average expedition requires four to five hours
of manual processing, usually performed by scientists themselves. With the use of
only the software, more resources would be freed and thus this time could be spent on
more important tasks and allow for the entirety of the research to move forward faster.
Similarly, other research initiatives that employ manual processing could benefit from
this solution, such as projects led by NGOs or governments and municipalities as it
will provide them with accurate biodiversity assessments in a timely fashion.

2.5
Company deliverables
Having established the technology and the market for this solution, the next step is to

3.1
Definition of Biodiversity
To properly understand the subject and its complexity, it is essential to establish the
definition of biodiversity. Having a clear outline of what is biodiversity and how it
relates to conservation biology is important as it will lead to the simplification of its
quantification in terms of monitoring.
The term biodiversity is coined by the combination of the words Biological Diversity.
In 1992, a treaty was signed by 150 nations at the Convention on Biological Diversity
in Rio de Janeiro, declaring that (Gaston and Spicer, 2004):
Biological diversity means the variability among living organisms from all sources in-
cluding, inter alia, terrestrial, marine, and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological
complexes of which they are part; this includes diversity within species, between species
and of ecosystems (United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), 1992).
Essentially, the term ’biodiversity’ captures the variety of life on Earth in species

abundance, genetic diversity, and the ecosystems they inhabit. Its definition is multi-
dimensional and understanding its essence is a fore step to establish the importance
of preserving it.
3.2
Biodiversity loss and preservation importance
Biodiversity loss can be classified as a phenomenon that has spatial and temporal di-
mensions and extends beyond species extinction. Its attributes are a decline in the
extent, condition, or sustainable productivity of ecosystems, species extinction, declin-
ing populations and distribution, and genetic erosion (United Nations Environment
Programme (UNEP), 1994).
3.2.1
Decline of populations & species
The WWF and the Zoological Society of London have coined the LPI, through the
Living Planet Project. The LPI offers great insights into vertebrate population decline
on a global scale. Even though their research might have some limitations due to the
lack of data for species in certain regions, it remains a great indicator of loss, especially
in regions where data is readily available (Ritchie et al., 2022).
Their research showcases that globally wildlife populations have experienced a steady
decline for almost 50 years. The baseline of the study is 1970 when the population is
considered to be at 100%. In 2018, the LPI was measured to be 30.89%, signifying
immense loss (Ritchie et al., 2022). The decline can be seen in figure 7.

3.2.2
Decline of populations & species in marine ecosystems
The ocean’s role in climate regulation and weather patterns is significant, as it can
impact rainfalls, droughts, and floods (Tanzer et al., 2015). Furthermore, the ocean
is capable of capturing great amounts of CO2 emissions and it is estimated that in
the last 200 years, it has managed to capture almost one-third of emissions driven by
human activities, which has led to ocean acidification at an increasing rate (Gattuso
et al., 2015). Consequentially, causing a demise of water quality, and reduction of the
ocean’s ability to provide sustenance (Worm et al., 2006).
Evidently, the ocean is under immense pressure as humans have considered it to be
a boundless area incapable of being affected by anthropogenic activities and an inex-
haustible sustenance source. The result of these actions has created a domino effect
impacting all marine species. The ocean has its limits and its resources are not used
sustainably by humans (Tanzer et al., 2015).
The decline in populations of marine vertebrates is found to be high, with a 49% es-
timated decline of LPI from 1970 to 2012. The main driver is human exploitation,
whereas habitat loss and degradation constitute major threats, as well (Tanzer et al.,
2015). Momentarily, 29% of fish stocks are overly exploited, the size of seagrass and
corals has reduced by 50% and at current rates, the ocean temperature is expected to
rise 3-5◦C by 2100, ensuring the extinction of coral reefs by 2050 (Tanzer et al., 2015).
As marine ecosystems exhibit a heightened sensitivity concerning climate change, over-
exploitation, and habitat degradation, these phenomena have instigated a 25% decline
in species (Worm et al., 2006). One example is rising temperatures due to climate
change, that aid in the development of pathogens that can lead to disease transmis-
sion. As a result, many species and their population is negatively impacted (Harvell
et al., 2002).
Essentially, the key risks associated with climate change and the ocean, create a cas-
cading effect. Rising temperatures and high CO2 absorption, lead to the destruction
of coral reefs due to acidification which in turn direly impacts biodiversity, fish-stock
levels and erodes natural coastal protection mechanisms. In turn, apart from the im-
pact on biodiversity itself, humans are and will be suffering from the consequences
as well, as the loss of settlements and reduced food supply will continue to intensify
(Tanzer et al., 2015).
As is evident, the unsustainable use of oceanic resources and marine biodiversity loss
can have dire results on the environment and humans. Therefore, it is essential to
ensure that biodiversity loss due to anthropogenic factors is mitigated and reversed if

possible.
3.2.3
Past initiatives on biodiversity preservation
As seen in the previous section, the decline in population and species is a phenomenon
that must be mitigated, due to the adverse impact it can have on the planet. Biodiver-
sity preservation has been an important topic not only within the scientific community
but also in the public sphere in recent years. The awareness and conversations about
it have evolved over time with various initiatives emerging throughout the years.
Biodiversity preservation gained more attention at the start of the 21st century, as
the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment of 2005 provided a wide-ranging overview of
ecosystem degradation on a global scale, shedding light on the consequences for human
well-being. The study concluded that in a span of 50 years, human activity has neg-
atively impacted nature more swiftly than ever recorded causing irreversible damage
and immense biodiversity loss (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2003). Further-
more, it was found that even though economic growth and high net gains were achieved
through the activities that led to biodiversity loss at the time, the benefits that future
generations can have from nature will be substantially less, due to the exacerbated
consumption of its resources. Ecosystem degradation is expected to worsen over time
unless significant changes in practices and policies are applied to mitigate the effects
of this phenomenon (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2003).
In 2010, the UN stated that 2011 to 2020 will constitute the Decade on Biodiversity.
This declaration served the purpose of raising awareness about the importance of
biodiversity conservation and the promotion of the ’Strategic Plan for Biodiversity’.
The mission of this plan was to:
"...take effective and urgent action to halt the loss of biodiversity to
ensure that by 2020 ecosystems are resilient and continue to provide
essential services, thereby securing the planet’s variety of life, and con-
tributing to human well-being, and poverty eradication. To ensure this,
pressures on biodiversity are reduced, ecosystems are restored, biological
resources are sustainably used and benefits arising out of the utilization
of genetic resources are shared fairly and equitably; adequate financial
resources are provided, capacities are enhanced, biodiversity issues and
values mainstreamed, appropriate policies are effectively implemented,
and decision-making is based on sound science and the precautionary
approach.” (Secretariat of the Convention on Biological Diversity, 2019)
The Aichi Targets introduced in the same year, were part of the scope of the strategic
plan for biodiversity. The 20 targets provided a framework to mitigate biodiversity
loss, within 5 distinct goals. The goals revolved around addressing rudimentary causes
of biodiversity loss, the reduction of the inflicted pressure, the improvement of its
status, the benefit enhancement derived from biodiversity, and the improvement of
knowledge management (Biosafety Unit, 2020).

According to Erdelen, 2020, regarding the post-2020 global biodiversity framework,
the ambitious goals set by the UN in 2010 and the Aichi Targets described above
were not met in the set time frame. To be more precise, CBD in their fifth edition of

also found that the flexible framework proposed removed the incentive for the imple-

3.2.5
Can nature be replicated?
In the previous sections, the initiatives for preserving and restoring biodiversity have
been presented. As previously seen, humanity failed to meet the Aichi targets and
in the latest report on the SDGs, the progress has not resolved the overall issue that
persists concerning marine biodiversity loss. A very interesting question emerges when
stressing the importance of biodiversity:
If all actions fail, can we as humans restore biodiversity artificially?
Can we regain everything that has been lost?
This question has no immediate, straightforward answer. It is simply impossible to
answer without examining every ecosystem that exists on Earth. Biodiversity is not a
singular, one-dimensional entity that can be easily replicated. It is an intricate system
with many variables, that even the slightest disturbance can affect potentially irre-
versibly (Díaz et al., 2020).
It is, however, of value to examine the scientific community’s take on ecosystem replica-
tion. For this purpose, a brief introduction to biodiversity offsets and other initiatives
that aim to rebuild ecosystems that have degraded due to human activity will be
presented. Offsets are the last step on the mitigation hierarchy and are to be used
when appropriate actions on avoiding, minimizing, and restoring the loss have failed
(OECD, 2016).
Page 17 of 67
==PAGE:29==
Biodiversity offsets have been defined by the BBOP as: ‘measurable conservation out-
comes resulting from actions designed to compensate for significant residual adverse
impacts arising from project development after appropriate prevention and mitigation
measures have been taken. The goal of biodiversity offsets is to achieve no net loss and
preferably a net gain of biodiversity on the ground with respect to species composition,
habitat structure, ecosystem function and people’s use and cultural values associated
with biodiversity’ (Business and Biodiversity Offsets Programme (BBOP), 2009).
In many countries, offset policies have started to form to reduce the adverse effects
of construction and promote biodiversity preservation, while it is also observed on a
corporate level as achieving no net loss or gain has gained traction in companies (Gel-
cich et al., 2016). Examples can be found globally, such as the Environmental Offsets
in Australia, where the objective is to improve or maintain protected environmental
aspects, the Policy for the Management of Fish Habitat in Canada, designed to achieve
no net loss in the country’s fisheries, the Impact Mitigation Regulation in Germany
aiming at preserving the existing ecological situation and the Conservation Banking
in the USA for offsetting impact on certain species (OECD, 2016).
As offsets are considered actions that promote biodiversity gain to account for loss
through construction, developers first have to avoid, minimize and rehabilitate any
adverse effect that might be incurred on biodiversity (Business and Biodiversity Off-
sets Programme (BBOP), 2012). A key component to offsetting is ‘no net loss’ and
‘in-kind compensation’. Both terms can have different meanings depending on the
type of habitat affected and therefore schemes might vary significantly in their ap-
proach. Understandably so, this makes offsets inconsistent which does not allow for
the creation of a consistent fungible unit that encompasses biodiversity loss and gain,
rendering biodiversity a non-tradable market commodity (Bull et al., 2013).
Offsets constitute a mechanism for estimating certain negative environmental impacts
on development projects that come at a cost. Based on this, three criteria have been
set that make offsets unique, namely (Bull et al., 2013):
• Provision of substitution or replacement of adverse impacts on biodiversity
• Measurement of loss and gain must be possible
• No net loss must be achieved
Even though, the idea of offsets might appear as a good solution to the decline of
biodiversity, they continue to receive criticism from the scientific community. For in-
stance, achieving all three criteria poses a challenge in offsets. The main underlying
problems are that there is no single metric that can be used across all types of offsets
to measure biodiversity, the definition of ’no net loss’ is vague, due to the lack of a
baseline for measurement, the challenge of establishing equivalence among different
ecosystems, the short time frame of these projects and the incurred time lag between
the project and the offset, among others (Bull et al., 2013). This is further solidi-
fied across the literature. According to Gelcich et al., 2016 biodiversity offsets have
conceptual flaws, and lack consistency in meeting conservation objectives due to the
difficulty of ensuring full compliance, whereas effective monitoring of the outcomes is
Page 18 of 67
==PAGE:30==
rarely applied. Quétier and Lavorel, 2011 comments that the lack of proper design
methods and requirements poses a problem in offset initiatives, whereas criticism also
revolves around the essence of offsets themselves. Trading biodiversity loss for uncer-
tain future gains can be problematic (Maron et al., 2016).
Examining the practical challenges of biodiversity offsets, issues in compliance, and
on the outcomes arise. There is a lack of information on the offset outcome post-
implementation. This can be attributed to non-existent monitoring, on the burden of
proof, and on the uncertainty of the capabilities of the offsetting entity to achieve the
desired outcomes (Bull et al., 2013).
The challenges observed in terrestrial and marine offset share a lot of similarities.
However, offsets intended for marine environments have a unique component that ren-
ders them more challenging than terrestrial offsets (UNEP-WCMC, 2016). The issue
lies in the scale and the degree of connectivity when it comes to marine environments
(Crowder and Norse, 2008). The offsets require a consistent policy framework and a
good knowledge base as the application risks are not well documented (Niner et al.,
2017).
Lastly, examining biodiversity offsets from an ethical standpoint is also important.
Karlsson and Edvardsson Björnberg, 2020 in their study ’Ethics and biodiversity off-
setting’ examine these initiatives and identify five main ethical objections.
1. Offsets are against nature’s intrinsic value - Considering and treating na-
ture as a commodity to serve economic growth rather than recognizing its in-
trinsic and inherent value is problematic. Nature must be protected because it is
nature. It should not serve the purpose of sustaining harmful human activities.
2. Loss cannot be compensated - it is impossible to quantify loss and create
equivalence. It is even harder to equate the offset gains with the original setting.
3. Insufficient information to make proper trades - too little is known for
making comparable offsets. Biodiversity is too complex to be replicated.
4. Risk of adopting the mentality of nature as a commodity - offsets create
the precedent of treating nature as a commodity that can be traded and replaced
for the needs of construction.
5. Negative justice implications - the potential benefits and costs of offsets are
distributed unjustly geographically or socially.
Hence, even though the attempt to replicate natural habitats appears appealing and
might even be considered an act of redemption to destruction on the altar of economic
growth, it has received criticism and is considered a questionable practice scientifically
and ethically.
Is there then any potential in offsets?
Page 19 of 67
==PAGE:31==
As the popularity of offsets continues to rise in policies and frameworks aimed at mit-
igating biodiversity loss, their potential might increase. However, to achieve proper
offsetting it is important to minimize the risks when implementing this practice, and
attention should be paid as it is not to be considered a panacea (Maron et al., 2016).
Various options such as offsets, concerning biodiversity loss are explored but the main
focus point should remain on what could be changed in behaviors and practices to not
further damage ecosystems and trigger an irreversible situation.

3.4.1
The startup setting
A startup can be defined as a novel company that is producing an innovative product
or service and its main operating resource is the knowledge, skills, experience, and
social capital of the founders (Skala, 2019).
The common challenges startups face are the lack of resources, small teams who have
little to no working experience, and the small time frame available for product release
among others. This highly volatile environment can be dangerous for a startup com-
pany. The transition from an idea to a company can be difficult, as extreme uncertainty
and high-risk settings can ’make or break’ a company (Giardino et al., 2014). Failure
cannot be attributed to one parameter. External factors such as a small customer base
or a restricted market or internal factors such as poor strategic and business planning
can determine success (Szathmári et al., 2024).
Flexible working methods that are ideal for such settings, can aid in the decision-
making process and help mitigate the risks of recurring challenges. Such practices
include the adoption of frameworks designed to provide easy and fast product adapta-
tion to market needs, ongoing feedback between the company and the customers, and
implementation of KPIs to track and learn from it. Furthermore, the use of tools for
product development facilitation and management is essential (Giardino et al., 2014).
However, technological development, market size, and team are not the only essen-
tial parameters determining success. Inadequacy and inefficient project management
might cause startups to face additional challenges. Even though various project man-
agement techniques are implemented across organizations when it comes to startups
their different priorities, make it hard for these tools to be used properly. This lack of
usage can incur issues such as chaotic practices and disorganization. Nevertheless, tra-
ditional project management techniques might not be appropriate in a startup setting,
as typically these companies experience immense growth indicating a rapid change in
their developmental stage, which renders traditional methods insufficient. This high-
paced and volatile environment requires innovative tools that account for this rapid
change (Mantilla, 2020).
In a study on project management practices in Portuguese startups, it was found that
implementing consistent and innovative project management methodologies gave them
a competitive advantage when it came to time and cost savings, stakeholder manage-
ment, risk mitigation, and scope definition. The most used methods encompassed an
agile approach, such as Kanban and Scrum, but it is highlighted that the scarcity of
Page 23 of 67
==PAGE:35==
resources prevented some of them from investing in the implementation of such ap-
proaches (Tereso et al., 2019).
Hence, it is of interest to explore innovative methods concerning project management
for a startup setting, where development might be rapid and the team might not be
able to efficiently handle the changes. Therefore, it would be beneficial for startups to
explore and potentially implement an agile approach or lean approach to their way of
working in general as well as on a project execution level.
3.4.2
Agile project management
The agile approach was first developed in 1991 and implemented in manufacturing,
nevertheless, its efficacy led to its extension in the Agile Manifesto that popularized
the agile approach for software development (Journal, 2013).
In its essence, the agile approach increases an organization’s ability to adapt to change
and unforeseen events and entails four principles that underline its value, which are
described in the agile manifesto. According to the approach, individuals and in-
teractions should be prioritized over the company’s processes and tools. Further-
more, having a working software is more important than producing extensive
documentation. Customer collaboration should also be prioritized over contract
negotiation and finally, a positive response to change constitutes a better approach
than blindly following a plan (Journal, 2013).
The agile approach described in the Agile Manifesto primarily addressed software de-
velopment, but the approach was later adapted into a more uniform methodology. The
three actions that should be prioritized are value over constraints, team leader-
ship over task management, and adaption to change over conformance to
plan (Highsmith, 2004).
This uniform approach can be utilized either in an organizational context or specifically
applied to projects as a generalized action plan that can be used in project management
where systems thinking, adaptation of the methodology, and creation of customer value
are integral to the successful planning and execution in uncertain settings (Ghezzi and
Cavallo, 2020).
This approach constitutes the Agile Project Management (APM)
Mindset and has five core principles (Cobb, 2011):
Rolling wave planning: Limited planning should be applied in the beginning
and a dedicated team should review and plan while the project progresses to
mitigate changes. It is important to determine the review frequency and engage
relevant stakeholders in the process.
Collective ownership: The outcome should be a collective result, where all
members hold some type of responsibility in the delivery. The project manager
must empower the team and control the limiting factors concerning the outcome.
Customer collaboration: Clients should be involved in the process to ensure
that the project provides value to them and fits their standards.
Page 24 of 67
==PAGE:36==
Validation: Validation is integral, as the correct product or outcome must be
delivered to customers.
Systematic improvement: The project can be split into increments or deliv-
ered in iterations. In this manner, the organization or the team can test and
validate the result before the delivery.
Some known techniques that can be used to implement the principles when delivering
a project are short daily stand-up meetings, consensus building where the team agrees
on decisions and aligns their expectations, and time-boxing that allows the work to be
split into set periods, where tasks must be completed in that time frame (Moran, 2015).
The startup setting could potentially be a good ground on which an agile approach
would flourish, as seen in section 3.4.1. Over the years, Scrum and XP, known agile
methods have been implemented in small or large companies popularizing the agile
approach (Highsmith and Cockburn, 2001). In a study involving 1526 startups, on
whether software startups are implementing agile approaches, it was found that most
implement agile approaches in different degrees. The most utilized approaches were
speed-related processes, whereas quality-related and communication methods were
overlooked (Pantiuchina et al., 2017).
All in all, agile adoption can be a complex
endeavor, as factors such as resistance to change, high need for support, and lack
of fitting organizational culture might contribute to the successful implementation
(Campanelli and Silva Parreiras, 2015). Furthermore, some practices might not even
be suitable for startups or small companies as their impact might negatively affect the
organization (Mkpojiogu et al., 2019).
3.4.3
Lean project management
The Toyota production system (TPS), developed by Taiichi Ohno constitutes a man-
agement method implemented across the company’s production lines to minimize waste
and optimize production (Ohno and Bodek, 2019). Taiichi Ohno identified seven types
of waste present in any process. This waste was found in transportation, where parts
were unnecessarily moved, in inventory where products were waiting to be completed,
in motion, where people moved around without purpose, in waiting times for the com-
mencement of the next steps, in the over-processing, over-production and defects of
products (Ohno and Bodek, 2019). The overall philosophy of TPS inspired lean think-
ing. Lean thinking considers an additional type of waste, namely goods and services
that do not provide any value to the customers and do not meet their needs (Womack
and Jones, 1996).
Lean is defined as doing more with less. The five core principles of this philosophy
encompass a methodology designed to reduce or eliminate waste (Womack and Jones,
1996):
1. Value should be defined by the customer to minimize complexity
2. The value stream from product to customer should be identified
3. A flow for value-adding activities must be created and departments that perform
singular tasks must be eliminated
Page 25 of 67
==PAGE:37==
4. The customer should be allowed to pull the product
5. The aim for perfection should always be part of the process
Initially coined as a tool to be used in production, the lean philosophy has expanded
into other sectors. In project management, lean constitutes a toolbox that can be po-
tentially utilized for reducing a project’s duration (Leach, 2006). Lean can be viewed
as a complementary method to traditional project management with a heightened fo-
cus on customer needs (Pomfret and DM, 2009), creating a more agile approach to
minimizing waste (Erne, 2022a).
The core principles of LPM are based on the values of lean. Essentially, the steps
for implementing this philosophy in project management are the identification of the
best possible cost-benefit ratio of the customer, the definition of value-creating work
packages or processes, the establishment of clear responsibilities, tasks, and authorities,
the continuous flow of results by minimizing work in progress and the immediate
identification and reparation of defects (Erne, 2022b). These steps aim at streamlining
processes, eliminating unnecessary tasks, and optimizing resource use. Some tools and
methods that fall under the LPM are (Erne, 2022b):
• Project Value Proposition Design to assess customer needs
• Requirements Elicitation from surveys, interviews, observations
• Triple-Decker Elevator Pitching
• Role assignment and description
• Delegation
• Time-boxing
• Collocation
• Daily standup meetings
In startups, lean thinking was applied in the creation of the concept of Lean startup by
Ries, 2017. This method embraces learning from failure and uses generic practices to
validate elements in the business model by employing quick iteration processes (Silva
et al., 2020), allowing for quick product development based on customer needs. It
should be noted that even though the concept has gained traction and has risen in
popularity in the past years the literature is limited (Silva et al., 2020)
3.4.4
Stakeholder analysis
In the ISO 21500 standard, a stakeholder is defined as "a person, group or organization
that has interests in or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by,
any aspect of the project" (“ISO 21500:2012 (E) Guidance on project management”,
2012).
Page 26 of 67
==PAGE:38==
Distinguishing between shareholders and stakeholders is of utmost importance. The
stakeholder analysis includes both aspects, as internal and external factors might affect
the project. Stakeholders are an indivisible part of the project and understanding their
role in it is vital for successful execution, as they are often integral in the decision-
making process. Consequently, stakeholders have to be identified and thoroughly an-
alyzed so that their importance and interests in the project are clearly defined. It is
the company’s responsibility to create an effective strategy for stakeholder engagement
and management as this will allow for good communication between involved parties
and will set a good precedent for future collaboration.
A typical stakeholder analysis would require stakeholder identification, data collection,
identification of priorities, determination of strengths and weaknesses, identification of
stakeholder support, prediction of behavior, and preparation of management strategy
(Cleland, 2007).
Stakeholder identification and characterization
The stakeholder analysis commences with the identification of the relevant parties
involved in a project, which can either be achieved through brainstorming and creat-
ing a list of potential stakeholders (Jepsen and Eskerod, 2009). The most common
stakeholders are directors, managers, employees, shareholders, competitors, customers,
suppliers, the government & policymakers, the media, the community, sponsors, and
consumers.
Stakeholder classification can be achieved with various methods that have been devel-
oped for this purpose, here three classification methods will be presented. Classification
can be done according to:
• Direction
• Power, legitimacy, and urgency
• Types of stakeholders
Categorizing stakeholders according to direction is the most straightforward among
the aforementioned methods. The stakeholders can have a downward, upward, or
outward direction (Bourne and Walker, 2008). Downward stakeholders represent in-
ternal entities such as the project management group. Upward stakeholders include
the project owners, clients, and anyone who has sponsored the project and has a
vested interest in its success. Whereas, outward stakeholders can either be internal or
external consisting of other employees of the company, suppliers, authorities, and com-
petitors. This model can be extended if categorization must be more diligent (Bourne
and Walker, 2008).
Stakeholders classification based on power, legitimacy or urgency is another use-
ful method for stakeholder classification. In this method, stakeholders can have one
or more attributes (Alves et al., 2014). To gain a better understanding, the three
attributes will be briefly explained.
Page 27 of 67
==PAGE:39==
Power refers to the stakeholders’ influence and power of negotiation in the con-
text of the organization.
Legitimacy refers to the relevance of the stakeholders on the project and their
legitimacy in that regard.
Urgency refers to the level the stakeholders’ needs must be met. Essentially, it
is an indicator of priority within the parties involved.
This model can be considered dynamic and multidimensional as it considers the change
of interests over time depending on social factors, the politics, and the intricate re-
lationships between stakeholders, e.g., conflict of interest.
Power, legitimacy, and
urgency are therefore attributed variables that might not be known to the stakehold-
ers themselves (Jepsen and Eskerod, 2009).
Classifying according to these three attributes can be further complimented by assign-
ing types of stakeholders according to their power, legitimacy and urgency. The third
methodology takes into account the variability of stakeholders and considers stake-
holders that have several attributes. For instance, a group or individual can have
legitimacy and urgency but no power over the execution and outcome of the project.
In contrast, another group might possess power but not have any interest in the project
and therefore exercise no influence on it, even if they would have the means to do so.
It is, therefore, important to monitor each stakeholder that might have a stake de-
pending on their relationship with the organization (Mainardes et al., 2012). In their
article ’A model for stakeholder classification and stakeholder analysis, the types are
defined as:
Dormant stakeholder: This type refers to groups or individuals that do not
have legitimacy or urgency (as defined above), but have the power to impose their
preferences and needs on the organization. As they do not have any stake in the
project, their power might be irrelevant and not affect the outcome. However,
the company should remain vigilant, keep track, and manage these stakeholders
continuously for efficient management.
Discretionary stakeholder: This type refers to stakeholders that have legiti-
macy on a project but do not possess any power or urgency. Oftentimes, these
stakeholders fall within corporate social responsibility and should be monitored.
Demanding stakeholder: This type has urgency but does not possess any
power or legitimacy.
Their demands are therefore low, but the organization
should be monitoring them in case they gain legitimacy or power.
Dominant stakeholder: This type possesses power and legitimacy and there-
fore has a great influence on the outcome. The organization should handle these
stakeholders very carefully.
Dangerous stakeholder: This type refers to stakeholders that have power and
urgency but do not have legitimacy. This group might exhibit coercive behavior
and become a threat.
Page 28 of 67
==PAGE:40==
Dependent stakeholder: This type refers to stakeholders that have legitimacy
and urgency, but do not have any power. They usually rely on other stakeholders
to have their needs met.

When the stakeholders have been identified and classified, tools such as a SWOT
analysis can be used to explore strengths and weaknesses to aid in the formation of
a specific engagement strategy appropriate for each stakeholder. This strategy should
contain various activities linked to the stakeholders’ interests and encompass their
expectations (Kujala et al., 2022).

Goals & Milestones
For the successful execution of the project, careful resource planning and the identifi-
cation of significant milestones early in the process is crucial. In Managing successful
projects with PRINCE2, a milestone is defined as an event on a schedule which marks
the completion of key activities (AXELOS, 2017). Milestones define the completion
of goals related to the project, therefore it is key to keep track of the progress on
each of these goals and ensure that the deadline set is met. Milestones are also an
important aspect of implementing an agile approach, as they allow for the breakdown
of the project into smaller more attainable goals.
Creating a project schedule is integral in defining milestones. The schedule requires
the definition, order, and duration of the project’s activities (Institute, 2021). As a
Page 29 of 67
==PAGE:41==
rule of thumb, the project’s milestones should be less than the deliverables but detailed
enough to ensure potential problems are solved promptly (AXELOS, 2017).
When defining project milestones or smaller goals, the SMART approach to setting
goals can be a useful tool. The acronym stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable,
Relevant and Time-bound (Doran, 1981).
Each milestone/goal should have these
attributes to ensure its completion. To evaluate whether the set milestones fit the
criteria of SMART, the following questions can be used:
Table 2: SMART Goals
Criteria
Questions
Specific
What will be achieved by this goal and how will it be done?
Measurable
How will you measure success? What data will be used?
Achievable
Does the company have the right employees with the appro-
priate skill set?
Relevant
Is the milestone in alignment with the company? What will
be the impact of the result?
Time-bound
What is the deadline for completion?
This model exhibits some limitations as the creation of the goals is dependent in the in-
herently flawed human nature and can lead to unfavorable outcomes. The SMARTER
model was developed to bypass the limitations of the SMART model. The additional
elements are Evaluated and Reviewed. The evaluation and review of the goal progress
can help in the creation of better, more attainable goals.

5.3.1
Agile & Lean in the company
So far, the company has participated in small projects as a stakeholder but has also
assumed the role of a project proposer. For the company, even though the project com-
mences with the inception of the project idea and is followed by a funding application,
not much is done regarding planning in case the application is successful. Usually, a
brainstorming session for potential projects leads to a funding application, and the
process is put on hold until the decision on funding becomes known due to constraints
on time and resources, as the company prefers to focus on product development. If
the application is successful, the company will resume planning and if it is not, the
idea might be revisited in the future but often is forgotten.
The process preceding a funding application can be considered chaotic and a well-
defined process could be beneficial for the company. However, as aforementioned a
small team, other priorities and limited time do not allow for this. As seen in sec-
tion 3.4.2, an agile approach to managing projects could be of value to the company.
Roll wave planning, advocating for limited planning in the beginning, and a dedicated
team to review the project plan and progress can help mitigate their current chaotic
approach without dedicating many resources.
Essentially, the brief brainstorming session which is the modus operandi now, can be
substituted by a working day where the team comes together to scope out a poten-
tial project. The team can assign some responsibility to each member of the team to
ensure collective ownership and document the basic activities and resources required
to complete the project. Aspects, such as the timeline and partners should also be
discussed, but careful and excessive planning should not take place as the funding
application might not be successful. In this manner, the company can mitigate the
lack of resources and can pick up the project planning if the application is successful.
When the funding is secured, the company can resume the planning. Role wave plan-
ning should still be part of the process to mitigate change and tasks should be reviewed
continuously. As indicated, the company prefers weekly tasks and some tools that can
aid in this process are time-boxing and completing tasks in increments. Weekly stand-
up meetings regarding the project can be included in their Monday meeting to engage
the team and assign tasks and decisions should be made collectively. When the team
commits full-time to the start-up, the weekly stand-up can be transformed into a daily
stand-up.
In the case of projects stemming from customers, all of the above apply. Furthermore,
the company should also involve clients in the process and ensure that the activities
or product features are value-adding.
Additionally, the company can implement some lean project management practices as
seen in section 3.4.3. These practices can help in eliminating wasteful practices and
save precious resources for the company, namely time but also funds as the company
is still in the startup phase and it is a crucial aspect. Some of these practices go hand-

in-hand with the agile approach discussed above. For instance, lean suggests assigning
roles and tasks to the team to ensure completion but agile suggests engaging in collec-
tive decision-making. These practices are not mutually exclusive as each team member
will be responsible for tasks, that would require agreement from the team, creating
personal and team accountability. Furthermore, both methods indicate daily stand-up
meetings as beneficial and focus on customer requirements and value-adding activities.
On a side note, the company can apply lean and agile to their overall processes. This
can be achieved by identifying the non-value-adding activities and simplifying them
as much as possible. Anemo Robotics can consider its product development process
as a project where sprints to achieve tasks can be used. For example, they can aim at
developing their product quickly to enter various markets based on potential customer
feedback. They can reach out to already established partners to gather feedback and
ensure that the product meets market requirements. Furthermore, the company can
assess its internal needs in terms of skills and when capital allows to hire employees
that can deliver the desired results.
As a newly founded company that is not subject to old processes with accumulated
complexity, implementing an agile and lean approach in this early stage allows for
future-proofing project management techniques and other processes, while also serving
as a mitigation tool to limited funds, resources, and time constraints. These practices
can be viewed as suggestions and can be subjected to a trial and error method where the
company explores and discovers what fills best their needs. The intent is to provide
some structure to be used in their upcoming project. It is also suggested that the
company evaluate this framework upon the project’s completion and make changes
were needed. The framework itself should be subject to an agile and lean approach,
where non-value-adding processes are eliminated, constant perfection is sought and
constant change and unforeseen events are a given.
5.3.2
Stakeholder analysis
The company has indicated that assistance when it comes to stakeholder analysis and
goal setting is required. They have stated that they"have never used any stakeholder
mapping tools or worked with these methods before". This can be attributed to the fact
that they have only executed small-scale projects, such as pilot tests where monetary
gain was not the objective, and therefore strict guidelines and client deliverables were
not important so systematic stakeholder mapping and engagement strategies were un-
necessary.
The first step in the stakeholder analysis is brainstorming about the potential project
stakeholders. This process will allow the company to reflect on the involved parties
and assess their knowledge of each of them.
Section 3.4.4 offers three stakeholder classification methods that the company can use.
The company should decide the level of detail in the stakeholder analysis. For exam-
ple, classification based on the direction can be used in smaller-scale projects, where
the timeline is short, only a limited number of stakeholders are involved, the company
Page 39 of 67
==PAGE:51==
mainly handles the project, and the relation of the project to the stakeholders is the
only important aspect that might pose risks. The template for this method can be
found in section C.1.
If the company wishes to dive deeper into the intricate relationships between stake-
holders to ensure engagement and good risk mitigation for large-scale projects, it can
use the classification based on power, legitimacy, and urgency and complete it with
assigning types. This approach constitutes a highly detailed analysis that breaks down
stakeholders into multiple categories when careful stakeholder monitoring for efficient
handling is necessary as each stakeholder, even when only possessing legitimacy could
potentially affect the outcome. A template can be found in section C.1.
The correct tool selection and proper classification of stakeholders can be challenging,
as there are many uncertainties in the initial planning phase, and not all stakehold-
ers might be known at this point. Therefore, these three methods can be tested in
the company’s upcoming project to evaluate the level of detail required for large-scale
projects to see which provides more value. For future projects, when a stakeholder
analysis is necessary the company can choose based on previous experience and re-
assess and redo the analysis if deemed necessary. Factors such as the project size to
pick the most relevant method should always be considered to avoid a disproportionate
amount of resources or time on a small-scale project.
Stakeholder identification and characterization must be followed by data collection,
identification of strengths and weaknesses, and an engagement plan. The company
should map out the stakeholders and compile any information available to be able to
create an engagement plan. The plan is dependent on the project and a specific guide
cannot be formulated without prior knowledge of the project specifics.

5.7
Case study: the MOSAR project
The framework that was developed in the previous section will be a useful tool for
the MOSAR project. The use of a case study is highly important for the company
and as was discussed in section 4, the implementation of the framework will serve as
a validation tool.
The MOSAR project was conceived in October 2023 when the company was exploring
the possibility of working on a large-scale project that would have a longer duration.
At the time, aside from Anemo Robotics’ wish to scale up on project size, they had
not yet defined a clear scope for a large-scale project and the outcomes were uncertain.
According to the CEO, earlier that month the company had established contact with
ReefCircular, a Danish company specializing in turning shell waste into sea life to
restore degraded marine ecosystems.
The two companies share a common mission and therefore decided that a joint project
and an ongoing collaboration would be beneficial for both parties. The initial scop-
ing and idea generation occurred in an unstructured manner via Teams and Anemo
Robotics took the lead on writing a proposal and applying for funding. DTU Aqua, a
department specializing in research on aquatic life became involved further down the
process.
5.7.1
Project description
The scope of the project was defined relatively easily as both companies had certain
goals they wanted to achieve and a mission to complete. After some brainstorming
between the two companies, they decided that:
The scope of the project is to build artificial reefs, deploy them in an
area with a degraded ecosystem, and monitor marine biodiversity for
Page 42 of 67
==PAGE:54==
one year.
The project summary was provided by the company and can be seen below:
The MOSAR project is a collaboration between the three partners. Each partner has
a distinct role in the project and based on the scope, the role of each partner can be
defined. ReefCircular will provide the infrastructure of artificial reefs, whereas Anemo
Robotics will provide long-term marine monitoring to test the efficiency of the reefs
and the prosperity of life. DTU Aqua will assist in the design of reefs, and footage
analysis and will advise on effective measures to create a thriving mini ecosystem
around the structure.
The target group of the project is degraded areas in Denmark that have suffered im-
mense marine ecosystem destruction. This project will serve as a pilot project to test
the efficiency of such structures and upon successful completion become the proof of
concept of this technology. The project would serve as the hallmark for these types of
projects and in the future, the stakeholders hope it would pave the way for increas-
ing Denmark’s export capacity in this sector, as well as strengthening national trade
within sustainability and the blue economy. Essentially, it will create a solution for
ecosystem degradation with an effective monitoring tool to assess the results.
The project provides innovative solutions to ecological restoration, biodiversity en-
hancement, and sustainable monitoring practices. It addresses biodiversity loss by
creating sustainable artificial reefs, contributing to the long-term restoration of ma-
rine ecosystems. Simultaneously, it promotes sustainable monitoring practices, miti-
gating the environmental impact of data collection. The result is a positive and lasting
change that enhances marine biodiversity while reducing carbon emissions in the ma-
rine industry. Lastly, it promotes export capacity by creating solutions that can be
used worldwide and fosters collaboration between companies and universities to create
more sustainable practices.

Discussion & Conclusions
6.1
Insights on literature review
Effective project management in startups can be challenging, as traditional methods
might be unsuitable for a high-risk setting subject to imminent changes. Agile or lean
approaches have found ground in these companies but are usually implemented in the
product development process, as this is the focal point for many of them. Research
on assessing the efficacy of agile or lean project management methods in startups was
found to be limited. As such, encountering success stories to draw inspiration was a
challenge. Specific PM methods are usually attractive to larger companies, that have
an established product, experienced personnel, and the resources to focus on effective
management to deliver their services to their clients.
Examining the case of Anemo Robotics, a company that operates in a highly niche
and traditional sector, examples of effective and innovative project management tech-
niques were non-existent. The literature review aided in understanding the sector and
its particularities but also highlighted the fact that biodiversity is a highly complex
subject that requires careful handling. Ecosystems are interconnected and even regu-
lators and policymakers struggle to capture their true essence and develop meaningful
goals that can mitigate degradation and biodiversity loss.
It is truly fascinating that frameworks and initiatives have been formed over the years
to alleviate the immense pressure on biodiversity and ocean resources, but one by
one fail to achieve the set goals and have a significant impact. For many, nature’s
commodities are considered inexhaustible goods. The situation is even worse for ma-
rine ecosystems, as the ramifications of human activities are not easily seen. Given
this fact, the repercussions of unsustainable use of oceanic resources might even pose a
bigger threat to the prosperity of humanity, due to the lack of attention on the matter.
Collecting data to bridge the knowledge gap on marine environments is crucial, as it
will lead to better-formulated policies, regulations, and strategies. Completing this
puzzle and shedding light on the current situation is key to forming action plans to
combat loss and restore ecosystems, leading to the completion of Vision 2050, where
humans live in harmony with nature.
6.2
Insights on the framework development
Developing a project management framework to accommodate the needs of the com-
pany and add value to its processes constituted a highly hands-on approach. The
interviews with the company offered many insights into their operations and were cru-
cial for the development of an effective project management guide. Furthermore, they
clarified the specific needs of the company. For instance, stakeholder management and
engagement were not done consistently if at all. Communication between the com-
pany and stakeholders was also inconsistent as not all members would participate in
conversations and updates, leading to miscommunication.
From the outset, the approach for the development included feedback loops with the
company to evaluate which approach was necessary. More traditional methods such as
Page 58 of 67
==PAGE:70==
rigid planning were explored as well. The intent was to evaluate whether the company
needed a highly organized working method to mitigate resource and time constraints,
where tasks would be known weeks in advance, or whether a more flexible approach
was best. It was observed that the company did not fare well with excessive planning
as tasks were overlooked until the deadline was approaching. Furthermore, it was real-
ized that as tasks would pile up, many of them became irrelevant due to changes and
rapid development. Hence, resources were spent to plan for tasks that were rendered
unnecessary, showcasing that traditional methods were inappropriate for this particu-
lar startup.
This led to the selection of a more innovative approach to engage the team and help
in efficient project management. For this purpose, agile and lean PM methods were
chosen and thoroughly examined. The findings were applied to the startup setting and
biodiversity-related elements were added to make the framework company specific.
The final result was well-received and therefore, partially implemented in the com-
pany’s current project. The project activities were set to commence in February of
2024, so it was only possible to test the framework in the project planning phase.
For this purpose, working days were organized which proved to be highly useful as
they provided some structure to the working method. Furthermore, stakeholders were
identified and goals were set. The initial planning was successful as the company com-
pleted the first milestone.
The insights from the MOSAR project case study were significant, as the theoreti-
cal concept was transformed into tangible actions. The implementation consolidated
that flexible approaches were best for the company, as the execution progressed fa-
vorably. However, as the strengths and weaknesses of the framework were explored,
some limitations became apparent. Risk mitigation techniques were not explicitly ex-
plored; solely through the prism of agile and lean where part of the methodology has
incorporated elements for risk management that might emerge due to uncertainty and
radical changes. Lastly, a concrete stakeholder engagement strategy was not developed
for MOSAR, as it was deemed out-of-scope. Formulating a stakeholder engagement
strategy is tied to the specifics of each project and since the purpose of the framework
is to provide the company with a generic guide for project management, a generic
strategy could not have been included. However, it would be highly important for the
company to create a stakeholder engagement strategy based on the insights from the
implementation and the identification.
The next step for the company is to implement the framework throughout the ex-
ecution phase, as well as at the end of the project.
Upon completion, the overall
performance and the results yielded by this approach should be reviewed and poten-
tial improvements must be noted. If the overall result is favorable, the improvements
can be integrated into the framework and the new version can be tested in the next
project. In case the framework brings mixed results, the company can explore other
project management methods and then perform a comparative analysis to evaluate if
the alternative is better suited to their needs. It is recommended that even if the com-
pany is satisfied, the team should always strive for perfection and therefore enhance
Page 59 of 67
==PAGE:71==
their processes after every completed project.
Additionally, it is worth mentioning that the framework was presented to a project
manager expert who evaluated it and provided validation. He recommended that the
company explore risk management methods and formulate smart goals and flexible
communication plans to accommodate their needs.
Lastly, a refinement of the framework and biodiversity-related KPIs by consulting
with marine scientists is suggested. The company can also consider the exploration
of effective communication methods and the implementation of return on investment
metrics in their projects.
6.3
Future research potential
The main limitation of the development of this framework was that is based on one
company since research was found limited regarding innovative project management
techniques in startups. Future research efforts can delve deeper into this topic. Young
entrepreneurs with no experience can benefit greatly if concrete methods are developed
to assist them in their endeavors.
To achieve this, a comparative analysis can be performed between startups that use
traditional project management methods, startups that prefer more innovative ap-
proaches, and startups that do not implement any concrete method.
The results
might serve as a guide to the implementation of the most successful approaches. The
study can also contribute to the research on the reasons why startups fail.
6.4
Outcome of learning objectives
The set learning objectives of this work were achieved through an extensive literature
review. Knowledge of the significance of biodiversity preservation and its complexity
was acquired and the startup venture setting was understood. Furthermore, innovative
project management approaches enhanced the author’s understanding of their nature
and implementation.
The data collection and development of the framework enhanced the author’s analyt-
ical reasoning skills, as she had to combine approaches and adjust them to specific
needs. The feedback loops and validation process offered insights into improvements
that prompted more learning and as the methodology itself can be considered agile
due to this process, a deeper understanding of this method was acquired. Lastly, the
reflection and discussion enhanced the author’s critical thinking skills by addressing
the problem statement and the framework’s limitations.

Conclusions
In summary, the overall impact of the project management framework on Anemo
Robotics’ processes was found to be favorable. The company was continuously in-
volved in the development of the framework to ensure compliance with their needs
and wishes.
The MOSAR case study showcased the potential and effectiveness of
an agile, flexible approach to project management for the company and set a prece-
dent for future research into agile and lean project management methods in startups
which could potentially lead to the creation of a solid methodology to enable start-ups
to overcome challenges and do efficient project management with limited resources.
Lastly, the framework received external validation, and recommendations were pro-
vided for future adaptations.
The problem statement was addressed by exploring the research questions, which were
answered throughout this work.
Research question 1 was answered by conducting interviews with the team and per-
forming a literature review on the complexity of marine biodiversity and the startup
venture setting. The main identified challenges are the complex nature of biodiversity
which hinders the company in formulating their deliverables after having processed
the data, the constant changes and risks associated with being a startup, and their
lack of experience in handling large-scale projects.
Research question 2 was answered by developing a framework adapted to their needs.
The company can enhance resource allocation and optimize its strategic planning
for projects by implementing a mixed agile and lean approach complemented with
biodiversity-related deliverables and traditional tools such as stakeholder analysis and
SMART goals formulation. This methodology will help the company mitigate risks
that can be attributed to the inherent nature of being a startup and operating in this
sector.
Research question 3 was answered by testing the framework in the MOSAR project
and providing recommendations on the future improvement process. The implemen-
tation of the framework in the project planning phase and their involvement in the
development and testing offered the team the opportunity to work with the adop-
tion of this method and alter their modus operandi, fostering a company culture that
involves more agile approaches. Furthermore, improvement techniques and methods
were offered in the project end section and the discussion. The company can consider
these recommendations and use them as a tool to strive for perfection and constant
improvements in their operations.
