ABSTRACT 
Over the centuries, there has been an increase of railway freight. To satisfy the demand, the 
capacity of railway network has to be increased. Increased traffic reduces the lifetime of the 
railway structure and therefore it has to be maintained.  Within the time, methods of 
maintenance evolved from maintenance performed manually by hands to fully automated ones 
with usage of huge machineries. For different projects, one methods can become more efficient 
than others, due to limitation of space, feasibility and cost. To investigate which methods are 
suitable for particular types of projects, this master´s thesis project was initiated, with the intent 
to estimate, analyse and compare different maintenance methods using various data sources 
and personal experience acquired from railway maintenance projects. 
The literature study focuses firstly on the different failures in a railway system. The second 
section of the literature study is related to different manual methods of maintenance. The last 
part of literature study is describing the common automated maintenance methods of railway. 
The analysis part of this thesis is based on a gathering and analysing the data from various 
railway and machinery owner companies, to be able to estimate the cost and performance for 
different machineries in different type of works. The cost estimation of different maintenance 
methods was performed for real life project Øst-Fyn, conducted in east part of Denmark in 
2016.  Using the data provided by railway - and machine owner companies, combined with the 
personal experience of the author and colleagues from the railway company Strukton Rail A/S, 
the model for cost estimation of maintenance projects, has been developed.  
The model display that there are many different variables, such as the extend of the project, 
the time of construction period, working conditions and others, need to be taken into 
consideration, while estimating the cost. The analysis shows that it is necessary to setup the 
priorities for different variables, as the change of variables may change the entire decision.

INTRODUCTION 
According to geological research, the first so-called ‘tracked’ roads appeared at least 2000 
years ago. At that time, many important trade routes were established by water.  For that 
reason, many goods, together with newly built ships, needed to be delivered to ports and 
harbours. People soon discovered that wheeled vehicles run more efficiently and require less 
maintenance, if they are driving in aligned grooves cut into the road surface. The wheeled 
vehicles were pulled by men and the aligned grooves, or ‘tracks’, prevented wagons from 
leaving the path. The demand of goods was growing from century to century, and tracked 
roads progressed from stone to timber, later to iron and nowadays to steel. Proper foundations 
were developed to increase the capacity and speed of railway transport, however, the larger 
the capacity, the shorter the lifetime, so smaller capacities are often adopted and maintained 
instead.  
Over the years, methods of maintenance also evolved. When demand increases, track traffic 
also increases, which mean that less time is available for maintenance. The reduction of 
available maintenance time means that the work needs to be carried out faster. For this reason, 
machinery started to be adopted for tasks, which were previously carried out using 
horsepower. When demand further increased and tracks went from one lane to two lanes, 
additional elements such as track junctions were introduced, and these could reduce the 
performance of machines, and increase the cost of maintenance. However, if the same track 
with track junctions could be maintained manually, performance could be higher and the 
maintenance cheaper than when using machinery. Maintenance methods started to change 
and nowadays some methods are more suitable for different type of projects, than others are. 
It is important to choose the proper method of maintenance, to avoid unnecessary delays and 
feasibility problems and minimize the cost. 
To investigate which methods are suitable for particular types of projects, this master´s thesis 
project was initiated, with the intent to estimate, analyse and compare different maintenance 
methods using various data sources and personal experience acquired from railway 
maintenance projects. The ambition of this thesis is to create a simplified guideline, which can 
be used to determine the most suitable railway maintenance method for a project. The 
application of the proposed analysis should be done with due consideration of the performance 
assumptions and simplifications described in this report. 
Objectives 
The primary aims of this thesis are to investigate different methods of railway maintenance, 
estimate, analyse and compare the cost of the different maintenance projects and provide a 
simplified guideline for various maintenance methods. This report covers a literature study, 
analysis of data provided by railway construction and machine owner companies, and 
assumptions based on personal experience of the author and colleagues from the railway 
company Strukton Rail A/S, acquired from various railway maintenance projects. 
The specific objectives of this thesis are to: 
• 
collect and analyse multiple sources of data relevant for the research 
• 
identify, describe and explain different track maintenance methods and discuss the 
benefits and drawbacks of these 
• 
estimate the cost and the time of the sample projects using different methods together 
with the acquired knowledge 
• 
investigate and identify the primary cost factors for maintenance projects

FAILURES IN RAILWAY TRACK SYSTEM 
Railway track can be defined as an infrastructure system, which is composed from several 
sub-components, where each sub-component fulfil its specific function. The essential functions 
of the railway track system is to support and guide trains that run on it. Normally horizontal, 
vertical and longitudinal forces, which may influence the functions of the basic components, 
affect track and it may turn into degradation and failure processes. The failures may not be 
very significant, like a micro crack, which may cause an unpleasant sound when driving 
compared to a derailment of a train, which may cause fatalities and spent material damage. 
Problems may appear in different elements of the railway track system and can be affected by 
different factors. This chapter describes separate failures in different track components and 
the reasons why those problems occur.  
1.1 Failures in Rails 
The failures in rails are normally divided into two general categories: critical defects and non-
critical ones. The first ones are those, which affect the safety of train operations. The non-
critical defects are those, which do not affect the structural integrity of the rail or the safety of 
the train operations over the defect (Tzanakakis, 2013). In this sub-chapter, these failures will 
be divided into different types of defects, to provide the better overview of each problem 
separately. 
Defective Rail Steel 
These defects usually appears due to bad quality of steel. If the quality of steel does not 
correspond to the standards, defects caused by unsatisfactory chemical composition, heavy 
segregation, inclusion, piping or flakes may appear.  
Transverse Defects 
This type of defects is very hard to identify in its initial stage, due to micro cracks inside the 
steel, which may not be identified by a human eye. Transverse defects is a progressive fracture 
that develops transverse to the cross-sectional area of the head of rail. When the defect 
reaches the rail surface (cracks out) it can be identified by the following characteristics: 
• 
A hairline crack, appears usually on the side of the head of the rail or at the fillet under 
the head. Sometimes it may also appear on the running surface. 
• 
Discoloration around the crack. Discoloration appears due to internal rusting and is in 
the railway sector usually called “bleeding” 
• 
A horizontal hairline crack on the side of the head which turns upward/downward at 
one or both ends. In addition a flat spot may appear on the running surface 
• 
A hairline crack extending downwards at a right angle to a horizontal crack is created 
by shelling of the upper gage corner of the head. Shelling can be identified through the 
presence of slight bleeding on the gage side of the running surface. 
Most common types of transverse defects are: transverse fissure (see Figure 1.1), compound 
fissure (see Figure 1.2) and engine burn fracture (see Figure 1.3). 
More information about the transverse defects can be found in (Nordco Rail, 2014)

Longitudinal Defects 
The longitudinal defect can be characterized as any fracture which has a longitudinal 
separation only. There are two most common longitudinal defects: 
• 
Horizontal head split 
• 
Vertical head split 
Horizontal head split (see Figure 1.4) is a longitudinal progressive crack, which is originated at 
the head of the rail. Normally it is accompanied with a flat spot on a running surface. It is 
impossible to detect the defect without special machinery, until it reaches the side of the rail 
head. The origin of the horizontal head split may be an internal longitudinal seam, segregation, 
or inclusion from the manufacturing process. The separation process is happening 
longitudinally and in horizontal direction, parallel to the top/bottom of the rail. There is also a 
chance that transverse separation may be initiated by the wheel impact, however in this case, 
the defect is usually classified as a compound fissures. Then, it may develop the service failure, 
which will be a complete transverse break of the rail. Once the horizontal separation defect is 
found, there is a chance that there may occur more of those defects within the same rail 
section.  
 
Figure 1.4. Horizontal head split (Source: Nordco Rail,2014 ) 
Vertical head split (see Figure 1.5) is a longitudinal fracture, which usually goes through or 
near the middle of the head of the rail. The origin of the defect is similar to the horizontal head 
split. It may be an internal longitudinal seam, segregation, or inclusion from the manufactory. 
In case of vertical head split, the separation is happening longitudinally and horizontally, 
parallel to the side of the head.  The growth of the defect is often rapid after separation opens 
up and it continues to grow rapidly until the split turns outwards.  Without special equipment, it 
may be impossible to detect the failure, until it will reach the surface. Once it reaches the 
outside of the rail, some additional visible effects may be visible, which may help to identify the 
defect. The defect may be provided with dark streak on the running surface and in some 
advanced stages, a bleeding crack is apparent at the fillet. This type of defect may be 
extremely dangerous, as it is not usually visible, until it has grown into a serious length. If the 
split happens on the gage side of the rail and breaks off in service, car wheels may climb to 
the top of the rail and cause a derailment. Upon service failure, the rail by itself may break into 
several pieces.

Web Defects 
A web defect is any progressive crack occurring in the web of the rail, which is primarily having 
a longitudinal separation. The most common web defects are: 
• 
Head and web separation 
• 
Split web 
• 
Piped rail 
If the fractures appears at or near the end of the rail, then those defects may be associated as 
joint area web defects. These types of defects usually appear in conditions resulting from 
bolted joints. The most common joints web defects are: Bolted hole crack and separation of 
the head and web at the end of the rail. 
The head and web separation (see Figure 1.6) is a progressive crack, which separates the 
head and web of the rail at the fillet under the head. 
 
Figure 1.6. Head and web separation. (Source: Nordco Rail, 2014) 
The origin of this failure may start due to acid used in some asphalt-based fillers at highway 
crossings, which may start the corrosion, where the head joints the web. Another reason may 
be gravel crossings, excessive speed on curves, or improper canting of the rail, which may 
cause an extra loading of the railhead.  Sometimes this type of defect may develop in the head

fillet area at the joint rail end, due to high stress conditions, created by jumping or swinging

early stages, wrinkled lines appear along the fillet. Later in advanced stages, it is possible to

Split web (see Figure 1.7) is a longitudinal crack, which may extend into or through the side of

defect may be dangerous, as the rail may break into several pieces under the load, and cause 
the derailment of the train. Usually this failure may be recognized by cracks or rusts streaks 
under the head of the rail, close to the web. In addition, some small pieces may split off the 
side of the railhead.

opens into a cavity in advanced stages of development. The reason of that type of defect is 
usually because of the manufacturing process (when the original bloom or ingot was cast). The 
length of this failure may be from few centimetre and up to the full length of the rail. Normally 
when rail has a seam, it does not grow either vertically or horizontally, however heavy loads 
may cause it to spread or open up in crosswise direction. The pipe rail failure is dangerous, as 
it may break the rail into several pieces, under the heavy load, causing the derailment of the 
train and fatalities. Usually this type of failure is provided with a bulging of the web on one or 
both sides.

Base Defects 
Base defects may be described as any type of break happening in the base of the rail. 
Nowadays there is no way to recognize a base defects with any kind of rail testing software. 
Most common base defects are: 
• 
Broken base 
• 
Base fractures 
Broken base is basically a crack in the base of the rail, with a vertical separation. Improper 
bearing of the ties or tie plates usually causes the origin of this failure. The growth of the current 
defect may depend from the location of the break and the loading of the rail. Usually the broken 
base begins with a half-moon break in the base of the rail, and may be easily detected. If the 
failure was not recognized in time, it may cause the weakened rail cross and the rail may break 
into several pieces.  
 
Figure 1.9. Base fracture (Source: Nordco Rail, 2014)

in a transverse plan. Usually this defect arises on the outer edge of the base. These type of

Surface Defects 
Surface defects are those, which appear on the rail surface during rolling in the steel mills. 
Some examples of surface defects can be seen in Figure 1.10. The amount of surface failures 
may be quite big. For that reason in this section, only most common failures are described, 
such as: 
• 
Seam 
• 
Lap 
• 
Mill defects 
Seam are the very fine lines on the rail surface and usually caused by blowholes near the 
surface or by faulty rolling. 
A lap is usually the cause in the rolling mill, when some distend piece of metal is rolled on the 
surface of the rail section. 
Mill defects are other failures, which may arise in the steel mills. This type of defect includes 
deformations, cavities or entrapped foreign material.

Service Defects 
This type of defects develops in rails during their service life. There are many different types 
of damage, deformations and imperfections, which occur during the lifetime of the rail leading

to their premature renewals. This section will describe only the most common failures. 
Additional information about different types of service defects may be found in book (Mundrey, 
1988) 
One of the common failures during the service life of rails are battered and crippled rail ends. 
When the train is running on a track, its wheels may cause an impact for the rail ends to be 
hammered down and flattered. This failure may occur particularly when the joint gaps are 
unnecessary big and fishplates do not match snugly and do not support the rail ends firmly.  
Another type of failure is associated with abnormal traffic effects such as heavy axle loads, 
high density of traffic, flat spot on tires and skidding of wheels due to severe braking. All those 
factors may cause such failures as wheel burns, scabbing, flaking, shelling and blacks spots. 
If the wheels of a locomotive are slipping, it may cause wheel burns, which invariably gives 
rise to hardened spots often accompanied with cracks. It may lead to rail fractures. Scabbing 
is a failure when patches of metal of different dimensions are dislodged from the surface of the 
rail table. Shelling is a crack in the form of horizontal separation of metal from the head of the 
rail. Sometimes it appears as a black spots spaced on the gauge face. 
One of unavoidable service defects is corrosion or rusting of the rails. Dampness and the acid 
gasses, which may be dissolved in the film of moisture coating the rail, cause corrosion. 
Rusting of rails is more prominent near water columns, ash cleaning pits and wet tunnels. It 
was marked that regular fast traffic rails corrode less than those on disused lines. 
Wear of Rails 
Wear is the process, which leads to loss of material from one or more solid surfaces in a sliding, 
rolling or impact motion relative to one another. In some literature, it is possible to see, that 
wear is divided into two categories, where the first one is a wear on a top of the railhead and 
the second one is the wear on a side of the railhead. 
The wear on a top of the railhead is usually happening due to abrasive actions of train wheels 
over the rails. Usually in dry areas where the climate is dry and blowing of sand and dust is 
constant, extra wear occurs due to the grinding action of the sand or dust particles between 
the wheels and rail surface. When the climate is damp, the rails are lubricated by water and 
wearing is diminished.  
In curves of the track, the wear is usually more significant due to skidding and slipping of the 
wheels rigidly connected by the axle. The centrifugal force of the wheel flanges against the 
side of the rail results in a grinding action, which makes the side of the rail wear (Lundmark, 
2007). 
1.2 Failures in Concrete Sleepers 
Railway track sleepers perform two important functions, where the first one is to hold the track 
to the gauge and the second one is to transmit and distribute the oncoming loads to the ballast 
underneath. In old times, sleepers were made from wooden, due to easy accessibility of trees. 
The wooden sleepers however had low service life and low durability. Over the past decades, 
wooden sleepers went out from use and were exchanged by pre-stressed or reinforced 
concrete sleepers. They had a higher durability and much longer service lifetime than timber 
ones.  For that reason, current sub-chapter takes into consideration only the modern pre-
stressed or reinforced sleepers. Unfortunately the concrete sleepers are not perfect and have 
their own problems, which may lead to certain failures. This sub-chapter will describe the most 
common reasons why concrete sleepers fail.

Deterioration of rail - seat 
One of the common modes of failures for concrete sleepers is the rail-seat deterioration (see

rail-seat occurs because of relative movements between the rail pad and concrete rail seat, 
which finally results in gradual wearing away of the cement paste from the concrete by frictional 
forces as the abrasive fine particles and the water penetrates the rail-seat pad interface 
creating the ideal situation for abrasion. There are several factors responsible for the rail-seat 
abrasion, such as heavy axle loads, the failure of fasteners, shoulders or sleeper pads, steep 
track gradients, and particularly track curves greater than two degrees.

Centre-bound damage 
In a heavy railway traffic, the tensile fractures may occur in prestressed concrete sleepers.

existence of fine rocks within its raw plugs can lead to increase in the transverse tensile stress 
in a sleeper and cause the appearance of the longitudinal cracks. Sometimes the maximum 
tensile stress between two raw plugs in a fastening system may be also responsible for the 
longitudinal cracking.

and vibrate. The machine is operated manually from the control centre. If the operator will 
make a mistake and place the needles on a sleeper, the machine will start to vibrate and break 
the sleepers. Some of the machines may tamp six sleepers at the time. If such a mistake will 
occur, six sleepers will be destroyed at once.

1.3 Failures in Ballast and Sub-ballast 
Many years people from the railway sector in many developed countries expend considerable 
effort and expense to maintain the geometry of the track and provide its level to a stage which 
will maintain the safe and comfortable ride. To provide a good track, it is very important to have 
a good and strong foundation, which will be able to distribute and handle the forces, coming 
from the train. It is important to keep eye on it and provide the maintenance in a good time, 
before it collapses. In case of improper or late maintenance different defects may occur, 
starting from small settlements and finishes with big failures as washout of the ballast bed, 
which may cause some fatalities. The current chapter describes different failures occurring 
within railway ballast and sub-ballast and repercussions what they may bring. 
Settlements in the ballast 
There are many reasons why settlements may appear in a ballast bed. Most common of them 
are missing material (see Figure 1.14), bad compaction of the material, too small density of 
the sub-ballast, big amount of water under the track (usually caused by bad drainage), too 
strong axle loads or wrong positioning of the sleepers. It is very important to maintain these 
settlements in time; otherwise, they will grow and may cause the destruction of the side of the 
track. In addition, water may get stuck in those settlements and in winter may create a hump 
in a ballast bed. 
 
Figure 1.14. Settlements in a railway ballast (Source: T. Telford, 1994)

Humps in a ballast 
If the track has settlements or holes in a ballast bed, water may stuck there. When the 
temperature drops down below zero, the dirt mixed with water will freeze. Freezing water 
normally expand up to 9%, which means that the surface above will rise. In some cases, those 
humps may rise the ground up to 200mm. If humps will be not maintained properly and in time, 
they may cause the deformation of the track geometry, uncomfortable driving and increased 
noise. In addition, there is a chance, that within the time, due to improper distribution of the 
force, the sleepers may be destroyed. 
Washout of the ballast 
In regions where the amount of snow or water is big, it is important to have a proper drainage 
system. During the spring when the snow starts to melt, there is a risk of increased amount of 
water in the ground. If the drainage system will not be able to handle the required amount of 
water, there may be a risk of washout of the ballast (see Figure 1.15). Small washouts may 
cause the deformation of the track geometry and reduce the capacity of the track. In some 
cases, washouts may destroy the entire track, and in case of late maintenance, cause an 
accident. 
 
Figure 1.15. Washout of the ballast after the rain (Source: Ags metalgroup, 2016) 
Vegetation in a ballast 
A modern train operation makes very high demands on railway tracks. These demands can 
only be met if the ballast bed and the verges are as much as possible free of vegetation. With 
the high speeds and loads of modern railway traffic, operating safety dictates that the ballast 
bed should be clear from plant growth.  In case of overgrowing vegetation in the track area, 
there is a danger for railway operations. A dirty or overgrown ballast bed (see Figure 1.16 ) 
loses the resiliency that is essential for high operating speeds. In addition, plant roots and dirt 
store water that can freeze and expand in winter. This leads to a change in track geometry, 
which in the worst case can lead to derailments and cause fatalities. In addition, plants that 
grow over the rails can reduce adhesion essential to power transmission and braking.

1.4 Forces on a railway system 
As it was mentioned previously, a railway track consists of elements, which together combine 
into s railway system. In order to be able to recognize all possible failures in a track; it is 
necessary to understand the damage mechanism of the railway. For that reason, it is also 
important to recognize all the forces affected by the railway track from a train running on a 
track and the response made to these forces. The forces acting on the track result from the 
trainloads. Those loads can be divided into three different groups: vertical, lateral and 
longitudinal loads. At the same time, those three groups of loads can be also divided into sub 
groups: static, quasi-static and dynamic loads, depending on their nature. This sub-chapter 
shortly describes which type of forces and how those forces affect the railway system. 
The static loads can be defined as simple loads, which are caused by the vehicle body mass. 
The quasi-static and dynamic loads are however, ones that are more complicated. Quasi-static 
loads (in some literature can be defined also as dynamic ride loads) are associated with vehicle 
movements. When a vehicle is running on a track, it applies certain forces to the track, due to 
the behaviour of the vehicle body and its components and geometrical irregularities of the 
track. These forces are low frequency forces. They are the combination of static loads and the 
effect of static loads at speed and they are classified in the frequency between 0,5 and 30 Hz. 
It is important to consider quasi-static loads, when the track alignment is designed. If the quasi-
 - 21 – 
==PAGE:24==
Cost and Benefit Analysis of Track Maintenance Automation 
Aleksi Stempen 
static loads are bigger than it was expected, it may unbalance the alignment of the track, which 
may cause problems such as uncomfortable driving or uneven wear out of the train axles. 
The dynamic forces come in two different categories. There are the so called “P1” and “P2” 
forces. P1 forces (in some literature also called impact forces) are very high frequency forces 
and are classified in a range between 100Hz and 2000Hz. Impact forces correspond to the 
surface irregularities of defects in rail and wheels and they produce a high impact to the rail 
and wheel. These forces occur at the point of the rail and sleepers resist the downward motion 
of the train wheel and the contact zone between wheel and rail is compressed. Impact forces 
are responsible for so called “rail hammering” just after the joint gap and it creates high 
stresses in the rail web. P1 forces are filtered out by the rail and sleepers, and they do not 
affect ballast or subgrade settlement of the track, however they produce a great influence on 
wheel and rail contact behaviour. Typical problems on a track due to P1 forces are hole failures 
in bolted rail joints and cracking of concrete sleepers. 
P2 forces are classified in a lower frequency range, between 30Hz and 100Hz; however, they 
have longer duration than P1 forces. These forces correspond to the movement of masses of 
the vehicle running on a track. The peak of a P2 force occurs in the area of the first running-
off sleeper, after the joint. The mass of the wheel set and rail together with sleeper move down 
together and compress the ballast beneath the sleeper. Those forces are primarily responsible 
for the degradation of the track alignment and for that reason they are of great interest to the 
track design engineers. The graph showing the impact of dynamic forces after a dipped rail 
joint can be seen in Figure 1.17.The summary of the dynamic forces on a track can be seen in

MANUAL RENEWAL AND MAINTENANCE OF THE TRACK 
Railway track rests on the floating foundation, when most other civil engineering structures, 
such as buildings, bridges and dams have solid foundation. The last type of foundation does 
not get unduly strained with oncoming loads. In the civil engineering field, the structures are 
massive and the live loads are relatively small on them, when in case of railway, the track is 
the tiny structure compared to the heavy, high-speed trains that run over it. This could explain 
the difference in maintenance needs between the structures. 
In railway, the major work in the track maintenance consists of correcting the surface geometry 
by rebuilding the deformed ballast. In addition, the heavy loads cause attrition and loss of 
ballast, wear and tear of different track components, which also require attention. Finally yet 
importantly, elements of nature such as rains, floods and winds make their own demand on 
the maintenance needs of the track structure. Even leaves dropped from the tree to the rail 
may increase the stopping distance of the trains and cause some troubles (See Figure 2.1). 
 
Figure 2.1 Train uses a high-pressure water jet to remove leaves from rails  
(Source: Geof Sheppard, 2009) 
The meaning of manual works in railway technology has been changed within the last 
centuries. In old times, the railway works were made literally manually, which means by pure 
human/horse power. Nowadays however people use machines and the contribution of the 
human force is significantly reduced. Mostly for every type of maintenance work, special 
machinery tools will do the job, and man will just check or operate the machinery. For example 
to detach a modern fastening system from the rail, a special machine is used, but man operates 
it, by using a physical power to push the machine forward and check if the machine does its 
job properly (See Figure 2.1). If the same machine would be able to perform the whole process 
by itself, then it would be already an automated maintenance. It is very important to understand 
and make the difference between methods.

Nowadays big projects cannot be performed by manual force only. According to rules in many 
countries, man can perform a tamping of the track manually, only if few sleepers were changed. 
It means that in every project, where more than few sleepers are replaced, man need to use 
an automated tamping machine. However if only few sleepers were affected, then tamping can

maintenance where the contribution of the man physical power is significantly high. All other 
methods of maintenance with special machinery is described in Chapter 3. Automated

2.1 
Manual methods of compact and exchange of ballast 
As it was mentioned previously the big role in track maintenance is playing a correction of the 
track and ballast geometry by rebuilding the deformed ballast. In common language, it is called 
“Packing of track”. In this sub-chapter most common manual methods of packing the track are 
described.  
Beater packing 
One of the first methods for packing the track was so called beater packing method. First of all 
a gangman by visual inspection needed to identify low areas on the track. This was done by 
looking along the length of the track with eye at rail level. Then the specialist should identify 
loose sleepers, by marking sleepers, which are moving under the passage of the train, or by 
just simple hitting sleepers with a hummer. After that, each side of the sleeper should be 
opened out to the bottom. The beater by itself is a pickaxe with one of its end blunted into tee 
shaped for driving the ballast under sleepers. Afterwards track needed to be lifted up to the 
required height, estimated by the gangman, using special jacks. The ballast that is located 
under the sleeper should be loosened by the sharp end of the beater and then beaten by the 
blunt end into the void under the sleepers. 
In the beginning of the 19th century, the beater packing was the only method used for the track 
maintenance. If it was carried out in a systematic manner by trained teams, it was capable to 
produce a good track top. However this method has own disadvantages. The pair of workers 
who was carrying out the beater packing needed to be about the same strength and activity to 
be able to achieve the uniformity of packing. In addition, it was impossible to avoid damage of 
the sleeper. Repeated use of beater packing damaged the undersides of the sleepers and the 
 - 25 – 
==PAGE:28==

Aleksi Stempen 
ballast making them rounded, due to the repeated blows of the beater and old ballast below 
the sleeper, which was loosened and weakened by beater packing. 
For over a century, beater packing was the only method, before the shovel packing method 
was introduced. 
Ordinary and Measured Shovel packing  
As it was described previously the beater packing method had many drawback. Because of 
that, a new packing method called "Shovel Packing" was introduced. This method consists of 
rising the track and filling small stone chips 5 to 10mm in size in the void to provide a firm 
bearing to the sleeper at the necessary level. The use of this method was limited to small lifts 
and its effectiveness depended on the skill of the gangmen, who should estimate the slack in 
the track and amount of chips required for each sleeper. In the middle of the 20th century, a 
"Measured Shovel Packing" method was introduced in France. 
In this method by simple levelling techniques, the amount of ‘slack’ in the track was significantly 
reduced. A set of three sighting boards were used to identify low spots in the track (See Figure 
2.3). Two boards were set on high spots of the track and the third board placed in between on 
a low spot. The gangman through the first board should sight the distant board on the high 
spot and signal his assistant to raise the intermediate board up until it completely covered the 
distant board. The amount by which the intermediate board should be raised was calibrated to 
give the number of chips required to raise the track to bring it in level with the high spots. Void 
meters were used to measure depression of loose sleepers into the track on passage of traffic. 
Measuring equipment was calibrated to give directly the amount of chippings required under 
each sleeper, which were then spread evenly 450 mm either side of the running edge of the 
rail to provide a firm bearing. The stone chips would consolidate by a small amount under initial 
traffic and it was general practice to pack the track slightly higher to allow for this consolidation. 
(Frazer, 1938)

Tamping using mechanized tools 
During the last century, the machinery progress made a huge step. The usage of hand power 
in ballast tamping was mostly switched by special mechanized equipment. There are big 
variety of tools, but the idea of them is mostly the same, to provide an effective compacting of 
the ballast under the sleepers. One of the common tools is a special hydro hammer, connected 
to the excavator or powered from separate engine, which can run form petrol or electricity. 
These tools are used in much the same way as the beater packing method, with a pair of 
hammers being used either side of the sleeper to be packed (See Figure 2.4). The user of the 
hammer need to have experience to be able to sense when adequate consolidation of the 
ballast has been achieved. It is necessary to take care to prevent too big crushing of the ballast 
or damage to the sleeper undersides. The ballast have to be free from fine material otherwise, 
the hammers will not be effective in packing, as the fine material would flow around the 
vibrating hammer (Cope & Ellis, 2001).This technique although useful for localized 
maintenance and around switches and crossings is unsuitable for use on wet spots as it could 
aggravate the problem of wet spots by creating holes in the ballast, allowing water to pond.

Manual exchange of the ballast 
The process of exchange the ballast manually is similar to the sleeper one (See Chapter 2.2 
Manual exchange of the sleepers). The difference in a methods starts after the sleepers are 
removed from the track. The old material has to be removed from the track. If the quality of the 
stone is still good, there are ways to sort the material through special machinery and reuse it; 
otherwise, old material is removed and disposed. If the target of the ballast maintenance is just 
renewal, then the thickness of the required materials is the same. In case of increasing the 
train speed, the ballast bed need to be upgraded to the required thickness. The material need 
to be filled in layers, where after each layer a compaction is required. Compaction have to be 
performed very carefully and it needs to reach a certain level, otherwise there is a risk of having 
settlements in the track, where afterwards the water may collect. Those problems may cause 
the failure of the track.

2.2 
 Manual methods of sleeper and rail maintenance 
Railway sleepers are a somewhat unglamorous component of the rail industry. However, these 
components, which are laid horizontally underneath tracks to hold rail lines in place at the 
correct gauge, form the backbone of rail travel. Apart from some experiments with stone block 
sleepers at the earliest stages of rail transport's development, timber has been the historically 
dominant material used for railway sleepers. During the last two centuries, the need to 
accommodate higher axle loads and faster speeds developed concrete sleepers.  The last one 
is nowadays mostly used type; however, at some special cases timbers can still be used.  In 
this chapter, main methods of manual maintenance of the sleepers will be described. 
Timber sleepers 
Wooden sleepers were used for many years, however as they were made from wood, it was 
necessary to prolong their service time. Simple timber is very sensitive to many factors, such 
as humidity, insects, vegetation and others. For that, reason people started to use creosote. It 
was used for more than 150 years. The idea of creosote was to provide the timber sleepers 
with additional functions. Timber sleepers treated with creosote were not so sensitive to the 
humidity. In addition, it helped to protect them from insects and vegetation. In 2003, it was 
investigated that creosote is also harmful for environment and can be toxic for people. 
Nowadays timber sleepers come with “green” pressure treatment, which is the latest 
environment friendly and non-creosote based preservatives and they are suitable for 
landscaping projects. Another alternative is to use timber sleepers made from some tropical 
hardwoods, which are naturally long lasting, and hence need no treatment. Example of modern 
timber sleepers can be seen in Figure 2.5. More information about timber sleepers can be 
found in [Kilgraney Railway Sleepers, 2015]

The need to find some replacement to the timber sleepers arose during the World War –II. 
Timber sleepers were not good enough to satisfy the demand and steel sleepers had many 
problems, due to high cost and fear of corrosion in the variable weather environment. Here 
came the idea to use the prestressed concrete sleepers. First mention of the prestressed 
concrete sleepers came in 1943, in UK. Along with UK, France developed own type of 
prestressed concrete sleepers, so called “twin block sleepers”.  It consisted of two trapezoidal

stability to the track. Due to the flexible tie bar in between the twin block sleeper the track 
requires extra maintenance to ensure that the blocks do not tilt differentially and the track does 
not loose gauge. 
Nowadays there are no special way to maintain concrete sleepers. If a failure appears in a 
concrete sleeper, it change to a new one. In some countries, concrete sleepers are recycled. 
The concrete is crushed into small pieces and sometimes used as a filling material in 
construction. The steel bars inside the sleepers are melted and reused for a production of new 
steel.  
Nowadays the monoblock sleepers are preferable for use. The lifetime of the twin block 
sleepers are shorter due to the open steel bar, which is affected by corrosion.

Previously it was described, that there is no special method to maintain the sleepers directly

methods of exchanging the sleepers may differ in various countries. In this section the Danish 
method of exchanging sleepers is described. 
First, it is important to make sure that there is no traffic in the track, which means that the track 
or section has to be closed for train traffic. Afterwards the process differs, depending on the

with exchange of the sleepers, the maintenance of the ballast may be performed.  
In case of changing the sleepers with maintenance of the ballast, the process will be totally 
different. First, the sleepers have to be detached from rail. In timber sleepers and old time 
concrete sleepers, the special bolts are normally used. In modern concrete sleepers, a special 
fastening system is commonly used. Before middle of 20th century, the detaching process was 
performed manually, using some simple tools as hammers and shovels, but nowadays special 
machines are used, normally powered from petrol. An example of a bolt detaching machine

to the Danish Railway standard. Using such short sections of rails, it becomes possible to 
remove the rail from the maintenance area very quickly, using a simple excavator and sheaves. 
After that sleepers can be removed from the track and ballast can be maintained. When the 
work is done, sleepers can be exchanged and track may be built together. According to Danish

AUTOMATED RENEWAL AND MAINTENANCE OF TRACK  
The idea of using automated machines for track maintenance dates back to the sixties. The 
demand of freight goods increased at that time, and the goods were transported mainly by 
road traffic. Road transportation was faster, but expensive and road depended. It was not 
always possible to increase the capacity of the roads anymore and heavy traffic was very 
harmful for the quality of the roads. In addition, in large cities it created issues related to 
environment, such as noise and air pollution. The problem of transportation increased every 
year and more countries started to think about diverting the road freight traffic to the railway 
sector. The heavy usage of railways has its own disadvantages as well. Railways, as roads, 
need to be maintained. However if in case of roads, the maintenance can be performed by 
partial closing of the lanes, in railway it is not always possible. In the past in many places there 
was only one railway line, and closing it meant stopping the entire traffic. The goods were late 
and bring dissatisfaction to the clients. Here came the idea of using special maintenance 
machines, which will be able perform maintenance fast and with minimal disturbance of the 
railway traffic. This chapter describes the automated railway maintenance machines and the 
principles of their application. 
3.1 Automated methods of compact, exchange and 
maintenance of the ballast 
Many years past since the first mechanized automated machines were introduced. Nowadays 
the number of different ballast maintenance machines is high. There are different types of 
ballast maintenance works to be done, such as compacting, cleaning, rebuilding, adding 
material. For every specific job, there is a particular machine which performs the task in the 
best way. There is a number of models of a given machine, but the principle of operation is the 
same. Difference can be in the speed, fuel consumption, size of the crew and others. For that 
purpose in this chapter there will be described different types of track ballast maintenance 
machines and how these machines work. 
Ballast tamping machine 
The objective of the tamping is to improve the vertical and the horizontal track geometry of the 
track and perform it with the minimum amount to disturbance of the traffic and minimum use 
of the ballast (Cope & Ellis, 2001) Tamping  ballast using machines is a process of lifting and 
laterally adjusting the track to its desired geometry, at the same time rearranging the upper 
portion of the ballast layer to fill resulting voids under the sleepers; it is thus a mechanization 
of the beater packing process. (Selig & Waters, 1994) When the sleepers are lifted together 
with rail, the special vibrating tamping tines are going inside the ballast on both sides of the 
sleepers. The vibrations help the tines go into the ballast. The frequency of vibration is chosen 
in such a way to be able to fluidize the ballast, and then compact it inwards and upwards, 
towards the bottom of the sleeper. Nowadays, the tamping machines are able to operate very 
efficiently and maintain more than 1200m of a track per hour (See Figure 2.8).

The best result, using the tamping tines is usually achieved on so called “good “track, which is 
not fouled. The ballast should be single graded stone and not smaller than 37mm, for the 
optimum performance of the tamping machine, otherwise to perform a proper tamping process 
can be difficult. This is happening due to the vibration frequency and the shape of the tamping 
tines (See Figure 3.2) is designed such that stones smaller than 37mm size cannot be 
compacted and will flow around the tamping tines. [Wood, 2002].  
 
Figure 3.2 Tamping tines attached to a power unit (Source: Selig and Waters, 1994) 
The tamping process using the machines is quick, but it does not produce a durable track 
geometry. The tampers of the machine disturb the compacted ballast under the sleepers and 
vertical track geometry deteriorates rapidly after tamping. After a rapid tamping, the resultant 
track profile assumes its original, pretamp condition, the phenomena called “ballast memory”.  
In addition, the tamping process damages the ballast and every tamping is crushing the 
material into dust, thus the repeated tamping of the track speed up the renewal of the track as 
the track becomes fouled. This theory was proven by Selig and Waters in 1994 (See Figure

horizontal stresses develop in ballast due to long term vertical loading and these locked in 
stresses help stabilising the ballast against movements. Tamping disturbs these residual 
stresses and results in increased settlements. (Calla, 2003)

For lifts lower than 25mm given by tamping, the track reverts to its original profile repeatedly 
after each tamping, which means that there is no change to the track inherent profile. Any 
improvement in inherent track profile is achieved only with lifts greater than 25mm. It was 
observed that for low tamping lifts, the ballast below the sleepers is squeezed upwards and it 
dilates into void between the sleeper and the ballast surface. Due to too low lifts on contacting 
the underside of the sleeper, further deformation of the ballast cannot be performed and 
therefore arrangement of ballast particles within the ballast is unchanged. The traffic of the 
train recompacts the ballast and the particles adopt their original position and the track 
assumes its original pretamp shape (Selig & Waters, 1994). The solution to it is to introduce 
high lift tamping. In lifting more than 25mm the ballast will have enough space for ballast 
dilation and rearrangement of particles will take place with new particles being absorbed into 
the ballast shape. The traffic afterwards will re-compact the ballast skeleton and introduce a 
new geometry to the track. After the high lifting tamping is performed, the ballast consolidation 
is required before the train traffic can run at its normal speed.

Ballast injection machines 
Ballast injection machines, so-called stone blowers, are used as a mechanization of the 
measured shovel packing process. These machines use compressed air to blow small, round 
20 mm stones under the sleeper creating a compact bed under the bed. This process takes 
advantage of the already compacted ballast bed instead of disturbing it as in the tamping 
method. The durability of the ballast profile, using this method, is higher, but then this method 
is not effective on track where the ballast is new or has already been disturbed by tamping 
process. The durability of the track top using the stone blowers can be up to eight times better, 
than using the tamping. (Cope & Ellis, 2001) It means that less stone blowers are required to 
maintain the length of the track than the tampers which leads to the savings in capital 
expenditure, machine maintenance, manpower and track possession. Another advantage of 
stone blowers is that it is possible to maintain the track with ash ballast. (Cope & Ellis, 2001) 
The stones used in stone blowers have a smaller size than the standard ballast, which allows 
developing more contact points under the sleeper. It allows for a better load distribution, thus 
ballast breakage is reduced. In 2001, the Coenraad noted that blowing stone helps to improve 
the track drainage, because it reduces or eliminates the vertical pumping action of the sleeper. 
Example of modern stone blower machine can be seen in Figure 3.5 .

(Source: C. Calla, 2003) 
Despites the fact that many research teams and authors have opinion that the stone blowing 
method has more advantages than tamping, the process is regarded with some scepticism by 
railway engineers. One reason is that the track after the stone blowing method looks worse 
than it was before, due to the, so called “design over lift” given to the track during the use of 
the stone blowing method. (Ball, 2003) During the implementation of the process the track 
needs to be raised up to the level required for correcting vertical geometry and plus extra 45 
mm to enable blowing of stones under the sleepers. When the track is raised, the amount of 
stones blown under the track is calculated using the height required for track geometry 
correction. The extra uplifts is to ease the stones below the sleepers. After the stone blowing 
track is lowered back on a new bed of stones which is blown under the sleepers, but the track 
does not settle through all those 45 mm uplifts. Approximately 50.000 tons of heavy traffic is 
required to settle back the track to its design level, which was designed for correcting the faults 
of the track geometry. (Cope & Ellis, 2001) This is the reason why just after the stone blowing 
process the track profile looks worse than it was before the stone blowing. However, after the

Dynamic track stabilizer 
The application of tamping method, it disturbs and loosens the ballast under the sleepers and 
the shoulder and crib ballast. Traffic on the track would compact the bottom ballast and the 
lateral stability would be also regained, but in this case, the speed restrictions needed to be 
placed on a track and in addition, there would be a loss of track geometry. Dynamic Track 
Stabilizer (DTS) machines are used to follow the tamping and lining work to consolidate ballast 
below and around the sleepers (See Figure 3.6). This process helps the align to get its lateral 
stability. (Cope & Ellis, 2001)

stones blown in by the stone blower and cause the loss in height achieved by packing. (Cope 
& Ellis, 2001)

Ballast cleaning machines 
Within the time, track ballast becomes worn, and loses its attributes. During the continuous 
vibration, the stones in ballast become rounded.  This hinders the tessellation of pieces of 
ballast with one another, and thus reduces its effectiveness. Attrition creates fine pieces of 
granite, like sand, dust and others. Combined with water in the ballast, these fine pieces stick 
together, making the ballast act like concrete. This hinders both track drainage and the 
flexibility of the ballast to constrain the track as it moves under traffic. In addition, track tamping 
machines cannot work effectively in dirty ballast. To improve track drainage, retain elasticity of 
track and to prolong the life of the materials, it is important to perform a ballast cleaning.  
Ballast cleaning removes worn ballast, screens it and replaces the "dirty" worn ballast with 
clean ballast. A ballast cleaner (See Figure 3.9) (also known as an under cutter) is a machine 
that specializes in cleaning the railway track ballast (gravel, blue stone or other aggregate) of 
impurities.  
 
Figure 3.9 Ballast cleaner PM 1000 URM (Source: EurailPool GmbH, 2016) 
There are many different types of ballast cleaners; however, all of them perform the same task, 
which is cleaning the ballast. The main advantages of using ballast cleaners are the following: 
• 
The quality of ballast cleaning is better than it can be acquired from manual work 
• 
The required slopes can be given to the formation 
• 
The loss of ballast is neglible. There is a saving of 10 to 15% of the ballast, which 
normally goes to waste with manual work. 
• 
Ballast cleaning can be done by an on-track machine without removing the rail and 
sleepers. 
A cutter bar runs beneath sleeper level excavating all of the ballast under the sleepers to a 
specified, variable depth. A conveyor then moves the ballast into the cleaner, where it gets 
forced through a mesh by a shaking chamber. Pieces of ballast, which are smaller than the 
mesh size, fall through and are rejected; those that are larger than the mesh are returned to 
the track along with fresh ballast. Some ballast cleaners have both ballast and spoil wagons 
attached to it, to which the materials are fed by a series of conveyor belts. Others simply 
undercut the ballast, and allow a work train to come through to dump fresh ballast. This process 
can be done in short possessions, meaning that track life can be considerably extended with 
the minimum of disruption.(Mundrey, 1988)

3.2 Automated methods of sleeper and rail maintenance 
The functionality of the railway track depends upon the condition of the individual components 
such as rails and sleepers. Within the time the elements become defective or the wear reach 
the condition, when they have to be changed. These parts should be replaced in appropriate 
time in order to avoid high subsequent costs and big interruptions to a traffic. In the past, these 
material-intensive and tiring tasks were carried out by manual force, but today there is a 
possibility to perform it using powerful automated methods. (Plasser & Theurer, 2016) Track 
renewal and track laying operations require high amount of personnel and machines. With the 
help of automated work methods, these values can be reduced. In addition higher quality can 
be achieved. 
A track renewal train is a work on-track machine (See Figure 3.10) that consists of many units 
combined together into one train. Materials required for track renewal (rail and sleeper 
replacement) are also carried by the track renewal train. As the track renewal train moves 
forward, the track renewal process will be done stage by stage to the completion of track 
renewal as it leaves the area. 
 
Figure 3.10 A modern track renewal train (Source: Strukton Rail, 2016) 
The supply of new sleepers and rails within the track is performed by automated operation. 
Accuracy and high working speed are the outstanding features of this automated method. The 
same applies for track renewal where firstly, the old track is removed and then the new track 
is laid directly behind. Other advantages are the careful handling of the material, particularly 
the rails, and better preparation of the subsoil. (Plasser & Theurer, 2016).  
To minimize the down time of track during a track renewal project, a track renewal train is used 
to automate most of the required process. Prior to the arrival of the track renewal train, 
preparation tasks should be done. The new rails usually need to be delivered to the centre of 
the track. Rail pieces need to be welded by track worker to form continuous welded rail and 
left at the centre of the track at the exact position required by the track renewal train to pick up 
at its arrival.

The general idea of Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) is to investigate if a decision is beneficial from 
all angles. In cases where there are several options to be considered, CBA allows review of 
them and development of reasoning for the most optimum choice. In addition, CBA can lead 
to emerging further options based on the analysis. 
4.1 Theory of Financial Cost-Benefit Analysis and Cost 
Effectiveness Analysis 
Let us consider a company, which has 1000DKK and is interested in investing this sum into a 
project. First of all, before investing money into the project, it is reasonable to know whether 
the decision is a good one for the company. One way of measuring this could be to find out 
the initial cost of the project and estimate how much money can be earned. To start the project, 
the company needs to spend 700DKK. Every following year, the company pays 300DKK to 
maintain the project, but it earns 200DKK of a clear profit. By prolonging this project for the 
next ten years there is then a chance for the company to earn 2000DKK.  
At first glance, it may seem that this investment is beneficial, however, there is more to the 
decision than this. Just because the company earns 2000DKK, does not mean that it is the 
best idea overall.  
If there is also an opportunity of buying corn for 1000DKK and selling it for a 4000DKK, then 
the company could earn 3000DKK. Therefore, the decision needs to be compared among 
other options. If the company invested the same money in stock, increasing by 25 % every 
year, for next ten years, then it would earn more money by doing nothing. It means that the 
decision of investing 1000DKK into the first project would not be an optimum one.  If the 
company decides to invest the same money into stock, but due to some economic crisis or 
some other reason it would earn only 1200DKK after ten years, then the idea with the first 
project is, in fact comparable to the first project and may have the same cost-benefits in the 
long run. Comparing these three options with respect to profit, benefits and risk in a methodical 
way helps us to weigh the options against each other and make an informed decision on which 
one to proceed with. Once we analyse the options in detail, further options can also surface. 
Primarily, the company is left with a clear basis for the decision.  This type of analysis is called 
Financial Cost-Benefit Analysis, and it investigates how decisions affect individual firms. 
Financial CBA is mainly used when it is necessary to consider different project options and 
choose the most appropriate one for a company, either cheapest one, with less risk or some 
other practical benefits. However, when the decision is made, it becomes important to analyse 
the proposed method of execution. To achieve this Cost Effectiveness Analysis (CEA) is often 
used. This type of analysis looks at the project, compare different methods of carrying it out, 
and finding the cheapest method of execution.  
When CEA is used, the options need to be transformed into monetary values. In the case of a 
railway maintenance project, most appropriate measurement is often the price of 1m ready 
track, measured in DKK/1m.

Theory of Economic Cost-Benefit Analysis 
Previously, analyses used to compare different methods were discussed, along with how 
decisions can affect private companies. When different methods of performing the same 
project are compared, it is not enough just to choose the one with the lower price. The impacts 
of decisions can be considered from a wider point of view, though, and this is Economic Cost-
Benefit Analysis.  
Let us consider that there is a railway maintenance project where the cheapest method has 
been chosen, however it takes a lot of time to perform. Due to this, many trains will be cancelled 
or delayed. When the delay increases to a significant level, people are likely to stop trusting 
train as a means of transport. At some point, more people will then choose to travel by car 
rather than train, increasing such problems as traffic jams, noise and pollution. When people 
start to use cars, it can be very difficult to return their trust to railway travel and start travelling 
by train again. 
Most freight trains operate at night. This helps to increase the amount of trains in daytime, 
when demand is much higher. One of the solutions can be to perform work at night, to reduce 
the disruptions to passenger traffic. For this reason, many freight trains can then be cancelled 
or delayed. There are many companies whose profit directly depends on trains to arrive time. 
For example, Sweden’s Volvo does not have large amounts of storage space, and their 
production is planned in such a way that a train with the required parts arrives just before a 
new shift starts. If a train is to be cancelled, then the entire production will be stopped. In worse 
case people may lose their job. In addition, performing works at night also reduces safety for 
maintenance workers. To find out which method results in which consequences, Economic 
Cost-Benefit Analysis needs to be used. 
Economic Cost-Benefit Analysis (ECBA) (also known as Social Analysis) looks at decisions 
from a wider perspective, with respect to how the chosen method will affect the people involved 
in the project.  When ECBA is used, it should be noted that it is important to clearly distinguish 
the methods used in the analysis and state the limit of detail or depth of the analysis.

Discussion of the essential cost calculation for Øst-Fyn project 
The essential cost results show that the use of the manual method of maintenance is cheaper 
than automated one, for this project. However, even though the price seems to be lower, there 
are many aspects which were not considered. The general idea of the calculation was to find 
the price for 1 m of performed maintenance works using one unit and unlimited time. In real 
life situation, this is not acceptable, because time is one of the important criteria, which can 
change the entire decision.  
Let us consider the calculation from the Øst-Fyn project regarding changing the sleepers and 
rails. Using the track renewal train, it costs around 294.000DKK for one 10h shift (excluding 
transport, setup cost and additional staff for the preparation works). The team of workers doing 
the same operation will cost around 179.000DKK. At first, it may seem that manual method is 
cheaper. However, the train can perform around 2000m for 10h shift, while the manual team 
(assuming good conditions) around 1000m. It means that to be able to perform the same 
amount of work manually, as it is performed with machines, in theory, it is necessary to double 
the resources required. In this case the cost of the manual method will also increase the same 
amount of times and it will cost around 358.000DKK.  
Another important criterion to consider is the length of the project. The price for the track 
renewal train was calculated with assumption that the length of the project is more than 30km. 
If the length of the project was around 10km, the price for the 10h shift could be higher. Using 
an example from previous project in Germany, for example, with a total length of 10km, the 
price could be around 374.000 DKK per 10h shift.  For this reason, to carry out a proper 
calculation of the cost for the different maintenance methods, it is necessary to specify some 
aspects which will be and will not be considered in calculation. 
The criteria that will be included in price calculation, are:  
• 
Length of project (the length of the project and the working period may change the price 
per shift) 
• 
Amount of resources (the number of resources may vary depending on the time 
constraints) 
• 
Working conditions (different working conditions may change the price per shift. 
Working conditions may be considered as amount of track junctions per km and the 
closed section of the track, i.e. free from any traffic) 
4.4 Analysis of the price criteria 
As was mentioned previously, the cost estimation requires some detailed research. It cannot 
be assumed only using constant values. Normally, the price per 1m of ready track will change, 
depending on variables mentioned in the previous section: Discussion of the essential cost 
calculation for Øst-Fyn project. In addition, the rate of progress of work may differ depending 
on the length of the project as people learn their task by performing it. This phenomenon can 
be defined as a Learning Curve.

At the beginning of every new project, the cost of the first shift is usually more expensive than 
the later ones. In addition, the work performance on the first shift of work is the smallest one. 
This is due to fact that the first shift includes a set up cost and the set up period.  
The construction period is usually defines the price per shift or amount of necessary resources. 
Normally, for every big project, there is a research team behind this, who performs a so-called 
set covering problem, using a mathematical model, where the general idea is to minimize the 
cost using the best possible time. 
To be able to perform a detailed maintenance cost estimation, these criteria need to be taken 
into consideration. For this reason, the variables mentioned above will be described in the 
following sub chapter. 
Learning Curve phenomenon 
Learning Curve is a phenomenon, which measures the relationship between increase in 
worker productivity and an improvement in labour skills acquired from the experience 
performing a task. 
The Learning curve effect leads to a decrease in the cost of performing 1m of track 
maintenance over time, because with increased involvement in the working process, workers 
become more and more familiar with that process. This, at the same time, leads to 
improvement in their efficiency level. In this case, “Efficiency” means the output generated per 
labour unit over the same amount of labour hours in the process of maintenance. 
This is due to the following factors: 
• 
The workers, who are engaged in the maintenance process, become familiar with the 
procedure of their work over the passage of time. Thus, they require less time to 
perform works on the same length of the track, which they were earlier producing by 
using more time. 
• 
Managers who are involved in the management and scheduling of the maintenance 
also become familiar with the process and are thus in better position to use the 
resources at their disposal in better manner, as well as scheduling the maintenance 
process more efficiently, thus leading to higher output for the same amount of input. 
A good example of the learning curve phenomenon can be seen on the track maintenance 
projects, based on data from Strukton Rail A\S. Through comparison of the working reports 
from different projects, where mostly the manual method of maintenance was used, it was 
possible to see, that at the beginning of the project, that the workers were able to perform 
around 800m of track per 10h shift. Later on, this value increased to 900m. Later, there were 
days where one team was able to maintain around 1100m per 10h shift. This increase is due 
to the Learning Curve effect.

Setup cost 
When machinery or team of people arriving to the project site, it require some time to perform 
all preparations before the beginning of work. Some machinery need to be built in together 
(sometimes the track renewal train and wagons arrives to the project site separately), people 
need to investigate the terrain and project site. There can be some problems with a logistics 
or cables as well. 
Setup costs include the costs of changing the tools or dies on the equipment, moving materials 
or components, and testing the initial output to be certain it meets the specifications. However, 
setup cost does not include the transportation cost.

those 9,5km, there are no stations in a middle and there are no traffic during the project time.

In this case, the first shift will be less productive due to setup period, but later on, the production

Usually many projects will have some stations in between. In such case, it is important to

has a passenger platform. Such stations are often built in precise size that passenger train can 
stop directly on a side of the platform and the gap between wagon and platform is minimized. 
Due to limited amount of space, such type of station cannot be maintained with big automated 
machines such as ballast cleaning train PM 200-1, and all works therefore need to be 
performed manually. However, the sections between the stations can be usually maintained 
by automated method using big machinery. 
To minimize the disturbance in passenger traffic projects, which are including passenger 
stations, are provided with only partial closing of the track, without a possibility to leave the 
machinery on the track for a night. For the machinery like PM 200-1, it require at least 1,5h of 
extra time whenever machine need to start or move to a new section, to be able to finish with 
all preparation works. It means that the length of maintained section in this case will be shorter.  
Moreover, the machinery may require additional permission to move from one closed track 
area to another. It is also not always possible to change the section during the traffic period. 
Therefore, such relocations may be performed during the night period, when the passenger 
traffic is smaller. 
To explain the problem here more precisely, let us consider the case when the distance 
between the stations is around 1300m. In the first day machine would perform around 900m, 
as it requires at least 1,5  hours to start the work. During the maintenance period, there is a 
possibility to leave the machinery on a project side. On the second day, the distance will be 
however only 400m. In theory, machine could easily make additional 500m in that shift, but 
due to limitation of relocation between the closed track sections, the machine will need to wait 
until the end of the shift in order to pass to another track section. The problem is that most of 
machines are paid by 10h shifts and not by specific hours of work. (This condition is important 
to take into consideration, as in some contracts there may be different prices for working period 
and the waiting period).  Even if the machine was working only half of the shift, the payment 
needs to be done for the whole 10h shift. The cost per 1m of the track for such project will be 
significantly higher even if the total length of the maintenance section is the same or even 
shorter.

Let us consider the example of the project shown in Figure 4.15. It is possible to see that the 
case is considering performing the maintenance works of the track including the sections 
between the signals and switches on a station area. The total length of the maintenance station 
is considered about 1100m. To explain the difference in cases in a best way it is assumed that 
the machinery can enter the area of the station at night, but it is possible to exit the station and 
proceed the maintenance works until the next station without waiting time. When the machine 
will reach the station, it needs to wait until the night period to enter the next closed section of 
the track. Machine cannot perform works on track junctions and for that reason, it will need to 
stop to be able to move through switches. Afterwards it will need to setup and prepare itself 
for work. Every time machine stop working and move to a new section, all preparation works 
normally take around 3h. It means that performed length in the day of moving will be at least 
300m shorter. In the case of the project shown on Figure 4.15, the total length of the section 
is around 12km, where around 11,6 km of it needs to be maintained with machinery (switches 
are excluded). For such project, it will be necessary to assume at least 16 shifts and in addition, 
some of the shifts will need to have overtime, to be able to finish the last tens of meters in time 
and move to a new closed section at night. Normally, machines usually allowed to work no 
more than 2h overtime per shift, otherwise it might be counted as a new shift afterwards. (This 
point is usually described in a contract, and therefore may change from project to project) 
Every additional hour (overtime) can cost around 18000DKK in case of track renewal train and 
around 44000DKK in case of ballast cleaning machine.

Discussion of the detailed cost calculation for Øst-Fyn project 
By comparison detailed calculations for two different method in Øst-Fyn maintenance project, 
it is possible to see that the price difference is about four million DKK. In addition automated 
maintenance method will use much less overtime hours, however both methods will include 
night shifts, to be able to perform all the works in time.  
In addition, the automated maintenance method for the current project has some additional 
benefits. First, it requires much less resources than the manual method. For example, in order 
to exchange the sleepers and the rails, the automated maintenance method need one track 
renewal train with a crew of 12 people. The manual method requires around 220 blue collar 
workers, 26 Huddig excavators, which will work 24 hours per day (it means additionally 52 blue 
collar workers who are operators of Huddig), 44 welders and 11 people responsible for safety 
on the site. It means that to exchange the sleepers and the rails, it will be necessary to have 
staff with at least 327 people.  
The time constraint of 14 days also means that all the works need to be done in parallel, what 
require more than 500 workers on site. This is an enormous number of workers for such 
project, and even big companies cannot always afford to have such a large amount of 
resources. Introducing the requirement for the manual maintenance method for the current 
project, could make it impossible for many companies to satisfy the staff demand. This can be 
one of the reasons, that in tender contract for the Øst-Fyn project it was stated that exchange 
and cleaning of the ballast need to be performed using the automated maintenance method.  
Moreover, the bigger amount of people working at the same time on a track, the higher the risk 
of accidents is. When there are many machines and many various actions happening at the 
same time on one track section, it becomes very difficult for workers stay alert to potential risks. 
From another side, manual method is more flexible and less dependent from working 
conditions than automated one. In case of stations with passenger platforms, it is not possible 
to perform maintenance with big machines, due to limited amount of space and therefore there 
is no other way than use the manual method. In situations where maintenance need to be done 
on a maintenance stations, with the partial closure of the track, manual method require less 
time to start the work and performance can be bigger than with automated maintenance.  
However, it is important to consider the available space on maintenance section and the traffic 
situation. Sometimes it is just not possible to send more than few teams to perform 
maintenance due to limited amount of space on the track. It is important to think about logistics 
and safety in case of the train traffic on neighbour track, as it is increasing the risk of accidents. 
Such factors may decrease the performance of the works.  
In case if time constraint would be increased up to 1 month, then the number of workers could 
be reduced down to 80-100 people. In this case, the risk of accidents would be smaller and 
the number of personal is acceptable. Moreover, there is a need to take into consideration the 
number of teams and the working schedule. Otherwise, if people work continuously for a long 
period of time, their productivity is decreasing and risk of accident is increasing, because 
people become tired and less responsible.  
Overall, after analysing the detailed calculation for different maintenance method it is possible 
to sum up, that for the project Øst-Fyn with those working conditions, automated maintenance 
method is more suitable. However, there were many variables, which may change from project 
to project.  For that reason the cost analysis is need to be performed for different type of the 
projects.

Discussion of the cost calculation for project Copenhagen – Høje Taastrup 
By analysing the cost of two methods, it is possible to see that for the project Copenhagen – 
Høje Taastrup, the manual method is around four million Danish krone cheaper than the 
automated one. One of the reasons is that the project is significantly short and has many track 
junctions. For such machinery as ballast cleaning train, the performance per shift will be lower 
if there are some track junctions included in a section and therefore the price per shift will be 
higher.  At the same time, manual method is more flexible and require less time to overpass 
the sections with track junctions. 
From another side, the manual method will require a large amount of people working on a track 
at the same time, which may increase the risk of accident.  It is not always possible to send 
unlimited amount of staff to the project are, as there may be a limited amount of space 
(especially on a stations) and therefore additional research may be required. Otherwise, the 
risk of being late can be increased. 
In automated method, the setup and transportation cost are constant independently from the 
project size and need to be included into the cost. It means that the smaller project is, the more 
expensive it may become to just even bring the machinery to the project site. 
In project between Copenhagen-Høje Taastrup the reasonable decision would be to use the 
manual method. The amount of track junctions and the closed track sections, reduce the 
performance of the automated method, and therefore the price per meter of track become 
more expensive. It means that for some type of project to use one of the methods is more 
reasonable that another.  
Analysing the data (performance and prices from current market situation) provided by 
Strukton Rail A/S together with personal experience it is possible to make calculation of the 
cost for various projects, compare and analyse the results and make and assumption when 
and which method is it more reasonable to use.

Automated method 
Each machinery has its own basic price. This is the assumed price (based on a market situation 
2016), what owners of the machinery can offer if the length of the project is more than 40.000 
km.  Afterwards, the less the length of the project the bigger will be the basic price per meter 
of work. The estimated growth of the pries can be described as follows (the estimation is based 
on experience from previous projects and information acquired from interview with the 
specialists from railway company Strukton Rail A/S and civil work specialists from company 
ARKIL A/S): 
• 
If the length of the project is smaller or equal to five km, then machinery prices 
increases for 50% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than five km, but not exceed more than ten km then 
machinery prices  increases for 40% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than ten km, but not exceed more than 15 km then 
machinery prices increases for 30% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than 15 km, but not exceed more than 20 km then 
machinery prices increases for 20% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than 20 km, but not exceed more than 25 km then 
machinery prices increases for 15% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than 25 km, but not exceed more than 30 km then 
machinery prices increases for 10% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than 30 km, but not exceed more than 40 km then 
machinery prices increases for 5% 
• 
If the length of the project is bigger than 40 km, then use the basic price, as companies 
may not offer lower price for their machinery

Manual method 
In case of manual method of maintenance, it is assumed that with the growth of the project 
length the cost per one meter of track will also grow. This assumption is based on a fact, that

together with the size of the project. The basic price in manual method is estimated (using the 
market situation of year 2016) for the projects shorter than five kilometres. Later on, it is 
assumed, that the price per meter of track will increase for 8% for projects that are longer than 
five and shorter than ten kilometres. Afterwards the cost will increase for 3% every additional 
ten kilometres.

CONCLUSIONS 
This report presents the findings of the research carried out for cost-benefit analysis of various 
railway maintenance methods. In this chapter, the overall conclusions are presented, and they 
are followed by recommendations for further work. 
6.1 Summary of the thesis findings 
The literature study presents failures of various railway structures, such as rails, sleepers, 
ballast and sub ballast. To prolong the lifetime of the railway structure, different methods of 
maintenance are used.  The maintenance methods may differ by manual or automated 
method, where manual method mean that maintenance is performed with small types of 
machinery operated by men. Automated method includes the usage of long trains, where most 
of the maintenance tasks performed by machinery, and operators are used for quality control. 
Different types of machinery used for maintenance continues to evolve and therefore some 
maintenance methods are more suitable for different type of projects, than for others. It is 
important to choose the proper method of maintenance in order to avoid unnecessary delays 
and feasibility problems and minimize the cost. 
The data collected from railway and machine owner companies was analysed and used, 
together with the personal experience of the author and colleagues from the railway company 
Strukton Rail A/S, to perform a cost and time estimation of different maintenance methods,  
required for the real life project Øst-Fyn, happening in Denmark in 2016. The results shows 
that usage of automated maintenance method more reasonable, due to cost and feasibility.  
Afterwards, using the same data received for Øst-Fyn and with advices of colleagues from 
railway company Strukton Rail A/S, the cost and time estimation was performed for the sample 
project Copenhagen-Høje Taastrup. The results shows that for the shorter projects, the usage 
of manual method is more reasonable, due to minimized cost and flexibility.  
The model was developed for cost estimation of various maintenance methods and it includes 
such variables as time, length, number of junctions and amount of earth works, where sub 
ballast need to be exchanged. The analysis of cost estimation results shows, that the same 
project, but with different set of variables, can be cheaper due to application of different 
method. 
Analysing different results acquired from the cost estimation model it is possible to conclude 
that for the projects, which are less than 18 km, it is more reasonable to use the manual method 
of maintenance, due to lower cost. The performance of manual method in overall smaller than 
in automated one, and therefore requires more time, for implementation. If the project includes 
extreme conditions, such as limited amount of time, it may be not feasible to use manual 
method of maintenance due to limited amount of resources in maintenance companies and 
space on the project site. 
6.2 Recommendations for further work 
The research of cost-benefit analysis performed for various railway maintenance methods in 
this thesis is based on data provided by railway and machine owner companies together with 
the experience of the author and colleagues from railway company Strukton Rail A/S, and 
therefore it includes assumptions and simplifications. For that reason the analysis may not be 
used as universal tool for any kind of maintenance projects. To be able to fit the analysis to the

most of the maintenance projects, following suggestions for subsequent research studies are 
proposed: 
• 
This research covers only financial cost-benefit analysis. Many other aspects of railway 
maintenance are described, however are not quantified. Therefore another types of 
analysis need to be included in further research. 
• 
The learning curve phenomenon and performance of the machinery is based on already 
experienced staff. Research on performance of unexperienced staff need to be added to 
the research. 
• 
The assumed time required for the maintenance projects is simplified. The proper 
estimation of time requires additional research and development of the model for set 
covering problem. 
• 
Overtime works need to be included into the cost estimation model. 
• 
Passenger stations with the platforms need to be included into the cost estimation model. 
• 
The Model used for cost estimation considers only situations, when rails and sleepers 
needs to be exchanged for the entire length of the project. A variable with different length 
of exchange of rails and sleepers need to be added.
