Abstract 
This study investigates the effect of the private car on quality of life in Copenhagen by comparing 
a base scenario with three different scenarios with air quality, noise pollution, space usage and 
congestion in mind. Literature review shows that air quality and noise pollution affect human 
health and congestion affects travel time. A survey on parking in Copenhagen shows that 40% of 
its residents think that green spaces are the most important aspect of Copenhagen land usage, but 
still 4.4% of all space is used only for parking spots. First scenario is business as usual, showing 
Copenhagen in 2050 if private car traffic increases in proportion with population. Scenario 2 is 
made with visions and goals that Copenhagen and Denmark have issued over the years. Finally, 
Scenario 3 is made as an utopic future with sustainability in mind, where the car free zone in the 
center is increased in size and all traffic consist of fleet sharing electric vehicles (EV). Private car 
traffic volume increases by 32% between base scenario and scenario 2 and decreases by 22% 
between base scenario and scenario 2 and 3. Comparison between scenarios were made in ArcMap, 
Microsoft Excel and Microsoft Access. The results show that air quality, noise pollution, space 
usage and congestion decrease with lower traffic volumes and a change to an all-EV fleet. To a 
achieve this, behavioral changes are needed, individuals would have to give up their private cars, 
which can be hard accomplish. 
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) shows that the utopic scenario is the most feasible when 
considering the four indicators used in this study. Scenario 2 made with visions and goals comes 
close behind the utopic scenario. Although it is estimated that utopic future scenario would be very 
expensive to implement and thus further research including a CBA would need to be conducted to 
make the best decision.

1. Introduction 
 Motivation 
Mobility is significant for human existence, although the means of travel is ever changing 
through the centuries. Mobility has always been essential, whether it was 60,000 years ago 
where modern humans migrated out of Africa and covered about 5,000-6,000 km per year 
(Metz, D., 2013) or in the year 2015 where an average Dane covered around 14,000 km by 
various modes (Statistics Denmark, 2017).  
The capital region of Denmark has published a report that focuses on regional growth and 
development strategy for the Greater Copenhagen area (two regions and 46 local authorities in 
Eastern Denmark, as well as 33 local authorities and a region in Southern Sweden). In this 
report quality of life, growth and sustainability is said to go hand in hand (Capital Region of 
Denmark, 2017).  As Copenhagen is part of the Greater Copenhagen area it will be the focus of 
this report and the effect of the private car on quality of life will be investigated and changes 
between scenarios measured. 
On one hand, the private car has made mobility easier and more convenient. It has increased 
employment, technological advancement and economic prosperity (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J.  and 
Khreis, H., 2016). Reliable transportation is a common barrier for employment (as not all 
workplaces are located in residential areas). Not only because car ownership has made it easier 
to manage your own time schedule, also because researchers have argued that it makes it 
possible to search for employment in a broader area. Recent literature has proven that car 
ownership increases the probability of being employed overall and lower unemployment rate 
generates better economic prosperity (Gurly, T. & Bree, D., 2005). Nowadays, some companies 
have started to challenge their employees to use public transport, walk or cycle to their 
workplace and providing cars that can be used at work for small errands. 
On the other hand, the private car (and other transport methods) has increased greenhouse 
emissions, noise disturbance, green space reduction, local temperature rise, health problems 
and human physical inactivity (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J.  and Khreis, H., 2016). For example, 
noise disturbance can generate actual health problems and traffic produces a significant amount 
of noise. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), noise from traffic can have 
damaging impact on health such as communication difficulties, headaches, sleeping difficulties, 
stress, high blood pressure, increased risk of heart diseases and hormonal effects. It is estimated 
that one million healthy life years are lost in Western Europe every year due to traffic noise 
(WHO, 2011). The total bill in the European Union (EU) for congestion, pollution and accidents 
linked to traffic is estimated to be 1,000 billion EUR annually (European Commission, 2019) 
and vehicle emission, congestion and dependency are a large factor that reduces quality of life 
in many cities over the globe (Kennedy, C., Miller, E., Shalaby, A., Maclean, H. & Coleman, 
J., 2005). 
==PAGE:11==
 
2 
 
For some, since cars are a big part of many people’s lives, car-free cities could be hard to 
imagine. However, some cities have already adopted long-term plans to reduce their private car 
fleet: Oslo, Helsinki, Madrid and Hamburg have announced plans to become partly private car 
free cities. Other cities such as Bogota, Brussels, Chengdu, Copenhagen, Dublin, Hyderabad, 
Milan and Paris have different plans to reduce motorized traffic (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J.  and 
Khreis, H., 2016). 
 Objective of thesis  
This study aims to investigate the effect of the private car on quality of life in Copenhagen by 
comparing four scenarios. The scenarios have different modal split, different population and 
number of trips. One scenario will only have electrical cars and a bigger car free zone in the 
center. The scenarios are compared using geographical information system and placed out 
visually on maps with the help of ArcMap and by using Microsoft Access and Microsoft Excel.  
Four indicators are investigated in each scenario, these are: Air quality, specifically PM10, PM2.5 
and NOx emission on one of the heaviest traffic streets in Copenhagen. Space usage where area 
used under parking spots are looked at. Noise pollution during day time, coming from traffic 
and the level of congestion on links. Initially, convenience indicator was used and travel times 
between zones in each scenario were planned to be calculated. Unfortunately, in the middle of 
the research an extension in ArcMap required for these calculations became unavailable and 
thus travel time is not estimated. Convenience indicator was therefore changed to congestion 
indicator.  
The study begins with background information about Copenhagen, following with a literature 
review that aims to provide knowledge about the indicators used in the study and sustainability 
in transportation. Afterwards the methods used are defined. The methods include the scenarios 
development, indicators measurement, Multi Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) calculation 
is introduced, assumptions used are listed and finally chapter about data collection and software 
usage. Following the method chapter results from the scenarios are listed and summarized and 
the result from the MCDA is presented. After listing of the results the thesis is summarized in 
discussion, where the results are evaluated and deliberated followed with recommendations. 
Finally, the study is closed with conclusion, where the end results are stated and evaluated in 
relation to the objective of the thesis.  
 Background information 
1.3.1. Historical transportation development 
Copenhagen is not afraid of changes in transportation. It is ever evolving which has impacted 
on how Copenhagen has developed as a city. In the late 1800s, bikes became more and more 
popular in the city which made it easier to sprawl the city, causing more numbers of people to 
move to the growing suburbs (Denmark.dk, 2019). At the same time, horse drawn carriages 
became somewhat of a tram in the city and thus the public transport started which changed to 
an electrical tram in 1901. The private car first came to Copenhagen in 1888 but did not stick 
on the market until some years later. To begin with it was very expensive to purchase a car and 
thus only served the wealthy upper-class population. When Henry Ford opened an assembly 
line in Sydhavn in 1919 (General Motors and Mercedes Benz followed shortly after) 
(Nationalmusset, 2019) cars became cheaper and thus the car population grew dramatically. By 
1933, around 117,000 cars were in used in Denmark, although cars didn’t become a common 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
 
property until the 1960s. By then it was accepted as the main mode share in the city. In the 
beginning of the 19th century, gasoline driven busses became a part of the Copenhagen transport 
system which grew rapidly in the time between the wars. In 1934, the first part of the S-line 
was opened and shortly after, in 1936, the second route was opened. In 1947, the Copenhagen 
finger plan was developed, which made sure that the population in the suburban areas would be 
able to move around the Copenhagen area easily (Mortensen, T., M., 2013). In 1973, a plan 
developed mainly for cars was issued and was the main plan until 1992, when another plan 
involving the metro was introduced in Copenhagen. The first metro line opened in 2002 and 
the second one in 2003, further lines are planned to open; the city ring in late 2019 and the 
Sydhavnsline in 2023 (Løvborg, E., 2019). 
1.3.2. Population and cars  
Copenhagen is the densest area in Denmark, with 5,800 people/km2 compared to 126 
people/km2 nationwide (City of Copenhagen, 2013). It goes up to 7,247 people/km2 in the inner 
Copenhagen city (Capital Region of Denmark, 2012). 
Population growth in Copenhagen is around 10,000 people a year (Statistics Denmark, 2017). 
Copenhagen’s municipalities’ population was 613.288 people in 2017. It is predicted that in the 
year 2030 there will be 715,226 individuals living in Copenhagen, with the number estimated 
to rise further to 755,783 by 2040 (Siemens, 2014). 
In Copenhagen there were it was 258 cars per 1,000 inhabitants in 2017 (Statistics Denmark, 
2017), which equates to 158,228 privately owned cars in Copenhagen. Assuming the current 
growth rate remains constant, there will be 208,721 cars in Copenhagen in 2050.  
1.3.3. Cycling and public transport 
Copenhagen is mostly flat and has good cycling infrastructure, thus making it easy, relatively 
safe and convenient to cycle around. Public transportation is also considered good, with 
different modes to select from (train, metro and busses). Modal split in Copenhagen shows that 
34% of trips are done in private cars, still leaving it at the highest split (City of Copenhagen, 
2017a) and creating problems in the form of congestion in the Copenhagen road system during 
peak hours.   
In order to serve the high number of cyclists in Copenhagen, the municipality has built around 
400 km of cycle paths, which all are separated from car lanes and sidewalks. Some of these 
cycle paths are so called superhighways, where commuters’ needs are given the highest priority, 
with a smooth ride and fewer stops provided. These superhighways have improved safety and 
connect work, study and residential areas. The highways are all close to stations so combining 
a trip with public transport is easy. In order to be categorized as superhighways the path needs 
to fulfill certain criteria including: air pumps, footrests, safer intersections, green waves and 
traffic lights timed to average cycling speed (Denmark.dk, 2019). This makes Copenhagen an 
excellent city with potential for high modal split for bikes.  
Although many might think that the people of Copenhagen ride bicycles for environmental 
reasons it is more often the case that travelling on bicycle around the city is faster and more 
convenient than driving. A 2006 survey showed that 54% ride bicycles for convenient reasons 
and only 1% did it to be eco-friendly. The same survey found that cycling in Copenhagen is 
very safe: 72% of cyclists feel safe on streets where specific cycle lanes are located. This is 
expected as, with a high number of cyclists, drivers are more aware of them in traffic and thus

injuries to cyclists drop with higher numbers of bike rides. In the years 1990-2000, distance 
traveled by bicycle rose by 40% while injuries dropped by 30% (Capital Region of Denmark, 
2016).

have been mentioned, namely, reduction of noise pollution and health impacts. Reduction of 
noise pollution not only has good impact on human health, it also is linked to house pricing. 
Finally, some experts see EVs as a stepping stone to autonomous driving which some see has a 
potential solution to the increasing traffic in Copenhagen (Aarhus University, 2017).

2.08% of all newly registered private cars since Denmarks’ Statistics started collecting data 
about new registered electrical cars in the country in 2009. In Table 1 new registration of

While Electrical vehicles were seen as providing Denmark with many benefits (reducing carbon 
emissions, local health impact and noise pollution) EVs have been facing an uphill battle due 
to tax reasons (Aarhus University, 2017). Denmark, being a country without auto 
manufacturing today, has high import tax on vehicles, ranging from 105% up to 180% 
depending on cost and weight of the vehicle. Electrical vehicles were excluded from this tax 
from 1983-2015 when laws were changed, this had the effect that EVs sales dropped 
dramatically after 2015 (deceasing from around 4,300 in 2015 to around 700 in 2017 including 
a 60% decrease in the first quarter in 2017), while other EU markets were getting stronger. This 
decreasing resulted in the Danish government to open a discussion to change taxes on EVs 
again (State of green, 2017, Autovista Group, 2018 & Hanley, S., 2018).

1.3.5. Air quality  
Denmark meets the EU's limit on values for particle pollution in the air. However, around three 
thousand Danes die prematurely each year and tens of thousands develop a severe illness that 
can be linked to fine particles in the air. Denmark‘s cities however do not meet the EU’s limit 
on hazardous nitrogen dioxide and Denmark's nitrogen dioxide levels are the cause of 
approximately 300-500 deaths every year nationwide (Press-Kristensen, K., 2014).

1.3.6. Space 
Cars, roads and parking spots take up large amount of already limited space found in cities. 
Space which could be used for other purposes that improve quality of life and wellness, such 
as: trees, parks and other green areas, which are often lacking in cities (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J.  
and Khreis, H., 2016).

parking, private parking, parking on private land, parking provided by the municipality and

1.3.7. Noise  
In Denmark, around 705,000 residents are exposed to traffic noise higher than the limit for 
construction of new dwellings (55dB). From those, 150,000 residents are affected by traffic 
noise exceeding 65 dB which can result in hypertension (The Road-Noise Group, 2003). WHO 
(2011) estimated that 30-50 premature deaths occur in Copenhagen every year that can be 
linked to traffic noise.  
Noise from passenger cars has been controlled since 1929 and new cars are now required to 
meet Europe-wide noise limits (74dB) and has been since 1996 (The Road-Noise Group, 2003). 
Thus, the Danish car fleet should mostly meet these laws, since the Danish passenger car fleet 
was on average 8.92 years old in 2016 (this level is the maximum level permitted and many 
vehicles produce lower levels of noise) (Statistics Denmark, 2017). 
1.3.8. Congestion and convenience  
Congestion and public transport are the two main problems Denmark faces in the transport 
sector. Congestion induced by cars has a great cost for both travelers and society (Aarhus 
University, 2017). Copenhagen network congestion for 2017 can be seen on Figure 21. This is 
an average daily value of congestion, where the red lines represent break down traffic. 
Value of time is a parameter that is used to convert time in to monetary value. The Danish value 
of time in 2004 was 67dkk in-vehicle time, this quickly adds up a large sum of money value 
which only increases in congestion because of higher travel time (Fosgerau, M., Hjorth, K. and 
Lyk-Jensen, S.V., 2007). 
Christensen (2000) investigated the impact of public transportation service on the traveler’s 
behavior, by using the Danish Transport Surveys (Transportvane Undersøgelsen) data. This 
investigation was conducted to better grasp the possibilities of change in modal choice, from 
car to public transportation. Larsen, M. K. (2010) found that many aspects interfere with mode 
choice; it ranges from aspects concerning the trip, the traveler, the home, the workplace, etc.    
While a car has usually five seats, the average amount of people per car is extremely low. From 
a car counting observation made during rush hour on Åboulevarden, the average amount per 
car was 1.12 persons. This results in a lot of extra unnecessary traffic in Copenhagen and thus 
congestion that could be avoided with more people using each car or change in modal split 
(Jensen, S.S. et al, 2013).  
A trip from Vanløse (Kærholmen) to the Inner City (Vendersgade) takes from 12 to 30 minutes 
by car according to Google.Maps (2019), depending on traffic, while biking takes 20 minutes 
and public transport 21 minutes at day time during weekday. This shows the huge impact 
congestion can have on travel time.

2.1.1. Cars in public space  
Cars use a lot of city space, whether it is space used for roads or parking. The amount of land 
used for transportation increases faster with increased car mode share than when other mode 
share increases. A commute home from work in a private car uses 90 times more space than if 
the same journey is made by public transport. Parking spaces typically are around 13-19m2 
which adds up to a high number quickly when car mode share is high (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J.  
and Khreis, H., 2016).   
2.1.2. Parking space policies affection on the transportation environment  
Actions in parking policies can affect transport behavior. Parking spaces do not only create 
spaces for cars already in the cities, but a greater number of parking spaces makes it easier to 
find a parking, meaning that traffic from vehicles looking for spaces will reduce. However, 
==PAGE:20==
 
11 
 
increased number in parking spaces will at the same time encourage increased car usage and 
bring more cars to the area. This generally causes more traffic which can lead to congestion 
problems, air pollution and noise pollution problems (Rambøl, 2016). 
By changing measures in parking policies and decreasing parking spaces, space can be reserved 
for other modes of transport for example bicycles or busses and making it less attractive to have 
a private car. That space could also be used for something else than transportation, for example 
green areas, urban nature, parks, which contribute to meeting the ambitions of a more bicycle-
friendly, livable, accessible, greener and more climate safe Copenhagen (Rambøl, 2016). 
2.1.3. Changing commuting from car to cycle  
Road vehicle emissions are one of the heaviest conductors to air pollution affecting human 
health. These emissions affect everyone in the neighborhood, both the drivers sitting in their 
cars due to air intakes exhaust emissions and people walking or biking alongside the road 
(Johansson, C., Lövenheim, B., Schantz, P., Wahlgren, L., Almström, P. Et al, 2017). Car traffic 
and related infrastructure affects three key environmental exposures: air pollution, noise 
pollution and local temperature rise (Nieuwenhuijsen, M., J. and Khreis, H., 2016). 
Many attempts have been made to estimate the impact of changing journeys from cars to 
bicycles from different sides of the world: in the Netherlands a study showed potential impact 
on all-cause mortality by changing to bicycles for a 7.5 or 15 km journeys. (De Hartog, J.J., 
Boogaard, H., Nijland, H. and Hoek, G., 2010). A similar study from Barcelona estimated the 
change in cyclist exposure to exhaust (Rojas-Rueda, D., de Nazelle, A., Tainio, M. and 
Nieuwenhuijsen, M.J., 2011). One study from New Zealand looks into shifting 5% mode share  
from car to bicycle (Lindsay, G., Macmillan, A. and Woodward, A., 2011) and an American 
study shifting 50% mode share on trips shorter then 8km was estimated (Grabow, M.L., Spak, 
S.N., Holloway, T., Stone, B., Mednick, A.C. and Patz, J.A., 2012). The last two studies have 
estimations on how the general population’s health would benefit the mode change.

Nitrogen oxides (NOx) consists both of nitrogen monoxide (NO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2), 
both are formed during combustion at very high temperatures when nitrogen from the air is 
oxidized. Nitrogen monoxide is not directly damaging to health while NO2 is. Exhaust from 
diesel engine typically consisted of 90% nitrogen monoxide and 10% nitrogen dioxide. 
Although, modernized diesel cars often have an oxidizing catalyst which reduces the diesel 
smell coming from the vehicle but increases the exhaust amount of harmful nitrogen dioxide 
up to 40% (Press-Kristensen, K., 2014). The largest sources of NOx in Denmark is the road 
transport sector, counting for 44% emissions in 2014 (Danish Center for Environment and 
Energy, 2017). Average maximum values allowed for NO2 is 40 µg/m3 and it can exceed 200 
µg/m3 maximum 18 times a year (Mortensen, K., 2018).   
Many health problems can arise from poor air quality. For example, cardiovascular diseases 
that can lead to premature death, airway diseases such as asthma, bronchitis, cancer, and other 
diseases such as diabetes. Scientists are even starting to investigate a connection between air 
pollution and brain diseases such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s. Mortality rates increase with 
higher air pollution: when PM2.5 increases by 10 µg/m3 the mortality rate increases by 6.2% 
and when NO2 increases by 10 µg/m3 the mortality increases by 5.5-8% (Kampa, M. & 
Castanas, E., 2008 & Press-Kristensen, K., 2014).

is used in district heating and thus it raises a problem of share of emissions to different sectors

2.3.1. Effects of noise 
Traffic noise is not a constant, it changes over time, as traffic is less at night time for example 
and more on peak hours. For environmental health assessment purposes a day-evening-night 
(Lden ) indicator is used to map annoyance across the EU in dB. Lden can then be divided up to 
Lday, Levening, and Lnight. Lnight has a maximum penalty of +10dB, which is considered because 
noise has more annoyance factor and people are more sensitive to noise effects in the night 
time. When mapping noise annoyance Lday should begin at 55dB and Lnight at 50dB (European 
environment Agency, 2014 & Passchier-Varmeer, Q. & Passchier, W., F., 2000).  
Passchier-Varmeer & Passchier (2000) found a conceptual model of the interaction of sound 
with the organism and the occurrence of effects of health and quality of life as can be seen on 
Figure 6. This model considers the effect of noise on health and quality of life as outcome of a 
processing of exogenous determinants or noise exposure. This exposure happens within 
economic and social environments and are modified by societal factors and thus lifestyle and 
concurrent exposure to other factors are considered. In quality of life research, the simple cause-
effect sequence seen in Figure 7 is not sufficient unless if simply a damage of the hearing organ 
is being looked at (Passchier-Varmeer & Passchier, 2000).

2.3.3. Noise factors for EVs 
Vehicles generate noise emission with tire interactions and the propulsion system. The noise 
emission increases with speed. Tire-road noise starts to increase more rapidly than propulsion 
noise at 50+ km/h and as a consequence dominates the noise emissions from the vehicle at this 
point. Thus, relevant difference in noise pollution can be found in internal combustion engines 
and EV’s in low speed areas, where EV’s are expected to be quieter by 3-4dB, but at a speed 
above 50 km/h reduction of engine noise has no effect (Verheijen, E. & Jabben, J., 2010 & 
Maffei, L & Masullo, M., 2014).

 Sustainable transport  
Generally, the thought of sustainable transport implies finding a proper balance between 
environmental, social and economic qualities for current and future measures, including indirect 
and non-market impacts (Litman, T., 2019).  
Many definitions of sustainability have been derived over the years. Sustainable transportation 
systems have the characteristics of allowing the basic needs of individuals and societies to be 
met safely and in a manner consistent with human and ecosystems health, and with equity 
within and between generations. It is a transport system that is affordable, operates in an 
efficient way, offers choices of transport modes and supports a vibrant economy. It is important 
that the transport system has limited emissions and waste, minimizes the consumption of non-
renewable resources, limits the consumption of renewable resources to a sustainable level and 
minimizes the use of land and noise levels (Litman, T., 2019). 
It is less clear how these qualities should be balanced. Ideally theory-based conceptions and 
operationalization of sustainable transport indicators should be developed and agreed upon, 
many performance indicators have been derived from current practices and stakeholders’ 
perceptions of sustainable transport. (Steg, L, & Gifford, R., 2005). 
Sustainability goals must balance economic, social and environmental goals, a list of goals can 
be seen in Table 8, this table suggested that each goal fits perfectly in a specific category, but 
the truth is that they often overlap each other, for example pollution is mentioned in the 
environmental category, but this is also a topic that effects human health and could there for be 
argued to fit in the social category as well (Litman, T., 2019).

2.5.1. Behavioral and technical changes towards sustainable transport 
Automobile usage has increased dramatically during the last decades; kilometers driven in a 
private car in Western Europe has increased by 90% between 1970 and 1990, which causes 
environmental, social and health problems (Metz, D, 2013). Many strategies have been 
proposed to try to come towards a more sustainable transport system. The changes can be 
divided into behavioral changes and technical changes. Behavioral changes are aimed at 
decreasing private car usage, by mode change, to less polluting transportation, travelling less, 
changing destinations and combining trips. Behavioral changes can lead to improved 
environmental quality, urban quality of life, and destination accessibility. Technological 
changes are directed to minimize negative impact for each car and each kilometer. For example, 
increasing energy efficiency of cars and developing new forms of road surface to reduce traffic 
noise. Combining these two changes gets us a step closer to a sustainable transport system. 
Behavioral and technical changes will have to be combined to reach the best outcome. For 
example, are accessibility problems too complex to be fixed within technical changes by itself, 
drivers may be tempted to use their new energy efficient cars more than their previous vehicle 
which could lead to more congestion. This is referred to as the rebound effect or Jevon’s 
principle (Steg, L., Gifford, R., 2005).  In this research the behavioral changes will solely be 
examined.   
Behavioral and technical changes are not only favorable towards sustainable transportation but 
can also influence quality of life of citizens (Steg. L. & Gifford, R., 2005). Linda Steg and Inge 
Sievers (2000) found that behavioral changes are generally harder to accomplish, as they 
usually require additional effort or decreased comfort for individuals. For example, reducing 
car usage involves changes in lifestyle which potentially will reduce freedom, comfort and 
convenience. Some people are more willing to make behavioral changes depending on their 
views of environmental risks. People’s view on environmental risks can be categorized by their 
coherence with four myths of nature: nature capricious (fatalist), nature perverse/tolerant 
(hierarchist), nature benign (individualist), and nature ephemeral (egalitarian). These myths can 
be illustrated by a ball in a landscape as seen on Figure 10, where the ball symbolizes the 
environmental risk behavior, whereas the line represents the vulnerability of nature. People 
grouped as nature ephemeral agree that it is necessary and acceptable to make policy measures 
aimed at reducing car numbers and that they could, and more importantly should, contribute to 
==PAGE:27==
 
18 
 
the solutions of car usage. While people categorized as nature benign and nature capricious felt 
their contribution was less important and would probably be against these kinds of policies. 
This shows that the latter group needs to be approached differently and should be informed with 
more effort on how their contribution can make a difference (Steg, L. & Stiever, I., 2000).

2.5.2. Sustainable transport indicators

2.5.3. Sustainable transport problems 
The measures used to reach the goals of sustainable transportation and to improve quality of 
life for the collective may conflict with individuals’short-terms interests. Individuals might 
have to adapt their lifestyles in order to work towards sustainable transport, this could mean 
that drivers may have to drive less and thus give up a certain freedom that the private car gives. 
The private car gives certain independence, flexibility, comfort, privacy and perceived safety. 
The private car even provides more status and pleasure than other modes in certain places, it is 
a means of self-expression and gives one the opportunity to control a powerful machine, rather 
than being a “tagalong” like while taking public transport (Steg. L. & Gifford, R., 2005). This 
means that improved quality of life of the majority of citizens might mean that drivers will have 
to give up some of their individual advantages given by their private car. This might be 
considered as a threat to their individual quality of life. If significant changes in travel behavior 
are needed, it is important to examine which transport policies are sustainable on the public 
level, that is to say if it is achievable to get the public to follow them. The definition of 
sustainable development upholds the importance of quality of life “meeting the needs of the 
present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own”. It 
emphasizes that quality of life depends on the extent that all generations can fulfill their needs 
(Steg. L. & Gifford, R., 2005).  
 Quality of life  
Quality of Life (QoL) was defined by Ed Diener (1997) as the extent to which important values 
and needs of people are fulfilled and is a multi-dimensional construct. It refers to well-being, 
perceived as person’s experience of life. There have been many different definitions of the term 
==PAGE:29==
 
20 
 
but they all come to a basic consensus on the meaning of QoL (Türksever, A.,N.,E. & Atakil, 
G., 2000). 
The history of QoL in medical sciences and public health starts in the Second World War in the 
United States, where social scientists involved in the war noticed a link in social and 
psychological forces that drove health and well-being. It was in 1948 that the World Health 
Organization (WHO) identified the importance of the QoL concept and stated that “health is 
physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity” 
(Land, K. C., 1996). 
Researchers from various fields have, since the 1930s, become more commonly interested in 
measuring Quality of life, especially in the last decades. Number and scope of QoL researcher 
have increased, while the definition of QoL differs among researchers. It has been argued that 
QoL perception and experience will become an important aspect of people’s spatial decision-
making and in repositioning cities (Türksever, A.,N.,E. & Atakil, G., 2000). 
The wellness concept came to the forefront in the 1950s when Dr. Halbert Dunn, who was the 
first director of the National Office for Health Statistics in the United states of America, 
introduced “an integrated method of functioning which is oriented toward maximizing the 
potential of which the individual is capable, within the environment where s/he is functioning” 
the idea of high-level wellness. (Land, K.C, 1996 & Dunn, H. L., 1961). This roused discussion 
about social indicators, to be able to measure QoL changes and as a goal of social and economic 
policy, QoL covers most or all domains of life and, in addition to individual material and 
immaterial well-being, such collective values as freedom, justice and the guarantee of natural 
conditions of life for present and future generations (Land, K.C, 1996). 
2.6.1. Assessing quality of life 
Effects of QoL in transportation can be assessed by indicating to which degree multiple 
scenarios of transportation will affect QoL indicators in both a positive or negative way. Then 
the changes can be weighted based on importance judgments of the relevant QoL indicators, 
since the indicators are not all the same in significances to individuals (Steg, L. & Gifford, R, 
2005).  
2.6.2. Health problems and benefits 
It has been observed that people that live close by large traffic streets in Copenhagen have over-
mortality. This is around 300-500 persons per year where fine particles and gasses are 
responsible for around 20 and traffic noise for 30-50 premature deaths. The remaining number 
are thought to be because of ultrafine soot particles that primarily come from traffic exhaust.  
Ultrafine particles have a high content of toxic soot and their surfaces are coated with heavy 
metals. These particles are deposited in the finest part of the lungs and transferred into the blood 
(Press-Kristensen, K., 2014).  
2.6.3. Changes to cycling 
People that cycle to and from work took fewer sick days from the year 2005 – 2012. In this 
period people living in Copenhagen have increased their cycling kilometers from 2.8 million to 
3.5 million km each day which a 25% increase. With that, one million fewer paid sick days 
were used. By cycling 1,200 km, each person saves one paid sick day in average (Capital Region 
of Denmark, 2012).

3. Method 
 Scenarios development 
Scenarios are useful to make an outline of the future, it can be an effective and useful method 
for analyzing the present to facilitate the creation of policies and goals. Scenario making helps 
with decision-making for today, so we can shape the development for our society towards our 
goals in the future (Maack, J., N, 2001).  
Effects of QoL in transportation can be assessed by indicating to which degree multiple 
scenarios of transportation will affect QoL indicators in both a positive or negative way and 
then the changes can be weighted based on importance judgments of the relevant QoL 
indicators, since the indicators are not all the same in significances to individuals (Steg, L. & 
Gifford, R, 2005).

 Indicators 
Sustainability process must be comprehensive and integrated, all significant objectives must be 
considered, as well as impacts and options. First a vision goal must be set (what we ultimately 
want to achieve), that helps to define the objectives which is the way to achieve the goals. Then 
set up targets which are specific, reasonable and measurable objective which we want to 
achieve. Then we will get the outcome which is the ultimate changes in impacts and activities, 
such as travel activity, pollution emissions etc. (Litman, T., 2019).   
When selecting indicators for measures, they should be helpful for our goals and objectives and 
should provide useful information and in most situations, one single indicator is not sufficient, 
therefore an index (list of indicators) should be used (Litman, T., 2019).   
Four indicators were selected to get a grasp of the effect cars have on quality of life in 
Copenhagen. The indicators were chosen with environmental factors in mind as well as people’s 
convenience, health and habits. Three of the indicators are objective and are all 
environmentally/social indicators: Air quality, space and noise, while the forth one is partly 
subjective and a social/economic indicator: congestion (first selected as convenience). The 
indicators are used to measure particular characteristic of interest and are used to understand 
the variable that the indicator is measuring. The indicators are relevant and reflect on quality of 
life for persons.

Congestion is interpreted in four intervals, where the lower the percentage the more congestion 
there is, which gives the level of congestion as follows: 
• <40% Break down 
• 41-60% Critical congestion  
• 61-80% Great congestion 
• >80% Incipient congestion  
Incipient congestion gives the road user an acceptable travel speed, travel speed is the average 
speed on a given link at a given point in time. Break down implies that the travel speed is very 
low, at the stop-go point. 
For the Base scenario the congestion was calculated with Traffic Analyst in Arcmap, 
specifically the Road Traffic Assignment tool and Modeling Route choose which are models 
based on network datasets, feature classes and tables, the models are capacity restraint and 
include stochastic effects. To implement the Road Traffic Assignment a geoprocessing model 
was created in ArcMap and set to run for 200 iterations.  
The OD matrix used is for a time period of 10 hours on an average weekday and thus the time 
period in the model input was set to 10 hours.

 Multi Criteria Decision Analysis  
Multi Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) is a good helping tool for decision-making process 
by narrowing down the best alternative. Many different MCDA are available but, in this report, 
the multiplicative REMBRANDT (Ratio Estimation in Magnitudes or deci-Bells to Rate 
Alternatives which are Non-DominaTed) will be used. MCDA considers and synthesizes 
several effects, quantitatively and qualitatively (Barfod, M., B. and Leleur, S., 2014). 
The four indicators were selected to compare the 4 scenarios with each other and were assigned 
values as follows, were 1 is the most important indicator to 4 – the least important: 
1. Convenience 
2. Noise pollution  
3. Space usage 
4. Air quality  
Convenience shall be weighted the highest in the project since it is what touches people most 
closely, thus it is easier to make them change, if they see results right away. Noise pollution 
was estimated to be the second most important because noise annoyance is easy for people to 
relate to. Space usage is placed in third place since survey mentioned in Chapter 1.3.6. shows 
==PAGE:41==
 
32 
 
that citizens of Copenhagen have a high preference for green spaces, leaving Air quality as the 
least important indicator.

8 depending on their preferences and put in a matrix where the value of each alternative for

5. Discussion   
As has been mentioned, some assumptions were made while conducting this study, that is that 
the cities’ buildings and roads do not change between scenarios, average speed on links does 
not change, brake wear, road abrasion and tire wear do not change, weather conditions remain 
the same in the future and estimations for the year 2030 for NOx emissions is used. All these 
assumptions might not hold, city landscapes are ever evolving, with change in traffic volume 
comes changes in speed on links, weather conditions are changing, and technology 
improvements happen every day. This does although not have a lot of impact on the comparison 
between scenarios since we may assume the numbers are estimation on the comparison would 
be the same with different conditions. Unless for the NOx emissions, while there could be big 
improvements in air filtering from combustion engines, then NOx emissions values for BAU 
and the Visions scenarios would be less. These improvements would although not effect the 
Utopic scenario, since it has no engines. This would lead to a difference between the Utopic 
comparison with the BAU and the Visions scenarios. 
The changes that is needed to be done to reach these scenarios are behavioral changes which 
are harder to accomplice than technical changes as mentioned in Chapter 2.6.1. This is because 
they require additional effort or decreased comfort for individuals and reducing car usage 
involves a big change in lifestyle for many people. Thus, further work is necessary to implement 
policies to successfully achieve the desired scenario. Especially would the Utopic future 
scenario need a big behavioral change since the car free zone in the city center has been made 
larger in size.   
To get a better comparison between scenarios a Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) would have to be 
made which would then be included in a COSIMA, a tool to combine results from CBA and 
MCDA together. This would help decisions makers with upcoming policies and, also, include 
stakeholders in the decision-making process. The current MCDA shows the Utopic scenario as 
the most feasible scenario, but it is reasonable to assume that this scenario would have a high 
CBA and thus would not do as well in the COSIMA. 
As mentioned in Chapter 2.1.2. actions in parking policies can affect transport behavior. 
Therefore, while making policies for the future scenarios, parking should be very well thought 
of. Increased number in parking spaces will encourage increased car usage and decreased 
number of parking spots will force people to use cars less. But also create an annoyance for the 
public and convenient problems, so finding the moments to reduce parking spots is a delicate 
matter. In the BAU scenario the parking zones would have to be increased in size and possibly 
investigated if a higher price for parking would be necessary. The Utopic scenario only has 
underground parking, this involves extremely high construction costs, although, in return 
Copenhagen would retrieve 4.1 million square meters of land back. This retrievable land could 
be used as green zones. Green zones have been recorded to improve happiness and as mentioned 
in Chapter 1.3.6. 40% of citizens in Copenhagen think green zones are the most important 
aspect in Copenhagen’s landscape, while only 7% believed it to be parking spots.  
Air pollution from traffic in Copenhagen changes dramatically between the scenarios, ranging 
from 3% increase of NO2 to no pollution at all in Utopic future due to electrical traffic only. 
PM10 and PM2.5 range from 32% increase to 22% decrease which follows the change in traffic 
in the scenarios. Increase in PM pollution could have a dramatic effect on human health in 
==PAGE:71==
 
62 
 
Copenhagen as seen in Chapter 2.3, the increase between Base scenario and BAU is 1.13 µg/m3 
for PM2.5 which could increase mortality rates related to air quality by almost 1%. Quality of 
life improvements for the Visions and the Utopic scenarios are significant and would lower 
mortality rates and improve health in the city. The BAU scenario is very close to EU’s limit for 
all categories.  
Noise pollution can cause various health problems as stated in Chapter 2.4. there is not a simple 
cause and effect model that shows noise pollution effect, as humans are differently annoyed 
and sensitive to noise. Noise pollution starting in 55dB can cause annoyance and other 
problems, therefore it is not acceptable that 63% of our environment is polluted with noise as 
happens in the BAU scenario. Visions scenario has noise pollution covering 35% and it goes 
down to 27% in the Utopic scenario. This is, due to the effect of the car free zone and the fact 
that EVs do not emit as much noise when driven under 50km/hour. From the same reason land 
exposed to noise higher than 75dB are relatively similar in the Visions and the Utopic scenario, 
due to noise pollution over 75dB happening on links with speed limit higher than 50km/hour. 
There has been some discussion about EVs being more dangerous to pedestrians and cyclist 
because they are so silent at low speed. This is something that could be looked better into and 
could be included in CBA as accident costs. 
Congestion is lowest in the Visions scenario, where congestion is incipient on 22% of links, 
Base scenario and the Utopic scenario come next with 28% and 29.6%. This is due to the fact 
that the Utopic scenario has the same amount of car trips as the Visions scenario but on fewer 
links. This means that the car traffic must move to other links and causes more traffic congestion 
to build up on them. Between the Base and the Utopic scenario there are few links that increase 
in traffic, this happens because the traffic has to find new routes, because of the bigger car free 
zone in the city center. 
For Copenhagen to get a bigger EVs fleet in trade of combustion engine, charging pods needs 
to be reachable within short time travel times everywhere in the city, as gas stations are 
nowadays. This means that it would be necessary to increase the number of charging pods and 
find them a suitable location so they are distributed well. Denmark should reconsider their tax 
policy, to make it once again more favorable to purchase EVs over combustion engines as EVs 
have some favorable characteristics over combustion engines, at least for short trips like in 
cities.     
Changes in modal split, where car split goes down, has other health benefits than what has been 
estimated in this study. As mentioned in Chapter 2.6.3. other studies have shown that by 
increasing bike shares in modal split by 25% one million paid sick days can be saved. This is 
not only economically beneficial, but also improves overall health and quality of life for 
individuals. While change in modal split can have positive impact on health, it can also have 
negative effect on convenience, were people do have to give up their comfortable cars that give 
them added freedom and change to a bike or public transport. This can specially be hard for 
families with small children and this effect must be looked into as well, when having quality of 
life in mind and is a project for future studies.   
The result show that change in car traffic volume influences current quality of life in 
Copenhagen. More traffic volume has indeed bad effect on noise pollution, air quality, space 
usage and congestion. Unfortunately travel time between scenarios could not be calculated after

the traffic analyst extension became unavailable so the convenience factor could not be included 
as was the initial plan.

6. Conclusion  
Based on the conclusions above, increased traffic has many unwanted side effects that affects 
quality of life. It produces more air pollution, noise pollution, demands more space usage and 
increases congestion. The comparison of the scenarios shows that there is link between better 
quality of life and less traffic, even so this study has not fully involved other aspects that effect 
quality of life as was the initial plan.  
The MCDA shows that the most feasible scenario is the Utopic future scenario when 
considering the four criteria in this study. Even though that scenario has the second highest 
congestion on links, there is a tradeoff made from the fact that this scenario has the lowest air 
pollution, noise pollution and space usage.    
Further research is needed to determine the cost effect to fully understand the impact assessment 
for each scenario. These researches should include a research of travel time, convenience, 
people willingness to make behavioral changes towards more sustainable future and CBA for 
the scenarios – to name a few.  
While making policies for the future, reduction in car traffic should highly be considered and 
while making these policies one must remember that people are not all the same. Different 
approaches must be used for different groups of people to go forward together to a more 
sustainable future, so we can meet our needs without compromising the ability of future 
generations to meet their own.
