Abstract 
The purpose of this master's thesis was to investigate the productivity of office 
workers and how to improve productivity in an economically sustainable way in 
connection with an indoor climate renovation. The motive for this study has 
been to create focus on indoor climate in relation to office workers' productivity 
and the large financial gain as a result of an energy renovation. The starting 
point was an existing office building on Amager, where a condition assessment 
was prepared. Three case scenarios were defined with the common purpose to 
improve the indoor climate without distorting the energy consumption in the 
building. All cases have been simulated with the simulation tool IDA ICE to 
extract information about the building's energy consumption and thermal 
environmental quality. The first case was prepared to classify the current 
condition of the building. The results from case 1 showed that the building's 
performance was low, in the form that the indoor climate did not live up to any of 
the environmental categories and the heat loss from the building parts was high, 
especially in winter. case 2 showed that the building, with some renovation 
measures could live up to the requirements of BR18. case 3 was based on the 
improved building in case 2, but with stricter requirements for the indoor climate. 
It was investigated what it took to comply with category A in DS / EN ISO 7730 
and category I in DS / EN 16798. In order to be able to answer the purpose of 
the assignment, a rough economic calculation has been made of the potential 
benefits of improved productivity for office work. The calculation is based on 
three different relationships between environmental quality and productivity. It 
could be concluded that the relationship between ventilation and productivity 
was the relationship that was closest to the real conditions, which therefore led 
to it being considered in the final cost-effective calculation. The calculation 
showed that case 3 with the relationship ventilation and productivity, had a 
factor of 3.9, which meant that the cost of the renovation has been paid back 
3.9 times over 30 years. Furthermore, it was concluded that case 3 was the 
most favorable renovation case.

Resumé 
 
Formålet med dette kandidatspeciale var at undersøge kontorarbejders 
produktivitet og hvordan man på en økonomisk bæredygtig måde kunne 
forbedre produktiviteten i forbindelse med en indeklimarenovering. Motivet for 
dette studie har været at skabe et større fokusområde på indeklima i relation til 
kontorarbejders produktivitet og den store økonomiske gevinst som resultat af 
en energirenovering. Der blev taget udgangspunkt på en eksisterende 
kontorbygning på amager, hvor en tilstandsvurdering blev udarbejdet. Tre 
scenarier blev defineret med det fælles formål om at forbedre indeklimaet uden 
af forvrænge energiforbruget i bygningen. Alle opstillede scenarier har været 
simuleret med simuleringsværktøjet IDA ICE til at udtrække oplysninger som 
bygningens energiforbrug og termiske miljø kvalitet. Den første scenarie blev 
udarbejdet for at klassificere bygningens nuværende tilstand. Resultaterne fra 
scenarie 1 viste, at bygningens ydeevne var lav, i form af at indeklimaet ikke 
levede op til nogen af kvaliteterne og varmetabet fra bygningsdelene var højt, 
især om vinteren. Scenarie 2 viste at bygningen med nogle renoveringstiltag 
kunne leve op til kravene i BR18. Scenarie 3 tog udgangspunkt i den 
forbedrede bygning i scenarie 2, men med skærpede krav til indeklimaet. Der 
blev undersøgt, hvad der skulle til for at overholde kategori A i DS/EN ISO 7730 
og kategori I i DS/EN 16798. For at kunne besvare opgavens formål er der i den 
sidste del af studiet blevet lavet en grov økonomisk beregning af de potentielle 
fordele ved forbedret produktivitet for kontorarbejde. Beregningen har taget 
udgangspunkt på tre forskellige relationer mellem miljøkvalitet og produktivitet. 
Der kunne konkluderes at relationen ventilation og produktivitet, var den relation 
der var tættest på de virkelige forhold, hvilket derfor medfødte at den blev 
overvejet i den endelige rentabilitetsberegning. Beregningen viste at case 3 
med relationen ventilation og produktivitet, havde en faktor på 3,9 hvilket 
betyder at omkostningerne til renoveringen er tilbagebetalt 3,9 gange i løbet af 
30 år. Endvidere blev der konkluderet at scenarie 3 er den mest gunstige 
renoveringssag.

INTRODUCTION  
 
The focus on sustainability has grown as there is a worldwide desire to reduce 
global CO2 emissions from fossil fuels. In addition, the construction industry is 
one of the biggest causes when it comes to CO2 emissions. Overall, 
construction accounts for almost 40% of global emissions, of which 28% is for 
building operations such as energy consumption for heating, cooling, and 
lighting (DAGENS BYGGERI, 2019). Sustainability can be divided into different 
scale ratios, a global scale, city scale, building scale and building component 
scale. This assignment focuses on the building and building component scale.  
It is known that indoor environmental quality has an impact on the occupant’s 
performance and health. The people that are affected the most by poor indoor 
environmental quality are the people who are exposed to it for the longest time. 
This is a major concern because most people spend up to 90% of their time 
indoors. The usual time spend in offices in Denmark is 8 hours a day i.e., 40 
hours a week which corresponds to 20-25% that office workers spend at work 
every year (Kraus & Šenitková, 2020). For office workers, poor indoor climate 
leads to “sick building syndrome” (SBS) which results in decreased 
performance efficiency. It is the job of the buildings to secure a comfortable and 
healthy indoor climate for the occupants. The building layout and construction 
can generally not complete the job therefore it needs systems that can help 
control the different indoor climate parameters. Many buildings suffer from 
inadequate performance, including high energy consumption, thermal comfort 
issues, and insufficient daylighting. The problems are generally caused because 
the design team is not considering these criteria from the start. To avoid a bad 
indoor climate, it is important to consider the indoor climate from the start in an 
integrated and holistic way (Kraus & Šenitková, 2020).

2.1 
Sustainability 
In this section, the concept of sustainability will be reviewed. Sustainability 
extends over 3 different dimensions which together provide a holistic idea of 
sustainability - the social, the environmental and the economic dimension. 
(MAAGØE, 2013) 
 
The focus on sustainability has grown as there is a worldwide desire to reduce 
global CO2 emissions from fossil fuels. In addition, the construction industry is 
one of the biggest causes when it comes to CO2 emissions. Overall, 
construction accounts for almost 40% of global emissions, of which 28% is for 
==PAGE:18==
Page 5 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
building operations such as energy consumption for heating, cooling and 
lighting. (DAGENS BYGGERI, 2019). In addition, there is an energy 
consumption in connection with the transport sector - the location of the 
building. Sustainability can be divided into different scale ratios, a global scale, 
city scale, building scale and building component scale. This assignment 
focuses on the building and building component scale. 
 
The 3 above dimensions for the definition of sustainability are; Social 
sustainability, environmental sustainability and economic sustainability. Social 
sustainability deals with the social conditions for planning and operating a 
building. In connection with this task, the social sustainability is associated with 
the comfort aspects such as thermal and acoustic comfort. The environmental is 
about building and renovating cities and buildings with as little CO2 emissions 
as possible. This type of sustainability is quantified using certification systems 
that compare the environmental performance of buildings. The economic 
sustainability is linked to the economic processes in a building for planning, 
construction, renovation, and operation. 
 
The aim of this assignment is to investigate office worker productivity and how 
to improve productivity in an economically sustainable way in connection with 
an indoor climate renovation. The answer to the task will be assessed with a 
social and economic sustainability perspective. 
2.2 
Indoor Environmental Quality 
The following section will review which parameters that have an impact on the 
occupant’s performance and health. Indoor air quality, thermal comfort, visual 
comfort, and acoustic comfort are the four categories of indoor environmental 
quality (IEQ) that needs to be considered to achieve optimum comfort for the 
occupants. The biggest impact on productivity is mainly IAQ and Thermal 
comfort, therefore the focus will be in these subjects. 
 
==PAGE:19==
Page 6 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
The primary cause of poor indoor environmental quality is mainly internally 
indoor pollution sources that release gases or particles into the air. Externally 
factors are outdoor pollutants that are carried into the building through an 
inadequate ventilation system or openings in the building envelope. The most 
common reason for an accumulation of pollutants in buildings is the lack or 
inaccessibility of indoor air replacement. A lack of air change can also lead to 
higher humidity and higher temperature which also creates discomfort for the 
occupants (Kubba, 2016).  
 
The people that are affected the most by poor indoor air quality are the people 
who are exposed to it for the longest time. This is a major concern because 
most people spend up to 90% of their time indoors. The usual time spend in 
offices in Denmark is 8 hours a day i.e., 40 hours a week which corresponds to 
20-25% that office workers spend at work every year. (Kraus & Šenitková, 
2020). It is now well documented that indoor climate has an influence on the 
human’s performance, health issues, and mood. For office workers poor indoor 
climate leads to “sick building syndrome” (SBS) which results in decreased 
performance efficiency.  
 
SBS is a phenomenon where building occupants complain of symptoms that do 
not match a specific illness and are difficult to trace to any specific source. 
Factors that may impact SBS include, noise, poor lighting, thermal discomfort, 
and psychological stress. Some SBS indicators may be: Acute discomfort, 
headaches and dizziness, nausea, poor concentration and forgetfulness, eye 
and throat irritation, etc. 
 
It is the job of the buildings to secure a comfortable and healthy indoor climate 
for the occupants. The building layout and construction can generally not 
complete the job therefore it needs an HVAC-system that can help control the 
different indoor climate parameters. Many buildings suffer from inadequate 
performance, including high energy consumption, thermal comfort issues, and 
insufficient daylighting. The problems are generally caused because the design 
==PAGE:20==
Page 7 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
team is not considering these criteria from the start. To avoid a bad indoor 
climate, it is important to consider the indoor climate from the start in an 
integrated and holistic way.  
2.2.1 
Indoor Air Quality 
Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) is determined by the concentration of contaminants 
presented in the air. In an office room with a high presence of occupants the 
most significant source of pollution is the occupants themselves. Humans affect 
the indoor climate with bioeffluents which contains water vapor, CO2, and 
various volatile organic compounds (VOC). VOCs are emitted gases from 
certain solids or liquids which generally contain in household products, cleaning, 
cosmetic, perfume, etc. (EPA, 2020) The level of activity and number of humans 
influence the production of bioeffluents. The concentration of CO2 in the air is 
used as an indicator of how well a room’s indoor climate is. There is a natural 
CO2 concentration in the outdoor air around 380 parts per million (ppm) while 
an adult sedentary office worker produces around 17 l CO2 per hour (Toftum, 
Wargocki, & Clausen, Indeklima i skoler – Status og konsekvenser, 2011).  
 
Other internal sources of pollution: dust, chemical compounds from cleaning 
products and cooking. Common external combustion sources such as tobacco 
smoke, gas, oil, kerosene, coal, and wood and emissions from building 
materials, furnishings, and various types of equipment. (Kubba, 2016) 
 
Ventilation is an important factor to limit the accumulation of pollutants level. 
The most common means of ventilation are natural ventilation and mechanical 
ventilation. The amount of ventilation that are needed in a room is defined by 
the concentration of CO2 which means that a room with a high presence of 
occupants needs more ventilation than a room with a low presence of 
occupants. Ventilation systems will be described in the upcoming sections.

2.2.2 
Thermal environment quality 
The quality of the thermal indoor environment is a factor that affects the 
occupants in noticeable ways. When it comes to spaces that are used for 
working or studying purposes and in general for longer periods, the importance 
of it gets even higher since the better the thermal indoor environment is, the 
more the productivity of the occupants is enhanced.  
 
To evaluate the thermal environment at a place of work it is necessary to 
measure the four physical parameters: Air temperature, mean radiant 
temperature, air velocity, and humidity. It is also necessary to evaluate the 
activity level and their thermal properties of the clothing (insulation and vapor 
resistance). Based on these parameters it is then possible to evaluate the 
combined influence by using a relevant thermal index (Olesen & Madsen, 
1995).  
 
One of the most widely used indices in moderate thermal environments, the 
PMV index (Predicted Mean Vote), predicts the mean value of the overall 
thermal sensation of a large group of persons as a function of activity (metabolic 
rate), clothing insulation, and the four environmental parameters air 
temperature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity, and air humidity. 
 
The PPD index is derived from the PMV index and predicts the percentage of 
thermally dissatisfied persons among a large group of people (Toftum, Toftum 
Handbook Human Factors Thermal comfort indices, 2004). Figure 1 shows PPD 
as a function of PMV.

4.2.3 
Building leakage 
To be able to perform the simulation of the office building, it is necessary to 
know about the building air tightness, also called infiltration. Infiltration is 
considered as energy waste when hot air flows out through leaks and cracks in 
the building envelope. According to BR18 the requirement for infiltration is 
maximum 1,5 l/s m2 (BR18, 2018). Requirements for infiltration was not 
introduced in the time the reference building was built. Therefore, a well-
considered assumption is made.  
 
The building has been continuously renovated, among other things, the 
windows on the top floor are reasonably new and therefore it can be assumed 
that the assembly around these windows and surrounding constructions has 
been tightened so the leakage is minimized. The buildings air tightness is 
assumed to follow the minimum BR18 requirements which is 1,5 l/s m2.

Potential benefit of improved 
indoor environment quality 
 
In this section, a rough economic calculation will be made of the potential 
benefit of improved performance of office workers. It is chosen to focus on three 
relations between indoor environment quality and work performance or health, 
as they are the most relevant for this thesis in relation to the renovation 
proposals that are given in section 4.3. The relations that will be focused on are: 
Ventilation rates and short-term sick leave, Ventilation rate and performance, 
Temperature and performance. Only zone 1, the landscape office, will be 
considered in this investigation as the occupants sits here throughout the day 
and the biggest performance change will occur in this zone. Figure 3 
(Seppänen, Fisk, & Lei, 2006), Figure 4 (Seppänen, Fisk, & Lei, 2006), and, 
Figure 7 (Seppänen, Fisk, & Lei, 2006) which is derived from former studies, 
shows the relation between Ventilation rates and short-term sick leave, 
Ventilation rate and performance, Temperature and performance, respectively. 
These figures will in the upcoming sections be replotted so a trend line and the 
associated polynomial equation can be drawn and enable further investigation 
regarding effective working hours in relation to performance. In each of these 
graphs, R-squared is inserted to see if there is variation between the variables 
and the trendline. R = 1.0 indicates that the relationship between the trend line 
and the variables fits 100%. Finally, the most profitable relation will be 
considered and included in the overall economic calculation, where the cost of 
the renovation measures is found. In this way, the most sustainable optimization 
of indoor climate including performance in office work is found. A summary of 
the economic benefit for all relations and performance is given in section 6.4.

Discussion  
In this section the process and the results of the project will be discussed. The

7.1 
Interpreting IEQ and occupant performance 
The indoor environmental quality and its underlying factors have been reviewed 
in section 2.2. First IEQ was reviewed, and which parameters have an impact 
on the occupant’s performance and health. It was discovered that the primary 
cause of a poor IEQ is indoor pollution sources that release gases or particles 
into the air in connection with the lack or inaccessibility of ventilation in the 
building. These factors lead to higher humidity and higher temperature which 
creates discomfort for the occupants. The poor indoor climate conducts SBS 
which results in decreased performance efficiency. The symptoms of SBS may 
be due to the thermal environment and/or the IAQ. It is also reviewed that many 
buildings suffer from inadequate performance because the indoor climate has 
not been considered from the start when designing buildings. Other parameters 
that may have an impact on the IEQ are lightning and acoustics. To get a better 
understanding of the relationship between office work performance and indoor 
climate a literature search was made. It was found out that the biggest impact 
on the occupant performance was a poor indoor air and thermal environment 
which is the underlying factors of IAQ. The symptoms of a poor IAQ are SBS, 
thermal discomfort, accumulation of pollutants in buildings, which leads to a 
poor IEQ and poor occupant performance and health. It has been examined 
that a poor IAQ has the biggest impact of occupant performance. IAQ is 
measured by the concentration of CO2 in the air and ventilation is an important 
factor to limit the accumulation of pollutants level.  
7.2 
The method of investigation 
In the first part of the investigation, the method used for to identify a building's 
problems was done with a field study. Based on the study, it was possible to 
==PAGE:91==
Page 78 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
evaluate which renovation measures that have the biggest impact on energy 
consumption and the indoor climate. In the second part of the investigation, IDA 
ICE was used to simulate the current conditions of the reference building and 
the proposed renovation measures.  
 
The field study was based on analyzing building components such as building 
envelope, windows and shading, building leakages, internal heat gains and 
HVAC systems. Unfortunately, the information present about the above-
mentioned building parts was very superficial so assumptions were made such 
as the buildup of the building parts and heat loss through them. For future 
practice, it will be recommended that more information is available about the 
case building, so that you can get a more accurate picture of the existing 
conditions. The information that was extracted was used for a simulation with 
IDA ICE of the existing conditions, thereby perpetuating an inaccuracy and 
creating an inaccurate result of the indoor climate conditions and energy 
consumption. In addition, it has not been possible to investigate classifying the 
acoustic environment in the project as IDA ICE has limitations in terms of being 
able to simulate the noise levels. The noise level has a significant impact on the 
indoor climate and the well-being of users. Lastly, IDA ICE has limitations in 
terms of measuring draught ratings. To ensure that the thermal environment is 
complied with according to DS 7730, it is necessary to perform field 
measurement or using another simulation program, to assess the local comfort. 
7.3 
Case results  
The aim of the scenarios was to is to achieve a high building performance 
regarding improvement in the indoor environment and improvement in 
performance in office work, without increasing the energy consumption of the 
building. The scenarios include measures such as improvements to the building 
envelope and the addition of ventilation in the building. All scenarios were 
simulated with the simulation tool IDA ICE to extract information such as the 
building's energy consumption and indoor environmental quality. Three cases 
were formed; Case 1 was based on the reference building, where the current 
==PAGE:92==
Page 79 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
condition of the building is assed, Case 2 was based on achieving the Danish 
building regulations requirements according to BR18 and Case 3 was based on 
improving the indoor environmental quality and achieving category A in DS/EN 
ISO 7730 and category I in DS/EN 16798.  
 
Common to the renovation proposals was that they all achieved the set 
requirements. IEQ were improved while not increasing the total energy 
consumption of the building.In the reference case, the building's total energy 
consumption resulted in 161.6 kWh/m2. An implication of the high energy 
consumption is the possibility that the focus only was on simulating the most 
critical spaces. The rooms that were considered were the meeting room and the 
landscape office on the second floor. These rooms can be qualified as the most 
critical rooms for the building as they have the largest surface area to outdoors, 
which means that they have a large heat loss which cause a high heat 
consumption. If the entire building were simulated, there would be a good 
incentive for the total energy consumption per. m2 would be smaller. Regarding 
the thermal environment in the reference case, it can be observed that both 
zones have a significant problem with too low temperatures. Zone 2 has a 
minimum temperature of about 13 °C, which is 4 °C below the temperature 
limits for category IV. For zone 1, the temperature is approximately 2.5 °C 
below category IV. After a talk with an employee, it can be stated that the 
workplaces in the middle of both rooms on both floors, are challenged in the 
winter, where it can get cold. The low temperatures are due to the poor 
insulation capacity, furthermore a fault can be made regarding assessing the 
heating systems input values in IDA ICE. It is possible that the office building 
already had a night setback system which ensures higher temperature in the 
zones in the occupied hours. Considering the IAQ in the reference build, the 
results showed that zone 1 had a satisfactory CO2 concentration during the 
occupied hours. These results are likely to be related to the total window and 
opening area in relation to the floor area. In the calculation, an opening area of 
15% of the window area is assumed. In addition, a high natural air exchange 
with an infiltration rate of 1,5 l/s m2 must be included in the assumption due to 
==PAGE:93==
Page 80 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
the poorly insulated building parts. Zone 1 complies with the requirements for 
category I. Zone 2 shows high levels of CO2 concentration and complies with 
the requirements for category III. The high concentration can be due to the high 
occupancy load in the room. 
 
In case 2 the energy consumption was simulated to 151,5 kWh/m2 which 
corresponds to a reduction of 10,9 kWh/m2. This reduction are likely to be 
related by post insulating the external wall and the roof structure. Thermal 
environment category II were obtained by post insulating building parts, adding 
night setback, changing the operative temperature of the radiators, and 
increasing the performance of 2 of the radiators in zone 1. The insulation in the 
existing exterior walls was replaced and the insulation thickness was increased 
to meet the requirements for the U-value according to the Danish building 
regulation BR18. The U-value of the external wall and roof were reduced with 
90,2% and 33,3%. To obtain a minimum temperature of 20 °C in the occupied 
time, it was necessary to make sure that the building was constantly added heat 
throughout the week at 22 °C. This also meant that the operative temperature 
for the radiators had to be changed to 22°C so the radiators were at fully 
capacity when it dropped below the setpoint temperature. Finally, a satisfied 
thermal environment was achieved by increasing the performance of two 
radiators in zone 1. In case 1 the total effect from the radiators was 13468 W 
which is in this case increased to 15750 W. With the changes above thermal 
environment in the case 2 was significantly improved and the meet the 
requirements for category II while the temperature limits are in category I for 
84% and 89% of the occupied time. Regarding the indoor air quality, ventilation 
was added to have a satisfactory CO2 concentration which meet the 
requirements for category II. The maximum ventilation rate in the simulation 
resulted in 0.98 l/s pr. m2 and 3.37 l/s pr. m2 which corresponds to 10,1 l/s per. 
person. 
 
Case 3 examines what it takes to comply with category A in DS/EN ISO 7730 
and category I in DS/EN 16798. In order to achieve category I and obtain a 
==PAGE:94==
Page 81 of 94 
 
 
 
 
 
satisfactory thermal environment, an ideal cooler was added in both zones and 
the temperature setpoints for the water radiators were changed, as the 
temperatures in case 2 exceeded the temperature limits values for category I. 
Initially, an attempt was made to comply with the requirements without adding 
cooling because this would be a disadvantage for the total energy consumption 
for the building. The simulations showed that it was not possible to maintain the 
upper temperature limit, therefore an ideal cooler was added in each room 
remove the excess heat from both rooms. An ideal cooler is a room unit that 
cools the zone with when there is no detailed information about an actual room 
unit. The ideal cooler gives an estimation of how much capacity the cooler must 
have to reach a given setpoint temperature. In case 2, the set point temperature 
of the water radiators was set to 23 °C for both zones this was downregulated to 
22 °C for zone 1 and 21,5 °C for 2, so as not to exceed the upper temperature 
limit of 25.5 °C. To maintain the lower temperature limit, the applied heat output 
in the zones was increased from 17.750 W to 20.000 W, for zone 1 and in zone 
2 the applied heat output was increased from 3196 W to 4268 W, which is 
equivalent to inserting an extra radiator of the same size as the existing ones. 
The changes in the parameters above, led to a satisfactory thermal environment 
which complies with category A in DS/EN ISO 7730. In connection with the 
stricter requirements for air quality, it has been necessary to increase the 
amount of air supplied in the rooms to maintain the limit value for CO2 
concentration in the zones. This is in case 3 increased to 1.36 l/s pr. m2 in zone 
1 and 3.53 l/s pr. m2 in zone 2 which corresponds to 15,7 l/s per. person.

viable. The calculated investment cost showed that the annual cost of case 3 
was higher than the annual cost of case 2. To determine the lifetime savings 
that was considered in the cost-effective, net present value (NPV) formular was 
applied. It was observed that the highest lifetime savings is achieved from case 
3 which resulted in approximately 5,2 million DKK in a period of 30 years. For 
case 2 the lifetime savings is 3,4 million. Regarding the cost-effective factor, it 
wasn’t a surprise that case 3 had the highest factor of 3,9. Case 2 resulted in a 
factor 2,92.  
 
From this it is concluded that the most sustainable way to improve productivity 
of office workers in connection with an indoor climate renovation is with 
conducting case 3, which is based on improving the indoor environmental 
quality and achieving category A in DS/EN ISO 7730 and category I in DS/EN 
16798.
