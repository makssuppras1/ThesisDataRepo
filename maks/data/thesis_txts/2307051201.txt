ABSTRACT 
According to the World Health Organization (WHO) about 50% of the population does not 
meet the daily physical activity recommendations. Active travel is a cost-effective, easy, 
enjoyable and ecological manner to increase the daily exercise level. Therefore, we need 
viable information on promoting cycling as a sustainable transport-related physical activity 
in car-oriented societies. Transnational migration from car-oriented to cycling-friendly 
environments provides a unique opportunity to understand the motivators for adopting a 
bicycle culture. The move from a car-oriented into a cycling-friendly environment, can be 
viewed as a life-course intervention that allows experiencing a new mobility culture, and 
empowers individuals to make informed decisions, and engage in shared responsibility 
towards sustainable development by shifting their mode choice. Bicycle friendly 
infrastructure, low traffic volumes, and perceived safety are positively associated with 
cycling. Moreover, the growing interests in electrically assisted bikes provide a unique 
chance to investigate what would make Poles use it. A few studies indicate that other 
factors such as social norms, activity participation, monetary and time savings, as well as 
gaining self-confidence and self-esteem,  may play a role in the decision to cycle. Therefore, 
cycling as a mode choice is likely related to the ability to cater for people’s functional and 
emotional needs.   
 
This study focuses on the role of existence, relatedness and personal growth in adapting to 
a cycling-oriented culture upon transnational migration from a car-oriented country. The 
study seeks to test the following hypotheses: (1) people cycle when cycling fits their needs; 
(2) adapting to a new travel culture, in this case-study from a car-oriented to a cycling-
oriented culture, is motivated by higher-order emotional needs; (3) if an individual would 
start to cycle in one country, he or she will continue this behavior in other places; (4) using 
electric bike-sharing systems instead of regular ones (at the same fare) is motivated by 
lower-order needs rather than higher-order needs. 
 
The development of a conceptual framework on the basis of Alderfer's ERG theory as a 
motivator for cycling willingness and intensity. Cycling willingness and intensity comprise 
of bicycle ownership and use of the bike-sharing system, cycling frequency, cycling 
distance, cycling purpose (utilitarian versus leisure), participation in the bike-to-work 
==PAGE:4==
 
3
campaign, feeling of well-being and enthusiasm related to cycling. Existence lower-order 
needs are time and cost savings, increased mobility, travel independence, activity 
participation, enlarging the opportunity space, exercise, health, caregiving and establishing 
daily-life routines. Nevertheless, existence also means coping with difficulties related to 
perceived safety, adapting to the road rules, weather conditions, lack of cycling experience 
and skills, and physical ability. Relatedness needs are social inclusion, place attachment, 
forming social networks, peer-acceptance, conforming to social norms. Personal growth 
needs are self-esteem, self-confidence, cultural identity, self-identity as a cyclist, and self-
actualization on environmental sustainability and fitness. The framework is complemented 
by individual characteristics, namely age, gender, education, income, travel habits and car 
/ bicycle availability in the origin country and after migration, migration duration, 
employment sector, residential location and its type, distance to work / university, driver’s 
license availability, structure of ICT social networks, temporary versus permanent 
relocation, and societal integration in terms of language and cultural familiarity. 
 
Web-based questionnaires were designed to collect data for the analysis. The attitudinal 
items were measured on a 5-point Likert scale. The first survey was administered to a 
sample of 1,200 Polish immigrants in Denmark, Netherlands, Germany, United Kingdom 
and Sweden. The second survey was conducted among a sample of 1,000 young people in 
three Polish cities: Gorzów Wielkopolski, Poznań, and Szczecin. 
 
Structural equations models (SEM) were employed for modeling cycling intensity. These 
models are particularly useful in accommodating the latent nature attitudinal constructs 
with the observed nature of travel patterns and socio-economic characteristics. The results 
show that higher-order emotional needs are associated with higher willingness to adapt 
from a car-oriented to a new cycling-friendly environment, and greater cycling intensity in 
terms of frequency and distance. The insights can be employed to encourage the gradual 
transition from car-oriented societies towards establishing a prominent cycling culture 
through attractive, creative and non-extreme solutions.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Changing the travel behavior of a population has become the challenge of our times. According 
to data from 2011, a half of all car trips in EU-15 is shorter than 6 km, which is a distance that 
can easily be cycled (European Environment Agency 2011). Therefore, it becomes essential 
to investigate what exactly motivates people to change.  
Assuming a decrease by 30% of car journeys below 6 km and change to bicycle use instead, 
we would note a 4% reduction in emissions of CO2 from road traffic. A commuter who use a 
bicycle instead car to reach 8 km distance to work, four days a week, avoids 3,220 km of car 
driven kilometers a year, which is the equivalent of 380l. of saved petrol and 0,75t of CO2 
emissions avoided (European Cyclist Federation 2009). 
 
The central question then becomes: how might the Alderfer’s ERG motivation theory (1969) 
affect the way in which migrants adapt to the new environment after the residential change. 
In the view of recent discovering, investigation of what exactly motivate people to change their 
travel habits and with which level of ERG model is it related growth the importance. This study 
aims to analyze five migrant’s community with Polish origin with current habitation in 
Denmark, The Netherlands, Germany, United Kingdom and Sweden in terms of their 
experiences with travel habits change after migration to different transport culture.  Rietveld 
and Daniel (2004) provided a meaningful example of the importance of this study. In their 
research about migrants in The Netherland, they showed that due to the different cultural 
background, newcomers prefer to use public transport or a car and are unlikely to cycle in the 
Netherlands. Therefore, it is interesting to investigate how would Polish migrants behave once 
facing different traffic conditions.  
 
This research consists of two combined studies. Electrically assisted bicycles are a convenient 
and environmentally friendly mode of transport. Moreover, are significantly cheaper than a 
car. The operational cost of the electric motor is lower than the engine, generates less noise, 
does not generate vibration and does not emit fumes. Due to that, the sale of e-bikes increases 
across Europe. However, share in Poland is still among lowest. Modern experience of cycling 
might result in higher interest in e-bikes. However, the scholar research about e-cycling is still 
insufficient and need more attention. Therefore, the second study would apply the same 
behavioral framework to investigate which needs might motivate people to use electric bikes.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW  
The following discussion introduces the theoretical background of the research. The first 
section describes existing theories about cycling benefits. Then, a scholar background about 
cycling intention is introduced in the second section. The third and fourth section provide a 
comprehensive analysis of the cycling phenomenon in places that are (1) car-oriented and (2) 
cycle-oriented. The fifth section fifth focuses on the relation between cycling and citizenship. 
Then, the context of migration is discussed in the sixth section. The last part of this chapter 
discusses the application of behavior theories that apply to this study from several 
perspectives. 
2.1  Cycling benefits 
Worldwide, every second person does not meet the recommendations for health-enhancing 
physical activity (WHO 2009). According to global recommendations, at least 60 minutes of 
psychical activity per day is required (WHO 2011). Therefore, transport-related physical 
activity might be seen as an easy and ecological way to increase daily physical activity. 
Physical activity has a direct effect on well-being and health and benefits from physical activity 
for health are obvious. However, nowadays people lead to reflections on how to make room 
in their lives to do sport. Therefore, active transport modes and using it as exercise time, seem 
to be the most reasonable and rational way to do this. Bauman et al. (2011), who indicate 
cycling as easy, cheap, widely spread and accessible solution for physical inactivity, support 
this hypothesis. Moreover, transport-related activity is easier in principle to adopt into a daily 
life routine and maintain than other forms of physical activity. As a result, adults who cycle 
regularly will have a body condition equivalent to being ten years younger. Also, their life 
expectancy will be two years above average (European Cyclist Federation 2009).  
  
Obviously, outcomes from cycling depend on intensity and frequency. So far, several studies, 
including Oja et al. (2011), have shown that cycling as a transport mode requires intensity 
that positively influences metabolic and cardiovascular system condition. Other research 
provided by Oja et al. (1991) found a dependency between maximal oxygen consumption 
(VO2max), which reflects the aerobic physical fitness with cycling. They found a 7% increase 
in VO2max after ten weeks of usage cycling as commuting mode for 3,75 days per week at 
approximately 65% VO2max. Research made by Hendriksen et al. (2000) is in line with Oja et 
al.’s findings and emphasizes the effects of commuting by cycling for six months at least three 
==PAGE:11==
 
10
times per week at approximately 60% VO2 max which resulted in a significant increase in 
men’s health.  
 
Bicycle traffic is not only environmentally friendly and influences the livability of the cities 
positively, but most of all it is good for health. Physical activity reduces the risk of 
cardiovascular disease, obesity, diabetes type 2 and a variety of other chronic conditions 
including some cancers. The strongest evidence in literature exists for colon cancer, whose 
risk of might is reduced by 40-50% due to regular physical activity. A study conducted in 
Shanghai (2014) found that regular commuting cycling in a long run protects from colon 
cancer and significantly modifies the BMI-associated risk (Hou et al. 2004). Recent studies 
also found the association between physical activity and the risk of breast cancer. Women who 
cycle regularly have the risk of breast cancer reduced by 34%. The relationship between 
cycling and risk of cancer is simple: as the amount of cycling increased, the risk of cancer 
decreased (Steinford et al. 2003, pp.157). Diabetes type 2 is currently the most common 
metabolic disorder worldwide. Physical inactivity is a major risk factor for the development 
of such kind disorder and increases the risk even by 50%. Literature found that cycling is 
associated with reduced relative risk for diabetes especially among men (Lynch 1996). 
Cycling contributes to weight reduction and stimulates fat metabolism by burning energy. 
Obviously, the extent to which cycling contributes to weight loss depends on diet and the 
amount and intensity of cycling. 
 
The immune system also gains from regular cycling. It protects the body from infections and 
diverse systemic diseases. Studies show that cycling as a moderate activity contributes 
significantly to a healthy life and strengthens the immune system.  
 
It is largely evaluated that cycling provides not only traffic benefits such as flexible mobility 
or support for multimodal transport connections, but also health benefits. Cycling also has a 
positive effect on emotional health – improves self-confidence, increases levels of well-being 
and stress tolerance, and reduces difficulties with sleep and tiredness (Byond et al. 1998). 
Cycling has a considerable relaxing effect. The physical and emotional functions of the body 
are stabilized thanks to cycling, which counteracts, depression anxiety and other 
psychological problems. The exercise and physical activity also control hormonal balance 
(Cavill and Davis 2007, pp.29).

Cycling affects physical body shape and attractiveness, which influences the perception of 
others, therefore influence individual’s well-being. Cycling can help with controlling body 
weight and muscular form. Moreover, skin benefits from the metabolic processes that are 
stimulated during physical activity as cycling.   
 
Cycling provides also greater independence for children (as well as for adults who do not have 
a driving license). Even though studies have not proved yet an existence of a direct link 
between physical activity and better academic performance, it might be indeed expected. 
Increased capacity for learning might be linked to mental health, which gains from physical 
activity. Cycling as physical activity can also help to increase adolescents’ self-esteem and 
reduces anxiety and stress (Sallis et al. 1999). Moreover, sport promotes skills such as 
teamwork, leadership, socialization, and self-discipline. Research has also shown that young 
people who participate in interscholastic sports are more likely to avoid drugs and being a 
smoker (Escobedo et al. 1993). The importance of setting good habits at an early age is 
essential and frequently cites in the literature. Dill and Voros (2007) concluded that cycling 
in childhood increases the likelihood of cycling as an adult. 
Also, elderly people might gain a lot from cycling. Physical activity contributes to reducing 
musculoskeletal pain and reduces the risk of diseases that might affect the ability to live 
independently. Cycling would improve coordination, body balance and strength, which are 
significant factors that might reduce the risk of fall. Also, flexibility and reaction time are 
improved thanks to training and are associated with the reduction in the risk of falls (Bruce 
et al. 2005). 
 
Cycling is an efficient and fast urban transport mode, requires less space than motor vehicle 
traffic, more mobile and has a wider accessibility than cars. To some extent, might be faster 
than motorized vehicles. The relation between the kilometers driven in a car to the time spent 
in a car and the additional time spent working to pay for its total running costs gives the 
average speed of a car to be 8 km/h.1

The modal shift from motorized transportation to cycling and others active commuting, would 
results in less congestion and a reduction in carbon dioxide, which is a major objective for 
many countries and will still gain with importance in nearly future (Shephard 2008, 
Woodstock et al. 2009). Air pollution has a direct impact not only on human health (lead to 
respiratory illnesses) but it also harmful for biodiversity (death of vegetation that leads to 
lower yields), built environment (surface corrosion), climate and cultural heritage (erosion of 
public buildings and landmarks). Only in the capital of Denmark, cycling reduces carbon 
emissions by 110,000 tons per year (Regional Cycling Report, pp.14). 
 
In the “Valuing the benefits of cycling- A Report to Cycling England” (2007) we can find that 
transportation demand management (encouraging the use of alternative modes of transport) 
is one of the ways to relieved congestion. It is widely recognized that cycling plays a prominent 
role in sustainable transport. The second one indicates the capacity of road infrastructure 
increase which is a significant incoherence since it is well known that the capacity increase 
leads to demand increase and therefore- congestion arise even more. This point is supported 
by Litman (2001) who argues that road capacity expansion often delivers fewer benefits than 
is expected and generated traffic often fills the new capacity. This argument proves that 
alternative modes to motorized vehicles, such as cycling, are the most appropriate way to 
make cities more livable by reducing congestion. Denmark proves that the significance of 
cycling in terms of traffic flow and congestion is enormous. The number of citizens cycling 
makes 112,000 fewer trips by car only during rush hour per day, which accounts for 660,000 
fewer kilometers driven by car (Regional Cycling Report, pp.13). The study conducted by 
Hamiltion and Wichman (2015) indicates that the presence of bike share docks leads to 2-3% 
reduction in traffic congestion.  
 
Even though cyclists are exposed to increased inhalation of oxidant smog, the air inside a car 
can be more polluted than the outside air breathed by the cyclist in the same place. In fact, it 
contains twice more CO2 as well as approximately 50% more nitrogen oxides, which clearly 
additionally influence the user’s health (Dekoster and Schollaert 1999, pp.13). The reason 
might be that the air intake of the cars is close to the exhaust tube of the car in front. A clear 
prove is provided by estimations by Hartog et al. (2010) that, due to increased physical 
activity, life expectancy increase by 3-14 months whereas the decrease life expectancy due to 
increased exposition to air pollution was 0.8-40.

Indirect benefits of cycling on health have also been investigated in the literature. More 
cycling, especially as an alternative to motor vehicle traffic, brings substantial health benefits 
for society as a whole, which is an effect of reduced noise and danger, improved air quality. 
The positive health effects from cycling, translated into money saved for societal and 
economic benefits of cycling, are indeed significant. In Denmark, each kilometer cycled instead 
driven in a car, gives the state of Denmark saving of 0,8 €. With 5,700,000 residents cycling 
average 1,5 km a day, Danish government saves daily 6,840,000€, mostly on health care costs 
(Cycling Embassy of Denmark’s 2015). Since regular physical activity promotes health and 
helps prevent disease, it decreases health care costs. The general social benefits from cycling 
are therefore significant. Also, employers can benefit. Physical activity programs related to the 
workplace can increase productivity by 2-52%, reduce short-term sick leave by 6-23% and 
reduce total health care costs by 20-55% (Freudenheim 1999). A study conducted by 
Andersen in 2000 on a sample of Danish adults shows that cycling to work is positively related 
to health. People who cycle to work have a 28% lower mortality rate. 
 
2.2 Cycling barriers  
Despite the many aforementioned advantages of cycling, only a minority of the world’s 
population is actually cycling for transport purposes. This is related to a variety of factors. It 
is widely discussed in the literature that, especially in the urban environment, there are many 
barriers to cycling, some relating to a lack of physical infrastructure, and some being 
perceptual or psychological (Bauman et al., 2008, Daley et al. 2007). 
 
A well-known fact is that the risk of injury or death in traffic crash strongly discourages people 
from cycling. (Pucher and Buehler 2006; Parkin et al. 2007). Injuries related to cycling have 
been discussed alongside a debate on mandatory helmet laws (de Jong 2012). Also, the 
benefit-to-risk ratio for cycling vs. injuries is overwhelmingly positive, as found in several 
studies (Bauman and Rissel 2009). Especially women tend to give their security as a reason 
for not cycling. (Daley and Rissel 2011, Vandenbulcke et al. 2011). Lack of safety (strongly 
associated with the lack of infrastructure) and slopes are perceived as strong obstacles for 
commuting by bicycle in the city. Therefore, insufficient infrastructure, especially in areas 
where cars are perceived as easier and more convenient to use (and hence with car-oriented 
==PAGE:15==
 
14
cultures), is often a crucial deterrent that limits cycling. (Heinen, et al., 2010; Kingham, et al., 
2011).  
 
Distance is a considerable barrier that limits cycling (Kingham et al., 2001; Dickinson et al., 
2003). An increase in cycling distance, hence also in travel time, results in lower cycling mode 
shares. According to Vandenbulcke et al. (2011), only people who live up to 5 km from their 
workplaces would ever consider cycling to work. However, wide research in the literature 
provides the view that accepted maximum distance is more complex and depends mostly on 
gender. Men usually cycle longer commuter distances than women (Pucher and Buehler 
2008). Other significant factors that might influence the acceptance of the maximum cycling 
distance might be ethnicity, city, and country where cycling is performed (Pucher et al. 2010). 
People that are interested in cycling to work are these living in close proximity to their 
workplaces. However, in terms of attractiveness, cycling to work is highly limited by 
organizational aspects such like dress code and availability of facilities in the workplace: 
showers, cycle racks, changing rooms (Heinen et al., 2009). Moreover, also town size might 
play a key role. Smaller cities perform better simply because they contain fewer barriers (e.g. 
high speed limits, motorways). Traffic density is also lower, and road conditions (speed limits, 
reduced traffic) are in favor to cyclist and influence safety (Pucher et al., 1999).  
Unsuitable weather, issues with transporting large goods and the physical effort needed might 
be discouraging. Short-term (weather) and long-term (climatic) factors like high or low 
temperatures, intense and frequent rain, strong winds as well as topography are well-known 
deterrents to cycling (Heinen et al., 2010, Parkin et al., 2008). Physical abilities, which often 
deepened with age, might also be an important factor in the choice to cycle. Some cycling 
conditions increase the physical effort required decrease the comfort and cause 
inconvenience.  
Barriers to cycling are embedded in the individual and social perception. This, on the other 
hand, is highly about particular experiences and therefore subjective. Pooley et al. (2011) 
highlight the effects social and personal barriers to utilitarian cycling uptake, emphasizing this 
high subjectivity as well as that they affect individuals to varying extents. The main issues are 
still the lack of infrastructure and safety, but it is worth to mention also these perceptual or 
psychological problems. Interestingly, these barriers vary between people and are depend on 
==PAGE:16==
 
15
the individual experience of cycling. Daley and Rissel (2011) found that these barriers are not 
static and vary from person to person and their experiences of cycling. Cycling might be 
perceived by some people as more inconvenient and unpleasant than other transport modes. 
There are found differences between experienced and inexperienced cyclists in literature 
(Heinen et al. 2010). Topography, for instance, can be interpreted very differently between 
these who cycle regularly and these who do not.  
Relatively little research has been conducted about how the perception of cycling and cyclists 
might influence people to ride or not ride. However, it is found so far there are many “social 
barriers” especially in places, where cycling is not popular. Skinner and Rosen (2007) 
investigate that the public images of cycling and possibility of being perceived as “foolish”, 
“hazardous”, “thoughtless” might discourage a non-rider’s choice of whether to use a bicycle. 
Also, Horton (2007) indicate the fear of being embarrassed in public space and the feeling of 
vulnerability on the bicycle. It is important to be aware that there are many types of cycling 
each kind is related with unique features. Horton (2007) also differentiates many different 
kinds of cycling: recreational, utility, commuting, racing, mountain biking, touring and hence, 
different kinds of experiences and potential barriers for users and non-users. Research has 
found that people, who do cycle, perceive barriers to be significantly lower than those who do 
not (Koorey, et al., 2009).  
 
2.3  Cycling intensions 
Current policies toward cycling promotion are focused mostly on transport demand 
management and on factors that affect bicycle use (Pucher et al. 2010). Moving forward in 
exploring more about the factors that affect the willingness to use the bike seems, therefore, 
imperative. Pucher (2010) also emphasizes that cyclist mode choice is to be analyzed by 
considering a larger than usual number of variables, which is also neatly confirmed by 
Fernandez-Heredia et al. (2016). In order to understand and model bicycle choice properly, it 
is necessary to identify and quantify precisely these variables as well as explore the way in 
which are perceived by the user. The very recent research made by Fernandez-Heredia et al. 
(2016) indicates users’ perceptions as a key element to understanding bicycle use.  
Fernandez-Heredia et al. (2016) introduce so two main issues that need to be solved: (1) 
improvement of identification of those factors; and (2) establishment of the way that would 
let investigate these factors effectively towards their future operational use. They have also 
==PAGE:17==
 
16
grouped elements that influence bicycle use into four main categories: (1) users’ 
characteristics, (2) trip characteristics, (3) environmental context, and (4) facilities and 
subjective perceptions. 
There is a strong background about the importance of cycling intentions and the importance 
of modal shift due to the necessity of facing nowadays mobility, environmental and health 
problems. Motivation to cycling as well as the benefits associated with cycling were widely 
investigated in the literature (WHO 2009, Parkin and Horton 2012, Horton et al. 2007).  
As Heinen and Handy (2012) stated, developing the knowledge of attitudes towards cycling 
as a mode of transport is essential for increasing bicycle use. They emphasize the need for 
differentiation among cycling behavior and attitudes across cities as required tool for effective 
strategies in promoting bicycle use. 
 “Mobility Paradigm” investigated by Urry (2007) emphasize the need of rethink the way in 
which we perceive transport and focus on movement process itself and what happens during 
it rather than what is preceded by. According to this perspective (Spinney, 2006), cycling is a 
sensory experience. Those kinds of experiences might be correlated with the fulfillment of 
human needs. It seems interesting to investigate to which extent cycling might affect these 
phenomena and both: (1) how influences the needs, and (2) how needs influence the cycling, 
if so.   
Passafaro et al. (2014) stated that there had been several studies conducted which aimed to 
analyze the main social and psychological factors affecting the spread of sustainable mobility 
in terms of urban planning, but most of them are focused on public transportation. There is 
not much known about emotional, social and psychological factors that might influence the 
mode choice. Also, Murtagh et al. (2012) studied the social identities associated with certain 
activities such as cycling.  
However, recent research by Lois et al. (2015) provided evidence for the existence of an 
association between cycling self-efficacy and social identity. Their foundlings go along with 
other studies: e.g. Murtagh et al. (2012), Steg et al. (2001) and clearly indicate that intention 
to use cycling for commuting is strongly related to identifying oneself as “a cyclist”. Their 
model also indicates that social identity is a significant predictor of cycle commuting intention. 
Noteworthy is the fact that they found a negative correlation between cycling for sport and

2.4 Cycling in car-oriented countries  
Over the past decades, cars and motorized vehicles rapidly spread around the World and 
developed from a luxury to a basic good in most developed countries. Despite representing a 
revolution in mobility and convenience (although it is getting less and less adequate due to 
rising congestion), cars has numerous of negative impacts on the society, environment, safety, 
health and traffic patterns. Even though cycling is emerging trend in Europe, beside few 
countries, who developed impressive cycling culture, things required more effort and 
research. The environmental and social impact produced by car have gained special attention. 
Currently, a half of European Union inhabitants use a car daily. However, there a significant 
disproportion among UE’s members. According to the survey conducted by European 
Commission, Europeans are more than twice less likely to cycle daily than to use the car. The 
sad true is that although we try to create Europe’s major cities friendly more for people than 
for cars, the scope of car-dependency in two of the densest European urban centers — London 
and Berlin — remain significant (Rode et al. 2015). 
An overwhelming majority of citizens in the European Union (81%) notice the problem of air 
pollution, noise (73%), road congestion (76%) and accidents (73%) and believe that these 
are important problems within cities. Interestingly, the third of Europeans believe that they 
are responsible for reducing traffic themselves. The majority (56%) believe that city 
authorities should be mainly in charge of this issue (European Commission 2013, p.5).  
The real cost of the journey made by car is actually covered by the driver only for 25%.2 In 
charge of the remaining 75% is the society. Cars are responsible for even 25-65% of air 
pollution in the urban areas (European Commission, 2014). Motorized vehicle usage is 
responsible in fact for the costs of road construction and maintenance, parking provision, 
traffic light implementation and maintenance, environmental externalities, building and 
monument renovation because of smog damages, maintenance of the administration of the 
safety of transport, struggle with noise and building a sound-absorbing barriers, maintenance 
of emergency services, congestion, treatment of injured in accidents and other many other 
economic costs. Even though the number of accident decreased by 43% in the past ten years,

In countries dominated by cars, commuting by cycling often suffers from unfavorable image

However, an effort has been made to promote alternative transport modes like cycling and 
walking, which is aligned with Poland's transport development strategy until 2020. For the 
years 2014-2020 there is €375,5 million dedicated to the needs of cycling as a transport mode.  
So far, there have been 18,000 kilometers of tourism cycling routes, and the total length of

transport mode to commute to work / school at any circumstances (OBOP, 1999). 
 
As part of the project Miasta dla rowerów (Eng. Cities for Bicycles), professional public 
opinion research on the attitudes of Poles toward bicycle was performed twice. Results show 
the bike is mainly used for utilitarian purposes, and it is a mass phenomenon, although 
primarily concerned with small towns and villages. Furthermore, Poles believe that bicycle - 
contrary to popular opinion – is resistant to the weather conditions and time of year. 12% 
OBOP (Ośrodek Badania Opinii Publicznej) survey respondents declared that they use the 
bike in the winter (mainly in rural areas). The research clearly shows that the cycling in 
Poland has huge underestimated potential, especially because over 24% of surveyed people 
stated that bicycle is their “ideal mode of transport”. Moreover, considering a second mode

studies also clearly indicate that lack of adequate infrastructure is the primary reason due to 
why people in Poland do not use bicycles. Approximately 25% of the survey respondents 
declared that the lack of bicycle paths is the main barrier for them to cycle (Polski Klub 
Ekologiczny, 2000). 
 
The current condition of infrastructure in Poland leaves a lot to be desired. Systems of cycle 
paths, first of all, are inconsistent. There is a significant lack of continuity of cycle paths, and 
implementation of new ones is often quite random due to lack of clear priorities. The route of

designed or unjustified. Simply, for a while, bicycle paths were built when it was easy, not 
where it was demanded. The applicable technical parameters are often incompatible with the 
rules, and logic and design in many cases are contrary to the interests of cycling (e.g. obstacles 
on the route, faults caused by too high curbs, too sharp bends, failures of traffic lights, the 
presence of the stairs on the cycling path).  
The general condition of the surface is generally poor (uneven surfaces, poor pavement 
durability, lack of routine maintenance, improper construction technology). The significant 
barrier is also an omnipresent lack of supporting facilities on the streets but also in the

accompanying infrastructure. The authorities explained that by the deficit of money and a long

paths in many cases is not consistent. Few examples are provided below. Report from 2011

funding on a multimillion scale since it requires its future reconstruction (Lubaczewska, 
2011).

Manchester, Newcastle and Sheffield. Noteworthy, in Cambridge 29% of its working residents 
cycle-commute, which is the highest rate in the UK (Census Analysis 2011).

2.5 Cycling in cycle-oriented countries  
The benefits of increasing the modal share of active transport are well researched (Davis 
2010; de Hartog et al., 2010; Oja et al., 2011). Some cities around the world are making a 
concerted effort to increase a bicycle usage trough successful policy making. Some of the cities, 
including Amsterdam, Copenhagen and a number of German towns and cities, lead the way in 
Europe. Currently, 12% of Europeans use a bicycle daily (European Commission 2013). 
 
Transport culture differs from country to country. Denmark and The Netherlands are 
considered “cycling nations” and cycling culture there is highly developed with the basis in 
history. The Danish and Dutch populations strongly identify themselves with cycling. Cycle-
friendly infrastructure and association of cycling as part of a national symbol help them 
overcome the omnipresent mass motorized traffic. It is inherent that the bicycle is an 
important element of traffic and therefore has its own space in road construction but, what is 
even more essential, equally important a place in people’s minds. Denmark and The 
==PAGE:25==
 
24
Netherlands, in contrast to most countries, increase cycle traffic flow every year (Carstensen 
and Ebert 2012). However, it is doubtful that either of these countries would have much better 
cycling statistic that others if there have not been made a significant effort to promote cycling 
throughout the years. Apart from the Netherlands and Denmark, the literature indicates also 
Germany as a cycling-friendly country (Pucher and Buehler 2008). Successful policy results 
that Denmark and The Netherlands, despite still a large car mode share, have a cycle trip share 
of approximately 17 and 27% respectively (Pucher and Buehler 2008).  
 
In the Netherlands, cycling is a prevalent mode of transport. Over 31% of the people use the 
bike as their primary mode of transport for daily activities, and cycling has a 27% modal share 
in all trips nationwide. Significantly high modal share for cycling is enabled by 
excellent cycling infrastructure but also by making cycling routes shorter, more convenient 
and more direct than routes for the car (Pucher and Buehler, 2008). The average Dutch cycles 
2.9 km a day. The total length of cycle paths in Netherlands is estimated at 35,000 km. Up to 
21% of sold bikes are electric which is a rise by 10% from 2008. In total, there were 1,400,000 
electric bikes in 2014 (Ministerie van Verkeer en Waterstaat 2009, pp.11). Approximately half 
of primary school pupils cycle to and from school (only 14% are brought and collected by car). 
Cycling share is even higher in the secondary school. 90% of schools’ students and individual 
children participate in the theoretical traffic examination. Average distance cycled per 
teenager per year is 2,000 km (twice more than per average person). Moreover, 25% of trips 
to work are made by bike. In terms of economy, Dutch spend a lot of money on their bicycles 
– nearly 1 billion € a year (Statistics Netherlands 2015). 
 
Although in Germany the level of car ownership is much higher than in the UK, for instance, 
the share of the bike is almost ten times higher in Germany than in the UK. German 
government devotes €100 million yearly for further bikeway extensions, cycling research and 
cycling related projects. Moreover, about €2 billion a year (revenues from the motor fuel tax) 
are earmarked for a special urban transport investment fund. This provides 70–85% federal 
matching funds for state and local governments aiming to build new cycling facilities. There 
are 70,000 km of cycle paths in Germany, and 9% of all trips are made by bike. The average 
length of a journey by bicycle in Berlin is 3,7 km. However, only 19% of bicycle trips are longer 
than 5 km. Cycling is ingrained in the German culture. German people have grown up riding a 
bike and still even the older generation cycle,- this makes drivers and pedestrians aware of

cyclist existence on the streets as well as the understanding and respect for them. The modal 
share of cycling for all trips only in Berlin was 13% in 2008. Berliners make 1,5 million cycle 
journeys a day, and the city aims to increase the share to 18-20% by 2025 (Pucher and Buehler 
2008). Interestingly, Hamburg has announced plans to become entirely car-free within the 
next two decades.3 
 
Denmark and its capital Copenhagen are the prime examples of well-developed bike 
infrastructure and brilliant policy-making in past years, aiming to be the best city for cycling 
in the world. That was, indeed, evaluated in the Copenhagenize Design Company’s ranking in 
2015. Cycling is not only a transport mode there; it is a significant part of the lifestyle in 
Denmark. Cycling accounts there for 17% of all trips and represents 4% of total traffic in 
kilometers. Only in Copenhagen, there is over 450 km of bike lanes, which are used to make 
1,240,000 km every day. Since 1990, the number of cyclists in Danish capital has risen by 70%. 
A fourth of all commuter trips is made with usage of the bicycle, and only in 2013 there were 
almost 500,000 sold, of which 18,100 electrical ones (Cycling Embassy of Denmark’s 2015). 
According to Danish Technical University's Travel Survey (2014) number of utilitarian cyclist 
increased rapidly between 2012 and 2013 from 36% to 45% and the car's modal share fell at 
the same time from 27% to 23% (Christiansen 2012). 
 
The key role in policy making in these countries plays the barriers and limitation for cars in 
the cities. Interestingly, construction of 1 km of Danish highway is 100 times more expensive 
than 1 km of the Danish bicycle highway (Cycling Embassy of Denmark’s 2015). Instead of 
expanding road infrastructure and parking facilities, policy is permanently focused on making 
cities people-friendly and livable rather than car-friendly. There are numerous of examples of 
such kind of policies.  The prime example is the Green Wave for cyclists in Copenhagen. 
Established for the first time in 2007 has spread quickly for the major intersection in the city. 
In the rush hours, the traffic lights are adjusting to the speed of 20 km/h, which allows cyclists 
to hit green lights all the way - and what goes along with this - make car traffic more 
inconvenient. Interestingly, the average speed of cyclist in Copenhagen is 16 km/h. A wave of 
20 km/h leads therefore to increase the cycling vigor, which leads to increased health benefits, 
and decrease the speed of those who cycle fast and therefore positively influence the safety. 
What is even more attractive, Copenhagen is currently testing a pilot project called “Green

Most residential streets in Dutch, Danish, and German cities have implemented traffic-calming 
and reduced the maximum speed limit to 30 km/h. Sometimes it imposes even more 
restrictions and requires cars to travel at walking speed. The examples from Germany, 
Denmark, and The Netherlands prove that often it is neither possible nor necessary to provide 
separate bike paths, especially on streets with relatively low traffic flow. There are also 
significant alterations to the street design itself: speed humps, raised intersections and 
crosswalks, extra curves and zigzag routes road narrowing. A very popular tool is also an 
artificial dead-end, created by mid-block street closures (e.g. by a concrete obstacle). 
Moreover, cities create extensive car-free zones in their downtowns (e.g. Copenhagen, 
Amsterdam, Muenster), intended them mainly for pedestrians but during off-peak hours also 
permitting cycling. In Dutch and German cities, we might also observe so-called “bicycle-
streets” which give absolute traffic priority over the entire width of the street for cyclists. This 
idea is popular in Muenster (Germany) were in 2007 were already 12 streets this kind and 
the concept has been so successful that the city plans to extent it in the nearly future. (Pucher 
and Buehler 2008).  
 
Infrastructure design is a key factor in such high cycle share. In cycling-oriented cities such as 
Amsterdam or Copenhagen, the signs that cycling is highly popular are findable everywhere. 
Highly visible bike lanes, often colored on intersections, turn restrictions for cars, additional 
traffic lights for cyclists or a large number of free bike parking facilities are only a few 
examples. Parking facilities are an important factor in increasing the cycling share since they 
increase the potential of multimodal trips. In Dutch, Danish and German cities, every main 
transport nodes like train stations, are equipped with a large number of cycling racks and 
indoor parking distributed along entire rail network. Moreover, even if generally most rail 
systems charge for taking the bike on board, the Copenhagen S-tog rail system (city light rail) 
provides the necessary facilities in the trains and therefore allows people to move easily with 
their bike.  
 
The reason for a high share of cycling is perhaps that cycling there is much safer than 
anywhere else. This is significantly linked especially for share among children, women and 
elderly, appear to be especially sensitive to traffic danger (Garrard et al., 2008). Thanks to the 
general perception of safety, cycling is popular in Denmark, Germany, and The Netherlands 
among all age groups.

Also, traffic laws are for cyclist. The Netherlands, Denmark, and Germany give special 
attention to the especially vulnerable situation of cyclists vs. motor and require the drivers to 
make special efforts to avoid potentially dangerous situations. Furthermore, most collisions 
with cyclists are assumed responsible for car drivers unless it can be proven that the cyclist 
consciously caused the crash. Having the right of way by law does not excuse motorists from 
hitting cyclists, especially children and elderly cyclists. The police and courts in the 
Netherlands, Denmark and Germany, strictly enforce the law. In combination with rigorous 
and comprehensive drivers’ and cyclists’ training, the strict enforcement of traffic laws 
significantly contributes to safer traffic conditions, which is beneficial both for cyclist and 
drivers.  
 
2.6 Cycling & citizenship 
Transport habits might be indeed interpreted as a part of culture, hence also citizenship. In 
places, where cycling is a strong element of culture and belongs to lifestyle (e.g. Copenhagen), 
it might influence the place attachment. Intense bicycle flow and its omnipresence for new 
residences might be overwhelming in the first exposure, but identifying oneself with such 
essential element of daily life routine is related with the adaptation in new conditions that 
migrants need to face. 
==PAGE:31==
 
30
There is a lack of research about transport habits and citizenship, especially in relation to 
cycling. Several explanations have been offered. According to research made by Horton 
(2007), cycling might have a broad range of social and cultural meanings, therefore also be 
related to the citizenship. Urry (2007) highlighted that facing two major global transport 
trends, which are (1) increase in distance traveled; and (2) increasing dominance of 
automobiles, transport and citizenship are indeed linked.  
Urry (2007) also emphasizes the effects of motorization patterns on social interaction on a 
local level. Omnipresent cars and motorized vehicle and increasing levels of its particular use, 
create the problem of congestion, air pollution and noise (Stanley et al. 2011). Moreover, car-
oriented cultures influence the social exclusion as well as progressive committing societies to 
the spiral of ever more intense car use (Jain and Guiver 2010). Sennett (1974) in his study on 
the case of New York described the phenomenon of „dead space“. He suggests that places 
deprived of the street (social) life which also occurs due to the domination of intense car flow 
on busy roads and surrounding areas have a deeply negative impact on public life, hence also 
on citizenship. Also, Freund and Martin (2007) and Garrard et al. (2012) emphasized the 
problem of car-demanding infrastructure and its outperformance upon pedestrians, cyclists, 
and public transportation needs, which according to Sennett (1974) influence the citizenship 
and place attachment. Even though Wickham (2006) investigated a link between citizenship 
and transport concerning public transport and private vehicles, therefore: motorized modes, 
his study might be relevant also to cycling. He indicated that reducing public space leads to a 
lack of social cohesion. He also proved that motorized road transport indeed destroys public 
space in a physical way. 
These explanations complement each other and help to illuminate the phenomenon of cycling 
being strongly associated with a sense of place and identity, whereas these are tightly related 
to existence, relatedness and growth needs. Given this evidence for the importance of 
citizenship and place attachment, an examination of the factors that impact upon adaptation 
in the new environment seems warranted.  
Fang Law and Karnilowicz (2015) also found a significant correlation between cycling and 
citizenship. They relate cycling to identity and place. According to their research, the 
establishment of an identity represented within adopted country must go along with further 
accommodation to fit within existing the cycling culture. The study emphasizes that active 
==PAGE:32==
 
31
cyclist participates in multiple interactions involving social and physical factors (e.g. social 
network and ties) which provide a unique perspective for investigating cycling within frame 
of place.  
Thus, following the reasoning set forth by Fang Law and Karnilowicz, it might be hypothesized 
that cycling is indeed strongly related to place and interactions with one’s inner self. Cyclist 
an active participant of city life and engages with elements of space and place. Fang Law and 
Karnilowicz (2015), refer to Convery et al. (2012), emphasizing that the place of cycling sets 
a center of one’s existence, which people are emotionally and psychologically tied. Moreover, 
this is a component of the complex process of identifying and defining oneself. 
Jones and Novo de Azevedo (2013) discussed how cycling fits – or not –in the society in terms 
of ongoing changes. Authors review the struggle with the marginalization of this mode in the 
socio-economic context based on the case of utilitarian cyclists in Brazil which are associated 
with low-income, while the people with higher income, cycle for recreation. It is an interesting 
point of view complementing Feng Law and Karnilowicz (2015) study where is found that 
cycling is associated with a particular social identity, status and class in the place of origin and 
is perceived differently in the current location (destination of migration) due to existing 
culture polarity. 
Alderd (2010) in his study aimed to prove that cycling is related to social relations (so also to 
relatedness) on a local level. He defines four dimensions of cycling citizenship which are: (1) 
responsibility for environmental issues; (2) taking care of oneself; (3) having roots in the 
locality; (4) responding to the social environment with openness. 
 
2.7 Migration context  
In the context of social, economic and political change, migrations have become increasingly 
common.  Despite the size and growth of the immigrant communities in many European 
countries, the travel behavior of immigrants and its impact on the transport system have 
barely been studied so far.  Immigration has significantly affected and constantly affects the 
transport patterns in the destination country. Therefore, it would be important to evaluate 
whether social and cultural determinants affect the adaptation to differently oriented 
transport culture among migrants. As it is proved by Chatterjee et al.’s (2013) research about 
the impact of life course events on the travel behavior, residential changes, hence migration, 
==PAGE:33==
 
32
appear as a possible driving force of transport behavior change. 
In the new environment, migrants deploy a range of social and other assets to adapt to new 
conditions. Such assets, for example education or social and economic capital, are unevenly 
distributed among migrants and might have an impact on developing social ties, and therefore, 
relatedness. Moreover, recent studies show that migrants are aware of the importance of 
adherence to social norms in combating difficulties with adaptation in a new milieu (Lyon 
2007). 
Even though demographics change rapidly nowadays due to intense migration, precise 
research about travel behavior among migrants is missing. The transition from a car-oriented 
culture to one with developed and emerging cycling environment, and the adaptation to it, 
must be conditioned by certain factors. It becomes crucial to understand what is the 
adaptation process driven by and what kind of needs and its hierarchy (if it applies) influence 
the adaptation to new travel behavior culture.  
 
Embedding transport in national and local cultures was highlighted by Alderd and Jungnickel 
(2014). They emphasize the lack of research on cycling and social identity and the importance 
of cultural context in such studies. Fang Law and Karnilowicz (2015) described identity and 
its establishment within adopted country are essential to understanding migrants’ behavior. 
Recent studies also show that migrants are aware of the importance of adherence to social 
norms in combating difficulties with adaptation in a new milieu (Lyons 2007). It is essential 
to emphasize that the literature already related the significant increase in cycling as a 
transport mode and cultural change (Pucher et al. 1999, Gaterslaben and Appleton 2006). 
 
The definition of “culture” in this context should be clarified. For this study, the term “culture” 
will be taken to mean the “way of life” and consensually with Alderd and Jungnickel (2014), 
we assume that everyday behavior is not visible since it is what people simply do with no prior 
consideration.  
 
Starting point for this study is Chatterjee et al.’s research (2013) about impact of life course 
events on the travel behavior. The analysis was conducted in a number of domains. Besides 
residential changes, authors also examined education, employment and family events which 
all apply to the study and which, if they occur, might lead to rethinking the way one travels 
==PAGE:34==
 
33
and change the habits. Chatterjee et al.’s analysis provides a unique perspective in which 
migration appear as a possible driving force of transport behavior change. The evidence from 
these studies suggests a variety of factors related to adaptation in a new environment and 
need of further investigation.  
 
Interestingly, Pucher et al. (2010) found that people who do not cycle but are surrounded by 
cyclist are potentially more likely to consider cycling as well as be more tractable to policy 
interventions. It, therefore, seems crucial to investigate people who after migration were 
embedded in different transport conditions than ones they knew from the country of origin, 
in this case: the shift from car to cycle-oriented culture. Moreover, according to Blumenberg 
(2009), immigrants are more likely than native-borns to use alternative modes of transport, 
including cycling. Blumenberg (2009) has used an assimilation framework to investigate 
transport behavior of migrants. She emphasizes the need of better understand the trends in 
migrants’ travel behavior to be able to provide better opportunities to understand the travel 
needs of immigrants better. In her study about immigrants in the U.S., she indicates the six 
major determinants of commute mode among immigrants. Factors that influence travel 
behavior of migrants are: (1) individual and household characteristics (gender, age, origin 
country, income, etc.); (2) spatial assimilation (residential choice, duration of migration, etc.);  
(3) access to ethnic-specific resources (economic and social networks); (4) employment 
patterns (labor market sector and its specification); (5) cultural differences (transportation 
patterns in the origin country, gender dependency); (6) external regulations (immigration 
laws and regulation, driver's licensing laws, etc.). 
 
Household habits indeed influence travel choices as well as its size and age. Myers (1997) 
found that in the U.S. immigrants who arrive at older ages are less likely to change their travel 
behavior in comparison to immigrants who were more adaptable to learning new behaviors 
and skills in young age. However, while household characteristics explain some of the travel 
habits differences between immigrants and native-born (as well as individual characteristic), 
some differences between them remain. These can be explained more by spatial assimilation 
process, therefore residential choices, time spent abroad and access to opportunities. 
Especially before Polish accession to the European Union, it was common that immigrants 
arrive through a process of chain migration. In this process, new migrants gain knowledge 
about opportunities and receive aid from family and friends already living in the migration 
==PAGE:35==
 
34
country. Boyd’s (1989) research suggests, that kinship networks not only motivate migration, 
but also that many immigrants rely upon immigrant networks to compensate for the 
sometimes limited availability of other forms of social capital. Employment patterns are 
relevant since some sectors are more likely than others to have non-standard work hours, 
vehicle requirements and variable work locations. Regarding culture differences, over time, 
most immigrants adapt to life in the different country but still maintain strong cultural 
identities. Therefore, assimilation may be unevenly distributed among the various aspects of 
immigrants’ lives, including transportation. For the purpose of this study, we chose cases in 
which significant disparate travel behavior culture indeed exist (car-oriented versus cycle-
oriented). Law regulations might also influence the travel mode choice. Barriers to obtaining 
driving license (also linguistic), high tax levels on automobiles, specific law regulations (e.g. 
in Denmark: the necessity to use only vehicles registered there; in the United Kingdom: left-
hand drive, etc.) indeed have an impact on the commute mode.  
 
In the literature, researchers are focused mostly on the low-status migrants, escaping from 
poverty and the way they find themselves in a different environment as well as how their 
native-born norms turn out there. In his study from 2010, Smart conceptualizes the typical 
image of an immigrant in the literature. According to his findings, it is a poor man 
(significantly more often than women), coming to a wealthy country in the west, looking for a 
life better that he found in a native-born country. This is in line with Nee and Alba (1997). 
Regarding transport, therefore, they are less likely than native-born adults to have the 
resources necessary to purchase, insure, and maintain vehicles. However, new trends in 
immigration show that the pattern of people moving abroad and their intentions are much 
more complex, and such simplification does not accurately capture current attitudes. 
 
2.8 Electric bikes 
An electric bicycle is a two-wheeled vehicle powered by a battery and an electric motor. E-
bikes typically have an operating speed up to approximately 30 km/h and weights ranging 
from 30 to even 80 kg. Because of this relatively lightweight and efficient motor, electrically 
assisted bicycle are known as one of the most energy efficient modes of motorized 
transport. Regarding energy consuming, e-bike consumes approximately 1/10 of energy 
that is required for an electric-car- around 1.8 kWh/100 km (Cherry et al., 2016). In fact, e-
bicycles are the most rapidly developing adoption of a sustainable transportation mode in 
==PAGE:36==
 
35
the history of motorization. In the literature, there is evidence available that e-bikes might 
be a convenient alternative for cars, which might encourage users to replace car trips and 
influence the travel patterns as a result (Popovich et al., 2014). In line with these findings 
is also the study by Jones et al. (2016) conducted in the Netherlands and the United 
Kingdom, where it is found that, thanks to the opportunity to increase cycling share, the 
use of e-bicycle leads to replacing personal journeys that might have been performed by 
car otherwise. Jones at al. also found social, technological and environmental barriers to 
using e-bikes, which include battery life, the weight of the bicycle, social stigma and 
infrastructure limitations and price of purchase (Jones et al., 2016). 
 
Sales and usage of electric bikes rise worldwide. The World market leader is China, where 
sales of e-bikes are projected to increase to $38 million in next two years. Annual sales of 
e-bikes there is about 30 million. At the beginning of the century, there were almost no e-
bicycles on Chinese streets while now it is estimated that approximately 150 millions of 
electrically assisted bicycle are present. In many cities, people use more e-bicycles than 
regular ones (Cherry et al., 2016). 
 
A high share of electrically assisted bicycles is also noticed in Germany and Netherlands- 
10% and 20% respectively (Astegiano et al., 2015). E-bikes have growing popularity also 
in Austria, Switzerland, and Denmark. Only in the Netherlands, e-cycling currently accounts 
for approximately 12% of total distance traveled by cycle, which is equivalent to 1,5 billion 
kilometers every year (Jones et al., 2016). E-bikes provide undoubtful advantages, 
especially for people who could not or would not otherwise make the same trip by 
conventional bicycle. So far, electrically assisted bikes make over 30% of all cycling 
kilometers driven by people age 65 and above. Interestingly, the journey distance 
performed by e-bike is 1.5 times longer than the one using a regular bicycle.

2.9 Behavior theories  
Behavior is determined not only by purely utilitarian motivations but also by the symbolic 
sequels for one’s self-identity. The role of social identity and belonging to certain group (e.g. 
cyclists) might be essential (Lois et al. 2015). It becomes interesting in terms of immigration 
since there is still a more improvement required in the area of the underserved population 
due to face that various subcultures (including migrants) are likely to have beliefs and 
==PAGE:39==
 
38
individual constraints not common for the general population. (Hill and Lafaye 2011) 
Understanding behavior theory in the attempt to change individuals’ travel choices gain 
importance and has been recognized in research as well as in policy development (Heinen, et 
al., 2010). As said by Schwanen et al., 2012,  “transport academics now agree that at least some 
level of behavior change is unavoidable if carbon emissions from transport are to be reduced 
significantly” (p. 522). 
2.9.1 Rational Choice 
The Rational Choice Theory is one of the most influential and far-reaching models of human 
behavior so far. This theory has its basis in Western neoclassical economics and has often been 
used in studies explaining behavior (Jackson, 2005). The rational choice is an outcome of The 
Expectancy-Value Theory developed by Atkinson (1950) in order to understand the 
achievement motivation of individuals (Jackson, 2005). The main assumption of the Rational 
Choice model is that people make decisions, deliberate choices to maximize their personal 
utility. Therefore, people must know outcomes of possible choices to be able to value cost and 
benefits of each action. However, this theory has already been proven as limited in terms of 
explaining behavior. Mode choice in transport is especially affected by these limitations since 
it is a recurrent choice. Transport is also perceived as socially motivated behavior (van den 
Bergh et al., 2000). Another limitation of the Rational Choice Theory is the fact that it assumes 
unlimited supply and demand for services and goods on the market. In reality, people are not 
able to cognitively consider all possibilities since the capacity and access to gather all required 
information is limited. Furthermore, individual’s opinion is predominated and biased. This 
occurs as an outcome of uncertainties in information and emotional affect (which is related to 
experiences and past behavior rather than reasoned), therefore, simply, habits. Van den Bergh 
et al. indicate that habits might lead to behaviors that not maximize utility and personal 
benefit. Moreover, decisions might be constrained by social context, and choice might be made 
base on benefits to others, e.g. environment, which is not explained by this model.  
 
2.9.2 Theory of Reasoned Action 
Ajzen and Fishbein (1980) proposed the Theory of Reasoned Action to fill the gap that 
Expectancy Value left. This theory derives from rational choice but includes the influence of 
subjective norms and individual’s beliefs. Subjective norms depend on what others might 
think. Attitudes, as a component of rational choice, are a set of beliefs about the outcomes of 
==PAGE:40==
 
39
certain behavior and the values that individuals attach to these outcomes. Those factors create 
an intention to behavior. However, this theory still does not incorporate emotional biases, 
social norms and habits. Moreover, it is already proved in the literature that intention to 
behavior and behavior itself are not perfectly correlated (Jackson 2005) unless people have a 
feeling of full control on their behavior, which was the milestone to modify the Theory of 
Reasoned Action into the Theory of Planned Behavior. 
 
2.9.3 Theory of Planned Behavior 
Many studies investigating bicycle usage are based on the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) 
by Ajzen (1991). TPB an adaptation of the Theory of Reasoned Action by Ajzen and Fishbein 
(1980) to account for situations where the individual does not have complete freedom to 
control their behavior. The theory assumes that before performing a certain behavior, 
individuals consider its consequences. Then intention becomes an important factor in 
determining behavior and its potential change. The individual perception of certain behavior 
might be positive or negative, and it is determined by the impression of the way in which 
society perceives the same behavior. The intention is therefore shaped by personal attitude 
and social pressure. Broadly speaking, it links the behavior to the beliefs and aims to 
understand how to create the change of people’s behavior through prediction of behavioral 
intention, which- according to Bamberg (2007)- is directly associated with the probability 
that one will act in a certain way. According to Ajzen theory, hence, intention is created by 
three types of beliefs: normative, behavioral and control; and behavior is a result of intention. 
Therefore, we assume that the intention to perform a given behavior is a function of three 
individual elements which are: (1) attitudes toward certain behavior, (2) subjective norms 
and (3) perceived behavioral control. Attitudes emerge from the realm of individual beliefs. 
Those are considered as the consequences of certain behavior. Furthermore, attitudes depend 
also on an assessment of the importance of those beliefs to oneself. Subjective norms, defined 
as the perceived social pressure, are based on the expectations and social influence. With 
reference to Fishbein & Ajzen (1975, p. 480), it comprises from two main factors: normative 
beliefs (opinion about how other people think that someone should behave) and motivation 
to comply (willing to adhere the normative beliefs accepted by the group). Perceived 
behavioral control is described as an individual perception of one’s possibility to execute a 
certain behavior. However, Ajzen theory has a significant limitation which is a gap between 
intentions and behavior (Hill and Lafaye 2011). This applies especially to the complex 
==PAGE:41==
 
40
behaviors like cycling. 
 
 
Based on the TPB, Dill and Voros (2007) conducted a study and found that attitudes towards 
cycling are usually less positive than those towards car use. They also highlight that a positive 
attitude towards cycling increases the likelihood of commuting by bicycle. Gatersleben and 
Appleton (2007), using this theory, found that people who consider cycling have a general 
more positive attitude towards cycling than those who do not (56% vs. 34%). A study by 
Bamberg and Schmidt (2003) compared Theory of Planned Behavior with the Norm-
Activation Theory and the Theory of Interpersonal Behavior. The results proved that to 
explain travel mode choice, TPB is the easiest to apply. However, the Theory of Interpersonal 
Behavior provides a great improvement by including factors such as beliefs and habits. 
 
2.9.4 Technology acceptance theory 
The technology acceptance theory (TAM) by Fred Davis and Richard Bagozzi (Davis et al. 
1989) is a modified version of the Reasoned Action model by Ajzen and Fishbein (1980) and 
one of the pioneering theories which aim to determine users‘ behaviors towards particular 
technologies. It is widely applied in the context of users‘ acceptance of new technologies.

2.9.5 Norm-Activation Theory  
A model for altruistic behavior created by Schwartz (1977) called The Norm Activation 
Theory is based on personal norms. It was used by Bamberg and Schmidt (2003) and modify 
four years later by Bamberg and Möser (2007) in transport choices analysis. In contrast to 
subjective norms, personal norms are not based only on what the individual believes others 
will think of their actions (Bamberg & Möser 2007). To clarify, personal norms depend on 
individual‘s beliefs about what they should or should not do which is associated with public 
opinion. Personal norms are constructed by the awareness of possible consequences of 
behavior and a feeling of responsibility related to these consequences. These two factors not 
only influence personal norms but also behavior. As stronger relation between awareness and 
sense of responsibility, as stronger is the relation between personal norm and behavior 
(Jackson, 2005). Stern (2000) found also that this correlation might be strongly affected by 
contextual factors from ABC theory, described below.  
 
2.9.6 Attitude-Behavior-Context Model  
Stern introduced the Attitude-Behavior-Context (ABC) model in 2000. According to Stern, 
there are two main factor groups. First, attitudes that are constructed by factors such as 
individual norms and values, specific personal beliefs, emotional biases which are related to 
the tendency to act in a certain way. Second, external conditions (or so-called contextual 
factors) are formed by legal, financial and institutional factors as well as physical capabilities 
but also by social norms and other interpersonal issues (e.g. group allegiances). Behavior is 
determined by how they would interact each other. According to Stern, attitudes have a strong 
impact on individual’s choice when external conditions are weak. When the situation is 
reversed, and contextual factors are strong in relation to a certain behavior, interestingly, 
attitudes are not so essential (Jackson, 2005). This model still does not account for past 
behaviors and habits, even though put more attention to social context.  
 
2.9.7 Social context 
There have been a few attempts to account for the impact of habit and social context in  
behavioral theories. However, the complexity of such models in most cases makes them too 
complicated to use in empirical analysis (Gifford, et al., 2011; Jackson, 2005). Nonetheless, 
since these factors indeed improve our understanding of the processes that determine 
behavior, it is not possible to ignore them in the analysis. Stern highlights it in his research 
==PAGE:44==
 
43
from 2000, that appropriate model of behavior should cover not only attitudes, contextual 
factors, and personal capabilities, but also habits. 
2.9.8 Stage model 
Bamberg (2007)’ Theory of Behavioral Change is in line with the theory by Prochaska and 
DiClemente (1982). Both are theoretically framed by the Rubicon model of action phases by 
Gollwitzer (1996). Hence, the behavior change is determined by “time-ordered sequence of 
qualitatively different stages” (Loist et al. 2015): (1) pre-contemplation, (2) contemplation, 
(3) preparation, (4) maintenance. According to this, there is no intention to the behavior 
change in the first stage, just awareness that it might be desirable or necessary. In the second 
stage, the change is considered. Individual assess pros and cons and therefore “behavioral 
intention” is formed. This formation makes an entry for the third stage, where a specific plan 
to execute this intention is made (e.g. creation of mental the link between a given future 
situation and execution/test of the intended new behavior). In the last stage, measures are 
taken to harden the new behavior and prevent a return to previous habits.  
Bamberg argues that this model has a significant advantage over the TPB, which is lacking in 
temporal perspective while stage models provide this perspective. Indeed, it is perceived as 
unrealistic to consider behavior change as a one-step process (Lois et al. 2015). Thus, better 
definition of the intentions is observed. Moreover, stage models provide a framework for the 
development of interventions that would be stage-matched. These are assumed to be more 
successful in creating behavioral change (Prochanska et al. 2008). 
2.9.9 Social identity 
Recent studies showed the value of including social identity into the original TPB model (Lois 
et al. 2015) as a further factor of behavioral intention. Lois et al.’s findings of the importance 
of identity in research about mode choice are consistent with other authors: Alderd (2013), 
Murtagh et al. (2012). 
The social identity theory introduced by Tajfel and Turner (1970) emphasizes the concept of 
a social identity as a possible to explain intergroup behavior. According to them, individual's 
self-concept arise from perceived membership in a relevant social group. The interpersonal 
behavior would be determined solely by the individual characteristics and interpersonal 
relationships among people. Authors emphasize that in social situations, in reality, behavior 
derive from two extremes cause purely interpersonal or purely intergroup behavior is 
==PAGE:45==
 
44
unlikely to be found (Tajfel 1978). The social identity theory focuses on the social structural 
factors that will predict which of these two dimensions will influence an individual’s behavior 
the most.  
2.9.10 Theory of Interpersonal Behavior 
The theory of Interpersonal Behavior by Triandis (1977) is based on assumptions similar to 
the ones of the Theories of Reasoned Action and Planned Behavior, namely that intentions 
immediately precede behavior. In addition to other theories, this theory includes also habits 
into the analysis (Morris 2002). According to this model, the intention is conditioned by 
attitudes, social factors, emotions responses, lifestyle and personal beliefs and habits and 
behavior is an outcome of intention. The strength of habits furthermore depends on the 
frequency of the behavior in the past. Therefore, future behavior is determined by the past 
behavior and deliberative thought. Scholar research (Bamberg and Schmidt 2003) found that 
this model much more complex in comparison to Stern’s ABC model, the Norm-Activated 
Model and the TPB. Although more complicated, the Theory of Interpersonal Behavior’s ability 
of comprehensively explaining behavior is significant, which applies especially to explain 
transport behavior.

2.9.11 Maslow’s hierarchy of needs  
The hierarchy of needs theory by Abraham Maslow is a psychological model proposed for the 
first time in 1943. Human needs, according to Maslow (1943), are hierarchical and follow with 
order: (1) physiological, (2) safety, (3) belongingness and love, (4) self-esteem, (5) self-
actualization. In his research, Maslow focused on the 1% of college student population that 
was mentally the healthiest (Mittelman 1991). The theory proposed by Maslow remains 
widely applied framework, especially in sociology studies.  
 
Physiological are simply requirements for human survival. These needs include basic life 
needs like food, water, air, sleep, shelter or sex. Safety needs start to dominate behavior once 
physical needs are relatively satisfied. These needs include protection, self-security, financial 
security, stability, health, and well-being. Love and belonging needs became important after 
the previous one are satisfied. These include a feeling of love, human relations, friendships, 
sense of acceptance, affection. Self-esteem needs derive from the need to be accepted, 
respected and valued by others. Human wants to be completive, gain prestige and achieve 
goals. Maslow believed that in order to process to the top level of needs, individual must 
master the previous one. Self-actualization refers to the need of realization, desire to 
accomplish everything that individual can perform the best to realize their self’s potential.  
 
The concept is pictured in the shape of a pyramid whose levels represent needs, from the most 
fundamental on the bottom. Interestingly, even though the pyramid shape is the most popular 
way to present it, Maslow himself did not use this method in his papers. The first four layers 
in the pyramid are called „deficiency needs“. Maslow's theory suggests that, in order to desire 
secondary needs, individuals must fulfill the basic needs. Different needs and motivations 
might occur at the same time since the human brain is complex and might run processes in 
==PAGE:47==
 
46
parallel. However, the human organism would be the most dominated by psychological needs, 
even if all other needs are unsatisfied. 
 
Figure 10 Maslow's hierarchy of needs 
 
 
Maslow's theory doesn't fully reflect the true complexity of human motivation and does not 
fully explain all human behaviors. According to Maslow, individuals apparently take the action 
that needs a higher order only after satisfying the needs of the lower level. It is important to 
emphasize exceptions of this rule. Contradictory to Maslow’s hierarchy of needs was a 
phenomenon of Polish theater existing during II World War under the German occupation and 
therefore, unmet need for security. Moreover, higher levels and lower levels of Maslow's 
hierarchy of needs are not universal. Therefore, they may vary due to individual differences 
and the availability of resources in the region or country. People also have different 
personalities, various beliefs and their social and cultural values are disparate and so do their 
needs.

2.9.12 ERG 
Alderfer (1969) developed a new model of human needs by compressing Maslow's hierarchy 
of needs from five to three: existence, relatedness, and growth. At the bottom level, individuals 
desire existence needs that refer to Maslow's physiological and safety needs. Relatedness 
needs relate to Maslow's social needs and the external part of self-esteem need. The need of 
growth equates to the internal part of self-esteem needs by Maslow and the self-actualization 
needs. 
 
Figure 11 Relation between Maslow's theory and Alderferd's theory 
 
 
 
There are three main relationships between the different categories of ERG theory: (1) 
satisfaction-progression; (2) frustration-regression; (3) satisfaction-strengthening. In the 
Maslow’s theory, satisfaction-progression plays a key role with gratifying the needs. The 
progression upward hierarchy is a result of satisfying lower order needs. Alderfer, on the 
other hand, argues that it is not necessary. In other words, move up in hierarchy from 
relatedness to growth does not necessarily presume the satisfaction of individual‘s existence 
needs. In case that a higher order need remains not gratified, individual may regress to lower 
order needs which appear easier to satisfy. Simply, the need that is already fulfilled might 
==PAGE:49==
 
48
become active again if it is impossible to satisfy higher order needs. Satisfaction-strengthening 
relation refers to the situation when already gratified need can maintain satisfaction or even 
strength this need iteratively when fulfilling higher order needs fails (Caulton 2012). 
 
Figure 12 ERG model 
 
 
Alderfer's ERG theory, similar to Maslow, maintains the existence of a general order for 
pursuing needs. However, he believed that this order is not fixed. Although existence needs 
have a higher priority than relatedness and growth needs, this order might change, depending 
on the person and the circumstances. Alderfer includes in his model s „frustration-regression“ 
element which applies to the situation when needs at higher level remain unsatisfied. Then 
individual will become frustrated, and go start over with pursuing lower level needs again. 
 
To sum up, there are three main differences between Alderfer’s and Maslow’s theories: (1) 
Alderfer believed that the strict progression from one level to the next does not necessarily 
exist and human behavior can be motivated by needs from more than one level at the same 
time; (2) Alderfer acknowledged the possible variation between individuals‘ importance of 
the needs; (3) if a higher order need is not fulfilled, the desire to fulfill a lower order need will 
increase and might lead individual to regress to a more basic needs category.

3. METHODOLOGY  
This study addresses policy makers by developing and testing a series of hypotheses 
indicating how the theory of needs developed by Alderfer affect the intention to change travel 
behavior during the process of the switch from car to cycle-oriented environment. The main 
goals of the current study conducted withinh the framework of ERG theory were: (1) to 
develop the questionnaire to measure the relation between ERG theory and intentions to use 
a bicycle, (2) to examine these relations with the aim of providing detailed hints to policy 
making.  
 
3.1 Research hypothesis 
This study aims to investigate what makes someone choose to cycle. Data collection and 
methodology are based on three main hypotheses regarding the intention to change travel 
habits after migration. As a nowadays challenge, it is necessary to understand in details the 
reasons why people choose a particular mode of transport. For future policy making, 
identification of groups willing to cycle would help to address policies and strategies to 
individuals with similar characteristics in more effective way. One additional hypothesis 
would be considered in the second study. 
 
We assume that transport is an essential element of the culture and after transition to 
different transport culture, people are more likely to use different mode of transport than they 
mainly used to do it (in this case: a bicycle instead a car) which is determined by needs of 
relatedness and growth more than existence. In other words, we assume that people are 
motivated to use bicycle by more than purely utilitarian purposes. Adaptation in differently 
oriented culture might be motivated also by the needs of belonging to the community, 
participating in city and social life as well as do not be strikingly differ from other community 
members in terms of their daily habits. Therefore, three main factor would be investigated in 
this study: existence, relatedness and growth needs. Their influence on the possible behavior 
change prior to significant change in life events would be examined in a double-dimension 
investigation.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:51==
 
50
First, the ERG model would be tested in terms of current residence and its traffic conditions, 
attitudes and perspectives according to the main hypothesis of this study:  
 
People cycle when cycling fits their needs. 
 
These needs might be nevertheless determined by numerous factors and some of them might 
become crucial in consequence of migration (e.g. need of establishing social ties in a new 
place). Owing to this and according to Chatterjee et al.’s (2013), it is important to evaluate 
how special life event as a migration influences in particular bicycle use among people raised 
in a car-dominated environment. Since each of case studies (Denmark, Germany, Netherlands, 
United Kingdom, and Sweden) have attributes of being more cycling-oriented culture than the 
respondents‘ country of origin, the second hypothesis that will be tested in this research refers 
to ERG concept and is stated as follow: 
 
Modal switch to cycling after migration to cycling-oriented cultures is 
motivated by higher-order needs rather than lower-order needs. 
 
In order to be able to indicate precise policy implications that might help to clarify directions 
for policy-making to move towards to increase the usage and promote the investment in 
cycling infrastructure in Poland and hence the modal switch to sustainable transport modes, 
this study also aims to investigate whether the place and environment of cycling is important 
or habit itself. Therefore, the third hypothesis to be tested in this research is: 
 
If an individual would start to cycle in one country, he or she will continue this 
behavior in other places. 
 
By other places, we assume also those less developed in terms of cycling culture. For this 
purpose, the scenario of moving back to the country of origin (Poland) would be implemented 
and therefore, the same ERG model would be tested in terms of perception for transport 
culture, conditions, and opportunities after having experienced a cycling-developed 
environment.  Applying the ERG model, in this case, would also allow determining major 
barriers to cycling in Poland from potentially experienced users. Since this research also 
==PAGE:52==

consists of an additional study conducted in Poland, where cycling share among other modes 
is significantly low, mostly due to  lack of perceived safety, required physical effort and lack of 
facilities that allows to use bicycle as main transport mode (therefore: existence needs) in 
example for trips to work, (e.g. showers in the workplace) we would like to examine the 
phenomenon of bike-sharing systems in three Polish cities by applying the scenario in which 
electrically assisted bicycles are available at the same fare as regular ones. The cycling culture, 
in this case, is supplemented by real-time infrastructure development proposed by each of the

would determine the more likely use of cycling as a transport mode. This study also

Using electric bike-sharing systems instead of regular ones (at the same fare) is

Since sales of e-bikes in Poland are the lowest among European countries, we would take the

3.2 Behavioral Framework  
For the purpose of this study, Theory of Planned Behavior seems the most appropriate to 
apply due to its ease of application to empirical research. However, Ajzen’s concept has a 
significant limitation which is a gap between intentions and behavior (Hill and Lafaye, 
2011), and since it applies especially to the complex behaviors like cycling, few mentioned 
changes are applied. Beside intentions, inclusion of habits might be considered as a crucial 
determinant of behavior. For the purpose of this research, instead of limited “subjective 
norm”, the wider category of a “social factor” (Bamberg and Schmidt, 2003) should be used 
as its equivalent. Moreover, beside intention and habits, the context in which an individual 
makes the decision goes alongside with the prior decision behaviors of the individual. 
==PAGE:53==
 
52
Therefore, in this case, it is important to include also a contextual factor, which would 
determine the possibility of performance of a certain behavior. Added value of habits, a 
contextual factor that verifies the possibility of individual’s choice and social factor as an 
equivalent to relatively constrained subjective norms, might reduce this gap.  
 
The theory of Ajzen assumes that before performing a certain behavior, individuals 
consider its consequences, in this case: how much cycling will gratify certain needs. 
Therefore, additional value to this model would be a new theory of norms by Alderfer. The 
ERG theory is based on three groups of needs: (1) existence (physical well-being and 
survival); (2) relatedness (interpersonal relations); and (3) growth (self-esteem and 
personal development). The development of the conceptual framework is based on 
Alderfer's ERG theory as a motivator for cycling willingness and intensity. Cycling 
willingness and intensity comprise of bicycle ownership and use of the bike-sharing 
system, cycling frequency, cycling distance, cycling purpose (utilitarian versus leisure), 
participation in the bike-to-work campaign, feeling of well-being and enthusiasm related 
to cycling. By including such factors, the attempt is made to measure to which extent each 
of the levels affect cycling. For the purpose of this study, as an existence needs safety, 
mobility, accessibility, time and money savings, fulfilling the basic needs like shopping and 
running errands and daily life routine are considered. Nevertheless, existence also means 
coping with difficulties related to perceived safety, adapting to the road rules, weather 
conditions, lack of cycling experience and skills, and physical ability. Relatedness needs are 
social inclusion, place attachment, forming social networks, peer-acceptance, conforming 
to social norms. Personal growth needs are self-esteem, self-confidence, cultural identity, 
self-identity as a cyclist, and self-actualization with respect to environmental sustainability 
and fitness. Based on the individual’s background and cultural environment, one set of 
needs may precede over others.  
 
 
3.3 Mathematical Model 
The formulation of structural equation models (SEM) allowed investigating the hypothesized 
behavioral framework. The commercial software M-Plus was used for the model estimation. 
SEM is a widely applied method, especially in behavioral studies. Recent studies applied SEM 
==PAGE:54==
 
53
to explore mode choice and intention to modal switch (Kaplan et al., 2014). SEM allows 
investigating hypothesis based on theory and empirical research by combine latent multi-
dimensional and observed unidimensional constructs and to account complex relationship 
patterns as well as measurement error when statistically analyzing data. This approach 
provides greater importance given to the validity of observed scores from given measurement 
instruments.  
The goal of the SEM analysis is to the determination of the extent to which the theoretical 
model is supported by the sample data. 
The parameters for modeling were estimated simultaneously by using maximum likelhood. 
Alongside the traditional descriptive measure of chi-square test of absolute model fit, 
comparative fit index (CFI) was performed as well as an additional descriptive goodness-of-
fit measure- the Root Mean Square of Approximation (RMSEA). The CFI examine the goodness 
of fit of a target model by testing the discrepancy between the hypothesized model and the 
collected data. A CFI value close to 0,9 indicates excellent goodness of fit between the model 
and the data (Yong and Pearce, 2013).  The RMSEA value describes how well the model would 
fit the populations’ covariance matrix. An RMSEA value close to 0,05 and lower representing 
a good model (Yong and Pearce, 2013).   
Specifically, in this study the SEM model contains three sets of equations: (1) measurement 
equations linking the latent constructs to observed indicators; (2) structural equations linking 
the latent attitudinal constructs to the cyclists’ socio-economic characteristics, (3) structural 
equations relating the latent attitudinal constructs to depended variables (e.g., cycling 
frequency, enthusiasm, distance, bike to work participation).

4. DATA COLLECTION  
Since there are few different places involved in this study, each separated from each other 
by a significant distance, this study is performed based on web-based questionnaires. The 
benefits of computer-aided recording are well known. It enables to record respondents' 
experiences and beliefs seamlessly. Surveys are anonymous, but in order to verify the 
sample, a prize raffle is proposed to encourage respondents to provide their personal data. 
Awareness of anonymous answers increases the reliability of the data shared by 
respondents. Online questionnaires also allow to capture the right answers and avoid 
misinterpretation or phrasing inconsistency, which might be common in verbal choice 
protocols. The significant advantage of web-based protocols is also the operational cost of 
the research. This is associated with time and human resources required to conduct the 
survey. Since the surveys are shared mostly among social media with cooperation with 
local media like radio and press, potential respondents are familiar with Internet 
technology, have access to the computer and therefore technology barrier is not a 
limitation in this case. Most of the respondents are also young people, familiar with that 
kind of activities. Moreover, with the widespread share of the Internet all over the World 
and among all age groups, this condition seems less and less a limitation for any of 
potentially tested population groups.

4.2 Survey design 
The results of this study might be used to develop and stimulate the cycling culture creation 
in Poland and other countries where the advantage of cycling as transport mode is still not 
sufficiently taken. Gaining the experience from places where implanting efficient transport 
policies in terms of promoting cycling is well developed such as Denmark and the Netherlands

For the purpose of this study, we also assume that behavior is influenced more by habits than 
a conscious and controlled process (Bamberg and Schmidt 2003). Therefore, daily routine, as 
an element of the existence needs, appears as a relevant. According to the social identity 
theory, we would investigate also relatedness since it is already proved in the literature that 
cycling is related to social relations (Alderd 2010). 
 
One way of encouraging people to change their habits and identify oneself as a cyclist is 
emphasizing benefits of cycling as a commuting mode for a larger scale: for the city, the 
environment, etc. Therefore, items related to growth would be tested in order to use them 
potentially in further policy making process. Jaśkiewicz and Besta (2014) in their recent study

as „This transport mode makes me feel like I am part of something bigger, something good“ or 
„By using this transport mode, I contribute to making the city an absolutely wonderful place 
to live“ are correlated with higher satisfaction with public transportation.

questionnaire. 
 
The target population was simply Polish migrants who live in one of the five analyzed 
countries: Denmark, Sweden, Germany, The Netherlands or the United Kingdom. There were 
no special requirements to take part in this exploration. There were also not only regular 
cyclist taken into consideration, but also those who do not cycle regularly or those who do not

cycle at all in order to investigate their fears and barriers which might be potentially

The most relevant part of this stage of work was to identify relevant key factors to be 
surveyed. This was performed based on the literature discussion and individual observations 
and experiences (especially about Polish conditions). The survey contained 34 questions 
divided into four parts. Noteworthy, there was one additional question in questionnaires for 
each country beside Denmark, which applied to the intention and probability of use electric 
bicycle within the next 5 years. The purpose was to be able to investigate migrants perception 
in the different kind of study conducted by the author as complement to the sample.  
 
The main content of the survey is divided into two parts. The first one measured attitudes 
according to the current country of residence. The second one presented a scenario where 
respondents were asked to hypothetically move back to Poland to look at the attitudes toward 
cycling in the entirely different condition than the present one. Questionnaire was 
complement by socio-economic characteristic and question related to transport habits. 
Besides Denmark, also question about intentions to use electric bicycle was included.  
 
The opening questions in the first part concerned that availability of the transport modes and 
the frequency of their use. Among transport modes were considered: car (as a driver, 
passenger, taxi), public transportation, bicycle and walk. Results were measured in both the 
country of origin and current residence. Availability was measured according to car, bicycle 
and bicycle sharing system also for both the country of origin and current country of 
residence. The following questions aimed to scope the transport habits of respondent: daily 
cycling distance, participation in the “Bike to work” campaign and oneself’s perception of 
which kind of cyclist they perceive to be.  
 
Further questions were constructed as follows. There were three types of needs to measure

those were embedded in one question with corresponding title: conditions - existence; society

country of residence as well as the probability of cycling in Poland in this certain scenario 
were measured.

At the most basic level of ERG are the existence needs. These correspond to Maslow's 
physiological and safety needs. Items related to existence needs include inter alia safety, daily 
routine, fulfilment of basic needs, increase of mobility and flexibility, money and time saving. 
The second level represents relatedness needs, where people fulfill need for interpersonal 
relationships. This level relates to According to Maslow's, relatedness corresponds with 3rd 
and 4th levels, which are social needs and self-esteem needs. Relatedness items describe how 
good about oneself people feel based on social relation with others. On the top level of ERG 
are growth needs. This equates to the higher-level part of Maslow's self-esteem needs and 
self-actualization needs. Here we can distinguish the aim to the personal growth and 
development, the sense of doing high quality and meaningful tasks (Alderfer 1969).

perception on cycling conditions. In total, there were 27 items in this part of the survey.

4.2.2 Study B 
The goal of the survey was to test the hyphotesis of the second study conducted for the 
purpose of this thesis which was how the existence, relatedness and growth needs influence 
the electric bicycle use after creation of cycle-oriented culture in three Polish cities. Data were 
collected by means of a tailor-made web-based questionnaire. 
 
The target population was young people, mostly students, who live in one of the three selected 
cities. In this case also not only regular cyclist were taken into consideration, but also those 
who do not or do not cycle regularly since the perceived fears and barriers might influence 
the overall results and has an impact on further analysis.  
 
Key factors for the survey were performed based on the literature discussion, individual 
observations and personal experience. The survey contained 30 questions divided into three 
parts: (1) cycling habits; (2) cycling attitudes; (3) e-cycling in Poland. Noteworthy, the survey 
included one question about individual’s lifestyle in order to investigate their possible 
influence for the future use of e-bikes. 
 
The main content of the survey was divided into two parts. The first measured attitudes 
according to the general cycling condition. The second part proposed a scenario in which the 
GoBike system (Copenhagen) was proposed as a newly implemented e-bike sharing scheme 
in each of these cities. The questionnaire was complemented by socio-economic 
characteristic. 
 
The opening questions in the first part regarded the frequency of use the transport modes: car 
(as a driver, passenger, taxi), public transportation, bicycle, e-bicycle and walk. The following

kind of cyclist (recreational / utilitarian), availability of bike sharing and its use, probable use 
of both bike sharing schemes (regular / electrically assisted) with assumption of the same fare 
for both , purpose that respondent is most likely to use a regular bicycle and e-bicycle or none 
(commuting to work / school; going for shopping and running errand; recreational cycling in

In order to understand what makes people cycle after migration, which allows determining 
how to create a cycling culture in Poland, this study aims to determine factors that 
positively influence the higher cycling share after migration. Among the set of questions 
about the traffic conditions in given country, three are selected to explain it briefly. 
Findings are in line with existing literature and prove that well-developed infrastructure, 
which determines safety, is one of the most important determinants to cycle. 74% admit 
that good quality and quantity of network positively influence their perceived safety and 
encourage the modal switch. Furthermore, almost 40% of respondents still feel unsafe in 
mixed traffic, where a bicycle path is not present and they need to share the lane with cars. 
On the other hand, an almost equal amount of respondents does not feel insecure in that 
kind of traffic conditions, which indicates that infrastructure is not necessarily the most 
essential element that determines the intention to cycle. The most crucial for this study is 
the result from question 13f, which clearly proves that the culture of drivers is the most 
essential element that makes people willing to cycle. Indeed, it is a serious problem in 
Poland. Broadly speaking, in the places where in general people do not cycle, drivers are 
not aware of cyclists in traffic. It is a serious issue in Poland since drivers do not respect 
cyclists and in most cases do not even consider their presence on the road, which leads to 
dangerous situations and significantly decrease the perceived safety. In places, where 
people are raised with omnipresent bicycles, they naturally gained the awareness, and it 
comes to them without any effort. This results proves how complex is the process of 
increasing cycling in total modal share and indicate that, to some extent, it is not enough to 
simply develop the infrastructure. The effort put in promoting cycling requires more 
sustained and complex actions regarding education and getting socially familiar with 
cyclists on the streets, especially among drivers. This is also strongly related to the need of 
safety as well as the need for proper interactions with other traffic participants and respect 
from other people, therefore: existence, relatedness and growth needs. In the figures 
below, distribution of answers for selected question is provided.

5.2 Explanatory factor analysis  
Explanatory factor analysis (EFA) is a statistical method performed to uncover the 
underlining structure of a relatively large set of variables by reducing the dimensions of 
the observations. This method assumes that any measured variable might be associated 
with any factor. EFA is commonly used by researchers to explore the underlining 
theoretical structure and identify the set of latent constructs of investigated phenomena.

EFA is most accurate when each of the factors is represented by multiple variables 
measured in the analysis, since single measured variables do not fully explain the 
correlations among measured variables. Moreover, EFA should be applied only when there 
is no prior hypothesis according to patterns of measured variables and possible factors. 
EFA is the first step before conducting confirmatory factor analysis and, since there is no 
predefined set of method, it requires a number of decision to make according to how the 
analysis will be conducted (George and Mallery, 2003). 
 
For the purpose of this study, the principal axis factoring method has been used. This 
method is a descriptive procedure, and it is the most appropriate when the goal is to focus 
on the collected sample instead of generalizing the results beyond it. The main advantage 
of this method in comparison to Maximum Likelihood is that it produces less improper 
solutions. On the other hand, principal axis factoring provides a limited range of indexes 
measuring goodness-of-fit. The structure of this method is set as follow: the first factor in 
principal axis factoring accounts for as much common variance as possible; the second 
factor accounts for the second most variance, the third for the third most variance, and so 
on (George and Mallery, 2003). 
 
In order to interpret the factor matrixes, factor rotation was performed. It aims to rotate 
factors in a multidimensional way to generate a solution that provides the best simple 
structure. There is an infinite number of the set of the factors that will explain the data with 
equal accuracy, and since a unique solution does not exist, a single solution from the set of 
infinite possibilities must be selected. There are two main types of factor rotation: (1) 
oblique rotation and (2) orthogonal. For the purpose of this study, the second one was 
applied.  
 
Orthogonal rotations require that factors are uncorrelated and therefore are less likely to 
generate outcomes with a simple structure. In this case, the varimax method has been 
applied, as in the most research. This allows simplifying the column of the factor matrix in 
a way where factors are clearly associated with each item.

It is recommended that the study sample should have at least 300 participants. Moreover, 
each variable that is associated with factor, should have minimum 5 to 10 observations. In 
order to prevent overestimation, cases with missing values are deleted during the process. 
However, EFA has some limitations. Most of all, proper naming of factors might be difficult, 
and therefore some name might not reflect all variables associated with the factor (Pearce 
and Yong, 2003).

5.3 Model estimation results   
After EFA, confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was conducted. CFA is a tool to verify the 
correctness of the structure of latent factor for the explaining correlation between 
indicators. At this stage, observed variables will be related to the latent factors based on 
the primary results from explanatory factor analysis. This part of the thesis provides the 
information about applied modeling performed with commercial software Mplus. In total, 
three models would be presented. For the study A, one model consisting from 2 equations 
is selected to present for the first part and two additional equations in the model for the 
second part. For the study B, also model with two equations is provided. However, it is 
important to emphasize that final models were selected after numerous attempts to 
achieve the best goodness of fit CFI score. During process, several configurations were 
tested but their CFI score was not high enough to assess its outcomes as reliable. For all of 
the models, delta parametrization was used.  
 
For the purpose of structural equation modelling, several goodness-of-fit statistics have 
been applied. Goodness of fit is defined as how proposed model fit to the collected set of 
observations. This study measures this value using two main indicators:  
(1) 
Comparative fit index, 
(2) 
Root mean square error of approximation. 
 
Root mean square error of approximation in general should be lower than 0,1, as lower 
value, as better fit. MacCallum et al. (1996) indicate the value of RMSEA below 0,01 as 
==PAGE:158==
 
157
excellent, 0,05 as good, and 0,08 as moderate. Comparative fit index exams the variance 
between data and model’s specification. Value of CFI ranges from 0 to 1. As higher the score, 
as better fit. Literature suggest that the value above 0,9 is consider as desired.

0.051).

6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION  
This research aims to determine what makes people cycle after the transition to a cycling-
oriented culture. The first study investigates migrants’ attitudes and the second one 
assumes the creation of cycling culture in Poland in line with real proposed changes by 
public authorities in each of cities. Four main hypothesis were formulated. An empirical 
research results in qualitative analysis and the semi equation models based on the theory 
of planned behavior by Ajzen and a new theory of needs by Alderfer. This chapter 
elaborates the results of this research and how these findings address the research 
hypotheses describe in chapter 3.  Policy recommendations as well as further research 
implications are discussed after concluding main findings of this research.  
 
6.1 Main results 
This research consists of two studies and addresses four hypotheses: (1) people cycle when 
cycling fits their needs; (2) modal switch to cycling after migration to cycling-oriented 
cultures is motivated by higher-order needs rather than lower-order needs; (3) if 
individual would start to cycle in one country, he or she will continue this behavior in other 
places; (4) using electric bike-sharing systems instead regular one (in the same fare) is 
motivated by lower-order needs rather than higher-order needs. The hypotheses were 
tested with data covering Polish migrants’ communities in Denmark, Germany, 
Netherlands, Sweden and United Kingdom (study A) and citizens of Poznań, Szczecin and 
Gorzów Wielkopolski (study B). The foregoing discussion has attempted to investigate if 
these hypotheses are true.  
 
In the first part of this research it is analyzed what influence people’s willingness to take 
part in “bike to work” campaign, weekly cycling distance and cycling frequency in the place 
of migration. Another measures investigated willingness to cycle in Poland and cycling 
frequency in Poland. Study B was focused on test what has an impact of e-cycling frequency 
and associated with e-cycling enthusiasm. These latent construct appear as the most 
relevant for creating the cycling culture in Poland.  
 
The analysis provided above proves that indeed people cycle when it fits their needs. 
Relatedness motivate people to cycle abroad the strongest with additional influence of 
growth and perceived safety whereas needs related to the most basic level of ERG model, 
==PAGE:178==
 
177
has the weakest influence among others. This analysis also confirmed the possible increase 
of cycling in travel patterns in Poland by at least 5%, according to the gathered data. 
Moreover, it is proved that people who did not cycle in Poland, indeed start to cycle abroad, 
where cycling conditions are more friendly. Cycling motivation increases along with the 
distance that the individual has to cycle. The willingness to cycle in Poland is associated 
both with willingness to cycle for daily purposes and with recreational cycling. 
 
Intentions to cycle frequently is linked with perceived cycling ease, pro-cycling attitudes 
and subjective norms in favor of cycling. Previous habits of cycling, both for recreation and 
utilitarian purposes, are also significant. People with middle education perceive more value 
in relatedness needs associated with cycling. It is clearly proved that people are motivated 
to cycling by much more than needs related with basic level of ERG model, but it is 
important to emphasize than needs of safety has a great influence on people’s intention to 
cycle. Furthermore, results from the empirical research and data analysis proved the 
hypothesis of the second study included in this thesis is confirmed. People associated 
cycling with good feelings, good memories and in general, cyclists in Poland have a 
relatively good image among society. The arguments given above prove that there indeed 
exists a correlation between modal switch after migration to differently oriented travel 
culture and higher order needs of the individual.  
 
Moreover, probably due to lack of experience, e-cycling is significantly less desirable 
transport mode that regular cycling. However, it is still an interesting option especially for 
the occasional use and, therefore, e-bike sharing schemes are desired. E-cycling is 
positively associated especially with socializing and commuting to work in the model 
describing e-cycling frequency. Socializing and going out as purposes for using e-bikes are 
also the most associated with enthusiasm related to e-cycling. It is clear that in case of e-
cycling, the great impact has previous experience with regular cycling, which strongly 
influence users’ perception. Models show gender differences in the attitudes towards 
cycling. Women are more likely to use e-bikes. Also recreational cyclists are associated 
more positively with e-cycling. Furthermore, as shown by results, motivation for cycling 
decreases with age and since e-bikes are appreciate more by older people, that might 
provide a good opportunity for further attempts to promoting e-cycling in Poland and 
increase cycling share in general. 
==PAGE:179==
 
178
 
Results show that people who live now in cycling-friendly environment are more sensitive 
for the value of safety. This is ensured by well-developed infrastructure in countries like 
Denmark or Netherlands and experience of cycling in favorable conditions does not 
positively influence the perceived safety in Poland. However, as more individual cycle, as 
less safety barriers can see in cycling. This is especially characteristic for utilitarian cyclists.  
People who cycle daily abroad, and therefore have a rich experience of cycling in favorable 
conditions, have negative perception of safety of cycling in Poland. The same apply to 
people who regularly use public transportation and those who used to travel by public 
transport in Poland.  
 
Ease of cycling and of e-cycling play a key role in people attitude to toward cycling. As easier 
they perceive it, as more they are willing to cycle. Moreover, in terms of location in Poland, 
Poznań is associated with the most positive feelings about cycling and perceive as the most 
cycle-friendly city. Interestingly, although Szczecin has significantly more developed 
cycling infrastructure, its citizens in compare to other case studies in this research, perceive 
it as the least cycling friendly city and among other. Moreover, they are willing to cycle less 
than others and especially in terms of social value, they are linked negatively with cycling.  
 
Empirical research and mathematical modeling lead to the conclusion that all four 
hypotheses are confirmed both by preliminary analysis of the data collected in the survey 
and mathematical model results. The SEM model fits the collected data. Clearly, some of the 
propositions that have been offered are not entirely unique, but this study enhances 
academic understanding of the factors associated with the cycling commuters’ behavior. 
 
6.2 Policy implications  
The implications of this research are manifold. Scholar evidence about cycling benefits for 
the society, economy, traffic and livability are undeniable. A better understanding of 
people’s attitudes toward cycling as a mode of transport rather than sport is critical 
because it is required to develop effective policy strategies to increase the cycling share in 
Poland. In emerging cycling environments, public authorities play the key role and are 
responsible for ensuring the continuation of the trend and quality of development. There 
is still a lot to desire in Poland. The arguments given above prove that infrastructure (e.g. 
==PAGE:180==
 
179
cycling paths, outdoors facilities) is an essential ingredient for stimulating the development 
of cycling culture. Quality and quantity of infrastructure have a direct impact on the 
perceived safety of bicycle use when is well-planed and well-maintained. The majority of 
respondents admit that they would cycle due to monetary and time savings. Therefore 
benefits of reduced travel time for cyclist should be taken into consideration during the 
planning process as well as improved comfort and safety. It is proved, that it is worth to 
invest in the development of cycling culture since it provides real financial benefits for the 
country. Moreover, even though perceived as expensive, the cycling investment might be 
even 100 times cheaper than the construction of highways and still provide advantages for 
the traffic (Cycling Embassy of Denmark’s 2015).  Encouraging the use of alternative modes 
of transport (transportation demand management) is one of the ways to relieve congestion 
which is a serious problem of car-dominated societies such as Poland.  
 
Results of this study are promising and indicate the huge potential of future cycling culture 
in Polish cities. However, education of society in this field is still insufficient and needs 
more attention to increase people awareness, which applies especially to drivers. Providing 
education tools focused more on cycling during future drivers‘ training might result in 
more cycling-friendly conditions even if the infrastructure is not well-developed. Creation 
of cycling culture is a complex and long run process. Results show that people who have a 
driving license negatively associated safety of cycling in Poland. That is the evedidence, that 
there is still a lot to do in terms of drives’ education. Therefore it should be well planned. 
There are numerous of good examples that Polish public authorities can use as 
benchmarks. Tangible benefits of solutions implemented in Netherlands, Germany and 
Denmark might encourage to cycle people who are willing to, but are concerned about their 
comfort and safety.  
 
Cycling motivation decreases with age, therefore attempts to increase the cycling share in 
total modal share in Poland should be perform as soon as possible. Encouraging to cycle in 
early age provides advantages for cycling as an adult, which goes in line with literature (Dill 
and Voros 2007). People who are 16 years old and older are associated with negative 
relation toward cycling attitudes, therefore the effort should be make in their childhood in 
order to prevent this trend in future. A good way of prevent it is to promote e-cycling that 
is associated more positively with people in higher age. Moreover, women perceive cycling 
==PAGE:181==
 
180
as less safety, which leads to conclusions, that in the process of policy making, special 
attention should be dedicated to maximize their feeling of safety in order to encourage 
them co cycle. On the other hand, women perceive more instrumental values associated 
with e-cycling and proper campaigns aiming to encourage women to use e-bike might be a 
way to increase number of cycling women in Poland. People who live now in cycling-
developed environment perceive cycling in Poland significantly less safety than those who 
live for example in United Kingdom, where cycling culture is emerging as in Poland. It 
requires effort to keep their habit also on Polish streets and those should be in special 
concern of policy makers. This apply especially to people who cycle daily abroad.  
 
Results from this study clearly show that in order to increase the cycling share in Poland, it 
is not enough to simply promote cycling. Additional effort should be made to discourage 
people to use their cars. Habit of driving frequently in the past and using car daily have 
strong negative impact on intention to cycling and perceived values of cycling in most of 
the factors. That proves that in order to achieve a real change in travel pattern in Poland, 
not only promoting modal shift by itself is required, but also make traffic for cars less 
convenient in favor of cycling.  
 
The foregoing discussion has attempted to clarify the dimensions in which policy-making 
should be performed. Results show that there are much more determinants to cycle than 
simply well-developed infrastructure. Therefore, public campaigns to encourage people to 
cycle, implementing the “bike to work” programs in workplaces, educational programs in 
schools and promotional events, are necessary. Linking cycling to health benefits in such 
campaigns might be also an efficient way to encourage more commuters to cycle. These will 
perform as a starting point to stimulate the development of a culture that is already 
emerging. There is still a lot of effort required until real changes would go on. However, the 
social, environmental, economy, health and traffic benefits that cycling entails are definitely 
worth to make such effort in Poland. Therefore, public authorities should increase actions 
taken toward facilitating cycling, but what is even more important than marginalization of 
cycling, is to make sure, that ongoing changes are consistent, and deliberate.

6.3 Further research  
Some restrictions on this study and areas for future research should be mentioned. First of 
all, the collected data does not fully represent the entire significant Polish migrants’ 
community abroad.  Further research might include the mode choice models of migrants. 
Interesting would to perform and experiment with GPS tracking routes to investigate the 
real purposes and destination that migrants follow abroad in terms of their culture 
familiarity and to measure which needs precisely are associated with them with cycling. 
Moreover, attempt to investigate their travel behavior after migration (transition back to 
origin travel culture) might provide a great insight of the real impact that living in different 
transport environment provides. For this purpose, Poland has a great potential due to a 
large number of people who live abroad and desire to come back to their origin country. 
On the other, investigating the electric bikes in terms of the needs that motivate people to 
use it in societies where it is more popular (Netherlands, Germany, China) might be a 
valuable fulfillment to clarify the dimensions in which promoting such a mode should be 
perform in countries like Poland and other, where cycling culture emerging as well.
