Abstract
Aluminium is found naturally in food or it is added as a food additives. Because aluminium
is found in food, humans are exposed to aluminium. EFSA have established a health based
guidance value, a TWI, of 1 mg Al/kg bw/week. A new method (prEN 17264) for determina-
tion of aluminium by ICP-MS in food has been suggested by CEN. This study has therefore
aimed to validate the method prEN 17264, use the method for determination of aluminium in
diﬀerent food items and perform a risk assessment of aluminium. The method was validated
and the following performance characteristic was found: speciﬁcity, linearity, precision (re-
peatability of 1.4% to 11% and within-laboratory reproducibility of 2.8% to 2.9%), trueness
(89% to 102%), LOD ( 1.7 mg/kg ) and LOQ( 5.5 mg/kg). Furthermore, the method was val-
idated in the concentration area from 1.7 to 1320 mg/kg. Applying the validated method on
selected food items it was found that spinach powders and tea had the highest concentrations
of aluminium (>1000 mg/kg) while baby food and fresh spinach had the lowest aluminium
concentrations (<7.3 mg/kg). The exposure estimation for three diﬀerent scenarios showed
that consumption of two diﬀerent baby foods did not lead to an exposure exceeding the
TWI. Furthermore, seven spinach shakes, with or without cocoa, together with the mean
aluminium exposure resulted in a total aluminium exposure above TWI. The third scenario
was a worst-case scenario based on four diﬀerent tea types and showed an exceedance of the
TWI occurs when consuming 28 cups per week.

1
Introduction
Aluminium is found naturally in the environment and is the third most abundant element in
the earth crust [1]. Aluminium is therefore a natural part of food since it is absorbed through
the soil and water. Furthermore, aluminium is added to food through additives. It has been
reported that the greatest contributor to intake of aluminium is food additives, but natural
occurrence of aluminium in food and migration from food contact materials also contribute
to the aluminium intake in humans [1],[2]. It has been suggested that aluminium has diﬀerent
toxicological properties. Aluminium has been shown to have an impact on nervous system
disorders, metabolic bone diseases, hepatotoxicity and to play a key role in Alzheimer’s dis-
ease in humans [3]. Furthermore, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) concluded
that aluminium did not show genotoxic and carcinogenic eﬀects relevance for humans ex-
posed through food [1].
There is no legislation of aluminium as a food contaminant the European Union (EU) [4],
but the Joint Expert Committee for Food Additives (JECFA) have established a provisional
tolerable weekly intake (PTWI) of 2 mg Al/kg bw/week [5] while EFSA have established
a tolerable weekly intake (TWI) of 1 mg Al/kg bw/week [1]. EFSA generally found values
below the TWI for adults while children were exposed to an aluminium concentration above
the TWI [1]. Because of the interest for aluminium in food in the past years, a new method
for determination of aluminium in food has been suggested. The new method, named prEN
17264, for determination of aluminium by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry
(ICP-MS) has been developed and proposed to Technical Committee CEN/TC 275. The
new features of the method are digestion with nitric acid (HNO3) instead of hydroﬂuoric
acid (HF) and a higher digestion temperature. Until now a multi-element method has been
used for determination, but this new method consists only of a procedure for digestion and
quantitative determination by ICP-MS of aluminium.
2
Aim of Project
The aim of this master thesis is to validate a method for determination of aluminium in food
by using ICP-MS. Furthermore, the method will be used to determine the aluminium content
in diﬀerent relevant food items. This master thesis aims to achieve the following:
• Validation of method prEN 17264 for determination of aluminium according to DTU
Food validation SOP [6] and NMKL’s procedure for method validation [7]
• Determine aluminium content in relevant food items
• Perform a risk assessment of dietary aluminium exposure

3
Aluminium
Aluminium is a non-essential element which occurs naturally in the environment. It is the
third most abundant element on earth after oxygen and silicon [8].
Aluminium is often
found as alumino-silicates, hydroxides, phosphates, sulphates and cryolite. The occurrence
of aluminium in the environment can be due to natural processes such as volcanic activity,
weathering of rocks and soil erosion. Furthermore, anthropogenic activities can release alu-
minium to the environment. These activities can be mining and production of aluminium
metal and other aluminium compounds [1].
3.1
Physical and Chemical Properties
Aluminium is a silvery, white and ductile metal. Furthermore, aluminium is non combustible
and non-magnetic [1]. Aluminium is the thirteenth element in the periodic table. Aluminium
is found naturally but because of the reactivity of aluminium it can not be found as a free
compound in the nature and exists only as Al3+ [1]. The chemical and physical properties

Risk Analysis
The aim of a risk analysis is to develop an estimate of the risks to human health and safety.
Furthermore, the risk analysis consists of identiﬁcation of the risk, implementation of control
measures and communication with stakeholders deciding how the measure should be applied.
A risk analysis is used to improve or develop standards, address food safety issues and
contribute to better food safety and human health [15]. A risk analysis consists of three
parts: risk assessment, risk management and risk communication, as it can be seen in ﬁgure
1.
Figure 1: Risk analysis modiﬁed from [16].

The communication between the three parts is essential for the risk analysis. The three
diﬀerent parts of the risk analysis have diﬀerent roles. The risk assessment is done by risk
assessors and is a scientiﬁc process consisting of four steps: hazard identiﬁcation, hazard
characterization, exposure assessment and risk characterization. The hazard identiﬁcation
should answer two questions [16]:
1. The nature of any health hazard to humans that a chemical may pose
2. The circumstances under which an identiﬁed hazard may be expressed.
To conduct a hazard identiﬁcation a variety of data from observations in humans to diﬀerent
animal studies are used to identify any toxicity or adverse health eﬀects [16].
The hazard characterization describes the critical eﬀect of the hazard. If possible, it should
include a dose-response relationship. Furthermore, the hazard characterization establishs
health-based guidance values such as tolerable intake (TI) or acceptable daily intake (ADI)
[16]. The exposure assessment is an evaluation of the occurrence and concentrations of the
chemical in the food, how much of the food that is consumed and if the consumers have
a high intake of the food. Usually diﬀerent scenarios are used to estimate the intake and
exposure e.g. average consumers versus high consumers or adults versus infants [16]. The
risk characterization is the last step in the risk assessment. Here all the information from
the hazard identiﬁcation, hazard characterization and exposure assessment are compared
and an estimate of the potential risk to human health is given. The risk characterization
must describe all assumptions, uncertainties and lack of science based knowledge [16]. The
risk assessment is the scientiﬁc basis for which decisions the risk management should take [16].
The risk management is done by a risk manager who needs to decide what to do for protecting
the consumers and their health. The risk manager must compare the risk assessment with
other policy alternatives and from this decide if any action should be taken [16]. The risk
communication is the part where the information and options about the risk, the risk related
factors and the risk perceptions, is communicated to the consumers, the industry and other
interested parties [16].

Chemical Risk Assessment of Aluminium
In the following a risk assessment of aluminium is done. The risk assessment is based on the
ﬁndings from EFSA in 2008 [1] and Vitenskapskomiteen for mat og miljø (VKM) in 2013 [4].
4.1.1
Hazard Identiﬁcation
Aluminium is a metal which occurs naturally in the environment. Aluminium is found in
drinking water, fruits, vegetables, grains, seeds and meat. The most common exposure ways

4.1.2
Hazard Characterisation
4.1.2.1
Toxicokinetics
The two main ways for aluminium to enter the body are oral or dermal. In this section oral
intake is described and discussed based on EFSA’s report from 2008 [1] and VKM’s report
from 2013 [4].
Absorption
The oral absorption of aluminium is poor. Only 0.1-0.8 % of aluminium is usually absorbed
[4]. The absorption of aluminium depends on diﬀerent factors such as the type of aluminium
compound, solubility and co-administration with water or food. When aluminium enters the
body, it ﬁrst passes through the gut lumen where the pH is below 5. Here the aluminium ions
are mainly [Al(H2O)6]3+] [4]. Afterwards, the food passes through the stomach and enters
the intestine, where the pH increases to a neutral pH, which results in insoluble complexes of
aluminium with hydroxide. In the intestine aluminium hydroxide is formed and it is excreted
through the faeces [4].
Distribution
The knowledge of the distribution of aluminium in humans is limited. In several animal
studies it has been found that transferrin is the main carrier of Al3+ in plasma while some
are bound to citrate. Uptake of aluminium is relatively slow in tissue and organs while the
uptake from aluminium bound to transferrin occurs by using a transferrin receptormediated
endocytosis.
Aluminium enter the brain through the blood brain barrier or the choriod
plexus. Further Al3+ accumulates in the lysosomes, cell nucleaus and chromatin [4]. Animal
experiments have shown that several factors can modulate the distribution of aluminium.
Deﬁciency of calcium and magnesium can result in accumulation in the brain and bones.
It is also shown that iron status has a negative eﬀect on accumulation in the tissue. Fur-
thermore, citrate and ﬂuoride reduce tissue accumulation and increase the renal excretion [4].
Metabolism
The metabolism of aluminium is not well studied, but it is assumed that aluminium is present
in the body as free ions, low molecular-weight complexes, physically bound macromolecular
complexes and covalently bound macromolecular complexes [4].
Excretion
Aluminium absorbed in the blood is mainly eliminated by the kidneys and excreted in the
urine but can also be excreted in the bile. It is shown that the concentration of aluminium

and also accumulation of insoluble aluminium compounds [4].
4.1.2.2
Toxicodynamics
Aluminium has been studied for diﬀerent toxicological properties. In the following, diﬀerent
studies on diﬀerent properties are summarised.
Acute Toxicity
The acute oral toxicity of aluminium of diﬀerent inorganic aluminium salts has been studied
for rats and mice and a LD50 from 162 to 750 mg Al/kg bw for rats and 164 to 980 mg Al/kg
bw for mice. The LD50 after intraperitoneal administration is much lower (25-82 mg Al/kg
bw for rats and 40-133 mg Al/kg bw for mice). This suggests that the toxicity of aluminium
depends on the systemic exposure and the bioavailability [1].
Subchronic Toxicity
An experiment with rats where they were given water with aluminium nitrate for 28 days
resulted in mild histopathalogical change in the spleen and liver [1]. Another rat study with
similar dosages of aluminium showed no histopathological changes but a decrease in body
weight [1]. Other studies with rats given aluminium sulphate or potassium aluminium sul-
phate also showed mild histopathological eﬀects in the kidney and liver [1]. Also, a study with
beagle dogs treated with sodium aluminium phosphate showed histopathological changes in
liver and kidney [1].
Genotoxicity
Studies with bacterial and mammalian cell systems showed that aluminium compounds were
non-mutagenic but some compounds produced DNA damage. Experiments found several
indirect mechanisms which can explain the variety of genotoxic eﬀects caused by aluminium
salts. EFSA noted that indirect mechanisms of genotoxicity only occur at high levels of
exposure and concluded that genotoxic eﬀects do not have relevance for humans exposed
through food [1].
Carcinogenicity
The carcinogenic data of aluminium compounds is limited. The studies are old and do not
contain details about the experiments. Furthermore, the International Agency for Research
on Cancer (IARC) has concluded that aluminium itself is not human carcinogenic. Based on
the limited studies and IARCs conclusions, EFSA concluded that aluminium is unlikely to
be carcinogenic for humans exposed through food [1].

Reproductive and Developmental Toxicity
Studies in mice showed that aluminium can produce testicular toxicity, decreased sperm qual-
ity and reduced fertility in male mice. Further studies showed reduced testicular weight in
male rabbits and dogs after daily intake of aluminium compounds. No eﬀects on female fer-
tility in rats were found but only two studies were available on female reproductive toxicity [1].
Neurotoxicity and Developmental Neurotoxicity
It has been suggested that aluminium can be implicated in Alzheimer’s disease and other
neurodegenerative diseases. However, these suggestions are controversial due to lack of ev-
idence.
Some studies of aluminium in water showed association between aluminium and
Alzheimer’s disease, while others did not. Both the German Federal Institute for Risk As-
sessment (BfR) [1] and the French Agency for Food, Environmental and Occupational Health
Safety (ANSES)[17] concluded that there was no relationship between food, including wa-
ter, and Alzheimer disease. From these authorities’ conclusion and the available data EFSA
concluded that exposure of aluminium through food does not aﬀect the development of
Alzheimer’s disease [1].
Tolerable Weekly Intake of Aluminium
In 2007 JECFA established a PTWI of 1 mg Al/kg bw/week [18]. This value was based
on several studies of the reproductive system and developing nervous system, where an in-
suﬃcient dose-response relationship was used [18]. In 2008 EFSA established a TWI based
on diﬀerent dietary animal studies in mice, rats and dogs [1]. In these studies there were
a lack of a dose-response relationship. EFSA based the TWI on two diﬀerent TWI values
based on a no observed adverse eﬀect level (NOAEL) and a lowest observed adverse eﬀect
level (LOAEL). The lowest NOAEL was 10 mg Al/kg bw/day, while the lowest LOAEL was
50 mg Al/kg bw/day. Both values were based on neurodevelopmental toxicity in mice. The
TWI was estimated to 1 mg Al/kg bw/week. In table 4 the studies of the diﬀerent authorities
and the values used for establishing the TWI or the PTWI can be found. Here it can be
seen that EFSA found a TWI equal to JECFA from 2007. JECFA values were also based on
several animal studies were the dose-response relationship was limited.
In 2012 JECFA decided to reevaluate their PTWI due to new available animal studies [5]. A
new study of developmental neurotoxicity resulted in an appropriated NOAEL of 30 mg/kg
bw/day. This resulted in a new PTWI of 2 mg/kg bw/week for all aluminium compounds in
food, including additives [5] and the PTWI from 2007 was withdrawn.

4.1.4
Risk Characterisation
EFSA and JECFA have established health based guidance values of aluminium in food as a
TWI or PTWI value. TWI is an estimate of the amount of a potential harmful substance
that a person can be exposed to weekly over a lifetime without appreciable health risks [19].
If the TWI is exceeded it represents a reduced safety margin and therefore the TWI is not a
threshold for toxicity [4].
The exposure estimates from EFSA and VKM were generally values below the TWI set by
EFSA and the PTWI set by JECFA. EFSA found that children from 1.5-4.5 years in France
had an exposure to aluminium above the PTWI and the exposure for 3-18 years olds in UK
was above the TWI. VKM found that highly exposed infants and two year old children had
an exposure above the TWI but below the PTWI.

to aluminium in Denmark is assumed to be equal to the exposure of the European population.

5
Analysis of Aluminium
Analysis of aluminium in foods consists of two steps. The ﬁrst step is digestion of the samples
and the second step is determination by ICP-MS. In the following diﬀerent digestion methods
and ICP-MS methods are described.
5.1
Digestion
Before trace elements can be detected by ICP-MS the samples are digested. Digestion is a
process where the samples are decomposed and dissolved by exposing the samples to high
temperatures (>180 °C) under pressure.
Digestion methods have been developed through the years. In 2002 DS/EN 13656 was the
only digestion procedure available. The sample amount added to the vessels are between 0.2
g to 0.5 g depending on the sample type. To the vessels three diﬀerent reagents are added
separately: 6 ml Hydrochloric acid (HCl), 2 ml Nitric acid (HNO3) and 2 ml Hydroﬂuoric
acid (HF) [21]. The problem with this digestion method is the use of HF. HF is a dangerous
and toxic chemical. Fluoride accumulates in the bones and can develop life threatening dam-
age [22]. Inhalation is corrosive and toxic and can e.g. lead to cramps, bloody vomiting, cold
sweats, inﬂammation and odeama in lungs, larynx and bronchi. Long-term inhalation can
damage lungs, liver and kidneys. Skin contact causes painful burns which will heal slowly.
It penetrates deep and causes tissue damage. In contact with the eyes it causes burns and
edema of the eyelids and can cause blindness. If HF is swallowed it can be fatal [22]. When
using HF personal protection must be used. Fume cupboards, respiratory protective device,
safety goggles, gloves, protective face shields and apron must be used [22].
As seen above it is diﬃcult, demanding and time consuming to use HF. Therefore a new
digestion method for trace elements in foodstuﬀs, EN 13805, was developed in 2014 [23]. This
method suggests a sample amount of 0.2g, 1 ml water and 3 ml HNO3. This procedure can be
used for almost all food samples but it also describes that some foodstuﬀmay require addition
of HF. Furthermore, the digestion temperature must be at least 180 °C [23]. The technical
committee CEN/TC 275 have now suggested a new digestion method for determination of
aluminium in food. The method, prEN 17264, is still under development but a working
document is available [24]. This method is relatively similar to EN 13805. The diﬀerence
between the methods are the amount of HNO3, water and the digestion temperature. This
method suggest a sample amount of maximum 0.5g, 2.5 ml water and 2.5 ml HNO3. The
digestion temperature must be minimum 200 °C [24]. Diﬀerent digestion methods have been
used in studies to determine trace elements in diﬀerent food items. In table 7 selected studies
using diﬀerent food items and digestion methods can be seen.

5.2
ICP-MS
ICP-MS is a technology used to determine most of the elements in the periodic table. The
ICP-MS system consists of ﬁve main parts:
• Introduction system
• Ionization / plasma
• Focalisation / vacuum interface
• Separation / ion optics and mass analyzer
• Detection / detector
Sample introduction and plasma is operated at atmospheric pressure and focalisation, sep-
aration and detection is operated under vacuum. In this project a iCAPQ ICP-MS from
ThermoFisher was used. In ﬁgure 2 a sketch of the ICP-MS instrument can be found. It
illustrates where the ﬁve main parts in the instrument are.
Figure 2: The ﬁve parts of the ICP-MS instrument. The ﬁgure is modiﬁed from [32].
Page 22 of 61
==PAGE:24==

The sample must be in gas or vapour form before introducing it to the plasma. Therefore,
liquid samples are converted into an aerosol by using a nebulizer which is a small device that
uses supersonic expansion of gas to turn the liquid into an aerosol [33]. After the nebulizer
a spray chamber is placed, where larger droplets are removed to waste, and only smaller
droplets below 10 µm enter the plasma [33].
In the plasma, four diﬀerent processes, where the particles are transferred into ions, take
place. In ﬁgure 3 a schematic illustration of the processes in ICP-MS is presented.
Figure 3: Processes in the ICP-MS from sample introduction to mass analysis [34].
The plasma consists of ions, electrons and atoms at an extremely high temperature (up to
10,000 K)[34]. A high temperature is used to make the transition of atoms to ions easier.
The most common plasma is the argon plasma because it forms single charged positive ions,
which are simpler to generate and interpret in the mass spectra [35]. The plasma needs a
lot of energy (up to 1600 W [34]) which is provided by a radio frequency (RF) generator.
The RF generator sends a high amount of power signal through a load coil which is placed
around the torch. The formation of the plasma is done when electrons from a spark source
ionize argon atoms within the torch gas ﬂows. These ions collide with other argon atoms
and cause a cascade of ionization which will form the plasma [35]. The plasma will form a
magnetic ﬁeld with the opposite direction than the RF generator which is synchronized, so
the magnetic ﬁeld from the plasma has minimal eﬀect and therefore the RF generator can
sustain it easier.
The next step is a crucial step of ICP-MS. Here the sample enters the high vacuum region
by the sample interface. In ﬁgure 4 the sample interface and the diﬀerent parts it consists of
can be seen.
Page 23 of 61
==PAGE:25==

Master Thesis
Figure 4: The ICP-MS sample interface [35].
The sample interface includes two water cooled cones. These cones make sure that the ions
enter the high vacuum part. To ensure the ions will be transferred to the high vacuum re-
gions, a series of vacuum chambers are used to go from atmospheric pressure to high vacuum
[34]. The ﬁrst pressure decreases leads to supersonic expansion of the ion beam which pulls
the ions into the high vacuum region through a skimmer cone. Behind the skimmer cone a
negatively charged extraction lens is placed which results in the movement of the ions into
the high vacuum region [35].
After the ions have passed the high vacuum region the ions are separated according to their
mass to charge ratios (m/z). The separation is done by a mass analyzer. The ICP-MS used
in these experiments use a quadrupole mass analyzer. There exist three diﬀerent types of
mass analyzers: quadrupoles, magnetic sector or time-of-ﬂight.
The most common mass
analyzer for ICP-MS is the quadrupole [34]. A quadrupole consists of four electrodes/poles,
two positively charged and two negatively charged. These poles create an electric ﬁeld where
the sample ions pass through, obtain energy and accelerate [35]. The electrical ﬁeld sepa-
rates the ions due to their m/z ratio [34]. Only one speciﬁc m/z is stable at speciﬁc settings
and is hence the only mass which can pass through the quadrupol to the electron multiplier
detector. The principles of the electron multiplier can be seen in ﬁgure 5.
Figure 5: Illustration of how the electron multiplier works [34].
Page 24 of 61
==PAGE:26==

As ﬁgure 5 illustrates, one positively charged ion is deﬂected onto the ﬁrst dynode. This
releases several free electrons which are repelled to the next dynode. Every time an electron
hits the second dynode it releases several electrons. When the cascade of electrons reaches
the ﬁnal dynode, the pulse is large enough to be measured as an ion count [34].
One of the major challenges of using ICP-MS is interferences. Interferences can be caused by
other atoms or polyatoms that overlap the same signal as the analyte resulting in a higher
signal than normal. Therefore, the interferences must be handled with to get the right result.
This can e.g. be done by applying collision cell technology. There are three main processes
which can occur in the collision cell: simple chemical reaction, charge transfer and energy
ﬁltering [36]. The simple chemical reaction and charge transfer reaction will not remove all
interferences, therefore energy ﬁltering can be eﬀective to remove the remaining interference
[36]. This can be done by applying the kinetic energy discrimination (KED) mode, where
helium is used as cell gas. The advantages of using helium as cell gas is that it does not
degrade the signal sensitivity because of helium’s low mass and that helium is an inert gas
and will not react with the analyte. In ﬁgure 6 an illustration of how KED works can be
found.
Figure 6: Illustration of how KED works from [36].
KED works by creating an energy barrier between the collision cell and the quadrupole. The
exit is more positively charged than the entrance of the collision cell and therefore the ions
are forced backwards into the cell [35]. The interferences pass through the collision cell and
collide with the He atoms. When they collide both the analytes and the interferences lose
some kinetic energy. The polyatomic ions have a larger collision cross section area than the
analyte ions and therefore they will undergo more collisions through the collision cell [36].
When the polyatomic ions and the analyte ions reach the end of the collision cell the poly-
atomic ions have lost more kinetic energy than the analyte ions as a result, only the analyte
ions will pass through the barrier and further to the detector [36]. Interferences relevant for
Page 25 of 61

Master Thesis
detection of aluminium are 12C15N+ or 13C14N+ [37].
Table 7 shows diﬀerent ICP-MS methods used for determination of aluminium in diﬀer-
ent food items. Besides ICP-MS the other methods used are single-particle spICP-MS and
ICP-Optical Emission Spectrometry (OES/AES). spICP-MS is used to characterize popula-
tions of nanoparticles in aqueous solutions [38]. By using spICP-MS the mean size of the
nanoparticles, size distribution, nanoparticle number concentration and nanoparticles mass
concentration can be measured [38]. ICP-OES is a diﬀerent technique which uses emission
spectra of elements excited within a high-temperature plasma to analyse the sample. The
plasma separates the elements in correlation with speciﬁc wavelengths of light. Every element
has a unique emission spectrum which is used to identify and quantify the elements [39].

Validation
By validating an analytical method information of performance characteristic is obtained.
The parameters considered in this validation are, speciﬁcity, selectivity, linearity, precision,
trueness, limit of detection (LOD), limit of quantiﬁcation (LOQ) and the measurement range.
The method used in this experiment is validated in accordance to DTU Food method vali-
dation SOP [6] and the Nordic Committee on Food Analysis (NMKL) validation procedure
for chemical analytical methods [7].
6.1
Speciﬁcity
The deﬁnition of speciﬁcity is the ability of an analytical method to distinguish the analyte
to be determined from other substance in the sample [7]. The speciﬁcity is used to describe
if the method is able to measure the intended analyte and if it is the analyte producing the
response.
6.2
Linearity
Linearity is evaluated by a calibration curve. A calibration curve is a function which describes
the correlation between the content of the analyte and the response. The calibration curve
consists of minimum ﬁve concentration levels including zero. After the analysis the curve is
evaluated based on the linearity, the equation, residual plot and the correlation coeﬃcient
(R2) [6].

Determination of aluminium is done by ﬁrst digesting the samples followed by detection by
ICP-MS. To give an overview of the working process in the laboratory a ﬂowsheet has been
made (ﬁgure 7). In the following sections the digestion method and ICP-MS method are
described in detail.
Figure 7: Overview of the laboratory work for analysing aluminium in food samples.
7.4.1
Digestion
Two diﬀerent digestion methods were used to compare the ratio between water and HNO3
and the inﬂuence of the temperature. The ﬁrst method is a digestion method used at DTU
for digestion of food samples prior to detection of diﬀerent trace elements, which is based on
an existing European standard method (EN 13805). The second method is a new method
speciﬁcally developed for determination of aluminium by German laboratories and proposed
as a new European standard method for determination of aluminium in food (prEN 17264).
The digestion oven had the following settings: Power of 1400 W, maximum pressure of 80
bar, maximum temperature of 280 °C. The digestion takes 40 minutes followed by cooling
for approximately 15 minutes. It takes the oven 25 min to reach the maximum pressure.
In table 8 the two diﬀerent digestion methods are present. The diﬀerence between the two
method procedures is the amount of water and HNO3 added.

7.4.2
ICP-MS
The ICP-MS used was an iCAPQ (Thermoﬁscher scientiﬁc) with a Cetac ASX-520 autosam-
pler. The ICP-MS had the following settings: Selected elements: Al, As, Cd, Pb, plasma
power 1550 W, helium gas ﬂow (KED) of 4.9 l/min, nebulizer ﬂow rate of 1.08 l/min, plasma
ﬂow rate of 15 l/min, number of sweeps 10 and number of replicates was 3. Internal standard
with rhodium (Rh) of 10 µg/l was prepared with 2 % HNO3 and was externally added to the
samples via the peristaltic pump of the ICP-MS.
A calibration curve was made from a multi element standard solution containing 29 elements
and 2% HNO3. The calibration curve was made in the concentration area from 0 to 400 µg/l.
The stock solution was dilution to two diﬀerent concentrations. In appendix A information
of the dilution of the stock solution and how the calibration curve was made can be found.

8.4.1
Exposure Estimation
The risk assessment in this work consist of an exposure assessment and a risk characterization
of the exposure. In the assessment, EFSA’s TWI of 1 mg/kg bw/week is used. Exposure
estimations are done for three diﬀerent scenarios to see the exposure from single food items
and how much the food items add or contribute to the overall exposure of aluminium from
food. The ﬁrst scenario is an infant scenario with consumption of baby food. The second
scenario is a ﬁtness scenario based on consumption of ﬁtness shakes and the third scenario
is for tea consuming people. The exposure is calculated based on equation 8:
Exposure = CAlinthefood · mfood
mBodyweight

Risk Characterisation
From the three exposure scenarios described above, it was found that consumption of the
two baby foods did not lead to an exposure exceeding the TWI. Furthermore, intake of seven
shakes (with or without cocoa) will contribute to a total aluminium exposure above the TWI.
Having an exposure exceeding the TWI for a longer period may aﬀect the health. Intake of
28 cups of four diﬀerent tea types leads to a total aluminium exposure close to or exceeding
the TWI. However, this was a worst-case scenario and in reality, these exposure estimations
on tea would be lower if including an infusion factor.

Conclusion
Validation of the digestion and ICP-MS method presented in this study fulﬁlls the require-
ments set by DTU Food and NMKL based on the validation parameters: speciﬁcity, selectiv-
ity, linearity, precision, trueness, LOD, LOQ and measurement range. The method provided
an accurate determination of aluminium in several food samples (reference materials and
selected food items). Furthermore, LOD and LOQ were improved by removal of the pump
placed on top of the HNO3 bottle.
Applying the validated method on selected food items it was seen that the highest concen-
tration of aluminium was found in spinach powders and four tea types (>1000 mg/kg) while
the lowest concentration was found in two baby foods and fresh spinach (<7.3 mg/kg).
EFSA has established a TWI of 1 mg Al/kg bw/week. The exposure estimation found that
consumption of two diﬀerent types of baby food did not exceed the TWI and did not pose
a health risk for infants. Furthermore, seven spinach shakes with or without cocoa sums up
to a total aluminium exposure above the TWI and can pose a health risk if consumped over
a longer period. Furthermore, a worst-case scenario of four diﬀerent tea types showed an
excess of the TWI if consuming 28 cups per week.
10
Future Perspectives
Based on the knowledge obtained from this report, it is suggested that the validation is
performed again. The validation must be conducted again where the pump of the HNO3 (67-
69%) bottle is removed. This decreases the aluminium content in the samples and therefore
it could be possible to use wheat ﬂour and durum ﬂour and hopefully get recoveries between
80-120%. Furthermore, it is suggested to use a digestion oven with better temperature con-
trol to treat all the samples with the same temperature through the digestion.
By assuming that the method is eﬃcient for determination of aluminium in food, the food
items selected for determination can either be retained or changed. If keeping the set of food
samples as used in this report, it is suggested to improve the data set by including more
samples of the same food item. If making a new set of food samples for determination of
aluminium, it is suggested to select wide number of common food items. From this data set,
exposure estimations of the dietary aluminium exposure in the Danish population can be
done.

Aim
The aim of this validation is to validate a new suggested CEN method prEN 17264 for
determination of aluminium in food in correlation to DTU’s SOP for validation[6] and NMKL
procedure for validation [7]. The validation consists of the following parameters: speciﬁcity,
selectivity, linearity, precision, trueness, limit of detection (LOD) and limit of quantiﬁcation
(LOQ).
Method Description
The digestion procedure is: 0.2 g dry sample is weighed in and 2.5 ml water are added. Add
2.5 ml nitric acid, close the digestion vessel and start the digestion. The digestion is done
with a maximum temperature at 280 °C. After the digestion the samples are analysed by
ICP-MS.
