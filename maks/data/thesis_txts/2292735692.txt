Abstract 
The motivation for doing this thesis was to find out, to what extend transportation affects the quality 
of life of people in cities in Denmark. 
“Quality of Life” (QoL) and “transportation” are concepts that have existed for thousands of years, 
but as to separate concepts. However, within recent decades an interest of the correlation between 
transportation and QoL has grown massively. This correlation is concerned with how transport 
systems affect the livability of a city. 
Liveability is defined as “suitable for human living”, and it is affected by the public safety, 
environmental quality, accessibility and mobility within the city - affecting the QoL of citizens 
(Merriam-Webster, 2011).  
City planning nowadays is focused on the human factor, which focuses on the well-being, and thus 
the quality of life. Transport is a major factor of the QoL as it is a vital element of city planning. We 
use transport every day to go to work and to have access to goods and services. Transport and 
mobility have a huge impact on the life quality of people - e.g. if people do not have access to 
transport, they are more likely to become socially excluded as their options are limited in regard to 
social activities and interaction. 
Quality of life is assessed by objective and subjective elements. Objective elements are the 
conditions that surround people - elements that cannot be controlled or manipulated by the 
individual. Subjective elements are elements that people have direct control of.  
Quality of life is subjective for each individual, but it is influenced by the immediate surroundings. 
For example, an objective element is the transport network in a city, which has an impact on 
people depending on variables like time, income, flexibility and accessibility.  
The purpose of this project is to develop a framework suitable for applying transport indicators, to 
assess the QoL of people living in Danish cities, in order to identify the areas that need 
improvement to enhance the citizens’ quality of life. 
 
The framework has two major purposes:  
1) To find out how transport affects the Quality of Life (QoL) of citizens within different domains 
(social, health, environmental and economic).  
2) To apply different transport indicators to state the “Quality of Life Performance” of the city and 
thereby the effect on its citizens. 
The study was conducted in two Danish cities, the city of Hillerød and the city of Roskilde. These 
cities were selected due to their size, geographic location, their focus on the citizens’ well-being, as 
well as the interest in the development of this thesis.  
Research was carried out to develop a framework with four different domains that have influence 
on QoL. After defining the framework, 23 transport indicators were developed within these four 
domains (Health and Environment; Safety; Connectivity and Economical). Only the most relevant 
indicators entered the framework. In order to find the indicators of high relevance, the Analytic 
Hierarchy Process (AHP) was used, where the indicators were compared to each other under 
different criteria, giving them a ranking of importance.   
==PAGE:4==
4 
 
The relevant indicators were evaluated for both cities, obtaining a Quality of Life Performance rate 
- a measurement of how the indicators are performing in the evaluated city. After evaluating all the 
indicators, an overall QoL performance rate was given to each city, stressing which domains need 
special attention and improvement.  
It is established that the main objective of this framework should lead to a larger awareness and 
accountability of the impact that transport has on society.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
Background knowledge obtained throughout our Master Degree in Transportation and Logistics –  
plus information gathered about the municipalities of Roskilde and Hillerød are the pillars for this 
project. It is important to understand that city planning involves many different elements which 
make city development complex. Transportation is one element of city planning and thereby an 
element that directly influences the lives of the citizens and their quality of life. Even though this 
has an immediate effect on the people, no framework focusing on transport indicators has been 
developed in Denmark. Cities all over the world, including cities in Denmark are trying to improve 
their livability, but without any documentation on how transport affects the citizens’ Quality of Life. 
The Quality of Life in relation to transportation has become an important matter due to the 
emerged environmental issues of sustainability and the change in people’s preferences and needs. 
The world today is not the same as it was 50 years ago and that also includes how people live, 
because the living conditions have changed due to technology, wealth, education, and the 
evolution of transport. Therefore, city planners have the complex task of developing liveable and 
attractive cities to fulfill the needs of the people living in the city. 
The rapid growth in car traffic in the last decade has resulted in city planning based on private 
transport and cities dominated by cars. This gave people both accessibility and mobility; however, 
it left the city empty of people where the car was dominating the street picture (Jan Gehl, 2010). 
The architect Jan Gehl, former professor at the Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts and founder of 
Gehl Architects, has been a consultant for a numerous cities around the world, where he assessed 
that traffic has a significant influence on people’s lives. This is constituted by the duality of 
transport, due to the positive and negative aspects. Traffic can bring people together, but it can 
also diminish opportunities for interactions, thereby having the opposite effect- separating people 
and communities. 
Sustainability is also an important issue for city planners nowadays. Cities have to evolve in a 
sustainable manner which is connected with transportation and thereby affecting the quality of life 
for the people(Steg and Gifford, 2004). Sustainability is important in order to develop a healthy city, 
but it is not the focus of this thesis, even though there are connections. Improving the Quality of 
Life will also affect some of the sustainability aspects of transportation but this is a secondary 
consequence of which we have to be aware in order to determine negative or positive outcomes. 
In order to answer our concerns of how transportation affects the Quality of Life of people in 
Danish cities, the following research question has been developed: 
 
“How does transport influences the QoL of the citizens of “Danish cities” and how to create a 
framework to select and apply transport indicators to assess Quality of Life performance in Danish 
Cities?” 
 
This thesis is assessed in two municipalities in Denmark; Hillerød and Roskilde, both located about 
30 kilometers from Copenhagen. Throughout the assessment, no focus was set on any specific 
population group; it is an assessment on the behalf of the general citizen.

2.1 Municipality planning in Denmark 
Denmark is divided into five regions, which all contain 98 municipalities. This was set by a reform 
passed by the Danish Government in 2007, where counties disappeared and were replaced by 5 
regions; Capital Region, Region of Zealand, Southern Region of Denmark, Central Region and 
Northern Region of Denmark, as shown in Figure 2.1. In the same reform, small municipalities with 
less than 20,000 inhabitants merged with each other to create one big municipality, narrowing 
down the number of municipalities from 271 to 98.

Today, the municipalities plan how Danish cities and landscapes are to develop within the overall 
state and regional guidelines. Each municipality has a municipal plan, which sets the overall 
targets and guidelines for the individual municipality's development for a period of 12 years 
(Danish Ministry of the Environment) 
Municipal governments are the closest authority that the citizens have. The main tasks and 
functions of the municipal governments are (Council of Europe 2014): 
 
 
Primary education 
 
Child care and care for the elderly and the disabled. 
 
Basic health services and promotion of health. 
 
Employment of citizens and integration of immigrants and refugees. 
 
Public utilities (water supply, electricity). 
 
Housing, urban development and planning.

 
Environmental protection. 
 
Roads and public transport.

 
City planning.

Chapter 3. Theory 
 
Before introducing the concepts of Quality of Life, Transport and how Transport affects Quality of 
Life, it is important to introduce the tools for the overall assessment of the thesis.  
As mentioned in the beginning of this project, the final goal is to create a framework with different 
domains where transport indicators can be applied in order to determine how they influence the 
Quality of Life of the citizens in cities. Thus, we need to understand both, the concept of framework 
and indicators. This chapter consist of two sections: 
1. The Framework, is one of the most important components, it is the system the indicators 
enter for a further assessment in different domains 
2. The Theory of the Indicators explains what an indicator is, the types of indicators and what 
the criteria of selecting indicators consists of. 
3.1 Framework 
A framework is a system that consists of a set of objects and a link between the objects and their 
attributes. Objects are the parts or components of a system and these parts are unlimited in 
variety. The attributes are properties of objects. “For a given system the environment is the set of 
all objects a change in whose attributes affect the system and also those objects whose attributes 
are changed by the behaviour of the system” (Hall and Fagen, 1956,20) 
The framework is the indicator system that will be used to assess the set of indicators for each of 
the selected domains.  
An indicator system or framework has four different functions (Gudmundsson, H., 2002 ,83), which 
are expressed in figure 4.2: 
1. Indicators that give information about the state of the system and create attention about 
tendencies and problems.  
2. Indicators which give information about progress in strategy. These also serve to maintain 
the decision makers focus upon their intentions and promises. 
3. Indicators for controlling strategies indicate the need for adjustments on implementation of 
strategies. 
4. Indicators for controlling systems can allocate resources for specific goals of the 
framework.

The framework for this study will be allocated in the attention objective where information will 
display the state of the system. The transport indicators will draw attention towards the “system” 
Quality of Life, and make the politicians aware of how the situation is in their specific municipality. 
3.2 Theory of Indicators 
Indicators are generally chosen from a number of statistics and data. They process this data in 
order to give information that supports monitoring, planning and management in different fields. 
There are different definitions for what an indicator is. Galloping (1997) defines indicators as 
“variables” that represent an attribute from a given system. The Ministry of Environment defines 
indicators more general as parameters that give information about an issue and turn complex 
issues into simple quantifiable information (Gudmundsson 2002) 
 
It can be inferred that indicators are variables chosen and designed for analysis of relevant data for 
a specific goal and context. Therefore, indicators have different meanings depending on the 
context of which indicator system or framework they will be part of. In this thesis, a set of indicators 
will be part of the framework “Quality of life by transportation”, and will be analysed to assess how 
they impact the quality of life of the people living in the cities. 
3.2.1 Types of indicators 
Essential indicators (Møller & Christensen, 2011):  
 
Qualitative indicators, describe the situation in verbal terms, usually with a yes or no 
answer, where quantitative indicators describe the situation in numbers or scales. 
 
Descriptive indicators, indicate the state of development, while normative indicators 
compare a situation with a norm or a target. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
 
Absolute indicators measure size of a given object, while relative indicators measure two or 
more objects and compare them to each other. 
 
Singular indicators measure on a single unit, while comparative indicators compare 
different units. 
The selection of indicators and their usability depend on the data available. 
3.2.2 Criteria for selecting indicators 
1. Representation - Indicators assessed in regard to their accurate representation of an 
impact: 
-Validity. It measures the issue or factor it is actually supposed to. 
-Reliability. It gives the same result when it is measured repeated times in the same way, 
on the same target population, and at almost the same point in time.   
-Sensitivity. It reveals important changes in the results due to changes in the priority of the 
criteria.  
 
2. Operation - indicators assessed in regard to operational success for practical and 
continued monitoring:  
-Measurability. It’s straightforward and relatively inexpensive to measure. 
-Data availability. It is based on data which are readily available, or that can be measured 
at a reasonable cost and time.  
-Ethical acceptability. It complies with fundamental human rights and requires only data that 
are consistent with morals, beliefs or values of the population. 
3. Policy application - Indicators assessed in regard to their pertinence and usefulness for 
policy and management decision making : 
-Transparency. It is understandable and possible to reproduce for the intended users.  
-Interpretability. It allows an intuitive and unambiguous reading. 
-Target relevance. It measures performance with regard to goals, objectives, targets or 
thresholds of interest.  
-Actionability. It measures factors that can be changed or influenced directly by 
management or policy action. 
 
These criteria are highly important due to their relevance for illustrating conditions about how 
transport affects the quality of life. The indicators have to be accurate in order to display the actual 
condition of the cities. The reason for using an indicator system within the transport sector is to 
address how transport affects QoL in a simplistic manner that can provide for a good overview of 
the context between QoL and transportation.

4.1 Research of the Theory 
The first step of carrying out this thesis was to do a thorough research to understand the concepts 
of Quality of Life (QoL), transportation and how transportations affects the Quality of Life of people 
 in Danish Cities. Quality of Life was investigated to understand the factors that influence people’s 
everyday life, while transport was investigated to understand how it is manifested in our lives and 
what the effects of transport are. In the end, it led to a greater understanding of the different 
aspects of how transportation influences Quality of Life.  
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
The research was conducted by searching for articles, reports and books at the DTU  electronic 
library, springer, google scholar search, the electronic library of the City of Copenhagen; as well as 
knowledge and theory obtained from former courses such as Sustainable Transport Assessment; 
Urban Planning and Sustainable Urban Development; and Smart, Connected and Liveable Cities, 
which gave a larger understanding of city development, livability and sustainability. These last two 
concepts are important to understand due to their connection to city planning and Quality of Life; 
however, they were not the main focus for the assessment of the framework.  
The process has been of an iterative nature, in regard to information that was researched 
throughout the process. The variety of sources resulted in a more insightful impact on the 
assessment. 
 
As mentioned before, the framework is to be applied in Danish cities/municipalities. Therefore, it 
was vital to investigate how a municipality is formed and what their tasks and functions are. For 
this thesis, the framework was applied in two Danish cities, the city of Roskilde and the city of 
Hillerød with the purpose to find the level of Quality of Life in each city, and compare these results. 
This will give an overview of which areas have to be improved in order to improve people's Quality 
of Life.  
 
We decided to apply the framework in these two cities because they both belong to the five 
provincial towns in the Greater Copenhagen Area; their relatively close distance to Copenhagen

4.4 The AHP Assessment 
The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) is a method used to conduct a Multi-Criteria Decision 
Analysis (MCDA) (DTU Transport Compendium, 2012). This decision-making method was used to 
narrow down the number of indicators so that only the most representative indicators for QoL 
would enter the framework. 
 
The MCDA is a set of methods to help make a decision when several decision criteria exists. It is a 
subjective analysis because it is based upon the expertise of the decision makers and their 
experiences. It also depends on the personality of the decision maker and the circumstances in 
which the decision takes place. The methodological approach of the MCDA can be indicated with 
the following quotation by the European Conference of Ministers of Transport (ECMT), Group of 
Experts (1981): 
 
“Multi-criteria decision analysis is a fairly recent method for assessing and selecting projects 
exerting complex socio-economic effects. In this method, the individual assessment elements are 
taken separately and measured in the appropriate dimensions. The criteria will have to be 
weighted among each other because they are not of equal relevance. Determining the weights 
requires much responsibility and expertise from the decision-maker as the weights have 
considerable influence on the results of the assessment. “ 
 
The essence of the AHP is to help the decision maker to structure a Multi-Attribute Decision 
Making (MADM) problem in form of an attribute hierarchy. 
The AHP method  hierarchy is divided into three levels: 
Level 1: Subject- the focus or the goal of the problem. 
Level 2: Criteria- multiple criteria or domains that define the alternatives. 
Level 3: Alternatives- competing alternatives. 
 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
For this thesis, the level 1 is how transportation affects quality of life, which is the main focus; the 
level 2 is the criteria that will be used to rank the indicators, and the level 3 are the indicators. 
The tool used to carry out the AHP assessment in this thesis was the Expert Choice, which is a 
decision-making software that uses AHP as the tool to carry out a Multi Criteria Decision Analysis 
(MCDA)  (13235 Planning Theory, DTU Transport).   
The Expert Choice ranks the indicators against each other under certain criteria, obtaining a 
ranking of importance for each indicator. These criteria also need to be ranked in order to give 
them a level of importance, thereby obtaining the best outcome. 
 
The method uses pair-wise comparisons to give weights to the criteria because it is much easier 
than doing a comparison of all the criteria simultaneously. The criteria are weighted against each 
other with respect to the subject or the goals, and the alternatives are weighed against each other 
with respect to the criteria. The nine-point scale created by Saaty in 2001 was used for this 
assessment. It is a nine point intensity scale of importance between two elements, which ranges 
from 1 to 9, and describes how much one alternative is prefered over another. The table below 
illustrates the nine intensities of importance of the scale.

Chapter 5. Conceptualization 
 
Quality of Life dates back to the great Greek philosophers and was described by Aristotle in 
“Eudemain Ethics” (Eudaimonia = Well being). Transport was used to visit people over larger 
distances, exploration and travel around the city. 
Quality of Life and Transportation are old concepts, but it is not until recently that those two have 
been connected as important aspects of city development. Today the concept of creating the best 
liveable city is based on connecting different components of city development, urban management 
and high quality of life in order to become a competitive city, both locally and in the international 
marketplace to produce the most optimal outcome for city life and city functions. 
The different components of urban development influence each other which make them 
codependent, however this has not always been taken into consideration. Over the last decade this 
has changed and the cities have realised that connecting QoL and Transportation is an important 
step towards creating a city for people (Jan Gehl, 2010) and not only a city of people; a city for 
people that find it comfortable and easy to move around the city and to interact with other people. 
Transport has become a major factor of the Quality of Life of people, as it consumes a lot of time in 
our daily life. We use transport in almost all of our everyday activities; our busy schedules today 
could not be possible without transportation 
5.1 The Concept of Quality of Life 
The concept “Quality of Life” has undergone a tremendous investigation over the past 70 years 
when Researchers sought to investigate trends and contents in people’s lives, but realized that it 
was not that simple (Noll,2004). The research was a lot more complex than first anticipated 
resulting in a much extensive research. This has led to different theories about the overall concept 
of Quality of Life. 
 
Wealth Versus Quality of Life 
Throughout history of every civilization, wealth has played a huge matter of quality of life. If you 
have money, you can buy everything. A country’s wealth, a city’s wealth is built on the amount of 
GDP it has. In many cities the wealth is shown by the quantity of buildings. A skyline with huge 
buildings displays the power and richness of the city. This is still taking place today, but in 1889 a 
man named Seth stated “we must not regard the mere quantity, but also the quality of “life” which 
forms the moral end” (Sonja Forward,2003,5), and 70 years later in 1967 the same statement 
appear by Galbraith “what counts is not the quantity of our goods but the quality of life” (Sonja 
Forward,2003,5). Even though there are 70 years between the two statements, the world has not 
changed. Many people today still believe that the bigger, the better; the two statements indicate 
that quality of life is more than quantity. The infrastructure can be defined by quality of design and 
material; but is this quality for the people? Does it make their lives better? The question is, what 
influences the quality of life of a person? 
Economist Manfred Max-Neef expressed that GDP does not describe anything about the people 
(Max-Neef,2008). The GDP describes the well-being of a society but GDP only measure what can 
==PAGE:30==
30 
 
be expressed in monetary terms. What does not generate money has no value. Going for a walk 
has no value, even though it might generate personal value. Manfred believes that the economy 
has to be rethought into “development process is best where the Quality of life for the people 
improves the most”. His argument is that “the quality of life depends on the possibility people have 
or have not to adequately satisfy their fundamental human needs“(Max-Neef,2008,3). Through 
research it became clear that wealth and materials were one out of many factors when it comes to 
QoL. A good life also depends on things such as health, education, personal freedom, enjoyment 
and welfare. When cities began to think of how to improve the lives of their citizens it was a huge 
step away from economic life quality to social Quality of Life (NOLL,2004). 
Economy however, does play a role in people’s lives since it affects the choices and abilities to 
travel and socialize. “Having a high quality of life at an affordable price” (Van Zerr & Seskin, 
2011,2) 
 
Objective and Subjective Quality of Life 
It has been established that quality of life is depended upon objective and subjective elements 
(Noll,2004). Objective elements consist of domains surrounding people. People do not have any 
responsibility for or direct influence on the domains surrounding them. Subjective impacts consist 
of an individual person's well-being. The individual is directly responsible and able to influence a 
given outcome or situation. The objective elements are conditions in which the subjective elements 
exist. 
 
“..expressions of satisfaction are themselves relative to the individual’s temperament and the 
circumstances and experiences that have shaped their frame of reference” (Sonja Forward,2003,6) 
  
Quality of life is subjective for each individual person, but it is depended on the state of objective 
elements, which we encounter on a daily basis. Every individual lives differently and thereby has 
different social environments; however, the perceived quality of life is not necessary different. It all 
depends on how we value various parts of our lives- what is important to us and what is not. It is 
the individual’s own evaluation that states the QoL but, it is defined by the things we know. If we do 
not know of better or worse conditions we perceive our own QoL either low or high, respectively. 
This is the comparison between the expected QOL and the experienced QoL, which define our 
well-being(Rogers, Halstead et al. 2010). (Wendy L. Way,1990, 67) “They theorize that overall life 
quality is a function of satisfaction with relevant domains of life experience and that satisfaction 
with any one domain depends on (a) the personal relevance of specific attributes of the objective 
reality and (b) the standards of comparison used to evaluate the worth of the perceived attributes 
of the reality”. One objective domain can be the traffic system which provokes a feeling in 
everyone. Everyone has an experience with the system and it has its impact on choices. However, 
we value the system differently depending on a various number of variables; income, time, 
flexibility, accessibility, among others. Therefore, how transport influences these needs has an 
important effect on the QOL, both positively and negatively.

“Being satisfied means feeling that life is the way it should be. When one's expectations, 
needs, and desires in life are being met by the surrounding world, one is satisfied. Satisfaction is a 
mental state: a cognitive entity.”(Søren Ventegod,2003,4) 
 
Human Needs 
Psychologist Abraham Maslow (1954) defined a hierarchy of needs that people strive to meet. It is 
a pyramid displaying the different stages of needs where each stage has to be fulfilled before 
climbing onto the next one. This means that the first need has to be fulfilled before the second 
need becomes a matter of interest. It is very fundamental in most human developmental theories 
that people will strive for further development or satisfaction, when one has been obtained. 
Professor of marketing and founding editor in applied research in QoL M. Joseph Sirgy uses the 
pyramid in order to display quality of life from a human developmental perspective. He describes 
that “Quality of Life goals can be defined as satisfaction of human development needs in 
community or society” (Sirgy,1986,331). He has used the concept of the pyramid “to develop the 
notion of a progression of satisfaction of needs in social development”. 
The theory contains 5 stages; biological needs, safety needs, social needs, esteem needs and 
self-actualization needs.

facilities.

This pyramid displays the different stages and the level of quality of life which becomes higher with

An assessment of individuals can determine the level of human development and QoL goals can

Domains Affecting Human Needs 
Bubloz, Eicher, Evers and Sontag (1980) address QOL in relation to a human ecological 
framework. It is a human ecosystem which consists of three parts. 1)The human environed unit 
(population), 2) environments and the interactions and 3) transactions between and within the 
elements. The human unit is in the centre surrounded by three environments; behavioral, natural 
and constructed. The human environed unit interacts with these environments and the 
environments interact with each other. 
 
Figure 5.2 The human ecosystem. 
Source: Margaret M. Bubloz, Joanne B. Eicher and M Suzanne Sontag 1978.  
 
This theory describes the human development as interactions between and within different 
environments. The human life consists of all the environments and their individual elements at the 
same time, thereby contributing to the needs simultaneously. Quality of life is the ultimate overall 
environment for the environed unit. It is the environment to strive for. Establishing goals and 
==PAGE:33==

optimal performance are important to determine the necessary resources to meet the needs in 
each environment. 
The environments which surround you, contribute to your life quality and affects the choices you 
make. A domain can influence you in different ways, but sometimes you influence it by challenging 
its structure instead of adapting to it. This is how interactions between a unit and domains change 
environments and how QoL improves or diminish its value. 
 
The main objective of investigating the theory of QOL lies in the importance of understanding what 
QOL is and how it is manifested in our lives. The relevance of the pyramid in figure 6.1 lies in the 
construction of levels. To understand that some needs are of a greater importance than others; 
and if some basic needs are missing, it will affect us even more than if high level needs were 
missing. However Denmark is a rich country, where most of the population is situated at the 
highest levels of the pyramid, so how can this theory be of any importance for this thesis? The 
answer lies in the other theory of the human ecosystem, that everything affects each other. A 
domain such as the transport system affects other domains and the different levels of needs exist 
in these domains. Therefore, transportation affects the needs of the people. 
 
Liveability and Quality of Life 
In the last decade cities all around the world have been focusing on developing liveable cities 
which depends on how well the city is at developing a lively, safe, healthy and sustainable city (Jan 
Gehl, 2010). Liveability is defined broadly as “suitability for human living” (Merriam-Webster, 2011) 
Jan Gehl says that the last 50 years of city development has been missing the human factor which 
is a vital instrument for the quality of life of the city. A liveable city is about centralizing the human 
aspect in city development, and through this planning ideology improve the QoL. The potential for 
a lively city is strengthened when more people are invited to walk, bike and stay in city space. 
Liveable cities require compact city structure, reasonable population density, acceptable walking 
and biking distances and good quality city space (Jan Gehl, 2010) 
The human factor is about focusing on the things which support the well-being and satisfaction of 
the people. The well-being is highly supported by the social capital a community or municipality 
has to offer. “The social capital is a measure of an individual’s or group’s networks, personal 
connections, and involvement.” (Rogers et al., 2010,1). They describe the social capital in 
municipalities as the design of physical infrastructure that has to provide connections between 
people and accessibility to important locations, such as recreational areas, shops, hospital etc.   
Liveability in general is affected by a city's public safety, environmental quality, community 
cohesion, friendliness, aesthetics, accessibility, pride and opportunity. (VTPI,2010)

5.2 The Concept of Transport 
Why do people travel? 
Traveling has been part of the human nature since the migration out of Africa millions of years ago. 
People´s reasons for travelling are many: to escape from poverty or war, to move to better 
conditions, to trade, to see other places, etc. Whatever the reason may be, people travelled to 
improve their conditions and quality of life in one way or another. Traveling in early times was 
uncomfortable, dangerous and very time-consuming. Some would argue that little has changed for 
travel, it is still dangerous, uncomfortable and time consuming (Erling Holden, 2007). Some studies 
suggest in fact, that the travel time nowadays is the same as it was five thousand years ago 
(Ausubel & Marchetti, 2001), assessed in constant travel-time budgets (TTB).  TTB means that 
people assign some time of the 24 hours they have in a day as the maximum amount of time they 
would be willing to spend on travel per day. It has been reported almost as an universal constant 
across time and space at 60 to 78 minutes per person per day (Ahmed and Stopher 2014). 
However, two aspects can be found in modern time travel that didn’t exist back then: a growth in 
mobility and a great increase in environmental and social consequences.    
 
Transportation 
According to Hoyle and Knowles (1998:1), transport exists “to provide for the movement of people 
and goods and for the provision and distribution of services. Transport thereby fulfills one of the 
most important functions and is one of the most pervasive activities in any society or economy”. 
Transport has been the founding element for the shape, growth and development of societies, as 
transport systems allow the transport of materials needed to build cities and towns. It also fulfills 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
the needs of people providing everyday goods, getting people to work and to participate in social 
activities. 
 
During the Industrial Revolution, the economy and culture of cities were totally changed by the 
arrival of the railway, increasing enormously the movement of people and goods through cities and 
forming new societies. In addition to the expansion of the railways, the appearance of the electric 
trams in the late nineteenth century was a big step for public transport. They were, however, 
replaced by buses in the early twentieth century. 
Trains, trams and busses allowed the middle and working class to live at relatively long distances 
from their place of work. Bicycles were an important mean of personal transport which had an 
impact from the late nineteenth century through the middle of the twentieth century. Before the 
appearance of the car in the 18th century, bicycles were a vital mode of transport for working-class 
people who biked to and from work (David S. Lawyer, 2005) 
 
In 1885 Carl Benz created the first automobile powered by the internal-combustion engine. 
However, at that time, cars were a product of manual labor and therefore only the rich could afford 
to buy a car. This changed in 1913 when Henry Ford introduced the assembly line production 
(Dietsche and Kuhlgatz 2015). 
By producing large amount of automobiles, cars stopped being an article of luxury, and they 
became accessible to almost everyone, creating mobility on a scale never seen before, changing 
the way of life of people and their social customs. In the days of horse-pull transportation, the 
longest travel distance was between 10 and 15 miles (16 and 24 kilometers), meaning that any 
community that was located further than 15 miles from a city, railroad or waterway was considered 
isolated from the social and economic life. 
Nowadays cars are not only used as a mode of transport. Results have revealed that people that 
drive a car usually dislike the public transport. For them, cars perform better than public transport, 
not only because of its instrumental function, but also because the car represents cultural and 
psychological values like freedom and independence, status and the pleasure and excitement of 
driving (Linda Steg 2003) 
 
Transport is the only major sector whose conditions tend to worsen as economic performance 
increases. The better the economy and higher income the higher the level of car and motorcycle 
ownership, leading to high levels of congestion and all the social problems related with 
motorization. On the other hand, when private motorization increases; accessibility and mobility 
decreases. (Lindfield and Steinberg 2012) 
During the twentieth century, there was a major change on how people travelled. People switched 
from public transport-railways and buses- to the private car, which resulted in the construction of 
large-scale transport networks with a huge amount of circulating vehicles.

5.3 Transportation affecting Quality of Life 
The interest in the impacts of transportation in quality of life has grown in the last decade setting 
focus on how transportation systems affects the liveability of a city or community, the levels of 
service, the user satisfaction, the sustainability and the accessibility. 
The links between mobility and transport have a huge impact in forming the quality of life for both 
individuals and communities. Many studies have shown that the link between transport and poor 
access to goods and services can contribute to social exclusion making it difficult for people to 
participate fully in society, affecting their quality of life. (Social Exclusion Eunit 1998; 2003; Hine 
and Mitchel 2003; 2001; Lucas et al. 2001; DETR 2000) 
Infrastructure improvement and modifications like multi-use pathways, bike lanes, pedestrian 
amenities, green areas, streetscape redesign, etc. are some of the things that are carried out in 
cities to improve the community and individual quality of life (Shafer, Lee and Turner, 2000; Leslie 
et al., 2007; Coulson et al., 2011). The next two sections describes the impacts of transportation 
and the connection to quality of life.

5.3.3 Conclusion  
Transportation is an important matter with respect to quality of life, because it affects significantly 
the basic human needs of health, safe, financial and social well-being. This is with respect to 
positive and negative consequences to people’s lives. It is a fact that transportation is a part of our 
daily life; and without it, mobility, accessibility and economic growth would almost be impossible, 
not only because the ability of travelling from one place to another, but also because of all the 
goods we consume have been transported from somewhere. Human beings do not have a natural 
basic need for means of transportation, but the urban development has made transport an 
implemental need, that we use to go to work and obtain the economical means, which is a basic 
human need. (Mortimer J. Adler 1997).  
In the pursuit to reach the high levels of needs, the lower levels of needs have been compromised, 
though without realizing at the time. The massive planning of infrastructure has been a derived 
consequence of obtaining the high level needs for the society and today we see the externalities

QoL aspects along with transport indicators. The framework is structured around the process

The Application 
The goal of the framework is to display the state of the city's quality of life. This is to raise 
attention concerning the current situation for the politicians and the citizens. 
 
The Aspects of the framework are:

System 


The system exists to address all the information, in this case, the indicators and create context

The system and the information are equally important for the success of the framework together

The Aspect of the Information 
By establishing the scheme of the four domains, information can be expressed accordingly. The 
information, which consists of the transport indicators, is the connections between transport and 
QoL. The QoL elements and the gains and pains of transport are expressed within each domain.  
 
There exist numerous frameworks addressing societal indicators, but they are structured 
differently. Frameworks deal with either objective; subjective or both objective and subjective 
indicators. There exist different opinions about including or excluding subjective indicators in the 
framework (Noll, 2004). One concern is that it only measures satisfaction according to the level of 
needs and thereby “how well people are adapted to their present conditions”. Another part believes 
that subjective indicators are indispensable because they determine the path to take and the 
success of it. 
Even though subjective indicators can directly display the well-being of the citizens, we have come 
to the conclusion that excluding subjective indicators is the best thing to do. The reason for this is 
that people change throughout life, and therefore the reason for their well-being also changes. This 
==PAGE:47==

depends a lot on which stages in life people are; young people have concerns different to adults. 
The value of an indicator will change according to this stage in life. Objective indicators are able to 
display the municipality’s ability to supply the citizens with quality of life and thereby the well-being 
of the citizens, because the objective indicators are based on the basic human needs.  
 
The Aspect of Context 
The context issue is important to establish, because the context between the domains constitute 
the QoL. The indicators complement each other, which means that they are interconnected and not 
only affect their own domain. Some indicators; for example the noise, affect the health as its main 
contribution, but it can also affect connectivity, because it keeps people from certain areas. This 
aspect of the interconnectedness between the indicators and the domains is important to grasp in 
order to obtain the bigger picture of how the transport indicators affect QoL of the citizens.

Definition of the Health & Environmental Domain 
The domain describes the city’s ability to secure the health of its citizens and a clean environment. 
Transport affects natural environments locally (quality of the air, noise, etc.) Most of these effects 
have a great importance for the present and future environmental status, affecting the health and 
quality of life of people. This domain is important due to the health risks, caused by pollution and 
noise that transport modes are inflicting upon the citizens, causing poor living conditions. 
 
Definition of the Safety Domain   
It is described in the human need hierarchy that safety is vital for a good quality of life. To feel safe 
is important for people's overall well-being and can therefore dominate people's movements and 
whereabouts. Safety can be crucial when choosing a route and a transportation mode. 
Transportation affects this feeling of security by the interactions of different transportation modes, 
the velocity, the landscape when driving, and the transport infrastructure in general- the safety of 
the infrastructure and the accidents that happen or can happen. 
 
Definition of the Connectivity Domain 
In the human need hierarchy it is described that people have a great need for interactions with one 
another, which depends on the transportation network in the municipality. Interactions are 
supported by recreational areas such as parks, lakes, pedestrian areas and shopping areas. To 
support human interaction, it has to be accessible for people to get to and from their homes to 
these areas and to school and work. The main goal for the connectivity domain is to make the city 
accessible by any transportation mode.  
 
Definition of the Economic Domain 
The domain describes how the city’s transport system affects its citizens economically.  Transport 
is important for the economy and reflects economic activity. It makes economic growth possible 
through transportation of goods and people, affecting positively the Quality of Life of the 
population. The economic domain describes the need for the people to be self-sustainable by the 
amount of money they have at their disposal. The transport sector holds different expenses for the 
municipality and the citizens, like time; household consumption; cost and how the economy affects 
transportation.

Chapter 7. The identification of Transport Indicators 
 
The description of the four domains will be the basis for identifying the transport indicators. The 
purpose of the indicators is to address the system in a more simplistic and measurable manner in 
order to communicate information. The indicators are specially selected to support the connection 
between transport and QoL and to display this development in society.  
 
Quality of Life indicators refer to the benefits derived from increased physical activity; breathing 
clean air; having access to transportation choices, jobs, housing, education, open space; having 
adequate opportunities for social interaction; living in a pleasant, safe and cohesive community; 
and having a sense of social belonging (Forkenbrock & Weisbrod, 2001) 
 
One objective criterion is that the indicators should display the direct effect transport has on QoL. 
This means that e. g. the speed limit does not tell anything directly about the quality of life of the 
citizens, but indirectly it can contribute to the safety, economy and connectivity of the citizens. 
Indicators can show a given situation in the municipality and address how this affects the citizens.  
The following 23 indicators were developed within the fours domains: 
7.1  The indicators of the Connectivity Domain 
 
1. Percentage of the population living within 500m of a bus stop or train station. 
The distance to a bus stop is a crucial aspect for people in order for them to consider 
public transport as an option for travel. Easy connection to, from and within the city can 
influence their choice of mode. This indicator gives citizens the feeling of a good 
connectivity and accessibility to public transport. The indicator shows the percentage of 
the population in a city that has a bus stop or train station within 500 meters, which 
takes about 8 minutes walking (Rejseplanen, 2015) 
2. Meantime that people travel to shopping, health, educational and recreational 
activities. 
People’s QoL depend on their connection to other areas of the city. The mean time has 
an influence on the choice of mode in order to get easily around and be connected to 
the city. The choice of mode will have an effect on other domains of QoL. The indicator 
shows how long it takes in average for people in a city to get to their main activities. The 
shorter the travel time, the better the feeling of Quality of Life of the people. 
3. Meantime to get to the city limits by different modes of transport. 
Many people work in other cities, therefore it is important to take into account the travel 
time to the city limits, as this has an influence on the choice of transport mode. The 
indicator shows how long in average it takes to reach the city limits by different modes 
of transport. The shorter the travel time, the better the feeling of Quality of Life of the 
people. 
==PAGE:50==
50 
 
4. Number of parking spots and their location. 
In other to connect the citizens in the municipality, parking spots and their locations are 
vital when choosing a transport mode. The indicator shows the number of parking spots 
and where they are located in the city for the use of the citizens. When the parking 
infrastructure is not good, or the prices of parking are too high, this can prevent people 
from biking or driving and this could affect their feeling of QoL. However, this would 
affect positively the QoL of people that are exposed to the daily car emissions. 
5. The difficulty degree of crossing a street. 
Social interaction is a human necessity.  If a street is difficult to cross, it separates 
people instead of bringing them together. The indicator shows how difficult it is to cross 
the streets of a city.  If a road is difficult to cross, this will prevent people from socialising 
or doing everyday activities, affecting their QoL. 
6. Percentage of population who can get to key locations door-to-door by public 
transport, walking and cycling. 
Getting door to door is important in order for the citizens not to feel they are wasting 
their time. The indicator shows the percentage of the population in a city who can get to 
locations door to door by the use of public transport, bike or walking. Long distances to 
the nearest bus stop/train station, affects people's feeling of mobility and accessibility. 
7. Persentage of households that have access to different transport modes (car, 
bicycle, public transport). 
The access to transport modes in a city is important to assess because it affects the 
QOL of the individual, but also affects the city. The indicator shows the percentage of 
the citizens in a city who have access to different transport modes. The aim of the 
municipalities is that all the citizens have access to a mode of transport to get to their 
everyday activities. When people do not have access to any mode of transport, they 
simply get isolated and this affects a lot their quality of life. 
8. Total length of cycle lanes in kilometres and the network. 
In order for the municipality to influence the selection of biking as the main transport 
mode, the conditions for the cyclists are important. The network of bicycle lanes is 
important for citizens that bike to and from work or school. If the bicycle infrastructure is 
not in optimal conditions, people will chose a less sustainable mode of transport. The 
indicator shows the total bike network of a city in kilometers. 
9. Percentage of busses/trains which are late more than 5 min, 10min. 
When public transport is late, people get frustrated and this affects their connectivity. 
They may choose to use another transport mode like the car, increasing the amount of 
emissions to the environment, affecting the QoL of the citizens. The indicator will show 
the percentage of busses and trains that are late in a city. 
10. Average waiting time in queue per person per day. 
Waiting in queue is one of the transport problems that affects the Quality of Life of 
persons the most because they lose their connectivity feeling. When cars are waiting in 
queue they have an increased emission of pollutants to the environment, affecting the 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
health of all the citizens. The indicator shows the average waiting time of a person per 
day when driving to everyday activities. 
 
7.2 The indicators of the Safety Domain 
 
11. Number of accidents. 
According to the WHO, “health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-
being. Accidents affect this state of health and thereby the QoL of the persons involved 
in an accident.  The indicator shows the total number of accidents in a city every year. 
12. Accident density and frequency. 
Accident density and accidente frequency are two risk measures. Risks threaten the 
safety of people, and safety is a measure of QoL. The more accidents, the less safe the 
roads are. This indicator will show the possibility of how often accidents happen and 
how many accidents per road-kilometers there are. 
13. Number of accidents per mode and their locations. 
It is important to know how many accidents there are for each mode of transport, and 
where these accidents are happening so that improvements can be done and less 
accidents will happen. The indicator shows the accidents per mode (car,bike, public 
transport) and where these accidents happen in the city. 
14. Number of injuries and deaths in traffic per year. 
Deaths and injuries in traffic represent costs for society (medical care, loss of 
productivity, compensations) and family. There is also a QoL reduction related to the 
pain of the loss. The indicator shows the number of people injured and death in traffic 
accidents every year. 
15. Percentage of bikers using helmets. 
Using helmet while biking reduces the number of injured and death and thereby saves 
medical costs and costs to society, as well as Quality of Life losses. The indicator 
shows how many people use bike helmet when using biking as their mode of transport. 
16. Percentage of roads/bike lanes in poor conditions. 
Roads and bike lanes in bad conditions are a risk for people. Bike lanes in bad 
conditions reduce the desire of people to bike, and they chose another mode of 
transport. The indicator shows the amount of roads and bike lanes that are in bad 
conditions for an optimal use. 
 
7.3 The indicators of the Health and environmental Domain 
 
17. Noise levels in dB during day and night. 
Noise causes stress and thereby affects people's health and performance. Noise is vital 
for the QoL of the citizens because it influences their choice of where to live, and where

to socialize. The indicator shows the levels of noise in the city, and which areas are

18. Emissions in µg/m3 per year.

20. Premature deaths per km2 related to emissions. 
Emissions deteriorate the health of people and are responsible for premature deaths. 
Premature deaths caused by emissions is a severe consequence of transport activity on 
the QoL. The indicator shows the number of premature deaths per year caused by the 
emissions in a city. 
 
7.4 The indicators of the Economic Domain 
 
21. Cost of transportation (DKK) per km. 
The cost of transport is an important aspect that affects the QoL of the citizens because 
if people cannot afford a transport mode, it affects their connectivity. A transport pricing 
policy where cars are very expensive could lead to an increased use of public transport 
and thereby an increased environmental quality. Though, for some people, the use of 
cars implies comfort, privacy and freedom, and therefore a good feeling of QoL. The 
less money you spend on transport, the more you have for social activities or leisure, 
which will improve you QoL. This indicator shows how much it costs to use a certain 
mode of transport in a city. 
22. Percentage of household consumption on transport. 
A reasonable part of the overall household consumption goes to transport. Transport 
pricing has a high influence on people's Quality of Life because it reflects the mode of 
transport people use. The less money a family can spend on transport, the more they 
can spend on social activities and holidays, which will improve the family’s well-being. 
This indicator shows how much people spend on transport every year in a city. 
23. Cost of accidents in transport per year. 
Traffic accidents represent a lot of costs, both for the people involved in them and for 
the municipality, who has to pay for medical care, rehabilitation, etc. These costs could 
be used to enhance other areas for the city development like recreational areas to bring 
people together. This indicator shows how much accidents cost, both for people and for 
the cities.

8.3 Assessing "The Influence on QoL" Criterion 
In order to evaluate the indicators to the “Influence on QoL” criterion, an investigation on how to do 
the assessment had to be carried out. The concept of QoL does not consider which indicators 
influence the citizens’ quality of life the most. The concept describes the different domains as 
having a high influence, but within each domain there is no evidence concerning which kind of 
indicators hold more influence than others. 
Two different papers have dealt with this dilemma and have used two different methods to come 
up with a valid result. 
The two papers are “A methodological framework for identifying and selecting sustainable transport 
indicators” by Castillo and Pitfield(2009) and “Selecting Indicators for Strategic Performance 
Management” by Marsden, Kelly and Snell(2011), both from the UK. 
Castillo and Pitfield are concerned about indicators belonging to the sustainable sector. They want 
to help the authorities by addressing indicators that have a maximized effect on this area. They 
have defined more than 200 indicators in 9 areas. 
Marsden, Kelly and Snell are concerned with effective indicators used for monitoring and 
performance outcomes that support the development of the strategy chosen. They investigated 16 
local and regional authorities which led to a huge amount of different indicators. There was no 
coherence between the gathered information in the different areas.   
The two methods applied are an AHP based approach and a survey based investigation among 
the local traffic planners. The AHP is used in order to investigate how the indicators influence a 
various number of criteria which are important for the specific area. The questionnaire is asking the 
planners, which indicators are important and which indicators are being monitored with satisfaction. 
They then illustrate the different importance of the indicators with respect to satisfaction. 
 
The two AHP based methods are great for the purpose of identifying how the indicators influence 
the QOL. One method is based on theory and the other is based on subjective evaluation among 
experts. The time perspectives are very different and the last method involves a various amount of 
people, whereas the first can depend on only one person. Considering the pros and cons, the AHP 
will be applied to investigate how the indicators influence QOL. This decision is primarily due to the 
short time at our disposal and on the basis of our QOL concept, which gives great information on 
what is important for the citizens’ QOL.

Chapter 10. The Framework: The Quality of Life by Transportation 
The Quality of Life by Transportation is formed by the 12 selected indicators which represent the 
different aspects of Quality of Life. It is these 12 indicators which will constitute the overall QoL 
level of the city and each of the domains’ level. Each individual indicator contributes with a specific 
measurement that illustrates the citizens’ experience of the city. 
 
Figure 10.1 The QoL by transportation framework with the final indicators.  
The Environmental/Health indicators: 
 
The four indicators will state the environmental status that affects the health of the 
citizens. The four indicators display two sides of the health situation, by having an active indicator 
to determine the physical health level of the citizens and passive indicators to determine the 
environmental health level of the municipality. The number of people whose quality of life is 
severely affected by the environmental pollution will pose an overall statement of the 
environmental health that the municipality can provide for the citizens. This is measured by the 
Premature deaths per km2 related to emissions. The amount of emissions and noise at different 
locations of the municipality will illustrate where the greatest health risks in the municipality are 
located. The physical activity by transportation is stated by the mode share distribution.  
==PAGE:80==
80 
 
The Safety Indicators: 
 
The three indicators will state the safety level of the municipality that affects the 
citizens security environment. The number of people whose quality of life has been affected by the 
conditions of the transport network will pose an overall statement of the security environment. The 
number of accidents will constitute the safety level of the road network which the municipality can 
provide for its citizens. The number of accidents will illustrate the safety of each mode and inform 
where the greatest risks in the municipality are located. 
The Connectivity Indicators: 
 
The four indicators will state the connectivity status that affects the social interaction 
of the citizens. The four indicators contribute with a statement of the municipality’s transport 
network in order to determine the level of connectivity by different transport options with the 
purpose of bringing people together and to important locations; school, work, recreational areas 
and shopping areas. There are two sides of the social interaction situation: indicators that can 
provide an obstacle for connection and indicators that can connect people with multiple choices.  
The Economic Indicator: 
The indicator will state the economic status of the transport system. It expresses the 
expenditure for transportation that the citizens would have to pay in order to access the different 
modes. This affects the economic situation of the citizens which influences their QoL.  
 
The complement of the indicators 
As stated earlier, there is a context between the indicators. This context is important to understand 
in order to determine the effect of these 12 indicators and to fully grasp the QoL of the citizens. 
Three indicators within the connectivity domain, the Difficulty degree of crossing a street; the 
Bicycle network and the Percentage of population living within 500 m. of a bus/train stop, can affect 
the choice of mode (the Percentage use of transport modes), which affect the Emissions in ug/m2 
per year and the Noise levels in dB during day and night. The Cost of transportation per km also 
has an influence of the choice of mode. Therefore, the connectivity and the economy affect the 
mode choice and thereby the environment of the city and its influence on the citizens’ health. The 
economy affects the citizens’ possibility for social interaction by the money that is spent on 
transportation which could be spent on other activities. The pollution and noise also affect the 
social interaction by limiting the possibilities for interacting. The safety of a city can influence the 
choice of mode and route, thus it influences the connectivity and the health of the citizens. The 
bicycle network can also illustrate the roads with no bike path (the citizens don’t feel safe enough 
to drive on those roads), affecting also the choice of mode. Of course this depends on aspects like 
road type, the amount of vehicles and speed. The access to different transport modes illustrates 
the different possibilities the citizens have in order to interact with each other and to engage 
themselves and explore the city life and the recreational areas.  
All the 12 indicators have an effect on each other in some way, illustrating that transport is a 
complex matter and therefore stating that QoL is also a complex matter. It is the evaluation of the 
12 indicators that can state the QoL of the citizens.

Chapter 11. Quality of Life Assessment by the Indicators 
The objective of the “Quality of life by Transportation” framework is to state the Quality of Life of 
the citizens in the evaluated cities in order to create awareness and identify areas of improvement. 
The framework can also be used as a tool to compare the Quality of Life among cities in Denmark. 
Therefore, it is important to develop a method to rate the performance of the indicators  in each city 
where the framework is to be used. This will generate an overview of how the citizens’ QoL is 
affected by transportation. 
The assessment of the indicators shows the level of QoL for each domain and for all the domains 
together. To assess the indicators, a scale to rate the Quality of Life Performance was developed 
for each indicator, and then the Spider Web chart is used to illustrate the QoL performance of each 
indicator. This QoL Performance rate goes from 0 to 10, where 0 displays a totally unacceptable 
level of QoL and 10 displays a perfect/acceptable situation of the QoL of the citizens.  
The scale will be compared with an Index of measurement based on the results of the assessment 
of each indicator, to find the QoL performance of the indicator. When developing the index of 
measurement for each indicator, both standards, the ideal QoL situation and the actual QoL 
situation was taken into account to state the unacceptable situation and the ideal situation. For 
example, the noise level is acceptable below 50 dB, therefore if nobody is exposed to levels of 
noise above 50 dB, then its Quality of Life Performance  would be 10, meaning that this is the ideal 
situation in the city.

12.1.1 Level 1: Environmental/Health indicators 
Emissions in µg/m3 per year and Premature deaths per km2 related to emissions 
A project called “air quality in your street” was developed by the municipality of Roskilde to assess 
the level of pollution in the municipality and figure out how this affects the Quality of Life of the 
people. The indicators assessed in this study were the Emissions in µg/m3 per year and premature 
deaths per km2 related to emissions. The pollutants selected were NO2, PM2.5, and PM10. 
NO2 was selected due to problems with exceeding the limit value at a monitor station at H.C. 
Andersens Boulevard in Copenhagen. PM2.5 and PM10 are chosen because these two pollutants 
are considered to have the greatest health impacts on people. 
The concentrations are calculated using air quality models, together with input data on emissions, 
meteorology, etc. There are two different contributions to air pollution. Regional air pollution is 
determined by all sources in Denmark and abroad. Urban background concentrations are 
determined by the sources in the city and the regional emissions, while air pollution in a street is 
determined by the vehicle traffic in the streets, as well the emissions from urban background.

their QoL by their ability to connect without too much difficulty and proving for good opportunities 
for the use of different transport modes. However, this indicator is performing a little better in 
Roskilde even though the difficulty degree is worse. The cycle network is the same which also 
could influence the choice of mode. The other indicators are providing for the same QoL 
performance, which means that citizens in the two cities are affected equally. Hillerød is performing 
slightly better than Roskilde, illustrating that the citizens have a better connectivity and safety 
environment. The health environment is more or less the same indicating that the two cities have 
some of the same challenges with regard to the noise levels and the choice of an active transport 
mode in the city.

Chapter 13. The Framework Evaluation 
The evaluation of the framework is about taking into account all the different aspects which can 
affect the frameworks further utilization.  
 
The utilization by the municipalities 
Taking a step back to the Municipality Planning in Denmark, the Danish Cities have an overall 
municipal plan which is reviewed every 12 years. It contains the targets and guidelines for the 
municipality’s development within the areas for which each municipality is responsible.  
The areas are: 
 
Primary education 
 
Child care and care for the elderly and the disabled 
 
Basic health services and promotion of health 
 
Employment of citizens and integration of immigrants and refugees 
 
Public utilities (Water supply, electricity) 
 
Housing, urban development and planning  
 
Environmental protection 
 
Roads and public transport 
 
Leisure, sport and cultural facilities 
 
City planning 
 
The Quality of Life by Transportation Framework contains the domains health/environment, safety, 
connectivity and economic, which influence several areas within transport ; Basic health services 
and promotion of health, environmental protection, roads and public transport, leisure(social 
interaction) and city planning. This indicates that the framework can be used in different elements 
of the municipal planning and that transport has a great impact on city development. The 
municipalities develop different projects to promote and improve the different areas. It is within this 
perspective that the framework has its purpose and effect. The framework can be addressed both 
before and after the development of projects in the municipalities. Before stating the conditions of 
the areas mentioned before, and after to evaluate the effects of the given project. The framework 
can also be a guide for the decision-makers towards different actions that can improve the 
domains of QoL and the lives of the citizens.     
 
SWOT analysis 
The purpose of this analysis is to evaluate different aspects of the framework which can affect the 
application by the municipalities. Four perspectives are taken into consideration; strengths, 
weaknesses, opportunities and threats. In the table below, some aspects have been pointed out for 
each perspective.

Table 13.1 SWOT Analysis of the Framework 
Strengths: 
The framework has a few strengths which are very beneficial for the municipalities and thereby the 
future application by the municipalities. It strengths involve the construction of the framework and 
the operation by the municipalities.  
This is especially due to the framework’s systematic approach in assessing the city’s QoL 
performance. It is straightforward for the municipalities to use and it gives a clear picture of the 
performances of the different QoL areas and thereby the citizens QoL. The specific scale methods 
for each indicator insure comparability between two or more cities, which is a benefit for the 
municipalities to find out if some have received a better QoL performance. It is a benefit for the 
framework that it does not contain too many indicators, which makes it manageable and less costly 
to produce. One of the framework’s strengths is the clear purpose of illustrating where to address 
their attention; the QoL area and within the area.  
 
Opportunities: 
The framework has two very good opportunities which can be very beneficial for the future citizens’ 
lives. The opportunities involve drawing attention to the QoL of the citizens and secondly 
interaction between municipalities.   
The framework can raise the recognition of how important traffic conditions and the infrastructure 
are for the QoL of the citizens. In order to improve the QoL conditions for the citizens the 
framework can draw attention to the different areas that need further investigation.  
The comparability can raise the opportunity for the municipalities to address each other in figuring

opportunity for the municipality to share knowledge and practices applied in municipal planning and 
projects.

Weaknesses: 
The framework has four weaknesses which can prevent the use of the framework by some 
municipalities. The weaknesses involve the “optimal” indicators and the data needed for the 
assessment.  
The framework has been established in cooperation with two municipalities as representatives for 
all the middle sized Danish cities. They have their own focus area and plans which have been used

The framework consists of twelve indicators which are very different, and demand different kind of

Threats: 
The framework has three threats which can prevent the use of the framework. The threats involve 
the task of data availability/collection. 
The municipalities have to assess twelve different indicators which can both be troublesome and

and after a project to see if the QoL performance has changed. For that, data needs to be collected

performance the data must not be too old otherwise it does not fully illustrate the QoL performance 
of today. If the data is too expensive to collect the framework may never be applied or if the data is 
too troublesome to collect, that they don’t believe it is worth the effort or investment. Another 
aspect is that the municipalities aren’t used to apply this kind of framework and it may not have the 
highest interest.   
The last threat is if the municipalities recognize a different framework to address the QoL of their 
citizens.

The rate scales were created for comparing the municipalities and address the indicators against

their own rate scale in order to illustrate where they are against where they want to be.

Chapter 14. Discussion and Conclusion 
 
Even though transportation is not a human need; it is a fact that we need transportation in our 
everyday life to be able to do our everyday activities, to socialize and to be connected with other 
cities/communities.  
The main objective of this thesis was to identify the effects of transport in Danish cities, to analyze 
how the use of transport affects the mobility, the security and the health of people; in order to 
create awareness of how transport affects their Quality of Life.  
To find out, we sought to answer the following question: 
“How does transport influences the QoL of the citizens of “Danish cities” and how to create a 
framework to select and apply transport indicators to assess Quality of Life performance in Danish 
Cities?” 
It is certain that transportation has both positive and negative effects on the life of people. In order 
to identify these effects, a set of Quality of Life elements was created based on the Maslow 
pyramid of hierarchy needs ((Sirgy,1986,331), which states that humans have five needs, where 
each need has to be fulfilled before climbing to the next need. These needs are: Biological needs: 
food, water, oxygen; Safety needs: physical and psychological security, health services; Social 
needs: social interaction, recreation, leisure; Esteem needs: achievement, success; Self-
actualization needs: creativity, integrity, and aesthetics. Each one of these stages represents the 
level of Quality of Life; the more stages we have covered, the better the QoL. The following 
statement supports the fact that these five human needs form the quality of life of people living in 
cities/communities.  
“Examples of quality of life include a community infrastructure that is accessible to all and supports 
the gathering of residents, healthy ecosystems that provide the region with valuable resources 
such as clean water and scenic vistas, and communities or regions that support the sense of place 
cherished by residents.” (Rogers et al., 2011,2)  
How is this definition of Quality of Life related to transport? We stated in chapter 5 that there are 
three principles that represent the connection between transportation and Quality of Life (M. 
VanZerr and S. Seskin,2011,3). These principles state that cities should improve air quality; reduce 
emissions; promote health; improve travel times to jobs, education and services; and invest in 
health, safe and walkable neighborhoods.   
Based on the QoL elements stated in chapter 5 and these three transport principles related to 
Quality of Life, we concluded that the effects of transport in the QoL of people are: 
 
 
Accessibility/Connectivity:  Transport connects cities and communities, and it allows people 
to travel wherever they want to, depending on aspects like money, time and health.  
 
Physical activity: Using car and public transport as a transport mode prevents people from 
being physically active however, biking and walking maintain people active, having a 
positive effect on their health.  
==PAGE:146==
146 
 
 
Economic growth:  Transportation supports economic growth by the transportation of goods 
and people. This gives access to large variety of goods, which supports the citizen's needs 
of food, socialization and leisure.  
 
Poor air quality and noise: Poor air quality affects the health of the citizens by causing 
respiratory illnesses, reducing their life expectancy and affecting their breathing conditions. 
Noise stresses people and prevents them from resting at night, affecting their performance 
at work or education. It also causes cardiovascular diseases, anxiety, depression and 
aggressiveness. (DTU Transport 2013) Poor air quality and emissions prevent people from 
socializing at locations where levels of pollution and noise are high. 
 
Congestion: Congestion causes pollution and makes travel time long. This affects the 
citizens' choices of transport mode and the route of the trip. Congestion creates a feeling of 
frustration and prevents people from spending time in other activities like social interaction, 
exercising or leisure.  
 
Accidents: Accidents create an unsafe environment and affect the health of people. It can 
prevent people from using certain transport modes and the choice of transport route.  
 
Cost of transportation: The cost of transport influences the choice of transport mode and 
the personal budget. Spending a lot of money on transport prevents people from spending 
it on activities like leisure, holidays and social activities.  
 
These positive and negative effects have an impact on people's everyday life and Quality of Life, 
but to what extent are these effects influencing people of Danish cities? This was answered in the 
second part of our research question, where we created a framework to apply transport indicators 
to measure the Quality of Life in the cities of Roskilde and Hillerød.  
The framework “Quality of life by Transportation” contains 12 transport indicators grouped in four 
domains: Health & Environment, Safety, Connectivity and Economic domain. Chapter 4 explained 
the process of development and application of the framework.  
It has been observed that the municipalities have different visions and plans for creating a livable 
city for their citizens. They recognize that the different domains (social, economic, health, safety, 
and environment) of transport planning have an effect on the citizens; however they do not 
consider these domains as a whole. The QoL is about recognizing and understanding that the 
different domains all together create the QoL. Separately they influence the QoL, but it is not until 
they are considered together that the situation of how transport affects QoL, and how it influences 
the citizens’ everyday life can be measured. 
With the application of the “Quality of life by Transportation” framework to the cities of Roskilde and 
Hillerød, it was possible to measure their QoL, by the Quality of Life Performance rate explained in 
Chapter 4, obtaining the following results:

The city of Roskilde obtained a QoL performance of 6, which is just above average. The areas that 
Roskilde needs to improve are: 
 
Levels of pollution: It was observed that the number of premature deaths in Roskilde is 
higher than the average in a Danish Municipality. 
 
Barrier effect: Some important roads are difficult to cross for pedestrians or bikers.  
 
Safety in roads: The number of accidents is higher than the average in a Danish 
municipality, as well as the number of injured and death.  
 
Active and green transport modes: 50% of the people still use the car as their main 
transport mode in the city.  
 
The city of Hillerød obtained a QoL performance of 7, which is a good QoL performance. However, 
no data from Hillerød was obtained regarding the levels of pollution or the number of premature 
deaths related to emissions. This could have changed the QoL performance of the city, but it is 
uncertain to say how much. From the data obtained, it is noticed that the areas that Hillerød needs 
to improve are: 
 
Safety in roads: The number of accidents is higher than the average in a Danish 
municipality, as well as the number of injured and death.  
 
Active and green transport modes: 60% of the people still uses the car as their main 
transport mode in the city.  
 
The application of the framework gives an overview to the city planners and politicians of which 
areas needs to be improved to improve people's QoL when they have to assign priorities and 
budget to transport projects.   
The overall goal of the framework “Quality of life by Transportation” is to secure Quality of Life of 
people in Danish cities and to create awareness among city planners and politicians of the benefits 
and consequences of transportation related to Quality of Life, for current and future plans and 
projects in the cities.  
 
We are aware that the framework “Quality of life by Transportation” has some limitations. The 
development of the criteria and the indicators was based on the knowledge, experience and 
research done by the developers, and therefore if they had to be developed and ranked by other 
decision makers, the outcome might have been different.  
An alternative way of constructing the framework could have been to ask the municipalities 
specifically which indicators they would have liked to form the framework. However, the indicators 
might not have been the same for both municipalities, ending up with two different frameworks. 
They did have the opportunity to rank the 23 indicators that were first developed according to their 
preferences and goals, but they found this task overwhelming and difficult. It is uncertain to say 
how much this could have changed the outcome.   
==PAGE:148==
148 
 
An alternative feature of the framework could be that the municipalities could be able to switch 
between the indicators of the framework, implementing those who they think have more priority 
and deleting those whom they do not find relevant for their goals and mission.  
Nevertheless, the feedback obtained from the municipality of Roskilde about the assessment of the 
framework, states that it is a good and useful framework. Hillerød municipality was not able to send 
feedback due to personal inconvenience.  
 
A possible further assessment of this framework would be to apply it specifically for different 
groups of people in Danish cities: elderly, disabled, families, people working outside the city, 
people working in the city, etc. This would be interesting, as it would show the perceived level of 
Quality of Life for each group of people living in a city.

Appendix I. Feedback from the Municipalities 
 
1. What do you think of the framework? 
 
“Overall, I think it is a good framework. You can talk about mobility in many ways, and an angle is if 
mobility is a mean to achieve a goal/destination or whether it is a goal in itself and the destination 
is not important. I would like that your framework had more focus on whether one can easily 
achieve his errands. You have a theme called connectivity indicators, which I would like included 
things like distance to work, school, shopping or recreation areas” [Original Version, Danish] 
 
2. 
What do you think of the 12 chosen indicators. Do they match with your interests as 
municipality? 
“ They overlap to some extent, but I would probably have to take some of the numbers from 
Region Zealand's health profile.  
Our policy is divided into four themes, each with its objective: 
 
1) Nice and easy access. 
- Traction on municipal roads must be improved for all road users. 
- Easy and attractive transfer options to get road users to combine flexible and more sustainable 
ways of transporting themselves. 
- Facilitate road users should be able to get more directly to major destinations. 
 
2) Health, movement and children. 
- There must be good access for all to walk or bicycle to natural and recreational experiences. 
- As much of all age groups should have the opportunity to be active and mobile in everyday life. 
- More children should cycle or walk to school. 
 
3) Green mobility 
==PAGE:188==
188 
 
- The sustainable transport must be more attractive, quick and comfortable so that it can be a 
viable alternative to the car. 
- The need for transportation will be reduced through good localization, as short distances in 
stronger national sustainable means of transport to the most obvious choice. 
- Bus transport must be made more environmentally friendly and less noisy. 
- More must walk, cycle and use public transport, so that in 2020, 50% of all trips are sustainable. 
 
4) The active and exciting city life 
- Roskilde should be a municipality with urban space without annoying noise and air pollution, 
where more people stay both more often and longer and give the city more life. 
- The urban spaces must be used flexibly and spontaneously and offer a framework for a diverse 
urban entertainment and commercial life that gives residents and visitors unique experiences on 
market squares, and supports local activities and communities. 
- It should be easy to find your way through cities and learn about interior design, commercial and 
cultural facilities. “ [Original Version, Danish]
