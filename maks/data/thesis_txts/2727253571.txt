1
Introduction
Groundwater contamination is a major environmental issue that poses a significant threat to human health and
ecosystems. Groundwater aquifers are susceptible to contamination from various sources, including agricultural
activities, industrial processes, landfills, and urbanization [1, 2]. The transport of contaminants in groundwater
is a complex process that is influenced by many factors, such as the characteristics of the contaminant, the
properties of the aquifer, and the hydrological conditionscite [3, 4, 5]. In this research project, the challenge of
predicting contaminant transport from point sources in groundwater aquifers is addressed using machine learning
techniques. A point source refers to a localized contamination origin, while groundwater aquifers encompass
subsurface formations that hold and transmit water. The movement of contaminants in these aquifers involves
intricate interactions, such as flow dynamics, infiltration, and interactions with aquitards, which are clayey till
layers that impede water flow [5]. The primary objective of this research project is to train a machine learning
model to predict the plume profiles in the aquifer from the point source based on establishing a training dataset
using the COMSOL model simulations.
1.1
Mathematical modeling of groundwater contamination transport
The mathematical modeling of groundwater contamination involves the use of numerical models to simulate
the transport and fate of contaminants in the subsurface since 1987 [6, 7, 8].
These models are based on
the principles of mass and energy conservation and are designed to simulate the behavior of the contaminant
under different conditions [9]. Among the commonly used approaches are the transport equation such as the
advection-dispersion equation (ADE), and the reactive transport model (RTM). The ADE is a simplified model
that describes the movement of a contaminant through an aquifer due to advection and dispersion [10, 11]. It
describes the overall movement of contaminants by accounting for their advective transport with groundwater
flow and the dispersion resulting from variations in flow velocities. The transport equation takes into account
the interaction between the contaminant and the porous media, such as sorption and degradation. This equation
serves as a general framework that can be adapted according to the conceptual model, incorporating aspects
such as dispersion, diffusion, dissolution, and reactions. When sorption and degradation processes are consid-
ered, the transport equation can be referred to as a form of reactive transport, although different definitions
exist for this term. On the other hand, the RTM is a comprehensive model that goes beyond the transport
equation, taking into account the complex interactions between contaminants, porous media, and microbial
processes. Microbial activity can significantly influence the fate and transport of contaminants in groundwater
systems [12, 13].
Although these models can provide valuable insights into the behavior of contaminants in groundwater, they
have several limitations. First, they require detailed information about the hydrogeological properties of the
aquifer, which may not be available for all sites [14]. Second, they can be computationally expensive and time-
consuming, which can limit their application to large-scale or real-time scenarios [14]. Third, they may not be
able to capture the complexity of the contaminant transport process, such as the effect of heterogeneity or the
interaction between multiple contaminants [15]. In most cases, a considerable amount of physical simulations
is necessary for conducting sensitivity analysis and uncertainty quantification [16]. Nevertheless, as physical
modeling typically demands a significant amount of CPU resources, executing the original model for all the
realizations becomes impractical [17, 18]. Hence, there is a great need to create an automated and computa-
tionally efficient modeling approach, such as a surrogate model, that can supplement the process-based model

1.2
Machine learning for groundwater contamination modeling
To overcome these limitations, machine learning (ML) has emerged as a promising alternative approach to
modeling groundwater contamination [19, 17, 18, 20]. ML models can learn the complex relationship between
input parameters and objective outputs based on a large dataset of simulation results from physical models or
observations from realistic cases, then use this relationship to make predictions for new cases [21, 22]. One of the
most widely used ML techniques for modeling groundwater contamination is artificial neural networks (ANNs).
ANNs are a type of ML model that is inspired by the structure and function of the human brain [23]. ANNs
consist of multiple connected layers of artificial neurons that can learn and generalize from input data. ANNs
can be trained using a variety of algorithms, such as backpropagation, to optimize the model’s performance. In
addition to ANNs, other ML techniques commonly employed in this context include support vector machines
(SVMs) and random forests.
Support vector machines (SVMs) are another popular ML technique used for groundwater contamination mod-
eling.
SVMs are based on the concept of finding an optimal hyperplane that separates different classes or
categories within the data [24]. They are particularly effective in handling non-linear relationships and can
make accurate predictions based on training data.
Random forests are an ensemble learning method that combines multiple decision trees to create a more robust
model. Each decision tree in the random forest independently makes predictions, and the final prediction is
determined by aggregating the results from all the trees. Random forests are known for their ability to handle
high-dimensional datasets and effectively capture complex interactions and non-linear relationships [25].
ANNs have been demonstrated to be effective in predicting CMD for contaminated sites, as shown in previous
works such as Li’s ANN-based surrogate modeling workflow [17] and Prasianakis [19]’ approach for process
coupling and parameter upscaling. Sprocati and Rolle [20] also proposed a methodology that integrates process-
based reactive transport modeling with machine learning techniques to improve the accuracy of predicting the
effectiveness of electrokinetic remediation for contaminated groundwater. To date, most research in the field
has focused on using artificial neural networks (ANNs) for contaminated sites using reactive transport models.
However, little research has been conducted on applying machine learning to advection-dispersion models. This
project seeks to address this gap by using COMSOL to generate a training dataset based on a 2D advection-
dispersion model and develop a machine learning model to predict vertical profiles downstream of the plume.
By exploring the application of machine learning techniques to advection-dispersion models, this project has
the potential to expand the range of models and tools available to predict CMD for contaminated sites.

subsurface. In addition, addressing these research questions can provide a comprehensive understanding of the
accuracy, the impact of modeling time and distances, model performance, and parameter variations in predicting
contaminant transport in the subsurface.

2.2
Surrogate model for contamination transport
Physical contaminated transport models can provide valuable insight into the behavior of contaminants in
groundwater aquifers. However, these models are often computationally intensive and require large amounts of
data. To address these challenges, surrogate models based on machine learning techniques have been developed.
Surrogate models can be used to approximate the behavior of complex physical models and reduce the com-
putational cost of simulations. In recent years, there has been a growing interest in integrating process-based
reactive transport modeling and machine learning for groundwater remediation applications. The combination
of these two approaches holds promise for enhancing the efficiency and effectiveness of groundwater remediation
efforts. The reasons behind this increasing interest may include the potential for improved prediction accuracy,
faster analysis, and the ability to handle complex interactions and uncertainties. By leveraging the advantages
of machine learning techniques, such as reduced computational requirements and the ability to generalize from
data, the integration of process-based modeling and machine learning can offer valuable insights and practical
solutions for groundwater remediation challenges.
Sprocati and Rolle [20] proposed a methodology for integrating process-based reactive transport modeling
and machine learning for electrokinetic remediation of contaminated groundwater. The authors used a neural
network surrogate model to predict the concentration of contaminants in groundwater, which was then used to
optimize the electrokinetic remediation process. The input to the model included the spatial coordinates, time,
and electrokinetic parameters such as voltage, current, and electrode spacing. The output was the concentra-
tion of contaminants in groundwater. The authors also incorporated uncertainty analysis to account for the
uncertainty in the surrogate model predictions.
Prasianakis[19] developed a neural network-based method for coupling physical models of reactive transport with
machine learning techniques. The proposed method allowed for the upscaling of parameters from small-scale
simulations to larger systems, reducing the computational cost of simulations. The input to the model included
the physical and chemical properties of the aquifer, such as porosity, permeability, and sorption coefficients, as
well as the boundary conditions and initial concentrations of contaminants. The output was the concentration
of contaminants in groundwater over time. The author used a combination of physics-based and data-driven
approaches to train the neural network and optimize the surrogate model.
Li [17] proposed an artificial neural network-based workflow for surrogate modeling in reactive transport simu-
lations. The workflow involved the generation of training data using physical models and the development of
a neural network surrogate model to approximate the behavior of the physical model. The input to the model
included the physical and chemical properties of the aquifer, as well as the boundary conditions and initial
concentrations of contaminants. The output was the concentration of contaminants in groundwater over time.
The authors also incorporated uncertainty analysis and sensitivity analysis to evaluate the performance of the
surrogate model.

2.3
Machine learning concept
Machine learning is a field of computer science that has been gaining significant attention in recent years.
Machine learning algorithms are used to automatically learn patterns in data and make predictions based on
those patterns [28]. The goal of machine learning is to build models that can accurately predict future outcomes
based on training datasets. Machine learning has applications in a variety of fields, including finance, healthcare,
and marketing. Machine learning algorithms can be broadly classified into three categories: supervised learning,
unsupervised learning, and reinforcement learning. One of the most popular approaches to machine learning
is supervised learning, which involves training a model on labeled data to make predictions or decisions about
new, unseen data [28]. One of the most powerful supervised learning techniques is artificial neural networks
(ANNs), which are modeled after the structure and function of the human brain.
2.3.1
Supervised learning
Supervised learning is a type of machine learning where the algorithm learns from labeled data to make pre-
dictions or decisions about new, unseen data [28]. The data used for training the algorithm consists of input
variables (features) and output variables (labels or targets). The goal of the algorithm is to learn a mapping
function between the input and output variables by minimizing a loss function. The loss function measures the
difference between the predicted output and the actual output. There are two main types of supervised learning:
regression and classification [28]. Regression is used when the output variable is continuous, such as predicting
house prices. Classification is used when the output variable is categorical, such as predicting whether an email
is spam or not. The most commonly used algorithms for supervised learning include linear regression, logistic
regression, decision trees, and ANNs.
2.3.2
Feedforward networks
Artificial neural networks are a class of models inspired by the structure and function of biological neurons.
ANNs consist of multiple layers of interconnected nodes, each performing a nonlinear transformation on the

input data. The nodes in ANNs are typically organized in a specific pattern, known as the network architecture
[29].
There are several types of network architectures, including feedforward networks, recurrent networks,
and convolutional networks. Feedforward networks are the simplest type of ANN, where the input data flows
through the network in a forward direction, and the output is produced at the output layer [29]. Each layer
performs its transformation on the input data and passes the output to the next layer as Figure 1.
Each node in the ANN receives an input from one or more nodes in the upper layer, performs a mathematical
operation on that input, and then outputs a signal to one or more nodes in the lower layer. The inputs and
outputs of each node are typically represented as vectors, while the mathematical operations performed by the
node are represented by a set of weights. In a feedforward neural network, signals flow in one direction through
the nodes of each layer represent as each circle in Figure 1, from the input layer shown as blue circles to the
output layer shown as green circles. The input layer receives the raw data, while the output layer produces the
final prediction or classification. The layers in between the input and output layers are called hidden layers,
as their output is not directly observed. The number of nodes and layers in an ANN can vary depending on
the complexity of the problem being solved. In general, more nodes and layers allow the model to learn more
complex patterns in the data, but may also increase the risk of overfitting, which occurs when a machine learn-
ing model becomes overly complex and starts to memorize the training data instead of learning the underlying
patterns and relationships. An overfit model is too tailored to the training data and may not capture the true
underlying patterns. As a result, when applied to new data, the overfit model may exhibit poor performance
and fail to provide reliable predictions.
Figure 1: Basic construction of feedforward networks, which illustrates the network architecture. Blue circles
represent input nodes, black circles represent inner nodes, and green circles represent output nodes. The lines
connecting the nodes depict the mathematical links and flow of information within the network.

introduce nonlinearity into the network, allowing it to learn complex patterns. Common activation functions

2.3.4
Loss function
The loss function in machine learning is used to estimate the extent to which the predicted value of the model,
f(x), does not agree with the true value. The loss function measures the discrepancy between the predicted
output of the network and the actual output, and the goal during training is to minimize this discrepancy.
The choice of loss function can have a significant impact on the performance of the network, and there are a
variety of different loss functions that can be used depending on the type of problem being solved. Mainly,
loss functions can be divided into two broad categories based on the type of learning task will be dealing with:
regression loss and classification loss.
For the regression loss, typical loss functions used are mean squared error (MSE), mean absolute error (MAE)
and Mean Bias Error (MBE). The mean squared error (eq.8) is measured as the average of the squared dif-
ferences between predictions and actual observations. On the other hand, the mean absolute error (eq.9) is
measured as the average of the sum of the absolute differences between predictions and observations. MBE
(eq.10) is essentially the same as MAE, the only difference being that it does not take absolute values. Although
less accurate in practice, it can determine whether the model has a positive or negative bias.

2.3.5
Backpropagation and learning rate
Backpropagation is the most widely used algorithm for training feedforward ANNs. It is a supervised learning
algorithm that involves updating the weights and biases of the neurons in the network to minimize the loss
function [29]. As the figure 2 below shows, it iteratively updates the parameters of the steep descent direction
of the loss function. During back propagation, the gradient of the loss function with respect to each weight and
bias is calculated. This gradient indicates the direction in which the weights and biases should be adjusted to
reduce the loss function. The size of the weight and bias updates is controlled by the learning rate, which is a
hyperparameter that determines the step size in the direction of the gradient. The backpropagation method is

loss function with respect to these parameters. This process continues until the loss function is minimised to
an acceptable level or until a stopping criterion is reached.

2.3.6
Optimization
Optimizers are used to update the weights and biases of the neurons during the backpropagation process. They
determine how the weights and biases are adjusted to minimize the loss function. There are various types
of optimizers used in ANNs, including stochastic gradient descent (SGD), momentum, and Adam.
Adam,
which stands for Adaptive Moment Estimation, is an optimization algorithm commonly used in training neural
networks [28]. It is an extension of the gradient descent algorithm that incorporates momentum and adaptive
learning rate techniques to accelerate convergence and improve the efficiency of the optimization process [28].

Regularization techniques

3.2
Developing of training data
In this study, physical modeling of the transport and fate of contaminants in a groundwater aquifer is addressed.
Specifically, it focus on a real-world scenario where a source area (as depicted in Figure 3) has been contaminated
with pollutants that have already reached the top of the aquifer. The objective is to simulate and analyze the
behavior of these contaminants within the aquifer to gain insights into their transport dynamics based on the
physical model. By developing a reliable physical model, it can better understand how contaminants spread
and interact with the aquifer’s geological and hydrological properties. Also it is essential to provide sufficient
information for training a machine learning model.
3.2.1
COMSOL model setup
In the COMSOL simulation, a source area(figure 4) is considered to be contaminated with a certain concen-
tration of pollutants, which is assumed to have already penetrated to the top of the aquifer. This means that
the contaminant source is already in place and the simulation is focused on the transport and fate of the con-
taminants within the two layers. To monitor the transport of contaminants, 10 observation lines are placed
downstream at regular intervals of 10 meters apart. The position of these observation lines is chosen to cap-
ture the behavior of the contaminant plume as it moves through the aquifer. The simulation output includes
important parameters such as the contaminated mass discharge, maximum concentration, depth of plume, and
center location of the plume, all of which are critical to understanding the fate and transport of contaminants
in the aquifer.
To develop a reliable model of groundwater flow and contamination transport, it is essential to specify appro-
priate boundary conditions for the system. In this study, fixed hydraulic heads were employed to define the left
and right boundaries of the site. Specifically, a hydraulic head of 30m was imposed at the left boundary, while
the right boundary was set to the difference between the left head value and the head gradient (i.e., the product
of the length of the study area and the head gradient). However, it is important to note that this boundary
condition applies only to the selected part for the aquifer, and assuming there is no flow in layer of clayey till.
This configuration ensures that the flow of water is constrained from the left to the right side of the model,
mirroring the natural flow behavior within the aquifer.
Regarding the transport of contaminants, we assumed a uniform flow of the contaminant across the upper
boundary of the aquifer. It is important to note that the upper boundary corresponds to the top boundary of
the model setup. By specifying this condition, we allowed for the outflow of contaminants from the aquifer,
facilitating the representation of the transport process. The equation Dxx * nx * cx = 0 was employed as an
outflow condition, where Dxx represents the dispersion coefficient, nx represents the outward normal vector,
and cx represents the concentration of the contaminant.
To prevent the inflow or outflow of contaminants at the remaining boundaries of the model, a zero flux boundary
condition was set. This condition ensures that there is no mass transfer of contaminants across these boundaries,
maintaining the integrity of the system and preventing any unwanted contamination inputs or outputs.

Model parameters
To fully simulate the movement of contaminants in an aquifer, Twelve different parameters with different val-

the direction of groundwater flow. The hydraulic gradient is the slope of the water table and is used to calculate
the direction and rate of groundwater flow. Porosity, which represents the void space in the aquifer, is also a
critical parameter for understanding the transport of contaminants. Other important parameters include the
distribution coefficient, which describes how strongly contaminants are retained in the soil. The source concen-

3.3
Construction of ANN architectures
In this project, the construction of different artificial neural network (ANN) architectures is achieved using
PyTorch. ANN architectures define the structure and connections of these layers, determining how information
flows through the network. To train the models, the root mean square error (RMSE) loss function is used. This
loss function measures the difference between the predicted output of the neural network and the true output
values. By minimizing the RMSE, the network learns to make more accurate predictions.
To prevent overfitting, an early stop mechanism is implemented during the training process. Overfitting occurs
when the network becomes too specialized in the training data and performs poorly on unseen data.
The
early stop mechanism continuously monitors the validation loss, which is calculated on a separate validation
dataset. If the validation loss starts to increase, indicating that the network’s performance on unseen data is

deteriorating, the training process is stopped. This prevents the network from overfitting and ensures better
generalization to new data. To explore various ANN architectures, different hyperparameter combinations are
tested. This includes varying the number of layers and neurons within the ANN. The training process loops
through these combinations, and the best-performing model is recorded based on the smallest validation loss.
To assess the modeling performance, two approaches are considered. Firstly, a single model is trained, where the
output is a single value corresponding to the input parameters. This model is trained independently. Secondly, a

the exploration of different strategies for involving these variables in the prediction process.

to the occurrence of large residuals. On the other hand, higher values of infiltration and the sorption coefficient
in the up layer are associated with high residuals. The percentage of training data falling within the ranges
associated with low groundwater velocities is relatively low, suggesting that the lack of data in this range may
contribute to the poor performance of the model in predicting output variables.
When considering all distances and years, similar trends are observed for maximum concentration and width
predictions. Low groundwater velocity parameters, including hydraulic conductivity and gradient, continue to
have a significant influence on the modeling residuals. Infiltration and the sorption coefficient also contribute
to the presence of large residuals, although their impact appears to diminish when compared to the analysis
with a single year and distance. The percentage of training data causing large residuals for these parameters is
relatively high, indicating that the issue is not solely attributed to insufficient training data.
When modeling width and center depth in scenarios with low groundwater velocity and high infiltration, the
model is difficult to capture the dynamics of the plume. This could due to the modeling concentration is low in
the aquifer. In such situation, the low groundwater velocity hinders the movement and spread of contaminants,
making it harder to accurately predict the width and center depth of the plume. Additionally, high infiltration
rates can cause rapid dispersion and dilution of the contaminants, further complicating the modeling process.
The determination of plume existence in COMSOL is often based on a concentration-to-source concentration
ratio threshold, such as 0.001. If the concentration in the aquifer is significantly lower than this threshold, the
model may not detect the presence of a plume, leading to confusion during machine learning training. This
confusion arises because the training data heavily relies on the COMSOL model outputs, and if the physical
model does not show a plume due to low concentrations, the machine learning algorithm may struggle to learn
the dynamics and characteristics of the plume accurately.
In conclusion, the analysis of residuals using box plots provides valuable insights into the relationship between
parameter values and modeling residuals. It reveals that low groundwater velocities, represented by hydraulic
conductivity and gradient, consistently contribute to the occurrence of large residuals across different predictions.
Additionally, higher values of infiltration and smaller values of the sorption coefficient are associated with high
residuals. The availability of training data within specific ranges varies for different parameters, suggesting
potential limitations that may impact the model’s performance. Also, when the physical model fails to exhibit
a plume due to low concentrations, the machine learning algorithm may encounter difficulties in accurately
grasping the dynamics and distinctive attributes of the plume.

Discussion
5.1
Summary of results
Through a comprehensive analysis of the Artificial Neural Network (ANN) results, it was observed that the
trained ANN model exhibited comparable performance to the physical model, successfully capturing the under-
lying dynamics of the system and providing valuable insights into the prediction of output variables including
CMDďź maximum concentrationďź and width of the plume. The trained ANN model performs well in pre-
dicting the center depth of the plume when the depth is above 10 meters underground. However, as the depth
increases significantly, the model faces challenges in accurately predicting the center depth.
From the results, it can be concluded that the single-output model, trained to predict a specific target variable,
outperforms the model trained for the simultaneous prediction of multiple output variables including CMD,
maximum concentration and center depth. The single-output model demonstrates superior accuracy and pre-
cision, indicating its effectiveness in capturing and predicting these target variables of interest more reliably.
However, the multiple outputs model exhibits superior performance in predicting the center depth of the plume
compared to the single output model.
Moreover, when incorporating a wider range of distances and years into the model, it becomes evident that
the task of capturing the underlying complexity and making accurate predictions becomes more challenging.
As a result, the model’s performance may be compromised, leading to decreased accuracy and reliability in
its predictions. This highlights the trade-off between incorporating more input variables and maintaining the
model’s predictive capabilities.
Furthermore, specific output variables exhibit varying levels of predictability. Parameters such as Contaminant
Mass Discharge (CMD) and maximum concentration demonstrate strong predictive capabilities, with the model
effectively capturing the underlying dynamics of these variables. However, challenges arise when attempting
to accurately predict the deep depth of the plume. The model’s performance in predicting the center depth
is comparatively lower, indicating the complexity and uncertainties associated with this particular variable.
For predicting the width, in scenarios where the plume formation is less likely or non-existent, accurate width
predictions become more challenging.
The analysis of residuals for the prediction of the maximum concentration, width, and center depth of the plume
revealed important findings. Firstly, the concentration data distribution exhibits a pronounced skew towards
lower values. As a result, the test datasets predominantly consist of instances with low concentrations, leading
to an insufficient representation of high-concentration cases. This disparity in concentration distribution under-
mines the model’s ability to accurately predict plumes with elevated concentrations, compromising its overall
performance in capturing the full range of concentration dynamics. Moreover, the analysis demonstrates the
model’s struggle in accurately capturing center depth and width in scenarios with very low concentrations. The
complex dynamics associated with low groundwater velocity and high infiltration pose significant challenges
for the model. These scenarios hinder the model’s predictive performance, making it difficult to adequately
represent the intricate behavior of the plume.

5.2
Limitations
Additionally, the determination of plume existence in COMSOL relies on a concentration-to-source concentration
ratio threshold, such as 0.001. When the concentration in the aquifer falls significantly below this threshold, the
model may fail to detect the presence of a plume. This discrepancy can lead to confusion during machine learning
training, as the training data heavily relies on the COMSOL model outputs. Consequently, the model may
struggle to accurately learn the dynamics and characteristics of the plume under low-concentration conditions.
5.2
Limitations
Despite the valuable insights and promising results obtained from the analysis of the ANN model, it is im-
portant to acknowledge certain limitations that are inherent to the project. These limitations may impact the
generalizability and applicability of the findings and should be taken into consideration when interpreting the
results. The key limitations of the project include:
1. Fixed Ranges of Training Parameters: One limitation of the project is that the ranges of the training
parameters are fixed. This means that the model is trained using a specific range of parameter values,
and it may not perform optimally when presented with parameter values outside of this range. The fixed
ranges may restrict the model’s ability to accurately predict plume characteristics in scenarios where the
parameter values deviate significantly from the training range. The inclusion of a wider range of parameter
values during training could potentially enhance the model’s performance and improve its generalizability.
2. Limited Prediction Scope: Another limitation of the trained model is its capability to predict only two
layers and one contamination source. The model’s performance is constrained to this specific prediction
scope, and it may not be applicable to scenarios with a different number of layers or contamination sources.
This limitation restricts the model’s versatility and may require additional development and training to
accommodate a broader range of prediction scenarios.
3. Simplified Representation of Plume Dynamics: The project assumes a simplified representation of plume
dynamics, which may not capture the full complexity of real-world scenarios. The model relies on certain
assumptions and simplifications to make predictions, which may introduce uncertainties and limitations
in its accuracy. It is important to consider the simplified nature of the model when interpreting the results
and applying them to real-world situations.
4. Absence of Uncertainty Quantification: Another limitation of the project is the absence of uncertainty
quantification in the model predictions. While the model provides predictions for various output variables,
it does not explicitly account for uncertainties associated with the predictions. Incorporating uncertainty
quantification methods would enhance the reliability and robustness of the model’s predictions by providing
a measure of confidence or error estimation.

Outlook

formance and applicability of the trained ANN models in predicting contamination transport in groundwater
aquifers.

Outlook
Firstly, expanding the range of input data used during training is crucial to improve the model’s predictive
capabilities. The current limitations imposed by the predefined range of input parameters can be overcome by
incorporating wider values. This can be achieved through extensive field studies and data collection efforts,
ensuring that the training dataset encompasses a more comprehensive representation of real-world scenarios.
By capturing a broader range of input parameter combinations, the model will be better equipped to handle
cases that lie beyond the existing boundaries and provide more accurate predictions.
Secondly, refining the plume identification criteria is essential for overcoming the limitations imposed by the
COMSOL software’s threshold based approach. A more robust and adaptable method for defining plume exis-
tence should be explored, one that considers factors beyond a fixed concentration with the source concentration
ratio. Incorporating additional indicators such as spatial distribution patterns or other relevant parameters can
enhance the model’s ability to identify plumes accurately, even in cases of low-contaminant concentrations.
Furthermore, exploring more complex network architectures for the ANN models holds promise for improving
their predictive performance. While the current models include distances and years as additional input nodes,
leveraging techniques such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks or other time series analysis methods
can provide more comprehensive insights into the time-dependent behavior of the aquifer system. By capturing
the temporal dependencies and trends in the data, these dynamic machine learning models can offer improved
predictions and a deeper understanding of how contamination evolves over time. Furthermore, incorporating
distance-related training methodologies can enable the models to account for spatial variations and better cap-
ture the complex interplay between distance, contaminant sources, and transport processes. This will enable
the model to capture intricate relationships and non-linearities within the data, potentially leading to more
accurate predictions and a better understanding of the system’s behavior.
Additionally, integrating other complementary modeling approaches with the ANN models can provide a more
holistic understanding of contamination transport in groundwater aquifers. Coupling the ANN models with
physics-based models or incorporating additional data-driven techniques, such as machine learning algorithms
or data assimilation methods, can enhance the overall predictive accuracy and robustness of the models. This
integration of different modeling paradigms can leverage the strengths of each approach and mitigate their
respective limitations, leading to more reliable and comprehensive predictions.
In terms of the training dataset, future research should prioritize increasing its size and diversity. By incorpo-
rating a larger number of cases with a broader range of input parameter values, the models will gain a more
comprehensive understanding of the system’s dynamics and improve their generalization capabilities. This can
be achieved through collaborative efforts, and the utilization of advanced data collection techniques, such as
remote sensing technologies or sensor networks, to acquire a wealth of high-quality data.
