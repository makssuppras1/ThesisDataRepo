Summary
1
Summary
The aim of this project is to investigate the eﬀect of the negative reinforcement on restrained shrinkage
cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs.
A literature review is made to provide a overview of the existing literature, and is used as a basis
prior to the experimental works. From the literature review it’s found that the restrained shrinkage is
limited to the early hours of the fresh concrete, and not many have assessed its inﬂuence on the elastic
modulus of the concrete, in which some of the outcome is questionable.
An experimental programme is designed to investigate the eﬀect of the negative reinforcement on re-
strained shrinkage cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs. Three SFRC slabs are cast, where two of the
slabs contains either Y20 or Y25 reinforcement bars, while the remaining one is without reinforcement
bars. The shrinkage crack development is tracked during 19 days of maturing of the concrete by a DIC
setup.
After 19 days of maturing the SFRC slabs are cut into smaller prisms/specimens, which are tested in
compression either with an LVDT to determine the stiﬀness or a DIC setup to visualize and assess
how the restrained shrinkage cracks get activated in compression.
The results from the experiments showed that the restrained shrinkage is limited to the early hours,
when the concrete is still fresh, and the presence of the negative reinforcement has a reductive inﬂuence
on the elastic modulus of concrete and hereby the stiﬀness of the slab.

Introduction
5
Introduction
5.1
Background and motivation
In a recent project at DTU, bending tests were carried out on two types of slack reinforced ﬁligree
decks. Both types were identically reinforced in the tension zone in the longitudinally direction. One
type of deck had no reinforcement in the compression zone while the other type had. The results from
the bending tests showed surprisingly, that the ﬁligree decks containing reinforcement in the compres-
sion zone acted less stiﬀthan the decks without reinforcement in the compression zone.
A hypothesis was formulated which stated that the reduction in stiﬀness could be traced back to the
fresh concrete, in which the negative reinforcement hindered the free movement of the concrete during
maturing which caused it to crack. These cracks had to be closed before the compression zone could be
fully activated, in which the ﬁligree decks with negative reinforcement would behave less stiﬀcompared
to those without.
Today’s testing standards for evaluating the material parameters of the concrete such as the stiﬀ-
ness are based on cylindrical concrete specimens, which are water cured under ideal conditions. These
conditions are not easy to replicate at the construction site, and if succeeded the concrete elements
usually still contain reinforcement. Nowhere in the standards is it given how to deal with the pres-
ence of reinforcement as the problem regarding its eﬀect on the elastic modulus is not commonly known.
This raises the question regarding the present assumption concerning the stiﬀness of the concrete
containing negative reinforcement. Designers today assume the compression zone to be fully activated
when designing concrete structures, but this assumption might not be fully correct. In order for this
assumption to be fully true, the cracks must be closed, and this will result in an initial deﬂection for
cases with beams and slabs. Prestressed concrete elements are also aﬀected of cracking due to the
negative reinforcement. Designers can be wrong when determining the prestressing force if part of the
force is lost when closing the cracks, and this will result in a lower prestressing force than assumed.
This is a huge concern for the engineering industry.
This project aims to get an understanding of the restrained shrinkage crack development due to the
negative reinforcement, and to assess the eﬀect of the reinforcement degree on the stiﬀness of SFRC
slabs. This is carried out by analysing the stress strain curve of SFRC containing either Y20 or Y25
bars, and then compare it to unreinforced SFRC.
5.2
Aim and objectives
The aim and objectives of this project is to investigate the eﬀect of the negative reinforcement on
the restrained shrinkage cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs.
This will be done by tracking the
development of the restrained shrinkage cracks in reinforced SFRC slabs at early age, and then assess
its eﬀect on the stiﬀness. Finally, the results will be compared to a unreinforced SFRC slab, and plain
reinforced concrete slabs. The objectives are as follows:
1. Classify restrained shrinkage by reviewing the diﬀerent mechanisms that takes place in the early
age of concrete, which will lead to the restrained shrinkage eﬀect, and to use existing literature
to provide a basis for evaluating the results.
2. Assess and quantify the eﬀect of negative reinforcement on cracking and stiﬀness in SFRC slabs,
and to assess the eﬀect of the steel ﬁbres on the stiﬀness as well.
3. Investigate the inﬂuence of diﬀerent sized reinforcement bars on the shrinkage behavior, cracking
and stiﬀness.

5.3
General methodology
This study aims to investigate the eﬀect of negative reinforcements inﬂuence on restrained shrinkage
cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs.
This study has been divided into 3 work packages (WP1-WP3):
• Literature review (WP1): Articles have been gathered, reviewed and rated in relation to
how relevant they are. The most relevant articles are brieﬂy presented, discussed and compared
to each other to provide an overview of what is known regarding the eﬀect of the negative
reinforcement on restrained shrinkage cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs.
• Experimental part (WP2): Three SFRC concrete slabs will be cast containing either Y25
bars, Y20 bars or no bars. The concrete mix and the amount of ﬁbres will be constant throughout
the entire experiment. Strain gauges will be attached to some of the reinforcement, and some
will be embedded into the concrete. The slabs will be stored in a chamber exposed to elevated
temperature, and low RH for 19 days. During the 19 days the crack development is tracked by
DIC. After 19 days, the slabs are moved from the chamber, and are inspected with a microscope
before they are cut into 12 prisms. The prisms are tested in compression to determine the elastic
modulus of the concrete, and the activation of the cracks is tracked by using DIC.
• Analysis (WP3): The cracks, which are found by DIC during the maturing, will be veriﬁed by
a microscope. The analysis will be a breakdown of the stress strain curve of the prisms to see the
eﬀect of the presence of reinforcement, and by DIC to see how the restrained shrinkage cracks
get activated during the compression test. Secondly restrained shrinkage is quantiﬁed from the
data obtained from the strain gauges and DIC. Conclusions will be drawn from here.
5.4
Scope and limitations
The scope of this study is as follows:
• Three SFRC slabs are cast containing either no bars, Y20 bars or Y25 bars.
• Strain gauges are installed on the reinforcement, and in the fresh concrete in order to track the
behavior of the reinforcement and concrete during shrinkage.
• The slabs are matured at elevated temperature, and at low RH to accelerate the shrinkage process
for 19 days.
• The restrained shrinkage crack development is tracked with a DIC setup.
• The slabs are cut into 12 prisms each which are tested in direct axial compression with LVDT in
order to determine the elastic modulus of the concrete, and on the same time tracked by a DIC
setup.
• The stress-strain curves of the concrete obtained from the compression tests are compared to the
results from the DIC setups during maturing & compression and the strain gauges.
The limitations of this study are:
• Insuﬃcient numbers of specimens tested to evaluate the stiﬀness of the concrete in order to
generalize, due to the limited time of the project.
• The start time when tracking the crack development in the fresh concrete is delayed by one hour
due to the casting process and the preparation of specimens.

5.5
Hypotheses
As concrete matures it shrinks which results in a contraction, and if reinforcement is present in the slab
it will restrain the free movement of the concrete. This will gradually build up the tensile stresses, and
when the tensile strength of the concrete is reached it cracks. These cracks will hamper the performance
of the concrete, and they will have to be closed before the concrete will perform as normally expected.
The work of the cracks will be seen on the stress-strain curve of the concrete as it will have a diﬀerence
stiﬀness. This is illustrated below along with the present assumption of the stress-strain curve, in
which the concrete is assumed to be uncracked.
Figure 1: Uncracked concrete vs. cracked concrete
The reduction in stiﬀness of the concrete will have a direct reductive eﬀect on the stiﬀness of the slab.
The negative reinforcement will have to carry an additional load in the areas of the cracks, in which
the reinforcement will experience a higher compressive stress. As the negative reinforcement deforms
the cracks will close gradually, and the concrete gradually loaded, as the load can be transferred in the
areas, where the cracks have been closed. Hereby will the slab act less stiﬀthan assumed, due to the
assumption of an uncracked concrete.
The use of steel ﬁbres in the concrete won’t inﬂuence the stiﬀness directly, but the ﬁbres will have an
eﬀect on the cracks in terms of width and propagation. The width of the restrained shrinkage cracks
are expected to be smaller for SFRC compared to conventional concrete, and consequently SFRC will
act more stiﬀcompared to plain concrete, since smaller crack widths have to be closed. This leads to
the following hypotheses of this study:
1. Restrained shrinkage induced cracks reduces the elastic modulus of the concrete and hereby the
stiﬀness of the slab.
2. The increase of the reinforcement ratio increases the reduction of the elastic modulus due to an
increase of restraining eﬀect of the concrete.
3. The reduction of the elastic modulus will be less for SFRC than for plain concrete, since smaller
shrinkage crack openings are expected.

6.1
Introduction - Steel ﬁbres in concrete
Concrete is a brittle material and its advantages lie primarily within its compression strength, low
cost and ability to protect the reinforcement from external degradation mechanisms such as chloride
ingress, ﬁre exposure etc. One major disadvantage with plain concrete is its low tensile strength, and
when reached the concrete starts to crack. If no reinforcement is present in the concrete the crack
will propagate through the concrete. If reinforcement is present the crack will form a gate way e.g.
chlorides to penetrate the concrete and the reinforcement will start to corrode. This is a huge problem
as the service life of the structure will be reduced considerably.
These problems can to some extend be improved with the use of steel ﬁbres to the concrete. Steel
ﬁbres enhances the tensile properties of plain concrete such as toughness/ energy absorption and crack
propagation [4]. The steel ﬁbres does not enhance the elastic properties of uncracked concrete e.g.
compressive strength or elastic modulus, but the ﬁbres are ﬁrst activated when the ﬁrst crack forms.
The ﬁbres bridging the crack opening will allow for tensile stresses to be transferred across the crack
as showed on the Figure 2 [4].
Figure 2: Plain concrete vs. SFRC, Illustration right[16]
In the ﬁrst area "(1)" the stresses in the concrete are transferred through the elastic bonding in the
concrete. At the peak the tensile strength of the concrete is reached and the ﬁrst crack will form. Plain
concrete will fail as nothing is hindering the crack to progress through the concrete. For the SFRC the
crack will continue until a ﬁbre is met. At ﬁrst the stress is transferred through a mix of interlocking
between the aggregates & the ﬁbre and shortly after the ﬁbre takes over completely as the ﬁbre and
concrete start to debond, which is illustrated in area "(2)" [21] [4]. The load is now carried by the
ﬁbre and is transferred to the concrete by the frictional bonding between the concrete and the ﬁbre.
SFRC will fail when the ﬁbre is fully pulled out.
Today steel ﬁbres has been used in many structures and in Denmark examples of such structures are
given in the following:
(I) In 2007 COWI ﬁnished a 3,9 kM tunnel carrying heating piping from Copenhagen to Amager by
only using SFRC [13].
(II) The high bay warehouse at the Carlsberg brewery in Fredericia has the inner parts of foundation
cast with steel ﬁbres.
The ﬁbres are typically chosen for its low cost and its mechanical properties.
Even though steel
ﬁbres are more expansive to produce than conventional reinforcement, the cost savings are found in
the time saved binding the reinforcement. Steel ﬁbres cannot replace reinforcement in areas with local

6.2
Quantiﬁcation of shrinkage in Steel ﬁbre reinforced concrete
In order to understand the mechanisms, which take place during restraining shrinkage, it is necessary
to quantify shrinkage in concrete in relation to: I) Types of shrinkage, II) Concrete mixture, III)
Environmental conditions and IV) Other properties of SFRC.
6.2.1
Types of shrinkage
Shrinkage is generally deﬁned as the volume reduction of the unloaded concrete, which is a three di-
mensional reduction. Shrinkage occurs when the concrete is mixed, and water & cement is starting
to react which is called hydration. The concrete will undergo volume changes due to shrinkage, in
which diﬀerent shrinkage types can take place depending on the environmental conditions, concrete
design etc. The ﬁrst type of shrinkage is Autogenous shrinkage which is shrinkage not caused by
thermal impacts, stresses from external loads or restrains, or loss of moisture to the environment. A
mechanism which is often seen within autogenous shrinkage is the self-desiccation of the concrete. The
concrete will self-desiccate when the cement is hydrating under sealed conditions, which means that
external water is not present. e.g. moisture in the environment, and under constant temperature. The
hydrating cement will start to use the water in the pore-system, and hereby create menisci within the
pores. These menisci will cause a negative pressure within the pore, which will result in a contraction
of the hydrating cement paste [5]. This mechanism is also known as Chemical shrinkage.
It has been reported that autogenous shrinkage is most critical in the early age concrete, as it develops
rapidly at this stage when the concrete hasn’t developed any suﬃcient mechanical properties, which
can cause cracking [5].
Another important mechanism that takes place when the concrete is still young is the evaporation
of water from the concrete surface. The water in the fresh concrete will start to move towards the
surface as the concrete starts to settle, this is called bleeding. The bleed water will lay on the surface
and evaporate. Once the bleed water has evaporated, the free water in the outer pore system will
be drawn to the surface by the continuing evaporation. This will result in a negative pore pressure
and cause a volume reduction[6]. This mechanism is called Plastic shrinkage. The upward moving
water/bleeding water in the fresh concrete is induced by another important mechanism named Plastic
settlement. Plastic settlement is the vertical volume reduction due to the gravitational settlement of
the aggregates, which is pushing the water towards the surface. While plastic shrinkage often leads to
visible cracking on the surface, the cracks due to plastic settlement may be present within the concrete
element [6].
Autogenous shrinkage, including chemical shrinkage, plastic shrinkage and settlement are most promi-
nent in the young concrete. As the concrete matures these eﬀects are getting smaller and other mech-
anisms will start to dominate. One of the most commonly known of these are Drying shrinkage.
Drying shrinkage is deﬁned as the volume reduction due to the loss of water to the environment[5].
This is almost the same as plastic shrinkage, but the diﬀerence is that plastic shrinkage takes place
when the concrete is still fresh, and drying shrinkage takes places when the concrete has harden. Dry-
ing shrinkage takes place as long as water is present in the pores, and it can take many years for the
concrete to dry, as the water has to be transported to the surface in order to leave the concrete. Drying
shrinkage is therefore present for many years, and is typically placed within the category of long-term
shrinkage eﬀects.
Other shrinkage eﬀects are Thermal shrinkage which is the volume changes due to temperature
ﬂuctuations [15] and the less known Carbonation shrinkage, which is the volume changes when the
cement paste reacts with the carbonate ions available in the water or carbon dioxide available in the
air, which results in precipitation calcium carbonate crystals [15] [7].
All these types of shrinkage are related to either the concrete mixture, the environmental conditions or

has to be giving to the concrete mixture and the environment.
6.2.2
Concrete mixture
The plain concrete mix design consists of cement powder, water, and aggregates. Pozzolans e.g. ﬂy
ash and silica fume are typically added to the mix as replacement of the cement powder, and chemical
admixtures are added to enhance the properties of the fresh concrete or how it hydrates.
When concrete shrinks the major part of the shrinkage is related to the cement paste. The cement
paste consists of the mixture between water, sand and cement. Sand do not have a big inﬂuence of
shrinkage but the mix of water and cement has, and this a natural starting point when considering
shrinkage from a concrete design view.
The water to cement ratio, w/c, is important for the pore structure to evolve. As the concrete hydrates
the pore structure will start to form, as the cement reacts with the water in the concrete will leave air
voids. The higher w/c ratio the coarser pore structure is obtained, since the water will be leaving larger
air voids, as there will not be enough cement present to continue the hydration. The other way around
a low w/c ratio gives a ﬁner/denser pore structure due to the increased cement content. It has been
reported that a dense pore structure leads to less shrinkage [3]. This is attributed to the fact that it
takes longer time for the water to be transported to the surface and evaporate. Especially autogenous
shrinkage is aﬀected by the w/c. If the w/c is high, 0.5<, autogenous shrinkage will play minor role as
self-desiccation of the concrete is limited, because more water is available than required for the cement
to fully hydrate. Therefore autogenous shrinkage has a more signiﬁcant eﬀect for concretes with a low
w/c, 0.4> [20].
Even though the major part of shrinkage is attributed to the cement paste, the aggregates have a huge
impact on shrinkage. The aggregates and sand may contain water in its pores which will contribute
to the hydration of the cement, the so called internal curing. This will have a strong eﬀect on the
self-desiccation of the concrete and hereby autogenous shrinkage [5]. On the other hand the aggregates
and sand can also be too dry and hereby absorb the water. The stiﬀness of the aggregates will also
reduce the overall shrinkage as the aggregates are hindering the free movement of the cement paste.
This will result in local stress development, and eventually cracking within the interfacial transition
zone surrounding the aggregates [5]. Overall the ratio between the cement paste and aggregates will
be determining the amount of shrinkage, as an increased aggregate content will reduce shrinkage [2],
due to the less amount of cement paste and increased restraining eﬀect.
As previously mentioned pozzolans are typically used as replacement of the cement powder where the
most commonly used are ﬂy ash and silica fume. Especially these two pozzolans have opposite eﬀects
on shrinkage. The addition of silica fume will result in smaller sized pores, which has a direct inﬂuence
on autogenous shrinkage, due to the increased capillary stresses. This is attributed to the fact that the
chemical shrinkage is four times higher than that of ordinary Portland cement [8]. Fly ash on the other
hand will reduce the amount of ﬁne pores in the concrete, and hereby reduce autogenous shrinkage
[22].
The addition of steel ﬁbres to the concrete mix will not reduce concrete shrinkage, but they will increase
the resistance against the crack propagation, decrease the crack width and the frequency. [5].
Shrinkage is therefore very dependent on the water and cement content. Cement is a constant factor
in the concrete mix, but the water content can vary. As stated above aggregates can add water to
the mix or absorb water, but the water content can also ﬂuctuate due to external impacts, such as
environmental conditions.
6.2.3
Environmental conditions
The environmental conditions have a huge impact on how the concrete matures, hereby gaining its me-
chanical properties, and how it shrinks. The two most important factors are the ambient temperature
and the availability of water.
When maturing at high temperatures, processes are accelerated in the concrete. The hydration pro-
8
==PAGE:15==
Literature review
cess is accelerated, and this means a acceleration of the strength development and the elastic modulus,
which typically is a good thing. As a consequence of a high temperature, the evaporation rate is accel-
erated, which increases plastic shrinkage [18] and drying shrinkage [22]. These two types of shrinkage
are eliminated when the concrete is water cured, but water curing at construction sites is diﬃcult and
instead the water is found in the moist. The relative humidity, referred as RH, will provide water to
the concrete if the RH is high. If the concrete is matured at high temperatures and low RH drying
shrinkage will further increase, but autogenous shrinkage will decrease due to the less availability of
water in the pores in order for the concrete to self-desiccate. High temperature and low RH maturing
also aﬀect the hydration process and therefore the mechanical properties of the concrete. The other
way around, it has been reported that maturing at a high RH results in less shrinkage during maturing
[10] but an increasing drying shrinkage after maturing has ended due to the high moisture level in the
pores. Generally maturing at high temperatures leads to higher shrinkage in the early age concrete
and smaller long term shrinkage [10] as illustrated in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Shrinkage of concrete cured at dry conditions
The decrease in shrinkage rate at high temperature maturing is due to the rapid evaporation of the
water. At some point the availability of water is limited which has a direct inﬂuence on the shrinkage.
This means that shrinkage is closely linked to the weight loss [2]. Investigations state that the ultimate
shrinkage of concrete is the same, given the same concrete mix, but is reached at diﬀerent times
depending on the maturing conditions [1]. Maturing of concrete is quite complex and there is no direct
answer on how exactly the concrete will react to the diﬀerent maturing conditions, only tendencies in
which the designer should be aware of. Another aspect, with regards to the maturing conditions, is
the size of the specimen. In high climate temperature the size of the specimen has a large eﬀect on
the shrinkage strain, as it takes longer time for the internal water to be transferred to the surface and
evaporate, in which it has been reported that the rate of shrinkage decreases with the increasing size of
the concrete element [2]. The exposed surface area is an important factor as well, in which evaporation
increases with the increasing surface area. In practice this is overcome by simply sealing of the surface
with a plastic sheet, and then the evaporated water will be trapped inside a sealed system.
Shrinkage is quite harmful for the concrete if precautions are not taken. Luckily, the concrete have
some properties which, to a certain point, neutralizes the negative eﬀects introduced by shrinkage.
6.2.4
Other properties of SFRC
Since the crack development due to the restraining eﬀect of the reinforcement is investigated, it’s
essential to know the properties of the concrete which diminishes the restraining eﬀect. The concrete
cracks when the tensile stresses reaches the tensile strength, but sometimes the concrete doesn’t crack
even when expected. Two phenomenons are introduced Creep and Stress relaxation. Creep is the
increase in deformation of concrete at a constant stress level, and stress relaxation is the decrease in

stress under a given level of sustained strain [17]. These two phenomenons have a substantial eﬀect as
cracking can be delayed and even not occur, which is illustrated in Figure 4 for stress relaxation.
Figure 4: Relaxation of shrinkage stresses [17]
As seen, the stress relaxation can have a huge inﬂuence. Typically creep and stress relaxation are
coinciding in which they can be hard to distinguish and measure directly.
Even if cracking occurs the concrete has the ability to repair its self to some extend, by diﬀerent mech-
anisms generally referred to as Self-healing. The cracks will either be sealed or completely be closed
and the damaged concrete is able to almost regain it’s mechanical properties. Many mechanisms can
contribute to the self-healing of concrete, but the two most important mechanisms are I) the continued
hydration of unhydrated cement paste and II) carbonation. Carbonation will seal the crack by precip-
itation of calcium carbonate crystals (CaC03) when the calcium ions in the concrete reacts with either
the carbonate ions in the water or C02 in the air. Carbonation is the most eﬃcient in terms of crack
sealing, but the continuing hydration is the most valuable for regaining the mechanical properties [7].
All these diﬀerent types of shrinkage and their relation to the concrete mixture, external impacts
and the neutralizing mechanisms are extremely complex. However these interactions are at the outta
most essential to understand, when investigating restrained shrinkage, in regards to designing the ex-
periment and to be able understand and interpret the outcome. As it will be seen in the next section
restrained shrinkage is a well known phenomenon, but not much literature covers the eﬀect of restrained
shrinkage on the mechanical properties.
6.3
Restrained shrinkage, macro scale studies
Restrained shrinkage can be divided into two types of restrains; external and internal. External re-
strains are often found in construction joints or when concrete is cast on a supporting layer e.g. a base
plate cast on the ground. Internal restrains are found in the reinforcement and aggregates. Especially
external restrains can lead to excessive cracking if the necessary precautions are not taken in which
minimum reinforcement is applied. With the addition of minimum reinforcement to the concrete, the
internal restraining eﬀect of the reinforcement is utilized to control the cracking. It has been discovered
that the reinforcement present in a suﬃcient quantity will distribute strains along the length of the
reinforcement resulting in the formation of more evenly spaced and narrower cracks than on single
crack [5] [14].
In the recent years the area concerning internal restrained shrinkage has started to regain researchers
attention as it’s eﬀect on the performance of the concrete is getting more and more important due to
the higher requirements of the concrete. The main focus of these researches have been on the cracking

Eﬀect of cover depth and rebar diameter on shrinkage behavior of ultra-high-performance
ﬁber-reinforced concrete slabs, 2017 [23]
The goal of this study is to investigate the shrinkage behavior of thin UHPFRC slabs restrained
by internal reinforcing bars.
The following experimental program is used.
12 UHPFRC slabs are cast with dimensions of 600x600x100 mm containing rebars with diﬀerent di-
ameters: 9.5mm, 15.9mm and 22.2mm.
The cover depth is also varied in the diﬀerent slabs, four cover depths are used: 5mm, 10mm, 20mm
and 30mm.
Strain gauges are mounted on the steel bars and embedded in the concrete to measure the concrete
and steel strains. The embedded strain gauges are placed in diﬀerent height through out the concrete:
13mm, 18mm, 28mm, 38mm and 50 mm.
The concrete mix has a w/c ratio of 0,25 and the steel ﬁber content is 2% (volume ratio).
The slabs are cured at 21,6 C and 62,2 % RH
The following is concluded
I) After 7 hours, the compressive strain in the steel bar steeply increased even though the rate of tem-
perature was reduced. Thus, the strain excluding thermal eﬀect started to increase from this point,
because the steel bar was deformed by concrete shrinkage with hardening rather than the temperature
gradient
II) The shrinkage strains increased very steeply with age until 16h and then they slightly expanded.
After expansion, the strains gradually reincreased and converged to a stable value.
III) The highest shrinkage strain after 30 days is found near the surface, which is due to the water
evaporation is most severe near the surface.
IV) The strain measured in the steel bar varied with temperature because steel is more sensitive to
temperature gradient than concrete.
V) The restrained shrinkage strains were much lower for the concrete without bar, in which the con-
crete was able to deform more. A diﬀerence of 69% is observed.
VI) The strain decreased with the increasing diameter of the bar, which was mostly observed at the
very early age. This is caused by the following reason: To deform the steel bar with a larger diameter,
a higher compressive force is required, compared to that with a smaller diameter. Consequently the
strain increased with the decrease of reinforcement diameter.
VII) Regardless of the bar diameter, all test specimens showed an increase in the strain with increasing
cover depth. This is attributed to the fact that a smaller cover depth was insuﬃcient to transfer the
full restraint force from the concrete to the steel bar.
VIII) In addition, smaller cover depth decreased the eﬀective volume of concrete forcing to deform the
steel bar, so that lower strains were obtained.
IX) The use of steel bar with a higher diameter and a lower cover is unfavorable as it deteriorates the
restrained shrinkage performance of UHPFRC: a higher degree of restrain normally leads to higher
residual stresses and cracking potential.

that restrained shrinkage is most signiﬁcant in the very early age, when the concrete is gaining it’s
properties. The heat from the hydration will cause the reinforcement to expand and after around 7
hours [23] the concrete will be stiﬀenough to compress the reinforcement. The tensile stresses can only
be build up when the concrete moves relative to the reinforcement [20] in which the restrained stress
build up is limited to the ﬁrst 7 hours. No stresses are build up when the reinforcement is moving in
the same way as the concrete when it contracts. On the contrary, Yoo, D.Y., et al.(2014) states that
the shrinkage stresses are build up when the reinforcement is deformed by shrinkage, which is a bit
opposing as the concrete will not be restrained when the reinforcement deforms in the same direction
of the contraction of the concrete with the same strain rate. The restrained stresses in the concrete
are introduced by the diﬀerence in the strain between the concrete and the reinforcement.
In agreement with the hypothesis it has been observed that the increase in reinforcement ratio leads
to a higher degree of restraint, in which the concrete deforms less. Beside from the reinforcement
ratio, the cover depth is inﬂuencing the restraining strain, due to the lag of force transfer between
the concrete and reinforcement [23]. Hereby smaller covers depths lead to smaller concrete strains,
which also mean a increase in restraining. Jafarifar et. al observes that the tensile strength of the
concrete is reduced by 50% in the top quarter of a concrete slab, which clearly shows the inﬂuence of
the restraining eﬀect on the performance of the slab.
IV) The gained knowledge will serve as a starting point in the design of the experimental setup
in order to meet the aim & objectives. It’s seen that restrained shrinkage is most signiﬁcant up until 7
hours, when the concrete will be stiﬀenough to deform the reinforcement. To maximize the restraining
eﬀect, the early age shrinkage of the concrete has to be maximized, in which the two most prominent
types of shrinkage are considered, namely autogenous shrinkage, including chemical shrinkage and dry-
ing shrinkage. Autogenous was seen to be the most signiﬁcant in the early age concrete, and was very
dependent on the concrete mix design. It was found that a concrete with a low w/c ratio increased
autogenous shrinkage, and the presence of ﬂy ash would reduce autogenous shrinkage. Hereby will ﬂy
ash be removed from the mix design and a concrete mix with a low w/c is chosen. When the concrete
shrinks the majority of the shrinkage is related to the cement paste, in which the cement paste ratio
to aggregates could be increased, to amplify shrinkage.
The concrete specimens in this experiment will be stored at high temperature as it was seen that high
curing temperatures led to an increase of early age shrinkage. The form work will also be removed
as soon as possible to expose all the surfaces, and increase the evaporation of water, in which drying
shrinkage will be accelerated. The relative humidity in the curing champer will be lowered to minimize
the water in the air, and hereby the self-healing eﬀect of concrete due to the continuing hydration but
also to increase the drying shrinkage, as reported in the literature. Not much literature mentions the
eﬀect by addition of steel ﬁbres in terms of restraining, only D. P. Bentz and O. M. Jensen states that
steel ﬁbres doesn’t reduce shrinkage but increases the resistance against crack propagation and are
limiting the crack width.

7.1
Preliminary work
A standard concrete mix was cast and poured into a form with the dimensions of 100x400x900. 4xY16
reinforcement bars were placed in the middle equally spaced in the longitudinal direction. The slab
was stored in a room temperature for 14 days.
After maturing the slab was visually inspected and surprisingly no cracks could be seen with the
naked eye. This was the starting point for an extensive literature research on quantifying shrinkage,
and in relation to the concrete mix and maturing conditions, as described in the previous section.
Many concrete tests were conducted with continuously corrections to the concrete mix along with
the literature study, in order to ﬁnd a concrete mix design, which is prone to shrinkage, see Appendix
chapter 1. This resulted in a concrete mix design with a low w/c, containing no ﬂy ash or plasticizer and
an increased cement paste volume ratio. Furthermore it was decided that the slabs for the upcoming
experiment will be stored at elevated temperature, and low RH to accelerate shrinkage based on the
literature study.

Casting
The mixing of the concrete mix is carried out by the following step wise procedure.
1. Dry mixing of the aggregates and cement for 2-3 minutes.
2. Water is added and the concrete is mixed for another 2-3 minutes.
3. The ﬁbres are added and distributed evenly while mixing, and the concrete is mixed for 1 min.
The concrete is checked for ﬁbre bundles and corrected if found.
4. Slump test is carried out to determine the workability of the concrete. This is done by ﬁlling
a cone with fresh concrete.
The cone is then removed and the settlement of the concrete is
measured as the diﬀerence between the height of the cone and the height of the settled concrete.
5. The air content is measured by using a pressure meter.
6. The concrete mix is poured into the formwork and cylinders gradually while the concrete mix is
vibrated with a handheld concrete vibrator for the slab and a vibrating table for the cylinders.
This is done to release the trapped air in the concrete.
7. The cylinders are sealed with a circular steel plate and a clamp, while the concrete slab is left to
settle for 45 mins. The surface is then smoothened and cleaned for any bleeding water appearing
on the surface of the concrete.

7.4.1
Compression test to determine the elastic modulus
The elastic modulus of the concrete specimens are determined from compression testing in a TONI
technik machine, and by using a LVDT to measure the strains. The elastic modulus is determined
in accordance to DS/EN 12390-13. A load cycle of three is used to determine the average initial and
secant elastic modulus. The load step is taken from the standard to 0,6 MPa/s. The initial elastic
modulus is found up to 15% and the secant elastic modulus up to 33% of their strength. For each test
of the elastic moduli is the ﬁrst load cycle rejected.

7.4.2
Removal of reinforcement
The reinforcement bar in the specimens is cored out. The specimens are then left to dry for one day
before they are tested in compression again. This is done to neglect the inﬂuence of the dry specimens
absorbing water which will have en inﬂuence on the elastic modulus. The reference specimens without
reinforcement bars, have also been cored out to verify that the coring doesn’t have an eﬀect on the
stiﬀness, see Appendix chapter 12.
7.4.3
GOM
GOM is a software, which is used to visualize and track the cracks in the specimens. The software
is detecting discrete signals in pictures, which indicate a grey value of a pixel. When tracking grey
values as signals, a maximum amplitude of the signal is required to detect the signal in which a black
speckle pattern is sprayed on to a white surface. Once the pictures are imported into GOM it creates
a grid consisting of facets, which are image areas, typically squared areas consisting of 20 pixels times
20 pixels, and with a point distance of 10 pixels to each facet element. These dimensions will satisfy
the requirement of a facet overlap between 25-50 %, which is deﬁned by GOM, in order to obtain
optimal results. GOM is tracking the movement of the facets in respect to their previous location
as the probability of ﬁnding an identical facet elsewhere on the specimen is extremely low. Hereby
is GOM able to calculate strains, displacements and stresses of the specimens, by determining the
average value of the adjacent facet elements.

Discussion
9
Discussion
In the following section the results are compared to each other and discussed based on the observations
made during the experiment. Based on the outcome of the results the general testing procedure given
by the standards are discussed as well.
9.1
Development of the concrete parameters
By tracking the development of the concrete parameters, it was seen that after approximately 5 days
the concrete strength didn’t evolve, and the elastic moduli were almost constant from day 1 for the
cylinders. This is due to the harsh environment, which was used to accelerate shrinkage. The tempera-
ture in the storage room was between 39,8-40,7 degrees, and the RH between 8,9-13,1%. A combination
of the high temperature and the low RH have dried out the cylinders completely after they were de-
molded. These conditions have almost stopped the hydration process, as no water was available for
the hydration to continue.
This harsh environment also aﬀect the slabs stored in the room, but due to the size of the slabs it is
not believed that the environment have dried out the slabs as fast as the cylinders.
By comparing the compressive strength and stiﬀness of the cylinders it’s seen that concrete batch
2/Y25 and batch 3/Y20 are relative equal. But the compressive strength and stiﬀness are a bit higher
for batch 1/Ref, approx. 22% in compressive strength and 8% in elastic modulus. By considered the
climate development, see Appendix chapter 4, it’s seen that the temperature and RH for the ﬁrst
day of maturing for batch 1/Ref is 36,6 deg & 24,3%. While for batch 2/Y25 and batch 3/Y20 the
temperature is 37,2 deg & 38,6 deg respectively and the RH is 15,6% & 14,7 % respectively.
The diﬀerence in temperature would not have caused the diﬀerence between the batches but the higher
RH could have. The increased RH could have caused a decrease in the rate of evaporation for batch
1/Ref in the ﬁrst 24 hours. Hereby more water would have been available for the concrete to develop
its mechanical properties. This is believed to be the relation between the diﬀerences in compressive
strength and elastic moduli between the cylinders.
9.2
Reinforcement and concrete behavior
The behavior of the reinforcement and the concrete relative to each other for the Y20 slab can be as-
sessed, from the plots of the strains obtained from the embedded strain gauges, and the strain gauges
on the reinforcement. It’s seen that the reinforcement bar expanded slightly in the very early hours due
to the heat generated by the hydration while the concrete contracts. It’s noticed that the compression
reinforcement at the top of the slab expands a bit more than the tension reinforcement at the bottom.
This is attributed to the fact that the reinforcement in the top is closer to the surface exposed to the hot
climate, where the initial temperature is higher at the top of the concrete. This is seen on the Figure 26
After ≈4,5 hours the strains of the concrete and reinforcement bar follows each other, in which
the concrete has become stiﬀenough to compress the reinforcement. The compression and tension
reinforcement start to expand at approximately 6 and 10 days, respectively. It’s the opinion of the
authors, that this expansion is caused by cracking. When the concrete cracks near the reinforcement
bars, it allows the reinforcement to expand. The measurements show that the compression reinforce-
ment expands, where it becomes longer than its initial length. One could think that it could be a fault
within the strain gauges, and the common phenomenon of a "drifting" strain gauge can be seen. The
strain gauge on the reinforcement bar are placed in specimen 6, and by inspecting the X-ray images
on Figure 32, a huge crack going through the whole specimen is seen. It’s believed that this crack
has allowed the reinforcement to expand. As the data logger started when the temperature in the
concrete was still below the temperature in the room, it makes sense that the ﬁnal length measured of
the reinforcement is bigger than the initial length.
The embedded strain gauges were used to measure the restrained shrinkage, in which the concrete
54
==PAGE:61==
Discussion
at the reinforcement would be hindered from moving freely by the reinforcement. By observing Figure
25 it’s seen that the concrete at the reinforcement moves more than the concrete located away from
the reinforcement. The strain gauges measuring the "free" concrete strain were located at the bottom
of the slab. As mentioned above, the temperature in the concrete at the compression reinforcement is
initially higher than the temperature in the bottom of the slab and as observed by other studies, the
temperature has a direct inﬂuence on the rate of hydration [10]. Hereby is the rate of hydration higher
at the embedded strain gauge in the top than the embedded strain gauge in the bottom, which is seen
as the increased strain on Figure 25 .
Restrained shrinkage is clearly visualized on Figure 24, where a big diﬀerence in strain is observed by
comparing the strain of the reinforcement and the concrete. The concrete contracts and the reinforce-
ment expands leading to tensile stresses. These stresses are increasing until the concrete is stiﬀenough
to compress the reinforcement limiting restrained shrinkage to the early hours of the fresh concrete as
reported by other studies [20] [23].
9.3
Shrinkage crack development
From the GOM results during maturing, it’s seen that the cracks develop in the very early hours when
the concrete is still fresh. The cracks can bee seen formed within the interval of 1-4 hours on Figure
20 - Figure 22.
During the casting, the cracks could already be seen in the surface before the speckle pattern was put
on, but in GOM it would seem that they developed one hour after starting the test, see Appendix

displacements so the ﬁrst picture, where the cracks could be seen on, would be the picture after one
hour after casting. This means that it’s the continuing crack opening, which is observed in the interval
between 1-4 hours at a fast displacement rate and the cracks, which are seen on the surface are plastic
shrinkage cracks.
By comparing the surfaces of the slabs obtained from the X-ray analysis on Figure 28 - 30 it can be
hard to distinguish between which of the surfaces are the most cracked one. The Y25 slab is slightly
more cracked in the surface compared to the reference slab and the Y20 slab, but no diﬀerence is seen
by comparing the reference slab and the Y20 slab. However, by comparing the sectional cuts it’s clearly
seen that the Y20 specimens and Y25 specimens are a lot more cracked internally than the reference
specimen. This is shown in Figure 31 - 33. It’s seen that the cracks are propagating through the entire
specimen, but also new cracks are formed internally.
By considering the crack widths, it’s seen that the crack widths measured by microscope are increasing

9.4
Crack activation
The activation of cracks could be seen from the GOM analysis, which showed how the cracks got
activated during compression.
The test showed that some cracks were instantly closed when the
specimen was loaded, and some were gradually closing as the loading was increased. From Table 8 and
Table 9 it’s seen that the cracks displaces less than the crack width measured during maturing. For
some of the cracks it’s due to a instant closure of the crack, and therefore it’s the displacement of the
concrete which is seen, and for other cracks it’s due to a gradual closure.
This is attributed to the following. As the concrete cracks it opens up. The crack is either only in
the surface or propagating into the concrete, either way the crack forms around the particles in the
concrete, which causes a variation in the crack width along the length. When initially loaded the crack
closes until a contact is made somewhere along the crack length, where the crack width is smallest or
a particle blocking the movement of the crack. The crack continues to close at a steady decreasing
rate as more contact points are forming along the crack length. The formation of the contact areas is
attributed to the fact that the direction in which the crack displaces during loading is not the same
direction as the crack opened up. This new direction of crack closure will cause the crack to close
gradually and even not fully close as the mirror opposite sides of a crack will not make perfectly
contact with each other.
This gradual closure of cracks are believed to have a direct inﬂuence on the elastic modulus of the
concrete, as the displacement in these areas are seen to be much higher than of the uncracked concrete
areas. This will result in a less stiﬀconcrete as it will deform more under the same load conditions of
a less severe cracked or uncracked concrete.
Another observation is made from the specimens, where a quarter is removed, and by looking into the
concrete. It’s seen that the same cracks tracked at the surface before removing a part of the specimen,
are now closing at a lower stress. This is due to the fact that some of the stabilizing concrete around
the crack has been removed and the crack is now available to "work" more freely.

9.7
Stiﬀness of slabs
Today when assessing the stiﬀness of a slab with negative reinforcement e.g. by hand calculations, it
is done by assuming the cross-section cracked in the tension zone up until the neutral axis and the
compression zone uncracked. The concrete and the negative reinforcement work together as a homo-
geneous material, in which the reinforcements contribution to the stiﬀness is added by transforming
the reinforcement area by multiplying it with the modular ratio α = Es/Ec.
The stress-strain curves of the specimens with reinforcement in Figure 38 are used to visualize how
the slab work at the negative reinforcement in compression. The stress-strain curves show a decrease
in stiﬀness when comparing the specimens, which are stored in the climate room, to the water-cured
specimens, which represent the stiﬀness the engineers use today. The gradual decrease in stiﬀness for
the secant elastic modulus was found to 13,6 %, 27,9 %, 22 % for the exposed, Y20 and Y25 specimens
with reinforcement respectively compared to the water-cured specimen.
The results show that the concrete at the negative reinforcement is not uncracked and the elastic
moduli is reduced. This means that the negative reinforcement is activated more at the cracks as
the concrete cannot transfer the load across the cracks. Hereby the stresses are increased in the re-
inforcement, in which it will be deformed at a lower load compared to a uncracked concrete. This is
seen in the percentage deviation, when comparing the stress-strain curve of the Y25 specimens with
the exposed ﬁctive specimens, which are calculated to 9,80 % and 22% compared to the water-cured
specimen. Hereby the slab will act less stiﬀthen assumed if it’s not accounted for the reduction of the
elastic moduli of the concrete at the negative reinforcement.
9.8
Testing standards
Based on the results obtained from the tests, several questions are raised towards the current testing
standards in relation to the elastic modulus. When testing concrete parameters the following condi-
tions are prescribed for the testing specimens before testing.
DS/EN12390-2 2012 : The testing specimens are sealed in the forms for at least 16 hours and maximum
three days. They are stored at 20±5 degrees. After demolding they are water cured or stored in a
climate chamber with a RH of 95% so they don’t dry out until they are tested.
ASTM C31/C31M - 18b: The testing specimens are stored for a period up to 48 hours at a temperature
range of 16-27 degrees in an environment that are preventing moisture loss from the specimens. After

Even though the results obtained from the tests are from harsh environments, it is seen from the

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the eﬀect of the negative reinforcement on restrained shrink-
age cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs.
A review of the existing literature in this area was done in order to provide an overview and a basis
prior to the experiment. The following key conclusions was found:
• Restrained shrinkage appears in the very early hours while the concrete is still fresh, in which
the concrete and reinforcement moves relative to each other.
• The restrained stresses are dependent on the stiﬀness of the reinforcement and the amount of
reinforcement.
• Two studies have tried to assess the reduction in elastic moduli: I) One found a reduction in
elastic moduli of 40% for uncracked concrete. This reduction was due to the gradual build up
in restrained stresses and creep. II) The other study stated the reduction in stiﬀness could be
estimated by
Ec
(1+1/(2ηρ)), in which it was concluded that reduction factor didn’t make sense as
the reduction in elastic moduli of concrete decreased with the increase in reinforcement ratio.
An experimental programme was designed to investigated the eﬀect of the negative reinforcement
on restrained shrinkage cracking and stiﬀness of SFRC slabs. Three SFRC slabs were cast along 36
cylinders, where two of the slabs had either Y20 reinforcement bars or Y25 reinforcement bars. The
remaining slab didn’t contain any reinforcement and was used as a reference.
Strain gauges were
mounted on the reinforcement and embedded into the concrete. The surfaces of the slabs were painted
with a white background colour and a black speckle pattern. The slabs were matured in a climate
room at a temperature of 40 degrees and RH on 10% to accelerate shrinkage. The maturing of the
slabs were tracked by a DIC setup.
After 19 days the slabs were removed and cut into prisms/specimens. In each specimen a reinforcement
bar were located in the center with the above mentioned size. The specimens were tested in compression
in order to evaluate the stiﬀness, and to see the activation of the restrained shrinkage cracks. For
evaluation of the stiﬀness the specimens were tested in compression with LVDT with the reinforcement
bar, and with the reinforcement bar removed.
In order to assess the activation of the restrained
shrinkage cracks, the specimens underwent three compression tests with a DIC setup: I) X-ray test
with pictures taken of the front and the inside surface when the specimen was unloaded and when the
loading was 40% of the strength, in order to visualize the crack pattern, II) Crack activation by taking
pictures of the surface every 20 kN up until 40% of their strength and III) A quarter of the specimen
was removed and pictures of the surface and inside is taken every 20 kN up until 40% of their strength
to see if the same cracks at the surface propagated into the concrete and to see them activated.
From the results obtained from the various tests the following are concluded:
• Restrained shrinkage is limited to the very early hours of concrete maturing in which the concrete
and reinforcement moves relative to each other. After 4,5 hours the concrete becomes stiﬀenough
to compress the reinforcement.
• After 6 and 10 days of maturing the reinforcement starts to expand, which is attributed to the
cracking which allows the reinforcement to move.
• The X-ray visualization showed that plastic shrinkage cracks could be seen on all surfaces for all
slabs, but the slabs with reinforcement are more severely cracked internally with cracks going
through the entire specimens.
• Cracks are seen gradually closing during the entire loading of the specimens in which the concrete
displaces more in these areas. This have a direct inﬂuence on the elastic modulus of the concrete.

Conclusion
• From the stress-strain curves for the specimens with reinforcement it could be seen that the
specimens with Y20 and Y25 bar acted less stiﬀcompared to the theoretical stress-strain curves,
which represents the present assumption.
• A direct correlation between the CMOD curve of a crack during compression and the stress-

the CMOD curve of a crack in which it becomes curved.
• A reduction of the initial elastic modulus of the concrete was found to 11,3% for SFRC specimens

Y20 specimens with the Y25 specimens.
• The size in reduction of the elastic modulus of concrete cannot be generalized from this experi-

proven.
• It couldn’t be concluded whether the presence of steel ﬁbres had any positive inﬂuence on limiting
the reduction in the elastic modulus compared to plain concrete.
• The reduction in the elastic modulus of the concrete will reduce the overall stiﬀness of the slabs.
