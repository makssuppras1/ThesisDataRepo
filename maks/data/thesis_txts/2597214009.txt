Abstract
The project addresses the development of autonomous exploration algorithms for con-
fined and constrained spaces. The solution is focusing on the inspection of ballast tank
ships and its compartments. The focus of the project is on three aspects: hole detection,
visual servoing, and exploration. The safe exploration and the final demo require solving
the hole detection and servoing problem for passing through the holes. The holes are
narrow, and they are entrances to the tank compartments. The project uses the stereo
camera for exploration and detection problems to have accurate collision detection, hole
detection, and pose evaluation estimates. The result of the thesis is flying inside the
ballast tank, exploring the current compartment, flying to the next compartment, and
continuing the exploration until the drone explores all compartments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
There are industries and areas where inspection is necessary. At the current state of
technology humans mostly do inspections manually or with some help of the machines.
A lot of these tasks are repetitive, tedious, error prone and even dangerous when a
human being is inspecting them. The inspections can be done autonomously with the
right equipment and the persistence to do them infinitely.
The idea is to solve the
aforementioned problems with the autonomous systems.
Work of this thesis is towards the drone inspection in complex and confined spaces.
Dark, closed and constrained environments are some of the most difficult cases for au-
tonomous drone navigation. The lack of GNNS solutions in addition to tight spaces
make it difficult for drones to safely and accurately navigate.
The thesis is part of the Inspectrone project. The project is under funding and
development from Innovation Fund Denmark, Force Technology and DTU. Focus
of the project is a drone inspection of ballast tank ships. The drone will use sensors
such as vision and touch-based sensors. At the moment ship inspection with drones is
mostly done by human manually flying inside while doing the visual inspection. This
can lead to many not classified damages as well as missed areas. Therefore optimizing
the drone to collect the data and inspect as much area as possible is a way to go. The
goal is to check the structural integrity of the ship, find corrosion, classify and localize
it while safely navigating through the areas.
Therefore there is a need to have accurate, fast and reliable autonomous inspection.
The inspection with the drone has to tackle numerous challenges such as safe navigation,
maximizing exploration, localization, feature detection (beams, holes, walls), defect de-
tection and it’s localization (structural damages, corrosion), optimal trajectory planning,
SLAM, etc. Solving these tasks is a challenging and rewarding job. The solutions to
these problems are relevant for other inspection sites such as mines, caves, building,
areas after natural or human caused disasters, etc.
1.1
Objectives
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate, propose and develop algorithms which can be
adapted for the autonomous inspection in the ballast tank ships. The objectives can be
split in three main tasks: hole detection, visual servoing and exploration. The structure
of the ballast tank compartments is that each of them is separated by the hole. The

drone explores inside of the compartment’s empty space. Flying from one compartment
to the other compartment the drone needs to detect the hole (hole detection), and fly
safely through the hole (visual servoing).
Hole detection
The drone needs to safely navigate through tight areas. In this thesis the tightest
part of the ballast tank is a hole. Usually the size of the drone is very close to the size
of the hole. As as result hole detection and classification algorithm is implemented.
Visual Servoing
When the hole is detected and the drone finishes exploring the current area, it needs
to enter the next area. Passing through the hole is critical task since of all the tasks,
passing through the hole is the most dangerous task for the drone. Development of
closed loop solution was necessary. The visual servoing is split in two tasks. Centering
at the hole and passing through the hole with estimated visual odometry.
Exploration
Once the tank is reached, exploration starts. Exploration is mainly used for two
things. Exploring the entire area, because everything needs to be captured for further
analysis to extract and classify damages of the ship. Exploration is also used for local-
ization. Since the algorithms is constantly searching for unexplored areas, it can find
new feature such as beams, walls and a hole to localize it’s self again.

CHAPTER 3
Hole Detection
3.1
Introduction
Hole detection is a stepping stone to the tasks such as safe exploration further and the
localization in complex confined spaces. In the scope of this thesis the detected hole is
used for safe navigation between tank compartments which is explained further in the
chapter 4, visual servoing.
Hole detection is critical in the safe exploration task. The drone explores an open
area of the tank that has a vast space to fly. Narrow holes separate compartments inside
of the tank. Therefore flying safely from one tank to the other requires accurate hole
detection during the exploration task, the drone must not pass the hole accidentally.
Hence whenever the drone detects the hole, the constraint where the drone shouldn’t fly
is set.
Detecting a hole gives an accurate pose estimation of a drone based on the current
hole location. Hence drone can estimate it is the position in the current tank frame and
the world frame. Furthermore this helps estimating where are the defects and damages of
the ship. Having the accurate pose estimate help people to plan and organize necessary
fixes.
In the test environment there are two different types of hole as seen in figure 3.1.
There is a smaller type hole Manhole, which in this test cases represents the separation
of the outer area and inner (tank) area and the big hole Passage that separates inner
tank compartments. The provided solution solves hole detection and their pose estimate
for both hole types.
3.2
Related Work
There are lots of different approaches to hole detection and its classification. The ap-
proaches are split into multiple categories. Working on-camera image data or working
on point cloud data. Furthermore, both approaches can be divided into classical ap-
proaches and machine learning approaches. Approach from [Per+18] uses images and
deep learning to train CNN to find potholes in the road. Their proposed solution is a
low cost. For the application in this thesis, the solution would require much less training
since there are only fixed types of holes. Another promising approach is [KV19]. The
approach is using depth images and deep learning to detect objects which are specified

3.4
Point Cloud Processing
3.4.1
Overview
The received point cloud data from the stereo camera is dense and structured. Point
clouds contain a large amount of data. Therefore it is near impossible to do analysis and
hole detection on the raw point cloud data in a suitable time. Even higher-end hardware
has a hard time processing analyzing raw point cloud. Hence, analyzing raw data on the
drone with limited resources is hardly possible. As mentioned in the environment setup
chapter, the camera in the tests are in simulation VI-sensor and real-world MyntEye-
D camera.
VI-sensor has a resolution of 640x480, which generates a point cloud of
maximum size 921,600. MyntEye-D has a resolution of 1280x720 with a maximum point
cloud size 2,764,800. Therefore point cloud filtering and downsampling is necessary to
have a moderately soft-real time solution. Two types of filters were used to lower the
amount of point cloud data. The filters that are used are passthrough filter and vox-grid
filter. The result of the filters is a sparse unstructed point cloud data. As a result, hole
detection can work both with unstructured and structured points clouds. The benefits
of having a structured point cloud would be, for example, using kernel functions like
filter, edge detections, etc.
3.4.2
PassThrough Filter
PassThrough filter passes points in a cloud based on constraints for the given particular
field. It iterates through the entire input once, automatically filtering non-finite points
and the points outside the interval specified by filter limits. PassThrough filter limits
the range of data of the given axis. The data far away from the camera is unnecessary

in the given moment. It also reduces the number of points that are analyzed. Another
reason for limiting the data range is because, in the sensors, stereo cameras often contain
noise in the disparity map, and it creates an inaccurate point cloud in certain positions.
Those positions are usually far away points and points at the edges of the image.
3.4.3
VoxelGrid Filter
Downsampling with VoxelGrid filter proved to be extremely useful because it can create
a very light and sparse point cloud without destroying edges and the structure, which
is essential for the hole detection.
In the voxel grid, all the points present will be
approximated (i.e., downsampled) with their centroid. Therefore underlying surfaces
are represented accurately.
3.4.4
Results
Depending on the filter parameters, number of points in the point cloud are drastically
reduced. The lower number of points, faster the performance. Reducing point cloud is
fine as long as it doesn’t influence hole detection algorithm, like not detecting holes or
doing false detection. In the figure 3.2 point cloud depth is set to 4.5m for PassThrough
filter and leaf size is set to 0.03m for the VoxelGrid filter. Reduced size in the given
example is approximately 9 times.
The algorithm that does hole detection doesn’t suffer in terms of performance with
having non filtered point cloud. On the contrary it prefers having all points, because
that way most of morphological operations are not necessary, and therefore integrity of
the image is not touched.

3.5
Multi-plane segmentation
3.5.1
Overview
The holes inside of ballast tanks are always on the planar surfaces. The hole can be
located on any given plane as long as the plane contains enough points and is big
enough. Therefore finding a plane and its equation gives information about the position
and the orientation of the hole.
Multi-plane segmentation is a heavy process, therefore previously explained point cloud
filtering is done to speed it up.
3.5.2
Algorithm
Since the surface that needs to be estimated is a simple plane, the choice of algorithm
is RANSAC [FP03] due to the robustness and simplicity. With the RANSAC from the
PCL on the point cloud segments only the biggest plane. There can be multiple holes in
the environment at any given moment, and it is always one hole per plane. Therefore the
segmentation of other planes is necessary. The algorithm 2 describes the process. Every
segmented plane and its normal is saved and then subtracted from the point cloud. The
process continues until there are not enough points.

CHAPTER 4
Visual servoing
4.1
Introduction
Visual servoing is also known as vision-based robot control. By definition, it is a tech-
nique that uses feedback information extracted from a vision sensor to control the motion
of the robot.
In the scope of this thesis, a visual servoing technique is necessary for the drone to
pass through the holes safely. The testing environment consists of a narrow holes. In
the test, the drone has a maximum of approximately 3 cm of free space to the plate
that contains the hole, if the drone is in the center of the hole. Hence visual servoing is
necessary.
This chapter is building towards the solution. Although generally visual servoing
also feature velocity control, in this control, the final result of the visual servoing is the
position control.
The visual servoing task is split into two subtasks. The first task is for the drone to
center at the hole at some distance. Therefore, before the first task can be completed,
the drone detects the hole, as the previous chapter explains. The second task is to fly
through the hole. For the drone to fly through the hole, it needs to estimate visual
odometry and correct its position accurately.
4.2
Related work
There are more and more research and development of visual navigation, visual odometry,
etc. Especially in the GPS-denied areas. Most of the influential research for this topic
is developed while researching VSLAM. There are numerous researches both for the
monocular and stereo camera approaches to visual odometry. The solutions substantially
focus on feature tracking. One such work is ORBSLAM [MMT15]. ORBSLAM uses a
monocular camera for tracking, mapping, relocalization, and loop closing.
Another
approach is to use a stereo visual odometry like in the paper [How08]. The benefit of
this approach is to have a depth map, which draws similarities to the solution proposed
in this thesis.
More often, a solution for aerial vehicles is to use visual-inertial odometry. The paper
[Dav19] fuses the IMU measurements and tracked features from the camera to estimates

4.3
Solution overview
The resulting control for the visual servoing only consists of position control. Therefore
after estimating the next pose of the drone, only the position is sent to the drone.
As explained in the introduction, there are two tasks in the visual servoing. Both of
the tasks estimate the next drone pose based on the visual features. Centering to the
hole uses a single point and the feature. The detected hole is the feature from which the
next pose is calculated. Passing through the hole is more complex tasks since it does
not use a stable feature, but it needs to detect features in every image and compare
them. Hence the task is called visual odometry. The visual odometry is estimated from
the monochrome image and the corresponding disparity image. The description of the
algorithm is the following. After taking two images where one of them is a reference, the
ORB algorithm [Rub+11] detects features, followed by matching and further filtering
with RANSAC algorithm. Subtracting the current pose from the reference pose gives
resulting the odometry.

4.5
Visual odometry
4.5.1
Overview
The definition of visual odometry is a process of determining the position and orientation
of a robot by analyzing the associated camera images. There are four overall images.
The two images are monochrome images used for feature detection, and two are their
disparity image pairs.
4.5.2
Algorithm
The algorithm behind the visual odometry is the following. Computations start when
two images are received. The first image is a reference image, followed by the second
image changes each time the algorithm finishes the estimation. The algorithm selects
the region of interest in the image. Upon taking the second image, the ORB algorithm
detects features in the images represented by keypoints and its descriptors. Points are
matched, filtered with RANSAC, and projected to 3d. For the following estimation,
multiple points around keypoint are used. The algorithm forwards the selected points
to 3d RANSAC algorithm, which uses SVD to estimate the translation and the rotation.
Since there can still be an inaccurate estimation, the algorithm takes the mean of multiple
solutions. The mean is the resulting odometry.

the performance of the running algorithm. Selecting just a part of an image influences
algorithm performance since a lower amount of pixels are investigated. In this task,
there are 2 different ways of how the region of interest is selected. The simulation test
requires reducing the image size due to the position of the camera. The camera captures
propellers which heavily influence feature detection algorithms.
The position of the
camera in the real world experiment does not capture the propellers. The problem is

Feature detection
4.5.4.1
Overview
Feature detection and matching is an essential task in many computer vision applications,
such as visual odometry, image retrieval, object detection, and more. Therefore the
implementation of an algorithm is used in this application. There are multiple feature
detection algorithms like ORB, SIFT, and SURF. Overall the algorithms give similar
results in terms of accuracy in their performance. Where they profoundly differentiate
is in terms of computational speed. ORB is faster by orders of magnitude compared to
SIFT and SURF. ORB is used as an algorithm for feature detection due to its speed
and reliability.
==PAGE:41==

4.5.4.2
ORB
The ORB (Oriented FAST and Rotated BRIEF) algorithm described in the paper
[Rub+11]. It is an influential paper because it reduces the computational power nec-
essary to compute the feature descriptors. Therefore it is good to use it as a feature
detector in the drone applications. The ORB is a fusion of two algorithms: keypoint
corner detector FAST and descriptor BRIEF.
The ORB (Oriented FAST and Rotated BRIEF) algorithm consists of the following:
• The addition of a fast and accurate orientation component to FAST
• The efficient computation of oriented BRIEF features
• Analysis of variance and correlation of oriented BRIEF features
• A learning method for decorrelating BRIEF features under rotational invariance,
leading to better performance in nearest-neighbor applications
FAST
FAST (Features from accelerated segment test) is a corner detection algorithm.
Given a pixel p in an array fast compares the brightness of p to surrounding 16 pix-
els that are in a small circle around p. Pixels in the circle is then sorted into three
classes (lighter than p, darker than p or similar to p). If more than 8 pixels are darker
or brighter than p than it is selected as a keypoint.
BRIEF
Brief(Binary robust independent elementary feature) takes all keypoints found by
the fast algorithm and convert it into a binary feature vector so that together they can
represent an object. Binary features vector also know as binary feature descriptor is
a feature vector that only contains 1 and 0. In brief, each keypoint is described by a
feature vector which is 128–512 bits string.

compute inaccurate pose estimates. Those points are wrongfully matched and would
increase the error of transformations.
The estimated transformation is between the
reference image and the current image. For the algorithm in place, there are three steps.
Using RANSAC to select N number of points which calculates the rigid transform. SVD
calculates the rigid transform. The estimated transform counts number of inliers based
on the distance metric. Finding enough inliers classifies the current rigid transformation
as good.

CHAPTER 5
Exploration
5.1
Introduction
Inspection of complex confined spaces is a hard problem that is under constant under-
going research. One step towards the inspection of the confined spaces is to have a safe
exploration. The drone needs to navigate safely. The ballast tank ships have a GPS-
denied environment, with the presence of magnetic and communication disturbances.
The drone needs to fly and explore ballast tanks. The space is mostly empty inside of
the tank. The problem is that there can be unexpected leftovers from the workers. The
tank might also be damaged and have plates and pieces in unexpected areas. The drone
has to fly between the compartments through holes in the narrow space. Even though
the environment is mostly known, the drone has to be careful. The drone is using a
single stereo camera for its navigation and exploration. The drone needs to explore the
environment in order to find and detect features such as beams, walls, holes, detect
defects (damages, corrosion), and localize itself if it is lost. Therefore maximizing the
information gain of the tank is the goal.
5.2
Related Work
There is a lot of undergoing research for the inspection of the GPS denied environments
like mines or ships, but also research regarding optimized path planning and aggressive
but save trajectory. Optimized trajectory planning enables drones areas more efficiently.
The area in this work is represented with the efficient 3D mapping framework Oc-
tomap [Hor+13] which is a probabilistic occupancy grid map. Another valuable approach
and more efficient in some cases is the Voxblox mapping framework [Ole+17]. This ap-
proach uses ESDF functions. The method generates in a map a slightly slower time
that the Octomap, but the lookup for a distance to the object is almost instant where
Octomap has to do raycasting to get a distance to the objects.
Different approaches find the good path planning with optimized trajectory planning
while also avoiding approaches. Therefore this thesis is influenced by those works. Ef-
ficiently exploring the areas first boils down to finding the best information gain in the
area. The approaches like [Bir+16] and [Dai+20]. Both papers build RRT in the space
to generate the next poses, while differently computing the information gain. The ideas
==PAGE:50==
40
5 Exploration
from the papers work well in an open environment, while for the confined spaces, they
need additional changes.
Another article is Structural Inspection Path Planning [A B15], where the goal is
to cover the whole surface area. The solution built an optimal path with RRT on the
surface, but as an input requires a mesh model.
5.3
Solution Overview
The solution is focusing on the exploration and path generation while not taking care
of optimized trajectory planning. The solution for the exploration is based on the work
from Andreas Bircher called Next-Best-View planner [Bir+16]. The main topic of the
article is to maximize the information gain in unknown areas and generate poses based
on that.
The algorithm needs a 3D mapping framework as a reliable and easy to use framework.
The choice is Octomap [Hor+13]. Octomap enables us to find the next poses and check
for safety and collision detection. The future poses are generated based on the next
best view. The next best view is the next pose, which contains the highest number of
unknown voxels inside the Octomap. The number of poses is generated at random, to
avoid analysis of the whole Octomap every time.
The drone flies to the generated pose, and the exploration continues. The exploration
stops when the drone cannot find unknown voxels or when the drone exceeds the time
limit for the single compartment. During the exploration of the compartment, the drone
detects the hole. After meeting the exploration stopping condition, the drone flies to
the hole and passes to the next compartment. If there are no more compartments to
explore, the drone returns home.
5.4
Octomap
The Octomap is an efficient probabilistic 3D mapping framework based on octrees. Other
notable pros of the Octomap are a representation of free, unknown, and occupied space.
It is stored efficiently on the disk and has efficient access and queries.
Having fast
access enables even a drone to analyze the space in a short period. Depending on the
application, the octomap can increase or decrease the resolution. Change the size of the
voxels.
The Octomap is extensively used throughout this exploration task due to its efficiency
and representation of free, unknown, and occupied voxel types. The tasks which use this
representation of voxels are collision checking and evaluation of unknown areas. Both
have a similar approach to evaluation with ray casting.

5.6
Pose validation
5.6.1
Overview
There is a possibility that generated poses are not accessible. The poses that are not
accessible are the ones that cannot guarantee drones safety. Only poses that contain
free voxels are valid. Occupied voxels would most likely cause the collision between the
drone and the environment, which must be avoided at all costs. Occupied voxels are
most of the time occupied, but they can also be the noise. The false occupied voxels
can appear because of the algorithm which generates Octomap or, more often, the noisy
point cloud data which camera generates.
There is always a slightly larger boundary box around the drone in which collision
checking occurs, due to safety reasons. For example, if the next drone position is close
to the wall, and the drone overshoots the target, the drone could potentially crash into
the wall.
The other type of voxels that are marked as not valid are unknown voxels. Unknown
voxels can be either free or occupied. Not knowing that information, the drone cannot
reliably fly into that space without seeing it first.
5.6.2
Algorithm
Pose validation algorithm uses octomap. The solution is to do the raycasting process.
The raycasting is to check all the points from sensor origin to the end pose and count for
the voxels. The algorithm generates an Nbox number of rays on which checking occurs.
The number of rays depends on the bounding box (bbx, bby, bbz) and the selected
resolution as the equation 5.1 explains.

voxels. In reality, there can always be a stray voxel. Therefore such voxels are filtered.
If the ray finds a number of an unknown or occupied voxel, then that pose is invalid
and is not taken in the further evaluation. The valid poses are further taken into gain

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The thesis address one of the active areas of scientific research within robotics during the
last couple of years, the inspection of confined spaces in GPS denied areas. Today, there
is a high demand for incorporating robots in the industry, and among those industries
is the inspection.
Deployed UAVs for inspection reduce the amount of work people
need to do manually, because inspection jobs are often dull and repetitive, sometimes
dangerous, and prone to human error. The idea is to have a more efficient, periodical
approach with less errors. Having autonomous inspection enables humans to focus on
other things while having quality inspection every time.
Therefore this thesis explores and develops solutions for one such inspection, an
inspection of ballast tank ships. Specifically, the project is about finding a good solution
to exploration and safe navigation through confined spaces. The project touches multiple
areas like hole detection, visual servoing, and exploration, and builds a solution towards
them. Hole detection algorithm detects the holes and their poses in the target frame.
Visual servoing takes care of passing through the hole while estimating visual odometry.
The exploration explores each of the tank compartments in a way that maximizes the
information gain.
Overall the developed system on the drone is somewhat able to navigate through the
tank and explore it.
