Preface
This master thesis project was carried out at the Department of Environmental
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU Environment), in the
period from the 23rd of January to the 23rd of June 2017. The project accounts for
30 ECTS points. The project was supervised by Professor Karsten Arnbjerg-Nielsen
(DTU Environment), Assistant Professor Hjalte Jomo Danielsen Sørup (DTU En-
vironment), Dr. Thomas Einfalt (hydro & meteo GmbH & Co. KG) and Marc
Scheibel (Wupperverband).
The project is using high resolution radar rainfall data to statistically describe
spatio-temporal varying rainfall with physically based variables. The project con-
cerns the preliminary steps to set up a weather generator which can simulate spatio-
temporal rainfall at a scale small enough to be relevant for urban hydrology. The
project is based on the radar rainfall data from the Wupper Association (Wupper-
verband), which is post processed by hydro & meteo GmbH & Co. KG.

Abstract
Future changes in precipitation patterns increase the frequency and magnitude of
extreme events. Rainfall is one of the most important input parameters in urban
hydrology models, and rainfall data is required in high spatio-temporal resolution in
order to describe processes leading to pluvial ﬂooding. This project examines spatio-
temporal varying rainfall characteristics of convective and front extreme events,
based on physical variables. The project is based on high resolution (5 minute tem-
poral resolution, 1x1 km spatial resolution) radar data from Wupper Association
district, North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany.
The radar data is initially analysed based on orographic eﬀect, seasonal distribution
and spatial correlation of extreme events. Extreme events were sampled by a Peak
Over Threshold method with type II censoring using two sampling strategies: 1-hour
maximum average intensity, targeting convective events, and 24-hour maximum av-
erage intensity, targeting front events. The two groups of extreme events (1-hour
and 24-hour) were characterised by 17 variables: 1 duration, 3 intensity, 2 area, 3
depth, 2 ratio, 2 rain cell identiﬁcation and 4 rain cell tracking variables. Rain cell
identiﬁcation and tracking algorithms were developed in the project. The statis-
tical methods Principal Component Analysis (PCA), cluster analysis and stepwise
regression were used to distinguish between types of the extreme events, understand
the dynamics of the variables and determine the dimensions of the variable space.
An attempt to select a number of independent variables for a weather generator was
conducted based on the results from the statistical methods.
The data was found applicable for description of extreme events and rainfall pro-
cesses at grid cell resolution. Seasonal variation of extreme events can be described
with two seasons: summer and other months. 16 variables contributed with infor-
mation to the event characteristic. The PCA indicated between 5-9 dimensions in
the extreme event characteristic data, though it was not possible it detmine any in-
signiﬁcant Principle Components (PC). The cluster analyses identiﬁed four rainfall
types based on the 16 variables: extreme convective, convective, front with con-
vective activity and front events. The stepwise regression identiﬁed independent
variables suggested for a weather generator which corresponded well with the vari-
ables important for deﬁning clusters in the data. 6 variables was suggested for a
weather generator (duration, maximum average 10 minute intensity, mean wet area,
ratio between maximum and mean depth, mean velocity and standard deviation of
direction). The project conducts the preliminary steps prior setting up a weather
generator. A weather generator based on the ﬁndings of this project could be a
solution to create artiﬁcial spatio-temporal varying rainfall series for application in
urban hydrology.

1 Introduction
1
Introduction
Climate changes resulting in a more extreme rainfall pattern together with urban
densiﬁcation put pressure on the urban drainage system (Semadeni-Davies et al.,
2008; Zhou et al., 2012; Larsen et al., 2009). Future changes in precipitation pat-
terns is likely to increase the frequency and magnitude of extreme events (Fowler
and Hennessy, 1995; Larsen et al., 2009). The demand for urban hydrological mod-
els increases in order to make cities more resilient to pluvial ﬂooding. A key input
parameter when modelling urban drainage systems is rainfall (Berndtsson and Niem-
czynowicz, 1988; Schilling, 1991; Thorndahl et al., 2008). A common way is to use
a model including a rainfall-runoﬀcompartment, with rainfall input as a long-term
rainfall series or a design storm (Butler and Davies, 2011; Willems et al., 2012).
The Danish practice proposed by the Water Pollution Committee of the Society
of Danish Engineers is to use a Chicago Design Storm, which simulates the same
intensity over the entire model area (Harremo¨es et al., 2005).
In urban hydrology response time is quick and extreme events at local scale can
cause pluvial ﬂooding (Schilling, 1991; Thorndahl et al., 2016). In order to simu-
late runoﬀprocesses in urban hydrology, rainfall data must be of high spatial and
temporal resolution (Berndtsson and Niemczynowicz, 1988; Schilling, 1991; Einfalt
et al., 2004; Ochoa-Rodriguez et al., 2015). Schilling (1991) and Einfalt et al. (2004)
have proposed resolutions requirements of 1-5 minute temporal resolution and 1x1
kilometre spatial resolution.
Regional Climate Models (RCM) is used to assess changes in future precipitation
(Christensen and Christensen, 2007; Kendon et al., 2012). The RCM output, which
is commonly available at a daily scale and spatial resolution of approximately 25 km,
have shown to be problematic in describing precipitation in a scale useful for urban
hydrology (Hanel and Buishand, 2010; Gregersen et al., 2013). In order to under-
stand climate change impact of precipitation patterns, spatio-temporal description
of rainfall have become important (Thorndahl et al., 2016).
Rainfall simulations, can beside RCM outputs, be built upon two types of data:
rain gauge and radar (RAdio Detecting And Ranging) data. Both types of data
have signiﬁcant strengths and weaknesses. which among others are addressed by
Einfalt et al. (2004). Strengths about rain gauge data is that it requires less data
treatment compared to radar data, and measurements are typical available for a
long time period. Rain gauges measure rainfall at ground level, which is the rainfall
of interest in hydrological modelling (Thorndahl et al., 2016), and often have a tem-
poral resolution of around 1 minute (Einfalt et al., 2004). A major weakness about
rain gauge data is the lack of information on rainfall movement, spatial variation and
coverage. Radar data, on the other hand, gives information about rainfall movement
and spatial coverage (Thorndahl et al., 2016), and have signiﬁcantly improved our
understanding of how precipitation is formed (Collier, 1989). Weaknesses of radar
data is that it measures reﬂectivity which when converted to rainfall intensities
can cause large uncertainties, especially for high rainfall intensities. Furthermore is
1
==PAGE:10==
radar data based on an instantaneous scan of volume high above ground which then
represents the reﬂectivity in the entire sampling time. This can lead to aggregation
errors and might not reﬂect the rainfall at ground level (Einfalt et al., 2004).
Models to simulate rainfall are also called weather generators. In the ﬁeld of rainfall
simulation, focus have until now been on models based on rain gauge data. Weather
generators can be based upon a dense network of rain gauges in order to include
some spatial variation in the model, examples are among others Jinno et al. (1993);
Willems (2001); Sørup et al. (2016), but do not describe the dynamics of spatial
rainfall. Downscaling of RCM outputs have been done to bridge between RCM out-
put scale and necessary scale for urban hydrology, with two main methods (Maraun
et al., 2010): Dynamic downscaling (e.g. Kendon et al., 2014) or statistical down-
scaling (e.g. Sørup et al., 2016). Further development of statistical downscaling is
required to describe the dynamics of small scale events which causes pluvial ﬂood-
ing. Dynamic downscaling is possible at a small scale useful in urban hydrology, but
is computationally extremely expensive (Maraun et al., 2010).
Radar data has been suggested as potential rainfall input in urban hydrology since
the mid 80’s (Einfalt et al., 2004).
Radar products have more recently become
available in spatial and temporal resolution fulﬁlling the resolution requirement in
urban hydrology, and within the last 1-2 decades become more frequently used in
urban hydrology along with increasing length of recording period (Thorndahl et al.,
2016). Weather generators based on high resolution radar data are very limited, and
often relies on statistically based variables, one example is Peleg and Morin (2014).
The downside of this is the absence of physically based variables to understand the
spatio-temporal variation in rainfall as well as limited possibilities of usage together
with RCM’s.
The project aims to quantify and describe spatial rainfall as a function of temporal
and spatial dynamics, rainfall types and seasonal variation. Descriptive statistical
method is applied to analyse selected physically based variables and their intern cor-
relation. The project aims to statistically describe spatio-temporal varying rainfall
using physically based variables, in order to assess the possibility of creating artiﬁcial
spatio-temporal rainfall series, of high resolution scale useful for urban hydrology,
based on the ﬁndings in this project. The project additionally analyse seasonal vari-
ation and spatial correlation of extreme events in the case area in order to verify the
applicability of the data.
The project is based on radar data from the Wupper Association in the North
Rhine-Westphalia. The data originates from the DWD radars: Essen, Flechtdorf
and Neuheilenbach. Data are post processed by hydro & meteo GmbH & Co. KG
and for this project recived in the hdf5 format in 5 minute resolution in a 1x1 km grid.
The case area and data is introduced in Section 2, background theory about rainfall
types, weather radars and terminology of spatial rainfall is presented in Section 3.

2
Case
This project based on high resolution radar data from the Wupper Association
(Wupperverband) in Germany. The data is used as a case to the methodology and
analyses developed and performed in this project. In the Wupper Association the
data is used for online and oﬄine purposes. Oﬄine purpose includes hydrological
design and critical values for ﬂood management, and online purposes are ﬂood event
management such as a ﬂood warning system (Marc Scheibel, personal communica-
tion, June 20, 2017).
2.1
Case Area
The catchment of the Wupper river, a tributary to the Rhine, represent the district
of the Wupper Association (Wupperverband, 2017). Wuppertal is the largest city in
the Wupper Association and is situated in western Germany, east from D¨usseldorf
and south from Essen, in the North Rhine-Westphalia (Stadt Wuppertal, 2017).
For simplicity the case area is deﬁned as a 38x48km square (1824 km2) around the
Wupper Association district, with top left corner in UTM coordinate U32 0355500
5686500.
2.1.1
Elevation
The district of the Wupper Association stretches from the Rhine lowland south-
west to the more hilly area in east, with steep valleys around the river of Wupper
(Lebendige W¨asser, 2017). The elevation diﬀerence can be seen in ﬁgure 1, which
show a digital elevation model of the area. The elevation varies with more than 400
meter, between the lowest elevation at 31 meters and highest elevation at 483 meter

2.2
Radar Data
The radar data from the Wupper Association spans over 13 years, from the 1st
of November 2000 to the 1st of November 2013.
The data amount is speciﬁed
as 1,372,608 radar images, 19,677 time series ﬁles and 4,749 daily precipitation
images. A total 384 GB of data. The data is a product from the DWD (Deutscher
Wetterdienst) polarimetric Doppler C-Band radar system. The case area is within
the range of the Essen radar and partly within the range of the Flechtdorf and
Neuheilenbach radars (see ﬁgure 3).
The data is a weighted composition of the
three radars: Essen, Flechtdorf and Neuheilenbach. In the following section a short
overview of the radar data and the post processing is given. Theory about weather
radars can be found in section 3.2.
2.2.1
Post Processing
The data is post processed by hydro & meteo GmbH & Co. KG on behalf of the
Wupper Association. Data is corrected in regards of blockage, clutter and attenu-
ation. The Z-R relationship is deﬁned as Z=256·R1.42. In the Wupper Association
district the radar data is adjusted to rain gauge data on daily basis, with a cor-
rection factor per gauge in a 1 km correction grid using inverse distance weighting.

2.2 Radar Data
Rain gauge data is beforehand visually inspected and compared to nearby gauges
in order to secure high quality rain gauge data. There are 60 rain gauges within the
Wupper Association. The post processed data have less than 5 % diﬀerence from
annual ground truth.
2.2.2
Data Format
Radar precipitation data for the case area are given in two data formats: Radar
images and precipitation time series. The radar images are post processed, and in

3
Theory
This section introduce the theoretical background knowledge about rainfall types
(3.1), weather radar (3.2) and spatial rainfall terminology (3.3).
3.1
Rainfall Types
In meteorology a general way to distinguish between types of rainfall events can be
made based on the atmospheric phenomena creating those: convection and fronts.
Both types of rainfall events can result in extreme events (based on the deﬁnition
in section 4.2.1), but the amount of precipitation in a given event depends on the
conditions in the atmosphere when the storm develops (Doswell et al., 2005). Ad-
ditional can orography be described as a rainfall generating process aﬀecting the
mean annual precipitation (Doswell et al., 2005).
3.1.1
Convective Rain Events
Convective rain events are created when parcels of air rise due to diﬀerences in
density (Plate, 1982). The diﬀerences in density occur when solar radiation causes
heaths the surface which locally warms the air in contact with the surface. The
warm less dense air will rise therefore rise.
If the parcels contain enough moist
the parcels can create consistently unstable conditions and large amounts of pre-
cipitation (Dingman, 2002). Convective rain events are often small scale weather
systems, with spatial areas of a few square kilometers and can cause very violent
weather as known from thunderstorms (Doswell et al., 2005). Convective thunder-
storms develop during warm days and causes most of the summer precipitation in
the European region. Convective rain events usually last less than an hour (Ding-
man, 2002) but in some cases thunderstorms are linked together creating a line of
thunderstorms interacting with each other. In this case the convective rain events
can grow in size and last for several hours (Doswell et al., 2005).
3.1.2
Front Rain Events
Front rain events origin from the main large scale weather systems called extratrop-
ical cyclones (Doswell et al., 2005). A front is made in a zone of transition between
warm moist air masses and colder air masses (Petterson, 1969). A warm front is cre-
ated if warm air displaces cold air near the surface and warm air rises, the opposite
phenomena is called a cold front (Dingman, 2002). Front rain events have a large
spatial area with a horizontal propagation often ranging from 100-1000 kilometers
(Doswell et al., 2005) and a lifespan of several days (Petterson, 1969). Front events
causes most of the winter precipitation in the European region (Doswell et al., 2005).
The Atlantic polar front has a main eﬀect on the European weather in the winter.
It moves from west and eastwards into Europe, stretching from Iceland in the north
to Portugal in south (Petterson, 1969).
3.1.3
Orographic precipitation
The orographic eﬀect describe how the precipitation pattern in a area is aﬀected
by the mountains (Dingman, 2002). Mountains causes modiﬁcations of the wind
pattern, which lift wind over the mountains where updrapft can be signiﬁcantly
7
==PAGE:16==
3.2 Weather Radar
increased (Doswell et al., 2005). When air masses are forced over mountains the air
is cooled down and producing a cloud which is either stable or unstable. While both
stable and unstable conditions will create precipitation, only unstable conditions
can trigger convection (Doswell et al., 2005). The dry air is warmed up wile moving
downhill on the other side, which result in appreciable less precipitation on this side
(Ahrens, 2003). The orographic eﬀect result in more annual precipitation in the
mountains than in the lowland (Dingman, 2002).
3.2
Weather Radar
Weather radars permits observations about the movement of precipitation and vari-
ation of rainfall intensities in space and time. This makes radar data important for
understanding spatial variation of extreme events (Thorndahl et al., 2016). This re-
port aims to use radar data to examine spatio-temporal variation of extreme events
and the correlation between variables describing the event characteristics, despite
this a short introduction to weather radars and problems with measuring precipita-
tion is given here. In depth description as well as the main assumptions in measuring
precipitation with a weather radar can be found in textbooks and articles as: Meis-
chner (2003), Collier (1989), Einfalt et al. (2004) and Thorndahl et al. (2016).
A weather radar operates at a given wave length. Weather radars are divided into
three main bands: X-band, C-band and S-band, referring to the wavelengths of
approximately 3, 5 and 10 cm respectively (Meischner, 2003). For a C-band radar
both attenuation and ground clutter can give signiﬁcant sources of disruption of
the received reﬂectivity, while a X-band radar is mostly aﬀected by attenuation and
S-band radars are aﬀected by ground clutter (Collier, 1989). The spatial and tem-
poral resolution of the radar data is often an indirect function of the radar range
(Thorndahl et al., 2016). C-band radars, which the data from this report is a prod-
uct of, have a maximum quantitative range of 100-130 km, which gives a typical
spatial resolution between 250-2000 m and a typical temporal resolution of 5-10 min
(Thorndahl et al., 2016). The beamwidth, called resolution in azimuth, also aﬀect
the spatial resolution (Einfalt et al., 2004). The spatial resolution is highest closest
to the radar and decreases as the radar beam becomes wider (Collier, 1989).
In short, a radar transmit a beam of electromagnetic radiation. The beam is scat-
tered and a minor part is reﬂected if obstacles are met (Maksimovic, 1996). The
reﬂectivity, Z, is collected by a radar antenna. A weather radar therefore measures a
three dimensional ﬁeld of reﬂectivity (volume) which can be used to estimate rainfall
intensities (Einfalt et al., 2004). Reﬂectivity is for weather radars often presented
in decibels of reﬂectivity (dBZ) (Meischner, 2003):
dBZ = 10 log10 Z
(3.1)
In a rain event, the rain drops will create an obstacle which will reﬂect some of the
beam. The reﬂectivity will be aﬀected by the variation of the drop size distribution
and density in the scanned volume (Einfalt et al., 2004). As the rainfall rate is
dependent on the drop size distribution too, the reﬂectivity gives an indication of

the rainfall rate, R. A common way to translate reﬂectivity to rainfall intensities is
a Z-R relationship which is an empirical relationship between the reﬂectivity and
the rainfall rate, R (Meischner, 2003).
Z = aRb
(3.2)
Where a and b are empirical constants. As the drop size distribution varies from
event to event, the Z-R relationship is aﬀected by the type of precipitation, for exam-
ple distinguishing between front and convective rain using diﬀerent Z-R relationships
(Collier, 1989). When measuring precipitation the choice of Z-R relationships can
have a large inﬂuence on the estimated rainfall rate, especially for high intensities
(Einfalt et al., 2004). As the distance to the radar increase, the scanned volume
increases as well, which aﬀect the reﬂectivity too. Radar data is therefore often
adjusted with other rainfall measurements, such as rain gauge data (Collier, 1989).
When adjusting radar data to rain gauge ground measurements, several problems
can arise due to the change in the scanned volume and wind drift of rain drops
(Thorndahl et al., 2016; Collier, 1989). Further away from the radar the radar beam
measures higher from the ground, which increases the time shift between the radar
measured precipitation and the rain gauge precipitation. This leads to an increases
in wind drift of rain drops why it can be diﬃcult to compare radar measurements
with ground measurements (Collier, 1989).
3.3
Spatial Rainfall Terminology
In order to describe and analyse spatial rainfall it can be necessary to divide and
identify smaller parts of the rainfall. The structure of spatial rainfall have been
described by Gupta and Waymire (1979), Waymire et al. (1984), Sivapalan and
Wood (1987), Willems (1999), among others. This section is a summary of before
mentioned articles which focus on the stochastic structure of rainfall. These will be
used deﬁne rainfall structures important for this project. Rain events often have
a hierarchical structure with increasing intensities for decreasing spatial scale, see
table 1. The largest sub-synoptic precipitation ﬁeld is called Large MesoScale Area
(LMSA). LMSA often appears as a band and have an area of approximately 103-
104 km2. LMSA’s move in relation to the movement of synoptic region and have a
lifetime of several hours. A synoptic region can consist of one or more LMSA’s but
as most areas of interest does not exceed 104 km2 and the structure of LMSA’s are
common to many types of storms, the diﬀerence between LMSA and rain storms
are sometimes neglected. LMSA’s can consist of multiple Small MesoScale Areas
(SMSA). SMSA’s have a spatial scale of 102-103 km2 and are also named ”cluster
potential regions”. SMSA’s consist of one or more rain cells. The cells within the
SMSA have similar velocities. SMSA’s have a lifetime of a few hours. The smallest
precipitation ﬁeld is a rain cell, which have a spatial extent of 10-50 km2. Rain cells
are deﬁned as having a high intensity relative to the surrounding intensity. Rain
cells can cluster, called ”cell cluster” and have a lifetime of several minutes to around
half an hour. Rain cells and cell clusters can be present inside SMSA’s or alone in
a LMSA.

The above mentioned structure of synoptic rainfall was developed based on cyclonic
storms, but can be used to describe a various ﬁeld of rain events with diﬀerent
synoptic properties and seasonal variation (Gupta and Waymire, 1979). It is clear
that local thunderstorms will not have the scale deﬁned for synoptic areas or LMSA,
but the structure ﬁts well with the rain cell and cell clusters described. As well as
frontal storms can be described with the structure of LMSA and SMSA. The size of
rain cells in relation to the size of urban catchments, makes the description of these
important in urban hydrology (Willems, 1999).
Rainfall will in this project be described in three diﬀerent scales: Rain event, rain
cell and grid cell. The latter of these is representing the smallest measurement scale
of the radar data (1x1 km) and therefore not a part of the above described me-
teorological based spatial rainfall structure. Rain events will represent any storm
type and have no lower threshold for spatial scale. Rain cells or cell clusters will
be deﬁned as areas with an intensity four times higher than the average intensity of
the rain event.

Time series data is used to get an overview of the data, testing the appropriateness

4.2.1
Identiﬁcation of Extreme Events
Precipitation time series data in 5 minute resolution derived from radar data is used
to sample extreme events for selected grid cells. Extreme events is deﬁned based on
extreme precipitation, deﬁned as the maximum average precipitation intensity for
a given duration (Madsen et al., 2002; Larsen et al., 2009). Two types of extreme
events are sampled: 1-hour and 24-hour, referring to the duration of the maximum
average intensity the events are sampled by.
Independent events are for 1-hour
extreme events deﬁned with a minimum dry period of one hour between events and
for 24-hour extremes, 24 hour dry period between events. The sampling method is
based on a Peak Over Threshold (POT, also know as Partial Duration Series) (Coles,
2001), with a threshold based on a speciﬁc number of events (Type II censoring)
(Mikkelsen et al., 1996). First the 15 largest events per year are sampled (total of
195 events), year 2000 and 2013 are considered as one year as the years together have
data for one year. This is done to secure a good distribution of extreme events over
the data period. From the 195 events an average of 3 extreme events per year of data
is sampled, which correspond to a threshold of 39 extreme events. The sampling
strategy is used for both 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events for the selected grid
cells. 39 1-hour and 39 24-hour extreme events are sampled.

4.2.3
Spatial Correlation
The spatial correlation of the data is analysed in two steps: First a simple com-
parison of events sampled in the ﬁve original grid cells is made, in order to get an
initial idea of how the events are correlated. Second the spatial correlation is cal-
culated using 100 randomly sampled grid cells, equal to 4950 pairs, calculating the
correlation between the 39 extreme events for each grid cell for 1-hour and 24-hour
extreme events (section 4.2.1).
The spatial correlation is calculated by estimating the correlation of extreme events
which are meteorological dependent from diﬀerent grid cells, applying the frame-
work of spatial inter-site correlation structures by Mikkelsen et al. (1996), also used
Gregersen et al. (2013). The unconditional correlation coeﬃcient ρ between a pair of
grid cells (A and B) is calculated by identifying concurrent events. If it is assumed
that the start (ts) and end (te) time of all events are known, concurrence between
the i’th event at grid cell A, ZAi and the j’th event at grid cell B, ZBj can be deﬁned
as:
{ZAi, ZBj} : [tsi −1/2∆t, tei + 1/2∆t]A ∩[tsi −1/2∆t, tei + 1/2∆t]B ̸= ∅
(4.1)
∆t is a lag time introduced to ensure that events can be concurrent events though
travelling time means that these events do not overlap in time.
∆t was in this
project set to 11 hours equal to the ∆t used in Gregersen et al. (2013). Based on
the sample of concurrent events and the sample of not concurrent events in a pair
of grid cells, the unconditional covariance can be estimated as:
Cov{ZA, ZB} = Cov{E{ZA|U}, E{ZB|U}} + E{Cov{ZA, ZB|U}}
(4.2)
Where U is a stochastic parameter which has the value of 1 for concurrent events
otherwise 0. The two terms in equation 4.2 are derived by Mikkelsen et al. (1996)
and deﬁned as:
Cov{E{ZA|U}, E{ZB|U}} = p(E{ZA|U = 1} −E{ZA})(E{ZB|U = 1} −E{ZB})
+(1 −p)(E{ZA|U = 0} −E{ZA})(E{ZB|U = 0} −E{ZB})
E{Cov{ZA, ZB|U}} = pE{Cov{ZA, ZB|U = 1}}
(4.3)
The unconditional correlation coeﬃcient ρ can now be estimated by dividing the un-
conditional covariance with the standard deviation for the two stations. Hereafter
similar to the procedure proposed by Gregersen et al. (2013) the data is divided into
bins based on distance between stations and the average ρ for each bin is calculated
in order to minimise noise in the data set. At last the data is ﬁtted to an exponen-
tial function, relating the distance between a pair of stations with the unconditional
correlation coeﬃcient ρ. A measurement of the correlation of extreme events based
on the distance between pairs of data points can be expressed as the e-folding dis-
tance. The e-folding distance, is the distance where the unconditional correlation
have decreased to 1/e, based on the ﬁtted exponential function (Gregersen et al.,
2013).

Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a statistical tool to estimate the correlation
structure of multidimensional data (Conradsen, 1984b). The PCA is additionally
used to analyse the number of dimensions in the data. The method uses linear
orthogonal transformation in order to describe the overall variance with linear com-
binations, called Principal Components (PC). The PC explaining most of the vari-
ance in the data is therefore the eigenvector related to the highest eigenvalue of the
covariance matrix. This PC is called PC1, PC2 explains the second most of the
variance and the rest follows subsequently. The number of PC’s is less or equal to
the number of variables explaining the data.

4.5
Cluster Analysis
A cluster analysis is the grouping of similar objects (Hartigan, 1975). The analysis is
used to group events and identify event types in order to understand which variables
are important for grouping and which is not.
4.5.1
K-means Clustering
The K-means clustering method is a partitioning clustering method. Partitioning
clustering means that each object belong to only one cluster. The clustering is done
in R using the algorithm by Hartigan and Wong (1979). The algorithm divides the
data into a speciﬁed number of clusters by minimising the sum of squared distances
within the clusters (Hartigan, 1975). The algorithm search for a local minimum de-
ﬁned so that no movement of data from one cluster to another will reduce the sum
of squared distances. The cluster analysis is done for the sampled extreme events
after performing the PCA. The cluster analysis is based on the projection of each
event on to the PC’s. Each event is therefore deﬁned in a multidimensional room,
with as many dimensions as PC’s considered.
The algorithm consists of four of steps. The following section is a short description
of the K-means clustering algorithm presented by Hartigan (1975) and Hartigan and
Wong (1979):
Step 1: Initially the events are clustered by selecting the cluster with the centre which
the event is closest to. The cluster centre has as many dimensions as number
of PC’s used in the cluster analysis and is deﬁned by the mean of each PC in
the cluster. B(l,i) is the mean of the jth PC for the events in the lth cluster.
The cluster centre is updated every time a new event is added to the cluster.
When all events are assigned to a cluster, begins the optimisation.
Step 2: For each event it is tested if the euclidean distance would be reduced, if the
event belonged to another cluster. l(i) describe the cluster where the event i
is contained, while l represent any cluster. nl represent the number of events
within cluster l. D[i,l(i)] denotes the euclidean distance between event i and
the centre of the cluster containing i. D[i,l] denotes the euclidean distance
between event i and the centre of any cluster. The increase in error by moving
an event from one cluster to another is calculated as:

4.5.2
Hierarchical Clustering
In hierarchical clustering an event is assigned to several clusters in a hierarchical
order. There are two types of hierarchical clustering: Divisive and Agglomerative
(Cormack, 1971). Divisive clustering start with all events in one cluster divides the
events in more and more clusters based on dissimilarities, until all events are in a
separate cluster. Agglomerative clustering starts with all events in separate clusters.
The process iteratively joins the two less dissimilar objects recursively moving on
until all objects at last are within the same cluster (Cormack, 1971). The last method
is used in this project. Calculation of dissimilarities varies between methods and
while many clustering methods are found in literature (Murtagh, 1983), this project
will use the Ward and the Average linkage method. The hierarchical clustering is
performed in R using the methods ”ward.2d” and ”average”. The two methods have
the same clustering algorithm containing four steps (Murtagh, 1983):
Step 1: Determine the dissimilarities between the events
Step 2: Form a cluster between the two closest events or clusters
Step 3: Calculate the new dissimilarities between new clusters and other events and
clusters. All other dissimilarities remain unchanged.
Step 4: Return to step 2 recursively until all objects are within the same cluster.
The calculation of dissimilarities is based on the Lance-Williams cluster update
method (Lance and Williams, 1966).
If it is assumed that data at this time is
divided into three clusters h, i, and j each with nh, ni and nj numbers of events, and
the dissimilarities between clusters are deﬁned as dhi, dhj and dij. The dissimilarities

new cluster k, with nk = ni+nj elements. The estimate of the dissimilarities between
the new cluster k and remaining cluster h is deﬁned as (Lance and Williams, 1966):

4.6
Stepwise Regression
Stepwise regression is in short, a way to determine how many independent variables
should be used in order to describe a data set (Conradsen, 1984a). The idea is to
ﬁt a linear model to the data with as few independent variables as possible without
compromising the ﬁt of the model signiﬁcantly. In this project the term stepwise
regression covers the procedure of a forward selection of variables which should be
considered in the model, and a backward test if any of the variables within the model
should be disregarded. The trade-oﬀbetween model simplicity and ﬁt is determined
using the Akaike’s Information Criterion (AIC) (Crawley, 2005).
AIC = −2 · log −likelihood + 2p
(4.13)
The lower AIC the better ﬁt. p is the number of parameters, and a penalty is there-
fore added each time an extra parameter is added to the model. If AIC decreases
when adding an extra parameter to describe the data, the improvement of the ﬁt is
larger than the penalty of adding an extra variable. The trade-oﬀbetween ﬁt and
simplicity therefore prefer the ﬁt as long as the AIC decreases.
Step 1 Calculate the AIC for a linear model only consisting of a constant.
Step 2 Add the variable which is most correlated with the dependent variable. Calcu-
late the AIC again. If AIC does not decrease, disregard the variable and stop
the stepwise regression. If the variable decrease the AIC, the variable stays in
the linear model.
Step 3 Add the variable which is most correlated with the dependent variable of the
remaining variables.
Test if the AIC have decreased, if not disregard the
variable and stop the stepwise regression. If the variable decrease the AIC,
the variables should be included. Afterwards it is tested if any of the variables
in the linear model can be removed without increasing AIC, these are then
disregarded.

Combined PCA for 1-hour and 24-hour Extreme Events
1-hour and 24-hour extreme events is joined to perform a combined PCA with a
combined normalisation (normalisation parameters can be found in appendix F, ta-
ble 28). For the combined PCA the two ﬁrst PC’s account for 57.4 % of the total
variability between the events which is 2.3-5.6 percentage-point lower than for the
two separate PCA’s. There were no non-signiﬁcant PC’s, using the approximate
test (equation 4.6 - 4.9). This could, as for the separate PCA’s also be due to the
steady decrease in magnitude of the eigenvalues (see table 11). For alternative ways
to determining the number of dimensions to describe the events, the ﬁrst 9 PC’s
should be considered in order to account for more than 95 % of the total variation
(see ﬁgure 16). Furthermore is the number of eﬀective PC’s (equation 4.10) calcu-
lated to 5.05 for the combined PCA. Between 5-9 dimensions should be considered
to describe the variability of the events in a combined PCA.

5.7
Variables for a Weather Generator
In order to set up an operational weather generator the number of variables should
be reduced from the 16 variables which have been used until now. Results from
the PCA, cluster analyses and stepwise regression have shown that it is reasonable
to assume that the dimensions in the data are smaller than the 16 dimensions the
variables describe. The reduction in variables is done by selecting a number of in-
dependent variables, and letting the rest of the variables be dependent. From the
combined PCA a suggestion could be around 5-9 independent variables (see section
5.4.2). The independent variables should be selected so they describe as much of the
variance in the events as possible and are somewhat independent from each other.
The results from PCA, clustering analyses and stepwise regression are combined in
this section.
After introducing season as a deterministic variable in the stepwise regression, there
is a change in the number of times each variable is used (see table 16 and 18). The
stepwise regression including seasons not give a clear indication of the importance
of including seasons as an independent variable, as seasons is only used four times.
Some of the reason could be that a deterministic variable does not include as much
information, but it could also be because other variables include the same infor-
mation. In ﬁgure 22 the variables duration, min depth and ratio depth is shown
with colours indicating the two seasons: summer vs. other months. The three vari-
ables shown is the variables showing most diﬀerence between the two seasons. The
three variables show a clear seasonal diﬀerence, but also a clear diﬀerence between
sampling methods. Min depth and ratio depth show a larger distinction between
seasons, while it could look like duration is more aﬀected by the sampling method.

6
Discussion
This section present a general discussions of the methodology applied in the project
and possible precautions and limitations of the results. The subjects discussed are:
Orographic eﬀect (6.1), extreme event statistic (6.2), spatial event characteristic
(6.3), PCA, cluster analyses and stepwise regression (6.4), variables for weather
generator (6.5) and future work (6.6).
6.1
Orographic Eﬀect
The orographic eﬀect in the case area could aﬀect the sampling of extreme events,
where extreme event from higher elevated areas are more likely to be sampled than
from lower elevated areas. The correlation between events and sampling place in the
catchment could be examined by selecting ﬁve new grid cells and see how the events
sampled from these grid cells diﬀered from the events sampled by the ﬁve original
grid cells. In order to analyse if the extreme events selected was representative for
the entire case area.
6.2
Extreme Event Statistics
6.2.1
The Original Grid Cells
The ﬁve original grid cells are selected to assess short distance variance in extreme
events. As these grid cells are also selected for initial analysis of extreme event
statistics, it should be discussed if these grid cells samples extreme events which are
representative for the entire case area. The ﬁve original grid cells are located in a
relative high elevated area (220-330 meter above sea level) compared to the general
elevation in the case area, and with a high mean annual precipitation (1160-1310
mm). This indicates that the ﬁve original grid cells do not represent the range in
neither elevation nor mean annual precipitation. For analysis of seasonal variation
in occurrence of extreme events it is not believed that the grid cells aﬀect the result
very much, as the variation is determined on larger scale. As the spatial correlation
of extreme events also samples 100 random cells in the case area, the initial analysis
of the ﬁve original grid cells contributes less to the results.
6.2.2
Data Veriﬁcation
The seasonal distribution of the extreme events from ﬁgure 10 has an expected pat-
tern based on the climatic region of the case area. This indicates that the data
at grid cell resolution describe rainfall processes which can be related to regional
climate. It has not been possible to ﬁnd studies which have analysed seasonal ex-
treme event statistic in the North Rhine-Westphalia or similar places. The large
diﬀerence between the seasonal distribution in occurrence of extreme event between
the two diﬀerent sampling strategies, have been conﬁrmed from studies describing
the seasonal diﬀerence in precipitation (Quirmbach et al., 2012; ExUS, 2010). Com-
pared to the seasonal distribution of extreme events in Denmark, based on the rain
gauge network from Water Pollution Committee of the Society of Danish Engineers
by Gregersen et al. (2013), the distribution in the case area is more singular for
1-hour extreme events (almost only in the summer) and very uniform for 24-hour

extreme events. This is consistent with the climatic diﬀerences between the North
Rhine-Westphalia and Denmark. The seasonal distribution verify to some extent
the sampling strategy by the diﬀerence in seasonal distribution between 1-hour and
24-hour extreme event, indicating that diﬀerent types of events are sampled by the
two sampling strategies.
The spatial correlation of extreme events also indicates that the data at grid cell

to a limitation by the case area which has the longest distance of around 60 km.
6.3
Spatial Event Characteristic
6.3.1
Sampling of spatial Extreme Events
The discussion on choosing spatial extreme events for event characteristic can be
divided into two parts: The deﬁnition of extreme events and the selection of spatial
extreme events for event characteristic.
Deﬁnition 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events are sampled by two diﬀerent sampling
methods and deﬁnitions of independency of events. This might cause prob-
lems when the two data sets are combined to one, as some variables might be
strongly aﬀected by the sampling method. The distinction between sampling
method is done in order to sample two types of extreme events: long ﬂat front
events and short peaked convective events. These events have very diﬀerent
event characteristics resulting in diﬀerent sampling strategies. It is not be-
lieved that a completely common sampling strategy can be applied without
putting threshold values so low that non-extreme events will also be sampled.
It might be possible to adjust the dry period between events to control for
independency (1 hour between independent events for 1-hour extreme events
and 24 hours between independent events for 24-hour extreme events). An
idea could be to increase the time diﬀerence for independency for 1-hour event
to 24 hours while still keeping the threshold for extreme event based on either
max 1hr or max 24hr. This will not create a similar sampling strategy but
might decrease some of the impact on the variables caused by the sampling
strategies. A possible drawback of this sampling strategy could be that several
convective events might be coupled which could result in a less clear convec-
tive tendency for the 1-hour extreme events. It could be tested how much the
event characteristic changes for 1-hour extreme events if the time diﬀerence
for independent events was increased to 24 hours, like for 24-hour events.

Selection Selection of spatial extreme events is done by selecting events sampled in 3
or more of the ﬁve original grid cells that were selected for extreme event
statistics. As the selected events are used to analyse the dynamics of spatial
rainfall in the area, it is important that these events are representative for the
rainfall in the case area. As the ﬁve original grid cells does not represent the
elevation and mean annual precipitation in the case area (section 6.2.1), nor
the extent of the case area, this could inﬂuence the distribution of events types
which are sampled. As especially 1-hour extreme events are local events, the
spatial extent of the case area might be important to cover. If there was a larger
distance between the grid cells, from which the extreme events are selected, a
larger variety in extreme events might be selected. If the grid cells was moved
further away from each other, the correlation in extreme events between the
grid cells is expected decrease. If extreme events should still be selected if
they were sampled in 3 or more grid cell, this might aﬀect the minimum size
or duration of the extreme events being sampled. On the other hand, if all
events in the ﬁve grid cells was selected for for spatial event characteristic
some of the events, though extreme events, might not be large enough to
cause ﬂooding. Another approach for selection of extreme events for spatial
event characteristic, could be to select days where insurance companies in the
case area or Wupper Association had registered ﬂooding or another indicator
of an extreme event.
6.3.2
Variables for Event Characteristic
The 17 variables initially selected for event characteristic (16 used in the further
analyses) describe a number of spatio-temporal varying characteristics of rainfall.
The maximum 24 hour intensity is deﬁned as the maximum 24 hour intensity within
the duration of the event, only considering rain inside the event duration. As no
24-hour events have a duration below 24 hours this does not cause any troubles,
but for 1-hour events where only 1-event have a duration of more than 24 hours this
might be problematic. The maximum 24 hour intensity for 1-hour events is therefore
directly correlated with the maximum depth (as seen in appendix D, ﬁgure 24). The
assumption of no rain outside the rain event, when calculating maximum 24 hour
intensity does not correspond well with the 1 hour time diﬀerence between indepen-
dent 1-hour events. It is therefore likely that it would rain within the 24 hours. On
the other hand if a large event would occur within the 24 hours this would aﬀect
the maximum 24 hour intensity. The maximum 24 hour intensity would reﬂect the
1-hour extreme event described and the other event within the 24 hour, which might
give an impression of a more uniform intensity than reality for the event described.
Compared to variables used in literature to describe spatio-temporal varying rain-
fall, this project have an extensive list of variables, except for variables describing
the spatial extend of the rain cells (Willems, 1999; Ochoa-Rodriguez et al., 2015;
Sørup et al., 2016; Peleg and Morin, 2012). Areal description of rain cells was not
included due to the strong dependency on the event varying threshold. A variable
describing the shape of the rain cell, for example the ellipticity could have been
included without the same inﬂuence from the event varying threshold. This would

6.3.3
Rain Cell Identiﬁcation
The rain cell identiﬁcation consists of 2 thresholds: A rainfall intensity threshold to
detect only the core of the rain cell (quarter of max intensity for the event, with min-
imum threshold separate for 1-hour and 24-hour events) and an areal threshold (10
km2) to exclude identiﬁed rain cells below a certain size. This method corresponds
well with methods developed in known cell identiﬁcation and tracking algorithms
(Kyznarov´a and Nov´ak, 2009; Handwerker, 2002; Peleg and Morin, 2012; Dixon and
Wiener, 1993; Hering et al., 2005; Johnson et al., 1998). The rain cell identiﬁca-
tion module in these papers have an areal threshold between 2-16 km2 to remove
noise, which corresponds nicely with the 10 km2 used in this project. The papers
introduce ﬁxed intensity thresholds between 35-45 dBZ which correspond to approx-
imately 5-30 mm/hr. This is either done introducing a single threshold (Kyznarov´a
and Nov´ak, 2009; Peleg and Morin, 2012) or introducing several thresholds in order
to select rain cells (Handwerker, 2002; Dixon and Wiener, 1993; Hering et al., 2005;
Johnson et al., 1998). This deviates from the method used in this project, where
the threshold varies from event to event, but this project does also in contrast to
the presented papers concerns both convective and front events. Dixon and Wiener
(1993) suggest diﬀerent thresholds depending on the type of storm to identify, which
in this project have been implemented as an event based threshold to emphasise the
diﬀerent event types sampled as extreme events. This could also have been im-
plemented as two ﬁxed thresholds. A minimum threshold of 20 mm/hr for 1-hour
events and 5 mm/hr for 24-hour events has been implemented in order to secure
that less intensive rain cells are not tracked and to distinguish between diﬀerent
storm types. A higher threshold results in a clear rain cell centre without as much
interaction between cells (Dixon and Wiener, 1993). This could imply that a ﬁxed
lower boundary as well as a possibility of a higher boundary based on the event max
intensity might end up reducing the noise in the rain cell tracking algorithm.
6.3.4
Rain Cell Tracking
The rain cell tracking algorithm developed in this project is a very simpliﬁed algo-
rithm managing birth, track and death of rain cells by connecting rain cells between
two consecutive images (5 minute time diﬀerence) by predicted position of the rain
cell in the next time step based on movement direction. Qualitative analyses of
the algorithm have shown that the algorithm identify the rain cells nicely and track
them until they have left the case area. The cell tracking algorithm results in a
moving direction which have been veriﬁed with the data. The major diﬀerence be-
tween this simpliﬁed cell tracking algorithm and the ones described in the literature
(Kyznarov´a and Nov´ak, 2009; Handwerker, 2002; Peleg and Morin, 2012; Dixon and
Wiener, 1993; Hering et al., 2005; Johnson et al., 1998) is the lack of managing
merging and splitting of rain cells in the algorithm. More extensive cell tracking
algorithms are evaluating the match of the rain cell from one time step to the next.
One method is to analyse the overlapping area between predicted position of the
54
==PAGE:63==

rain cell in t+1 and where a rain cell is positioned in t+1 in order to evaluate if this
rain cell could be the same rain cell as found in t. This method of evaluation in
tracking of rain cells is for example implemented in the Swiss TRT (Hering et al.,
2005). Another method is to minimise a cost function using distance or correlation
to link rain cells from one time step to another, where rain cells between two time
steps are matched so that the overall distance or correlation (e.g. areal diﬀerence)
for the rain cells in the event is minimised. This method is used in TITAN, SCIT
and CELLTRACK (Dixon and Wiener, 1993; Johnson et al., 1998; Kyznarov´a and
Nov´ak, 2009). Common for both evaluation methods is that in order for these al-
gorithms to track rain cells, the evaluation is dependent on the algorithm to handle
merging and splitting rain cells, otherwise the tracking would not work. For the sim-
ple algorithm presented in this project, it is not necessary, as no evaluation further
than the link between previous rain cell and the rain cell within the predicted search
area is made. This imply that the chosen search area becomes very important in
order to be able to track the rain cell without linking the cell to another rain cell
which accidental is within the search area. This furthermore imply that the simple
algorithm in this project can connect two rain cells which is not physically the same.
The error of this is minimised by a larger threshold. In the case of splitting, the
algorithm track the rain cell with the highest intensity while the other is identiﬁed
as a new rain cell. In case of merging one of the original rain cells is further tracked,
while the other is assumed dead.
The search area in this project is set to 5x10 km, which in the shortest direction
corresponds to an approximate velocity of 17 m/s with a 5min temporal resolution.
If a rain cell is found within the search area the centre of the rain cell does not
have to be within the search area. The minimum distance threshold for search area
(5 km) corresponds well with the CELLTRACK algorithm (Kyznarov´a and Nov´ak,
2009), while a bit lower (usually up to approximately 10 km) than TITAN (Dixon
and Wiener, 1993), SCIT (Johnson et al., 1998) and the modiﬁed CELLTRACK in
Peleg and Morin (2012). Other algorithms have similar distance thresholds when
beginning tracking, but adjust the threshold to the velocities measured for the rain
cell (Handwerker, 2002; Hering et al., 2005). As the cell centre does not have to be
within the search area the actual distance threshold is larger.
The largest uncertainty in velocity and direction of rain cells, in the current de-
sign of the cell tracking algorithm developed in this project, is assumed to be due
to the deﬁnition of the cell centre. The impact of the uncertainty could be the
reason why the standard deviations for velocity is very high, see appendix C table
26 and 27. In ﬁgure 8 there is a large jump in cell centre from t=7 to t=8 for the
red marked rain cell. As seen on the ﬁgure this is mainly due to the maximum
intensity cell moves from one peak in the rain cell to another. One can discuss if the
rain cell identiﬁed in t=7 should have been two separate rain cells which merge in
t=8. This is a result of the threshold and the same picture could have been present
even though merging and splitting of rain cells was accounted for. As long as rain
cells are deﬁned based on a threshold, more peaks in one rain cell is unlikely to be

avoided. Most splitting and merging of rain cells is due to the implementation of
a threshold for rain cells and happens rarely in reality (Dixon and Wiener, 1993;
Handwerker, 2002). The jump in cell centre from t=7 to t=8 in ﬁgure 8 is instead
mostly due to the deﬁnition of the cell centre where most cell tracking algorithms

The rain cell tracking have only been performed within the case area. The tracking

area, compared to if the whole rain cell was tracked. This could be corrected by
tracking the entire rain cell and not only the part of the rain cell which lies within
the catchment area. Compared to the jump in rain cell centres the systematic error
at the boarder, which only aﬀects the velocity in a few time steps, is assumed to be
minor.
6.4
PCA, Cluster Analysis and Stepwise Regression
In the following a overall discussion for the Principal Component Analysis (PCA),
cluster analysis and stepwise regression will be presented. The results are prelimi-
nary discussed in the result section (5.4, 5.5 and 5.6) and compared in section 5.7.
The correlation between the 16 variables in the spatial event characteristic are in
general very low, while for a few variables (as expected) there is some correlation. In
the statistical analyses the correlated variables might inﬂuence the result, as several
correlated describing the same characteristic will result in weight of this character-
istic is increased (Jolliﬀe, 2002). This might lead to interpreting a characteristic as
important only because it is described by several correlated variables. An example
is the characteristic depth. Depth is described by three correlated variables, min,
max and mean depth. This implies that depth might be weighted three times higher
than a independent variable. On the other hand does the the depth variables mu-
tual describe spatial variation in the event and a single depth variable might reduce
this information. The correlated variables are mainly depth and intensities, which
are also variables which are important in all of the statistical analyses. It could be
tested if statistically methods might be inﬂuenced by the correlated variables by
disregarding some of the correlated variables to test if this would change the overall
result.

In eight cases a 24-hour extreme event also contain a 1-hour extreme event. When
combining 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events in one data set this makes some of
the observations non-independent. Within each of the two separate data sets inde-
pendence is assured by the length of a dry period between events. PCA has as many
other statistical methods an assumption of independent identically distributed ob-
servations, though when PCA is used for only descriptive purposes non-independent
observations does not cause major eﬀects on the objective (Jolliﬀe, 2002). The PCA,
which is also used in the cluster analyses, is in this project mainly used for the de-
scriptive purpose of understanding the multidimensional variable space and the rest
of the observations are assumed independent, the iid assumption is not considered
critical. Furthermore is dependencies between observations actively explored in the
cluster analyses, and directly in the PCA based on seasons or sampling methods.
The three statistical methods (PCA, cluster analysis and stepwise regression) have
all indicated the necessity of a many dimension variable-space in order to describe
the variability of the events. All three methods imply the importance of the major
variable groups (duration, intensity, area, depth and cell tracking), why no clear re-
duction of the 16 variables can be made. Even though 5-9 PC’s might be reasonable
to describe the variability of the events, based on alternative methods, reduction in
numbers of PC’s was not signiﬁcant with the approximate test. Furthermore does
the ﬁve ﬁrst PC’s identify all 16 variables as important. The cluster analyses seems
to be less dependent on the cell tracking variables (cell num, cell life, mean and sd
vel/dir) when identifying diﬀerent event types, while PCA and stepwise regression
put greater emphasis on these. One reason could be that the simpliﬁed cell tracking
algorithm does not describe these variables good enough and therefore blurs the
tendencies in these variables. On the other hand, both PCA and stepwise regression
recognise the importance of the cell tracking variables, why an explanation could be
that these variables explain more about the inter-group variance than the variance
between event types. As both cluster analyses are based on the result from the PCA,
the cell tracking variables still inﬂuence the cluster analyses as they inﬂuence the
result of the PCA. The stepwise regression in general conﬁrms the result from the
PCA and the cluster analyses, while also pointing out that some variables, especially
the cell tracking variables, are very independent, which also explain why it is hard
to deﬁne the importance of these variables in the cluster analyses.
6.5
Variables for Weather Generator
The number of variables in a given weather generator should be an optimisation
between the explanatory power of the linear models of dependent variables and the
number of independent variables. While an increase in number of independent vari-
ables will increase the description of the dependent variables, a lower number of
independent variables would make a weather generator more robust. Furthermore
should it be discussed if there is a lower limit of the r2 value in order to reason-
able model the variables in the weather generator. In section 5.7 the reduction of
independent variables were based on the overall best performance measured in AIC
and r2. It could be discussed if the selection of independent variables instead should

focus on reducing AIC and increasing r2 for the most important/sensitive variables
for the purpose of the weather generator.
The variables which have been pointed out as the most important to distinguish
between 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events (see table 20), should be further dis-
cussed. Throughout the project, focus has been on the diﬀerence between 1-hour
and 24-hour events, as very diﬀerent events are sampled with the two sampling
methods. The diﬀerence in extreme events is one of the main aims of this project
to describe, but it can be discussed how much the variables which distinct between
1-hour and 24-hour extreme events are aﬀected by the sampling method. One of the
variables which might be most inﬂuenced by sampling method is duration due to
the diﬀerence in time lag between independent events. Duration distinguish clearly
between 1-hour and 24-hour events, but does not clearly explain the diﬀerences
between convective and front events though the cluster analyses showed that the
duration distinguished between two types of front events. The seasonal diﬀerence
for duration in ﬁgure 22a could be an eﬀect of the sampling method, since none of
the 24-hour summer events has duration similar to the 1-hour summer events. In
PCA duration was found to be one of the most important variables in the distinc-
tion between 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events in PC1 and PC2. As 1-hour and
24-hour extreme events also represent two diﬀerent types of events, it is important
that the distinction between 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events can be described by
the selected variables unaﬀected by the sampling method. If the distinction between
1-hour and 24-hour extreme events is not present without the inﬂuence of the sam-
pling method on the variables, this would imply that the variables does not describe
the events on a basic level. Testing if the PCA for combined events would still show
a clear distinction between 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events if duration was disre-
garded from the PCA (new PCA made) showed a clear distinction between 1-hour
and 24-hour extreme events. PC1 and PC2 was primarily inﬂuenced by depth and
intensity variables. The depth variables are assumed to be inﬂuenced by the sam-
pling method to some extent. Despite this have the cluster analyses shown that the
depth variables group events across sampling method, in groups with meteorologic
characteristics in common. It is therefore believed that the depth variables are less
aﬀected by the sampling method as duration.
6.6
Future Work
Beyond the scope of this project, a natural next step would be to set up a weather
generator in order to create a spatio-temporal varying artiﬁcial rainfall series based
on the ﬁndings in this project. A preliminary status on the performance of such
a weather generator will indicate which possible adjustments could be done in the
analysis spatial rainfall. In the above sections several suggestions have been made
on how to improve the results and which precautions there might be in relation to
the ﬁndings in this project. A weather generator based on a number of independent
variables described in this project will result in a spatio-temporal varying rainfall
descriptions from high resolution radar data.

6 Discussion
A rainfall series from a future weather generator will have properties (given by
the variables in this project) which can be related to physical terms. One could
consider how to adjust the weather generator to account for future changes in pre-
cipitation patterns or if any variables would be more useful as independent variables
in order to account for climate changes.
This will give the possibility to simu-
late spatio-temporal varying artiﬁcial rainfall series accounting for climatic changes,
which could be compared to the results from dynamic downscaling of RCM by
Kendon et al. (2014).
Based on the discussion on the eﬀect of the sampling method on the variables
and the implementation of seasons as an independent variable, another suggestion
could be to make two separate weather generators based on 1-hour and 24-hour
data respectively. This would avoid problems with the sampling methods, as well
as dependencies of events. As the weather generator creates one event at a time,
two separate weather generators might also make it easier to control the number
of convective and front extreme events generated, taking into account how climate
changes will impact the precipitation pattern in the future (Fowler and Hennessy,
1995; Larsen et al., 2009; Sunyer et al., 2015).

7
Conclusion
The radar rainfall data from Wupper Association was found applicable for the pur-
pose of this project, based on the analysis of the seasonal distribution and spatial
correlation of extreme events. Seasonal distribution in occurrence of extreme events
at grid cell resolution (1x1 km) for 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events showed an
expected climatic pattern. From the seasonal distribution in occurrence of extreme
events it is concluded that seasonal variation in the case area can be described with
two seasons (summer/other months). The e-folding distance describing the spatial
correlation of extreme events indicates rainfall properties at grid cell resolution. The
e-folding distance was found comparable with measurements of spatial correlation
of extreme events in Denmark. It is concluded that the orographic eﬀect have in-
ﬂuence on the mean annual precipitation variation in the case area and should be
considered for further work with the data.
17 variables based on a physically description of spatio-temporal varying rainfall
was identiﬁed. It is concluded that 16 of the variables contributed with information
and there were little correlation between the variables. A rain cell identiﬁcation and
tracking algorithm was developed to estimate 6 of these variables. It is concluded
that the algorithm estimated the direction of the rain cells well, while a few prob-
lems in the estimate of velocity were identiﬁed. It is concluded that the rain cell
identiﬁcation and tracking algorithm was applicable to the purpose of the project,
though very simple compared to literature.
From the signiﬁcance test of PC’s, it was not possible to reduce the number of
PC’s from 16. Alternative methods to suggest a number of dimensions the the data,
indicated between 5-9 dimensions. From the PCA and cluster analyses it was pos-
sible to distinguish between 1-hour and 24-hour extreme events and identify four
diﬀerent storm types: severe convective events, convective events, front events with
convective activity and front events. The variables found important in the PCA and
cluster analyses are also concluded to be important based on the results from the
stepwise regression. 6 independent variables (duration, maximum average 10 minute
intensity, mean wet area, ratio between maximum and mean depth, mean velocity
and standard deviation of direction) was suggested for a future weather generator
based on the results of the PCA, cluster analyses and stepwise regression.
It is concluded that the project conducts the preliminary steps prior setting up
a weather generator. Separate weather generators for 1-hour and 24-hour extreme
events was suggested in order to avoid inﬂuence of sampling method on variables,
and correlation between events. It is concluded that a weather generator based on
the ﬁnding of this project could be a solution to create artiﬁcial spatio-temporal
varying rainfall series for application in urban hydrology.
