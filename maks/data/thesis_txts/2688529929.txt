abstract
Abstract
Disentanglement studies heavily rely upon the assumption that the latent space of the
variational autoencoder is Euclidean, accordingly their proposed metrics measure disentan-
glement with respect to the Euclidean axes of the latent space. Unfortunately, the Euclidean
representations found in the latent space are arbitrary, and thus disentanglement cannot
be guaranteed. Instead, we propose to investigate disentanglement from a non-Euclidean
approach. To this end, we ﬁrst modify our variational autoencoder (VAE) to have less
conﬁdent variance estimations by introducing an RBF network as the decoder variance
network. This allowed us to ﬁt the locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) to the latent
distribution of the VAE. Initial experiments were conducted using a subset of MNIST digits.
We observed that using multiple classes result in the latent distribution becoming discrete
and not suited for traversal. Subsequently, we repeated our experiment using only MNIST
class 1. Here, we see some potential usage for our approach. However, as we evaluating
the latent distribution in a non-Euclidean manner, classical disentanglement tools cannot
be applied to measure our performance, complicating performance evaluation. Instead, we
evaluated the bodies data set, which is a synthetic data set of bodies varying only in pose
and body shape. The aggregate posteriors of these models do not adhere to the Euclidean
axes and are thus not ideal in terms of classical disentanglement. However, upon manual
inspection, it is clear that the true generative factors of variation have been identiﬁed and
were separable. Here, we found potential use of our principal geodesics and even achieved
disentanglement according to the deﬁnition of Bengio et al (2014). However, our model
suffers from being unstable and largely dependent on the variance network and its artifacts.

Introduction
Classiﬁcation is the process of predicting the class of a given data point. Classiﬁcation and
regression are two of the most well studied machine learning tasks. Most classiﬁcation algorithms
primarily attempt to identify discriminative features in order to separate data into different
classes.
Algorithms such as support vector machines’ sole task is to identify the decision
boundary between classes. For such tasks, often only a very limited understanding of the data
is required. Imagine having to distinguish between pictures of black cats from yellow cats.
The classiﬁer could primarily rely on the predominant color hue of the image. Whereas, if we
are tasked to generate new samples to add to our data set, it requires a much more complete
understanding of the data and its distribution. Creating an image ﬁlled with a yellow smear is not
sufﬁcient to generate an image of a yellow cat. A generative model must understand or at least
attempt to model most of the underlying distribution in a much more comprehensive manner.
If the underlying distribution is successfully modeled, it is possible to evaluate likelihoods of
samples, compress data, impute data, and sample new data etc. Thus, being able to estimate the
underlying data distribution is incredibly useful for other downstream machine learning tasks.
To contrast it back to the classiﬁcation task, the classiﬁers can be considered as describing the
data, while we now demand that our generative model needs to be able to explain the data. Then
again, this is a very ambitious task and rarely accomplished by our generative models. Due to the
difﬁculty of this task, many generative models choose to neglect this part in its entirety. Instead,
they focus on transforming data from a simple distribution to the data distribution. This can
be done with a probabilistic approach, but some are even trained relying solely on adversarial
learning [1, 2, 3, 4].
In this thesis, we will attempt to identify patterns and extract information from our estimated
underlying data distribution. Speciﬁcally, we will investigate the latent representation of the
variational autoencoder (VAE). We attempt to learn more about the latent representations of data
by leveraging the induced geometric structure of the VAE. We initially set out to investigate the
disentangling properties of the variational autoencoder, but due to our experimental premise and
design, we cannot apply the usual methodologies presented in disentanglement studies. We will
simply qualitatively contrast our experiments to those studies instead.
1.1
Disentanglement
The performance of a machine learning algorithm heavily depends on the input data and its
representation (features). Thus, data preprocessing and feature selection is critical prior to any
machine learning task. This process ranges from more simple feature selection to heavy data
transformation. Unfortunately, this part of the data analysis frequently relies on taking advantage
of prior domain knowledge to extract information from the data. This usually requires manual
work by domain experts. Thus, it is of great interest to automate this process, and efforts to
do so has become known as representation learning. Representation learning has a rich history
in classical machine learning with linear methods such as PCA, ICA, and CCA etc. While, the
==PAGE:7==
introduction
2
classical methods take a linear approach, we will be exploring the variational autoencoder’s
ability to ﬁnd non-linear feature representations in an unsupervised manner.
The initial hurdle of representation learning was and still is to deﬁne what constitutes a good
representation. One obvious deﬁnition is that it is a representation that improves the perfor-
mance of downstream machine learning processes by being more computationally convenient.
Unfortunately, such a deﬁnition heavily depends on the speciﬁc downstream task and its hyper-
parameters. Thus, this deﬁnition makes performance evaluation close to impossible. Bengio et al.
(2013) laid the ground works, while they did not explicitly deﬁne what a good representation
is, they presented various properties of a good representation [5]. Firstly, good representations
should be distributed, meaning they should be expressive and of reasonable size. Secondly, they
should also be invariant, which pertains to being invariant to local changes in the data. Instead, it
should only capture high level features. Finally, the representations should be disentangled with
respect to factors of variation (FoV). A change in one factor of variation should be independent
of the other factors. The ﬁrst two aforementioned properties can be achieved in variational
autoencoders by creating a sufﬁciently expressive network and choosing a small latent space.
A small latent space forces the latent variables to store high-level features, as opposed to local
changes. However, the ﬁnal property is not guaranteed and has been a key research area within
variational autoencoder research. A major problem in disentanglement research is the lack of a
formal and mathematical deﬁnition of disentanglement. Consequently, there is also no formal
method of evaluating whether a representation is disentangled or not. Therefore, each article
provide their own deﬁnition of disentanglement, then again, these deﬁnitions are usually very
similar to the one provided by Bengio et al (2013). The individual papers tend to tweak their
deﬁnition to ﬁt their scope and usually their proposed metric for measuring disentanglement. To
get a better understanding of what disentanglement refers to in a variational autoencoder setting,
we must ﬁrst garner a better understanding of the model.
1.2
Variational Autoencoder
1.2.1
Autoencoder
The variational autoencoder was developed from the traditional autoencoder that is much older
[6]. The traditional autoencoder, like the variational variant, is an artiﬁcial neural network that
attempts to reproduce the input data in an unsupervised setting. Importantly, the model contains
an information bottleneck that limits the information allowed to pass through the network. The
parts of the network prior the bottleneck are known as the encoder. On the other hand, the
layers after the bottleneck that reconstruct the data samples are known as the decoder. We will
sometimes refer to this part of the model as the generator/generative model. The bottleneck
is created by restricting the amount of information allowed to be stored in the internal layers.
This is usually done by forcing the middle layers of the network to have less capacity. In this
bottleneck, the model is thus forced to learn a simpler representation of the data as it has to be
able to reconstruct the input sample. This simpler representation can then be used to facilitate
further analysis. This all occurs through the inductive bias of the network architecture, the model
==PAGE:8==
introduction
3
is trained purely through a reconstruction error, which could be a binary cross entropy loss. The
traditional autoencoder can be considered a lossy compression algorithm, as the information in
the bottleneck can be treated as a compressed version of the data. The reconstructions are not
perfect, and information is lost throughout this process, hence, lossy. The traditional autoencoder
is most commonly used to denoise data and detect outliers.

When expressed in this manner, the lower bound, L(x), is also known as the Evidence Lower

1.3
Variational Autoencoder in Practice
Now that we have derived the ELBO loss so that we can optimize our variational autoencoder.
Let us translate this loss to practical terms. In practice the approximate posterior, q(z|x), and
our observational model p(x|z) will be modeled by universal function approximators in neural
networks. The latent variables, z, comes from a chosen prior distribution p(z), typically from an
exponential family. Remember that z is assumed to be the underlying set of variables that the
samples, x, were generated from. Thus, they are in most cases of much lower dimension than x.
Figure 1: A: A graphical schematic of the variational autoencoder model with the observation model on
the left and the inference model on the right. B: A structural overview of the neural network
used in practice to infer the latent variables and reconstruct the original samples.
As most neural networks, the VAE is optimized via stochastic gradient descent that is back-
propagated throughout its layers. An issue that comes with the network structure of the variational
autoencoder is that the gradients do not readily ﬂow through the network. An engineering
trick to facilitate smooth back-propagation is known as the reparameterization trick. We will
use the Gaussian prior as an example, as it is the most commonly used prior. First, instead of
using a multivariate Gaussian distribution, we instead force these to be independent Gaussian
distributions. Additionally, instead of sampling directly from the approximate posterior, we
introduce a noise variable, ϵ, to sample from instead. Our latent distribution thus becomes
z = µ + σ ⊙ϵ,
ϵ ∈N (0, 1).
(11)
This reparameterization trick ensures that the gradients can easily ﬂow back through the entire
network [7, 8].

1.3.1
Variance network
To reconstruct its input samples, the variational autoencoder outputs a distribution of the data,
given the latent encoding, p(x|z). It uses this output distribution to give an estimate of the input
data. A Bernoulli observation model is frequently used for black and white image data, where
each pixel value is interpreted as the probability of the binarized pixel value being 1 under
the Bernoulli distribution. For more complicated settings, such as colored images, discretized
distributions are typically used. In our case, we will be using a normal distribution with a
diagonal covariance matrix. Our neural network must estimate both the mean and variance
of the output normal distribution. While neural networks are excellent at mean predictions,
their variance prediction leave a lot to be desired. Neural networks are frequently overconﬁdent,
returning very low variance estimations [9]. The reason for this is not entirely clear. It may
be due to the lack of principled uncertainty estimation or perhaps that the task of estimating
variance is fundamentally different from mean prediction. Thus, our current neural network
design theory and engineering tricks do not translate well to variance prediction. An illustration
of this phenomenon can be observed in ﬁgure 2. In the ﬁgure from Detlefsen et al. (2019), it
is clear that the estimated variance of the samples even far away from the manifold remain
meaningful with very little noise. Instead, we would expect the samples far away from the latent
manifold to be more noisy than those on the manifold. In their proposed variance estimation
network, Detlefsen et al. ﬁnd that samples away from the data manifold are estimated to have
much higher variances. We will attempt to reproduce this property in our model. Note that
we will sometimes refer to this variance estimation property as more robust/strict than regular
variance estimation.
Figure 2: Left: The decoded samples from a classical variational autoencoder with a normal distribution as
its observation model. Notice that samples away from the latent manifold is likewise estimated to
have very low variance. Right: The corresponding results of the model proposed by Detlefsen et
al. [10]. Their network estimate much higher variances away from the supported latent manifold.

If we were to utilize the classical variational autoencoder for our experiments, the low variance
estimation would result in a very smooth variance approximation in the latent space. This leads
to overly conﬁdent predictions of out-of-distribution samples [10, 11]. But more importantly
for our experiments, this results in the data manifold being overly smooth around the latent
representation. Instead, we prefer if the data manifold is concentrated around the latent rep-
resentations of the data. This will allow us to investigate the data while traversing the latent
manifold, as it creates clear boundaries of the manifold. Overly smooth variance estimations
blurs the boundaries between areas supported by data and areas not supported.
To ensure we have our desired properties in our variance estimation, we modify our variational
autoencoder and its optimization process to improve variance estimation. Again, improving in
this case refers to the variance network gaining our desired properties. First, we separate the
training of the mean network and variance network completely. By training the mean network
ﬁrst and only subsequently training the variance network, we see much less conﬁdent variance
estimations [10]. Whether this is better is up for debate, however, for our experiments we desire
that the variance network has high variance in areas without support of the data. Thus, our
methodology cater to this property. Second, we will be using a radial basis function (RBF)
network for variance estimation, which consists of a mixture of RBF kernels [12],

1.4
Disentanglement in a Variational Autoencoder
As mentioned previously, representation learning research in variational autoencoders tends to
focus on achieving disentanglement. An issue in this research area is the lack of both a formal
and mathematical deﬁnition of what disentanglement entails. Each paper propose their own
deﬁnition and method of measurement. They tend to not stray too far away from the deﬁnition
presented by Bengio et al [5], who deﬁned disentanglement as a change in one factor of variation
being be independent of the other factors. However, this still lacks a mathematical deﬁnition. In a
variational autoencoder setting, this usually translate to meaning a change in one latent code only
changes one ground truth factor of variation. This is of course in broad strokes, as individual
papers deﬁne it in their own manner.
Unfortunately, the variational autoencoder suffers from an identiﬁability problem. The latent
representations, despite being equally optimal, is an arbitrary instantiation. This problem stems
from the ELBO loss only caring about the overall latent distribution, it does not care about the
internal structure of the data. Two latent distributions that are considered equally optimal may
look wildly different. An example could be an invertible rotation transformation,
g(z) = R(z, θ),
θ = sin(π ∥z∥),
(14)
where θ is the rotation parameter. This transformation can be thought of as placing the points
on separate rotating discs and rotating them in different directions. The illustration of this
transformation from Hauberg (2018) can be found in ﬁgure 3B. This obviously contorts the latent
representations greatly, but it does not incur any additional loss in the ELBo [13]. The latent
encodings are considered equally optimal before and after the transformation. This also causes
problems in measuring distances in the latent space, which we will delve into shortly. Locatello
et al. formalized this issue a bit more and presented it as the impossibility result in their paper
[14]. In their proof, they construct an inﬁnite family of orthogonal matrices that can be applied to
the latent representations without changing the marginal distribution. These transformations are
generated using householder transformations, which are linear transformations that reﬂect the
data in a reﬂection plane. This way, the transformation matrices that are applied to the latent
encodings are constructed to be non-zero in all elements of the matrix, and thus they can be
considered a non-independent transformation of the latent representations. As Locatello et al.’s
deﬁnition of disentanglement heavily depends on independent latent codes, they have shown
that the variational autoencoder cannot achieve reliable disentanglement. Additionally, Locatello
et al. conducted extensive experiments (2.52 GPU years) to also experimentally demonstrate
that the classical variational autoencoder does not have the necessary inductive bias to reliably
disentangle data. Instead it is largely dependent on individual model seeds. Since then, studies
tend to focus on introducing the necessary inductive bias into the variational autoencoder.

1.5
Distances in the Variational Autoencoder
Another issue that comes with the identiﬁability problem is that distances in the latent space
do not carry meaning. We continue with the previous rotation example (see ﬁgure 3B for
illustration), which incurred no change from an optimization point of view. This transformation
changes pairwise distances between points completely, but this change cannot be measured
in the ELBO loss. This clearly illustrates that distances between points are uninformative, as
they are arbitrary depending on the latent representations instantiation. Another issue is that
the inter-class distances are often heavily compressed. To optimize the KL-divergence in the
ELBO, the variational autoencoder will ensure that the latent representations mimics the standard
Gaussian prior as much as possible. This is in most cases a difﬁcult task, remember that the
model still has to store all the necessary information to reconstruct the high dimensional data
sample from this latent data encodings. Consequently, the model cannot afford to distribute the
data in the latent space in a meaningful manner. Instead, it compresses the latent distribution
of the data to lower the KL-divergence, which results in the latent space distances losing their
meaning. This especially affects the inter-group distances, as these would ideally be placed far
apart. The low density areas between groups are heavily compressed.

1.7.1
Study Overview
The initial goal of our study was to investigate disentanglement in variational autoencoders by
exploiting its geometry. However, it quickly became apparent that our study deviated from usual
disentanglement studies. Disentanglement studies heavily rely on the Euclidean latent space and
its coordinates to measure its level of disentanglement. However, as we are attempting to ﬁnd
non-linear structures in the latent space through leveraging the generative model’s geometry, we
cannot directly compare our results to these studies. Our newfound geodesics no longer span a
basis for measurement and we cannot rely on their metrics to measure our performance. Instead
we will qualitatively evaluate our results and compare them to the Euclidean approach.
An overview schematic of our work can be found in ﬁgure 6. The initial hurdle was to implement a
model with our desired variance properties. To overcome this, we modify the classical variational
autoencoder training and utilize an RBF network as our variance network. Following this, we
will be using the Riemannian geometry of the latent space and generative model to ﬁt a locally
adaptive normal distribution. From this locally adaptive normal distribution, we will study the
principal geodesics, which are the geodesics that have the eigenvectors of the LAND covariance
matrix as their velocity vectors. This is a gross simpliﬁcation of our work. Naturally, each step of
this process comes with its own set of challenges. The details of which we will now delve into.

Estimating the Normalization Constant Efﬁciently
The constant of the Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND) has no closed form solution.
Instead we must approximate it via numerical integration by evaluating the function at points in
a grid that we deﬁne. Doing so each iteration is both computationally expensive and impractical.
The LAND gradients consider each geodesic between the points in the grid, p, and µ. Naturally,
this quickly becomes very memory intensive. Even in our two-dimensional latent space, a grid

identify which parts of the latent space contribute the most to the constant. This was done by

2.3
Fitting a Locally Adaptive Normal Distribution (LAND)
After investigating the metric grid size and determining the necessary number of importance
samples, we settled on a grid size of 100 by 100, and 512 importance weighted samples. Using
these hyperparameters, we ﬁt our locally adaptive normal distribution parameters, µ and A
(A⊤A = Σ−1), to the latent representations of our variational autoencoder. To initialize the LAND
distribution, we test 10 different initializations and use the one that returns the lowest negative
log likelihood. Each initialization, we initialize µ via Sturm’s mean [19]. Sturm’s takes two points
connects them using a geodesic and uses the middle point as the current mean. It then samples
==PAGE:27==
results
22
another point and goes one third along the geodesic between the mean and this point, and then
one fourth towards the next and so on. It was important to utilize Sturm’s mean, as initializing
the µ in a low density area of the latent space causes model instability. The A matrix is initialized
randomly by drawing from a uniform distribution between [-0.05, 0.05] for each entry of A.
Remember that the A is deﬁned as A⊤A = Σ−1, hence the very small initialization values. Once
again, the µ training and A training were separated. This time, we alternated training every ﬁve
epochs. Both were trained using Adam optimizers with a learning rate of 0.001. Each epoch
we ﬁnd the latent representation using the VAE and sample from the approximate posterior,
meaning each aggregate posterior is slightly different between epochs.
Following this, we explored the latent space by calculating eigenvectors of the LAND covariance
matrix. By exponential mapping these, we found our principal geodesics. We normalize the
length of the eigenvector to be one in the data space. Subsequently, we manually tuned the length
of the geodesic in order to have it span across the entire manifold.

3.2
Importance Weighted Sampling for LAND Constant Estimation
Fitting the locally adaptive normal distribution (LAND) to our latent representations is very
computationally demanding. Due to the normalization constant having no closed-form solution,
we must evaluate it across a grid in the latent space to estimate its value. To save computations
and speed up our algorithm, we introduced importance weighted sampling. To determine which
distribution we should sample from, we visualized the values of the square root determinant
of the metric in the latent space. This was done using a model trained on a subset of MNIST
digits (0, 1, 2, 3), but the similar results are observed when repeated using other data sets, see
appendix A.4. Our visualization in ﬁgure 10 (left) indicates that it is from the edge of the latent
manifold that it is important to sample from. It is in this minor area of the latent space, where the
large metric values subside. As we will see shortly, the shape of the latent manifold may differ
signiﬁcantly. Thus, in order to ensure that we would sample from the edge of the latent manifold,
we deﬁned the importance sampling distribution to be dependent on the metric value itself, see
equation 42.

principal geodesic only traverses the area in between classes. Initially, this can feel counter
intuitive, but this makes a lot of sense. We deﬁned our metric to penalize the geodesic when
it crosses areas of changing variance. This means that when the geodesic travels from a low
variance area encounters the high variance of the interclass area, they tend to shy away. As the
minor region between classes harbor fewer data points, these areas have slightly higher variances.
Thus, the geodesics tend to be penalized more heavily for leaving one class to travel into another.
Accordingly, in the µ of our LAND (the point at which the principal geodesics cross), we observe
that the principal geodesic travels slightly into the 3-digits of MNIST. Subsequently, the geodesic
tends to travel in the in between class area. This is due to the aforementioned metric penalty.
As the geodesic is already subsiding in the high variance in between class area, entering a class
would mean entering a low variance area, which is penalized. Instead, the geodesic chooses to
continue in the high variance area between classes.
To conﬁrm the aforementioned hypothesis, we must plot the eigenvectors of the metric matrix,
which we have visualized in ﬁgure 12. This visualizes the direction in which the the metric
penalizes the geodesic the most for traversing. The geodesics travels in the orthogonal direction
of the ﬁrst eigenvector. This explains the sharp turn that we observe right before it enters the
MNIST 1-digits at [-1,1]. Going into that class would require the geodesic to go in the direction
of most variance, so instead it turns and continues in this low density area in between classes
that has slightly higher variance. This is because the metric places a large penalty traversing
through an area with a change in variance, thus our geodesic never truly goes into any class.
This highlights the importance of variance estimation with our desired properties. Unfortunately,
estimating the variances using these hyperparameters leads to large difference in between seeds,
see appendix A.6, as minor differences change the geodesics entirely as we will highlight later on.

Using Bodies Data to Attempt to Clear Ambiguity
So far, our results have been inconclusive. Due to the nature of the data we have analyzed, we
have not been able to conclude anything with certainty. Instead, we took to analyzing bodies
data, previously shown in ﬁgure 8. This is a synthetic data set, which has some special properties
when encoded via a VAE, some troublesome, and some desired ones. Firstly, the shape of the
latent manifold is frequently not very Gaussian, see ﬁgure 15. This is unexpected, as it carries a
larger penalty in the ELBO loss due to its shape. The latent distribution comes in many shapes
and forms, from an almost straight manifold to extreme curvature. Depending on the seed, it may
even come as disentangled with respect to the Euclidean axes (body shape on one axis and pose
on the other). This underlines that the latent manifold is an arbitrary instantiation dependent on
the seed. An illustration of the extremes of the latent manifold can be found in appendix A.11. In
these plots, we have shown the mean of the aggregate posterior. There is a discrete nature that

grid to ensure a wide variety of data. This is similarly reﬂected in the mean of the approximate
posteriors of the data. Importantly, this discrete nature disappears when sampling from the
approximate posterior distribution.

4
Discussion
4.1
When are geodesics useful? And when are they not?
Until now, we have seen cases with informative geodesics and not so informative geodesics.
So that begs the question, when is our method applicable? In our initial experiments on a
subset of MNIST digits (0, 1, 2, 3), it is clear that the geodesic is uninformative. Due to the
low density areas between classes which causes the latent distribution to be almost discrete, the
metric penalizes traversal from one class to another. Instead, the geodesic prefer to travel in the
orthogonal direction and ends up snaking its way between groups the entire time. When the
method succeeds in traversing to another class, it rarely traverses all classes present. Indicating
==PAGE:38==
discussion
33
that applying our methodology on data with very distinct classes, or at least distinct enough
that the VAE recognizes them as distinct, is not the ideal setting. This may change when you
have more latent dimensions than classes. Then again, from the MNIST 1-digits, we observe
that the variational autoencoder recognizes almost three distinct groups of numbers. The two
smaller groups include 1s written with serif ("hats" and bases). These two groups have few
enough samples that they can be considered outlier groups, which heavily inﬂuence methods
such as principal component analysis (PCA) since PCA is well known to place heavy emphasis
on outliers. Instead, due to the local nature of our geodesics, our method can ignore the inﬂuence
of the outliers. Accordingly, we observe a difference in direction determined to have the highest
variance. This could be one of the potential setting, where the locally adaptive normal distribution
(LAND) can contribute with something else. However, a major problem is that we are unable to
measure whether the directions that the LAND identiﬁes are superior to those of the PCA. We
can only assume they perform better due to the inﬂuence of the outliers on the PCA results.
It is becoming well-established that the classical variational autoencoder does not have the
necessary inductive bias to reliably disentangle data. While there is no formal or mathematical
deﬁnition of disentanglement, it usually involves measuring the disentanglement of data along
the Euclidean axes. It is especially apparent in our bodies data experiments that this is in no way
guaranteed. The latent manifold comes in a variety of shapes, and despite the model identifying
the true generative factors, they are in no way guaranteed to be along the Euclidean axes. When
evaluated using classical disentanglement metrics such as the disentanglement, completeness,
and informativeness (DCI) metric [20], the VAE would score low in almost all cases. Only when
the latent distribution instantiation are axes aligned would the DCI metric be able to rely on a
signel Euclidean axis to predict a single factor of variation. Our half-moon shaped latent spaces
score close to 0 in terms of disentanglement. From a glance, one would conclude that the model
had not achieved disentanglement. However, when you qualitatively evaluate, it is clear that
the model has identiﬁed the ground truth factors of variation, and they are clearly separable.
They are just not dependent on the Euclidean axes. Instead throughout the manifold, it is clear
the body pose is a function of how far along the half-moon shaped manifold you are, and the
body shape is dependent on which side of the manifold you are on. Here, we see potential in
the locally adaptive normal distribution to identify the disentanglement that was missed using
classical disentanglement tools.
4.2
Why our Results are not Directly Comparable to Disentanglement Studies
As we have just gone over, classical disentanglement study tool heavily rely on the model isolating
factors of variation along the Euclidean axes. This means most metrics and and traversals cater to
such. Unfortunately, as our principal geodesics are unable to be used as a basis for a coordinate
system, we are unable to measure performance using their proposed metrics. Moreover, we do
not assume that the latent space is Euclidean, which also goes against their methods. Finally, due
to disentanglement metrics catering to disentanglement along the Euclidean axes, designing their
experiments with fewer factors of variation than latent dimensions would be counterproductive.
This means that if we use our current two-dimensional latent space for a classical disentanglement
==PAGE:39==
discussion
34
study, we would at most include two factors of variation. It is unclear what one should expect
with regards to disentanglement if there are more factors of variation than latent dimensions.
Additionally, this is unlikely to happen in real world scenarios, usually you instead experience
latent collapse as you have superﬂuous latent dimensions. This leaves us only able to traverse the
principal geodesics and compare our results in a rudimentary manner. From our plots, see ﬁgure
15, it is clear that traversing the half-moon shaped latent representations using the Euclidean axes
or even the principal components yield poor results. It is important to mention that the principal
components are not usually applied in the latent distribution of variational autoencoders, as
the latent distribution is assumed to be standard Gaussian, the PCA would be expected to be
uninformative. They purely serve as a baseline for comparison between directions of most global
variance and local variance. Note that despite the standard Gaussian prior, latent distribution are
not guaranteed to be informative, and the PCA can thus still be informative. However, in the
MNIST class 1 models, we are unable to determine whether the principle components presents
more informative directions than either the geodesics or the Euclidean axes.
4.2.1
Worse ELBO with RBF Decoder
As previously noted, we observe that the ELBO of our RBF decoder VAE is worse than the
baseline model. The opposite is observed in Arvanitidis et al. (2018) [12], where the RBF decoder
network achieves slightly higher ELBOs. It is unclear as to why this happens, but luckily it is not
a major concern. In disentanglement studies, the goal is naturally to achieve disentanglement. To
this end, studies frequently introduce different hyperparameters or training methods that come
at a slight cost in the ELBO. An obvious example would be the β-VAE that raises the β parameter
in front of the Kullback-Leibler Divergence term in the loss. This frequently results in models that
are slightly worse at reconstructing the input data, since the model instead emphasizes the shape
of latent distribution. Seeing that it is only a slight decrease in ELBO, we did not investigate it
further. Additionally, you can consider our desired variance property, high variance away from
the latent manifold, as a penalty to the latent space. From this perspective, it is only natural that
introduction of a penalty would result in slightly worse ELBOs.
4.3
Future Works
We have found that our principal geodesics can ﬁnd within-class non-Euclidean structures,
however, many open questions remain. The primary bottleneck is striking a balance between
having a meaningful manifold without just a reﬂection of RBF kernel artifacts and avoid being
too ﬂat. Unfortunately, due to the variance network issues of the classical variational autoencoder,
we have been using the RBF decoder. The reality is that it has been difﬁcult to ﬁne-tune the
variance network to ﬁnd a balance, highlighted in ﬁgure 16. The RBF decoder does not scale well
to high dimensional latent spaces, however, luckily disentanglement studies rarely use more than
10-dimensional latent spaces. On the other hand, it remains unclear how our current methodology
will translate to latent spaces of higher dimensions.
To improve our work, the most immediate problem to solve is how to reliably train a network
that is not too ﬂat and does not have too many artifacts of the RBF kernels. We suspect that
==PAGE:40==
conclusion
35
somehow tuning the number of kernels and the α-parameter can strike a balance, but doing so
without manually ﬁne-tuning has shown to be difﬁcult. The hyperparameters do not appear to
translate well between models. Additionally, we can also consider how to determine the location
of the kernels, currently, they are placed on top of the means from the approximate posterior.
Note that this is solely due to having as many kernels as data samples in the aggregate posterior.
As an alternative, perhaps it is better to ﬁt a Gaussian mixture model to sampled samples from
the approximate posterior instead. This presents the primary roadblock ahead, and it is likely to
that the bulk of our time will be spent solving this issue.
Furthermore, another minor addition would be a method of scaling the eigenvectors in a more
principled manner prior to exponential mapping. Currently, it has manually been tuned to span
across the entire manifold. Ideally, we would hope that we would be able to scale the eigenvector
with 3 ·
√
λ in order to imitate the 99.7% quantile of the normal distribution. Unfortunately this
leads to the geodesic continuing far outside the stable area of the manifold. Another method that
could be interesting would be to use Morpho-MNIST [21]. Castro et al. have introduced a method
of calculating different factors of variation for MNIST data. Using their proposed framework,
it would be possible to evaluate how the samples along the principal geodesics differ from one
another, and how they perform against traversal of the Euclidean axes and principal components.
However, as they have multiple factors of variation (area, length, thickness, slant, width, and
height) it can be difﬁcult to know what to expect. Remember that disentanglement studies do not
study variational autoencoders with fewer dimensions than factors of variation in the training
data. However, a possibility could be to focus solely on slanting and thickness, as these were the
primary factors we identify. We would then have to measure whether our geodesics managed to
isolate the slanting or thickness more than the Euclidean axes.
Finally, we have until now found one scenario in the bodies data set, where our methodology
has potential. Expanding to other data sets would be a natural progression. However, a limiting
factor of our data choice is to choose data sets with continuous factors of variation and a
sufﬁcient number of samples. Having frequently non-continuous factors of variation such as
color, frequently leads to a discrete latent distribution. Such latent spaces are not ideal to traverse
using continuous measures such as our geodesics. An obvious next candidate would be the
dsprites data set using only the continuous factors of variation [22]. This data set is a stable in
disentanglement studies and have three continuous factors of variation, resulting in 6,144 unique
samples.
5
Conclusion
We set out to investigate disentanglement using non-Euclidean tools. Current disentanglement
studies heavily rely on the assumption that the latent space is Euclidean and as such measure
the disentanglement with respect to the Euclidean axes of the latent space. Unfortunately, this
assumption has been shown to be troublesome and not always beneﬁcial [12, 23]. Instead, we
sought to explore the latent space in utilizing the non-Euclidean geometry. The locally adaptive
normal distribution (LAND) presents a method of generalizing the Gaussian distribution to
==PAGE:41==
conclusion
36
non-Euclidean manifolds. We utilized the framework and geometry of the variational autoencoder
to ﬁt the LAND to the aggregate posterior of our VAE. From this, we calculated the eigenvectors
of the LAND covariance matrix from which we calculate our our principal geodesics, which we
have deﬁned to be the geodesics that have the eigenvectors of the LAND distribution as their
tangent vectors. Our approach struggles in multi-class setting, but show promise when applied to
single class problems. The primary source of difﬁculties has been the variance estimation network,
which is overly conﬁdent when composed of neural networks [9, 10]. This necessitates a different
approach, and we used an RBF decoder network as our decoder variance network. Unfortunately,
this approach leaves artifacts, which inhibits our performance as the variance network heavily
dictates the shape of the latent manifold. If a more stable method of estimating the variance of the
latent manifold in data space is found, our method shows promise of identifying non-Euclidean
disentanglement.
