Abstract
For genetic circuits that control irreversible actions, we must be able to design them so that
they do not misbehave even in a dynamic environment where the inputs they have been
designed to respond to can vary. In this work, we present an algorithm to design genetic
circuits with any given digital logic behavior whose high and low values are guaranteed
to be optimally far apart. We show this enables us to improve the behavior over time
of almost any circuit, and in particular a well-known misbehaving circuit from the genetic
circuits literature.
Resumé
For genetiske kredsløb der styrer irreversible handlinger er det nødvendigt at de er de-
signet således at de opfører sig korrekt selv i et dynamisk miljø hvor de inputs de reagerer
på kan ændre sig. I dette projekt præsenterer vi en algoritme der designer genetiske kred-
sløb med adfærd som vilkårlig digital logik, hvis høje og lave værdier er optimalt adskilt.
Vi viser at dette tillader os at forbedre næsten et hvilket som helst kredsløbs adfærd, og
illustrerer dette ved at forbedre et kredsløb, der er kendt fra litteraturen for at opføre sig
uhensigtsmæssigt.

1
Introduction
The genetic design automation tool Cello [1] enables the use of digital logic as an ab-
straction when designing novel behaviors of biological systems. Towards this goal, it au-
tomatically designs genetic circuits whose behavior represent that of specified digital logic
circuits. More specifically, it heuristically designs logic circuits composed only of gates for
which it has biological implementations available, and either heuristically or exhaustively
performs so-called gate assignment, assigning biological elements to implement each log-
ical gate, optimizing for the highest fold-change between 0 and 1 output states at steady
state.
However, this abstraction is, as all abstractions inevitably are, leaky: the non-ideality of the
underlying substrate (in our case, genetic circuits in a biological system) “leaks through”
and prevents us from treating the circuit we produce as a perfect digital implementation
of some logical function. Our gates act as imperfect approximations to their desired log-
ical functions [2], and circuit behavior is disturbed by cell-to-cell variability [3] as well as
context effects [4]. Solving or mitigating these issues is crucial to making the digital logic
abstraction work as a tool for design of genetic circuits.
These are all issues of genetic circuits, but there are also issues that are shared with
physical imple- mentations of digital logic. One such issue is hazards, roughly described
as situations where one or more inputs changing simultaneously could lead to the circuit
glitching, that is, temporarily outputting a wrong value depending on the the delay intro-
duced by the wires and gates that make up the circuit. So-called function hazards exist
that are intrinsic to the function itself, and cannot, in theory, be avoided [5]. However, as
they only result in glitches under specific delay scenarios, their impact can be mitigated
when designing the physical implementations of such circuits. Other hazards, known as
logic hazards, are merely an implementation artifact, and can be avoided when designing
the logic circuit.

Motivation
2.1
Problem statement
To further the goal of improving the accuracy with which we can design genetic circuits
that conform to the abstraction of digital logic, and to enable irreversible effects such as
cell death to be placed under the regulation of such circuits, we need to be able to control
their behavior as a function of time, not just at an idealized steady state.
Additionally, we desire control over aspects such as response time, and while the exis-
tence of function hazards implies that not all sources of glitches can be eliminated at the
abstract level, a design that minimizes their impact might mitigate the problem sufficiently
to allow circuits containing them to nevertheless find practical use.
2.2
Hypothesis
Can the utility of genetic circuits implementing digital logic be meaningfully improved by
taking into account their behavior over time as part of an automated design process?
2.3
Scientific aim
To develop an automated tool to design genetic logic circuits that can optimize not only
for steady-state behavior but also aspects of circuit behavior as a function of time.

we must first enumerate all relevant logic circuits. Specifically, we will enumerate acyclic

inputs, whose nodes have at most 2 inputs.

3.1.3
Extending
Initially our set of graphs is the single 1-node graph. Then, we iterate, building the n + 1-
node graphs from the set of n-node graphs as follows: for each n-node graph, identify
the set of nodes of in-degree < 2, that is, the nodes that can receive new edges. Now,
generate its powerset, excluding the empty set (ensuring that the new node will have at
least one edge out). For each of the subsets in the powerset, add a graph to the set of
n+1-node graphs that contains both the existing graph as well as a new node with edges
out to exactly the nodes in that subset.
3.1.4
Isomorphism testing
As our algorithms are independent of the labeling of the graphs and only depend on the
structure, we wish to ignore graphs that are isomorphic to graphs already in our set. To
accomplish this, we need to perform isomorphism testing against all graphs already in the
set. We can save significant computation by observing that, for any value we can compute
which depends only on the structure of a graph, two graphs that differ in this value cannot
be isomorphic. In our particular case, as we add nodes and edges to our graphs, we can
cheaply store and maintain their in-degree and out-degree spectra. As such, we store
our set of graphs as a hash-table from a tuple of spectra to a set of graphs, and, when
attempting to add a new graph to the set, only perform isomorphism testing against the
set of graphs with equal degree spectra. Finally, two graphs with equal degree spectra
must also have the same number of nodes.
Having established that two graphs could possibly be isomorphic, how do we actually
test whether they are? As they have the same structure and only differ in the labels,
we can attempt to create a bijective function M (a matching) from nodes in one graph
to nodes in the other, such that for every edge a →b in the first graph there exists an
edge M (a) →M (b) in the other. To find such a matching, we exploit the structure of our
graph: a DAG with a single sink. Numbering the nodes by when they were added, we
can attempt to match that sink, node 0, in both graphs: if it has the same in-degree, we
can proceed, and otherwise the two graphs cannot be isomorphic. Now we can proceed
through the rest of the nodes of the first graph in topological order, knowing that every
node will have edges out. If we are processing node a, and there is an edge a →b, then
due to processing the nodes in topological order, we know that there must be a match
for b, and we know that a needs to be matched with a node in the other graph that has
edges in to M (b). If b (and thus M (b)) only has a single edge in, then that edge must
be M (a) →M (b), and we have found M (a). Otherwise, b must have 2 edges in, giving
us 2 options for M (a). This means that for each node with 2 edges in, and thus 2 nodes
with edges into it, we have the choice between mapping (without loss of generality) the
”earlier” of the two nodes in the first graph in its topological order to the ”earlier” or ”later”
of the equivalent two nodes in the second graph in its topological order. However, multiple
in-degree-2 nodes can have edges in from the same node, which could disagree on how it
should be mapped. We solve this by designating, for each non-sink, a ”parent” node: the

earliest node it has an edge out to. This parent is the node that determines how it should
be mapped: either to the ”earliest” or ”latest” child of the parent’s match. We search
through the two options depth-first: the first time we process a node with in-degree 2, we
mark it for mapping the ”earliest” node with an edge in to it to the ”earliest” node with an
edge in to its match. We continue doing this until we either have a match for every node,
in which case the two graphs are isomorphic, or we reach a contradiction, such as finding
that the proposed mapping for a node does not have the same in-degree or out-degree,
or that it has an edge out a →b where a corresponding edge M (a) →M (b) does not
exist. In this case, we bactrack. The second time we process an in-degree-2 node, we
instead attempt to match its ”earliest” node with an edge in to it with the ”latest” node with
an edge in to its match. Since we are doing depth-first search, if we backtrack all the
way past node 0, this means that we have unsuccesfully attempted every possible way to
match the nodes of the two graphs, and we can conclude that they cannot be isomorphic.
3.1.5
Adding inputs
Finally, we need to ensure that our graph has the desired number of inputs N. We could
simply filter our graphs and keep only those that contain precisely N in-degree-0 nodes,
declaring those as the inputs, but this is wasteful. Instead, we take each graph, add an
additional N nodes as inputs, and add edges out from them to the existing graph in every
possible way where each input node will have at least one edge out and where the inputs
will be the only remaining in-degree-0 nodes. Specifically, we add an edge from either 1
or 2 input nodes to each in-degree-0 node and an edge from either 0 or 1 input nodes
to each in-degree-1 node, and then discard the graph if it does not have edges out from
every input node.
3.2
Hazard analysis
Once we have a circuit, we can compute the truth table of the logic function it imple-
ments. If we take logic levels to represent presence (1) or absence (0) of a given species
or its active variant, this corresponds to repressor-based logic where several repressible
promoters control the same transcript. If either promoter can lead to transcription inde-
pendent of the other, we are essentially OR-ing together the absence (NOT) of active
repressors, which corresponds to a NOT gate in the 1-input case and, by De Morgan’s
laws, a NAND gate in the 2-input case. We then consider, for each input, whether it is 0 or
1: the input state. When changing from one input state to another, we have a transition,
represented as a pair of input states, and based on the timing of the changes propagating
through the circuit, we might observe undesirable behavior: specifically, for a transition
between two states where the circuit will output 0 (resp. 1), it might intermittently output
1 (resp. 0). We’ll call this behavior glitching. Since glitching is timing-dependent, we will
say that if it is possible for a circuit to glitch during a given transition, then the circuit has a
hazard for that transition. If glitching behavior could theoretically be triggered only by the
timing of the input changes, we specifically call this a function hazard (as it is due to the
function that the circuit implements), as opposed to a function hazard.
From the truth table, then, we can determine if a given transition has a function hazard
or not. But can we prove that it does not have any logic hazards? And does this proof
extend to the case of biological circuits where our values become continous?
3.2.1

it will not be explained in further detail, but a noteworthy quality of it is that it is symmetric,
meaning that for two input states x and y, it will give the same result for x →y and y →x.

Graph walk
Consider a DAG, the state DAG, of the possible ways in which the internal state of a
circuit can evolve based on the input changes. Exploring this DAG depth-first, if we reach
a state where the output has changed its logic value, this path through the state DAG
has a glitch(!) and thus this transition is hazardous. We can propagate this information
upwards in the state DAG and reuse it, since two different transitions might reach the
same state (at least, two different transitions with the same end state will both reach that
end state).
3.2.2
Partial order
So far we have considered only digital logic. Now let’s extend our analysis to continous
values. Consider a logic circuit implemented as a biological circuit, mapping repressor-
promoter biological logic gates to each node. Since we do not know at this stage how the
mapping will be performed, and thus the relative strength of the promoters, we are limited
to working with a partial order. However, despite that, since we know that no promoter will
provide perfectly off 0 values and infinitely high 1 values, we do have some information
that we can use to compare the signal strength at a given node under different conditions.
Then, we have a simple way to determine whether a given path through the graph contains
a hazard: for a transition where the output of the circuit is 0 (resp. 1) both at the beginning
and end, the output strength of the circuit in some intermediate state must be less than
or equal to (resp. greater than or equal to) the output strength of the circuit at either the
beginning, the end, or both. This works for any partial order as long as it’s consistent with
the possible behaviors of any possible implementation of the circuit.
Actual partial order used
To compare the output level of the same node in two different states, we first compare
their logic levels. If one is greater than the other (where 1 > 0), we are done. If not, we
compare the logic levels of their inputs (if any), using this same procedure recursively.
The base case is that the node is a circuit input, in which case the comparison returns
equal. If the node has one input, we return the comparison of the input. If the node has 2
inputs, and they either both agree, or one input compares equal while the other compares
inequal, we return that comparison. If the 2 inputs disagree, or any input is incomparable,
we return incomparable for this node as well.

Upper/lower set as antichain
We are concerned only with whether all states reached on a path through the state DAG
are greater than or equal to (resp. less than or equal to) our beginning or ending states.
A set with the property that it contains all elements greater than or equal to (resp. less
than or equal to) any element in it is called an upper (resp. lower) set, and the smallest
possible upper (resp. lower) set for a given set is called its upwards (resp. downwards)
closure. As such, we can rephrase: we are interested in whether the upwards (resp.
downwards) closure of the set of states reached on any path through the graph from
the beginning to the end is equal to the upwards (resp. downwards) closure of the set
containing only the beginning and end states. To detect this efficiently, we propagate the
set of states reached upwards through the graph, maintaining it in the form of an antichain:
a set whose members are all mutually incomparable. This exploits the property that every
upper (resp. lower) set is equal to the upwards (resp. downwards) closure of the set of its
maximal elements, which is an antichain. Thus, we only need to maintain this antichain
of maximal elements, for which both adding a single element, taking unions, and testing
for both membership of single elements and set inclusion can be done efficiently. Starting
with the antichain of maximal elements of the upwards closure of the set containing only
the beginning and end states, if we observe any state that is not already in the set, we
know we have detected a hazard according to the partial order.
3.3
Gate assignment
With the structure of our circuits defined, we need to consider gate assignment, picking the
specific proteins that will represent each gate. In order to perform this search efficiently
while achieving a guaranteed-optimal result, we use branch and bound.
The general idea of branch and bound is to repeatedly split the set of solutions (”branch”),
calculating a bound on how good any solution contained in each subset could be (”bound”),
enabling us to disregard any subset whose solutions cannot be better than our current best
solution, a process called pruning or fathoming. When we reach a singleton set, we have
reached a feasible solution, which we can compute the score for and possibly replace our
current best solution with. Starting from the set of all feasible assignments, this gives us
a tree structure, with sets of solutions at the nodes, and single solutions at the leaves.
Rather than enumerating every possible assignment, we will represent a set of possible
assignments by a data structure containing assignments for only some of the nodes. For
example, an entirely unassigned circuit will represent the set of all possible assignments.
Our bounding function will then take these partial assignments as input. If we design our
bound carefully, it will coincide with the actual score for a fully assigned circuit, and we
can use a single function for both purposes. Finally, by allowing the bounding function
to consider information about the current best solution (such as its score), and allowing
a return value indicating that the bound for the input partial assignment is no better than
the score of the current best solution, we can save significant computation, since in that
case we do not need to actually compute the specific value.
For the branching, we have a simple solution: in topological order, starting with the output,
assign one node at a time. This ensures that we are starting with the node that will most
strongly affect the final output, and only assigning nodes whose values flow into other
assigned nodes, which has a greater impact on reducing the uncertainty about their values
than an assigned node flowing into an unassigned node.
Finally, we need to decide how to explore the tree. We use a simple depth-first search. We
keep a stack of nodes (representing sets of possibilities) yet to be visited, starting with the

3.3.1
Scoring/bound function
For the scoring function, we extend the Cello score from [1], or rather, its logarithm, which
we’ll call the steady-state score, to function over time. Specifically, where the steady-state

4.1
Hazard analysis
Performing hazard analysis on all 842133 circuits containing 7 nodes or smaller and with
3 inputs, we find that larger circuits generally have a higher propensity for hazards, but

4.2
Fontanarrosa et al.'s 0x8E circuit
Recreating the ”0x8E” circuit from [7] illustrated in Figure 4.9, and using the parameters
specified in their supporting information, we can simulate all transitions between input
states with equal output logic value. We find that despite a steady-state score of 1.43,
the ODE score is actually negative at −0.117, and indeed on Figure 4.2 it’s apparent
that the lowest value during the transition 5 →6 (which should output a consistent 1)
is actually below the highest value during the transition 7 →1 (which should output a
consist 0). That is, the glitching behavior is sufficiently strong that it becomes impossible
to distinguish 0 from 1 if it’s assumed the circuit may be undergoing a transition, rendering
this an infeasible implementation when transitions need to be taken into account.
Then, we can maintain the original structure of the circuit, but perform ODE-score-optimizing
branch and bound, resulting in the circuit illustrated in Figure 4.10, with the performance
seen in Figure 4.3. In this way we achieve feasibility when considering transitions: the
ODE score is now positive at 0.793. This does not come at any cost to the steady-state
score, which in fact increases to 1.80 due to lowered leakiness in the 0 state (note the axes
compared to Figure 4.2). This is likely possible due to the fact that Cello, which is the tool
used to design the original circuit, generally uses a heuristic method, namely simulated
annealing, for gate assignment [1], where we use branch and bound resulting in globally
optimal gate assignment.
Finally, we can design a new circuit for the same truth table, thus enabling the circuit
structure itself to be redesigned to further improve the ODE score, resulting in the circuit
illustrated in Figure 4.11, with the behavior seen in Figure 4.4 and an ODE score of 0.959.
This does come at a slight cost to the steady-state score which is decreased to 1.737, but
if behavior during transitions is of concern, this is likely tolerable in return for the reduction
in glitching behavior.
For an overview of the process of improvement, Figure 4.5 illustrates the worst pairs of
each of the 3 circuits, showing how the optimizer in this case mostly operates by reducing
leakiness, which is however still beneficial even in the cost of significant lowering of the
maximal output values.
4.3
Tradeoffs
As [7] uses approximated and equal time constants for all gates, we proceed from here

Looking at Figure 4.6, and bearing in mind that the steady-state score is an upper bound
on the ODE score, we see that the circuits optimized for steady-state performance do not,
in general, have good behavior during transitions, with the steady-state optimized circuits
ranging from optimal ODE scores for only the very simplest of circuits down to negative,
where the highest value representing 0 experienced during a transition actually ends up
greater than the lowest value representing 1 experienced during a transition, as is the
case in the circuit of Figure 4.2. This can be the case even for a circuit with essentially
the highest achievable steady-state score.
Figure 4.7 shows the trade-off more explicitly. We can see that optimizing for ODE score
is generally quite inexpensive in terms of steady-state score. In fact, the mean loss of
steady-state score for any given truth-table is only 0.16 compared to a mean gain of ODE
score of 1.21. If we look at the median, the difference is even more promising, with a
median loss of steady-state score is 0.059 compared to a mean gain of ODE score of
1.32.
Figure 4.7 seems to show two different groups of truth tables. To better tell what’s go-
ing on, Figure 4.8 shows a close-up of the data of Figure 4.8, albeit distorting the relative
scales of the steady-state and ODE scores. We can see the two groups separated clearly:
one group comprises circuits which end in a single NOT gate, consistently implemented
by ”P1-PhlF”. ”P1-PhlF” is the promoter with the highest ratio of ymax to ymin, and addi-
tionally has a very high n, allowing it to function as essentially a comparator at the end
of a circuit implementing the negation of the desired truth table, producing strongly sepa-
rated outputs from relatively weakly separated inputs, explaining why this group reaches
essentially equal, maximal, steady-state score when optimized for this. By similar logic,
when optimizing these circuits for ODE score, we are essentially optimizing a 1 node
smaller circuit for the negated truth table, and unless this negated circuit has terrible per-
formance, it will be corrected by the ”comparator”, explaining how almost every circuit in
this group achieves a near-optimal ODE score when optimizing for this, without needing
to compromise on the steady-state score.
However, given our condition of smallest feasible circuits, this requires that the truth ta-
bles are such that this negation of the negated truth table is among the smallest feasible
implementations. When this isn’t the case, we see greater variety in the circuit designs,
and a greater loss in ODE score. In fact, for the ”P1-PhlF” NOT-gate group, we observe a
mean of 0.038 and median of 0.031 steady-state score loss for a mean of 2.0 and median
of 2.1 ODE score gain, as compared to a mean of 0.23 and median of 0.27 steady-state
score loss for a mean of 0.57 and median of 0.53 ODE score gain in the other group.
Finally, we see a very small group at (0, 0) in Figure 4.7 and the upper-right corner of
Figure 4.8, of truth tables that are so simple that their smallest implementation is naturally
free of glitches.

5
Discussion
As expected from our results on tradeoffs, when improving on [7], we find that improving
the ODE score comes at a small but non-zero cost to the steady-state score. However,
we are able to improve on both compared to the original design, and even achieve ODE
score feasibility without increasing the circuit size. This is especially significant since the
re-designs in [7] intended to improve the circuit’s glitching behavior come at the cost of
increased circuit size, which is undesirable. We note that said re-designs have been the
subject of analysis for several years [8]. Our approach shows that increasing the circuit
size is unnecessary, and that better results can be achieved simply by the use of a design
algorithm that directly optimizes the desired property. In fact, we find that no 6-node
circuits exist that can perform better than our 5-node circuit!
5.1
Limitations
The approach outlined and implemented in this work has a number of limitations. Chief
among them, its mathematical model of gate/circuit behavior is based on parameters cor-
responding to cell population behavior and so does not take into account the noise intro-
duced into circuits by cell-to-cell variability, and cannot speak to the probabilities of ob-
serving any given behavior in a single cell or across a population. If the required data were
available, however, it would at least be rather straightforward to extend the ODE bounds
to provide the I-score defined in [3], but stochastic modeling would likely be needed for
an accurate model of the probabilities of any given single-cell behavior.
The approach also assumes that inputs change simultaneously (although not the outputs
of the input regulators), or at least on a sufficiently fast timescale compared to the time
constants of the regulators. While function hazards are unavoidable, robustness to small
delays in the inputs is a worthwhile goal. Likewise, the approach assumes that transitions
only occur from one steady state to another, missing out on valuable information about
how the circuit would behave if inputs change several times within a shorter interval.
We do not model context effects. While [4] shows that circuit behavior can in fact be
reasonably bounded even in the presence of context effects, this was considered out of
scope for this work. Likewise, for comparison with [7], we do not consider roadblocking,
despite [6] showing its relevance for biological logic based on tandem repressible promot-
ers. This, however, would be a relatively simple addition as all that is needed is to modify
ySS to take roadblocking into account.
The computation of the ODE score bounding is wasteful in several ways. The numeri-
cal integration could be stopped once a sufficiently strong glitch is seen to disqualify a
candidate, and transition time series only need to be computed in the first place if those
transitions are candidates for the worst transition within a given output logic level. Addi-
tionally, the solver library employed only gives access to dense output despite using an
adaptive step size, but with access to the interpolation polynomial, integration could be
stopped before even reaching the timestep at which a glitch develops sufficiently to be
disqualifying.
5.2
Future work
A number of avenues exist for expanding or building on top of this work. The partial order
based state graph approach could be extended to cover more gate types and possibly

==PAGE:30==
cyclic circuits, and then be employed directly to analyze abstract circuits or circuit design
patterns. In fact the type-2 incoherent feed-forward loop was used during development
and debugging of this part of the system, being small enough to easily analyze by hand
and compare to the results of this module, but the automation would make much larger
abstract circuits amenable to analysis.
The hazard analysis can be optimized through trace theory to limit the number of paths
through the state graph that need to be evaluated, and better partial orders could be
developed that might allow proving more transitions safe. Integrating hazard analysis
more strongly with the ODE score bounding might also allow proving certain transitions
to be safe for a given partial assignment of a circuit.
A number of tradeoffs could be investigated. The tradeoff between steady-state score
and ODE score is an obvious example, and optimization methods exist that could find
the Pareto frontier. However, another interesting option is the tradeoff between burden
and performance, looking at how scores improve as larger than necessary circuits are
allowed.
The ”comparator trick” of feeding a circuit that computes the negation of the desired
truth table into a NOT gate with appropriate characteristics is a design pattern that might
generalize, not only to hand-designed circuits, but more broadly: a repressilator needs
comparator-like functionality from its gates to push it away from steady state.
In addition to optimizing for minimum difference between logic levels, having bounds on
the circuit behavior over time allows branch and bound optimization of other circuit be-
haviors. For example, circuit response time could be the optimization objective, or one
objective in a multi-objective framework.

6
Conclusion
In this work, we developed a performant tool to synthesize circuits for any given truth-
table whose high and low values remain as far apart as possible even while in the middle
of responding to changes in their environment, and to do so in a way that guarantees
that these circuits will be truly optimal both with respect to their structure as digital logic
circuits and with respect to the details of how said digital logic circuit is implemented in
the particular biological system.
Existing systems today are often based on heuristic methods or can only predict misbe-
havior without being able to avoid it or even state if avoiding it is possible. For genetic
circuits controlling irreversible actions such as a kill switch, being able to ensure that cir-
cuit behavior does not cross safety boundaries dynamically is necessary, and this work
provides a tool that ensures that designers working on such systems can not only receive
the necessary assurances, but can iterate on their designs with reliable feedback and
speedy feedback from their tools that under their provided design conditions, no better
performance than a certain level is possible. We show that this is achievable in practice
by improving significantly on a circuit whose misbehavior has been known for the last half
a decade.
