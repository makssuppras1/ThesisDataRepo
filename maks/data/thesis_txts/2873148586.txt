Abstract 
This study explores the formulation and evaluation of thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) as a 
targeted drug delivery system using remote loading techniques. The liposomes were composed 
of 1-Myristoyl-2-Stearoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (MSPC) and DSPE-mPEG2000 in a 
95:5 molar ratio, designed to release clenbuterol hydrochloride in response to mild 
hyperthermia (approximately 40°C). Stealth liposomes composed of HSPC, cholesterol, and 
DSPE-mPEG2000 (3:1:1) served as a control. Liposomes were synthesized by hydration 
followed by high-pressure extrusion, and remote loading of clenbuterol hydrochloride was 
performed using an ammonium sulfate gradient. Dynamic light scattering confirmed uniform 
size distribution (<200 nm) and PDI (<0.3). RP-HPLC was used to quantify and evaluate drug 
encapsulation efficiency and in vitro release. Results demonstrated that thermosensitive 
liposomes achieved effective encapsulation and triggered release of clenbuterol hydrochloride 
upon heat exposure, with performance influenced by lipid bilayer composition and drug-to-
lipid ratios. These findings support the potential of MSPC-based thermosensitive liposomes 
(TSLs) for controlled and localized drug delivery, with the long-term goal of advancing toward 
animal studies for the treatment of muscle atrophy.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Scientific Background 
Drug delivery systems have transformed the pharmaceutical industry and modern healthcare by 
optimizing the therapeutic potential of existing and novel drugs. These systems improve key 
drug properties and offer several advantages such as increased solubility, stability and 
bioavailability. Additionally, they allow controlled drug release and targeted delivery to 
specific tissues or cells, which helps reducing off-target effects. Such systems have been 
successfully applied to a wide variety of therapeutics, including small molecules, proteins, 
antibodies and nucleic acids [1]. Furthermore, advanced drug delivery technologies are 
considered essential for the future of personalized medicine. These systems can be tailored to 
account for variability between patients in pathophysiology and drug response.  For example, 
by modifying the characteristics of liposomes, such as composition, size and surface charge, 
drug delivery systems can be specifically designed to match the characteristics of a particular 
disease [2]. 
 
Among emerging technologies, stimuli-responsive drug delivery systems offer special interest 
due to their ability to release their payload in response of internal or external triggers, such as 
pH, enzymes, redox gradients, light and temperature. A particularly interesting example is 
thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs), which are able to release their encapsulated drug when 
exposed to mild hyperthermia (typically 39–42°C). This allows for both spatial and temporal 
control over drug release, which has been extensively studied for cancer treatment [3]. 
1.2. Liposomes 
Liposomes are nanosized vesicles composed of one or more phospholipid bilayers surrounding 
an aqueous core. They spontaneously form when lipids encounter an aqueous medium by 
undergoing self-assembly [4]. Their amphiphilic structure features a hydrophilic core and a 
hydrophobic bilayer shell, allowing them to encapsulate both hydrophilic and hydrophobic 
molecules, as illustrated in Figure 1.1. Polar (hydrophilic) drugs are typically enclosed within 
the aqueous core, while non-polar (hydrophobic) drugs are incorporated into the lipid bilayer 
[5].   
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
 
Figure 1.1 Schematic representation of unilamellar liposome [6]. 
 
Liposome membranes can be composed of either natural or synthetic phospholipids. Common 
natural phospholipids include phosphatidylcholine (PC), phosphatidylethanolamine (PE), 
phosphatidylserine (PS), phosphatidylinositol (PI), phosphatidylglycerol (PG), and 
phosphatidic acid (PA). Frequently used synthetic phospholipids include 1,2-dipalmitoyl-sn-
glycero-3-phosphocholine 
(DPPC), 
1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine 
(DSPE), 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DSPC) and unsaturated lipids such as 
hydrogenated soy phosphatidylcholine (HSPC) [6].  
 
Cholesterol is often incorporated into liposome formulations at concentrations below 30% of 
the total lipid content. It plays a key role in modulating membrane fluidity, permeability and 
stability, especially in biological fluids such as blood and plasma. By intercalating between 
phospholipid molecules, cholesterol enhances membrane rigidity and reduces permeability, 
minimizing unwanted drug leakage [6]. 
 
Liposomes vary in size, with those used in clinical applications typically ranging from 50 to 
150 nm in diameter [6]. Based on the number of bilayers, they are classified as either 
unilamellar or multilamellar. Another classification system is based on functionality, including 
categories such as conventional, stealth (PEGylated), ligand-targeted and stimuli-responsive 
liposomes [4].  
 
Stimuli-responsive or “triggered-release” liposomes are designed to release their contents in 
response to specific physical or biochemical stimuli such as pH changes, temperature, redox 
conditions, enzymatic activity, electric or magnetic fields and ultrasound. These stimuli induce 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
alterations in membrane permeability and stability, enabling controlled drug release. 
Thermosensitive liposomes are a prominent example within this class [6]. 
 
Liposomes possess several advantageous characteristics, such as biocompatibility, low 
immunogenicity and biodegradability. This makes them one of the most widely used 
nanocarriers for drug delivery. Their lipid bilayer structure mimics natural cellular membranes, 
which contributes to their safety and effectiveness. Moreover, liposomes enhance drug 
solubility and protect therapeutic agents from degradation [6]. 
 
Despite these benefits, conventional liposomes can exhibit limited stability and circulation time 
due to rapid clearance by the reticuloendothelial system (RES). To address this, liposomes are 
frequently coated with polyethylene glycol (PEG) to form PEGylated liposomes, also known 
as stealth liposomes. PEGylation reduces immunogenicity and clearance and extends 
circulation time, thereby improving drug accumulation at the target site [6], [7].  
 
Furthermore, liposomes can be surface functionalized with targeting ligands to enable active, 
site-specific drug delivery. This targeted approach allows for selective accumulation in diseased 
cells, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing toxic or off-target side effects [6]. 
 
Several methods are available for the production of liposomes, with thin-film hydration 
followed by extrusion being the most commonly employed. This approach enables precise 
control of vesicle size and ensures a uniform size distribution, both of which are critical for 
meeting regulatory standards. Other methods, such as reverse-phase evaporation, are also 
employed depending on the desired liposome characteristics [6], [7]. The incorporation of 
techniques such as high-pressure extrusion in liposome production processes allows for 
working with larger volumes and concentrations, which is a significant advantage when scaling 
up to commercial production [8]. 
 
Over the years, liposomes have established themselves as effective drug delivery systems. 
Several lipid-based formulations have received FDA approval, with the first and well-known 
example being Doxil®, a PEGylated liposomal formulation of doxorubicin, originally approved 
in 1995 for the treatment of Kaposi’s sarcoma and later approved for ovarian and breast cancers. 
Its composition includes HSPC, cholesterol, and DSPE-mPEG2000 in a molar ratio of 56:39:5 
[4], [7].

1.2.1. Lipid packing parameter 
An important parameter to consider when designing liposomal systems is the lipid packing 
parameter (P), which predicts the type of lipid aggregate structure that will form [9]. First 
defined by Israelachvili et al., this parameter is determined by the molecular geometry of the 
lipid and is calculated using the following formula:

where V is the volume of the hydrophobic alkyl chains, A is the cross-sectional area of the 
hydrophilic head group, and l is the length of the alkyl chains [10], [11]. 
 
By calculating the packing parameter, one can predict the resulting lipid structures, which 
include micelles, bilayers or inverted micelles [12]. When P < 0.5, spherical or cylindrical 
micelles typically form. For values between 0.5 and 1, flexible bilayer vesicles (such as 
liposomes) are expected. A packing parameter close to 1 results in more rigid bilayers, while P 
> 1 leads to the formation of inverted micelles [12].  
 
This parameter is particularly important in the formulation of thermosensitive liposomes. By 
combining lipids with different packing parameters, it is possible to fine-tune the bilayer 
composition to ensure effective drug encapsulation, minimize premature leakage and enable 
controlled drug release at specific trigger temperatures. Therefore, a thorough understanding of 
the lipid packing parameter is essential for optimizing liposome design and performance [9]. 
1.3. Drug loading 
In addition to the lipid composition of the system, a critical factor in designing an optimal drug 
delivery platform is selecting a drug loading method that aligns with the specific properties of 
the drug. It is essential to consider the drug’s physicochemical characteristics when choosing 
an encapsulation strategy, as this choice directly influences the system's stability, efficacy, 
release profile and overall therapeutic performance. Ultimately, the goal is to achieve the 
highest possible encapsulation efficiency while preserving the stability of both the drug and the 
liposomal carrier to ensure the intended therapeutic effect is achieved. 
 
There are two main approaches to loading drugs into liposomes: passive loading and active 
loading. Passive loading involves incorporating the drug during liposome formation. In this 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
method, the drug is typically dissolved in an organic solvent along with the lipids, which are 
then hydrated in an aqueous buffer [13]. However, this technique has several disadvantages, 
including low encapsulation efficiency, reduced bilayer stability, poor drug retention, and a 
higher risk of premature drug release. These limitations often lead to the need of higher drug to 
lipid ratios in order to achieve effective therapeutic amounts of drug encapsulated and also 
produces too much drug waste [6], [13]. 
 
Active loading techniques, also referred to as remote loading, involve the post-formation 
encapsulation of drugs into liposomes by exploiting a transmembrane chemical gradient, most 
commonly a pH or ion gradient, across the lipid bilayer [6]. This gradient functions as the 
driving force for drug uptake. The underlying mechanism relies on the drug being 
predominantly in its unionized form in the external aqueous environment, allowing passive 
diffusion across the hydrophobic lipid bilayer. Upon entering the liposomal interior, where the 
buffer conditions favour ionization, the drug becomes protonated or charged. This makes the 
drug impermeable to the membrane and ensures effectively trapping it inside the liposome 
aqueous core [13]. The protonation state of a given molecule depends on its pKa and the 
surrounding pH [8]. 
1.3.1. Advantages of remote loading 
Overall, remote loading is often the preferred method when designing liposomal drug delivery 
systems due to several key advantages. This approach enables more stable drug encapsulation 
and offers greater control over drug release. A major benefit is that the loading process is 
performed after liposome formation, making it independent of the liposome preparation 
conditions. This separation simplifies process optimization and improves reproducibility. 
Additionally, remote loading typically results in significantly higher encapsulation efficiency 
[14].  
 
High encapsulation efficiency is particularly critical for drugs that are expensive or available 
only in limited quantities, such as many chemotherapeutic agents [6]. Moreover, achieving a 
high drug-to-lipid ratio is essential to ensure therapeutic efficacy while minimizing the required 
administration volume. This reduction in liposome volume helps limit patient exposure to lipid-
based excipients, thereby decreasing the risk of toxicity [13].  
 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
The vast majority of drugs currently in clinical use are weak bases containing primary, 
secondary or tertiary amine groups, which makes them well-suited for encapsulation using a 
transmembrane pH gradient. Due to their pKa values, these molecules remain predominantly 
unprotonated in the external buffer, allowing them to diffuse across the lipid bilayer. Once 
inside the acidic interior of the liposome, they become protonated and get entraped [8]. For 
drugs that are not inherently weak bases, chemical modification can be employed to convert 
them into weak base prodrugs, enabling their compatibility with remote loading techniques [6], 
[14].  
 
Once encapsulated, the stability of the drug within the liposome largely depends on its 
physicochemical properties, particularly its aqueous solubility and the presence of ionizable 
functional groups. Drugs that possess low solubility or readily ionizable groups tend to 
precipitate and accumulate within the liposomal core, facilitating their retention. In contrast, 
drugs lacking such properties may be more difficult to retain and are prone to leakage from the 
liposome [14]. To enhance drug retention, several strategies can be employed. These include 
encapsulating the drug at concentrations above its solubility limit to induce precipitation, or co-
encapsulating the drug with stabilizing carriers such as amino-modified cyclodextrins, which 
can form complexes and improve retention inside the liposomal compartment [14].  
1.3.2. Remote loading techniques 
There are various remote loading methods available, and the selection of an appropriate 
technique depends on the specific physicochemical properties of the drug. For amphipathic 
weakly basic drugs, like doxorubicin, ammonium sulfate and citric acid gradients are commonly 
used. In contrast, for amphipathic weakly acidic drugs, a calcium acetate gradient can be used 
[13], [15]. Additional buffer-based gradient methods include phosphate and EDTA gradients. 
An alternative strategy involves metal ion-based loading methods, which do not require a pH 
gradient to drive drug encapsulation [13]. 
 
The ammonium sulfate and citric acid gradients are typically established by hydrating the lipids 
in an internal solution of ammonium sulfate (pH 5.5) or citrate buffer (pH 4.0), followed by 
exchange of the external buffer with a higher pH medium, such as HEPES or phosphate-
buffered saline (PBS) at physiological pH (~7.4) [8]. This transmembrane gradient facilitates 
efficient drug uptake into the liposomal core. The ammonium sulfate method, in particular, has 
demonstrated encapsulation efficiencies exceeding 90% [16], [17]. Doxil® is a good example 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
of remote loading by an ammonium sulfate gradient [18]. The mechanism behind these 
gradient-based loading techniques is illustrated in Figure 1.2. In the ammonium sulfate gradient 
method, the intraliposomal aqueous phase is filled with ammonium sulfate ((NH₄)₂SO₄). A 
transmembrane ammonium gradient is then established by removing the external ammonium 
sulfate, typically through dialysis. The higher concentration of ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) inside 
the liposome leads to the diffusion of neutral ammonia (NH₃) across the lipid bilayer to the 
external medium. As each NH₃ molecule diffuses out, a proton (H⁺) is left behind, resulting in 
proton accumulation and acidification of the liposome interior. This proton gradient enables 
remote loading of weakly basic drugs such as doxorubicin. In its uncharged form, the drug is 
permeable to the lipid membrane and tends to enter the acidic core, where it becomes 
protonated. Once charged, the drug is unable to cross back through the membrane, effectively 
trapping it inside the liposome [19].   
 
 
Figure 1.2 Mechanism of drug loading with (A) ammonium sulfate method and (B) calcium 
acetate method [8]. 
 
The calcium acetate gradient has proven to be an effective method to encapsulate weak acids 
into preformed liposomes, with an encapsulation efficiency of almost 100% been reported, 
particularly in the case of diclofenac [20], [21].

1.4. Thermosensitive systems 
Thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) are specialized drug delivery systems designed to release 
their payload in response to mild hyperthermia, typically in the range of 40–43°C [22], [23]. 
The first TSLs were introduced in 1978 by Yatvin et al., who demonstrated temperature-
dependent release of neomycin from liposomal carriers, leading to inhibition of in vitro protein 
synthesis pathways [24]. 
 
These delivery systems are typically administered systemically and are often combined with 
localized hyperthermia techniques to achieve site-specific and rapid drug release in target 
tissues. This makes TSLs particularly effective for the treatment of solid tumors, since 
hyperthermia has been shown to enhance cellular uptake of chemotherapeutic agents. The 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
combined effect allows for both spatial and temporal control over drug release, thereby 
minimizing systemic toxicity while improving drug bioavailability and overall therapeutic 
efficacy [22]. 
 
Hyperthermia can be applied using a variety of techniques, including deep tumor heating with 
radiofrequency ablation (RFA) [7], high intensity focussed ultrasound (HIFU), microwaves, 
near infrared (NIR) [25] and magnetic resonance-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS) [26]. 
Water bath induced mild hyperthermia is also a simpler and non-invasive alternative commonly 
used in preclinical settings [25]. The choice of hyperthermia method, along with the applied 
temperature and duration of heating, plays a critical role in influencing drug release and overall 
therapeutic efficacy [22].   
1.4.1. Mechanism of heat-induced drug release 
The mechanism of traditional thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) is based on the phase transition 
phenomenon of phospholipid bilayers. At a specific phase transition temperature (Tm), the lipid 
bilayer undergoes a reversible change in its physical state, transitioning from a gel phase (solid-
like) to a liquid-crystalline phase (fluid-like). These liposomes are engineered to “melt” and 
become more permeable when heated above this transition temperature, enabling the controlled 
release of their contents [27].  
 
As the temperature approaches Tm, the molecular organization of the lipids begins to change, 
affecting both their conformation and mobility. In the gel phase, the hydrophobic acyl chains 
of the phospholipids are fully extended and tightly packed, while the polar head groups remain 
relatively immobile. This ordered arrangement results in a rigid and low-permeability 
membrane. Upon heating, increased thermal motion induces conformational changes in the 
lipid tails, specifically a shift in carbon-carbon bonds from trans to gauche configurations. This 
leads to increased disorder and enhanced fluidity of the bilayer [28]. 
 
During the transition, some areas of the membrane remain solid while others become fluid, 
which creates mismatched regions where the two states meet, resulting in local disruptions in 
lipid organization known as packing defects. As shown in Figure 1.3, the permeability is highest 
around Tm due to the coexistence of both phases, which creates what is known as grain 
boundaries. Once the temperature exceeds Tm, the entire membrane becomes fully fluid, 
making it more leaky and allowing the release of its contents [29]. 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 
Figure 1.3 The effect of temperature-dependent membrane phase transition on permeability 
[29]. 
 
When designing a thermosensitive liposomal system, several factors must be considered that 
influence Tm, including the hydrocarbon chain length, charge and degree of saturation. In 
general, increasing the chain length or incorporating double bonds tends to raise the Tm of the 
lipid, and consequently, the Tm of the overall liposomal formulation [30]. 
1.4.2. Types of thermosensitive liposomes 
Since the development of the first traditional thermosensitive liposome (TTSL), significant 
advancements have been made in the field of thermoresponsive drug delivery systems. A 
variety of thermosensitive liposomal platforms have since been developed, including lysolipid-
based, polymer-modified, targeted and metallic nanoparticle-based thermosensitive liposomes 
[31]. 
1.4.2.1. 
Traditional thermosensitive liposomes (TTSL) 
The composition of the first traditional thermosensitive liposomes (TTSL) was a combination 
of 1,2-dipalmitoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DPPC) and 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-
phosphocholine (DSPC), with respective phase transition temperatures (Tm) of approximately 
41°C and 55°C [28], [32]. Later formulations incorporated hydrogenated soy 
phosphatidylcholine (HSPC) [28]. Later, the addition of DSPE-PEG2000 was studied to 
improve the circulation time of the system, and it was reported that a 5% of the total lipid 
content was the most effective ratio in order to improve stability without compromising the 
system’s temperature sensitivity [33].  
 
Despite these advances, TTSLs exhibited some limitations, most notably a lack of burst release, 
which reduced their efficiency for rapid and localized drug delivery. These shortcomings 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
motivated the development of more responsive systems, such as lysolipid-containing 
thermosensitive liposomes (LTSLs) and polymer-modified thermosensitive liposomes [34]. 
 
1.4.2.2. 
Lysolipid containing thermosensitive liposomes (LTSL) 
Lysolipid containing thermosensitive liposomes (LTSLs) are a modified version of the 
traditional thermosensitive liposome achieved by incorporating thermosensitive phospholipids 
known as lysolipids. These lipids have a single acyl chain and a polar head group larger than 
the tail, which destabilizes the lipid bilayer upon heating. As illustrated in Figure 1.4, compared 
to a TTSL the lysolipid structure increases membrane permeability by introducing defects that 
create transient pores when exposed to heating [23]. 
 
Figure 1.4 Grain boundary formation comparison in a traditional thermosensitive liposome 
and a lysolipids containing thermosensitive liposome [23]. 
 
This concept was first introduced by Anyarambhatla and Needham in 1999, who incorporated 
a lysolipid (MPPC) into the lipid bilayer. They formulated a system composed of l,2-
Dipalmitoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (DPPC) and 1 -Palmitoyl-2-Hydroxy-sn-Glycero-
3-Phosphocholine (MPPC).  The resulting system showed enhanced drug release and a reduced 
Tm in the range of 39–40°C [35]. Nowadays, a more used standard composition would be 
DPPC/lyso-PC/DSPE-PEG2000 in a molar ratio of 90:10:4 [29]. LTSLs are characterized by 
requiring a lower phase transition temperature to achieve drug release, making them more 
suitable for clinical applications. This reduced Tm not only enables rapid drug release under 
mild hyperthermic conditions but also addresses the key limitations of traditional systems, such 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
as delayed release, the absence of a burst effect and increased risk of heat-induced side effects 
[23]. 
 
Notably, LTSLs have demonstrated ultrafast release kinetics, with up to 80% of encapsulated 
doxorubicin released within 20 seconds at 41.3°C [36], highlighting their potential for use in 
combination with localized hyperthermia for targeted cancer therapy. 
1.4.3. Thermosensitive liposomes applications 
The use of thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) in combination with hyperthermia for the targeted 
delivery of chemotherapeutics into solid tumors has been extensively investigated. Notable 
applications include treatment strategies for breast cancer [37], lung cancer [38] and liver cancer 
[39]. This combined approach offers several therapeutic advantages for the treatment of cancer. 
 
TSLs enable localized drug release at the tumor site, which reduces systemic toxicity and 
enhances therapeutic efficacy. Unlike many conventional nanocarriers, thermosensitive 
liposomes do not rely solely on the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect. The EPR 
effect is a pathophysiological mechanism that allows nanoparticles to accumulate in tumor 
tissues over normal tissue due to leaky vasculature and poor lymphatic drainage. Although this 
mechanism is widely used, it has shown inconsistent and often suboptimal outcomes in clinical 
applications [22], [25]. Instead, TSLs primarily function through intravascular triggered release 
within the tumor microvasculature, offering more reliable drug delivery at the desired site [40]. 
However, one limitation of this approach is its reduced efficacy in the treatment of metastasis, 
where localized hyperthermia is more difficult to apply [22]. 
 
In addition to facilitating drug release, hyperthermia itself provides therapeutic benefits. It 
increases blood flow and vascular permeability within tumors, enhances drug uptake by tumor 
cells and sensitizes those cells to chemotherapy. Furthermore, tumor cells often exhibit poor 
heat tolerance due to hypoxia and impaired stress responses, making them more susceptible to 
hyperthermia-induced damage [28]. These combined effects help overcome previous 
challenges related to insufficient tumor accumulation and inefficient drug release [25]. 
 
While the application of TSLs has primarily focused on cancer therapy, the use of these systems 
for non-oncological indications is an emerging area of research. These applications face 
additional technical challenges because they require precise control over temperature and 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
targeted heating. Nevertheless, studies have explored the potential of TSLs for the delivery of 
antibiotics, antithrombotic agents and anti-inflammatory therapies [22], [41]. 
1.4.4. Clinical Example: ThermoDox® 
One of the most extensively studied clinical examples of thermosensitive liposome technology 
is ThermoDox®, developed by Celsion Corporation. ThermoDox is a lysolipid-containing 
thermosensitive liposome designed to encapsulate doxorubicin and release it in response to mild 
hyperthermia, typically within the temperature range of 39.5°C to 42°C [42] [43]. The 
composition of the liposome consists of the combination of 1,2-dipalimitoyl-sn -glycero-3-
phosphocholine (DPPC), 1-stearoyl-2-hydroxy-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (MSPC), and 
1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-(carbonyl-methoxypolyethyleneglycol-
2000) (DSPE-mPEG-2000), in a molar ratio of 86:10:4 [44].  
 
ThermoDox is administered intravenously and combined with localized mild hyperthermia, 
commonly through radiofrequency ablation (RFA). This combination is intended to enhance 
drug accumulation in solid tumors and the surrounding tumor microenvironment [43]. 
Compared to conventional intravenous administration of doxorubicin and other liposomal 
formulations, ThermoDox has shown superior drug concentration at the tumor site [7]. 
 
Following successful preclinical studies and early clinical trials, ThermoDox progressed to 
Phase III clinical development, including major trials such as HEAT (targeting primary liver 
cancer) [43], OPTIMA (ThermoDox and RFA for the treatment of hepatocellular carcinoma) 
[45], and TARDOX (Phase I study investigating ThermoDox combined with focused 
ultrasound for liver tumors) [46].  
 
Despite early promise, the system encountered significant challenges during late-stage trials. 
Both the HEAT and OPTIMA trials failed to meet their primary endpoints. In the HEAT trial, 
ThermoDox combined with RFA did not show a significant improvement in progression-free 
survival compared to RFA alone. The OPTIMA trial, which implemented a standardized 
heating protocol, was later terminated [47].  
 
These outcomes prompted scepticism within the scientific community regarding the 
effectiveness of thermosensitive liposomes in cancer treatment. It has been suggested that RFA 
may not have been the most appropriate hyperthermia modality for this formulation, as it can 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
elevate tissue temperatures to approximately 50°C. In response, recent clinical strategies have 
shifted toward the use of mild hyperthermia techniques, which are more compatible with the 
temperature sensitivity of TSL based systems[48].

1.5. Formulation and design of the studied Thermosensitive System 
This master thesis focuses on a thermosensitive liposomal drug delivery system composed of 
1-Myristoyl-2-Stearoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (MSPC) and DSPE-mPEG2000 in a 
95:5 molar ratio (shown in Figure 1.5 and Figure 1.6), encapsulating clenbuterol hydrochloride.  
 
It is important to clarify that in the literature, the abbreviation MSPC has been used to refer to 
two different phospholipids, which may lead to confusion. The lipid used in this study is 1-
Myristoyl-2-Stearoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine, an asymmetrical phospholipid consisting 
of a myristoyl (C14:0) and a stearoyl (C18:0) fatty acid chain linked to a glycerol backbone 
with a phosphocholine headgroup. It has a molecular weight of 734.0 g/mol and a phase 
transition temperature (Tm) of approximately 40°C [56], [57]. This differs from 1-Stearoyl-2-
Hydroxy-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine, a lysophospholipid with only one stearoyl (C18:0) 
fatty acid chain, also abbreviated as MSPC in some publications, including those describing the 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
clinically studied ThermoDox® formulation. The lipid used in ThermoDox is not the one used 
in this work, as our MSPC in not a lysophospholipid. 
 
Phospholipid tail asymmetry has shown to increase membrane fluidity and reduce bilayer 
thickness. Studies have demonstrated that asymmetric phospholipids, in which the two fatty 
acid chains differ in length, disrupt the regular packing of the lipid bilayer. This leads to 
increased disorder and fluidity compared to symmetric lipids such as DPPC, which contains 
two identical C16 chains and is commonly used in traditional thermosensitive systems [58], 
[59]. Based on this, we hypothesized that our thermosensitive formulation, using MSPC as the 
main lipid, could potentially promote a faster drug release due to its enhanced membrane 
fluidity and its lower Tm (approximately 40°C), more suitable for a clinical setting.

1.6. Clenbuterol Hydrochloride 
Clenbuterol hydrochloride is a sympathomimetic agent, meaning it stimulates α-adrenergic and 
β-adrenergic receptors, with a preferential activity on β2-adrenergic receptors in smooth 
muscle, classifying it as a selective β2 agonist [61], [62].  
1.6.1. Therapeutic and illicit uses 
In certain countries, clenbuterol is approved for medical use as a bronchodilator in the treatment 
of reversible airway obstruction, such as asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease 
(COPD), due to its ability to relax smooth muscle tissue in the airways and thereby improve 
respiratory function [61]. However, it is not approved for human use in many countries, 
including Denmark, due to its potential side effects, which include cardiac arrhythmias, tremors, 
gastrointestinal disturbances and, in rare cases, cardiac arrest. Beyond its bronchodilatory 
effects, β2-receptor activation by clenbuterol is also associated with hypertrophic, lipolytic, and 
anabolic effects, including increased skeletal muscle mass [63], [64]. Its potential application 
in the treatment of muscle atrophy, which represents a long-term objective of this research, has 
been previously explored with promising results [65]. 
 
Due to its muscle-building and fat-reducing properties, clenbuterol is also misused as a doping 
agent and is classified as a prohibited substance by the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) 
[66]. Although its performance enhancing effects have been demonstrated in animal models, 
there is insufficient scientific evidence supporting similar outcomes in humans [67]. 
Furthermore, clenbuterol has been illegally used in livestock to increase lean body mass and 
reduce fat content, practices that are banned in many countries due to potential toxic effects in 
humans consuming contaminated meat [68].

1.6.2. Structure and physicochemical properties 
The molecular structure of clenbuterol consist of a benzene ring substituted with an amino 
group at the 4 position, two chlorine atoms at the 3 and 5 positions and a and a tert-
butylaminoethanol side chain (see Figure 1.7). The drug is commonly formulated as its 
hydrochloride salt to improve water solubility. 
 
Figure 1.7 Structure of Clenbuterol Hydrochloride [68]. 
 
Among some of its physicochemical properties, it is worth highlighting it has a molecular 
weight of 313.6 g/mol and a pKa of 9.63, classifying it as a weak base [69], [70]. For drug 
loading purposes, its predicted pH dependent lipophilicity was evaluated in this project (see 
Figure 1.8), to assess how the drug distributes between lipophilic and hydrophilic environments 
at different pH values.  
 
The logD profile shows a clear shift in lipophilicity is between pH 7.4 and pH 5.5, which 
correspond to the external and internal pH values of the liposomal formulations used in this 
study. At pH 7.4 clenbuterol shows moderate lipophilicity with logD value of 0.14. At pH 5.5 
the logD drops to -0.85, meaning a high degree of ionization and reduced membrane 
permeability under acidic conditions. This behaviour is favourable for the expected mechanism 
of remote loading with ammonium sulfate gradient previously, where the drug must be 
sufficiently lipophilic at extracellular pH to cross the liposome bilayer and become ionized 
within the acidic core to ensure retention. 
 
In theory, this physicochemical profile makes clenbuterol a suitable candidate for remote 
loading into liposomes using an ammonium sulfate gradient. However, the relatively low logD 
at pH 7.4 indicates that only a limited fraction of neutral species is available to cross the 
liposomal membrane. One of the aims of this project is to confirm whether this theoretical 
suitability translates into effective encapsulation efficiency in practice.

2.2. Liposome formulation 
For stealth liposomes, a lipid mixture of HSPC, DSPE-mPEG2000 and cholesterol (molar ratio 
3:1:1) was hydrated at 70°C for 2 hours in a 250 mM ammonium sulfate solution under constant 
stirring, resulting in a final lipid concentration of 40 mM. For thermosensitive liposomes, 
MSPC and DSPE-mPEG2000 were similarly hydrated at 70°C (above the phase transition 
temperature) for 2 hours in 250 mM ammonium sulfate to a final lipid concentration of 30 mM. 
The resulting multilamellar vesicles were sonicated at 60°C for 30 minutes to ensure complete 
dispersion of the lipid film. 
 
Following hydration, liposomes were extruded to reduce vesicle size. In some batches, hand 
extrusion was performed with 21 passes, though sample loss due to extruder malfunction was 
noted. In later batches, high-pressure extrusion was used to improve consistency and increase 
sample volumes. Extrusion was carried out sequentially through polycarbonate membranes of 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
decreasing pore size (0.8 µm, 0.4 µm, and 0.1 µm; one pass each) at 70°C to achieve the desired 
size. Dynamic light scattering (DLS) was used to measure particle size. All measurements were 
performed in triplicates and results are reported as mean and standard deviation (SD). 
 
To establish the transmembrane gradient for remote loading, unencapsulated ammonium sulfate 
was removed by dialysis at 4°C for 17 hours using 10,000 Da MWCO dialysis cassettes against 
500 mL of 150 mM phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). One buffer exchange was performed 
during dialysis to ensure minimum ammonium sulfate attached to the lipid surface, which could 
interfere with remote loading efficiency. Liposomes were stored at 4 °C until loading. 
2.3. Remote Loading with the ammonium sulfate gradient method 
Remote loading of compounds into preformed liposomes was performed using a 
transmembrane ammonium sulfate gradient. A similar procedure was used for both acridine 
orange and clenbuterol hydrochloride. The appropriate amount of drug was weighed and 
directly added to the preformed liposomes to achieve the desired drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio. The 
mixture was then incubated at 60°C with continuous stirring for 1 hour in the case of acridine 
orange, or for 30 minutes for clenbuterol hydrochloride. Following incubation, samples were 
cooled to room temperature and unencapsulated drug was removed by dialysis at room 
temperature using 10.000 Da MWCO dialysis cassettes against 500 mL of PBS.  Dialysis was 
performed for 16 hours for acridine orange and 6 hours for clenbuterol hydrochloride, with one 
buffer exchange. Loaded liposomes were stored at 4 °C until further use. 
2.4. Liposome characterization using dynamic light scattering (DLS) 
Liposome size was measured using dynamic light scattering (DLS) with a Malvern Nano ZS 
Zetasizer (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK) at room temperature. The instrument itself 
performs the measure in triplicates. Prior to analysis, samples were diluted 1:100 in Milli-Q 
water. The size calculation is based on average particle diameter (Z-average). The 
polydispersity index (PDI) was also determined to assess the uniformity of the liposome size 
distribution. A PDI value close to 0 indicates a homogeneous population, while values greater 
than 0.4 suggest a highly heterogeneous liposome population [76].

Drug release from the thermosensitive liposomes was determined by dialysis at controlled

All liposome batches formulated in this study were physicochemically characterized by average 
particle diameter (Z-average) and polydispersity index (PDI) as described in Section 2.4. The

techniques and because these batches were the most relevant for this study. Reported values

3.2. Lipid loss estimation during manufacturing process based on cholesterol 
measurements 
A central objective of this study was to achieve high encapsulation efficiency while optimizing 
the drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio. This is essential to maximize the amount of drug encapsulated in 
each liposome and minimize the amount of lipid carrier administered. To achieve this, we aimed 
for precise D/L ratios by accurately quantifying lipid content in each formulation, allowing us 
to accordingly calculate the drug amount required for remote loading. 
 
For stealth liposomes, cholesterol concentrations were directly measured using RP-HPLC and 
used as a surrogate marker for total lipid content, which allowed accurate calculation of the 
drug quantity required to achieve the desired D/L ratio. However, this strategy could not be 
applied to the thermosensitive liposomes, as their formulation does not contain cholesterol. 
Furthermore, direct quantification of their main lipid component, 1-myristoyl-2-stearoyl-sn-
glycero-3-phosphocholine (MSPC), was not possible due to the absence of chromophores, 
making it undetectable by our RP-HPLC/UV method. To address this limitation, we estimated 
an average lipid loss indirectly by tracking cholesterol loss in the stealth formulation, under the 
assumption that cholesterol incorporation reflects overall lipid retention. Previous studies have 
shown that during extrusion-based liposome production, the amount of cholesterol that actually 
gets incorporated into the lipid bilayer is often lower than expected, supporting its use as an 
indicator of lipid loss [81]. The resulting lipid loss profile for the stealth formulation was 
assumed to be the same for the thermosensitive system.

3.3. Comparison of NanoDrop and HPLC as quantification techniques  
In the early stages of this project, NanoDrop spectrophotometry was evaluated as a potential 
alternative to reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) for 
quantifying small molecule drugs encapsulated in stealth liposomes. Acridine orange (AO) was 
selected as a model compound due to its strong UV absorbance at 490 nm. While NanoDrop is 
primarily used for nucleic acid and protein quantification, it has also been explored for 
measuring certain small molecules [84], [85]. The method offers advantages such as rapid data 
acquisition, minimal sample volume requirements (1–3 µL) and operational simplicity, which 
makes it ideal for rapid preliminary assessment.  
 
To evaluate its potential utility, stealth liposome samples containing AO were analyzed using 
both NanoDrop and HPLC at 490 nm, following dilution in a 50:50 (v/v) acetonitrile:Milli-Q 
water solution containing 0.1% formic acid (pH 4.5) to disrupt the lipid bilayer. Dilution factors 
ranging from 11x to 1100x were tested to see how the sample composition and concentration 
affect the accuracy of the measurements. Table 3.2 presents the results. At high dilution levels 
(1100x, 550x, 220x), NanoDrop significantly overestimated the AO concentration compared to 
HPLC, with percent differences over 100%. This reflects the inability of this method to 
distinguish individual components within the sample, specially in complex lipid based systems, 
leading to the absorbance of other substances being mistakenly attributed to AO. At a moderate 
dilution (110x), results from the two methods were more comparable, showing only a 14.45% 
difference. However, at the lowest dilution (11x), NanoDrop underestimated the AO 
concentration. This could possibly be due to light scattering or absorption interference from 
intact or partially disrupted liposomes. 
 
These findings are consistent with previous reports that highlight the limitations of 
spectrophotometric techniques such as CD spectroscopy when applied to complex mixtures like 
nanocarriers, as the observed absorption may be impacted by the light scattering of the 
nanocapsules and other substances present in the sample [86]. In our case, despite efforts to 
disrupt the liposomal membrane with organic solvent, incomplete disruption or residual lipids 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
may have contributed to inaccurate absorbance readings. In contrast, HPLC offers key 
advantages in specificity and resolution, allowing separation of the active molecule from 
excipients, lipids and degradation products. Although NanoDrop may still have utility for 
preliminary screening under well-controlled conditions, we conclude it is not suitable for 
reliable quantification of small molecule drugs in liposomal formulations. Based on these 
findings, we chose not to pursue NanoDrop for further quantification experiments in this 
project.

3.4. Remote loading in different liposomal formulations 
The primary objective of this project was to evaluate whether clenbuterol hydrochloride could 
be effectively encapsulated into liposomes using the ammonium sulfate gradient remote loading 
technique, as described in Section 2.3. This method was selected due to its well-established 
mechanism and proven efficacy in other liposomal systems, including Doxil®, where 
encapsulation efficiencies over 90% have been reported [18], [87]. Based on the predicted logD 
profile for clenbuterol hydrochloride and the calculated logP and pKa values (see section 1.6), 
the molecule was in theory considered a good candidate for efficient remote loading and 
retention inside the liposome core. 
 
Remote loading was tested using two liposomal formulations: stealth liposomes and 
thermosensitive liposomes (see Table 1.3), at a 1:4 drug-to-lipid (D/L) molar ratio. The loading 
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
procedure was carried out at 60°C for 30 minutes. Drug amounts needed were calculated 
assuming a 31% lipid loss, as detailed in Section 3.2. The thermosensitive formulation, 
composed of MSPC:DSPE-mPEG2000 in a 95:5 molar ratio, was designed to release its content 
at approximately 40°C, which is the phase transition temperature (Tm) of MSPC, its main 
component. Stealth liposomes (HSPC:DSPE-mPEG2000:Cholesterol in a 3:1:1 molar ratio) 
were used as a positive control, not because remote loading was guaranteed to succeed in this 
formulation, but because it represents a simpler and well-established liposomal system. This 
formulation was particularly suitable as a reference due to its similar composition to Doxil® 
(HSPC:Cholesterol:DSPE-mPEG2000 in a 56:39:5 molar ratio) [18].  
 
The reason to have a control was due to the elevated temperature used during loading. At 60°C, 
membrane permeability is increased, facilitating drug encapsulation. In the thermosensitive 
formulation, this temperature is above the Tm (approximately 40ºC), which may destabilize the 
membrane and lead to drug premature leakage, especially given the absence of cholesterol in 
the lipid bilayer. In contrast, the stealth formulation includes cholesterol and has a higher Tm 
(approximately 53°C) [88], which stabilizes the bilayer and helps prevent premature drug 
release. For this reason, the stealth formulation was used to ensure that any drug leakage 
observed in the thermosensitive system was due to membrane destabilization and not a failure 
in the loading method.

Figure 3.3 shows results of encapsulation efficiencies (%) for the two types of liposomal 
formulations (stealth and thermosensitive) at a 1:4 drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio. Both formulations 
show moderate encapsulation efficiency with minor differences, 63 ± 6.8% for stealth 
liposomes and 55 ± 4.4% for thermosensitive liposomes. The slightly lower encapsulation 
efficiency observed in the thermosensitive formulation compared to the stealth formulation may 
be due to the presence of cholesterol in the lipid bilayer. Cholesterol is known to enhance 
membrane rigidity and reduce permeability, helping to prevent drug leakage during the loading 
process. This could explain the difference between 63% for stealth and 55% for thermosensitive 
liposomes. In addition to stabilizing the membrane, it has previously been discussed the effect 
of cholesterol improving loading could be explained because it makes the lipid bilayer more 
hydrophobic so that hydrophilic drugs encapsulated in the liposome core have a harder time 
leaking out [76]. However, following the example of Thermodox®, we decide not to include 
cholesterol into our thermosensitive system formulation to ensure a sharp phase transition 
temperature [26]. In conclusion, these results confirm that clenbuterol hydrochloride can be 
successfully remote loaded into both stealth and thermosensitive liposomes using the 
ammonium sulfate gradient technique. 
3.5. Optimization of remote loading parameters 
Following confirmation of successful remote loading of clenbuterol hydrochloride into 
liposomes using the ammonium sulfate gradient method, the next objective was to optimize 
loading conditions to maximize encapsulation efficiency. Initial loading experiments (Section 
3.4) were conducted at 60 °C for 30 minutes using a 1:4 drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio, based on 
previously reported conditions for similar liposomal systems [15], [89]. 
 
For the optimization of our remote loading protocol, three different parameters were evaluated: 
drug-to-lipid ratio, loading temperature and loading incubation time. These optimization 
experiments were performed loading clenbuterol hydrochloride into stealth liposomes at 
different conditions, because as previously discussed we considered it a standard for its similar 
composition to Doxil® [18]. Four stealth liposome batches were prepared under different 
combinations of these parameters (see Table 3.3).

To evaluate each parameter individually, initial conditions (D/L ratio 1:4, 60 °C, 30 min) were 
used as the reference, and encapsulation efficiencies under alternative conditions were 
compared accordingly (Figure 3.4).  
 
As shown in Figure 3.4A, encapsulation efficiency increased significantly with a lower D/L 
ratio. Specifically, a D/L ratio of 1:3 resulted in 64.6 ± 1.1%, while a ratio of 1:5 resulted in 
93.0 ± 9.7%. Previous results (Section 3.4) indicated encapsulation efficiency of 63.0 ± 6.8% 
for D/L ratio of 1:4.  These results suggest that decreasing the D/L ratio from 1:3 or 1:4 to 1:5 
significantly improves drug loading, while further decreases offer no additional benefit and may 
introduce complications due to excessive lipid content. This trend reflects the principle that 
lower D/L ratios, with more lipid relative to drug, result in higher encapsulation efficiency. This 
is likely due to the increased availability of internal liposomal volume per drug molecule, which 
prevents saturation and allows most of the drug to be encapsulated. However, at very low D/L 
ratios, while overall encapsulation efficiency remains high, the drug load per individual 
liposome may be insufficient to achieve therapeutic efficacy, potentially limiting potency on a 
per-particle basis [90]. At a 1:4 D/L ratio, the number of liposomes is more limited relative to 
the amount of drug when compared to 1:5 D/L ratio, making saturation more likely. Once 
saturation occurs, the remaining drug stays unencapsulated, reducing overall efficiency [90]. 
On the other hand, the drug content per liposome is likely higher at higher D/L ratios, which 
may improve therapeutic potency. From a clinical perspective, minimizing the total amount of 
lipid and excipients is beneficial to reduce the risk of toxicity and immunogenicity and improve 
drug release [91]. Therefore, the 1:5 D/L ratio was selected as the optimal ratio for effective 
loading while minimizing lipid content as much as possible. This finding is consistent with 
previous work by Mao et al., who also reported superior performance at a 1:5 D/L ratio [92]. 
Additionally, moderate instead of low D/L ratios are often preferred to preserve membrane 
stability and controlled release properties [91], [93]. Based on these findings, a D/L ratio of 1:5 
was selected for future experiments. 
==PAGE:39==

To optimize loading temperature, encapsulation was compared at 60 °C and 45 °C (Figure 
3.4B), both during 30 minutes at a 1:4 D/L ratio. At 60 °C, encapsulation efficiency was 
63.3 ± 6.8%, while loading at 45 °C increased it to 80.2 ± 2.4%. This improvement suggests that 
excessive temperatures may compromise membrane integrity or the transmembrane proton 
gradient, reducing loading efficiency. This is interesting for the following experiments, since 
our hypothesis was that a loading temperature closer to the Tₘ of our thermosensitive system 
would be more effective, as the lipid bilayer is most permeable to the drug at its phase transition 
temperature. This hypothesis is supported by Bhatia et al., who reported optimal encapsulation 
when liposomes were prepared at or near the phase transition temperature of the lipid bilayer 
[94]. During the literature review, we also noted that studies involving thermosensitive systems 
commonly used lower remote loading temperatures[95], [96]. Consequently, 45 °C was 
considered the more favourable condition. 
 
Figure 3.4C represents the effect of loading incubation time at 30 minutes and 1 hour, both at 
60ºC and a 1:4 D/L ratio. Increasing the loading time from 30 minutes to 1 hour resulted in 
encapsulation improvement, from 63.3 ± 6.8% to 70.5 ± 3.1%. This could suggest that sufficient 
time is needed to achieve equilibrium loading of the drug. Other studies have shown that lower 
loading temperatures may require longer incubation periods to reach maximum efficiency [97]. 
Therefore, extending the incubation time seems to be beneficial, especially when using lower 
temperatures such as 45 °C. However, prolonged exposure to heat may risk drug leakage or 
compromise liposome stability. This led us to believe that although longer times were not tested 
here, incubation beyond 1 hour may not provide additional benefits and could potentially be 
negative.

To evaluate the effectiveness of the optimized remote loading protocol, a final loading 
experiment was performed using a D/L ratio of 1:5, a temperature of 45 °C and an incubation 
time of 1 hour. This experiment was carried out on both stealth and thermosensitive liposomes 
for comparison. As shown in Figure 3.5, stealth liposomes achieved a significantly higher 
encapsulation efficiency (72.0 ± 4.4%) compared to thermosensitive liposomes (54.2 ± 2.3%).   
The fact that the improvements observed under these optimized conditions did not translate to 
the thermosensitive formulation highlights the superior loading capacity of stealth liposomes. 
This difference is likely due to variations in membrane composition, which influence bilayer 
permeability, stability and the maintenance of the proton gradient during remote loading. 
 
When comparing this thermosensitive batch to the one prepared under previous conditions (D/L 
ratio 1:4, 60 °C, 30 minutes; encapsulation efficiency 55.0 ± 4.4%), no significant improvement 
was observed. Therefore, it can be assumed that an encapsulation efficiency of approximately 
55% may represent the upper limit for our thermosensitive liposomal system under all the 
conditions tested.

3.6. Drug stability test 
A preliminary study was performed to assess the chemical stability of clenbuterol hydrochloride 
in liposomal formulations. Two types of liposomes were tested: thermosensitive (MSPC:DSPE-
mPEG2000) and stealth (HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/Cholesterol), both prepared at a drug-to-
lipid (D/L) ratio of 1:4. Samples were analyzed by HPLC right after drug loading and again 
after approximately three weeks of storage at 4°C. Refrigerated storage at 4°C is considered a 
standard condition for maintaining liposomal stability. 
 
The results, summarized in Table 3.4, indicate that clenbuterol concentrations remained 
relatively stable in both formulations. In the MSPC:DSPE-mPEG2000 formulation, the drug 
concentration increased slightly from 0.656 mg/mL to 0.664 mg/mL. In the stealth formulation, 
the concentration decreased from 1.228 mg/mL to 1.156 mg/mL. The percentage of initial drug 
content retained was 101.2% for the thermosensitive liposomes and 94.2% for the stealth 
liposomes, corresponding to fluctuations of +1.2% and −5.8%, respectively. These variations 
fall within the expected range of experimental variability and do not indicate significant drug 
degradation.

3.7. Temperature triggered release in vitro 
An in vitro drug release study was conducted to evaluate the temperature-dependent release 
profile of clenbuterol hydrochloride from the developed thermosensitive liposomes. The 
experiment was carried out at two temperatures: 40°C, corresponding to the phase transition 
temperature (Tm) of the liposomal formulation, and 37°C serving as a physiological control. 
The procedure followed the methodology described in Section 2.8, using the final batch of 
thermosensitive liposomes produced under optimized conditions (D/L ratio of 1:5, loading 
temperature of 45°C and incubation time of 1 hour). 
 
As shown in Figure 3.6, at 40°C, the drug release was rapid and significantly higher, reaching 
over 100% cumulative release within the first 6 hours (360 minutes), indicating complete drug 
release from the liposomal system. In contrast, at 37°C, drug release was significantly slower, 
more sustained and incomplete, reaching only 79.7 ± 7.2% after 20 hours (1200 minutes). A 
closer examination of early time points at 2, 4 and 6 hours, reveals that the cumulative release 
at 40°C was consistently about two times greater than that observed at 37°C. This behaviour 
confirms the thermosensitive nature of the liposomal formulation and highlights its potential 
for triggered release under mild hyperthermic conditions. The increased release at 40°C can be 
attributed to the increased membrane permeability due to the phase transition of the lipid 
bilayer, which facilitates rapid drug diffusion. Meanwhile, at 37°C, the liposomes remain in a 
more rigid, limiting drug leakage and allowing a more sustained release profile. These findings 
align with release profiles reported from Mao et al. [92], and support the potential utility of this 
delivery system for targeted drug release upon mild hyperthermia. 
 
Although the thermo responsive behaviour of the system was clearly demonstrated, the release 
rate was slower than what has been reported in some previous studies. Rahim et al. observed 
100% release within 1 to 2 hours using a lysolipid-containing thermosensitive liposome [77].

could be explained by the additional packing defects introduced by the lysolipid in the lipid 
bilayer. Needham et al. [99] also reported this difference between lysolipid-containing systems 
(LTSL) and traditional thermosensitive liposomes (TTSL). Their results are more consistent 
with our findings, as they observed 40% release within 30 minutes, while our formulation 
released approximately 30% at the same time point. 
 
Another possible explanation for the slower release is the assumption that the Tm of our system 
is exactly 40°C. This value was based on literature reports for MSPC, but no experimental 
verification was performed. Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) could be used in future 
work to accurately determine the Tm of our formulation, as is standard in many studies [95], 
[100]. 
 
In addition, the choice of release medium may have influenced the results. While we used 
phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), other studies often use media that more closely mimic 
physiological conditions. Burke et al. [101] demonstrated that the buffer system significantly 
impacts release kinetics, showing that the fastest release occurred in human plasma and the 
slowest in PBS. Therefore, the use of PBS in our study may have contributed to the relatively 
slower drug release observed.

3.8. HPLC accuracy test 
This experiment was carried out by my colleague, Tómas Frostason, who kindly provided the 
resulting data. 
 
During the month of May, the HPLC instrument began to show signs of malfunction, which 
was reflected in inconsistent and unreliable results. To verify whether the observed inaccuracies 
were due to equipment malfunction rather than experimental error (such as incorrect dilution 
preparation) an accuracy test was performed. The test involved repeatedly analyzing samples 
of known concentrations to assess the precision and reliability of the instrument. Two samples 
were used for this purpose, with known concentrations of 1.0 mg/mL and 0.5 mg/mL. The 
results are summarized in Table 3.5. In both cases, the measured concentrations were much 
lower than expected, with average values of 7.58 µg/mL and 11.51 µg/mL, respectively. The 
relative standard deviations (RSD) were extremely high, at 107.57% and 141.26%, indicating 
serious inconsistency in the measurements. The fact that the lower concentration sample (0.5 
mg/mL) resulted in a higher measured concentration than the higher concentration sample, 
further supports our hypothesis of a malfunctioning autosampler. This could be due to the 
autosampler not taking enough volume or doing so inconsistently across replicates. Inconsistent 
injection volumes directly affect AUC measurements and calculated concentrations. This 
equipment malfunction may have affected results from the in vitro drug release experiment (see 
section 3.7).

4. Conclusions 
Although the thermosensitive liposomal formulation investigated in this study has not been 
extensively characterized to date, our findings demonstrate its potential as a novel strategy for 
temperature-triggered drug release. The system is composed of 1-Myristoyl-2-Stearoyl-sn-
Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (MSPC) and DSPE-mPEG2000 in a 95:5 molar ratio. It was 
specifically designed to enable temperature-sensitive release, based on the low phase transition 
temperature of MSPC (40 °C) along with the influence of phospholipid tail asymmetry on 
membrane fluidity. 
 
A 
comparative 
analysis 
with 
a 
stealth 
liposome 
formulation 
(HSPC:DSPE-
mPEG2000:Cholesterol in a 3:1:1 molar ratio) served as a critical control, helping to identify 
key limitations in the thermosensitive system and areas for further optimization. Both 
formulations exhibited particle sizes below 200 nm and polydispersity index (PDI) values under 
0.3, which fall within the acceptable range for extruded liposomal drug delivery systems. 
Regarding drug loading, while we were able to improve encapsulation efficiency in the stealth 
formulation, these improvements did not translate effectively to the thermosensitive system. 
This may be due to differences in bilayer composition and inaccuracies in drug-to-lipid (D/L) 
ratio calculations. As direct measurement of lipid content was not possible for the 
thermosensitive formulation, lipid loss was estimated based on the cholesterol loss data on 
stealth liposomes. This estimation could have introduced errors in the calculated D/L ratios for 
the thermosensitive system, potentially affecting encapsulation accuracy.  
 
Despite limitations in the in vitro drug release data due to HPLC malfunction, temperature-
triggered release was observed. However, the thermosensitive liposomes showed significant 
drug leakage at 37 °C compared to similar formulations reported in the literature. This suggests 
that further improvements are needed, such as incorporating cholesterol into the lipid bilayer. 
Supporting this, Sadeghi et al. reported that the inclusion of cholesterol in lysolipid-based 
thermosensitive liposomes improved release characteristics and showed reduced leakage at 
physiological temperatures [102]. 
 
Finally, it is important to differentiate the MSPC used in our formulation, 1-Myristoyl-2-
Stearoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine, from the compound with the same abbreviation used 
in ThermoDox®, 1-Stearoyl-2-Hydroxy-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphatidylcholine. The second 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
compound exhibits cytotoxic activity against cancer cells, including leukemia cells [103], 
which makes it effective for drug delivery in cancer therapeutics.  However, it has also been 
reported to cause cytotoxic effects on smooth muscle cells [104]. Therefore, for our intended 
application in the treatment of muscle atrophy, our thermosensitive system presents a clear 
advantage, as it is based on a different compound. This compound, 1-Myristoyl-2-Stearoyl-sn-
Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (also abbreviated as MSPC in some literature), is not cytotoxic.

5. Future perspectives 
With the completion of this project, it is evident that the proposed thermosensitive liposomal 
system has potential for improvement in several key areas. Although the produced liposomes 
met the desired morphological criteria, the differences observed in encapsulation efficiency 
between the two different formulations suggest that the remote loading process can be further 
optimized. For example, Alrbyawi et al. reported very high encapsulation efficiencies and rapid 
drug release profiles using a thermosensitive liposomal formulation composed of 1-Myristoyl-
2-Stearoyl-sn-Glycero-3-Phosphocholine (MSPC), in combination with DPPC, DSPE-
mPEG2000, and cholesterol, to encapsulate daunorubicin (DNR) at a drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio 
of 1:5. These findings suggest that future studies could benefit from exploring other lipid 
compositions, particularly by incorporating different ratios of these components into our 
thermosensitive bilayer [105]. 
 
Additionally, since the stability of our liposomal formulations was not assessed during this 
project, this represents another important area for further research. For long-term applications, 
it would be essential to use preservation techniques such as freeze-drying (lyophilization), a 
well-established method that improves liposome stability and shelf life by removing water to 
prevent degradation during storage [98]. This consideration is especially important given that 
other thermosensitive formulations, such as Thermodox®, have shown to require strict storage 
conditions [77]. 
 
This project was carried out with the long-term goal of progressing toward in vivo testing. As 
a preliminary concept for experimental design, animal testing could be conducted in mice via 
intravenous administration (e.g. tail-vein injection) combined with localized mild 
hyperthermia, possibly using laser-mediated heating. In our most successful batch, the 
thermosensitive liposomes achieved an encapsulation efficiency of approximately 55%, 
corresponding to a clenbuterol hydrochloride concentration of 0.629 mg/mL, with a total lipid 
concentration of around 21 mM. Assuming administration of a 200 µL dose to a 20 gram mouse 
[106], this would result in a clenbuterol dosage of approximately 6.29 mg/kg. This is 
considerably higher than doses reported in previous studies. For example, Pellegrino et al. used 
1.5 mg/kg/day via oral administration in mice [65], and Burniston et al. reported that 
subcutaneous administration of a lower dose of 10 μg/kg/day induced muscle growth without 
myotoxic effects in rats [107].  
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
Therefore, keeping in mind that IV administration should be more potent due to direct 
bloodstream delivery, this encapsulation efficiency appears sufficient for achieving effective 
dosing in future animal experiments. However, dilution of the thermosensitive liposome 
solution prior to administration should be performed to avoid potential toxicity.
