ABSTRACT 
A semi-outdoor space is an additional transparent shield or envelope, which can connect different buildings 
as well as cover one or more buildings. Through using simulation programs like IDA ICE and DELPHIN, the 
study investigated what conditions that affected the air humidity in the semi-outdoor space and if the semi-
outdoor space can be used as a renovation option for existing buildings.  
 
An initial analysis with two different families was made, to make a detailed study of the relative humidity in 
the semi-outdoor space, and of the indoor climate and energy use of the inner house. Furthermore, it was 
investigated if the indoor climate influenced the humidity in the semi-outdoor space, by having a family, the 
bad user, which was not very good at opening their windows in the inner house and a family, the good user, 
which was good at opening their windows. These two families were the base case throughout the report.  
 
To investigate what conditions that affect the humidity in the semi-outdoor space, different analyses were 
made. The humidity analysis investigated the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space against the 
requirement set for the semi-outdoor space in this report, which was between 20%-70% relative humidity. 
As well as an evaluation of the risk of mold growth in the semi-outdoor space. Furthermore, a condensation 
analysis was made to calculate how many hours of condensation on the windows of the semi-outdoor space 
that occurred. These analyses were made for different conditions. The conditions investigated was the 
orientation of the semi-outdoor space and different moisture sources added to the semi-outdoor space, like 
drying clothes, cooking, having a jacuzzi or having plants. Furthermore, the difference between having one-
layer glass and polycarbonate plates as windows for the semi-outdoor space was also investigated as a 
condition.  
 
The analyses made to investigate whether the semi-outdoor space can be used as a renovation option were 
a study of the moisture balance in the outer wall construction of the inner house and a mold growth 
evaluation on the façade of the inner house. The wall constructions investigated were wall constructions 
before and after the energy crisis and light and heavy outer wall constructions.  
 
The results showed that the indoor climate of the inner house had no significant influence on the relative 
humidity of the semi-outdoor space and when implementing a semi-outdoor space, the energy use for the 
inner house decreased with 33%-56% for the bad and the good user, respectively. Furthermore, there were 
no problems with low relative humidity, however all the cases had hours in the range 70%-100% relative 
humidity, which was above the requirements set for the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space. This 
led to risk of mold growth for all the cases, however when implementing the semi-outdoor space, the mold 
index decreased to nearly no mold growth on the façade of the inner house, because the semi-outdoor 
space functions as a climate screen. Additionally, the condensation in the wall constructions before and after 
the energy crisis was removed, when the semi-outdoor space was implemented.  
 
Condensation on the windows of the semi-outdoor space only occurred during the night and mostly in the 
colder months. The condensation occurred for both transparent materials, however there were fewer hours 
for the polycarbonate plates, because of the lower U-value.  
 
All of the moisture sources had an effect on the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space and increased 
the risk of mold growth. Therefore, it is important to install a control opening system on some of the 
windows in order to force a natural ventilation in the semi-outdoor space, because higher airflow decrease 
the high relative humidity values. Furthermore, all wall constructions investigated can be used for the inner 
house, when implementing a semi-outdoor space, which concludes that the semi-outdoor space can be used 
as a renovation option without causing condensation in the wall construction.

RESUMÉ 
Et tredje rum er en ekstra transparent konstruktion som kan samle to bygninger, eller dække en eller flere 
bygninger. Igennem brug af simuleringsprogrammerne IDA ICE og DELPHIN har dette projekt lavet en 
undersøgelse af, hvilke forhold der påvirker den relative luftfugtighed i et tredje rum, og om det tredje rum 
kan bruges som en renoveringsmulighed for eksisterende bygninger.  
 
En indledende analyse af to forskellige familier var lavet for at lave en detaljeret undersøgelse af den relative 
luftfugtighed i det tredje rum, og af indeklimaet og energiforbruget for det indre hus. Derudover blev der 
også lavet en undersøgelse af om indeklimaet fra den indre bygning, havde en påvirkning på luftfugtigheden 
i det tredje rum, ved at have en familie, den dårlige bruger, som ikke var særlig god til at åbne deres vinduer 
i det indre hus, og en anden familie, den gode bruger, som er bedre til at åbne deres vinduer. Disse to 
familier blev brugt som standardcasen igennem de resterende analyser. 
 
For at undersøge, hvilke forhold der påvirker luftfugtigheden i det tredje rum, er forskellige analyser blevet 
lavet. Luftfugtighedsanalysen undersøger den relative luftfugtighed i det tredje rum op imod de krav, som er 
sat for det tredje rum i denne rapport, hvilket er mellem 20%-70% relative luftfugtighed. Samtidig med er en 
evaluering af risikoen for skimmelvækst i det tredje rum lavet. Derudover er der lavet en kondensanalyse, 
som udregner hvor mange timers kondens på ruderne i det tredje rum, der opstår. Disse analyser var lavet 
for forskellige forhold. De forhold som blev undersøgt, var orienteringer af det tredje rum og forskellige 
fugtkilder tilføjet til det tredje rum, så som at tørre tøj, lave mad, have en jacuzzi eller planter. Derudover 
blev forskellen mellem et-lags glas og polykarbonat plader, som ruderne i det tredje rum, undersøgt som et 
forhold.  
 
Analyserne som blev lavet for at undersøge om det tredje rum kan bruges som en renoveringsmulighed, var 
et studie af fugtbalancen i en ydervægskonstruktion for det indre hus, og en skimmelvækst evaluering på 
facaden af det indre hus. Vægkonstruktionerne som blev undersøgt, var vægkonstruktioner før og efter 
energikrisen, og lette og tunge ydervægskonstruktioner.  
 
Resultaterne viste, at indeklimaet fra det indre hus ikke havde nogen påvirkning på luftfugtigheden i det 
tredje rum, og ved at implementere et tredje rum reduceres energiforbruget for det indre hus med mellem 
33%-56%, for henholdsvis den dårlige and gode bruger. Derudover var der ingen problemer med for lav 
relative luftfugtighed, men alle casene havde allesammen timer i intervallet 70%-100% relativ luftfugtighed, 
som var over kravene sat for den relative luftfugtighed i det tredje rum. Dette førte til risiko for 
skimmelvækst for alle casene, men ved at implementere det tredje rum faldt mug indekset til stort set ingen 
skimmelvækst på facaden af det indre hus, fordi det tredje rum fungerer som en klimaskærm. Samtidig med 
fjernes den kondens dannelse som opstod i vægkonstruktionerne før og efter energikrisen, når det tredje 
rum blev implementeret.  
 
Kondensen på ruderne af det tredje rum opstod om natten og mest i det kolde måneder. Kondensdannelsen 
opstod for begge typer transparent materiale, men der var færre timer med kondens for polykarbonat 
pladerne, fordi de havde en lavere U-værdi. 
 
Alle casene med fugtkilderne tilføjet til det tredje rum havde en påvirkning på den relative luftfugtighed i det 
tredje rum, og risikoen for skimmelvækst steg. Derfor er det vigtigt at installere et åbningssystem på nogle af 
vinduerne i det tredje rum for at skabe en naturlig ventilation inde i det tredje rum, da højere lufthastighed 
nedsætter de høj relative luftfugtighedsværdier. Derudover kan alle vægkonstruktioner undersøgt, blive 
brugt til det indre hus, når et tredje rum er implementeret, hvilket fører til en konklusion på, at det tredje 
rum kan bruges som en renoveringsmulighed, uden at skabe kondens i vægkonstruktionerne.

1 INTRODUCTION 
In recent years, it has become a worldwide goal to minimize the use of fossil fuels. This can be done by 
finding energy-saving and sustainable solutions, as well as through conversion into renewable energy 
sources. In the construction sector, it is important that, in the attempt to achieve this goal, no compromise is 
made with either indoor climate or architectural terms.  
 
In Denmark the energy used in buildings accounts for around 40% of the total energy consumption, which 
means that there is a potential for a reduction of CO2 emissions through new solutions. Therefore, stricter 
requirements to the energy use in buildings have been introduced in recent years. To comply with these 
requirements, it is not only important to have more effective heating systems or installing solar cells, but 
also to aim for passive solutions. These could for example be natural ventilation, orientation and shape of 
the house, or design of daylight and shading systems. (Frost 2012) 
 
Another passive solution could be a semi-outdoor space. A semi-outdoor space is an additional transparent 
shield or envelope, which can connect different buildings as well as cover one or more buildings. The semi-
outdoor space can be made of glass or other transparent materials and can provide shelter to withstand 
rain, wind and sludge. The shield can create a comfortable environment by storing solar radiation and can 
lead to reduced heating load of the inner house during cold periods. The comfortable environment the 
shield provides can extend the useful area of buildings and can increase the creativity in the building sector.  
 
In recent years several studies about semi-outdoor spaces have been carried out, where the thermal 
comfort and energy performance has been in focus (Papachristou et al. (2016), Winther Jørgensen & 
Leenders Møller (2015), Skammelsen Tranekjær (2018), Andersen & Sauerberg (2016), Lin & Zmeureanu 
(2006), Lin & Zmeureanu (2008) and Søgaard Møller (2014)). However, a detailed study of the humidity in a 
semi-outdoor space has not been made. This project will therefore investigate what conditions that affect 
the air humidity in a semi-outdoor space.  
 
Furthermore, there is a potential for a CO2 reduction through energy efficient retrofit of buildings. Energy 
efficiency in buildings has become one of the major issues in building science and the building industry as a 
consequence of the worldwide energy crisis in 1970s. Substantial savings in heating energy suddenly 
appeared as one of the key factors in the necessary optimization of energy consumption in the society. The 
building standards were updated, and this changed the classical concepts of building construction in a rather 
significant way because a variety of new problems appeared which were rarely experienced before and 
required new physical and technological solutions. (Toman et al. 2009) For example condensation in the 
construction and mold growth on the façade.  
 
Additional or retrofitted thermal insulation system of existing buildings appeared as the most 
straightforward way to achieve almost immediate improvements in heating energy consumption. (Toman et 
al. 2009) The external insulation of the façade is one of the most useful insulation systems and even though 
it is one of the safest and most effective kinds of insulation, it is often not feasible for historic buildings. 
Because an alteration of the façade is not an option and only internal insulation is possible. Internal 
insulation has, however in several cases led to mold problems due to a higher water content as it reduces 
the heat flow from inside to the outside of the external wall thereby creating a steeper temperature 
gradient. (Finken et al. 2016) The semi-outdoor space can therefore maybe be an alternative solution to 
retrofit of buildings in a way where the façade is untouched, and mold problems avoided. This project will 
therefore also conduct an investigation on the semi-outdoor space as a renovation option for existing 
buildings, without creating condensation in the wall construction and increasing the risk of mold growth on 
the façade of the inner building.

2 BACKGROUND 
The following contains a description of the semi-outdoor space and a review of earlier projects evaluating 
semi-outdoor spaces. Sections about fabricated houses and the changes that happened in the houses after 
the energy crisis as well as a description of the only company in Denmark making semi-outdoor spaces are 
also included.   
2.1 WHAT IS A SEMI-OUTDOOR SPACE? 
Different definitions of the semi-outdoor space have been suggested, according to how much the room is 
affected by the ambient climate. Figure 1 illustrates the different types of semi-outdoor spaces, which Chun 
et al. (2004) have defined. Type 1 is a transitional space contained within a building, such as a hotel lobby or 
entry atrium, where conditions are constantly mixed as people move in and out of the building. Type 2 is 
categorized by an attached, covered space connected to the building or between buildings, where outdoor 
conditions predominate, such as a balcony, porch, corridor, covered street or arcade. Type 3 transitional 
space is not attached to a building and is essentially an outdoor room, entirely influenced by how the design 
of the structure modifies the outdoor climate, such as bus stations or pavilions. (Chun et al. 2004) 
 
Figure 1 Three types of transitional spaces (Chun et al. 2004) 
However, there is also the kind of semi-outdoor space which covers one or more buildings, creating a space 
between the shield and the building which the occupants can use. 
2.2 EARLIER STUDIES 
The concept of the semi-outdoor space is not a new concept and was first thought of in the 1960s by 
Buckminster Fuller. (Budds 2016) Afterwards the construction of the semi-outdoor space was in focus and 
only in the recent years has a focus been on the thermal comfort in the semi-outdoor spaces, however the 
studies are still limited.  
 
Semi-outdoor spaces covering a building has been investigated in terms of the thermal comfort and the 
energy savings of the inner house by simulation programs like IDA ICE through different studies. Winther 
Jørgensen & Leenders Møller (2015) found an increase in the temperature of 5 °C between the outdoor and 
semi-outdoor space and a decrease in the energy consumption of the inner house. Papachristou et al. (2016) 
found that the semi-outdoor area could provide more than double the comfortable occupancy hours 
compared to outdoors and the semi-outdoor space can be roughly used for 45% of the time out of a year. 
Furthermore, a decrease in the energy demand for the inner house of around 15% was found. Skammelsen 
Tranekjær (2018) found a reduction of the energy consumption for the inner house at around 40% in 
average, and that the semi-outdoor space could provide thermal comfort 23-27% of the time throughout the 
year when a 18-27 °C comfort interval was used. Lin & Zmeureanu (2006) found a reduction of about 19.1% 
of the annual heating load of the inner house when covered by a semi-outdoor space through a 
mathematical model using the heat balance equations.  
 
Other semi-outdoor environments have also been investigated according to the thermal comfort, where 
simulation programs were used. Bouyer et al. (2007) analysed two sports stadia where the thermal index 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
Page | 5 
 
PET was used, and the results showed that wind comfort evaluation alone cannot provide a comprehensive 
assessment of comfort in stadia. (He & Hoyano 2010) analysed a membrane structure where the aim was to 
develop a computational simulation tool for designing a comfortable urban environment. The results 
showed that the proposed simulation method was capable of quantifying spatial distributions of surface and 
air temperature, air velocity and moisture under membrane structures. (Lin & Zmeureanu 2008) analysed a 
semi-outdoor space be using a three dimensional thermal and airflow (3D-TAF) model to predict the impact 
of such dome on the heating load of the protected house.  
 
However other methods to evaluate the thermal comfort in semi-outdoor environments have also been 
used, like questionnaires. Zhang et al. (2018), Spagnolo & de Dear (2003) and Dahlan & Gital (2016) used 
questionnaires to conducted a thermal comfort study of pavilions and other semi-outdoor locations. The 
results showed higher thermal comfort index, OUT SET, of 26.6 °C than the indoor, SET, of 24 °C (Spagnolo & 
de Dear 2003) and that PMV is an acceptable alternate to predict thermal sensation immediately after a 
down step thermal transition for people living in a hot-humid climate country. (Dahlan & Gital 2016)  
 
The semi-outdoor environments and spaces have also made it possible to combine new sustainable energy 
technologies with the design of new buildings. Pagliarini & Rainieri (2011) have investigated evaporate 
cooling on the glass roof of a semi-outdoor space and the results showed that roof evaporative cooling 
coupled with glass sheet high solar radiation absorptivity may offer an attractive way for the control of a 
semi-outdoor environment. Saranti et al. (2015) investigated the combination of experimental shading with 
integrated PVs and analysed if it could achieve better view for the users while performing as energy 
production and reduction machines.  
 
For a more in depth review of the studies regarding the thermal comfort in semi-outdoor spaces and 
environments see (Goshayeshi et al. 2013).

3 PROJECT OBJECTIVE 
The aim of this study was to investigate the conditions affecting the humidity in semi-outdoor spaces and 
the associated risk of mold growth. The study was inspired by the research on the thermal indoor climate in 
buildings with a semi-outdoor space already done, and the lack of a detailed study of the humidity and the 
risk of moisture problems in a semi-outdoor space.  
 
In addition, it has been attempted to find solutions for renovation of homes where the renovation process 
should not spoil the appearance of the façade. Internal insulation can create moisture problems, which can 
cause condensation and long-term mold growth. The project investigates therefore also whether a semi-
outdoor space is a good renovation option for existing and new homes.  
 
The analyses were made with the simulation programs IDA ICE and DELPHIN, and suggestions to prevent or 
solve moisture problems is given.

4 THEORY 
This chapter addresses the history of the requirements for the relative humidity and how to calculate the 
relative humidity. Furthermore, the water activity, natural ventilation, condensation, mold growth and 
moisture balance in outer walls are addressed.  
4.1 RELATIVE HUMIDITY 
There are no requirements to the relative humidity in a semi-outdoor space. Therefore, different standards 
for other building types were investigated to be able to determine these requirements.  
 
ASHRAE standards, where thermal comfort is included, can be tracked back to 1967. (Sreshthaputra 2003) In 
ASHRAE 55 (1981) the comfort range according to the humidity ratio (g/kg) was specified and kept in most of 
the following ASHRAE standards. In ASHRAE 55 (1992) the maximum air relative humidity was set to 60%, 
because this is the limit for where mold and mildew start to grow. In ASHRAE 55 (2004) no lower humidity 
level was recommended. The reason is that the humidity for thermal comfort is negligible. However, as 
stated in the same standard, it might have secondary health implications. For instance, at low relative 
humidity, the increase in evaporation rate may cause skin drying and irritation. (Djamila et al. 2014) In the 
same standard, however an upper humidity ratio was set to 0.012kg/kg, which corresponds to an upper 
relative air humidity level of about 80% at a low dry bulb temperature of about 20 °C. (ASHRAE 55 2004) The 
upper humidity ratio from ASHRAE 55 (2004) was kept in ASHRAE 55 (2010) and again the standard does not 
specify a minimum humidity level. However, the standard does state that other factors may place limits on 
the minimum humidity level, such as dryness of skin and eyes.  
 
In other standards like DS/EN ISO 7730 (2005) it was stated in the informative part of the standard as 
opposed to the mandatory part, that in moderate environment a rise of 10% in relative humidity is 
comparable to an increase in operative temperature by 0.3 °C. This has a modest impact on thermal 
sensation, but the situation might be different for higher activity levels. The same observation was noted 
earlier by Nevins et al. (1966) in the tested temperature range of 18.9 °C and 27.8 °C, even though the 
reason behind the selection of 0.3 °C in the standard is not known. (Djamila et al. 2014)

humidity in occupied spaces where humidification or dehumidification systems are installed, are presented

Other studies have been made about the relative humidity and thermal sensation. For example, Tanabe et 
al. (1987) did not observe in their thermal comfort studies any increase in thermal sensation due to higher 
air relative humidity. Fountain et al. (1999) found few differences in human response to air relative humidity 
exposure between 60% and 90% in the temperature range of 20 °C – 26 °C while sedentary. The few 
differences were presumed to be a result of the increased skin temperature and sweating. McIntyre (1980) 
reported that the difference in air relative humidity at 20% and 70% is undetectable and within the comfort 
zone. Bauman et al. (1996) observed that the humidity affects occupant thermal sensation under increased 
metabolic rate. However, they did not register any considerable effect of higher air relative humidity within 
the temperature range of 15.6 °C and 40.6 °C and air relative humidity of 20% and 90%. (Djamila et al. 2014) 
Toftum et al. (1998) developed a model that predicts the percentage of persons dissatisfied due to humid 
skin as a function of the relative humidity of the skin and applies for sedentary, thermally neutral persons. 
The model predicted that the relative air humidity may be close to 100% without causing much discomfort 
from humid skin among thermally neutral persons performing sedentary work. (Toftum et al. 1998) 
 
There is however as mentioned earlier a limit for the relative humidity in order to avoid mold growth. As a 
rule of thumb mold growth can be avoided if the relative humidity on the surface of the material is kept 
below 70% relative humidity. (Brandt 2013) 
 
Based on the standards and earlier studies the requirements for the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor 
space in this project were set to have a minimum relative humidity of 20% to avoid dryness and irritation of 
skin and eyes, and a maximum relative humidity of 70% to avoid risk of mold growth.

4.2 NATURAL VENTILATION 
Natural ventilation in a building occurs by thermal buoyancy and wind pressure.  
 
Natural ventilation comprises three kinds of ventilation principles. One-sided ventilation, where there only 
are openings in one outer wall. Cross ventilation, where there are openings in two or several outer walls. 
Buoyancy driven ventilation, which are in rooms with high room height and where the openings are located 
in the outer walls and roof. (Jensen 2005) Illustrated in Figure 5. 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
Page | 11 
 
 
Figure 5 The natural ventilation principles. Left: One-sided ventilation. Middle: Cross ventilation. Right: Buoyancy driven ventilation. 
 
The driving forces of natural ventilation are the pressure differences over a building’s openings created by 
temperature differences between room air and outdoor air and wind pressure on facades. During thermal 
buildup, the air exchange occurs due to the difference in density between outdoor air and room air. Density 
differences are mainly due to differences in temperature between outdoor air and room air but may also be 
due to differences in moisture content. The difference in density between outdoor air and room air causes 
pressure differentials that pull air into and out of openings in the building’s climate screen (buoyancy driven 
ventilation). Also, the vertical distance between intake and exhaust openings generates a thermal driven 
pressure and thereby an airflow. The largest airflow through a given space is obtained by placing the 
openings in the facades with the greatest possible vertical distance. (Jensen 2005) 
 
Wind pressure on exterior building surfaces causes pressure differences that can drive outside air into the 
building through openings in the wind side and room air out of openings in the leeward side (cross-
ventilation). This allows for more efficient ventilation and room depths of up to 5 times the room height can 
therefore be allowed. (Jensen 2005) 
 
The two driving forces, thermal buoyancy and wind pressure can perform separately, but usually they occur 
at the same time. The thermal buoyancy will be dominant at low wind speeds, while the wind pressure will 
be dominant at higher wind speeds. (Jensen 2005) 
 
The ventilation principles used in the semi-outdoor space are cross ventilation and buoyancy driven 
ventilation.

4.3 CONDENSATION 
Air contains moisture and the temperature of the air determines how much moisture the air can hold. Warm 
air can contain more moisture than cold air. Condensation occurs when warm, moist air comes in contact 
with either a surface or air that is colder. The warm air is unable to retain the same amount of moisture as it 
did, and the water is released either into the colder air or onto the colder surface, which Figure 7 illustrates. 
When this happens, the air reaches the ‘saturation point’ and the temperature reached at saturation point is 
called the ‘dew point’. At dew point temperature the air has a relative humidity of 100%. (Brinsmead 2017) If 
condensation occurs the risk of mold growth increases significantly.  
 
 
Figure 7 The concept of condensation. 
There are several types of condensation and some of these are, cold-bridge condensation, warm-front 
condensation and interstitial condensation. (Brinsmead 2017) In a semi-outdoor space it is the cold-bridge 
condensation, which is a risk. Cold-bridge condensation occurs when warm, moisture heavy air comes into 
contact with surfaces at or below its dew point. This can occur at the base of external walls, on windows, 
where it may cause cills to rot and on the underside of the roof. (Brinsmead 2017)

4.4 MOLD GROWTH 
Both extrinsic and intrinsic factors, as defined by Blackburn (2000), influence mold growth. The extrinsic 
factors are related with the climate, like air temperature and relative humidity, while the intrinsic factors 
depend on the characteristics of the building materials. (Fantucci et al. 2017)  
 
Dampness and molds can occur in a variety of different scenarios. For example, in the existing building stock, 
poorly insulated envelopes lead to low internal surface temperatures. In new constructions, highly air tight 
envelopes, together with insufficient ventilation, cause high internal relative humidity. (Fantucci et al. 2017) 
 
Most fungus species have optimal growth at 25-30 °C. At temperatures outside this range, growth decreases, 
and it will normally completely cease over approximately 50 °C and below 0 °C. Mold growth occurs over 
time, and mold levels and growth depend on how long the growth conditions are present. In practice, mold 
growth occurs often in short periods of high relative humidity, replaced by periods when growth conditions 
are not met. (Brandt 2013) 
 
One of the ways to investigate if there is a risk of mold growth on a surface is by using the VTT model that 
was developed at the Technical Research Centre (VTT) in Finland. (Viitanen et al. 2008)

WATER ACTIVITY 
Water activity describes the energy status or escaping tendency of the water in a material sample. It 
indicates how tightly water is bound, structurally or chemically in products. Both the water content and the 
water activity of a sample must be specified to fully describe its water status. (Decagon 2006) 
 
Water activity’s usefulness as a quality and safety measurement was suggested when it became evident that 
moisture content could not adequately account for microbial growth fluctuations. The water activity (aw) 
concept has served microbiologists and food technologists for decades and is the most commonly used 
criterion for safety and quality. (Decagon 2006) 
 
Water activity is the ratio of the vapor pressure of water in a material (p) to the vapor pressure of pure 
water (p0) at the same temperature. Relative humidity of air is the ratio of the vapor pressure of air to its 
saturation vapor pressure. When vapor and temperature equilibrium are obtained, the water activity of the 
sample is equal to the relative humidity of air surrounding the sample in a sealed measurement chamber. 
Multiplication of water activity by 100 gives the equilibrium relative humidity (ERH) in percent.

Microorganisms have a limiting water activity level below which they will not grow. Water activity, not

microorganisms is 0.6aw. (Decagon 2006) Several reports on mold growth in buildings and building materials

growth occurs. (Campbell et al. 2007) 
 
Water activity is easily and accurately measured using benchtop instrumentation, but in situ measurements 
are more difficult. (Decagon 2006) 
4.5 MOISTURE BALANCE IN OUTER WALLS 
Moisture can be transported in vapor and in liquid form. Of importance to moisture transport is moisture 
and temperature conditions in the material and building surroundings like the moisture bond to the material 
and the structure of the material. In vapor form, the moisture transport can occur by diffusion and 
convection and in liquid through capillary suction. In some cases, external forces like wind and gravity have a 
significance. (Lundgreen 2010) 
 
Diffusion is water transport through the surfaces of the structure due to vapor pressure differences. Inside 
buildings, there is usually greater moisture content in the air than outdoors. A higher vapor pressure indoors 
is therefore expected compared to outdoors. The vapor pressure difference will cause the moisture to 
diffuse out through the construction, in order to equalize the vapor pressure on both sides of the 
construction. This happens even when the air stands still, and it also happens for the air inside a material. 
Building materials have different ability to prevent vapor diffusion, but many common used building 
materials like wood and gypsum, have high vapor diffusion. A risk of condensation on the inside of a 
diffusion tight cladding can therefore occur. On the other hand, if a damp-proof course is placed, on the 
inside of the wall, the moisture is prevented from penetrating into the construction and damage is avoided. 
(Lundgreen 2010) 
 
Convection is moisture transport through leaks in the surfaces due to air pressure differences. Pressure 
differences arise due to wind, thermal buildup of the room air and mechanical ventilation. A ventilation 
system that creates excessive pressure in the house should therefore be avoided as it increases the risk of 
damage due to convection. A difference in air pressure on either side of a wall can result in air passing 
through the construction and carry moisture with it. (Lundgreen 2010) The types of condensation that 
mostly occurs in houses are the interstitial condensation, which happens when warm, moist air diffuses into 
a vapor-permeable material, such as fibrous insulation. If the material is warm on one side and cold on the 
other, the moisture will be deposited in liquid form within the material. This is a particular problem in 
heavily insulated or air-conditioned homes. (Brinsmead 2017) 
 
The air in the majority of homes tends to have 50-70% relative humidity. Problems occur when the moisture 
content has become too high, because of structural defects in a building. For example, when an old house 
has no damp-proof course and when there is inadequate ventilation. Period homes often have no damp-
proof course, which means moisture from the soil beneath the house rises up into ground floor rooms, 
whilst other homes suffer from bridged damp-proof course or damaged guttering. (Brinsmead 2017)

5.4 THE SEMI-OUTDOOR SPACE AS A RENOVATION OPTION 
Can the semi-outdoor space be used as a renovation option for existing buildings? To answer this question 
an investigation of whether the semi-outdoor space creates condensation in the outer wall of the inner 
house was made. If there is condensation a risk mold growth in the construction occurs. The moisture 
balance in the outer wall was investigated using the simulation program DELPHIN 5.9.5 (Bauklimatik-
Dresden). Simulation models of the different wall constructions were made, with and without the semi-
outdoor space. A yearly simulation was made to evaluate if there were different periods throughout the 
year, where the risk of condensation was higher than others.  
 
5.4.1 DELPHIN 5.9.5 
DELPHIN is a simulation program for coupled heat, moisture and mass transport in porous building 
materials. The program can be used for calculation of thermal bridges, including evaluation of hygrothermal 
problem areas such as surface condensation and interstitial condensation, which was used in this 
investigation to evaluate the moisture balance in an outer wall. (Bauklimatik-Dresden) 
 
In the program the different wall constructions were modelled in a 1D construction (planar horizontal 
transport). It is also possible to model 2D constructions, like corners and wall meeting foundation. However, 
in the following analysis a 1D construction was used. Materials from the material data base were assigned to 
the selected layers in the construction. The material data base had a long list of materials to choose from 
and the most representative materials were chosen.  
 
Furthermore, the boundary conditions were assigned to the construction. IDA ICE simulations were made for 
the different scenarios and the temperature and relative humidity for the inner house and outside/ semi-
outdoor space were exported and processed. The climate data files were imported to DELPHIN and used to 
setup the external and internal boundary conditions for the wall constructions. Additionally, the solar 
radiation and driving rain conditions were assigned to the surface facing the outside or the semi-outdoor 
space. However, for the models with the semi-outdoor space implemented only the solar radiation condition 
was assigned to the surface, because the semi-outdoor space acts as a weather shield and therefore protects 
the house from wind and rain. Heat conduction and vapor diffusion boundaries for the inside and outside of 
the wall were also assigned.  
==PAGE:36==
 
 
Page | 31 
 
 
The program includes sets of moisture mass balance equations such as convective liquid water flux, 
convective water vapor flux and diffuse water vapor flux. Internal energy balance equations, like heat 
conduction flow and total internal energy density, solve the system of partial differential equations fully 
coupled. The numerical solution is achieved by semi-discretization in space using a finite/control volume 
method and subsequent integration in time. (Fantucci et al. 2017) 
 
Lastly, the outputs were setup and assigned. Relative humidity outputs were assigned to different points in 
the construction in order to be able to evaluate if condensation occurs. Hourly outputs were setup and a 
yearly simulation was run.

5.4.3 RISK OF MOLD GROWTH 
In the following the risk of mold growth was evaluated and the same two scenarios as for the moisture 
balance were investigated, which means that the wall constructions before and after the energy crisis and 
the light and heavy outer wall constructions were used. Simulation models where the semi-outdoor space 
was implemented and models where it was not, were made. This was done to evaluate if the semi-outdoor 
space creates a higher or lower risk of mold growth.  
 
The risk of mold growth was found through the mold index. The mold index was found by using the VTT 
model equations, which are implemented in DELPHIN 5.9.5. To obtain these results the surface temperature 
and relative humidity were needed, along with sensitivity class and corresponding material. To get a more 
accurate estimate of the risk of mold growth, a simulation running for at least 2 years was made. Climate 
files containing 4 years of data were therefore made to import into DELPHIN. To generate these climate files 
the data from IDA ICE were used. IDA ICE only simulates one year of climate data, so it was assumed that the 
following three years would look the same. The investigation of mold growth was on the surface of the outer 
wall facing the semi-outdoor space or the outside, because the relative humidity is higher and therefore 
causes a higher risk of mold growth.

6.2 THE SEMI-OUTDOOR SPACE AS A RENOVATION OPTION 
It was investigated if the semi-outdoor space could be used as a renovation option for existing buildings by 
evaluating the moisture balance in the wall construction and the risk of mold growth on the surface of the 
wall. Wall construction before and after the energy crisis was used. Furthermore, was it investigated if the 
semi-outdoor space also could be used for thermally lighter houses.  
 
It was chosen only to look at the good user, because the results for the good and the bad user were very 
similar. The good user was chosen, because of the higher relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space, which 
increases the risk of mold growth.

in the construction. A yearly simulation with hourly outputs was performed.

7 DISCUSSION 
In the following the different analyses will be discussed and compared in order to evaluate 1) what 
conditions that affect the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space and 2) can the semi-outdoor space be 
used as a renovation option. However, first a reflection of what the results overall showed is presented.  
 
Overall the results show that the semi-outdoor space can be used as a renovation option for existing 
buildings and for historical protected buildings where the façade cannot be changed. The semi-outdoor 
space can extend the life span of the buildings, because the semi-outdoor space functions as a climate 
screen. Furthermore, in relation to new buildings being built with a semi-outdoor space the damp-proof 
course in the construction can be avoided. Additionally, the semi-outdoor space can still be used by the 
occupants, where they are thermally comfortable, even though the high relative humidity values occur. 
(Toftum et al. 1998)  
 
The semi-outdoor space also creates the possibility for a new way of think according to how houses in the 
future should be built. The heated floor area can be down scaled, because the semi-outdoor space creates 
new square meters, which the occupants can use in long periods throughout the year. At the same time the 
semi-outdoor space decreases the energy use of the inner house. This kind of passive energy saving could be 
an important piece in the further research within zero-energy houses. Furthermore, the potential for 
combining different passive solutions with the design of buildings is made possible.  
7.1 HUMIDITY 
For all the cases the number of hours for the relative humidity in the range from 0%-20% were very few. This 
is positive for the occupants in the semi-outdoor space, because then they are not exposed to irritation of 
skin and eyes. However, for all the cases there were many periods in the range 70%-100% relative humidity, 
which was outside the requirements of the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space. These relative 
humidity values can lead to condensation and increased risk of mold growth. Therefore, this range is 
discussed in the following for the different cases.  
 
7.1.1 BAD USER VERSUS GOOD USER 
The difference between the bad and the good user was not that significant, when looking at the humidity 
conditions of the semi-outdoor space. The relative humidity behaved as could be expected. The values of the 
relative humidity were high in the colder months, where the temperature was low, which results in the air 
not being able to contain the same amount of water as when the temperature is higher. It was lower in the 
warmer months. However, it was possible to detect a difference between the bad and the good user when 
looking at the indoor climate of the inner house. Here, the good user was overall better than the bad user 
when comparing the results with the requirements for dwellings from DS/EN 15251 (2007). There was also a 
large energy saving by implementing a semi-outdoor space, around 140 kWh/m2, which corresponds to a 
reduction of 56% for the total energy use between the house with no semi-outdoor space and the good 
user. The reduction of the total energy use between the house with no semi-outdoor space and the bad user 
was 33%. Which indicate that the good and the bad user did have an influence on the energy use of the 
inner house.   
 
The good user was the case with the highest values of the relative humidity in both the semi-outdoor space 
and in the inner house. A solution to decrease the relative humidity could be to install a ventilation system 
where the exhaust air from the inner house is lead all the way outside of the semi-outdoor space. This 
prevents the warm moist air to be mixed with the air from the semi-outdoor space.  
 
Especially in the winter, the warm moist air can condense or increase the relative humidity in the semi-
outdoor space, because the air in the semi-outdoor space is somewhat colder and thus cannot contain the 
same amount of water as the warm air from the inner house. This leads to the dew point temperature for 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
Page | 64 
 
the warm air to decrease when it is mixed with the cold air from the semi-outdoor space and thereby the 
relative humidity increases.  
 
Another reason to decrease the relative humidity is also to decrease the risk of mold growth in the semi-
outdoor space. From the comparison between the operative temperature and the relative humidity it was 
possible to locate measuring points, which were above the critical relative humidity curve in Figure 23 and 
Figure 24 for the bad and the good user, respectively. This indicate that these measuring points lied in a 
favorable area for mold growth, where the conditions are met in relation to the temperature and the 
relative humidity, which means that there is a risk of mold growth. However, it is not possible to predict how 
much mold growth there would be, because this depends on how long the period is, for which the 
conditions are met. In order to be able to say something about this, the mold index had to be investigated.  
 
Besides the relative humidity, temperature and the exposure time, the building material which is exposed, is 
also important to know. The mold growth intensity and rate and even the possibility for growth depends on 
the nutrition and pH level of the material surface and the material type. All this takes the mold index into 
account. The correct way to interpret the results of the present model is that the mold index represents the 
possible activity of the different mold fungi. The model does not make any distinction between the mold 
species, but it represents the risk for any possible mold growth on the material surface. (Viitanen 2007) 
 
The mold index was found for the good user with the light outer walls of the summer house. The results with 
and without the semi-outdoor space can be found in section 6.2.2 Risk of mold growth under light and heavy 
outer walls. After four years the mold index for when the semi-outdoor space was implemented was 1.1, 
which indicate initial stages of growth and a small amount of mold on the surface. Without the semi-outdoor 
space, the same surface reached 4.5 in mold index after four years, which indicate moderate growth and 
30%-70% of the surface covered with mold. This shows that the semi-outdoor space decreases the risk of 
growth of mold on the façade of the inner house, because the semi-outdoor space functions as a climate 
screen for the inner house and removes the façade’s exposure to wind, rain and sludge.  
 
7.1.2 ORIENTATION 
In relation to the orientation of the semi-outdoor space there was no significant difference between the 
relative humidity for the two orientations. There was fewer hours of the relative humidity in the range 70%-
100% for the east/west facing gables, but only with 30 and 50 hours for the bad and good user, respectively. 
The same was observed for the evaluation of the risk of mold growth. Here there was no difference for the 
good user between the two orientations and it was 0,7% lower for the east/west facing gables in relation to 
the north/south facing gables for the bad user. From this it can be concluded that the orientation of the 
semi-outdoor space does not have an influence on the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space.  
 
7.1.3 MOISTURE SOURCES 
For all of the cases with moisture sources the relative humidity was higher than with no moisture source. 
Especially for the case with the plants, where the relative humidity in 94% of the hours out of a year lied in 
the range 70%-100%. This was also visible in the relative humidity graph, Figure 32, which looked very 
different from all the other relative humidity graphs with the moisture sources. The high relative humidity 
values can be explained by the fact that this was also the moisture source which added the most moisture to 
the semi-outdoor space out of all of the moisture sources. However, there is an error in these results, which 
is that the plants also consume some of the moisture and water in the air, which was not possible to 
simulate in IDA ICE. Therefore, is it assumed that the results for the plants look worse than they are in 
reality. From a talk with Drivadan owners of greenhouses and houses with the semi-outdoor space 
implemented experienced a “low” humidity in the semi-outdoor space. This resulted in that the owners had 
to water the plants more, especially in the summer. It should however be mentioned that the high relative 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
Page | 65 
 
humidity values occurred mostly during the colder months, where the temperature was lower. However, the 
results for the case with plants should still be evaluated with this error in mind. 
 
If there is a wish to have plants in the semi-outdoor space, it is important to be aware of having natural 
ventilation in the semi-outdoor space that manages to ventilate the warm humid air.  
 
In the semi-outdoor space, it is assumed that there is natural ventilation and more specifically buoyancy 
driven ventilation, because of the way the window openings are located. This is also documented by 
Skammelsen Tranekjær (2018). This form of natural ventilation was chosen, because it is the most optimal in 
relation to the geometry of the semi-outdoor space. This kind of ventilation promotes ventilation of the 
more warm humid air that occurs in the semi-outdoor space, which can lead to condensation on the 
windows when the air comes in contact with the colder surfaces and to increased risk of mold growth.  
 
In order for the buoyancy driven ventilation to occur the windows in the bottom and top of the semi-
outdoor space have to be open. The windows in the semi-outdoor space were temperature controlled. 
However, a more optimal solution could be to make the control system for the windows relative humidity 
controlled in the winter, because the high relative humidity values occurred mostly in that period, and 
temperature controlled in the warmer months.  
 
Another solution could be to make a schedule for the windows in the winter, where the windows open on a 
specific time of the day in a preset period. In that way it is possible to force a natural ventilation in the semi-
outdoor space even though the temperatures are low.  
 
For the other moisture sources, the relative humidity profiles progressed as expected, which can be seen in 
their average relative humidity values and standard deviations, because they were very similar and did not 
differ from the base case. The relative humidity was higher in the colder months and lower in the warmer 
months. The moisture source which had second most hours in the range 70%-100% relative humidity was 
the case with cooking in the semi-outdoor space, with 535 hours more than no moisture source. Here it was 
also in the winter that the high relative humidity values occurred and a possible solution to avoid these high 
values could be to avoid cooking in the semi-outdoor space in the colder months and only do it in the 
warmer months. This is probably also a more likely approach to be using the semi-outdoor space in relation 
to cooking.  
 
With most of the hours of the relative humidity in the range 70%-100% for all of the moisture sources it was 
also expected that the risk of mold growth would increase. If the periods in the range of 70%-100% for the 
relative humidity increase, then the periods above the critical relative humidity curve increases as well. 
Which means the risk of mold growth increases. It can be concluded that as the temperature remains 
relatively the same for all the cases, then when the relative humidity increases, the risk of mold growth 
increases. This was also proven by Viitanen (2007). Taken this into account it was logical that it was the case 
with the plants that had the highest number of hours above the critical curve. Simulations were not made to 
evaluate the mold index for these cases, but as there were initial stages of mold growth for the good user 
with no moisture source in the semi-outdoor space, it is assumed that there is also mold growth on the 
façade of the inner house for all the cases with moisture sources. In order to be able to say more specifically 
how much mold growth there would be, simulations should be made.  
 
This means that if the occupants want to implement or do some of these activities in the semi-outdoor 
space, a control system on some of the windows should be installed so that natural ventilation occurs in the 
semi-outdoor space. This is also supported by Skammelsen Tranekjær (2018) who could conclude that larger 
airflows in the semi-outdoor space decreased the relative humidity in the semi-outdoor space. The control 
system should be set so that the windows also open in the winter, because it is in this period where most of 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
Page | 66 
 
the hours with too high relative humidity values occurred. Furthermore, it should be considered that these 
activities only should take place in the warmer months.  
7.2 CONDENSATION 
When there were too high relative humidity values there was also a risk of condensation on the windows of 
the semi-outdoor space, because the surfaces are colder than the air temperature. This was also the case for 
the moisture source with drying clothes. 
 
7.2.1 MOISTURE SOURCE WITH DRYING CLOTHES 
Generally, there were more hours with condensation for the case with drying clothes than the case with no 
moisture source and the sides of the semi-outdoor space had more hours of condensation than the roof of 
the semi-outdoor space. The number of hours where there was condensation was higher in the colder 
months, because the surface temperature decreases when the outdoor temperature decreases and is thus 
more often lower than the dew point temperature in those periods. October was the month where there 
were highest risk of condensation. This month could still be a month where the occupants would stay in the 
semi-outdoor space as comfortable temperatures could still occur. Therefore, it was investigated when the 
condensation occurred throughout a day in October. The condensation typically occurred during the night, 
which is outside the periods where the occupants would stay in the semi-outdoor space. This was valid for 
the entire month of October and it is assumed to be the same for the majority of the months. Which means 
the occupants should not be worried about condensation on the windows of the semi-outdoor space during 
the day. This also corresponds with the experience from Drivadan.  
 
However, it is important to be aware of which material is chosen as frames of the windows and as structural 
system of the semi-outdoor space, because the material should be able to withstand to be exposed to quite 
a lot of periods with condensation. If a material is exposed to condensation for so many periods, it can lead 
to mold growth, if there is the right conditions and enough nutrition on the material.  
 
7.2.2 MATERIALS 
The sides of the semi-outdoor space had more hours of condensation than the roof of the semi-outdoor 
space for the analysis where the transparent material was replaced by polycarbonate plates instead of one-
layer glass. Here it was still during the night that the condensation occurred and the highest number of hours 
where there was condensation, was in the colder months. The difference in number of hours where there 
was condensation between the two materials was however small. The largest difference was 27 hours, 
which one-layer glass had more number of hours of condensation. The difference in U-values were not very 
large, which explains the small difference in hours between the two materials. However, it still shows that if 
a material with lower U-value is used, the hours of condensation is decreased, because the surface 
temperature increases. Therefore, it could be interesting in a further investigation to look at other 
transparent materials with lower U-values than the already tested materials. A new material which has come 
on the market is transparent wood. It offers excellent mechanical properties, including strength, toughness, 
low density and low thermal conductivity. (Gaitens 2017) However it is still a relatively new material, so it 
has not yet been used in buildings.  
 
However, it is important to be aware of the material properties, for there should not be a breeding ground 
for the mold to grow on the surface of the material.  
7.3 MOISTURE BALANCE IN ANALYSIS AND THE RISK OF MOLD GROWTH ANALYSIS 
From the analyses made for the different wall constructions it can be concluded that the semi-outdoor space 
removes condensation in a wall, because it functions as a climate screen.  
 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
Page | 67 
 
7.3.1 ENERGY CRISIS 
This was valid for both the wall types constructed before and after the energy crisis. For the outer wall made 
after the energy crisis there was condensation at all the construction layers, except at the internal façade of 
the wall. It is therefore important to evaluate if insulating the air gap between the two brick walls is the best 
solution, because it makes the house more air tight and thereby condensation can occur in the wall. In this 
comparison the outer wall before the energy crisis was actually better, if only looking at the moisture 
balance in the wall without implementing a semi-outdoor space. In the cases where the semi-outdoor space 
was implemented both walls could be used as no condensation occurred in the wall construction.  
 
However, it could be interesting to look at the inner house’s indoor climate and energy use in relation to the 
two wall constructions, as this also can have a large influence on which wall construction to choose. 
  
There was a large difference in mold index for both the walls compared to the semi-outdoor space being 
implemented or not. Without the semi-outdoor space, the mold index after four years reached 2.4 for both 
wall constructions, which indicate that less than 10% of the surface would be covered with mold and with 
the semi-outdoor space there was no risk of mold growth.  
 
7.3.2 LIGHT AND HEAVY OUTER WALLS 
In relation to the light outer wall there were only few periods where the relative humidity on the façade of 
the inner house reached 100%. When looking at the moisture balance there was no risk of condensation in 
the wall construction neither with nor without the semi-outdoor space. However, the average relative 
humidity through the wall construction got lower when the semi-outdoor space was implemented. 
Nevertheless, when evaluating the mold index of the same outer wall there were a large difference on the 
mold growth on the surface with and without the semi-outdoor space. As earlier mentioned, the mold index 
decreases with 3.4 when the semi-outdoor space was implemented, because it functions as a climate screen. 
 
The conclusion is that it is possible to use all wall constructions investigated for the inner house, when 
implementing a semi-outdoor space. However, if wood is chosen as the cladding it should be considered to 
treat the wood to increase the pH level of the surface of the material in order to suppress the growth of 
organism on the material.   
 
7.3.3 FURTHER STUDIES 
Better ventilation in the semi-outdoor space is one of the solutions to decrease the high relative humidity 
values in the semi-outdoor space. However, in order to be able to find the most optimal ventilation in the 
semi-outdoor space, a further study of the natural ventilation should be considered. Here, a CFD simulation 
could be made, to study the airflow in the semi-outdoor space. Furthermore, a study of how much and when 
the windows in the semi-outdoor space should be open could be made. Additionally, could the placement of 
the windows that can open in the semi-outdoor space have an effect on the optimization of the airflows in 
the semi-outdoor space. Besides the windows being controlled by a schedule, could the windows also be 
controlled by an opening system. Here, a study of which parameters that should control the system could be 
made. Maybe in the winter should the opening system be controlled by the relative humidity and in the 
summer by the temperature.

8 CONCLUSION 
Overall the purpose of this study was to investigate 1) what conditions that affect the relative humidity in 
the semi-outdoor space and 2) can the semi-outdoor space be used as a renovation option? Simulations in 
IDA ICE and DELPHIN were used to answer those questions.  
 
There were no problems with too low relative humidity values and the occupants in the semi-outdoor space 
would therefore not experience irritation of skin and eyes due to this. However, all the cases analysed had 
hours in the range of 70%-100% relative humidity, which was above the requirements set for the relative 
humidity in the semi-outdoor space in this study. This led to risk of mold growth on the façade of the inner 
house for all the cases, because they all had periods above the critical relative humidity curve. However, 
when implementing the semi-outdoor space, the mold index decreased to nearly no mold growth on the 
façade of the inner house, because the semi-outdoor space functions as a climate screen and removes the 
façade’s exposure to wind, rain and sludge. Furthermore, the energy use of the inner house decreased and 
the condensation in the wall constructions before and after the energy crisis was removed when the semi-
outdoor space was implemented.  
 
The indoor climate of the inner house had no significant influence on the humidity of the semi-outdoor 
space as well as the orientation of the semi-outdoor space, however adding different moisture sources did 
have an influence on the relative humidity and the risk of mold growth. Therefore, it is important to install a 
control opening system on some of the windows in order to force a natural ventilation in the semi-outdoor 
space, because higher airflow decrease the high relative humidity values.  
 
The high relative humidity values also resulted in condensation on the windows of the semi-outdoor space, 
however only during the night and mostly in the colder months. The change of the transparent material for 
the windows did have an influence on the hours of condensation that occurred, however the small 
difference in U-values also resulted in a small difference for the hours of condensation. Nevertheless, a 
lower U-value resulted in fewer hours of condensation on the windows.  
 
The overall conclusion is that all wall constructions investigated can be used for the inner house, when 
implementing a semi-outdoor space and that the semi-outdoor space can be used as a renovation option 
without causing condensation in the wall constructions. Furthermore, can the risk of mold growth be 
minimized by having natural ventilation in the semi-outdoor space and by choosing a façade material which 
is resilient towards mold growth.
