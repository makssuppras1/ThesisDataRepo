Summary
Epilepsy affects approximately 1% of the world’s population and the majority of
them lives in the low and middle-income countries (LMICs).
Epilepsy is usually
diagnosed by a human interpreter that reads through an EEG recording. The main
barrier for people being diagnosed and treated for epilepsy, in LMICs, is the limited
accessibility of EEG and the lack of interpreters. McKenzie et al. (2017) describes a
study were data from portable EEG scanners is sent abroad for analysis by voluntaries.
This thesis examines how to shorten the time spent manually interpreting the EEG
recordings, by using novelty detection. A preprocessing pipeline is suggested and two
methods for novelty detection are examined.
The thesis uses two real EEG data sets to validate the process. The first data set is
from the Children’s Hospital in Boston and includes records with labeled seizures. The
other one is recorded in Guinea with a portable EEG scanner(SBS2) and contains no
labels. The methods are first analyzed and developed with the labeled data. These
methods are then tested on the data from the SBS2.
In the preprocessing steps
artifacts are removed by classifying Independent Components. Features are extracted
and a Principal Component Analysis is used for dimension reduction. An interesting
observation was made when the Median absolute deviation (MAD) of the signal was
inspected between subjects.
The MAD values were in general higher for subjects
showing epileptic discharge, indicating that it could be used as a global discriminant.
Two methods based on Gaussian distribution were used for novelty detection which
showed similar results. The first method was to fit a Gaussian distribution to the
data and the latter was to use a robust Gaussian Mixture Model based on the work
from Roberts and Tarassenko (1994). Abnormal data points were detected, however,
there was no difference between subjects with and without epileptiform discharges.
These first steps of running experiments on the data from the SBS2 show promising
results. They offer an option to implement; a data quality control, a global discrim-
inant using MAD and novelty detection to localize the abnormal data points. From
a practical point of view, this thesis contributes an analysis on how to shorten the
time spent on interpreting the data. This analysis could be used as preliminary work
for future studies on the interpretation of SBS2 data from epilepsy patients in the
LMICs.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Epilepsy and EEG
Epilepsy affects around 50 million people worldwide and more than 80% of them live
in the low and middle-income countries(LMICs) (McKenzie et al., 2017). Epilepsy is
characterized by epileptic seizures, both the duration and frequency of these episodes
can vary significantly.
To detect and diagnose epilepsy an electroencephalogram
(EEG) recordings can be used.
The EEG was discovered in 1929 by the German psychiatrist Hans Berger. He
and his colleagues showed on the EEG, what was then refereed to a petit mal(absence
seizures) epilepsy (Smith, 2005). Because of its convenient and inexpensive ways, the
EEG has since then played a large role in diagnosing patients with seizure disorders,
i.e.
epilepsy.
It measures the electrical potentials on the scalp that arise due to
neuronal communication within the brain (Hari & Puce, 2017). In other words, it
measures the voltage difference between two recording sites. If the EEG shows inter-
ictal epileptiform discharge (IED) it indicates that the patient has epilepsy. However,
the EEG has some disadvantages. For example, around 10% of epileptic patients will
never show any IED and an extensive areas of the cortex, have to be activated at the
same time in order to generate enough potential for changes to be registered by the
electrodes (Smith, 2005).
A recent survey of key respondents from 37 countries shows that availability
of EEG is 63% in low-income countries and 71% in lower middle-income countries
(McKenzie et al., 2017). The waiting time in these countries can be long, and only
40% from the low-income countries consider EEG to be affordable. The limited avail-
ability of EEG service is tough for individuals or clinicians to solve, i.e inconsistent
electricity supplies, the high cost of EEG equipment, the low number of skilled tech-
nologists and interpreters of EEG recordings, and centralization of services in cities
(McKenzie et al., 2017). On the other hand, the medicine against epilepsy is inex-
pensive, 20$ per year, and the patients, or subjects, show a good response to it, so
availability of aid after diagnosis is not a limitation. Therefore, it is clear that better
access to EEG could improve the quality of life for epilepsy patients in the LMICs.
A study from McKenzie et al. (2017) used an equipment that could help overcome
most of these previously mentioned barriers regarding EEG and will be the focus of
the next section.

1.2
The research from McKenzie et al. (2017) on SBS2
In April 2017 a research was published, where the ability of a smartphone-based EEG
application, the Smartphone Brain Scanner-2(SBS2), was assessed (McKenzie et al.,
2017). The SBS2 system consists of an Android tablet that is wirelessly connected to
a 14 electrode EasyCap headset. The system was tested on patients with suspected
epilepsy in Bhutan, where the median age of the participants was 24 years and the
recordings were then analyzed by interpreters. The detection of EEG abnormalities
recorded by SBS2 was then compared to recordings from a standard EEG.
The SBS2 can help overcome previously mentioned barriers regarding EEG. For
example, staff without formal EEG training performed the EEG recordings with SBS2
and training them took less than an hour. Compared to the standard EEG record-
ings, that were completed on Xltek machines by board-certified EEG technologists
or supervised research staff. The standard EEG has greater complications than the
SBS2. The development of mobile-compatible electrode caps provides an opportu-
nity for EEG systems that are low-cost, portable and more user friendly compared
to the standard EEG technology. This system could therefore lead to an increase in
availability of EEG service (McKenzie et al., 2017).
The interpretation of the data in McKenzie et al. (2017) was carried out by a
board-certified pediatric and adult neurologists, referred to here as interpreters. This
was done for both the SBS2 and standard EEG recordings. The interpreters were
instructed to categorize the EEGs and register their interpretation on a standardized
spreadsheet. The recordings were either classified as normal or abnormal overall, and
whether they had epileptiform discharges, or IED, present or not.
1.3
Research objective
There was no prepocessing nor modelling done to classify the data in the study
from McKenzie et al. (2017). The readers only based their interpretations on their
prior knowledge of EEG signal readings. All the work that is done to interpret the
recordings is voluntary. It would be optimal to decrease the time spent on reading
through them. To do so, data collected in Guinea, similarly to the data collected in
Bhutan that was used in McKenzie et al. (2017), will be modeled and tested using
different machine learning methods. Those methods are e.g. artifacts removal and
novelty detection. This is done to analyze the data and to be able to classify the
EEG. The program that will be used to analyze the EEG recordings is EEGLAB, an
interactive MATLAB toolbox, that can be used to read continuous EEG signals.
For simplification, the whole process is displayed in the diagram in Figure 1.1,
where the blue box represents where the work from this thesis will be placed in the
process. As mentioned, the work done to interpret the recordings is voluntary. This is
why machine learning methods will be used to minimize the time and ease the process
of analyzing the recordings from the SBS2 system. The main objective is therefore
to classify the EEG recordings and shorten the time spent on interpreting the data.

The SBS2 data, both from Guinea and Bhutan, included a spread sheet that contains
information regarding the subjects and their recordings. Even though a recording
was marked positive for having IED present, it is not labeled where and when the
discharge occurs. Considering this, and the fact that SBS2 recordings are collected

The base
data set would preferably be from pediatric patients, as well as better defined and
easier to test on, compared to the SBS2 data. Therefore, a data set from PhysioNet

seizure and non-seizure recordings that were labeled. The modelling process would
then be tested on the CHB-MIT data, using a recording from each subject as a
training data and then a seizure recording from that same subject as a test data. The
objective is to be able to detect the seizures, since they are the abnormalities labeled
in the data.

changed from the one used for the CHB-MIT data. From a practical point of view

the interpreter receives multiple recordings simultaneously. Then the interpreter reads
the recordings, until a sufficient amount of recordings are labeled as ’clean’, or without
IED. These recordings are then used to form a training set and the rest of the recorded
data as test set. The novelty points, detected by the modelling process, would be

CHAPTER 2
Clinical Background
2.1
Electroencephalogram(EEG)
EEG plays an important role in diagnosing and management of subjects with seizure
disorders such as epilepsy (Smith, 2005). The limitations and a short explanation
was provided in the introduction chapter 1. Further explanation on EEG, as well
as description of the equipment used to record the EEG data in this thesis, will be
provided in the following sections.
2.1.1
EEG recording standards, rhythms and characteristics
EEG can be measured by means of electrodes placed directly on the cortex or on the
scalp (Blinowska & Durka, 2006). The EEG measured on the cortex is sometimes
called electrocorticogram(ECoG). EEG recorded as a response to external or internal
stimulus is called an event-related potential (ERP) and EEG generated in the absence
of external stimulus is called spontaneous EEG. EEG of a normal subject in the
awake state with scalp electrodes has an amplitude of 10-100 mV. However, the EEG
amplitude may increase by almost an order of magnitude in the presence of epilepsy,
so that in the cortex the amplitudes are in the range of 500-1500 mV (Blinowska &
Durka, 2006).
In this thesis, EEG registered by means of electrodes placed on the scalp was
used. The exact position of each electrode is very important for both interpretation
of a single recording as well as a comparison of results (Blinowska & Durka, 2006).
Therefore, scalp EEG setup has a standardization, called the 10-20 electrode system.
The system states positions, related to specific anatomic landmarks of the nasion and
inion, so that 10–20 % of the distance between them is used as the electrode interval
(Herbert & Jasper, 1957).
The EEG is a measure of potential difference between electrodes. The referential,
or unipolar, setup measures relative to the same electrode for all derivations (Bli-
nowska & Durka, 2006). There does not exist a universal consent regarding the best
position of the reference electrode, so it can for exampled be placed on the earlobe,
nose or scalp center. In the bipolar setup, the potential difference between two partic-
ular scalp electrodes is registered. For display and further processing, data recorded
in the unipolar setup can be transformed into any bipolar montage.

2 Clinical Background
The necessity of artifact rejection is universally acknowledged (Blinowska & Durka,
2006). An EEG artifact can for example come from muscle or heart activity, eye
movement, an external electromagnetic field, poor electrode contact and the subject’s
movement. Such activities can be separated from the EEG data to some extent and
will be discussed in more detail later.
With visual analysis of EEG signals, several brain related disorders can be iden-
tified by clinical experts(Sanei & Chambers, 2013). These EEG signals show brain
rhythms that have amplitude and frequency. They can change according to the age
of the individual and the state they are in, for example, between wakefulness and
sleep.
Different frequency ranges are the characteristics of the five major brain waves.
These frequency classes of EEG signals are Delta (0.5-4 Hz), Theta (4-8Hz), Alpha
(8-13 Hz), Beta (13-30 Hz) and Gamma (above 30 Hz) according to Blinowska and
Durka (2006). There, it is stated that the slow activity characteristic in young children
constantly reduces during adolescence and EEG is said to evolve with age until 30
years, when the final character is achieved, and then starts to change again in old
age. Figure 2.1 from Blinowska and Durka (2006) shows the five characteristic EEG
rhythms as well as EEG during epileptic seizure.
Figure 2.1: Characteristic EEG rhythms, from the top down the figure shows for
Delta (δ), Theta (θ), Alpha(α) and Gamma (γ) as well as EEG during epileptic
seizure. Note, that the amplitude scale is an order of magnitude larger (Blinowska &
Durka, 2006)

2.1.2
Smartphone Brain Scanner
The technical details and verification on the Smartphone Brain Scanner (SBS2) were
explained in the following two articles, Stopczynski, Stahlhut, Petersen, et al. (2014)
and Stopczynski, Stahlhut, Larsen, Petersen, and Hansen (2014). As mentioned in
the introduction chapter 1, the research from McKenzie et al. (2017) assessed the
SBS2 ability to detect epileptiform abnormalities compared to standard clinical EEG.
These three articles will be used as a reference for this section.
Most neuroimaging experiments have been recorded when the patients are not
moving. However, there is a well known need in neuroscience for devices that support
quantitative measurements in ordinary, everyday settings. Wireless neuroheadsets
have made mobile brain imaging a reality. With electrodes placed on the scalp, they

2.2
Epilepsy
Epilepsy is a neurological disorder that appears in repeated seizures. The disorder
is characterized by the presence of abnormal synchronous discharges of large group
of neurons in the brain (Exarchos, Tzallas, Fotiadis, Konitsiotis, & Giannopoulos,
2006).
These discharges appear either during seizures (ictal periods) or between
seizures (interictal periods), and are often referred to as ”paroxysmal activity”. In
daily practice, neurologist examine short recordings of interictal periods. According
to Exarchos et al. (2006) the most common forms of interictal activity found in EEG
are individual spikes, the sharp wave and the spike-and-wave complex, where the
spikes are observed in the majority of subjects with epilepsy. Therefore, detection
of interictal epileptiform discharge (IED) plays an important role in the diagnosis of
epilepsy. However, according to Tzallas, Tsipouras, and Fotiadis (2009) the brain is
not in a clinical seizure during an isolated spike. In an ictal period a different EEG
pattern is observed, that consists of polyspike activity, rhytmical waveforms for a
broad variety of frequencies, low-amplitude desynchronization, as well as spike-and-
wave complexes. Diagnosis of epilepsy is usually based on observed epileptic seizure,
even though IED can offer evidence of epilepsy.
Pillai and Sperling (2006) however state that the interictal spike remains the
hallmark of epilepsy, clearly demonstrating cortical hyperexcitability and hypersyn-
chrony, and is present in the ”normal” interictal state. There it is mentioned that
interictal spike may help; verify a clinical diagnosis of epilepsy, define the epilepsy
syndrome, give information useful for deciding drug management and to assess can-
didacy for epilepsy surgery. A list of the criteria for interictal spikes and sharp waves
is shown in Pillai and Sperling (2006). It can be found in table 2 in the article, and
is as follows:
1. The discharge should be clearly distinguished from background activity and
paroxysmal.
2. The discharge usually shows a sudden change in polarity occurring over numer-
ous milliseconds, resulting in a sharp contour or spikiness.
3. The duration should be less than 200 ms. Sharp waves last between 70 and 200
ms, and spikes last between 20 and 70 ms.
4. The discharge must have a physiologic field, so that the discharge recorded from
more than a single electrode and a voltage gradient should be present.
5. Spikes or sharp wave are usually negative in polarity.
6. Most spikes are accompanied by a slow wave.
The interictal epileptiform discharge, or IED, is usually detected with the help
of EEG, however, that is not always the case. Pillai and Sperling (2006) discussed
some limitations to the scalp EEG recordings. For example, the recording time can
be insufficiently short to detect IED, a large area of cortex (6cm2) is needed in a
spike discharge for it to show up on the scalp recordings and scalp recordings can be
affected by muscle action and other artifacts. Smith (2005) also talks about the use
of EEG to diagnose epilepsy and its disadvantages, e.g. around 10% of patients with
==PAGE:27==

epilepsy never show IED and IED can be detected in a small percentage of normal
subjects, that never develop epilepsy.
According to Smith (2005) there are also a number of things that can effect
whether or not subjects with epilepsy will show IED in the EEG. Children are more
prone to show IED compared to older subjects. The location of an epileptogenic zone
is important as a majority of subjects with temporal lobe epilepsy show IED, com-
pared to epileptic foci in mesial or basal cortical regions are less likely to demonstrate
spikes using scalp EEG. Subjects with frequent seizures, for example one each month,
are more likely to have IED compared to subjects with one seizure per year.
The timing of the EEG recording can be a crucial factor. For example, within 24
hours of a seizure, 51% of subjects show IED but only 34% if the EEG is recorded
later. Sleep can be another factor, since some subjects show discharges primarily
during sleep. Consequently, and because only 50% of subjects with epilepsy show
IED in the first EEG test, there is no single solution, or recording type, that fits all
subjects. By doing multiple recordings of routine EEG, the yield in adults can be
increased and sleep studies can increase the yield for all ages. All these limitations
indicate how essential it can be to have EEG available at all times.
As stated before, EEG is crucial for epilepsy diagnosis. Smith (2005) says that
ideally a reported EEG with monitoring facilities should always be available but that
is clearly not the case today, especially for patients in the low or middle income coun-
tries(LMIC). This, and all of the limitations described above, indicate how essential
it is to increase access to EEG for patients with epilepsy or the ones that are in dire
need of a diagnosis.

State of the Art
Epilepsy is characterized by the presence of abnormal synchronous discharges of large
groups of neurons in the brain (Exarchos et al., 2006). As previously mentioned in
the section 2.2, on epilepsy, these discharges appear either during seizures (ictal peri-
ods) or between seizures (interictal periods). The identification of ictal and interictal
periods plays an important role in the diagnosis of epilepsy. However, the task of de-
tecting intericatal epileptiform discharges (IEDs) can be challenging (Halford, 2009).
Their morphologies vary a lot and can be similar to waveforms that are part of arti-
facts or normal background EEG activity.
Classification of artifacts in EEG
is a widely used method. EEG data is of-
ten contaminated by artifactual, nonneural electrical activity originating from non-
physiological sources, (Frølich, Andersen, & Mørup, 2015). This activity can in some
extent be separated from the data by using Independent Component Analysis (ICA).
Main focus has been on the binary problem of classifying neural and non-neural ICs
(Winkler, Haufe, & Tangermann, 2011). However, some studies have been done on
the multiclass problem at the level of across subject generalization within a study
(Viola et al., 2009). Frølich et al. (2015) uses multinomial regression to classify ICs
of EEG data into multiple artifact classes.
Seizure detection
using EEG signal began in the 1970s with the vision of Viglione
and Walsh (1975). It was not until 20 years later that the technology catched up with
their idea (Litt & Echauz, 2002). Several factors influenced the explosion of interest
since 1990, one of which was the wide acceptance of digital EEG technology. Gotman
(1990) reported automated seizure detection with 76% detection accuracy. In 1995
Qu and Gotman (1997) achieved 100% detection accuracy using time- and frequency-
domain features that were classified by a k-nearest neighbor classifier. Seizure detec-
tion based on artificial neural network classifiers has provided a good result (Tzallas
et al., 2009; Weng & Khorasani, 1996). Acharya et al. (2012) tried seven different
methods for automatic detection of normal-, interictal- and ictal- periods with en-
tropy based non-linear features. The Fuzzy Sugeno Classifier gave the best result
with 98.1% accuracy.
The Support Vector Machine and Gaussian Mixture model
gave also good results.

Background

4.2.5
Classification of independent components in EEG
As described in previous sections, the electroencephalogram (EEG) can be recorded
with electrodes placed on the scalp and used to provide information about brain
activity.
Brain activity as well as a specific cognitive task during an experiment
can be reflected as signals of the EEG (Winkler et al., 2011). Each neural activity
generates a very small electrical field which can only be detected with EEG if a
number of assembled neurons display compatible behavior at the same time.
To
interpret these recordings can sometimes be difficult, especially when the EEG data
is corrupted with artifacts. Artifacts can be electrical activities of non-neural origin,
with both physiological sources, such as patients’ heartbeat, eye movement or muscle
activity, or non-physiological sources, such as electrical background noise and loose
electrodes (Frølich et al., 2015).
The signal recorded by a single electrode reflects a mixture of multiple sources of
activity with different origins, both inside and outside of the brain (Chaumon, Bishop,
& Busch, 2015). This is one of the main problems when it comes to interpreting the
scalp EEG. Independent component analysis (ICA) can, to some extent, be used to
detect and separate these non-neural activity from the data.
Makeig et al. (1996) introduced the ICA method to EEG analysis and it has
been generalized with EEGLAB, an interactive MATLAB toolbox. Chaumon et al.
(2015) describes that ICA provides isolation of independent components(ICs) as linear
combination of electrodes. A set of inverse weights, specifying the projection of the
independent source onto the electrode cap, and a period of time can then be used
to characterize each IC. ICs can be considered in many ways like single electrodes,
since they are linear combinations of the original signal. A bad electrode can easily
be discarded after being recorded, and the same can be done for ICs. They can be
subtracted from the signal because the ICs are linear combinations of the original
electrode signal. The remaining signal, after subtracting an artifactual IC, is then
without the artifacts that were caught by that IC.

4.5
Novelty detection
An outlier is defined as a data point that is very different from the other data based
on some measure (Aggarwal & Yu, 2001).
Information regarding some abnormal
behavior of the system described by the data can often be contained in outliers. These
data points can arise due to numerous reason, for example, mechanical faults, human
error, fraudulent behaviour or simply by natural deviation in the data. Detecting
outliers can help identify system faults and fraud before they grow into potential
disaster (Hodge & Austin, 2004).
Novelty detection is closely related to outlier detection, where the test data is
found to differ in some sense from the data that is available during training. Markou
and Singh (2003) describe novelty detection as the identification of an unknown or new
data that a machine learning system does not know about during training. Several
studies indicate that novelty detection can be a challenging task, therefore, there are
several models of novelty detection that perform well on different types of data. The
success of the model does not only depend on the type of method used but also the
statistical properties of data handled.
There are a number of significant issues related to novelty detection that need
to be considered (Markou & Singh, 2003). For example, the method used must be
capable of robust performance on the test data, so that it maximizes the exclusion of
novel samples and simultaneously minimizes the exclusion of known samples.
In this thesis the outliers are not considered to be system faults or error, but
abnormal data points occurring as natural deviation from the data. This indicates
that novelty detection would be the appropriate measurement.
4.5.1

4.6
Performance evaluation
Class imbalance is a situation where the classes in a dataset are not represented
equally(Herlau, Schmidt, & Mørup, 2017). This problem can distort the ability to
fairly assess the performance of the model using for example accuracy, or the fraction
of the correct predictions.
Class imbalance frequently occurs in data sets and in
a variety of fields, an overview of which can be seen in Chawla (2009).
Receiver
operating characteristic (ROC) and Area under curve (AUC) are measurements, or
ways, to deal with class imbalance. These methods will be considered in the next
sections, but to understand and use these methods it is necessary to take a look at
the confusion matrix first.
4.6.1
The confusion matrix and thresholds
To start with, consider a standard two-class classification problem with observation
xi and output yi where yi = 0 and yi = 1 means that the observation i belongs to
the negative and positive class, respectively(Herlau et al., 2017). Lets say a classifier
is made, that assigns to each observation i a number ˆyi where
ˆyi = f(xi, w).
The number ˆyi will then be a continuous and express a relative ’propensity’ for i
to belong to a specific class, according to the classifier. The question is then, how
to evaluate such a classifier?
The first step would be to translate or change the
continuous numbers ˆyi to a binary class-prediction. A simple way to do so, is to
introduce a threshold parameter, θ. All observations i where ˆyi > θ belong then to
the positive class, and all i where ˆyi ≤θ to the negative class. Note that different
thresholds can then have a large influence on the behavior of the classifier. When
looking at the performance of the classifier f, the threshold is chosen randomly so
the different values have to be taken in to consideration.
Figure 4.9: Confusion matrix of the classifier(Herlau, Schmidt, & Mørup, 2017)
The confusion matrix gives a summary of the classification performance of a clas-
sifier(Sammut & Webb, 2011). It is a two dimensional matrix, where one dimension

46

indicates the true condition of an object and the other by the predicted condition that
the classifier assigns. This matrix is the basis for many common metrics(Fawcett,
2006). One case of the confusion matrix is frequently used, where the classes are
either negative or positive. This case is shown in Figure 4.9, where the four cells are
true positive(TP), false positive(FP), true negative(TN) and false negative(FN). Each
threshold level θ gives a confusion matrix, see Figure 4.9, where the four cells shown
with red and green colour in the matrix, can be calculated. Description of the cells,
with respect to the threshold θ, is the following(Herlau et al., 2017):
• True positive(TPθ): The number of observations that are actually positive yi =
1 and the classifier correctly classifies as positive, ˆyi > θ
• False positive(FPθ): The number of observations that are actually negative
yi = 0 and the classifier incorrectly classifies as positive, ˆyi > θ
• False negative(FNθ): The number of observations that are actually positive
yi = 1 and the classifier incorrectly classifies as negative, ˆyi < θ
• True negative(TNθ): The number of observations that are actually negative
yi = 0 and the classifier correctly classifies as negative, ˆyi < θ

it is common to normalize with the class size. Therefore, the true positive rate(TPR)

4.6.2
Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve
The Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) graph is a useful technique for organiz-
ing and visualizing the performance of classifiers(Fawcett, 2006). It is also used for
determining the cut-off point where optimal sensitivity and specificity can be achieved
(Akobeng, 2007). Spackman (1989) was one of the first to use ROC graphs in machine
learning, by evaluation and comparing algorithms using ROC curves.
ROC graphs are two dimensional graphs where the true positive rate(TPR), see
equation 4.47, is plotted on the y-axis and the false positive rate(FPR), see equation
4.48, on the x-axis. The graph illustrates the trade-offs between benefits(TP) and
costs(FP) (Fawcett, 2006).
A graph from Fawcett, 2006 shows the ROC space with single points in the space,
see Figure 4.10. These points represent discrete classifiers, that outputs only a class

Various points in the ROC space are necessary to note. The point in the lower left
==PAGE:62==

the unconditionally issuing positive classification, located in the upper right corner

be used. A binary classifier can be produced by such a ranking, or a scoring classifier

4.6.3
Area under the curve(AUC)
A single figure as a measure of the classifier’s performance is usually desirable. Using
AUC as a performance measure for machine learning algorithms has therefore been
used in various analysis, for example in Bradley (1997), where AUC are used to
compare the performance of six algorithms.
The value of the curve will always be between 0 and 1, since the AUC is a portion of
the area of the square unit of the ROC space. However, with a classifier that exploits
some information in the data, the ROC curve is expected to lie above the random
guessing diagonal line between (0, 0) and (1, 1) in the ROC space. The triangle under
the diagonal line has an area of 0.5, so AUC should not be less than 0.5 (Fawcett,
2006). If the AUC is 1, then the ROC curve goes through point (0, 1), which means
that for some threshold, θ, the number of false positives is 0 and the number of true

positives is equal to the total number of positives observations, i.e. the classifier works
correctly(Herlau et al., 2017).

graph. From the graphs it is clear that some classifiers with higher AUC than another
classifier can perform worse in a specific region on the ROC space. The graph on the
right shows (A), the AUC for a discrete classifier, and (B), a probabilistic or scoring
classifier.

order to average the effect between points trapezoids are rather used than rectangles.

properties, it gets rid of dependence on threshold θ by integrating and is independent
of class imbalance in the test data(Herlau et al., 2017). The AUC in this section has
however only been discussed for two classes. Hand and Till (2001) is referred to, for
further discussion on use of AUC for multiple class classification.

To start with, an ICA decomposition on the EEG data set was preformed, using
runica from Makeig et al. (1996).
The algorithm uses the logistic infomax ICA
algorithm of Bell and Sejnowski (1995) with the natural gradient feature of S.-i. Amari,
Cichocki, and Yang (1996). As recommended by Makeig et al. (1996), the 'extended'
option was used, with the extended-ICA algorithm of Lee et al. (1999). After the ICA
decomposition, the algorithm updates the ICA weight matrix and ICA sphere matrix.
Next, feature extraction was performed, the features in the established spatial
feature set is explained in Frølich et al. (2015).
Before training, the features are
standardized by subtracting the mean and standard deviation of the spatial features.
Then a Multinomial Regression model is used to predict probabilities of class member-
ship of ICs, where the number of classes are six, representing six artifact types. These
probabilities are then used to assign each IC to the class with the highest probability.
To visualize all the classified ICs, a scalpmap of all ICs in the EEG data structure
can be plotted with the function pop_ICMARC_selectcomps. To display this plot here,
a data called Sample Data provided with EEGLAB was used, and Figure 5.5 shows
the 32 ICs classified from the data. Under each scalpmap, a labeled button with each
ICs class is plotted below. The buttons can be clicked to inspect the power spectrum
of each IC.
Finally, the artifacts that will be removed are specified. In this thesis, all artifacts
that are neither neural nor mixed were removed, so the removed artifacts were eye
blinks, heartbeat artifacts, lateral eye movement and muscular artifacts. A figure of
each component removed was plotted on a scalp map using the built in EEGLAB
function pop_topoplot. The Sample Data was used again and can be seen in Figure
5.6, where the three artifacts that were removed are shown. Figure 5.6a shows a
blinking artifact, IC number 5, Figure 5.6b shows two heart artifacts that are ICs
number 8 and 10 and 5.6c displays a lateral eye movement artifact, IC number 18.
To remove the artifacts listed, the function pop_subcomp was used. The function
removes components, that are specified in an input vector, from an EEG dataset.
This removed all artifacts classified as eye blinks, heartbeat artifacts, lateral eye
movement and muscular artifacts. By experiment it was found that it is preferable
to remove artifacts, the validation can be found in Appendix E.

6.1
CHB-MIT data set
This chapter is on the two data sets used in this thesis, selection of data used and
how preprocessing was changed between the two data sets. The first section is on the

6.1.1
Background
A base data set was used in this thesis to develop methods as it is better defined
than the SBS2 data. Data set from PhysioNet (Goldberger et al., 2000 (June 13))
was chosen as the base data, called CHB-MIT scalp EEG data. The data base was
considered a good base data for the experiments, since the subjects are children, just
like in the SBS2 data base, as well as they are recorded in a more conventional envi-
ronment for EEG recordings. The data base can be downloaded from the PhysioNet
website (“CHB-MIT Scalp EEG Database”, 2018). It is described in an article from
Shoeb (2009), where machine learning approaches are used to detect the onset of an
epileptic seizure in the data.
The data was collected at the Children’s Hospital Boston and consist of continuous
scalp EEG recordings from 23 pediatric subjects undergoing withdrawal of anti-seizure
medication. The subject were from 3 to 22 years old, 5 males and 17 females. Each
subject had between 9 to 42 continuous recordings where most of the recordings were
one hour long but some up to 4 hours. The records were marked as seizure and non-
seizure records. The time span of the seizures were also listed, and some recordings
contained up to three seizures (Shoeb, 2009). All the EEG records were sampled at
256 Hz and the international 10-20 bipolar system of EEG electrode positions and
nomenclature was used. Number of EEG signals, or channels, differed between files
from 18 to 28 signals, but most files contained 23 EEG signals (“CHB-MIT Scalp
EEG Database”, 2018).
6.1.2
Selecting data
The experiments in this thesis were done to detect novelty in the data. By using
this data base, the seizure should be considered as a part of the the abnormal data

that will be detected. By using the CHB-MIT data some labeled abnormal data is
included and therefore makes the results from the modelling the data more concrete.
It was decided to choose the test data as the first recording where each patient had
a seizure. The first recording from each subject that contained no seizure was set as a
training set, as a ”pre-seizure” recording. The decision was based on the fact that it
is ideal for novelty detection to train on ”clean” data. However, there is a possibility
that these recordings consist of some abnormal data. For example, it is not stated
in the information regarding the data set if the patient already had a seizure before
the recording started. In the section 2.2 on Epilepsy, it is stated that 51% of patients
show interictal epileptiform discharge(IED) behavior within 24 hours after a seizure.
Therefore, it is not certain that the data used as training set did not include IED,
or some other abnormal behavior. To make use of this information, another decision
was made regarding the choice of training data. Some subjects had seizures in the
first recording, thus having no pre-seizure recordings. As a result, those 7 patients
were excluded from the experiments.
Another aspect was also looked at to decide which recordings should be used for
this thesis, the channel types. As previously mentioned, the number of EEG signals
differed between files, between 18 and 28 signals, but most files contained 23 EEG
signals. The files that contained 28 signals had five ”dummy” signals that could be

This concluded that two recordings for 16 subjects, out of the 24 subjects, were

6.2.3
Setup for modelling
The SBS2 data only has one recording for each subject, thus there is not a recording
that can be considered as ’clean’ (without epileptic form discharges or novelties) EEG
recording for each subject. Therefore, the approach to model the data set needed to
be changed from the one that would be used for CHB-MIT scalp EEG database.
One approach would be to train on the first part of each recording, and then test
==PAGE:86==
72
6 Clinical Data
the rest of the data. As mentioned in section 4.5 on Novelty Detection, it is crucial to
train on a clean data set. The epileptic form discharges in the SBS2 are not labeled,
only marked as positive or negative for showing the behavior. Thus, training on the
first part of each set does not ensure that the data being trained on to be necessarily
clean. This approach was therefore not applicable.
Another approach was to train on a set of recordings that had been marked as
negative to show the epileptic form discharges. The test data set would then consist
of both negative to epileptic form discharges recordings and positive.
This poses a problem since the training will be performed on recordings from
different subjects.
Subjects can have different absolute frequency of rhythms and
depending on factors such as subject sex and age (Rezek & Roberts, 1998).
There are numerous methods that try to normalize the power of each channel by
dividing with the maximum power of each channel, or by standardize using Z-score
for each subject recordings. However, that can risk loosing important information.
Another method was therefore used, where all recorded point in each channel of
the preprocessed data was normalized by divided by the Median absolute deviation
(MAD) of that channel. The MADj for each channel j is calculated as follows,
MADj = median(|Xj −MEDj|)
where Xj is each channel of a recording and MEDj is the median of each channel.
To see the difference in the EEG data after normalization, the mean variance
for each channel, for each of the 22 subjects was examined. The mean variance was
therefore 14 numbers, the same as the number of channels, and the sum of these
variances was calculated. The same was done for all 15 recordings of the EEG data
from healthy subjects, or Non IED, as well as for all 7 recordings of the EEG from
subjects With IED. The results from these calculations can be found in Table 6.5.
The outcome of the total variance shows the effects from normalizing in each
channel. The total variance drops from 113,799.60 to 244.4, showing that there is
a much lower variance in the data after normalizing. This indicates that the data
set is more feasible for joining the recordings together and making a training set for

Implementation
This chapter describes the implementation of the two methods used to detect novelties.
The first two sections are for the Maximum likelihood for Gaussian distribution and
the Robust Gaussian Mixture Model, for the base data set CHB-MIT. The other
sections describe the same models for the SBS2 data.
7.1
Maximum likelihood for Gaussian distribution:
CHB-MIT data
In this section a model for novelty detection, based on normal distribution will be
described. The model was trained and tested on the CHB-MIT data, described in
section 6.1. Novelty detection, as defined in section 4.5, is the identification of an
unknown or new data, that a machine learning system does not know about during
training. Therefore, to do a proper novelty detection, the model was trained on ’clean’
data. For the CHB-MIT data, a clean data was considered as the first record for every
patient that is seizure free. Medical signals can vary a lot between patients (Rezek &
Roberts, 1998), therefore, the model was trained on a clean recording for each patient
and then tested on a recording from the same subject, where a seizure occurs. The
aim is then to detect the seizure, using novelty detection.

7.2.2
Randomness in the model
The robust GMM takes in a random training point in every iteration, this means that
for every single run of the algorithm, the results can vary. For the sake of robustness
it was decided to run this algorithm s times. If a test point was classified as a novel
vector in any of these s runs it was marked as a novel vector. The effect of increasing
s was investigated by looking at how many new novel vectors where detected in each
run and how it affected the sensitivity and specificity. The two graphs in Figure 7.12
show how the average sensitivity and specificity change with an increasing number
of runs. The figure shows that the sensitivity is slowly increasing with more runs
while the specificity decreases more rapidly after the first few runs and then slowly
decreases. The number of new novel vectors also decreases with time. This behaviour
indicates that after few runs the algorithm has identified as many of the true positive
points, or seizure points in this case, that it can. After that, few new novel vectors
are detected and they are mostly false positives. It was decided to choose s = 5 to
try to maximize the sensitivity without taking in too many false negatives.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This chapter discusses the main finding from Chapter 7.
For simplification, the
chapter is divided as follows:
1. This section discusses the results and method performance for the CHB-MIT
data set.
2. The next section provides discussions on the results from the SBS2 data set.
There, preliminary results are referred to the ones found in section 7.3.1 on
abnormal behavior in the data. The finding for Mean absolute deviation(MAD)
from section 6.2.3 on setup for modelling will also be discussed.
3. The last section is on the Use case, for how the findings and the methods can
be used to reach the objective of this thesis.
8.1
Performance on the CHB-MIT data
The results for the CHB-MIT data can be found in sections 7.1.4 and 7.2.3. The
two methods used to detect seizures in the data set were the maximum log likelihood
and the robust Gaussian mixture model(GMM). For the first method, both the sen-
sitivity and specificity was found for the single percentage threshold and an optimal
threshold for each subject. The same was done for the robust GMM method for the
chosen set of parameters. To be able to compare the two methods, the Energy of
the signal and Frequency-Based Features(Feature set 2) will only be reviewed. The
percentage threshold will be used for comparison, since the optimal threshold is found
by optimizing the sensitivity and specificity in an individual subject.
It is difficult to assert which method performed better in seizure detection. For the
maximum log likelihood the sensitivity was equal or higher for all cases except for one
subject. However, the same cannot be said for the specificity. The robust GMM had
a higher specificity for almost all subjects, thus, being more robust for false positives.
Subject 2 is an extreme example of this, that emphasizes this difference between
the methods. The subject has 90.2% sensitivity for the maximum log likelihood and
only 64.6% in the robust GMM method. However, the maximum log likelihood gave
40.1% specificity and the robust GMM 91.4% specificity. Thus, the general difference
between the results of the two methods is that the maximum log likelihood classifies
more true positives, with the cost of classifying more false positives compared to the
robust GMM. Note that this performance of the maximum log likelihood method
==PAGE:124==
110
8 Discussion
could however be explained by the chosen percentage threshold. If a lower threshold
would have been chosen, the sensitivity might have decreased, presumably with an
increase in the specificity.
The challenge of seizure detection depends both on the sensitivity and specificity,
and was considered equally important in these experiments. The sensitivity is clearly
important, since it explains how the method performed in finding the abnormal seizure
points. However, the main objective was to shorten the time spent on interpreting
the recordings, and a low specificity indicates that there are too many false positives
that the readers would need to observe. Thus, it can be argued that the sensitivity
and specificity is equally important for the final results.
The methods are quite consistent when comparing sensitivity as well as specificity
for each subject. Subjects that had a relatively low score, in either sensitivity or speci-
ficity, were the same for both methods, except for subject 2, which was previously
discussed. This could suggest that, since both models are based on Gaussian distri-
bution, a similar performance might be expected. The sensitivity and specificity were
not sufficiently high for some subjects. It is known that the Gaussian distribution is
not robust for outliers, which might explain why the method is not able to sufficiently
capture the abnormalities of seizures for these subjects. There could also be other
reasons, one explanation could have been that the seizure points for these subjects
were so artifactual that they were removed by the artifact removal. However, the
performance was evaluated before and after the artifact removal, and the results of
the seizure detection was not affected by removal of artifacts. Another explanation
could be that the chosen feature set cannot discriminate the abnormal behavior in
the seizures for these subjects. However, the same feature set was used for the other
subjects where seizures were detected.
Even though the optimal case would be to detect all seizure points and include
no false positives, the methods can still be used. Note that a part of the seizure for
all subjects is detected since the sensitivity is always higher than zero. If the use case
is considered, where an interpreter reviews the abnormal points, then both methods
would at least show a part of the seizures.
Comparing the novelty points detected from both methods could have been an
interesting approach. For example, if both methods classify the same false positive
point as novelty. That would give a strong indication that the EEG data should be
looked at closer, as the data might show artifactual behavior or other abnormalities
that would suggest improvements to the preprocessing or artifacts removal process.
This could be done using the zero-one signal plots that were made for both methods,
however this was only implemented for the SBS2 data.
The CHB-MIT data had multiple seizure recordings for each subject. Therefore,
it would have been interesting to see how the methods would perform on another
seizure recording from the same subject. Results from such an experiment would
have given an even better conclusion to the novelty detection. However, there was
not enough time to experiment with this.

8.2
Performance on the SBS2 data
The results from the SBS2 data set can be found in sections 7.3.4 and 7.4.3. The
objective was to perform novelty detection on subjects and the hypothesis was that
subjects classified positive for epileptiform discharges(With IED) would show more
novelties. The detected novelties would then be the target for interpreters to examine.
Both methods were trained on a group of subjects classified as negative(Non IED),
considering them as ’clean’ recordings. The results were unexpected when comparing
the With IED and Non IED subjects. The number of novelties for the With IED
subjects were lower than for the Non IED subjects.
The abnormal behavior in the recordings for both groups were shown in section
7.3.1. The preliminary results were found using maximum log likelihood with mean
and standard deviation from all recordings in each group. Those experiments show
that abnormal data points, referred to as outliers, could be found in both groups.
The number of outliers found with the same threshold for both groups was on average
higher for the With IED subjects.
The training set consisted of the first 8 recordings of the Non IED subjects. The
unexpected results from the methods might be explained by the fact that these record-
ings were not ’clean’ for outliers as the preliminary results indicated. The possibility
of cleaning the training set for outliers could be considered. A single percentage of
outliers could then be removed and the method retrained. Roberts and Tarassenko
(1994) mentioned the possibility of pruning the trained network, by removal of com-
ponents with small priors for the robust GMM method. However, dealing with such
complex data, as EEG signals, it would be problematic to choose a suitable percent-

age of outliers to remove or which priors to prune. This strategy would have to be
investigated in detail.
The unexpected results could be affected by the feature extraction that was ap-
plied. The extracted features are based on Tzallas et al. (2009). There, a part of the
objective was essentially the same as here, to classify interictal behavior. However,
the SBS2 data is not labeled where interictal, or epileptiform discharges, occur. There-
fore, it cannot be determined if in fact the novelties detected in the With IED subjects
are the epileptiform discharges. Analysis of these novelties would be preferable and
clarify the method’s performance.

8.3
Practical usage
From a practical point of view the analysis and methods applied in this thesis sought
to shorten the time spent on classifying the EEG recordings. This section will high-
light how the experiments and analysis in this thesis can be used in practice.
As previously described, the SBS2 data is collected where access to EEG service is
limited. Multiple recordings are then sent to an interpreter who is not located where
the recordings are performed. The interpreter evaluated the quality of the recordings
on a scale of 1-10 and answered questions regarding the analysis of the data. Bearing
the experiments and analysis from this thesis in mind, the following steps will be sug-
gested to reach the objective of the thesis, to classify the EEG recordings and shorten
the time spent on interpreting the data.
The first step would be to improve the quality control. Using the implemented
preprocessing pipeline and artifact detection could assist in evaluating whether the
recordings are too contaminated. An appropriate limit, or threshold, of the number
of removed channels and artifacts could be found. If the threshold is exceeded, the
recording would be classified as too low in quality. This could even be implemented
during the recording, so that it could be repeated. The subject could be asked for
example to try to stay still or the person supervising the recording asked to adjust
some of the equipment.
The next step would be to start evaluating the MAD values of the EEG data. This
could be used as a global discriminant. High values of MAD should indicate which
recording is expected to have epileptiform discharges present. This would suggest
which recordings, out of the multiple ones received, the interpreter should begin to
evaluate.
The third step would then be to use the methods to locate the novelties in the
recordings. The fact that the methods find more novelties in the recordings without
epileptiform discharges is still an issue. However, the approach made in this thesis
could still be used, where it would indicate where the interpreter should start to ex-
amine the recordings.
The training of multiple subjects that are considered to be without epileptiform
discharges present might be done with a different approach. As the preliminary results
showed, it is difficult to consider them as without abnormalities or ’clean’ recordings.
One hypothesis is that since the Gaussian distribution is sensitive to outliers, the
extreme outliers in the training set could effect the model performance and thus, not
detecting the epileptiform discharges. A possible set up for modelling the data would
be to start by interpreting a subset of the recordings, until a sufficient number of
recordings without epileptiform discharges have been found. Outlier detection would
then be performed on these recordings, for example with maximum log likelihood.
Then the recordings with the least number of outliers would be chosen as the train-
==PAGE:130==
116
8 Discussion
ing set. The methods would then use that biased training set and perform novelty
detection on the rest of the recordings, as a test set. However, this might increase
the number of false positives (non epileptiform discharges) detected. Note that the
epileptiform discharges are not labeled, so it is impossible to know if the performance
of this approach would be better until an interpreter would examine the novelties.

Conclusion
McKenzie et al. (2017) assessed the ability of the Smartphone Brain Scanner-2(SBS2)
to detect epileptiform abnormalities compared to standard clinical EEG.
The article covers the barriers to access to EEG services, for low and middle-
income countries(LMICs), and shows that easier access to EEG could improve the
quality of life for epilepsy patients in the LMICs.
The article covers the barriers to EEG service for low and middle-income coun-
tries(LMICs), and shows that easier access to EEG could improve the quality of life
for epilepsy patients in the LMICs. The SBS2 can help overcome these barriers and
lead to increased availability. The recordings of the EEG data could be performed
by staff without formal EEG training and the records sent to an interpreter located
elsewhere. Currently, the work done by the interpreters is voluntary. This thesis
focuses on preprocessing and modeling the data to ease the process of analyzing the
recordings from the SBS2 system. The main objective is, therefore, to classify the
EEG recordings, shorten the time spent on interpreting the data and thereby giving
new options for LMICs .
The SBS2 data set is not labeled to indicate where epileptiform discharges occur.
Therefore, a base data set that contained labeled seizures was used. The purpose
of the labeled data set was to develop a preprocessing pipeline, analyze the effect of
removing artifacts and evaluate performance of the novelty detection methods. The
methods used were the maximum log likelihood and the robust Gaussian mixture
model. The results were promising, where for example, the Area under the curve
(AUC) for the maximum log likelihood had an average score of 0.87 and 0.83 for the
two feature sets. This showed that fitting the data to a simple Gaussian distribution
was remarkably successful for detecting the seizures. However, the abnormalities de-
tected by the methods were not only the seizure points. The steps used for the base
data set were then applied to the SBS2 data set. The results were unexpected, since
more abnormalities were detected in the recordings without epileptiform discharge
compared to the ones containing epileptiform discharge. However, the preprocessing
pipeline and the methods showed potential for reaching the objective of the thesis.
When SBS2 data is collected, multiple recordings are sent to an interpreter that
evaluates the quality of the recordings and answers questions regarding the data. The
experiments and analysis made in this thesis provide suggestions for a step wise use
==PAGE:132==
118
9 Conclusion
case to ease this process. The preprocessing pipeline could serve as quality control.
The quality of the recording would be considered too low, if many of the channels or
artifacts are rejected, and could even be evaluated while the recording is conducted.
The global discriminant, or the Median absolute deviation(MAD) value, is a good
indicator for evaluating which recordings have epileptiform discharge present. For fu-
ture work, more subjects would be needed to establish such a discriminant threshold
in order to find which recordings could be excluded. Finally the two methods would
work as a local discriminant, indicating where the abnormalities are located in each
recording.
These first steps of running experiments for the SBS2 show promising results.
From a practical point of view, this thesis contributes an analysis on how to shorten
the time spent on interpreting the data. This analysis could be used as preliminary
work for future studies on the interpretation of SBS2 data from epilepsy patients in
the LMICs.
9.1
Future work
Feature extraction for classification of EEG signals is essential.
There are multi-
ple articles that suggest different types of features, depending on various criteria for
classification. Therefore, using a different feature set could be suggested. The en-
tropy based non-linear features used by Acharya et al. (2012) might be an interesting
approach. There, positive results were obtained by using different methods for au-
tomatic detection of normal, interictal and ictal periods. Feature selection provides
a base for choosing the features which are most suitable for a specific classification
(Übeyli, 2009). For future studies, a further analysis of feature selection would be
advised.
A quality control measurement could easily be implemented using the preprocess-
ing steps from this thesis. Experiments with a wider range of quality, ranging from
1 to 10 should then be used. A suitable threshold for channels and artifacts removed
could then be chosen and recordings breaking that threshold would be classified as
unusable or non-interpretable.
Several recordings of SBS2 data have been made, but as previously stated the oc-
currences of epileptiform discharges are not labeled within the SBS2 data sets. The
optimal case would be to have a sufficiently large labeled database in order to get
more accurate results. This could help make the process even more automated for
several reasons. For example, to find a suitable threshold for the global discriminant,
or the Median absolute deviation (MAD) value, and to be able to asses the perfor-
mance of the novelty detection. More complicated methods, such as Support vector
machine(SVM), could then be a part of future studies. The long term goal would be
to make the classification of the recordings fully automatic.
