Abstract
The project seeks to further develop current Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) methods
for simulating industrial spray dryers by improving modeling accuracy of droplet trajectories
produced during rotary atomization.
Experiments are performed in a pilot plant spray dryer fitted with a 120 mm-diameter
vaned rotary atomizer operating at 15 200 and 19 000 rpm. Data on outlet parameters and
product deposition pattern is sampled for validation and comparison purposes. Direct imag-
ing of the droplet trajectories and spray patterns produced by rotary atomization is con-
ducted at ambient temperatures, indicating that air pumping effects of the rotary atomizer
might be of importance to the produced spray pattern of the pilot plant dryer in question.
A CFD model is set up based on common industrial approaches to simulate the overall
spray drying process. Good agreement is found when comparing with outlet parameters
of experiments, while less agreement is noted in numerical and experimental estimations of
product deposition fluxes on internal dryer walls and particle trajectories.
Extended simulations are carried out to evaluate the effect of air pumping on produced
droplet spray pattern, taking internal vane geometry and atomizer rotation into account.
Trajectories of smaller sized droplets are found to be highly influenced by the presence of
the rotating atomizer as it significantly impacts the main jet of inlet drying air. This effect
is concluded to be the result of the large magnitude of atomizer peripheral speed relative to
the tangential swirl component of the inlet air velocity and the amount of air pumped by
the atomizer, estimated at about 5% of the mass flow of air through the dryer.
Proper agreement between experimentally observed and numerically predicted spray pat-
terns is found using the rotating atomizer CFD setup, and a suggestion for improved bound-
ary conditions of the initial model is made from the findings. Compared to the CFD model
of industrial practice, implementing the suggested boundary conditions is found to improve,
at similar computational costs, the physical behavior of the droplet trajectories.
In conclusion, the study demonstrates the necessity for taking into consideration atomizer
rotation in CFD modeling of spray dryers fitted with vaned rotary atomizers in small scale
facilities in particular, when seeking to accurately predict trajectories and wall deposition
patterns.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Spray drying is a processing system that utilizes atomization of liquids or slurries and sub-
sequent drying of formed droplets to obtain powders for consumer and industrial products,
ranging from baby foods and milk powder to catalysts and pharmaceuticals.
The primary atomization process of the spray dryer is of utmost importance for the effec-
tiveness and efficiency of the process as well as the produced powder. Commonly, pressure
nozzles or rotary atomizers are used for industrial atomization purposes. The rotary atom-
izer utilizes centrifugal energy for atomization of liquid feedstock. Feed is introduced onto a
spinning disc or wheel, moving towards the periphery, where it is disintegrated into a spray
of droplets in contact with the drying air. Pressure nozzles convert pressure energy into
kinetic energy by forcing the feed through a small orifice, creating an unstable film that
rapidly breaks into droplets.
Due to the size of full-scale spray dryers and their hostile environments, the designs of
both atomizers and spray drying chambers are primarily of heuristic and partly empirical
nature. Accordingly, immense effort has been dedicated to developing tools for computa-
tional fluid dynamics (CFD) modeling of spray dryers over the past decades, in order to
optimize the spray dryer designs to reduce wall depositions of product and mitigate the risk
of dust explosions. Most studies have been conducted on modeling of pressure nozzle atom-
izers, with less attention dedicated to rotary atomizers (Huang et al., 2005). The complexity
of the applied models varies depending on the purpose of the simulations. For obtaining
increased insight into the mechanisms of spray drying, only a handful of attempts to incor-
porate advanced models of primary and secondary atomization, droplet breakup, collision,
coalescence, separation, agglomeration, drying, and wall deposition have been implemented
with varying success, adopting both Euler-Euler (Kuhnhenn et al., 2017a; Lin et al., 2013),
Euler-Lagrange (Anh Ho et al., 2002; Oakley, 2004; Pawar, 2014; Verdurmen et al., 2004;
Woo et al., 2008a), and Lattice-Boltzmann methods (Dietzel et al., 2013; Hölzer et al., 2009)
to simulate spray dryers and similar systems.
In terms of industrial applications, a compromise between modeling accuracy and com-
putational resources has to be made, limiting the simulations to focus primarily on drying
of droplets using an Euler-Lagrange approach, for which non-interacting spherical point-
particles are defined as droplet or particles. For simulation of spray dryers fitted with rotary
atomizers, such modeling approaches tend to neglect transient flow effects of the rotating
atomizer as well as the complex nature of the liquid breakup process associated with the
atomizer. Recent studies by Kuhnhenn et al. (2017a) and Lin et al. (2013) have attempted to
outline the effects of the rotating atomizer wheel on the produced droplet size distribution
and trajectories, although at computational costs vastly exceeding the budget for simula-
tions carried out in the industry. Accurate predictions of not only the drying kinetics of the
droplets but also their trajectories within the spray dryer are of importance, as performance
and safety of the process is highly dependent on wall depositions and the regions of occur-
rence. In this relation, initial conditions of the atomized droplets are expected to have a
pronounced effect on their trajectories, hence also drying behavior, residence time, and wall

interaction. Accurate predictions of such initial droplet conditions and trajectories would
present a major advantage in numerical modeling of spray dryers in both early and later
dryer design stages. This would facilitate a shift in the design process from being heuristic
to deductive in nature, ultimately enabling improved performance, safety, and reliability of

Only a few studies of either experimental or numerical nature have been conducted to
investigate the particle trajectories of spray dryers fitted with rotary atomizers for the pur-

obvious advantages. The purpose of such investigation should be to improve upon the sim-

prediction accuracy of present CFD models at the same level of computational cost.
1.1
Objectives
The project seeks to improve the current industrial CFD models of spray dryers fitted with
rotary atomizers by adding to the description of droplet trajectories produced by such pro-
cess. An investigation of the atomization process within a pilot plant spray dryer is con-
ducted using experimental techniques. The main goal of the project is to obtain a better
understanding of the complex atomization process and associated flow conditions of a rotary
atomizer used for spray drying purposes. Such experiences are to be used in Euler-Lagrange
spray dryer simulations of spherical point-mass particles for improved prediction of particle
trajectories of rotary atomization and quantitative assessment of the obtained effect.
Summarizing, the objectives of the study are:
1. To adopt experimental methods for investigation of droplet trajectories and spray
pattern in a pilot plant spray dryer fitted with a rotary atomizer.
2. To model the behavior of the pilot plant spray dryer using an Euler-Lagrange approach,
similar to ones found in literature, allowing for baselining and validation.
3. To conduct a numerical investigation on relevant effects of a rotating atomizer on fluid
flow and liquid breakup behavior, related to predicting droplet trajectories.
4. To improve upon the Euler-Lagrange modeling approach to spray dryers fitted with
rotary atomizers in terms of prediction accuracy of droplet trajectories, based on ex-
perimental and numerical findings on pilot plant scale.
1.2
Organization of thesis
The thesis is divided into four main parts. The first part includes the motivational and
theoretical background for the project. A brief outline of the relevant aspects of spray drying
is provided in Chapter 2 with underlying governing equations presented in Chapter 3.
The experimental parts of the study are combined in Part II, entailing two sets of ex-
perimental investigations, for which a separate chapter is dedicated to each; Chapter 4 and
Chapter 5. Part III describes the numerical procedures and results related to CFD simula-
tions of the overall pilot plant spray dryer behavior in Chapter 6 and a more detailed flow
and droplet behavior study in Chapter 7. Each chapter is concluded with a summary and
discussion on main results.

CHAPTER 2
Spray drying process
As previously motivated, prime focus will be dedicated to spray dryers fitted with rotary
atomizers and their mode of operation, hence an introduction to such process is necessary
for appreciating the theoretical, experimental, and numerical investigations performed in
subsequent chapters.
The following chapter provides a brief overview of a generic spray
drying process and the main steps involved, with special attention dedicated to the spray
dryer systems fitted with rotary atomizers. The reader is referred to the work of Masters
(1991) for an exhaustive survey of the principles of spray drying.
Spray drying is a method for transforming a slurry or feed from liquid state into dry
powder by spraying the feed into a hot drying medium. The drying process is related to
other processes such as flash drying and fluid bed drying, but offers a unique feature in
that both particle formation and drying is performed in a one-step, continuous operation
(Masters, 1991, p. 1).
Spray drying is mostly used at the end of processing lines within all major types of in-
dustries, requiring different and unique product characteristics, whether it be high quality
and sanitary powders for food and pharmaceutical uses, such as milk products, fruits, antibi-
otics, and enzymes, or high throughput of mineral ores and clays. Spray drying is a suitable
technique for handling different requirements of such industries, as the characteristics of
the final product, in terms of density, particle size, residual moisture etc. can be precisely
controlled through the stages of the process (Masters, 1991). The process of spray drying
can be broken into four stages of operation: (1) atomization of liquid feed into a spray, (2)
contact between the spray and the drying medium, (3) droplet moisture evaporation, and
(4) separation of dried powder from gas stream (Anandharamakrishnan, 2013).
The four stages will be elaborated on in the following, focusing on the first two stages of
the operation.
2.1
Atomization
The atomization of a pumpable feed to form a spray of droplets is the corner stone in the
spray drying process, distinguishing it from many other drying approaches unable to handle
liquid or slurry feedstock. The main purpose of atomization is to increase the surface area
of the feed for improved mass transfer conditions during evaporation. Hence, small droplets
are desired to increase heat, mass, and momentum transfer between the droplets and the
drying medium, preferably with uniform sizes to promote homogeneous evaporation rates
and product characteristics (Walzel, 1993). Lapple et al. (1957) defines a sequence of steps
involved in the atomization process, listed below.
1. Formation of liquid sheets, jets, or films by acceleration of the fluid.
2. Introduction of small disturbances to the liquid surface in terms of ripples, waves, or
perturbations.

3. Formation of ligaments of the liquid under effects of fluid pressure and shear forces.
4. Collapse of ligaments into droplets under action of surface tension.
5. Further breakup of the formed droplets by action of the pressure and shear forces of
the gaseous medium.
It is usually possible to categorize the atomization process into a primary stage, involving
the first four steps mentioned above, and a secondary stage, involving the last step. The
primary atomization process, also referred to as the primary breakup, is categorized as the
disintegration of the jet into droplets or globules, depending on the kinetic energy of the
liquid upon release. Various characterizations of primary breakup in terms of size and shape
of the liquid droplets depends on the ratio of pressure forces to surface forces. Depending on
the mutual magnitude of the two opposing forces, steps of the primary atomization process,
as outlined above, can be reduced or complete skipped by e.g. increasing the release speed
of the liquid (Lapple et al., 1957; Malatkar, 2010).
The secondary atomization process involves breakup of coarsely shape globules or droplets
produced by primary breakup of the jet. The ratio of dynamic pressure forces of the gaseous
medium on the droplet to the surface tension force is defined by the Weber number

2.2
Spray-air contact
Spray-air contact relates to the manner in which the spray of droplets contact with the
drying air. This is influenced by the location of the atomizer relative to the air inlet. In a
co-current flow setup, the product and drying medium passes through the drying chamber
in the same direction. Hence, droplets enter the drying chamber at the top, where inlet gas
of high temperature is also added. In a counter-current configuration, product and drying
medium enters at opposite ends of the chamber, often with the hot inlet air entering at
its base. In addition, a mixed flow approach, combining both flow design can be adopted
(Anandharamakrishnan, 2013).
For spray dryers fitted with rotary atomizers, flow configurations are almost entirely of
co-current nature, resulting in a parabolic, downward pointing spray pattern emerging from
the tip of the rotary atomizer, as the inlet jet of hot air deflects the droplet trajectories
(confer illustration in Figure 2.1). The heated inlet air is most often passed through a gas
distributor located on top of the drying chamber to create a uniform flow distribution and
to introduce a specific amount of swirl to the jet by use of swirl vanes.
The parabolic shape of the spray pattern requires the diameter of the drying chamber to
be relatively large for wall deposition of product to be avoided. Deposited material will be
prone to overheating and degrading, impacting final product quality and in some situations
resulting in dust explosions, depending on the location of the deposits.
The rotation of the atomizer wheel will also affect the flow field in the region of initial

spray-air contact. An inherent phenomenon of the wheel design under rotation is the gen-
eration of a low pressure region at the center of the atomizer. As the vanes rotate, air is
moved radially outwards creating the low pressure region. This region establishes an inward
air flow along the clearance gap between the rotating and stationary part of the atomizer,
as illustrated in Figure 2.1. As a result, air is circulated into the wheel, while liquid feed is
transported towards the wheel tip along the vanes. Air is often drawn into the wheel from
the hot inlet drying air, introducing drying and aerating of liquid feed within the wheel. This
can lead to undesired product qualities and over-drying of particles deposited on the internal
walls of the atomizer, resulting in the risk of fire and explosions (Chegini et al., 2012). The
phenomenon of air circulation is often referred to as air pumping and can have an effect on
the primary atomization process and resulting spray pattern, although the degree is largely
uncharted, and will potentially vary depending on the specific spray dryer setup (Chegini
et al., 2012; Masters, 1991).
2.3
Product formation and separation
Evaporation of moisture from the surface of the droplets starts upon contact with the hot air.
The large surface area of the spray droplets ensures a fast evaporation, keeping the droplets
at wet-bulb temperature and cooling the hot inlet gas (Anandharamakrishnan, 2013). At
high moisture contents, drying rate is governed by mass transfer of vapor from the droplet
surface to the surroundings. As drying continues, particles with crusts are formed and the
drying rate is governed by the rate of transport of moisture through the particle. Hence,
the term droplet will refer to a subdivision of the feed created by the atomizer, for which
surface moisture is present. The term particle will refer to a component of the product, with
shape and characteristics determined by its atomization and drying behavior. The particle
trajectories within the spray dryer are governed by the air flow pattern as well as the initial
trajectories upon atomization, which in turn affects the drying behavior of the particles as
well as the wall deposition rate.
The final stage of the process involves separation of final product from the air stream.
This can be done by primary product discharge at the base of the drying chamber in combina-
tion with a secondary product discharge, where a downstream separation equipment (e.g. a
cyclone) extracts the remaining product. An alternate method is to recover all the available
dried product in the separation equipment as shown in Figure 2.1 (Handscomb, 2005). Ad-
ditional configurations involving fluidized beds and pneumatic conveyors are often adopted
in commercial spray drying plants for improved control of dried product characteristics.
2.4
Spray dryer modeling
Due to the number of process stages in the spray drying process, numerical modeling is a
formidable task, difficult to simplify without failing to represent the physical behavior.
The simplest method of modeling is to apply overall energy and mass balances for the
system, rendering overall process parameters such as outlet temperature, humidity, and
product moisture available for estimation.
At the other end of the modeling scale, CFD techniques have been adopted over the past
decades for improved modeling and comprehension of the spray drying process. This level of
modeling entails simulation of both the continuous (gas) and dispersed phase (droplets/par-

ticles) for determination of particle trajectories, local drying rates, areas of wall deposition,
risk of dust explosions, and particle-particle interactions such as collision, agglomeration,
coalescence, and attrition (Handscomb, 2005).
Currently, empirical or semi-empirical models as well as the experience of system designers
and operators are used for both design and control purposes. Such heuristic approach is
prone to experience a significant amount of uncertainties, off-spec products, and risk of dust
explosions. Controlling and design of spray drying plants based on fundamental equations of
conservation can improve on some of these shortcomings. In this respect, CFD is an obvious
tool, although validation is difficult to achieve, as experimental investigations of large-scale
as well as pilot plant spray dryers present a formidable challenge due to their complexity,
hostile environment and large scales (Mujumdar et al., 2010).
One of the most widely cited studies by Verdurmen et al. (2004) on the numerical modeling
of agglomeration in spray dryers fitted with pressure nozzles, is considered a state-of-the-art
use of advanced CFD tools, incorporating numerous submodels to capture particle collision,
agglomeration, and drying.
Less advanced and computationally inexpensive simulations
conducted by Huang et al. (2004a), Birchal et al. (2006), Woo et al. (2008a), and Handscomb
(2005) have focused on improved modeling of droplet and particle drying kinetics, defining
the unofficial industry standard for such models.
Very little public literature deals with
simulation of spray dryers fitted with rotary atomizers as limited experimental data exists
on these configurations, in either full or pilot plant scale.
Some attempts to model the
process have been made by e.g. Huang et al. (2004b) and Woo et al. (2008b), adopting
models similar to those used in simulating spray dryers fitted with pressure nozzles. The
aim of these investigations have been to predict overall process parameters such as outlet
temperatures, humidities, and powder characteristics as well as to assess particle deposition
rates, using simplified analytical and empirical relations for handling the atomization process.
Recent attempts by Lin et al. (2013) and Kuhnhenn et al. (2017a) to simulate the primary
atomization of rotary atomizers using extremely computationally expensive approaches, have
provided increased insight into the complex process of droplet formation. Nonetheless, no
attempts to improve the methods proposed more than a decade ago, for simulating particle
trajectories, have been made. Thus, evaluation, investigation, and development of currently
available models and methods for simulating particle trajectories of rotary atomization would
be immensely beneficial, as accurate prediction of such will allow for improvement of spray
dryer design to reduce wall product deposition, that can lead to off-spec product quality and
risk of dust explosions.
The purpose of the study is to fill this gap in the literature through experimental and
numerical investigations, allowing for improved droplet trajectory prediction of rotary atom-
ization.

Governing equations
The process of spray drying can be categorized as a gas-droplet (particle) flow with a dis-
persed phase of particles and a multicomponent single-phase continuous phase in the form
of moist air. As the particle is subject to mass, velocity, and temperature changes during its
residence time within the spray drying chamber, equations to handle not only the continuous
phase, but also the dispersed phase are required for modeling the particle-laden flow. The
degree of these changes is highly dependent on the distance between the dispersed phase
elements. The volume fraction of the dispersed phase αd is a measure of dispersed phase
loading, generically defined as:
αd = Vd
V ,
(3.1)
with Vd being the volume of the dispersed phase in the total system volume V . A similar
expression can be written for the volume fraction of the continuous phase αc, naturally
implying that
αd + αc = 1.
(3.2)
The volume fraction of the dispersed phase can be used as a rule of thumb for determining
whether the flow is dilute (αd < 0.001) or dense (αd > 0.001). The dilute suspension enables
sufficient time for particles to respond to local fluid dynamic forces in between particle-
particle collisions. By increasing the concentration of particles, the average time between
collisions is reduced, resulting in more of the particle trajectories being controlled by particle-
particle collisions (Crowe et al., 2012, p. 28-29).
Hence, depending on the particle-flow regime, different modeling approaches and effects
are considered important. In the context of spray drying, the dispersed volume fraction is
low enough to constitute a dilute flow, although larger volume fractions are expected in the
vicinity of the atomizer, affecting the coupling between the continuous and the dispersed
phase. Coupling refers to the exchange of mass, momentum, and energy between the two
phases. Mass coupling is achieved through evaporation. Momentum coupling is achieved
mainly through drag force on the particles resulting from their movement in the continuous
medium. Energy coupling is achieved mostly through the transfer of heat between phases.
Two overall methods for handling the couplings can be adopted; one-way or two-way coupling.
The choice of method depends on the nature of the flow. If the flow of one phase (often
the continuous one) affects the other with no reverse effect, the flow is said to be one-way
coupled, while the flow is said to be two-way coupled if the phases affect each other mutually
(Crowe et al., 2012, pp. 28-30).
In the following sections, equations for the dispersed and continuous phase, necessary for
solving for the flow field and particle behavior within a spray dryer, are outlined. Special
emphasis is placed on the theoretical description on the coupling between the two phases.
The chapter is finalized with a section on the adopted techniques for numerically modeling of
such flows in the commercial CFD software STAR-CCM+ (Software Siemens PLM, 2018).

3.2
Continuous phase equations
The ideal approach in handling multiphase flows is to solve the complete Navier-Stokes (NS)
equations, or the corresponding Reynolds averaged (RANS) ones, for the continuous phase,
taking into consideration boundary conditions for every single particle or droplet in the fluid
domain. This would require resolving each and every particle in both time and space, making
it extremely time consuming to solve for the complete behavior of a spray pattern. Instead,
methods for treating the continuous phase as a single Eulerian component and the dispersed
phase as a Lagrangian phase, with a mutual coupling with respect to mass, momentum, and
energy, allows for solving particle-laden flows at significantly lower computational cost.
The commonly known RANS equations (confer e.g.
(Versteeg et al., 1995)) apply to
single-phase flows, where point velocities are decomposed into a mean and a fluctuating
component, and averaged with time. The approach yields a Reynolds stress term, requiring
closure of the equations using turbulence models for approximating this term. Modifications
to the RANS equations are needed to encompass the presence of multiple phases, for which
a volume averaging approach to the Navier-Stokes equations should be adopted as suggested
by Crowe et al. (2012). For multiphase flows, the addition of droplets or particles requires
a volume averaged representation of the continuous equations, as larger point volumes are
needed due to the presence of the dispersed phase.

3.3
Numerical modeling
Two approaches to modeling of multiphase flows are available in the current version of the
commercial CFD software STAR-CCM+ (Software Siemens PLM, 2018): the Euler-Euler
and the Euler-Lagrange approach. The Euler-Euler approach, also referred to as the two-fluid
model or the volume-of-fluid (VOF) model, considers both the dispersed and the continuous
phase to be governed by continuum fluid mechanics, solving a set of governing equations
for each phase.
The method is extremely useful when simulating flows of high particle
loadings, as the computational time does not depend on the number of particles, but rather
on the number of distinctive phases. On the other hand, particle-particle and wall-particle
interactions are not modeled in detail and the phases must be internally homogeneous,
preventing variations in material properties of phases to be defined. The minimum particle
or droplet size is directly related to the minimum size of the volume mesh. Hence, volume
cells with sizes below that of the smallest possible droplets are required to obtain a proper
==PAGE:36==
22
3 Governing equations
representation of the phase, making the approach costly for large fluid domains (Blais et al.,
2015).
For lower particle loadings, the Euler-Lagrange approach provides a faster solution
method, as particles are not resolved on the Eulerian field, but rather defined on a sub-
grid level with modeling rather than solving of phase interactions (Software Siemens PLM,
2018, p. 7359). STAR-CCM+ distinguishes between discrete element method (DEM) and
discrete parcel method (DPM). DEM considers individual solid particles, for which the gov-
erning equations are solved. As each particle is coupled to the continuous phase through
mass, momentum, and energy source terms, the DEM framework enables tracking of every
particle including forces of rotation and contact to be modeled. Solving the equation of mo-
tion for all the particles within the fluid domain provides the properties of the particle cloud.
This puts a limit on the amount of particles to be simulated, as the computational time is
proportional to the number of particles (Software Siemens PLM, 2018, pp. 7359-7360).
For large numbers of particles, a statistical approach is more appropriate to adopt, repre-
senting the total number of particles by a smaller number of parcels. Particles are grouped
in such a way that their properties are the same, allowing for the parcel to be represented
by a single computational particle in the dispersed phase equations (Crowe et al., 2012, p.
236). Illustration of the discrete parcel method is provided in Figure 3.3.
Contrary to DEM, DPM enables use of material particles for defining single and mul-
tiple liquid components to constitute the computational particles, useful for modeling of
evaporating slurry droplets in spray drying applications.

source terms as well as the dispersed volume fraction, to ensure numerical stability.

can be adopted, employing a backward differentiation approach that uses the solution at the
current time level and one (first order) or two (second order) from the previous time levels
(Software Siemens PLM, 2018, pp.

The implicit scheme is used in favor of
explicit schemes, as it is unconditionally stable for any chosen size of time-step (Versteeg
et al., 1995, p. 173).
As mentioned above, ∆t should be small enough to ensure fluid dynamic forces on the
computational particles to remain constant. Ferziger et al. (2002) suggest that the time-step
be chosen so that Lagrangian particles do not cross more than one cell during each time
marching. In STAR-CCM+, a local time-step is adopted for each parcel. The local time-
step is adjusted dynamically to control the accuracy of the Lagrangian solution. Adjustment
is conducted within substeps of the time-step, for which the number of substeps and their
individual sizes are controlled by a maximum and minimum Courant number. For a steady-
state simulation, for which the temporal derivative of the continuous governing equations
are disregarded, the particle trajectories must still be computed based on the same dispersed
equations used in transient simulations. This dilemma is circumvented using a parcel-time
that only accounts for the individual parcel movement through the steady flow field. Each
parcel is time marched using the parcel time-step controlled by the Courant number, until
all parcels have left the computational domain, or in other ways been depleted. The steady-
and unsteady-state parcel tracking methods are schematically outlined in Figure 3.3.

CHAPTER 4
Preliminary experiment
Experiments to gather empirical data on the behavior of the Niro A/S Production Minor
(PM) pilot plant spray dryer, located at the Department of Chemical and Biochemical
Engineering of the Technical University of Denmark, are conducted to capture overall process
parameters and the specific fluid flow effects related to aerodynamic behavior of atomized
droplets. Such experiments are necessary, as very little or no empirical data on spray dryers
fitted with rotary atomizers is available in public literature, for which both geometries,
boundary conditions, and operating conditions are thoroughly described.
Two sets of experiments are performed. The first set, referred to as the preliminary ex-
periments, will allow for overall process parameters, such as inlet and outlet temperatures,
pressures, flow rates, humidities etc. to be determined for the experimental rig. Such exper-
imental data will enable one-to-one comparison with predictions of numerical approaches.
The second set of experiments, referred to as the primary experiments, is to provide in-
creased insight into the behavior of the rotary atomizer with respect to the atomization
process and produced spray pattern, for improved modeling of such phenomena.
A chapter is dedicated to each experimental approach, outlining the experimental setup,
instrumentation, facility, and procedure. Within each chapter, results of the experiments
are provided and followed by a discussion.
4.1
Equipment
The Niro A/S PM pilot plant co-current spray dryer is made from stainless steel, consisting
of a cylindrical part with a height of 0.78 m and an inner diameter of 1.195 m. A conically
shaped lower part is fitted to the cylindrical part with a relative angle of 60◦and a vertical
height of 0.90 m, yielding a total inner chamber volume of 1.21 m3. The PM is fitted with a
rotary atomizer wheel composed of 24 rectangular vanes. A frequency converter allows for
easy adjustment of the atomizer speed within an operating range of 15 000 to 24 000 rpm.
Air is heated by a built-in heater located outside of the chamber. The heater supplies
an adjustable power ranging from 1.5 kW to 36.3 kW. The hot air is passed through a gas
disperser on top of the drying chamber, upon entering through its ceiling to heat up the
atomized feed. The feed, composed of liquid and solid content, is evaporated within the
chamber, increasing the absolute humidity of the air while decreasing the liquid content of
the droplets. As water is evaporated from the droplets, solid particles are formed and led
into a cyclone through a connecting piping at the bottom of the cone. Larger particles are
separated from the main air flow within the cyclone, from which particles are collected at
the bottom, and hot air is led out of the building through an exhaust piping system. Smaller
particles, not separated from the air flow in the cyclone, are collected by a bag filter.
The main fan, driving the flow through the system, is located downstream of the drying
chamber, above the cyclone. A secondary fan is positioned further downstream of the system
to help drive the flow through the bag filter.

4.4
Discussion
A series of uncertainties are associated with the presented data of the preliminary experiment.
These uncertainties are related to the accuracy of the experimental equipment, as well as to
display digitization, noise, and signal fluctuations of monitors, complicating the readings.
Due to the method of determining the dry air mass flow rate, a significant level of error
on the obtained values is expected. The hot-film sensor requires proper alignment with the
flow direction and knowledge of the specific location of the sensor, to determine the volume
flow rate via integration. The measurements are further complicated by an upstream 90◦
pipe bend, causing increased velocity fluctuations and effects of recirculation zones near
the inner pipe wall. For improved estimations, the velocity would have to be measured at
locations across the entire cross-sectional area of the exhaust pipe, rather than just along a
single horizontal line. An uncertainty level of ±0.174 m/s is estimated based on variation in
velocity readings for each measured velocity, which corresponds to approximately ±11 kg/h
in terms of moist mass flow rate. The level of uncertainty is estimated using an error prop-
agation method on the basis of the equations for calculating the mass flow rate, listed in
Appendix B. Additional uncertainties with respect to the numerical methods for estimating
the flow rate from the sampled velocity magnitudes as well as uncertainties related to ap-
proximating the flow over the entire pipe cross-section by a single profile, are not accounted
for. Despite the estimates of the dry air mass flow rates in the two operating conditions
being slightly different, the values are within the uncertainties of the measurements and
method of determination, hence found to be acceptable.
Additional uncertainties are related to the methods for determining the feed flow rate of
the slurry, reducing the values of the feed flow rates in Table 4.1 to estimations of actual
values.
The particle size distributions, displayed in Figure 4.2a, have shapes that would be ex-
pected from a Rosin-Rammler distribution, depending on the choice of exponents.
The
resolution of the particle size distribution is directly dependent on the amount of sieves and
the difference in their mesh sizes, as the technique only provides a discrete representation of
the actual particle size distribution. Hence, additional sieves to resolve high curve gradients
would smoothen the obtained particle size distribution curves.
As previously noted, and found from Table B.2, product is lost throughout the pilot plant,
most likely due to powder deposition on the internal walls of the spray dray chamber and
connecting pipes. Product deposition was found on the chamber cylinder and around the
inlet. No deposited particles were observed on the conical part of the chamber. This is most
likely attributed to high accumulation rates of product on the surface, eventually causing
lumps of dried and semi-dried product to slide down, depending on its residual moisture
and stickiness. The estimated deposition of product on the chamber walls is related to a
degree of uncertainty, as the experimental data is collected throughout the duration of both
operating conditions, hence accumulated. Additionally, the wall deposition rate is estimated
from the mass balance, based on on the feed and air flow rate estimates, hence also subject
to the level of accuracy of these values.

Primary experiment
Having conducted the preliminary experiments, and thus provided the necessary background
for understanding the drying process and for comparing with corresponding numerical sim-
ulations, additional insight to the behavior of the atomized feed is sought.
The purpose of the primary experiment is to determine the spray pattern produced from
rotary atomization within the spray dryer both in the vicinity of the rotary atomizer and
further away, enabling qualitative data for comparison with CFD predicted droplet trajecto-
ries. Further, information on the process of atomization, primary stage especially, and the
size and shapes of the produced droplets would be valuable, as only a very limited amount
of such in-situ measurements have been reported in the literature.
Several methods for spray size and pattern determination have been proposed over the
years, collected in works of Lefebvre et al. (2017), Mayinger (1994), and Tropea (2011),
and adopted by several authors, including Kuhnhenn et al. (2017b), Teske et al. (2001),
Langrish et al. (1992), and Southwell et al. (2000). Depending on the aim of the study,
various measurement techniques for non-intrusive particle characterization and spray patter-
nation exist, among which direct imaging and interferometry principles are the most widely
used. For estimation of the size distribution of a spray, measurement techniques such as
Phase Doppler Anemometry (PDA), laser diffraction, and holography can be used, yielding
information on droplet velocities, sizes, and refractive indices. A key assumption for such
methods is that droplets are spherical, which is not necessarily true for the process industry
of spray drying, as it concerns itself with liquid fragmentation in the shape of non-spherical
droplets and ligaments (Tropea, 2011). To fulfill this requirement, sampling locations are
positioned downstream of the primary and secondary atomization region, at which a cloud of
spherical droplets is expected. Even so, this can provide valuable quantitative information
on the produced particle or droplet size distribution as shown by Kuhnhenn et al. (2017b)
and Teske et al. (2001).
The use of direct imaging to investigate the velocity, size, and shape of droplets and
particles has proven useful in measurements of pilot plant flow fields conducted by Langrish
et al. (1992) and Southwell et al. (2000), as less sensitive equipment and calibrations are
needed for obtaining quantitative and qualitative data. Such measurement techniques span
from simple shadowgraphy to particle image/tracking velocimetry (PIV/PTV) and glare-
point separation with increased requirements for light sources and detector devices (Tropea,
2011). The imaging-type patternators allow for estimation of droplet locations independent
of shape or size, by directing a beam of light into the spray. Scattered light from droplets
in the spray is collected by a digital camera, allowing for evaluation of the produced spray
pattern as well as size and velocity estimation, if the speed and resolution of the camera is
sufficient.
In line with the purpose of the primary experiments, and due to the limited space within
the drying chamber of the PM pilot plant spray dryer, spray pattern investigations are
conducted using direct imaging, during operation of cold flow cases, without droplet evapo-

5.1
Setup, instrumentation, and procedure
To perform direct imaging of the spray pattern, sufficient optical access to the spray drying
chamber is required.
Only a limited number of access points are available in the origi-
nal design of the pilot plant, leaving only a few options for optical access if non-quiescent
(convective) conditions with cold drying inlet air flow are to be investigated.
Thus, a new hatch was designed and constructed with a 300 mm × 320 mm rectangular
glass window for improved optical access. The hatch was drawn up based on the overall
dimensions and outer shape of the original hatch, to ensure an air tight sealing required for
a correct inlet air flow rate. Work drawing with overall dimensions of the designed hatch is
enclosed in Appendix A.
The improved optical access provided by the rectangular glass window allows for the
directing of a laser beam into the chamber from behind the window, while simultaneously
recording droplet movements with a camera, also placed outside of the drying chamber
behind the window. Such a setup was not possible with the Ø110 mm porthole installed in
the original hatch. By placing both camera and light emitter outside of the chamber instead
of mounting them inside, arduous challenges of vibrations transmitting from fan and rotary
atomizer to the camera and wet spray contacting with sensitive electronic equipment are
circumvented.
To detect the droplet trajectories, a sheet normal to the viewing direction of the camera is
necessary, requiring a mirror to be mounted on the cylindrical surface of the inner dryer wall.
The setup is illustrated in Figure 5.1. The position of the laser sheet is mainly controlled
by a tripod allowing for manual rotation about all three axes, on which the laser and optics
are mounted. The vertical laser is sheet centered in middle of the rotary atomizer.
A stand constructed of Bosch Rexroth aluminium profiles is used for mounting and po-
sitioning the camera. Back-scattering of the light source from the stainless steel walls of
the internal drying chamber is reduced by mounting sheets of black felt on the parts of the
cylinder exposed to light scattering. Photographs of the experimental setup in practice are
enclosed in Appendix A.
The image acquisition system consists of an aCA 1300-200µm Basler ace USB 3.0 camera
with a monochrome CMOS sensor, an Edmund Optics lens with a 12 mm fixed focal length
and an F-number of 1.8, and a MGL-FN-532 continuous-wave operated 532 nm green laser of
up to 1.5 W fitted with an optical assembly for creating a vertical beam of light. The small
Basler camera has a resolution of 1024 px × 1280 px and a pixel size of 4.8 µm × 4.8 µm. The
camera is operated in global shutter mode to capture changes in the field of view (FOV) at
every distinctive frame. Using the 12 mm fixed focal length, the FOV for the given setup is
approximately 264 mm×330 mm, resulting in a resolution of approximately 258 µm/px. This
resolution is not nearly high enough to resolve the droplets created during the atomization
process. In addition, since the maximum frame rate of the camera is 203 frames per second
(fps) at fast sensor readout mode, the image acquisition setup is not able to detect stepwise
droplet motion at high image frequency and resolution. Instead the system allows for a
global determination of particle locations and spray pattern at instants.
The temporal

Experiments with and without inlet air flow are performed for different feed flow rates
and atomizer speeds, in accordance with the test-matrix of Table 5.1, in which acquisition
frame rate is also indicated for each experimental condition. Feed flow rates are determined
using the change in mass of T1 over time (confer Figure 4.1), and listed in Table 5.1. Using
the method outlined in Chapter 4, velocity at probing points within the exhaust pipe are
determined along with temperature and absolute humidity, ultimately yielding an estimated
of the mass flow rate of the inlet air. Experimental conditions at which air flow measurements
are conducted are marked by a ∗symbol in Table 5.1.
Acquisition of a sequence of snapshots for each experimental operating condition is com-
menced by bringing the rotary atomizer up to operating speed and turning on the fan (if
required by the experimental condition), allowing the flow within the chamber to develop
for a few minutes before valve V1 of Figure 4.1 is opened and the feed pump turned on.
Sampling of still images is initiated just prior to adding of the feed in order to increase the
sampling time until droplets adhere to the inner surface of the window, drastically reducing
the sharpness and quality of images.
Generally, such condition is reached after approxi-
mately 10 seconds of sampling, depending on the experimental conditions. At this point
most of the window is covered by droplets, and the sampling is ceased. To prepare for an-
other sampling sequence, the window of the hatch is dismounted, wiped, and cleansed. The
mirror, mounted on the inner cylindrical wall of the drying chamber, is wiped before the
window is put back in place and fastened to the frame of the hatch.
As the extent and time consumption of the cleansing process is significantly increased
when using a suspension of water and maltodextrin as feedstock, all experiments are carried
out using only demineralized water. Hence, effects of viscosity on the atomization process
are not investigated in the present set of experiments, although possible with the current
setup.
Although the hatch is constructed of aluminium, with a glass window capable of with-
standing temperatures above the expected drying air temperature (∼460 K), window gas-
kets, along with the felt and plastic mount for the mirror located on the inside chamber
walls, are not capable of tolerating such temperatures. Not to mention the heat conducted
through the solid hatch, increasing the temperature on its outer face to several times the
ambient temperature, raising serious safety issues when handling during cleaning between
image samplings. Hence, experiments are conducted without heated air, neglecting effects
of evaporation on droplet atomization and trajectory.
In addition to the experimental procedure of direct imaging of the spray patterns, esti-
mation of the inlet air conditions is conducted experimentally. Specifically, tufts of magneti-
cally coated, polyester-type plastic film from cassette tapes are attached to the fixed skirt or
mount of the atomizer, allowing for estimation of the degree of swirl through direct imaging.
Rotary atomizer is held stationary during the investigation and no feed of water is supplied.

LCE processing technique of direct imaging data is used as basis for further analysis of the
spray patterns.

5.4
Discussion
Estimation of the uncertainty level related to the direct imaging is difficult to quantify due
to its qualitative nature. Most of the uncertainties of the primary experiment are associated
with the inlet conditions of both feed and air. Due to the method of estimating the inlet air
mass flow rate using the hot-film probe, the average moist air flow rate of ˙mi = 438 kg/h
at Ti = 296 K is only accurate down to approximately 25.3 kg/h, using the same error
propagation methods as adopted in the preliminary experiment. In addition, the method
of feed flow determination using change of feed in the tank over time makes it somewhat
difficult to determine the actual conditions under which the direct imaging is conducted.
Results presented in the previous section should be interpreted with this in mind.
This
being said, the image sequences as well as the time averaged ones allow for clear evaluation
of the effect of inlet air on the spray pattern. This is especially the case in the far field at
which the jet of droplets starts to breakup, but to some extent also in the vicinity of the
atomizer, where the high intensity jet is observed to deflect downwards under the influence
of the inlet air. Contrary, the jet is deflected slightly upwards in the quiescent conditions.
The upward motion of droplets is most likely due to the flow induced by the rotation of the
atomizer, causing fluid flow along the underside of the atomizer from its center, accelerating
towards the edge. This secondary flow is of minor strength compared to the inlet air, thus
highly diminished during use of inlet air. No droplets were detected in the region below
the point of injection, although droplet are expected to be subjected to a pressure force
in downward direction exerted by the inlet air jet. This can be attributed to the droplet
detectability of the acquisition system being too low to capture smaller sized droplets in
this region. Even so, it is likely that the initial momentum of droplets upon shearing from
the wheel tip is large enough to prevent entrainment in the central air jet or that the jet is
affected by the air pumping effect of the rotating atomizer, to produced the observed spray
pattern.
Throughout all acquired images, more intense light scattering is observed close to the
rotary atomizer, and especially near the bottom of the vanes. The pixel intensity is found
to be the lowest in the center of the vanes, slightly increasing towards the upper face of
the vanes, at which a narrow jet of droplets is observed, confer Figure 5.5. Due to high
ratio of Coriolis relative to gravitational acceleration, the feed is presumed to flow in a thin
sheet along the rear vertical wall of the vanes, in line with observations of Kuhnhenn et al.
(2017a). Even so, the distribution of feed in the inner part of the atomizer is expected to
have a significant effect on the feed distribution within the vanes. Towards the upper and
lower corners of the rear vane walls, the straight angles of the rectangular vane, enables
feed to adhere to both walls, yielding more feed to be sheared. Such effect would lead to
increased feed concentrations and light scattering in these regions, as observed in the direct
imaging. The ejection of high concentration and high velocity feed clustered at the bottom
of the vanes could also be attributed to the air pumping effect producing an uneven air flow
distribution within the vanes, affecting the feed and atomized droplets.
Since light is scattered on all droplets on its way, regions of high droplet concentration
of the spray may deflect large portions of the laser beam, creating a difference in pixel
intensity not related to the concentration of droplets. Despite this effect being limited in
sparse regions of the spray, the high intensity jet in the lower part of the vane may result in
a shadowing effect, reducing the light scattering from droplets injected above.
Experiments were carried out at ambient temperature conditions, at which spray dryers do
not operate. Langrish et al. (1992) reported slightly different atomizer air pumping effects at

CHAPTER 6
Baseline model
The purpose of the following chapter is to set up a numerical CFD model for simulating
the behavior and performance of the PM pilot plant spray dryer for which experiments have
been conducted and overall process parameters determined. The numerical model should
be in line with most of the requirements listed by e.g. Sommerfeld (2015), Fletcher et al.
(2006), and Mujumdar et al. (2010) on modeling of spray dryers. Hence, the purpose of
the baseline model is to provide a reference of current industrial approach in simulating
of spray dryers at moderate computational costs, while laying the foundation for further
development and assessment of relevant aspects in modeling of spray dryers fitted with
rotary atomizers. The following sections provide an overview of the basic elements involved
in CFD modeling of spray dryers including geometry, boundary conditions, physics, and
discretization approaches. All numerical simulations are carried out in STAR-CCM+ 13.02
(Software Siemens PLM, 2018).
6.1
Geometry
The geometry used as input file in the CFD model is based on the overall dimensions
of the PM spray dryer.
The scope of the project is limited to simulation of the spray
drying chamber. Consequently, no attempts to model separation systems downstream of the
chamber or processes upstream are made. An excerpt of the Ø98 mm piping to the cyclone,
attached to the bottom of the cone, is included to improve numerical stability. The space
between the inner region of the chamber and its outer walls, occupied by stagnant air and
wall thickness, is not included as part of the solving domain.
As the ambient air is heated by HE1 (confer Figure 4.1), it is fed to a gas disperser
located on top of the dryer ceiling. The purpose of the gas disperser is to distribute the
flow to create as close to uniform flow conditions as possible, using guide vanes to induce a
swirling motion. As the exact gas disperser geometry is not known for the PM spray dryer,
a simple flat inlet surface is added on top of the chamber geometry, allowing for the drying
air to enter the chamber parallel to the wall of the atomizer mount.
As done also by e.g. Huang et al. (2008) and Woo et al. (2008b), the internal geometry of
the rotary atomizer is not included in the fluid domain to reduce computational costs. Main
dimensions of the fluid domain are presented in Figure 6.1a, with the diameter, radius, and

6.2
Physics modeling
A number of models are included in the CFD setup to capture the physics of the Eulerian
and Lagrangian phase. The main models for each phase are summarized in Table 6.1. Most
of the models included in Table 6.1 are used throughout all simulations of the project, only
varying in terms of whether temporal scales are resolved and in terms of the feed properties.
For the baseline model, a steady-state approach is adopted based on the governing RANS
equations, as proposed by Anandharamakrishnan (2013) for spray dryer simulations. The
governing equations are phase averaged and modified to account for the dispersed Lagrangian
phase, as described in Chapter 3. Turbulence is accounted for through the Reynolds stress
term of the RANS equation, for which closure is provided by the two-equation k-ω shear
stress transport (SST) model by Menter (1994) with a curvature correction as suggested
for such applications by Woo (2017). A 2nd order segregated convection scheme is adopted
with the SIMPLE (Semi-Implicit Method for Pressure Linked Equations) pressure-velocity
coupling implemented in Software Siemens PLM (2018).
As recommended by Software
Siemens PLM (2018, pp. 3998-4004), a fluid enthalpy model is adopted, solving the total
energy equation and computing the temperature from the enthalpy according to the equation
of state. The drying medium is modeled as an incompressible ideal gas composed of mixed
water vapor and dry air. Sutherland’s law of Software Siemens PLM (2018, p. 2975) is used
to determine the dynamic viscosity of both components, while the specific heat is defined as
a polynomial function varying with temperature at atmospheric pressure.
The Lagrangian phase is governed by the two-way interaction between the computational
particles and the continuous Eulerian phase, as recommended by Mujumdar et al. (2010).
Turbulent dispersion is included to simulate the effects of turbulent eddies on the particle

Results
Based on the proposed simulation setup, including the obtained knowledge from the parcel
and mesh study, a CFD simulation including parcel tracking through the domain of the PM
pilot plant spray dryer is conducted.
To reduce computational time, a single-phase solution is obtained by solving for the flow
and temperature field over the first 1 500 steady-state iterations. The Lagrangian solver is
switched on after 1 500 iterations, tracking injected droplets during evaporation and move-
ment within the spray drying chamber until they reach the outlet face or a wall with escape
condition imposed on it. To prevent the time consuming process of solving for particles
retained for long residence times due to e.g. recirculating zones, a maximum of 25 000 and
35 000 substeps, for the 15 200 rpm and 19 000 rpm case, respectively, is defined for solving
each particle track. A maximum residence time of 100 seconds is defined. At the end of
each particle tracking sequence, data on residence time, position, temperature, size, velocity,
mass fractions etc. is stored and extracted from STAR-CCM+ for data analysis. Simulations
are carried out for 25 000 steady-state iterations to reach a convergence in both Eulerian
and Lagrangian phases. Using this approach, simulation times on four nodes containing two
sets of Xeon E5-2670 8-core CPU’s (Central Processing Unit), each running at 2.60 GHz,
amount to approximately 24 hours.

6.7
Discussion
A steady-state Euler-Lagrange simulation setup has been set up for modeling the behavior
of a pilot plant spray dryer, for the purpose of obtaining swift and adequate results. Based
on the experimental results presented in Chapter 4, percentile discrepancies below 5% of
the Eulerian phase are observed when comparing to the obtained numerical results with
similar boundary conditions. A complete validation of the numerical setup is difficult to
establish, based solely on the overall process parameters such as outlet temperature and
humidity.
Further, the uncertainties and level of error related to the experimental data
and fluctuations in numerical simulations render it difficult to asses whether the found
discrepancies are the results of the experimental uncertainty level or the modeling approach,
or perhaps a combination of the two.
In addition to the uncertainties related to the estimation of the drying air inlet mass flow
and the inlet feed flow rate, as discussed and quantified in Chapter 4, the air disperser geom-
etry of the PM pilot plant spray dryer is unknown. As the functionality of the gas disperser
is not expected to be ideal, non-uniform flow distribution at the annular face of the drying
air inlet is to be expected. As these conditions are difficult to capture numerically without
the full disperser geometry, the swirling plug flow condition adopted for the baseline simula-
tions is bound to inflict a mismatch between the experimental and numerical findings. The
degree of this mismatch is difficult to estimate, but incorporating the tangential component
of the drying air is expected to have the largest impact on the Eulerian flow field as well as
on droplet evaporation and trajectory.
The use of escape boundary condition on all walls has an effect on the mass flow of product
through the outlet face as well as the residual moisture of recorded particles. As particles
are removed from the domain at first interaction with domain walls, their residual moisture
content is extracted as well. The liquid water in the domain entails a potential for reducing
chamber temperature, as energy from the drying air is needed to evaporate the moisture. By
evaluating the mass weighted average residual moisture content and temperature of escaped
particles on each boundary, a potential reduction in outlet air temperature ∆To can be
estimated, assuming that all water is evaporated from the particles. For the first operating
condition ∆To = 0.93 K. For the second operating condition, larger particle mass flow rates
through the upper part of the drying chamber, i.e. ceiling and upper part of cylinder, are
observed at lower field temperatures (confer Figure 6.10 and Figure 6.12b). Hence, a higher
potential for extracting energy from the Eulerian phase is found, resulting in ∆To = 1.4 K.
Nevertheless, as is it not possible to evaporate all residual moisture in particles sticking to
the walls, especially at lower temperatures, only a fraction of the temperature reduction
potential can be exploited by changing of the wall boundary conditions. Thus, difference
in outlet temperatures observed in Table 6.4 can not be explained solely by the escape
condition.
The escape condition results in a limited product mass flow through the outlet face com-
pared to the experimental case, where particles are forced through the outlet or deposited
on the chamber walls. The deposition of product on the chamber walls was found to be
0.87 kg/h in the preliminary experiment, of which most particles deposited on the cylindri-
cal chamber part. Comparing to numerical values of the solid particle mass flow rate through
the cylindrical part of the chamber, found to be 0.41 kg/h at 15 200 rpm and 0.50 kg/h at
19 000 rpm, neither coincides with the experimental values. As this comparison does not
take into account the residual moisture of the particles and their stickiness, it is not directly
applicable, since only a fraction of the particles hitting the chamber wall will deposit (Som-

merfeld, 2017). Hence, the baseline model tends to underestimate the maximum amount of
particle deposition per unit time on the cylinder, indicating that the particle trajectories are
not correctly modeled in either operating condition.
This inconsistency is further underlined by comparing the predicted droplet trajectories
of Figure 6.11 to those observed in the primary experiment shown in Figure 5.4 for n =

stressed that simulations are conducted in a heated medium, while primary experiments
are performed at ambient room temperatures with different feed compositions, hence not
directly comparable.
The use of an overall wall heat transfer coefficient for the external walls of the simulated
geometry resulted in a lowering of the outlet face temperature for both operating conditions.
As some degree of uncertainty is related to the estimation of the heat loss, the applied
heat transfer coefficient is connected to the same, or higher, level of error. Nonetheless, as
the value of the coefficient is in line with ones adopted for similar simulations, the reduced
outlet temperature is estimated to be of higher physical relevance than the one obtained for
adiabatic conditions.
As the main purpose of the baseline model is to establish a base for further development
and ensure that this base is physically founded, estimation of e.g. powder quality is not
of main concern, as this would required outlet temperature estimations to be more accu-
rate. The numerical model has shown itself capable of predicting main trends in the spray
dryer with respect to outlet parameters, especially when taking wall thermal convection ef-
fects into consideration. Some inconsistencies are found with respect to observations of the
experiments, especially with regard to wall deposition and particle trajectories.
In the attempt to establish a method for simulating the flow field and droplet behavior
within a spray dryer fitted with a rotary atomizer, a series of assumptions and shortcuts
have been made to reduce complexity and computational time of the model. The following
list entails some of the most prominent assumptions made. The list is arranged in order
of relevance for the aim of the project with respect to modeling of droplet and particle
trajectories.
• Steady-state.
• Stationary atomizer wheel.
• Empirical droplet size distribution.
• Empirical droplet release velocity.
• Spherical droplet approximation.
• Uniform feed properties.
• No particle-particle interaction.
• Escape boundary condition.
• Uniform particle shrinkage due to evaporation.

The first point concerns itself with the steady-state assumption made when adopting a
steady-state flow solver in STAR-CCM+. Due to the inherently transient nature of the flow
within the spray drying chamber, as reported by e.g. Woo (2017) and Woo et al. (2012),
steady-state conditions are not achievable, especially not when solving for the particle-laden
flow. To properly resolve the time scales of the flow, efforts should be made to model the
flow behavior using an unsteady-state RANS solver for increased confidence in the model
predictions.
One main aspect of the present method for simulating the behavior of the spray dryer is
to reduce computational cost in terms of mesh size by neglecting the air pumping effect of
the rotary atomizer, as air is allowed to circulate through the internal geometry of the vaned
atomizer wheel in the PM spray dryer. Neglecting this effect influences the flow field along
with the initial conditions of the Lagrangian phase, resulting in different particle paths and
drying patterns.
As the atomizer is held stationary, the primary and secondary breakup process associated
with the shearing of droplets at high velocities at the periphery of the atomizer wheel is
reduced to a mathematically formulated droplet size distribution. This distribution is based
on an empirical relation for the characteristic droplet sizes produced by the specific atomizer
during specific operating conditions.
As droplet sizes are crucial for their corresponding
trajectories, improved prediction of the actual droplet size distribution would most likely
improve the prediction of the overall spray dryer performance.
Although easily pointed
out, the method for obtaining information about the initial droplet size distribution requires
detailed knowledge about the atomization process and its outcomes from either experimental
or numerical approaches, proving tedious and expensive in previous studies by e.g. Teske
et al. (2001) and Kuhnhenn et al. (2017b).
Droplet velocities upon release are empirically estimated, especially the radial component,
hence associated with a large degree of uncertainty.
Increasing the initial radial droplet
velocity is expected to have a significant effect on the particle trajectories, forcing them
to escape closer to the top of the domain. Hence, uncertainties related to estimation and
prediction of the radial droplet release velocity should be of concern when attempting to
accurately model particle trajectories.
Using the DPM approach, droplets are injected and tracked through the domain as spher-
ical particles undergoing air residence and evaporation. Several investigations have been con-
ducted by e.g. Sinha et al. (2017), Domnick (2008), and Ghaemi et al. (2008) to determine
the sphericity and behavior of droplets created by different types of atomizers, suggesting
that the assumption of spherical droplets might not be valid, hence crumbling the foundation
of the adopted Schiller-Naumann drag model.
Additional effects of feed properties, particle interactions, particle porosity and drying
behavior, as well as the neglected effects of particle-wall collisions are expected to influence
the performance of the baseline model. Nonetheless, particle trajectories within the primary
and secondary atomization regions are assumed less affected by these modeling issues.
In order to obtain a better understanding of the behavior of the flow within the spray
dryer, particle trajectories, air pumping effect, and their mutual impact, transient simula-
tions including rotation of the vaned atomizer wheel will be conducted in the following. In
addition, such simulations will allow for improved tracking of the particles during drying as
well as provide foundation for evaluation of several of the assumptions at the top of the list
within the time frame of the study.

7.1
Transient flow field effects
As previously suggested, the flow field within the drying chamber is inherently transient in
terms of large- and small-scale temporal fluctuations. These effects can be included in the
CFD simulation by adopting an unsteady approach for solving the time-dependent terms of
the RANS equations. In order to simulate the flow field in unsteady state, discretization in
temporal space is performed using a 2nd order accurate implicit Euler scheme, as suggested
by Woo (2017). Using boundary conditions equal to those of Table 6.2 at the first operating
condition, the single-phase (Eulerian) flow field is solved for, not the dispersed phase. Mod-
eling of the Eulerian phase is set up in accordance with Section 6.2. The geometry of the
PM unit is used without resolving the internal atomizer geometry. Hence, the volume mesh
of Section 6.4 with a cell count of 1.8 × 106 is adopted.
To reduce computational time, a steady-state flow field is simulated until a convergence
in pressure drop over the system and the flow field has been achieved.
The transient
solver is turned on, and the simulation is run for 25 s.

7.2
Single-phase air pumping in hot flow
The air pumping effect of the rotating vaned atomizer installed in the PM pilot plant spray
dryer can be accounted for in the CFD model using several approaches offered in STAR-
CCM+ (Software Siemens PLM, 2018). Modeling of rotating objects is mostly done using
moving reference frames (MRF), sliding interfaces, or rigid body motions (RBM). The MRF
approach can be adopted in both steady- and unsteady-state simulations by translating
and/or rotating a local coordinate system with respect to the inertial one, while keeping
the mesh stationary. In terms of the Lagrangian phase, the MRF introduces a body force
acting on the computational particle. The force is dependent on the local speed of the MRF,
altering the two-way coupling source terms and particle trajectories in the entire domain
(Software Siemens PLM, 2018, p. 7368). Hence, the MRF approach can not readily be
adopted for Euler-Lagrange simulations in STAR-CCM+.
The use of sliding interfaces requires an interface to be defined in the vicinity of the rotary
atomizer, often in the shape of a cylinder, inside which the fluid is rotating. In turn, this
can introduce non-physical flow behavior in the vicinity of the sliding interface. Depending
on the application, such behavior is often negligible.
For the present case, non-physical
flow behavior near the injection of droplets could potentially alter their trajectories and
counteract the attempt to improve upon the modeling accuracy.
By resorting to the RBM approach, non-physical behavior of the flow field and droplet
trajectories are avoided by rotating the entire volume mesh in temporally discretized steps.
The following sections elaborate on the incorporation of RBM in the unsteady-state spray
dryer setup of Section 7.1, including appertaining convergence studies and results.
7.2.1
Geometry and boundary conditions
The RBM approach requires an axi-symmetric domain to ensure that fluid is not accelerated
unevenly by centrifugal forces. The axi-symmetric chamber geometry of Figure 7.5 is adopted
for the transient RBM simulations in order to account for the effect of the rotating atomizer
on the flow field. Different axi-symmetric chamber geometries were tested to ensure that
the flow field resembles that of the original geometry with a bent outlet piping to as large
extent as possible. The results of the study are included in Appendix D.
Based on the results, an axi-symmetric geometry with a straight Ø98 mm outlet piping is
chosen. To reduce potentially increasing swirl conditions within the straight outlet piping,
a vortex stabilizer is introduced into the straight pipe. The vortex stabilizer is simply an
Ø49 mm flat disc with a thickness of 5 mm, placed in the geometric center of the outlet pipe.
The vortex stabilizer is similar to that of Derksen (2003), used in the vortex finder of a
cyclone.
The internal geometry of the rotary atomizer is included in the fluid domain, with the 24
vanes and the clearance gap between the moving and stationary parts enabling air pumping
during rotation.
The height and width of the vanes are 8 mm and 5.5 mm, respectively.
Isometric exploded representation of the 120 mm-diameter atomizer wheel of the PM spray
dryer is shown in Figure 7.5. A rotating spindle constitutes the mechanical link between
the stationary part, marked in gray, and the rotating part, enclosed by the yellow colored
surface. As the flow of liquid feed into the atomizer is not modeled, the feed piping is not
included in the geometry. Additional information on the exact atomizer design and measures
is enclosed in Appendix C.

Single-phase air pumping in cold flow
Simulation of single- and multiphase flows field within the spray dryer in ambient condi-
tions allows for comparison of spray patterns obtained in the primary experiments with the
transient CFD model predictions. A setup similar to that of the previous section is used to
simulate the single-phase flow in cold case, using the boundary conditions of the primary
experiment.
To reduce the immense computational requirements and time for conducting simulations
of the full chamber domain using the RBM method, a segmented domain is adopted to
capture only the flow in the near-field of the atomizer. The segmented domain, covering a
45◦angle of the full domain and entailing 3 vanes, is shown in Figure 7.13. The height of
the segmented domain corresponds to 18.6% of the full chamber length Lc, while the wedge
stretches to 66.6% of the chamber radius Rc. The segmented domain is discretized by using
0.9 × 106 polyhedral and prism layer cells, four times fewer than those used for the full case.
Additional cells are used to refine the area close to the sharp edge of the wedge for numerical
stability, and in the jet vicinity.
Boundary conditions as defined in Figure 7.13 are imposed on the wedge domain. The
lower side of the domain is imposed with a zero gauge pressure. Ceiling, atomizer, and the
restricted cylinder wall are imposed with a no-slip condition in the stationary reference frame.
The two vertical faces of the wedge are imposed with a periodic boundary condition, linking
the two using an interphase rotating at the speed of the RBM. The interphase allows for cyclic
repetition of information across the two spatially separated faces, such that fluxes of field
properties crossing one of the boundaries are applied to the other (Software Siemens PLM,
2018, p. 1582). The inlet face is applied with a temperature of 296 K at an axial velocity
yielding a mass flow rate of 438 kg/h, corresponding to that of the primary experiments.
To compare with the spray patterns of the primary experiments at both convective and
quiescent air flow conditions, an additional simulation is carried out without air flow applied
to the inlet face. In this case, a zero gauge pressure is applied to the inlet face.
Flow fields of the two cases are compared in Figure 7.14 after 0.9 s of simulation time.
For the quiescent condition visualized in Figure 7.14a, an upward pointing air pumping jet
is observed, forcing the air towards the ceiling and down along the restricted cylinder wall.
Air is entrained into the jet from both the lower face of the wedge and the inlet face of the
chamber. A recirculation zone is produced between the ceiling and the atomizer due to the
upward flow of air. The recirculation zone is shifted away from the atomizer as flow of cold
inlet air is imposed on the inlet face.
Maximum radial and tangential velocities in the vicinity of the atomizer are found to be
approximately the same as for the hot flow case, with the ambient temperature resulting
in a lower speed of sound compared to the one of elevated temperatures. Consequently, a
maximum Mach number of 0.33 is reported.
The restricted cylinder wall affects the location and extent of the upper recirculation zone,
altering the air pumping induced jet. This is underlined in Figure 7.15, where the radial
velocity contours of the jet produced by air pumping are compared for the hot and cold flow
case, with the latter resulting from the segmented fluid domain. The air pumping effect is
found to be slightly higher in the cold flow case, constituting 6.2% of the inlet air mass flow
rate compared to 5.1% for the hot flow case. This produces a more horizontally aligned air
pumping induced jet, contributing to the result of Figure 7.15, although the main cause is
suspected to be the domain size restriction.

7.5
Improvements to baseline model
Results of particle-laden transient simulations presented in the previous section are confined
to a more qualitative evaluation, as parcel tracking over long sampling periods are required to
obtain results of statistical independence. Thus, tracking of DPM particles in unsteady-state
conditions is not a viable solution in the industry, generally dealing with short term projects.
Instead, suggestions for incorporating the effects observed from the primary experiments
and the extended transient simulations are made to improve the accuracy of the baseline
model with respect to particle trajectories and wall deposition predictions.
Woo et al. (2012) and Woo (2017) have proposed to account for atomizer rotation by
applying a local slip velocity tangential to the surface of the atomizer without modeling the
inner geometry. Very limited changes to the flow field are observed using this approach, as
the influence on the near-field by the atomizer is obtained only through viscous fluid effects.
As indicated by previous simulations, the main effect on the flow follows from translation of
air radially outwards along the vanes, and imparting it with a high tangential velocity upon
shearing at the wheel tip. Neglecting rotation and internal geometry of the atomizer, such
effect can be resembled by defining a surface on which air is added to the fluid domain with
a radial and tangential velocity component. The latter should correspond to that of the
peripheral wheel speed and the former can be defined to yield the mass flow rate of the air
pumping effect. Figure 7.19 illustrates the location of such velocity inlet face in the shape
of a rim (marked in orange). The height of the inlet rim face is set equal to that of the vane
height.
To satisfy the overall mass balance of the spray dryer, the flow of air into the domain by the
radial surface velocity should be extracted elsewhere in the domain. This can conveniently
be done by defining an outlet rim face placed at the location of the inlet to the clearance
gap, illustrated in Figure 7.19 (marked in light blue). The underside of the atomizer, not
including the the spindle screw, is imposed with a rotating no-slip wall condition.

the first operating condition using the non-adiabatic wall thermal specification. Averaged

7.6
Discussion
Investigations of transient effects within the spray drying chamber have been conducted for
both stationary and rotating atomizer conditions.
From the stationary unsteady-state simulations of the PM spray dryer vortex break-
down of the swirling inlet air jet was found in single-phase conditions, not observed during
steady-state particle-laden simulations. Single-phase solutions of steady- and unsteady-state
simulations were compared, suggesting that the steady-state approach performs rather well
on average, considering the vast difference in simulation time.
As reported by Woo (2017), rotation of the atomizer is found to stabilize the highly
fluctuating flow field found when only simulating the central inlet jet.
Simulations with
rotating atomizer was conducted for significantly less physical time than the corresponding
stationary atomizer simulations. Hence, potential large-scale temporal variations in the flow
field during atomizer rotation are not captured. Still, the stabilizing trend of Figure 7.10a
is expected to continue with increased simulation time.
Rotation of the atomizer produces circulation of air within its internal geometry resulting
in high tangential and radial air velocities at the tip of the wheel. Despite tangential fluid
velocities reaching up to 111 m/s near the atomizer tip, resulting in Mach numbers of up to
Ma = 0.33, no effects of air compressibility were encountered.
The air pumping effect of the atomizer was found to be 5.1% of the mass flow rate of hot air
through the inlet face. A higher ratio of 6.2% was found for the segmented cold flow domain.
The difference is mostly attributed to the domain size restriction, as the periodic boundary
conditions and the limited amount of resolved vanes will influence the flow field and averaged
mass flow estimation. Additionally, effects of temperature and air compressibility can have
an effect, although not directly observed from the results.
The normalization of the air
pumping effect with respect to the inlet air mass flow rate is associated with the uncertainty
level related to the estimation of the mass flow rates from experiments. Langrish et al. (1992)
found higher air pumping effects (between 9 and 17%) for a similar sized atomizer operating
at higher speeds. The amount of air pumped by the atomizer per unit time is expected to
increase with increasing rotational speed, hence increasing the ratio of air pumped mass flow
rate to that of the central jet, although at a decreasing rate due to increased pressure drop.
As a result of the modified flow field, different particle trajectories and spray patterns
were observed upon modeling of the atomizer rotation. Behavior was found to be similar
for the hot and cold cases, with the cold flow cases of quiescent and convective air flow
conditions allowing for comparison of numerical spray pattern predictions with observed ones
of the primary experiments. Good agreement with quiescent and convective experimental
spray patterns was found in the cold flow setup, despite using a wedge segment of the full
chamber domain to reduce computational costs. As touched upon by (Woo, 2017, p. 24-
25) a segmentation of full chamber domain leads to restricted flow behavior in terms of
transient fluctuations and turbulent structures. Nevertheless, the flow field of the eighth
wedge segmented fluid domain is found to provide an acceptable average representation of
the actual flow field in the upper part of the domain, especially as the air pumping effect is
found to reduce vortex shedding in the wake of the atomizer.
Regardless of the attempts to reduce simulation times, particle-laden simulations were
restricted by the time frame of the project. Hence, steady-state conditions with respect to
both the Eulerian and Lagrangian phase were not achieved. The flow fields were found to
settle quickly, while thermal equilibrium in the Eulerian phase requires additional simulation
time to reach convergence. Results of the multiphase simulations in unsteady-state were
==PAGE:111==
7.6 Discussion
97
obtained with a limited number of parcel injections to reduce computational costs. As in
the steady-state baseline model, convergence studies on the number of injected parcels and
the injection frequency should be conducted to ensure generally valid results, although such
study is of lower priority than achieving steady-state conditions in the boundary flux of
parcels.
The presented results of the transient Euler-Lagrange simulations should be viewed in
light of the uncertainties related to determining the boundary conditions, including feed flow
rates, inlet air mass flow rates, and uniformity of inlet air pattern, as previously touched
upon. This is especially the case when comparing experimental and numerical results. The
predicted flow field within the rotary atomizer was found to be highly symmetric due to the
uniform inlet plug flow of drying air and the neglected effects of supplied feed.
No attempts were made to model the pulsating flow of the feed produced by the variable
speed pump in the experiments, as lengthy simulation times would be required. Hence, some
artifacts of the pulsating behavior are prone to be incorporated in the time averaged spray
patterns of the experimental data, not accounted for in comparison with the numerically
predicted ones.
The suggested modifications to the boundary and inlet conditions of the baseline model
yield predictions in better agreement with those of the transient hot flow case. Significantly
higher particle escape rates in the upper part of the chamber are predicted by the modified
baseline model due to the altered particle trajectories. Where the baseline model underpre-
dicts the cylindrical wall deposition, the modified model greatly overpredicts it, comparing
with the estimated rate of the preliminary experiment. Generally, both models will tend to
overestimate the wall deposition on the cylindrical chamber wall due to the escape condition,
not considering stickiness of particles and their ability to deposit on the wall. Estimation
of the radial velocity component accounting for the air pumping effect of the atomizer in
steady-state conditions requires information on the size of the air pumping effect, as it will
depend on the size, geometry, and rotational speed of the atomizer.
The ratio between
the inlet air and the air pumping flow rate is not expected to be constant during varying
atomizer sizes and geometries as well as chamber sizes and operating conditions. In addition,
a fixed temperature is imposed on the inlet rim face, not necessarily representative of the
true physics, presumably leading to higher drying rates on the seeded droplets compared to
actual conditions.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
A series of experimental and numerical investigations of the Production Minor pilot plant
spray dryer have been conducted in the attempt to map the overall behavior and specifically
particle trajectories caused by atomization and spray-air contact.
Quantitative data has been collected from the preliminary experiments in terms inlet and
outlet properties of flow and particles. The primary experiments allowed for a look into the
process of rotary atomization in cold flow conditions for varying operating conditions. The
purpose of the experimental part of the study was to not only provide a suitable basis for
validation and comparison of CFD simulations of the PM spray dyer, but also to provide a
better understanding of the atomization process for improved modeling.
As the operating conditions in terms of inlet mass flow rates of feed and drying air
were estimated from methods associated with a pronounced level of uncertainty, validation
and even proper comparison with numerical results proved to be a non-trivial task. As the
obtained boundary conditions were implemented in the CFD simulations, it must be stressed
that all presented results on the PM spray dryer should be considered with this in mind.
Ideally, validation of the CFD simulations should be based on quantitative flow field data
obtained within the spray dryer chamber. Such information is difficult to obtain. Some at-
tempts to perform measurements gathering flow field data, mostly in single-phase flow fields
of pilot plant spray dryers, have been reported in the literature. Nevertheless, comparison
with CFD predictions and such quantitative flow field data requires proper information on
the geometry and boundary conditions, which is rarely provided. Hence, main validation
of the numerical part of the study has been based on comparing predictions of the sim-
ulated outlet conditions to those obtained experimentally. Such foundation of validation
can be considered slightly fragile, as different flow field behaviors can lead to similar outlet
properties.
The results of the primary experiment performed in ambient conditions can not be directly
applied to elevated temperatures of operating conditions. As argued, evaporation effects
can be neglected to some extent due to the high initial droplet momentum, at least for
larger sized droplets. Regardless of the validity of this assumption, the experimental cold
flow spray patterns allowed for direct comparison with predictions of transient rotating
mesh simulations.
As agreement between the two was observed, the basis of numerical
validation was extended from being limited to chamber outflow conditions to incorporating
qualitative flow field data. From this comparison, increased confidence in the flow fields
and droplet trajectories predicted by the same CFD model at elevated temperatures of pilot
plant operating conditions is obtained. Although yielding proper qualitative data, the setup
of the primary experiment was not able to provide data of sufficient quality to quantify on
droplet speeds, shapes, or sizes during atomization. Hence, all reported results of particle-
laden simulations were obtained using the same method of seeding Lagrangian, spherical
droplets based on a predefined size distribution.
Based on both numerical and experimental investigations, the atomizer rotation is found
to have a pronounced effect on the spray dryer flow field and particle trajectories. The air
==PAGE:116==
102
8 Discussion
pumping effect was found to produce a horizontal jet from the atomizer vanes with a high
radial and tangential velocity. In combination with the inlet of drying air, either hot or cold,
a jet angled relative to horizontal was observed, stabilizing the otherwise highly fluctuating
central air jet. The amount of air pumped by the 120 mm-diameter vaned atomizer wheel
rotating at 15 200 rpm was found to be 5.1% and 6.2% of the drying air throughput at 459 K
and 296 K, respectively. The difference in the two values is most likely attributable to the
segmented wedge fluid domain used to obtain throughput values in the cold flow case.
The transient air pumping effect was considered crucial for the behavior and modeling
of the PM spray dryer. Several authors including Chegini et al. (2012) and Woo (2017)
have argued that the air pumping effect will be less relevant in large scale spray dryers for
which different chamber dimensions, drying air throughputs, feed flow rates and so on tend
to be different from those of the pilot plants, both absolutely and relatively speaking. Even
though the relative magnitude of the air pumping effect is likely to decrease with increasing
dryer size, the air flow pattern in the vicinity will be disturbed on all scales of equipment
size. Thus, it is important to account for the air pumping effect in CFD modeling of spray
dryers of different scales, as it is prone to impact droplet trajectories and patterns of product
wall deposition, with the prediction of the latter being crucial in estimations of final product
quality and risk of dust explosions.
Other investigations would also be highly relevant in terms of evaluating the accuracy of
some assumptions made, specifically with respect to the adopted droplet size distributions
and escape wall conditions.
Such simulations would require additional and longer CFD
simulations, hence left to future studies.
As computational time is generally an issue in modeling of spray dryers including drying of
droplets and particles, the transient simulations incorporating rotation of the atomizer wheel
are not a viable approach for short term projects. The significantly less computationally
expensive steady-state approach of the baseline model was modified to artificially encompass
effects of transient air pumping. The suggested boundary condition modifications allowed
for improved modeling of the spray pattern, comparing with results from both the primary
experiments and the transient simulations. The downside to the proposed alteration is that
it requires information on the air pumping effect of the atomizer in operating condition
in order to estimate the radial velocity component to be imposed on the surface of the
atomizer. Such information can be troublesome to acquire for large scale dryers fitted with
rotary atomizers, in which case additional CFD simulations, analytical estimates, literature
reviews, or similar should be undertaken.
==PAGE:117==
CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The objective of the study was fourfold. Firstly, to experimentally determine overall process
parameters of a pilot plant spray dryer fitted with a rotary atomizer and to investigate the
produced spray pattern. Secondly, to develop a CFD setup based on operating conditions
of the experiments for baselining and validation purposes. Thirdly, to conduct a numeri-
cal investigation on relevant effects in relation to predicting droplet trajectories of rotary
atomization using CFD. Fourthly, to modify the baseline model using gathered data and
knowledge for improving prediction of particle trajectories and wall deposition pattern at
low computational costs.
The preliminary experiments were used as foundation for subsequent experiments and
CFD simulations under operating conditions of the Niro A/S Production Minor pilot plant
spray dryer. Results of the preliminary experiments were confined to data on overall process
parameters including inlet and outlet temperatures, humidities, and mass flow rates. Wall
deposition was found to be quite extensive for the selected operating conditions of the pilot
plant spray dryer, with the most of the product being deposited on the cylindrical wall of
the drying chamber.
Primary experiments were conducted to further investigate the spray drying pattern and
droplet trajectories in cold flow conditions using direct imaging.
A hatch was designed
and constructed to provide sufficient optical access to the chamber.
Experiments were
successfully carried out for various operating conditions to outline the effect of feed flow
rate, rotational speed, and inlet air on the produced spray pattern. Based on the qualitative
results of the experiments, only minor differences across selected operating parameters were
detected with the setup in question. An uneven distribution of feed was found along height
of the vanes clustering near the corners, presumably due to the liquid feed and air flow
distribution within the vanes and internal atomizer geometry. Inlet air was found to have
a pronounced effect on the spray pattern, although direct imaging revealed effects of either
initial droplet momentum or atomizer air pumping, preventing droplets of all sizes from
being convected in vertical downward direction by the central air jet.
Based on the geometry of the Production Minor spray dryer and boundary conditions of
the experiments, a CFD model was set up using a steady-state RANS approach with a k −ω
SST turbulence model in an Euler-Lagrange framework.
The setup allowed for tracking
of spherical liquid droplets undergoing evaporation within the chamber. Simulations were
performed at atomizer speeds of 15 200 and 19 000 rpm, similar to those of the experiments.
Agreement between CFD predicted and experimental outlet temperatures and humidities
was found, especially when applying an empirically estimated heat transfer coefficient of
4.76 W/m2K to the dryer walls. Nevertheless, wall deposition fluxes on the cylindrical part
of the drying chamber were severely underpredicted by the baseline model, while particle
trajectories did not resemble those of experimental findings for smaller sized droplets. Con-
sequently, validation of spray drying models using CFD techniques should be based on a
complete set of empirical data including internal airflow patterns rather than simply com-
paring predicted and experimental values at the outlet, as different drying behaviors can be
==PAGE:118==
104
9 Conclusion
observed for approximately the same outlet values.
Additional numerical modeling was performed to address the inconsistency between the
experimental data and the baseline model predictions by adopting a transient simulation
approach with moving meshes simulating the atomizer rotation at 15 200 rpm. Simulations
were carried out in full three-dimensional and segmented chamber domains at both elevated
and ambient temperatures for comparison with baseline predictions and experiments. Single-
phase air pumping effect was found to be between 5.1% and 6.2% of the inlet air throughput
in hot and cold conditions, respectively. Proper agreement between primary experiments and
numerical prediction of the produced spray pattern was found when incorporating a rotating
atomizer in the CFD model. The results suggest that both the radial and tangential velocity
component, imparted to the air during circulation through the internal of the atomizer, is
of importance to the droplet trajectories. As the steady-state solution provided results in
line with those of the time averaged unsteady-state simulation under stationary atomizer
conditions, improvements to the baseline model were sought to keep computational costs
at a minimum.
Hence, the baseline CFD setup was modified by imposing a radial and
tangential velocity component to the surface of the atomizer. Spray patterns resembling
those of the transient simulations were observed and an overprediction of the experimental
wall deposition rate on the cylinder was found, in line with expectations when using DPM
escape condition on the walls. Outlet temperatures were lowered by a few degrees compared
to the baseline model, thus less consistent with the experimental value. Additional efforts
should be made to improve upon the suggested modeling technique.
In conclusion, the study shows that atomizer rotation has a significant effect on the
spray and wall deposition pattern.
The necessity of including the air pumping effect in
numerical modeling will vary depending on the scale of the spray dryer. Nevertheless, effect
on both particle trajectories and wall deposition patterns will be experienced in any case,
if no attempts to reduce the air pumping effect have been made. Hence, it is of utmost
importance that the air pumping effect on particle trajectories be taken into account in one
way or the other, if accurate and physically sound predictions of wall deposition, vital to
estimating final product quality, are to be obtained.
==PAGE:119==
CHAPTER 10
Further work
Further work on both the experimental and numerical part of the project should be con-
ducted to increase the generality of the results, by spanning a larger range of operating
conditions and modeled physics.
Due to the limited time frame of the project, convergence in the Lagrangian and Eulerian
fields was not obtained for the multiphase transient simulations.
To provide additional
insight into the difference in assuming steady-state as opposed to unsteady-state conditions,
these simulations should be carried out until adequate convergence has been obtained.
With regard to the Production Minor pilot plant spray dryer, the air pumping of the
rotating atomizer was presumed to have the most influential effect on the droplet trajectories
in the vicinity of the atomizer. Hence, additional effects of wall-particle interaction and
atomization process of feedstock have been left for later studies. The particle rebound effect
on the walls would be a valuable contribution to the CFD model in comparing predicted
wall deposition fluxes to those estimated from experimental data.
The use of a Rosin-
Rammler initial droplet size distribution is a rather crude approximation to the atomization
process. Further work would naturally include a two-fluid simulation of the internal atomizer
geometry to evaluate the validity of the statistical droplet size distribution as well as to
determine the effect of the liquid feed on the flow field behavior and air pumping jet.
Scale-up of results obtained at pilot plant scale is not a straightforward task without
additional modeling. Hence, further work would entail simulations of large scale spray dryer
facilities to determine whether the presented results for the small scale pilot plant can be
applied directly to larger scales, and if not how scaling of the results might be performed.
In terms of experimental work, the described setup with the constructed hatch can be used
to conduct experiments at proper in-situ conditions during evaporation of liquid water as well
as slurries of suspended solids, for improving the validation foundation of the CFD model.
Additionally, the use of experimental equipment of higher quality would allow for increased
acquisition speeds and image resolution to obtain information on droplet sizes, velocities,
and shapes during the stages of atomization. Such data would provide a quantitative basis
for further validation and development of the present CFD modeling techniques.

APPENDIX D
Investigation of axi-symmetric
chamber geometries
In modifying the chamber geometry to fulfill the axi-symmetric requirement of the RBM
approach in STAR-CCM+ it is important that the flow field resembles that of the original
geometry to as large an extent as possible. An axi-symmetric geometry with a straight Ø98
mm outlet piping stretching 178 mm downwards is proposed, referred to as the Straight
case. To reduce potentially increasing swirl conditions within the straight outlet piping, a
vortex stabilizer (VS) is introduced into the straight pipe, referred to as the Straight VS
case. The vortex stabilizer is simply an Ø49 mm flat disc with a thickness of 5 mm, placed
in the geometric center of the straight outlet pipe.
The original chamber geometry with the bent outlet pipe is simply referred to as the Bent
case. Simulations of the flow field within each of the three drying chambers are conducted
at a time-step of ∆t = 5.0 × 10−4 s. A total physical time of 30 s is simulated, averaging
over the last 15 s to obtain a representative time averaged flow field.
Results of the study are presented as time averaged Line integral Convoluted (LIC) flow
fields in Figure D.1 and quantified using plots of velocity magnitudes along different profiles
in Figure D.1. Generally, similar flow fields are observed with respect to the location of the
vortex breakdown and connected recirculation zones in the cone of the chamber. A leveling in
the flow field near the bottom of the cone is observed for the flow fields of the axi-symmetric
geometries, although having little or no effect on the flow field further upstream.
Some differences are observed in the horizontal velocity magnitude profile and the vertical
centerline profile. Nevertheless, uncertainties related to the time-averaging process make it
difficult to distinguish the profiles from each other.
The pressure drop across the chamber is reduced from ∆P = 140.6±2.9 Pa to ∆P = 48.8±
1.1 Pa, when adding the straight pipe, as a result of the removed separation zone located
along the inner bend of the bent pipe. A more similar pressure drop to that of the Bent case
is found for the Straight VS case, experiencing a pressure drop of ∆P = 133.7 ± 2.4 Pa.
