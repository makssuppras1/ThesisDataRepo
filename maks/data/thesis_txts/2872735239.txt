Abstract 
The rapid increase in Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) presents significant 
environmental and economic challenges. With the aim of reducing the amount of Electrical and 
Electronic Equipment (EEE) to be disposed of or recycled, this study explores strategies to reduce 
electronic waste by analyzing and assessing repair-friendly product design, spare part 
management methods, and efficient repair processes in Europe. Key focus is on product design 
strategies such as modular concepts, design for disassembly, and optimal placement of wear-out 
parts. Additionally, the research assesses sustainable business models that ensure actual repair. 
This is divided into commercial product repair, manufacturer-controlled product repair, free repair 
organizations and customer self-repair supported by manufacturer provided manuals. 
Additionally, empirical data from the Copenhagen Electronics Repair Project (CERP) are used. 
CERP is initiated by the author of this work and conducted as a part of this thesis. A total of 133 
products were physically analyzed, documented, and assessed for repairability. Key variables 
such as repair success, fault category, spare part costs, product age, and repair time were 
evaluated. Statistical methods, including ANOVA and regression analysis, were used to identify 
economic patterns and influencing factors. 
The findings reveal that 84% of products could be successfully repaired, with electrical and 
mechanical issues being common, but dirt-related defects surprisingly accounting for the largest 
share. Only 42.8% of the devices required spare parts, underscoring the importance of non-
material interventions such as cleaning and simple adjustments. The study also explores 
correlations between product design and repair cost, age, and time value. Interviews with 
stakeholders provide additional insights into consumer behavior, repair motivations, and barriers. 
Based on the results, targeted design improvements, policy recommendations, and repair- 
supportive business models are proposed to enable repair at scale. The work contributes a 
comprehensive case study that supports the transition toward a more repair-oriented circular 
electronics economy.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background & Problem Statement 
Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) is among the fastest-growing waste streams 
globally, creating substantial environmental, economic, and social challenges (Balde et al., 
2015). The disposal of electronics contributes to resource depletion, toxic emissions, and 
increased landfill volumes (Kumar et al., 2017). In the context of the circular economy, such 
inefficiencies contradict the core objectives of reducing material input, maximizing product use 
phases, and keeping resources in circulation for as long as possible. Repair, as a life-extending 
strategy, plays an important role in transitioning from linear production and consumption models 
toward circular economy systems. 
Compared to recycling or remanufacturing, repair maintains the highest degree of product 
integrity and functionality, thus yielding the greatest environmental benefit (Wieser & Tröger, 
2018). By extending the use phase of products, repair contributes to what the European 
Commission describes as "slowing the loop". Lengthening product lifetimes through design for 
durability, reparability, and ease of maintenance. This complements "closing the loop" strategies 
like recycling, which seek to reintroduce materials at the end of a product's life (Knobloch, M., 
n.d.). When successful, slowing the loop reduces the material intensity per unit of service over 
time, making the product lifecycle both longer and leaner as shown in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: This diagram adapted from Knobloch, M., (n.d.) illustrates the two central strategies of a circular economy: 
closing the loop and slowing the loop. Closing the loop refers to recycling and material recirculation, while slowing the 
loop emphasizes extending product lifespans through design for durability, reuse, repair, refurbishment, and 
remanufacturing. Together, these approaches reduce material throughput and environmental impact by maximizing the 
use-phase of product. 
Despite its potential, repair remains underutilized. Many electronic products are discarded 
prematurely due to design barriers that make disassembly difficult or impossible. In parallel, the 
economics of repair are often unfavorable. The service is performed in high-wage countries, while 
the manufacturing of new devices occurs in low-cost regions (McCollough, 2009; Bovea et al., 
2017; Diddi & Yan, 2019). The price of repair has grown far more rapidly than that of new products, 
making it less attractive both for consumers and businesses (King et al., 2006). From an industry 
perspective, repair is often seen as unprofitable and incompatible with fast-paced market cycles 
(Maitre-Ekern & Dalhammar, 2016). 
Additionally, Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) commonly restrict access to spare parts, 
service manuals, diagnostics, and software. These restrictions, reinforced through legal and 
technical means, serve to inhibit third-party repair and reinforce consumption-based business 
models (Wiens, 2018; Deloitte, 2016). In some cases, OEM lobbying actively works against the 
implementation of right-to-repair legislation and product transparency, seeking to preserve 
unidirectional product cycles (Christaller, 2024; Rossmann, 2020; Zhilyaev et al., 2021).

As a result, the repair sector has contracted over recent decades (McCollough, 2009). Products 
are increasingly treated as short-lived consumables (Sabbaghi et al., 2017), and the concept of 
obsolescence (whether planned, functional, or psychological) has become embedded in both 
design and consumer culture (Rivera & Lallmahomed, 2016; Hennies & Stamminger, 2016; 
Svensson-Hoglund et al., 2021). In addition, software-based restrictions, such as firmware locks 
that block third-party components, further reduce repair feasibility (Barte et al., 2016). 
Most of these repair barriers originate at the design stage. Complex and integrated components, 
glued housings, and proprietary fasteners hinder disassembly and component replacement 
(Deloitte, 2016). In contrast, modular product design is widely recognized as a key enabler of 
repair. By allowing individual parts to be replaced or upgraded without discarding the entire 
device, modularity supports multiple circular economy goals: longer use cycles, lower material 
throughput, and easier remanufacturing (Sonego et al., 2018). 
Product complexity often undermines repairability (Sabbaghi et al., 2016; Bovea et al., 2017; 
Wieser & Tröger, 2018). As product functions multiply and form factors shrink, maintenance 
becomes more difficult. To address these challenges, policymakers and researchers have 
advocated for standardized repairability scoring systems, particularly within the EU (Dalhammar 
& Richter, 2017) and have explored optimal design lifespans in the face of rapid technological 
advancement (Qi & Sawhill, 2014; Jia & Zhang, 2013). 
Repair also offers socio-economic benefits beyond material efficiency. It can improve brand 
perception, especially when companies provide repair services directly, which reinforces 
consumer trust and brand loyalty (Munten & Vanhamme, 2023). On the labor side, repair work is 
often cleaner, more skilled, and less physically demanding than recycling, enhancing worker 
satisfaction and offering better training opportunities (Lepawsky et al., 2017). Additionally, repair 
can educate consumers about product function and design, potentially leading to more 
thoughtful purchasing behavior and greater demand for durable, well-constructed goods (Pérez-
Belis et al., 2017; Laitala et al., 2021). 
This thesis addresses this gap by investigating the practical, economic, and design-related 
dimensions of repair through a detailed case study: the Copenhagen Electronics Repair Project 
(CERP). CERP is a volunteer-based refurbishment initiative developed and initiated by the author 
in collaboration with the City of Copenhagen and local recycling centers. This unique setup 
provides insights into products that would have otherwise been treated as waste, allowing a rare 
view into the reasons why products are discarded and what makes repair viable. 
The goal of this thesis is: 
1. Develop and implement a volunteer-based electronic waste repair system (CERP) in 
collaboration with the City of Copenhagen, aiming to create a replicable model for future 
initiatives. 
2. Investigate design strategies that enhance product repairability by making disassembly 
and part replacement easier. 
3. To derive recommendations for design, policy, and business models that could help 
enable repair at scale and reduce electronic waste. 
4. Assess the economic and environmental viability of repair-based approaches in Denmark 
by evaluating costs and break even points for consumers and repair business, aiming to 
find win-win-win situations where consumers, business and the environment all benefit.

5. To analyze the repair success rate and fault types of 133 examined devices, revealing 
patterns in design flaws, maintenance neglect, and common failure points 
In addition to quantitative analysis, this study incorporates qualitative data from interviews with 
stakeholders involved in CERP, manufacturers, and sustainability initiatives. This mixed-methods 
approach provides a comprehensive view of the interplay between design, economics, and 
consumer behavior in shaping repair outcomes. 
By focusing on the real-world conditions of discarded electronics, this thesis aims to contribute 
to the emerging field of sustainable product design and to inform efforts for a more repair-friendly 
circular electronics economy. Ultimately, it seeks to move the repair discussion beyond theory, 
showing where practical interventions can make meaningful change. 
1.2 Research Questions 
• 
RQ: How can product design and repair strategies contribute to reducing electronic waste 
and extending product lifecycles? 
• 
RQ1: How can repair-friendly product design strategies, including modular concepts, 
design for disassembly, and optimal placement of wear-out parts, enhance product 
longevity and reduce electronic waste? 
• 
RQ2: What are the key challenges and opportunities in spare part management, and how 
can sustainable business models support long-term spare part and documentation 
availability to facilitate repairs? 
• 
RQ3: What are the most effective pathways for electronic product repair, and how can 
volunteer-based refurbishment initiatives contribute to reducing waste and extending 
product lifecycles? 
1.3 Literature review 
The repairability of electronic products is gaining momentum as a practical and necessary 
strategy in the broader shift toward circular economy principles. With growing concern over the 
environmental and social costs of electronic waste, researchers, policymakers, and practitioners 
are increasingly exploring ways to extend the lifespan of products during their use phase. Across 
the literature, a few key themes have emerged: the importance of product design, particularly 
modularity; the role of supply chains and spare part logistics; the diverse range of repair models, 
from grassroots initiatives to commercial ventures and the behavioral and policy factors that 
shape repair culture. This thesis contributes to these ongoing discussions with a particular focus 
on repair models and economic attractivity of repair in Denmark. It explores how better product 
design, effective repair pathways, and locally grounded initiatives can reduce e-waste and which 
repair models are suited to give a viable alternative to product disposal and purchase of new 
produced electronics. It also analyses a set of data collected in CERP on repaired electronics, 
faults and repair cost in connection to new price and age. On these data, statistical analyses 
including clustering, histograms, regression and ANOVA analysis including post-hoc-test is 
conducted. Even though there are bigger datasets in literature and on open source platforms such 
as the open repair alliance, none of these collects that many different datapoints on price, value, 
repair cost and age as the dataset from CERP which was conducted as part of this work. To 
compare the data of CERP, some analysis methods were also conducted on open repair alliance 
data. 
One of the most widely discussed strategies to enhance repairability is modular product design. 
The idea is simple but powerful: by designing products in a way that allows components to be 
==PAGE:8==
Sebastian Christaller 
8 
 
individually accessed, removed, and replaced, repairs become easier, cheaper, and more 
frequent. Studies by Amankwah-Amoah (2017) and Bakker et al. (2014) have been foundational in 
identifying modularity as a way to counteract premature obsolescence. Amankwah-Amoah 
(2017) in particular highlights how modularity allows for selective upgrading, thereby reducing the 
need to discard an entire product just because one part has failed. This directly supports design-
for-sustainability principles, where disassembly, repair, and upgradeability are built into the 
product from the start. 
More recent empirical work has strengthened these arguments. For instance, Amend et al. (2022) 
found that users of modular smartphones are significantly more likely to engage in repairs, largely 
because the process is more affordable. Sonego, Echeveste, and Debarba (2022) add that 
modularity doesn’t only help individual users. It also supports the development of wider repair 
ecosystems, including professional services, peer-to-peer networks, and stewardship programs. 
From a production point of view, modularity can be a win-win: it supports mass customization 
while making maintenance easier. Ipaki and Hosseini (2025) argue that when modularity is aligned 
with manufacturing strategies, it can pave the way for upgradable electronics that are also easier 
to fix. Boix Rodríguez et al. (2023) build on this by suggesting how disassembly scores can help 
manufacturers evaluate how easily their products can be repaired. 
Durability and modularity often go hand in hand. As Qi and Sawhill (2014) point out, the optimal 
lifetime of a product varies depending on how quickly technology moves. Modularity offers a way 
to extend that lifetime, by allowing targeted updates that don’t require starting from scratch. 
Similarly, Proske (2022) uses life cycle assessments of smartphones to show that even a slight 
increase in the environmental cost of manufacturing is outweighed by the benefits of a product 
that is designed to be fixed over and over again. 
Of course, even the most modular design is of little use without access to the right spare parts. A 
recurring theme in the literature is that spare part availability is one of the main bottlenecks in 
making repair viable at scale. Sabbaghi et al. (2017) identify poor part availability, proprietary 
components, and economic disincentives as major obstacles. Kim, Dekker, and Heij (2017) make 
the case for smarter forecasting models based on actual usage data, while Wu, Fu, and Xia (2025) 
bring machine learning into the equation to make these forecasts more accurate and responsive. 
Boujarif et al. (2025) take a broader view, showing how effective spare part supply chains are 
crucial for both economic viability and environmental performance. Li, Li, and Zhang (2025) 
discuss the importance of reverse logistics systems and trade-in schemes to help circulate 
components back into the system. These discussions are very relevant to this thesis, which seeks 
to better understand how the repair ecosystem in Denmark and Europe can be improved, also by 
bringing end of life products and parts back into circulation. 
Technologies like additive manufacturing are opening new possibilities in this space. Butturi, 
Marinelli, and Lolli (2022) evaluate the environmental impact of 3D-printed spare parts and find 
strong benefits, particularly for less commonly available parts. Producing parts on-demand and 
closer to the point of need reduces both inventory pressures and transportation emissions. This 
directly addresses one of the key challenges highlighted by Sabbaghi et al. (2017), who noted that 
supply chain constraints often make repair economically unfeasible. 
Also the business models built around repair are evolving. Zufall et al. (2020) explore how 
companies are rethinking their roles of not just selling products but also offering repair services 
and spare part provisioning. These models have shown promise in extending product lifespans 
and reducing waste, but they also depend heavily on financial viability, market acceptance, and 
often, supportive policy frameworks. This is where the second and third research questions of this 
==PAGE:9==
Sebastian Christaller 
9 
 
thesis come into play: by comparing commercial and volunteer-based repair pathways, and by 
evaluating the costs and benefits of each in the Danish context, the goal is to identify those “win-
win-win” scenarios where the environment, consumers, and businesses all benefit (Christaller, 
S., 2024). 
Repair cafés and other community-based initiatives have been an area of study in recent years as 
well. These spaces do more than just fix broken electronics. They also educate, empower, and 
shift cultural norms. Privett (2018) and Diddi & Yan (2019) both point to their value as social 
learning environments, while Rogers, Deutz, and Ramos (2021) offer a compelling case study from 
Hull, UK, showing how repair cafés can be embedded in local economic development strategies. 
Lundberg et al. (2024) and Sandez et al. (2023) emphasize the need to consider access to tools, 
repair knowledge, and confidence as key factors in promoting self-repair. These studies resonate 
closely with this thesis’s project CERP. By working directly with the municipality, the project aims 
to explore how the concept of repair cafés can be implemented in the waste stream of 
Copenhagen to not just offer a service of repair but diverting broken electronics from actual 
disposal. 
Consumer attitudes are also very relevant to this topic. Cox et al. (2013) show that people’s 
perception of a product’s lifetime influences whether they consider repair worthwhile. 
McCollough’s longitudinal research (2007, 2009, 2019) traces how rising incomes and the 
convenience of fast consumption have undermined repair culture. But this trend is not 
irreversible. Environmental concerns, economic pressures, and the growing visibility of repair 
movements are starting to make a difference. Studies by Dorland et al. (2025) and Jørgensen et al. 
(2023) provide insight into the specific motivations and barriers people face when deciding 
whether to repair or replace, including design, cost, and social influence. Jørgensen et al.’s survey, 
focused on Denmark, is particularly relevant here. It offers a useful reference point for 
understanding the current state of public attitudes and what might encourage a shift toward more 
sustainable behaviors. 
The environmental case for repair is also well-established. Life cycle assessments from Bovea, 
Ibáñez-Forés, and Pérez-Belis (2020) consistently show that repairing small electronics has a 
lower environmental impact than replacing them. Proske (2022) and Hennies & Stamminger 
(2016) reach similar conclusions with smartphones and household appliances. Zhilyaev et al. 
(2021) add that effective repair can meaningfully delay disposal and reduce the stock turnover of 
e-waste. These studies strengthen the background of this thesis’s assessment of environmental 
and monetary break-even points: in many cases, repair is not just good practice. It is the best 
option from both a resource and economic perspective. 
Finally, it’s important to recognize that policy plays a significant role in shaping what’s possible. 
The EU’s Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) Directive (2012) is often cited as a 
policy that shifts responsibility back onto producers. Complementary regulations, like right-to-
repair laws and extended producer responsibility (EPR) schemes, can create incentives for repair-
friendly design and better access to parts and information (Svensson-Hoglund et al., 2021). At the 
same time, voluntary initiatives and labelling schemes are helping to promote transparency in 
product durability and repairability (Dalhammar & Richter, 2017). These policy directions frame 
the wider ecosystem within which repair strategies succeed or struggle. This thesis contributes 
valuable insight into how local governments can act as facilitators of repair. Not just through 
regulation, but through partnerships, education, and infrastructure. 
Promoting repair is a multi-dimensional challenge that touches on product design, supply chains, 
user behavior, business models, and policy. While many promising solutions exist, the task ahead

is to connect the best strategies in every area. To develop systems that make repair easy, 
appealing, and accessible. This research enters the conversation by offering both, analytical 
depth and practical experimentation. By focusing on real-world implementation, this thesis has 
the potential to move the field forward. Not just in theory, but also in practice. 
2. Methodology 
This study uses a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative data from CERP and an 
online survey with qualitative interviews and physical disassembly investigations. Insights from 
research literature are complementing each topic structured in a way that first more theoretical 
research is presented and afterwards complemented by data from the repair project and the 
survey. The goal is to generate a holistic understanding of repairability in electronic products and 
to analyze the economic feasibility of repairs, consumer attitudes, and the influence of product 
design on repairability. 
2.1 Physical Product Analysis 
The core dataset for this study is based on an in-depth physical analysis of 133 electrical and 
electronic products collected through conduction of CERP. Each product was carefully examined 
and documented to build a comprehensive dataset. The data collected included the following 
elements: 
• 
Product identification: Name and model number (where available), along with 
categorization based on the WEEE Directive (Directive 2012/19/EU of the European 
Parliament and of the Council, 2012). 
• 
Fault diagnosis: Identification of the defective component and a description of the 
observed or suspected malfunction. Each repair attempt was classified as either 
successful or unsuccessful. In parallel, repairability was assessed and rated as repair-
friendly, partially repair-friendly, or not repair-friendly. 
• 
Product specifications: Estimated product age, derived from manufacturing date 
stamps where possible, or otherwise inferred based on product design and brand. Product 
weight was measured for use in lifecycle impact assessment. The original retail price was 
researched using online sources, adjusted for inflation where applicable, or estimated 
based on comparable models when direct data were unavailable. Additionally, current 
second-hand market value, from now named as time value, was estimated using 
platforms such as eBay, DBA, or other resale marketplaces. 
• 
Repair metrics: The time required for each repair was standardized using a benchmark 
skill level equivalent to a “medium-skilled repairer” (defined as three months of practical 
repair experience). This approach allowed for consistent time comparisons across repair 
cases. Spare part costs were either directly recorded or estimated. A minimum threshold 
of 5 DKK was applied to account for negligible part costs and to maintain consistency in 
cost reporting.

2.2 Survey Data 
A digital survey was conducted using Microsoft Forms to gather data on consumer attitudes and 
willingness to pay for repairs. Questions were designed based on gaps in literature. The survey 
targeted a general audience and the link to the survey was distributed through social media 
platforms and within repair sessions. It consisted of questions on a five point scale ranging from 
strongly agree to strongly disagree. The survey was anonymous and voluntary, and no personal 
data such as name, address and demographics were collected. Data from the completed surveys 
were exported to Excel and analyzed there. Figures were generated inside the Microsoft Teams 
survey tool analysis environment. 
2.3 Qualitative Interviews 
To complement the quantitative analysis of electronic product repairs, a series of qualitative 
interviews were conducted. The purpose of these interviews was to gain a deeper understanding 
of the motivations, barriers, and perspectives that shape repair behaviors and infrastructure. The 
findings from the statistical dataset were contextualized with real-world experiences and 
stakeholder insights. 
The interviewees were selected to represent three key stakeholder groups within the broader 
repair ecosystem: 
1. Volunteers participating in CERP: Volunteers engaged in CERP were interviewed to 
explore practical repair challenges encountered during the project. These discussions 
also examined intrinsic motivators for participating in the project, such as sustainability

This qualitative component serves as a narrative counterpart to the quantitative analysis. 
2.4 Repair friendly product design 
This study investigates repair-oriented product design through a multi-faceted analysis of key 
variables influencing product reparability. The primary areas of examination include disassembly, 
accessibility, reassembly, and spare part availability. These elements were selected based on 
their established influence on the success of repair efforts (Bogue, 2007; Duflou et al., 2008; 
Sabbaghi et al., 2015). 
To evaluate these factors, a case study approach was employed, including hands-on product 
teardown, expert interviews and review of industry documentation. Product examples were 
chosen to reflect a diversity of categories. A specific emphasis was placed on observing housing 
design (glued, clipped, or screw-based), internal component accessibility, and modularity across 
production years. 
Furthermore, data was collected on spare part generation strategies, including spare part 
stocking, reverse logistics (Boujarif et al., 2025), and additive manufacturing applications (Butturi 
et al., 2022). Literature on reverse supply chains (Li, Li, & Zhang, 2025) and spare part forecasting 
(Wu, Fu, & Xia, 2024) was integrated to assess how manufacturers manage after-sales part 
availability. Industry cases were included to understand real-world applications of refurbishment 
and spare part reuse. 
The role of 3D printing was studied using an interview and secondary data sources. Different 3D 
printing technologies (FDM, SLA, SLS) were assessed for spare part suitability in terms of cost, 
precision, and mechanical performance.

2.5 Pathways for repair 
2.5.1 Case study: Volunteer repair project 
To investigate how volunteer-based repair initiatives can influence the circular economy and 
reduce e-waste and also to contribute with a positive impact to the real word while writing this 
thesis, CERP was initiated. CERP delivers a fundamental dataset for this work and a remaining 
benefit for the municipality of Copenhagen. The project is initiated by the author of this thesis and 
implemented as a collaboration between the municipality of Copenhagen and two local recycling 
centers: Hørgården and Lykkebazaren. 
The project’s goal is to divert electronics from recycling and disposal streams by facilitating local 
repair and redistribution. The repair process often takes only 15–45 minutes per device and most 
repairs were doable for volunteers with varying skill levels. 
The conceptual groundwork was laid in October 2024 by Sebastian Christaller. Planning involved 
consultations with stakeholders from the Municipality of Copenhagen's Teknik & 
Miljøforvaltningen department. By December 2024, legal concerns were addressed, posters 
requesting defective electronics were distributed, and two repair dates were scheduled in January 
2025. The first repair session was held on February 6th 2025, at Lykkebazaren. 
Excluded product groups were determined based on five criteria: 
1. White goods (e.g., washing machines, stoves) were excluded due to their size and testing 
requirements. 
2. Data-sensitive electronics (e.g., smartphones, tablets) were excluded to protect personal 
data and due to their repair complexity. 
3. Low-demand products (e.g., old or worn-out devices) were excluded to avoid wasting 
volunteer effort. 
4. Low-impact products (e.g., basic kitchen scales, clocks) because of high repair need 
compared to low value and resource intensity. 
5. Hazardous items (e.g., bicycle airbags, smoke detectors) were excluded due to safety 
concerns, even if technically repairable. 
The repair process began with a safety briefing that taught volunteers to inspect devices using the 
senses of sight, smell, and touch. After the initial check, devices were powered using switchable 
outlets and further tested. Once unplugged, repairs commenced using available tools and 
reference materials (manuals or online videos). After repair, the device’s functionality was tested, 
cleaned, and prepared for redistribution. 
Redistribution was managed through a two-tier approach: 
• 
Low-demand products were placed in the Bytterum (swap shelves) with clear labeling as 
"repaired." 
• 
High-demand products were distributed via raffles or events to avoid individuals taking 
multiple items and to increase fairness.

2.5.2 Impact assessment 
To evaluate the environmental effects of CERP, this study employed a structured CO₂ impact 
estimation framework adapted from Privett (2018) and the Centre for Sustainable Design (n.d.). 
Although originally developed for use in traditional repair cafés, the tool was deemed applicable 
due to methodological similarities, particularly in quantifying the avoided emissions through 
repair activities. 
The method centers on calculating greenhouse gas emissions avoided or generated through 
successful product repairs. The primary input variable for these calculations is the weight of 
repaired products, which was collected for each item in the dataset of CERP. The model 
distinguishes between different categories of environmental impact, each reflecting a specific 
mechanism by which emissions are either prevented or introduced during the repair process. 
One core category is replacement product savings, which estimates the CO₂ emissions avoided 
by extending the lifespan of existing devices instead of producing new ones. This category 
assumes that repaired products functionally replace new purchases and thereby offset the 
environmental burden associated with industrial manufacturing, logistics, and distribution 
(Privett, 2018). 
To ensure accuracy, the methodology also incorporates product displacement loss, which 
accounts for cases where repaired items do not serve as true substitutes. For example, if a 
repaired product is taken by someone who would not have otherwise made a new purchase, the 
avoided emissions are lower or absent. Also, if the repaired product does not last as long as a new 
produced product falls under product displacement loss. 
The framework further includes repair-related emissions, such as emissions from transportation 
of volunteers, energy consumption during diagnostics and testing, and the production or reuse of 
spare parts. These values are factored into the balance to assess the net benefit of repair activities 
(Privett, 2018). 
Additionally, the methodology quantifies landfill and recyclables savings, capturing emissions 
avoided by diverting products from waste or recycling systems. Since both landfill and recycling 
incur environmental costs, prolonging the use-phase of devices through repair yields measurable 
environmental benefits (Privett, 2018; Centre for Sustainable Design, n.d.). 
The tool also includes embodied GHG emissions associated with the transportation of products 
to and from the place of repair, as well as indirect effects such as rebound behavior, where savings 
from avoided purchases may lead to other emissions-intensive spending (Privett, 2018). 
The final environmental assessment is based on the cumulative weight of repaired products and 
their associated impact factors as defined in the input of the tool by Privett (2018) published by 
the Centre for Sustainable Design (n.d.).  
The survey and qualitative interviews further determined whether repaired items replaced new 
purchases (essential needs) or fulfilled optional wants. Since different people take the items in 
CERP than the people who delivered them, it is very likely that the product displacement loss is 
higher. The reason for adding a repaired element to the household can be because it has no cost 
for the person or because it is seen as green since the product has no or very little emission 
(Blackburn, R. et al., 2025). The consumption behavior regarding products seen as green by the 
consumer is detailed analyzed by Eunike, I. J., et al. (2025). Therefore, a more detailed analysis is 
included where the consumer attitude towards free products is asked, and a pyramid of needs is 
==PAGE:15==

developed and validated qualitatively with participants. From this, the share of optional products 
and products that really replace new purchases in the dataset of CERP can be calculated. 
To evaluate the extent to which repaired and redistributed electronic products substitute the 
purchase of new items, a classification of product necessity was implemented. Participants who 
received repaired items were asked whether they would have purchased the product anyway or 
whether the item served a more optional purpose. Based on these responses, each product was 
categorized as either a “need” or an “optional” item. Examples of optional products include 
devices like ice machines, disco lights, or juicers, while essential products include vacuum 
cleaners, kettles, or blenders. This classification was further validated through qualitative 
interviews with recipients of repaired items and visitors to the recycling station. The objective of 
this classification is to distinguish between products that are likely to displace a new purchase 
and those that add value in other ways, such as increasing access to devices among individuals 
with limited financial means.

2.5.4 Manufacturer Remanufacturing Strategies 
To show the opportunities and practice of manufacturer-controlled repair, a more literature based 
analysis was conducted focusing on design principles, operational models, and supply chain 
integration. Key sources include peer-reviewed studies and industry reports (Aydin et al., 2023; 
Kerber et al., 2021; Motes, 2025), which provide insights into how manufacturers implement 
remanufacturing both in centralized and decentralized systems. Additionally, product design 
implications were assessed, particularly how design-for-remanufacturing parallels but differs 
from design-for-repair (Motes, 2025). The study also incorporated findings on reverse logistics, 
modularity, and the role of operational integration in managing inventories (Aydin et al., 2023). To 
provide comparative context, case studies from leading firms such as Dell were reviewed, 
alongside empirical findings on environmental outcomes of centralized versus decentralized 
refurbishment models (Mann et al., 2022). 
2.5.5 Consumer self-repair 
For certain products, particularly where repairs are relatively simple and safe, guided self-repair 
is considered a viable option. Evidence from other sectors, such as clothing and furniture, shows 
high levels of consumer competence in self-repair (Dorland et al., 2025). Within electronics, 
similar potential exists when adequate instructional support is provided. In this context, 
manufacturers can facilitate self-repair by supplying guidance in the form of manuals, video 
tutorials, technical drawings, or service documentation (Lundberg et al., 2024). 
To better understand the effectiveness of guided self-repair, this study references the controlled 
experiment conducted by Sandez, Pérez-Belis, Juan, and Bovea (2023), in which participants were 
instructed to repair a kettle with two induced faults. Instructions were provided in three formats: 
video, step-by-step text, and a written guide. The impact of the chosen instruction format and 
prior repair experience on repair success were key variables. Furthermore, consumer attitudes 
toward manufacturer-guided self-repair and community-based repair options such as repair 
cafés were assessed through survey questions. 
2.5.6 Comparison of repair and recycling 
To assess and compare different repair strategies for electronic products to recycling, a 
qualitative analysis was conducted. The analysis evaluated key repair pathways: manufacturer-
controlled refurbishment, open-market commercial repair, customer self-repair, repair cafés, 
volunteer-based repair initiatives, recycling, and disposal/incineration. Each pathway was 
analyzed based on multiple dimensions.

2.5.7 Business models for managing sustainable products 
To explore alternative business models that could enhance product longevity and reduce 
environmental impact, this study examines lease, rental, and Product Service Systems as 
frameworks for increasing repair help to more sustainable consumption. Literature and case 
studies were analyzed to assess the economic and ecological implications of these models, with 
particular focus on their impact on product lifetime, consumer behavior, and life cycle emissions. 
The analysis includes cost and life cycle assessment (LCA) data comparing long-lifespan lease 
models with traditional product sales. LCA methodology is based on the definition of a functional 
unit as the provision of a service over time (Amasawa et al., 2020; Proske, 2022). The research 
also considers consumer perspectives on repair services and the perceived risks associated with 
repair reliability. Key points include willingness to pay, uncertainty about repair outcomes, and 
product design suitability for multiple-use cycles. Data from commercial examples such as high-
end appliance leasing (McKinsey & Company, 2015) were included to assess practical viability. 
The study evaluates not only environmental benefits and cost savings but also structural risks 
such as the potential monopolization of repair services by manufacturers. Concerns around self-
repair exclusion and market competition were identified in the literature (Svensson-Hoglund et 
al., 2021; Rogers et al., 2021). 
2.5.8 Willingness to repair for different product types 
To explore how product characteristics, pricing, and consumer attitudes affect the likelihood of 
repair, this study combined literature analysis with survey data and CERP data. Previous research 
identifies high cost as the primary deterrent to repairs (Pérez-Belis et al., 2017; Bovea et al., 2017). 
This includes not only repair costs but also the price of new products and the perceived additional 
lifespan gained through repair (Torca-Adell et al., 2025). 
To assess consumer decision-making, the study builds on surveys such as that by Borthakur and 
Govind (2018). Similarly, Jørgensen et al. (2023) analyzed if repair decisions are financially driven 
and which role environmental considerations play (Dorland et al., 2025). 
Product type and cost were also taken into account. Hennies and Stamminger (2016) analyzed if 
cheaper items are more likely to be discarded. The typology proposed by Cox et al. (2013) who is 
categorizing products into workhorse, investment, and up-to-date was used to interpret 
consumer repair behaviors based on functionality, emotional value, and trend sensitivity. 
To investigate real consumer behavior further, a question in the survey was included. Participants 
were asked whether they would repair products at different repair cost thresholds (30%, 50%, and 
70% of the new product price). This aimed to test and expand upon the 20% threshold proposed 
by McCollough (2007). Additionally, the survey included questions on consumer preferences 
regarding receiving their original repaired item versus a similar replacement, and willingness to 
repair different product types (Hi-Fi systems vs. vacuum cleaners).

• 
Partly repair-friendly: repairs possible but complicated by minor design limitations 
•

2.6.4 Repairability and product type benchmarking 
Repair cost was calculated as the sum of spare part cost and the labor cost, with labor cost 
calculated by multiplying the repair duration (in hours) by a benchmark hourly wage of 600 DKK. 
This benchmark wage reflects a realistic proxy for labor in professional repair contexts.  
To evaluate how repair costs differ across product types, boxplots were created for each category 
using the Tier 1 product grouping of the European WEEE framework. It allows insight into which 
product types present higher financial burdens for repair and where low-cost repairs are more 
common. 
A further hypothesis tested was the potential correlation between a product’s physical weight and 
its repair cost. Weight is treated here as a proxy for product size, complexity, or functionality 
density. These are factors likely to influence repair effort and parts required. A scatter plot was 
used, clustering by product category, to visualize whether heavier items generally incur higher 
repair costs.  
To explore product longevity and patterns of failure over time, each product’s age at the time of 
repair was plotted. This visualization aimed to identify whether products tend to fail within 
specific lifecycle windows (e.g., early obsolescence vs. late-stage wear-out). The dataset allowed 
for the assumption that most products had not undergone prior repairs, as only 4 out of 133 units 
exhibited evidence of past service. This assumption justified using age as a proxy for first-time 
failure. 
The Hourly Wage Value is used as a Proxy for Economic Attractiveness. This metric reflects the 
economic “gain” (or value reclaimed) per hour spent on repair, calculated by dividing the new 
product price by the total repair time. This provides a time-efficiency metric that indicates which 
products are more “worth repairing” from an economic perspective. 
This value was then clustered by both Tier 1 and Tier 2 product categories to assess whether 
specific types of devices systematically yield higher or lower hourly repair value. Scatter plots 
were used to visualize distribution and compare across groupings.

2.6.5 Quantitative and Qualitative Data Analysis 
The depreciation of electronic products over time and its relevance at the point when a repair 
becomes necessary is analyzed. Depreciation was not calculated on an annual basis. Instead, 
the analysis focused on the monetary depreciation from new purchase price to the product’s time 
value at the moment repair was required. Consumers or repair practitioners are typically 
concerned with the product’s value at the time of failure when deciding for or against repair. 
The time value was assessed through estimated resale market value for comparable products in 
similar condition. These values were derived from available online marketplaces and adjusted 
based on observable product wear. 
The analysis visualizes the relationship between each product’s original new price and its time 
value at repair, allowing a general assessment of how much value products retain by the time 
repairs become necessary. A key assumption underlying this analysis is that products with a 
higher time value are more likely to be considered economically viable for repair, especially when 
the estimated repair cost is lower than the product’s residual value. 
Two ellipsoids were added to the plot to represent qualitative zones of likely repair and unlikely 
repair. These zones were based on combined observations of time value thresholds and 
corresponding repair costs previously examined in other chapters of this thesis. They are not fixed 
decision boundaries but serve to frame the trade-off space between product value and likely 
repair cost. 
To test whether the observed trends held true within a more homogeneous product group, the 
analysis was repeated exclusively for coffee machines (Tier 1 Group 2; Tier 2 Group 8). This group 
includes several subtypes: fully automatic machines, barista-style machines, pad machines, and 
grinders. 
2.6.6 ANOVA Analysis 
To evaluate whether statistically significant differences exist between group means of repair cost 
across products classified as repair friendly, medium repair friendly or not repair friendly and the 
different defect categories electrical, mechanical and dirt, a one-way Analysis of Variance 
(ANOVA) was employed. ANOVA is a statistical technique used to determine if there are any 
statistically significant differences between the means of independent groups. This method is 
particularly suitable when the dependent variable is continuous, and the independent variable is 
categorical with more than two levels (Abdi & Williams, 2010). In the case of the repair data of 
CERP, the independent variables repair friendliness (repair friendly, medium repair friendly and 
not repair friendly) and defect category (electrical, mechanical and dirt) are used in two different 
ANOVA analyses. Also the two independent variables (repaired and not repaired) and the new 
==PAGE:21==
Sebastian Christaller 
21 
 
price of a product was analysed with ANOVA. For two independent variables ANOVA delivers the 
same result as the simpler two-sample t-test, but since the one way ANOVA was needed anyway 
for analyses with three or more independent variables, ANOVA is used for all Variance analyses. 
To see if there are statistically significant differences between the repair cost of different product 
categories, ANOVA was conducted on the Tier 1 product category levels and within Group 2 of 
product categories on the second tier level. Since even in this grouping different product types are 
present such as filter coffee machines, barista coffee machines, fully automatic coffee machines 
and pad machines, a further grouping within product technology type was conducted and an 
ANOVA analysis was done on the repair cost and the economic viability of repair. The economic 
viability of repair is defined as the time value of the product minus the repair cost. The repair cost 
is calculated based on the spare part cost and the repair time with the benchmark of 600 DKK of 
repair time cost per hour used.  
To assess the potential differences between group means, the following hypotheses are 
formulated: 
• 
Null hypothesis (H₀): The means of all groups are equal. This implies that the 
independent variable has no effect on the dependent variable. 
• 
Alternative hypothesis (H₁): At least one group mean is different. This suggests that the 
independent variable influences the dependent variable. 
The ANOVA analysis partitions the total variation in the dataset into two components: 
• 
Between-groups variation: This reflects the variability in the dependent variable that can 
be attributed to differences between the clusters grouped according to the independent 
variable. If the group means differ greatly, the between-group variation will be relatively 
large. 
• 
Within-groups variation (error term): This captures the variability that exists within each 
clustered group. It represents individual differences or other unexplained variability not 
attributed to the grouping variable. 
Each source of variation is associated with a Sum of Squares (SS) and a corresponding Degrees 
of Freedom (df). From these, Mean Squares (MS) are calculated by dividing SS by their respective 
df. The F-statistic is then computed as the ratio of the mean square between groups to the mean 
square within groups (Abdi & Williams, 2010). 
The significance of the F-statistic is assessed by comparing it to a critical value (F crit) based on 
the chosen significance level (α = 0.05) and the degrees of freedom associated with the test. 
Additionally, a p-value is calculated to indicate the probability of obtaining an F-statistic as large 
as (or larger than) the observed value under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. 
If the p-value is less than the significance level, or if the F-statistic exceeds the critical value, the 
null hypothesis is rejected. This indicates that at least one group mean is significantly different 
from the others. 
If the ANOVA test reveals significant differences among the group means, a post-hoc test such as 
Tukey’s Honest Significant Difference (HSD) can be done (Abdi & Williams, 2010). This allows for 
pairwise comparisons between groups to determine specifically which means differ from one 
another.

3. Product Design for Repair 
Repair-oriented product design primarily focuses on three key aspects: disassembly, 
accessibility, and reassembly. If the internal components of a device are inaccessible, repair 
efforts become unfeasible from the outset. Similarly, if a defective part is obstructed by numerous 
cables and other components that must be removed or damaged in the process, successful repair 
is inhibited, often resulting in additional waste due to the need for replacement parts. 
Furthermore, if components are difficult to reassemble it significantly reduces the feasibility of 
repair. This can be due to deformation during disassembly or the complexity of the mounting 
process. Consequently, this study examines these three critical aspects in detail. 
Beyond the physical aspects of repairability, the availability of spare parts is equally essential. If 
a defective component cannot be sourced, repair becomes impossible, regardless of the 
product’s design. Therefore, strategies for ensuring long-term spare part supply are also analyzed 
in conjunction with the disassembly, accessibility, and assembly processes. Additionally, product 
documentation plays a crucial role in facilitating repairs. In the 1970s, for example, it was 
common practice for manufacturers to provide circuit diagrams with electronic products, 
significantly enhancing repair accessibility. In Appendix A a circuit diagram in an envelope in the 
inner side of a housing of the B&O Beogram 1000 record player is shown. Nowadays, products are 
more complex and in most cases no service information is available. 
3.1 Disassembly 
The disassembly step is mainly inhibited by two problem groups: Proprietary fasteners and glued 
or clipped housings. While proprietary fasteners can be opened with the right specific tools, glued 
housings can usually not be opened without damage. Clipped housings can sometimes be 
opened with the right method, repair approaches by beginners often result in damage of the 
housing. All three groups inhibit the repair of products by the customers (Bogue, 2007). 
3.1.1 Proprietary Fasteners 
Certain manufacturers employ specialized fasteners, including proprietary screws, rivets, or 
clips, that require unique tools or knowledge to disassemble (Bogue, 2007). These design choices 
serve as barriers to independent repair, as individuals without access to the right tools or training 
are unable to perform even basic maintenance (Duflou et al., 2008). A notable example is Jura, a 
premium coffee machine brand, which utilizes a proprietary oval-head screw that can only be 
removed using a specialized screwdriver (Jura Ovalkopfschraube, n.d.). This design decision lacks 
any functional justification from an engineering perspective and appears to be implemented 
solely to discourage unauthorized repair. Moreover, the use of proprietary screws increases 
manufacturing costs, as they are not standardized mass-produced components. By contrast, 
competing brands such as DeLonghi, Philips, and Siemens utilize standard fasteners, thereby 
eliminating unnecessary repair barriers, at least for accessing the product housing. A proprietary 
triangle screw of a Stelton water kettle is shown in Appendix A.2. 
3.1.2 Glued and Clipped Housings 
Another common impediment to repairability is the use of glued enclosures, which often prevent 
non-destructive disassembly (Bogue, 2007). Such designs are frequently observed in low-cost 
==PAGE:23==
Sebastian Christaller 
23 
 
products and direct imports from Chinese manufacturers (Franke et al., 2021). In many cases, 
glued housings can only be opened by breaking them apart, rendering reassembly impossible. 
Sonos for example uses completely glued and clicked housings, which cannot even be opened 
and repaired by the manufacturers themselves. They are not even repairing warranty cases (HIFI-
Klubben Interview, 2025). Another case where this practice is prevalent is power converters, 
where safety regulations necessitate tamper-proof designs (Franke et al., 2021). Given that 
transformers are inherently modular components and pose significant safety risks when 
improperly modified, their non-disassemble able construction aligns with principles of 
sustainable and safe product design (Franke et al., 2021). For all other housings, gluing should be 
avoided to allow maintenance and repair. 
Plastic clip-based enclosures are widely used in mid-range and high-end products that offer some 
level of repairability. These clips allow manufacturers to refurbish returned products efficiently 
while maintaining an accessible design for authorized service to a certain extent. However, such 
designs present challenges for untrained individuals attempting repairs for the first time. Clips are 
often positioned in a manner that requires a thin prying tool for proper disengagement, and 
improper handling can lead to breakage. Additionally, tools such as screwdrivers used for prying 
may leave visible scratches on plastic surfaces, resulting in aesthetic degradation even when the 
repair itself is successful. An example of damage caused by the opening of clipped housing 
without the specific tools a manufacturer has is shown in Appendix A.3. 
Lego has done a wide range of research on disassemble able clip and stick connections (Casa de 
Bricks, 2021). Since Lego builds not only have to be built by non-trained people, it also must be 
possible to disassemble the builds. Therefore, Lego has even higher criteria than necessary for 
housing and components in electric products, but their research can be a good benchmark and 
give ideas for optimizing connections to facilitate repairability. 
3.2 Accessibility and Exchangeability 
3.2.1 Modular Design 
Modularity is a fundamental aspect of repair-friendly product design, serving two primary 
functions: facilitating the replacement of defective or worn components and enabling 
technological upgrades to maintain compatibility with evolving standards (Sonego et al., 2018). In 
some cases, both objectives are addressed simultaneously. More integral products are leading to 
difficulty in repair (Sabbaghi et al., 2016, 2017; Bovea et al., 2017). In the last decades, a trend to 
more integral and less modular products could be seen (Wieser and Tröger, 2018). By disassembly 
of products from different production years it can also be seen that the modules get bigger, 
meaning five components that have been single modules 30 years ago are now welded on one 
PCB and thus replacement of one component means necessarily replacing significantly more 
material than 30 years ago (Franke et al., 2021). This also comes together with higher costs for the 
spare-part since it is more material and thus higher repair cost, which makes repair less attractive 
pushing more people to buying new products.  
For example, in audio amplifiers, relay components may degrade due to sulfide buildup, 
necessitating replacement. This component can be clicked or connected with cable shoes or 
welded to a PCB. In the second case, most people could not do the repair anymore, since it is a 
more difficult task. A complex PCB of a modern vacuum cleaner is shown in Appendix A.4. 
In consumer electronics, modularity allows for straightforward technological upgrades, such as 
transitioning from a 30-pin iPod dock connector to a Lightning or wireless charging interface. If 
==PAGE:24==
Sebastian Christaller 
24 
 
this interface is not designed modular, changes to new standards mean that many people would 
throw away their product if it cannot be adapted to new generations of signal transfer.  
In the case of e-bikes, battery modules can be refurbished by replacing aged lithium-ion cells with 
newer, often higher-capacity alternatives. Thereby full functionality can be restored and even new 
generations of cells with higher energy density can be built into the existing battery housing. 
Also, for small kitchen equipment like toasters and kettles, modularity and re-design for 
disassembly could lead to a higher repair quota (Jørgensen, M. S. et. al., 2023). These product 
groups are among the least modular ones and the new prices are usually low, pushing the 
consumers to new purchases instead of repair. 
3.2.2 Strategic Placement of Wear-Prone Parts 
In addition to modularity, the positioning of high-wear components significantly impacts 
repairability. Ideally, frequently failing components should be more accessible than those that 
experience minimal degradation over time. A well-executed example of this principle can be 
observed in DeLonghi and Siemens coffee machines, where the brewing unit is designed for easy 
removal. Since brewing unit failures are among the most common defects after descaling issues, 
this design facilitates both routine maintenance and repairs. The unit can be detached via a 
simple click mechanism, allowing users to clean and lubricate it as needed. Furthermore, if 
plastic components within the brewing unit break, they can be replaced without requiring access 
to the entire internal assembly. 
For more complex repairs involving internal components such as the heating element, pump, or 
main printed circuit board (PCB), disassembly of the housing is required. 
3.3 Reassembly and Reversibility 
Obstacles encountered during reassembly often mirror those present in disassembly. However, 
additional challenges arise when components are easy to remove but difficult to reposition 
correctly. Often only design for disassembly or design for disposal was considered in the product 
development phase, while design for reassembly was omitted (Bogue, 2007). 
A common example is the rubber drive belt used in CD players to connect the motor to the gear 
mechanism responsible for extending and retracting the disc tray. Over time, these rubber belts 
degrade and lose elasticity. While the removal process is relatively straightforward, installing a 
new one can be significantly more complex. 
The difficulty arises from the necessity of precise alignment and the confined space available for 
maneuvering the replacement belt. To disassemble the old belt, it typically just needs to be pulled 
out. In many cases, the gear assembly is accessible only through a narrow gap between other 
internal components and the plastic housing, making manual installation challenging. This issue 
exemplifies the importance of considering reassembly feasibility during the design phase, 
particularly for components that are prone to wear and require periodic replacement (Bogue, 
2007). 
3.4 Spare Part Management and Business Models 
The price and limited access to spare parts are one of the main factors that reduce the feasibility 
of repair. To promote repair, spare parts must be available during the entire life cycle of products, 
also after the end of production (Sabbaghi et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:25==
Sebastian Christaller 
25 
 
Spare parts can first be differentiated into new produced parts of same properties and production 
technology and differently generated parts. These can be retrofits, upgraded or improved 
components, 3D printed parts of in most cases different material and used parts which can be 
remanufactured or just tested and cleaned. The easiest way to have spare parts is to have still a 
supplier collaboration or stock of the component. The mass production method is nearly always 
the cheapest.  
Companies must decide how many spare parts will be produced in the final production run to 
meet future demand. This decision may be difficult, since the demand for spare sparts is 
influenced by consumer decisions and is intermittent (Kim et al. 2017). There is research by Wu, 
Fu, & Xia, 2024 on how to predict spare part needs with regression models for electronic products. 
This is not always possible, since for some products the end of production has already passed. 
If there are no original spare parts left, they can be generated from worn out products. 
3.4.1 Reverse Supply Chain (RSC) 
A reverse supply chain for spare parts focuses on collecting, refurbishing, and reusing used 
components as spare parts for sale, in own refurbishment programs or even in new products. 
Manufacturer take-back programs are a key approach, where companies collect old or defective 
products, recover functional parts, and use them in refurbished or remanufactured goods or sell 
them as spare parts (Boujarif et al., 2025). E-waste collection centers and authorized repair shops 
can also serve as hubs for gathering spare parts, particularly when partnered with manufacturers 
or government initiatives (Li, Li, & Zhang, 2025). 
When a product is no longer functional, manufacturers can dismantle it, test individual parts, and 
reintegrate them into refurbishment processes (Duflou et al., 2008). Remanufacturing processes 
ensure these components meet quality standards by repairing, cleaning, and sometimes 
upgrading them before reuse (Boujarif et al., 2025).  
In order to ensure economic viability, companies frequently implement core deposit systems, 
wherein customers return used components in exchange for discounts on new or refurbished 
items (Li, Li, & Zhang, 2025). Standardizing components across product lines also simplifies the 
recovery and reuse of parts. While examining the economic benefits of repair services and reverse 
supply chains, it is essential to acknowledge the corresponding challenges in repair practices. A 
prevailing challenge in the EoL recovery processes is the presence of various sources of 
uncertainty (Boujarif et al., 2025, Mashhadi et al., 2015). The uncertainty in the quality of the 
collected products can intensively affect the performance of the spare part generation practices 
and is influenced by the consumers usage behavior (Sabbaghi et al., 2015). For spare parts 
generation and refurbishment processes it is also critical if the same components are broken in 
every device, because this part would be needed for the products to be repaired as well. Thus, an 
even distribution of defects over all components would be the ideal case for having a reverse 
supply chain since a maximum of working products could be built with the core taken back. This 
is usually not the case and defective parts are often centered on a handful of components, often 
wear out parts. 
Companies in various industries like soundboks (HIFI) (SOUNDBOKS, 2025), Technivorm 
Moccamaster (coffee machines) (Moccamaster, 2025) and Rimowa (Quality suitcases) (RIMOWA, 
2025) have programs to take back products and also offer refurbished products for a cheaper 
sales price on their websites. Rimowa also offers 250 Euro discount on a new or refurbished 
product when handing in a Rimowa Aluminum suitcase. With this approach they try to collect 
suitcases to generate spare parts for their refurbishment or repair programs. 
==PAGE:26==
Sebastian Christaller 
26 
 
If there is no take back option or a spare part is worn out in almost every taken back product, new 
parts in smaller series have to be generated. Therefore, 3D printing is a good option with nowadays 
mechanical properties that suit most use cases. 
3.4.2 3D Printing of spare parts 
3D Printing of spare parts is a good solution if no manufacturer supplied spare parts are available 
or even for a manufacturer to produce small series of spare parts (Butturi et al., 2022). For many 
plastic parts, injection molding is used as a production technology. This has high fixed costs and 
a longer process duration. With 3D printing, small series can be produced cheaper and faster. The 
German police use 3D printing to manufacture special mounts for equipment in their cars and the 
German train company Deutsche Bahn uses 3D printing to generate spare parts for trains 
(Qiumeng Li, personal communication, February 13, 2025). Trains, for example, often have a 
longer product lifecycle than spare parts are available and printing offers a fast and flexible 
solution, especially for plastic parts or mounts in the interior. 3D models can be generated by 
scanning a component and it is possible to exchange properties by modelling and printing 
afterwards. The manufacturer can also use old CAD models for that purpose if there is the need 
to produce a small series of spare parts (Qiumeng Li, personal communication, February 13, 
2025). Fused deposition modeling (FDM), stereolithography (SLA), and selective laser sintering 
(SLS) are the three most common types of 3D printers used.  FDM uses filament and is the least 
precise but cheapest method. SLA uses resin and SLS uses powder which can be metallic or non-
metallic (Formlabs2, n.d.). For spareparts, SLA and SLS will be the most used ones. 
The best 3D printing technology for spare parts depends on the application and required 
properties (Butturi et al., 2022). Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) is the most cost-effective 
option, ideal for basic replacement parts, prototyping, and non-load-bearing components in 
consumer electronics and household items. Stereolithography (SLA) offers high precision and 
smooth surfaces, making it suitable for small, intricate spare parts (Formlabs2, n.d.). For durable, 
high-strength spare parts, Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) is the best choice, commonly used in 
industrial and automotive settings, where functional mechanical components like brackets, 
gears, and enclosures are required. While FDM is affordable, SLA ensures fine detail, and SLS 
delivers superior mechanical performance (Formlabs2, n.d.). 
4. Paths for Repair 
Repair is crucial not only for environmental reasons but also for manufacturers. Successful repair 
stories can significantly benefit them, as positive repair experiences influence future consumer 
decisions. When repairs are successful, they enhance the perceived quality and reliability of a 
product, which can lead to positive word-of-mouth recommendations and repeat purchases from 
the same manufacturer (Mashhadi et al., 2016). As a result, manufacturers have an interest in 
offering suitable repair options. Additionally, municipalities and volunteer organizations are 
motivated to support repair initiatives primarily because of their potential to reduce waste. 
The following section explores various repair pathways in detail.

4.1.1 Product Selection 
For CERP, a targeted product group had to be selected. In general, all products that consume 
electric energy were considered. However, to simplify logistics and testing, white goods were 
excluded from the outset. White goods refer to large household appliances, such as refrigerators, 
washing machines, and stoves, which were traditionally made in a ceramic white color. These 
==PAGE:28==
Sebastian Christaller 
28 
 
products also require a tap water supply and, in some cases, a heavy current supply to be properly 
tested, making them impractical for inclusion. Therefore, there was no question about the need 
to exclude them. 
To address data privacy concerns, the municipality of Copenhagen requested the exclusion of 
smartphones, tablets, and computers. Additionally, from an operational perspective, the repair of 
these devices is more specialized, requiring specific tools and expertise. Furthermore, the rapid 
pace of technological advancement means that these products quickly become obsolete. For 
instance, a 15-year-old laptop is generally less desirable to consumers, while older Hi-Fi systems 
or kitchen appliances often still provide comparable functionality and serve as viable alternatives 
to new products. 
A third product category was excluded based on the demand for repaired items. Since the goal is 
to put repaired products back into circulation and offer them as substitutes for new purchases, 
products that were unlikely to find a new home were excluded to avoid wasting time and repair 
resources. This includes items that are technologically outdated or heavily worn. 
A fourth product group was excluded after visual inspections. Applying the Pareto principle which 
is aiming to achieve 80% of the impact with only 20% of the effort, products with minimal resource 
or use value but a high predicted repair need were excluded, such as basic kitchen scales or 
simple alarm clocks. 
Finally, products posing serious safety hazards were excluded. This includes items like bicycle 
airbags (e.g., the Swedish Hövding) or smoke detectors. Some products were excluded before 
repairs, while others were excluded after an assessment during the repair process. For example, 
a Danish-designed Stelton electric kettle had to be excluded due to a malfunction in its power-
cutting mechanism. The bimetallic component in this mechanism had become bent and 
displaced as shown in Appendix A.5. Even if repaired, a repair on components like this is too risky. 
A malfunction could lead to overheating of the heating element, potentially causing the kettle's 
lower part to melt or, in the worst case, ignite a fire. 
4.1.2 Repair Process 
The repair process always begins with a safety briefing for new volunteers or interested 
participants. This briefing emphasizes the importance of using all the senses to identify potential 
issues: 
1. Sight: Visual inspections are key to identifying defects. For instance, a defective lithium-
ion battery is often noticeable by its expanded interior. Capacitors, as well as broken or 
melted fuses, can also be detected visually. Overheated components tend to darken the 
surface of metal or PVC housings. Plastic housings may show signs of melting in 
thermoplastic materials or darkened surfaces in duroplastic materials. Visible damage to 
PCBs and soldered joints is also common. 
2. Smell: Some defects can be identified by smell. For example, an overheated amplifier or 
melted plastic around the motor of a mixer may emit a distinct odor. If a device has an 
unusual smell, it should be thoroughly inspected before being plugged in, as it could pose 
a fire risk if not addressed properly. 
3. Touch: If there is still uncertainty after using sight and smell, it is important to open the 
device's housing and check its internal components when possible. Never plug in a device 
that may have suffered a short circuit or overheating without performing a thorough 
inspection first. 
==PAGE:29==
Sebastian Christaller 
29 
 
After performing sensory checks without electrical power, the next step is testing the device. It is 
crucial to be attentive during this phase and avoid handling the device directly. In some cases, 
people may be seen holding products in their hands while testing them in recycling center swap 
areas, which is a dangerous practice. Electrical plugs with integrated switches enhance safety, 
allowing a device to be plugged in without being turned on immediately. This step can be done 
after plugging in the device at the power switch. 
Once plugged in and tested, the device should be unplugged again before further analysis for 
additional issues. Simply switching the device off is not enough, as it may still retain power. 
For the repair, all necessary tools and equipment are provided during each session. As discussed 
in the "Repair-Friendly Product Design" chapter, opening the housing of a device can sometimes 
present challenges. In many cases, manuals or YouTube videos can provide helpful guidance on 
how the housing is assembled. Once the device is successfully opened, the next step is to identify 
the problem. Visual inspections and electronic measurement tools can assist in this process. 
Often, the issue is easy to pinpoint, whether it is due to dirt or a clearly damaged component. After 
replacing the faulty part, cleaning the device, or simply ensuring everything is properly 
reassembled, the housing is closed. Then the functionality is tested to ensure the repair was 
successful. Finally, the device can be given light cleaning to enhance its appearance before being 
placed on the swapping shelf, ready for its next home. 
4.1.3 Redistribution Plan 
Repaired and fully functional electronics hold significant value compared to other items found in 
recycling centers and their swapping areas (Bytterum). Therefore, the redistribution of these items 
must be managed equitably. It's important to prevent individuals from taking multiple items or 
selecting the best ones immediately. To achieve this, a mixed distribution approach is 
implemented: Less-demanded items are placed on the swapping shelf with a post-it note 
indicating they have been repaired, while more sought-after items are distributed through a raffle 
or special events designed to promote the project. This ensures that high-demand items aren’t 
simply taken but are instead allocated randomly to interested individuals. 
Most items are placed directly on the swapping shelves of Lykkebazaren during the event, allowing 
volunteers to provide additional explanations about the devices and ensure that no one person 
takes everything. A high-end hi-fi system with speakers was featured as a raffle on Instagram. 
People expressed their interest by commenting on the post, and the winner was randomly 
selected from those who participated. This method guaranteed fair chances for all and 
simultaneously helped raise awareness of the project.

4.1.5 Challenges and Limitations 
Spare Parts: Since the repaired products are donated free of charge, the investment in spare parts 
is kept minimal. Only small, standard components are purchased, with a budget limited to 30–50 
DKK per product. Whenever possible, free spare parts are reused. These are typically salvaged 
from other non-repairable devices. To support this, a stockpile of irreparable items is maintained. 
This allows for the disassembly and reuse of cables, screws, and other common components 
during repairs. 
Logistics: The project operates out of Lykkebazaren, located in Amager in the south of 
Copenhagen. Initially, only two donation stations were available. Two months after launch, this 
was expanded to include five recycling stations across Copenhagen. Due to organizational 
constraints and the outsourcing of larger recycling centers to the company ARC, it has not yet 
been possible to integrate these large centers into the project’s electronics collection network. 
Transportation to Lykkebazaren is typically managed via cargo bike, or occasionally by car, 
depending on employee availability and the location of the donation points within Copenhagen. 
WEEE Regulations: In accordance with WEEE regulations, items cannot be retrieved from official 
electronic waste containers. This makes the project heavily reliant on direct communication with 
the public and encouraging separate handing in at the smaller recycling stations where the 
cooperation exists. Unfortunately, many people remain unaware of the initiative. This is resulting 
in the majority of discarded electronics still ending up in standard e-waste bins. To remain 
compliant, the CERP cannot get items from these containers. The only viable solution is to 
increase public awareness so that items are handed directly to the project at the smaller recycling  
centres. 
==PAGE:33==
Sebastian Christaller 
33 
 
Volunteer Retention: Volunteers are unpaid and often face opportunity costs by dedicating time 
to the project instead of paid work. To maintain motivation, it is important to provide coffee, 
snacks, and opportunities for learning. Engagement must remain interesting, as it may reduce 
participation otherwise. Currently, the number of volunteers is stable and slightly growing, 
suggesting that the repair events offer sufficient value. Volunteers particularly appreciate the 
chance to connect with like-minded individuals, share knowledge about product repair, and 
engage in their niche technical interests. 
4.1.6 Potential for Scale 
The model is specifically adapted to the Copenhagen context, where "Bytterum" (swapping 
rooms) facilitate the distribution of repaired electronics. In other cities, alternative methods for 
both the distribution and collection of electronics would need to be developed. For example, 
handovers could be organized during or immediately after repair sessions. Collection could be 
coordinated in partnership with local recycling centers, or by setting up designated drop-off 
points in accessible public spaces such as libraries or town halls. This flexibility means the model 
can be adapted and implemented in various forms in other cities or countries. Within 
Copenhagen, CERP has already expanded from two to five collection centers which proves 
scaling is possible.

4.3 Manufacturer Remanufacturing Strategies 
Manufacturers in the electronics industry are increasingly turning to remanufacturing as a 
strategic approach to reduce costs, meet environmental regulations, and strengthen their brand’s 
sustainability image (Motes, P., 2025). Unlike basic recycling, remanufacturing involves restoring 
used products to like-new condition through a comprehensive process that includes 
disassembly, repair or replacement of worn components, and rigorous quality testing (Aydin, R. et 
al., 2023; Motes, P., 2025). This strategy helps to reduce electronic waste and minimizes the use 
of raw materials and still maintaining a high number of sales. 
Different models of remanufacturing exist, depending on a company’s capabilities and 
objectives. Some manufacturers choose to keep operations in-house to maintain control over 
quality and intellectual property. Others outsource to specialized third parties or implement take-
back programs that rely on consumers returning used devices in exchange for discounts (Aydin, 
R. et al., 2023). Regardless of the model, successful remanufacturing requires thoughtful product 
design that allows for easy disassembly and modularity as discussed earlier, along with an 
efficient reverse logistics system to handle the collection and processing of used items. The 
design for successful remanufacturing is similar to the design for repair (Motes, P., 2025). One 
main difference is that proprietary fasteners can be used in product design, since the aim is not 
to ease repair for everyone with non specific tools but only for the processes in the own 
refurbishment stream. Also open source availability of data, software and spare parts on the free 
market is no specific aim in design for remanufacturing. 
Regarding the conduction, operational integration is essential. Manufacturers must adapt their 
supply chains to accommodate the flow of returned products, manage inventories of both new 
and reclaimed parts, and ensure that remanufactured goods meet high standards to maintain 
customer trust (Aydin, R. et al., 2023). Although remanufacturing can lead to significant cost 
savings and environmental benefits, it also presents challenges. These include consumer

4.4 Customer Self-Repair & Manufacturer Support 
Cohen and Whang (1997) argued that after-sale services play a key role in enhancing the 
perceived quality of products, a strategy that manufacturers adopt to increase customer 
satisfaction and loyalty. These services can involve repairs or remanufacturing carried out at the 
manufacturer's facilities as mentioned before, or, alternatively, empowering consumers to 
undertake specific repairs themselves. For products where repair is simple and safe, guided self-
repair can be a viable option. Self-repair practices demonstrate high consumer competence in 
clothing and furniture repairs (Dorland, J., et al., 2025). Some repairs in electronics can also be 
simple and feasible with the right guidance. In such cases, manufacturers can provide repair 
guidance through online videos, manuals, technical drawings, or detailed service information 
(Lundberg, P. et al., 2024). Ideally, manufacturers should also supply spare parts to consumers. 
In the future, the possibility exists for manufacturers to share CAD data, enabling consumers to 
3D print non-mechanically demanding plastic parts themselves. However, this remains a 
futuristic idea, as not every household currently has access to 3D printing technology. Until then, 
the supply of spare parts remains an attractive revenue stream for manufacturers, especially 
when managed efficiently (Lundberg, P. et al., 2024). 
Sandez, Pérez-Belis, Juan, and Bovea (2023) conducted an experiment to explore the success of 
guided self-repair for a kettle with two induced faults. Participants were provided with repair 
guidelines in three formats: video, step-by-step instructions, and a guide. The study found that 
61.4% of participants preferred video instructions, 24.6% favoured step-by-step instructions, and 
14.0% opted for the guide. Of the participants who followed the instructions, 63.2% successfully 
repaired the kettle. The study also demonstrated that prior self-repair experience had a 
statistically significant impact on the success of the repair. Participants with previous repair 
experience achieved a success rate of 40.4%, compared to only 22.8% for those without such 
experience (Sandez et al., 2023). This finding supports the notion that learning how to repair, and 
gaining hands-on experience, significantly increases the likelihood of successful self-repair in the 
future. 
Despite the potential benefits, companies often worry that offering repair services or enabling 
self-repair could undermine their business. While this concern is valid, it does not necessarily

have to result in a loss of revenue. With the right pricing strategy, companies can generate 
significant profit from spare parts. In industries such as electronics and communications, after-
sale service revenues can account for up to 30% of the total product revenue (Cohen et al., 1997). 
For example, if the production cost of a spare part is only 2% of the full product, it can be sold for 
20% of the product's price. This results in a margin that can be 10 times higher than that of a new 
product. 
Moreover, when looking at spare parts markets, the difference in margins becomes evident. If a 
product were assembled entirely from spare parts, the cost would likely be at least twice as high 
as purchasing the product as a complete unit. In some cases, certain parts, such as bicycle 
frames and forks, are not available for sale as individual components. When considering the price 
of all other parts needed to assemble a bicycle, the cost of these individual parts would far exceed 
the price of a complete bike, excluding the frame and fork. This highlights the economic value of 
purchasing a full product rather than individual spare parts. Even though a bicycle is no electric 
product, it is a good example for looking at spare part prices since bicycles have a higher share of 
components available as spare parts than electronic products.

4.6 Business models for managing sustainable products 
The cost of repair services has risen significantly more than the price of new products over time 
(King et al., 2006). This increase is largely driven by higher labor costs, especially in developed 
markets (McCollough, 2019). As a result, businesses must adapt their models to maintain repair 
services as a competitive and attractive option. 
Lease and rent models shift the responsibility of product lifecycle management from the 
consumer to the manufacturer or service provider. In these models, the producer determines how 
long a product stays in operation and is repaired, ensuring that products remain functional for an 
extended period. A product with a high initial cost but low yearly operating costs and a long 
lifespan becomes a more viable and competitive option. The focus of these models is not to sell 
products, but to provide long-term use value to the customer (Duflou et al., 2008). 
However, such models may not appeal to consumers who prioritize short-term usage or those 
with limited financial resources to make a high initial payment (Amasawa, E. et al., 2020). 
Additionally, certain products, such as a child's buggy, which are only needed for a short time, are 
unlikely to be viable as long-life products. For instance, by adopting a rental model, where 
customers pay for each year of use, companies can design products that last much longer and 
serve multiple families over their lifetime. 
Swapfiets is an example of this type of model, providing bike rental services to students, who only 
need a bike for a limited period (e.g., four months). McKinsey & Company (2015) also highlights 
this approach with high-end washing machines. By leasing washing machines instead of selling 
them, households would save roughly a third per wash cycle, while the manufacturer would earn 
around a third more in profits. Over a 20-year period, leasing one 10,000-cycle machine instead 
of five 2,000-cycle machines would save 180 kg of steel and more than 2.5 tons of CO2e 
emissions (McKinsey & Company, 2015). 
Lease and rent models can thus better align consumption patterns with product lifecycles, 
offering a more efficient way of meeting various consumer needs (Amasawa, E. et al., 2020). If 
consumers' willingness to pay is the same, the cost of the product can be significantly higher in 
the lease or rental model (McKinsey & Company, 2015). Unlike the purchase model, where the 
focus is often on keeping costs low, the lease or rent model prioritizes cost-effectiveness over the 
product's lifecycle (Amasawa, E. et al., 2020).

Consumer hesitation to pay for repairs often stems from uncertainty about the reliability of the 
repair and the limited warranty that typically accompanies it. If the manufacturer would take the 
risk of repair reliability, consumers would likely feel more confident in opting for repairs or product 
exchanges. In a PSS model, the company would bear the risks associated with repair success, 
making consumers less concerned about whether a product is repaired or replaced (Amasawa, 
E. et al., 2020). The PSS Model addresses repair reliability and stimulates activity in the repair 
sector (Rogers et al., 2021). 
A potential downside of this centralized, manufacturer-controlled model is the risk of 
monopolization, where manufacturers control all repair decisions and could drive smaller repair 
businesses out of operation. It also raises concerns about limiting consumer options for self-
repair (Svensson-Hoglund et al., 2021; Rogers et al., 2021). If manufacturers exclusively conduct

repairs, the repair sector would shift to the manufacturer, potentially increasing the overall repair 
quota but also reducing competition and self-repair opportunities for consumers. 
4.7 Product clusters and their likelihood of repair  
According to Pérez-Belis et al. (2017) and Bovea et al. (2017), high cost is a key factor behind nearly 
80% of repairs not being carried out. The issue of cost involves not only the price of the repair 
service but also the cost of purchasing a new product and the added lifespan a repair might 
provide (Jørgensen, M. S. et. al., 2023; Torca-Adell, L. et al., 2025). In a survey conducted in India, 
Borthakur and Govind (2018) found that 68.1% of respondents would opt to buy a new product 
instead of repairing their existing one due to high repair costs. For most respondents a lower cost 
for repair compared to buying a new product is the reason for having an electronic product 
repaired (Jørgensen, M.S. et al., 2023). Financial reasons are the main motivator for repair 
decisions, while environmental considerations play a minimal role (Dorland, J., et al., 2025). 
In general, cheaper products are more likely to be replaced because the repair cost is often close 
to or exceeds the new product price (Torca-Adell, L. et al., 2025). More expensive products are 
more likely to be repaired, as they are often of higher quality and may have a longer lifespan before 
requiring repair (Hennies and Stamminger, 2016). Also, the likelihood that the product repair cost 
comes close or exceeds the new price is smaller. 
The tendency for high-quality products to be repaired is also supported by the product typology 
proposed by Cox et al. (2013). The authors identify three types of products: workhorse, 
investment, and up-to-date. According to their research, this typology influences repair 
behaviour. Workhorse products are valued for their utility and reliability, and they are expected to 
have a long lifecycle. Repairing a workhorse product involves weighing the additional life it will 
gain, the cost, and the inconvenience of the repair process. Investment products, due to their high 
initial cost, are more likely to be repaired, and an emotional attachment may also play a role in 
this decision. Up-to-date products, in contrast, are typically driven by trends and social identity. 
These products are more likely to be discarded or replaced due to changes in technology and style 
rather than malfunction. According to Jørgensen, M. S., et al., (2023), the main reasons across the 
three product types they analyzed in their study for buying a new product instead of a used 
product is a wish to get the newest model of product and not believing a used product will have a 
long lifetime. 
A good example of this is the Bowers & Wilkins Zeppelin Air, a docking station for iPhones and 
iPods. While it is a relatively expensive product with a retail price of around 5000 DKK, it would 
typically fall into the investment product category, like other high-end audio equipment. However, 
due to its reliance on Apple's 30-pin connector, this product has characteristics of an up-to-date 
product because it uses a now outdated standard. The Zeppelin Air is often discarded as newer 
Apple devices phased out this connectivity standard. The design decision to place the 30-pin 
connector at the center of the product effectively rendered it an up-to-date product, making it 
more prone to being replaced and less likely to be repaired compared to more traditional Bowers 
& Wilkins speakers. 
Another phenomenon can be observed with many high-end electronics, where a deposit-like 
system is in place, akin to bottle or can deposits. If we consider the higher price of such products 
as a premium, it parallels the deposit paid when purchasing a beverage container. Many high-end 
products retain resale value, even when broken. This is particularly true for products that are 
modular and repairable, as their resale value can approach that of a functioning unit. On 
platforms like eBay, dba, and others, one can find that high-quality products are often bought and 
==PAGE:45==
Sebastian Christaller 
45 
 
repaired by individuals or companies, despite being defective. The original owner, or "primary 
customer," can thus sell the broken product and recover some value at the end of its lifecycle 
again, similar to the bottle deposit system. 
For lower-end products, however, this effect is not seen. Few people are willing to invest the time 
and effort to repair a product with a minimal new price (Torca-Adell, L. et al., 2025). Moreover, the 
resale value for a defective, low-end product is so small that the original owner is unlikely to make 
the effort to list it on these platforms. In this sense, the transaction of secondhand products 
requires time investment from both the buyer and seller, leading to a "break-even point" effect. 
For products of low quality, this break-even point is often too high for them to be considered worth 
repairing or reselling. In contrast, high-quality products are more likely to be traded, even in 
broken conditions. The perceived value of a product is higher when it is of better quality, and the 
attractivity for repairers is higher if the product is modular and repair friendly.  
Hennies and Stamminger (2016) found a clear connection between product lifespan, price, and 
quality, and the likelihood of a product being repaired. Their research analyzed how many 
products had been repaired before reaching the end of their life. The results show that 50% of 
washing machines, 68% of computers, 97% of kettles, 71% of TVs, and 94% of hand mixers had 
never been repaired before being discarded. Particularly among small household appliances, 
such as kettles and hand mixers, the rate of products thrown away without repair is notably high. 
Also research on hair dryers in professional and private use shows that most of them are thrown 
away, often not even in the right e-waste recycling stream (Torca-Adell, L. et al., 2025). This is 
largely due to repair-inhibiting product designs and the low initial price of these items. 
McCollough (2007) estimated that consumers are typically willing to pay up to 20% of a product's 
replacement cost for repair services on small electronics. However, due to rising labor costs for 
qualified repair services, particularly in developed countries, and the decreasing cost of new 
products as manufacturing technology improves and labor costs in developing countries are 
lower, the price of repairs has increased. Additionally, the environmental costs associated with 
new product manufacturing (e.g., resource consumption and pollution) are not accounted for in

be better protected from exposure to food residue or mineral buildup. In the case of coffee 
machines, design improvements could include wider nozzles or pipes to reduce the likelihood of 
clogging due to limescale. From a user-side perspective, employing filtered water or reverse

5.2.1 Repair Stories without spare parts involved 
To give examples, two common cleaning tasks are described. 
Vacuum cleaners, especially bagless models, feature multiple filtration stages. After the cyclone, 
they typically have one or two pre-engine filters, followed by a HEPA filter step after the motor. 
When these filters become clogged, the motor has to work harder, drawing air through a restricted 
membrane that barely allows airflow. As a result, the motor becomes louder and overheats when 
running at maximum capacity. In the case of battery-powered vacuums, this filter clogging 
significantly reduces run time. This can lead users to believe the battery is near the end of its life. 
Often, this is not the case. A simple routine of cleaning, washing, or replacing the filters restores 
the vacuum’s cleaning power and extends battery life. This maintenance procedure usually takes 
around 20 minutes and can prevent the vacuum from being prematurely discarded. 
For coffee machines, descaling is a critical maintenance task. Over time, limestone buildup in the 
system can reduce heat conductivity in the heating unit, increase noise from the water pump, and 
cause blockages in the nozzles. Regular use of a descaling solution specifically designed for 
coffee machines helps prevent these issues. It’s important to note that using the wrong descaler 
can worsen the problem, as some descaling agents may create limestone balls that easily block 
the nozzles. To avoid this, it is important to always use a descaler that is compatible with the 
specific appliance. If a nozzle does become clogged, using the steam function can often help to 
clear it. Coffee machines with steam capabilities, such as fully automatic machines or espresso 
machines with a single water circuit, operate at two pressure/heat stages: the steam function runs 
at higher pressure and heat, which can help loosen a limestone plug in the nozzle. It is important 
to be cautious, as the built-up pressure could cause water to shoot out of the nozzle with high 
pressure after the plug became loose. 
==PAGE:56==
Sebastian Christaller 
56 
 
Both of these tasks are common maintenance issues that can be resolved with simple steps and 
no spare parts. Regular cleaning and descaling can restore full functionality to appliances and 
extend their lifespan.

In many devices, replacing a damaged cable is a common repair task. There are various reasons 
for cable issues, including insulation wear, abrasion, or even a broken or cut cable. Most of these 
problems are relatively easy to fix. Typically, the device housing needs to be opened, and a 
terminal block if available allows for the faulty cable to be removed and replaced. The new cable's 
phases can then be screwed in place. 
It's crucial to ensure that in devices with three-phase wiring, one of the wires is always the earth 
(ground) phase. The phases are often color-coded, making it easy to match them. If there is any 
uncertainty, the color scheme can be cross-checked, as these color codes are universal, even for 
non-European products. In cases where there is no terminal block, the wires can be welded and 
insulated with proper insulation materials, such as heat shrink tubing or electrical tape. It is 
important to use only materials specifically designed for electrical insulation, as using improper 
tape or materials can be very dangerous. 
In addition to cables, plugs and switches sometimes need to be replaced. While many modern 
plugs and switches are molded into onto the cable, screw-type replacements are still available 
and can be especially useful when a plug or switch needs to be swapped out. 
A common issue in vacuum cleaners is loose connections in the cable. This can happen if the 
spring in the cable roller mechanism is too tight, pulling the cable with excessive force, causing 
the plug connection to loosen. While replacing the entire cable in the roller can be difficult, 
swapping out the plug is often a simpler and more effective solution. 
Another specific example of a repair without spare parts is replacing a belt in a CD player. Similar 
tasks may also apply to cassette players or some record player models. To access the belt, the 
housing must be opened, and the reader mechanism disassembled enough to reach the belt 
driving the CD tray in and out. Most CD players use a motor below the tray or a gear system to 
rotate the CD, so not all models require a belt for the rotation purpose. Once the old belt is 
removed, installing the new one can be tricky due to limited space. Replacement belts can often 
be bought, but in many cases, a simple rubber band (such as those used to seal food packaging) 
can serve as a cost-effective substitute, especially for less critical components. 
When deciding whether to use a replacement part or a cheaper alternative, it's important to 
consider the function’s significance, the value of the product, and the potential consequences of 
==PAGE:57==
Sebastian Christaller

a failure. A faulty CD tray is not safety critical, thus a less durable replacement may be acceptable. 
In CERP opting for the inexpensive rubber band solution is the only possible solution, as the cost 
of an original replacement part would be too high, making the repair otherwise unfeasible.

barista machines and filter coffee machines to fully automatic machines, pad machines, and 
grinders. They are each exhibiting distinct usage patterns and repair timelines. Generally, barista 
and filter machines demonstrate higher durability, while fully automatic and pad machines tend 
to require repairs earlier in their lifespan. Interestingly, both pad machines, which are relatively 
inexpensive, and fully automatic machines, which are usually more expensive, show a higher 
likelihood of earlier failure. However, due to their higher initial cost, fully automatic machines are 
more often repaired, whereas pad machines are typically discarded. Consequently, even within a 
narrowly defined product group, the correlation between price and longevity remains ambiguous, 
although a slight trend suggesting that higher-priced devices may be repaired more frequently 
exists.

5.6 Depreciation of Electronic Products until repair is necessary 
Based on the analysis of 133 datasets, repairs are, on average, required 11.3 years after the date 
of production. Figure 48 explores the relationship between a product's original purchase price and 
its remaining monetary value which is referred to as the time value at the moment a repair is 
needed. A general trend is observable that products with higher original prices tend to retain more 
value at the point of failure. While the exact age at which a repair becomes necessary varies 
across product types, this analysis focuses not on fixed depreciation intervals (e.g., annually or 
over a decade), but on depreciation up to the repair event. The value retained at this critical point 
serves as a key determinant of whether repair is economically viable. 
Higher-priced products that still hold considerable time value are more likely to be repaired, as 
their residual worth justifies the associated repair costs. Conversely, if a product has already 
depreciated substantially and holds minimal value, the likelihood of repair diminishes. Even for 
low-cost products, repair may remain viable if sufficient value is retained. This is particularly true 
as spare part and labor costs tend to be lower for inexpensive items, a finding established earlier 
in this study. 
Figure 48 visualizes the relationship between new price and time value at the repair point. Notably, 
the figure includes two ellipsoids that qualitatively delineate zones where repairs are less or more 
likely to occur. These zones are not defined by an absolute time value threshold, but rather 
account for the factor that higher-priced products typically involve higher repair costs. Thus, the 
decision to repair depends not only on time value but also on the proportionality between residual 
value and anticipated repair expenses.

5.8 Repair behavior and pareto principle 
This study combines data from various research sources with the practical experiences of 
founding two repair cafés (in Germany and Portugal) and CERP, a recycling-integrated repair 
project in Copenhagen. Clear differences emerged between these projects in terms of product 
quality, willingness to repair, and complexity of repairs. 
In Germany, participants usually had mid- to high-quality products with moderate to difficult 
repairs, while in Portugal, products tended to be cheaper or mid-range and had often undergone 
previous attempts at repair. In CERP, repairs were generally simpler and spanned a broad range of 
product quality. 
A comparison of vacuum cleaner faults and coffee machines found that CERP dealt with easier-
to-fix problems. Typically, visitors at traditional repair cafés had already attempted simple fixes at 
home, leaving more complex tasks for volunteers. 
The Pareto principle, suggesting 80% of results come from 20% of efforts, is especially relevant 
here. Most repairs require minimal effort in CERP, while only a few are complicated or resource-
intensive. By prioritizing easy repairs, particularly in a wealthy area where people are less driven 
to visit repair cafés, CERP maximizes impact per volunteer effort. 
6. Key Findings & Recommendations  
6.1 Key Findings  
This masters thesis provides a comprehensive analysis of the barriers and possibilities 
surrounding repairability in small and mid-size electronic products. Disassembly, accessibility, 
and reassembly are found as the principles of repair-oriented design. Proprietary fasteners, glued 
or clipped housings, and inaccessible or overly integrated internal components were found to 
significantly hinder repair, especially for untrained users. To make a product repairable for a broad 
range of people, untrained consumers with limited technical knowledge should be able to perform 
non-safety-critical repairs. Even technically repairable products are often rendered impractical to 
fix due to risks of damage during opening or reassembly. Modular design and strategically located 
wear-prone parts facilitate both professional and user-led repairs. 
==PAGE:90==
Sebastian Christaller 
90 
 
One of the most critical challenges is the availability of spare parts. Regardless of physical 
repairability, products quickly become obsolete if replacement parts cannot be sourced. Long-
term spare part strategies, including remanufacturing, reverse logistics, and emerging 
technologies like 3D printing, are essential to maintaining repair viability over time. Also, the 
decline in publicly available technical documentation further reduces self-repair opportunities. 
Repair is not only environmentally important, but also a strategic opportunity. For manufacturers, 
offering repair services and spare parts can improve brand reputation, customer loyalty, and 
revenue. Municipal and volunteer-led initiatives, such as CERP, demonstrate scalable, low-cost 
models for reducing e-waste. In CERP, 111 repaired products prevented over 7,370 kg of CO₂ 
emissions, with 74% of items estimated substituting for new purchases. The remaining 26% which 
do not substitute new purchases are seen as improving welfare for people with limited financial 
means and thus reducing social inequality. 
Despite logistical and regulatory barriers (e.g., WEEE regulations, limitations in accessing formal 
e-waste streams), stable volunteer engagement and municipal collaboration show the  value and 
potential scalability of projects such as CERP.  
From a commercial perspective, truly independent repair businesses in Denmark are rare, and 
viability depends heavily on strategic product selection. A cost model developed in this study 
shows that for repair to be profitable at 600 DKK/hour (a benchmark derived from HIFI Klubben), 
only 80 of 133 products were viable. Profitability rises when only high-margin items are selected 
for repair. Although the model assumes no residual value for broken items, which may 
overestimate potential profits, emotional attachment or practical need could increase consumer 
willingness to pay. 
Government incentives could further support the repair economy. While evidence of large-scale 
impact is limited, even small support may shift consumer behavior and increase repair. 
Self-repair also presents unused potential. For non-critical items, consumer-self-repair is viable 
with support from the company. This can be spare parts and instructional resources. 
Experimental evidence shows that guided repairs improve outcomes, with prior experience 
significantly increasing success rates. While some manufacturers fear cannibalizing new product 
sales, spare parts can serve as profitable revenue streams, often sold at significant markups. 
Consumer interest in self-repair and community repair programs is strong when supported by 
access to parts and instructions. Future technologies like 3D printing of simple components 
could expand decentralized repair options even further. 
Cost remains the most significant barrier: around 80% of repairs are foregone due to high repair 
prices. Consumers typically reject repairs exceeding 50% of the cost of a new product, and only 
about a third are willing to pay that much. Most are only comfortable at or below the 30% 
threshold. Environmental concern is rarely a decisive factor in repair decisions. The financial 
considerations dominate. 
Product value also determines repair behavior. Low-cost items are frequently discarded due to 
high cost-to-value ratios when looking at the repair costs. High-end or emotionally valuable 
products are more likely to be repaired. 
When repairing, two-thirds of users prefer to receive their own product back after repair. For some 
products emotional attachment and hygiene concerns play a significant role. Additionally, 
consumer willingness to repair varies by product type. Hi-Fi systems, for example, are more likely 
==PAGE:91==
Sebastian Christaller 
91 
 
to be repaired than vacuum cleaners, despite a smaller-than-expected difference in consumer 
willingness to repair. This indicates a mismatch between interest and available repair services. 
A core finding is the high success rate of repairs in the CERP dataset. 112 out of 133 products 
(84%) were successfully repaired. This demonstrates that many consumer electronics retain 
significant functional potential beyond their initial failure point, particularly when addressed in 
settings where skilled volunteers or semi-professionals are available. 
Defects were classified into three categories: Electrical (E), Mechanical (M), and Dirt/External 
Contamination (D). Surprisingly, dirt-related failures were the most common, surpassing both 
electrical and mechanical issues. This suggests that poor maintenance and lack of cleanliness 
are critical but often overlooked contributors to product failure, especially in kitchen 
environments. 
Moreover, the perception among participants that electronics predominantly fail due to electrical 
or mechanical issues was shown to be misaligned with empirical evidence, showing the value of 
user education in preventative maintenance practices. 
Spare parts were required in only 42.8% of successful repairs. This is indicating that a substantial 
proportion of issues can be addressed through cleaning, reassembly, or minor adjustments. 
Notably, repairs without spare parts tended to have lower costs and higher economic viability. 
When parts were needed, costs were generally proportional to the product's new price. Lighting 
equipment showed the highest spare part involvement, aligning with its defect characteristics 
(e.g., broken bulbs, transformers, or glass components). 
68% of products were rated as “repair-friendly,” with 23% being “partly repair-friendly” and only 
9% classified as “not repair-friendly.” The bar for “repair-friendly” was set relatively low to avoid 
labeling most products as non-repairable. 
Repair cost, defined as the sum of spare part cost and labor (600 DKK/h), varied across products 
but showed no statistically significant correlation with product category. However, more 
expensive products tended to be more economically viable to repair, offering higher hourly 
returns. Repairs for cheaper products, while often simpler, were less attractive financially due to 
low resale value. 
On average, products required repair 11.3 years after manufacture. Higher-priced products 
showed a tendency to retain greater residual value at the point of repair, making their repair more 
likely. However, within specific product groups such as coffee machines, this relationship was 
blurred by variations in design, features, and perceived value. 
ANOVA analysis revealed that: 
• 
Repair costs differ significantly across fault categories, with mechanical faults being more 
expensive than dirt-related issues. 
• 
Repair success was not significantly influenced by the product's original price. 
• 
Repair cost did not differ significantly between products grouped as repair-friendly, 
medium, or not repair-friendly. This is indicating that even “repairable” products can be 
costly to fix. 
==PAGE:92==
Sebastian Christaller 
92 
 
• 
Statistically significant differences were found within Tier 2 subgroups of small household 
appliances, suggesting that more detailed categorization is necessary for meaningful 
insights. 
The CERP repair success benefited from the collection of discarded but often easily repairable 
items. In contrast, repair cafés tend to receive more complex cases due to often prior failed user 
self-repair attempts. This difference underscores the potential of low-barrier repair initiatives.  
6.2 Recommendations 
The findings clearly show that repairability is determined by design choices, availability of spare 
parts, consumer behavior, and broader economic and regulatory frameworks. To promote repair 
as a viable alternative to disposal, a series of strategic recommendations are proposed. 
First and foremost, product design must prioritize repairability as a fundamental principle. 
Manufacturers should focus on making products easier to disassemble, access, and reassemble. 
This includes avoiding the use of proprietary fasteners, glued or clipped housings that complicate 
opening, and overly integrated internal components that increase the risk of damage during repair 
attempts. Using modular design principles and locating wear-prone parts in accessible locations 
will facilitate repairs by both professionals and self-repairing-consumers. For instance, small 
household appliances and toys often have design barriers such as glued housings or non-
standard screws. These should be replaced by standardized components where opening the 
device does not pose a safety hazard. Products should be designed  to reduce dirt ingress by using 
sealed motors and heating elements. Consumers should be encouraged to perform routine 
maintenance including filter cleaning and descaling frequently to increase product lifetime.  
Equally important is ensuring long-term availability of spare parts and access to repair 
information. Even the most repair-friendly products lose their practical repairability if 
replacement parts cannot be sourced. Strategies such as remanufacturing, reverse logistics, and 
the use of emerging technologies like 3D printing must be supported to maintain part availability 
throughout a product’s lifespan. The decline of publicly available technical documentation poses 
a significant barrier to self-repair and independent repair businesses. Providing clear, accessible 
repair manuals helps consumers and open repair shops to repair. Creating platforms where 
consumers can easily find spare parts and repair services would further increase repair and 
affordability. 
Furthermore, companies should recognize the value of repairable products, not only from an 
environmental perspective but also as a source of after-sales and servitization revenue. A shift 
toward a circular economy involves moving away from the traditional model of frequent new 
product sales and instead focusing on revenue generation through product maintenance, 
updates, and other after-sales services 
Community and municipal repair initiatives are a scalable, low-cost model for diverting products 
from waste streams. Projects such as CERP demonstrate the environmental benefits achievable 
through volunteer based repair projects, including significant CO₂ emission reductions and reuse 
that often substitutes for new product purchases. To amplify the impact of these efforts, local 
governments should provide logistical, regulatory, and financial support to sustain and expand 
repair cafés and similar projects. Integrating repair activities further within formal waste 
management systems has a potential to increase repair rates. 
On the consumer side, there is a huge potential for self-repair, especially for non-critical and 
simpler products. This potential can be realized through offering instructional resources, 
==PAGE:93==
Sebastian Christaller 
93 
 
workshops, and digital repair tutorials that guide users through common maintenance and repair 
tasks. Encouraging consumers to perform routine maintenance can prevent many typical faults 
and extend product life. The development and adoption of technologies like 3D printing for 
producing simple replacement parts will further help consumers to address minor faults without 
professional intervention. 
Not all products require the same level of expertise or resources for repair. Publicly supported 
initiatives such as CERP should focus on simple repairs that yield the most waste diversion per 
volunteer hour. More complex repairs can be referred to skilled professional repairers. 
Aggregated repair data such as from CERP or the Open Repair Alliance should be systematically 
analyzed and outcomes integrated into EU-level lifecycle assessments, eco-design regulations, 
and digital product passports. Special attention should be given to fault clustering, product 
durability, and actual repair outcomes. 
Municipal waste stations could integrate triage processes to redirect reusable products toward 
repair initiatives like CERP to avoid premature disposal. Lightly defective but high-value 
electronics should be prioritized for these interventions. 
Given the heterogeneity within product groups (e.g., various types of coffee machines), future 
research should isolate single product categories and analyze them in depth. This would enable 
more targeted recommendations on lifespan, failure patterns, and design improvements. 
Despite this potential, huge cost is the main barrier to repair. The study found that roughly 80% of 
repairs are not conducted due to high costs. Consumers are generally unwilling to pay more than 
30–50% of a product’s replacement price for repair services. To overcome this, repair pricing 
models must be aligned with consumer expectations, and financial incentives should be 
introduced to encourage repair. Governments could consider subsidies or tax reliefs for repair 
services and spare parts. Manufacturers should develop repair services or lease models that 
include repair. Independent repair businesses should be substituted by the government to 
become viable for a higher share of products while eventually cross-substituting repair of lower 
revenue products by products with higher revenue. 
To make repair a mainstream, viable alternative to disposal, holistic strategies are essential. 
These include better product design, increased part availability, clear access to repair 
information, financial incentives, and community repair support. By implementing these 
recommendations, stakeholders can collectively transform repair from a niche activity into a 
mainstream. Creating the win-win-win situations where customer, company and climate all three 
benefit. 
6.3 Limitations 
While this study provides valuable insights and works with primary data, several limitations must 
be acknowledged. 
First, the primary dataset from the Copenhagen Electronics Repair Project (CERP) consists of 133 
electronic products. Although this number is sufficient for exploratory analysis and to identify 
indicative trends, it limits the statistical power and generalizability of the results. Subgroup 
analyses such as those focusing on Tier 2 WEEE categories or individual product types like coffee 
machines or vacuum cleaners are based on very small sample sizes. This restricts the ability to 
draw firm conclusions or to detect statistically significant differences across groups. 
==PAGE:94==
Sebastian Christaller 
94 
 
Second, the economic model used to assess repair costs and economic viability relies on several 
simplifying assumptions. A standardized labor rate was applied uniformly across products, 
assuming a medium-skilled repairer with defined hourly costs. While this approach enables 
comparability, it does not account for real-world variation in wage structures, shop overheads, or 
brand-specific repair pricing strategies. Moreover, consumer-specific factors such as individual 
time preferences, opportunity costs, or willingness to pay for sustainability are not reflected in 
this model. 
Third, the assessment of repair-friendliness was conducted using a qualitative categorization 
system. Although guided by defined criteria, such as the accessibility of components, type of 
fasteners, and availability of spare parts, this process inevitably involves subjective judgment. As 
such, the classification may be influenced by assessor bias or inconsistency, despite attempts to 
maintain objectivity. 
Finally, and most critically, the study's analytical focus on economic parameters does not fully 
capture the behavioral complexity of real-world repair decisions. Consumers are not perfectly 
rational agents. Their decisions to repair or discard products are influenced by non-economic 
factors such as perceived reliability after repair, emotional attachment, personal convenience, 
availability of service options, and broader social or cultural attitudes toward repair. These 
behavioral dynamics are difficult to quantify but essential to understanding the actual uptake of 
repair practices in society. 
Taken together, these limitations show the importance of interpreting the results as exploratory 
and context-specific rather than universally generalizable. They also point to key areas for future 
research. 
6.4 Future Work 
To build upon the findings of this study and address its limitations, future research should aim to 
collect larger and more comprehensive datasets, ideally encompassing several thousand 
products across a wider range of WEEE categories. Such datasets would allow for greater 
statistical robustness and enable more precise subgroup analysis. Collaborations with municipal 
waste authorities, repair cafés, and commercial repair service providers could significantly 
enhance data availability and ensure representation across diverse product types and regions. 
Focused studies on individual product categories such as fully automatic coffee machines, HIFI 
equipment, or vacuum cleaners would be particularly valuable. By narrowing the scope to 
homogeneous product types, researchers could better isolate the influence of specific design 
features on repairability and reduce variability caused by differing technological architectures. 
This would allow for stronger statistical inferences and the identification of design factors that 
either facilitate or obstruct repair. 
Experimental research could further contribute by systematically varying key design parameters 
such as fastener types, levels of modularity, or the use of adhesives in controlled settings. Such 
studies would offer causal insights into the impact of product design on repair time, spare part 
requirements, and overall repair success rates. These findings would be relevant for informing 
eco-design guidelines and regulatory frameworks. 
In addition to technical and economic assessments, future work should also incorporate 
behavioral research to better understand consumer decision-making around repair. Surveys, 
interviews, and field experiments could shed light on the psychological, logistical, and social 
factors that influence whether individuals choose to repair or replace products. Insights from this 
==PAGE:95==
Sebastian Christaller 
95 
 
could guide the development of policy interventions, such as awareness campaigns, financial 
incentives, or service accessibility improvements. 
Ultimately, an interdisciplinary approach combining engineering analysis, behavioral science, 
and policy research will be essential to fully unlock the potential of repair as a key pillar of a 
circular economy.

A1: Service manual included within the easily accessible housing of a Beogram 1000 record player, produced between 
1965 and 1972. This example shows a highly service-friendly product design and a manufacturer commitment to 
enabling self-repair. The integration of documentation directly into the product supports both professional and user-led 
maintenance. A stark contrast to many modern devices. 
==PAGE:103==

A2: Proprietary triangular screw head on the bottom of a Stelton water kettle, designed to restrict access and hinder 
unauthorized repair by private individuals. While some components such as the bimetallic thermostat used to cut 
power when the water boils are indeed safety-critical, many other repair scenarios do not pose safety risks and could 
be handled safely by trained individuals. Nonetheless, such proprietary fasteners are commonly found even on non-
critical parts, unnecessarily limiting repair accessibility. 
==PAGE:104==

A3: Stelton water kettle with integrated heating element and control components. In this case, the repair was not carried 
out due to a fault in the safety-critical overheating protection system. Repairs involving such components pose safety 
risks and are often not feasible without certified replacement parts or specialized knowledge. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
A4: Broken clip from the housing of a KitchenAid filter 
coffee machine. When housings rely on hidden clips 
rather than accessible screws, untrained repairers often 
damage the enclosure during disassembly. The use of 
standard screws instead of fragile clips would enable 
easier and safer access to internal components, 
significantly improving the product’s repairability.

Copenhagen Wheel exemplifies 
how highly integrated products 
can become prematurely 
obsolete due to software 
dependencies. As the product 
requires a proprietary app and

the discontinuation of production 
and shutdown of backend

critical limitation in modern 
product design, where lack of 
software support can lead to 
otherwise functional hardware 
ending up at recycling facilities.

and the dependency on full-board 
replacements rather than modular

A7: Rubber components of a high-end hi-fi speaker by Audio Note, showing typical signs of material degradation over 
time. Despite the deterioration, the speaker remains repairable. New rubber surrounds can be glued in place. 
Specialized repair shops offer this service for approximately 800 DKK per unit, which is economically viable considering 
the original purchase price of around 15,000 DKK. This example illustrates how high-quality products can maintain long-
term repairability.
