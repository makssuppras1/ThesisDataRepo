Abstract
The active control of ultralight deployable space telescope mirrors is investigated. An ef-
ficient method for finding the initial shape of a reflective membrane, leading to a spherical
shape upon application of a uniform pressure load, is developed. In-plane actuation by
piezoelectric control, and out-of-plane control by electrostatic forces are compared. Elec-
trostatic control leads to smoother control, but piezoelectric control is close in performance
if thickness, radius of curvature, and electrode size are well-selected.
The thermal design of a 1 meter diameter reflector using a piezoelectric laminate with
PVDF-TrFe as the active layer is investigated. It is found to be highly sensitive to the
thermal environment. Two different thermally balanced low CTE laminate stackups are
proposed, performing significantly better. The active correction of the 1 meter reflector
using 340 electrodes is investigated, and it is found to correct the thermal disturbance of
the improved design to nearly diffraction limited performance at wavelength λ = 500nm.

Introduction
Over the last 5 decades, space telescopes have expanded our understanding of our planet
and the cosmos. Looking away from the Earth, the next generation of space-based obser-
vatories will further increase our understanding of the universe, and aid us in the search
for life outside our solar system [1]. Looking towards Earth, space telescopes will continue
to be instrumental in areas such as agriculture, climate change modelling, and disaster
management [2].
Regardless of pointing direction, the primary performance driver of a telescope is its res-
olution and signal strength [3]. An exception is factors such as extreme stability, which
prove to be important for specific instruments, such as coronagraphs [1]. The resolution
will be driven by the accuracy and diameter of the telescope, whereas the signal strength
is driven by the effective area. With launch costs constraining mass and volume, there is
a strong desire to attain large, lightweight, deployable, and accurate reflectors. Attaining
such would pave the way for constellations of high resolution Earth Observation satellites,
or bolder astrophysics missions [4].
The areal density of space telescope mirrors has already been reduced significantly since
the Hubble Space Telescope’s mirror (Figure 1.1), which weighed 180kg/m2. The James
Webb Space Telescope, with its hexagonal segmented mirror design (Figure 1.2), attains
a primary mirror areal density of 25kg/m2 [3]. JWST is the next generation of space
telescope, and will launch on Christmas Eve this year. The next change in paradigm is
envisioned to be the use of highly controllable mirrors to reduce the areal density by yet
another order of magnitude.

Conventional optics uses heavy low-CTE (Coefficient of Thermal Expansion) glass mirrors
for its dimensional stability and ability to be polished to high precision. An alternative to
polishing is an optical replication process, where optical surfaces can be molded from a
polished master. This allows thinner geometries and a wider choice of materials, while
dimensional stability remains challenging [5].

This report will explore and compare active control using in-plane or out-of-plane forces
to attain the required dimensional stability for a thin-geometry mirror. Two concepts for
the actuation of the primary mirror will be compared:
The first actuation concept considers a thin membrane subjected to electrostatic forces
from control electrodes. Voltage applied in small electrode areas will induce a charge,
attracting the membrane towards the electrode array. An example of such a design is
shown in Figure 1.3. The electrode array must be fixed, and is thus mounted on a deploy-
able truss mesh. Such an electrostatically actuated membrane design has been explored
and tested already in [6], for use as a large deployable radio reflector for satellites.
The second concept utilizes in-plane forces from piezoelectric actuators incorporated in
a laminate. Figure 1.4 shows an example of such a laminate. When a voltage is applied
between the patterned electrodes and the ground electrode, the piezoelectric PVDF-TrFe
will contract or expand, controlling the shape of the reflector. A 20cm demonstrator of
this technology has been shown operating in open-loop in [7]. Since the laminate is made
from flexible materials, a rollable reflector could be made, which would deploy from its
internal strain energy. As opposed to the electrostatic membrane concept, the piezoelec-
tric concept would require no supporting structure, because of the thicker substrate. A
thicker substrate can be used since the piezoelectric forces are larger than the electro-
static forces.

Although the active primary is the focus of the report, sensing is explained such that the
system may be understood. An example of a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor is shown
in Figure 1.7. The wavefront from a distant object is initially plane. When it reflects off
the aberrated primary mirror, the wavefront takes on the shape of the mirror1. Thus, the
shape of the mirror can be measured by simply measuring the reflected light.
The wavefront sensor has an array of small lenses which focus a part of the wavefront
on to a camera sensor, in which the centroid location can be measured. If the local slope
of the wavefront is non-zero, this is equivalent to a beam of light at an angle, resulting
in a displacement of the measured centroid. From the local slope measurements, an
approximation of the wavefront can finally be computed.
The required accuracy of the reflector will depend on the wavelength of the light it is used
to collect. Light is meant in the broad sense, the considerations of the report are applicable
to visible, infrared or radio telescopes alike. Generally, an accuracy of RMS = λ/14 is
deemed sufficient, where RMS is the root mean square error with regard to a perfect
surface, and λ is the wavelength. For visible light, this leads to required accuracies of
around 500nm/14 = 36nm.

2
Modelling
The overall modelling approach will be described in this section. A simplified electrostatic
and piezoelectric model is presented. They will be used together with commercial FEM
software COMSOL to create a numerical model of the reflector. Subsequently, a control
scheme will be formulated to determine control inputs that give a best match to a desired
target shape.
2.1
Electrostatic Model
Electrostatic forces arise whenever electrically charged particles are present. Thus, if
a large amount of charge can be built up, the electrostatic effect can be utilized as an
actuator. Consider a charge build-up on the surface of a plate conductor from an applied
voltage. The electric field from the charge will repel any further charge from building up.
However, if an oppositely charged plate, separated by an insulator, is placed close to the
original plate conductor, the electrical field is reduced and thus the amount of charge on
the plate can be increased. This is the basic working principle of a capacitor, and the
same principle will be used for the electrostatic control of the reflector as shown in Figure
2.1. The insulator is the vacuum of space.

The mechanical model is handled by COMSOL. It is a membrane model, in which bend-
ing stiffnesses are neglected. This is only valid for very thin materials, where the bending
stiffness becomes negligible compared to the membrane stiffness. The numerical model
is geometrically nonlinear, which will be necessary to capture the effect of the initial shape
of the membrane. The membrane solver of COMSOL is explained in detail in [8]. The
implementation of the COMSOL model is explained in further detail in Appendix A.2. Gen-
eral COMSOL modelling tricks which may be helpful for the interested reader are also
explained in Appendix A.2.
In previous work [9], the membrane solver in COMSOL was compared against analytical
results. An expression for the deformation of a circular flat membrane subjected to a
uniform pressure [10] was used, and the numerical solution converged to an identical
result.
2.2
Piezoelectric Model
The piezoelectric effect can arise in crystal structures with no centre of symmetry. When
the material is deformed, it changes polarity due to the changing shape of the lattice. If
a material consists of such crystal structures, oriented randomly on a macroscopic scale,
they can be aligned by poling, during which a large electric field is applied while the mate-
rial is cooling. When the material has cooled and the dipoles are aligned, the piezoelectric
effect is prevalent on a macroscopic level. Deforming the material will lead to an electric
field and vice versa [3].
For a 1-dimensional piezoelectric material, the constitutive equations may be written in
standard form as [3]:
D = ϵT E + d33T
(2.2)
S = d33E + sET
(2.3)
where D is the electric displacement, E is the electric field, T is the stress, S is the strain,
ϵT is the permittivity under constant stress, d33 is the (direction-dependent) piezoelectric
constant, and sE is the compliance under constant electric field. The super scripts (T
and E) in general indicate which parameter the value is constant with regard to. The
subscript of d33 indicates the direction of poling and piezoelectric effect for the first and
second subscript respectively. Thus, in this example case, the actuation is happening in
the same direction as the poling. When considering only actuation and not sensing, as
is the case in this report, only Eq (2.3) is needed to describe the deformation. Effects of
hysteresis are ignored, as well as other nonlinear effects.
In general, the piezoelectric effect is anisotropic. The direction of concern in the piezoelec-
tric laminate is shown in Figure 2.2. The laminate is poled perpendicular to the substrate.
When an electric field is applied over the piezoelectric material, deformations will occur
anisotropically, according to the piezoelectric constants d33 (parallel to poling direction),
d31 (perpendicular to poling), and also in shear. However, all deformations except those
perpendicular to the poling direction (d31) will produce no significant deformation of the
substrate.

For an actuator with no sensor feedback, governed only by Eq (2.2), the behaviour can be
modelled as a thermal equivalent. In the piezoelectric laminate, the actuator is isotropic
in the plane, so the coefficient of thermal expansion is simply set to CTEeq = d31. The
thermal field applied should then be Teq = V /tf. where V is the voltage applied across
the film thickness tf. This means that commercial FEM code with the ability to simulate
thermally induced stresses can be used to model piezoelectric effects, when the above
mentioned assumptions hold.
In the case of piezoelectric control, the forces are applied in-plane, inducing a bending mo-
ment. Since bending stiffnesses are now significant, the membrane model will no longer
be applicable. A Reissner-Mindlin shell model is used instead in COMSOL. The COM-
SOL implementation can model general orthotropic materials such as fiber composites
in multiple layers. The layer properties are reduced to equivalent single layer properties,
which can then be solved using the shell solver. The composite theory as used in COM-
SOL is explained in [11]. An equivalent (simpler) theory for isotropic composites can be
found in [3]. The COMSOL shell solver solving the reduced system is explained in [12].
The modelling of the piezoelectric effect will use the described thermal equivalence, and
the implementation is explained in Appendix A.2.
In Appendix A.1, a verification of the COMSOL model is shown, where numerical results
are compared to analytical results for a flat circular disk with attached an uniform strain
actuator.

Initial Shape of Electrostatic Membrane
Before proceeding with control, it must be noted that the electrostatic control can operate
only in attraction. Thus, an initial shape of the membrane must be found, such that an
applied uniform bias pressure leads to a spherical shape of the membrane, as shown in
Figure 3.1. In previous work by the author [9], the initial shape of the membrane was
found by iteratively subtracting the error (between resulting shape and spherical) from
the initial shape of the membrane. This method does converge to a reasonable result,
but has some disadvantages. Firstly, the error at the boundary is always zero, since the
reflector is constrained at the outer edge. This isn’t optimal, it will be seen that an initial
displacement of the boundary should be allowed. Secondly, the previously implemented
method was not mesh-independent.

Influence Functions
The reflector will be partitioned into a number of electrode areas. Applying a unit control
input Cj to electrode number j will result in a displacement field across the reflector. This
displacement field is called the influence function of the electrode. The influence functions
of all electrodes will be used later in the control algorithm to determine control inputs. In
the electrostatic case, the control input is the pressure, removing the nonlinear pressure-
voltage dependence of Eq (2.1). The voltage can be found later. In the piezoelectric case,
the control input is the voltage. For the electrostatic case, geometrically nonlinear simu-
lations are used, since this is necessary to establish the initial shape. In the piezoelectric
simulations, linear analyses are used.
A reflector is modelled for both electrostatic control and piezoelectric control, using the
parameters in Table 4.1. The reflector is illustrated in Figure 4.1. The outer edge of the re-
flector is clamped, and only a single central electrode is considered. For the piezoelectric
control, the laminate geometry is illustrated in Figure 4.2.

Spatial Frequencies
The distribution of errors across spatial frequency is investigated, which will be of impor-
tance if a secondary deformable mirror is to be added to the optical system for further
correction of the wavefront. In a normal modern adaptive optics system, wavefront error
correction can happen by a flat deformable mirror. The mechanism of such a mirror is
similar to the mechanism of the large active primary discussed in the report. If a flat de-
formable mirror is added to the system, it must be noted in what range of spatial frequency
the residual correction must happen. It is a concern that the piezoelectric control could
add more high frequency error to the shape, which will be more difficult to correct.

residual error after correction. The decomposition produces the best fit sum of Zernike
modes, up to a specified degree, to match the residual error. The implementation is similar
in method to the previously described control algorithm: a matrix of the Zernike modes
is constructed, which is analogous to the Jacobian. The Zernike coefficients can then be
found using Eq (5.3) as described in Section 5. ndc = 200 coefficients are computed, the
first 100 are shown for the target, after piezoelectric control, and after electrostatic control

6.8
Findings
It was found that electrostatic control is in general better at attaining a shape with low resid-
ual error. However, the difference is small. This finding is only true when the electrodes
are small as compared to the boundary layer size (Figure 6.12). For a very large reflector
with moderate accuracy requirements and low electrode counts (i.e. a radio reflector), the
electrostatic approach may be best. However, for the operation in visible wavelengths,
a high amount of electrodes is required. In this case, the two control types are similar
in performance. However, attaining an initial high degree of accuracy is expected to be
easier for the shell laminate than for the electrostatic membrane. This will be discussed
further in Section 9. For these reasons, the next part of the study will focus only on the
piezoelectric control. The thermal design of the shell laminate will be investigated.

Thermal Design of Piezoelectric Reflector
When the reflector is located in the space environment, it will be heated by the sun directly,
and indirectly by reflection from the Earth. Since the reflector is curved, it will not be
uniformly heated. Further, there may be times when the reflector is in full or partial shadow
behind the Earth. In the baseline design for the piezoelectric reflector, the CTE of the
involved materials is high, and the laminate is thermally unbalanced. Thus, the effect of
varying temperature fields on the deformation of the reflector should be considered.
A 1-meter diameter reflector is analysed. The reflector is simply supported in the inner
ring. That is, only the vertical and tangential translation is constrained. An illustration of
the reflector is shown in Figure 7.1. The actual constraint on the reflector will depend on
how it is implemented from a systems perspective, including the deployment mechanism.
It is expected to be more likely that the reflector will be constrained near the centre rather
than on the outer edge.

and geometry used. Thermal deformations will be found for two different thermal fields
and multiple layer configurations. The desired result is a layer configuration with low
thermal deformation for both thermal fields. Later, the deformation results will be used as
input for active control to find the final residual error.

9
Discussion
Electrostatic vs piezoelectric control
It was desired to compare the performance of electrostatic and piezoelectric control for
the active primary of a space telescope mirror. The electrostatic control was found to be
smoother, but the effect is not highly significant when the parameters of the piezoelec-
tric control are well-selected. While the piezoelectrically controlled shell lends itself well
to various deployable concepts, the lack of bending stiffness in the electrostatic control
means that a supporting structure would need to be added and subsequently deployed.
Depending on deployment concept, errors from the deployment of the supporting struc-
ture could be imparted onto the membrane surface, and would be difficult to correct.
Parameter selection
For piezoelectric control to be effective, large amount of electrodes must be used, and
the radius of curvature should be as high as possible. Further, the substrate must not be
too thin. The radius of curvature will be determined by the optical design of the telescope,
whereas the thickness can be selected for best performance. The thickness should be
selected such that the effect of the boundary layer length is minimized (Figure 6.12), while
control voltages are limited. The eventual rolling of the reflector for use as a deployable
mirror would also set constraints on the substrate thickness. For the control of the 1m
reflector with 340 electrodes as shown in Figure 8.5, the maximum resulting voltage is
around 100m for a thickness of 400µm. Keeping voltages at these levels is advantageous,
as it simplifies control of many channels. Control of hundreds or even thousands of actu-
ator at these voltage levels may be feasible. For instance, deformable mirror controllers
operating 1024 channels up to 200V have been developed already for space use[16].
The controller takes up a 10x10x5cm volume, meaning it could feasibly fit in a standard
cubesat design. Whether such a control unit will be sufficient to drive the active primary
mirror will depend on the power requirements, which have not yet been investigated.
One driver of the power requirements will be the thickness of the active layer. If the thick-
ness of the layer is increased, the leakage current of the PVDF-TrFe will drop. However
this will come at a severe disadvantage, namely the increase in overall CTE of the mirror.
Assuming leakage current levels are acceptable, the PVDF-TrFe layer thickness should
be selected based on the breakdown voltage, which is around 5 · 105V/mm [3]. This sug-
gests that for control in the 100V range, PVDF-TrFe thickness could be slightly reduced
(5 · 105V/mm · 5µm = 2.5kV). However, experience1 has shown that the breakdown volt-
age is lower in practice. A reduction of PVDF-TrFe layer thickness would reduce thermal
deformations significantly.
Thermal Design
In the investigation of the thermal design (Section 7) the displacements due to thermal
imbalance were investigated. It was found that the baseline design, using a high CTE sub-
strate and unbalanced layout was very sensitive to the thermal environment. Balancing
alone was found to be insufficient. To keep thermal deformations to a reasonable level,
the design must be thermally balanced and the overall CTE kept low by selecting specialty
engineering plastics such as Novastrat 905 with a CTE of around CTE = 0.04 · 10−6 [15].
Although the thermal balance was attained by making the layout symmetric, which was
easiest to implement, this isn’t the only way of balancing the substrate. An equivalent

Multiple deformable mirrors
In this report, the proposed system architecture as explained previously (Figure 1.6) fea-
tures the primary mirror as the only active part. However it has also been suggested to
operate the system with a second (flat) deformable mirror [3]. In this architecture, the con-
trol is split into low and high frequency components, with the active primary correcting only
low frequency errors. In a sound-system analogy, it is sometimes called a woofer-tweeter
arrangement. Although as was seen in Figure 8.5, the residual error when many elec-
trodes are used is of high spatial frequency. Thus, the further correction of this residual
would set large demands for wavefront measurement and require a very high actuator-
count secondary deformable mirror. Such a system is unlikely to fit in a nanosatellite (e.g.
standard 10x20x30 cm CubeSat) but may be applicable for larger satellites.

Conclusion
Comparisons have been made for the control of an electrostatic membrane and piezoelec-
tric shell laminate. An efficient method for finding the initial shape of an electrostatically
controlled membrane mirror has been found. As opposed to previous work [9], mesh in-
dependence has been demonstrated, suggesting that the shape will be applicable in real
manufacturing.
Due to electrode boundary effects, the residual error of the piezoelectric control is in-
creased if the substrate is thin, if the electrodes are large, or if the radius of curvature
of the reflector is small. If parameters are selected carefully, good control can attained.
The manufacturability of a deployable membrane reflector has been discussed, and it is
believed that a piezoelectric shell is easier to implement for operation in the optical wave-
length regime. The slightly better control of the electrostatically controlled membrane is
unlikely to make up for manufacturing difficulty.
In the baseline design of an unbalanced high-CTE laminate, a very high sensitivity to ther-
mal environment has been demonstrated. Thermal balancing of the laminate alone has
been shown to be insufficient. Balancing and selection of a specialty low-CTE substrate
can produce a laminate with more modest thermal displacements.
A 1m mirror with 340 electrodes has been analysed. A random temperature field with an
amplitude of 2K lead to Peak to Valley thermal displacements of 13.97µm. With active
control, the PV-error drops to around 0.3µm and RMS ≈60nm. For a diffraction limited
optic in the visible regime, the desired error was RMS ≈34nm. A diffraction limited
reflector can be attained by increased electrode count or possibly by reduced PVDF-TrFe
layer thickness.
The next step for the project will be to demonstrate control authority in thermal-vacuum1
of one of the proposed low-CTE balanced layouts (Figure 7.9 or Figure 9.1). The goal will
be to demonstrate that the control authority of the reflector exceeds the thermally induced
deformation in a relevant environment. This would raise the technological readiness level
of the technology, paving the way for full-scale testing in a real mission.

First, the overall program structure will be described with an example program flow. Then,
some details of the specific COMSOL implementations will be given. Lastly, code exam-
ples which are deemed interesting are shown. The full codebase is not documented since
it is large. The code is available at [17]. The implemented codebase may be run on a
personal computer using COMSOL Multiphysics 5.6 with MATLAB, or on the DTU HPC
Cluster by submitting the included batch script. The cluster code is platform specific. The
full details of the COMSOL models are not explained either, but they are available at the
same location. The Appendix is meant to give a broad overview of the simulation im-
plementation. The code is written and implemented by the author, with exception of the
Zernike decomposition code, which has been supplied by Kainan Wang.
COMSOL was used since, in the beginning of the project, the exact method of analysis
was not chosen. Since it can analyse a wide variety of problems, it was deemed suitable.
Some things may have been implemented in more efficient ways in other software e.g.
reduced simulation times for linear shell elements in software not relying on multiphysics
simulations. In other aspects, such as the use of adjoint methods (SNOPT) to optimize
the shape of the nonlinear membrane, the software worked very well.
In past work [9], the COMSOL implementation was based extensively on the COMSOL
MATLAB LiveLink API. An entire model can be created in the COMSOL GUI, and then
be exported as a MATLAB file. Then, the MATLAB file can be modified by adding code
snippets, for instance to do the computation of the Jacobian. This approch however, is
very cumbersome if significant changes are to be made on the original file, since the
MATLAB file will be very long and difficult to interpret. A different approach is used in
this report. First, a COMSOL model is made in the GUI, which serves as a skeleton.
This skeleton can then be automatically imported into MATLAB, where it can be modified.
Functions which are needed to e.g. modify the COMSOL geometry are written as scripts in
COMSOL, and can then be called from MATLAB using the imported model. This approach
is advantageous as the skeleton can be modified in the GUI without having to export
the model as a MATLAB file. Also, there is a more clear distinction between COMSOL
functions (numerical simulation setup) and MATLAB functions (optimization algorithm and
postprocessing).
The structure of the codebase is shown in Figure A.1. 3 types of files are shown, MATLAB
files denoted .m, COMSOL files denoted .mph and finally Model Methods, which are scripts
inside COMSOL. parameterSweep.m controls the overall simulation, and parameters are
set and stored in simData.m, which is a class containing simulation parameters, sent to
the functions from parameterSweep.m.
An example program flow, utilizing Algoritm 1 as explained in Section 5, is:
1. Parameters are specificed in parameterSweep.m.
2. Using the simData.m class a parameter file is exported.
3. simJac.m calls upon electrostaticSkeleton.mph to generate the model according
to the parameters.
4. The model is generated by Model Methods setParameters followed by generateGeometry.
Then, generateLoadsSweep creates the loads to be solved for.
5. simJac.m calls upon the solution of the model and the data is exported from COM-
SOL using Modell Method exportData.

With this implementation, post processing can happen on previously stored data, or new
targets can be tested without having to re-compute the Jacobian. The next sections will
describe the COMSOL implementation for piezoelectric and electrostatic control respec-
tively.
A.2.1
Piezoelectric control
The piezoelectric skeleton Model Tree, from which the important parts of the simulation
setup can be explained, is shown in Figure A.2a. The Shell interface is used, with the
Layered Linear Elastic Material node. One Thermal Expansion node is used per
electrode, and here the load is specified.The Layered Linear Elastic Material gets
the material properties of the laminate from the Layered Material node under materials,
which combines materials properties from the 3 different materials shown. As explained
above, the Model Methods shown are responsible for the generation of the geometries
and specification of electrode loads. The discretization used is quadratic.
A.2.2
Electrostatic control
The electrostatic skeleton Model Tree is shown in Figure A.2b. The Model Methods con-
tained are similar to those shown in Figure A.2a, and are hidden. The model has two major
components, the 2D axisymmetric case and the 3D case. The axisymmetric model uses
a General Optimization node to implement the optimization algorithm as was explained
in Section 3. 4 studies are shown. First, 2D initial computes the axisymmetric prob-
lem with no change in shape, saving the maximum deformation. The shape optimization
is started using 2D shape optim. Then 3D Jac computes the Jacobian. MATLAB com-
putes control inputs based on the Jacobian and the final result can be computed using 3D
initial. The discretization used is cubic in both the axisymmetric and 3D models.
A.2.3
Thermal simulation
The thermal simulations do not require any optimization algorithm and thus can be han-
dled entirely in the COMSOL GUI. The model tree is shown in Figure A.3. The materials
are implemented by a Layered Material node, similarly to shown in Figure A.2a. The
implementation is different however in terms of modeling thermal strains. The Thermal
Expansion node as was used for modeling the electrodes takes as input a specific value.
Thus, a Multiphysics interface is used instead, tying the temperature field to the strain.
Note that heat transfer effects are ignored, since the temperature field is specified over
the entire domain.
