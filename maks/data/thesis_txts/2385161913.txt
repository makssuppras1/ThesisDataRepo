Abstract
Environmental pollution of plastics have become ubiquitous throughout the marine environment
and is recognized as an emerging key challenge for our society on a global scale. As plastic debris
accumulated in the marine environment it is subject to degrading forces which, in time, will
make the plastics progressively fragment into smaller pieces, the so-called microplastics. The
smaller sizes of plastics, will alter the availability to aquatic organisms. Amongst organisms of
lower trophic levels, notably ﬁlter-feeders such as mussels eﬃciently ﬁlter seawater and can, as
a consequence, also ﬁlter smaller particles like microplastics. Few studies have worked with lab-
oratory exposures of mussels to microplastics, assessing ingestion and egestion. Moreover, none
of these have yet worked with environmentally realistic exposure concentrations of microplastics.
In this study, the mussel, Mytilus edulis, was used to assess eﬀects of microplastics in the ma-
rine environment. In order to gain insight into this, ﬁrstly, experiments with stress responses,
ingestion and egestion were conducted. Secondly, the learnings therefrom were leveraged to
assess the distribution and fate of MPs in the surrounding environment.
Furthermore, an
enzymatic digestion protocol was developed to ensure proper quantitative assessment of mi-
croplastics in mussel tissue.
The assessment of three stress responses; Body Conditioning Index; Clearance rate and
Byssus thread regeneration was investigated during a continuous three-week exposure with two
exposure concentrations (5.3 and 25.5 microplastic particles mL–1). No statistical signiﬁcance of
any of these tested stress responses was observed at any of the used exposures concentrations as
a result of the three-week exposure. It is suggested that the three-week period should have been
prolonged. Ingestion and egestion was assessed at two even lower exposure concentrations (5
and 100 microplastic particles L–1) to be considered environmentally realistic and was measured
at two exposure time points of 10 and 40 minutes. Microplastics were found in tissue of all
mussels being exposed to microplastics, in all treatment combinations. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in time was observed (2-way ANOVA: p=5.43e-07), indicating the exposure time increases the
microplastic uptake. There was not found an eﬀect of varying concentrations which suggests
that the proportion of intake by the mussels is similar regardless of the quantity of microplastics
they are exposed to. No signiﬁcance of egestion was found (comparing percentages left in tissue
before and after egestion), however the short egestion period of the experiment implies this
needs further investigation and longer egestion periods. The average clearance rates, based on
the amount of MP beads, were found to be lower than the average clearance rate of algae,
suggesting a selective feeding and/or evasive behavior in the Mytilus edulis. To ensure a proper
quantitative assessment of MPs in mussel tissue in the ingestion and egestion experiment an
enzymatic digestion protocol was developed initially in the study. A comparative test of six
industrial enzymes established that enzymes to be advantageous for recovery of microplastics
from animal tissue and that the optimal enzyme used to digest mussel tissue was Alcalase
(2.5%). When employed according to the protocol, the enzyme was found to be highly eﬃcient
in digesting the tissue (on average 12.39% organic matter remaining). These ﬁndings provide
important learnings to guide future assessment of MPs uptake and eﬀects in ﬁlter-feeders such as
mussels. Finally, the new enzymatic digestion protocol contributes with a precise and practical
method to employ in similar assessments.

Introduction
2.1
Problem In Question
Plastics have within the last decades become an inevitable part of everyday life on this planet.
Plastic has achieved widespread importance due to the many favorable properties of plastics; it
is lightweight, durable, strong and inexpensive (Andrady, 2011; Kershaw et al., 2011). Thus, one
ﬁnds it applied in a variety of industrial productions, e.g. the transport sector, in constructions,
food products and packaging, telecommunications and medicinal applications (PlasticsEurope,
2016; Kershaw et al., 2011). As a result, the global production of plastics are continuously
increasing and in 2015 the global annual production reached 322 million metric tons (Plastic-
sEurope, 2016). Poor waste management practices, accidental loss and indiscriminate discard
of plastics, however, entail that plastics are accumulating in the marine and land environment
at an uncontrollable rate (Thompson et al., 2009; Wright et al., 2013b). Moreover, plastics
persist remarkably well in the environment if not properly disposed and therefore a property
such as durability is a noteworthy and ironic reason for why plastics present an environmental
threat (Thompson et al., 2009). In fact, it is estimated that all plastics ever released into the
environment are still present to some degree (Thompson et al., 2004, 2005). In recent years
this environmental pollution of plastics has begun attracting signiﬁcant attention and is now
arguably recognized as an emerging key challenge for our society on a global scale (Woodall
et al., 2014; UNEP, 2015).
Currently, the annual load of plastics to the environment are estimated to be in the range
of 12.2 million tons. Of this volume, 94% of the plastics entering the ocean are expected to
end up on the sea (Eunomia, 2016). It is estimated that at least 5.25 trillion plastic particles
weighing 268,940 tons are ﬂoating on the surface in the ocean basins (Eriksen et al., 2014). A
tendency which is expected to increase in future years, as the global plastic production is also
increasing (Jambeck et al., 2015). Furthermore, in the ocean there are hot-spot zones of very
high concentrations of plastic debris and these are located in the ocean gyres (Cózar et al.,
2014). Apart from the hot-spot zones, beaches of coastal areas are often where the plastics
ends up, especially the plastics ﬂoating on the sea surface such as plastic bags (Moore, 2008).
Once entered into the environment, it is especially the properties of persistency and density
that determine the "fate" of these plastic from there forward.
Firstly, density is the main determinant of where plastics will end up in the marine envi-
ronment. Depending on the density of plastics relative to that of the surrounding saltwater as
well as the dynamics of wind-induced mixing in oceans plastics will be found either on surfaces
or migrate down the water column into the deeper sea (Eriksen et al., 2014). That is, diﬀer-
ent densities aﬀect the buoyancy of plastics and entail diﬀering vertical distributions in the
water column (Kukulka et al., 2012; Reisser et al., 2014). While processes of weathering and
biofouling may alter the density of a plastic such that it settles on sediment at the seabed, if
buoyant the plastics become subject to wind- and ocean-driven transport, ultimately allowing
for widespread distribution (Moore, 2008).
Secondly, although considered relatively persistent in the environment plastics are nonethe-
less exposed to degrading forces that, in time, will make the plastics progressively fragment
into smaller pieces and accumulate in the oceans (Andrady, 2011; Sundt et al., 2014). This
persistence is determined based on the type and composition of plastic (Andrady, 2011) and
the presence of photolytic, mechanical and biological degradation forces (Andrady, 2011) such

as UV-B radiation, physical abrasion by wave action, biofouling by microorganisms and/or
re-suspension from oil or sediments (Andrady, 2011; Reisser et al., 2014).
This progressive
fragmentation of macro-plastic debris will lead to an increase of smaller and smaller plastic
particles, the so-called microplastics (MPs), accumulating in the oceans. In addition to frag-
mentation of larger marine plastic debris (Arthur et al., 2009), the accumulating microplastics
are also introduced directly from human-based activities such as maritime industry and con-
sumer products washed down the drain (Hammer et al., 2012; Arthur et al., 2009).
While presence of plastics can cause an aesthetic issue, actual deleterious eﬀects on more
than 250 marine species have also been demonstrated (Wright et al., 2013b).
The marine
organisms have been aﬀected by ingestion or entanglement of plastic debris present in the marine
environments (Wright et al., 2013b). The impact of this includes reduced feeding activity, energy
reserves (Wright et al., 2013b) and growth, diminished predator avoidance, and abrasions of
the digestive system (Wright et al., 2013a). The continuous fragmentation of plastic particles
renders more and more aquatic species, especially in the lower part of the food chain, exposed
hereto (Mendoza and Jones, 2015; Browne et al., 2008). Organisms with selective feeding of
particles in a speciﬁc size-range, as low trophic suspension-, ﬁlter- and deposit feeders have
shown to be one of these exposed groups (Browne et al., 2008). Here the MP particles can
be ingested or mistaken for food, in some cases leading to an accumulation which have caused
severe eﬀects on tissue and cellular levels (von Moos et al., 2012).
Microplastics have also
been shown to be ingested by invertebrates such as crustaceans, polychaetes and bivalves (Van
Cauwenberghe and Janssen, 2014; Browne et al., 2008; von Moos et al., 2012; Xu et al., 2016),
by vertebrates such as ﬁsh and whales (Lusher et al., 2013; Fossi et al., 2012) and planktonic
organisms such as zooplankton taxa, copepods and larval ﬁsh (Cole and Galloway, 2015; Cole
et al., 2013; Setälä et al., 2014).
Furthermore, as plastic debris becomes smaller this can result in a change of properties of
the particle (Eriksen et al., 2014) e.g. so as to become an adsorbent for organic contamination
(Hale et al., 2010; Ivar do Sul and Costa, 2014). That the MPs can act as a carrier of hazardous
pollutants already present in the environment and make these available for aquatic organisms
adds a new layer to the concern of eﬀects on aquatic species (Teuten et al., 2009).
The animal class of ﬁlter-feeders play an important role in the marine ecosystem. As they
eﬃciently ﬁlter particles from the several liters of seawater daily streaming through the organ-
ism the consequence is a cleansing of the water allowing other marine-life to thrive. In this
regard, the exposure of MPs to the ﬁlter-feeders is a rising concern (Riisgård et al., 2011). The
blue mussel, Mytilus edulis (M. edulis), is commonly used as a model organism in that it plays
a signiﬁcant role both in ﬁltering away microorganisms (von Moos et al., 2012), bacteria and
toxins and as a food source to animals of higher trophic levels (Riisgård, 1988; Zorita et al.,
2007; Karlsson et al., 2017). Blue mussels are in addition commonly harvested by humans as
food supply, making their thriving and reproduction of high economical value.
To this day, scientiﬁc studies have typically not worked with environmentally realistic MP
concentrations in their experimental design when exposing aquatic organisms (Lenz et al., 2016).
Especially when assessing eﬀects, very high concentrations of MPs have been used in the labora-
tory tests. Moreover, hitherto, the focus of laboratory studies has not been on the relationship
between ingestion and egestion of MPs in mussels (Wright et al., 2013b). Finally, most studies
monitoring mussel MPs content have done so with direct samples from the uncontrollable envi-
ronment of the ﬁeld and typically at single points in time (Courtene-Jones et al., 2017; Bagaev
et al., 2017; Van Cauwenberghe and Janssen, 2014). This makes for an insuﬃciently explored
and signiﬁcant subject in environmental research and the problem in question for this thesis to
strive to unravel.

2.2. AIM OF STUDY
2.2
Aim of study
The problem in question has thus become to investigate three aspects of the phenomenon of
MPs in the ﬁlter-feeder, M. edulis. Namely, eﬀects of ingestion and egestion at environmentally
realistic concentrations of MPs; insight into removal and distribution of MPs; and implications
of stress responses as a result of continuous thee-week exposure. This has resulted in one overall
aim of this thesis study.
Overall Aim:
To expand the understanding concerning ingestion and egestion of microplas-
tics in mussels and eﬀects related hereto.
The overall aim has been operationalized into the following three subsumed aims:
• Sub-aim 1: To evaluate the eﬃciency of diﬀerent industrial enzymes in digesting the
tissue of the blue mussel, M. edulis, and thereby establish an optimal digestion protocol.
• Sub-aim 2: To identify whether a continuous exposure to MPs can induce stress re-
sponses in the blue mussel, M. edulis.
• Sub-aim 3: To assess the ingestion and egestion of MPs in the blue mussel, M. edulis,

3.1.2
Persistence, distribution & degradation
Plastics are persistent and account for a large fraction of all marine debris in the Baltic Sea,
the North Sea, the Celtic Sea, the Bay of Biscay and diﬀerent areas in the north-western basin
of the Mediterranean Sea (Galgani et al., 2000), with plastics accounting for more than 70% of
collected debris in Galgani et al. (2000). Plastics in the open oceans are subjected to ocean cur-
rents and wind mixing, inﬂuencing the geographical transport of plastics (Kukulka et al., 2012).
As the plastics are transported both in horizontal and vertical direction by ocean currents and
wind mixing (Kukulka et al., 2012), it has been observed that MPs tend to accumulate high
concentrations at speciﬁc hot spot zones, which have often been referred to as the ocean plastic
gyres (Eriksen et al., 2014). The term hot spot zones, indicates greater concentrations of plastic
debris compared to the rest of the open water bodies.
As most plastics are low density polymers compared to the density of seawater, they tend to
be present at the surfaces of the oceans (Andrady, 2011). Nevertheless, several processes at sea
such as weathering and biofouling can alter the density of the polymers (Reisser et al., 2014),
which in turn aﬀects the buoyancy and thereby the composition and location of the polymers
in the water column (Ziccardi et al., 2016). Sedimentation of plastics are also highly observed
especially for the plastics with higher densities that seawater (Eunomia, 2016). In the marine
environment, plastic degrades due to photolytic, mechanical and biological processes (Browne
et al., 2007; Andrady, 2011). The degradation processes are often slow, and plastic items can
persist in the environment for decades (Ryan et al., 2009a). If plastics are ﬂoating on the ocean
surfaces UV radiation will make the polymers brittle. With mechanical disturbance to brittle
polymers, waves can cause the polymers to fragment into smaller particles (Browne et al., 2007;
Moore, 2008). Biological degradation from bacteria and fungi, can also enhance the process of
fragmentation (Browne et al., 2007). If plastics are ﬂoating on the ocean surfaces UV radiation
will make the polymers brittle. With mechanical disturbance to brittle polymers, waves can
cause the polymers to fragment into smaller particles (Browne et al., 2007; Moore, 2008).
Biological degradation from bacteria and fungi, can also enhance the process of fragmentation
(Browne et al., 2007).
Eventually, as a result of biofouling and fragmentation, one of the fates of plastic in the
marine environment is sedimentation (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2015; Cózar et al., 2014; Arthur
et al., 2009).
However, when polymers fragment into smaller and smaller constituents, the
polymers never fully degrade, as the carbon-carbon double bonds in the monomers cannot be
fully broken down by bacteria living in the marine environment. It is expected that all plastics
ever introduced into the marine environment, is still present as whole items or fragmented into
smaller particles (Thompson et al., 2004).

3.2. MICROPLASTICS
3.2
Microplastics
In the scientiﬁc literature plastics are distinguished according to size as either macro-, micro- or
nanoplastic. There is, however, to date no oﬃcial deﬁnition of microplastic (MP) (Hidalgo-ruz
et al., 2012), but in many contexts the term microplastics have been deﬁned and applied to
plastic particles less than 5 mm in diameter in their longest dimension (Arthur et al., 2009;
Sundt et al., 2014). The lower size limit of MPs are usually, for sampling convenience, 0.33 mm
(Sundt et al., 2014). Though it is expected that plastic debris are present down to even nano -
and picoscale in size (Cole and Galloway, 2015). The latter is based on model estimates, as it
has not yet been possible to measure nano - and picoscale plastics (Eriksen et al., 2014).
Microplastics can further be distinguished by their origin, as being either primary or sec-
ondary MPs (Lassen et al., 2015). Primary MPs are intentionally produced in small sizes for
direct use, either as virgin resin pellets, used in the manufacturing of plastic products, or as
micro beads which are spherical plastic particles added in consumer products e.g. cosmetics and
personal care products (Arthur et al., 2009; Lassen et al., 2015). Secondary MPs are the product
of environmental degradation and fragmentation of larger pieces of plastic debris (macroplas-
tics) into smaller plastic fragments (Sundt et al., 2014). Microﬁbers are also secondary MPs, ad
these are synthetic ﬁbers from fabrics used to make clothes or robes in form of polymer types
such as PE, nylon and acrylic materials (Bagaev et al., 2017). The microﬁbers are seen to been
dominate as MP ﬁndings the recent years (Karlsson et al., 2017; Bagaev et al., 2017).

3.2.2
Ingestion and eﬀects of microplastics in aquatic organisms
The continuous fragmentation of plastics into smaller particles, make them available for inges-
tion by smaller marine organisms (Browne et al., 2008; Wright et al., 2013b). Ingestion of larger
plastic particles to marine organisms, have already been found (Wright et al., 2013b).
The concern continues to increase, as the number of smaller MPs increases in the marine
environment (Eriksen et al., 2014). Due to the small size of MPs, they are available to lower
trophic levels of organisms, such as ﬁlter- and deposit feeders, as the MPs can migrate down
the water column and/or deposit on sediment (Andrady, 2011). Important factors that con-
tribute to the bioavailability of MPs are: Size, density and abundance of the MPs (Wright et al.,
2013b). Laboratory and ﬁeld studies have demonstrated ingestion of MPs by deposit-feeding
and ﬁlter-feeding organisms (Browne et al., 2008; von Moos et al., 2012; Wegner et al., 2012;
Xu et al., 2016; Avio et al., 2015; Karlsson et al., 2017). A review of Rochman et al. (2016)
review 283 papers on eﬀects regarding impacts of marine debris foun a total of 96 of these
perceived threats were tested, 83% of which were demonstrated, and a large portion hereof
(89%) assessing sub-organismal eﬀects (i.e. molecular, cellular, tissue). Rochman et al. (2016)
concludes that the quantity and quality of research requires improvements, however suggests
that suﬃcient evidence exists to begin to mitigating problematic plastic debris now, to avoid
risk of irreversible eﬀects. Yet no direct toxic eﬀects to aquatic organisms of MPs are found,
the intake of macro and microplastics from marine species are high (Wright et al., 2013b).
In addition, some speciﬁc eﬀects regarding the blue mussels M. edulis was assess more thor-
oughly.
First a study of Browne et al. (2008) investigated the ingestion, translocation and
accumulation of polystyrene MPs (3.0 and 9.6μm) at a concentration of 5.1 g L–1 in M. edulis.
The results of the study demonstrated persistence of MPs in the hemolymph over 48 days of
exposure. The study further revealed greater translocation from the gut to the circulatory sys-
tem and accumulation of smaller microspheres in the tissues of the mussel compared to larger
spheres (Browne et al., 2008). Accordingly, Karlsson et al. (2017) examined the presence of
MPs in M. edulis collected from the coast IJmuiden Pier in the Netherlands which adjoins the
North Sea. The mussels tissue was analyzed with an adapted enzymatic digestion protocol using
proteinase K. In a sample of 15 individual M. edulis an average of 5-19 particles per individual
mussel was found. Around 50% of the MPs were ﬁbers (Karlsson et al., 2017). A study of
von Moos et al. (2012) investigated uptake and eﬀects of exposure to high-density polyethy-
lene (HD-PE) particles (0-80 μm) M. edulis for up to 96 h at 2.5 g HD-PE L–1. The HD-PE
presence of MP on the gills, inside the digestive system and in connective tissue of the mussel.

Enzymes
In this thesis, six industrial enzymes were applied for digestion of animal tissue. As all these
six enzymes are serine proteases a short description of this enzyme group is given.
3.4.1
Serine protease
The serine proteases are a family of enzymes that cut certain peptide bonds in proteins. The
speciﬁc activity of the enzyme depends on which amino acids residues that is present, at the
enzymes active site. One of the active sites is always a serine, thus accounting for their name
(Denniston et al., 2007). Serine proteases have diﬀerent important functions in digestion, blood
clotting etc. There are three major classes of protein-digesting enzymes secreted by the pan-
creas of mammals of serine proteases, these are; Chymotrypsin, Trypsin and Elastase. These
three protein-digesting enzymes have diﬀerent substrate speciﬁcities, to where they cleave the
diﬀerent peptide bonds during protein digestion (Denniston et al., 2007). Serine proteases found
elsewhere in nature, e.g. subtilisin which is a serine protease secreted by the bacterium Bacillus
subtilis. Although it has the same mechanism of action as the serine proteases of mammals, its
primary structure and tertiary structure are entirely diﬀerent (Denniston et al., 2007).
3.4.2
Enzymatic digestion of animal tissue
Although other methods have previously been used to digest animal tissue (Witte et al., 2014;
Li et al., 2016; Van Cauwenberghe and Janssen, 2014), the use of enzymes have slowly been
emerging over the last couple of years (Claessens et al., 2013). Enzymatic digestion methods
are favorable, as these do not alter the MP structure (Claessens et al., 2013).
In a study, the enzyme Trypsin (0.325% dilution) was used to successfully digest M. edulis
tissue and expose potential MP with a high enzyme eﬃciency (88±2.5%) (Courtene-Jones et al.,
2017). Other animal tissue has also been digested with enzymes and Cole et al. (2014) used
diﬀerent proteolytic enzyme proteinase-K to digest zooplankton. Karlsson et al. (2017) used
diﬀerent proteolytic enzyme proteinase-K to digest M. edulis and found a high eﬃciency (>97%)
and no altering of MP beads. Lastly, Liu et al. (2008) digested the Paciﬁc oyster (Crassostrea
gigas) with the enzyme Alcalase 2.4 L from Novozymes in a dilution of 3% volume of enzyme
weight of raw material (50◦C, pH 8.5 for 3 hours, then adjusting to pH 5.5) and continuously
hydrolyzing with bromelin (3 hours). The study was not assessing MP extraction, but used to
isolate a novel cysteine-rich antimicrobial peptide (Liu et al., 2008).
An overview of enzymatic studies in digesting animal tissue was reviewed, as the present
study aimed to further develop a standardized enzymatic digestion protocol, testing six indus-
trial enzymes.

of animal and vegetable origin, such as softening wheat gluten.
6. Glutamyl endopeptidase (pHopt = 9) is a serin protease used in the leather industry, to
loosen hairs on skind or hides.
4.1.2
Part A: Initial test
An initial experiment was conducted to get an overview of the diﬀerent enzyme eﬀectiveness
of digesting mussel tissue. Commercially sold blue mussels, bought fresh at the store (MENY,
Seafood A/S, caught in the Baltic Sea) was defrosted one hour prior to dissection, which is
described in the latter Section 4.1.5 ﬁnal digestion protocol I.
For each enzyme, it was chosen to add either a 5% solution of the enzyme ’stock’ solution
or add the a given amount of enzyme ’stock’ solution which corresponded to 0.25 AU (Active
Units, Novozymes). As the AU unit is a Novozymes trade secret, only the ’ratio’ of AU could
be compared.
All of the enzymes were diluted with Milli-Q water, to obtain the tested dilutions (5% or
0.25AU). The digestions were run in 25mL blue-cap bottles, weighed beforehand (Figure 4.2)
with and without the wet weight (ww) of the blue mussel tissue. The steal-ﬁlters, which were
used to ﬁlter the digested tissue-solutions after incubation, were prepared by numbered, pre-
burned in 550◦C for 1 hour and weighed. Once the enzyme dilutions and Milli-Q water were
added to the ww of the mussel tissue, the solutions were incubated (to digest) overnight at 37◦C
on a shaking table.

4.1.4
Part C: Microplastic-Enzyme test
Once the two eﬃcient enzyme dilutions were obtained, a ﬁnal test was performed, to assess the
enzymatic impact on MPs. To compare the MP appearance, the optimal enzyme dilutions were
added to the MPs in the optimal dilutions and assessed under the microscope. This was done
by adding MPs to solutions of: 2.5% Alcalase and 5% ProAct (without mussel tissue), as well
as running controls with only Milli-Q water and MPs (all run in triplicates).
The four tested plastic types were: Black polystyrene beads (50μm), pink polyethylene
fragments (≥63μm), green nylon fragments (63-125μm) and blue rubber fragments (≥125μm),
all weighed to approximately 0.001 grams. No mussel tissue was added, as the main focus was
to ensure that the enzymes do not aﬀect or degrade the MPs. A visual comparison under the
microscope of the four MP types were made, comparing the enzyme treatments with added
MPs to the controls with only Milli-Q water and MPs.

Final digestion protocol I
On the bases of the above trial experiments, an overall ﬁnal protocol was established. This
protocol was used in the method of assessing the enzymes, thus Final digestion protocol I, as
later adjustments to the protocol are implemented.

The steps of the ﬁnal digestion protocol I are shown below:
1. Mussels were defrosted in zip-log bags (frozen at DSC) 1-2 hours prior to the dissection.
2. Once thawed, the mussels were placed head up for 10s to drain water and then placed
on the ﬂat side (Storch. and Welsch., 2009, p. 150). With a pair of sharp scissors, the
mussel was along the middle, towards the head, so the outer shells could be separated.
3. All tissue was removed from the right shell with a scalpel and then scraped from he left
shell, having all the tissue in a clean petri dish.
4. Inside the petri dish, the tissue was cut into smaller pieces with a pair of scissors, to allow
the enzymatic treatment to have a larger surface area to digest.
5. The shells are cleansed into the petri dish and discarded.
6. Wet tissue was transferred to a glass bottle (25ml) and the petri dish rinsed with deionized
water into the blue-cap.
7. The stock enzyme solution Alcalase was added into the blue-cap bottle. A volume of
0.5mL Alcalase and 19.5mL of Milli-Q water was added, to reach a ﬁnal concentration of
2.5% Alcalase stock-solution.
8. The blue-cap bottles containing enzyme dilution and mussel tissue were put on a shaking
table to digest overnight in a 37◦C climate room.
9. After incubation, the digested solutions are ﬁltered through a steal-ﬁlter mesh (10μm).
10. Steal-ﬁlter and wet weigh of the sample were then used to determine the percentage of
water, OM and IOM (as described in Section 4.1.2 and shown in Figure 4.2).

4.3.7
Filtering seawater samples
Once the experimental exposure and egestion period was completed, ﬁltration of the 10L seawa-
ter was conducted. All buckets were rinsed thoroughly with distilled-water to ensure recovery
of MPs. The water was ﬁrstly sieved through a 20μm mesh sieve which retained 50μm PS beads
while removing R. salina algae (size-range 8-12μm). Afterwards, the algae-mesh was removed
from the sieve, washed into a glass beaker and ﬁltered through a vacuum-ﬁltration system us-
ing SUE Heto vacuum pumps. All equipment was rinsed with distilled-water in between each
step of the process. The treatments where ﬁltered onto 10μm algae-mesh (D: 4.7cm), and the
controls were ﬁltered on WhatmanTM Glass Microﬁber Filters (1.5μm particle retention). To
avoid cross-contamination, two diﬀerent vacuum ﬁltration systems were used for treatments
and controls. Once all treatment and control water-samples had been ﬁltered and placed into
clean, labeled petri dishes, the number of PS beads from each exposure water and egestion
water could be counted (minimum twice) in the Microscopes, by using clickers.
4.3.8
Digestion of tissue samples
The exposed mussels and the associated controls were digested according to the ﬁnal digestion
protocol I (Section 4.1.5), however some few adjustments to some of the steps (highlighted in
italic) were made and resulted in the Final digestion protocol II:
1. Mussels are defrosted in zip-log bags (which they were frozen in back at DSC) 1-2 hours
prior to the dissection.
2. Once thawed, the mussel was placed on the ﬂat side (see (Storch. and Welsch., 2009, p.
150)). With a pair of sharp scissors, the mussel was along the middle, towards the head,
so the outer shells could be separated.
3. All tissue was removed from the right shell with a scalpel and then scraped from he left
shell, having all the tissue in a clean petri dish.
4. Inside the petri dish, the tissue was cut into smaller pieces with a pair of scissors, to allow
the enzymatic treatment to have a larger surface to digest.
5.
Shells are kept inside the same pre-marked zip-log bags, as initially frozen and kept in.
6. Wet tissue was transferred to a glass bottle (25ml) and the petri dish rinsed with deionized
water into the blue-cap.
7. The stock enzyme solution Alcalase was added into the blue-cap bottle. A volume of
0.5mL Alcalase and 19.5mL of Milli-Q water was added, to reach a ﬁnal concentration of
2.5% Alcalase stock-solution.
8. The blue-cap bottles containing enzyme dilution and mussel tissue were put on a shaking
table to digest overnight in a 37◦C climate room.
9. After incubation, the digested solutions are ﬁltered through a 10 μm plankton mesh.
10. Everything belonging to this sample was rinsed into the ﬁltration system several times;
the matching shells, the inside of the zip-log bag (used for the mussel), the inside of the
blue-cap bottle.
11. The algae-mesh was then assessed for MPs under the microscope and counted manually
with clickers (min. twice). If necessary, the petri dish could be kept for later recounting
or imaging.

4.4. STATISTICS
4.4
Statistics
Null-hypotheses were addressed using a p-value limit of 0.05. The tests were run in the Statis-
tical Software R Studio.
All data-set’s diagnostics are checked by using the Shapiro–Wilk test to test the normality
of the data, and Fligner-Killeen test to test data for homogeneity of variance to ascertain
the assumptions of further statistical testing.
Stress responses exposure
Three stress parameters are tested, in the three-week exposure scenario; BCI, clearance rate
and byssus thread regeneration, respectively.
Based on the value of the BCI’s, an 1-way ANOVA is performed as the concentration is
the single factor, and the BCI’s where only measured at the end of the exposure period. The
null-hypotheses are as follows:
• Null-hypothesis: There is no diﬀerence in BCI’s between the diﬀerent exposure concen-
trations and controls.
For the byssus thread regeneration and clearance rates, a repeated measures ANOVA
is needed, as both byssus thread regeneration and clearance rates are followed for the same
experimental units both at the start and end of the experimental period of three weeks. The
null-hypotheses are as follows:
• Null-hypothesis: The regeneration of the byssus threads from the mussels does not get
aﬀected by concentration and do not vary with time.
• Null-hypothesis: The clearance rate of the mussels does not get aﬀected by concentration
and do not vary with time.
Ingestion and Egestion experiment
In the Ingestion and egestion experimental design we have two factors ’time’ and ’concentration’,
for which 2 levels are assessed for each of these factors. As we have two time points (10 & 40min),
and two concentrations (low & high). Additionally, having a control group for each factor. We
therefor use a 2-way ANOVA. We use a 1-way ANOVA to ensure that all clearance rates
are equal for all treatment groups, for these values the data were sqrt transformed to fulﬁll
normality.
The null-hypotheses are as follows:
• Null-hypothesis: The initial clearance rate is equal for all the treatment groups.
• Null-hypothesis: The fraction (percentage) of ingested MPs does not vary with time.
• Null-hypothesis: The fraction (percentage) of ingested MPs not vary with exposure con-
centration.
• Null-hypothesis: The fraction (percentage) of ingested MPs is not aﬀected by the inter-
action of the factors ’time’ and ’concentration’.
For any further testing diﬀerences among groups a TukeyHSD.

Discussion
As devised in the Aim of study section, this thesis has had three sub-aims to investigate by
virtue of three corresponding experimental approaches. To provide a clear overview and trans-
parency in examination of each the discussion section has been structured accordingly with
three dedicated parts.
6.1
Optimization of enzymatic digestion
Sub-aim 1: "To evaluate the eﬃciency of diﬀerent industrial enzymes in digesting
the tissue of the blue mussel M. edulis and thereby establish an optimal digestion
protocol."
A enzymatic digestion experimental design was designed with a focus of testing enzymes’ diges-
tion of bivalve tissue without causing any adverse eﬀects to the MPs. A standardized protocol
was developed to make a quantitative approach manageable, when assessing MPs content in
tissue of the mussels. This protocol was used in Part 3 (Ingestion and Egestion experiment).
Previous studies have employed various digestion methods to disassociate MPs from the
animal tissue, using; Van Cauwenberghe and Janssen (2014) used nitric acid in, Witte et al.
(2014) used nitric - and perchloric acid, Li et al. (2016) using hydrogen peroxide, respectively.
The eﬀect of these compounds used for digestions was tested (i.e. nitric acid, hydrogen per-
oxide, sodium hydroxid.) which found that the substances had detrimental impact on certain
plastic polymers (Claessens et al., 2013). This highlights the value of developing a standardized
protocol using alternative substances to digest faunal tissue and easily extract and quantify MPs.
In developing this new protocol, six industrial enzymes were tested. The enzyme eﬃciencies
are in this study determined as the OM (percentage) remaining after digestion subtracted from
the initial ww (100%). The eﬃciency of the six enzymes was found to range between 78.8%
to 94.5%.
Two enzymes (Alcalase and ProAct) were preferable and selected for the subse-
quent experimental procedure, as these had a higher digestion eﬃciency of 90.8% and 94.5%,
respectively. In the next step, a further selection between the two enzymes found the enzyme,
Alcalase, at a dilution of 2.5% is the optimal choice in digesting mussel tissue. Alcalase degraded
the bivalve tissue eﬃciently, leaving only 12.39% OM remaining after digestion. Furthermore,
the Alcalase solution rendered removal and assessment of the four types of added MP particles
easy. Alcalase did not alter the visual appearance or show any signs of degradation to the
MP particles. The enzyme, ProAct at a dilution of 5%, was discarded as this enzyme induced
adhesion of the MP particles to the glass-ware. ProAct had 12.17% OM remaining after the
digestion yet the adherence to the glassware rendered recovering the added MPs infeasible for
this purpose. The reason for this adhesion, however, is unknown since detailed information on
the enzyme is a trade secret of Novozymes. Nonetheless, when assessing the MPs exposed to
the ProAct solution aggregates of the MPs were observed. This indicates that ProAct to some
degree alters the surface tension or surface properties of the tested MP particles.
In a study of Courtene-Jones et al. (2017) a mean eﬃciency of 88±2.25% was found at a
0.3125% Trypsin enzyme dilution with Milli-Q water. This was similar to the eﬃciency found
for Alcalase 2.5% having a mean eﬃciency of 87.61%. Yet, the methods used to calculate en-
zyme eﬃciency varied between this study and the study by Courtene-Jones et al. (2017). In
this study, it was preferable to use dry weight measures and the % OM left after digestion given

that a fraction of IOM cannot be removed and should therefore be removed from the overall
weight measures. In Courtene-Jones et al. (2017) only the wet weight measures of shells and
tissue after digestion were used which makes the ﬁnal eﬃciency measured more imprecise. In
Cole et al. (2014) a higher eﬃciency range was found (54-97%) using a diﬀerent proteolytic

proteinase-K. Similarly in this study, no altering of the MPs were found from the Alcalase
(2.5%) digestion suggesting that enzymes in general are more optimal in digestion protocols
than e.g. acids.
Considerations about making the method easily manageable, cheap and time-eﬃcient was
likewise of high priority. To do so, the method focused on a enzymatic digestion which could
function optimally at a temperature of 37◦C, as most laboratories have a 37◦C room available.
Furthermore, it was also an advantage to run the digestion in a 37◦C room as this makes it
possible to digest several samples simultaneously. The mussel could be preserved in a frozen
state until further assessed. This is an easy way to keep the mussels preserved and eases the
daily workload. In addition, freezing the mussel prior to digestion did not alter the extraction of
MPs. In contrary, freezing the mussels made the dissection easier compared to dissecting them
freshly. This is supported by the study of Courtene-Jones et al. (2017) who also found that
freezing mussels prior to assessment does not alter the particles. The method was furthermore
time-eﬃcient as there was no need for to targeting speciﬁc organs during the dissection step.
In this way, a whole mussel could be scraped from the shell and cut in smaller pieces. Cutting
the tissue, gave the enzymes a larger surface area of the soft tissue to digest simultaneously.
Occasionally the enzymes did not fully degrade all the mussel tissue and structures from the
adductor mussels or mantle skirt were the most commonly structures remaining. Even though
these organs were not always 100% digested, they were not considered regions in the mussels
where it is likely for the MPs to accumulate. Most importantly, it was seen that Alcalase 2.5%
was able to digest all the soft tissue with the ideal targeted organs including the respiratory,
digestive and reproductive organs.
In terms of optimizing the enzyme eﬃciency and having less OM left it could be considered
to advance the dissection step in the protocol. As the adductor mussels and mantle skirt were
often the remaining part of the tissue, these could be removed by dissection in advance leaving
only the soft tissue to be digested. Dissection protocols on removing these parts were conducted
by following the dissection manual in (Storch. and Welsch., 2009, p.151). While this is certainly
feasible it is worth taking into account that adding this step will increase the overall time of
the workload. Nevertheless, the digestion protocol could be ideal for digesting speciﬁc parts of
the soft tissue in the mussels such as the digestive tract or another part of particular interest.
The enzymes would here be able to achieve an even higher eﬃciency.

6.2. STRESS RESPONSE EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
6.2
Stress response experimental design
Sub-aim 2: "To identify whether continuous exposure to MPs can induce stress
responses in the blue mussels, M. edulis."
Overall the results from the measured stress responses in the three-week exposure did not imply
any clear patterns. All the assessed stress responses (BCI, clearance rate, Byssus regeneration)
did not show any signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the treatment groups nor the controls. The only
mentionable pattern observed was the eﬀect of time which for all tested stress responses was
signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from exposure start (Day 0) to exposure end (Day 21).
In the stress response design concentrations of 5.3 beads mL–1 and 25.5 beads mL–1 were
used. Importantly, the approach was not to assess environmentally realistic concentrations,
but to identify whether any of these chosen concentrations could induce stress responses. Still,
the used exposure concentrations were lower than other published studies on eﬀects of MPs
in mussels (Browne et al., 2008; von Moos et al., 2012). The study by Browne et al. (2008)
used 1·1012beads mL–1 to assess ingestion, translocation and accumulation in blue mussels; von
Moos et al. (2012) used 1·106 – 1 · 109beads mL–1 to assess the formation of granulocytomas
and lysosomal membrane destabilization. The concentrations were converted to mL–1 from the
publication of Lenz et al. (2016). Interestingly, the exposure concentrations of 5.3 beads mL–1
and 25.5 beads mL–1 used in this study are nonetheless values which within the future could end
up being environmentally realistic. Levels of marine litter and thereby also MPs are expected
to increase in the future (Jambeck et al., 2015), from both the continuous fragmentation of
macro plastics and from direct released of primary MPs (Eriksen et al., 2014; Thompson et al.,
2004). Moreover, as the values here are lower than exposure concentrations found in the studies
reviewed during this thesis this could also be a representation of the Lowest Observed Eﬀect
Concentration (LOEC).
Clearance rate based on algae counts, which was one of the measured stress responses, had
an average clearance rate of 3.41-4.43L/h at exposure start (as seen in Table 5.5). Comparing
the measured clearance rate to the correlation of clearance rate and length in mussels found
by Riisgård et al. (2014), using the given correlation (Equation 3.1), this showed that the ini-
tial theoretical ﬁltration was expected to be 4.72-4.95L/h. Comparing the measured clearance
rate to the theoretical clearance rate, the measured clearance rate is slightly lower, however
acceptable, implying that the mussels’ clearance rate were not aﬀected by the three-week ac-
climatization period. Nevertheless, large SD was observed in the initial clearance rates (Figure
5.6), caused by a big variations in the individual clearance rates of the mussels (Appendix 10.2
Table 10.6). In a study by Wegner et al. (2012) they detected that NP beads were seen to cause
a lowering of ﬁltration (based on the valve opening in mm of valves). Wegner et al. (2012)
further found that the ﬁltering activity was signiﬁcantly dependent on the nano PS concentra-
tion. No signiﬁcant responses on the clearance rate could be concluded from this study as there
was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the diﬀerent treatment groups and controls clearance rate
(ANOVA: p=0.621).
A signiﬁcant diﬀerence of time was observed for both of the treatments and the controls
(ANOVA: p=0.001). This argues the diﬀerence found in clearance rates over time, was not
due to the plastic exposures as this group was aﬀected equally to the controls. The mussels
were kept in a static system with constant aeration, changing the water every 2-3 days. This
practice is used in other studies (Wegner et al., 2012; Garrido et al., 2012b; Rist et al., 2016;
Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2015) and therefore considered acceptable. Each replica had 800mL
seawater per mussels changed every 2-3 day, which is quite low if the mussels’ ﬁlters 4-5L/h.
Ideal conditions would have been to keep the mussels in a ﬂow-through system which constantly

supplies fresh seawater. This allows them continuously to ﬁlter which requires less energy for
the mussels (Riisgård et al., 2015). The mussels were kept for a period of six-weeks in the
climate room and a pulse-ﬁltration was observed in that the mussels were only actively ﬁltering
during feeding or water exchange. Only during these processes, the mussels could sense colloids
in the water column and start ﬁltration (Riisgård, 1988; Riisgård et al., 2011). It has been
established that a discontinuous feeding can aﬀect the energy consumption in mussels (Riisgård
et al., 2015). Thus, it is probable that the mussels in the stress response exposure of this study
have used more energy as well as being in a inactive state during most of the exposure than
would be the case with a ﬂow through or larger pump system available.
BCI is a measure which is used as an indicator of starvation and the BCI (weight based) as
seen in Equation 4.3 was discussed by Davenport and Chen (1987) to be an accurate, stable and
easy measure to determine starvation. In von Moos et al. (2012) the CI is also calculated using
weight as measure. However, von Moos et al. (2012) used the ww to calculate CI, whereas in the
present study dw measures were used. Dry weight measures often vary less, as the ww can be
easily aﬀected by evaporation (Davenport and Chen, 1987). No signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the BCI
measures (ANOVA: p=0.424) between the diﬀerent exposure treatments, background mussels
(initially frozen), nor the controls was found. However, a diﬀerence in the BCI measurements
was observed for the mussels which during the experiment had spawned. Here, BCI was on
average 0.05 lower for the spawning mussels. BCI was aﬀected by spawning as the mussels,
during this process, eject either sperm cells or eggs into the water in large quantities (Fearman
et al., 2009). As the gametes (sperm or egg cells) develop within follicles in the mantle tissue
these are released during ejection and the mussels lose body weight and use the glycogen energy
reserves in the process (Fearman et al., 2009).
The last assessed stress response was the regeneration of byssus. The formation of byssus is
a sign of the viability and ﬁtness of the organism (Silverman and Roberto, 2010). As the pro-
cess of forming the protein-based byssus threads is energy consuming (Silverman and Roberto,
2010), a stress response would be expected to result in a decrease in regeneration of byssus
over time. A diﬀerence over time (ANOVA: p=0.012) was found for all the treatment groups,
showing that the experimental period of the three weeks inﬂuenced the regeneration of byssus.
On the contrary to a decrease, the results in the study indicated byssus formation actually
increased in the treatment groups during the exposure and the controls remained stable (shown
in Figure 5.7). A large individual variation between the mussels was observed which increased
the SD. In addition, no signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the treatment group and controls could
be demonstrated (ANOVA: p=0.619). The manual handling of the water change and transfer to
exposure set-up may have inﬂuenced the number of regenerated byssus formed at exposure start
(day 0). In a study by Rist et al. (2016) the Asian green mussel, Perna viridis, was exposed
to PVC particles (1–50μm) for two 2-hour-time-periods per day in a total period of 96 days at
several concentrations; 0mg/L, 21.6mg/L, 216mg/L, 2160mg/L. After 40-44 days a signiﬁcant
decrease in byssus production were observed for the second and highest PVC pollution level.
Suggesting, that clearer eﬀect on the byssus could be observed in a prolonged exposure.
The overall ﬁndings showed that stress responses in the clearance rate, BCI or Byssus thread
regeneration was observed for the two exposure concentrations, 5.3 and 25.5 beads mL–1. The
stress response design highlighted further the importance of optimal laboratory conditions,
emphasizing that organisms ﬁrstly need to adapt and acclimatize to the experimental conditions.
As this stress response assessment only took place over an exposure period of three-weeks, this
suggests these stress responses are not aﬀected by MPs within this time frame even in the
laboratory conditions. These considerations were supported by Roberts (1975), deﬁning a step-
wise protocol of how to assure proper conditioned ﬁeld organisms.

6.3
Ingestion and Egestion experimental design
Sub-aim 3: "To assess the ingestion and egestion of MPs in the blue mussel, M.
edulis, using environmentally realistic concentrations of MPs and determine the
aﬃliated distribution and clearance rate of MPs."
The results from the three-week exposure documented in the previous discussion (Section 6.2)
was used to improve the conditions of the preparation and design of the Ingestion and Egestion
experiment. Optimal conditions for the mussels is essential, thus an eﬀort was put into getting
as close as feasible to simulating mussel behavior in their natural environment. The ingestion
and egestion experimental design was decided to be executed at DSC, Nykøbing Mors, where
ideal equipment and gear was provided to run the experimental set-up and hereby avoid the
same issues challenging the stress response experiment.
To ensure the viability and ﬁtness of mussels, they were kept in a ﬂow through system prior
the start of the experiment. The constant in- and outlet of seawater in the ﬂow-through system
made the mussels ﬁlter more naturally. Initial measurement of the experimental population
was conducted to ensure comparison between treatment groups. Firstly, the experimental pop-
ulation measurements were found to be comparable to sampled population and a low SD was
observed within the experimental population (Table 5.6). Next, clearance rates were measured
repetitively prior to exposure to ensure proper and continuous ﬁltration from the mussels. This
was necessary as ingestion from an active ﬁltration was one of the main focus points.
No
statistical diﬀerence between the clearance rate measures, based on algae counts, measured
prior to exposure was observed (ANOVA: p=0.181). These ﬁndings made the diﬀerent runs of
treatments comparable, as all mussels had the same ideal start conditions.
6.3.1
Ingestion
It was found that the mussels were able to ingest the MP beads in all combinations of time (10
& 40min) and in all concentrations (5 & 100 PS beads L–1). In agreement with our results,
other studies have demonstrated ingestion of MPs in the blue mussel, M. edulis, (Browne et al.,
2008; von Moos et al., 2012; Wegner et al., 2012) and furthermore ingestion has been demon-
strated in other bivalves (Wright et al., 2013b; Rist et al., 2016; Avio et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016).
The aim was to test an environmentally realistic concentration of MPs which can be challeng-
ing to establish since a gross variation in naturally measured concentration have been reported
(Hidalgo-ruz et al., 2012). The local concentrations are likely to depend on sampling locations
(e.g. beach, open ocean, and coastal waters) as well as the distances from centers of human
population (Eriksen et al., 2014; Courtene-Jones et al., 2017; Bagaev et al., 2017; Cózar et al.,
2014). The sampling equipment is also an important factor in the measurement of local con-
centrations as the used mesh-sizes can determine the lower limit of found particles (Hidalgo-ruz
et al., 2012). Lastly, natural conditions such as weathering, ocean currents and wind-mixing
will also aﬀect the concentrations found (Kukulka et al., 2012; Ryan et al., 2009b).
The review study by Hidalgo-ruz et al. (2012) found a range between 0.000022 to 8.6 items
L–1 for environmentally sampled concentrations in sea surface samples. The exposure concen-
trations 5 beads L–1 was evaluated and chosen as an environmentally realistic concentration.
Although 5 beads L–1 is still in the high end it is nonetheless within reported values. Hidalgo-
ruz et al. (2012) also accentuated that the relationship between the mesh-size used for sampling
and the abundance is of great inﬂuence of sampled concentrations. This argues that many of the
lower reported ﬁeld concentrations could underestimate the ﬁeld MP concentration, especially
considering that higher MP concentrations were reported in the studies using smaller meshes
(Hidalgo-ruz et al., 2012). The high exposure concentration of 100 beads L–1 is in the very high

==PAGE:58==

end of environmentally realistic exposure. The value is based on the most polluted environ-
mental concentrations found to 102 particles L–1 in Swedish waters near production plants of
PE (Norén, 2016). The use of environmentally realistic concentrations has however been given
too little attention. It is argued in the scientiﬁc community that eﬀects observed in laboratory
studies using orders-of-magnitude higher concentrations than those found in ﬁeld studies must
be interpreted in light of environmental concentrations (Lenz et al., 2016). If not, there could
be cases of overreaction or misinterpretation of MPs results. The criticism pinpoints that many
eﬀects from MPs in aquatic organisms are using orders-of-magnitude higher concentrations that
reported in the environment, e.g. as pointed out in two studies regarding bivalves von Moos
et al. (2012) using and Browne et al. (2008) using 1·1015particles L–1 and von Moos et al. (2012)
using 1·109 – 1 · 1012particles L–1.
The black PS MP beads used in this experimental design had a size of 50μm. In comparison,
blue mussels are shown to ﬁltrate particles within size range 4-62.8μm (Zhang et al., 2010). This
bead size is thus in the high end compared to their natural common food source, the algae cells,
which are more in the range of 8-12μm (Vu et al., 2015). A study by Browne et al. (2008)
revealed a greater accumulation and translocation into the circulatory system in MP beads of
smaller size (3.0μm). This argues that an even higher ingestion could have been observed, had
the particles been within a range more similar to their naturally food-source like algae, yet that
further studies will be needed to examine this. In addition, Cole et al. (2013) suggest that
irregularly shaped MP, as a result of environmental fragmentation, can be ingested and become
trapped within the intestinal tract which potentially result in gut-blockage and greater GRT
and thereby inﬂuence the physical health of mussels.
6.3.2
Egestion
The observed egestion of MPs was low and no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was found in MP in the
tissue between the mussels which have had time to egest and the mussels there were sampled
directly after exposure (2-way ANOVA: p=0.555). The percentage of PS beads found to be
egested was between 0.64-2.94% of the total counts for both exposure concentrations which is
a small fraction of the total. Nevertheless, high standard deviations of the egested counts were
found (Table 5.7). It must be emphasized that the egestion percentage is only based on one
mussel of the two exposed - as only one mussel was used for the egestion experiment. Thus, the
egestion (%) refers to how many MPs were egested relative to how many were ingested upon
exposure.
Considerations were given to the re-uptake of egested MP beads as the mussels have a high
hourly ﬁltration (Riisgård et al., 2015). However, the blue mussel’s exhalant siphon functions
as a exhalent jet which minimizes the possibility of re-ﬁltration and recirculation of the water
once ﬁltered (Riisgård et al., 2011). Each replica was contained in a plastic bucket with 10
L seawater, thus re-uptake could have been observed. A study by Bayne et al. (1989) tested
for 6 diﬀerent experimental diets fed to the blue mussels and found that the blue mussel has
an average Gut Passage Time (GPT) between 1.81-3.23 hours. Our experimental design only
had an egestion period of 2 hours, which could have been too short. The mussels might not
have enough time to egest the MP beads and therefore further assessments with longer egestion
times would be a next step for future assessment.
Not much scientiﬁc information is available on egestion of MPs by marine organisms (Wright
et al., 2013b). Van Cauwenberghe et al. (2015) assessed the MP content of faeces from M. edulis
and found 0.1±0.2g–1 faeces, collected during a 24 hour clearance period. Another study found
prolonged gut passage time in the lungworm, Arenicola marina, upon ingestion of MP particles
(UPVC, 130 μm) which implies that MPs are being retained and subjected to extensive diges-
tion, costing energy as MPs have no nutritional value (Wright et al., 2013a). This implies, as
before, that a 2 hour egestion time could have been reasonable for normal food sources yet the

MP beads could have induced a longer GPT leading to no signiﬁcant amounts of MPs being
found in the egestion water.
6.3.3
Distribution
Very high recovery rates of MP were found in the distribution assessment, as averages found
between 93.3-119.2% of the added MP beads could be recaptured in the assessed compartments
of tissue and water. For the low exposure concentration with approx. 50 MPs added to the
experimental system, between 52.5-59.6 MP beads were recovered per replica. For the high ex-
posure concentration with approx. 1000 MP beads added to the experimental system, between
936.3-993.3 MP beads were recovered. This conﬁrms that the method is highly applicable for
recovery experiments.
In this thesis, given the distribution of MPs in the diﬀerent compartments (water and tissue),
no statistical diﬀerence was found in the mussel tissue distribution (percentage) for the diﬀerent
exposure concentrations (2-way ANOVA: p=0.555). The ingested percentages were more or less
the same when exposed for an equal amount time for the low and the high treatment. At time
point of 10min, 14.10% (low) and 18.39% (high), and at time point of 40 min, 46.84% (low)
and 52.17% (high), MPs found in mussel tissue. Moreover, this oﬀers some important insights,
as even the low environmentally realistic MP concentration the mussels ingest the MPs equal
proportions.
Contrary to ﬁnding no eﬀect of exposure concentration level on the proportion of ingestion,
the fractions (percentage) of MPs in tissue, was found to be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent between the
exposure time of 10 and 40 minutes (2-way ANOVA: p=5.43e-7) (Figure 5.10). The fractions
(percentage) of MPs in tissue was seen to vary between 14.10-18.39% at time 10 minutes and
between 46.84-52.17% at time 40 minutes thus, demonstrating a clear increase in ingestion with
an increase of time. As 40 minutes of exposure is very short compared to a whole lifespan of
a mussel, this raises concerns for the viability of the organism given that low egestion values
were found subsequently.
In a study by Van Cauwenberghe et al. (2015) an estimation of
the total exposure of MPs during a life span in M. edulis was calculated. Based on average
environmental concentrations of 0.4±0.3 MP particles L–1 and a ﬁltration of 2L/h a mussel
can potentially ingest 10 particles per day (Van Cauwenberghe et al., 2015). Extrapolating to
a theoretical value a mussel should ﬁlter 14,000 MPs from the seawater during a life span of
4 years. However, Van Cauwenberghe et al. (2015) also estimates a body burden of 0.3±0.2
particles g dw–1, which results in an extremely low retention eﬃciency of 0.003% empathizing
that the mussels somehow can reject or egest the MPs after intake.
6.3.4
Clearance rate
The clearance of MP beads were 3.35 L/h (low exposure) and 5.29 L/h (high exposure) in-
dicating that the clearance of MP beads increased with more particles in the water column.
Nonetheless, the SD of the clearance rate of MPs were overlapping (Figure 5.11) thus making a
true diﬀerence between the clearance of MP beads an expectation but not unequivocally infer-
able. The clearance rate based on the algae is, however, certainly higher than the clearance rate
for MPs. The average clearance of algae was found to be 8.36-10.99 L/h (all treatment groups)
and all the initial clearance rates were furthermore found not to be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from
each other (ANOVA: P=0.168 & TukeyHSD: p>0.5).
The clear diﬀerence in clearance rate based on either MP or algae cell counts argues for
either a selective feeding behavior or a less eﬃcient ﬁltration in the addition of MPs to the

6.3.5
Faeces and pseudofaeces
Faeces and pseudofaeces is a part of egestion and was, due to time limitations, not examined
in this study. In any case, the recovery of the added MPs were very high (93.3-119.2%) which
makes the fraction of MPs remaining to be found very small. It is thus not expected that
signiﬁcant amounts of MPs will be present in the faeces and/or pseudofaeces. In addition, the
egestion period was chosen to be 2 hours long which most likely has been in the low end for the
mussels to egest the ingested MPs from the exposure. This is supported by Bayne et al. (1989);
Wright et al. (2013a) and the low values of egestion found in the present study. In the study by
Wegner et al. (2012) PS NP were found in pseudofaeces from M. edulis present on the bottom
of the aquarium after an exposure of 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3g/L of nano PS after 8 hours of egestion.
6.3.6
Spawning
The blue mussels reproduce in May-June (Danish Shellﬁsh Center, 2017) and the ingestion and
egestion experimental design took place 2-15 of June. Therefore, spawning was observed in some
of the diﬀerent replicas during the diﬀerent exposures. As spawning can increase the variance
in behavior, it was noted which replicas spawned (Appendix 10.3.6). Temperature and food
availability are two important factors which can trigger the spawning of blue mussels (Leinio
and Lehtonen, 2005; Fearman et al., 2009). During some of the measurements of clearance
rate spawning was observed simultaneously, possibly triggered by the sudden addition of high
concentrations of R. salina. It was established that the mussels did not ﬁlter signiﬁcantly less
during spawning as the clearance rate rates during spawning were found to be similar (ANOVA:
P=0.0181, TukeyHSD: p>0.05). The replicas which spawned were thus still considered true
replicates and retained in the results.
6.3.7

The natural background contamination of MPs in the mussels sampled from the Limfjord was

microﬁbers per mussels being plastic microﬁbers. Conﬁrmation with e.g. Raman spectroscopy

CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
6.4
Implications from ﬁndings
Mytilus edulis was found suitable as indicator organism for MP pollution and for providing
valuable information on MP distributional patterns. The mussels are able to ﬁlter large quanti-
ties of seawater, including potential MP particles present in the marine environment. Based on
the ﬁndings in the present study, as well as knowledge gained from scientiﬁc literature on MPs,
it is expected that mussels can alter the distribution of MPs in the water column by either;
ﬁltration, ingesting, accumulation, translocation inside tissue and egestion hereby possibly em-
bedded in faeces or pseudofaeces. The blue mussels in the present study showed a clearance
rate based on MP beads of approx. 3-5 L/h, which during a life span of a blue mussel could
add up to ﬁltration of several thousand MP particles from the water column. Relative to the
factor of time, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in exposure durations were found to impact the ingestion
in the present study. By MP removal from the above water column, blue mussels can decrease
the exposure to other marine life that thrives there. However, as the mussels are limited in
their ability to move (Riisgård et al., 2015), the ﬁltering service is limited to sea water (which
further is aﬀected by the locomotion of surround water movement) available to mussel. Many
of the ﬁeld-collected mussels showed relatively low MP numbers inside the tissue, suggesting
some extent of egestion. If MPs were egested as faeces or pseudofaeces, these would have a
tendency to sediment (Wegner et al., 2012). An altering of the sinking rate was also observed
for zooplankton fecal pellets containing MPs (Cole et al., 2015).
Some of the highest ﬁeld
sampled concentrations of MPs are found in marine sediments, and is in general often orders
of magnitude higher than the concentrations found in the water column or at the sea surface
(Hidalgo-ruz et al., 2012). This causes further concern for benthos that live and feed on, in or
near the seabed. Over the past century, there has been a dramatic increase in studies assessing
the harmful eﬀects of MPs to aquatic organisms (Rochman et al., 2016).
Further, a rise of concern has been found regarding MP content in mussels and thus human
exposure, through intake of these. Fisheries and shellﬁsh agricultural farms want to ensure, that
presence of MPs in mussels and seafood does not pose a threat to food safety. A study by Van
Cauwenberghe and Janssen (2014) assessed this concern and estimated the maximum human
consumption of MP particles from seafood ingestion. Basing the estimations on average load
of 0.36±0.07 particles g–1 for ﬁeld collected M. edulis, Van Cauwenberghe and Janssen (2014)
extrapolates these data to an average human consumption. European top human consumers
are expected to ingest 72.1 g day–1 of mussels per capita. Using the average load found, an
annual dietary exposure to European top consumers was calculated to an ingestion of up to
11,000 MPs year–1 and for the lower consumers calculated to 1800 MPs year–1. However, this
study works with an average load of 0.36±0.07 particles –1 for ﬁeld collected mussels. This
could possibly be in the low end, based on our small pilot study assessing the ﬁeld collected
mussels ﬁnding 31.2 microﬁbers per mussel (and an average ww of 5g), this adds to a much
greater annual dietary exposure to European top consumers of 170,000 MPs year–1. This is
nevertheless a very rough estimate, as the mussels used for this part of the study are only based
on a small sample (n=9). Yet it still emphasizes that local concentrations are varying.
Although no direct toxic eﬀect has been shown (Wright et al., 2013a), these annual dietary
exposures are high. Additionally, leaking of harmful additives (added during plastic production)
and MPs adsorption of pollutants from the surrounding water e.g. PCB has been found to have
negatively eﬀects on wildlife and human health (Rochman et al., 2016). An increase of MPs
in the ocean is expected, as human use of plastics increases. In addition, an increase has been
found for poor waste management practices as well as intentionally and unintentional losses
(Thompson et al., 2009), no conclusions have been drawn on eﬀects.

7
Conclusion
This study has set out to expand the understanding of eﬀects related to microplastics in mussels,
in relation to both ingestion, egestion, distribution and common stress responses. Moreover,
the study has sought to contribute with methodological insight.
Firstly, an enzymatic digestion protocol was developed to ensure a proper quantiﬁcation of
the MP assessed in bivalve tissue. Enzymes were seen to be an ideal substance to recover MPs
from animal tissue, compared to acid digestions. In the developed enzymatic digestion protocol,
the industrial enzyme, Alcalase, at a dilution of 2.5%, was established as the optimal enzyme to
use in digesting blue mussel tissue and recovering MPs without causing any deleterious eﬀects
on the MPs. Alcalase (2.5%) had on average 12.39% OM left from the total wet weight after
the digestion, the remaining tissue types (adductor mussels and mantle skirt) furthermore not
expected regions in the mussels where MPs are likely to accumulate to comprise low levels of
MP content. The procedure of the protocol was further developed to be easily manageable
(less equipment), cheap (industrial enzyme) and time-eﬃcient (preserved frozen, many samples
simultaneously, and simple dissection). The protocol, although specialized for the purpose of
this thesis, can be also be used to target more speciﬁc organs of interest and can be modiﬁed
to assess speciﬁc purposes regarding MP in bivalve tissue.
Secondly, the thesis has assessed common stress responses in blue mussels using two exposure
concentrations of MPs during a three-week exposure period. In this study, the concentrations
of 5.3 and 25.5 PS MP (20μm) beads mL–1 entailed no stress responses from the mussels, as
no signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the treatment groups and control groups were found in any
of the tested stress parameters (BCI, clearance rate & Byssus regeneration). The results in the
stress response exposure were seen to have large SDs mainly caused by big variations between
the individuals. Implications in data showed that the spawning of mussels alters the BCI nega-
tively. The eﬀect of time from the start to the end of the three-week exposure had a signiﬁcant
impact on all the treatment groups and on the controls. This suggested that the experimental
design had an impact on the mussels. Containment in a static system with insuﬃcient water;
manual handling during water-change and addition of MPs; and discontinuous ﬁltration which
compromised the mussels’ ability to preserve energy could all be laboratory and methodical
factors inﬂuencing the diﬀerence observed in time.
The third part of the experimental design assessed the MP ingestion and egestion of the
blue mussels. Employing the aforementioned enzymatic digestion protocol, an ingestion of PS
beads (50μm) by M. edulis at both exposure concentrations of 5 and 100 beads L–1 could be
registered. This is the yet lowest exposure concentration ingestion demonstrated to this day
amongst laboratory studies with M. edulis.
As for the egestion assessment, only a very low fraction of beads (percentage) was found
in the egestion water. Moreover, the faeces and pseudofaeces were not assessed in this study,
as a total recovery of 93.3-119.2% MPs was obtained. This suggests only tiny amount are left
to be recovered, thus the faeces and pseudofaeces are considered negligible. The egestion, was
not signiﬁcant when comparing the tissue percentage (after exposure) to the tissue percentage
(after egestion), which implies that either M. edulis did not egest the PS beads or that the gut
retention time is longer than expected hence rendering the egestion period of 2 hours too short.
However, the high recovery (93.3-119.2%) suggests that it is only a tiny amount remaining to
be found.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
Another of the major ﬁnding revolves around the distribution of the MPs between water
and tissue. Utilizing the developed enzymatic digestion protocol, it was possible to assess the
distribution of the MP beads for the very low concentrations exposed to the treatment groups
and subsequently recovered. Amongst four treatment groups (L-T10, L-T40, H-T10 and H-T40)
total recovery 93.3-119.2% was found. In the distribution assessment, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in the tissue fractions (percentage) over time was found, which argues that the ingestion in-
creases over time, potentially leading to an accumulation of MPs. The other tested factor of
concentration level showed no statistical diﬀerence on tissue fractions (percentage) between the
diﬀerent exposure concentrations. Interestingly, this observation indicates that even at the low,
environmentally realistic concentrations, the level ingestion is occurring in a similar proportion
to higher concentrations.
Clearance rate was another informative ﬁnding, which based in the counts of MPs was found
to be 3.35 and 5.29 L/h for the low and high exposure concentration, respectively. This argues
that there is a slightly higher clearance of MP beads in the high exposure, yet SD makes this
hard to state with certainty. Nonetheless, a clear diﬀerence of clearance rate based on the algae
cells was found, which for the algae clearance was to 8.36-10.99 L/h. This is worthwhile not-
ing, as this shows a clearly higher clearance rate based on algae counts, compared to clearance
rate based on MP beads, which implies a selective feeding behavior from the M. edulis or an
avoidance via a lowering of ﬁltration.
A small portion of mussels were assessed to determine the natural background contamina-
tion of MPs in the mussels used in the experiment. No black MP beads were found in any of the
analyzed mussels, supporting that the black MP beads found in our experimental population
originates from the exposure concentration. In addition, several microﬁbers (average of 32.1
particles per mussels) were found in a small sample size of 9 digested ﬁeld mussels, indicating
that microﬁbers is a highly abundant MPs to be considered for future assessments. Beads were
however used in the present study, until methodologies and learnings were obtained from work-
ing with beads, as working with microﬁbers adds a new layer of complexity to the laboratory
performance.
The key contributions of this study are noteworthy ﬁndings about the impact and the dis-
tribution of MPs in mussels and their surroundings. This study has demonstrated M. edulis’
ability to ingest MPs at environmentally realistic concentrations down to 5 MP beads L–1,
which is the lowest concentration assessing ingestion found in current laboratory studies. Fur-
ther, the observed level of MP ingestion was found to follow the relative concentration in the
surrounding water column proportionally. This suggests that, should the accumulation of MPs
in the environment continue to increase so will the level of ingestion in the mussels. Moreover,
time is a very determining factor for ingestion since it increases progressively. However, a clearer
understanding of egestion given longer time duration is called for in this regard, as it will be
interesting to investigate the distribution of MPs in faeces. Minor adjustments, like smaller
sized particles (relative to natural food source of algae 8-12μm) and exposure to other more
commonly MP particles found in the ﬁeld like fragments or microﬁbers, are also qualiﬁed to be
worthwhile investigating further. MPs are at this point not proven to be hazardous in regard
to human consumption, however greater eﬀorts are needed to ensure that the constant input
and accumulation of litter and MPs in the marine environment is evaluated and either ruled
out as hazardous or mitigated.

Outlook
The present study found a range of well implemented methodologies to; extract MP from ani-
mal tissue via enzymatic digestion; assess distribution and obtain a close to 100% recovery of
added MPs in rather large experimental replicas (10L of water); and assess "natural" content of
MPs inside ﬁeld collected mussels working in clean and controlled conditions in the laboratory.
These methodologies would be ideal to further develop and be used to assess some of the seen
limitation of this study.
In future work, it would be interesting to look further into the more commonly found MP
particles sampled in the environment such as microﬁbers, fragments or even smaller beads within
the same size range as the algae M. edulis ingests. Further investigation and experimentation
into a clear understanding of egestion of MPs is strongly recommended as well as how the
presence of MPs in fecal pellets can determine the distribution hereof. More broadly, research
is also needed to determine stress responses, ideally exposing M. edulis for a longer exposure
periods than three weeks. Lastly it is recommended that further research be undertaken working
within environmentally realistic concentrations, if not the aim is to obtain a LOEC.

10.3.1
Preparation of exposure concentration
A stock of black 50μm Polystyrene (PS) beads where purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc
and the exposure concentration calculations made. according to the following correlation given
by the supplier. This is shown in Equation 10.1 to estimate the sample volume (V). The desired
count of particles in the sample volume could be achieved by:
V = (D · 1000) ∗(F – P )
C – F
(10.1)
Where,
D = amount of diluent (mL)
V = sample volume (mL)
C = initial concentration (particles per mL)
P = particle concentration in diluent (particles per mL)
F = desired count (particles per mL)
The Equation 10.1 was used to estimate the concentration of the sample volume (V) for
5 and 100 PS beads L–1 seawater. respectively. The stock had an initial concentration (C)
of 1.5 ·107 beads mL–1. given in the supplier Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc Inc. product speciﬁcs
(KB050T). From the Equation 10.1 it could be calculated that a sample volume (V) of 4.2 of
82μL of the stock should be added to the 10L of seawater. in order to corresponded to 5 and
100 PS beads L–1 seawater.
A sample of the calculated sample volume (V) was pipetted onto a petri dish and counted
manually under the microscope, to ensure the concentration given from the calculations from
Thermo Scientiﬁc where consistent with the count expected.
From the stock, a further 1:10 dilution with saltwater was made and 1L of this diluted stock
was prepared. Now the volumes needed were 42 and 820μL to be added to the 10L seawater to
obtain a concentration of 5 and 100 PS beads L–1. It was preferred to do the dilution. in order
to be able to pipette larger volumes. As two small amounts could create inconsistency during
the pipetting.
