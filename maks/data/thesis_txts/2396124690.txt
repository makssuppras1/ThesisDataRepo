Abstract 
 
The world is threatened by an ever increasing number of people requiring an ever increasing number of 
food sources. As the demand grows, so too must the agricultural systems grow and improve, to combat 
threats such as pests and plant diseases, and seize opportunities to increase production. Unfortunately, the 
fungicides and pesticides used by many are insufficient to offer total protection, as well as being known to 
be involved in a range of adverse effects, on humans, as well as plants. 
To combat these challenges, one solution could be the development of effective Biological control agents 
(BCAs), which is the utilization of naturally present microorganisms, such as Pseudomonas. However, there 
are many obstacles to cross in order to create BCAs, and much more research must be done in order to 
develop reliable, and economically feasible BCAs.  
Hence the purpose of this study has been to discover the optimal growth conditions on the optimal media, 
and to successfully isolate and test the bioactivity of isolated Pseudomonas samples from the soil. These 
have been characterized by UV light for the determination of fluorescence, and then verified by 16S rRNA 
sequencing in Germany. Various methods of conducting bioassays have been tested in order to determine 
the optimal setup. 
The methods used in this study can in turn be used for larger scale experiments, as well as lay the ground 
work for more detailed analysis of isolates, in order to one day create an effective BCA.

1 Aim of Study 
 
The aim of this study was to develop high-throughput virulence assays, by first isolating and 
identifying bioactive Pseudomonas from soil samples, and then performing bioassays on said 
samples. Though this is not a new method, it is a relatively new method of working within this lab, 
and as such, even the methods being used have been under constant evaluation. The method of 
isolating bioactive Pseudomonas was first to be chosen based on results from various types of 
growth media, as well as the conditions under which optimal growth is achieved. Once this was 
achieved, the method for conducting the most effective bioassay was tested rigorously. The 
purpose of this was to optimize on the methods of isolation and conducting bioassays, with the 
explicit future perspective of conducting larger, and more specific methods of analysis, of 
biological control mechanisms in a larger scale.

2 Introduction and theory 
 
The rift between the global demand for agricultural produce and the actual production of 
agricultural produce has been an ever widening gap in the recent decades, culminating in the food 
crisis of 2008. This rift is ever growing, and as such has called for a demand for optimization of 
crop yield, to accommodate the ever growing global needs. animals, pests, diseases and weeds, 
are altogether responsible for between 20% to 40% of global agricultural losses. (Savary, Ficke, 
Aubertot, & Hollier, 2012) 
 
This however, does not effectively reflect the true cost to consumers, farmers, public health, 
society, environment, etc. since it does not take into consideration, physical and economic access 
to food, or the accumulation of mycotoxins, thus making the societal yield even smaller.(Savary et 
al., 2012)  
 
There have been proposed and tested various solutions to these issues, with plant protection 
being at the foremost since the 20th century, focusing on protecting crops from yield losses due to 
biological and non-biological causes. This problem however, remains in the 21st century, with 
even more complexity being added to it, as environmental, economic and social factors begin to 
have a larger impact. This is a direct result of shrinking natural resources, such as water, 
agricultural land, biodiversity, human labor, fertilizers, and the restrictions of those, and the 
availability of non-renewable energy.(Savary et al., 2012) 
 
A general, global confusion of how to properly assess crop loss(Savary et al., 2012) has led to a 
slowing down of the initiatives, and as such, properly identifying which crops are the most urgent 
to protect, is still a missing element As a result, research prioritization has not been properly 
determined as of yet(Savary et al., 2012). In addition, crop pests’ and pathogens’ geographical 
distributions are still poorly understood, and recent studies have been done to increase our 
understanding of the geographical distributions importance in combating said pests and 
pathogens.(Bebber, Holmes, & Gurr, 2014)  
 
Some of the proposed solutions to these problem statements involve ecology, epidemiology, 
biology, agronomy, public health medicine, economics, just to mention a few, and this project’s 
purpose is partly to determine whether Pseudomonas strains can be used as a substitute or 
supplement in crop protection. Not all bacteria that live in the rhizosphere are pathogenic or 
parasitic in nature, in fact many are known as plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR). These 
are free-living, soil-borne bacteria, which when present in the soil or when applied to the soil, can 
facilitate growth of plants by providing nutrients in a symbiotic manner, or reduce the damage 
soil-borne plant pathogens can inflict.(Ahemad & Khan, 2011)  
 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
Current methods used for crop protection include pesticides, fungicides, biological control agents 
(BCAs) to mention the most widely applied methods. Pesticides have many positive aspects to its 
function, though it comes with inherent disadvantages. It is documented(Ahemad & Khan, 2011) 
that the ever increasing use of pesticides has led to frequent and deliberate contamination of 
agricultural soils. As a consequence, such agrochemicals, when allowed to rise beyond certain 
threshold levels, pose a serious threat to the rhizospheric organisms and their associated biotic 
processes. This is entirely dependent of the rate of application, the toxicity and activity spectrum 
of used pesticides, and the persistence and availability of these chemicals in the soil.(Ahemad & 
Khan, 2011) furthermore, the constant addition of new pesticides and fungicides in the market, 
requires even further examination of their post-application consequences.  
 
BCA’s can range in diversity in equal measure to pesticides or fungicides, and must go through as 
many pre-approvals and tests as pesticides and fungicides, if not more. The toxicological risks must 
be thoroughly mapped, as well as for its various primary and secondary metabolites, as well as 
being tested upon not only the crop that is its intended user, but also the surrounding 
environment related to it. This includes the impact on human health and human food sources, as 
well as animals and plant life. 
To give an example, Haas and Keel has expressed concern in regards to the antibiotics produced by 
fluorescent Pseudomonas, and whether they are too broadly toxic, and not specific enough in 
targeting the plant pathogens of interest(Cook et al., 1995). They offer their own suggestion of a 
strategy, that involves identifying Pseudomonas biocontrol strains producing antibiotics that 
specifically inhibits only the plant pathogens in question. This is an ideal solution, though the work 
required to isolate such a perfect strain can be both challenging and long.  
 
Concerns like these needs to be addressed, and the procedures for resolving these concerns must 
be under stringent control and done with rigorous documentation.  
 
However, the diversity of the biocontrol abilities among numerous bacteria species, such as 
Pseudomonas and Bacillus are huge, and it is very common that BCAs can display several different 
biological control mechanisms(Loper et al., 2012), it has been theorized that the interaction 
between rhizospheric bacteria and associated plants have led to an accumulation of genes 
contributing to numerous beneficial traits for the plants. based on this theory, studies in 
narrowing, isolating and utilizing these bacteria can be done.

 2.1 Pseudomonas Putida, a fluorescent Pseudomonas 
 
Several strains in this study have been identified to be Pseudomonas putida, which is a well-
documented species for its beneficial plant enhancing capabilities. Pseudomonas putida is a rod-
shaped, flagellated, gram-negative bacterium commonly found in soil and water habitats that are 
rich in oxygen. these polar flagella helps guide the Pseudomonas putida towards the seed of the 
plants which provides nutrients to the bacterial cell.(Dworkin & Falkow, 2006) 
 
 It grows at its optimum at 25-30°C and can easily be isolated by King +++ plates. Several strains 
are already documented, including the KT2440, to colonize plant roots and establish a mutual 
relationship between the two. The surface of the root, already referred to as the rhizosphere (part 
of it), allows the bacteria to thrive and grow from the root nutrients. in return, Pseudomonas 
putida induces both plant growth and protects the plant from pathogens, which has been 
illustrated in the bioassay part of the results of this study.  
 
Various strains of Pseudomonas has been proven through the literature to have biocontrol 
capabilities towards a numerous amount of plant pathogens, including all five used in this study. 
Fusarium was also tested as a potential plant pathogen candidate for this study, but due to no 
usable results, will only be discussed in theory. It should be noted that this study did not identify a 
strain that had inhibitory effects on all five plant pathogens. The beneficial effects of Pseudomonas 
Putida in promoting plant development, has made it an ideal candidate for bioengineering 
research, to use as a biocontrol agent and improve general plant health(Espinosa-Urgel, Salido, & 
Ramos, 2000). 
 
Pseudomonas Putida has a very diverse, aerobic metabolism, which can degrade organic solvents 
(Toluene, f. example) and can convert styrene oil into biodegradable plastic 
polyhydroxyalkanoates. A very noteworthy characteristic, considering polystyrene foam was 
previously considered non-biodegradable. All of these qualities have led to many researchers 
wanting to use Pseudomonas putida to study bacteria-remediated soil processes(Venter, 2002) 
 
Pseudomonas putida has also proven very helpful in the research of other species in the 
Pseudomonas genus, especially Pseudomonas aeruginosa, which is well known to lead to fatal 
diseases in humans. Despite Pseudomonas putida being saprophytic, it can aid in the research of 
cystic fibrosis, the inherited disorder caused by a defective CFTR chloride transport, leading to 
recurrent opportunistic infections by Pseudomonas aeruginosa. These two are so closely related, 
and as such share similar sequenced genomes (approximately shared: 85%), with the exception of 
Pseudomonas putida lacking the genes to determine virulence. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
Being non-pathogenic, very versatile and easy to handle, Pseudomonas putida proves a prime 
target for research.  
Pseudomonas putida has the ability to tolerate and survive environmental stresses due to its 
diverse control of proteins, including peptide / protein secretion and trafficking, modification and 
repair of protein, stabilization and folding of protein, and also degradation of peptides, proteins 
and glycopeptides (IGR Comprehensive microbial resource). It has a very complex metabolism, due 
to the proteins control of a particular pathway that not only depends on the signal received, but 
also on the specific regulators and promoters in the pathway. Once these signals are received, it 
can then inform the cell of the exact oxygen and nutrient available. This supplies it with the tools 
needed to be used as a biocontrol agent in various climates and conditions.(Ruiz-manzano, Yuste, 
& Rojo, 2005). 
 
In addition, Pseudomonas putida has lipids that are specifically developed as an adaptation 
mechanism that responds to both physical and chemical stresses. Pseudomonas putida is able to 
change its degree of fatty acid saturation, the cis/trans-isomerization and the cyclopropane fatty 
acids formation.(Dworkin & Falkow, 2006) This essentially means that depending on the phase, 
the cell can change its characteristics to better respond to the specific environment.  for example, 
during transition from growth to the stationary phase, a higher degree of saturation of fatty acids 
is seen, as well as a higher membrane fluidity which improves substrate uptake. This effectively 
regulates the cell. All these characteristics result in Pseudomonas putida’s ability to survive deadly 
toxins in soil and thrive in even contaminated areas. The conversion of harmful organic solvents 
into nontoxic composites which are essential to bioremediation, is all thanks to Pseudomonas 
putida’s metabolism. (Härtig, Loffhagen, & Harms, 2005) 
 
Pseudomonas putida also interacts with various other organisms present in the soil. An example of 
such is Saccharomyces cerevisiae, where their interaction in the rhizosphere leads to beneficial 
effects on the state of Pseudomonas putida. Such interaction, and other similar, could prove 
essential to developing biocontrol agents, not just one strain or species, but a beneficial 
community of various bacteria. This study has only done mixed strain bioassays, though doing 
cross species bioassays could reveal important information and usability.  
 
The fungi Saccharomyces cerevisiae produces glucose and maintains favorable pH values, which 
both benefits Pseudomonas putida. This interaction, and others like it, help regulate plant health, 
and provides a great maintainer of healthy and abundant plant life. At the same time, the 
production of various siderophores, such as pyochelin and pyoverdine, helps protect the plant 
from fungal pathogens.  
In return, Pseudomonas putida is able to reside in the rhizosphere and inside the plant seeds, 
where it even supplies vital nutrients to the plant.(Espinosa-Urgel et al., 2000)

2.2 History of Pseudomonas putida pathology 
 
As early as 1982, the US national institutes of Health designated Pseudomonas putida a safety 
strain, which allows it to be used to clone genes from other bacteria from the soil(Romney, 
Sherlock, Stephens, & Clarke, 2000). Over time, some strains have been designated pathogenic, 
despite most being not, due to improper care. An example is the discovery of Pseudomonas putida 
in a commercial anti-fog product, and in StaKleer unopened stock solutions. Improper sterilization 
lead to infection.  
Over time, studies have determined Pseudomonas putida to be saprophytic and thus deemed a 
safe bacterium, though other species in the genus are known to be opportunistic pathogens. 
(Pseudomonas aeruginosa and syringae). 
 
Pseudomonas putida has recently been tested as a potential BCA, and studies dating back to the 
90s has classified 74 strains of Pseudomonas and tested them for potential decrease in plant wilt 
and infection from Fusarium.(Lemanceau & Alabouvette, 1991)  
 
Their success, combined with the general threat of Fusarium, leads to a proposal of doing 
bioassays with Fusarium, in addition to the five plant pathogens associated with this study, as well 
as future studies with soil-borne Pseudomonas. 
 
2.3 The six plant pathogens 
 
2.4 Fusarium avenaceum, oxysporum, and graminearum 
 
All three species of Fusarium are associated with plant pathogenicity, be it fruits or various types 
of grains, leading to a requirement for measures to be taken. Though this study did not lead to any 
useful interaction between Fusarium species and the various collections of Pseudomonas, a small 
section is relevant for potential future studies. 
 
Fusarium produces mycotoxins, which leads to pathogenicity in humans, and can cause diseases 
such as Fungal keratitis, Onychomycosis and Hyalohyphomycosis. Several species have shown a 
quite remarkable phenotypic stability, showing both high virulence, and capabilities to grown on 
many different kinds of culture medias, and is known to sporulate.(Lemanceau & Alabouvette, 
1991)  
 
These traits alone, not only make it a good candidate to experiment with, but also gives an 
indication about just how diverse and adaptable is can be in different environmental conditions. 
Fusarium oxysporum is at the same time famous for being extraordinarily hard to eradicate 
without complete sterilization. As a result, Fusarium is one of the most important species of plant 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
pathogens to combat, and any progress in a biocontrol agent with repressive capabilities would be 
extremely valuable in the industry.(Lemanceau & Alabouvette, 1991)  
 
The different species of Fusarium have various ways of infecting healthy plants, though general for 
all species are the infection of the plant roots, either by its sporangial germ tube or mycelium. This 
can either be done at the root tips, or through any wounds in the roots.  
Once inside the roots, the mycelium grows through the root cortex intracellularly. When it reaches 
the xylem (Xylem is one of the two types of transport tissue in vascular plants, phloem being the 
other). From here, the mycelium remains in the vessels, where it then can advance towards the 
stem and crown of the plant.  
 
When the microconidia germinates, the mycelium can penetrate the upper wall of the xylem 
vessel, thus enabling even more microconidia to be produced in the next vessel.(Gonsalves & 
Ferreira, 1993)   
 
2.5 Erwinia carotovora 
 
Erwinia carotovora is a rod shaped bacterium, named for the carrots from which it was first 
isolated. It infects a wide variety of vegetables and plants, amongst here are carrots, cucumbers, 
onions, lettuce, tomatoes, rice and potatoes(Garbarino & Carolina, 1998) The microbes can be 
found in soil, guts of insects, suspended in aerosols in the air, or in water.(Dworkin & Falkow, 
2006)  These impressive methods of motility mean they can invade crops in both fields and in 
storage, turning plant tissue soft and watery, and eventually foul-smelling and slimy in 
appearance. The different sub species of the pathogen have adapted to different hosts and 
temperatures, ranging from temperate temperatures to much warmer temperatures.(Bell et al., 
2004)  
 
 
Though the genome sequencing of Erwinia carotovora is very limited, the highlight of this is the 
presence of eleven horizontally acquired genomic island which are predicted to allow for nitrogen 
fixation, the production of adhesion proteins used for host attachment and phenazine antibiotic 
production and acquired genes for opine catabolism.(Hueck, 1998) It contains all six Gram-
negative protein secretion systems, and its type one and two secretion systems produces plant 
cell-wall degrading enzymes and other pathogenic determinants. The type three secretion systems 
secrete proteins into eukaryotic cells where they further contribute to pathogenesis by disrupting 
both cellular processes, as well as host cell signal transduction.(Hueck, 1998)   
 
 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Erwinia carotovora causes cell death through plant cell wall destruction through the creation of an 
osmotically fragile cell, achieved by the production of the Plant-cell wall degrading enzymes 
mentioned above.(Bell et al., 2004) Examples of these are extracellular pectic enzymes and 
cellulase, which breaks down pectin and cellulose as the name indicates.(Dworkin & Falkow, 2006) 
All these factors contribute to the degradation into a foul-smelling, semi-liquid crop, unfit for 
consumption, and with the numerous methods of transportation, both airborne, waterborne and 
soil borne, coming up with BCA’s capable of preventing this spread is of great importance, both 
scientifically and economically.  
 
2.6 Pectobacterium carotoverum 
 
Pectobacterium carotovorum is a bacterium now belonging to the Enterobacteriaceae, though it 
used to be a member of the genus Erwinia, which has just been described in the above section. It 
is a plant pathogen with a diverse host range, but widely similar to Erwinia carotovoria, targeting 
agriculturally and scientifically important plant species. It functions by producing pectolytic 
enzymes capable of hydrolyzing pectin between individual plant cells, similar to Erwinia 
carotovoria, and even induces the same symptoms in plants.(Dworkin & Falkow, 2006) Most 
plants are known to be able to resist a wide array of plant pathogens, with just the help provided 
by the rhizosphere, however, the presence of any wound greatly increases the chance of infection.  
 
Since harvested crops have a wound where it has been harvested from the fields, and as such the 
postharvest losses are the primary concern, and since high humidity and temperatures around 
30°C are common in a lot of countries in terms of storage, and these particular conditions highly 
favors the development of decay.(I. Toth, Bell, & Holeva, 2003) 
 
The most well-known virulence factors include: cellulases (Plant wall degradation), pectinases, 
proteases, lipases, xylanases and nucleases, in addition to regular pathogenic virulence factors 
such as iron acquisition, multiple global regulatory systems or LPS integrity.(I. Toth et al., 2003)

2.7 Xanthomonas campestris, Pectobacterium atrosepticum and Dickeya solani 
 
Xanthomonas campestris is another bacterial species that causes a variety of diseases in plants, 
including “black rot” which infects plants such as cabbages, as well as causing a bacterial wilt in 
other plants. visible brown or blackish spots on leaves can be Xanthomonas campestris.(“Black Rot 
Of Crucifer Crops,” 2002)  
Pectobacterium atrosepticum causes blackleg in potatoes, which can result in stunting, wilting, 
chlorosis of leaves, a decline in crop yield, necrosis of tissue and in worst cases, death of the 
potato plant. The name originates from the blackening and decay that is visible in the lower stem 
portion, also called the leg, of the plant.(S. H. De Boer, 2004; Kwasiborski, Mondy, Beury-Cirou, & 
Faure, 2013)  
 
The last pathogen, Dickeya solani, also causes blackleg and soft rot in potatoes, and has symptoms 
that are very often indistinguishable from those caused by Pectobacterium species, however, 
Dickeya solani is more virulent, causes disease from lower levels of inoculum and is capable of 
spreading more efficiently through the plant.(I. K. Toth et al., 2011) 
Their virulence factors are very similar to the above mentioned pathogens. 
 
All of the plant pathogens tested in this study share a lot of common ground in terms of virulence 
factors, targets of diseases and diseases caused, which leads to the theory that protecting the 
crops in question might be achievable through one BCA that remains to be developed. Until then, 
recommended precautions to take to prevent infection and crop losses, are identification of 
potential disease sources, and management of pests capable of causing spread, as well as 
engineering methods to increase host resistance, Proper sanitation is also key in preventing 
diseases, and as such, creating proper risk assessments could lead to a reduction in crop loss.  
 
All of these current methods however, only deal with prevention, and at this point, if a crop 
harvest has been infected, there are rarely methods to avoid discarding the whole harvest and 
properly sanitizing the facilities in hope of a better harvest next time.(“Black Rot of Crucifers Fact 
Sheet,” n.d.)

2.8 The benefits and downsides of multiple plant-growth promoting traits 
 
It has already been stated, that the diversity of the capabilities among fluorescent pseudomonas 
such as Pseudomonas putida is significant, and it is quite common for BCAs to display numerous 
different biological control mechanisms.(Loper et al., 2012) In addition to this, it has been 
reasoned that these multiple traits provide more benefit to the plants than bacteria with fewer 
traits, with the reasoning being that it ensures that at least one mode of action is expressed in 
particular environmental conditions, and that the effects of these differing mechanics are added 
up quantitatively.(Bruto, Prigent-Combaret, Muller, & Moënne-Loccoz, 2014)   
 
Most studies showing that the most effective plant growth promoting rhizobacteria are the ones 
with several different plant beneficial properties(Almario, Muller, Défago, & Moënne-Loccoz, 
2014; Bruto et al., 2014) at the same time, removal of just one of these mechanisms often greatly 
reduce the plant-beneficial effects. However, despite a great amount of time invested doing 
research in plant growth promoting rhizobacteria, there have yet to be a proper definition to the 
molecular features, and as such, the status of plant growth promoting rhizobacteria is still not well 
defined. Despite this, some genes of importance for plant-bacteria cooperation have been 
identified already, and there are more studies currently researching this in various bacterial 
isolates, doing research similar to this project, but in greater detail, in an attempt to gain a more 
definable insight to the qualities and potential associations of plant-beneficial traits.  
 
a common and widespread approach to identifying and utilizing plant growth promoting strains is 
to acquire samples, grow them, and screen through the isolates for bioactivity towards plant 
pathogens, or for plant growth promoting properties, by testing them on humanly used crops. 
Examples of both these approaches are well documented by other researchers(Rana et al., 2011)  
 
Some inherent disadvantages unfortunately do exist, and these issues are not just restricted to 
their field application. fluorescent pseudomonas, such as the ones being used in this study, lose 
some of their viability when left in storage for extended periods of time. This has a negative effect 
on its concentration, and as such may not have the required concentrations required for effective 
biocontrol.(Haas, Defago, & Défago, 2005) These factors must all be considered when creating a 
BCA, and these advantages and disadvantages differ from bacteria to bacteria. Another example is 
Bacillus, which is also being studied currently at DTU. When comparing the two, it has been 
documented that Bacillus is able to produce resting spores, whereas Pseudomonas is not, and 
where the quality of resting spores has a direct impact on storage, since resting spores protect 
from stresses during formulation and storage.  
 
However, efforts have already been made to remove disadvantages such as this through methods 
such as biopolymer formulations which function by protecting the cells during transportation and 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
storage. (using pseudomonas for integrated). Still, a need for further research in the search for 
optimal products, and the process leading to a product, is greatly required, and just one of the 
barriers to overcome in the pursuit of the optimal BCAs.

2.9 Possible solutions 
 
There is a motivation behind the need for using BCAs, and ideally, a mixture of such. It is to 
increase the disease suppressive effects, and it can be done in multiple ways, two of which has 
been tested in this study. By using a combination of Pseudomonas strains, or using other potential 
candidates for BCAs, an attempt to use the exhibited disease-suppressive mechanisms in 
conjunction and complementary, or by using biocontrol strains which are known to stimulate each 
other in the expression of desired biocontrol genes, and as such enhance the disease suppressive 
qualities of the BCA towards plant pathogens. There are limitations to this approach, as any good 
BCA will have to accommodate for fluctuations in environmental conditions, as well as dealing 
with storage and production that does not lead to functional decomposition.  
 
These methods have been tested before(M. de Boer et al., 2003; Guetsky, Shtienberg, Elad, 
Fischer, & Dinoor, 2002), by Guetsky et al and Boer et al, where Boer et al were able to 
demonstrate that a combination of Pseudomonas putida strains were able to enhance disease-
suppression compared to using single strains, and Guetsky was able to demonstrate that 
combinations of more diverse origins, in this case Pichia guilermondii and the bacteria Bacillus 
mocuides were able to suppress the pathogen Botrytic cinerea on strawberries. This beneficial 
relationship was proven to be exhibited by one species competing with the pathogen for 
nutrients, while the other was enhancing plant systemic resistance.  
 
More examples of solutions exists, such as different species enhancing DAPG biosynthesis of one 
another, which is an antibiotic against wheat pathogens(Maurhofer, Baehler, Notz, Martinez, & 
Keel, 2004). Methods like these can be monitored by using various reporter genes, such as GFP, 
RFP or Lux reporter genes. More stable commercial bio pesticides have also been created by 
adding nutrients when introducing a new BCA, since evidence has suggested that minerals and 
carbon sources plays an important role in the biosynthesis of antimicrobial compounds produced 
by Pseudomonas BCAs. In addition, adding nutrients is an easy and economical way of improving 
biocontrol of introduced bacteria and indigenous bacteria alike (potential role of pathogen). This 
could be a possible solution to the decomposition of Pseudomonas BCAs. Moenne-Loccoz et al 
showed that though seeds are normally pelleted and dried for storage purpose, which increases 
survival, and as a result, biocontrol efficacy, though the addition of nutrients to these seeds 
resulted in positive results in terms of survival for the introduced BCAs.(Moënne-Loccoz et al., 
1999)  
 
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
Just as any other living being, adding nutrients and creating beneficial symbiosis is not enough, as 
the microbiome composition of plants and the rhizosphere around its roots are susceptible to 
stress and disease. A possible solution to this could be complete profiling of the crop microbiomes, 
and more detailed analysis into exactly how introduced microbes will actually influence this 
community. Understanding the exact impact of introducing BCAs, is just as important as 
developing a good BCA under laboratory conditions.(Picard & Bosco, 2008)

3 Materials and methods 
 
Five sampling sites have been visited and samples have been extracted on the date of August 15th, 
from Zealand in Denmark in 2017. This project is in continuation of a previous project in 
Pseudomonas sampling and characterization, with the following changes, gone into detail later in 
this section: Kings +++ modified medium (KB+++) has been used over the PAB +++ modified 
medium, due to higher pseudomonas selectivity. At the same time, a thousand-fold reduction in 
added chemicals have been applied on all accounts, due to a reference error that was carried over 
into the previous project.(Vacheron et al., 2016)

3.1 Characterization of isolates by 16S rRNA Sequencing 
 
In order to determine that the isolated and purified colonies were only species of Pseudomonas, 
16S rRNA sequencing was performed using the two primers: 16S_8F and 16S_1492r with the 
sequences AGA GTT TGA TCC TGG CTC AG and GGT TAC CTT GTT ACG ACT T respectively (PCR 
primer design). Selected colonies chosen previously was subjected to colony PCR according to the 
procedure in Appendix XXX, as well as subjected to the program listed in the same Appendix.  
 
afterwards, the amplified DNA was purified using NucleoSpin®Gel and PCR clean-up kit (Macherey 
Nagel), where afterwards 2 µL was run on a 1% agarose gel (Invitrogen) which was stained with 5 
µL of ethidium bromide in TAE buffer. The gel results would then give an indication whether or not 
the product had the appropriate size and was fit for sequencing, which was prepared diluting to a 
concentration of 50 ng / µL on the Nanodrop (DeNovix). Once this was completed, the sample was 
sent to GATC Biotech in Germany for sequencing and Analysis. The results were then opened in 
CLC workbench and a locus alignment was done to make sure that samples showing near identical 
sequences were sorted to avoid duplet results, and the sequence extracted from CLC workbench 
was then run using the NCBI BLAST tool, selecting megablast and standard settings.

3.2 Bioactivity 
  
The five sampling points resulted in a total of 14 strains of Pseudomonas that were subject to 
multiple, different bioactivity assays. The first bioassay was conducted on 25 strains previously 
isolated, but the samples showing no kind of bioactivity towards any plant pathogen was 
discarded. All samples, both plant pathogens and Pseudomonas strains were adjusted with a 
photometer to OD of 1 to get a common foundation for measuring clearing zones. 
 
The first method consisted of growing the freeze stocks created previously of the Pseudomonas 
and the six plant pathogens described in their separate sections earlier in the report. This step was 
identical for all used methods.  
A lawn consisting of 200 µL of each plant pathogen was spread unto separate LB plates agar, 
which had been dried already for one hour. Immediately after this, 10 µL spots of Pseudomonas 
samples were spotted on top of the plate. The plates were then left for one days at room 
temperature in darkness, where after clearing zones (or lack of) was documented photographically 
every 24 hours for three days, and the most promising photographs were kept for appendix C.  
 
Table 2 - name and origin of the plant pathogens used in this study. 
 
 
The second method was a spot by spot bioassay, with reference spots of both plant pathogen and 
Pseudomonas sample being present. 10 µ spots were spotted on an LB plate, and left to grow, 
with photographic evidence being taken every 24 hours. 
 
The third method used for the bioassay consisted of growing 10 µL spots of Pseudomonas samples 
on LB agar for 24 hours, where after the plates were subject to Chloroform gas for one hour, and 
drained of gas on a desiccator for 45 minutes. A 1% Bacterial grade agar was then heated to 
melting point, and subsequently cooled down to 45°C, where one of the plant pathogens were 
then added and vigorously shaken, for then to be poured on top of the LB plates with the dead 
Pseudomonas spots on.(Bauernfeind & Burrows, 1978) To create a frame of reference, 
Pseudomonas protegens CHAO or P9K21 (the best candidate from the previous study to this

one)(DSMZ, Germany) was used on all plates. Clearing zones were measured and documented 
photographically at 24h intervals.   
 
The fourth method used is very similar to the first method, but instead of applying droplets to the 
LB plates directly, 0.45 µm thick filter paper was applied unto the dried LB plate, and then 10 µL 
were applied on top of the filters. The colonies were left in darkness at room temperature for two 
days, before the filters with the growing Pseudomonas colony was removed. A 1% Bacterial grade

plates with the secondary metabolites of the Pseudomonas still present. Clearing zones were

used. This selection was based on how well they performed in the first bioassay.

4 Results 
 
4.1 The isolation of Pseudomonas species from the soil 
 
There have been several methods of isolating Pseudomonas from the soil (A new selective 
medium for) for quite some time, but since it is a relatively new method of lab work in Lars 
Jelbak’s group, so an important factor in this study was not just to yield results in forms of usable 
Pseudomonas strains for future work, but also to optimize on methods and device the most 
effective method of isolating Pseudomonas and testing it for bioactivity.  
 
Different medias and temperatures of incubation was tested on the isolates derived from the 
original five sampling sites listed in table 1. The temperatures of incubation were tested at both 
room temperature (Around 20°C), at 4°C and at 30°C. Monitoring growth and bioluminescence 
lead to the conclusion that room temperature was the optimal growth conditions. Dilutions of 
samples was set at 2*102 for bioassays, however, a one-time large serial dilution was made to 
determine the Colony Forming Unit count (CFU) of the samples. a 1000-, 10.000- and 100.000-fold 
dilution was made, and a CFU of 65.400.000 was determined. This knowledge is helpful in 
determining exactly how large serial dilutions one should make to be able to see growth, and 
individual colonies for selection.  
 
Several different medias were tested (see attached recipe list in Appendix B), including PAB++, 
KB+++, and Pseudomonas Isolation Agar (PIA, modified).  
 
The first soil samples were grown on PAB++ plates and PIA plates. There was very little growth on 
either of them after 48 hours. The experiment was repeated several times, before attempting to 
isolate Pseudomonas from KB+++ plates instead. The modified KB+++ contained cycloheximide, a 
toxic fungicide. The results were a large change in Pseudomonas growing on the plates, drawing 
the conclusion that though the toxicity was higher in the KB+++ plates, the benefit of using it far 
outweighed the additional safety measures that were required.  
 
Once colonies showing desired morphology and bioluminescence was selected, they were re-
plated unto diluted KB+++ media (ratio 1:4). The incubation time on KB+++ plates were as low as 
24 hours before colonies could be selected.  
 
Freeze stocks had been prepared as described in the methods section, though it should be noted 
that when reusing freeze stuck, careful measures were taken to prevent damaging the cells by 
thawing and refreezing.(HARRISON, 1955) Instead, frozen stock was removed with an inoculation 
graft by scraping the top of the frozen stock, and thereby extracting Pseudomonas for reuse.

5 Discussion 
 
5.1 Different medias used in this study  
 
This study has attempted isolation of Pseudomonas on several different types of plates, PIA plates, 
KB+++ plates and PAB plates. These are chosen based on their qualities that allows Pseudomonas 
species to produce siderophores which displays fluorescence when exposed to UV lights. However, 
these medias have not displayed equal means for growth, as the composition of the media 
(sulphate, phosphate, magnesium, potassium, etc) is vital to allow for the optimal growth of 
Pseudomonas.(Bhattacharya, 2010)  
 
Since medias are very rich in nutrients, modifications for specific selections are added to these 
medias, such as ampicillin, chloramphenicol, cycloheximide, or others, in order to make a media 
that is very rich, but also very selective. (Simon & Ridge, 1974)).  
 
The isolation on PIA and PAB plates were largely inconclusive, with the primary concern for PIA 
being that the Pseudomonas Supplement needed for the plates, were decomposing after six 
months of storage, and while PAB media was allowing for Pseudomonas growth, the growth was 
limited, as well as allowing for fungi growth.  
KB+++ media proved to be very rich in growth conditions for Pseudomonas and at the same time, 
very selective. This is due to the dipotassium hydrogen phosphate and magnesium sulphates 
present in KB+++.(Control, 1954) 
 
Though all media selective for fluorescent Pseudomonas contain chemicals that induce pigment 
production through siderophores, as was visible for even the plates showing little growth, the 
KB+++ allows for the most diverse species of Pseudomonas to grow, and as a result of this, was the 
only media this study continued to use.  The effectiveness of KB+++ is due to its very non-selective 
media, so by modifying it and adding fungicides and other bacteria inhibiting chemicals, we 
allowed for a broad selection of Pseudomonas selection while limiting undesired growth from 
other microorganisms. This is further aided by the fact that Pseudomonas species have few 
nutritional common denominators, but have quite a few antibiotic resistance.(Sands & Rovira, 
1970)

5.2 The colony morphology 
 
Almost all assays were spot assays, though it was theorized that the inhibition zones of bioassays 
containing living Pseudomonas, had Pseudomonas growth as well, though the spots looked 
unchanged through a microscope. literature has proven that stress signals might trigger the 
formation of biofilms,(Aka & Haji, 2015) and that could explain the ring-like formation on the outer 
parts of the spotted on Pseudomonas colonies. If this was the case, the growing of Pseudomonas 
outside the biofilm would look slightly different, due to a lack of homogenization. This biofilm 
formation is a response to the added antibiotic, and could therefore be theorized to have been the 
case in the spot assays. Since the detection of Pseudomonas has been done by UV light, the 
intensity is hard to determine, and as such, the only visible evidence that is obtained, is that 
intensity increases as cell density does, making the spots more visible than the immediate 
surrounding area, where Pseudomonas is also theorized to be growing.(Bhattacharya, 2010)  
 
5.3 The rhizosphere 
 
The rhizosphere is not a well-documented area of BCA development and crop growing, despite 
how important it is. The rhizosphere is the immediate community of microorganisms around plant 
roots, which leads to both symbiotic and parasitic relationships between microorganisms, and the 
plant. But this community is exposed to more diverse conditions than any humanly known 
pathogen, due to the changing of the seasons. The rhizosphere will not be the same in February, 
as it will be in August, due to changes in pH, temperature, moisture, exposure to sunlight, 
etc.(Dunfield & Germida, 2003) And to add to these factors, the variation of the plant that the 
rhizosphere is associated with, has a direct effect on the diversity and activity of the microbial, and 
vice versa.  
This ever changing environment results in hard to compare results, such as having completely 
different results based entirely on the season and location of the soil sampling. To minimize these 
variables, it is advised to create as many constants as possible, such as very specific sampling sites, 
which are subject to repeated sampling, and creating libraries of desired bacterial samples 
harvested often throughout a full seasonal cycle. Understanding and harnessing the conditions 
and variables associated with the rhizosphere, is key to conducting successful soil-based 
experiments in the pursuit of effective BCAs.

Several studies have looked directly into some of the factors behind the rhizosphere,(Almario et

5.4 Pseudomonas identification based on 16S rRNA 
 
The sequencing results in this study helped verifying that all samples were indeed Pseudomonas, 
but most of the results showed several hits with identical identity and query coverage. This makes 
it all but impossible to identify the exact Pseudomonas species, and it could be related to the use 
of a universal primer pair.(Janda & Abbott, 2007; Miyazaki, Sato, & Tsukuda, 2017) The BLAST 
results often showed different results from the forward primer compared to the reverse primer, 
but this could be adjusted for by aligning the two sequences in CLC workbench and finding the 
overlap. However, if the sequence is too long, and there is no overlap, the alignment becomes 
very difficult to create, as a larger and larger “blind” spot appears. Janda et Al. has provided 
evidence for the universal primers used that though it is very efficient at determining the identity 
at the genus level (>90%), it is far less effective at determining the species (Between 65% and 
83%).(Janda & Abbott, 2007) As a result, this primer pair is very efficient at determining if samples 
contain Pseudomonas, but for more precise identification of species, a primer which has a higher 
evolutionary rate or one able to target genes more specific to Pseudomonas should be 
implemented.  
 
5.5 The different bioassays 
 
The primary aim of this study has been to identify the optimal method for conducting bioassays. 
four methods have been used throughout the study, where the chloroform based bioassay proved 
to be the optimal one. The method has allowed for the identification of bioactive Pseudomonas 
isolates, and even though laboratory bioassays are very artificial compared to the actual 
rhizosphere, it is very useful for determining candidates for further studies, in greater detail and 
more representative to the actual rhizosphere.  
 
Since Pseudomonas is known to be swarming, which was evident in the fourth method, where a 
top agar was placed on living Pseudomonas (the remnants of the colony that was removed from 
the filter paper). This could be the explanation for why this bioassay was so unsuccessful. Since the 
plant pathogen grew up clouded and chaotic, there might have been Pseudomonas spread around 
in the liquid agar, which was creating inhibition.

6 Conclusion 
 
In conclusion, the optimal method for conducting bioassays on Pseudomonas isolates from the soil 
has been determined based on the four different methods used in this study. We have been able 
to successfully isolate Pseudomonas samples with bioactivity from soil samples collected on 
Zealand, based on the two previous studies done by Lone Gram’s group and Lars Jelbak’s group, 
and do so under prime conditions for Pseudomonas growth. 
 
The most effective method for isolation and growth was determined to be on KB+++ plates, with 
the remaining two, PAB and PIA, being discarded. The addition of cycloheximide, chloramphenicol 
and ampicillin was required to prevent unwanted fungi and bacteria from growing on the plates. 
The optimal conditions are growth for 24-48h depending on the KB+++ ratio (1:1 for 24h or 1:4 for 
48h), and at room temperature in darkness. Once grown, select colonies were extracted and 
restreaked unto KB+++ plates in the ratio 1:4. 
 
Through the optimal bioassay method, we were able to show inhibition on four out of five plant 
pathogens (Fusarium experiment yielded no inhibition either), by using mixed Pseudomonas 
samples. The mixed Pseudomonas samples proved to be exhibiting greater plant pathogen 
inhibition, than any of the single samples, giving rise to further study in creating BCAs with 
mixtures of species.  
 
All samples used in this study have freeze stocks created, which can be used for further studies, as 
well as a complete library of photographic documentation of various successful bioassays.

7 Future perspective 
 
Despite testing on a selection of different media, further studies could potentially look into medias 
more comparable to the soil, or growing in soil itself. It would be of great interest to see if 
Pseudomonas is still able to produce secondary metabolites capable of inhibiting plant pathogens, 
when grown on less nutrient rich plates. Another alternative would be to grow Pseudomonas in 
soil with the plant pathogens. This could be done by inserting an antibiotic resistance gene in the 
Pseudomonas samples, and isolating it with the same antibiotic treatment afterwards.  
If it is possible to grow on nutrient limited plates, a second possibility would be to use iron-binding 
agents, which has been proven to increase fluorescent pigment production.(Lamichhane & 
Varvaro, 2013) There are alternative medias that could be used once isolation is complete, to test 
for compatibility with more realistic soil conditions.(Sharma & Johri, 2003) 
 
Another approach that would be of great interest, but was not possible within the time frame set 
for this project, would be to metagenomics analysis of the samples. This would help to create a 
diverse profile of the samples collected, and serves as a powerful lens to observe the microscopic 
world present in the soil samples. It could be a way to observe hidden diversity that is not visible 
by doing sequencing as we have done in this project.  
 
To carry on from a topic in the discussion, isolate libraries would benefit greatly from being 
sampled during select points in the different seasons, as the life stage of a plant, as well as the 
conditions of the environment surrounding the plant, have a great impact on the composition of 
the rhizosphere. Doing this would not only give a greater diversity in samples, but more 
importantly, potentially pinpoint a particular period where Pseudomonas is particularly abundant, 
or bioactive.  
By creating a larger set of samples, the chances of isolating Pseudomonas that shows larger 
inhibition on plant pathogens might be possible, as well as potentially inhibiting Fusarium. 
Literature indicates that larger sample sets, leads to better chances of finding great BCA 
candidates.(M. de Boer et al., 2003)  This is not to say that larger is always better, as creating too 
large libraries will make the work cumbersome and show, and prone to missing key isolates. A fine 
balance is required, in order to get the greatest results. 
 
Since the primer pair used in this study was too unspecific, developing a new set could also be 
beneficial to future research. This could potentially be done in CLC workbench, which has a primer 
design function, which allows the user to create primers which are very selective for 
Pseudomonas. Repeating elements are known to be highly conserved across even different species 
of Pseudomonas, and this has previously been used as guiding markers to locate a region within 
the 16S gene. Doing this, it has been possible to find consensus sequences across as many as fifty 
different species of Pseudomonas. Four repeating patterns that were showing more than 80% 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
consistency, led discovering continuous regions of bases. The continuation of finding consensus 
patterns and sequences, has thus previously led to the invention of very specific primer pairs, 
which could either be used in further studies, or serve as an inspiration to create our own primer 
pairs.(Purohit, Raje, & Kapley, 2003)  
 
Lastly, biofilm formation was only mentioned as a theory in this study, however it offers a 
microbial community increased survivability. Considering the hostility of soil environments, it is a 
factor that could affect BCA’s effectivity. Conducting research into natural occurring biofilm 
formation in soil microbial communities, both by Pseudomonas and plant pathogens, could reveal 
important information that must be taken into consideration in the continuation of developing 
BCAs.(Miquel Guennoc, Rose, Labbé, & Deveau, 2017)   
 
Biofilm formation is a preferred lifestyle of bacteria in soil, but despite of this, very little 
documentation exists. Biofilm formation is documented to not happen at random, but is directed 
by several biotic factors. Discovering these biotic factors could be of importance in determining 
the exact impact biofilm formation might have.
