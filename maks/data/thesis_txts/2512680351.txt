Abstract 
The emergence of natural biopolymers in hydrogel platforms for tissue engineering 
applications is in high demand since they are non-toxic, biocompatible, biodegradable, and 
inexpensive. The reinforcement of hydrogels with two-dimensional materials is a desirable 
approach to enhance biocompatibility, cell proliferation, and viability. Herein, we have developed 
a system that is both mechanically stable and capable of conducting current.  This project aimed 
to manufacture a nano-reinforced hydrogel carrier system, which can support cell spreading, 
proliferation, and growth within a three-dimensional (3D) network, namely muscle progenitor 
(C2C12) and adipose stem cells (ADSCs). To design this system, we have synthesized methacrylate 
gelatin (GelMa) and added different amounts of MXenes (0.005,0.0125, and 0.025%) (w/v) to the 
polymer solution to increase cell spreadability and electrical property of the crosslinked 
hydrogels. Ultraviolet (UV) photo-crosslinking method was employed to obtain crosslinkable 
hydrogel, and the polymer mixture was exposed to UV light for 60 seconds. Subsequently, for 
characterization studies, the hydrogels were kept in an incubator at 37 °C for 12 hrs. These 
GelMa/MXene hydrogels were characterized by Scanning electron microscopy (SEM), Proton 
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR), Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), Differential 
Scanning Calorimetry (DSC), X-Ray Diffraction (XRD), and Electrochemical impedance 
spectroscopy (EIS). To investigate the biocompatibility of the composite hydrogels, C2C12 and 
ADSC cells were encapsulated and cultured in GelMa/MXene system. The cell viability results 
displayed the viability of more than 85% for ADSCs after 7 days for all combinations. Moreover, 
the results from fluorescent microscopy confirmed that cells spread more in GelMa-
0.005%MXene hydrogels compared to the control sample (pure GelMa hydrogel). The results of 
the present research work have provided the principal knowledge to date regarding the biological, 
mechanical, and electrical properties of MXenes in a 3D hydrogel system in the presence of adipose 
stem cell and C2C12 muscle cell lines.

1.Introduction 
Generating porous substrates, which closely mimic the native extracellular matrix (ECM) 
to promote the regeneration of damaged tissues, has always been a significant challenge in tissue 
engineering. Therefore, during the last decades, there have been several attempts to produce 
constructs 
that 
encompass 
physicochemical 
properties 
of 
the 
native 
cellular 
microenvironment(2). As an example, according to the statistics, over six million bone damages 
occur in the US every year, which needs approximately one million bone grafting procedures. 
Hence, tissue engineering is one of the most promising strategies for the treatment of bone 
injuries. In the conventional method, the source of bone for implantations has been from the 
patient's own body (autografts) or from cadavers (allograft), both of which have significant 
drawbacks such as in allograft donor tissue may not always be accessible, and the risk of rejection 
is higher in the allograft. In autografts, more surgical time is required to obtain the autograft 
sample from a different part in a patient's own body. Consequently, to overcome these 
disadvantages, successful application of bone tissue engineering can address some of the 
challenges related to the autografts or allografts. In this regard, tissue engineers have proposed 
the development of three-dimensional (3D) microenvironments, which can guide embedded cells 
into mature tissue and integrate in vivo with dysfunctional tissues for healing (3–5). This tissue 
engineering strategy includes the use of scaffold materials in combination with cells and 
biological clues. During recent years, hydrogels as a pioneer 3D structures are being applied in 
the most promising techniques such as electro-sniping and 3D printing. Hydrogels are composed 
of cross-linked polymeric networks; they are soft, highly hydrated structure and versatile scaffold 
materials (6). Hydrogels are capable of absorbing and retaining a high amount of water. Their 
aqueous features provide a native-like state for the cell encapsulation. Moreover, hydrogels are 
both physically and chemically tunable, injectable, and they have been used over the years for 
tissue engineering and various drug delivery applications (2). One of the significant features of 
these hydrogels is porosity, which can provide the necessary support for cells to attach, spread, 
and differentiate (7–9). In general, it approved that a highly porous 3D microstructure with 
interrelated pores and a large surface area is beneficial to tissue in-growth. Finding an ideal 
hydrogel pre-polymer that can mimic the biology of human tissues in terms of structure, function, 
and performance remains a challenge for researchers. Therefore, natural biopolymers hold

immense promise, since they provide a combination of unique properties; they are non-toxic, 
biocompatible, biodegradable, and inexpensive.  
In this study, GelMa/MXene hydrogel was developed and investigated in terms of 
mechanical and chemical properties as well as biocompatibility. For this purpose, adipose stem 
cells (ADCs) and a muscle cell line (C2C12) were encapsulated into the hydrogel system. 
1.2. Stem Cells 
            Stem cells are special types of cells, which are capable of being developed and 
differentiate into different cell types. They can also undergo self-renewal to generate more of 
the same kind of stem cells. The stem cells are classified based on their origin: (i) embryonic; (ii) 
fetal; (iii) adult and (iv) induced pluripotent1. In other words, this classification can simplify as 
embryonic and adult mesenchymal. In adult organisms, the role of stem cells and progenitor cells 
is to repair the body and replace adult tissues (10,11). The three well-known accessible adult 
stem cells in humans are derived from bone marrow, adipose tissue, and blood that can be used 
to transplant organs, tissues, or even particular proteins from one part of the body to another 
part (12,13).  Stem cells possess two remarkable properties: 
• Self-renewal: It is the ability to go through several cycles of cell division while keeping the 
undifferentiated state. 
• Potency: The stem cells can differentiate into specific cell types. In other words, this ability 
requires totipotent or pluripotent stem cells to be able to give rise to any mature cell type 
(Fig.1.1), although multipotent or unipotent progenitor cells are also referred to as stem 
cells. The source of pluripotent stem cells is developing embryos. 
Stem cells can proliferate and renew themselves; therefore, it is possible to expand them in vitro, 
and that nominates them in many cell-based therapeutic approaches.

Adipose tissue-derived stem cells (ADSCs) are mesenchymal cells with the capability for 
self-renewal and multipotential differentiation. This multipotentiality enables them to become 
adipocytes, chondrocytes, myocytes, osteoblasts, and neurocytes, among other cell lineages 
upon differentiation. Among stem cells, adipose tissue-derived cells have demonstrated 
promising results in regenerative medicine and tissue engineering (13). 
Cui L et al. and Smok C et al. have used ADSCs to treat osteoporosis in rat models where 
differentiated stem cells were implanted to regenerate the bone tissue. According to their study, 
over two years, regeneration of affected bone tissue was achieved (11,15). The main challenge 
in the application of stem cells in upcoming cell replacement therapies is to keep them viable and 
to control their differentiation in specific tissues. In this sense, there is a range of cultivation 
methods such as reprogramming strategies, genetic manipulation, epigenetic modulation as well 
as developing 3D three-dimensional matrices and directing stem cells to the parts where they are 
needed (10). Hence, a viable stem cell delivery system requires a carrier in order to save cells 
from the disruptive shear forces during the injection; otherwise, the subpar number of surviving 
donor cells will reduce the treatment efficacy of the therapy. Simultaneously, such systems need 
to hold the encapsulated cells within the targeted tissue and provide them with a stable three-
dimensional (3D) microenvironment to permit tissue repair and regeneration(16).

myoblast muscle cell line) were encapsulated in a 3D-hydrogel structure via the UV cross-linking

1.3. Hydrogels 
Human organs are made of elastic, rigid, and electroactive tissues that can spontaneously 
heal. Therefore, the microenvironment within most tissues is also highly dynamic and load 
bearing(16–18). Hence, 3D tissue-engineered scaffolds should have the same mechanical and 
electrical properties as those found in natural tissues. Hydrogels are one of the significant 3D 
microstructures in tissue engineering. Hydrogels can provide a biomimetic and hydrated 3D 
microenvironment to support cell function (19). Moreover, besides the biological and structural 
factors, the native microenvironment of tissues within the body relies on mechanical (i.e., bone, 
muscle) and electrical cues (i.e., cardiac, nerve). Tissue-engineered hydrogels aim to integrate 
within native organs that are mechanically durable and electroactive. In this regard, main 
attempts have been directed toward engineering of the hydrogels that can simultaneously fulfill 
mechanical properties while they are self-healable and electroactive (20). Additionally, 
injectability is a desirable property of hydrogels that can be used to deliver stem cells to the 
target tissue in a minimally aggressive manner. The main benefits of conductive hydrogels include 
not only their physical property but also their adequate electrical property, which can provide 
electrical signals to cells to stimulate them to spread more (21,22). 
1.4. Materials and methods 
One of the essential components in hydrogels is polymers, which can together with water 
develop 3D microenvironments for cells to attach, grow, and differentiate. Hydrogels are 
hydrated 3D structures stabilized via cross-linked hydrophilic polymers; so, their aqueous 
features enable a native-like state for cell encapsulation. Moreover, these polymers should be 
capable of functionalization appropriately in order to modify their chemical and physical 
characteristics according to the targeted therapy. Among natural polymers, alginate and gelatin-
based hydrogels are two main groups, currently being used in the clinic. Alginate-based hydrogels 
are usually characterized by brittleness and low mechanical stability, and the gelatin-based ones 
tend to degrade rapidly within the body since they act as a substrate for collagenase(23,24). In 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
6 
 
addition, the gelatin-matrix generates a local microenvironment for cell assembly and acts as a 
lubricant during injection through a fine catheter. For cellular scaffolding, hydrogels are generally 
used because of their compositional similarities to an extracellular matrix (ECM) and tunable 
structures for cellular survival and proliferation(18).  
To fabricate hydrogels, both natural and synthetic polymers can be employed. Naturally 
derived polymers, such as collagen, chitosan, hyaluronic acid (HA), alginate, gelatin, elastin, 
chondroitin sulfate, and heparin, are good candidates for biological applications due to their cell 
signaling and cell-interactive properties, and biodegradability. However, they suffer from 
drawbacks such as low mechanical properties, inability to control their degradation and 
structure, and potential immunogenicity (24). On the other side, synthetic hydrogels, such as 
poly(vinyl alcohol)(PVA), poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG), polyacrylamide (PAM), and poly(2-
hydroxyethyl methacrylate) (PHEMA) possess controllable degradation and microstructure, 
generally show high mechanical properties, but lack biological property(25–27). According to the 
distinctive properties of both natural and synthetic hydrogels, hydrogels based on the 
combination of these two classes of polymers have attracted significant attention for biological 
and biomedical applications. In the construction of hydrogels, materials with proper 
characteristics should be chosen for the intended application. 
1.4.1. Ultraviolet (UV) cross-linking 
 Numerous crosslinking methods, including chemical and physical approaches, have been 
employed to construct polymer networks and maintain their 3D structures in aqueous 
circumstances. In the physical crosslinking method, physical interactions between polymer chains 
such as polymer entanglements, ionic interactions, or hydrogen bonds prevent dissociation of 
the hydrogel, while in chemically crosslinked gels, covalent bonds between polymer chains 
establish stable hydrogels. Various approaches can be employed to form stable hydrogels, such 
as ionic interactions, pH stimulation, and light exposure. 
To form a hydrogel under UV exposure light, a photocurable hydrogel precursor is 
combined with a photoinitiator, which initiates the crosslinking reaction. Although a range of 
wavelengths can be used, ultraviolet (UV) light is mostly used to induce the photo-initiator to 
generate free radicals. Then, the activated functional groups form covalent bonds with free 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
7 
 
radicals to produce crosslinked networks. Subsequently, an unreacted polymer is washed out 
upon completion of the crosslinking process. Photo-crosslinkable hydrogels offer several 
advantages over other types of crosslinking schemes. For instance, they enable controlled spatial 
crosslinking of the hydrogel to control the microstructure of the resulting material, which can be 
used to modify cellular behavior (e.g., adhesion, migration, and proliferation)(28–30). 
Furthermore, photo-crosslinking is a simple, rapid, and cost-effective technique.  
1.4.2. GelMa 
Gelatin is a protein derived from partly denatured collagen and is a hydrophilic natural 
polymer material with excellent biocompatibility and biodegradability properties. Since the 
hydrolysis product of collagen, the primary component of ECM in most tissues, contains many 
arginine-glycine-aspartic acid (RGD) sequences that can promote cell attachment, as well as the 
target sequences of matrix metalloproteinase (MMP), which are suitable for cell remodeling (31). 
GelMA is made of this natural polymer, gelatin, via the one-step chemical modification. To form 
covalently crosslinked hydrogels form a gel, GelMA undergoes photoinitiated radical 
polymerization, under UV light exposure in the presence of a photoinitiator (Fig.1.2). Besides UV 
light exposure, GelMa hydrogel can also be microfabricated using different techniques including 
micro-molding, photomasking, bioprinting, self-assembly, and microfluidic, to generate 3D 
constructs with controlled microarchitectures.

Hybrid hydrogel systems can form by mixing GelMA with nanoparticles such as carbon 
nanotubes (CNTs), graphene oxide (GO), and other polymers to form networks with desired

1.4.3. MXene 
MXenes are inorganic materials containing two-dimensional transition metal carbides 
and nitrides with the general formula of (Mn+1XnTx), where M is a transition metal, X can be carbon 
or nitrogen, and Tx is surface functional termination such as –OH, –F, –O(34). Furthermore, 
MXenes combine metallic conductivity of transition metal carbides and hydrophilic nature of 
hydroxyl or oxygen terminated surfaces. Moreover, MXenes have antibacterial and 
biocompatibility properties(35). MXenes can be produced by selectively removing aluminum 
layer from layered MAX phases. MXenes can hold many ions and molecules between their layers 
by an intercalation process, which is sometimes a necessary step in order to develop the 
materials’ unique properties(34). 
 
Recently, it has been reported that by using a combination of chemical exfoliation and 
sonication, the synthesis and mass production of 2D materials from three-dimensional (3D) 
layered compounds with chemical bonding between the layers is achievable. In this regard, it has 
been demonstrated some members of the MAX phase family can be exfoliated into 2D transition-
metal carbide and nitride layers, so-called MXene, by using hydrofluoric acid (HF) solutions and 
sonication(36)as shown in Fig.1.3(34,37). Consequently, depending on the transition metals at 
the surface, thickness, and surface functional groups, they present quite different electronic, 
magnetic, optical, and electrochemical properties compared to their original MAX phases (38).

Two-dimensional (2D) transition metal carbides (MXenes) demonstrate outstanding 
performances in many applications, such as energy storage, optoelectronics, electrocatalysts, 
and biomedicine. However, by the conversion of the titanium carbide to titanium dioxide under 
ambient circumstances, the colloidal solutions of Ti3C2Tx MXene flakes deteriorate rapidly(39). In 
a recent study reported, the oxidation stability of the Ti3C2Tx flakes could be noticeably improved 
in a system where both temperature and water molecules are well-controlled (39,40). Zhao X et 
al. reported that an aqueous solution of Ti3C2Tx MXene could be chemically stable for more than 
39 weeks when its storage temperature was sufficiently low approximately (−80 °C) to stop the 
oxidation processes. Additionally, it was also found that if the Ti3C2Tx flakes dispersed in an 
organic solvent such as ethanol, the degradation process can be significantly delayed even at 5 
°C (41). Moreover, MXenes tend to oxidize and degrade quickly in a matter of days, thus to 
improve the stability of MXenes using of antioxidants, such as sodium L-ascorbate (Ascorbic acid), 
is shown as a practical approach to delay the oxidation of colloidal and dehydrated Ti3C2Tx MXene 
nanosheets (41). According to the chemical structure of MXene and its properties, it can be used 
as a reinforcement agent in composites, similar to clays or graphene, in order to modify 
mechanical, electrical, and biological properties. 
1.5. Properties of Hydrogels 
Biocompatibility and biodegradability are the most important properties that should be 
considered in cell encapsulation process and tissue engineering applications (42). The hydrogel 
system, which used in tissue engineering, should be non-toxic and non-antigenic (33,43). Based 
on the literature review, GelMa is a biocompatible material and a suitable candidate for the 
fabrication of hydrogels. 
Furthermore, most of the tissues in the human body are very dynamic; thus, a hydrogel 
carrier for stem cell delivery should be able to endure continual and cyclic mechanical forces. 
Therefore, the mechanical property of a hydrogel plays a crucial role in directing stem cell 
differentiation into a specific tissue (5). For instance, research studies have shown that a typical 
Young’s modulus range is around 25-60 (kPa) in order to support optimal osteogenesis, while 
typical moduli for supporting differentiation into neural and muscle lineages are 0.1-1 and 8-17 
(kPa), respectively (44,45). In a research study, Yue.K et al. reported that the compressive 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
10 
 
modulus of 5% (w/v) and 10% (w/v) gelatin methacrylate (GelMA) hydrogels with ∼80% 
methacrylation degree were defined in the range of ∼3-5 and 14-16 (kPa) respectively, for 
experiments dealing with viable cell encapsulation (31). However, the mechanical property of the 
GelMa/MXene hydrogel was not previously reported.   
The degradation rate depends on the intended application. If the purpose is to develop a 
retrievable and immunoprotected hydrogel, long-term stability and low degradation are required 
(29). Whereas, if the hydrogel only serves as a carrier until the transplanted cells integrate into 
the tissue, lower stability and higher degradation rate are necessary. 
Encapsulated cells in a hydrogel system must be able to interact with the surrounding 
environment and tissues in order to enhance cell survival, viability, proliferation, and 
differentiation (21,46). This function is only possible if the system has a continuous and 
interconnected inflow of nutrients and outflow of metabolic waste products. To achieve this 
purpose, an interconnected hydrogel network with controlled porosity and pore size is required 
to keep the encapsulated cells alive. In this regard, recent research studies have shown that a 
hydrogel with a pore size >100 (μm2) is typically needed to ensure that simultaneously allowing 
an efficient waste and nutrient exchange happens with the surrounding environment and tissues 
(5,21).

1.6. Objectives 
The UV photo-crosslinking technique allows the production of a hydrogel, i.e., substrates 
with pores diameter in the same order of magnitude of the extracellular matrix (ECM), and 
provides better cellular adhesion, growth, and proliferation. In the UV light cross-linking process, 
the biological characteristics with the high porosity and surface area make the obtained substrate 
useful in many biomedical applications as the biocompatible and biodegradable polymers can be 
processed in the UV cross-linking method. 
This master thesis aimed to produce and characterize a proper hydrogel carrier based on 
GelMa/MXene for improving ADSCs and C2C12 cell viability and proliferation. It was 
hypothesized that by encapsulating ADSCs and C2C12 in the reinforced hydrogel system, the 
viability and functionality of the encapsulated cells would increase compared to cells 
encapsulated in a hydrogel without MXene. For this purpose, different percentage of MXene was 
incorporated into GelMa hydrogel. This hydrogel carrier was chosen due to its properties, such 
as biocompatibility and biodegradability. The overview of this study is presented in Fig.1.4.

2.1. Synthesis of GelMa 
GelMA was synthesized according to a method described by Yue.K et al. (31). Briefly, 30 g 
of gelatin (from porcine skin Type A, Sigma Aldrich, Germany) was slowly added to 300 ml of 
warm Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline (DPBS) (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) at 50 °C and stirred 
until fully dissolved. Then, under N2 gas, 17.4 ml of methacrylic anhydride (Sigma Aldrich,

lyophilized for 7-10 days to obtain a solid product, which was kept at -20 °C. The schematic

2.2.1. Fabrication of Photo-Cross-Linked Hydrogels 
The photo-cross-linked GelMa/MXene hydrogels were fabricated by adding dissolving 
freeze-dried GelMa polymer with different concentrations of MXene into a 0.5% (w/v) photo-
initiator (PI) solution. Then, the prepared polymer solutions injected on a glass slide, and a glass 
coverslip was separated by a 2mm spacer and then cross-linked by ultraviolet (UV) light ∼7 
mW/cm2 for 1 min to form the hydrogels. Ultraviolet (UV) light is commonly used to induce the 
PI to produce free radicals. Therefore, these free radicals form covalent bonds with activated 
functional groups to produce crosslinked networks. Subsequently, for further characterization 
studies such as FTIR, DSC, SEM, and XRD, the hydrogel samples were washed with MQ water and 
freeze-dried overnight.

2.4. Cell culturing 
  Cell culture is the process that cells are grown under controlled conditions, generally 
outside their natural environment in an incubator at 37°C, 5% CO2, and 90% humidity. These 
conditions can differ for each cell type, but generally contain a suitable vessel with a medium 
that supplies the essential nutrients (such as carbohydrates, amino acids, vitamins, minerals), 
growth factors, hormones, and gases (CO2, O2), and controls the physio-chemical environment 
(pH buffer, osmotic pressure, temperature). In this study, Adipose stem (ADSCs) and C2C12 
obtained from (Aalborg University, Denmark), were used in all processing experiments. Firstly, a 
1 ml aliquot of the cell solution containing 700,000 ADSCS cells was thawed very quickly in the 
water bath at 37℃. Then ADSC cells were transferred to a proper culture flask (T175 cm2) 
containing 50ml warm prepared Sigma DMEM/F-12 Ham culture media consisting of 10% Fetal 
Bovine Serum (FBS) (Gibco, Thermofisher, MA, USA) and 1% Penicillin/ Streptomycin (Gibco, 
Thermofisher, MA, USA). Then, the flask was shaken gently to ensure the medium covered the 
surface of cells completely. One day after thawing the cells, the medium discarded, and the flask 
was rinsed once time with warm DPBS, then 30ml of the warm medium was added. C2C12 cell 
lines were cultured in DMEM High glucose media containing 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) 
(Gibco, Thermofisher, MA, USA) and 1% Penicillin/ Streptomycin in T-75 flask (450,000 cells) at 
37℃ under a 5% CO2 atmosphere. 
2.4.1. Sub-culturing (Passaging) 
The ADSC and C2C12 cells were grown in the incubator at 37°C, 5% CO2, and 90% 
humidity. Every two days, the medium of each cell type was changed until the cells reached ≈70% 
confluency. The passaging cells were done by removing and discarding the culture medium and 
washing it with pre-warmed 10 ml Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline for ADSCs and 5ml for

3.1. Hydrogel properties 
This thesis aimed to develop a nano-reinforced hydrogel for targeted cell delivery. In 
order to evaluate the nano-reinforcement effect, 0.005%,0.0125%, and 0.025% MXene were 
incorporated into GelMA hydrogel and characterized in terms of chemical, mechanical, and 
electrical properties. Moreover, to investigate the cell viability, proliferation, and optimal

3.1.1. 1H NMR analysis 
The chemical modification of GelMa was quantified by using 1H NMR spectroscopy. The 
lyophilized GelMa (1% (w/v)) was mixed in D2O and placed in NMR tubes to characterize the 1H 
NMR spectroscopy. Fig. 3-1 (b) shows the 1H NMR spectra of gelatin and GelMA. In the GelMa 
spectrum, new signals were detected at c = 5.36 ppm and b = 5.6 ppm, which were assigned to 
the methacrylate groups. The new signal at d = 1.84 ppm was assigned to the methyl function (R-
CH3) of the introduced methacrylate. Consequently, it has been concluded that methacrylate 
groups have been successfully grafted to the gelatin molecules. The intensity peak at a=2.92 ppm 
of the lysine methylene of the gelatin after the reaction decreased in the GelMa spectrum, which 
confirmed that almost all the lysine methylene of gelatin residues was reacted with lysine 
methylene of GelMa. According to (eq.2.1), the degree of methacrylation was determined by 
~60%.

Conclusion 
Tissue engineering aims to develop therapeutic products mimicking the native 
extracellular matrix (ECM) to promote the regeneration of damaged tissues. In this regard, a 
combination of scaffolds with viable cell systems or active biomolecules derived from such cells 
is necessary for the repair, restoration, and regeneration. In this project, we have synthesized 
GelMa from natural polymer gelatin, which is a biocompatible, biodegradable, and non-toxic 
biopolymer. Hereof, GelMA undergoes photoinitiated radical polymerization, under UV light 
exposure in the presence of a photoinitiator, to form covalently crosslinked hydrogels. We 
hypothesized by the addition of MXene as a 2D inorganic material into GelMa hydrogel; the 
thermal stability and biocompatibility might be increased. Incorporation of MXene into GelMa 
hydrogel was confirmed by FTIR, XRD, DSC. GelMa/MXene hydrogels displayed lower Young’s 
modulus, and mechanical toughness that was attributed to the fact that the proper cross-linking 
was not obtained as MXene has a sharp absorption peak in the same range of UV cross-linking 
(320-500nm). On the other side, GelMa/MXene hydrogels showed higher conductivity property, 
which can stimulate ADSC cells to spread more compared to pure GelMa hydrogel. In all 
GelMa/MXene hydrogel samples, the porosity was higher than 100 µm2 that would allow the 
exchange of nutrients and waste products. In a 3D culture system, C2C12 and ADSC cells were 
encapsulated in GelMa/MXene hydrogels. We have demonstrated that ADSC cells growing, 
spreading, and proliferating inside GelMa/MXene hydrogels improved, and also cell viability was 
more than 85% up to one week in all GelMa/MXene hydrogels, whereas the proper concentration 
of MXene for C2C12 cell line was 0.005%(w/v) to obtain cell viability more than 80%. 
In conclusion, the characterization and cell study results suggest that GelMa hydrogel 
containing (0.005,0.0125, and 0.025) %(w/v) Mxene hold promising properties as a 3D 
biomaterial to be used in tissue engineering applications. According to the cell viability, chemical, 
and mechanical characterization results, GelMa10%/0.005%MX has promising features for both 
ADSCs and C2C12 cell lines, and it can be used as a nano-reinforced hydrogel carrier for mending 
damaged tissues through targeted cell delivery.

Future Perspectives 
Improving proliferation and osteogenic differentiation of human-adipose-derived stem 
cells and C2C12 muscle cell via a nano-reinforced hydrogel system as well as enhancing the 
hydrogel mechanical properties leads to the optimization of the 3D hydrogel for transplantation 
and clinical applications. This project represents promising results for ADSCs and C2C12 cell 
viability in the presence of 2D inorganic material MXene in the 3D hydrogel system. Nevertheless, 
further study and experiments should be done to enhance the efficacy of MXene within the 
hydrogel system to maintain the proliferation and differentiation of C2C12 muscle and adipose 
stem cells. Firstly, to obtain proper crosslinked GelMa/MXene hydrogel using visible light 
crosslinking (380-740) nm instead of UV cross-linking (320-500) nm could be an alternative 
method for achieving proper crosslinked hydrogel.  
Moreover, the osteogenic differentiation of human adipose-derived stem cell study 
through GelMa/MXene nano-reinforced hydrogel should be conducted to investigate the effect 
of MXene on cellular biocompatibility and cell differentiation within the 3D hydrogel system. 
Furthermore, alkaline phosphate (ALP) activity and ALP staining assays could be performed to 
evaluate the osteoblast activity and the osteogenic differentiation of ADSCs, whether MXene 
increases the formation of the mineralized matrix, compared to the control hydrogel.
