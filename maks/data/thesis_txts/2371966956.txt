Abstract
Human behaviour is characterised by identifiable patterns and shows a high degree of predictability.
Two of the most notable behavioural aspects are human mobility and social interactions. This study
addresses a gap in existing literature regarding the correlation of the two from an individual’s perspective.
We analyse a dataset of both location and communication data (≈863 people, ≈2.5 years) and find that
patterns of human behaviour in these fields show a manifestation of the exploration versus exploitation
dilemma. In the mobility data, we discover two distinct classes of individuals: spatial returners and
explorers, and by observing different time periods, we find that this dichotomy exists regardless of
temporal resolution or population size.
By detecting tie de-activation in social networks, we find
a diverse range of communication strategies. We discover a linear relation between communication
capacity and activity, and quantify what defines social returners and explorers. Finally, we explore
the relation between spatial and social exploration and find no correlation. The behaviour patterns of
these characteristic classes can be valuable resources for social studies and the implementation of better
diffusion models. We believe this study can benefit further research to examine the correlation between
individual communication and mobility in a more combined approach.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Humans are amazing beings, capable of extraordinary feats and accomplishments, excelling in sciences
while trying to understand the world around them. However, one of the most interesting aspects of
humanity is that it is continuously trying to understand itself.
1.1
Understanding Human Behaviour
The science of Psychology explores the behaviour and mind as well as conscious and unconscious ex-
periences and thoughts in the process of understanding human individuals and groups [40]. There is a
big interest and desire in finding out why people act in certain ways and how or why they take their
decisions. As humans, we set goals in our life and try to achieve them, thus psychic life is determined
by one’s goal, the knowledge of which is essential to any understanding of behaviour [1]. Apart from
the individual, we are also interested in learning how one fits into the whole and the formation and
change of relationships between individuals in a larger scale - society. Sociology is the science which
studies the social behaviours between humans, as well as society itself [109].
These sciences, although different, they often become intertwined in the process of understanding hu-
man behaviour even better. Social psychology studies how norms define aspects of our behaviour, with
Cialdini et al. [21] stating that between the different norms “an individual’s actions are likely to con-
form to the dictates of the type of norm that are familiar even when the other types of norms dictate
contrary conduct.”.
With the existence of these scientific fields, along with many others such as Philosophy, Anthropology
and other social sciences, one can understand how complex humans are and how difficult it is to
understand them and their behaviour.
However, although every individual is unique and different,
Eagle et al. [37] argues that “there are repeating and easily identifiable routines in every person’s life”.
They presented a new approach in modelling human behaviour and uncovering daily patterns: the use
of structures called eigenbehaviours, “which are the principal components of an individual’s behavioural
dataset”. With the use of these characteristic vectors they managed to predict 79% of a day’s remaining
behaviours for their test subjects.
Nevertheless, even though our daily life is dictated by sometimes heavily defined routine patterns,
to claim that decision making inside and outside of these patterns is a simple process would be far from
truth. What is interesting is not only the patterns themselves, but also how and why they are created
through the decision process. Why does an individual take one decision instead of another?
In order to connect all these aspects of human behaviour, one of the major topics that this paper
focuses on is the exploration versus exploitation dilemma. This refers to “an individual’s search for
a balance between exploring the environment to find profitable actions while taking the empirically best
action as often as possible” [5]. According to Baum et al. [12, p. 768] “exploitation refers to learning
gained via local search, experiential refinement, and selection and reuse of existing routines. Exploration
refers to learning gained through processes of concerted variation, planned experimentation, and play”.
This topic presents itself across many different fields and studies and has been the focus of various
research approaches. Social learning studies how this dilemma is present, not only in decision making
but also throughout our learning process. When faced with a problem, an individual can either explore,
thus pioneer and introduce new knowledge or exploit, thus take advantage of existing knowledge, usually
by copying it. Rendell et al. [95], organised a computer tournament where participants submitted
strategies specifying how to use social learning and its asocial alternatives and subsequently found that

social learning and copying existing knowledge proved to be the most effective strategy. Furthermore,
exploration vs exploitation plays a significant role in organisational learning, where “the relation between
the exploration of new possibilities and the exploitation of old certainties is examined” [68]. The term

1.2
Research Questions & Hypothesis
This study sets out to examine the interplay between exploration and exploitation in two
specific aspects of human life: human mobility and social networks. We narrow down the
focus of our research into examining human behaviour in these two specific fields. The aim of this
research is initially to examine real life data and seek significant patterns in daily human behaviour and
subsequently to combine the findings from these two fields into a unified conclusion.
More specifically, on the human mobility aspect, Pappalardo et al. [85] showcase that there exists
a clear dichotomy in recurrent mobility patterns between individuals that can separate them in two
distinct classes: explorers and returners. In this paper we will try to reproduce and validate this
finding by using the Sensible DTU Project dataset (see section 3) as well as analyse the data specific
to this project. Secondly, on the social networks aspect, Miritello et al. [73], state that “separating
communication capacity from activity reveals a diverse range of tie activation strategies, from stable to
exploratory.”. We are going to attempt to reproduce these findings and apply their techniques on our data
and in combination with the research of Miritello et al. [74], arrive at a conclusion regarding the bigger
picture around social interactions and human communication. The final goal of the research project
documented here is to experiment and combine the findings from different papers in two different fields
by using one unified and rich dataset and examine what are the correlations between an individual’s
mobility patterns and social and communication behaviour. The uniqueness and novelty of our approach
lies predominantly on the dataset which we are using, as it contains both individuals’ location as well
as communication data, thus giving us the opportunity to explore and examine a person’s behaviour
in a combination of human mobility and social interaction perspectives. The topics in question of this
study are:
• Does exploration versus exploitation manifests into human mobility data? We attempt to answer
this by exploring whether the distinct classes defined by Pappalardo et al. present themselves in
our own data, thus making the dichotomy structure more general and clear.
• Does the same dichotomy presents itself in social communication data? We attempt to tackle
this problem by using the research methods of Miritello et al. in order to find communication
strategies in our own data.
• Main Question: Is there a correlation between an individual’s mobillity and social communication
patterns?
• An answer to the above question will help us reply to more common and real world questions: “Is
a spatial explorer, who likes to travel a lot and visit new places, also a social explorer, who has
a lot of friends and distributes her communication time between several other individuals? Does
a person who enjoys the daily routine of commuting between their home and work keep a small
social circle?”

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
In this section an overview of related work is presented. The focus is initially split in two parts: Human
Mobility and Social Networks. First, the reader is met with an overview of human mobility studies and
its interdisciplinary applications, followed by a more in depth review of studies around human movement,
focusing on patterns, models and a better understanding of its nature. Next, an introduction to social
networks is presented, discussing notable work in the field, along with a short historical overview and
reference to some of the most significant scientists and their work. Application and studies of this field
follow, in a process to cover its broad scope. The focus then moves in the combination of the two, where
the interplay between the two different fields is discussed, along with how this study relates to the field.
Finally, there is a part dedicated to data collection, its historical evolution, technological advancements
and the privacy issues which have arisen. Last but not least, in both mobility and social sections, there
exists a specific subsection which gives a short overview to the two papers that inspired this study.
2.1
Human Mobility
2.1.1
Overview
The nature of human movements has always been an intriguing field of study. The basic laws governing
human motion are of high importance and subsequently the subject of human mobility has been the
central focus of many diverse and interdisciplinary studies as it is not just a single constrained entity,
but it can be classified in three main aspects: spatial, temporal and social [57, 18].
It can be found in epidemic modelling [39, 55, 24, 23, 7]:
Eubank et al. [39] “explore the use of dynamic bipartite graphs to model the physical contact pat-
terns that result from movements of individuals” with outstanding results, as they use them to analyse
smallpox spread and arrive at a conclusion that “outbreaks can be contained by a strategy of targeted
vaccination combined with early detection without resorting to mass vaccination of a population”. Fur-
thermore, Hufnagel et al. [55] “introduce a probabilistic model that describes the worldwide spread
of infectious diseases, which can be used to predict the worldwide spread of future infectious diseases
and to identify endangered regions in advance”. Colliza et al. [24] motivated by the spread of the
severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS), developed a stochastic model by considering data from 220
countries and found out that “the integration of long-range mobility and demographic data provides
epidemic models with a predictive power that can be consistently tested and theoretically motivated”.
In another study, Colliza et al. [23] used the same approach to study the spread of the H5N1 avian
influenza virus with the use of airline travel data and once again concluded that the probability of
global outbreaks is highly connected to the air transportation. Similarly, Balcan et al. [7] created a
model integrating mobility and transportation data which generates stochastic realisations of the H1N1
epidemic evolution and provided estimates for the number of hospitalisations and the attack rate for
the next pandemic wave.
Another prominent field is traffic prediction and urban planning [59, 54]:
Kitamura et al. [59] showed that “individuals’ daily travel patterns can be synthesized in a practical
manner by micro-simulation”. Horner et al. [54] studied endogenous hub locations on a network and
presented results relative to cities which act as centres through which large flow passes; results relevant
to transportation planning and transport geography. Human mobility studies have also been used in
economic forecasting [43] and understanding human migration patterns [102].

2.1.2
Patterns, Predictability and Understanding
In order to understand the nature of human movement, first the concept of movement itself had to
be defined. In physics, the term Brownian motion refers to the random movement of particles [41],
while in mathematics, the stochastic process that describes a path which consists of random steps, is
known as random walk [88]. Since there exist significant patterns in any kind of movement, several
approaches have tried to model and build systems that explain and simulate it. Metzler and Klafter
[72] studied various general diffusion models which have been built in order to explain all those systems
whose statistics is governed by ubiquitous power laws. They argue that “Brownian motion (BM) can
be generalised using the continuous time random walk (CTRW) model to subdiffusion or dispersive
transport (SD), to Lévy flights (LF), or to Lévy walks (LW)” [72, p. 164]. The model which has been
heavily used in all mobility studies and which plays a major role in the field of human mobility, is
the Lévy walk. Developed by the French mathematician Paul Lévy, extends the Brownian motion to
be a more general random walk model [56]. The model has been used to describe the movements of
animals, such as albatrosses [113] and jackals [4], in physics [101] as well as general topics [117]. In the
human mobility field, Brockman et al. [14] studied the movement of bank notes as a proxy for human
movement and concluded that “the distribution of travelling distances decays as a power law, indicating
that trajectories of bank notes are reminiscent of scalefree random walks known as Lévy flights”.
Besides defining and modelling human movement, another interesting approach is that of predicting
it. Song et al. [106] studied data of mobile phone users and found a 93% of potential predictability in user
movements. They highlight the lack of variability in predictability and how that is independent from
the distance travelled. They conclude that “most individuals are well localized in a finite neighborhood,
but a few travel widely.”[106, p. 1021]
Even though the Lévy walk is the most studied and used modelling framework, it is important to
say that human movement is not entirely random and deviates from the model. Song et al. [107, p. 819]
note about visitation frequency that “the probability f of a user to visit a given location is expected
to be asymptotically (t →∞) uniform everywhere (f ≈const.) for both Lévy flights and CTRWs. In
contrast, the visitation patterns of humans is rather uneven, so that the frequency f of the kth most
visited location follows Zipf’s law”. Likewise, González et al. [45] found that contrary to random walks,
an individual has a significant probability to return to a few highly frequented locations and argue that
with agent-based models they [45, p. 781] “can obtain the likelihood of finding a user in any location”.
With this evidence of human movement not being entirely random, there was a need of deeper
understanding of the motives behind an individual’s movements. Why does an individual move in a
specific way and what are the decisions they take in order to do it? Barabási [8] showed that “the bursty
nature of human behaviour is a consequence of a decision-based queuing process” by studying e-mail
activity patterns, highlighting the effect of human intention as a significant factor of what differentiates
human behaviour from a Poisson process. In conjunction to this, Rhee et al. [96] claim that human
movement is not a random Lévy walk and conclude that they are similar, with the most distinct common
pattern between them being the heavy-tail distribution of flight lengths, but not the same. Furthermore,
they argue that this difference is not caused by geographical constraints, but by human intention. In
this process of further understanding human mobility, Karamshuk et al.
[57] deeply explored and
reviewed the most notable literature in the field and presented a thorough analysis of different mobility
models. Moreover, they discussed the knowledge around human movements and further associated it
with the aspects of predictability and patterns.
They reached a conclusion that there is a need of
more realistic models that combine all the existing techniques and a better understanding between the
different statistical properties of human movements.

2.2.1
An introduction to Social Networks
The study of social interactions is not new. Since the start of the 19th century, researchers have tried to
understand the social relationships between individuals and the need for a more precise and specialised
field, apart from pure sociology, presented itself.
Here we will attempt a short but comprehensive
historical overview of the social networks field of study. As the author of this paper is not a graduate
of sociology or a field of human sciences, the knowledge of renowned scientists will be borrowed in the
process of presenting an outline of the field instead of an in-depth analysis.
Wasserman and Faust [115] authored a thorough review of network analysis methods and presented an
overview of the field for social networkers. They highlight the importance of the network part, as they
argue that it provides a leverage for answering standard research questions by giving information on
the underlying structural environment. They introduce social network analysis as a clear and distinct
research perspective inside the social and behavioural sciences, as it is based on the foundation of the
importance of relationships among the interacting units it studies. These relational concepts (which
are defined as links between units) are the most important part of defining a social network analysis
along with a set of four principles [115, p. 4]:
• Actors and their actions are viewed as interdependent rather than independent, autonomous units
• Relational ties (linkages) between actors are channels for transfer or “flow” of resources (either
material or nonmaterial)
• Network models focusing on individuals view the network structural environment as providing
opportunities for or constraints on individual action
• Network models conceptualise structure (social, economic, political, and so forth) as lasting pat-
terns of relations among actors
The aspect of the network perspective is significantly crucial, as it enables researchers to get a more
broad insight while studying phenomena, as it does not focus solely on individual attributes, but in
the associations among these attributes or their usefulness for predicting another attribute. This is a
fundamental difference from the standard social science research methods.
As far as the historical foundations of the field are concerned, Wasserman and Faust set the founder
of the social network concept to Barnes [11], who in 1954 presented his two year fieldwork in Norway;
Scott [98] seeks the roots even earlier, in 1920s, in the work of Radcliffe-Brown on social structures
[90]. The predecessor of the social network analysis, sociometry, can be found in the work of Moreno
[76], who in the early 1930’s invented the sociogram models: a graphic representation of social links
that a person has. A sociogram is a graph drawing that plots the structure of interpersonal relations
in a group situation (figure 2.1). From the 1930s to the 1970s, social anthropologists and sociologists
built on the work of Radcliffe-Brown and his concept of social structure. Especially after the 1950s the
need for more precise definitions and translations on the notions of social network and web arose, and
after the 1970s significant technical work and specialisations appeared, leading to the birth of the key
concepts behind social network analysis [98]. The field continued to grow stable and moderately, where
we find the above works of Wasserman and Faust, Scott and several others such as Degenne and Force

[31] bringing us to the 2000s, where major changes and advancements happened. Watts [116] studies
this change in his work “The ‘New’ Science of Networks”, a term which was defined by him and his
contemporaries, such as Buchanan [15] and Barabasi [9], and refers to “the progress on a number of
previously intractable problems, reformulating old ideas, introducing new techniques, and uncovering
connections between what had seemed to be quite different problems” [116, p. 243]. This progress

Applications and Studies
Social network analysis, in the same manner as human mobility, has been used in several different fields
with notable results.
Social Interactions. Onnela et al. [84] studied data from 3.9 individual users and emphasised
on the analysis of large-scale, one-to-one social interaction networks. As the main focus was social
interactions, they used the properties of call duration and number of calls in order to measure the
strength of a social tie. Finally, they used the concept of overlapping links in order to measure the
connection between link weight and the overlap of the neighbourhood in the vicinity of a tie. They
concluded that for 96% of the links in their network, the strength of the tie and the overlap are correlated.
With this finding they confirmed that weak and strong links have qualitatively different global roles in

social networks and verified the weak tie hypothesis which suggests that more novel information flows
to individuals through weak than through strong ties [49]. Hidalgo et al. [53] studied CDRs from 2
million users in a process of studying the coupling between the structure of a network and the temporal
stability of its links. Their main focus was on studying the persistence of the ties between individuals by
examining the perseverance of ties across time and concluded that persistent links tend to be reciprocal
and are more common for people with low degree and high clustering. In a similar manner, Martin et al.
[70] studied persistence in a broader temporal space by examining factors related to the persistence of
ties after a 12 year period. They present evidence of triadic effects, stating that “people are more likely
to remain in contact with others when they share patterns of contact with third parties.”. However, when
focusing on friendships instead of just contact, they conclude that “triadic effects can be decomposed
into some effects pertaining to hierarchy and other effects pertaining to reciprocation.”, a result which
is in compliance with the reciprocal influence on persistence found by Hidalgo et al..
In order to examine the social interactions from another angle and focus not on the ties themselves,
but how and why these ties are created, Kossinets and Watts [62] studied the homophily principle (the
tendency of individuals to associate and bond with similar others [71]) in a network of 30,000 individuals
in a university community. They suggest that tie formation is heavily biased by triadic closure and focal
closure, which effectively constrain the opportunities among which individuals may select and that the
formation of some particular relationship is in part a consequence of individual preferences and in part
the result of the opportunities available at the time.
Health and Disease Spreading. Similarly to human mobility data, social networks have been
widely used to address health issues as well as study and model disease spreading.
Salathé et al.
[97] created a social network based on data from a high school and used it in order to simulate and
study the spread of an influenza-like disease, and presented that the results were in good agreement
with absentee data during an influenza season.
Madan et al.
[66] used mobility and behavioural
data to help understand the link between behaviours and symptoms at an individual level. On the
social network aspect, features such as total individual communication, late-night and early-morning
communication patterns, as well as communication diversity were used in order to simulate characteristic
behavioural changes associated with respiratory symptoms, fever, influenza, stress, and depression.
They concluded that it is possible to devise a classification scheme that identifies when individuals
are likely to be symptomatic. Furthermore, they explored the role social exposure has in obesity and
suggested the extension to other health related situation such as mood contagion, affecting sleep loss
or job productivity. Obesity has been the focus in other social network studies as well, with Christakis
and Fowler [19] studying a social network of 12,000 people assessed repeatedly from 1971 to 2003. They
concluded that a person’s chances of becoming obese increased by 57% if he or she had a friend who
became obese in a given interval, as well as the effect of family ties in the spread of obesity, with an
increase of 40% in the probability of a sibling becoming obese if the other became obese first. Likewise, if
a spouse became obese, the likelihood for the other increased by 37%. In a shift from physical to mental
health, Fratiglioni et al. [42] studied the influence of social networks on the occurrence of dementia,
and found out that an extensive social network helps to protect against it. People living alone or with
a poor social network have an increased 60% of developing dementia. For a more in-depth analysis, the
reader is referred to the work of Kawachi and Berkman [58], as they review and explore the connection
between social ties and mental health.
Socioeconomics. Granovetter [49] studies the effects of social structures on economic outcomes
and argues that economic models should not exclude non-economic aspects of social life and act as a
black box. He argues that many social phenomena strongly affect costs and available techniques for
economic action. Eagle et al. [35] combined data from a national communication network with national
census data on the socioeconomic well-being of communities and found that social network diversity is
highly correlated with the economic development of a community.
Urban Studies. Krings et al. [63] analysed data from 2.5 million mobile users in Belgium in order to
study inter-city communication and conclude that “inter-city communication intensity is characterized
by a gravity model: the communication intensity between two cities is proportional to the product of
their sizes divided by the square of their distance.”.

2.3
Interplay between Human Mobility & Social Networks: a
combination
When studying human nature, the aspects of mobility and social communication often blend, interact
and influence one another. This is not surprising since the notion of sociality is deeply engraved into
mobility itself, as it was referred in section 2.1.1. As a result, many scientists decided to not split the two
aspects, but rather study them as a whole, in a more holistic approach. The existence of mobile phones
and the advancement in the field of data collection, gave the opportunity to study rich datasets that
consisted of both CDR data, as well as proximity data. What was left, was to ask the right questions
and uncover significant patterns.
Eagle et al. [38] highlight the advantages of collecting not only communication information but
also location and proximity data. In their Reality Mining study, they gathered data for 94 individuals,
containing call logs, Bluetooth devices in proximity of approximately five meters, cell tower IDs, appli-
cation usage, and phone status. With a combination of this data, they concluded that it is possible to
accurately infer 95% of friendships, focusing on friend dyads which demonstrate distinctive temporal
and spatial patterns in their physical proximity along with calling patterns. Wang et al. [114] focus on
the same idea. They argue that most of the research has focused on the social space only, leaving an
important question unanswered: how mobility patterns impact and shape the social network. In order
to address that, they tracked the trajectories and communication records of 6 million mobile phone users
and showed that the similarity between two individuals’ movements correlates with their proximity in
the network. In addition, they explored the predictive power of using mobility data to predict the
creation of new links by using a supervised classifier. Cranshaw et al. [28] examined traces of 489 users
of a location sharing network and presented a model for predicting friendship between two users by
analysing their location trails. Furthermore, they showed a positive relationship between the entropy
of the locations the user visits and the number of social ties that user has in the network. Backstrom
et al. [6] used Facebook data in order to asses the relationship between geography and friendship, and
they developed an algorithm capable of predicting an individual’s location. Last but not least, Cho et
al. [18] used mobile phone and social network data and showed that short-ranged travel is less impacted
by the social network structure, while long distance travel is more likely to happen near an existing
friend. They conclude that the influence of friendship on one’s mobility is two times stronger than the
influence of mobility on creating new friendships.
At this point, we need to note how this thesis fits into the above mentioned research as well as what
differentiates its approach. As presented in this section, the researchers who combined proximity with
communication data, did it in order to decipher the interplay between the two and its impact on the
social network. However, the focus is mainly on the overview of the whole - on a community/society
level.
Friendships, face-to-face meetings, social ties based on distance etc.
are the object of these
studies. In this study, we aim to look at the same interplay but from a different perspective: that of
the individual. We use the combination of proximity and communication data in order to discover
patterns not between persons, but between the individual itself. Does the way a person commute tell
us about the same person’s way of communication?

For the social scientists, the only available source of data for years was self-report data, often on

Location Resolution. The first thing that had to be done was to group some location points

Visitation frequency acts as a weight on every location.
This can be
defined in two ways: either as how much time the individuals has spent in each location, or how many
times they have visited it. In this study, we decide to use the latter as each location already contains
a notion of time as the sampling frequency was set in 15 minutes. We regard the 15 minute interval as
adequate enough so that each location record is considered a visit (the individual visited it and not just

Centre of mass and geographical distance. In our case the centre of mass is the geographical
centre or geographic midpoint between all of the individual’s locations.
There are several ways to
calculate this found in literature with the two most common being: the average coordinate for a set
of points on a spherical earth and the mathematical average of the latitudes and longitudes of a set of
points. The first method is used when locations contain weights (i.e. one location is more important
than another one) and contains more complex calculations in order to take into account the spherical
shape of the earth as the longitude and latitude have to be converted in Cartesian coordinates and then
multiplied by their weights before being converted again back to latitude and longitude. As in this
study the individuals’ locations are weighted the same, we use the second method and thus rcm is the
average between all of the locations in L.
The next thing we have to define is the ri −rcm entity. As both ri and rcm refer to geographic
locations, their subtraction is a distance and can not be calculated as if they were mathematical values
since the earth is spherical. In order to calculate geodetic distance there are two ways: the Vincenty and
the Haversine formulas. Between the two, the Haversine formula is more computational efficient and can
run faster, but the Vincenty formula is more accurate [67, 112]. Here, we decided to use the Vincenty
formula as the magnitude of data we handle allows us to do so without being significantly affected by
the slower computation. However, the reader should be advised to use the Harvesine formula if their
data size is of high magnitude. Moreover, the distances calculated here refer to points between the
same country, and mainly in the same city, so the two formulas would have given the same approximate
result.

4.4.3
Radius of Gyration: Saturation Hypothesis
In order to filter the data we were working on, a specifc hypothesis has to be validated:
After a certain amount of location points, rg starts to saturate.
This was an empirical hypothesis: when more data points start to be added to equation 4.1 rg keeps
increasing until it reaches a certain point where it stabilises or starts to decrease. We call this the
saturation point. There is a significant meaning to this point, as it provides a filtering tool. Individuals
with less amount of recorded locations than the saturation point can, and have to, be discarded as we
can safely assume that they do not have enough location points for their rg calculations to be considered
satisfactory. Here, we present the process of testing and validating this hypothesis.
We initially split one month of data in 4 weeks, chose users with sufficient number of measurements
and plotted the rg for cumulative increasing measurements (1 week, first and second week, first and
second and third etc.) We observed that the rg for some users remained stable but for others it was
increasing as expected. Thus, we repeated the same process with 2 months of data and split them in
total of 8 weeks. As expected, a knee-point appeared at the 7th/8th week were rg seemed to stabilise
or started to decrease. In order to validate this further, we repeated the process with 3 months of data
and with 12 weekly cumulative sets. After this was plotted, it was evident that there was a visible
knee-point at the 7th week as well as for the 9th week for a few individuals (figure 4.4 : A). Many
individuals with a small rg value portrayed a very linear/stable rg behaviour. We isolated and zoomed
in on those and we observed that their radius of gyration also saturates around the 7th week (figure 4.4
: B and C).
For the last part, we had to translate what 7 weeks mean in terms of readings/measurements, so we
plotted the count for every increasing week for our high measurement users. As it can be seen in figure
4.4 : D, 7 weeks translate to to ≈5000 points. However, since for some users, their rg saturates later
around 8 and 9 weeks, we choose a cut-off of 6000 points for our filter. As a result, any participant
with less than 6000 recorded location points, will not be considered in this study.

8.1
Social Interactions: Activating & Deactivating Social Ties
In order to understand the social behaviour of the population, one has to understand the evolution
of social relationships. To do that, the understanding of what mechanisms control the dynamics of
activating or deactivating social ties is of paramount importance to make progress in the characteri-
sation of human behaviour [73]. Since human communication is bursty [8], the method usually used
in communication network studies and which assumes that a tie is present if it shows any activity in
the observation window, is prone to errors. Miritello et al. notes that large inter-event times between
interactions are likely and thus they might be unobserved or mistaken as tie decay or formation, espe-
cially if the observation window is short. As a result, they propose of a slightly different method for
investigating tie activation and deactivation.
To assess whether a tie has been activated or deactivated in the observation window Ω, this method
focuses on the observation of tie activity in time windows before and after Ω: if tie activity is observed
in the window before Ωthen it is considered an old tie (cases (a) and (d) in figure 8.1) on the other
hand, if activity is observed in the window after Ωwe assume that the tie persists (cases (b) and (d) in
figure 8.1). In any other case, we consider that the tie is activated and/or deactivated in Ω(cases (a),
(b) and (c) in figure 8.1). Miritello et al. defined their windows in the following order:
• Pre Window: 6 months
• Observation Window Ω: 7 months
• Post Window: 6 months
In our study, as we work with the data of one calendric year (2014), we define the following windows:
• Pre Window: 3 months (January to March)
• Observation Window Ω: 6 months (April to September)

(spring semester and summer vacations). The definition of the observation windows can be seen in

investigate the time between tie communication events δtij. For each individual we calculate the time

This validates that the number of

finding, Miritello et al. suggest that it resembles the preferential attachment: the process by which tie

9.2.1
Principal Component Analysis
We split our values in two vectors: one vector for nα,i and one for κi. We then normalise them, by
subtracting their mean out of them. Then we create a centered matrix X with the two vectors as
columns. Then, we take its transpose XT and create their covariance matrix. By using this matrix, we
find the corresponding eigenvectors and eigenvalues. As we have 2 dimensions, there are 2 orthogonal
eigenvectors, the principal one and the secondary [105, 100]. Specifically, we found out the following
two eigenvectors:
• First eigenvector [-0.44 , -0.898] with an eigenvalue of ≈177.89
• Second eigenvector [-0.898, 0.44] with an eigenvalue of ≈15.32

9.2.2
The Nature of β & a Comparison Between Studies
The quantity β represents the linear correlation between communication activity and communication
capacity of an individual. A β = 0.75 in the network of Miritello et al., means that as the capacity grows,
the activity grows proportionally as well, but slowly (0.75 < 1). In order to increase their social circle,
they need to activate many new ties. In our network, a β = 1.11 means that as the capacity grows, the
activity is increased by a little over the same amount. Or conversely, in order to increase their social
circle, the participants in the SensibleDTU project need to activate less new ties. The difference in our
data that lead to a higher slope is mainly explained by two things: The lack of variance in our data and
the high activity and capacity of our participants. The low variance means that the individuals in the
SensibleDTU project are similar in the way they build their social network. As students, they activate
and deactivate ties in a high rate (higher than in the dataset of Miritello et al.) and increase their social
circle fast. Considering that ≈60% of the participants were 1st year students [3, p. 20] this makes
sense, as when students start their higher education for the first time, they are known for meeting new
people, activating new ties and making connection and, in the same manner, deactivate ties fast as
they stop communicating with people they just met. The natural characteristic of our dataset, that it
is composed of students, encloses a higher similarity in communication activity than the one obtained
from a more random population such as the one obtained from mobile company CDR records.
9.3
Social strategies: Social Returners & Social Explorers
Now that we have identified the quantity β, which defines the characteristic behaviour of the commu-
nication activity as a function of the communication capacity for the users in our network, we can
explore the different communication strategies of the individuals. As we showed at figure 9.9, there
are many different combinations of communication capacity and activity, displaying different strategies.
We follow the concept of Miritello et al. and encode that disparity by defining the ratio γi = nα,i/κi,
as social strategy and which gives information about the balance between the communication capacity
and the communication activity for an individual. As we showed in section 7.2 there is no dichotomy of
the population and in section 7.3 we found that there are two small groups of individuals with low and
high entropy. In the same manner, the social strategy γi encodes this disparity of the population and is
a tool for uncovering these groups of individuals where their social communication behaviour deviates
from that of the majority of the population. Thus, for γi ≃β, individuals have a normal social strategy:
their communication capacity and activity are well balanced. We consider as social explorers, the
individuals whose communication activity is significantly larger than their communication capacity and
thus they activate/deactivate social ties in high numbers compared to their social circle and compared
to the normal majority of the population. Thus, we define social explorers, as the individuals whose
γi ≫β. Similarly, we consider as social returners, the individuals whose communication activity
is considerably lower than their communication capacity and who activate/deactivate a small number
of connections compared to their capacity. Thus, we define social returners, as the individuals whose
γi ≪β. Miritello et al. refers to these two strategies as social keeping (γi ≪β), meaning that these
individuals keep a very stable social circle, and social exploring (γi ≫β), meaning that these individuals
activate new ties and deactivate existing ones at a high pace. We suggest that these two different social
strategies are a manifestation of the exploration versus exploitation dilemma in social interactions.
We plot the probability distribution of γi and then we calculate the bottom and upper 25% per-
centiles of our population, which correspond to γi = 0.71 and γi = 1.5 respectively. We then use these
quantities in order to define our two classes: users with γi < 0.71 are labelled as social returners,
users with γi > 1.5 are labelled as social explorers. A schematic representation of this process can be
seen in figure 9.10.
We have successfully managed to identify the two specific classed of individuals we were looking for
and define what characteristics of social communication patterns describe social returners and social
explorers. In chapter 11 we attempt to find a correlation between these two classes and their spatial
counterparts.

Social Network Studies
In this chapter we take a short deviation from our central research on the interplay between human
mobility and social networks, in order to study social networks deeper. At this chapter we focus on the
participants of the SensibleDTU project and their interactions, isolated from the outer world. Since
we are interested in studying the interactions in the community itself, we create social networks where
the nodes are the individuals that participated in the study. The links between the nodes, indicate
a communication event between the two. We create two types of networks: a multi-directional graph
where two nodes can have as many number of directed edges between them, and a directed graph, where
nodes are connected by a single edge in each direction, irregardless of the volume of communication
events. The decision for creating two different networks lie in the nature of their types. Each kind
of network can help us study different characteristics of the underlying behaviour. The multigraph,
emphasises the volume of communication between two nodes (i.e. how much two individuals talk),
while the simple digraph emphasises on the quantity of unique connections of the nodes (i.e. how
many people a person is connected to). Furthermore, we create this pair of networks both for the phone
call and the sms datasets. At this point, we have to highlight the inserted bias in this approach. These
networks does not give us the whole picture of an individual’s communication patterns in general. They
indicate only how students participants of the project communicate with each other.

CHAPTER 12
Overall Discussion & Future
Work
After what seemed like a long trip in the world of human mobility and social communication patterns,
this study comes to an end. In the sense that the core of this research study was to reproduce the
findings of two well-renowned papers, it is considered successful.
12.1
Discussion
The finding of two distinct mobility classes in our dataset is very important as it revalidates the findings
of Pappalado et al. [85] in a different dataset consisting of a much smaller and characteristically different
population. In conjunction with our findings in the stability studies, we extend the current knowledge by
suggesting that this dichotomy is apparent regardless of the time period of the observation, the season or
the size of the population. Individuals in our study presented this behaviour in all scales and resolutions
we investigated. This is a fact which further research can acquire information from, as findings regarding
human mobility patterns can be generalised in many different areas of discipline. Knowing that the
population can be split in two distinct classes and having clear mathematical tools to identify them
using their radius of gyration, opens the opportunity for the creation of better diffusion models, epidemic
modelling or social behaviour studies. From a social point of view, the fact that humans move in such
clearly defined patterns shows the effect of the modern way of life in our commuting patterns and how
the home-work pair defines the majority of a social returner’s mobility. In addition, the fact that the
other class of individuals exhibits directly opposite behaviour, poses the question of whether human
beings, when considering their mobility patterns, are so simply and tightly defined in two sets.
After finding that human mobility is so well defined by these two distinct classes, our expectation was
that the same behaviour would be found in human interactions and communication as well. However,
our results proved us wrong, as there is no clear dichotomy in social communication. Not only are
humans not so well defined in their communication patterns, but they also exhibit many different
communication strategies, even when having the same social connectivity, revalidating the results of
Miritello et al. [73]. Nevertheless, we managed to uncover the same two classes, although they do not
split the population in two this time. They are outside of the normal majority and in this study were
considered to be the top and bottom 25% of the social strategy distribution. These two classes exhibit
either stable or exploratory strategies. These classes in the same manner as their spatial counterparts,
can be of use for navigation, influence and information diffusion studies as well as provide evidence for
the divergence between the static and dynamic characterisations of human interaction.
Our last part of the study, and the one that set out to draw more novel conclusions between the
two aspects of human life, mobility and communication, was also the one that produced unexpected
results. Expectations for finding a correlation between the spatial and social exploratory and stable
classes were high. However, the results clearly showed that such a correlation does not exist - at least
it was not able to be uncovered with the methods of this study. Although this was a negative finding,
it is still an important one. First of all, it shows that even though humans exhibit easily identifiable
and predictable routines and patterns in the aspects of their lives, they are not so easily defined when
trying to combine these aspects together. From a social perspective, we can say that humans are not

black & white. The fact that an individual travels a lot does not necessarily mean that they also have a
big social circle or that they spend very long time communicating. Even though our results showed no
correlation, we still believe that this study sets the foundation for further research to draw upon and
examine the correlation between individual communication and mobility in a more combined approach.
12.2
Future Work
As the time for delivering a study in the form of a thesis is limited, not all ideas and aspiration for
research were formulated. Here we state some of the plans for future work that could directly continue
on top of this study.
One major idea is to expand from the individual’s perspective, this study had, to their close social
circle. We are interested in finding whether a spatial explorer’s (or returner’s) friends are also explorers
(returners). Likewise, since the social network studies showed that our networks are assortative with
the node’s degree, we want to validate whether a social explorer’s best friends and more contacted
contacts are also social explorers. This can bring interesting perspectives regarding homophily and
social behaviours.
A second plan that was not implemented, was to look further in the modularity and communities
of the social networks. Our graphs showed well defined communities though at this point they serve
just visual purposes. Our hypothesis is that these communities are heavily influenced and formed by
the students’ chosen fields of study. As they spend most of the time together with students of the same
disciple, we hypothesise that the majority of their communication would be directed towards contacts
that they met through their studies. As community detection is a research area of its own and there
can be many more influences to community forming, as well as overlapping communities for the same
individuals, the time needed for performing this research fell out of the current study.
Last but not least, the application of the same ideas and methods on other datasets is of high interest.
Especially considering the social interactions part, we are interested in reproducing these findings in
other forms of communication such as Facebook or instant messaging applications. These are the forms
of communication of this (and probably the next) generation and as sms text messages and phone calls
tend to be slowly driven out of our lives, longitudinal studies in other forms of more modern data
sources are needed in order to explain the continuously evolving form of human communication.

CHAPTER 13
Conclusion
In this final section we summarise and present the most important findings of this study.
By using the concept of radius of gyration, we managed to reproduce the findings of Pappalardo et
al. [85] and show that there is a clear dichotomy in the population regarding their mobility patterns.
k-returners are individuals whose characteristic mobility can be described by their k most frequented
locations, while k-explorers are individuals for whom, the k most frequented locations are not sufficient
for describing their total commuting behaviour. Moreover, we found that as k increases, all explorers
slowly transition to returners, with k = 10 locations being enough to account for the biggest part of the
total travelled distance for the majority of the population. It is important to note that the size of our
populations was smaller than the one of Pappalardo et al., indicating that the dichotomy can be seen
regardless of population size and we contributed to general knowledge as by splitting a calendric year
in 4 time periods, we proved that the dichotomy is also present in different time periods. Moreover,
we also expanded the work of Pappalardo et al. on the individual’s persepctive, as we showed that the
classification of an individual is not permanent but can change through time. As far as the SensibleDTU
population is concerned, we find that they present a more exploratory nature and they reach a balance
between k-returners and k explorers for k = 8.
Last but not least, we showed that returners and
explorers are characterised by a different spatial distribution of the visited locations. As the radius of
gyration increases, the most frequented locations transition from being close to each other to moving
further apart for returners, while for explorers they remain close and other clusters of locations emerge
away from them.
While studying social networks we repeatedly observed behaviours indicating that phone calls define
a more personal form of communication while text messages tend to be used for high volume communi-
cation to a broader set of contacts. We found that there is no clear dichotomy in the population similar
to mobility and showed that four contacts are enough to explain most of the individual’s communication
time. We identified that the sms network contains higher entropy than the call network, indicating that
it is significantly harder to predict who an individual exchanges text messages with, but also found a
small percentage of individuals holding low and high entropy values, indicating stable and exploratory
behaviour. By reproducing the work of Miritello et al. [73], we defined an observation period Ω= 6
months and examined the activation and deactivation of communication ties. The individuals of our
study showcase an average time between communication events of ≈5 days and can handle ≈20 ties
on average. We found that despite ties being activated/deactivated continually, the communication
capacity for each individual remains almost constant indicating that individuals tend to balance the
activation/deactivation of ties in such a way that the number of active relationships remains stable
over time. In addition, the individuals of this study exhibit a social persistence of 70%, indicating that
although in a given time period users activate and deactivate many connections after a period of 6
months they maintain on average the 70% of their initial social network. The most important finding
was that the communication activity presents a linear correlation with an individual’s communication
capacity and with the method of PCA we found that for our study it is nα,i = 1.11ki, a value higher
than that of Miritello et al., indicating our population was characterised by higher activity as well as
lack of variance. Finally, by encoding disparity in the social strategy ratio, we were able to successfully
quantify what defines a social explorer and social returner.
By studying the call and sms social networks of the population we find that individuals send much
more text messages in quantity than they make phone calls, but they deliver these messages to roughly
double the amount of contacts indicating that students tend to use text messages to communicate much
==PAGE:78==
68
13 Conclusion
more than they prefer to make phone calls. By examining the degree distributions of the networks we
found that they follow a power-law and that, in the sms network, higher total degrees are a result of
reciprocal, two way communication.
Last but not least, we found that there is no correlation (Pearson coefficient ≈0.1) between an indi-
vidual’s mobility patterns and communication behaviour and thus a spatial explorer is not necessarily
a social explorer as well. There might be a slight tendency of social explorers to also be spatial, but the
results are not robust and further research is needed for more conclusive findings.
To sum up, by examining a rich and novel dataset which consists of both proximity and social com-
munication data, allowed us to explore a person’s overall behaviour and examine the relations between
mobility and communication patterns. We state that the exploration versus exploitation paradigm man-
ifests itself clearly in the human mobility aspect of human lives, but also appears between the edges
of the out-of-normal communication spectrum. Identifying the characteristic classes of individuals who
are the main part of the interplay of exploration and exploitation in the form of returners and explorers
is of high importance. These significant behaviour patterns can be valuable resources for social studies
and transportation and urban research, as well as help in the implementation of better models for
information diffusion and epidemic simulation. There is also need of further research in the topic of
combining human mobility with social communication studies, especially with modern, high volume
and precision data, in order to draw more sufficient conclusions regarding the influence and correlation
of human mobility with social interactions.
