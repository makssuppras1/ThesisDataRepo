Abstract
As the world collectively grapples with climate change and the ever-increasing demand for
electricity, the need for large-scale renewable energy sources and the adoption of green fuels via
Power-to-X technologies is becoming more urgent. In line with Denmark’s ambition to reduce
carbon emissions and achieve climate neutrality by the year 2050, the government has decided
to construct the world’s two first Energy Islands (or Offshore Energy Hubs), one on the island of
Bornholm and another artificial island in the North Sea. These offshore energy hubs represent
an extreme version of energy systems with high levels of renewable energy sources, forming a
100 % renewable energy source based system.
Balancing active power in such systems can prove to be particularly challenging. The lack of
system inertia coupled with the unpredictability of offshore wind power means that conventional
methods of active power balancing cannot be applied. This challenge is amplified in an AC
interconnected hub, where multiple components are connected to the same system, and the
reliance on a single component for balancing can lead to instability and the potential shutdowns
of the entire system. One promising solution being considered for the offshore energy hubs is
integrating hydrogen production using electrolyzers, that is Power-to-X.
This thesis explores the feasibility of integrating an Alkaline electrolyzer into the interconnected
AC offshore energy hub to coordinate active power control amongst all units of the system. The
simulation tool PowerFactory RMS was used to build a model of the 4 GW interconnected AC
offshore hub with an electrolyzer, implementing a coordinated control scheme. The system is
then tested under two main fault scenarios. In the first scenario, a fault causes one of the
offshore wind power plants to be disconnected, resulting in the loss of scheduled generated
power causing the system to experience underfrequency. In the second scenario, a pole fault
occurs, leading to an overfrequency situation where wind power exceeds transmission capacity.
Both scenarios are tested with various amounts of support from the electrolyzer to evaluate it’s
performance. The primary objective is to assess whether the offshore energy hub can main-
tain self-reliance during a fault and avoid dependence on the onshore system for compensation,
while minimizing wind curtailment. The findings aim to determine the effectiveness of elec-
trolyzers in enhancing the resilience and stability of offshore energy hubs, ensuring they can
sustain continuous and eﬀicient energy supply despite fluctuations in power generation and
consumption.

To support this transition with the necessary energy, the Danish government announced plans
to build the world’s first two energy islands (or offshore energy hubs). One will be on the
island of Bornholm with a planned capacity of 3 GW, and the other will be an artificial island

In May 2022, Denmark, alongside the Netherlands, Germany, and Belgium, further advanced
this initiative through the Esbjerg Declaration [5]. This agreement marks a new era of energy
collaboration, aiming to transform the North Sea into a green power plant for Europe. The
green power plant will consist of multiple connected offshore energy hubs with ambitious targets
for increasing wind energy capacity to 65 GW by 2030 and 150 GW by 2050. Additionally, a
target of 20 GW of electrolysis capacity by 2030.
1.2
Motivation
Active power control is essential for the smooth operation of power systems, as it maintains
the balance between generation and demand. Traditional AC power systems are dominated by
synchronous generators that rely on system inertia to maintain and control of the power system.
These generators are rigidly synchronized and can counteract any fluctuations or imbalances in
the grid by adjusting its speed, thereby stabilizing the system’s frequency [6].
However, the transition to large-scale renewable energy systems, such as the offshore energy
hubs (OEHs), introduces significant challenges to system stability. Unlike conventional power
sources, renewable energy sources (RES) like wind power cannot be reliably predicted or con-
trolled, leading to increased complexities in maintaining energy balance in steady-state condi-
tions. These RES typically lack inertia, making it more diﬀicult to stabilize frequency fluctua-
tions in the grid [7].
Additionally, the inherent complexity of these offshore hubs, with their multiple high voltage
direct current (HVDC) converters, wind turbine converters, electrolyzers, and other intercon-
nected components, introduces various control interactions. If not properly managed, these
interactions can cause instability and potentially result in system-wide shutdowns [8].

1.3
Project objectives
The aim of this project is to address the challenges associated with active power control in
renewable energy systems by investigating the feasibility of integrating hydrogen electrolyzers
into an interconnected AC offshore energy hub to maintain system stability and control. Specif-
ically, it seeks to determine how electrolyzers can be employed to coordinate balance among
the units within the offshore hub, thereby enhancing the reliability and stability of system
operation.
This involves developing a model of a 4 GW AC interconnected offshore energy hub with
hydrogen production and implementing a coordinated active power balancing control scheme
with a emphasis on electrolyzers. To achieve this, the simulation tool PowerFactory RMS will
be used. As the primary focus of this project is the development of the control strategy that
enhances stability and reliability, the models used for both OEH and electrolyzers will be kept
generic.
In this project, the control method of the OEHs will be optimized through the integration of
electrolyzers, with the aim of developing a coordinated control scheme for electrolyzers and the
HVDC components to enhance the capabilities for active power control.
1.4
Scope and limitations
This thesis is defined by the following simplifications:
• This project focuses solely on the offshore part of the offshore energy hubs and does not
simulate onshore systems, generators, or loads.
• The electrolyzer model used in this project will focus on the electrical characteristics,
assuming constant temperature and excluding hydrogen production calculations.
• The system will be simplified by using only one grid forming converter.
• The wind power plants are assumed to exhibit identical behaviours and the wind power
output is assumed constant during simulations.

Offshore energy hubs (OEH)
Denmark, along with the Netherlands, Germany, and Belgium, has committed to increasing the
offshore wind capacity of the North Sea to 65 GW by the year 2030 and 150 GW by 2050. The
current near-shore sites for wind power are not suﬀicient for meeting these goals. Therefore,
new large-scale offshore wind farms will need to be constructed further off the coast, where the
wind conditions are more robust and capable of generating higher power outputs [9].
Given the distance of these wind farms from the shore, traditional high-voltage AC transmission
is no longer viable. Instead, high voltage direct current (HVDC) systems will be used, requiring
large-scale converters to be installed near of the production sites [9, 10]. That is where offshore
energy hubs (OEHs) come into play.

Offshore energy hubs are offshore platforms designed to optimize the collection and distribution
of wind energy from offshore wind farms. These hubs will revolutionize the process of trans-
mitting offshore wind power by linking multiple wind farms to a single point. By centralizing
energy production and distribution, OEHs aim to improve the eﬀiciency and effectiveness of
integrating offshore energy [7, 11].
Although the concept of OEHs bring numerous benefits, their construction also presents sub-
stantial technical and logistical challenges due to their unprecedented scale and complexity.
One major challenge in OEHs is maintaining system stability, especially due to the lack of
inertia in a 100% RES-based system [9].
To address these concerns, the integration of hydrogen production using electrolyzer has been
suggested.
The consortium of the North Sea Wind Power Hub (NSWPH) programme has
demonstrated that the integrating of electrolyzers can be beneficial due to their ability to
convert excess wind energy into hydrogen.
This capability could allow the OEH to avoid
curtailing wind energy, thereby enhancing the overall stability hubs. This process not only
provides a flexible way to balance supply and demand while delivering valuable green hydrogen
to demand centers without requiring significant additional onshore electricity infrastructure
[11].
Given these challenges, effective active power control strategies are essential for a eﬀicient and
safe operation of OEHs.
2.2
HVDC technologies
HVDC converters are a major component of offshore energy hubs, ensuring eﬀicient transmission
of electricity over long distances. Unlike the traditional AC system, HVDC systems convert
AC to DC for transmission and invert it back to AC for distribution.
2.2.1
VSC and MMC technologies in HVDC systems
There are two primary types of HVDC converters: line-commutated converters (LCCs) and
voltage source converters (VSCs). Unlike their predecessors, the LCCs, VSCs use insulated
gate bipolar transistors (IGBTs), enabling high-frequency pulse-width modulation (PWM).
This technology allows the VSC to precisely control active and reactive power seperately and
to quickly reverse power flow without needing to change DC voltage polarity [12].
Over the past decade, modular multilevel converters (MMC), have become the favoured type of
VSC [13]. MMCs consist of multiple submodules (SMs) connected in series, providing increased
scalability and higher fault tolerance due to greater redundancy. Additionally, MMCs operate
at a higher eﬀiciency and produce cleaner AC outputs [12, 14]. This makes MMCs well suited
for large-scale renewable energy applications, such as the offshore energy hub.

Half-bridge and full-bridge MMC-VSC
The MMC can be classified into half-bridge (HB) or full-bridge (FB) submodules. Most installed
MMC-HVDC today use HB submodules [13]. The HB submodule consists of two IGBTs, two
diodes and a capacitor. It offers two possible voltage outputs, 0 and +Vc [15].
Figure 2.2: Modular multilevel converter (MMC) topology with Full-bridge submodule [15]
The FB submodule, illustrated in Figure 2.2, includes four IGBTs, four diodes, and a capacitor,
enabling three possible voltage outputs, 0, +Vc, and −Vc. Here, Vc represents the voltage across
the submodule capacitor [15]. This ability to produce three distinct voltage states improves
system redundancy, as FB submodules can block or mitigate DC faults more effectively than
HB submodules [12]. This makes FB-MMCs better suited for the offshore energy hub.
2.2.2
HVDC configurations
There are three main configurations of HVDC systems, symmetrical monopole, rigid bipole and
bipole with a dedicated metallic return cable.
Symmetrical monopole
The symmetrical monopole configuration, illustrated in Figure 2.3, features of two HVDC
converters, one located offshore and the other onshore. These converters are connected by two
HVDC cables operating at the same voltage but with opposite polarity [16]. The configuration
is known for its low capital expenditure (CAPEX) and has been widely adopted for offshore
systems with capacities up to 900 MW, particularly by TenneT in Germany [17].

Additionally, in the event of a pole-to-ground, the voltage of the remaining healthy pole would

Bipole configuration
The rigid bipole configuration, consists of two HVDC converters offshore and two onshore. The
key advantage of this configuration is that, in the event of a converter pole fault, the system
can be reconfigured to function as a asymmetrical monopole by utilizing the cable of the faulty
converter pole as a metallic return. This adjustment allows the system to maintain up to half of
its transmission capacity. However, the prolonged use of ground current operation is prohibited
in the North Sea [17], making the rigid bipole configuration unsuitable for the OEH system.
Consequently, the bipole configuration with dedicated metallic return, seen in Figure 2.4,
emerges as the preferable option for the OEH. Although this configuration requires a higher
CAPEX, it enhances system stability and availability.
Notably, it can withstand faults in
the both converter pole and the HVDC cable without needing to disconnect from the system,
thereby preserving half of its transmission capacity [17].

2.3
Converter control methods
In the offshore energy hub, the offshore wind power plants rely on the VSC-HVDC converters to
connect to the power grid. These converters can be controlled in two main ways, grid following
or grid forming.
2.3.1
Grid forming converters
Grid forming (GFM) converters are essential for weaker grids, such as the OEH, as they provide
voltage and frequency support, as well as virtual inertia and black start capability. They are
designed to create their own voltage and frequency, similar to how traditional synchronous
generators operate.
This ability allows them to establish a reliable reference point for the
power system, ensuring that the power generated offshore can be eﬀiciently transmitted to
shore [18].
Figure 2.5: Control scheme for GFM converter [19].
GFM converters are often operated in islanded mode, as is illustrated in Figure 2.5. In this
mode, GFM converters must automatically establish and stabilize the system’s frequency and
voltage. This is achieved using by using droop control, frequency droop control manages the
active power output by adjusting the frequency, while AC voltage droop control manages the
reactive power output by adjusting the voltage. This allows the converters to share load changes
eﬀiciently [20].
After adjusting the frequency and voltage, the refrence signals are transformed from the dq
coordinate system to the abc coordinate system. That signal is then sent to the modulation
technique, such as PWM, to control the power electronic switches.

2.3.2
Grid following converters
Grid-following (GFL) converters are designed to operate in alongside the existing grid. They
rely on the grid’s voltage and frequency as a reference to inject active and reactive power,
thus they cannot operate in islanded mode. GFL converters synchronize to the grid using a
phase-locked loop (PLL) to determine the phase angle (θ). The measured phase angle is then
used to for current control to determine the desired active and reactive power output [20].
Figure 2.6: Control scheme for GFL converter [19].
As illustrated in Figure 2.7, the control of the direct axis reference current (id,ref) can be used
to manage either the the active power or DC voltage. Similarly, the quadrature axis reference
current (iq,ref) is used to control either AC voltage or the reactive power at the PCC. This
allows the GFL converter to inject the desired amount of active and reactive power into the
grid.
GFL converters can also implement droop control to regulate their power output in
response to changes in the systems frequency and voltage. [19].
The reference currents are then fed into a current limiter to ensure they do not exceed the
maximum current. These signals are subsequently processed by the inner current controller,
which converts them into reference voltages (vd,ref and vq,ref). Finally, these reference voltages
are transformed back into the abc frame and sent to the modulation stage, similarly to the
GFM converters.

2.3.3
Active power control of OEHs
In a report from NSWPH consortium [21], it was demonstrated that active power balancing of
the OEH can be achieved using a frequency-droop based control, where all converters operate
in grid-forming mode.
This approach diverges from the conventional V/f control, which is
typically applied in offshore wind farms via HVDC transmission system. The frequency droop
method allows for power sharing among HVDC systems, enabling converters to adjust their
active power output to balance power mismatches and maintain stability in the hub. This
method will be implemented.
Figure 2.7: Frequency droop.
The offshore system has the capability to self-balance without relying on the onshore system
to a certain extent. This can be achieved through methods of downregulating wind power in
instances of excess power and upregulating when operating below maximum capacity. The
second report [8] highlights how the AC hub can increase wind availability by redistributing
power in cases where the one of the HVDC converters is unavailable. However, the wind energy
production might need to be curtailed to prevent overloading and potential damage to the
remaining converters during periods of high wind speeds. By integrating electrolyzers they
could enhance the systems reliability and self-suﬀiciency.

Electrolyzers
Electrolyzers are devices that utilize electricity to produce hydrogen through a process called
electrolysis. This process involves an electrolysis cell, which consists of three main components:
an anode, a cathode, and an electrolyte. The anode and cathode facilitate the flow of electrons
that drive the chemical reaction, while the electrolyte provides a medium for ion exchange.
When a suﬀicient amount of current passes through the electrolysis cell, water is dissociated
into hydrogen and oxygen [22]. The electrolysis process can be expressed as:

2.4.1
Hydrogen production and potential
Hydrogen, referred to as the ”energy carrier of the future”, offers a promising solution to these
challenges due to its ability to store intermittent RES, such as wind and solar power [23]. This
is especially promising for systems like offshore energy hubs, where electrolyzers can be used to
convert surplus wind power into hydrogen, thereby enhancing energy management and stability.
As of the end of 2021, 96% of global hydrogen production came from fossil fuels, with only
4% produced via electrolysis. Given that only 33% of global electricity was from renewable
sources, just about 1% of hydrogen production was from renewable energy [24]. However, this
percentage is expected to rise significantly with the European Commission’s target of 20 GW
electrolysis capacity by the year 2030 [11].
2.4.2
Role of electrolyzers in power system stability
Electrolyzers are increasingly recognized for their ability to provide ancillary services in sys-
tems with significant amounts of renewable energy. Several studies have explored electrolyzers’
capabilities to enhance system stability through various control strategies.

Recent research [25] has shown that both alkaline (ALK) and proton exchange membrane
(PEM) electrolyzers can act as responsive loads, adjusting their power consumption to reduce
frequency deviations and support grid stability. This was done using virtual synchronous ma-
chine (VSM) control approach, which enables the electrolyzer to react rapidly in the event of
a fault. It was also shown that they can provide secondary frequency response. enables elec-
trolyzers to provide virtual inertia, allowing them to perform comparably to large-scale battery
energy-storage systems.
Another study [26] has demonstrated that PEM electrolyzers could operate in grid-forming
mode, providing voltage and frequency regulation similar to traditional synchronous machines
and battery storage systems. This operation is beneficial in low-inertia system, enhancing grid
robustness and reliability.
However, it is important to note that these studies have primarily been conducted on onshore
systems with a 50% renewable energy scenario, rather than fully inverter-based 100% RES
system.

Proton exchange membrane electrolyzers
PEM electrolyzers have shown good potential in assisting power systems with frequency de-
viations.
They are gaining market traction due to their compact and flexible design, wide
operating range and a rapid response time, making them suitable for grid balancing [27, 25,
26].
Alkaline electrolyzers
ALK electrolyzers have been used extensively in the chemicals industry. They have the ad-
vantage of being a more established and less expensive technology compared to the PEM
electrolyzers. However, their longer response time makes them less suitable for grid balancing
purposes [27, 25, 26].
Solid oxide electrolyzers
SOE have demonstrated significant potential for higher conversion eﬀiciencies, superior ionic
conductivity, and higher operating temperatures compared to both ALK and PEM electroy-
lyzers. However, SOE technology is less mature and is still under development [27, 28].

AC hubs are ideal for exporting wind power due to their ability to reroute power during faults,
ensuring a stable supply. Additionally, AC hubs are cost-effective because of the maturity and
simplicity of AC technology. However, they require advanced control techniques for HVDC
stations due to shared grid-forming functionality and have higher power losses from multiple
AC/DC conversions [8, 29].
Other hub topologies
While this project focuses on AC interconnected hubs, the other possible topologies are briefly
introduced:
• DC interconnected hubs, shown in Figure 3.5, couple on the DC side, forming a multi-
terminal HVDC (MTDC) system. They offer reduced power losses, better power exchange
between different onshore areas, and easier scalability. However, the immaturity of the
DC technology can lead to higher initial costs and less established control mechanisms
[8, 29].
• The hybrid hubs combine AC and DC systems, providing an interconnected AC system
alongside a MTDC system. This topology has the highest initial cost but offers more
operational flexibility [8, 29].

3.3
HVDC system
The offshore system connects to the 400 kV offshore bus, with each wind power plant connected
to its respective pole of the HVDC system, as shown in Figure 3.6. The HVDC system is
modeled using two sets of MMC-HVDC bipolar links, each rated at 2.2 GW, that transmit the
power generated offshore to the onshore grid.
Figure 3.6: The HVDC system diagram on PowerFactory model.
The wind power plants (WPP 1, WPP 2, WPP 3, and WPP 4) are connected to the 400/300
kV step-down transformers, which then connect to the AC/DC converters. These converters
are part of the HVDC system, which includes both positive (+525 kV) and negative (-525 kV)
poles. The system also includes the neutral (N) points, or the dedicated metallic return (DMR),
which is grounded on the offshore side, providing a path for fault currents and helps maintain
consistent voltage levels. The converted DC power is then transmitted through the 525 kV DC
cables to the onshore grid, where it is converted back to AC power by the DC/AC converters.
The power is then stepped up using 300/400 kV transformers before being fed to the external
grids (Grid 1 and Grid 2), which represent two different onshore systems.

Test 4 - Electrolyzer for secondary frequency control
Because of the restricting ramping rates of the ALK electrolyzer, it was tried using for secondary
frequency control.
It has been demonstrated that electrolyzers can be used for secondary
frequency control, or automatic generation control (AGC) [25]. AGC refers to the control that
occurs after the fault has been cleared, that restores the system back to its optimal values.
A similar concept to the AGC, the Master Controller, has been considered for the Bornholm
energy islands [36], where the Master Controller would be used to get system back to a optimal
operational point following a fault.
An AGC controller similar to the one proposed in [25] was implemented, where an additional
control loop was added to the frequency droop control of the electrolyzer, shown in Figure 5.15.
Figure 5.15: AGC controller added to the electrolyzer.
The controller periodically adjusts the electrolyzer’s consumption based on the change in fre-
quency:
PEl,sec = PEl,init + ∆f · β
(5.15)
PEl,sec is the adjusted consumption of the electrolyzer , PEl,init is the initial consumption, and
β is the frequency droop gain.
For this controller, the same time step as outlined in [25],
was used TAGC = 4s.
With the effort to optimize the reaction of the HVDC converters,
the frequency droop of the electrolyzer was deactivated. This approach allowed the HVDC
converters to stabilize the frequency initially, followed by the electrolyzer’s response through
the AGC mechanism.
For this implementation, the droop settings of the HVDC converters and the wind curtailment
were kept the constant, while various values of β were tested using a trial-and-error method.
This simplified approach was intended to observe if the electrolyzer could be utilized to bring
the system back to its initial condition.
Overall, the AGC mechanism works effectively to adjusts the setpoints of the HVDC converters,
bringing them closer to their initial set points and thereby enhancing system stability.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the feasibility of integrating an Alkaline (ALK) electrolyzer into an
interconnected AC offshore energy hub to coordinate active power control among all units
of the system. Using the simulation tool PowerFactory, a model of the interconnected AC
offshore energy hub was developed. A coordinated control scheme was then implemented using a
simplified a frequency droop control method, where one grid forming converter was implemented
to control the frequency of the system and maintain stability. The system was then tested under
two main fault scenarios using different capacities of the electrolyzer.
In the first scenario, a pole fault was simulated, causing the wind power generation to exceed
the offshore energy hub’s transmission capacity.
The coordinated frequency droop control
method proved to be a successful method of active power control. Integrating the 1000 MW
ALK electrolyzer yielded the best results, allowing the offshore energy hub to gain more self-
suﬀiciency, minimize its reliance on the onshore systems, and the need for wind curtailment.
However, the restrictive ramp-up rates of the ALK electrolyzer model caused the model to
reach stability slower.
In the second scenario, a fault caused one of the offshore wind power plants to be disconnected,
resulting in a deficit of power transmission. Again, the frequency droop control proved to be
successful in maintaining the stability. The integration of electrolyzers reduced the reliance on
the onshore system and demonstrated an improved response due to the modeling properties of
the ALK electrolyzer model.
To address with the limiting ramp rates of the electrolyzer, a simplified automatic generation
control (AGC) mechanism was implemented for Scenario I to assess if the ALK electrolyzer
could be better handle secondary frequency control. The findings were promising, with the
electrolyzer optimizing system stability by adjusting the systems components closer to their
initial setpoints.
Overall, these findings underscore the feasibility and effectiveness of integrating electrolyzer in
enhancing the stability and reliability of the interconnected AC offshore energy hub. The results
support the projects aim of developing a coordinated control scheme between electrolyzers and
HVDC converters, improving active power control capabilities.
Future investigations could
include the integration of an adjustable droop, exploring the advantages of PEM electrolyzers,
and further investigating the uses of ALK electrolyzers for secondrary frequency control.
