Investigation of rodent brain vasculature and perivascular space dynamics in
miniscope recordings
by Sheyla BARRADO BALLESTERO
Accurate segmentation of cerebral vasculature from miniscope images in freely
moving mice would help us to evaluate blood flow in order to get a better un-
derstanding of the glymphatic system.
However, this is still a challenging task
due to the complexity of the vascular imaging data and the fact that the miniscope
has never been used for this purpose before. In this pilot study, a semi-automatic
pipeline for cerebral vascular segmentation from miniscope images was designed
and implemented.
This would allow us to study how artery and vein constric-
tion/dilation attributes across brain states (awake, NREM, REM) contributes to the
glymphatic system. It consists of a method to skeletonize the binary segmented map
in order to generate a minimum spanning tree. Cross sectional lines of the vessel are
randomly generated as input regions of interest for the diameter algorithm imple-
mented to estimate the vessel diameter. Finally, the vessel diameter mean, standard
deviation and coefficient of variation are extracted and classified according to K-
Means clustering (k=3). This approach allows us to classify the vessels into three
classes: (i) small vessels with low pulsatility, (ii) small vessels with high pulsatility,
and (iii) big vessels. An example of how the resulting vessel diameters can be used
to analyze their changes through the brain states is also shown. This tool opens the
possibility of a semi-automatic and simultaneous analysis of multiple vessel profiles
in miniscope data, leading to faster and more reproducible studies in the study of
cerebral blood flow in miniscope recordings.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The brain is one of the most metabolically active organs in the body. This high bioen-
ergetic state of the brain is paralleled by high metabolic waste production. However,
the brain lacks a traditional lymphatic system for metabolite clearance. Hence, the
brain must clear extracellular proteins by an alternative mechanism. Recently it was
discovered that instead of lymphatic vessels, the brain utilizes a unique system of
perivascular channels, formed by astroglial cells to eliminate soluble proteins and
metabolites from the central nervous system [1]. The clearance pathway was called
the glymphatic system. Recent work suggests that cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) from
the subarachnoid space moves into the brain along paravascular routes surround-
ing penetrating cerebral arteries, exchanging with brain interstitial fluid (ISF) and
facilitating the clearance of interstitial solutes [2], but the exact processes involved
in toxic waste clearance from the brain remain incompletely understood.
As the Monro-Kellie dogma states, because the skull is a rigid box, the sum of
the volumes occupied by the parenchyma, the connective tissue, the vasculature,
the meninges and the CSF must be constant [3]. Therefore, because the sum of the
volumes are constant, we expect that a change in blood volume (BV), which hap-
pens during vasodilation and vasoconstriction, will affect the volume of CSF and
thereby the glymphatic system. Hence, it is important to study the blood changes.
According to Mestre et all [4], CSF is pumped in the paravascular space (PVS) by
arterial wall motions. However, it still remains unknown how do vessel diameter
(VD) changes affect CSF flow in the PVS and in the brain. In a series of studies, J.
Iliff and colleagues reported that a key driver of CSF flux into and through the brain
parenchyma is the cerebral arterial pulsatility, and suggested that accumulation and
deposition of toxic solutes might be due to changes in arterial pulsations [2].
Despite all the controversy behind the glymphatic system mechanism, all the
evidence seems to agree on the fact that it occurs during sleep, while it is mainly dis-
engaged during wakefulness [5]. Although it is not exactly known why glymphatic
flow is only permitted during sleep, it may be related to the fact that because nore-
pinephrine (NE) levels are reduced during natural sleep, it results in a higher CSF
flow due to the decreased resistance caused by the increase of volume in the inter-
stitial space [6]. Moreover, it has been found that the constriction/dilation of brain
vasculature differs between brain states. We hypothesized that a high BV (as we e.g.
see in REM) might either help “pushing” and distributing CSF around in the brain,
or it could help pushing CSF out of the brain. Then, as a secondary thing, it would
be interesting to see if there are any differences in arterial pulsations between states,
as this will probably affect the flow in the PVS, but not necessarily in the brain.

2
Chapter 1. Introduction
Based upon these findings and hypotheses, in this present project we propose to
use for the first time the use of a miniscope, a novel image technique, to visualize
brain blood vasculature. The main advantage is that it will allow us to image the
brain in freely moving and naturally sleeping mice while simultaneously recording
the electroencephalogram (EEG), and the electromyogram (EMG) to distinct among
brain states: wakefulness, rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, and non REM (NREM)
sleep.
Since it is a novel technique, we proposed a semi-automatic segmentation pipeline
for mouse brain blood vessels in miniscope recordings followed by an algorithm to
compute the VD. The goal of this project is to create a powerful tool that can be use in
the future to address how changes in the BV play a role in regulating the glymphatic
flow and to evaluate artery and vein constriction/dilation kinetics across brain states
(awake, NREM, REM) because of noradrenaline (NA) changes.
1.2
Project description
During sleep, the brain is cleared from waste metabolites via what has been termed
the glymphatic system. Removal of waste products from the brain is conducted
by CSF. Emerging evidence shows that driven by arterial pulsations, CSF moves
along arteries in a fluid-filled gap called the PVS, wherefrom it enters the brain
parenchyma. It is not known exactly why glymphatic flow is only permitted dur-
ing sleep, but one parameter is likely to be constriction/dilation of brain vascula-
ture, which has been found to differ between brain states. We use a miniscope,
which is a miniature microscope weighing ≈3 grams, to image brain vasculature
in freely moving and naturally sleeping mice while simultaneously measuring EEG
and EMG. This allows us to compare the level of vasoconstriction/dilation between
brain states. In order to facilitate the task to the user and make it faster and re-
producible, we design and implement a segmentation pipeline for the miniscope
recordings followed by an algorithm to measure the VD.
1.2.1
Objectives
The main goal of this project thesis is to design and implement a segmentation
pipeline in miniscope recordings for BV and the PVSs around them. That allows
us to generate a random number or regions of interest (ROIs) across the vessel that
can be used to measure the VD, and later on to study how blood changes affect glym-
phatic flow. To achieve that goal, I have used data consisting of multiple miniscope
recordings that were already recorded, as well as miniscope videos that has been
recorded during the development of the project. Here are detailed the objectives
that have been proposed partially in order to accomplish the purpose:
O1.- Design and implement a segmentation pipeline for the miniscope record-
ings.
O2.- Implement an algorithm to measure the VD
O3.- Characterize the blood vessels based on their characteristics and behavior.
O4.- Use the VD to evaluate artery and vein constriction/dilation kinetics across
brain states (awake, nREM, REM).

2.1
Anatomy and terminology of the glymphatic system
In gaining a better understanding on how metabolic waste clearance from the cen-
tral nervous system (CNS) occurs while we sleep, it is necessary to consider the basic
anatomy. Because of the complexity of the anatomical profile of the network, we will
briefly discuss and review the general anatomy and physiology from the perspective
of the glymphatic pathway. It is important to mention that since this is a relatively
new discovery, the amount of scientific literature surrounding the glymphatic sys-
tem is rapidly increasing, and therefore its definition is continuously being renewed.
This has caused controversy surrounding both the directionality and the anatomical
space in which this system resides.
Working inwards from the skull (Figure 2.1), there is a thick supporting layer
of tissue (the dura), another connective tissue layer (the arachnoid membrane), the
pia mater and the brain parenchyma with its glial outer surface. The arachnoid
membrane and pia mater are separated by the subarachnoid space that is bridged
by chordae and larger trabeculae (anatomic part of the image that has been zoomed).
The parenchyma is made upon cells, e.g. neurons and glia, the extracellular matrix
and ISF that pervades the matrix and fills the spaces between the cells. Arteries
and veins run through the subarachnoid spaces with branches penetrating into the
parenchyma. Veins leaving the parenchyma drain into the various venous sinuses
and thence to the jugular veins.
The principle of CSF transport and waste solute removal via the glymphatic
pathway is illustrated in the zoomed part of Figure 2.1. As represented, CSF flows
alongside the arteries through the periarterial space, whose outer perimeter is sur-
rounded by a sheath of astrocytic end-feet on which AQP4 water channels are strate-
gically positioned to facilitate rapid water exchange across the capillary-endfeet com-
plex, and is forced into brain parenchyma. There, it interchanges with ISF facilitating
waste removal. Finally, the CSF-ISF and waste leaves the brain parenchyma along
perivenous channels. This whole transport system has been designed glymphatic
pathway or (g)lymphatics due to its dependence on glial cells [7]. The recent dis-
covery of the glymphatic system has opened new opportunities in developing treat-
ments that by enhancing glymphatic flow, reinforce the clearance of neurotoxic ag-
gregates, characteristic to many neurodegenerative disorders including Alzheimer’s
disease and Parkinson’s disease.

2.2
Arteries vs Veins
To automate the classification of the blood vasculature in the miniscope recordings
based on the hemodynamics, and vessel characteristics, it is necessary to include
some of the basic concepts of the main blood vessels: arteries, and veins.
Veins are structurally and functionally different from arteries. The main func-
tional difference is defined by the direction of the flow of blood that they carry.
Arteries carry blood away from the heart, whereas veins carry blood towards it.
Regarding structural aspects, arteries have thick, elastic, muscular walls due to their
high pulsatility. This characteristic is important because arteries need to withstand
the high pressure from ejected blood due to their proximity to the heart. In contrast,
veins have thin walls with few elastic fibres, which makes them less pulsatile. More-
over, arteries also have narrow lumens, necessary for maintaining the pressure of
blood as it is transported, whereas veins have larger lumens since they carry blood
under low pressure.
Considering these aspects, we can summarize that veins have higher VD while
lower pulsations. Hence, it is the constriction/dilation of the vessels along with their
VD which will allow us to classify them. As stated, we will expect a lower mean but
higher standard deviation of the vessel diameter when talking about arteries, and
vice versa in the case of veins.
2.3
Effect of Norepinephrine in blood vasculature
Norepinephrine (NE), also called noradrenaline (NA) or noradrenalin, is an organic
chemical in the catecholamine family that functions in the brain and body as both a
hormone and neurotransmitter associated with the ‘fight or flight’ response to per-
ceived danger [8]. Yet the role of NE in regulating vertebrate sleep/wake states re-
mains unclear, it is believed that it plays a key role in the brain waste clearance. The
NA released varies with the sleep-wake cycle, being more active during wakeful-
ness and least active during sleep state. During natural sleep, levels of NE decline,
leading to an expansion of the interstitial space and a subsequent potentiation of

terstitial space [9]. In contrast, during sleep deprivation, NE secretion is increased
while glymphatic fluid transport is reduced (see Figure 2.2). This occurs because NA
release triggers vasoconstriction (narrowing of blood vessels) increasing blood pres-

2.4
Sleep
Sleep is a fundamental biological phenomenon widely observed in the animal king-
dom, and therefore it must serve at least one basic function across this wide range
of species. Over the past decade, the role of sleep in glymphatic clearance has been
conclusively demonstrated, and since the vast majority of clearance occurs during
sleep, the glymphatic system can simply not be investigated without examining the
basic aspects of sleep [6].
During natural sleep or anesthesia the interstitial space increases enhancing the
exchange of CSF with ISF via the glymphatic system, removing potential neurotoxic
waste products such as Amyloid-β (Aβ) that accumulate during waking periods.
This mechanism contributing to the restorative nature of sleep was unknown in spite
of the numerous negative consequences of sleep deprivation: prolonged reaction
times, reduced cognitive performance, increased risk of seizures or hallucinations,
and even death in a matter of days for flies or weeks for rodents [10]. These results
support a new hypothesis to answer the age-old question of why sleep is necessary,
to eliminate metabolic waste from neural activity.
Sleep stages can be divided into two major phases, NREM and REM sleep. In
the context of humans, there can be a further subdivision of NREM sleep into stages
1, 2, 3, and 4, although recent research has combined stages 3 and 4 into slow-wave
sleep (SWS) because of the inherent difficulties in distinguishing these latter two
stages of deep sleep (2.3 left). However, in mammals, sleep is broadly comprised,
as mentioned, of the stages of NREM or SWS and REM or paradoxical sleep [11].

Chapter 2. State of Art
In healthy subjects, REM sleep is always preceded by one or several NREM sleep
periods [11], but there might either be NREM or awake after REM.

The brain activity pattern differ among these states. REM sleep is defined by
a rapid and low-voltage/high frequency EEG, similar to the activity of the brain
during waking hours, with rapid and random movement of the eyes. Interestingly,
during this sleep phase almost all muscles of the body are paralysed (REM atonia).
In contrast, NREM sleep is characterised by slow, low-frequency EEG patterns, and
there is usually little or no rapid eye movement and muscles are not paralysed (2.3
right). However, while the dynamics of neural activity during sleep are well charac-
terized, the cerebrovascular manifestations of sleep are less clear. Previous studies
suggest that cerebral bloodflow (CBF) changes between states. However, the hemo-
dynamic signals changes are not well understood.
According to the study performed by Turner et al. [13], there is an increase in
cerebral blood volume ([HbT]) and arteriole diameter both in NREM and REM sleep
relative to the awake state. Based on this line, we pose to keep investigating on
the CBF and its relationship with the glymphatic system in freely behaving mice by
looking at both, veins and arteries.

sion bandpass filters for further spectral cleanup [18]. For superficial brain imaging,
as it is the case, we image through a cranial window. An example of such miniatur-

combined with calcium imaging, dual imaging of cellular activity, neurotransmitter

3.2.1
Preprocessing
The miniscope image usually has imperfections like poor contrast and noise, which
need to be reduced or eliminated before the segmentation. So preprocessing is a
necessary step to be followed, which can include different sub-steps.
Bias field correction
In general, blood vessels have lower reflectance and appear darker than other struc-
tures in the image. However, this will depend on the type of experiment. In this case
blood vessels will appear darker because the virus in the background illuminates.
Typically, bright areas can be seen in the center of the image because of the light
emitted by the miniscope. For vasculature segmentation, it is useful to remove these
bright reflections to provide a better vessel-background contrast. Those background
lightening variations are removed, and a shade-corrected image is generated. This
is so called bias field correction (BFC).
==PAGE:38==
18
Chapter 3. Materials and methods
Gaussian blur
The simplest way to perform the BFC is by simply applying a Gaussian filter
which blurs the image and yields the background image, Ib. Assuming a multiplica-
tive model (i.e., the corrupted image is the result of multiplying the expected image
with the bias field), the shade-corrected image can be obtained by dividing the raw
image by the estimated bias field, i.e. Ib. This allows us to get rid of the change of
intensity in the corners and across the image to be able to apply a threshold for the
binarization afterwards.
Regarding the parameters, the scale (σ) is defined as the standard deviation of
the Gaussian kernel used for the analysis. GridSearch was used with the aim for
finding a good scale at which the VD are expected to be found. The set of different
scales used for the Gaussian filtering ranged from 10 to 30 with a step size of 5.
Vessel enhancement
Next sub-step in the preprocessing block will consist of contrast enhancement. How-
ever, as stated by Khawaja et all [15], it has an inherent tendency to aggravate the
noise, and therefore, impede accurate vessel detection. Therefore, we proposed in-
stead an strategy based on the Frangi filter, one of the most famous methods for
vessel segmentation since it is fast as well as efficient.
Frangi filter
The Frangi filter is typically used to detect vessel-like or tube-like structures with
the purpose of enhancing the blood vessels with the eventual goal of vessel segmen-
tation. It is based on the hessian matrix method that relies on the calculation of the
second order derivative of the image in horizontal, vertical and both left and right
diagonals to obtain the Hessian matrix. This is due to the fact that in a linear struc-
ture, the intensity increase rapidly from the border to the center line and decrease
again from the center line to the opposite border [24].
First, histogram equalization is used to enhance the contrast of the bias filter
corrected image to facilitate the task of distinguishing the blood vessels from other
background regions.
Now that the image has the same lighting conditions, the
Frangi filter was applied using the parameters depicted in 3.1 as they have shown
good results for the tested images.
3.2.2
Binarization
The next processing step is image binarization, a non-linear operation that converts
every pixel of the image Ii,j into just two tones, black and white, leading to the la-
bel image I_seg. In image segmentation, these two levels of black and white are
usually associated with background and foreground, respectively. In our problem
it refers to the blood vasculature or foreground, and the rest of structures or sim-
ply background. This is done by selecting a good threshold value that allows the
differentiation.
In short, binarization thresholding involves applying an algorithm to assign all
image pixels to be either black if Ii,j is less than a fixed value called the threshold T,
or white if it is greater [25]. Despite it is the simplest method of segmenting images,
selecting the most optimum threshold for binarization is a difficult task due to the

3.2.5
Skeletonization and graph path
The idea behind the graph algorithm is to connect all the pixels so that we can build
a path that will be useful later on to perform the vessel analysis. A graph consists
of a finite set of nodes (or vertices) and a set of edges connecting these nodes. Two
nodes are said to be adjacent if they are connected to each other by the same edge.
Skeletonize
First, we obtain a skeleton using scikit-image from the binary image. The skele-
tonization process consists of reducing the foreground regions in the binary image
to one pixel wide representations so that we get a skeletal remnant that largely pre-
serves the extent and connectivity of the original region while throwing away most
of the original foreground pixels. The skeleton is made by making successive passes
of the image. On each pass, border pixels are identified and removed on the condi-
tion that they do not break the connectivity of the corresponding object. The process
iterates until no more pixels can be removed.
Graph path: minimum spanning tree
The generated skeleton consists of just pixels but it has lack of the knowledge of the
path. Hence, it is used to build a graph path considering 8-connectivity, i.e. pixels
up/down, left/right, as well as the diagonals are considered as neighbors. Later on,
this graph is used to implement a minimum spanning tree (mst) based on Kruskal’s
algorithm. It consists of a subset of the edges of the graph that connects all the
vertices with the minimum sum of edge weights and consists of no cycles.
Removal of Irrelevant Side-branches

tation. They may correctly reflect the anatomical situation, but may also be the due
to noise. Hence, a recursive algorithm has been developed to prune the branches be-
low a threshold. It is based on the idea that there are three different types of nodes:
terminal nodes (only one neighbor), intermediate nodes (two neighbors), and junc-
tion nodes (three or more neighbors). Because we have built a mst, we have a net-
work that gives us all that previous information. Hence, starting in a terminal node

3.2.6
Vessel diameter algorithm
Final step of the process before the analysis consists of implementing an algorithm
to measure the blood vessel diameter. The estimation has been performed imple-
menting the proposed algorithm in [28], and it is described in the next paragraphs:
Finding ROIs
As we intend to measure the diameter of a blood-filled vessel in front of a lighter
background, we need to specify a line selection across the structure of interest. The
general idea is based on the construction of parallel lines to the mst prunned with the
aim of calculating their normal to create a cross section of the vessels. Those lines
will be made up based on the random selection of the nodes that define the MST.
From each of those randomly selected nodes, we construct a branch by travelling

3.2. Segmentation pipeline
23
the mst prunned. Branches with less than 20 nodes are removed. Finally, among
the remaining branches, we will consider the number of branches specified, which
is basically the number of ROIs (number of lines) the user is interested in.
After that, the nodes comprising the branch are used to generate a parallel line
to the mst prunned by fitting the nodes to a 1st order polynomial. In order to plot
the parallel lines in the image, a director vector is constructed from the slope of the
line and the vector equation of the line is used to plot it. Also, we generate the mid
point of each line, which will be used to define the perpendicular lines, i.e. the cross

Vessel Diameter algorithm
Now that we have the necessary cross sectional lines of the vessels we can proceed
with the implementation of the algorithm. It requires a stack of grey-scale images
along with the previous lines of interest. It is important to mention that the profile
is calculated in the original stack of images without any modification. The main
idea behind the algorithm is to take advantage of the change in intensity across the
vessel. As previously mentioned, because the virus illuminates, the vessels appear
darker. Hence, a roughly explanation to find the profile is based on the idea that
coming from a light area, it becomes darker when entering the vessel, and again
lighter when going back to the perivascular space. The steps to obtain the vessel
diameter can be summarized as follows (Figure 3.8):
==PAGE:44==
24
Chapter 3. Materials and methods
• Obtain the profile.
Pixels below the line are calculated and the profile is obtained interpolating
them using a 1st order polynomial. In order to achieve better results, a sav-
gol filter with a window size of 9 and polynomial order 3 was applied to the
obtained profile.
• Divide the profile into three regions
The profile is separated in three parts by finding two positions (a1, a2) with the
maximum difference between the mean intensity of the three adjacent points
to both sides, i.e. by doing the first derivative of the profile.
• Calculate the reference intensities in each region, which is defined as the max-
imum intensity in the perivascular areas (b1, b3), and the minimum intensity
within the vessel (b2).
For the all of the pixels in that region, we compute the mean considering the
two adjacent neighbors. The reference intensity is calculated iterative updating
the result until all the pixels have been travelled.
- b1, b3: lightest areas outside the blood vessel
- b2: darkest area within the vessel
• Obtain two thresholds (c1, c2) averaging the adjacent reference intensities.
- c1 : average intensity between b1 and b2
- c2 : average intensity between b2 and b3
• Obtain a linear trend of the intensities of the two positions (left and right) of
the boundary (a1, a2).
• Get the boundaries of the diameter (d1, d2) at the intersection point with the
threshold.
• Obtain the VD subtracting the boundaries of the diameter (d1, d2).
Note that because a pipe has half of its maximal thickness at √
4/3 of the ra-
dius, so the resulting (d1, d2) are multiplied by this value as a correcting factor
to ensure the VD has been calculated correctly.
The results of the vessel diameter are saved in an excel file for future analysis.
Moreover, the boundaries of the vessel, (d1, d2), are also saved to evaluate the per-
formance of the algorithm using the kymograph. For further information consult
the detailed explanation of the algorithm at [28].
Vessel diameter postprocessing
A median filter using a kernel size of 5 has been used for noise removal, followed
by a mean filter to smooth the VD. Median filter plays an important role in the VD
postprocessing as it is well known for noise removal while preserving the informa-
tion. It is necessary to apply it since due to the quality of the generated stack of
images they can compromise the VD estimation.

Chapter 5
Discussion and conclusion
The miniscope is a powerful tool with a wide variety of utilites. In spite of that, sci-
entists have mainly use it to observe the cellular activity. Based on its possibilities,
we decided to image the brain to visualize the blood vasculature in order to analyze
how blood flow affect the glymphatic system. However, because it is a novel tech-
nique, it requires the development of new pipelines. In the present project we have
demonstrated a method for semi-automatic segmentation of brain blood vascula-
ture from miniscope recordings in freely moving and natural sleeping mice, and the
subsequent generation of the VD. This technique can be applied to analyze blood
vessels across brain states based on their hemodynamics and their relation to the
glymphatic system.
There are multiple critical steps in this protocol. Minimization of motion during
the imaging is essential for generating movies that are usable for blood vessels flow
analysis using the implemented Diameter algorithm. However, it is complicated to
achieve since we image freely moving mice, and also flashes emitted by the minis-
cope can ruin part of the signal. The median and mean filter during the vessel diam-
eter post-processing have shown good results dealing with this problem.
Another aspect to take into account during image acquisition is the number of
frames acquired per second since it will determine the maximum frequency of the
vessel fluctuations that can be measured based on the Nyquist theorem.
Moreover, it was difficult to measure the performance of the segmentation since
we are not interesting in getting an accurate segmentation map, but useful vessels.
This plays an important role during the bias field correction. The selection of the
hyper-parameter σ for the Gaussian filtering will determine the vessels that will be
segmented later on. Bigger values of σ include larger vessels, whereas small val-
ues allow to segment the small ones. Multi-scale approaches are often successful in
combining segmentation results derived in different resolutions. However, despite
it would have helped in the detection of small vessels, there need to be a trade-off
between the amount of segmented structures with respect to the ones that are truth-
fully vessels and the ones that are not.
Following, tidiness is one of the most sensitive parts. This is because the parame-
ters depend on the physical attributes of the image. Hence, this block of the pipeline
is really attach to the specific stack of images, which means that the user will need
to determine the parameters based on the characteristics of the specific data.
Additionally, we do not really care about a good segmentation, but about an
accurate skeleton. Skeleton line segments should lie near the midpoint of the vascu-
lature in order to correct measure the blood vessel diameter.
==PAGE:54==
34
Chapter 5. Discussion and conclusion
In general, giving values to the hyper-parameters can be another of the tricky
parts of the protocol. However, we aim to facilitate the task to the user by pro-
viding a range of values that have shown good results on the tested images. As
stated above, choosing a low σ for Gaussian filtering will detect smaller vessels and
vice-versa. In the case of the binarization threshold, the higher the value, the more
structures in the image will be segmented. Hence, we advice to start with a low
value and to update it if necessary. The threshold to remove islands is a physical
parameter. The higher the value, the less structures are kept.
The vessel unsupervised-classification is unavoidably subject to some uncertainty,
since we do not know the ground truth. The classification using the mean vessel di-
ameter, and the coefficient of variation as features is also subject to discussion. Those
metrics where computed using the complete time series, independently of the states.
However, it has been depicted that those properties change over the time and the
brain states. Hence, the classification of each profile may be more robust if we con-
sider its properties in the different brain-states, and not only a general metric over
the whole recording.
To conclude, although the VD across states results are not as expected, since
Turner et al. [13] showed that during both NREM and REM sleep mice showed
large increases in cerebral blood volume ([HbT]) and arteriole diameter relative to
the awake state, we cannot extract any conclusion since only one VD profile was
included for a specific ROI. However, the ability of the algorithm to measure the VD
poses this protocol as a key tool that opens the door for a wide range of investiga-
tions of the glymphatic system.
