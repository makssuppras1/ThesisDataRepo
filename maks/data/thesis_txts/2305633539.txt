ABSTRACT 
Currently the major hindrance in the field of tissue engineering is perfusion of the in 
vitro engineered tissues and body parts. Development of functional vasculature will be 
beneficial not only in the field of tissue engineering but also in ex-vivo studies of different 
diseases including cancer, cardiovascular disease and many others. Furthermore in vitro 
fabricated blood vessel is crucially beneficial in the field of pharmacological modeling, drug 
testing and also in connecting multiple organs in body-on-a-chip applications. Unfortunately 
despite the huge effort, the formation of functional bio mimicking 3D vasculature network 
have remained elusive.  
Hydrogels are gaining popularity in the field of tissue engineering due to their 
numerous advantages regarding ECM resemblance and high diffusion and porosity. One of 
the most extensively used hydrogel families are poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG)-based hydrogels 
due to their non-immunogenicity. PEG-based hydrogels matrices have been extensively 
used for controlled drug delivery and cell delivery vesicles in wound healing.  
The aim of this study is endothelialization of open channels of dimensions close to a 
small biological vein and establishment of an assay for quantifying and visualizing cellular 
behavior on the hydrogel. The findings in this thesis are beneficial in forming an intact 
monolayer of endothelial cells inside a perfusable channel of micro-scale dimensions. The 
system will be used for studying transendothelial migration of cancer cells.  In this thesis, 
PEGDA hydrogels have been fabricated using stereolithography-based 3D printer and 
chemically modified with adhesive proteins. Endothelial cells are seeded on the hydrogel 
surfaces and characterized regarding their morphology, attachment and viability.

CHAPTER 1 INTRODUCTION 
 
This chapter covers the motivation behind the work carried out for this thesis and 
introduces the current techniques employed for in vitro fabrication of blood vessels. Finally, 
the techniques used in this study are introduced. 
 
1.1 MOTIVATION  
 
Tumor metastasis is the leading cause of death in cancer patients.1,2 Often, by the 
time the primary tumor is surgically removed; metastases have developed in other body 
tissues, which can be difficult to detect. Metastasis is a multiple step process, beginning 
with cell phenotype modulation, followed by dynamic homotypic and heterotypic cell-cell 
interactions, angiogenesis, and cell migration1,2. During angiogenesis, at normal 
physiological condition, endothelial cells (ECs) escape the constraint of the basement 
membrane and sprout into injured or deoxygenated areas in order to increase oxygen 
supply and to bring immune cells to the site of injury through trans-endothelial migration. 
Tumor growth is facilitated by the same properties facilitating the dissemination of cancer 
cells into and out of the blood circulation (a process called intra- and extra-vasation, 
respectively) to form new micro-metastases at other body locations.3,4   
 
Accurate and early identification of metastatic cells to prevent/eliminate them is the 
key to treatment in cancer patients. Consequently, detailed understanding of the molecular 
and cellular mechanism underlying the metastatic cascade process is required. In order to 
study the mechanism behind metastasis, various in vivo and in vitro models have been 
employed but despite the effort, a proper model to address the complex behavior of tumor 
cells is still lacking.  
 
In vivo animal models fail to clarify the contribution from an isolated factor effect 
and parameters affecting e.g. tumor progression including oxygen tension, nutrient 
gradients, and mechanical forces cannot be controlled or manipulated. Furthermore 
observations and quantifications are challenging in animal studies and can be complicated 
by ethical issues of sacrificing large numbers of animals, as well as being expensive and time 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
consuming. Lastly, the species are different from humans, making the results controversially 
comparable to what actually happens in humans.5 Hence an in vitro model that addresses 
the essential properties of in vivo disease will have benefits in disease research and drug 
screening studies.  
Using a more humanized in vitro model (e.g. if human primary cells are employed) 
enables better predictability of new drug compounds for human therapeutics..6 It will 
provide a practical solution to many of the issues presented by animal testing including 
providing control over experimental variables, species relevance, portability, cost and time 
efficiency. Hence building a perfusable endothelialized synthetic microvasculature network 
permitting trans-endothelial migration will serve as a high quality in vitro model for drug 
screening and diseases such as cancer and will contribute to molecular studies of the early 
stage of metastasis, intravasation, angiogenesis, and tumour microenvironment.  
 
Building a perfusable synthetic microvasculature would also benefit Tissue 
engineering (TE) applications. Currently, in vitro manufactured tissues are limited to a few 
millimetres in thickness due to insufficient perfusion techniques and lack of functional 
vasculature. Thus, the main challenge within the field of tissue engineering is building three-
dimensional (3D) perfusable microvasculature to supply tissue cells with oxygen and 
nutrients, while removing metabolic waste for sustaining cell viability and differentiation. 
Since endothelial cells play a vital role in homeostasis of blood vessels and also in different 
disease such as cardiovascular disease and cancer metastasis, they cannot be avoided.   
 
The aim of this thesis is to establish methods to endothelialize synthetic perfusable 
micro-channels on a microfluidic system using reproducible, fast, low cost, and efficient 
techniques for in vitro diseases studies and drug screening, as well as perfusion of in vitro 
manufactured body tissues and parts. Since three-dimensional soft biomaterial scaffolds are 
essential for long term cell cultures in regenerative medicine and tissue engineering, the 
focus in this project is on hydrogel as biomaterial. The working hypothesis is that covalent 
binding of RGD and gelatin in bulk and at the surface could successfully enhance cell 
adhesion and spreading while retaining cell viability on the hydrogel used in this study.

lumen have crucial functions in vascular homeostasis through secretion of different

1.3 HYDROGELS IN BIOMEDICAL APPLICATIONS 
 
Hydrogels can be applied for many biomedical applications, including for implants, 
TE, contact lenses10 and in wound dressing11 and drug delivery12. Hydrogels are very 
advantageous due to their excellent diffusion properties, ECM resemblance and high water 
content. Hydrogels can absorb water up to thousands time more than their dry weight and 
Cell survival rate in hydrogels are high due to their excellent diffusion properties13. 
Compared to rigid surfaces, hydrogels mimic the ECM to a higher degree and allow cell 
encapsulation. Furthermore they permit excellent control on different physical, chemical 
and biological factors including biodegradability, cell adhesion, spreading, migration and 
proliferation14. Hydrogel stiffness is tunable by changing polymer concentration and 
polymer molecular weight, crosslinking degree, and type of crosslinking. Changing the 
polymer molecular weight would also change the pore size of the hydrogel. Changing pore 
size is sometimes desirable in order to control diffusion rate and mass transport across the 
hydrogel15. Increasing pore size would give lower stiffness on the other hand, thus a balance 
between pore size and mechanical strength is necessary13,16,17. 
 
Hydrogels can be classified according to the types of polymers they are made from 
into two groups; (a) natural hydrogels and (b) synthetic hydrogels.  Each of these 
subcategories is listed in Table 2 with their advantages and limitations.

1.4 CURRENT FABRICATION TECHNIQUES  
 
So far different techniques have been employed for fabrication of vascular 
structures. Various 2D and 3D models have contributed to our understanding of different 
diseases and have built the base for future in vitro TE of vasculature network. Traditional 2D 
systems23,24 are limited with respect to 3D geometry and its inherent structural and 
mechanical properties25. Hence sophisticated 3D models permitting flow are more faithful 
models for in vivo structure and more predictive of clinical outcome. They permit analysis of 
more complex interactions between different cells, molecules and extracellular matrix 
(ECM) under physiological conditions.  
 
Micro-scale technologies and microfluidic systems together with hydrogels have 
emerged as tools to overcome the challenges of developing an artificial microvasculature. 
Microfluidic systems provide continuous perfusion and physiological flow which offers a 
more advanced in vitro models of disease microenvironment and they enable immediate 
perfusion of the structure improving proliferation and growth of cells from the very 
beginning. Besides they are small in scale and therefore are more efficient in using patient 
cells and samples26. This is advantageous in allowing numerous studies at low cost and in 
future microfluidic systems can be used as a tool to understand the unique characteristics of 
each patient’s tumor at reduced time and labor.  
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
 
One technique employed for fabrication of microvasculature for TE and in vitro 
disease models is called vasculogenesis- and angiogenesis-induced technique which is based 
on biological formation of blood vessels through vasculogenesis and angiogenesis (self-
organization of ECs by themselves or with mesenchymal cells in hydrogel). This is induced ex 
vivo through use of biochemical and biophysical cues. As these techniques may improve 
outcomes within the field of tissue engineering, they lack complete control over the 
formation of a microvascular network and different factors (due to dependence on cell 
response). Controlling the parameters that affect the outcome helps in understanding a 
single factor influence. Hence techniques enabling full control are more advantages for 
studying diseases.  
Previous studies have developed some traditional technique which include 
molding26,27, soft lithography28–31, photopatterning32 etc. These techniques are simple but 
have some limitations including cumbersome manual handling, time demanding due to the 
iterative process, difficulties in fabricating interconnecting complex network, lack of 
structural freedom in 3D designs, difficulties in reproducing the results and they apply 
considerable design constraints on the materials, channels and cells used during 
fabrication26,27. Thus a more sophisticated technique for fabrication of vasculature network 
is necessary33,34. 
In recent years 3D printing has become widespread in the field of biomedical 
engineering because of its single step automated fast fabrication techniques. There are 
different types of 3D printer and in this study we have employed a light polymerized based 
3D printer called stereolithography (STL), which is a fast and cheap technique and allows 
fabrication of complex vasculature network with high degree of interconnectivity. It further 
allows use of heterogeneous materials and enables control on both internal and external 
architecture of the vasculature network.

1.5 POLY (ETHYLENE-GLYCOL) DIACRYLATE HYDROGELS 
 
For a material to be printable using the STL printing system, it is required to be 
photo-polymerizable. In Poly (ethylene-glycol) diacrylate (PEGDA), both end groups of PEG 
are modified with acrylate, which functionalizes the polymer for photopolymerization 
reactions (Figure 2 a)39.  
  
PEGDA is one of most widely used material in biomedical application due to its 
remarkable advantages including water solubility and low-cytotoxicity39–41. PEGDA hydrogel 
has been used for encapsulation and immobilization of cells and enzymes and as non-
inflammatory modifier of drugs and implant surfaces for reducing protein adsorption, 
platelet adhesion and thrombogenicity42,43. It has also been used in combination with 
protease-degradable sites, adhesive ligands and vascular endothelial growth-factor (VEGF) 
to induce angiogenesis in implants in vivo39.

Furthermore PEGDA polymer is non-adhesive toward proteins and cells. This 
property makes PEGDA an excellent material for in vitro disease studies and drug delivery 
screening because any biological response to such a material must be attributed only to the 
incorporated biological factors44.  
Gelation of PEGDA hydrogel happens by covalent binding through chemical 
crosslinking in the presence of light and a photoinitiator. The photoinitiator dissociates upon 
exposure to light to form free radicals. These free radicals initiate polymerization of PEGDA 
monomers through free radical polymerization. The photoinitiator used in this study is 
lithium phenyl-2,4,6-trimethylbenzoylphosphinate (Figure 2 b), shortly called LAP, which is 
water soluble (up to 8.5 wt %) and cytocompatible at the concentration used in this study45. 
The curing depth of light must be slightly higher than the layer thickness in order to 
establish adherence between layers but it should not penetrate deep to previous layer 
resulting in undesired curing of them. The curing depth depends on the light penetration 
depth in the resin, and can be controlled by employing photo-absorber. In this study 
Quinoline Yellow (QY) has been employed as photo absorber which is biocompatible and 
water soluble46. LAP, at the concentration we used, can be initiated at the wave length 365 
nm, which is the wavelength we use in our printing system.

1.6 BIOFUNCTIONALIZATION OF PEG 
Cell adhesion to a material is only possible through interaction between cell 
membrane receptors (mainly integrins) and short peptide sequences on adhesion proteins 
(e.g. vitronectin, fibronectin and laminin) on the substrate. PEG-based hydrogel is non-
adhesive toward proteins and subsequently cell. This is due to poor protein adsorption to 
the hydrophilic and nonionic PEG-based hydrogel surface and its steric repulsion and high 
water affinity39,47–49.  
One way to make PEG-based hydrogel bio-adhesive is chemical conjugation of 
collagen associated proteins (CAPs). The most common CAPs used for cell attachment 
modifications are Arg–Gly–Asp (RGD) containing proteins and networks of ECM 
components50–52. RGD is the cell attachment site of a large number of adhesive extracellular 
matrix proteins such as vitronectin, fibrinogen, fibronectin, von Willebrand factor, 
thrombospondin, laminin, entactin, osteopontin and tenascin51 and was originally 
discovered in fibronectin in 198453. In a study by Hubbell it was shown that RGD alone was 
enough to support morphologically complete cell spreading40. Hence cell adhesion onto 
PEGDA can be accomplished by chemical incorporation of this short synthetic adhesion 
peptide alone. We used acrylated RGD peptide to improve biological responsive of PEGDA 
hydrogel. The RGD sequence used in this experiment is KGGGMGYRGDS where lysine (K) is 
used to attach acrylate group to RGD to enable photo cross-linkage of RGD to the PEGDA 
hydrogel surface. 
Establishing a single step straight forward fabrication technique without need of 
manual handling is highly desired because it will decrease errors coupled with complexity of 
multistep techniques. Hence in order to avoid post modification at hydrogel surface, it is 
desired to mix RGD (or any other CAP) in polymer solution before fabrication (hereafter 
designated bulk modification) forming a hybrid hydrogel. However this must be 
compromised with costs associated with using huge amount of protein.   
Gelatin is an inexpensive replacement for RGD and different papers indicate 
successful use of gelatin for facilitating cell adhesion onto hydrogel surfaces27,54,55. In a study 
by J. W. Nichol et al.,56 where they investigated cell binding, elongation and migration 
properties of GelMA microgels, cells bound to and spread on GelMA surface of 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
concentrations 50, 100 and 150 mg/mL. They further discovered that HUVEC cells 
proliferated on GelMA hydrogel and the proliferation’s rate increased with increasing 
GelMA concentration.  In a study by H. Bae et al.57 pullulan methacrylate (PulMA) was mixed 
with GelMA at 50 and 100 mg/mL concentration to enhance cell attachment, proliferation 
and elongation within that composite hydrogel. They observed that HUVEC cell adhesion 
and proliferation increased significantly with the addition of GelMA to PulMA polymer. 
Gelatin has also been commonly used for in vitro fabrication of vasculature network58.  
Gelatin is hydrolyzed product of collagen and has matrix metalloproteinase sensitive 
degradation sites and cell binding motifs including RGD (Arg-Gly-Asp)59. Gelatin 
methacrylate (GelMA) is derived from gelatin, where the amine group is modified with 
methacrylate (MA) functional group (Figure 
3 a). This would make GelMA 
photopolymerizable meaning that upon exposure to light, methacrylate groups on GelMA 
and free acrylate groups of PEGDA bind together through covalent binding (Figure 3 b)60,61. 
 
1.7 CHOICE OF MATERIAL 
In this study we have used PEGDA 700 as a suitable biomaterial to conduct our study 
because the acrylate groups on PEG chains allows photo-polymerization of PEGDA 
molecules, enabling use of STL fabrication technique. PEGDA 700 is inexpensive compared 
to those of higher molecular weight and can therefore be used for numerous in vitro 
studies. It allows adequate diffusion of nutrients to ensure cell survival and is chemically and 
mechanically stable22,62,63. Finally PEGDA is non-adhesive toward proteins and cells and 
provides us with a non-fouling background for in vitro disease studies. 
RGD is a short synthetic adhesion peptide, increasing the reproducibility in the 
system. It is a ligand for the most highly expressed adhesion receptor on ECs, the αVβ3 
receptor64. However RGD is relatively expensive and our goal is to establish a cost effective 
system for numerous in vitro studies.  
Methacrylated gelatin (GelMA) has been used in this study because it is cost 
effective and has long chains and therefore is expected to be more available to cells for 
adhesion. Methacrylate groups make GelMA amenable to microfabrication techniques (i.e. 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
STL) and allow chemical cross-linking of GelMA in to other molecules. The RGD motifs on 
gelatin do not contain groups that will react with methacrylic anhydride and are free to bind 
to cells, making GelMA a suitable adhesive sites for cells55.  
 
1.8 CHOICE OF DESIGN 
 
The designed 3D model for hydrogel fabrication is a simple design in order to 
increase the speed of screening studies. It includes a bowl structure with open channels of

1.10 METHOD OF CELL CHARACTERIZATION  
Various techniques were utilized to characterize cells on the PEGDA hydrogel. The 
primary technique employed to observe cells was by phase-contrast microscopy. In order to 
characterize cellular behavior, fluorescent and laser scanning confocal microscopy were 
used. The amount of cells was estimated by staining the cells with Hoescht 33342 which 
allows a fast cell counting using ImageJ software. By using the stain Calcein AM, information 
about cell morphology and spreading on the surface was gained. Finally, dead cells were 
counted and visualized by staining with Propodium Iodide (PI). Hoechst 33342 is fluorescent 
staining of DNA which binds to adenine-thymine-rich regions of DNA in minor groove66. It is 
excited with UV light and its emission spectrum is 460-490 nm.  Calcein AM is a live staining 
of cell cytoplasm. Calcein AM is converted to green fluorescence after hydrolysis of 
acetoxymethyl ester by intercellular esterases. The excitation and emission wavelength of 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
Calcein AM are 495 and 515nm respectively. PI stains dead cells since it cannot permeate 
the cell membrane. On binding to nucleic acids, PI emits red fluorescence. Its excitation and 
emission wavelength are 535 and 617 nm respectively. 
3D hydrogel structures were printed on coverslips. In order to enable coverslips 
adhesion to the hydrogel structures, the coverslips were modified with a monolayer of silyl 
methacrylate (section 2.3). For visualizing cells on the structure using 10×/0.25NA objective 
on confocal microscopy, a holder was designed to bring the structure within the working 
distance of the objective (6-8 mm). The holder should fix the structure during tile-scanning 
in order to avoid motion blur. Tile-scan provides multiple partial images from the sample 
and stitches them together to form a complete image. Using tile-scan, large field of view can 
be scanned with high resolution. A big reservoir is designed inside the holder which is 
connected with the volume around the hydrogel through four indentations. The purpose for 
that was to allow nutrient and oxygen supply to the cells through the hydrogel matrix and 
diluting molecular probes.  Finally the holder was fixed inside a microtitter 6-well plate. 
Further details on the holder design are provided in section 2.4.  
Other techniques used for characterizing cells on PEGDA hydrogel include 
trypsination and viability assay. Further information is provided in section 2.10.

2.2 PREPARATION OF POLYMER PRECURSOR SOLUTION 
The QY concentration determines the penetration depth of light. If concentration is 
too low, the layers do not adhere to each other and if it is too high the designed features 
are not achieved. Hence the concentration of QY has been optimized during this thesis. The 
concentration of other components in polymer solution has earlier been optimized by 
Rujing Zhang to obtain homogeneous hydrogels. In pre-polymer solution, the different 
component concentration used in this study were 5 mg/mL of QY, 5 mg/mL LAP and 200 
mg/mL of PEGDA (MW 700) in Milli-Q water.  
 
2.3 MODIFICATION OF COVERSLIPS 
The 3D structure hydrogels were printed on coverslips. In order to enable coverslips 
adhere to hydrogel, they were modified with a monolayer of silyl methacrylate using 2% 
(v/v) 3-trimethoxysilyl propyl methacrylate. The procedure includes plasma treatment 
(power 50 W) on coverslips for 2 minutes to removes any foreign contaminants present at 
the surface, making it more suitable for further processing and also makes the surface 
hydrophilic. Then coverslips were immersed into 2% (v/v) 3-trimethoxysilyl propyl 
methacrylate solution (in 95/5 ethanol/water with pH adjusted to 4-5 using acetic acid) for 
10 minutes. Samples were flushed three times with ethanol (96%) in order to remove 
unbound molecules. They were then dried in an oven at 100oC for 20-40 minutes.

2.5 FABRICATION TECHNIQUES 
During this study three different printing systems were used. For fabrication of 
hydrogel structures, a modified commercial STL printing system (Envision Tec Micro 405 nm 
illumination) was used which was later replaced by a faster homemade printer (PolyChrome 
- made by professor Niels Bent Larsen). The stand structures were fabricated using the 
makerbot replicator 2. Each of these printers is introduced in following.  
 
2.5.1 ENVISION AND POLYCRHOME STEREOLITHOGRAPHY SYSTEMS  
Fabrication of hydrogel structures were performed using Envision Tec Micro 3D 
printer (410 nm), which is a light projection based STL printing system (Figure 1). This printer 
enables fabrication of square channels with dimensions 100 × 100 µm2. The exposure time 
for each layer was optimized to 60 seconds.  
Since the printing time of the commercial printer was too long, it was replaced by a 
homemade printer, PolyChrome (Figure 8). Polychrome allows fabrication of the hydrogel at 
higher speed and better resolution. It allows fabrication of square channels with 50 µm 
cross-sectional dimension which is very close to the dimensions of capillaries. Polycrome is a 
projection-based micro-stereolithography (µ-STL) with the same working principle as normal 
STL but with higher resolution. The reservoir is filled with polymer precursor solution (Figure 
8 b) followed by exposing light at wavelength 365 nm using a UV LED light source. The digital 
photomasks generated from CAD designs are used to produce cross-sectional images of the 
desired 3D structure and 2D patterns are made from the photosensitive resin. Upon 
movement of the built plate in z-direction, the 2D patterns crosslink to the previous layer 
building a 3D structure. A matlab code controls the light pattern generation and the 
movement of the fabrication platform in the z-direction.   
The exposure time for each layer with this printer is optimized to 3 seconds and the 
step height is set to 25 µm. Since the curing depth is slightly higher that the platform step 
height, good adherence between layers is established. 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
 
2.5.2 MAKERBOT REPLICATOR 2 
Stands (presented in Figure 7) are printed using Makerbot Replicator 2 printing 
system, which is based on fused filament fabrication technique. A continuous filament of 
thermoplastic material is forced out of the heated extruder head onto the building plate. 
The temperature inside the extruder and print speed are 230oC and 150 mm/s respectively. 
Infill density is 50%, filament diameter is 1.77 mm, layer height is set to 0.20 mm, and the 
material used is polylactic acid (PLA).

2.7 MODIFICATION OF PEGDA WITH GELMA  
A cheaper replacement for RGD is GelMA which can be used in higher concentration 
for better adhesion results. From a certain concentration and above, gelatin undergo gel 
transition at room temperature (RT) which is reversible by increasing temperature (physical 
entanglement)59. In addition to physical entanglement, GelMA can also become gel upon 
exposure to UV light in the presence of LAP due to photopolymerization of the methacryloyl 
substituents59. So with GelMA, there is double gelation mechanism. In order to avoid 
gelation during modification, GelMA concentration needs to be optimized for both gelation 
routes.  
 
2.7.1 OPTIMIZATION OF GELMA CONCENTRATION 
For optimization of GelMA concentration, 500 µL of GelMA solution at 
concentrations 50, 25, 12.5, 10 and 8 mg/mL were added into 3 mL glass vials (Figure 9). The 
glass vials were turned upside down for 2 minutes and turned back again. GelMA underwent 
==PAGE:32==

gelation transition at concentration 50 mg/mL within 2-4 minutes. At concentration 25 
mg/mL, the solution became very viscous but did not undergo gel transition. At 
concentrations 12.5 mg/mL and below, no gelation was observed within 2 hours of study. 
Thus concentrations up to 12.5 mg/mL were used in the remaining work.  
As mentioned earlier, GelMA concentration in presence of LAP and light exposure 
needs to be optimized as well. For that, PEGDA solution was prepared (5 mg/mL of QY, 5

inside the light box for cure for 5 minutes. 200 µL of GelMA at concentrations 12.5, 10 and 8

2.10 BIOCHEMUICAL ASSAY 
The most challenging and time demanding part of this project was establishing a 
technique to study cell attachment and morphology on hydrogel surfaces modified with 
different concentrations of GelMA and RGD peptide. Various biochemical assays and 
techniques have been employed to find a suitable method for studying cell behavior.  
Cell viability assay, alamarBlue (AB), was used in this study, that is based on cell 
metabolism and it establishes a quantitative correlation between number of living cells and 
measurable fluorescence from cell metabolic conversation of enzymatic substrates. Since 
the metabolic rates vary significantly between cell types, the metabolism assay must always 
be calibrated with a series of known concentrations of the cell type of interest. Since other 
culture components will give rise to background or attenuation of fluorescence, performing 
an equivalent analysis on culture medium without cells is necessary to compensate for 
those effects. This was performed using the alamarBlue cell viability assay protocol69. For AB 
10% v/v of reagent was added to the well containing cells and after 1h incubation in a cell 
culture incubator (at 37 OC), 100 µL of the supernatant was transferred into a 96 well-plate 
and the fluorescence was measured using the Microplate. The 96 well-plate was kept in a 
fridge until measurement.

CHAPTER 4 DISCUSSION 
 
4.1 CYTOTOXICITY TEST 
The cytotoxicity test was performed on PEGDA hydrogel as explained in section 2.9. Data 
obtained from cytotoxicity test show that our material is not cytotoxic after the 
recommended rinsing procedure explained in section 2.12. Student t-test was performed to 
ascertain statistical significance between cell viability in control (cells seeded in fresh media) 
and the sample of interest (cells seeded in the media which was incubated with hydrogel for 
a week) (Figure 11). By conventional criteria, (P-value=0.5, n=3), the difference between the 
two samples are not statistically significant. Overall, high cellular viability indicated the 
eventual remaining unbound molecules had minimal/no effect on ECs survival.  
 
4.2 SURFACE MODIFICATION WITH RGD  
Lack of cell-responsive features associated with use of PEGDA hydrogel as 
biomaterial, limits cell proliferation, migration, elongation, and viability. We used RGD 
peptides to improve biological responsive of PEGDA hydrogel and studied cell attachment to 
the surface when the RGD peptides are chemically cross-linked onto it. Results presented in 
Figures 13, 14 and 15, have demonstrated that RGD supports cell adhesion and spreading 
onto PEGDA hydrogel. Initially on day 1 of culture, ECs exhibit a round morphology (Figure 
13 (d). Over time cells spread to reach their neighboring cells, forming cell-cell junctions. 
Cells on non-modified PEGDA accumulate forming multilayered cell clusters. Red arrows on 
Figure 13 - f show cells with rounded morphology on RGD modified surface 6 days after 
being seeded. The rounded morphology is likely due to limited cell binding sites at the 
substrate.  
 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
4.3 MODIFICATION WITH GELMA  
Although cells attached to the RGD modified surface, it was observed that cells were 
easily detached from the surface by the smallest shear stress such as changing media from a 
corner of the structure. This is problematic because the final purpose is to perfuse channels, 
and cells should be able to overcome the hydrodynamic forces by changing their 
morphology rather than detaching from the surface. Besides it is not cost effective to use 
high amount of RGD.  GelMA is an inexpensive replacement for RGD, and has longer chains 
that are more available to cells for adhesion. The concentration of GelMA should be 
optimized to remain GelMA in liquid form at RT and during exposure to UV light in the 
presence of a photoinitiator. The optimization results show that the maximum non-gelation 
concentration of GelMA is 10 mg/mL. This matches well with the literature, where several 
authors state 10 mg/mL as the lowest limit for gelation68.  
 
4.3.2 BULK MODIFICATION WITH GELMA 
Results from bulk modification of PEGDA hydrogel with GelMA (Figure 18) were 
similar to those from non-modified PEGDA (Figure 14). Cell adhesion was not established 
and cell clusters were formed on bulk modified hydrogel. One reason behind this could be 
that GelMA concentration used for bulk modification of PEGDA was not enough to 
immobilize sufficient binding sites for cells on the hydrogel surface. Higher concentration of 
GelMA cannot be used as GelMA undergoes gel transition at concentration above 10 mg/mL 
and can therefore not be printed. Overall it is concluded that even with the maximum 
optimized GelMA concentration (10mg/mL), bulk modification of PEGDA hydrogel does not 
establish enough binding sites for cell attachment at the hydrogel surface.  
In many studies, GelMA-PEGDA hybrid hydrogels were synthesized and studied for 
their cell adhesiveness and physical properties. For example in a study by Hutson and his 
colleagues41, 50-150 mg/mL GelMA was added to 100 mg/mL PEG and the cell adhesiveness 
of the hybrid hydrogel was studied. The studies revealed that GelMA enhanced cell 
attachment on hybrid hydrogel with the highest number of attached cells obtained from the 
highest GelMA concentration. The minimum GelMA concentration they used was 50 mg/mL 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
which is 5 times higher than the concentration we used. Hence their success in terms of cell 
attachment is due to the high GelMA concentration they used. Though, this high 
concentration of GelMA is not applicable to our fabrication method.  
 
4.3.3 SURFACE MODIFICATION WITH GELMA 
The surface of PEGDA was modified with 10 mg/mL GelMA and results show that 
cells have attached and spread well onto the surface (Figure 19). It was also observed that 
cells attached to channel sides (Figure 20). Confluent cell layer was established at day 6 by 
increasing the seeding density from 50.000 cells to 60.000 cells.  
Different concentrations of GelMA were used for modification PEGDA surface (Figure 
22). The number of attached cells increased with increasing GelMA concentrations (Figure 
22) and the best result was obtained on 10 mg/mL GelMA coating, where cells formed an 
almost confluent layer on the top of the channels (Figure 22 (j)).  
The main purpose of this study was to make an intact monolayer of ECs inside 
channels of the same magnitude as biological small veins (200 – 300 µm)70. The 
morphologies of ECs are desired to be close to those in biological blood vessels. Most 
endothelia in human body consist of ordered continuous monolayer and it is an essential 
concept of the endothelium as a continuous barrier. ECs  are aligned in the direction of 
perfusion71 and attached to ECM that is rich in collagen, laminin and elastin which give the 
vessels their mechanical properties and regulate cellular communications. Receptors on cell 
membrane (especially integrin receptors) anchor to ECM and determine how cells interpret 
biomechanical and biochemical cues from their surrounding environment72. Hence the 
cellular microenvironment plays an essential role in how successful an in vitro fabricated 
tissue is. It has consistently proven difficult to propagate a functional monolayer of ECs in 
vitro due to failure in preservation of native cell-cell and cell-matrix interactions. In this 
study we did not provide cells with their original microenvironment which include growth 
factors and proteins that are present in subendothelium. Some researchers use a 
commercially available ECM extraction called Matrigel as a successful gel for resembling 
ECM components73,74. Matrigel is taken from mouse tumors and contains proteins such as 
collagen, laminin and growth factors and enzymes and some other unknown molecules and 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
proteins74. It is emphasized that we were not interested in complete recapitulation of the 
biological activities of the native blood vessels. We are rather interested to study cancer 
metastasis across the EC-layer lining inside the closed channels.  For that a confluent 
monolayer of ECs would serve our goal properly. Incorporation of Matrigel to provide cells 
with their biological environment in order to improve their cell-cell contact and cell-matrix 
attachment will be at the expenses of having background signal from Matrigel in our in vitro 
disease model. Since the content of Matrigel is not fully understood, it is not a proper 
material to include into our system. Cell alignment is not the focus of this study since this 
study is performed in a static environment. However it is expected that cells align in the 
direction of blood flow when exposed to unidirectional perfusion inside closed channel. 
It is important to explore the influence of ECM stiffness on tumor progression. 
Various studies have shown that tumor cells migrate in the direction of greater stiffness 
(durotaxis) and they also change morphology toward a more malignant morphology75–79. 
Our system is highly versatile allowing studies of e.g. tumor progression on rigidity gradient 
across the substrate generated by changing exposure time, or varying monomer to cross-
linker ratio. Surface topologies have also shown to affect cell adhesion, proliferation and 
migration, as cell surface receptors sense the topological cues and respond to them by 
reorganizing their cytoskeletal, changing signaling pathways etc. which affects cell fate. In a 
study by P. Kim and his colleges63 it was observed that HUVECs cultures on nano-patterned 
PEGDA-GelMA surface exhibited enhanced cell adhesion and spreading when compared 
with those lacking63. Hence one can employ both surface features and biochemical cues 
(e.g. growth actor) to guide cell behavior.  
 
4.4 CHALLENGES WITH QUANTITATIVE ANALYSIS  
The most challenging and time demanding task in this project was to develop a 
method for quantifying and visualizing cellular behavior on PEGDA hydrogel. Several 
techniques were employed including trypsination, live/dead assays, and staining techniques.  
Performing viability assays on hydrogels remains a large challenge. It was observed 
that the fluorescent product of the enzymatic assay diffuses into the hydrogel, attenuating 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
the signal. The solution to this is to calibrate the assay with a series of known concentrations 
of ECs on hydrogel. This way, assay diffusion into the hydrogel is taken into account. This is 
based on the assumption that the diffusion rate of the viability assay into the hydrogel is the 
same for different samples as the hydrogel composition and environmental factors affecting 
diffusion (e.g. Temperature) are kept constant. Moreover it is important that the cells used 
for calibration have the same growth condition including adhesive state as those used for 
analyzing. Since equivalent growth condition could not be provided for all samples, this 
technique was ruled out for this study because of the complicated viability assay. 
While phase-contrast microscopy offers an alternative way of visualizing the cells on 
hydrogel surface, it is insufficient at quantifying cellular properties. It is also not suitable for 
hydrogels printed with Polychrome printer because they do not possess optimal optical 
properties for phase-contrast microscopy. The voxel size of the printed structure is 14 × 14 
µm2, which is close to the size of ECs (between 10 × 10 and 20 × 20 µm2). This makes the 
visualization of cells on the printed structures challenging (Figure 23). Red circles in Figure 
23 show some of the cells that are not observable on the surface. Cells marked with white

Use of conventional fluorescent microscopy also proves challenging, because some

During this study, it was observed that ECs became rounded in morphology after 
being exposed to staining multiple times (Figure 24). Since it was not possible to rinse the 
staining from the hydrogel after visualization, the dye remained and accumulated inside the 
hydrogel after each time points. It is well-known that organic solvents e.g. DMSO are toxic 
to cells80. The toxic response to DMSO depends on the type of cell and cell line but different 
studies suggest 2.5 mg/mL as the maximum concentration that can be used without toxic 
effect81. In this study, DMSO was used for dissolving dyes and it is expected to diffuse out of 
the hydrogel matrix into the media reservoir. Hence DMSO concentration was too small to 
have any effects on cells.  In this study a new sample was used for every time points.

4.5 PROBLEMS WITH REPRODUCIBILITY 
Unfortunately results in later experiments from PEGDA modification with various 
GelMA concentrations were not reproducible. Cells in all replications died even with the 
maximum GelMA concentration. A representative image from each coating is presented in 
Figure 23. The lack of reproducibility was not studied in depth, but was thought to be due to 
a series of unfortunate circumstances that inhibited the experiments to go their full length. 
One set of experiments was sealed in a plastic box during an incubator cleaning disrupting 
the pH and temporal environment of the cells causing asphyxiation and cell death. A second 
series of experiment was disturbed by improper handling probably causing cell detachment. 
Another hypothesis is that the modification did not work due to improper handling, storage, 
or treatment and GelMA batch to batch variation.

SUMMARY, CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS 
In this study PEGDA hydrogel was used for fabricating open micro-channels of 
dimensions close to a small biological vein (200 - 300 µm) using STL based 3D printer. 
Diacrylate functional group on PEG makes it amenable to photo-polymerization and 
covalent binding to other molecules. PEGDA monomers were covalently bound to each 
other upon exposure to light in presence of the photoinitiator, LAP. The photo absorber QY 
was used to define the vertical printing resolution. The printed hydrogel was tested for its 
cytotoxicity. The data revealed that eventual remaining unbound molecules after rinsing the 
hydrogel as explained in section 2.8, had no effect on cell survival.   
During this study the hydrogel was modified with cell-adhesive sites (RGD and 
GelMA) to enable cell adhesion to PEGDA hydrogel. Modifications with adhesive proteins 
were achieved by functionalizing adhesive proteins with an acrylate moiety, thereby 
enabling copolymerization of adhesive peptide with PEGDA molecules upon photo-
initiation. Cells from cell-line CRL-2922 were seeded and characterized in terms of their 
resemblance to biological endothelium regarding morphology. The most challenging part of 
this project was establishment of a method for quantifying and visualizing cellular behavior 
on PEGDA hydrogel. For that combined confocal and wide field fluorescence microscopy 
was found to be the most optimal method.  
In the present study the PEGDA hydrogel surface was modified with RGD peptide at 
concentration 0.2 and 0.4 mg/mL. On non-modified surface cells accumulated in some areas 
forming cell clusters while on RGD-modified surface cells attached and spread with spindle 
form morphology. Both concentrations showed similar results in terms of cell adhesion, 
hence 0.2 mg/mL was used in the remaining work. Some observations indicated that cell 
binding to RGD peptides was not stable enough for our purpose. The endothelial cell 
monolayer was disturbed or removes cells from the surface by mixing staining inside the 
hydrogel-well or changing media. Besides RGD is expensive and cannot be generously used 
for bulk modification of PEGDA.  
GelMA was used as a more cost effective substitute for RGD. Besides GelMA has 
longer chains and more accessible to integrin receptors on cell membrane. The minimum 
gelation concentration of GelMA was studies and found to be 10 mg/mL. GelMA was used 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
for bulk modification of PEGDA, also called PEGDA-GelMA hybrid hydrogel. Hybrid hydrogel 
of GelMA/PEGDA at concentrations 5/195 and 10/190 mg/mL were prepared. Cells were 
seeded onto the hybrid hydrogel and imaged at day 3 using confocal microscopy. Results 
revealed that GelMA concentration was not enough for immobilizing sufficient binding sites 
at the hydrogel surfaces for cell attachment. PEGDA surface was modified with GelMA at 10 
mg/mL, and cell adhesion was studied at day 3 and 6 using confocal microscopy. Cells 
attached and spread very well. The seeding density was increased from 50.000 cells to 
60.000 cells and a confluent layer was achieved at day 6 of culture.  
The effect of GelMA concentration on cell attachment was studied by modifying 
PEGDA surface with GelMA at concentrations 1, 5 and 10 mg/mL. Cells were seeded on the 
surface at density 50.000 and imaged at day 6 of culture using confocal microscopy. 
Percentage of attached cells was calculated using ImageJ. It was observed that number of 
attached cells increased by increasing GelMA concentration. Besides cells on 10 mg/mL 
GelMA formed an almost confluent monolayer at the top of the channels. 
In future work ECs could be seeded on fenestrated micro-channels (spatially aligned 
pores) to study cancer cell migration into the endothelialized channel through pores 
incorporate in the channels. Spatially aligned pores can expectedly be introduced as an 
integral part of the microchannels design printed by micro-stereolithography. The space 
between the channels (Figure 25) will subsequently be filled by cancer cells encapsulated in 
a natural hydrogel material, e.g. Matrigel or collagen. Figure 25 represents how Rujing 
Zhang is seeding ECs inside close channel modified with 10 mg/mL GelMA (Figure 25 a). The 
seeded structure is immersed in DMEM, enclosed in a box (Figure 25 b). The box is rotated 
every 30 minutes to allow cell seeding on each channel side. Schematic representation of 
the designed structure is presented in Figure 25 (d).

CELL SUBCULTURING82 
Volumes used in this protocol are for 75 sq cm flasks. 
 Remove and discard culture medium. 
 Briefly rinse the cell layer with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to remove all traces 
of serum which contains trypsin inhibitor. 
 Add 2.0 to 3.0 ml of Trypsin-EDTA solution to flask and observe cells under an 
inverted microscope until cell layer is dispersed (usually within 5 to 15 minutes). 
Note: To avoid clumping do not agitate the cells by hitting or shaking the flask while 
waiting for the cells to detach. Cells that are difficult to detach may be placed at 37
°C to facilitate dispersal. 
 Add 6.0 to 8.0 ml of complete growth medium and aspirate cells by gently pipetting. 
 Transfer cell suspension to a centrifuge tube and spin at approximately 125 X g for 5 
to 10 minutes. Discard supernatant. 
 Resuspend the cell pellet in fresh growth medium. Add appropriate aliquots of the 
cell suspension to new culture vessels.  
 Incubate cultures at 37°C. Subculture when cell concentration reaches between 8 X 
104 and 1 X 105 cells/sq. cm. 
 Change media every 2 to 3 days

 Remove the rinsing PBS solution and add the required volume of fresh PBS for 
staining.

Dissolve Calcein-AM pellet in an appropriate amount of DMSO.  Keep

SYNTHESIS OF GELMA83  
First, a 10w/v% gelatin solution was prepared by dissolving gelatin (Type A, 300 
bloom from porcine skin, Sigma) in Dulbelco’s phosphate buffered saline (DPBS) warmed to 
60 OC for 2 h under vigorous stirring. The solution temperature was maintained between 60 
OC and 70  OC during gelation dissolution, after which it was lowered to 50 OC. To produce 
GelMA with a 50% degree of methacrylation, 0.14 mL of methacrylic anhydride was then 
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
added drop-wise to the gelatin solution for each gram of gelatin in solution. The gelatin 
methacrylation reaction was allowed to proceed for 4 h at 50 OC under vigorous stirring. The 
methacrylation reaction was then quenched by dilution of the reaction solution with DPBS 
warmed to 40 OC to yield a GelMA concentration of 4.5 w/v%. To remove excess methacrylic 
acid and methacrylic anhydride, GelMA was precipitated overnight by the addition of ice-
cold acetone to the reacted solution at a 1:4 ratio of GelMA to acetone. Acetone was then 
decanted from the precipitated GelMA, and the precipitate was dried under flowing air for 
30 min before being dissolved in 10w/v% in DPBS warmed to 40 OC. Tis warm GelMA 
solution is vacuum filtered through a 0.2 µm filter, transferred to a 12-14 kDa molecular 
weight cutoff (MWCO) dialysis tubing, and dialyzed agained deionized (DI) water for 3 days 
(the dialysis media was changed twice daily) to remove any remaining methacrylic acid and 
salts from the DPBS. Lastly, the GelMA was frozen overnight at -80 OC, lyophilized for four 
days and stored at -20 OC.
