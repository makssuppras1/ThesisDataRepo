Abstract
The need to limit the rising global temperatures is urgent. Efforts are being made all
over the world to reduce the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and move away from
fossil fuels as much as possible. Electrification of the transport sector can play a big
part towards this. However since this will increase the load on the power system, using
electric vehicles (EVs) to provide grid support services is a good way of helping to keep
the system stable. Though it could affect the battery life of the EV in a negative way.
The main purpose of this study was to first validate/update the battery model pre-
sented in [1] and then analyse the degradation of the battery for different operations -
calendar ageing (parked) only, driving only, frequency regulation only and the combined
operation of driving plus frequency regulation. All the operations were simulated for two
different starting state of charge (SoC) conditions of 80% and 60%. The degradation of
the battery was analysed after one day and 5 years of operation. The driving profile and
the frequency regulation profile was kept constant for each day. Finally the economic
value of providing frequency regulation with the EV was analysed.
The main outcome from the thesis was the validity of the battery model: the model
behaved similar to an actual EV battery of Nissan LEAF 2018. The degradation due to
the combined operation is not too drastic as compared to other individual operations.
The SoC level plays an important part in the overall degradation of the battery - higher
the SoC level of the battery, higher the degradation of the battery. Finally, provid-
ing frequency regulation is economically lucrative for the EV owner, giving profits of
upto 1000 €/year even though there is an approximately 1.5% of extra battery capacity
degradation as compared to when only used for driving.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Climate change is a threat looming large over the future. There is a global effort to reduce
the extent of this change. As of 2017 the global temperature increase has been around 0.8 to
1.2°C from the pre industrial levels which is a cause for concern [2]. If this temperature change
goes beyond 1.5°C it could cause adverse effects on our planet like more extreme conditions in
summer, winter, rains etc. This has a serious impact on the whole ecosystem [3]. Thus there is
an urgency to contain this temperature change to within 1.5 °C. The biggest example of this is
the Paris agreement. Under this agreement the aim is to stay within 2 °C increase in the global
temperatures and to make efforts to pursue the 1.5 °C goal. 195 countries are a part of this
agreement [4]. In recent developments even the big companies (28 companies with combined
market value of $ 1.3 trillion) are committing to this cause of limiting the temperature rise to
1.5 °[5].
Since the transport sector is a huge contributor to the (GHG) emissions ( 24% as of
2016)[6], electrification of this sector will help reduce the GHG emissions considerably. On
top of the Paris Agreement, Denmark has its own ambitious climate policy, in which they
plan to have the energy supply including the transport energy consumption based completely
on renewables by 2050 [7]. This again backs up the fact that EV play a major part in all of this.
The market for the EVs has grown at a considerable rate in the past few years as can be
seen from Figure 1.1 [8]. More penetration of EVs will increase the load on the grid, but the
EVs can be used smartly to support the grid by providing grid support during certain hours
when they are not being used for transportation purpose. This added utility of the EVs could
help them migrate even faster into the transportation network.

1.2
Thesis Objective
The thesis will look into how the battery of the EV responds to different type of uses in terms
of its degradation. After that it will be checked if it is economically viable for a consumer to
use their EV for grid support provision. The battery model being created is trying to emulate
the battery of a Nissan LEAF.
The primary objective is: to validate a battery model of a real EV and analyse its
performance under different uses. Is it technically and economically feasible for
the end user to provide services with the EV? This will be executed in the following
steps:
• How to tweak the given battery model to be closer to the actual battery
of Nissan Leaf? The more accurate the battery model the more realistic will be the
analysis of the battery degradation. However certain limitations will be present as it
might lead to over fitting the model.
• How much does the battery degradation change depending on the use of
the EV? The degradation of the battery will be analysed under 3 different scenarios
- Calendar ageing (parked only), driving only and grid support.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical Background
In this chapter the considered power system (Denmark) and the basic EV concepts are pre-
sented. In section 2.1, the power system and the frequency regulation in Denmark are described.
The next section 2.2 describes the reason for modelling this particular battery and gives addi-
tional info regarding the frequency regulation profile and the driving profile.
2.1
Power System and Frequency Regulation
Denmark’s power system is split into two zones: DK1 i.e the Western Denmark which is a part
of the ENTSO-E RG Continental Europe grid and DK2 i.e the Eastern Denmark which is a
part of the ENTSO-E RG Nordic grid. The total electricity consumption in 2018 for DK1 and
DK2 from different energy sources is shown in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Electricity consumption from different sources for Denmark in 2018
As it can be seen, there is a significant difference in how the electricity is obtained in both
the regions. DK2 relies more on interconnection and fossil fuels whereas DK1 uses the wind
farms a lot more.
==PAGE:26==
6
2 Theoretical Background
This high reliance on renewable energy sources and interconnections affects the stability
and reliability of the grid. To help with the grid stability and reliability, ancillary services are
provided. Ancillary services are grid support services required by the power systems - trans-
mission or distribution system operators (TSOs or DSOs) - to maintain integrity, stability and
power quality or the power system (transmission or distribution system). Ancillary services
can be provided by connected generators, controllable loads and/or network devices. Some
services are set as requirements in Grid Codes and some services are procured as needed by
TSOs and DSOs to keep the frequency and voltage of the power system within operational
limits or to recover the system in case of disturbance or failure [9].
The ancillary services are regulated by the ENTSO-E Continental Europe Operation Hand-
book, the Joint Nordic System Operation Agreement and by Energinet’s regulations for grid
connection.
They are provided by the conventional generators, distributed generators and
CHP plants. These services vary depending on the region they are supplied to.
The different ancillary services provided in Denmark are :
• Primary reserve
• Frequency controlled disturbance reserve
• Frequency controlled normal operation reserve
• Automatic and manual frequency restoration reserve supply ability
Frequency regulation or better known as frequency containment reserve (FCR) is a service
required in both the regions among other services. For DK1 region, FCR is provided by the
TSOs within the ENTSO-E RG Continental Europe’s synchronous area jointly.
The com-
bined requirement of the ENTSO-E RG Continental Europe grid is ±3, 000 MW. Depending
on the production in DK1, its share of capacity to provide this service is determined. In 2017
the requirement was ±20 MW. According to the rules of ENTSO-E RG Continental Europe,
import/export of primary reserves is allowed. For DK1 they are allowed a maximum import/-
export of ±90 MW. The frequency must be supplied for a frequency deviation of up to ±200
mHz relative to the reference frequency of 50 Hz. This implies that in the range of 49.8-50.2
Hz. A deadband of ±20 mHz is permitted. Bids are submitted by players no later than 15.00
on the day before the day of operation and a minimum of 0.3 MW must be bid [10].
In DK2 the FCR-N is provided by the TSOs within the ENTSO-E RG Nordic grid area
jointly. The combined requirement of the ENTSO-E RG Continental Europe grid is 600 MW.

The battery used in Nissan LEAF has prismatic pouch cells. Comparing to the traditional
cylindrical cells, prismatic pouch cells have a lighter housing, this allows them to have a higher
energy density. Adding to this is also the fact that the folded or stacked compound design
of the prismatic pouch cells gives them a higher volumetric and gravimetric energy density [15].

3.2
Battery Capacity Fade Model
The capacity of the battery takes a hit due to two kinds of losses:
1. Calendar Degradation
2. Cycle Degradation
The calendar degradation is the battery degradation caused by internal chemistry of the
battery. It is a function of time, temperature and SoC.
The cycle degradation is the degradation of the battery caused by the charging and dis-
charging of the battery. It is also dependant on the temperature, current and the charging/dis-
charging rate.
3.2.1
Calendar Degradation
Calendar degradation is primarily caused due to evolution of the solid electrolyte interface
(SEI) at the anode due to decomposition of electrolyte.
The formation of this layer leads
to loss of Li material and this loss is irreversible. Thus, it leads to loss of battery capacity.
However, this layer provides kinetic stability to the electrolyte. It leads to reduction of material
loss from the future cycles. This effect is promoted by a high SoC and temperature [25] [26].
The Figure 3.14 shows how temperature and SoC affect the calendar degradation. The power
fade of the battery is not considered since a dynamic internal resistance of the battery is not
considered in this model.

facilitate this, it is assumed that the user will have the same travel habits for the next 5

is a necessary assumption to consider due to lack of actual data for such a long duration of time.

duration than one day, the SoC level keeps on falling if it is left to be developed naturally.

frequency regulation operation start at the appropriate SoC and don’t fall to lower values after

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The work done in this thesis intended to answer if it is technically and economically feasible to
provide frequency regulation along with the normal driving use of the EV and To determine
the impact it would have on the battery capacity as compared to other operating scenarios of
the EV.
The first step was to validate/update the given battery model. Experiments were con-
ducted on an actual Nissan LEAF 2018 to validate and/or modify certain parameters of the
battery model. Pertaining to the electrical parameters, the relationship of the resistance of the
battery with SoC was found and modified in the model. After this with regards to the thermal
parameters of the model, the value for the thermal capacitance and resistance was calculated
and updated in the model which improved upon the battery temperature characteristics. This
would help to get a better evaluation of the capacity degradation of the battery. Another part
added to the model was to provide threshold limits to the SoC modelling of the battery. If
the SoC was allowed to develop naturally it was continuously falling as days progressed due
to energy losses. To avoid this, there was an upper and lower threshold limit was set which
prevented the SoC from either going above or falling below the limits to have proper develop-
ment of the SoC of the battery.
Once the model was working for one day operation, it was simulated for 5 years. The
different operating scenarios simulated were - parked only, driving only, frequency regulation
only and finally the combination of both driving and frequency regulation. The driving con-
sumption was 8.7 kWh per day and the frequency regulation provided was ±9 kW for 14 hours
per day. All the operating scenarios were simulated for two different starting SoC cases of
80% and 60%. This was the SoC value that the battery must have when it is time to start
driving the EV. Hence the frequency regulation is provided while keeping this range in mind.
The combined operation of EV lead to maximum degradation of the battery for both the 80%
and the 60% SoC cases being 10.52% and 7.71% respectively. This was followed by frequency
regulation only, driving only and finally parked only. The difference in capacity degradation
between combined operation and parked only was 2.76% and 3.09% for 80% and 60% SoC
case respectively. Calendar degradation was the primary source of battery degradation, al-
ways accounting for more than 50% of the total battery capacity degradation. The calendar
degradation was found to be decreasing as the time duration increases, with the losses being
almost 4% after 18 months and then being under 8% at the end of 5 years for the 80% SoC
case. Similar trend was found for the 60% SoC case. The two different SoC cases also showed
that maintaining a lower SoC level leads to less calendar degradation and a higher SoC level
increases the calendar degradation. However, for the cycle degradation the different SoC cases
had the opposite impact with 60% SoC case causing higher cycle degradation as compared to
the 80% SoC case. Also the cycle degradation was found to be increasing every year though
==PAGE:88==
68
7 Conclusion
at a decreasing rate.
From the technical results an economic evaluation was performed. Taking the hourly aver-
age price of night time of frequency capacity payments for the past 4 years, the revenues were
found to be 1381 €/year. However while performing frequency regulation the losses experi-
enced due to the efficiency of the charger is an extra cost. These costs amounted to 264 €/year
(industrial prices). Furthermore, using the EV for driving and frequency regulation reduces
the capacity of the battery as compared to parked only scenario and that is also an added cost
which amounted between 80-90 €/year depending on the SoC case. For the combined operation
these costs are higher for the 60% SoC case as compared to the 80% SoC due to the influence
of cycle degradation. Taking into account that cost as well, an EV user can make profits in
excess of 1000 €/year. Further performing sensitivity analysis gave some more positive results.
Sensitivity analysis on the hourly average frequency regulation capacity payment showed that
only if the payment prices fall below 6 €/MW/hour would the EV user experience losses from
performing frequency regulation. Otherwise there is profit to be made. Also the sensitivity
analysis on the efficiency of the charger showed that if the efficiency of the charger goes upto
90% the costs incurred by EV users will decease by 53%.
Overall, the results of this thesis show that using EVs for performing grid services along
with normal driving operation is a feasible prospect. The battery degradation experienced
due to performing frequency regulation along with normal driving operation is not too severe
as compared to isolated operations. Plus it helps the EV user earn money as well. However,
there were limitations to the design of the battery model and the output of the simulations.
A more detailed battery model and daily real life usage data of EV for driving and frequency
regulation would yield a more accurate results.

CHAPTER 8
Future Work
More detailed battery modelling
A more detailed model of the battery can be modelled with more insight on the electrical
and the thermal parameters.
Acquiring data from battery manufacturers or from the EV
manufactures can be a way to approach this if they consent.
This would help model the
battery characteristics more accurately and could lead to more accurate results in regards to
the degradation of the battery.
Driving and frequency regulation profile
This simulation was run using the data only for one day and was assumed to be consistent
for the whole 5 year duration. However, the driving and frequency regulation profiles could
vary almost everyday. Thus, it would provide more details with regards to battery degradation
and can change the loss distribution of the capacity. For starters profiles for a week could be
analysed, then one month, after that 2 and so on. It would useful to have data from different
time duration of the profiles so as to better generalize the degradation.
Length of simulation
The battery degradation analysis can be done till the end of the lifetime of the battery to
see how long the battery can actually last while performing different operations. Or just try
to exhaust the capacity of the battery to get an idea how long the batteries can last while
performing driving and frequency regulation.
