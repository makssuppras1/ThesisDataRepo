Abstract
In this Master Thesis we will see what function ﬁelds are and what properties they
have. We will show how to construct them from algebraic curves. Next, we deﬁne
what algebraic geometry codes are and how to construct them from function ﬁelds.
Lastly we will give two examples of Algebraic Geometry codes (AG codes) from
famous algebraic curves, such as the Klein Quartic and the Hermitian curve.

Introduction
Let us consider a geometric object X such that P1, . . . , Pn ∈X.
Let L be a
vector space over Fq of functions on X that take values in Fq.
The mapping
evP : L →Fn
q deﬁned as evP(f) = (f(P1), . . . , f(Pn)) ∈Fn
q where f ∈L is a
linear mapping. Thus the image of evP, Im evP, naturally induces a linear code.
Using this intuition we see that geometric object arise naturally in coding theory.
Furthermore, deﬁned this generally, we can not say much about the dimension and
minimum distance of Im evP and its dual.
Instead of considering any geometric object X, we now consider X to be a
subset of the aﬃne or projective space as the zeros of some given set of polynomials,
called a variety.
Let now P1, . . . , Pn ∈X be points whose coordinates are in
Fq. The functions will be polynomials or quotient of polynomials, also known as
rational functions. We will call this algebraic geometric (AG) codes if some theory
of X allows us to give bounds on the dimension of the vector space L and the
minimum distance of code.
A simple and classical example of the above is given by Reed-Solomon (RS)
codes. In this case X is the aﬃne line over Fq. The points are n distinct elements
of Fq and L is the vector space of polynomials of degree less that k −1 with
coeﬃcients in Fq. We will show that RS codes have parameters [n, k, n −k + 1] if
k ≤n where n ≤q.
Hence it is natural to consider the case where X is an algebraic curve (aﬃne
or projective). In this case L is the set of functions in an algebraic function ﬁeld.
The idea of using algebraic curves and function ﬁelds was originally ﬁrst explained
by V.D. Goppa in [12] in order to construct AG codes.
Before Goppa’s construction of AG codes, algebraic curves and algebraic func-
tion ﬁelds where mainly used in pure mathematics. Furthermore, the paper [18],
states that all linear codes can be expressed as algebraic geometry codes. This
further motivates the study and closely analyze function ﬁelds which are the basis
of algebraic geometry codes.
The aim of this thesis is the following. We will ﬁrst explore in detail properties
about function ﬁelds, build the divisor group and deﬁne Riemann-Roch spaces
on a function ﬁeld, see Chapter 2. These Riemann-Roch spaces are the building
block of AG codes, hence understanding them is a crucial part to the theory of
AG codes. Subsequently, we will show very brieﬂy how to construct function ﬁelds
from algebraic curves, see Chapter 3. After that in Chapter 4, we ﬁrst show some
basic results of linear codes and then deﬁne what AG codes are precisely. We next,
show some general results of AG codes and then give more concrete results that
are speciﬁc to Rational Codes. Lastly, we ﬁnish the thesis by giving two examples
1

of AG codes over function ﬁelds constructed from algebraic curves and giving a
brief discussion on how to proceed with what we have learned while writing this
thesis.
In order to be able to thoroughly understand this thesis, we expect the reader
to know and understand basic results in ring theory as well as ﬁeld extensions.
Even more, we also presume the reader to know the basics of the projective space
and how to include the aﬃne space into the projective one.

Deﬁnition 2.1.1. Let K be a ﬁeld. An algebraic function ﬁeld F/K of one variable
over K is an extension ﬁeld F ⊇K such that F is a ﬁnite algebraic extension of
K(x) for some element x ∈F which is transcendental over K.
For a given function ﬁeld, F/K, if there exists a transcendental element x ∈F
over K, such that F = K(x), then F/K is called rational.
Rational function
ﬁelds are the simplest example of a function ﬁeld. Rational function ﬁelds will be
explored in detail in Section 2.7, and in Chapter 3 we will show how to construct
function ﬁelds from algebraic curves.
Deﬁnition 2.1.2. Let ˜K := {z ∈F | z is algebraic over K}.
˜K is trivially a
ﬁeld, and is a subﬁeld of F. We will call ˜K the ﬁeld of constants of F/K.
We will show a characterization of the transcendental elements of F over K.
Remark 2.1.3. z ∈F is transcendental over K if and only if the extension
F/K(z) is of ﬁnite degree.
Proof. We have that F is a ﬁnite algebraic extension of K(x) for some x ∈F that
is transcendental over K. Let z ∈F be transcendental over K such that x = z.
We want to show that [F : K(z)] < ∞. Since z ∈F then there exists f(x) ∈K[x]
such that z = f(x). Thus K(x, z) ⊆K(x) and
[F : K(z)] = [F : K(x, z)] · [K(x, z) : K] ≤[F : K(x)] · [K(x, z) : K] < ∞.
Reciprocally, if for some z ∈F we have that [F : K(z)] < ∞and z is algebraic
over K then [K(z) : K] < ∞. Therefore
[F : K] = [F : K(z)] · [K(z) : K] < ∞
This would imply that [K(x) : K] < ∞which is impossible since x is transcen-
dental over K. Thus z can not be algebraic over K, meaning it is transcendental
over K.
Remark 2.1.4. We observe that K ⊆˜K  F.
Furthermore, F/ ˜K is also a
function ﬁeld. We will say that K is algebraically closed in F, or K is the full
constant ﬁeld of F, if K = ˜K.

Deﬁnition 2.1.5. A valuation ring of the function ﬁeld F/K is a ring O ⊆F
with the following properties:
(1) K  O  F.
(2) for every z ∈F we have that z ∈O or z−1 ∈O.
Proposition 2.1.6. Let O be a valuation ring of the function ﬁeld of F/K. Then

(a) O is a local ring, i.e. O has a unique maximal ideal P = O \ O∗, where
O∗= {z ∈O | ∃w ∈O, zw = 1} is the group of units of O.

3.1
Aﬃne Algebraic Curves
Let K be an algebraically closed ﬁeld. The n-dimensional aﬃne space An = An(K)
is the set of n-tuples of elements in K, meaning
An(K) = {(a1, . . . , an) | ai ∈K for all i ∈{1, . . . , n}}.
Deﬁnition 3.1.1. V ⊆An is called an aﬃne algebraic set if there exists M ⊆
K[X1, . . . , Xn] such that
V = {P ∈An | F(P) = 0 for all F ∈M}.
K[X1, . . . , Xn] is the ring of polynomials in n variables.
Remark 3.1.2. Given an aﬃne algebraic set V ⊆An, the set
I(V ) = {F ∈K[X1, . . . , Xn] | F(P) = 0 for all P ∈V }
is an ideal of K[X1, . . . , Xn].
Proof. Let f, g ∈I(V ). Hence, for h := f −g, we observe h(P) = f(P)−g(P) = 0
for all P ∈V , since f(P) = g(P) = 0. Thus h ∈I(V ). Given p ∈I(V ) and
q ∈K[X1, . . . , Xn], we have that w := q · p ∈I(V ).
This is due to w(P) =
q(P) · p(P) = q(P) · 0 = 0.

the AG code C := CL(D, G) over the function ﬁeld F8(X), as G := 10Q where
Q = (0 : 0 : 1) ∈X and D the divisor as the sum of the other 23 F8-points.
In [16], we see that F8(X) has genus 3, as the projective curve has genus 3. Thus
(G) = deg G+1−g = 10+1−3 = 8 since deg G = 10 ≥2g−1 = 2·3−1 = 5. By
Corollary 4.2.3, this gives rise to a code C with parameters [n, k, d] where n = 23,
k = (G) = 8 and d ≥n −deg G = 23 −10 = 13.
4.4.2
Hermitian Curve
Let q be a prime power, and let us consider the perfect ﬁeld Fq2. We will consider
the smooth projective curve, as the zeros of g(x, y, z) = yqz + yzq −xq+1 in the
ﬁeld Fq2. Hence, we will consider the function ﬁeld Fq2(H)/Fq2 where
H = {(x : y : z) ∈P2 | g(x, y, z) = 0}.
It is well known in the literature that the genus of this function ﬁeld is g =

2q(q−1), and that the the polynomial g(x, y, 1) has q3 zeros in Fq2. We will denote
these points as P1, . . . , Pq3. Thus there are a total of q3 + 1 Fq2-rational points.
The last point is obtained by taking z = 0, giving us the point P∞= (1 : 0 : 0).
We will deﬁne the code C := CL(D, G), where D := P1 + · · · + Pq3 and G :=

and d ≥n −deg G = n −m = n −k −g + 1.

5
Discussion
To conclude this thesis, we provide in this section a short discussion on how one
could proceed with everything learned in this manuscript.
We ﬁrst note that we have not examined methods on how to compute the genus
of a function ﬁeld. We can use Plucker’s formula which is explained in [16] to
easily calculate the genus of a plane algebraic curve, which in turn will allow us to
compute the genus of a function ﬁeld. Other known methods are given by studying
the relation between the genera of function ﬁelds in a function ﬁeld extension, see
Chapter 3 of book [21], Baker’s theorem in [1] and [5] or Castelnuovo’s techniques
in [10].
Furthermore, we could also now read the literature on existing AG codes and
try and construct new AG codes which have yet to be explored and analyzed. Many
interesting constructions of AG codes with diﬀerent properties and from diﬀerent
function ﬁelds are in fact available in the literature, see [14], [17], [22], [20], and a
deep investigation of those would be a natural continuation of the work started in
this thesis.
Even more, we have only shown how to construct AG codes. We could also
proceed by trying to learn about algorithms that can decode AG codes, see [9],
[7], [8], [11], [13], [19].
Finally, since it is a growing research ﬁeld, it would be interesting to understand
whether further applications in information theory of algebraic function ﬁelds and
algebraic curves are available and/or can be found. Examples are application in
quantum codes or locally recoverable codes for distributed storage, see [15] [4], [2],
[3].
