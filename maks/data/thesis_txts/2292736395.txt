Abstract
This Master Thesis project deals with the design of plunger-type wedge shaped
devices for ocean-wave generation and absorption.
The devices will be em-
ployed at the wave ﬂume facilities of DTU Section for Fluid Mechanics, Coastal,
and Maritime Engineering and it will be used for the generation and absorption
of deep water waves and breaking waves. We evaluate the kinematics and the
dynamics of the 2D structures through potential ﬂow codes and experimental
tests. We aim to dimension the generation system and to deﬁne an optimal
shape for generation and absorption purposes. We observe that the numeri-
cal potential ﬂow calculations give a good estimate of the actual behaviour of
the structure. Thus, as an optimal design, we deﬁne a concave-shaped wedge
which can generate deep water breaking waves and eﬃciently absorb waves over
a large range of frequencies.
Keywords
Deep water, Breaking Waves, Wave generation, Wave power absorption,
Plunger-type, Wedge, Potential ﬂow.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Concerns continue to grow over fossil fuel combustion and its negative impacts
on the environment, such as CO2 emissions. Renewable energies are one of
the alternative ways that has being explored so far in order to reduce the
reliance on traditional sources of energy. Among all, wind energy has proven
to be reliable, considering its technological advances and developments. Over
the years, the wind power industry has moved from onshore installation to
coastal and oﬀshore solutions. Nowadays, the trend is to go further away from
the shore, deeper in water. Due to higher, more consistent wind speeds on
the sea, oﬀshore wind turbines can generate substantially more energy than
their onshore and coastal counterparts. Oﬀshore wind power plants may reach
capacity factors in the range of 50%. When going in deep water, substructures
have a key role in withstanding the wave load, and their design is currently
a challenge for the oﬀshore wind energy industry. Many researches focus on
developing and testing new substructures which can work in deep water. The
possibility of generating scaled waves which can accurately reproduce deep
water waves is particularly appealing for research purposes.
As oﬀshore wind energy expand further, structures that can absorb wave
energy become increasingly attractive in order to protect oﬀshore objects or
even wind farms from the hydrodynamic loads. For this purpose, the idea is to
design highly eﬃcient wave absorbers whose task is to reduce the hydrodynam-
ics load on the wind turbine substructure, while at the same time generating
useful energy.
Although the strength of the waves challenges the oﬀshore installation, the
power of the waves is an invaluable source of energy. It is a clean technology
with limited environmental impacts. Wave power at a given site is available
90% of the time (Mørk et al., 2010) and wave climate can be forecast 3 days
in advance. The theoretical global wave power ﬂux is estimated to be about
26,000 TWh/year (Mørk et al., 2010), which is the same order of magnitude
as the world’s 2007 electricity consumption. Wave-energy converters (WEC’s)

have both economical and technical potential to serve as clean renewable en-
ergy sources.
Even though underwater generator technologies are still in a
pre-commercialization phase and numerous steps must be completed before
large scale plants can be installed, wave energy conversion oﬀers the highest
theoretical energy potential.
In this project we are working to build a pair of removable, wedge-shaped
devices which can work both as wave generators and wave energy converters.
The wave generators are capable of producing high quality deep water waves.
Thus, this equipment will improve the accuracy of wave generation for research
purposes. The wave energy converters will be used for educational purposes to
enhance the understanding of ocean wave energy extraction amongst engineer-
ing students, practising marine engineers, and renewable-energy developers.
1.1
Motivation
The section for Fluid Mechanics, Coastal, and Maritime Engineering at DTU
uses a range of experimental water tanks to generate realistic currents and
surface waves. Currently, these facilities are primarily used for experimental
studies of sediment transport and coastal protection, which generally require
the production of shallow-water waves. However, present and future research
investigating the performances of ﬂoating renewable energy devices will require
the accurate and repeatable generation of deep-water waves, where the depth
is large compared to the wavelength.
A recent study of an ocean wave energy device used in Flume 1, Building
114 at DTU, indicates that generation of deep-water waves presents some new
challenges using the existing equipment. Flume 1 is a water wave tank which
is 25 m long, 0.6 m wide and 0.8 m deep. A piston wave generator is situated
at one end of the tank, consisting of a vertical plate that oscillates back and
forth horizontally. Water is present both in front and behind this plate. Lin-
ear potential theory is used to calculate the motion of the paddle required to
generate the speciﬁed wave amplitude. The appropriate control signal is sent
to a hydraulic system that drives the paddle.
==PAGE:21==
1.2 Thesis concept
3
Figure 1.1: The existing, horizontally-reciprocating wave paddle (red) [13].
The present piston wave generator has diﬃculties producing deep-water
waves with the requested wave amplitude. These diﬃculties arise from two
sources. Firstly, the horizontal drive causes a standing wave behind the paddle
that can violently disrupt the control of the paddle’s motion. Secondly, the
horizontal motion drives a relevant ﬂux of liquid around the edges of the paddle,
resulting in a signiﬁcant transfer of energy to the generation of vorticity on both
side of the paddle. As a result of these problems, currently, the required wave
amplitude can only be generated by constructing a series of transfer functions
relating the amplitude set point to the desired value. These transfer functions
are time-consuming to construct and may not be stable in the long term.
1.2
Thesis concept
To address the diﬃculties which arise when generating deep water waves, a
wave generator is required that radiates as little energy as possible behind
the paddle, and generates as little leakage as possible around the edge of the
paddle. A promising solution is represented by a plunger-type wedge-shaped
wave generator, whose sketch is presented in Figure 1.2. Inspiration for such
solution is taken from the ”Berkeley Wedge”, designed and tested by Madhi et
al [6]. This device consists of an asymmetric heaving ﬂoat and power-take-oﬀ

(PTO) system. Its geometry ensures minimal eﬀects from viscosity and eﬃcient
energy extraction.

The region behind the wave maker is isolated from the rest of the tank by
a vertical wall. The wall prevents the radiation of waves to develop behind.
Furthermore, using a plunger-type device allows to employ the same ﬂoat for
wave absorption purposes. Coupling two identical plunger-type devices, one
working as generator, the other as an absorber, would be an exceptionally
good way to demonstrate one of the key principles of wave energy extraction,

Linear theory is valid as long as the wave steepness is small and the eﬀects of
second and higher orders related phenomena are negligible. When the condi-

linearised, giving a simple solution for the surface elevation, the velocity poten-

the linear theory is not valid anymore and non-linear eﬀects must be taken into
account.

2.2.3
Deep water breaking waves
When the wave amplitude reaches a critical value, the wave base can no longer
support the top of the wave, thus the wave collapses. Breaking waves are clas-
siﬁed into: depth-induced breaking waves and steepness induced breaking wave.
These two categories classify waves according to the sea depth where breaking
wave occurs. The ﬁrst type of waves develops in deep water while the second
==PAGE:40==
22
2 Theoretical framework: waves and wave eﬀects
type arises in shallow water in the form of spilling breaker, plunging breaker
or surging breaker. Although shallow water breaking waves are more common
than deep water ones, we focus our attention on depth-induced breaking waves.
Deep water breaking waves might drastically aﬀect structures mounted in deep
water, as well as vessels.
Deep water breaking waves are unaﬀected by the sea bed, in contrast to
the shallow case. In deep water, the unidirectional nature of real ocean waves
results in non-linear wave-wave interaction, which ampliﬁes the height of in-
dividual waves, and consequently their steepness. If the steepness of a wave
produced by waves’ interaction becomes suﬃciently large, the waves become
unstable and they break as a result.
Direct study of the hydrodynamics of breaking wave is diﬃcult since it
is an unsteady phenomenon. Empirical criteria have been deﬁned in order to
determine the maximum wave height. We adopt the Battjes criterion [2], which
is valid both for shallow and deep water. Its expression is

The plunger-type structure substitute the existing paddle wave maker because

The present paddle wave generator showed diﬃculties producing deep-water
waves with the requested wave amplitude. As previously explained, we esti-

the tank’s wall and the paddle’s edges. Signiﬁcant energy variations linked to

results and experimental results not satisfactory.

control of the wave generator as well as the quality of the generated waves. A
plunger-type wave generator should represent an improvement with respect to
the paddle wave maker. Firstly, the region behind the ﬂoat can be isolated
so that here standing waves do not develop . Secondly, the heave motion of
the ﬂoat should reduce the edge ﬂux and, as a consequence, the generation of
vorticity.
At the same time, the plunger-type device represents a promising solution
for wave power absorption purposes. Indeed, we can design an asymmetrical
ﬂoat which allows an an energy capturing eﬃciency grater than 50%. Further-

and, as a consequence, minimal energy losses.

4.2.1
Domain deﬁnition and geometry of the wave absorber
OceanWave3D solves the radiation problem in a ﬁnite 2D domain. The length
and the discretisation of the domain are case related. The deﬁnition of the
domain depends, for example, on the desired accuracy, on the dimension of the
analysed structure or on the frequency of the generated wave.
The structure is interpreted by OceanWeve3D using the immerse boundary
method. The idea is to separate the points of the domain which belong to
the body from the points of the domain which belong to the ﬂuid. Therefore
quantities such as velocity potential and free surface elevation are exclusively
computed for the points belonging to the ﬂuid region.
Computational and Physical domains
The solution for the velocity potential is found in the ﬁnite 2D physical domain
x×z = [0, Lx]×[−h, η(t)]. The physical domain is mapped into a computational
domain: ¯x× ¯z = [0, 1]×[0, 1]. While the physical domain varies along the time,
the computational domain is time-independent. The use of the computational
domain allows an important saving of the computational cost: diﬀerential op-
erators which solve the radiation problem are computed once along the entire
routine.
A mapping function is used to move from the physical domain to the com-
putational one. In the x-direction, the physical grid is simply scaled in order
to ﬁt the computational grid. So, in the x-direction an even spaced grid is
mapped into another even spaced grid. In the z-direction, the domain extends
from the sea bottom to the free-surface, which is time dependent. It is conve-
nient to make a change of variable which maps the time-variant solution to a
time-invariant domain using the non-conformal σ-coordinate transformation

Domain Boundary condition
In order to solve the radiation problem for ﬁnite depth waves travelling in a

The radiation boundary condition is imposed on the right end wall of the
tank. This condition states that the wave associated with the radiation velocity
potential must radiate away from the body. For a ﬁnite domain, such as the
OW3D one, introducing the radiation condition avoids wave reﬂection, thus
the formation of standing waves in the tank domain. The radiation condition
is based on the fastest speed in the domain, v = √gh and will let the long-wave
energy escape the domain. While this boundary condition works eﬀectively for
the longer waves, it does not do so for the shorter waves. A further restriction
is thus implemented in order to avoid reﬂection phenomenon. The simulation
runs for a limited period of time so that the waves travelling with velocity
equal to the waves celerity, v = c, do not hit the right wall, generating a
standing waves. It is proven that the combination of this conditions makes
the reﬂection negligible phenomenon. Although the domain is ﬁnite, inﬁnite
domain condition can be recreated.

7.1
Optimization criteria and geometrical constraints
Inspired by the Berkeley Wedge concept and the ”Wave Bridge” ﬂoat’s shape,
we design a 2D geometry through an optimization process. The objective func-
tion of the optimization depends on the ﬁeld of application of the device.
The ﬁrst and most important requirement imposed to the ﬂoat is related to
its capability of generating breaking waves for a large span of frequencies. The
ﬂoat needs to be able to generate waves approaching the breaking wave limit
for the widest range of frequencies. For the triangular wedge, low frequency
breaking waves cannot be generated as the amplitude of the motion of the
ﬂoat exceeds the geometrical constraint imposed by the clearance between the
bottom of the tank and the lowest edge of the wedge.
We design diﬀerent
geometries in order to move this limit to lower frequencies and improve the
capabilities of the wave generator.

As a second characteristic, the maker should generate waves whose pro-
ﬁles well resemble ﬁnite-depth waves. This means that the eﬀects of the wave
structure interaction of the velocity ﬁeld should not drastically inﬂuence the
generated waves.
At the same time, the ﬂoat should eﬀectively work as a wave power ab-
sorber for both irregular and monochromatic waves.
We refer to the wave
absorber as the device which couples the ﬂoat and a stiﬀness-damping PTO.
When working in an irregular wave environment, the device should be able to
work for the widest range of frequencies. In this way, a higher portion of the
energy of the incoming waves could be absorbed and converted into electrical
energy. Thus, maximizing the range of frequency where breaking waves can
be generated turns out to be, at the same time, a functional feature for the
wave absorption in irregular waves. On the other hand, the ﬂoat can work to
absorb monochromatic waves, and the best performance is obtained when the
coupled PTO system can be tuned to resonance at diﬀerent frequencies. In res-
onance conditions, the wave maker over-excites the structure motion and the
absorbed energy is maximized. The PTO system can be tuned to resonance if
the optimal required PTO stiﬀness coeﬃcient is positive. As an additional, but
not restrictive requirement, we prescribe that the PTO system can be tuned
to resonance for the widest range of frequencies.
A further constraint to the choice of geometry comes from the attempt of
minimizing viscous dissipation. The Berkeley Wedge ﬂoat tested at University
of California [6] assures minimal inﬂuence from viscosity thanks to the high cur-

theory and we do not perform viscous calculations. In order to limit viscous
eﬀects we select, by visual inspection, a geometry whose lowest edge is similar
to the Berkeley wedge one. However, we stress that only viscous calculations
or experiments can conﬁrm the negligible eﬀects of viscosity on the problem.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion and
future work
In the project we investigated the design of wave makers and wave energy con-
verters for deep water waves. The devices are movable structures and they can
be used in Flume 3, Building 114 at DTU in order to improve the wave ﬂume
facilities of the DTU Section for Fluid Mechanics, Coastal, and Maritime En-
gineering. Beneﬁciaries of the project will be engineering students, practising
marine engineers and renewable-energy developers.
Firstly, we focused the attention on the design of a simple wave maker,
namely a triangular plunger-type device. We performed a numerical analysis
of the wave-structure interaction which provided us with a complete character-
isation of the wave maker kinematics and dynamics. Results of the numerical
analysis were used to dimensionalise the wave generation system.
Then, we conducted experiments in Flume 3 at DTU in order to test the
newly designed device, and we performed a comparison between the results
of the numerical analysis and the experimental data. The alignment between
numerical and experimental results could be improved using more sophisticated
measurement equipment.
Next, we designed a wave energy converter for absorption of monochro-
matic as well as irregular waves by coupling the plunger-type wedge with a
PTO. We developed a code that can solve the diﬀraction problem and can thus
be employed for the characterisation of the wave power absorber. Here, the
generation-absorption combination for terminator-type devices is exploited in
order to validate the code. Moreover, we investigated diﬀerent setups of the
WEC working in irregular waves as we attempt to deﬁne the optimal conﬁgu-
ration for wave energy absorption. We observed that the performance of the
absorber depends on the dynamics of the structure as well as on the irregular
wave sea-state.
Finally, diﬀerent shapes for the ﬂoat working both as a wave generator and
==PAGE:140==
122
8 Conclusion and future work
a wave energy absorber were analysed. We aim to obtain a device that can
accurately generate deep water waves and breaking waves. At the same time,
low forces need to be applied by the wave maker actuator and good perfor-
mance is expected by an identical ﬂoat working as a WEC. We opted for a
concave-shaped wedge whose geometry is strictly related to the velocity ﬁeld
of the generated waves.
The observations and the results we obtained during this project bring us
to some improvements or future work that would be interesting to investigate
:
• OW3D currently performs wave-structure interaction analysis for linear
waves. In order to obtain a characterisation of devices generating and
absorbing non-linear waves, OW3D should be further extended in order
to perform analysis when non-linear waves are involved. The develop-
ment of a OW3D version based on non-linear waves theory is in progress.
Therefore, the analysis of the kinematics and dynamics of the structure
we performed through a linear version of OW3D should be implemented
with the upcoming non-linear version.
• Experiments have been performed with a very crude actuation and force
measurement system. The next step is to perform tests with more sophis-
ticated facilities. In detail, a motor should be used in order to displace
and control the movement of the ﬂoating structure. Furthermore, the
data acquisition system should be improved to gather information about
the applied ﬂoat displacement and actuator force. Finally, a wave eleva-
tion controller could be added in the tank in order to continuously adjust
the ﬂoat movement.
• The diﬀerent wedge shapes we described and analysed for the geome-
try optimization, as well as the structure working as a WEC, could be
analysed through experimental tests.
• The analysis of the kinematics and dynamics of the structures should
be performed through CFD viscous calculations. This would provide an
estimate of the impact of viscous forces on problem.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, 
education, innovation and public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new 
opportunities and technology for the global and Danish exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key 
technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind energy and provides 
the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within 
nine research programmes organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and 
Basics for wind energy.
