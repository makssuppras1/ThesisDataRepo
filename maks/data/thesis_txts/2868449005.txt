Abstract
Visual Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (vSLAM) is a crucial technology for auôÙ
tonomous mobile robots, capable of realôÙtime localization and environment reconstruction
using cameraôÙbased perception. This thesis investigates and compares stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart
vSLAM algorithms, contrasting traditional featureôÙbased methods, such as ORBôÙSLAM3
and RTABôÙMap, with emerging differentiableôÙoptimizationôÙbased approaches. These inôÙ
clude NeRFôÙbased models such as LoopyôÙSLAM, as well as methods leveraging 3D
Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), including PhotoôÙSLAM and GSôÙICPôÙSLAM. The study evaluôÙ
ates these methods in terms of accuracy, robustness, computational efficiency, and pracôÙ
tical deployment feasibility.
Through a systematic literature review, TUM and Replica were identified as primary benchôÙ
marks for evaluating localization and photorealistic reconstruction, while KITTI provided
additional realôÙworld complexity. Quantitative evaluations revealed that traditional SLAM
pipelines remain more robust and resourceôÙefficient, delivering realôÙtime localization on
CPUôÙlimited systems. Conversely, emerging methods enable richer, more photorealistic
mappings but demand highôÙend GPU resources, making them less suitable for extended
deployments in larger environments and resource constrained platforms.
Building on these findings, the thesis implements and tests PhotoôÙSLAM, a decoupled
3DGSôÙbased framework that inherits frontôÙend tracking from ORBôÙSLAM3. Simulations
in Gazebo facilitated safe integration into the Robot Operating System (ROS) navigation
stack, while realôÙworld experiments on the Clearpath Husky UGV validated the methodãs
potential and limitations. Tests on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin demonstrated realôÙtime
tracking capability but showed that mapping remains bottlenecked by optimization and
high memory usage.
Ultimately, although emerging 3DGS approaches exhibit remarkable mapping fidelity and
open avenues for advanced tasks such as novel viewpoint synthesis, they currently lack
the efficiency and robustness needed for broad onôÙrobot applications. Future research
integrating multiôÙsensor fusion, hardwareôÙspecific optimization, and improved memory
management is poised to bridge this gap, paving the way for more powerful, realôÙtime
SLAM solutions in mobile robotics.

Introduction
Autonomous robots have become increasingly widespread in fields ranging from wareôÙ
house logistics to selfôÙdriving vehicles. A core requirement for these systems is the ability
to navigate their surroundings without human intervention. At the core of this challenge
lies Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM), a fundamental technology that enôÙ
ables robots to build an internal representation of the environment while tracking their own
pose in real time. Over the past two decades, research on Visual Simultaneous LocalôÙ
ization and Mapping (vSLAM) ã which relies primarily on camera data ã has made sigôÙ
nificant progress, yielding robust and accurate systems. These techniques have proven
especially effective when fused with additional sensors such as an Internal Measurement
Unit (IMU) to enhance pose estimation and drift correction [1, 2].
Despite the progress, SLAM research remains a rapidly evolving field and is still facing
challenges related to dynamic environments, largeôÙscale mapping, multiôÙagent cooperaôÙ
tion, and sensor fusion strategies [3]. At the same time, emerging representations such
as Neural Radiance Field (NeRF), Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), and 3D Gaussian SplatôÙ
ting (3DGS) offer a leap forward in capturing detailed, highôÙfidelity 3D information. These

2.1
FrontôÙend
The frontôÙend of a SLAM system is responsible for processing raw sensory data to exôÙ
tract meaningful features and estimate the cameraãs motion. This plays a crucial role in
building a reliable representation of the environment and the camera trajectory, providing
the foundational data necessary for the backôÙend processing to create a consistent and
accurate map.
2.1.1
Visual Sensing
To achieve effective motion estimation and feature tracking, the choice of visual sensors is
paramount. Visual sensors used in visual SLAM are typically categorized into monocular,
stereo, and RGBôÙD (RedôÙGreenôÙBlue Depth) cameras, based on how they capture visual
information. Advances in computer technology and electronics have led to significant
improvements in camera resolution, pixel accuracy, and focus, making them costôÙeffective
and accurate solutions for SLAM applications [2].
2.1.2
Visual Odometry
One of the primary objectives of Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) is to esôÙ
timate a globally consistent camera trajectory within a map. To achieve this, the system
incrementally estimates the local trajectory through odometry or tracking. By decoupling
this process from mapping and executing it in parallel, performance is significantly imôÙ
proved as it avoids the need to update the map with every new frame. This paradigm of
parallelized tracking and mapping was first introduced in Parallel Tracking and Mapping
(PTAM) [8].
In visual odometry, two primary approaches are commonly used: FrameôÙtoôÙFrame (F2F)
and FrameôÙtoôÙMap (F2M). FrameôÙtoôÙFrame (F2F) estimates the motion by comparing the
current frame against the previous frame, while FrameôÙtoôÙMap (F2M) compares the curôÙ
rent frame against a local map constructed from features of past frames.
FrameôÙtoôÙFrame estimation (F2F)
In F2F, the visual odometry system computes the relative rigid body transformation Tkã1,k
between consecutive frames k and kã1. The global camera poses C0:k are then obtained
by concatenating these relative transformations as follows [9]:
Ck = Ckã1Tkã1,k
This approach is computationally efficient but is inherently prone to drift due to the accuôÙ
mulation of errors over time, especially in long trajectories without loop closures or global
corrections.
FrameôÙtoôÙMap estimation (F2M)
F2M alignment, the current frame is compared against a local map that has been built
using features extracted from previously observed frames. This allows the system to
achieve more robust localization by anchoring the current pose to a broader spatial conôÙ
text, thereby reducing the susceptibility to drift compared to F2F methods. The local map
can either consist of sparse features or a dense representation depending on the SLAM
method being employed [10].
Additionally, depending on the method for motion estimation, visual odometry can be perôÙ
formed using featureôÙbased techniques (indirect methods) or by directly using image inôÙ
tensity values (direct methods) between adjacent frames [10].
2.1.2.1
FeatureôÙpoint (Indirect method)
The choice of feature extraction method plays a crucial role in the performance and roôÙ
bustness of visual odometry. In the featureôÙpoint method, sparse features are extracted

from images using a feature detector, followed by the assignment of feature descriptors.
Subsequently, matching is performed between frames based on these descriptors. The
cameraãs position and orientation are then estimated by applying geometric constraints,
such as the epipolar constraint [2]. The featureôÙpoint method can deliver real time perôÙ

Feature Detection
The feature extraction pipeline begins with a feature detection algorithm to identify key
features in the image. These features must be efficiently extracted and possess distinctive
characteristics. Corner detectors are particularly effective for this purpose, as they are
computationally lightweight and capture intensity changes in all directions, unlike edge
detectors, which lack information along the edge direction. Robust and distinctive corner
features are essential for many visual odometry and SLAM tasks.
For instance, ORBôÙSLAM employs the Features from Accelerated Segment Test (FAST)
algorithm to detect corners [6], while RTABôÙMap uses Good Features to Track (GFTT)
[10], an extension of ShiôÙTomasiãs corner detection based on Harris Corner Detection.
While the FAST algorithm detects all corners in the image, GFTT ranks detected features
by quality, allowing the selection of a specified number of highôÙquality features and disôÙ
carding lowerôÙquality ones. This ensures that only the most reliable features are retained
for further processing.
Feature Descriptor
Once the features have been identified, a more robust description is required. At this
stage, only the locations of the features have been extractedëƒ however, for effective
matching, it is essential to have points that remain stable under changes in perspective.
Various types of feature descriptors exist, with commonly known examples including Scale
Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT), Speeded Up Robust Features (SURF), and Oriented
FAST and Rotated BRIEF (ORB). A shared characteristic of these favored descriptors is
their scale and rotation invariance. While SIFT is a robust descriptor, it is computationally
intensive. SURF builds upon SIFT by addressing the speed and computational efficiency
drawbacks. In contrast, ORB employs the FAST algorithm for feature detection and incorôÙ
porates a rotation of the Binary Robust Independent Elementary Features (BRIEF) test
pattern to achieve descriptor rotation invariance. This makes ORB lightweight and suitôÙ
able for realôÙtime applications [2]. ORBôÙSLAM employs ORB descriptors primarily due to
their fast extraction time. ORBôÙSLAM requires a feature extraction method that processes
each image in under 33 ms, whereas methods like SIFT and SURF are reported to take
approximately 300 ms per image [7]. RTABôÙMap employs BRIEF descriptors but supports
all the variety of descriptor types available in OpenCV [10].
Feature Matching
To estimate the camera pose, it is necessary to match the feature descriptors extracted
from two or more frames to identify corresponding features. These matched feature deôÙ
scriptors can then be utilized to estimate the camera pose by applying constraint relationôÙ
ships among the identified features. ORBôÙSLAM matches features using the Hamming
distance. Since ORB features are represented as binary strings, the Hamming distance
allows for bitwise comparison. The distance is determined by counting the number of
bits that differ between two descriptors [6]. Matching between frames and the map in
RTABôÙmap is performed through a nearest neighbor search, with the Nearest Neighbor
Distance Ratio (NDDR). In this process, the extracted image features are compared to

Pose Estimation
The pose estimation technique used in SLAM is influenced not only by the type of SLAM
algorithm but also by the algorithmãs current state. An initial pose estimate is computed
and passed to the next stage of the pipeline for further refinement. This refinement of the
estimate typically relies on the PerspectiveôÙnôÙPoint (PnP) algorithm with Random Sample
Consensus (RANSAC) then subsequently optimized with Local Bundle Adjustment. PnP
refers to the problem of estimating the relative pose between a calibrated camera and a
set of n 3D points in the scene [9].
The approach depends on the tracking state. For example, in ORBôÙSLAM, if tracking in
the previous frame was successful, a constant velocity motion model is used to predict
the current camera pose. This prediction guides a more efficient search, minimizing the
need for extensive feature matching before PnP is applied for pose estimation. If trackôÙ
ing is lost, a more exhaustive search across all keyframes is performed to find matches
and correspondences, identifying relevant map points for PnP. Other SLAM systems may
follow a similar approach but with variations depending on their specific algorithms. Both
ORBôÙSLAM and RTABôÙMap utilize PnP for camera pose estimation, followed by Local
Bundle Adjustment to optimize the pose and map points [6] [10].

2.2
Loop Closure Detection
Visual Odometry (VO) on its own can be considered a dead reckoning approach, where
errors accumulate over time without feedback. While VO can serve as a standôÙalone
solution, it is the integration of loop closures that significantly improves the accuracy and
robustness of SLAM, by reducing error both in the map and trajectory. This is in broad
terms done by finding a revisited area in the frontôÙend and then optimizing the map and
trajectory in the back end.
Loop closure can be divided into four main steps:
1. Image representation: Features are mapped into visual word vocabulary in Bag of
Words (BoW) to efficiently encode and compare frames.
2. Loop candidates selection: Based on appearance, the histogram of the current
frame, generated by BoW, is compared to the keyframe histogram database.
3. Loop transformation: Estimating the relative transformation (rotation and translation)
between the current frame and the selected keyframe(s), typically using featureôÙ
based methods. This step is often processed in the backôÙend to refine the loop
closure hypothesis.
4. Loop verification: Based on geometric representation the transform is evaluated
(often using RANSAC)

tection by discretizing visual features into a histogramôÙbased representation. Keyframes

2.3.1
Filter based
FilterôÙbased SLAM methods use probabilistic filters, such as the Extended Kalman FilôÙ
ter (EKF) or Particle Filters, to estimate the robotãs state and map recursively. By modeling
SLAM as a Markov process, these methods simplify state estimation and are particularly
suitable for realôÙtime applications in smallerôÙscale environments. However, filterôÙbased
SLAM faces significant limitations, including computational inefficiency as the number
of landmarks increases, due to the quadratic growth of the covariance matrix, and the
approximation errors introduced by linearizing nonôÙlinear motion and observation modôÙ
els. These limitations make filterôÙbased methods less suitable for largeôÙscale or highly
nonôÙlinear environments. While this thesis primarily focuses on optimizationôÙbased techôÙ
niques, a more detailed description of filterôÙbased methods can be found in Appendix
A.2.
2.3.2
Optimization based
OptimizationôÙbased methods, which are more commonly used in modern SLAM systems,
rely on graphôÙbased optimization. In this approach, the poses of the robot and constraints
between them are represented as a graph, where nodes represent poses (keyframes),
and edges represent spatial constraints (transforms). A common method for optimizing
this graph is nonlinear leastôÙsquares optimization (e.g., using g2o or Ceres solvers), which
iteratively minimizes the error between connected nodes to refine both the trajectory and
map.
2.3.2.1
Keyframe Management
In optimizationôÙbased SLAM, keyframe management plays a crucial role in ensuring that
the system remains computationally efficient while maintaining accurate map and pose
estimates.
Unlike filterôÙbased approaches, which maintain continuous state updates,
optimizationôÙbased methods rely on selective representation of the environment by usôÙ
ing keyframes which is a subset of all frames that capture the most relevant information
about the environment. Thereby keyframes serve as anchor points in the optimization
process, reducing the amount of data needed to be processed in backôÙend optimization.
Selecting the right keyframes is critical for achieving both accuracy and efficiency in largeôÙ
scale environments.
ORBôÙSLAM is an example of an optimizationôÙbased system that uses keyframe manageôÙ
ment extensively. In ORBôÙSLAM, keyframes are added quickly due to a low threshold
for inclusion, ensuring detailed mapping. However, they are also removed rapidly using

By carefully selecting and managing keyframes, optimizationôÙbased SLAM systems like
ORBôÙSLAM and RTABôÙMap achieve realôÙtime performance without overwhelming the
computational resources. This is especially important in largeôÙscale environments, where
storing and optimizing every frame would be computationally prohibitive.
2.3.2.2
Loop Closure, Pose Graph optimization & Full BA
Keyframe selection is essential not only for ensuring efficient map creation but also for faôÙ
cilitating loop closure and graph optimization ôÙ two critical backôÙend processes in optimizationôÙ
based SLAM. These processes help improve the accuracy of the robotãs localization and
the consistency of the generated map. In ORBôÙSLAM, loop closing is performed in two
steps, first, a loop has to be detected and validated, and second, the loop is corrected
optimizing a pose graph [6].
Graph optimization is the central framework that involves adjusting camera poses to miniôÙ
mize errors in transformations between keyframes. In ORBôÙSLAM, Pose Graph OptimizaôÙ
tion (PGO) is executed, when a loop closure is detected and validated, and it distributes
the loop closing error along the graph [7]. In addition to the PGO, global BA is also applied
afterwards. Applying both enhances the overall accuracy of the map and robot poses,
correcting errors across the entire trajectory.

Theory: 3D Scene Representation
The methods discussed in this chapter focus on advanced techniques for representing
and reconstructing 3D environments in SLAM systems. Traditional methods like Structure
from Motion (SfM) photogrammetry widely used in offline 3D reconstruction are generally
unsuitable for realôÙtime applications and fall short in terms of realistic and novel view
synthesis quality [15, 16].
Among the most influential recent developments in this area are Neural Radiance Fields
NeRFs and Gaussian Splatting 3DGS. These approaches have drawn significant attenôÙ
tion due to their ability to deliver highly detailed and realistic scene reconstructions, esôÙ
tablishing themselves as central innovations in the field [5].
NeRFs use implicit representations, encoding the scene directly within the weights of a
neural network. By training on a set of input frames and their poses, the network can genôÙ
erate highly realistic images of the scene from any novel viewpoint [17]. In contrast, 3DGS
takes a more explicit approach by using point clouds to place the mean of anisotropic 3D
Gaussians within the scene. Thereby it only requires optimization of the parameters of the
Gaussians and no MLPs and thus improving computational efficiency significantly. AddiôÙ
tionally, the discrete structure of 3DGS makes it straightforward to modify or edit specific
parts of the scene, adding practical flexibility [18].
The next section introduces the concept of radiance fields, which provides a useful frameôÙ
work for understanding how light is distributed within 3D scenes. This framework is key
to both implicit methods like NeRFs and explicit ones like 3DGS.
3.1
Radiance Fields
A radiance field is a mathematical representation that describes how light is distributed in
a 3D scene. It captures the interactions of light with various surfaces and materials in the
environment [4].
Formally, a radiance field is defined as a function L : R5 ãR+. This means that for any
given point in space (x, y, z) and a specific direction indicated by spherical coordinates
(ö¡, ü), the function L(x, y, z, ö¡, ü) returns a nonôÙnegative value representing radiance.
In the context of rendering, this radiance value indicates the amount of light traveling in a
particular direction from that point in space. Essentially, it helps to determine the color of
a pixel in the final image, modeling how intense the light is as it moves from any point in
the scene toward the viewer [4].
3.1.1
Implicit vs Explicit Radiance Fields:
Radiance fields can be divided into two main categories: implicit and explicit. Each type
has its own characteristics regarding how scenes are represented and processed.
3.1.1.1
Implicit Radiance Fields
Implicit radiance fields, applied in NeRFs, do not explicitly store information about how
light is distributed in a scene. Instead, they calculate the radiance at any point by queryôÙ
ing a neural network (MLP). This method allows for highôÙquality rendering but can be
computationally demanding. The representation can be defined as:

3.2
NeRFs
NeRFs introduced by [17] fundamentally reconstructs 3D scenes using neural networks.
It leverages MLPs to approximate a function that maps a 5D input, which consists of the
3D spatial position x (x, y, z) of a point in space and a 2D viewing direction d (ö¡, ü), to the
sceneãs visual and geometric properties. As mentioned in Section 3.1.1.1 NeRF leverages
radiance fields to generate highly realistic scenes. Specifically, NeRF models each pointãs
volume density (how solid or transparent the point is) and RGB color (the emitted light at
that point), thereby capturing both the shape and appearance of the scene. After training a
NeRF to represent a 3D scene, the process of generating an image from a novel viewpoint
(inference) can be broken down into the following steps:
Step 0: Scene Representation
The 3D scene is represented by a neural network (typically a MLP) that predicts the volôÙ
ume density and RGB color for each point in space. The input to the network consists
of the 3D position of a point and the 2D viewing direction. To ensure that the network
can capture highôÙfrequency details, these inputs are first transformed through positional
encoding, which helps the network differentiate fine details in the scene.
Step 1: Ray Casting
Given a camera position and viewing direction, Point of View (POV), rays are cast from
the camera through each pixel of the desired output image. Each ray passes through
the scene, sampling multiple points along its path. These sampled points serve as query
inputs to the network, representing the location in the 3D scene, where each point will
influence the final pixels of the image. This is illustrated in Figure 3.1.
Step 2: PointôÙwise Prediction
For each sampled point along a ray, the network predicts two key properties:

==PAGE:30==
1. Volume Density: How much matter or opacity exists at the point, indicating whether
it contributes to the structure of the scene.
2. RGB Color: The light emitted by the point when viewed from the current camera
direction.
Step 3: Volume Rendering
To compute the final color for each pixel, volume rendering is applied along the ray. This
technique simulates the accumulation of light along the ray by performing integration on
the predicted colors and densities of the sampled points. The volume density controls
how much each point contributes to the final pixel color, with nearer, denser points having
a stronger influence.
Step 4: Image Formation
The result is a 2D image, synthesized by combining the contributions from all rays traced
from the cameraãs position through the scene, effectively rendering a view of the 3D scene
from that particular angle.
NeRFs excel at accurately modeling complex 3D scenes and capturing fine details. HowôÙ
ever, as these highôÙlevel steps demonstrate, the process involves significant computaôÙ
tional overhead, as it requires casting rays for each pixel, sampling numerous points along
each ray, predicting both density and color for every point, and then integrating this inforôÙ
mation to generate a single 2D image. The following will delve into key aspects of NeRFs,
providing a more detailed evaluation of their underlying mechanisms and relevant theoôÙ
retical components.
3.2.1
Network architecture
The originally proposed network architecture of NeRFs consists of a MLP with 10 fully
connected layers, with nine of them containing 256 channels and one with 128. Rectified
Linear Unit (ReLU) activation functions are applied between all layers, except for the
secondôÙtoôÙlast layer, where a sigmoid activation function is used to output the volume
density. Additional enhancements, such as skip connections, concatenation at specific
layers and positional encoding to input vector, are also incorporated [17].
NeRFãs use of deep learning is quite different from traditional applications. Typically, deep
learning models are trained to generalize across a variety of scenarios and use cases.
However, in NeRF, the model is intentionally overfitted to a specific scene or environment,
enabling the fully connected network to effectively represent or describe the particular
scene for the purpose of synthesizing novel views.

point and its surrounding area to the scene structure. Based on these outputs, the samôÙ

then uses this more informed sampling to make a more accurate prediction, leading to
improved rendering quality and efficiency.[17]. This is illustrated in Figure 3.2, where uniôÙ

3D Gaussian Splatting in SLAM
3D Gaussian Splatting has seen advancements in several areas, including dynamic scene
reconstruction, largeôÙscale scene reconstruction, SLAM, and autonomous driving appliôÙ
cations. In the context of this thesis, the use of 3DGS in SLAM is particularly relevant. The
fundamental goal of SLAM is to create a map of an unknown environment while simultaneôÙ
ously determining the deviceãs location within that map in real time [4]. SLAM presents sigôÙ
nificant challenges for computationally demanding scene representation methods, such
as the neural implicit techniques discussed in Section 3.2.3. Traditionally, SLAM systems
have used point clouds, surfel clouds, or voxel grids to represent environments. In conôÙ
trast, 3DGS leverages anisotropic Gaussians to achieve a more realistic representation
[4]. Recent implementations of SLAM with 3DGS ã such as GSôÙSLAM, PhotoôÙSLAM, and
GSôÙICPôÙSLAM ã demonstrate the promising potential and versatility of this approach.
One intuitive application of 3DGS in SLAM is the use of 3D gaussians as the fundamenôÙ
tal unit for dense map representation, which can also be optimized to enhance tracking
performance [4].
Like iNeRF, as discussed in Section 3.2.3, GSôÙSLAM relies exclusively on the acquired
modern map representation for pose estimation. The GSôÙSLAM algorithm is fully differôÙ
entiable, enabling the optimization of an initial pose estimate through backpropagation of
a photometric loss using gradient descent. The optimization is performed in two stages,
referred to as coarseôÙtoôÙfine optimization. In the first stage, lowôÙresolution (coarse) imôÙ
ages are rendered to optimize the pose over a fixed number of iterations. In the final
stage, reliable 3D Gaussians are selected based on the pose obtained from the coarse
optimization. These Gaussians are used to render fullôÙresolution images, while noisy 3D

The initial pose estimate relies on a constant velocity assumption, where the last known

reliable 3D Gaussians are selected to guide the algorithm toward refining the pose by

both photometric and depth errors for refinement [31].

on Euclidean distance. GôÙICP uniquely integrates multiple ICP metricsëƒ pointôÙtoôÙpoint,
pointôÙtoôÙplane, and planeôÙtoôÙplane. It does this by representing surface points using coôÙ
variance matrices, and depending on the shape of the covariance matrix, it dynamically
applies the most appropriate ICP method for the surface geometry between a source and
target point cloud.
A key innovation in GSôÙICPôÙSLAM is the dual use of 3D Gaussian information, specifiôÙ
cally the 3D point and covariance matrices, for both ICP tracking and 3DGS mapping,
eliminating the need for additional postôÙprocessing. As illustrated in Figure 3.17, the proôÙ
cess begins by generating a point cloud X and its associated covariance set C1, which
is obtained through downsampling and reprojection of depth images. GôÙICP then comôÙ
putes a transformation T to align the source Gaussians (current frame) Gs = {Xs, Cs}
with the target Gaussians from the map Gt = {Xt, Ct}. If the current frame is selected
as a keyframe, the source Gaussians are used to initialize the 3DGS mapping as new
primitives. Concurrently, the 3D Gaussian map is optimized in the 3DGS process and fed
back into the ICP tracking pipeline, maintaining a continuous loop of refinement between
tracking and mapping [33].
By exploiting the bidirectional flow of Gaussian information between tracking and mapôÙ
ping, GSôÙICPôÙSLAM achieves high computational efficiency, running at up to 107 FPS for
rendering, while maintaining accurate scene representations [33].
3D Gaussian Splatting presents significant potential for SLAM by leveraging optimizaôÙ
tion techniques derived from NeRFs and deep learning, while maintaining high efficiency
through fast rendering. However, the current stage of development is still in its infancy,
leading to relatively immature implementations. Although 3DGS appears ideal for largeôÙ
scale SLAM due to its realôÙtime capabilities, most implementations lack crucial compoôÙ
nents such as loop closure and global bundle adjustment, both essential for maintaining
longôÙterm accuracy in SLAM. Moreover, 3DGS reconstructions shows noticeable degraôÙ
dation when dealing with sparse view inputs and they are sensitive to factors such as moôÙ
tion blur and depth noise [5, 4], further highlighting the need for more robust and refined
approaches. Despite these limitations, the potential of 3DGS for realôÙtime SLAM remains
compelling, and further evaluation through implementation and performance analysis is
necessary and will following in the upcoming chapters.

PhotoôÙSLAM builds upon ORBôÙSLAM by integrating with its framework without modifying
the original interface. This integration introduces an initial mapping phase during which
PhotoôÙSLAM awaits for ORBôÙSLAM to initialize a sparse map. Once this initialization
is complete, the Gaussian backend processes the initial map points, converts them into
Gaussian representations, and enriches the keyframes with additional data required for
highôÙfidelity mapping. While this initialization introduces a delay, typically lasting a few
seconds (depending on the dataset), it enables the online enhanced mapping accuracy.
Figure 5.13 illustrates how reliable tracking begins only after this phase is completed.

higher pose accuracy and feature richness. This ensures that the optimization process

5.6.2.4
Discussion and Implications
The KITTI evaluation reveals three main issues that hinder 3DGSôÙbased SLAM methods
in largeôÙscale outdoor robotics:
1. FrontôÙEnd Sensitivity and Depth Quality: GSôÙICPôÙSLAMãs reliance on ICPôÙbased
tracking emphasizes the need for dense and accurate depthëƒ sparse LiDAR or noisy
stereo data led to frequent drift. While PhotoôÙSLAMãs featureôÙbased approach parôÙ
tially offsets these limitations, it remains susceptible to incomplete depth, resulting
in ãfloatingã Gaussians and inconsistent mapping.
2. Lack of Viewpoints: Incompletely observed objects ã captured from only a few
viewpoints ã commonly lead to misplaced or ãfloatingã Gaussians, disrupting visualôÙ
ization and rendering. This was less noticeable in TUM and Replica due to multiple
viewing angles.
3. Scalability and Memory Constraints: Both algorithms consumed over 9 GB of
GPU memory despite the sequence lasting only 43 seconds. This rapid memory
growth stems from accumulating numerous 3D Gaussians without advanced prunôÙ
ing or compression, raising concerns for longer trajectories or larger environments
where subôÙmap merging and loop closures might become essential.
Algorithm Comparison
Ultimately, PhotoôÙSLAM outperformed GSôÙICPôÙSLAM by leveraging ORBôÙSLAMãs robust
visual tracking over the depthôÙbased ICPôÙtracking. This results in better reconstructions
but it is important to note that both methods face significant challenges when it comes
to novel viewpoints and poses. Both approaches struggle with accurately placing and
representing the 3D Gaussians in space, which impacts the quality and accuracy of the
3D Gaussian map. Future investigations could explore:
ãÂ Full 360ôÙDegree LiDAR Scans: Potentially reducing occlusion issues and improvôÙ
ing multiôÙview consistency.
ãÂ Advanced Depth Fusion: Combining stereo and LiDAR to minimize sparse reôÙ
gions, or employing specialized methods (e.g., KISSôÙICP [34]) for more robust point
cloud alignment.
ãÂ Efficient 3D Representations: Designing map representations that mitigate large
memory footprints, particularly for unbounded or loopôÙrich environments.
3D Gaussian Representation Limitations
A final consideration involves the inherent challenge of modeling outdoor scenes with
3D Gaussians, which do not necessarily align with realôÙworld surfaces. Unlike meshes
or point clouds, Gaussian means can ãfloatã when depth data are sparse, generating
ambiguous or obstructed views (Figures 5.26a and 5.26b). By contrast, 2D Gaussian
splatting [76] could preserve visual coherence more effectively under limited viewpoints,
although it sacrifices volumetric detail which in this case may be for the better.
In summary, the KITTI experiments reveal that, while PhotoôÙSLAM shows promise for
largeôÙscale, visually guided SLAM, both tested 3DGS approaches struggle under sparse
or noisy depth conditions. As realôÙworld robotics demands robust, memoryôÙefficient, and
multiôÙviewôÙaware SLAM systems, these findings emphasize the need for continued reôÙ
finement of 3DGS methods or alternative approaches that better handle the complexities
of large outdoor domains.

The initial Docker workflow developed during the benchmarking phase was intended for
deployment on the NVIDIA Jetson device. However, the Jetson platform utilizes an ARMôÙ

process. The original Docker images, designed for the amd64 architecture, required subôÙ

process involved updating dependencies such as the CUDA toolkit, OpenCV, LibTorch,
and other libraries, many of which have strict version compatibility requirements. ResolvôÙ

and configurations is available in the PhotoôÙSLAM GitHub repository.
Hardware modifications were also necessary to optimize the Jetson devices for SLAM
applications. The default 64 GB eMMC storage was insufficient for storing Docker images
and datasets. To overcome this limitation, a spacious SSD was installed via the M.2 slot,
to accommodate for the plug and play portability of our Docker workflow. On the AGX Orin,
the NVIDIA Software Development Kit (SDK) Manager was installed on a host machine

applied per frame. This is insufficient for accurate mapping, as gradient descent requires
multiple iterations to converge to an optimal solution. To address this limitation, PhotoôÙ

where the pose is estimated with the highest confidence. By concentrating optimization
on keyframes, the system can perform multiple iterations on the most significant data,

found to be a critical factor for achieving highôÙquality mapping, as discussed in Section

ing 17.85 FPS for Mapping may be insufficient for fully online applications. The results
from benchmarking were achieved with tail optimization, where the system continued to
refine the map after capturing and processing the data. This additional offline optimizaôÙ
tion allowed Mapping to reach the required number of iterations per keyframe and obtain
the desired map quality. Without such tail optimization, the mapping quality may degrade
significantly in a purely online setting.

Discussion of Results
Both ORBôÙSLAM3 and PhotoôÙSLAM employ the same frontôÙend for feature extraction and
trackingëƒ hence, their localization performance is expected to be fairly similar. Indeed,
results from Table 6.3 and Figure 6.17 show comparable performance between the two
methods6. Nevertheless, PhotoôÙSLAM ôÙwhich incorporates a 3DGSôÙoptimized backôÙend ôÙ
achieves slightly lower mean ATE RMSE in both experiments. This advantage aligns with
previous findings from the benchmarking chapter, although the gains are marginal. This
slight improvement likely results from minor modifications to the ORBôÙSLAM3 codebase,
as it is copied into their repository rather than pulled from the original source. Despite the
marginal accuracy gains, PhotoôÙSLAM shows a notably higher rate of tracking failures
(over 100 frames in Experiment 2), indicating reduced robustness under more complex
or fastôÙpaced scenarios. By contrast, ORBôÙSLAM exhibited fewer tracking failures, sugôÙ
gesting that it may maintain more stable performance, particularly under conditions where
fast motion or environmental complexity can challenge the SLAM pipeline. These obserôÙ
vations highlight a highôÙlevel tradeôÙoff:
ãÂ PhotoôÙSLAM: Achieves slightly better accuracy (lower ATE RMSE), but suffers from
more frequent tracking failures, indicating lower robustness.
ãÂ ORBôÙSLAM: Offers strong robustness with fewer tracking failures, though its overall
accuracy is marginally lower than that of PhotoôÙSLAM.
In practical robotics applications, robustness and consistency often take precedence over
marginal improvements in accuracy, as even brief periods of tracking failure can disrupt
downstream processes such as navigation, path planning, and autonomous decisionôÙ
making. While PhotoôÙSLAM demonstrates promising accuracy improvements under conôÙ
trolled conditions, its observed instabilityãmanifested in a higher rate of tracking failuresã
raises concerns for realôÙtime deployment in dynamic or complex environments.
Moreover, it is important to note that both algorithms experienced multiple failures during
evaluation, which were excluded from the reported statistics. This exclusion limits the
scope of the conclusions drawn and suggests that neither algorithm demonstrated a level
of robustness that would be ideal for practical use without further optimization. AdditionôÙ
ally, the trajectory errors reported for both algorithms either overlap significantly or show
only marginal differences, further weakening the assertion that PhotoôÙSLAM achieves
substantially better localization performance than ORBôÙSLAM. These findings emphaôÙ
size the nuanced tradeôÙoffs between accuracy and robustness, highlighting the need to
consider applicationôÙspecific requirements when selecting SLAM systems for realôÙworld
deployments.

Summary
This chapter examines the challenges of deploying stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart vSLAM algorithms on
a mobile robot, addressing RQ4 with a focus on computational efficiency, containerization,
algorithmic robustness, and realôÙworld constraints in autonomous navigation.
SimulationôÙdriven SIL testing enabled safe and efficient integration, demonstrating that
vSLAM methods based on ORBôÙSLAM3, such as PhotoôÙSLAM, can be adapted to the
ROS navigation pipeline. However, the sparse pointôÙcloud representations in ORBôÙSLAM3
require additional processing to generate useful occupancy grids for navigation.
Hardware limitations, particularly GPU memory and power consumption, restrict the feaôÙ
sibility of combining SLAM with advanced scene representations like 3DGS. Deploying
PhotoôÙSLAM on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin 64 GB revealed the high computational deôÙ
mands of emerging methods, emphasizing the need for further optimization.
RealôÙtime deployment on the Clearpath Husky UGV demonstrated the feasibility of runôÙ
ning both traditional and emerging vSLAM in a semiôÙoutdoor, confined environment. While
the featureôÙbased ORBôÙSLAM frontôÙend achieved acceptable tracking, robustness reôÙ
mains a concern. Furthermore, novel viewpoint reconstruction in PhotoôÙSLAM was conôÙ
strained by the singleôÙcamera setup, limiting scene coverage and generalization.
Overall, the findings highlight the importance of ROSôÙbased system design, prior simuôÙ
lation, and SIL testing for integration. They also underscore the need for highôÙmemory
GPU hardware for emerging SLAM methods and emphasize the critical role of loop cloôÙ
sure in ensuring tracking reliability. Finally, the current 3DGS backôÙend proves incapable
of reconstructing full scenes from a single fixedôÙmounted RGBôÙD camera, reinforcing the
necessity for further advancements in scalability and robustness.

Future work
The findings of this thesis highlight several promising directions for future research and
experimentation. This section outlines potential avenues for extending the current work,
organized approximately from the most straightforward to the most complex tasks, conôÙ
sidering their feasibility and expected impact.
7.1
Online Deployment of PhotoôÙSLAM on the Husky
A natural next step is to deploy PhotoôÙSLAM online on the NVIDIA AGX Orin platform
mounted on the Husky robot, potentially extending the evaluation to autonomous naviôÙ
gation, as proposed in this thesis. While no significant improvements in performance or
outcomes beyond those described in this thesis are anticipated, this deployment would
further assess PhotoôÙSLAMãs feasibility and the challenges of realôÙtime onôÙrobot operaôÙ
tions.
7.2
MultiôÙview Data Collection
An interesting extension involves collecting data from multiple viewing angles. For inôÙ
stance, an aerial vehicle (e.g., a drone) could complement or replace the ground robotãs
perspective [81], or a tilting camera could be installed on the robot to capture elevated
or more diverse viewpoints. Such multiôÙview data collection could enhance map comôÙ
pleteness by providing additional constraints to refine Gaussian parameters in 3DGS,
potentially reducing uncertainty. However, it would also increase frontôÙend tracking comôÙ
plexity.
7.3
Extended 360ô¯ View
Incorporating a 360ô¯ camera or multiple cameras to achieve full environmental coverôÙ
age could enhance both tracking and mapping performance. Recent studies suggest that
panoramic input improves robustness and accuracy in ORBôÙSLAM [82], while others highôÙ
light its potential for enhancing 3DGS mapping [83, 84]. The extended field of view may
mitigate occlusions and improve feature tracking, leading to more stable localization and
denser reconstructions.
7.4
Fusion of LiDAR and Camera Data
Another direction is the integration of LiDAR with cameraôÙbased 3DGS for SLAM. Recent
research on LiDARôÙvision 3DGS demonstrates the feasibility of achieving fast (7 FPS) and
accurate mapping with novel view synthesis [85]. However, this approach remains a disôÙ
entangled methodology, similar to PhotoôÙSLAM, rather than a fully endôÙtoôÙend framework.
Exploring a tighter integration between LiDAR and GaussianôÙbased representations could
lead to improved localization accuracy and robustness. Papers utilizing 2D Gaussian surôÙ
fels [86] discuss the potential integration of this sensor fusion approach within SLAM and
identify it as a direction for future work.
7.5
Alternative Gaussian Representations
Replacing 3D Gaussian Splatting with 2D Gaussian Splatting [76] or Gaussian Surfels
[87] has gained interest to reduce computational overhead while preserving or even enôÙ
hancing spatial relationships. Flattening Gaussian ellipsoids into surfels, coupled with
normalôÙdepth consistency, has shown to improve efficiency and reconstruction fidelity.

Investigating how these alternative Gaussian representations could integrate into PhotoôÙ
SLAM, particularly in terms of runtime performance and reconstruction quality, could offer
valuable insights into optimizing SLAM systems for realôÙtime deployment.
7.6
EndôÙtoôÙEnd Pose Optimization with 3D Gaussian
Splatting
A fundamentally different approach for future work is refining endôÙtoôÙend 3DGS methods
through algorithmic optimizations or sensor fusion. EndôÙtoôÙend frameworks such as iNôÙ

are currently computationally demanding, incorporating sensor fusion techniques ã such

A potential application of this strategy in PhotoôÙSLAM is the integration of an intermediôÙ

endôÙtoôÙend framework to refine the initial pose estimate from ORBôÙSLAM3ãs VO, accuôÙ
mulated drift could be mitigated before final optimization in the backôÙend. This hybrid
approach aims to combine the robustness of featureôÙbased tracking with the highôÙfidelity
reconstruction capabilities of differentiable SLAM frameworks, potentially improving both
localization accuracy and computational efficiency.

Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the implementation, evaluation, and deployment of vSLAM alôÙ
gorithms for mobile robotics, comparing traditional featureôÙbased methods and emerging
approaches that rely on differentiable optimization. In doing so, four research questions
(RQ1 ã RQ4) were addressed through benchmarking, simulationôÙbased validation, and
onôÙrobot experiments.
RQ1: Commonly Used Traditional and Emerging SLAM Models.
Traditional solutions, such as ORBôÙSLAM3 and RTABôÙMap, rely on sparse feature trackôÙ
ing and geometric optimization, delivering robust realôÙtime performance under CPU conôÙ
straints, primarily due to keyframe management including loop closure mechanisms that
mitigate drift. In contrast, emerging frameworks, exemplified by LoopyôÙSLAM (NeRFôÙ
based) and PhotoôÙSLAM (3D Gaussian Splatting), employ photometric loss minimization
on GPUs, enabling detailed photorealistic scene reconstruction. While the enhanced fiôÙ
delity is beneficial in featureôÙsparse environments, these techniques are resourceôÙintensive
and less mature in terms of reliability.
RQ2: Proper Benchmarks for Emerging vSLAM Algorithms.
A review of relevant literature identified TUM and Replica as standard benchmarks, priôÙ
marily for localization accuracy (ATE RMSE) and photorealistic mapping and reconstrucôÙ
tion (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS). Although these datasets provide ground truth trajectories
and controlled environments for direct comparisons, they fail to capture the complexities
of mediumôÙtoôÙlargeôÙscale or outdoor deployments. Furthermore, they lack comprehenôÙ
sive metrics for evaluating global map quality and novel view synthesis. Incorporating the
KITTI dataset introduced a more realistic outdoor scenario in a mobile robotics context,
but further research should adopt or develop additional metrics to assess global map conôÙ
sistency, contributing to a more comprehensive evaluation of emerging vSLAM methods.
RQ3: Comparison of Traditional vs. Emerging Methods.
Benchmark evaluations showed that emerging frameworks can match or slightly exceed
the tracking accuracy of traditional SLAM in certain cases, but their overall robustness reôÙ
mains inadequate. Loop closure in featureôÙbased methods proved essential for maintainôÙ
ing consistency over extended trajectories ã a feature often left out in newer algorithms.
While the representational richness of emerging methods enables highôÙfidelity reconstrucôÙ
tions, it comes at a significant computational and memory cost, limiting their applicability
to small, confined environments. NeRFôÙbased solutions, in particular, exhibited extreme
computational inefficiency, failing to run online even with substantial hardware resources,
and were significantly outperformed by realôÙtime capable 3DGS methods in both perforôÙ
mance and accuracy.
Among these, PhotoôÙSLAM demonstrated the most practical balance between accuracy
and efficiency, maintaining reliable tracking in a realistic and challenging scenario. By
inheriting ORBôÙSLAM3ãs tracking pipeline and reserving GPU resources for mapping,
it achieved realôÙtime performance while reducing computational overhead. In contrast,
endôÙtoôÙend approaches required GPU processing for both pose estimation and map reôÙ
finement, increasing their computational demands. Decoupled architectures like PhotoôÙ
SLAM, which separate frontôÙend tracking from backôÙend mapping, currently offer the most
feasible path toward realôÙtime, onôÙrobot SLAM deployment.

RQ4: Challenges in RealôÙWorld Robot Integration.
Finally, deploying PhotoôÙSLAM within the ROS framework on a Clearpath Husky UGV
required extensive adaptation, including the development of custom ROS wrappers and
simulationôÙbased testing in Gazebo for safe, rapid prototyping. The online deployment
confirmed the computational and robustness challenges identified through benchmarking.
Although the GPUôÙenabled platform, NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin (64 GB), supported realôÙ
time tracking, PhotoôÙSLAM remained constrained by the optimization demands of the deôÙ
coupled 3DGS process. As environments become more complex, memory requirements
increase, and novel viewpoint reconstruction degrades. This underscores the need for
optimized data structures and algorithmic refinements to make highôÙfidelity 3DGS SLAM
feasible for mobile robots.
Overall Implications and Future Directions.
Traditional featureôÙbased SLAM remains the more practical choice for onôÙrobot applicaôÙ
tions requiring efficiency and realôÙtime reliability. Nevertheless, 3D Gaussian Splatting
techniques show early promise for scene reconstruction and novel viewpoint generation,
particularly if their computational and memory requirements can be reduced. Future reôÙ
search could advance these emerging methods by integrating multiôÙsensor fusion, such
as IMU or LiDAR, to reduce the optimization space and enhance pose estimation serving
the differentiable pipelines and 3DGS for faster convergence. This progression could enôÙ
able a new class of highôÙfidelity, robust vSLAM systems capable of meeting both scientific
and industrial demands in autonomous navigation.

A.1
Bag of Words Implementation for Loop Closure
Detection
The key to loop closure detection lies in calculating the similarity between images. A
straightforward approach would involve direct feature matching, where features from the
current frame are compared with those from previous keyframes. However, this is comôÙ
putationally and memory inefficient, especially as the number of keyframes grows. To
address this, the Bag of Words (BoW) model [89] has become a popular solution in SLAM
algorithms [1] used in both ORBôÙSLAM and RTABôÙMap.
BoW was originally introduced for scene understanding but has since been adapted for
recognizing revisited scenes in SLAM. The process can be broken down into several
steps (see Figure 2.5), starting with (1) feature extraction and according descriptor vecôÙ
tors. To improve speed some algorithms like ORBôÙSLAM [6] uses the same descriptors as
used for the visual odometry. Once the descriptors are extracted, they are (2) clustered
(commonly using kôÙmeans clustering). Each cluster forms a group of descriptors that are
visually similar. The center (centroid) of these clusters represents a ãvisual wordã in the
BoW model, effectively discretizing the continuous feature space into a set of represenôÙ
tative words. After clustering, (3) a dictionary or vocabulary is built, where each cluster is
stored and represents a unique visual word leading back to a representation of a type or
category of features. The first level of the tree depicts the clusters that were generated
in the clustering / discretization process. Additional levels can be generated by repeating
the clustering within clusters and thereby organizing visual words into a hierarchical strucôÙ
ture (e.g., using a vocabulary tree). This hierarchical structure is commonly implemented
using the DBoW2 framework, as seen in ORBôÙSLAM, allowing a coarseôÙtoôÙfine matching
process for efficient visual word retrieval [90].
The vocabulary tree containing the dictionary is a set of learned visual words that repôÙ
resent typical patterns found in the images. In most cases it is generated offline. If not
environmentôÙspecific, a preôÙtrained vocabulary based on a large dataset can be reused
across various environments, though environmentôÙspecific vocabularies may yield higher
precision in certain structured settings.
Finally, (4) for each new image, its descriptors are quantized by assigning them to the
nearest visual word (cluster) in the dictionary. Assigning all the image features to visual
words, this process results in a histogram of visual words, where the frequency of each
word indicates its occurrence in the image. Notably, this approach discards spatial inôÙ
formation, focusing purely on appearance. The final frame histogram is the visual word
vector for this individual frame, a compact representation of the imageãs content. This
process from (1) to (4) is visualized in Figure 2.5.
Once each image is represented by a visual word histogram, (5) similarity scores between
histograms can be computed using metrics like cosine similarity or chiôÙsquare distance,
which are wellôÙsuited for measuring the distributional differences between visual word
vectors. This enables efficient image matching for tasks such as loop closure detection,
where the goal is to identify previously visited locations by comparing current images with
stored keyframes (see Figure 2.6).

A.2
Detailed Description of FilterôÙbased Methods
FilterôÙbased methods for SLAM rely on recursive state estimation using probabilistic filters
such as the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) or Particle Filters. These filters are designed
to continuously update the robotãs belief about its state as new sensor data becomes
available. The core idea behind filterôÙbased methods is to model the SLAM problem as
a Markov process, where the future state depends only on the current state and not on
previous states directly. This assumption helps in reducing the complexity of state esôÙ
timation, making it more computationally efficient, which is why filterôÙbased approaches
are often used in smallerôÙscale environments where realôÙtime performance is critical.

In this approach, the state vector at time k(denoted as xk) represents the robotãs current
pose (position and orientation) as seen in Figure A.3, while the control vector uk repreôÙ
sents the input or control signal that influences the robotãs motion between time steps,
such as velocity and direction, used to update the robotãs position. In visual SLAM this
control vector is estimated from the visual odometry by tracking features across consecôÙ
utive frames as explained in section 2.1.2.
The environment is represented by a set of landmarks whose locations are also estimated.
The position of each landmark is denoted by a vector mi for the iôÙth landmark. A landmark
is essentially a feature that has been selected, based on criteria like stability, distinctiveôÙ
ness, and ability to be consistently reobserved, to become a longôÙterm reference point to
represent part of the map and thus a specific location or object.
The estimation of the robotãs pose and the landmarksã positions is updated recursively in
two main steps: the prediction step and the update (or correction) step.
Prediction step:
In the prediction step, the filter predicts the next state of the robot (e.g., its position at the
next time step) based on its current state and control input. The prediction is governed
by the kinematics model of the robot, which describes how the robot moves through the
environment.
At time k, the state of the robot, xk is predicted from its previous state, xkã1, and the
control vector uk, using the following equation:
p(xk|xkã1, uk) ãxk = f(xkã1, uk) + wk
Here:
ãÂ f(ã) is the motion model (or kinematics model) of the robot, which predicts how the
robotãs state changes based on its previous state and the control input. This can be
a general linear motion model or represent a specific vehicle motion model.

ãÂ wk represents the process noise, which accounts for uncertainties in the motion
model (e.g., slippage of wheels, errors in the control commands). It is typically
modeled as Gaussian noise with zero mean and some covariance.
The goal of the prediction step is to provide an estimate of the robotãs pose and the uncerôÙ
tainty associated with this estimate before incorporating the latest sensor measurements.
Update step:
After the prediction step, the update step uses the actual sensor measurements to correct
the predicted state of the robot and landmarks. The robot collects sensor data (such as
range or visual observations) to detect landmarks in the environment. The key goal in
this step is to compare the predicted landmark positions from predicted sensor readings
(based on the current state and map) with the actual sensor readings with according
landmarks to minimize the error between them. This process helps to adjust and improve
the estimated state of the robot and landmarks.
The observation model that relates the robotãs state xk and the landmarks m to the sensor
measurements zk is given by:
p(zk|xk, m) ãzk = h(xk, m) + vk
Where:
ãÂ zk is the actual sensor measurement (e.g. features detected in an image)
ãÂ h(xk, m) is the predicted measurement. This prediction is calculated from h(ã) which
is an observation model that given the current estimated state of the robot xk and
the landmark positions m predicts a measurement such as visual features.
ãÂ vk represents the measurement noise, which accounts for uncertainties in the sensor
readings.
This equation says that the actual measurement zk should ideally match the predicted
measurement h(xk, m) plus some noise vk. The residual (difference between the actual
measurement zk and the predicted measurement h(xk, m)) tells us how much correction
is needed. If there is a significant difference/residual the update step adjusts the state
estimate to minimize this difference by incorporating the Kalman gain in Kalman filters (or
other similar techniques in particle filters), which determines how much weight to give to
the sensor measurements versus the predicted state. After the update step, the new state
estimate should align more closely with the actual observations, thus reducing uncertainty
in the map and robotãs pose.
Limitations of FilterôÙbased Methods
FilterôÙbased SLAM, such as EKF SLAM, becomes computationally expensive as the numôÙ
ber of landmarks and robot states increases. This is because the filter must maintain
and update a covariance matrix that grows quadratically with the number of landmarks.
Additionally, the linearization of nonôÙlinear motion and observation models (as done in
EKF) introduces approximation errors, which can accumulate over time, especially in
large, nonôÙlinear environments. For these reasons, filterôÙbased methods are generally
more suited for smaller, structured environments, whereas optimizationôÙbased methods
are more commonly used in largeôÙscale SLAM systems.

A.3
Positional Encoding
When directly operating on the 5D input (x, d), the neural network often struggles to capôÙ
ture fine details in the scene. This is because neural networks inherently have a bias
towards learning lowerôÙfrequency signals, which can lead to oversmoothing of the reconôÙ
structed views, particularly in regions with highôÙfrequency details like sharp edges or texôÙ
tures. Positional encoding addresses this issue by transforming the input space to better
preserve highôÙfrequency information. Without it, the model may find it difficult to distinôÙ
guish between spatially close points as their numerical values are very close, leading to
a loss of critical geometric and visual information. By applying positional encoding, the
coordinates are enriched with higherôÙdimensional representations that enable the model

In the first implementation of 3D Gaussian Splatting, An SfM algorithm is used to obtain
camera poses for the input images. Additionally, since the SfM algorithm generates a

SfM is the process of constructing a 3D representation of the environment from a series of

prerequisite for SfM is determining the cameraãs calibration and position, often expressed
as a projection matrix.

A.5
Renderingëƒ Context and Background
Rendering can be viewed as the reverse of scene reconstruction. While scene reconstrucôÙ
tion builds a 3D model from raw data, such as images or sensor inputs, rendering takes
an existing 3D model and transforms it into a 2D image. This process converts complex
computerôÙgenerated data into pixelôÙbased visuals for interpretation [4]. This means that
after densifying the scene representing it with 3D Gaussians, the next step is to project
this 3D representation onto a 2D image plane, effectively rendering a view in the scene.
In the context of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS), image formation rendering involves calôÙ
culating how each Gaussian contributes to the final image and rasterize (i.e. coloring the
individual image pixels), accounting for projection, depth, and transparency.
A generic 3DGS image formation pipeline consists of the following key principles:
1. Perspective Projection (from 3D to 2D): The 3D Gaussians are projected onto the
2D image plane according to the cameraãs perspective, mimicking how a realôÙworld
camera or human eye captures depth and scale.
2. Clipping (Frustum Culling): Gaussians that lie outside the view frustum are clipped
to optimize the rendering process.
3. Depth Sorting (ZôÙbuffering): The Gaussians are sorted by depth to ensure proper
rendering order for both opaque and transparent overlaying objects.
4. Alpha Blending (Transparency and Opacity): Overlapping splats are blended
together, with transparency and opacity handled using alpha values.
The following sections will explore each of these individual general principles providing a
deeper understanding of how 3DGS works to render realistic images.

A.5.3
Depth Sorting (ZôÙbuffering)
As an important part of rendering, depth sorting, traditionally often handled using a ZôÙ
buffer algorithm, is used to correctly resolve the visibility of overlapping objects, ensuring
that closer objects occlude those further away, which is crucial for realistic image formaôÙ
tion.
In 3DGS, depth sorting works by comparing the zôÙcoordinates (depth values) of the proôÙ
jected 3D Gaussians. During the perspective projection stage, each Gaussian is projected
onto the 2D image plane, and the depth value z for each splat is preserved in the ZôÙbuffer,
which is a 2D array that stores the depth of the closest Gaussian at each pixel (see Figure
A.6).
As each Gaussian is projected as a splat and rendered:
ãÂ The depth of the current Gaussian at a given pixel is compared with the value already
stored in the ZôÙbuffer for that pixel.
ãÂ If the current Gaussian is closer to the camera (i.e., has a smaller zôÙvalue), the
ZôÙbuffer is updated with this new depth, and the pixel color is updated accordingly.
ãÂ If the current Gaussian is further away than the one already stored in the ZôÙbuffer,
it is discarded for that pixel, as it would be occluded by the closer Gaussian.
This process ensures that only the closest Gaussians are visible in the final image, and all
occluded Gaussians are correctly handled. ZôÙbuffering is essential in maintaining the corôÙ
rect depth relationships between overlapping splats, contributing to the realistic portrayal
of the scene.
In the case of transparent Gaussians, the approach differs from traditional ZôÙbuffering.
In the original paper [18], a fast Radix sort algorithm is employed to sort the Gaussians
based on their depth values. Instead of discarding farther transparent Gaussians, they are
sorted from farthest to closest and rendered in this backôÙtoôÙfront order. This depth sorting
is essential for proper öÝôÙblending, where the final pixel color is computed by blending the
colors of semiôÙtransparent Gaussians according to their transparency values. By ensuring
that transparent Gaussians are blended in the correct depth order, this method produces
realistic transparency effects, which significantly enhances the quality of the final rendered
image.

B
Sensors
As the name suggests, vSLAM relies on visual data, meaning cameras are the primary
sensors used to capture the environment. When selecting a camera, the first consideraôÙ
tion is whether the application requires color images (RGB) or if grayscale images are
sufficient.
Next, the choice must be made between employing a monocular camera setup, which
does not provide depth information, or selecting from the available alternatives as cateôÙ
gorized by [63]: Structured Light, Time of Flight (ToF), Stereo Camera Sensing, or a
combination.
B.1
Structured Light
A structured light sensor, also known as an Active Stereo Camera, operates by proôÙ
jecting structured patterns, such as stripes, onto a scene. Using pattern codification
techniques, the sensor analyzes the distortion of the projected patterns to generate a
dense depth map. This approach is more efficient than traditional methods that rely on
projecting individual points and triangulating each point separately [63]. However, it is
sensitive to environmental lighting conditions, particularly when using active IR outdoors,
where performance may be affected.
B.2
Time of Flight
ToF sensors estimate the distance to an object in a scene by measuring the time taken
for emitted light to travel to the object and back to the sensor. Consequently, ToF sensors
rely on the time it takes for a light wave to reach a point in the scene, be reflected, and
return to the sensor. This process is repeated for every pixel in the sensorãs field of view.
The sensor calculates the distance for each point in the scene, and the collection of these
distances forms a depth map [63].
B.3
Stereo Camera Sensing
The creation of a depth map using a stereo vision system involves calculating the depth
of each point in a scene by analyzing its projection from two different camera perspecôÙ
tives. This process utilizes epipolar geometry, which describes the geometric relationôÙ
ship between the two camera views. By locating the pixel corresponding to a point P in
the image plane of camera A and comparing it to the position of the corresponding pixel
for the same point P in camera B, depth can be computed through triangulation. This
calculation incorporates the camera intrinsic parameters, the baseline distance (the
separation between the cameras), and the disparity between the corresponding points in
the two images [63].
B.4

The choice of camera technology for SLAM is heavily dependent on the specific use case
and whether the underlying SLAM algorithm supports the chosen modality. As detailed in
the literature review of SLAM algorithms Table 4.1, while some algorithms can operate with
a monocular camera setup, the majority utilize RGBôÙD cameras. Consequently, RGBôÙD
cameras have been selected for this thesis.

E.2
Stereo to Depth Estimation
Following the LiDAR to depth conversions it is clear that this data is accurate though
very sparse. Therefore another approach to generating a depth image is taken utilizing
traditional stereoôÙvision to create a depth image by calculating the disparity between corôÙ
responding pixels in the left and right images. The process involves the following steps:
Rectification
First the cameras need to be calibrated to determine intrinsic and extrinsic parameters and
furthermore rectified, transforming the images so that the epipolar lines are horizontal and
in vertical correspondance, i.e. the corresponding points lie along the same horizontal line
[95]. In the KITTI dataset [72] this is already done, which greatly simplifies the process.
Stereo Disparity

After rectification a disparity calculation must be performed. Disparity refers to the horiôÙ
zontal shift between corresponding pixels (or blocks) in the left and right rectified images.
It is calculated using OpenCVs implementation of stereo matching algorithms, specifically
in our case the semiôÙglobal block matching (SGBM) [96], which balances local accuracy
with global smoothness by combining pixelôÙlevel matching with constraints to enforce spaôÙ
tial consistency. The algorithm operates in three main stages:
ãÂ Matching Cost Calculation: For each pixel in the left image, the SGBM algorithm calôÙ
culates a matching cost across a range of possible disparities by comparing pixel
intensities or small blocks between the left and right image. This cost reflects the
similarity between corresponding regions in the two images and serves as the founôÙ
dation for estimating disparities. A common method for cost computation involves
using the Census Transform, which encodes intensity patterns around a pixel into a
bitôÙstring representation, followed by a Hamming distance comparison to measure
differences between the transformed left and right image patches.
ãÂ Cost Aggregation: Once the initial matching cost is computed, SGBM aggregates
the costs to ensure spatial consistency while preserving edges. Aggregation is perôÙ
formed along multiple oneôÙdimensional (1D) paths radiating from each pixel, typiôÙ
cally in five or eight directions (in the directions of neighboring pixels). This cost
aggregation process incorporates smoothness constraints which penalizes (by P1
and P2) large changes in disparity between neighboring pixels but is still able to
preserve edges. For each pixel and disparity, the aggregated cost is the sum of the
minimum costs from all considered directions. This step ensures a balance between
local detail and global coherence in the disparity map
ãÂ Disparity Selection and PostôÙProcessing: After cost aggregation, the disparity with
the lowest aggregated cost is selected for each pixel. This step provides an initial
estimate of the disparity map, which is then refined through postôÙprocessing. The
postôÙprocessing can include subôÙpixel interpolation (disparity values are refined by
interpolating), Uniqueness Checks (rejects ambiguous matches where multiple disôÙ
parities have similar cost), and Occlusion Handling and Filtering (corrects lack of
corresponding pixels, such as object edges).

Initial Testing and Challenges
Initial testing was conducted using the default camera configuration of 640û480 resoluôÙ
tion (aspect ratio 4:3) at 15 FPS. This setup resulted in unsatisfactory feature recognition
and consequently poor localization performance with ORBôÙSLAM3. The low resolution
produced insufficient image detail, resulting in suboptimal map construction due to limôÙ
ited scene information and less detailed depth data. Furthermore, the low frame rate
introduced significant frameôÙtoôÙframe displacements, worsening the feature tracking diffiôÙ
culties as features moved substantially between frames.
Additionally, the Huskyãs highôÙfriction wheels, designed for outdoor terrains such as mud
and sand, introduced challenges in the environment of the ASTA arena. Unlike vehicles
with explicit turning such as the Ackermann steering geometry, where the front wheels
pivot independently to facilitate turning, the Husky employs a skidôÙsteer mechanism. In
skid steering, the wheels on each side of the vehicle rotate at different speeds to create
a turning motion, which inherently relies on wheel slip to maneuver effectively. On the
highôÙfriction asphalt floor, this mechanism caused small ãjumpsã during turns, with more
pronounced vibrations occurring at smaller turning radii. These vibrations contributed to
motion blur in camera frames, further degrading the quality of input data for ORBôÙSLAM3.
Improvements to Input Data
To address these challenges, adjustments were made to improve the input data quality:
ãÂ Increased Resolution and Frame Rate: The camera configuration was updated to
1280û720 resolution (aspect ratio 16:9) at 30 FPS. The higher resolution provided
greater detail in both RGB and depth data, enhancing feature recognition and map
quality. Additionally, the wider field of view (FOV) offered by the 16:9 aspect ratio
allowed features to remain within the frame longer during turns, improving tracking
continuity. However, this configuration required a USB 3.0 connection due to the
increased data transfer rate of approximately 140 MBps, resulting in higher memory
consumption.
ãÂ Reducing FrictionôÙInduced Vibrations: To mitigate vibrations caused by the Huskyãs
highôÙfriction wheels, the wheels were wrapped in lowôÙfriction material (standard
packaging tape). This significantly reduced vibration levels, thereby minimizing moôÙ
tion blur in camera frames and improving localization accuracy.
These modifications yielded noticeable improvements in localization and mapping perforôÙ
mance. By providing higherôÙquality input data, ORBôÙSLAM3 could produce better feature
tracking and map reconstruction, thereby addressing the limitations encountered during
initial tests.
