Abstract
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) is an imaging method used to reconstruct im-
ages from backreﬂection, similar to ultrasound. But it is using light in near infrared
spectrum (NIR) and is based on the principle of low coherence interferometry [1].
OCT is able to provide images with high resolution and the relevance of OCT in di-
agnostics is currently growing [2]. However, OCT is limited in its penetration depth
and its application on low scattering media [3]. Furthermore, understanding its light
interaction with multiple scattering media, which is relevant for signal modelling, is
still a research topic [4][5]. One aspect is the contribution of multiple scattering to
the OCT signal. Literature describing the propagation of multiple scattered light in
tissue and elaborate OCT models considering multiple scattering exists [6]. Accord-
ingly, it has been shown, that multiple scattered light is a relevant component of the
OCT signal and provides particle information such as size, geometry and density
of the scattering particles in the tissues [6]. Regarding the design of OCT systems
novel applications, which include a spatial offset (SOOCT) to increase the amount of
signal from multiple scattered light, are under investigation. The aim of this thesis
is to design and built such a system on a relatively small wavelength, which has not
been used before. Moreover, the system should be evaluated on different offsets and
in comparison to a conventional, zero offset application. To achieve this, an experi-
mental set-up will be developed, where the offset can be adjusted manually.
The evaluation of that system was based on a simple experiment comparing the A-
scans of multilayered samples. If multiple scattered light has its proposed relevance
for OCT imaging, more layers could be counted at depth. A range of different offsets
on one sample were measured and analysed qualitatively.
The acquisitions of those offsets were successfully, but with a limitation in the size
of the offset due to ﬁber coupling of the reference signal. The results of those exper-
iment proofed the main hypothesis. However, more of such investigations would
increase conﬁdence of those results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) is a non-invasive optical imaging method with
a relatively high resolution up to 1-15 µm [1]. Therefore, OCT is of great interest
for morphological imaging, which can be used for diagnostics. OCT relies on the
principle of low-coherence interferometry, which obtains reﬂective information from
backscattered light in the near-infrared region. Thereby, a beam from a broad band-
width source, such as a laser or superluminescence diode (SLD), will be split into a
sample and reference path and mixed in the detector. Then the structural informa-
tion of the sample can be extracted from the resulting interference pattern [1].
However, this application is still limited regarding its penetration depth due to scat-
tering at larger depth [3]. Even though in current OCT image quality degrades due
to multiply scattered light, at the same time scattering events provide information
regarding structure [3]. However, the OCT signal contains a high amount of single-
scattered light, the ballistic signal, which do not contribute to imaging. Therefore, it
is of interest to detect more scattered light by ﬁltering out the ballistic signal. A pos-
sible solution is to introduce a spatial offset between the illumination and detection
path of the sample. By detecting slightly apart from the incoming light beam, the
signal would contain light, which had experienced more scattering events [7].
The hypothesis is that light from multiple scattering contributes to the OCT signal.
The resolution and signal to noise ratio (SNR) might even be improved by increasing
the amount of those highly informative signals. To do so, a small Offset (<100 µm)
between the point of illumination and the point of light collection will be added.
This should enhance imaging of turbid media at higher depth (≥1 mm) [7]. The
scope of this thesis is to develop an OCT system using a pulsed laser source with
a centered wavelength of 800 nm to investigate the impact of the spatial offset. For
that purpose, a set of A-scans without and with different offsets will be acquired.
Those will be compared regarding resolution and SNR. It is of interest if deeper lay-
ers of a multilayered sample can be distinguished by applying the offsets.
This knowledge and a successful approach to the Spatial Offset OCT (SOOCT) sys-
tem would be valuable for the development of OCT systems to improve the SNR by
giving more structural imaging information in depth. This would be of high interest
to use OCT in diagnostics such as in cancer research and endoscopy.

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
Introduction Optical Coherence Tomography
Optical Coherence Tomography is a similar imaging technique, such as ultrasound,
whereby depth proﬁles can be constructed from the time delay of waves after re-
ﬂection. Analogous, from one dimensional depth proﬁles, A-scans, cross-sectional
two dimensional images, B scans as well as volumetric scans can be constructed [1].
While ultrasound is based on an acoustic signal, in OCT the depth proﬁles will be
obtained from backscattered light in the near-infrared (NIR) domain. Those wave-
lengths have the advantage of minimal absorption in biological tissue. OCT images
provide a better resolution of up to 1-15 µm, which makes it possible to provide
morphological information of tissue abnormalities [1].
A disadvantage is the limited penetration depth of two to three millimetres for
most biological tissues [1].
With those characteristics, OCT based diagnostics are especially common in medi-
cal ﬁelds, such as dermatology and ophthalmology [1]. Another high advantage of
OCT is, that it provides a non-invasive way of imaging without risks such as tissue
damage and exposure to ionizing radiation. So, OCT is a promising alternative to
surgical biopsy and is currently becoming more relevant for cancer differentiation,
i.e. for melanoma [8] [1].
2.1.1
Coherence
Coherence means, that the phase relationship of an electromagnetic wave remains.
One can distinguish between spatial coherence and temporal coherence. While spa-
tial coherence compares the phases from two points, temporal coherence states the
phase relation in a plane perpendicular to the propagation direction. Practically, if
light is coherent, it is able to interfere with a sharp contrast between the fringes. With
loss of coherence, those fringes appear more blurry and incoherent light does not in-
terfere at all [9]. The phase, however, might be shifted. This would be observed as
a shift in the interference pattern, [10] as shown in the two pictures of interference
patterns with different phases in ﬁgure 2.1. The circular rings correspond to dust.
Another criterion for generating a clear interference pattern is, that the frequency
or wavelength of two light beams needs to be the same [10]. Due to the relation be-
tween coherence and interference, it determines the axial resolution of OCT systems,
which is the coherence length.

2.1.2
The Michelson Interferometer
Conventional OCT systems have the set-up of a classical Michelson-Interferometer
or Mach-Zehnder Interferometers, which is a conﬁguration, where two beamsplit-
ters will be used, so the reference and sample paths are decoupled [11]. Those in-
terferometers can either work in free space (bulk optics) or with ﬁber optics, where
a ﬁber coupler splits the beam. For the Michelson interferometer, three main tech-
niques exist, as shown in ﬁgure 2.2 [12]. The ﬁrst type of Michelson interferometers
FIGURE 2.2: Three common Michelson interferometers [12]
(MI) is built by two mirrors, which are incident to the optical axis of the beam. Ac-
cordingly, the beam will be reﬂected back straight. As some laser sources might
be damaged if parts of the light reach back into the laser again from the BS, an-
other conﬁguration (type II. in ﬁgure 2.2) uses tilted mirrors. A disadvantage of this
construction is, that misalignment errors causing differences in path lengths, might
occur. The third type of MI uses retroreﬂectors instead, therefore the light beam
remains parallel to the optical axis [12].
2.1.3
Principle of Low Coherence Interferometry
The underlying principle of OCT is low coherence interferometry (LCI). Whereby
low coherence refers to a short coherence length λc, which is the length a light wave
can be said to be coherent [10]. LCI is a sensitive technique to measure the time
delay of backscattered light and differences in optical path lengths. A LCI consists
of a beamsplitter (BS), usually with a ratio of 50/50, which is dividing the incoming
light into two paths by transmission and reﬂection, as seen in ﬁgure 2.3.
For this reason, an interference pattern is observed on the detector only if the
path lengths of both interferometer arms are similar. Differences in pathlength should
not exceed the coherence length of the source. At the end of both paths, the light will

be reﬂected back by a mirror and recombined by the beamsplitter and afterwards de-

condition that both pathlengths match. The difference in pathlength should be in
the range of the coherence length [1].
2.1.4
Frequency Domain OCT
A more effective method than time domain OCT (TDOCT) is OCT in the frequency
domain (FDOCT), which is a standard method for current OCT systems. Analo-
gous to TDOCT, FDOCT relies on the principle of low coherence interferometry, but
instead of scanning the reference arm over depth, the reference path is ﬁxed. Ac-
cordingly, the reﬂectivity proﬁles of several tissue layers over a range of depth can
be acquired simultaneously. This enables a gain in speed and made real-time imag-
ing with OCT possible [2]. To separate all depth information from each other, two
main techniques exist: Spectral domain OCT (SDOCT), shown in ﬁgure 2.4 on the
left, and Swept-source OCT (SSOCT), in ﬁgure 2.4 on the right. In SDOCT a spec-
trometer detects the signal, where the light will be diffracted into a camera. That
signal, will be translated to a depth proﬁle by a Fourier transformation (FT). SSOCT
systems use a swept-source laser instead of a broad bandwidth source, where a laser
beam scans rapidly over a range of different wavelengths, so the depth proﬁles can
be detected by a photodetector analogous to TDOCT [2].

The Light Interaction with Tissue
The performance of OCT systems are especially determined by the model used to
establish the relation of the tissue’s structure and optical properties of the measured
parameters [5] [20]. This section gives an overview of the light interaction in tissue
and how it contributes to the OCT signal:
Due to differences in refractive index between tissue layers, OCT is able to obtain a
reﬂection proﬁle [2]. While travelling through turbid media, such as tissues, the light
is attenuates and scatters. This results in three main categories of the back reﬂected
signals:
• Ballistic (single scattered)
• Near-Ballistic (multiple scattered)
• Diffuse (highly multiple scattered)
The interference signal reaching the detector consist of a high amount of directly
back scattered light, the ballistic signal, which occurs at the surface layers and there-
fore limits penetration depth. Near-ballistic signals experience at least one scattering
event inside the tissue and therefore transfer information of the optical properties of
the turbid medium. Diffuse optical signals, which scatter several times, can cause to
speckle artefacts in current systems, as modelling the light propagation of those dif-
fuse signals is still a challenge. Therefore, conventional OCT systems rely on ballistic
and near-ballistic signals for imaging [21].
Relevant for OCT is elastic scattering, which is one component of light atten-
uation. Therefore, a range of models exists, which can determine scattering and
estimate attenuation coefﬁcients. Those models can be divided into two main cate-
gories, relying on single scattering (SS) and others taking multiple scattering (MS)
into account [5].
3.1
Scattering Models
3.1.1
Single-Scattering
The simplest models for the OCT signal assume, that the incoming light will be
backscattered just once, the so-called single scattering models [4]. Thereby, the signal
intensity I at a depth z can be described by Beer’s law (see equation 3.1 [5]). From
that will be assumed that the intensity decays from its initial value I(0) exponential,
depending on the attenuation coefﬁcient µt in a uniform tissue. This tissue speciﬁc
coefﬁcient can be determined by methods, such as curve ﬁtting and is a composition
of scattering µS and absorption µA and can be expressed as µt = µA + µS [5] [4].

3.1.2
Multiple-Scattering
On the one hand, multiple scattering (MS) could lead to the occurrence of noise, seen
as speckles in the image [3]. On the other hand, multiple scattering (MS) also carries
valuable image information, such as anisotropy, shape, and density of the scattering
structures. While SS models might be applicable for weakly scattering samples or
shallow imaging, multiple scattering events occur more frequently at higher imag-
ing depth and in case of dense optical media. Then, SS models are not sufﬁcient for
reliable imaging and models considering MS are necessary [22] [4].
Modelling MS inside the tissue is more complex than SS, as other parameters such as
scattering angle, depending on scattering size and probability of scattering events,
depending on particle concentration [21], inﬂuence the light propagation. Leading
to a higher amount of photons being directed back improves probing depth and
SNR [21]. For modelling the OCT signal this means, that the tissue attenuation µt
is smaller than the sum of linear scattering coefﬁcient µS and absorption coefﬁcient
µA [4]. This can be expressed by a mapping function of the attenuation coefﬁcient

3.2
Elaborate OCT Models
A more detailed model describing the OCT signal is based on the principles of the
extended Huygens-Fresnel principle (EHF) and shower curtain effect (SCE), which
is a geometrical principle, which describes how contrast decreases with smaller de-
tector distance to a scattering media. This section gives an overview of relevant OCT
models describing the light interaction in tissue.
3.2.1
Extended Huygens-Fresnel Principle
The Huygens-Fresnel principle uses scalar wave equations to describe the propaga-
tion of electromagnetic light in a turbid medium [9]. The principle states, that vari-
ations in the refractive index n of the medium lead to changes in the amplitude and
phase of the electromagnetic ﬁeld of the light [9]. The scalar wave equations con-
ducted from the Huygens-Fresnel principle can provide the electric ﬁelds of light
with a speciﬁc wavenumber k at a certain position while propagating through a
medium with refractive index n. But it has been difﬁcult to use those equations in
the case of biological tissue, where the refractive index is an unknown and random
parameter [1].
A solution for the limitations of the Huygens scalar wave equations for random me-
dia provides the extended Huygens-Fresnel principle (EHF). It is fundamental for
current OCT models. Also, for describing the signal of the SOOCT it is relevant (see
in section 3.2.4. This principle is an extension of the previous scalar wave equations,
by adding a factor into the calculations of the electric ﬁeld amplitude of a light wave
[1]. This factor, the Greensfunction includes perturbations in the ﬁeld’s amplitude
and phase due to random inhomogeneities of the medium [24]. The EHF principle

Spatial Offset OCT
4.1
Main principle of the Spatial Offset OCT
The signal of a conventional OCT system contains mostly a ballistic compound,
which occurs mainly from shallow depths. As that signal carries less valuable imag-
ing information [3] and is overwhelming the signal from MS events, imaging at fur-
ther depth can be impaired. This reduces the imaging depth and worsens the SNR of
images at depth. Therefore it would be of advantage to improve the balance weight
between single and multiple scattered signals [21]. For these reasons, the ballistic
signal component should be reduced mechanically before the signal processing.
The hypothesis of the Spatial offset OCT (SOOCT) system is that the ballistic com-
ponents can be ﬁltered by introducing a spatial offset in the sample path. Meaning
the signal backscattered from the sample travels parallel and slightly apart from the
incoming signal towards the sample [7]. By separating the MS light from the ballistic
component the reference path could be matched with the parallel travelling sample
path. So, the reference signal interferes with just that signal component at the beam-
splitter. To ensure that the interference of the non-ballistic signal will be detected,
the offset should also be considered while placing the detector entrance. Then, the
relative number of multiply scattered photons will be increased. Also, the signal in-
tensity for MS decreases, but by increasing the integration time more detail in depth
would be observed.
Advantages of the SOOCT against an earlier approach in the literature, using an
angular offset between the objective lens and sample [28], is that beam propagates
parallel at the focusing area. This decreases the impact of the offset on the image
quality. The spatial offset might also be more efﬁcient in detecting MS light, which
decays more quickly [7], as the path length inside the sample does not change.
In conclusion, the principle of SOOCT provides a promising solution to identify ﬁne
structures at depths and the design of such a system is of great interest.
4.2
Realisation of the Spatial Offset in the OCT Design
4.2.1
Previous considerations
Before ﬁnding a solution to generate the spatial offset, some general characteristics of
the OCT system have been deﬁned. Those decisions have been made by brainstorm-
ing and literature review. The aim of the design is to be as simple as possible and
telecentric. Because a simple design with fewer optical components and those placed
in a collimated beam reduces risks, such as misalignment and unwanted aberration
effects [1].
==PAGE:32==

TDOCT or FDOCT
The design of the system has been decided to be based on FDOCT due to its advan-
tages over TDOCT. Those are imaging speed, higher SNR[2] and the fact that the
reference mirror can be ﬁxed. So no motorised construction is needed. The FDOCT
system should be based on a femtosecond laser source and detection with a spec-
trometer.
Fiber Optics or Bulk Optics
As the spatial offset of less than 80µm can not be realized by two ﬁbers, which each
have a cladding diameter of more than 100µm [18], the system will be built in free
space. Lenses and a beamsplitter will guide the beam, but the combined heterodyne
signal must be coupled into a ﬁber. A single-mode ﬁber conducts the signal into the
spectrometer.
Type of Interferometer
The simple MI interferometer type ensures a faster and easier implementation and
will probably be less affected against noise than a Mach-Zehnder interferometer [29].
Another advantage is that the MI needs fewer components, as one BS splits and
recombines sample and reference paths. Accordingly, this interferometer system
has been chosen. Those arguments have been weighted against the Mach-Zehnder
interferometer, which on the other hand would provide a decoupled reference and
sample path [11].
4.2.2
First Drafts of the SOOCT System
After the general design of the OCT system has been decided, the next task was to
construct the spatial offset. The main criterion was to ﬁnd a geometry, where the
back coming reference and sample beam match the BS at the same position with the
same angle. Simultaneously, the outcoming beams must be well separated from the
incoming and ballistic beams.
One of the ﬁrst ideas was to implement the offset by a retro reﬂective mirror in the
reference path, as illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1. This solution should create the offset by
guiding the reference signal back to the BS parallel to the optical axis. Accordingly,
the reference beam interferes with the multiple scattered signal from the sample
path. In addition, the ﬁber entrance must be shifted. So the spectrometer detects
the signal with the offset and not the ballistic component. One disadvantage of this
construction was that the offset can not be varied without changing the reference
path length.

Laser Source and Beam Guidance into the System
As a light source, the Femtosource Scientiﬁc from the FEMTOLASERS Produktions
GmbH has been used, which is an ultrafast (<12 fs pulse duration) Ti-Sapphire oscil-
lator. The laser generates a broad bandwidth beam with a central wavelength of 800
nm [31]. A 532 nm laser source excites the Ti-Sapphire crystal of the Femtosource
Scientiﬁc. The laser will be modelocked to create a train of ultrashort laser pulses.
This creates a broad bandwidth range in frequency, which is necessary to detect the
OCT signal [1].
With two mirrors (model BB1-E03 Thorlabs mirror for NIR light), with an angle of
45° respectively to the propagation direction the laser has been guided from the laser
cavity into the interferometer. Those mirrors have been mounted by post holders.
The height of the mirrors has been set so that the laser beam hits them in the centre
(at 10-11 cm from the table ground). This should enhance the beam alignment. The
holders were ﬁxed on the lab table by clamping forks provided by Thorlabs.
==PAGE:38==

Two neutral density ﬁlters (ND-ﬁlter) decreased the intensity and thereby ensured a
ﬂat spectral response [18]. A variable ﬁlter (Thorlabs model NDC-100S-4M) is placed
between the two 45° tilted mirrors. Another ND-ﬁlter was placed before entering the
interferometer.
Michelson Interferometer
Based on the Michelson interferometer principle, the light will be divided by a 50:50
non-polarising NIR beamsplitter (BS) cube (model BS017 Thorlabs), towards a ref-
erence mirror (BB1-E03) and the sample path. The mirror was ﬁxed in a mounting
with adjustable angles, centred on the optical axis. The BS was placed with its cen-
tre at the optical axis. The mounting of the BS cube had angular freedom in three
directions, which made alignment possible. After the alignment on a non-offset in-
terferometer has been done sufﬁciently, the position of the BS has been kept. The
reference mirror was placed in a holding with adjustable angles in x-, y- and diag-
onal position. It was mounted on a stage to adjust the length of the reference path.
This gave accuracy in the range of microns and ensures that sample and reference
path lengths match. An iris in front of the reference mirror compensated for the
lower intensity of the sample path due to lower reﬂection.

Offset Measurements
After the ﬁrst measurement at different samples, no signal has been obtained by
using the offset. Therefore, the alignment and set-up has been optimised again.
Some signiﬁcant changes are:
• ﬁxing the ﬁber entrance again
• realigning the laser beam at the BS and ﬁrst two mirrors along vertical axis
back on the optical axis
• additional ND ﬁlter and compensation of the reference signal by an iris
After that reﬁnement, it was no issue to image the multilayered sample of tapes and
projector foil, which has been decided to be a good representative for turbid media.
With its thickness of 1.65 µm the sample provides the region of interest for offset
measurements.

Experimental Procedure of the Offset Measurements
Firstly, the non-offset signal has been set, as described in section 5.1.1. If the sample
signal was maximised, both pathlengths were matched and the reference intensity
was adapted by the ND-ﬁlters, the offset could be implemented. The desired offset
was set by adjusting the mounting of the ﬁber entrance apart in the horizontal di-
rection. While the sample arm was blocked again, the reference mirror was coupled
in the shifted ﬁber again by adjusting the transverse angle of the reference mirror. A
maximal intensity in the spectrum showed when that was the case. Afterwards, the
sample has been placed. By varying just the angle of the mirror and the transverse
position of the ﬁber entrance, the path length has been kept constant and no further
alignment was necessary.

Discussion and Concluding Words
6.1
Discussion
First of all, it should be noted, that constant peaks from the reference or another
part of the OCT system have also been present in the offset measurements. Figure
6.1 shows an A-scan of the reference signal before implementing the offset measure-
ments. In accordance with that those peaks have been misinterpreted as the signal,
FIGURE 6.1: An A-scan of the reference signal only shows peaks
they have not been considered in the analysis. However, they can also cancel out the
signal by destructive interference.
The qualitative analysis, which found, that the signal peaks travel further into the
sample by applying the offsets could be argued to be quite subjective. This analytical
method relied on the individual to decide if the peaks refer to the signal or not. This
could have been difﬁcult due to the surrounding noise level. Also, the sensitivity
roll-off effected the determination of the signal.
It should also be noted, that the alignment of the beam and a therefore a telecen-
tric beam propagation is necessary for trustful results. Otherwise, it might have an
impact on the offset measurements. However, during the build-up of the system,
it has been found that this is not always easy to achieve and measure. In addition,
effects such as internal reﬂection in the BS, reﬂections from other parts of the system
or dispersion effects might have an impact on the results. Also, air bubbles in the
sample and constant interference patterns between the layers might have inﬂuenced
==PAGE:52==
36
Chapter 6. Discussion and Concluding Words
the A-scans. Another aspect is, that the provided sample might not be comparable
with tissue, so different materials could have been investigated.
The overall image quality of the SOOCT system has not been determined with this
project, as artefacts such as speckle effects affecting a B-scan, might be present.
Finally, it would have been crucial for the statistical accuracy of the results to repeat
the measurements of each offset. A better experimental design might have been a
randomised range of measurement of eight repetitions for each parameter. There-
fore, it is questionable, if the results are reproducible. But this has not been done
due to limited time. Previous issues with the alignment of the laser source and the
difﬁculties in aligning the SOOCT system have affected the project plan.
Even taking those aspects into account, the results of this thesis have proofed the hy-
pothesis and provided useful knowledge in the aspect of the contribution of multiple
scattering to the OCT system. The design and realisation of this novel and individ-
ual OCT system were successful. As it provides to implement the spatial offset in a
simple mechanism and is able to acquire A-scans with those.
6.2
Conclusion
With this Master thesis, an OCT system set-up has been designed, which is capable
to implement and vary the offset manually in a simple approach. The underlying
mechanism is a transverse shift of the ﬁber entrance and matching of the reference
by angular adjustment of the mirror. The system has been evaluated successfully by
simulation studies and built.
Non-offset measurements were performed at different samples and a multilayered
sample of different layers of tape and projector foil has been used for the offset mea-
surements. It has been able to acquire A-scans with offsets up to 20 microns. The
hypothesis, that multiple scattering contributes to the OCT signal has been proofed.
The so obtained results also indicate that the intensity peaks of the A-scans travel
further in depth by the increase in offset.
However, the so performed analysis was quite subjective and not statistically repre-
sentative. Also, artefacts occurring from the system might have an impact on those
results. But the ﬁndings of this novel system were fundamental for the understand-
ing of the OCT system and further development. Therefore, more investigations of
this system are of interest.
6.3
Perspective
To obtain more certainty of the results, more investigations could be performed.
Firstly, Offset measurements with more repetitions and randomised or blocked ex-
perimental design could be performed. This would ensure the reproducibility of the
results. The qualitative analysis might also be performed by different individuals to
become more objective. Secondly, biological samples could be investigated, such as
milk, as those provide comparable optical properties with tissue. Thirdly, the acqui-
sition of B-scans and further analytical methods such as SNR calculations, contrast
measurements and determination of speckle artefacts would be useful to evaluate
the image quality of such a system. But for such experiments, high-performance
electrical equipment, such as a high-resolution spectrometer, would be beneﬁcial. If
