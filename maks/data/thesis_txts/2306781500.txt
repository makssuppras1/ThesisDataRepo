Abstract 
Although the transport sector is a major contributor to several environmental impacts, recent 
technological advancements show great opportunities for improvements. Life cycle assessment (LCA), 
which quantifies the potential environmental impacts of systems in a life cycle perspective, need to be 
applied to ensure that these developments are made with as low impact as possible. Until now, LCA 
has rarely been applied with a systemic approach to transportation systems; it is often used in analyses 
with restricted system boundaries and without a future-oriented perspective. This study aims to bridge 
these gaps by assessing the time-differentiated deployment of electric vehicles (EV) at a full urban 
scale, using the city of Copenhagen for the period 2016-2030 as case study. The assessment is 
performed and interpreted at two levels: (1) vehicle level, where four types of non-conventional cars, 
i.e. battery-based, range-extended, fuel-cell-based and hybrid EV, are compared to internal 
combustion engine vehicles on the basis of 1km driven in Copenhagen, and (2) city level, where LCA 
has been applied to the entire private car fleet of the Copenhagen area from 2016 to 2030, including 
the vehicles, the charging infrastructures and the electricity and fuel support systems. These three 
major components of the transport systems are all differentiated in time. Four scenarios have been 
developed, differentiated by different degrees of implementation of the different EV technologies in 
Copenhagen. Results show that fuel-cell EV perform better in general, having the best scores in ten 
categories and being the worse only for Ozone Depletion. Range-extended EV present a high potential 
as a transition technology and future LCA studies should thus take them into consideration. Charging 
infrastructures had a minor contribution to the total impacts of the system compared to vehicles and 
the energy system. In the systemic analysis, the ranking of the scenarios varies across the different 
impact categories. However, S4, a scenario promoting disruptive technological advances related to fuel 
cells EV, appears to be the best option at midpoint for nine impact categories out of 15 and at endpoint 
for the three areas of protection. Though the analysis focuses on the case of Copenhagen, these results 
suggest that the focus of technology developers and urban planners should be put on pushing the 
development and deployment of fuel-cells and range-extended EV on the market.

1. Introduction 
The transport sector has a major role in multiple problematics human kind is facing today. Mainly 
based on fossil-fuels, it participates considerably to global resource depletion (Capellan-Perez et al., 
2014). Accounting for 23% of the global carbon dioxide emissions (IEA, 2009), it is one of the main 
contributors to anthropogenic greenhouse gases (GHG) emissions, and thus to climate change. As such, 
it also presents potential for considerable improvements and recent technological advancements show 
great opportunities in that sense. Indeed, more and more vehicles driving on electricity or hydrogen 
are developed and sold. They have no CO2 emissions during the use phase, not to mention that electric 
motors are more efficient than internal combustion engines (Sadek, 2012). As such they would be more 
environmental-friendly alternatives to conventional cars. However, assessing the overall reduction of 
environmental impacts versus fossil-fuel cars requires considering the bigger picture. Indeed, even 
though they do not have tail-pipes emissions, manufacturing and assembly of the vehicles as well as 
the production of electricity and hydrogen needed to power the cars are not carbon-free, not to 
mention other potential environmental impacts. Additionally, scarce resources are needed in the 
production of some of the complex but essential components of these vehicles. This might result in a 
burden-shifting from the use stage, where the fossil-fuelled vehicles have the most impacts, to other 
part of the life cycle of the technology system, such as manufacturing or end of life of full or partly 
electric vehicles. Thus, before beginning the implementation of game-changing measures that would 
aim to encourage intense deployment of non-conventional vehicles, decision makers need to have 
access to the adequate assessment tools which would allow them to estimate in which extend this 
alternative is better in the long term for human health and the environment.  
One of the well-developed tools to compare the effects of different scenarios on ecosystems and 
human health is Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). This internationally standardised methodology is used to 
quantify emissions and resource consumptions of a product or a system, and allows assessment of 
their impacts on human health and the environment (ILCD, 2010a). The principle is to take into account 
a part or the complete life cycle of the object under study, from cradle to grave, in order to give an 
overview of all impacts relative to humans, nature and resources. Numerous LCAs can be found on 
electric vehicles in the literature. However, a literature review based on all LCA studies applied on 
Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs) (available in Appendix A) have highlighted various possibilities of 
improvement, especially concerning the method. As a matter of fact, LCA methodology is only partially 
respected in half of them, which leads to conclusions and recommendations that are difficult to 
compare. The goal and scope definition is often incomplete or missing, despite the fact that its 
importance has been highlighted by various reviews in the past (Nordelöf et al., 2014; Hawkins et al., 
2012). The impact assessment is limited to greenhouse gases emissions and energy consumption in 
half of the studies, and the proportion that takes the complete life cycle of the vehicles into account 
hardly reaches 60%. As shown in Figure 1, only 56% of the studies adopt a systemic perspective and 
include the technology in a bigger system with which it interacts.

LCA is particularly adapted for the comparison of complex systems due to the large variety of impact 
categories it can consider. Despite its accuracy, the systemic approach is still in its early stages of 
development and has not been used extensively in LCA studies thus far. Contrarily to a majority of 
papers that do LCAs focusing on independent products or processes, a systemic perspective shows 
great promise in numerous fields of study, and permits a more rigorous description of reality. This is 
particularly the case when it comes to studies willing to compare the effects of policies implementation 
or important change in societal habits. The challenge of the systemic approach is that it requires a 
wider knowledge of the change occurring and requires giving special attention to all potential 
implications of that change. However, some of the papers approaching the problem in a systemic 
perspective have developed interesting methods in order to assess entire fleets, combining research 
of disparate fields such as energy systems and agent-based modelling. Their results are globally 
homogenous and show many advantages for the deployment of electric vehicles.  
The over-arching purpose of this study is to compare the different alternative technologies to internal 
combustion vehicles. This is first done through a vehicle-based LCA of the different technologies of 
vehicles, followed by a fleet-based LCA of the deployment over 15 years of non-conventional vehicles 
in Copenhagen, taking into account the vehicles, the charging infrastructures and the change of 
electricity mix through the years. Both LCAs are conducted with a systemic approach: the first LCA aims 
to compare vehicles representing the average cars in a specific external environment which is the city 
of Copenhagen and the second LCA considers the entire fleet of the city. This will contribute to provide 
useful information to decision makers and other stakeholders of the transport sector and will give 
them tools to take adequate decisions concerning the implementation of various types of electric 
vehicles. It will also provide guidance for LCA practitioners to do a comprehensive and consistent LCA 
on EVs systems. Copenhagen has been chosen for its ambitious climate plan of being the first carbon 
neutral capital by 2025 (København Kommune, 2009). Two basis scenarios will be investigated, inspired 
by McKinsey & Company (2011): a “Below 100” or Business As Usual (BAU) scenario, where the current 
trends keep developing the same way, leading to an average carbon footprint of the well-to-wheel 
phase hypothetically below 100gCO2/km in 2050, and a “Below 10” scenario where a strong emphasize 
is put on incentive measure to buy non-conventional vehicles and environmental consciousness-raising 
campaigns, leading to an average carbon footprint theoretically below 10g CO2/km in 2050. Two extra 
scenarios will be built and considered based on the primary results of the analysis to investigate how 
to diminish more the environmental impacts.

2. Methodology 
2.1. Assessment framework 
LCA is a multiple step method that is applied iteratively (ILCD, 2010a). It starts with a goal and scope 
definition, which describes the system and details the methodology to be used. A functional unit is 
then defined, which is a quantitative measure of the function the system provides and acts as a 
reference to compare the different elements. Then data are collected for the analysis. Finally, the 
impact assessment is conducted on the system, using the materials and emission flows calculated from 
the data collection. As shown in Figure 2, interpretation all along the process is necessary to conduct 
an accurate study.  
In this study, two complementary LCAs have been conducted. The first one compares the vehicles 
alone (vehicle-based) and constitutes a basis for the second LCA that considers the whole private 
vehicle fleet of Copenhagen (fleet-based). Each assessment has a specific scope, with different 
functional units and system boundaries.  
2.2. Scope 
2.2.1. Vehicle-based LCA: comparison of five different technologies 
In order to have a thorough overview of the system’s components, the different technologies of vehicle 
will be assessed in a first time. Five technologies of powertrain are considered: 
- 
ICEV: Internal Combustion Engine Vehicles, or conventional vehicles, are powered by normal 
or optimised engines, and are driving on fossil-fuels. 
- 
BEV: Battery Electric vehicles are powered by an electric motor and carry a battery as energy 
storage. 
- 
HEV: Hybrid Electric Vehicles can be powered by an electric motor or a combustion engine, 
and carry a battery. This category includes plug-in hybrids (PHEV) that are charging their 
batteries thanks to the electricity grid and non-plug-in hybrids (nPHEV) that are charging their 
batteries thanks the combustion engine.

- 
REEV: Range-Extended Electric Vehicles are powered by an electric motor and carry a battery 
as principal energy storage, but a combustion engine is used to generate additional energy in 
order to extend the autonomy of the car. 
- 
FCEV: Fuel-Cells Electric vehicles are powered by an electric motor and carry hydrogen fuel-
cells as energy storage. 
A functional unit of “1km driven by a private vehicle in normal driving and meteorological conditions 
in Copenhagen in 2016 and in 2030” has been chosen. This includes the cradle-to-grave of the vehicles 
(manufacturing, use, maintenance and end-of-life) and the well-to-wheel of the carburant (fossil fuels, 
electricity or hydrogen) as well as the roads. The assumption is made that charging infrastructures for 
EVs and hydrogen supply infrastructures are negligible, and are thus not included in this part. Having 
a detailed vision of the vehicles’ impacts will help understand the results of the complete system and 
avoid speculations about what causes the impacts. 
2.2.2. Fleet-based LCA: comparison of different scenarios 
Even though the vehicle-based approach will give useful information about the differences between 
vehicles, it is not sufficient to draw conclusions concerning private transportation in a specific city. 
Therefore, a second study is conducted with a fleet-based perspective that aims to assess the 
deployment of electric vehicles in Copenhagen, by running an LCA that include vehicles, energy 
consumption, charging infrastructures and electricity generation. This will allow a more accurate 
description of reality. The strategy is to build a system that models private transportation in the city as 
accurately as possible by using the trends and driving behaviour of the inhabitants. Furthermore, this 
type of study requires a differentiation in time for the elements of the system that will change through 
the time frame. The composition of the electricity mix changes yearly and traduce the evolution of 
Danish energy policies. The characteristics of the vehicles are adjusted every five years to simulate the 
technological improvements. The results are thus specific to the Copenhagen area (here the provinces 
of København Omegn and København By) and to private passenger cars.  
The system includes the vehicles (cradle-to-grave), the energy production, the charging infrastructures 
and the electricity grid (see Figure 3). The main function of the system is to supply to all private 
transportation via motorized vehicles needed in the area of Copenhagen (defined here as København 
Omegn and København By) from 2016 to 2030. The functional unit could thus be defined as “15 years 
of private vehicle transportation in Copenhagen”.

The full life cycle of the vehicles is considered: natural resources extraction, manufacturing, assembly, 
distribution, use, maintenance, roads wear and end of life, as well as the well-to-wheel of the carburant 
(gasoline, diesel, electricity or hydrogen). Charging infrastructures are also considered cradle-to-grave, 
as illustrated in Figure 3. Construction, use and decommissioning of conventional fuel stations are 
considered, but the hydrogen stations are not considered, since the number of vehicles driving on 
hydrogen is very limited in both scenarios and they appear late in the time frame. 
2.3. Definition of the scenarios 
In its report “Boost!” written in 2011, McKinsey&Co elaborated on scenarios of evolution of the global 
market shares by powertrain technology until 2050. The different scenarios have been constructed 
based on the hypothetical carbon threshold that will be reached in 2050 when considering the well-
to-wheel emissions. In this study, the ones corresponding to 10 and 100 g CO2eq/km are considered 
as base scenarios.  
The “Below 100” scenario can be seen in Figure 4a and corresponds to a Business As Usual (BAU) 
evolution. CO2 emissions reduction is moderate, and electric vehicles have a limited deployment, even 
in 2050. However, HEV and REEV are quickly gaining ground to the extent of conventional vehicles 
which are finally disappearing at the end of the time scope. FCEV are still inexistent of the repartition 
in 2050. This means that according to McKinsey’s findings the average private vehicle in 2050 in this 
scenario will have well-to-wheel emissions of less than 100 g CO2eq per kilometre.

infrastructures and the electricity grid.

The “Below 10” scenario can be seen in Figure 4b and corresponds to a significant augmentation of 
policies and awareness campaign favouring the purchase of an electric vehicle. REEV and HEV are 
transition technologies toward BEV and FCEV, which finally share 100% of the market in 2050.  
The two scenarios seem to be exaggeratedly different because of the radically opposed situation in 
2050, but the evolution is looked over 35 years and the uncertainties are so large that both scenarios 
are actually plausible (Dansk Energi, personal communication, February 1st, 2016). They reflect two 
divergent political lines that the Danish government could choose to follow in the coming years.  
 
In order to assess how the environmental impacts of transportation could be reduced significantly, two 
explorative scenarios have been developed in order to investigate a range of possibilities: 
- 
BEV++: this scenario follows the tendencies of B10, but with faster changes. The proportion of 
BEV increases faster, at the expense of HEVs (see Figure 5a). It uses the realistic scenario B10 
as a base and represents the potential evolution of the fleet if even more efforts were put on 
the deployment of non-conventional vehicles via policies and regulations. 
- 
S4: this fourth scenario describes a disruptive technological advancement in favour of the 
FCEV, that would accelerate its introduction and totally change the market from 2019. 
Additionally, REEV are highly promoted in the beginning of the time scope in spite of HEVs, as 
a transition technology (see Figure 5b).

2.4. Data collection 
After defining the frame of the study in the goal and scope definition, data are collected on the flows 
of materials constituting the system. Details about calculations and data sources are available in 
Appendix B.  
2.5.1. Primary data source  
General data about Denmark have been retrieved from the website Statistik Banken (e.g. the predicted 
evolution of the population of the zone until 2030 or the proportion of people living in single houses). 
The Danish National Travel Survey has been used to describe the current Danish situation regarding 
passenger cars. The main assumption is that current trends in transportation habits, like the average 
distance driven per day with a passenger car by an inhabitant of this zone or the average number of 
vehicles per inhabitant of this zone will barely change until 2030 in Denmark, and are thus considered 
constant. The database Ecoinvent version 3.1 (Wernert et al., 2016) was used as a base for all the 
inventory flows.  
To define the motivation factor, a decision-context has to be chosen. The decision-context influences 
the way the co-products are handled in the impact assessment. Because of the effects the 
development of these technologies can have on electricity generation, fossil-fuel consumption and 
rare resources use in industry, decision-context is a macro level decision support (situation B according 
to ILCD, 2011). Thus, a consequential approach is used and system expansion is considered for the 
multi-processes modelling, meaning that the additional functions related to co-products are integrated 
in the system boundaries.  
2.5.2. Modelling the vehicles 
Modelling vehicles during the next 15 years is done using data from governmental organisation (Dansk 
Elbil Alliance, 2016; ICCT, 2015; USABC, 2014; EPRI, 2014), scientific papers (Garcia et al., 2015; Del 
Duce et al., 2014; Simons and Bauer, 2015; Edison Consortium, 2011) and the automobile industry 
(ACEA, 2014; all the manufacturers of EVs). For all the details of the characteristics’ definition, please 
refer to Appendix B section 3.

Private vehicles technology is evolving fast, especially for alternatives to internal combustion engine. 
Since the last decade, the main focus has been on improving fuel efficiency, which leads generally to 
less, or at least different impacts. As this trend is likely to continue in the future, it is crucial to consider 
a change of the cars’ characteristics between 2016 and 2030. Different models of cars have been built 
on a limited number of parameters inspired by the Ecoinvent v3.1 processes: vehicle weight, fuel 
consumption, lifetime of the vehicle and weight and lifetime of the battery (when applicable). For each 
technology, a base model is used for the beginning of the time scope and is defined based on the 
current average Danish vehicle. ICEV base-model is inspired by data from the ICCT report (2015), which 
contains the average characteristics of the cars sold in Denmark since 2009, by year. A private vehicle 
having an average lifetime of 10 years (Querini and Benetto, 2015), the average over the whole period 
of time available in ICCT (2015) is a good approximation of the average characteristics of the current 
vehicles driving on the Danish roads. Concerning BEV base-model, Dansk Elbil Alliance and Danske 
Bilimportører have given us data about the exact composition of the BEV fleet in Denmark per model 
of car. Information related to the models has been retrieved from the official manufacturers’ 
brochures. A factor has been applied on the fuel consumption based of recent studies highlighting the 
important difference between theoretical data and real-life consumption (Del Duce et al., 2014). 
Moreover, a constant has been added to traduce the need of externalities (heat, air-conditioning, radio 
and lights) that electric vehicles undergo. The need of additional heating is due to the very low heat 
loss of electric motors compared to conventional vehicles, and as a matter of fact, heating is 
indispensable almost permanently in Denmark. The HEV, REEV and FCEV base-models are inspired by 
the BEV base-model since these technologies are barely present in the Danish market today.  
Constructed on these base-models, an average vehicle has been created every five years (so three 
models per technology in total, except for the FCEV which enters the market in 2025 so required only 
one model). A constant yearly decrease has been applied to the vehicle weight, the fuel consumption 
and the battery weight (when applicable). The vehicle and battery lifetimes are predicted to stay 
constant until 2030, and equal to the data used in Ecoinvent v3.1 (150000 km for the vehicles and 
100000 km for the batteries).  
2.5.3. Modelling of the infrastructures 
BEV, REEV and PHEV need specific charging infrastructures that are not largely available yet. Usually, 
the user charges its vehicle mainly during night, trying to avoid to charge outside its home. Therefore, 
the assumption is made that 100% of the owners of one of these three technologies will install a home 
charger if they have the possibility to do it. This depends on the type of accommodation they are living 
in. Individual and shared houses have a capability to install a charger in their private parking spot or 
garage. On the contrary, multi-dwelling houses might not have this option, except in few cases. 
Unfortunately, no statistics about the proportion of multi-dwelling houses with their own parking lot 
is available, so the assumption is made that occupants of multi-dwelling houses are not able to install 
a charger.  
The number of normal public chargers that will be built until 2030 for a comfortable use of electric 
vehicles can be calculated according to different theories. Various methods can be found in literature, 
with different levels of accuracy and based on different types of data. Lucas et al. (2012; 2013) have 
used two different techniques of calculations in two articles focusing on charging infrastructures. The 
first one was based on the average service ratio (chargers/cars) needed in 2020, which had been 
calculated by a Portuguese charger infrastructure implementer. Despite its simple applicability, the 
results of this technique is limited to Portugal. In their following paper, Lucas et al. used a service ratio 
formula, based on the yearly driven distance per vehicle. More precise than the first one, this 
technique permits a better adaptation to the specific frame of the study. On a more factual way, a 
Danish research team (Edison Consortium, 2011) studied the situation of Denmark in case of relative 
deployment of BEVs in the whole country. The driving range and the geographical applicability are 
well-defined and precise, increasing the level of reliability as compared to the previous methods.

Another and more technical approach consist of assessing the area under study to identify the peak 
consumption of an average day (Nègre, 2011). The number of chargers needed is then simply deduced 
from the energy required to satisfy the peak, and represents the optimal number needed in the area 
to answer the demand. Nevertheless, finding the peak consumption is highly challenging and requires 
a complex and detailed study of the transportation trends of the area under study. Furthermore, this 
implies that 100% of the chargers need to be used to answer the demand at peak hour, which may be 
difficult in reality. Another “top-need approach” has been developed by EPRI (2014), but instead of 
using at the theoretical peak energy needed, it establishes the average parking patterns, and makes 
the assumption that users will chose to charge their car every time they have the possibility to do it, in 
other words every time they are parked (EPRI, 2014). This technique does not give a theoretical optimal 
amount of chargers needed, because of the extreme nature of the underlying assumption.  
A new method is developed in this paper, built on a different approach. All the methods described 
above were developed to estimate the optimal number of chargers needed for the future fleet. But 
the goal here is to predict the number of chargers that will actually be built in the future, which may 
not correspond to the optimal number of chargers needed. First, BEV owners who do not have the 
possibility to install a charger at home will need public chargers for their primary charge. One public 
charger for two cars would be sufficient for a comfortable use of the cars (Dansk Energi, personal 
communication, February 1st, 2016). On the contrary, because of the short time of charge, REEV and 
PHEV will not require the installation of extra public chargers. As the market share for electric vehicles 
grows, companies, as well as public and private institutions will gradually install normal chargers in 
their parking lots to meet the increasing demand of their employees, customers and users. It can easily 
be imagined that shops which add charging stations for their customers’ vehicles will be preferred by 
the majority of BEV, REEV and PHEV owners. This would encourage the installation of charging stations 
in all the main parking lots of the city. Companies would potentially be willing to increase the 
convenience to their employees. This could potentially lead to a much higher number of charging 
stations installed than the optimal needed to meet demand.

substantially increase the convenience of owning a BEVs since it allows the owner to drive a bigger 
distance than the driving range in one travel. Indeed, because of the long time needed to charge 
entirely a BEV with a normal charger (from 6 to 8h in average), trips of longer distance than the 
autonomy of the vehicle are not feasible without the possibility of fast charging. The report of EPRI 
(2014) concludes that 5 fast chargers per 1000 electric vehicles are enough to drastically increase the 
proportion of long trips possible with a BEV.  
Concerning all vehicles working with fossil fuels, the fuel supply infrastructures are included in the 
study since it is part of the material flows of the vehicles in the database used as a base for the 
assessment (Ecoinvent v3.1). However, the hydrogen supply chain is not taken into account for the 
fuel-cell electric vehicles because of the low amount of vehicles penetrating the market in the two 
scenarios considered.

2.5. Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) 
Once all the data have been collected, the flows of substances coming from and going to the different 
environmental compartments (air, water, soil, sediment and biota) are retrieved using a LCIA software. 
An IA method is then selected and used to calculate the contribution of the system to different impact 
categories thanks to characterisation factors and finally express it as one score per category called 
characterised (e.g. for climate change in g CO2 eq). In order to compare the categories, normalisation 
and weighting can then be applied to the characterised scores. The software SimaPro (v.8.1.0.60) has 
been used in this study to build the model and conduct the impact assessment. Two IA methods have 
been chosen to quantify the contribution of the different scenarios on various impact categories: ILCD 
2011 Midpoint+ (v.1.07) (ILCD, 2010b; Hauschild et al., 2013) and ReCiPe Endpoint (Europe H/H) 
(v.1.12) (Goedkoop et al., 2013; Huijbregts et al., 2015). They have been chosen because they consider 
different levels of environmental mechanisms: Midpoint level indicators consider a higher level of 
details by considering one specific mechanism for a variety of substances within a specific impact 
category whereas Endpoint level indicators model all the way to the impact on the three areas of 
protections, i.e. Ecosystems, Human Health and Resource Depletion, thanks to additional modelling 
elements (ILCD, 2010a).  
ILCD Midpoint presents 16 impact categories: climate change, ozone depletion, human toxicity (non-
cancer effects), human toxicity (cancer effects), particulate matter, ionizing radiation (human health), 
ionizing radiation (ecosystems), photochemical ozone formation, acidification, terrestrial, freshwater 
and marine eutrophication, freshwater and marine ecotoxicity, land use, water resource depletion and 
mineral, fossil and renewable resource depletion. They are connected to multiple areas of concerns, 
such as human health, ecotoxicity in different compartments, air pollution or resource depletion. 
However, the impact category Ionizing Radiation (Ecosystems) is still at a development stage and thus 
is not recommended (Hauschild et al., 2013); it will not be considered in this paper. ILCD is using an 
equal weighting among the different impact categories (EC-JRC, 2012). 
ReCiPe Endpoint considers the three areas of protection that are Ecosystems, Human Health and 
Resource Depletion, and its Hierarchist version is used. Normalisation values for Europe are used and 
weighting is applied based on the Hierarchist perspective. 
2.6. Uncertainty and sensitivity analysis  
Even though data about the current situation are genuine and reliable, uncertainty is large concerning 
future vehicles and transportation habits and trends, and predictive studies in this field are rare. 
Scenarios can be developed analysed past trends, but the future is always unknown and can be 
potentially surprising. Another type of the uncertainty concerns the system modelling that is based in 
average values and models. As it is impossible to obtain real flows of materials constituting the average 
Danish vehicle, the model presented in Ecoinvent v3.1 is used as a basic model of car. However, it has 
not been developed to represent Danish vehicles and it is not possible to evaluate how different it is 
from the actual Danish vehicle.  
A Monte Carlo analysis should be conducted to quantify precisely the uncertainties. However, it was 
not possible to use it in this assessment due to software limitations. However, a number of 
uncertainties have been addressed through sensitivity analysis on multiple parameters. An overview 
of these parameters and of how much they vary is available in Table 2. Six sensitivity analysis will be 
conducted, three concerning methodological choices and three varying the input parameters.

the future fuel consumption will depend on the technologies’ development within the next decades, 
and is thus hard to anticipate. An average yearly reduction rate of 2,5% for fossil fuels and 1,25% for 
electricity is adopted for the study, which is increased by 10% for the sensitivity analysis. Finally, a new

3. Vehicle-based LCA 
The five powertrain technologies assessed in this study present different advantages and 
disadvantages in term of burdens for the environment and human health. Therefore, an overview of 
their impacts before examining the scenarios will allow a better understanding of the system. Gas 
stations are taken into account in the model, whereas charging stations and hydrogen supply stations

impact (less than 2% of the total score) so the decision was taken to neglect them here. Characterised

3.1. Environmental hotspots in the technologies’ life cycle 
The complete life cycle of a vehicle includes four phases: manufacturing, use, maintenance and end of 
life. The use phase consists of production and distribution of fuel or electricity, roads, exhaust 
emissions and brake, tyre and road wear emissions. The five technologies of vehicle present two 
important phase contributors: manufacturing and use. Together, they represent more than 70% of the 
score in at least 12 categories out of 15, including climate change and human toxicities (See Figure 7 
and Figure 8). The distribution among different phases remains globally the same between 2016 and 
2030 for all the technologies.

3.2.2. Climate change 
The category of climate change is essential when it comes to transportation. Indeed, the transport 
sector counts for one quarter of EU greenhouse gases emissions, which makes it the second biggest 
contributor (EU, 2016).  
When talking of private transportation, conventional cars equipped with an internal combustion 
engine are considered as great emitters of greenhouse gases because of their exhaust emissions. 
However, Figure 8a shows that it is not the technology that have the biggest impact on climate change 
in this model. The highest score is found for the current BEV (2016) and is caused primarily by the 
electricity consumption. This reflects the fact that the Danish electricity mix in 2016 has a particularly 
high level of greenhouse gases emissions. It is composed of more than 60% of coal, which is the 
electricity production’s mean with the highest carbon footprint. In 2030, the BEV score is only slightly 
lower than the ICEV one because even though the electricity impacts decrease, the ICEV fuel 
consumption reduces also thanks to technology optimisation aiming to decrease the exhaust 
emissions. BEVs suffer from the lack of maturity of the technology, which results in a lower yearly 
reduction rate of fuel consumption, and prevents it making a real difference by this impact category in 
2030. This finding is in contrast to the majority of studies comparing conventional vehicles to electric 
vehicles. An overview of the results found in other papers is available in Figure 12. To cite only few, 
Noshadravan (2015) found that climate change impacts of BEV is 40% lower than ICEV in 2015, Bauer 
(2015) found a reduction of 30% in 2012 and 60% in 2030 and Onat (2015) identified a diminishment 
of 30% in favour of the current BEV. The carbon footprint of electricity production is one of the main 
contributors, and it is likely that other countries have lower electricity impacts than Denmark, which 
partly explains this difference. This is illustrated in Huo’s results (2015), who considered various 
electricity generation portfolio in China and the USA, in order to assess the influence of the study’s 
location. They have observed that the score per kilometre varies by up to 200% depending on the 
assumption. Nevertheless, their maximum emission rate in 2015 corresponds to the number found in 
this study.

Another factor that explains the difference between the results of this study and other papers is the 
BEVs’ characteristics. Indeed, the average BEV considered here for 2016 is much heavier and consumes 
more electricity per kilometre than the regular electric vehicle from Ecoinvent, which is usually used 
in the other studies. Nevertheless, the characteristics used in this study to define BEVs are based on 
real-life data in Copenhagen area, retrieved from the actual composition of the electric vehicles fleet. 
Copenhagen BEV fleet is composed of a large amount of luxury cars, which at the same time represents 
a very low proportion of the ICEV fleet. Therefore, the average BEV is considerably more powerful, 
bigger, heavier and of higher standing than the average ICEV in Copenhagen. It makes it complicated 
to compare the two technologies, which are meant to provide services of different quality. Only the 
most advanced and expensive BEVs are truly attractive for customers today because of the novelty of 
the technology and thus the lack of maturity of the models available on the market. Furthermore, the 
vehicle used in this simulation have an average electricity consumption of 22,7 to 28,3 kWh/km 
whereas the one in Ecoinvent consumes only 19,9 kWh/km (Del Duce et al., 2014). This is again related 
to the difference of average size and comfort of the vehicle considered as well as the externalities (i.e. 
need of fuel related to secondary functions such as heat, air-conditioning and lights). Even though they 
have been calculated using a same procedure than in Ecoinvent, Denmark is a relatively cold country 
in Europe, so the amount of electricity used for heating the vehicle is substantially higher than the one 
obtained for the average vehicle in the database. 
Nevertheless, it needs to be highlighted that the main contributor to climate change in the case of the 
ICEV is the exhaust emissions whereas it is the electricity production in the case of BEV. This implies 
that GHG are emitted on a different way and, most importantly, in totally different places. For ICEV 
they will contribute to air pollution where the cars are driving, so most likely in the cities. On the 
contrary, electricity production is usually offset and takes place in the country side, most likely far from 
inhabitants, and thus have less impact on human health than ICEV’s emissions. The LCIA method used 
here do not take these differences into account. Therefore, even though the quantities of CO2 emitted 
are comparable in 2030, BEV is preferable to ICEV. 
To understand what the above numbers in g-CO2eq per kilometre corresponds to in real life, the results 
can be normalised on the annual CO2eq emissions per person and the number of kilometres driven per 
year. The resulting score corresponds thus to a percentage of the annual production of CO2eq by one 
person, and can be compared to the European numbers concerning transportation. The different 
technologies have a normalised score of 0,02 to 0,06 person-equivalent, corresponding to 2 to 6% of 
the annual production of CO2eq by one European inhabitant. The EU has calculated that cars are 
responsible of approximately 12% of the European CO2eq emissions. The number obtained could thus 
seem low, especially since the number retrieved by the EU is very likely to consider only exhaust 
emissions and not manufacturing ones, which represent a great part of the score obtained here. 
However, it needs to be highlighted that the city considered is Copenhagen, where an especially low 
number of kilometres driven per person per year is witnessed. Indeed, an average amount of 1778 
km/person/year has been used in this study based on the research of The Danish National Travel 
Survey (2013), whereas e.g. the French average is nearly 11000 km/person/year (Planetoscope, 2016). 
The difference is explained by, on the one hand, the fact that the average kilometres driven per day is 
lower in capitals and big cities in general, and on the other hand because of the intense use of biking 
in Copenhagen (København Kommune, 2012). All in all, the percentage of 2 to 6% is coherent. 
Except for FCEV, which is still in a development phase, REEV is the technology that has the lowest 
impacts for climate change. This technology is poorly represented on the market so far, but seems to 
be highly promising. Indeed, its low greenhouse gases emissions are mainly due to its low fuel 
consumption compared to an ICEV. Using the combustion engine to produce the electricity is expected 
to require five times less fuel than if it is used to drive the vehicle directly like in a conventional car 
(Tate et al., 2008). REEV are so to speak never considered in LCA studies looking for the best alternative 
to ICEV, and this lack of attention leads most of the authors to miss its very interesting advantages.

3.2.3. Mineral, fossil and renewable resource depletion 
Consumption of resources is an important topic when it comes to transportation. Conventional 
vehicles consume considerable amounts of petrol and diesel, which could lead to a lack of these limited 
resources in several decades. Some studies show pessimistic futures concerning the availability of 
energy for transport, such as Capellan-Perez et al. (2014) who concluded that it was unfeasible to 
follow the demand of energy for transport until 2020 because of resource scarcity. The ILCD 
methodology logically comports an impact category considering fossil fuel depletion, which gather also 
metals, minerals and renewables consumption. 
As shown in Figure 9b, ICEV from 2016 has the highest impact in this category, with HEV and REEV not 
far behind. The only technology that makes a difference is the full electric one, due to the surprisingly 
positive impact of lithium-ion battery. In Ecoinvent, the end of life of batteries includes recycling and 
re-use of almost 100% of the cobalt and lithium components, which then avoid the extraction of these 
metal from mines. This crediting is debatable, since the manufacturing of BEVs will need to answer a 
hypothetical increasing demand that will require fast and consequent extraction of lithium and cobalt. 
This also arises ethical issues, since the largest single lithium reserve worldwide is situated in Bolivia 
under the very touristic salt desert of Salar de Uyuni, and its exploitation would partly destroy the main 
source of revenue of numerous local inhabitants (USGS, 2014).  
As Figure 9b shows, fuels used during use phase have an impact smaller than 1% and can thus hardly 
be seen in the graph. This highlights the fact that even though fossil fuels are intensively used during 
the use phase, other resources are depleted for the manufacturing of the vehicles that might be even 
more problematic. However, this depends entirely on the importance given to the different resources 
in the elaboration of the methodology (here ILCD 2011 Midpoint), which is a subjective process 
depending on the data used by the authors and their disposition. In the ILCD method, metals are 
considerably more important than minerals and energy (Rørbech et al., 2014). This explains why fossil 
fuels of the use phase contribute so few compared to the crediting of metals. 
It is difficult to compare the results obtained in this impact category to the findings of other studies 
because the different papers use a variety of methods which consider different elements. Whereas 
some focus on the energy used, others consider only metals or minerals. 
3.2.4. Human toxicity (cancer and non-cancer effects) 
The three battery-equipped vehicles (BEV, HEV and REEV) have up to almost four times more impacts 
on human toxicity categories than ICEV. The BEV from 2016 has a score more than three times higher 
(see Figure 9c) for non-cancer effects and almost two times higher for cancer effects than the ICEV 
from 2016 (see Figure 9d). This is mainly due to the use of heavy metals for the battery, which have a 
non-negligible impact on human health. They can be absorbed by the human body through various 
ways, including food, water and air. Even though they are elements naturally present in small 
quantities in the environment, some anthropogenic actions, like mining operations, emit extra 
quantities and thus increase their concentration in the environment. This leads to an abnormal 
absorption of these substances by humans creating health issues (Duruibe et al., 2007). Solid waste 
leaches during mining operations are responsible of the great majority of human toxicity impacts 
(Norgate et al., 2006). In conventional life cycle impact assessment methodologies, it is assumed that 
all metals are leaching and penetrate the environment. However, Giurco et al. (2000) demonstrated 
that if the real mobility of metals is considered, ecotoxicity and human toxicity would be reduced by 
factors 5,5 and 3 respectively. Therefore, the numbers obtained here might be overestimated.

4.1. Overall performances of the scenarios 
When looking at the cumulative scores for the entire time scope, the two scenarios are ranked

problematic.

the characterisation factors. The results have been calculated again using another method: ReCiPe 
Midpoint (Europe H/H). The results are available in Appendix E, Table E1 and show that B10 performs 
better for 7 impacts categories, B100 shows better scores in 8 categories and in the three last ones 
they present equivalent results. This is similar to what have been found using ILCD methodology i.e. 
one scenario cannot be designated as considerably better than the other at that stage. When looking 
at the results in detail, the differences are roughly equivalent in the two methods. One divergence 
between methods is observed concerning resource depletion because mineral, fossil fuels, metals and 
renewables are combined in a single category “Mineral, fossil & renewable resource depletion” in ILCD 
whereas it is split in “Metal depletion” and “Fossil depletion” in ReCiPe where minerals and renewables 
depletion are not considered. Therefore, metal depletion in ReCiPe is much higher (+166%) for the 
scenario B10 because of the BEVs, whereas the combined category in ILCD promotes B10 as slightly 
better (-7%). This is due to the definition of the characterisation factors for the different material flows. 
In a second time, endpoint method has been used in order to determine if one scenario make a 
difference when looking at the overall picture. As indicates Figure 14, B10 is better than B100 in the 
three areas of protection but of just few percentage points. As for the results per category, this low 
difference can still be significant if the system uncertainties compensate each other between the two 
scenarios (see part 4.3.). 
When long-term emissions are not taken into account (sensitivity analysis No. 1), the results of the two 
scenarios vary slightly. It impacts 6 categories out of 15: human toxicity (cancer and non-cancer effect), 
ionizing radiation HH, freshwater ecotoxicity and marine and freshwater eutrophication. Their scores 
are decreasing, which is normal since less emissions are considered, and the difference between 
scenarios always change in favour of the B10 scenario (See Appendix E, Table E2). This is due to the 
reduction of impacts of the mining processes and deposition of heavy metals on site. This impacts B10 
more than B100 because in this scenario a lot more vehicles equipped with batteries are deployed, 
element composed mainly by rare metals like lithium. Even though few categories are impacted, they 
undergo an important change, and the difference between scenarios changes by up to 12 percentage 
points. Thus, the way in which long-term and short-term emissions are differentiated and weighted 
have a substantial impact on our system.  
4.3. Stability of the system relatively to its inputs 
The results show that the two scenarios differ by a 30 percentage points. This can be meaningful if the 
system is robust so that the errors are compensating each other from one scenario to the other. To 
check the stability of the system, a sensitivity analysis has been conducted on different parameters.

After changing the proportion of large BEV (sensitivity analysis No. 4), the results of two scenarios 
change very slightly and the difference between the two scenarios fluctuates of less than 2%. The 
system is not very sensitive to this parameter. The fuel consumption reduction rate has been increased 
by 10% for the sensitivity analysis No. 5. The results show that the system is not sensitive to that 
parameter either since the difference between the two scenarios does not experience any remarkable 
change. Finally, a new electricity mix has been considered to simulate a mix composed by more 
renewable energies in 2030. Just as for the other parameters, the change of the electricity mix has a 
small influence on the difference between scenarios (1% or less, depending on the impact category). 
All in all, each and every tested input parameter present no impacts on the results of the study. The 
system has numerous input parameters which have a considerable inertia since making them vary 
individually hardly influence the final results. A great majority of the errors present in one scenario are 
also present in the other one and thus compensate each other. This is explained by the fact that the 
two scenarios are composed by exactly the same elements (the five types of vehicles and the three 
types of charging infrastructures), but in different quantities, which leads to a system with a low 
sensitivity in general. Consequently, the system can be considered as very robust, and even a small 
percentage of difference gives a real advantage to one of the scenarios.  
4.4. Vehicles types and infrastructures contribution 
In this paper, three ways of calculating the number of infrastructures were tested. The impacts of the 
infrastructures themselves change from simple to triple depending on the choice of the method. 
Indeed, the total number of charging stations to be installed vary of up to 200% from one method to 
the other. However, the contribution of the infrastructures to the final score never represent more 
than 2% of the final score of the impact categories (see Figure 15). Additionally, it does not impact the 
difference between scenarios. It is thus clear that the infrastructure method does not influence the 
results even in the case of method 3, which has been developed for this paper and estimates a much 
higher number of infrastructures needed compared to the other methods (see Section 5.3.2.). Even in 
this case, infrastructures are the smaller contributor to the final results contributing for less than 1% 
in 14 impact categories and 2% in Freshwater Ecotoxicity. However, the different types of charging 
infrastructures do not change from 2016 to 2030, whereas new way of charging cars might be found 
and implemented with the “regular” ones (e.g. non-contact charging) which would change the impacts.

that the materials flows have been over-estimated when it comes to the fuel cells manufacturing and 
the vehicle powertrain used for FCEVs. All in all, this scenario might be plausible if an important 
breakthrough happens in this fuel cells technology and that hydrogen fuel becomes more accessible 
to consumers.

5. Conclusions & recommendations 
On a methodological perspective, it is important that more than one category be considered when a 
LCA on transportation is conducted to gain a more complete assessment. Furthermore, more options 
than the usual ICEVs and BEVs need to be examined to consider all the opportunities of environmental 
improvements possible. This study finds that non-conventional vehicles can reduce environmental 
impacts versus internal combustion engine vehicles. Depending on the impact category, the ranking 
between the various technologies vary, but overall the best options to focus on are FCEVs in long-term 
and REEVs as a transition technology. However, fuel-cells cars are still far from having a substantial 
share of the private vehicle market, and as such will not become a serious option for customers in the 
next decade without a major technological breakthrough. Based on these findings, decision makers 
could encourage and promote research and development in fuel-cells and hydrogen supply chains. 
Another option would be to incentivize car manufacturers in order to accelerate the entrance of REEVs 
on the market. This would provide a more environmental-friendly option before the deployment of 
FCEV. Concerning BEVs, this technology is only relatively interesting in Denmark, for two main reasons. 
The first one is that Denmark is a quite cold country, so an extra consumption of energy is required 
because of the low loss of heat of the electrical powertrain compared to an internal combustion 
engine. This increases the electricity consumption in a non-negligible manner. The second reason is 
that the Danish power system has high environmental impacts. When conducting LCA analyses, the 
electricity generation portfolio becomes an important parameter. To get the full benefits of BEVs, 
government efforts would need to focus on reducing the environmental footprint of electricity 
generation, in collaboration with the other countries that are part of the continental European 
electricity network. This would partly balance with the need of heat production in the passenger cabin. 
Finally, efforts could continue to be made to reduce the average size of BEVs in Copenhagen, which 
was encouraged before by the absence of taxes for electric vehicles. The potential benefits of REEVs 
and FCEVs are intriguing area for future research, as well as the hydrogen supply chain and the 
feasibility of FCEV as a dominant private transportation mode of the future.

Abstract Appendix A 
Transportation accounts for one quarter of the global CO2 emissions and is a main contributor to fossil 
resources depletion. Electric vehicles (EVs) represent a promising alternative to internal combustion 
engines for private transportation and need to be assessed in order to determine the actual 
environmental benefits. This review aims to analyze how the numerous life cycle assessments (LCA) 
on EVs have been conducted in order to highlight methodological trends and find out what are the key 
parameters which need a special attention. This will help future studies to select important factors to 
include and write accurate analysis. 39 recent publications have been examined, selected on precise 
criteria. The respect of LCA guidelines and methodology has been found heterogeneous among the 
papers. In particular, the scope definition is often neglected, even though this issue has already been 
spotted in previous reviews on the subject. This leads to ambiguous conclusions and recommendations 
that are hardly comparable. Additionally, more than one impact category should be considered and 
the complete life cycle of the vehicles should be taken into account to avoid misleading results and 
construct a thorough overview of the impacts. Some key contributors need to be studied with special 
attention. The main one is the modelling of the electricity mix. This is the first driver of the operation 
phase, which is presenting the highest environmental impact. Technological improvements should be 
considered in the case of a future time scope. Finally, the driving mode, the size of the vehicle and the 
end of life of the EVs need to be examined because of their important impact on the results. We thus 
recommend to carefully respect the LCA methodology, which will increase the average quality of the 
future studies and to show a complete transparency in the life cycle inventory. So far the conclusions 
that should be taken from the set of LCAs analyzed are ambiguous, and recommendations for decision 
makers are difficult to support. More studies should be conducted on large-scale systems, in order to 
allow a thorough comparison and, more important, reduce the high uncertainty of this type of 
assessment.

A.1. Introduction 
Accounting for 19% of global energy use and 23% of energy-related greenhouse gases (GHG) emissions 
(IEA, 2009), the transport sector contributes highly to two crucial current problematic: fossil-fuel 
depletion and climate change. Yet, this sector is growing rapidly, and energy use and CO2 emissions 
due to transport are expected to increase by nearly 50% by 2030 if no shift occurs in transportation 
trends (IEA, 2009). Given that climate change have already worldwide impacts on human health and 
ecosystems (IPCC, 2015), a change in the global transportation system is required within the next 
decades. One option to be investigated is the deployment of battery and hybrid electric vehicles, 
because of their promising characteristics that will potentially reduce GHG emissions. The main 
argument in favour of this affirmation is that full electric vehicles do not present any tailpipes 
emissions, and that they would be more energy efficient than internal combustion engines (Sadek, 
2012). However, the electricity used to power the vehicle as well as the manufacturing phase are not 
carbon-free. Complex technologies using scarce resources are needed, principally for the conception 
of the battery. 
Therefore, the real benefits of switching from conventional internal combustion vehicles to electric 
vehicles need to be assessed before any further deployment. Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a coherent 
tool to use when it comes to analyse the advantages of electric vehicles for society, as it can take into 
account the whole life cycle of the vehicle. However, carrying a LCA can be done in slightly different 
ways depending on the choice of the system definition, the hypothesis made about the data choice or 
the geographical and temporal scope. Comparing different studies is hence difficult due to the 
heterogeneity of their frames, and sometime the lack of consideration of crucial life cycle elements or 
stages. This can lead the authors to make various and sometimes contradictory conclusions, which are 
hardly comparable because of factors such as cited above. 
Reviews about LCA of electric vehicles (EVs) have already been conducted, aiming to summarize the 
results or to assess their relevance. Hawkins et al. (2012) established guidelines defining what should 
be a state-of-the-art LCA on EVs, and compared it to 55 studies found in the literature. Their conclusion 
is that none of the 55 papers comply with their definition of a thorough, complete LCA of EVs. Few 
years later, Nordelöf et al. (2014) reviewed 79 studies, arguing that the goal and scope definition is the 
key component for an accurate LCA, and concluded that this specific part is often neglected and lacking 
essential elements. Therefore, LCAs conducted on EVs in scientific literature are very unequal and 
seem to generally lack a frame that would prevent authors from disregarding important aspects and 
make the studies more easily comparable. 
This paper is making a literature review of recent LCA conducted on electric vehicles. Its goal is to 
identify key drivers of this type of study in order to determine crucial factors to be developed 
thoroughly in future LCAs. This can significantly improve the reliability of their results through the 
establishment of indispensable components and parameters to be included in a complete LCA in the 
field of electric mobility. Lessons learned from the articles will be discussed and recommendations will 
be stated to different types of stakeholders. 
A.2. Methodology 
 
A.2.1. Scope of included studies 
In 2014, Nordelöf et al. reviewed 79 papers that have been running LCA on electric and hybrid vehicles. 
It main methodological finding was that few studies define the goal and scope in an accurate way and 
that some crucial elements of the LCA methodology are often missing such as the functional unit. The 
goal of this study is to assess all recent publications in order to (1) analyse if and how Nordelöf’s 
recommendations have been implemented and (2) define an LCA framework to follow for future 
studies about electric mobility.

Only literature published in English is considered in this paper.  
The term “LCA” implies the use of a very specific methodology ruled by requirements and guidelines 
defined in ISO 14044 standards. In this review, all peer-reviewed articles and reports producing a “LCA” 
of EVs are considered regardless of how compliant they are to the ISO 14044 standards, as well as 
some papers presenting an environmental impact analysis that includes at least energy consumption 
and air emissions impacts, but do not literally specify that they are producing a LCA. 
Electric vehicles include full electric vehicles, or Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs), and Plug-in Hybrid 
Electric Vehicles (PHEVs).  
The 79 papers reviewed by Nordelöf et al. (2014) have not been assessed in details, but will be

criteria are covering all the main steps of a LCA: goal definition, scope definition, life cycle inventory, 
life cycle impact assessment and interpretation.

A.3.1. General considerations and goal of the study 
A.3.1.1. Temporal evolution 
The number of studies conducting LCAs on EVs have been relatively stable from 1998 to 2005 with up to 
three studies per year, before suddenly increasing until 2012, when the peak number of 18 studies can be 
witnessed. It went down again in 2013 and is increasing slowly since then, as showed inFigure A19. This 
illustrates a rising interest of the scientific community for the electric mobility problematic, especially. 
However, the increasing rate since 2012 have slowed down. This could be the proof that this technology 
is now well implemented in our everyday life and does not seem as revolutionary as it could have been in 
the past.  
Figure A19: Total number of articles per year 
A.3.1.2. Geographical and temporal scope 
Defining a clear temporal and geographical scope is crucial to conduct an accurate LCA on EVs, and is too 
often neglected (Nordelöf et al., 2014). In 15 studies out of the 39, at least one of these two characteristics 
were not explicitly defined. 
Concerning the temporal scope, most of the studies are considering implicitly the “current situation”, 
meaning technology, electricity network and driving habits in place when the paper is written. However, 
they do not state explicitly which year they are considering for their life cycle inventory, e.g. which 
electricity mix is used. This is problematic for the comparison of the results with other papers and, to a 
larger extend, with the use of the results in general. Indeed, they are highly dependent on the technology 
considered, the carbon footprint of the electricity production and the extraction of the raw material, as 
well as plenty other factors which can all vary with time.  
The one third of the studies working with a future perspective were generally good in defining the origin 
of the data they were using.

The geographical scope of the studies was generally well described. Only two papers of the set did not 
specify in which country or continent the study was run. Most of the papers have been studying the USA, 
as it can be seen in Figure A2. A considerable number of studies have been conducted on Portugal, 
compared to the size of the country. 
A.3.1.3. Object of the study 
Different approach of the LCA of EVs have been developed within the last decade, leading to different 
possible focus. Figure A21 shows that 65% of the studies are centred on the vehicles themselves, 
considering the complete life cycle of the object, or what the authors consider as equivalent to it. Some 
other studies are limiting their focus on one object of the system, like the battery or the infrastructures.  
From a totally different perspective, three of the papers are limiting the study to the Well-to-Wheel (WTW) 
life cycle. Their argument is that the impacts of manufacturing of conventional vehicles and electric ones 
are so similar that they can be neglected in a comparison. This type of approach was frequent in the 2000s 
(Nordelöf et al. (2014) reviewed 26 WTW LCA out of 79 articles), but reviews showed that this hypothesis 
can be inaccurate because of battery manufacturing. Only three authors of our set of papers have chosen 
to follow this approach, which may signify that the findings and recommendations of reviews like the one 
conducted by Nordelöf (2014) have been considered by the scientific community.

A.3.2. Scope definition  
A precise and thorough scope definition is important to allow the reader to understand a LCA study. The 
possibilities are so wide in term of system boundaries and frames that a lack of details can be at least 
misleading, and in the worst case it can erase totally the relevance of the assessment.  
A.3.2.1. Functional unit 
The choice of the functional unit is decisive for the rest of the assessment and the interpretation of the 
results. More than half of the studies reviewed have chosen a distance as a functional unit (Figure A22). 
This presents some advantages like allowing to compare vehicles with different lifetime. As a matter of 
fact, another common way to define the functional unit in LCAs of EVs is one “vehicle or battery life”, 
defined by a certain number of years or kilometres driven in the whole life of the object. But usually these 
studies assume the same lifetime for all the vehicles they are comparing, which is usually unrealistic. 
Figure A22: Functional unit categories 
Some papers enlarge their assessment to the whole fleet of a city, a region or a country. They usually study 
a period of time and establish a model representing the evolution of the fleet during this period. A small 
amount of studies has used energy as a functional unit. 
Generally speaking, the functional unit definition was vague and short, or inexistent. Almost 40% of the 
papers do not define it explicitly. When this happened, the functional unit presented in Table A3 have 
been deducted from the lecture of the paper. Even when the authors state it explicitly, they frequently 
lack a temporal and geographical frame, and remain imprecise about the driving cycle, the electricity mix 
and the charging habits.  
A.3.2.2. Handling of the multifunctional processes 
Hardly any study has stated explicitly the way they are handling multifunctional processes. Out of the 39, 
only five declared they have been using an attributional approach and a single one specified they have

been following a consequential one. This illustrates again the poor respect of LCA methodology in general 
in the batch of publications under study. 
A.3.2.3. System boundaries 
Because of the systemic dimension of electric mobility, LCA studies on EVs can be conducted with a 
technological or a systemic approach. A technological perspective implies that the authors have focused 
their attention on one or several specific technologies, considering only inert products or process (vehicles, 
batteries or well-to-wheel) as it is manufactured, without taking into account any interactions with the 
external environment. A systemic perspective considers a whole system, introducing a notion of average 
characteristics and aims to include the product in the environment it is used (e.g. considering 
infrastructures, roads, etc). It is challenging in term of data collection and modelling, but often it offers a 
more complete and accurate view of a situation. 
In our set of papers, 56% used a systemic approach of LCA. It should be mentioned that it is rarely explicitly 
specified and that the classification has been done based on information given in the study. 
In some cases, the choice between systemic and technological was debatable because of a lack of 
transparency about the modelling of the system and the scope of the LCA. This is also problematic in the 
interpretation of the results. 
In the case of a systemic approach, different elements have been taken into account in the system 
boundaries, as illustrated by Figure A5. While half of the papers considered only vehicles, some others 
have added roads or charging infrastructures to the assessment. Arguments supporting these choices 
could not be found in the majority of the cases. On the other hand, three papers adopted a systemic 
approach but considered only the batteries for the EVs. This choice was always well explained, the reason 
being that the rest of the vehicles could be considered as equivalent between the different technologies 
examined.

is crucial for the results as well as for their interpretation. Authors usually chose to use the average mix of 
a country or region (Table A4). Data about the electricity of a country or region are often available, which 
makes the life cycle inventory easier, and makes sense in most of the contexts. Particularly in the case of

A.3.4. Impact coverage 
As seen in Table A3, the majority of the studies focus their impact assessment on GHG emissions and 
energy consumption. Four of them make only a sum of emissions without further assessment, not even 
aggregating them as an indicator. Only half of the papers under consideration in the review are considering 
other indicators than GHG emissions and energy consumption, and less than a quarter considered more 
than 5 other categories. This lead to weaknesses in the conclusions, as they consider a limited amount of 
criteria. Indeed, examining only one or two categories is not enough to conclude (Ercan and Tatari, 2015; 
Bauer et al., 2015) because the results can be drastically different depending on the categories considered. 
For example, GHG emissions and water withdrawal usually show completely opposite conclusions, which 
is also usually the case for toxic impacts, such as human health or ecotoxicity.  
 
A.3.5. Findings of the studies (lessons learned) 
An overview of the key results presented by the studies allows to highlight some general conclusions about 
LCAs on EVs. However, it should be remembered that the publications reviewed here are studying different 
situations with various scopes and parameters. Thus, every single conclusion is intimately linked to the 
frame of its paper, and should not be considered as a general statement. 
A.3.5.1. Comparison between different technologies  
Generally, battery electric vehicles (BEVs) are found as having lower GHG emissions and energy 
consumption than conventional internal combustion engine vehicles (ICEVs). However, four papers 
concluded that hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) would be an even better option to reduce global GHG 
emissions today. This is due to more efficient technologies available for HEVs than BEVs on the market for 
==PAGE:51==
Master Thesis 
 
Florence Alexia Bohnes 
XII 
 
the moment (Abdul-Manan, 2015). Regarding the models sold nowadays, the energy and GHG emissions 
of battery production increases with the degree of electrification of the vehicle, and these impacts are not 
compensated by the savings during the operation phase (Lewis et al., 2014). For this reason, He and Chen 
(2013) recommend to wait until the technology of BEVs is more efficient before starting to seriously 
promote it through ambitious policies and regulations, and that promoting it synchronously with HEVs 
would be the best strategy in term of emissions reduction.  
Nevertheless, electric mobility is a constantly evolving technology, which is and will be undergoing 
significant improvements during the next decades. Energy efficiency and GHG emissions of the current 
models are likely to evolve drastically in the future before reaching the maturity of the technology. 
Therefore, it is particularly important to consider expected technological developments in studies 
assessing future scenarios (Bauer et al., 2015; Garcia et al., 2015).  
A.3.5.2. Contribution analysis and key parameters 
Operation phase is determined as the main contributor to environmental impacts of EVs in a majority of 
papers. This phase is highly driven by electricity production, hence the importance of the right modelling 
of electricity mix. Indeed, electricity supply is explicitly stated as being the first key parameter of LCAs of 
EVs in 22 publications of our set. However, the carbon footprint of electricity production varies drastically 
from one region of the world to the other. For example, some regions in the USA present up to 60% lower 
GHG emissions and up to 95% lower air pollutant emissions per unit of electricity produced compared with 
Chinese regions (Huo et al., 2015). Therefore, benefits from the deployment of EVs in carbon intensive 
electricity production regions can be inexistent to negative. MacPherson (2014) even recommend to stop 
people from buying electric vehicles in these areas.  
The second contributor to environmental impacts of EVs is the manufacturing phase. Some studies have 
even found the production phase and the use phase in the same order of size (Zackrisson et al., 2010). 
Considered alone, manufacturing phase is typically higher for EVs than ICEVs because of the battery and 
the significant amount of resources required in its fabrication. 
Another influencing parameter in LCAs of EVs is the driving mode. In general, a lower average driving cycle 
has a negative impact on fuel consumption of vehicles (Ercan and Tatari, 2015). However, the difference 
is lower for EVs than for ICEVs, which makes the use of EVs especially interesting in low average driving 
cycle situations with frequent stops (Yuan et al., 2015). This is also showed by Hutchinson et al. (2014), 
who demonstrated that in cities benefits of EVs are obvious, whereas this is not the case concerning 
highway trips. Additionally, driving aggressiveness can have a significant environmental impact for ICEVs 
as well as BEVs (Noshadravan et al., 2014). Thus, the influence of the driving behaviors and conditions 
chosen by the authors have a considerable impact on results and should be the object of a special attention 
during the elaboration of the scope of the LCA. A light use can decrease the kilometer impact by 42 to 50% 
compared to an intensive use (Faria et al., 2012). 
Other parameters can influence the results of LCAs on EVs, like the size of the vehicle. As a matter of fact, 
heavier vehicles have a larger environmental impact than small ones (Messagie et al., 2014). This is also 
true, to a greater extent, for EVs, since batteries needed in heavy vehicles are much bigger than in small 
ones. It can then switch the tendency for other indicators than GHG emissions and energy consumption 
and obtain a worst score for BEVs than ICEVs (Querini and Benetto, 2015). 
Modelling of a realistic end of life for the vehicles is often neglected, certainly driven by the lack of 
knowledge of the authors concerning the handling multifunctional processes (See section A.3.2.2). 
Nevertheless, the ones that have consider it conclude that e.g. the recycling rate of the battery can be 
significant in the results (Yu et al., 2014). This is supported by Manzetti et al. (2015) in their review of the

different approaches of LCAs on EVs. Messagie et al. (2014) also found that end of life is a key factor of the 
assessment. 
A.3.5.3. Focus on studies using broader impact assessment 
Considering only GHG emissions can be misleading because of the wide panel of environmental impacts 
that have EVs. Thus, GHG emissions or global warming potential is not representative of the other 
categories (Sanfélix et al., 2014) and the need to consider more than one indicator has been stated by 
multiple articles (Bauer et al., 2015). Consequently, half of the publications in this review have been 
considering other categories than GHG emissions and energy consumption. 
While environmental benefits of EVs concerning reduction of GHG emissions are widely recognized and 
repeatedly proved, it is not the case for other categories. The assessments are usually mitigated, and the 
overall conclusions highly depend on the importance the authors put on the different categories. However, 
this consideration is personal and implicit, as none of them have aggregated them in a single score using 
a specific life cycle impact assessment methodology.  
In general, other categories than global warming potential show contradictive conclusions within the set 
of articles considered. For example, some articles have found that BEVs have a higher score in particulate 
matter formation compared to ICEVs (Querini and Benetto, 2015; Lang et al., 2013), while other have 
showed a decrease (Girardi et al. 2015). The fact that EVs have worst results regarding human toxicity than 
ICEVs is ambiguous as well. Girardi et al. (2015) and Bauer et al. (2015) found that EVs have a much bigger 
impact than ICEVs (+40 to 80% and +400% respectively) whereas Messagie et al. (2014) found them 
comparable and Luk et al. (2015) and Onat et al. (2014) found them slightly lower. This illustrates the 
strong dependence of the results on the set of hypothesis regarding e.g. the modelling of electricity mix. 
Comparing studies is extremely hard because of the multiple parameters influencing the findings. In the 
last example, Girardi have used the European marginal mix, Bauer and Messagie the European average 
mix, Luk 100% natural gas source and Onat the average USA mix, which may be one of the reasons to the 
huge difference witnessed in their results. 
Nevertheless, some other categories are coherent within the studies. Water withdrawal is found to be way 
higher for BEVs than any other type of vehicle in the two studies that consider it (Ercan and Tatari, 2015; 
Onat et al., 2014). This important demand of water could turn it to a critical resource in the transportation 
sector if the shift to electric mobility progress as expected.  
Contrarily to what would be expected, BEVs show better results than ICEVs when it comes to abiotic 
resource depletion, even though the battery manufacturing requires an important amount of rare and 
non-rare metals (Girardi et al., 2015; Messagie et al., 2014; Querini and Benetto, 2015; Notter et al., 2010).   
A.3.5.4. Focus on fleet-based studies 
A study using a functional unit taking a whole fleet into account can be run in at least two different ways. 
The first one establishes a precise evolution of the fleet over a period of time and compare the impacts of 
different scenarios over the whole period. That is how Querini and Benetto (2015) have studied 
Luxembourg and Lorraine from 2013 to 2020 and Garcia et al. (2015) focused on Portugal between 1995 
and 2030. This could be called a “period approach”. The second way to proceed to a fleet-based study is 
to define a target year, to establish the profile of the fleet for this year (which could be in the present or 
in the future) and to make yearly balance of impacts. Buekers et al. (2014) established an assessment of 
every European country in 2010 and 2030, MacPherson et al. (2014) studied the yearly emissions of the 
USA from 2009 to 2035 and He and Chen (2013) examined the situation of the Chinese fleet in 2030. In 
most cases, scenarios need to be developed regarding the trends of deployment of the different types of 
==PAGE:53==
Master Thesis 
 
Florence Alexia Bohnes 
XIV 
 
vehicles, the evolution of electricity mix and the driving habits. This type of assessment is usually used to 
examine large-scale impacts of new/hypothetical policies or regulations. 
That is the case for Querini and Benetto (2015), who studied the implementation of a new policy and its 
impacts for the fleet until 2020 in Luxembourg and Lorraine. They state that a mass scale deployment will 
not occur before the end of their scope, so that their results are likely to be verified. In the case of a mass 
deployment, the assessment of EVs impacts will be highly more complex. However, implementation of the 
new policies will have a positive impact compared to a scenario without EVs in six categories out of ten. 
But e.g. ionizing radiations will increase a lot, due to the electricity generation mainly based on nuclear 
power in France. Thus, one of the recommendations of the paper is to switch French mix to other mean 
of production.  
On the contrary, Buekers et al. (2014), who are studying the deployment of EVs to 5% in the 27 European 
countries, concluded that it would actually be more beneficial in France because of the low carbon 
footprint of its electricity mix thanks to nuclear power. However, they do not assess ionizing radiation, 
which leads to this contradictory conclusion. Still, they recognize that impacts of nuclear power are 
debatable. In parallel, they determined that a deployment of EVs in Europe of 5% in 2030 would be better 
than business as usual in 23 counties out of 27. 
Garcia et al. (2015) also assess that an important share of EVs in the fleet will not happen before 2025. 
They reached the conclusion that if the deployment of EVs wants to be beneficial, the current carbon 
footprint of electricity needs to decrease. Additionally, when taking into account technological 
improvements of ICEVs and their increasing energy efficiency, the advantages of BEVs become ambiguous. 
MacPherson et al. (2014) established that the deployment of EVs can be highly beneficial for the US, even 
though without a drastic shift in policies, these benefits will be very limited. They suggest to base the level 
of credit in case of a purchase of a EVs on the carbon footprint of electricity mix of the region the customer 
buys the car, highlighting once again the importance of the electricity mix. 
He and Chen (2013) studied the case of China, and supported that a deployment of energy efficient EVs is 
crucial for the achievement of the Chinese goals in term of emissions reduction. Thus, the technology 
needs to keep improving in order to reach a much higher efficiency in few years, which would allow the 
Chinese government to reach their threshold in term of GHG emissions. Furthermore, promoting HEVs and 
BEVs in parallel would be the more interesting choice of policy.  
A.3.5.5. Focus on studies considering the infrastructures  
Four of the paper reviewed in this article are considering the infrastructures needed for the deployment 
of EVs. Ercan and Tatari (2015) are including them in its comparative LCA of different technologies of buses, 
and finds out that infrastructures are negligible for all types of vehicles. On the contrary, Lucas et al. (2013) 
are considering passenger vehicles and concludes that the infrastructures can account for up to 12% of 
the life cycle GHG emissions, which is not negligible. 
A.4. Conclusions and recommendations 
This review has been analyzing 39 LCAs conducted on EVs through different criteria. A high number of LCA 
have been conducted in this field, but their quality is highly variable. The goal and scope definition is poorly 
detailed in most of the papers, which conduct to misleading results and interpretation of the results. 
Furthermore, the studies can hardly be compared one to another. Even though these issues have already 
been highlighted in past reviews and methodology analysis, it has not been taken into account in all the 
new publications. It seems that the LCA methodology is poorly known by the authors in general, which 
==PAGE:54==
Master Thesis 
 
Florence Alexia Bohnes 
XV 
 
question their competence in this field and the quality of the peer review when the study has been 
published in a scientific journal. Although LCA is a useful tool when it is used wisely, it loses all its value 
when used approximately by novices in the field. Therefore, the following recommendations concerns in 
particular authors not familiar with the field of LCA but who are willing to use this method to assess a 
technology, a product or a system. They of course also apply to all the authors willing to conduct a LCA on 
EVs. 
On the methodology: 
- 
Before beginning a LCA on EVs, get familiar (or reconnect) with the structure and the guidelines of 
such an analyzing tool (See ILCD Handbook (2010) and ISO 14044). 
- 
Follow thoroughly the steps of the LCA, with special attention on the scope definition. 
- 
Pay special attention to the transparency of the life cycle inventory. 
- 
Consider a complete LCA, composed of at least the cradle-to-grave of the entire vehicles and the 
WTW. 
- 
Observe more than one impact category (e.g. GHG emissions). 
- 
Link the results in an obvious way to the hypothesis, to avoid misleading interpretation of the 
results for the readers who would go directly to the conclusion. 
On the content:  
- 
Pay special attention to the following parameters: 
o Modelling of electricity mix 
o Technological improvements in the case of a time scope in the future 
o Driving mode 
o Size and mass of the vehicle 
o End of life 
By respecting all these suggestions, future LCAs conducted on EVs will be easier to interpret and, to a larger 
extent, to compare. This will allow decision makers to have a better overview of the advantages and 
disadvantages of electric mobility and help them taking the right decisions about if and how to accelerate 
the deployment of this technology.  
Indeed, so far the conclusions that should be taken from the set of LCAs analyzed are ambiguous. All the 
studies do not present equivalent results, and sometimes the link between these results and their advice 
directed to decision makers is questionable. Even though the majority of the studies show positive 
environmental implications to the deployment of electric vehicles, most of them are based on biased 
assumptions and debatable hypothesis. Only a limited part of the picture is considered. Nevertheless, 
some interesting studies have been conducted on large-scale systems, but the important number of 
parameters to take into account rises drastically the uncertainty of the results. Therefore, more studies 
should be conducted on large-scale systems, in order to allow a thorough comparison and, more 
important, thwart the high uncertainty of this type of assessment.

B.1. Main hypothesis 
Only passenger vehicles are considered. The zone under study is called Zone Z and corresponds to 
København Omegn and København By. Five powertrain technologies are considered: 
- 
ICEV (Internal Combustion Engine Vehicle): normal combustion engine. 
- 
BEV (Battery Electric Vehicle): electric motor and battery as energy storage. 
- 
HEV (Hybrid Electric Vehicle): with (PHEV) and without plug-in (nPHEV), combustion engine and 
electric motor. 
- 
REEV (Range-Extended Electric Vehicle): electric motor and combustion engine to generate energy 
for range extension. 
- 
FCEV (Fuel-Cell Electric Vehicle): electric motor and fuel-cells as energy storage.

CALCULATIONS: 
Vehicle life: 
The life time of the vehicles is assumed to be the same for all the models of vehicles and stays constant 
through the years (even though it is differentiated for the components such as the battery). 
LM = LSimaPro, ∀M. 
Battery life: 
The life time of the batteries is supposed to be the same for all the models of vehicles that have a battery 
and stays constant through the years. 
LbM = LbSimaPro, ∀M. 
 
Vehicles weight: 
The weight of the vehicles is assumed to decrease every year thanks to technology improvements and the 
rate of decrease is the same for all types of fuel and stays constant through the years. 
 
mM t2 = mM t1 * (1-Dm)5 
 
mM t3 = mM t1 * (1-Dm)10 
Fuel consumption: 
The fuel and electricity consumption decreases every year thanks to technology improvements and the 
rate of decrease is the same for all types of fuel and stays constant through the years. 
 
FCM t2 = FCM t1 * (1-DFC)5 
 
FCM t3 = FCM t1 * (1-DFC)10 
 
ECM t2 = ECM t1 * (1-DEC)5 
 
ECM t3 = ECM t1 * (1-DEC)10 
Due to factors like occupancy rate, type deflation, driving behavior and air conditioning, the real-world 
fuel consumption differs from the theoretical one with the NEDC cycle. All of these factors are gathered in 
a rate to apply to fuel consumption. 
 
FCreal M = (1+µ)*FCM 
Due to factors like occupancy rate, type deflation and driving behavior, the real-world electricity 
consumption differs from the theoretical one with the NEDC cycle. Additionally, the heating, the air 
conditioning and the radio, lights, etc, consume more electricity. This is gathered in a constant per km 
(they are considered to work 0,83%, 0,04% and 33% of the time respectively). 
 
ECreal M = (1+α)*FCM+ αSC 
Remark: the constant is considered only for the BEVs, to avoid double counting. 
Battery weight: 
Thanks to technology improvements, battery density will increase in the near future and stay constant 
from 2021. At the same time, the capacity of the battery on board vehicles will increase. (Garcia et al., 
2015) 
Thus, the battery decrease rate from 2016 to 2020 is the following: 
 
DBW=1- (BC2020/BD2020)/ (BC2015/BD2015)

and size of the glider) for the average REEV than for the small-medium BEV, it has been chosen to take the

heavier vehicle.

m{electrical powertrain} REEV t1= m{electrical powertrain} BEV SM t1 
 
M REEV T1 = M{glider + electrical powertrain} REEV t1+ Mb REEV t1 + M{ICE} REEV t1

m {glider } REEV t1= Pglider/ICE * M{ICE} REEV t1

Method 3: Need per location 
 
There are two types of charging: primary charging, which happens mainly at night and at home, and 
secondary charging, which happens anywhere the driver is, with the only purpose to finish its trip.  
- 
Primary charging: If the people who do not a private parking spot want to be able to have an 
electric car, a certain amount of public chargers are needed.  
- 
Secondary charging: depends entirely on the driving range, which keeps evolving, and less and less 
vehicles need to charge again during the day. However, multiple places will take the decision to 
build a charger in their parking lot. Among others, the companies, and especially the ones with a 
lot of employees.
