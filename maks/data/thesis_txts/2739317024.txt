Abstract 
Digital dermatitis (DD) is a prevalent infectious disease in cattle, leading to lameness, reduced milk 
production, and significant economic losses in the dairy and beef industries. Characterized by 
painful lesions, particularly in the interdigital area, DD results from complex interactions between 
bacterial pathogens and the host's immune response. This study conducted a comprehensive 
statistical analysis to examine the impact of DD on gene expression, utilizing logistic and linear 
regression models, as well as t-test analyses. Significant molecular markers distinguishing different 
stages of DD were identified, with a focus on the upregulation and downregulation of genes 
involved in inflammatory pathways and tissue remodeling. 
The study also explored the correlation between epithelial thickness and specific gene expressions 
in non-affected biopsies, revealing that higher expression levels of key genes were associated with 
thinner epithelial layers. Notably, STAT6, STAT3, STAT5B, and IL23P19 were linked to slower 
epithelial thinning, while GATA3 and TLR4 were associated with faster thinning. Additionally, 
upregulated expression of pro-inflammatory genes (IL8, IL6, IL1B, IL1A) and MMP-13 in lesions 
indicated a robust inflammatory response and active tissue remodeling. Conversely, the 
downregulation of TIMP3 and the upregulation of MMP13 suggested an imbalance between MMPs 
and TIMPs, highlighting the presence of affected tissue. 
These findings provide valuable insights into the local immune response and inflammation in 
bovine digital dermatitis, offering potential targets for therapeutic intervention and advancing our 
understanding of the disease's pathogenesis. This research not only contributes to the broader 
understanding of DD but also paves the way for developing more effective treatment strategies, 
ultimately aiming to reduce the economic impact on the cattle industry.

1. Introduction 
Bovine digital dermatitis (BDD) represents a significant health challenge within the global dairy 
industry, resulting in substantial economic burdens and compromising the overall health and 
productivity of cattle (Caddey et al., 2021). It is characterized by the development of painful, 
ulcerative lesions on the hooves, particularly in the interdigital region. These lesions are linked to 
the presence of various Treponema species, a group of anaerobic bacteria that thrive in the moist 
and suboptimal conditions prevalent in many modern dairy farms (Faezi et al., 2022). The infection 
by these bacteria leads to the formation of proliferative lesions around the heels, which are 
extremely difficult to treat effectively within herds (Palmer & O’Connell, 2015).  
The enduring nature of these lesions contributes to chronic lameness, reduced feed and water 
intake, and impaired reproductive performance, worsening the economic and ethical implications 
of the disease. This infectious hoof disease primarily affects the skin of digits of dairy cattle, 
manifesting through symptoms such as lameness and reduced milk production. These symptoms 
are not only indicative of the animal's distress but also lead to significant economic losses for dairy 
farmers due to decreased productivity and increased veterinary costs (Urban-Chmiel et al., 2024). 
The disease was initially reported in Italy by Cheli and Mortellaro and has since been 
acknowledged as a universal cause of lameness in cattle across numerous countries worldwide 
(Nally et al., 2015).  
The prevalence of lameness cases caused by digital dermatitis (DD) varies significantly depending 
on geographical regions and farming practices, ranging from 10% to 40% (Wilson-Welder et al., 
2015) . In Denmark, BDD poses a significant challenge for the dairy industry. Recent studies have 
shown that the prevalence of BDD among dairy cows exceeds 20% (Thomsen et al., 2008), with 
the disease present in approximately 85% of dairy herds (Capion et al., 2008). 
Despite the availability of treatments, managing BDD remains a challenge. Traditional treatment 
methods involve the application of topical antimicrobial agents, the use of footbaths, and the 
administration of systemic antibiotics. However, these interventions often yield inconsistent 
results, with a high incidence of disease recurrence (Mudroň et al., 2021). This inconsistency 
underscores the need for a more nuanced understanding of the disease's pathogenesis and the local 
immune response.

1.1 Problem Statement 
The clinical and epidemiological features of BDD have been widely investigated, yet the precise 
molecular processes underlying BDD pathogenesis remain unclear. The complexities of the bovine 
immune response to the disease pose a significant challenge in understanding BDD, particularly in 
deciphering the exact dynamics and balance of pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory responses. 
This includes the roles of various cytokines, toll-like receptors, and metalloproteinases. This 
knowledge gap hampers the development of targeted treatments and preventive measures, thereby 
slowing progress toward more specific and effective interventions that could mitigate the impact 
of BDD, enhance animal welfare, and reduce economic losses in the cattle industry. 
1.2 Purpose of the Project 
The aim of this project is to gain deeper insights into the skin's immune response during different 
stages of DD by investigating the expression of key genes (see Appendix 1, List of Gene Names), 
such as cytokines (e.g., IL1A, IL1B, IL6) and toll-like receptors (TLR4), in cattle with active and 
chronic DD lesions. Specifically, this study seeks to explore the relationship between the expression 
levels of these genes and various factors, including epithelial thickness (in both healthy skin and 
DD lesions) and the severity of DD, as assessed by DD-score and M-stages. 
Statistical tools and analyses, including linear regression analysis and logistic regression analysis, 
will be employed to thoroughly examine these gene expression patterns, aiming to identify 
biomarkers that could enhance diagnosis, facilitate targeted treatment options, and improve the 
management of BDD.  
1.3 Hypotheses  
In addressing the complex relationship between gene expression and BDD, this research is 
structured around several key hypotheses:  
I. 
Specific genetic expressions are hypothesized to correlate with varying degrees of epithelial 
thickness in affected cattle.  
II. 
Specific gene expressions are hypothesized to be associated with an increased DD score. 
III. 
Differences in gene expression are hypothesized to exist between the various M-stages. 
More specifically, the gene expression profiles are expected to differ between M4.1- stage 
and M1-stage and between M4.1-stage and M2-stage.

2. Background  
2.1 Overview of Bovine Digital Dermatitis 
2.1.1 Histological Description of Digital Dermatitis Lesions 
DD lesions are commonly found on the plantar surface of the hind foot in bovine species, especially 
dairy cows. Histologically, these lesions exhibit several distinct features, including an initial loss 
of the stratum corneum and/or stratum granulosum. The epidermal layers show significant 
disruption, particularly with the invasion of the stratum spinosum by spirochetes. This bacterial 
infiltration is a hallmark of DD, often leading to epidermal hyperplasia, where the epidermis 
becomes thicker due to an increased rate of cell proliferation (Wilson-Welder et al., 2015). 
The dermis beneath these lesions shows signs of intense reactive inflammation. This inflammation 
is characterized by the infiltration of various immune cells, including neutrophils, plasma cells, 
lymphocytes, and eosinophils. These cells are part of the body's immune response, attempting to 
combat the bacterial invasion and repair tissue damage. The presence of these inflammatory cells 
contributes to the ulcerative and erosive nature of the lesions (Wilson-Welder et al., 2015). 
As the condition progresses, the lesions may enlarge and develop papilliform projections, which 
are small, strawberry-like extensions on the surface of the skin. These projections can further 
worsen the condition, making the lesions more prone to ulceration and physical trauma. Over time, 
the chronic inflammation and ongoing infection can lead to significant tissue damage, with areas 
of both active hyperemia (increased blood flow) and chronic proliferation present within the same 
lesion (Wilson-Welder et al., 2015). 
Pain upon palpation and lameness are common clinical signs associated with DD lesions, although 
these symptoms are not universally present in all affected animals. The lesions are often prone to 
bleeding when touched due to the fragile nature of the damaged tissue. 
2.1.2 Microbial Etiology and Pathogenesis of BDD 
BDD has been recognized as a major infectious disease affecting dairy cattle since its first 
description in 1974. Even after extensive research, the exact etiology and pathogenesis of BDD 
remain unknown. Several bacterial species and environmental factors seem to play a role in the 
development of this multifactorial disease. 
BDD is primarily associated with multiple species from the genus Treponema. These spirochetes 
are consistently found in the superficial and deeper layers of the epidermis, especially at the 
==PAGE:13==
Page 12 of 71 
 
interface between healthy and diseased tissue (M. W. Nielsen et al., 2016). Numerous Treponema 
species were found in these locations, suggesting that they contribute significantly to the 
pathogenesis of BDD. Among the most commonly identified species in BDD lesions are 
Treponema phagedenis, Treponema medium/"vincentii"-like, and Treponema pedis. As a result of 
their fastidious nature and slow growth, these spirochetes are challenging to culture and study 
(Faezi et al., 2022). 
Several dairy farming environments provide conducive conditions for BDD transmission, such as 
prolonged moisture exposure and relatively anaerobic conditions. 
BDD is considered a polybacterial complex due to the fact that multiple types of bacteria have been 
cultured and identified from active lesions. Aside from Treponema spp., other bacterial genera have 
also been implicated in the disease process, often acting as secondary invaders such as 
Mycoplasmopsis, 
Bacteroides, 
Porphyromonas, 
Campylobacter, 
Fusobacterium, 
and 
Dichelobacter (Megahed et al., 2022). 
Studies have shown that the prevalence of different Treponema phylotypes can vary according to 
the stage of lesion development and their location within the lesion. For example, T. phagedenis-
like and T. vincentii-like phylotypes are frequently found at the interface of healthy and affected 
tissues (Wilson-Welder et al., 2015). This distribution suggests a dynamic bacterial population 
during the progression of the disease. 
While spirochetes are commonly seen in BDD lesions and are frequently the dominant bacterial 
genera, their exact role is still partly unknown. This emphasized how complex the etiology of BDD 
is and highlights the need for further research to fully understand the interactions between these 
pathogens and the host. 
2.1.3 Clinical Presentation  
A qualitative classification system, often referred to as the Mortellaro (M) scoring system, has been 
established to describe the progression of DD lesions. This 5 M-step scoring system, was first 
described by Dörte Döpfer in 1997 (Döpfer et al., 1997). 
 
 
 
==PAGE:14==
Page 13 of 71 
 
The lesions are categorizing as follows (Figure 1): 
• 
M0-stage: Represents normal and healthy skin with no visible DD lesions. 
• 
M1-stage: This early stage is characterized by a small, circumscribed granulomatous area 
that is moist, ragged, and mottled red-gray, measuring 0.5–2 cm in diameter. These lesions 
lie at or just below the epithelial surface (up to 2 mm) and are generally not painful. 
• 
M2-stage: Known as the classical ulcerative stage, M2 lesions are minimum 2 cm in 
diameter, located more than 2 mm beneath the epithelial layer, and associated with 
significant pain upon palpation. These lesions typically appear near the coronary band. 
• 
M3-stage: This stage signifies the healing process of an M2 lesion, characterized by the 
formation of a scab over the ulcerated area. The color of the scab varies depending on the 
topical medication applied. 
• 
M4-stage: Represents chronic lesions with hyperkeratosis, showing a proliferative aspect 
that can vary from papilliform to mass-like projections. These lesions are generally not 
painful but remain infectious and can transition back to an active M1 lesion.  
• 
M4.1-stage: Chronic M4 lesions with a small, active, and painful M1 focus within them.

healthy skin, (M1) Initial small, active lesion, (M2) Ulcerative and painful lesion, (M3) Healing or scabbed lesion, (M4) Chronic 
and proliferative lesion, and (M4.1) Chronic with new lesions. Images used in this figure are adapted from Kofler et al. (2020).

The morphological characteristics of DD lesions are often interrelated and can be challenging to 
distinguish visually. The etiopathogenesis of these conditions may overlap, making precise 
identification difficult. Not all affected animals exhibit clinical lameness, even if a lesion is painful 
upon palpation. Visual inspection, therefore, remains a subjective yet crucial diagnostic tool. 
In addition to the M scoring system, other methodologies are utilized to assess the severity and 
progression of DD lesions. One such system, adapted from Nielsen et al. (2016) evaluates both 
epidermal damage and the inflammatory response in the dermis, providing a detailed 
histopathological assessment. 
The DD score categorizes the degree of epidermal damage on a scale from 0 to 3. A score of 0 
represents a normal epidermis without any visible damage. A score of 1 indicates mild or focal 
epithelial proliferation and hyperkeratosis. Moderate epithelial proliferation and acanthosis 
(thickening of the skin) are classified as score 2. The most severe category, score 3, denotes 
extensive or diffuse damage, characterized by severe epithelial proliferation, acanthosis, exudation, 
erosion, or necrosis of the dermal papillae, see Figure 2. Similarly, the dermis score assesses the 
inflammatory response within the dermis, also on a scale from 0 to 3. A score of 0 reflects no 
alteration in the dermis. Mild inflammation, indicated by a slight increase in the number of 
lymphocytes and mononuclear cells, is scored as 1. A moderate increase in inflammatory cells 
corresponds to score 2. Score 3 represents severe and diffuse inflammation, with a significant 
increase in inflammatory cells throughout the dermis (M. W. Nielsen et al., 2016).

to Döpfer et al. (1997), reveals varying degrees of epidermal and dermal alterations across different segments. On the left side, the 
tissue appears unaffected/healthy (green) with a score of 0. Moving towards the center, there is moderate epithelial proliferation and 
acanthosis (score 2, dark green), followed by extensive epidermal degeneration characterized by severe epithelial proliferation, 
exudation, and erosion (score 3, red). On the far left, near the center of the lesion, the tissue shows severe exudation and ulceration

2.1.4 Symptoms and Impact of Digital Dermatitis on Cattle 
Digital dermatitis significantly impacts the health, performance, and welfare of dairy cattle, 
resulting in considerable economic losses. This section details the effects of DD on various aspects 
of cattle health and farm productivity. 
Health and fertility 
DD causes significant pain and discomfort, which primarily manifests as lameness. Lame cows 
have limited movement, impairing their capacity to feed and reducing their water intake. This, in 
turn, increases their susceptibility to other health problems. Chronic discomfort from DD also 
causes physiological stress, which weakens the immune system and makes cattle more susceptible 
to infections and diseases (Urban-Chmiel et al., 2024). Lameness caused by DD has a negative 
impact on fertility since it prolongs the time between calvings and reduces conception rates. The 
stress caused by chronic pain and limited movement affects normal reproductive cycles. Studies 
have indicated that lame cows have a 30-50% longer interval between calvings and a 15-20% lower 
conception rate than healthy cows (Tsousis et al., 2022). 
Performance and milk yield 
DD significantly reduces milk production. Lame cows consume less feed due to discomfort and 
difficulties getting feed and water. This results in reduced nutritional absorption and body condition 
scores, which directly affect milk supply. According to research, cows with DD can produce up to 
25% less milk than healthy cows, with the most severe reductions occurring in cows with acute 
lesions (Useni et al., 2014).  
Digital dermatitis can induce stress and discomfort during lactation, resulting in inconsistent 
milking patterns and reduced milk flow. The necessity for numerous treatments and extended 
healing periods interrupts normal lactation cycles, adding to additional decreases in milk 
production (Useni et al., 2014).  
Mental well-being 
The constant pain associated with DD has a major impact on the mental health of affected animals. 
Stress signals include frequent lying down and changes in social relationships within the herd. 
These behavioral changes are symptomatic of distress and a lower quality of life. Chronic pain and 
suffering can also cause depressive-like states in cattle (Steagall et al., 2021).  
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 71 
 
Lameness influences the herd's social structure. Lame cows are frequently downgraded to lower 
social categories due to their inability to compete for resources. This can lead to increased stress 
and isolation, worsening the harmful effect on mental health (Proudfoot & Habing, 2015).  
Lameness and mobility 
Digital dermatitis is the major cause of lameness in dairy cattle, causing severe pain and noticeable 
weakness or unwillingness to walk. Cows are susceptible to repeated lameness, ongoing pain, and 
decreased movement even after treatment due to the chronic nature of the disease. According to 
studies, more than 90% of DD lesions occur in the hind feet, and 70-80% of affected cows have 
considerable lameness (Cortes et al., 2021).  
Impaired mobility caused by DD has an impact on food behavior and availability to water, resulting 
in lower nutrient intake and poor body condition. Limited mobility hinders effective eating, 
resulting in uneven grazing consumption, which negatively impacts herd health and productivity 
(S. S. Nielsen et al., 2022).  
Economic Losses 
The direct costs associated with DD include veterinary treatments, medications, and preventive 
measures such as footbaths and hoof trimming. Frequent treatments and the need for specialized 
care increase labor costs. Additionally, the equipment used for hoof trimming and footbaths 
requires regular maintenance and disinfection to prevent the spread of infection, adding to the 
operational costs (S. S. Nielsen et al., 2022).  
Indirect costs are significant and include reduced milk production, and lower reproductive 
performance. Farms with high DD prevalence may also experience lower market values for affected 
animals, reducing overall farm profitability (Capion, 2021). 
2.1.5 Risk Factors for Digital Dermatitis in Cattle 
DD in cattle is influenced by a variety of risk factors spanning genetic, individual, and 
environmental aspects. Research indicates that individual cows exhibit significant variability in 
their susceptibility to DD. There are identified three distinct groups of cattle: permanently sick, 
intermittently sick and permanently healthy (Capion, 2021). This variability suggests a genetic 
component to DD susceptibility. 
==PAGE:18==
Page 17 of 71 
 
Physical characteristics such as hoof conformation play a critical role in DD. Lower heel heights 
and specific claw angles have been shown to increase the risk by facilitating prolonged contact 
with slurry and damp conditions, which are conducive to DD (Capion, 2021). 
The stage of lactation significantly influences susceptibility to DD. Cows in peak lactation or 
immediately post-calving are more susceptible to DD due to immune suppression and metabolic 
stress during these periods. During peak lactation, cows are typically fed a high-energy diet to 
support milk production. This diet can lead to larger quantities of more liquid feces, increasing heel 
contamination with slurry and, consequently, the risk of DD (Capion, 2021).  
Herds with higher levels of leg cleanliness have lower DD prevalence. Solid floors, especially those 
with grooves, tend to retain more slurry, increasing DD risk (Capion, 2021). Biosecurity practices, 
including the introduction of new animals and hoof trimming practices, are also notable. 
Hoof trimming practices, especially those involving professional trimmers who work across 
multiple farms, can spread DD if equipment is not adequately sanitized between cows (Capion, 
2021). Proper cleaning of hoof trimming equipment between cows significantly reduces the risk of 
spreading DD-causing bacteria (Capion, 2021). 
2.2 Current Treatment Approaches for Digital Dermatitis 
2.2.1 Traditional Treatments and Their Limitations 
DD management has historically relied on early detection and intervention strategies, primarily 
using antiseptic solutions in footbaths and the application of antibiotics. These traditional methods 
have been the cornerstone of controlling and mitigating the impact of DD in dairy herds. 
Antiseptic footbaths 
A common technique for treating and preventing DD lesions is the use of footbaths. It consists of 
cleaning and disinfecting cattle feet with a variety of chemical treatments. Two of the most widely 
used antiseptics are formaldehyde and copper sulfate (Cu2SO4). 
• 
Copper Sulfate: Copper sulfate has been extensively used due to its potent antibacterial 
properties. When used in footbaths, it helps to reduce the bacterial load on the cattle’s hooves, 
thereby preventing the formation of new lesions and aiding in the healing of existing ones 
(Laven & Logue, 2006). However, the environmental impact of copper sulfate is a significant 
concern. When footbath solutions are disposed of, copper accumulates in the soil, leading to 
==PAGE:19==
Page 18 of 71 
 
phytotoxicity and toxicity to aquatic organisms (Bolan et al., 2003). This environmental hazard 
has prompted the search for more eco-friendly alternatives. 
• 
Formaldehyde: Formaldehyde is another effective antiseptic used in footbaths. It has strong 
antibacterial properties that help control DD. However, formaldehyde is a known carcinogen, 
posing health risks to both animals and humans. Due to these risks, the use of formaldehyde in 
footbaths has been recommended for prohibition within the European Union (Wolkoff & 
Nielsen, 2010). This regulatory stance highlights the need for safer alternatives in managing 
DD. 
Antibiotics 
Antibiotics, particularly tetracycline, are frequently used in both topical applications and footbaths 
to treat DD. Tetracycline helps to reduce bacterial infections effectively but comes with significant 
concerns: 
• 
Tetracycline footbaths: The off-label use of tetracycline in footbaths has been common due to 
its efficacy in treating DD lesions. However, this practice raises several issues, including the 
risk of antibiotic residues in milk and meat, which can affect food safety (Cramer et al., 2019). 
Moreover, the widespread use of antibiotics in agriculture contributes to the development of 
antibiotic-resistant bacteria, posing a significant public health risk. 
The limitations associated with traditional antiseptics and antibiotics have driven research towards 
alternative treatments that are both effective and environmentally sustainable. 
2.3 Bovine Immune Response 
The immune system is a complex network of biological structures and processes designed to protect 
an organism from disease. Its primary function is to identify and respond to a wide variety of 
pathogens, such as viruses, bacteria, fungi, and parasites, while differentiating these harmful agents 
from the body's own healthy cells and tissues. The immune system is divided into two main 
components: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system, both of which work 
together to provide a comprehensive defense against infections (Turvey & Broide, 2010). 
2.3.1 The Immune System Overview  
The innate immune system 
==PAGE:20==
Page 19 of 71 
 
The innate immune system represents the first line of defense against pathogenic invasion, 
characterized by its rapid response. This system is evolutionarily ancient and is present in all 
multicellular organisms, providing an immediate response to a wide array of pathogens without the 
need for prior exposure (Turvey & Broide, 2010). 
Physical and chemical barriers 
The most fundamental components of the innate immune system are the physical and chemical 
barriers that prevent pathogen entry. The skin, being the largest organ of the body, acts as a 
formidable physical barrier. It is composed of multiple layers of tightly packed epithelial cells and 
is fortified with keratin, a tough, protective protein. The skin's surface is also slightly acidic due to 
the presence of sebum and sweat, creating an inhospitable environment for many pathogens 
(Turvey & Broide, 2010). 
Phagocytic cells 
Phagocytic cells, including macrophages, neutrophils, and dendritic cells, are essential to the innate 
immune response. These cells possess pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that detect pathogen-
associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), which are conserved molecular structures found on 
pathogens but not on host cells. Toll-like receptors (TLRs) are a well-characterized family of PRRs 
that recognize various PAMPs and initiate signaling cascades that lead to the activation of innate 
immune responses (Li & Wu, 2021).  
Upon encountering a pathogen, phagocytic cells undergo a process known as phagocytosis. The 
pathogen is engulfed into a phagosome, which then fuses with a lysosome to form a 
phagolysosome. Within the phagolysosome, the pathogen is subjected to an array of antimicrobial 
mechanisms, including acidification, production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), and the action 
of hydrolytic enzymes, leading to its destruction (Rosales & Uribe-Querol, 2017). 
Natural killer cells 
Natural killer (NK) cells are a critical component of the innate immune system, particularly in the 
defense against viral infections and tumors. Unlike T cells, NK cells do not require prior 
sensitization to recognize and kill their targets. They possess a repertoire of activating and 
inhibitory receptors that allow them to distinguish between healthy cells and those that are infected 
or transformed (Björkström et al., 2022). 
==PAGE:21==
Page 20 of 71 
 
NK cells exert their cytotoxic effects through two primary mechanisms: the release of cytotoxic 
granules containing perforin and granzymes, which induce apoptosis in target cells, and the 
engagement of death receptors on target cells through ligands such as Fas ligand (FasL) and tumor 
necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) (Ramírez-Labrada et al., 2022). 
Inflammatory response 
A hallmark of the innate immune system is the inflammatory response, a complex biological 
process triggered by infection or tissue injury (Chen et al., 2018). The primary objectives of 
inflammation are to eliminate the initial cause of cell injury, clear out necrotic cells and tissues, 
and establish a repair process. This response is characterized by the classical signs of inflammation: 
redness, heat, swelling, pain, and loss of function (Chen et al., 2018; Wilson, 2011). 
Upon tissue injury or pathogen invasion, resident immune cells such as macrophages and mast cells 
release pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., interleukin-1 [IL-1], IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor-
alpha [TNF-α]) and chemokines (Kany et al., 2019). These molecules induce vasodilation and 
increase the permeability of blood vessels, facilitating the influx of immune cells, proteins, and 
nutrients to the site of infection or injury. Neutrophils are typically the first responders, followed 
by monocytes that differentiate into macrophages upon entering the tissue. These cells work in 
concert to phagocytose pathogens, secrete additional cytokines, and recruit other immune cells, 
thereby strengthening the inflammatory response (Wilson, 2011). 
The inflammatory process is tightly regulated to prevent excessive tissue damage. Anti-
inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-10 and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β)) and regulatory 
mechanisms, such as the activation of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and the clearance of apoptotic 
cells, contribute to the resolution of inflammation and the promotion of tissue repair. 
2.3.2 Immune Response in Bovine Digital Dermatitis 
The immune response to DD is a complex interplay between innate and adaptive immune 
mechanisms, influenced by the pathogen's virulence factors and the host's genetic and 
immunological landscape. 
Innate immune response in digital dermatitis 
The initial immune response to Treponema species in DD is mediated by the innate immune system. 
Upon infection, PRRs such as TLRs on macrophages, dendritic cells, and keratinocytes recognize 
PAMPs (Evans et al., 2014). A particularly important PAMP detected by TLR4 is 
lipopolysaccharide (LPS), a key component of the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria. 
==PAGE:22==
Page 21 of 71 
 
When LPS binds to TLR4, in combination with its co-receptors MD-2 and CD14, it initiates 
intracellular signaling cascades, see Figure 3, (Dai et al., 2022a). This recognition triggers 
intracellular signaling cascades, notably the NF-κB pathway, leading to the production of pro-
inflammatory cytokines and chemokines. These cytokines are key components of the inflammatory 
process, and their release leads to the classic signs of inflammation, such as redness, heat, swelling, 
and pain (Dai et al., 2022a).

The inflammatory environment is further characterized by the production of matrix 
metalloproteinases (MMPs), such as MMP-13, which are involved in tissue remodeling and repair 
(Akkoc et al., 2018).  
MMP is an enzyme family that degrades extracellular matrix proteins, including collagen, that are 
integral components of the extracellular space (interstitial space) between cells. Metalloproteinases 
possess zinc ions and calcium ions as cofactors, allowing them to function enzymatically (Evans 
et al., 2014). The MMP family of enzymes plays a crucial role in a variety of physiological 
processes, including wound healing and tissue remodeling (Subramaniam et al., 2018). In the 
absence of proper regulation, MMP activity can result in excessive degradation of the ECM, leading 
to ulcerative lesions on the skin (Vaalamo et al., 1997).

It has been determined that MMPs play a significant role in the development of BDD. Human cells 
are known to express MMP more frequently in hypoxia, a condition characterized by low oxygen 
levels and often associated with an anaerobic environment conducive to BDD. It has been reported 
that hypoxia induces MMP-13 enzymes and could contribute to the pathogenesis of BDD through 
the development of in vivo lesions (Akkoc et al., 2018). 
Adaptive immune response in digital dermatitis 
The adaptive immune response to DD involves both humoral and cell-mediated components, 
although its effectiveness varies among individual cattle. The humoral response is marked by the 
production of serum antibodies against treponemal antigens (Wilson, 2011). However, the 
magnitude and specificity of these antibody responses are inconsistent, potentially due to the 
diversity of treponemal phylotypes and antigenic variation within lesions (Oliveira et al., 2017). 
Studies indicate that innate and inflammatory responses, particularly the activation of neutrophils 
and macrophages, are crucial in controlling these infections, highlighting the importance of 
focusing on molecular mechanisms that enhance these responses (Vlasova & Saif, 2021). 
Gene expression and pathway analysis in DD lesions 
Transcriptomic analyses have provided insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying DD 
pathogenesis (Vermeersch et al., 2022). Studies comparing gene expression profiles between 
healthy skin and DD lesions have identified significant alterations, including the upregulation of 
pro-inflammatory molecules, antimicrobial peptides, and the activation of the IL-17 signaling 
pathway. IL-17F, is highly expressed in both acute and chronic DD lesions, implicating it as a 
central mediator of the inflammatory response (Vermeersch et al., 2022). IL-17F, produced by Th17 
cells and γδ-T cells, induces the production of chemokines such as IL-8 and IL-6, which attract 
neutrophils, monocytes, and other immune cells to the infection site, amplifying the inflammatory 
process (Vermeersch et al., 2022).

4.1 Linear Regression 
Linear regression is a fundamental statistical method used to model the relationship between a 
dependent variable (response variable) and one or more independent variables (predictors). The 
method assumes that there is a linear relationship between the dependent and independent 
variables, meaning that changes in the independent variables are associated with proportional 
changes in the dependent variable. 
The statistical model is mathematically represented by the equation:

In this model, α represents the intercept, representing the expected value of Y when 𝑥= 0. The

represents the number of observations included in the analysis. 
4.1.1 Model Check 
To ensure the validity of the linear regression model, a model check should be performed. The 
model check involved several key diagnostic steps: 
I. Normality of the residuals 
To evaluate the normality of the residuals, Q-Q (quantile-quantile) plot are generated. In these 
plots, if the residuals are normally distributed, the data points should align closely along a straight 
diagonal line. Deviations from this straight line suggest that the residuals do not follow a normal 
distribution, indicating potential issues with the model's assumptions. 
II. Variance homogeneity 
To assess variance homogeneity, residuals are plotted against the fitted values (𝑦̂𝑖). 
Homoscedasticity is expected, where the residuals should exhibit constant variance across all levels

of the independent variable. If the plot reveals a trumpet shape or other discernible patterns, this 
will indicate heteroscedasticity, suggesting that the residual variance is not constant. 
III. Linearity 
The linearity assumption was examined by plotting residuals against the explanatory variable (𝑥𝑖). 
For a well-fitting model, these plots should display a random scatter of residuals with no discernible 
patterns. The appearance of curves or S-shapes would indicate non-linear relationships, suggesting 
that the linear model may not be appropriate for the data. 
IV. Independence 
The independence assumption in linear regression ensures that each observation in the dataset is 
independent of the others. This means that the error term (residual) for one observation should 
not be related or correlated with the error term for another observation. This assumption is crucial 
for obtaining valid and unbiased results. 
To check for independence, a residual plot can be used. In a valid residual plot, there should be no 
obvious patterns or trends, indicating that the errors are uncorrelated.

The analysis of the gene expressions data performed during my bachelor thesis, “Investigation of

The data used in this study is based on Cq (quantification cycle) values, which was obtained from 
qPCR experiments. Cq-values represents the cycle number at which the fluorescence signal of the 
PCR product crosses a specific threshold, which indicates the presence of the target gene.  
A lower Cq-value correspond to a higher gene expression level, as it indicates that fewer cycles 
were needed to detect the target gene.  
To ensure data integrity, melting curves were examined to validate the specificity of PCR products, 
avoiding problems such as primer dimers or contamination. Contamination was assessed in non-
template (NTC) and minus reverse transcriptase (-RT) controls. Standard curves were used to 
quantify PCR efficiency. To reduce experimental bias, data was normalized. Finally, Cq-values 
were converted to relative quantities on a linear scale, which is a more interpretable measure of the 
gene expression. It allows a direct comparison across the samples and ensures that the data reflects 
relative gene expression levels. These relative quantities, rather than the Cq-values, are the gene 
expression data used for a more advanced statistical analysis in this study.

The analysis aimed to investigate hypothesis 2, which states that specific gene expressions are

the strongest relationships with lesion status based on their p-values. 
Following the initial screening, the gene with the strongest relationship and the lowest p-value was 
chosen as the starting point for future investigation. In the next phases, logistic regression models 
were repeatedly constructed by adding one gene at a time to the baseline model. The goal was to 
see whether each added gene significantly improved the model's ability to explain the link between

gene significantly enhanced the model's ability to describe the link between gene expression and 
lesion state. 
At each step, the analysis aimed to identify genes that, when added to the model, contributed 
significantly to explaining the variation in lesion state. The approach was repeated until a group of 
genes was identified that provided the most complete and statistically significant model. 
After identifying the key genes, the analysis involved conducting correlation analysis and 
hierarchical clustering. These techniques were used to explore the relationships between the 
significant genes and to identify patterns of co-expression, helping to uncover how these genes 
might interact or be related in their expression profiles. 
5.3.2 Evaluating the Impact of Lesion Status on Gene Expression 
This part of the investigation used linear regression models to investigate how the presence or 
absence of lesions influences gene expression levels. Unlike the previous study, which investigated 
how gene expression predicted lesion status, this study examined the opposite relationship—the 
impact of lesions on gene expression. 
A linear regression model was fitted for each of the 67 genes in the dataset, where the log-
transformed expression level of each gene was used as the dependent variable, and lesion status 
was the independent variable. 
After fitting the models, diagnostic plots were created to assess linear regression assumptions such 
as linearity, homoscedasticity, and residual normality. These diagnostic checks were required to 
validate the validity of the models. 
To determine whether genes showed a statistically significant change in expression associated with 
lesion status, the p-values for the independent variable (my.Lesion) coefficients were taken from 
each model. Genes with p-values < 0.05 were considered significant, showing a strong relationship 
between lesion presence and gene expression levels. 
In addition to statistical significance, the degree of the effect was assessed by calculating the log2 
fold change for each significant gene. This measurement revealed whether the gene was up or 
downregulated in the presence of a lesion.

5.4 Part 3 – Specific Gene Expression Correlation with M-stages  
The present analysis was conducted to investigate hypothesis 3, which proposes that there are 
significant differences in gene expressions profiles between various M-stages. Specifically, 
between M4.1 and M2, and M4.1 and M1.  
To test this hypothesis the gene expressions data of the 67 genes and the M-stages were analyzed.  
First step in the analysis was to log-transform the gene expression data to each of the expression 
value. This is done to stabilize the variance across the data.  
Following the log transformation, independent two-sample t-tests were performed to compare the 
mean log-transformed expression levels between the M2 and M4.1 stages for each gene. The same 
was done for each gene's expression levels in the M1- and M4.1 stages. The t-test was conducted 
to determine if the differences in gene expression between the two stages were statistically 
significant. 
The p-value from the t-test was calculated for each gene to determine the significance of the 
observed differences. To identify genes with significant differential expression, a p-value threshold 
of 0.05 was used.  
In addition to p-values, log2 fold changes in expression were calculated between the M2 and M4.1 
stages, as well as the M1 and M4.1 stages. The log2 fold change was calculated as the difference 
between the log2-transformed mean expression in the M4.1 stage and the log2-transformed mean 
expression in the appropriate M-stage (M2 or M1). Positive log2 fold increases suggest 
upregulation during the M4.1 stage, whereas negative log2 fold changes imply downregulation.

7 Discussion 
 
7.1 Part 1 – Epithelial Thickness and Correlation with Specific Gene 
Expressions  
The present study provide insight into the relationship between gene expression and epithelial 
thickness in healthy biopsies from cattle, focusing on the role of several significant genes.  
The significant genes identified in this study, ACVR2A, TLR4, TGFB2, GATA3, STAT6, IL23P19, 
STAT5B, and TIMP3, demonstrate to a clear relationship between the epithelial thickness and the 
gene expression levels of these genes, suggesting in the maintaining epithelial homeostasis.  
Based on the results illustrated in Figure 11, where the correlation between the expression of the 
significant genes and the epithelial thickness was investigated, a decrease in the epithelial thickness 
was observed for the significant genes. Although the rate of decrease in the thickness varies across 
the different genes. This may suggest that even in the absence of digital dermatitis, these genes are 
actively involved in regulating the structural and functional properties of the epithelial tissues.  
Compared to GATA3, TLR4, TGFB2, ACVR2A, and TIMP3, a slower rate of decrease in epithelial 
thickness observed with genes like STAT6, STAT3, STAT5B, and IL23P19, which are genes the 
primarily are involved in cytokine signaling and immune regulation, indicates that they have a role 
in modulating immune activity without causing rapid or damaging tissue changes. This is in 
consistent with the known roles of STAT6 and STAT3 in regulating immune response, particularly 
in balancing pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory responses. In context with their expression 
in a healthy tissue, it likely suggests a state of immune readiness, where the tissue is ready to 
respond to potential threats while maintaining its structural stability.  
On the other hand, genes such as GATA3, TLR4, TGFB2, ACVR2A, and TIMP3 was observed to 
have a faster rate of epithelial thinning with increased expression, when compared to the genes 
mentioned above. 
GATA3 and TIMP3 are genes associated with tissue remodeling, inflammation and extracellular 
matrix regulation. The expression of GATA3, which is a transcription factor essential for the 
differentiation of Th2 cells, suggest its involvement in tissue remodeling and immune regulation. 
This finding aligns with findings from previous research, which identified GATA3 as a crucial 
factor in keratinocyte homeostasis, activation, and proliferation, particularly in conditions like 
psoriasis where GATA3 expression is downregulated (Rácz et al., 2011). 
==PAGE:56==
Page 55 of 71 
 
In psoriasis, reduced GATA3 expression leads to keratinocyte hyperproliferation and altered 
differentiation, emphasizing its role in maintaining epithelial integrity (Rácz et al., 2011). Findings 
from this study suggest that GATA3 is crucial for controlling epithelial structure even in healthy 
tissues, likely through immune regulation, making it a key player in both normal tissue maintenance 
and pathological remodeling. 
A significant regulation of TLR4 was observed in this study. TLR4 is a receptor that recognizes 
pathogen-associated molecular patterns and initiates the innate immune response. Previous studies 
have shown that TLR4 expression is notably higher in various skin conditions, indicating its role 
in producing inflammatory cytokines essential for tissue repair (Chen et al., 2013; Dai et al., 
2022b). 
Figure 10 shows that higher TLR4 expression is associated with thinner epithelial layers. This 
could be due to the timing of tissue sampling. If biopsies were taken during a chronic phase rather 
than an acute one, the levels of LPS (lipopolysaccharides) might have been low at the time. In a 
chronic state, the tissue may not be exposed to high LPS levels, typically seen in acute bacterial 
infections. Consequently, lower TLR4 expression might still correlate with a thicker epithelial 
layer, reflecting an ongoing repair process.

7.2.1 Evaluating the Impact of Lesion Status on Gene Expression 
The impact of the lesion status on gene expression in digital dermatitis samples was studied my 
linear regression modelling. Significant genes, with a p-value <0.05, were identified. 
The study reveals an upregulation of IL8 in digital dermatitis lesions with a Log2-fold change of 
6.22, which indicates a significant upregulation in IL8 expression. This finding is consistent with 
the role of IL8 regarding the inflammatory response, particularly in the recruitment and activation 
of neutrophils to the site of infection. In a related study, IL8 showed an upregulation at the M2-
stage of DD, which according to the M-scoring system is the acute stage. The significant 
==PAGE:58==
Page 57 of 71 
 
upregulation of IL8 observed in both studies highlights the significant role of IL8 in the 
pathogenesis of DD.  
The upregulation of the pro-inflammatory in this study is highly observed. The upregulation of IL6 
(Log2-foldchange of 5.20), IL1B (Log2-foldchange of 4.31) and IL1A (2.88), highlights the strong 
inflammatory environment associated with lesion presence. As known, these pro-inflammatory 
cytokines are central cytokines that drive the acute phase response. In particularly IL6, which is 
involved in promoting differentiation of T-cells and B-cells. Both IL1A and IL1B, are involved in 
driving the local inflammation, which both contribute to the recruitment and activation of immune 
cells at the lesion site.  
A significant upregulation of MMP-13, log2-foldchange of 6.1 was observed. Its significant 
upregulation in ulcerative skin lesions indicates the active tissue remodeling, and this finding is in 
accordance with previous studies mentioned in the background section (Subramaniam et al., 2018). 
The downregulation of haptoglobin (HP) gene expression observed in biopsies with lesion, with a 
Log2 fold change of -2.8858. This suggests that the local expression of HP in the affected skin is 
decreased. HP is an acute-phase protein and is produced in the liver as part of the acute response 
to inflammation. A suggestion is, that as HP is synthesized in the liver, its systemic levels are 
typically a reflection of overall inflammation rather than local immune responses. Because of the 
chronic inflammatory environment typical for DD, its expression at local tissue sites, including the 
skin, may be suppressed. 
The results in Table 7, shows a downregulation of TIMP3 (Tissue Inhibitors of Metalloproteinases 
13), log2 foldchange of -2.4155. 
TIMPs play an important role in protecting the extracellular matrix from degradation by MMP’s. 
Considered that a significant upregulation of MMP13, a suggestion for the downregulation of 
TIMP13 in context to DD lesions may be that the chronic inflammatory environments disrupt the 
balance between MMP’s and TIMP’s. To further explain this imbalance, it is known that without 
sufficient TIMP activity to inhibit MMPs, the ECM is progressing degraded, leading to the 
breakdown of dermal-epidermal junction, and the formation of painful, ulcerative lesions 
(Mukherjee & Das, 2024).

7.3 Part 3 – Specific Gene Expression Correlation with M-stages  
7.3.1 Comparison of M4.1 and M1 
The comparison of gene expression between the M4.1 and M1 stages of digital dermatitis provides 
valuable insights into the pathophysiological changes that occur as the disease progresses from an 
acute inflammatory phase (M1) to a more chronic, possibly reactivating phase (M4.1). 
The upregulation of STAT5B in the M4.1 stage compared to the M1 stage, as indicated by the Log2 
fold change of 0.3881, suggests that STAT5B plays a more prominent role in the chronic phase of 
digital dermatitis. STAT5B, as a member of the STAT family, is crucial in mediating responses to 
cytokines and growth factors. The increased expression in the M4.1 stage compared to M1- stage 
may suggests an increased demand for this regulatory function as the disease shift from an acute to 
a chronic state. 
CCL2 (C-C Motif Chemokine Ligand 2) is significantly downregulated at the M4.1 stage (Log2 
fold change of -0.5696). CCL2 is required for attracting monocytes to areas of inflammation, which 
is critical during the acute M1 phase. The significant decrease in CCL2 expression in M4.1 
indicates a decrease in active immune cell recruitment, which aligns with that the inflammation is 
no longer acute but has transitioned to a more regulated or resolved state.  
A significant upregulation of IL23P19 in M4.1 compared to M1-stage is observed based on the 
results in Table 8. IL-23P19 is an essential subunit of IL-23 that aids in the maintenance and 
expansion of Th17 cells. An important feature of Th17 cells is IL-17 production, which is a 
proinflammatory cytokine that drives chronic inflammation (Beringer et al., 2016). 
Compared to M1, M4.1 features chronic inflammation instead of an acute inflammatory response. 
In the M4.1 stage, IL23P19 expression is upregulated, suggesting that the IL23/IL-17 axis is more 
active, sustaining the chronic inflammatory environment and supporting Th17 persistence (Liu et 
al., 2020). A crucial component of Th17 cell survival and proliferation is IL-23. As a result of the 
increase in IL23P19 expression in M4.1 as compared to M1, more Th17 cells are needed to 
maintain the chronic inflammatory state.  
7.3.2 Comparison of M4.1 and M2 
Based on the analysis and result presented on Table 9, a downregulation of several key 
inflammatory markers, including IL6, IL8, and MMP13 in the M4.1 stage compared to the M2 
stage, is observed. 
==PAGE:60==
Page 59 of 71 
 
Inflammatory cytokines such as IL6 and IL8 play a crucial role during the acute phase of 
inflammation.  
IL6 regulates the immune response by enhancing T cell differentiation and increasing acute-phase 
protein synthesis. IL8 is a chemokine that attracts neutrophils to areas of illness or damage. The 
downregulation of IL6 and IL8 in the M4.1 stage indicates a decrease in acute inflammatory 
activity, implying that the M4.1 stage may reflect a transition to a more regulated phase of 
inflammation. 
MMP13 is involved in the cellular breakdown of extracellular matrix components, which is 
essential for tissue remodeling and healing after inflammation. The considerable downregulation 
of MMP13 at the M4.1 stage suggests a decrease in tissue remodeling activities, which could 
indicate tissue structure stabilization when inflammation decreases. 
STAT6 is a transcription factor that is mainly activated by the cytokines IL-4 and IL-13, which 
promote the Th2 immune response. The upregulation of STAT6 during the M4.1 stage indicates a 
change toward a Th2-dominated response, which is frequently associated with anti-inflammatory 
activities and tissue healing. This increase could imply that the immune system is shifting toward 
resolving inflammation and promoting healing. 
Transforming Growth Factor Beta 2 (TGFB2) is a cytokine that helps regulate immune responses 
and promote tissue regeneration. The upregulation of TGFB2 in the M4.1 stage of digital dermatitis 
indicates that tissue repair and remodeling activities are engaged. Studies highlights that TGF-β 
signaling is essential for successful tissue regeneration (Liarte et al., 2020).

8 Conclusion 
This study investigated the impact of bovine digital dermatitis on gene expression using various 
hypotheses and statistical models. The initial analysis examined the relationship between gene 
expression and epithelial thickness in healthy biopsies. Linear regression identified several 
significant genes (p-value < 0.05), indicating a clear correlation between gene expression levels 
and epithelial thickness. 
Significant genes identified, including STAT6, STAT3, STAT5B, IL23P19, GATA3, and TLR4, 
were consistently associated with epithelial thickness changes. Specifically, STAT6, STAT3, 
STAT5B, and IL23P19 were linked to a slower rate of epithelial thinning when having a higher 
gene expression, compared to GATA3 and TLR4 which were associated with more rapid thinning 
when the gene expression increases. 
The investigation into gene expression in DD lesions identified four key genes—ITIH4, IL7, IL18, 
and PPIA—suggesting their potential as biomarkers for disease severity. Cluster analysis indicated 
that IL7 and ITIH4, as well as PPIA and IL18, tend to co-regulate, pointing to their interaction in 
DD pathogenesis. 
Further analysis highlighted a significant upregulation of pro-inflammatory genes (IL8, IL6, IL1B, 
and IL1A) and MMP-13 in DD lesions, underscoring a robust inflammatory response and the role 
of MMP-13 in tissue remodeling within the lesion microenvironment. An imbalance between 
upregulated MMPs and downregulated TIMPs was observed, accelerating ECM degradation and 
exacerbating tissue damage. 
Finally, differences in gene expression profiles across M-stages of DD were notable. The M4.1 
stage exhibited significant upregulation of STAT5B and IL23P19 compared to the M1 stage, 
suggesting these genes' roles in disease progression. Conversely, inflammatory markers like IL6, 
IL8, and MMP13 were downregulated in the M4.1 stage relative to M2, indicating reduced acute 
inflammation and tissue remodeling as the disease advanced. The upregulation of STAT6 in the 
M4.1 stage further supports its involvement in the later stages of DD. 
Overall, these findings enhance our understanding of gene expression dynamics in bovine digital 
dermatitis and provide insights into potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets for managing the 
disease.
