Introduction 
 
We as humans have all experienced stress, we feel a bit under the weather or upset as well as 
our heartrate might go up, we start to breath quicker and me might start to sweat, e.g. before 
we meet new people, if we have a big presentation coming up or if we have an important sports-
game to play. In the work/study environment we live in today, we see and hear the words stress 
and stress management everywhere we go, whether it is in the newspaper, libraries, in TED 
talks or on the radio, but what is stress and why is it so big and an important factor of our daily 
lives? 
 
The term “stress” first arose back in the early 1930s with Hans Selye. Whilst doing his research 
he came to believe that stress is the state of an organism when it is adapting to changes in the 
environment (Vilner, 1999). Selye, often called the father of stress research, gave us new names 
and definitions as mentioned earlier such as “stressor” which is a factor that triggers the “stress” 
response (Sandor, Yvette, & Somogyi, 2012). Selye considered stress in health and disease to 
be medically, sociologically and philosophically the most meaningful subject for humanity that 
he could think of. For the past 90 years stress has been widely researched but in the initial 
phases it was researched through a narrow perspective but today, stress research has caught 
attention in many disciplines such as genetics, chemistry, medicine, neurosciences, physiology 
and many other (Robinsson, 2018).  
 
Stress has become a significant problem of our times, and with the rise of entrepreneurship 
many decide to pursue that carrier which is often considered a stressful carrier path. Especially 
for engineering students with all the knowledge that they possess concerning technology which 
is crucial in the digital transformation the humankind is experiencing. That is why the aim of 
this study is to determine whether the overall stress of engineering students would decrease by 
enhancing their knowledge and skills regarding their stress levels. 
Stressors 
 
What one person perceives as a stressful situation might not affect the person standing next to 
him at all, and stressors that cause stress to an organism can come from various sources 
(Papathanasiou, Tsaras, Neroliatsiou, & Roupa, 2015). Stressors can be divided into various 
groups based on intensity, duration and character. When looking at the duration of stressors it 
can be split into two separate groups, acute and chronic stressors (Pacak & R.McCarty, 2007). 
Furthermore, stressors can be split into two major types based on brain functions, physical or 
systemic stressors which produce disturbances of physiological status such as injury or pain, 
secondly there is psychological or emotional stressors that threaten one’s current state (Dayas, 
Buller, Crane, Xu, & Day, 2001). Acute stressors are discrete life events such as getting a single 
speeding ticket, and to be able to adapt to acute stressors, physical processes and psychological 
processes are important. Chronic stressors occur on daily basis and have longer duration, and 
they can be split up into five groups. Firstly, persistent life difficulties, long duration life events 
such as family member being in an accident which leads to disabilities. Secondly, role strains, 
such as working, parenting or trying to fulfil multiple roles at the same time. Thirdly, chronic 
strains which can occur between social groups, such as discrimination due to race, gender or 
ethnicity. Fourthly, community-wide strains, such as living in a bad neighbourhood with high 
crime rate. Fifth and final group is daily hassles, e.g. being stuck in traffic (Romas & Sharma, 
2017). Additionally, non-events can be described as stressors, but those are defined as

classified as non-events (Horwitz & Scheid, 1999). There are certain stressors that most people

on people and only some people would consider them stressful, such as noise (Patton, n.d.). 
 
Back in 1967 Holmes and Rahe created a stress scale, which they called the social readjustment

Stress Theories 
 
Researching the definition of stress gives oneself a wide-ranging answer both based on how 
different disciplines define the term and often the idea of stress can be as subjective as an 
individual’s experience of stress (Robinsson, 2018). Gillian Butler argues that there are at least 
three ways of defining stress, firstly there is a stimulus based definition, secondly a response 
based definition and lastly stress as a dynamic process (Butler, 1993).  
Stimulus Based Definition 
A stimulus based definition is the most common definition of stress and it focuses on external 
sources of stress, where situations cause stress, events in the environment such as illness or 
being fired from your job. Life events can be looked at as negative or a positive experience 
based on cognitive and emotional factors (Walinga, 2010). Lazarus and Folkman argue that the 
stimulus based approach assumes that certain situation is in fact normatively stressful, but 
ignore important variables such as individual differences in the evaluation of events, 
environment, personality and other factors such as past experiences (Lazarus & Folkman, 
1984). 
Response Based Definition 
A response based definition focuses on physiological responses to noxious and aversive stimuli 
and is often used in the field of biology and medicine, this definition correlates to Selye’s initial 
ideas of stress, called the general adaptation syndrome, where the physiological responses 
follow three stages which is illustrated in Figure 1 (Butler, 1993).

Firstly, the individual enters the alarm stage where it is being triggered by a threatening 
encounter, known as stressor (Lipsky, Kentor, Rothman, & Gerin, 2004). This body’s response 
is often called the fight or flight response or acute stress response, and is ideal when an 
individual faces a short-term physical danger, e.g. when hiking up a mountain and comes across 
a mountain lion, then the body is prepared to either fight the threat or flee it (School, 2018).  
When we face a stressful condition the body’s nervous system is activated and the level of 
various hormones are released in the body, those hormones include cortisol, catecholamines, 
vasopressin, gonadotropins, thyroid hormones, growth hormone, prolactin and last but not least 
insulin (Reetu & Ranabir, 2011).  
 
Cortisol is often referred to as the most crucial “stress hormone” and is essential to maintain a 
normal blood pressure, furthermore, it affects the body in various ways. If the body receives a 
lot of excess cortisol it has consequences on the cardiovascular system, which is linked to 
elevation of blood pressure, obesity and hyperinsulinemia which is higher amount of insulin 
than is considered normal in the blood, which can increase the odds of getting type 2 diabetes. 
Hyperglycaemia can cause serious health problems if not treated, such as damaged vessels, 
heart diseases, strokes and nerve problems. Furthermore, it affects insulin resistance, where the 
cells do not respond well to insulin and the body does not use glucose as an energy source, so 
the body automatic response is to produce more insulin and in the long run the blood sugar 
levels rises (Whitworth, Williamson, Mangos, & Kelly, 2005). Additionally, cortisol plays its 
part in individuals’ sleep cycle, where elevated cortisol levels can be linked to chronic insomnia 
and is the primary cause of sleep disturbance (Bush & Hudson, 2010). When cortisol level 
arises in the body it enters the brain, and if an individual is in a long lasting condition with 
elevated cortisol levels it can be linked to impaired cognitive performance (Soravia & De 
Quervain, 2012). Recent studies illustrate that higher cortisol level is related to worse memory 
and visual perception, but that is the brain’s ability to absorb what it sees and making sense of 
it, hence reading (Echouffo-Tcheugui et al., 2018). Researches indicate that adrenal stress 
hormones such as cortisol play a big part in consolidating lasting memories, during or after 
emotionally arousing experiences (McGaugh & Roozendaal, 2002). “Memory consolidation is 
the process by which a fragile short-term memory trace is transferred into a stable long-term 
memory” (Soravia & De Quervain, 2012). The opposite of memory consolidation is memory 
retrieval process, which is the process of collecting information that has already been stored 
during the memory consolidation process. Soravia and De Quervain argue that cortisol reduces 
memory retrieval of emotional memories, but emotional memories play a critical role in the 
development of anxiety disorders. Furthermore, studies have shown that stress and cortisol 
decrease working memory performance, but that is the process where information is constantly 
updated and temporary stored (Soravia & De Quervain, 2012).

Catecholamines are adaptive and maladaptive hormones which include three different 
molecules, dopamine, norepinephrine and epinephrine which also goes by the name adrenalin 
on a daily basis. The catecholamine’s activate both behavioural and physiological processes to 
help the body to overcome stress and plays an important role as neurotransmitters. When 
catecholamines are released into the blood stream the blood pressure increases, pushing blood 
to the heart and the brain, the heart rate increases, the liver starts producing and releasing more 
glucose (Liangyou, 2014) (Tank & Wong, 2015), the pupils dilate so the individual has better 
vision and the blood flow to the intestines decreases. Interestingly, these catecholamines are 
important for the organisms to survive, but only in short-term, if they are ongoing for a long 
period of time they might result in illnesses such as, cardiac hypertrophy, hypertension and 
posttraumatic stress disorder (Tank & Wong, 2015).  
 
Vasopressin is one of three major hormones that acts on the blood vessels and kidneys and are 
involved in the preservation of fluid and balance of electrolyte in the body, but those are e.g. 
sodium, calcium, potassium and magnesium which the body receives through food and fluids. 
During stressful environmental challenges, vasopressin is released into the blood stream to  
prevent the loss of body fluid (Antoni, 2017).  
 
Prolactin is an adaptive hormone that controls immune response, angiogenesis which is the 
formation of new blood vessels, energy metabolism, and helps with balancing electrolytes in 
the body. In recent years, prolactin studies have shown that there is correlation between high 
prolactin levels and psychological distress, and patients that have higher level of prolactin have 
addressed that they feel more hostile and anxious. However, more information is needed on 
how influential prolactin is during the stress response (Torner, 2016). 
 
After the alarm stage the individual enters the resistance stage, where the body begins to repair 
itself and resolve the stress, where the hormonal levels, heart rate and other factors try to return 
back to normal and reach a stable equilibrium (homeostasis). The alarm stage and the resistance 
stage often act as opposites since a lot of the characteristics of the alarm stage disappear or are 
reversed during the stage of resistance (Selye, 1950). If on the other hand the stressor is not 
removed and the stressful situation is continued for some period of time, the body will remain 
on high alert, and become adjusted to the high level of stress where stress hormones are still 
being produced and will in the end reach the third and final stage, exhaustion. The exhaustion 
stage introduces the signs of alarm stage which results in the body’s resources do not function 
as normal due to the long stressful situation and the individual becomes likelier to develop 
stress-related illnesses, become more expectable to diseases and worst case scenario, the 
individual dies (Selye, 1976). 
Stress as a Dynamic Process 
Butler’s third and last definition of stress is stress as a dynamic process, often called 
transactional theory of stress and coping (TTSC), which was developed by Richard Lazarus. 
His definition of stress looks at “the relationship between the person and the environment, 
which takes into account the characteristics of the person on the one hand, and the nature of 
the environmental on the other” (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984), and requires psychological, 
physiological and/or behavioural efforts to manage the situation and its consequences. The 
main idea in the theory is that one’s perception of stress is the most important factor. According 
to the theory, an individual enters a situation and has to make assessment if there is a threat to 
begin with and categorise the encounter, by definition cognitive appraisal. Lazarus and 
==PAGE:8==
 
5
Folkman argue that appraisals can be split into three different components, primary appraisal, 
secondary appraisal and reappraisal (Walinga, 2010).  
 
Primary appraisal is differentiated as irrelevant, benign-positive and stressful. Irrelevant 
appraisal is when the situation has no value for the individual and therefore it has no physical 
or emotional consequences (Shebilske, 2007). Benign-positive appraisal arises when an event 
is seen to have good implications and result in positive emotions for the individual, such as 
joy, love, happiness and peacefulness. Not all people will believe that the desirable state will 
last for long, so in some cases it can generate guilt or anxiety which anticipates that appraisals 
are complex and depend on individual factors (Brannon & Feist, 2010) (Lazarus & Folkman, 
1984). Stress appraisals arise when an event or a situation have the potential to have bad 
implications and negative physical or emotional ramifications (Shebilske, 2007). First kind of 
stress appraisal is harm/loss, when an individual experiences harm or loss, such as injury, 
damage to self-esteem or loss of a person, damage has already been sustained. Secondly, threat 
appraisal is assessing the possibility of threat, which they identify as harm and losses that have 
not taken place, and what makes threat different from harm and loss is that it allows anticipatory 
coping, which is preparing for a stressful consequence that is very likely to happen, e.g. when 
you are about to do public speaking or take an exam (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984). Threat 
appraisal is often correlated with maladaptive tendencies and is often characterized by negative 
emotions (Williams & Cumming, 2012). Third and last kind of stress appraisal is challenge, 
which is quite similar to threat appraisal but it focuses more on gain and the growth that can 
come out of a situation and is considered more adaptable approach. When looking at athletes 
the challenge state can lead to better performance where an individual can prove his worth and 
allows pleasurable emotions such as eagerness and excitement. Threat and challenge are not 
mutually exclusive and they can occur at the same time, e.g. when a student gets a student job, 
and is eager to learn but at the same time the student is afraid of not being able to step up to 
the job and perform as well as expected (Williams & Cumming, 2012) (Lazarus & Folkman, 
1984).  
 
After primary appraisal comes secondary appraisal. When an individual experiences threat or 
a challenge the situation has to be managed, either something has to be done or left alone. If 
there is something that can be done, it also has to be taken into consideration what is at stake, 
what are the resources and what are the options for coping, as well as the likelihood that an 
individual can apply an effective strategy. The stress response and the emotional reaction is 
based on the interact between secondary and primary appraisals, if the person is not capable to 
deal with the situation the stress will be greater than otherwise, leading to negative stress 
response (Lazarus, 1992) (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984).  
 
Reappraisal is an ongoing process which refers to a changed appraisal on the basis of new 
information from the environment, where the individual reappraised the nature of the stressor 
or the resources he has to respond to it (Walinga, 2010). Cognitive reappraisal focuses on 
changing the interpretation or appraisal of the stimuli, e.g. if you have an important deadline 
and you are nervous about meeting it, you can reappraise that nervous feeling that it is your 
body helping you stay alert, and that leads to the deadline being a challenge that you are excited 
about instead of considering it a threat (Buhle et al., 2014) (McLaughlin, 2020).

Albrecht’s Types of Stress 
Back in 1979, Dr. Karl Albrecht a management consultant came to the conclusion that in his 
belief there were four types of stress, time stress, anticipatory stress, situational stress and 
encounter stress.  
 
Time stress is probably the most common type of stress in the current environment we live in 
today, but the definition of time stress is constant worry about time and usually the lack of it, 
with time pressure at work or at school, as well as having kids and having to take care of a 
household can be stressful for a lot of people (Albrecht, 1979). Anticipatory stress, often called 
future stress is an emotion-based feeling, where an individual experiences that there is a future 
threat that he cannot control which leads to autonomic arousal, sometimes it can be just one 
event such as having a deadline for a school project in five months which is constantly on your 
mind (Grogan & Murphy, 2011). Situational stress as the name indicates refers to when an 
individual is in a situation beyond his control, were the individual is unsure whether they will 
meet the responsibilities and demands based on the recourses and abilities that they have at a 
given time. Situational stress can be short-term such as sitting in traffic knowing you will be 
late for an important academic meeting and there is nothing you can do about it, but it can also 
result from e.g. a health crisis such as the COVID-19 pandemic which can be considered an 
overburden for many people (Oreg & Berson, 2013). Encounter stress refers to interaction 
between individuals or a group of people, e.g. doctors or social workers experience high level 
of encounter stress since they are working with people all day long were they can be drained 
due to a lot of personal interactions (Albrecht, 1979).  
 
Human Function Curve 
Even though stress is often considered an unwelcome guest in our society, it cannot be 
overlooked that stress has its benefits depending on the situation. As applies to many things we 
often seek for stability and balance in our lives, whether it is our day to day diet or our work-
life balance. The same applies to stress as the cardiologist Peter Nixon clearly identified back 
in 1982 when he developed the Human Function Curve which is illustrated in Figure 2, that 
considers the balance between positive and negative stress (Murden, 2018).  
 
 
Figure 2: Stress curve (Posen, 1995). 
 
Peter Nixon’s model goes hand in hand with Selye’s ideas where he identified four main areas 
of stress, positive stress (eustress), negative stress (distress), overstress (hyperstress), and 
understress (hypostress) (Cooper & Dewe, 2004).  
==PAGE:10==
 
7
In relation to the stress curve, hypostress is insufficient amount of stress where the level of 
performance is relatively low, resulting in the feeling of restlessness, lack of inspiration and 
often boredom where people stop growing in what they are doing. Eustress on the other hand 
is a positive response to a stressors resulting in a positive psychological state, where the level 
of performance is at its highest (Hargrove, Nelson, & Cooper, 2013). Eustress can be 
psychological or physical and it prepares the heart, muscles and body for what is to come. 
Eustress has many benefits such as increased creativity, it stimulates the immune system for 
optimum operation for health benefits, boost optimal adrenaline to the body to think clearer 
and move faster, as well as leading to a feeling of fulfilment, satisfaction and wellbeing. 
Example of positive stress is, engaging in sports, receiving promotion and planning a vacation 
(Eredoro & Egbochuku, 2019). In demanding jobs individuals can feel hope, feel like they are 
fully present as well as being focused and at the top of their game (Agnihotri, 2018) (Hargrove, 
Nelson, & Cooper, 2013). Being exposed to positive stress without being able to manage it 
sufficiently can drain the body’s recourses and can transform positive stress into negative one 
(Eredoro & Egbochuku, 2019). Distress is the kind of stress which is negative, unpleasant and 
harmful and can often be seen as maladaptive response to stressful situations where the 
performance on an individual decreased gradually (Selye, 1974) (Eredoro & Egbochuku, 
2019). When routine is constantly being changed or readjusted it creates a lot of unresolved 
feelings and emotions such as discomfort which can lead to physiological and behavioural 
symptoms such as, increased blood pressure, aggression and other negative coping 
mechanisms. Examples of bad stress is, death of a partner or family member, illness, financial 
problem or being neglected. Distress can be further categorized into acute stress, and chronic 
stress (Eredoro & Egbochuku, 2019). Chronic distress results from a long-term stress and has 
been defined as a stress that lasts for several hours per day for weeks or months. It is the kind 
of stress that effects our health. Chronic stress can be seen in people e.g. who live in endless 
poverty and dysfunctional families (Dhabhar, 2018).  
 
Hyperstress can occur when a person is going beyond its limits and the stress level becomes 
more as well as the performance level decreases, and exhaustion starts to creep in. Hyperstress 
often results from being overworked and overloaded (Agnihotri, 2018). 
Stress and Illness 
 
Researches are showing that majority of the body’s systems are affected by chronic stress and 
thus it comes to no surprise that stress, especially long-term or chronic will have some 
consequences on the body which can have negative effects on one’s health (Salleh, 2008). 
There are a lot of factors that can affect the relationship between psychosocial stressors and 
disease, such as number, nature and persistence of the stressors, as well as the learned patterns 
of coping, resources and biological factors such as genetics (Schneiderman, Ironson, & Siegel, 
2005). When the body is trying to recover from stress, inflammation becomes one of the body 
responses, and diseases such as cardiovascular dysfunctions, diabetes, cancer, autoimmune 
syndromes and mental illnesses have been linked to inflammation and stress (Mariotto, 2015).  
 
Chrousos and Kaltsas argue how psychosocial stress and activation of the whole stress system 
in the body leads to obesity and metabolic syndrome resulting in the development of 
cardiovascular diseases, which is the major cause of mortality and morbidity in the western 
part of the world (Chrousos & Kaltsas, 2007). Cardiovascular diseases affect the heart and the 
veins and include heart attack, heart failure, strokes and blood clot. According to The European 
Heart Network, cardiovascular diseases cause more than 3,9 million deaths in Europe, which 
==PAGE:11==
 
8
accounts for 45% of all deaths in Europe and 37% in the EU countries (Wilkins et al., 2017). 
Studies have been illustrating that chronic work stress increases the risk of cardiovascular 
diseases, as well as chronic stressors such as caregiving and discrimination (Dimsdale, 2008). 
 
Many reports and studies are suggesting that stressful experiences trigger the onset of diabetes, 
but in the field, there is a lot of controversy. There is a clear link between depression and 
prevalent and incident diabetes, as well as depression is a well-known epilogue of chronic 
stress (Mariotto, 2015). Mooy et al. have demonstrated links between stressful experiences and 
diagnosis of type 2 diabetes in large populated studies. Bjorntop has as well tried to explain the 
physiological links between stress and diabetes, but he argues that stressors lead to high cortisol 
among other biological factors which contribute to insulin resistance, with elevated glucose 
levels which is key component in type 2 diabetes (Lloyd, Smith, & Weinger, 2005) (Joseph & 
Golden, 2017).  
 
Cancer is one of the diseases in the modern world that is widely discussed, but there were 
around 3,91 million new cases of cancer in Europe, and 1,93 million deaths in 2018. The same 
controversy applies in the relationship between cancer and stress, but over the past 30 years, 
clinical and epidemiological studies have identified that psychosocial factors such as chronic 
depression and stress can be linked as risk factors for cancer progression, whereas the 
physiological stress response is considered the most likely factor (Moreno-Smith, Lutgendorf, 
& Sood, 2011). Scientist have also leaned towards the conclusion that there is an association 
between chronic stress and cancer incidence, as well as increase in tumour growth (Chiriac, 
Baban, & Dumitrascu, 2018) (Moreno-Smith, Lutgendorf, & Sood, 2011). 
 
Further studies are needed to get a better understanding of the relationship between stress and 
autoimmune syndromes, where the body starts to attack its own tissues, furthermore 
autoimmune syndromes come in various forms. Recent Swedish study conduce the possibility 
that stress may cause these autoimmune syndromes, since it illustrated that people who were 
previously diagnose with stress-related disorders had higher incidence of these autoimmune 
diseases (Song et al., 2018).  
 
Over the past decades, glucocorticoids, primarily cortisol has been widely investigated both in 
terms of neuroendocrine profile of stress diseases, and in relation to different outcomes of 
stress, such as cognitive deficits or psychopathologies. It comes to no surprise that chronic 
stress has an impact on cognition and psychopathologies since cortisol travels to the part of the 
brain which is responsible for memory, emotion and emotional regulation (Marin et al., 2011). 
In 1995, studies started to provide evidence that stress-induced cortisol had effect on human 
learning and memory, especially memory impairment syndromes such as Alzheimer’s disease 
(McEwen & Sapolsky, 1995). Large number of studies over the last year seem to illustrate 
evidence that stress response can act as a trigger for many mental illnesses (Yuen et al., 2012). 
Van Praag illustrated with his study that stress may cause depressive features such as anxiety 
and aggression, where he demonstrated that chronic stress cause changes in cortisol systems, 
which have similar features as the previous mention depressive features (Praag, 2004). 
Furthermore, individuals can obtain panic disorder which is a form of anxiety disorder, where 
they have regularly panic attacks which is defined as a period of overwhelming distress and 
anxiety (Hoppe, Ipser, Gorman, & Stein, 2012).  
 
Burnout is the state of mental, emotional and physical exhaustion caused by chronic emotional 
and interpersonal stressors concerning one’s job. Burnout can be defined by three variables, 
exhaustion, cynicism and professional inefficacy, where the exhaustion variable is the stress

gastrointestinal disorders, muscle tension, hypertension, cold/flu episode and sleep 
disturbances, and these symptoms are usually correlated with the exhaustion variable of 
burnout (Maslach & Leiter, 2016). It is clear that stress affects the body, emotions, mind and 
behaviour of individuals.  
Stress Management and Coping 
 
Lazarus argued that coping with stress can be separated in two groups, problem-focused coping 
were the causes of the stress in the environment are targeted, actions are taken and the situation 
is changed or removed. The second group is emotion-focused coping were the main idea is to 
reduce or change how the negative stressor is appraised (Lazarus, 1993). Coping research is 
constantly evolving with time and improved knowledge, with new concepts arising. Future-
Oriented Proactive Coping looks into preparing people to cope in advance to potential stressors 
and thus making sure that it will have minimum impact on the individual, e.g. dealing with 
results of an exam in the near future. To be able to respond to future stressors, Aspinwall and 
Taylor argued that five interrelated components are needed, recourses, accepting the stressor, 
appraisals of the stressor, coping efforts and lastly being able to learn and use feedback about 
success experiences (Folkman & Moskowitz, 2004). Dual Process Model of Coping is a 
dynamic process were an individual dangle between loss-oriented coping and restoration-
oriented coping. Loss-oriented coping can be considered as grief work, were a person denies 
and avoids changes related to restoration, while restoration-oriented coping looks at mastering 
new skills and responsibilities, accepting and understanding the stressor, and in case of loss of 
a loved one that could be for a wife to become and accept being a widow (Folkman & 
Moskowitz, 2004). In general, it is often considered that coping types can be split in five 
groups, problem-focuses coping, emotion-focused coping, seeking-understanding coping were 
an individual focus on understanding the problem and the meaning of the experience, seeking 
help and lastly avoiding the problem or potential solutions (Beradi, Glantsman, & Whipple, 
2019). 
 
There are a lot of techniques and opportunities to cope with as well as reducing stress and 
promote good health, which can be cognitive, physical, environmental or of other forms. 
Therapy, especially cognitive-behaviour therapy, were the core premise of the treatment is to 
change maladaptive cognitions such as beliefs, that leads to emotional distress and problematic 
behaviours. In stress management intervention as well as depression and anxiety disorders, 
cognitive-behaviour therapy has been more effective than many other treatments at achieving 
the overall goal which is to reduce the symptoms, improve the person in functioning and remiss 
the disorder (Hofmann, Asnaani, Vonk, Sawyer, & Fang, 2013). Furthermore, there is another 
method which has been working well with cognitive-behaviour therapy and that is progressive 
muscle relaxation where the idea is to maintain tension in 16 different muscles for 5-7 seconds, 
which results in less tension in the body and the individual learns how to relax (McCallie, 
Blum, & Hood, 2006). 
 
Mindfulness has been becoming more popular in the past years were people participate in 
mental training to calm the body and mind, as well as relaxing the body and muscles where 
oneself is aware of the body and mind. Mindfulness-based stress reduction is a 8-10-week 
group program including 10 – 40 participants, that uses mindfulness meditation to deal with 
and alleviate suffering with psychiatric, physical and psychosomatic disorders. The method 
assumes that awareness will lead to more correct perception of the situation which reduces the 
==PAGE:13==
 
10
negative effects and improves one’s ability to cope. Mindfulness-based stress reduction method 
has shown great benefit to individuals who are dealing with anxiety disorders, depression and 
stress (Grossman, Niemann, Schmidt, & Walach, 2004).  
 
Meditation is a simple technique that has the potential of triggering the body’s relaxation 
response. Meditation can be divided into two main groups, concentrative meditation and 
mindfulness/insight meditations. Concentration meditation focuses on giving attention to 
specific activity, that could be a breath, image or a mantra, Transcendental Meditation is an 
example of concentration meditation (Jr., Laiting, & Everyly, 2012). Transcendental 
Meditation is often considered the most effective mediation technique and should be practiced 
twice daily for about 20 minutes, sitting upright in a comfortable position, with the use of the 
technique to achieve a state of deep relaxation, research show reduction in psychological 
distress, including decreased anxiety, emotional distress and depressive symptoms. 
Transcendental Meditation increases alpha electroencephalogram (EEG) coherence and 
synchrony to necessary groups for motor, sensory and cognitive behaviour (Elder, Nidich, 
Moriarty, & Nidich, 2014). Mindfulness/insight meditation on the other hand pays attention to 
thoughts, senses and physical sensations (Jr., Laiting, & Everyly, 2012).  
 
Deep breathing or diaphragmatic breathing is the process of expanding the stomach and take 
deep inhalation and exhalation, which results in decreased respiration frequency and 
maximisation of blood gases. Psychological studies are showing that five minute daily deep 
breathing for 30 days can significantly decrease anxiety, depression and stress, as well as 
breathing practice is widely used in clinical treatments for all kinds of mental conditions. 
Physiological evidences indicate that a single breathing practice, reduces blood pressure, 
increase heart rate variability and oxygenation, enhance pulmonary function and improves 
respiratory muscle strength (Ma et al., 2017).  
 
Music has been a part of human history for centuries, and often can music be linked to various 
experiences, whether it is a breakup, wedding, funeral, concerts, or just a joyful time with good 
friends. Being exposed to music can reduce cortisol levels and stress, but that does not apply 
for all music genres, e.g. classical music such as Beethoven’s 6th Symphony reduces cortisol 
levels while “techno-music” seems to increase the level of stress hormones. Furthermore, there 
is a difference between major and minor music, in that sense that major music reduces stress 
and cortisol more than minor music. When a musical stimulus is used to induce relaxation, the 
tempo should be below what is identified as a resting heart rate (<80 beats per minute), but 
often it can vary from 60-100 beats per minute in normal adults. Additionally, the music has to 
have fluid melodic movements, pleasing harmonies, regular rhythm without sudden changes, 
and it is important that the music has tonal qualities such as strings, piano or flute. Musical 
stimuli can then be used as a synchronizer through entrainment to influence changes in 
physiological responses, such as heartbeat, blood pressure and respiratory rate. It is important 
to keep in mind when music is used as stress relief that it can in fact evoke stress, anxiety and 
intolerance for people, and often can cultural differences be the reason why people respond 
differently to music. Even though music techniques in stress management can stand on its own, 
studies have illustrated that combined guided imaginary and music and progressive muscle 
relaxation and music result in significant improvement in the state of relaxation (Yehuda, 
2011).  
 
Guided imagery has been practised for decades and is a mind-body therapy that evokes and 
generates mental images and patterns that the mind creates. Guided therapy can be done by 
individuals on their own and can be used in stressful situations whether it is at home, in the 
==PAGE:14==
 
11
work setting or on a subway. The idea is to use the imaginations to create mental images that 
results in positive emotions and physical effects for the individual (Hart, 2008). Van Kuiken 
argues that there are four types of guided imagery. Firstly, pleasant imagery, such as positive 
memories that generate feeling of calmness or comfort. Secondly, physiologically imagery, 
focusing on a function that needs healing, e.g. imagining the cancer cells in the body being 
destroyed. Thirdly, mental rehearsal or reframing, imagining a task or performance prior to the 
event and the emotions connected to it. Lastly, receptive imagery, which refers to an individual 
scanning the body for direct healing (Kuiken, 2004). Thoughts can be very powerful, while 
people can think of negative images that can increase stress, anxiety and pain, same can be 
applied with positive and calming images, which can decrease pain and other symptoms. 
Guided imagery is most effective in group settings, and when it is used together with music 
(Hart, 2008).  
 
In general, there is no one right way of dealing with and reliving stress, e.g. one can choose to 
go to therapy, meditate, practice mindfulness, engage in hobbies, read, do yoga, listen to music, 
do physical exercise, go to a spa, pray, take prescribed medication, etc. 
Students and Stress 
 
According to The Danish Health Authority young adults in Denmark are becoming more and 
more stressed, where one in every four from the age range 16-24 are experiencing high levels 
of stress (Damsgaard, 2017). Young adults aged 16-34 are most likely to be affected by mental 
health problems, such as depression and anxiety, but approximately 20% of the Danish 
population will experience these problems over a course of one year. Furthermore, some 
scholars argue that there is a correlation between education and mental health problems, but 
people with higher level of education seem to show lower sign of mental health problems than 
those who have only finished compulsory education (Sommar, 2016). Mental illness has been 
a growing concern within the academic community, especially when it comes to graduate 
education, but a recent study with 2279 participants from 26 countries, illustrated that graduate 
students are more than six 6 times likelier than the general population to experience anxiety 
and depression (Evans, Bira, Gastelum, Weiss, & Vanderford, 2018).  
 
It can often be a stressful process to make that decision to enrol at a university, and usually 
students have high expectations of how their life at the university will turn out. Often people 
leave the comfort zone at their own homes and move away for the first time, some even 
crossings boarders where they do not know the language and have to adjust to new social 
environment. The first days and weeks revolve around meeting new people and being 
comfortable in moving around the campus area and adapt to the new educational environment. 
In a study conducted by Reda Abouserie in 1994, university students had to rank their degree 
of stress they were experiencing in various situations that could be stress inducing. Those were, 
ranked from the highest impact to the lowest, examinations and their results, studying for 
exams, too much to do, amount to learn, need to do well (self-imposed), essays and projects, 
financial problems, lack of time for study, timing, spacing of assignments, need to do well 
(imposed by others), unclear assignments, worry over future, forgotten assignments, unclear 
course objectives, knowing what is important to study, lack of time for family and friends, lack 
of time for own interests, making choices about career, learning new skills, uninteresting 
curriculum, family crisis, boring classes, conflict with people you live with, personal health 
problems, problems with housing, conflict with spouse/partner, loneliness, peer pressure, 
==PAGE:15==
 
12
conflict with college system, conflict with peers, home sickness, conflicts with lectures, and 
lastly sexual problems (Abouserie, 1994).  
 
In a more recent study, a student-life inventory was used to collect data from 877 participants 
about stressors and reaction to stressors. The most common stressors according to the students 
was self-imposed, such as everyone in class had to like them. Second most common stressors 
were those that resulted from pressure, such as competing with a peer who gets the highest 
grade. Third most common stressor were conflicts, e.g. between one or more desirable 
alternatives. Fourth most common stressors were changes, e.g. disruptive life or various 
changes at once. The least common stressor were frustrations, e.g. daily hassles and failure to 
reach a set goal (Gadzella & Master, 2005) (Hamaideh, 2011). When it came to the student’s 
reactions to stress, the most common response was cognitive, e.g. students thinking about 
whether the strategies they used were effective. Secondly, students reacted with emotional 
responses such as fear, worry and anger. Thirdly, physiological response such as sweating, 
headaches and stuttering, and the least common response was behavioural, e.g. crying, being 
abusive to others or of self (Hamaideh, 2011).  
 
Engineering programs are often considered a high stress field of study, and often stress can be 
developed based on culture and social norms, where it is perceived that engineering students 
always work hard and it is complete normal to be constantly stressed out, because that is what 
engineers do, stress and high workload is a part of the decision to pursue an engineering carrier. 
In a 2012 study the reason why most students leave an engineering program is based on the 
fact that they feel like they do not belong, but having stress as a norm can give the students 
idea that only certain personalities are accepted or can be successful engineers, which can have 
detrimental effects. Additionally, students addressed that the reason why they left was that they 
found the pace too fast and the amount of workload too high. A 2018 study illustrated that 
22,4% of engineering students reported moderate to severe stress, 29,9% reported moderate to 
severe anxiety and 29,9% moderate to severe depression (Jensen & Cross, 2018). In a 2012 
study, they looked at why college students with suicidal thought did to seek treatment, the 
second most common answer was due to the fact that stress was normal in college (Downs & 
Eisenberg, 2012). 
 
In a 2013 meta-analysis, psychological interventions to reduce stress in university students 
were evaluated. It identified that moderate stress-related mental health concerns, including 
depression and anxiety, is affecting half of the university students which is a critical issue for 
the universities. In result of the meta-analysis, cognitive, behavioural and mindfulness-based 
approaches can reduce effects of stress, as well as levels of cortisol response, depression and 
anxiety amongst university students (Regehr, Glancy, & Pitts, 2013). 
Entrepreneurs and Stress 
 
Entrepreneurship refers to begin something, launch or undertake, and is often considered to be 
consisted of three main features, “individuals who are able to identify opportunities and exploit 
them to generate value for potential customers, themselves and the whole of society” (Tavakoli 
& Fayolle, 2017). Defining the word entrepreneur offers multiple solutions since it has a very 
broad definition, but in general, entrepreneurial process is well suited in private, public or non-
profit sectors, especially in public service, but it also takes into account intrapreneurs, those 
who act like entrepreneurs within an organisation (Tavakoli & Fayolle, 2017).  
 
==PAGE:16==
 
13
Entrepreneurship, innovation and creativity are interrelated. Creativity is defined as useful 
ideas and products, which can be connected to the cognitive process of identifying 
opportunities (Parthasarathy, Doboli, & Paulus, 2011). Creativity is the foundation of all 
innovation (Amabile, Conti, Coon, Lazenby, & Herron, 1996), but innovation can be defined 
as “the multi-stage process whereby organisations transform ideas into new/improved 
products, service or processes, in order to advance, compete and differentiate themselves 
successfully in their marketplace” (Baregheh, Sambrook, & Rowley, 2009). Some might say 
that entrepreneurship is the outcome of innovation, but one has to keep in mind that the value 
creation in entrepreneurship, making an idea into a business opportunity, is not solely 
dependent on innovation (Tavakoli & Fayolle, 2017). 
 
Entrepreneurship has been rising in universities, especially since open innovation has been 
growing steadily while closed innovation has been cutback. Organisations are increasingly 
sourcing external knowledge instead of operating in their own innovation environment, which 
is ideal for universities. In relation to engineering students, they both operate in technology 
pull and push environment, the demand for technological innovation has been growing over 
the past decades, where universities are key sources for all kinds of innovation, as well as 
universities pushing technology to the industry (Rothaermel, Agung, & Jiang, 2007).  
 
Those who decide to pursue an entrepreneurial carrier often experience more occupational 
stress than those who choose to work in established organisations, which some scholars suggest 
its due to role ambiguity (Wei, Cang, & Hisrich, 2015)(Aitken, Saltstone, & Fraboni, 1999). 
When one thinks of entrepreneurship, high-risk taking, heavy workload, job uncertainty and 
decision conflict, are words that often come up, which can lead to fear, loneliness, stain anxiety 
and other negative feelings and emotional exhaustion (Wei, Cang, & Hisrich, 2015). 
Entrepreneurs have also been perceived as flexible, persistence individuals that have high 
tolerance for frustration, and can withstand obstacles (Aitken, Saltstone, & Fraboni, 1999). In 
a study from 1999, entrepreneurs perceived that high workload was the greatest source of on-
the-job stress, were on average they spent 56,7 hours on their business. Furthermore, they 
thought that underutilisation of their skills and role ambiguity were the second and third 
greatest source of stress (Aitken, Saltstone, & Fraboni, 1999). Additionally, scholars have 
identified the need to achieve, need for power, need for affiliation, competitive comparison, 
demands of knowledge, managing responsibility and resources can be linked to entrepreneurial 
stressors (Wei, Cang, & Hisrich, 2015). Furthermore, there are evidence that role stressors can 
lead to exhaustion which makes entrepreneurs more likely to withdraw from their venture 
(Wincent, Örtqvist, & Drnovsek, 2008).  
 
Entrepreneurs psychological well-being and coping is considered to impact performance, but 
it is perceived that prior start-up experience can be looked at as knowledge and information 
resource, which can prepare the individual for which coping strategies can be used when a 
stressful situation occurs. This might indicate that combining students, entrepreneurship and 
stress theories might positively affect stress and mental health of the student in the future if 
they choose to continue with a carrier in entrepreneurship. There are though signs that active 
coping within entrepreneurs seems to have positive effects on the psychological-well-being of 
the individuals involved, without having prior experience of start-ups (Uy, Foo, & Song, 2013).

Methodology 
 
Setting and Sample 
Participants were found by approaching students from a technological entrepreneurship course 
(X-Tech) at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) where each team was responsible to 
nominate one random student from their team to participate in the study. The course is set up 
in an accelerator format, where students create a technology demonstrator and a business plan 
which is pitched to examiners and investors. The experiment ran for approximately 11 weeks, 
from March 11th 2020 – May 20th 2020. The participants for the study were in the beginning 
16, but 12 (male: 7, female: 4, non-binary: 1) participants were considered eligible at the end 
of the study. Average age of the students was 25,73 years.  
 
Students were randomly split up in three groups where every participant had to complete daily 
PSS (perceived stress scale) questionnaire among other questions concerning stressors and 
stress relief methods which participants received on their mobile phones. To make it less of a 
burden to the participant to answer the questions, they were split up in three different formats 
which was initialised every third day as can be seen in Table A. 1. The three different groups 
were approached individually, group A received no intervention and were asked to answer a 
daily questionnaire concerning their stress level. Group B had an intervention, where they got 
information about stressors, physical and psychological symptoms of stress and how it can 
have a positive and negative effects. They were introduced to stress relieving methods such as 
meditation, music, breathing, along with a list of good apps that could be used and encouraged 
to find other stress relieving methods that would work for that individual. Group C got the 
same intervention as group B, but additionally participants had to measure their stress level 
with Sumondo medical device.  
Percieved Stress Scale 
The perceived stress scale (PSS) is a self-report measure to which situations in one’s life are 
perceived stressful based on their ability to cope, which was developed by Cohen using 
Lazarus’ transactional theory of stress and coping. The scale has become one of the most 
widely used measure of perceived stress (Taylor, 2015). The 10 items of the PSS are presented 
below but instead of looking at perceived stress over the last month, it was measured on a daily 
basis. 
 
1. Today, how often did you feel upset because of something that happened 
unexpectedly? 
2. Today, how often did you feel that you were unable to control the important things in 
your life? 
3. Today, how often did you feel nervous and stressed? 
4. Today, how often did you feel confident about your ability to handle your personal 
problems? 
5. Today, how often did you feel things were going your way? 
6. Today, how often did you feel like you could not cope with all the things you had to 
do? 
7. Today, how often did you feel like you had control over irritations in your life? 
8. Today, how often did you feel that you were on top of things? 
==PAGE:18==
 
15
9. Today, how often did you feel angry because of things that were outside of your 
control? 
10. Today, how often did you feel like difficulties were piling up so high that you could 
not overcome it? 
 
Participants completed the questions on a Likert-type scale, ranging from 0 (Never), 1 (Almost 
never), 2 (Sometimes), 3 (Fairly often) and 4 (Very often). Before the PSS score is determined, 
scores for questions 4, 5, 7 and 8 have to be reversed since they indicate positive items, e.g. 
0=4, 1=3, 2=2, 3=1 and 4=0. PSS scores are obtained by summing across all 10 items. 
Individual scores can range from 0–40, but scores ranging from 0-13 are considered low stress, 
scores ranging from 14–26 are considered moderate stress and scores ranging from 27-40 are 
considered high stress. 
Sumondo Medical Device 
Participants in group C had to use Sumondo medical device that uses heart rate variability 
(HRV), artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) to diagnose mental stress 
(Sumondo, 2020). Group C had to measure their stress level at 10am every day for 5 minutes, 
and throughout that time participants could see in an app how their stress fluctuated based on 
a scale of 0 to 20, where 0-4 (Calm), 4-6 (Active), 6-8 (Lighter stress), 8-12 (Stress) and 12-20 
(Toxic stress). To be able to compare the perceived stress and measured stress it was assumed 
that calm and active would fall under the category “low stress”, lighter stress and stress would 
fall under the category “moderate stress” and toxic stress would fall under the category “high 
perceived stress”. The Sumondo app offered exercises for stress relieving, such as breathing, 
music therapy and guided meditation.  
Statistical Analysis 
All statistical analysis was performed using SPSS, including descriptive statistics (n, mean, 
standard deviation). Pearson correlation analysis was performed where all statistical 
significance level was presented. Missing values from the PSS, were predicted with the linear 
trend at point method in SPSS.

Discussion 
 
Even though one might think that stress would affect students’ academic work, the overall 
response score indicates that on average, students believe that stress almost never affects their 
work but it should be taken into consideration that the body might have become adjusted to the 
high levels of stress, where in the long run it might push them to exhaustion. Interestingly, 
students in group A have the lowest average response score, while students in group B had the 
highest. The reason for that might be that the answers are too individualised due to few 
participants, meaning that a stressed individual, e.g. with underlying condition might be 
affecting the overall score.  
 
Main sources of stress for students in their lives was clearly their studies, but stressors in the 
academic environment that were reported are in general similar to those that can be found in 
the literature. While other studies have reported exams and studying for exams, as the stressors 
with the highest impact, students in Denmark reported workload and work/study balance, 
which according to the literature has been addressed as one of the most common reason why 
students decide to drop out of engineering programs. Clearly there is a difference between 
countries what students perceive as stressors. This difference might be due to the fact that there 
is different emphasis in academic work between countries where students in Denmark tend to 
work more in groups, and often work on bigger projects throughout the semester instead on 
only focusing on theoretical aspects. This approach can have its pros and cons, but relationships 
with other students seem to cause more stress then what can be found in the literature. 
Work/study balance might be ranked so high due to financial incentives set by the Danish 
authorities where some students are viable to receive monthly benefits if they work concurrent 
with their studies. The problem might also be a social factor, where students might experience 
pressure coming from external factors such as it being the norm to be working while getting 
education. It also has to be mentioned in this context that financial problems might be pushing 
students to work which might be causing stress. Even though students did not address COVID-
19 as a major stressor it might have affected them more then they realised. When students had 
to use their own imagination at the end of each week to identify at least three major stressors 
from the passing week, COVID-19 was the second most influential factor. Switching to online 
classes in the middle of the semester might have made students feel inadequate due to external 
factors, where some might have adapted better than others. In a way the five big stressors could 
all be linked to COVID-19, since students might have lost their jobs or had to adapt to online 
work as well as distance teaching, which might have led to financial insecurity or uncertainty 
about how to finance their basic needs in the near future. COVID-19 could also have been 
affecting the health of the students, not only the fear of being infected but also being ill when 
basic healthcare might be in minimum range of activities, and where you have to follow rules 
on social distancing. Students did not address issues such as time stress which is often 
considered the most common type of stress according to Albrecht, but with economy slowing 
down and social distancing, students might have experienced less time stress. 
 
There is no clear correlation between the literature and the students concerning entrepreneurial 
stressors, even though the course is set up in an incubator format. It might be the security and 
the environment around the students which might be the reason why the stressors differ. 
Furthermore, it might be that the individuals participating in this study might not be as invested 
in the future of their project and the possibilities they offer. What students and entrepreneurs 
seem to have in common is workload being the greatest source of stress. Stressors related to 
the course correlates well with other course work, and even though it is unclear, stressors linked 
==PAGE:30==
 
27
to relationship with other students might in fact include known entrepreneurial stressors such 
as need for affiliation, competitive comparison, demands of knowledge and managing 
responsibility and resources.  
 
The difference between groups on how they perceived stress comes as a surprise. Group B 
perceived way higher stress than other groups. It could be that other groups are stronger in 
psychological, physiological and behavioural efforts to manage a situation, hence have found 
their comfort zone between eustress and distress. However, a likelier explanation could be that 
there is a difference in how often groups participated, as can be seen by the answer ratio from 
the PSS in Table A. 5 where group B had higher answer ratio and included more people. The 
literature illustrated that 22,4% of engineering students report moderate to severe stress, 
whereas this survey illustrated that 36% of the time engineering students report moderate to 
high stress. This difference does not necessarily mean that engineering students are getting 
more stressed, since it has to be taken into consideration the students participating in the study 
were facing a global pandemic that has no analogue.  
 
Students in Group A showed fewer correlations in relation to feeling nervous and stressed. 
Interestingly, if group B and C perceived that they had been nervous and stressed, it also 
increased the feeling that things were not going their way and they felt they could not cope 
with everything they had to do, while those in group A almost had neutral correlation.  
 
The most interesting finding is the difference between perceived stress and measured stress, 
but only one individual has similarities between his perceived stress and measured stress. 
Unfortunately, it is difficult to compare if there is a difference due to lack of data but measured 
stress seems to be higher than perceived one. This difference might be due to the fact that the 
individual was moving when the stress measurement was being carried out, or more 
interestingly that physically measured stress appears sooner than cognitive measured stress, 
which could mean that detecting it earlier could in fact allow the student to decrease it before 
they might experience stress-related illnesses. Furthermore, individuals tend to be biased when 
self-reporting which might lead to PSS indicating lower stress then what is perceived in reality, 
but individuals 2 and 3 only report low stress according to the PSS, but their measured stress 
indicates that 45% and 70% respectively of the time they are experiencing low stress, otherwise 
moderate or high stress.  
 
Interestingly, students in group A were more effortful when it came to relieving their stress, 
while students in group C, which indicated how and when they were stressed, and offered stress 
relieving methods did nothing more than half of the time. It might be disbelieving in the 
technology if it is perceived that the stress level is lower than it actually is, since the data 
illustrates some time spent in moderate or high stress fields, or the fact that they were not that 
invested in lowering their stress levels. It comes as a surprise that students in group B did not 
use meditation as a stress reliever, even though they were presented with the tools, why that 
might be remains a mystery, but it might be that podcasts and music are convenient since it can 
be used while doing chores, cooking or when a person is on the go. Even though students did 
not always report relieving their stress level, overall they came up with diverse and creative 
methods to decrease their stress level to find what fits them the best, as well as using methods 
which the literature addressed, such as meditation, music, mindfulness and breathing.

Conclusion 
 
The main conclusion that can be drawn from this study is that enhancing students’ knowledge 
and skills concerning their stress levels does not decrease the overall stress for the students. 
The study focused more on restoration-oriented coping methods, where students master new 
skills, but it did not work as one had hope, with that being said it might be that students had 
needed more time. Problem-focused coping might be a better approach for universities to 
consider, where the causes of stress in the academic setting are targeted, actions are taken and 
the situation is changed or removed, but more research is needed in terms of interventions and 
programs for engineering students who are participating in the entrepreneurial settings before 
any recommendation can be made.  
Limitations 
 
Initially, data was only considered eligible if a student had participated in 70% of the study, 
but due to lack of participation, students with lower answer ratio had to be taken into 
consideration, which might have made the data analysis wage and biased. Additionally, data 
from three out of four students in group C could be retrieved, but with the amount of data that 
had been collected from the PSS, it was considered that the fourth individual was eligible for 
this study, which most likely caused inaccuracy in the data analysis. Due to the difference 
concerning perceived stress and measured stress, statistical analysis in SPSS could not be 
performed. Data was missing concerning question number 8 in the PSS which had an impact 
on the overall stress score.
