Abstract
Non-destructive testing (NDT) is a crucial technique in the manufacturing and qual-
ity control of wind turbine rotor blades. The current state-of-the-art method, ultrasonic
inspection (UT), suffers from limitations such as loss of depth resolution when scanning
through the thickness/height and shadowing effects, making it unable to detect and char-
acterize multiple-layered cracks or delaminations, damages in sandwich structures, or
wrinkle defects in composite manufacturing. Terahertz (THz) radiation is non-ionizing,
and can easily penetrate most nonconductive materials, such as glass fiber, resin, and
balsa wood, making it an ideal candidate for wind turbine blade NDT techniques. How-
ever, current terahertz time domain spectroscopy (THz-TDS) systems are costly and
limited to coupon specimens. To address these issues, this study investigates terahertz
cross-correlation spectroscopy (THz-CCS) as a novel non-destructive testing method for
composite materials, aiming to enhance image quality and overcome the penetration lim-
itations of conventional ultrasonic testing. A GLAZE Technologies Carmen THz-CCS
system was used with two distinct sensor head configurations to detect and characterize
common composite damage types and geometries, including delamination cracks, wrinkles,
and sandwich structures. The THz-CCS system was used to identify and characterize the
delamination crack within a 16.9 mm thick unidirectional glass/epoxy double cantilever
beam sample. The specimen was held open so that the opening displacements gradually
decreased from 1.5 mm to a closed crack. Depending on the scanning parameters, the
THz-CCS device identified the crack with a crack opening resolution of 0.07 - 0.28 mm.
It was also used to examine wrinkle and sandwich specimens. The system successfully
traced the surface displacement of a wrinkle specimen and estimated the critical angle
of the wrinkle within 1 degree. THz-CCS scans were also able to estimate the refractive
index of the core material and measure the thickness of the top and bottom skins of a
sandwich specimen. Given the relative maturity of UT compared to THz-CCS, there is
enormous potential for further improvement in THz-based inspection technology. The
results demonstrate the feasibility of using THz-CCS to detect subsurface damage at sig-
nificant depths, underscoring its potential and current limitations as an emerging quality
control and detailed inspection technology for thick composite wind turbine blades.

Introduction
The average offshore wind turbine blade has grown from under 40 m in length in the early 2000s
to over 90 m in modern offshore wind turbines [1]. Wind turbine blades (WTB), are costly,
making up 15-20% of the total costs of a wind turbine, and are made of complex materials,
predominantly glass fiber reinforced polymer (GFRP) composites and balsa wood [2]. Larger
wind turbine blades can produce more power, but they become increasingly vulnerable to
both manufacturing defects and fatigue damage as the structures become thicker and more
sophisticated [3]. Nondestructive testing (NDT) methods are a vital aspect of wind turbine
quality control as well as maintenance, allowing the user to locate and identify damage within a
structure without altering it in any way. As WTBs become larger, conventional NDT methods
such as ultrasonic testing (UT) struggle to penetrate the increasingly thick composites used in
WTBs [4]. New technologies that can penetrate deeper without increasing the cost or difficulty
of use will be needed to extend the life of large WTBs.
This thesis investigates terahertz cross-correlation spectroscopy (THz-CCS) as a novel non-
destructive testing method for composite materials, aiming to enhance image quality and over-
come the penetration limitations of conventional ultrasonic techniques. A GLAZE Technolo-
gies Carmen THz-CCS system was used with two distinct sensor head configurations to detect
and characterize several common composite geometries and damage types, including sandwich
structures, delamination cracks, and wrinkles.
A comprehensive literature review of the state of the art in THz-based non-destructive test-
ing (NDT) and ultrasonic testing (UT) for the inspection and quality control of wind turbine
blades is presented in section 2. The research questions guiding the investigation of delamina-
tions, wrinkles, and sandwich structures in GFRP composites are outlined in section 3. The
experimental setup, including THz systems, UT equipment, and data processing techniques, is
detailed in the methods section (section 4).
The results are presented in three sections, each corresponding to a different damage type
or structure. section 5 covers the detection of delamination cracks using the THz V0 sensor
head, with comparisons to the V1 sensor and UT. subsubsection 2.2.2 focuses on the analysis
of a fold-type wrinkle using the V1 sensor, including surface displacement tracking and the
extraction of critical geometric features, again with UT comparison. section 7 explores two
composite structures: first, an 8-ply unidirectional laminate is examined using the V0 sensor
to assess sensitivity to individual ply layers; second, two sandwich structures are analyzed and
measured.
In section 8, the broader implications, limitations, and future directions are discussed.
Finally, section 9 summarizes the key findings in relation to the research questions and provides
concluding remarks.

2.1
Wind Turbine Blade Structure
Wind turbine blades are large, highly complex structures. The aerodynamic shells form the
exterior of the blade and are responsible for collecting energy from the wind by generating
a lift force that is transferred to the spar caps and then from the spar caps to the circular
root of the blade [5]. Most WTB have two shells, one on the pressure side facing towards
the wind and one on the suction side facing away from the wind. The shells are joined with
adhesive at the leading and trailing edges, as well as to the spar caps in the center. Between
adhesive joints, shells are designed to be as light as possible. Sandwich structures, where thin
composite skins are bonded to a core material, typically balsa wood or polymer foam, provide a
lightweight, buckling-resistant alternative to thicker composite plates in these regions, shown in
Figure 1b. Spar caps are typically constructed from thick, predominantly unidirectional glass
and/or carbon fiber epoxy composites and run the full length of the blade. They provide the
majority of the bending strength of the blade. Shear webs hold the spar caps apart, allowing
them to behave like an I-beam. Shear webs come in many configurations and can be constructed
from solid composite plates or sandwich panels. They are typically separate structures that are
adhesively bonded to the spar caps. WTB composites can be >60 mm at the base [5]. Most of
the damage in a WTB is concentrated around the widest section of the blade, or the max chord
section, labeled in Figure 1a, and the root transition zone, where rapid changes in shape cause
stress concentrations. These areas of the WTB are also more prone to manufacturing defects.

2.2
WTB Damage
Composite structures such as WTBs are analyzed differently depending on the length scale being
considered. When discussing large structures, they are typically analyzed on the macro scale,
where the composite is treated as a homogeneous material, as shown on the left in Figure 2.
However, composite laminates are not homogeneous. They are constructed from layers of fibers
stacked at different angles to provide the desired material characteristics and held together by
a matrix material. Each individual layer is referred to as a ply or lamina within the composite
laminate [6]. The resin layer between each ply is approximately 5 µm thick [7]. The fibers within
each layer can take many forms but typically are grouped in bundles and either woven into a
fabric or stitched together with all the fiber bundles parallel to each other in a unidirectional
(UD) ply. Individual E-glass fibers are typically 5-24 µm in diameter [8]. At the meso scale of
analysis (mm), shown in the center in Figure 2, ply layers are modeled to account for different
failure behaviors, but fibers and fiber bundles are not considered. The meso level allows for
examination of composite failure modes, such as debonding or delaminations. At the smallest
micro (µm) scale, shown on the right in Figure 2, each individual fiber is considered. [9].
According to Mishnaevsky 2022 [10], the leading causes of fatigue failure in wind turbine
blades are leading edge erosion, which is visible from the outside of the blade, interface de-
bonding at adhesive joints in the leading edge, trailing edge or between the shear webs, spar
caps, and shells, or between layers of the composite laminates, especially in areas with ply
drops. Blade fatigue failures typically arise from many failure modes and damage types growing
together until they reach a tipping point and the structure becomes unstable, rather than from
a single source [9]. Examination at the meso level to understand what is happening may help
to prevent catastrophic failures and prolong the lifespan of WTBs.
While many types of blade damage are visible from the surface, delaminations within the
spar caps and sandwich skin-core de-bonding, highlighted in Figure 1b, as well as many early-
stage adhesive bond line failures are invisible below the surface or in locations that are very
difficult to see with visual inspection methods [10][11]. These failures can often stem from
manufacturing defects that cause stress concentrations, like dry spots in the laminate where
the matrix material was not sufficiently infused, or fiber misalignments such as wrinkles [10].
To catch these defects before they cause major structural issues, either before they are installed
in the case of manufacturing defects, or before they can develop further for fatigue damages,
NDT methods are required [12].
2.2.1
Delamination Cracks
Damages in composites tend to propagate along weak interfaces or weak planes. When a crack
initiates, it typically follows the path of least resistance, propagating in the fiber direction
through the softer matrix material [6].
Delaminations split layers within the laminate and
are not visible from the surface, particularly if a coating has been applied. Technicians and
engineers must rely on NDT technologies to locate and determine the size and depth of delam-
inations [4]. Common initiation sites for delaminations include material discontinuities such as
inclusions, resin-rich zones, regions of high porosity, ply terminations (drop-offs), or manufac-
turing defects like fiber wrinkles [4]. They can also grow from matrix cracks under cyclic loading
[9]. While delaminations may not cause immediate or catastrophic failure, because the newly
created sublaminates are still able to carry loads, like smaller beams, they can severely compro-
mise the structural integrity of the component by reducing stiffness, strength, and load-carrying
capacity [6]. Delaminations tend to propagate, and the higher stresses in the sublaminates ini-
tiate additional delaminations. The damaged composite is also more vulnerable to buckling
because each individual beam is weaker [6]. Early detection and accurate characterization of
multilayered delamination defects is essential for ensuring long-term performance and safety of

2.2.2
Wrinkles
Wrinkles or out-of-plane fiber misalignment are one of the most common and difficult to detect
manufacturing defects in composite parts [13]. Wrinkles typically form in areas with complex
geometries where the fabric plies have to conform to a small tooling radius relative to the
thickness of the part or where there are significant ply drop-offs, such as T-joints, or in the
max chord region of a wind turbine blade [14]. They can occur at any ply within the laminate
and take on many shapes from small-scale out-of-plane oscillations to folds in the fabric layers
[13]. They can affect the surface of the composite, causing a bump or valley, or they can be
invisible from the exterior [14]. The criticality of a wrinkle is determined by its geometry as
well as its location within the laminate and the percentage of the thickness that is affected
by the wrinkle [15]. Wrinkles are typically described by their wavelength, L, amplitude, A,
and maximum angle, θmax, as shown in Figure 3 [13]. The wavelength amplitude ratio, the
wavelength angle ratio, or the amplitude thickness ratio often characterizes the criticality.
Generic single wave wrinkles, like the one pictured in Figure 3, are typically modeled as bell
curves, with more complex formulas used to model the shape of individual plies through the
thickness [14]. Nartey et al 2020 [16] found that maximum angles of 12 degrees in 3.5 mm thick
composite plates could reduce the compressive and tensile strength by 20-30%. Mishnaevsky
2022 [10] reported in his review that in sandwich structures, wrinkles can lead to an up to 55%
reduction in strength. NDT methods capable of detecting and characterizing the criticality of
wrinkles in thick composites are key to reducing failures in wind turbine blades.

2.3.1
Ultrasonic Testing
Ultrasonic testing (UT) is one of the most common NDT methods employed in the wind
industry [17]. It is a robust and well-developed technology that transmits a short acoustic
wave pulse into the specimen, typically with frequencies between 0.5 and 10 MHz for glass fiber
composite materials [4]. Elastic waves transmit poorly between materials with significantly
different densities, such as air and GFRP. As shown in Figure 4a, in a pulse echo UT device,
when the acoustic wave transmitted into the sample encounters a defect, the wave is reflected.
These reflections appear as peaks in the resulting waveform. The time delay between the peaks
indicates the location of the damage. The reflected or transmitted portion of the original wave
signal can then be analyzed to find low density damaged regions [17]. However, because the
waveform is fully reflected, a “shadowing effect” prevents further characterization of material
below the detected change in density, making it impossible to detect layered delaminations with
a diameter smaller than the uppermost delamination [18]. This phenomenon can also make it
difficult to inspect sandwich structures with very low-density core materials, as most, if not
all, of the pulse will be reflected at the bottom of the near-side skin. To allow the pulse to
travel from the transmitter into the specimen itself, most UT technologies require a coupling
medium, usually water, connecting them to the test specimen [19]. This increases the difficulty
of use and adds another potential source of error if there is insufficient contact between the
transducer and the specimen.
In UT, the axial resolution or the distance at which the peaks of two consecutive echoes
are no longer distinguishable, is typically considered to be one-half of the spatial pulse length
[20]. The spatial pulse length is defined as the wavelength in the specimen material multiplied
by the number of cycles in a pulse [20]. In the case of this thesis, a 2.25 MHz transducer with
a single-cycle pulse was used, yielding an axial resolution of 700 µm at v = 3118.5 m/s, as
shown in subsection 4.8. Higher frequency transducers can achieve better axial resolution due
to their shorter wavelengths, but have lower penetration depths because the higher frequency
pulses carry less power [20]. The achievable resolution is highly dependent on the speed of
sound within the material and the overall signal attenuation. UT performs particularly well in
homogeneous materials, such as metals. In contrast, the inhomogeneous nature of composites
can lead to variations in the speed of sound throughout the material and lower data quality [21].
A composite will behave more like a homogeneous material if the thickness of the fiber layers
is significantly smaller than the axial resolution [21]. On the other hand, if the frequency of
the transducer is close to the resonant frequency of the composite, Smith et. al 2016 [15] found
that the instantaneous phase of pulse echo wave form increases 2π radians through each ply and
used this phenomenon to model ply drops and wrinkles in carbon fiber reinforced composites.
However, on an industrial scale, wrinkle identification and characterization in GFRP with UT

2.3.2
Terahertz Spectroscopy
Terahertz (THz) based NDT is a promising emerging technique that utilizes electromagnetic
waves with frequencies from 0.1 to 10 THz to investigate materials on the mm to sub-mm
spectral range [11][2]. While the first THz images were captured in the 1970s, development of
the technology stagnated until the turn of the century, when femtosecond lasers became more
readily available [23]. THz radiation is non-ionizing, meaning that it is not harmful to humans
or materials, and can easily penetrate most nonconductive materials, such as glass fiber, resin,
and balsa wood, making it an ideal candidate for wind turbine blade NTD [24]. To obtain a
measurement, a short terahertz pulse is sent from the transmitter into the material. A portion
of the wave will be reflected at each material interface with a change in refractive index (n),
as shown in Figure 4b. These reflections appear as peaks in the resulting waveform, with their
time-of-flight (TOF) delays used to determine the sample thickness, as well as the depth and
size of defects [25]. Because THz waves are able to propagate through both high and low-density
12
==PAGE:13==
materials, they can be used to detect and characterize multiple defects stacked on top of each
other, overcoming the shadowing effect found in UT [26].
THz systems are configured in either transmission or reflection modes.
In transmission
mode, the transmitter and receiver are placed in line on opposite sides of the part, and the
receiver collects the final transmitted signal. Transmission mode allows for much simpler signal
processing but is less practical for industry settings. It requires the sample to have a high enough
transmittance, the fraction of the wave that is not reflected or absorbed by the material, relative
to the thickness, for the signal to travel all the way through. The sample thickness must also
be known to determine the location of the damage [23].
In reflection mode, the transmitter and receiver are placed on the same side of the material,
and the reflected signal is collected. Reflection mode requires both a more complicated system
design and post-processing. However, it is independent of and can be used to calculate the
sample thickness [23]. A system that can be used on one side of the sample is often required
to inspect large, complex structures, making it more promising for large-scale NDT, especially
in industrial settings [23].

Most investigations employed THz-TDS systems from manufacturers such as TeraView or
TeraMetrix, typically in reflection-mode geometries. These setups often include material prop-
erty extraction, such as refractive index or absorption coefficient. A key limitation across much
of the literature is the use of thin samples—typically less than 2 mm thick—with a few notable
exceptions. For example, Liu et al. (2024) [22] examined 20–30 mm thick sandwich panels, and
Im et al. (2013) [25] studied a 35–85 mm thick section of a WTB trailing edge. Although defects
as small as 25 µm were sometimes detectable, resolution and signal quality were inconsistent.
The reported refractive index of GFRP varied slightly across studies, generally ranging from
2.1 to 2.38 depending on frequency. TOF thickness measurements were consistently successful,
particularly when supported by advanced signal processing, including wavelet transforms and
machine learning [33], [37]. While such techniques were outside the scope of this thesis, they
will be critical to the future application and development of THz-based NDT.
Dong et al. (2015) [24] directly compared THz-TDS and UT C-scans of a 1.85 mm thick
GFRP specimen containing simulated delaminations (via embedded Teflon films). Their com-

parison of lateral and axial resolution showed that THz outperformed UT in both metrics.
Operating at 1 THz, the THz system achieved lateral and axial resolutions of 0.3 mm and 45
µm, respectively, compared to 1.95 mm and 500 µm for UT at 5 MHz. They applied the 6 dB
drop, full-width half-maximum, method for defect sizing and also observed secondary peaks
associated with unidirectional ply interfaces. These results underscored the potential of THz
imaging as a high-resolution alternative to UT in GFRP inspection.
Han et al. (2018) [28] employed THz-TDS in both time- and frequency-domain imaging
modes to assess multilayer delaminations in a 0.8 mm thick, 4-ply plain weave GFRP compos-

generally yielded more information. While frequency-domain C-scans could reveal defects based
on phase or spectral magnitude, time-domain images offered greater clarity. Their frequency
slicing technique enabled spatial tuning, where lower frequencies enhanced visibility of large
features like delaminations, while higher frequencies resolved finer details such as fabric weave
patterns.
Nsengiyumva et al. (2023) [27] used a TeraView TPS 4000 system to characterize a three-
level delamination specimen. Delaminations were introduced using Teflon disks of varying crack
opening displacement.
By applying a solitary wavelet transform to analyze the data, they
determined the GFRP refractive index to be approximately 2.157 and achieved a delamination
crack opening measurement error of just under 4%, validating the precision of THz analysis for
defect characterization.
Liu et al. (2024) [22] investigated thick sandwich panels with artificial damage, including
cracks, delaminations, and porosity. They reported minimum detectable crack lengths of 3 mm
and delamination thicknesses of 1 mm. Although a 0.053 mm defect was faintly visible, the
signal-to-noise ratio was low, suggesting a lower detection limit for subtle damage remains a
challenge.
Only a few studies have directly addressed THz-based inspection of WTB materials. Martin
et al. (2018) [40] explored the use of terahertz inverse synthetic aperture radar (ISAR) for WTB
inspection, comparing it with thermography and X-ray to inspect delaminations, wrinkles,
inclusions, and high porosity regions. THz-ISAR was able to detect large, dry areas and high
porosity regions, but was unable to detect out-of-plane fiber waviness. However, this radar-
based technique was not optimized for NDT applications and suffered from limited penetration
and long acquisition times.
A more extensive body of work by Im et al. (2012–2019) systematically explored the ap-
plication of THz-TDS in WTB structures. Their 2012 study demonstrated THz’s ability to
overcome UT shadowing effects using a through-sensing configuration with the emitter and
receiver on opposite sides of the specimen [26]. In 2013, they repeated this experiment on an
actual WTB trailing edge with saw-cut defects [25]. By 2014, they had developed a method
to extract the refractive index of blade materials in reflectance mode, reporting a value of 2.19
for GFRP [38]. Finally, in 2019, they demonstrated that THz-TDS could penetrate up to 105
mm in transmission mode [29], confirming its potential for deeper inspections under optimal
conditions.
The existing body of research has firmly established THz-TDS as a viable method for de-
tecting delaminations, debonding, and porosity in GFRP composites and sandwich structures.
However, most work has been limited to thin samples and artificially introduced defects. There
remains a significant gap in the literature concerning the detection of complex flaws, such as
wrinkles and delaminations, in thicker and more realistic composite configurations. Despite the
clear potential of THz-based NDT methods, their adoption in large-scale, real-world structures
has been limited, primarily due to the size, cost, and complexity of conventional THz-TDS
systems. Although THz-CCS offers a more compact and cost-effective alternative, it has yet to
be applied to the inspection of composite materials. This Thesis aims to address these limita-

Methodology
To test the feasibility of using THz-CCS to detect subsurface damage at significant depths,
a series of experiments were conducted using two different sensor head configurations of the
GLAZE Technologies device, as shown in Figure 8. Initial investigations to determine whether
the THz beam could detect layers in a unidirectional composite sample and characterize a
simple crack utilized a prototype sensor head with a focused beam in an angled reflection setup,
typical for THz-CCS applications in other industries. Delamination cracks in 16.9 mm thick
unidirectional glass/epoxy double cantilever beam (DCB) specimens were evaluated. Scans of
the unloaded DCB specimen were compared to scans with a wedge inserted to keep the crack
open, allowing for direct comparison between closed and open crack states and between in situ
conditions and those typical of fracture mechanics testing. The depth of focus and time window
limitations of this setup were also evaluated.
Because the original sensor setup was not designed for thick specimens and demonstrated
limited depth of focus, a second sensor head with a collimated beam normal to the specimen
surface was developed and subsequently tested. This configuration was used to re-examine the
DCB specimens and to assess additional damage types, including wrinkles and sandwich core
features.
Pulse-echo ultrasonic testing scans of the DCB and wrinkle specimens were conducted for
comparison, providing a benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of THz-CCS.
The measurement devices and analysis techniques used in this thesis are outlined in the
following subsections. The two THz systems are described in detail in subsection 4.1, followed
by the data processing and cleaning methods used for the V1 sensor head in subsection 4.2.
The approach for locating the crack tip (or any key specimen feature) within the THz sensor
reference frame is explained in subsection 5.4. The windowing-based peak detection method
developed to isolate features in the THz B-scans is presented in subsection 4.6. Finally, subsec-
tion 4.8 describes the ultrasonic device used for comparison scans and outlines the corresponding
time-of-flight calculations.
4.1
GLAZE THz-CCS device
THz measurements were performed using the Carmen THz Cross Correlation Spectroscopy
platform from GLAZE Technologies. It utilized 2 THz bandwidth with 70 dB peak dynamic
range at 100 GHz in transmission mode. All scans were taken with 1200 scanning points per
pulse, with a 0.6 s acquisition time per pulse. Two different reflection sensor head configurations,
V0 with a 30 degree angle of incidence and V1 with a 0 degree angle of incidence, were employed
throughout the thesis.
The full-width half-maximum time duration of the pulse, shown in

purposes of this study, all measurements in the sensor coordinate system will be expressed in

4.2.1
Background noise subtraction and time delay stretching error correction
At all V1 sensor head heights, a consistent background noise waveform is present. The waveform
can be easily removed by taking a reference scan >30 mm away from the edge of the sample
in u or v at the same sensor head height in w. This ensures that no additional signal from
the sample is present in the scan and that the reference noise matches the background noise
in the measurements. Figure 10 shows the background noise wave forms at w = 62 and 81,
in gray and blue respectively. The difference between the two waveforms is shown in orange.
While the noise maintains the same general shape and amplitude range, there is a sufficient
difference that subtracting noise from a different sensor head height will decrease the quality
of the results.
The sensor also has an inconsistent linear time delay error, causing the recorded time spacing
between the data points to either stretch or contract depending on the scan. According to
GLAZE technologies the delay is likely due to changes in temperature causing the optical
fibers in the optical delay unite, the mechanism governing the delay between the time the pulse
is emitted and the time the receiver begins recording data, to stretch slightly more or less than
the device is calibrated for stretching or compressing the optical path. This type of error can
be identified by looking at the frequency of the water absorption lines in the frequency domain
[41]. THz radiation easily excites the hydrogen bonds in water molecules, causing the energy
of the radiation to be absorbed, resulting in noticeable dips in the spectral density at specific
frequencies often referred to as water absorption lines [42]. Depending on the scan, when the
raw dynamic range is plotted, the water absorption lines do not line up with the scientifically
accepted values of 0.5572 THz, 0.7523 THz, 0.9879 THz ext.[43]. To correct for this error in V1
scans, the ratio of the sensed to actual water absorption frequency was determined and used
to scale the time vector, ensuring that the water absorption lines in the resulting pulse were
properly aligned [41]. Scaling factors varied from 0.99 to 1.05. When the absorption lines were
not clearly visible, as was often the case in scans where the front reflection was not visible,
the correction calculated for the most recent scan or that sample taken on the same day was
applied. When that was not available, no correction was made, though it should be noted that

4.2.2
Sensor head alignment
Accurate alignment of the sensor head is critical to ensure reliable reflection measurements in
normal-incidence configurations. When the V1 time-domain THz system was initially installed,
the scan head was found to be tilted by approximately 1.5 degrees to the left along the u axis
(when facing the sensor). This slight angular misalignment significantly reduced the amplitude
of the front-surface reflection and degraded the overall signal quality. After identifying the issue,
the sensor head was realigned to be orthogonal to the scanning stage. Figure 12 compares
the V1 pulse characteristics before and after sensor alignment, shown in blue and orange,
respectively. Both pulses were taken at the same point on the metal sample stage with the
front peak aligned at approximately 20 ps. While the misaligned pulse appears visually less
noisy in the time domain, it exhibits a 48% reduction in peak amplitude, 278 arbitrary units
(A.U.) compared to 538 A.U. for the properly aligned case. The misalignment also causes a
significant decrease in bandwidth as shown in the spectral density plot.

4.3
Constructing longer time window visualizations
To build A- and B-scan images with a time window longer than the sensor’s built-in limit,
multiple scans were taken at different positions along the optical path by moving the sensor
closer to the sample surface in w and then stitched together. Each scan captured a different
part of the overall time window.
The non-overlapping sections of each scan were cut and
concatenated based on the time shift, ∆t, introduced by moving the sensor between scans.
This approach works fairly well, but it does result in an inconsistent sampling rate.
The size of this step in time was calculated for each scan based on the change in w relative
to the first scan, using Equation 1, and added to the time vector. This generated a list of
scans aligned in time. Non-overlapping sections of each pulse were sampled using the PyGlaze
cut function. The cut function extracts a segment of a pulse between two specified times. It
works by identifying the indices in the pulse’s time array that correspond to the specified start
and end times of the desired interval. It then slices both the time and corresponding signal
arrays to isolate the data within that time window. The result is a new pulse that contains
only the portion of the original signal between the given times. These pulses could then be
easily concatenated to form one longer pulse.
In the special case of constructing the V1 pulse on metal discussed in subsection 4.4, where
the step size was 0.2 mm, the cut function was not used. Instead, ∆t was assumed to represent
the non-overlapping position of the data. To convert the time shift into a number of data
points, nsamples, ∆t was divided by the sampling interval and rounded to the nearest integer. A
central target time, in this case 50 ps, was then chosen, and the index closest to this time in
each scan was identified. A near symmetric range of data points was then selected from each
pulse, centered around this index, extending ±nsamples/2. If nsamples was not an even number,
the first index was rounded down and the second rounded up. For the first and last scans in
the sequence, the time not covered by the other scans was included in the final pulse. The
time shift ∆t was then added to the sample time vector. These time-adjusted segments were
concatenated to reconstruct the full pulse, and the final result was visually compared to the
original individual pulses to confirm proper alignment. This ensured that every cut decision
was made consistently for the very small sample sizes involved.

4.5
Crack tip location
To relate the THz scan data to the physical specimen and assess the ability of the THz device to
sense the crack in the DCB specimen, the location of the crack tip in the sensor reference frame
must be determined. As shown in Figure 15, the edge of a thin metal sheet, approximately 1.5
mm thick, was aligned with the crack side of the crack tip. Then, the sensor head was moved
from the undamaged section towards the metal plate until it began to show in the A-scan.
The location was then recorded when the edge of the spot came into contact with the plate.
The process was then repeated, aligning the metal plate with the opposite edge of the crack
tip and approaching from the damaged side. The average of the two points was then found
and used as the approximate crack tip location. The crack tip location was used to convert
the position coordinates from sensor uvw coordinates to the DCB xyz coordinates using the
following formula: y = vcrack−tip −v + ycrack−tip. This strategy was also employed to locate
features in other specimens.

4.6
Windowing peak detection method
To isolate and quantify the location and amplitude of features, such as crack or material inter-
face reflections, within the B-scan data, a windowing peak detection method was developed. If
the dimensions of the specimen are known, the expected TOF between the front reflection and
the feature was calculated and compared to the TOF of any prominent reflections within the
B-scan. A time window width relative to the calculated TOF that would fully encapsulate the
reflection was then determined. The GLAZE Python library’s cut function was used to isolate
the time window. For each A-scan along the B-scan path (i.e., across the DCB specimen’s
length), the minimum amplitude, the maximum amplitude, the zero crossing or point between
the minimum and maximum where the signal amplitude equals zero, and their corresponding
time of flight were identified. These values were then overlaid onto the original B-scan. The
value that best captured the feature geometry, usually the minimum amplitude, was then se-
lected and used to calculate the TOF if the front reflection was available or further analyzed
in different ways.

UT device
Figure 16: HFUS 2000 pulse-echo ultrasonic imaging system with coordinate system labeled.
A high-frequency HFUS 2000 single probe pulse-echo water bath immersion ultrasonic imag-
ing system (Ingenieurb¨uro Dr. Hillger, Germany), shown in Figure 16, was used to scan the
DCB1 and wrinkle specimens. The system operates over a frequency range of 0.5 to 80 MHz
and includes an X, Y, Z stepper-motor scanning system with a spatial resolution of 0.01 mm.
The water tank dimensions are 1000 mm × 700 mm × 500 mm.
A 2.25 MHz transducer was selected to achieve sufficient penetration and enable clear de-
tection of back-wall reflections in the relatively thick GFRP samples. The system has one
cycle per pulse, meaning that the spatial resolution is approximately equal to 0.67 mm. All
ultrasonic scans were conducted with a 0.5 mm step size, consistent with the step size used for
corresponding scans acquired using the V1 THz system. During scanning, specimens were sub-
merged in the water bath and supported on coins to prevent direct contact with the mirrored
specimen stage at the bottom of the tank.
The system collects both A-scan and C-scan ultrasonic data and processes signals using
three operator-defined gates. Gate 1, shown in red in the A-scan images, typically captures
the front surface or interface echo; Gate 2, shown in blue, generally spans the region between
the front and back-wall echoes; and Gate 3, shown in yellow, is usually used to define the
back-wall echo. Gate 1 is used to define the top surface of the specimen based on the TOF
of the maximum amplitude peak corresponding to the interface echo within the gate. Gates
2 and 3 are positioned relative to the front surface echo identified by Gate 1, allowing for
consistent spatial referencing across all measurements. The start and end times for each gate
are determined manually by the operator. Within each gate, the system records either the
maximum or minimum amplitude value and the TOF of any peak that exceeds a defined
amplitude threshold. These TOF values can be used to estimate the location of damage or to
30
==PAGE:31==
calculate the material thickness, provided that a clear signal is present above the background
noise. It is important to note that the system does not provide access to raw amplitude and
phase data. Instead, it outputs A-scan and C-scan images. The C-scans can display amplitude
values from individual gates or TOF values calculated between Gate 1 and either Gate 2 or
Gate 3, or between the transducer signal and Gate 1.
4.8.1
UT time of flight calculations
In UT, the TOF is the time interval between the emission of an ultrasonic pulse and the
detection of a corresponding echo.
The TOF is directly related to the distance the pulse
travels through the material, under the assumption of a constant sound velocity. For through-
transmission or pulse-echo configurations, this allows for the calculation of either the thickness
of the specimen or the depth of a reflecting interface, such as a defect or a material boundary.
The distance d is calculated using the formula,
d = v · TOF

Accurate TOF extraction requires a well-defined peak exceeding a specified amplitude threshold
to distinguish it from background noise.

Results: Delamination Crack
This section presents the use of the V0 THz sensor head to detect delamination cracks in double
cantilevered beam (DCB) specimens, with results compared to V1 sensor head and ultrasonic
(UT) scans of the same samples. The specimens are introduced in subsection 5.1, followed by
a comparison between measured and analytical crack opening displacements for DCB1 with
a 1.5 mm hex wrench inserted at y =20 mm in subsection 5.2. Scanning parameters for the
V0 device are detailed in subsection 5.3. A comparison of V0 scans of the open and closed
DCB specimen is provided in subsection 5.4, and the effect of sensor head height sensitivity is
discussed in subsection 5.5. Differences in scan quality across the three DCB specimens and
the influence of raster step size are examined in subsection 5.6. Finally, comparative results
using the V1 sensor head are discussed in subsection 5.7, and UT system scans are presented

5.1
Double cantilevered beam specimens
DCB specimens are used to assess the THz-CCS device’s ability to detect simple delamination
cracks. DCBs are a common configuration to study fracture mechanic properties such as de-
lamination cracks in composite materials [45]. Once a DCB specimen has been used to study
crack growth, it is left with a sufficiently large crack that can be opened or closed to change the
width of the damage. The crack spans the entire width of the specimen, allowing the damage
to be sufficiently characterized using a line scan or B-scan showing an amplitude heat map of
depth vs distance traveled along the specimen [46].
Three DCB specimens, DCB1, DCB2, and DCB3, were used in this study, though DCB1,
pictured in Figure 17, was used for the majority of the scans. y = 0 is located at the end of the
slip foil, 70 mm from the left edge of the specimen. The DCB specimens were initially used to
study mixed-mode cohesion laws by Erives et al. 2023 [45]. They are 500 mm long, 30 mm wide,
and 16.9 mm thick. The crack is located at the geometric mid-plane, approximately 8.45 mm
through the thickness. DCB specimens 1, 2, and 3 had a crack lengths of 165.5 mm, 203 mm,
and 163 mm, respectively. The DCB specimens were cut parallel to the fiber direction from
a 20-ply unidirectional stitched glass fiber epoxy composite plate. E-1182 Saertex glass fabric
with the backing material facing downwards for all layers was used with a Hexion RIMR035c
epoxy matrix material.
While the composite is near-symmetric, the stitching of the fibers
(roughly perpendicular to the fibers) causes an asymmetric interface at the mid-plane [45].
Table 4 presents the expected TOF values for the crack and back-wall reflections when using
the V0 and V1 sensor heads. For the V1 system, the expected TOFs, 121.8 ps for the crack
reflection and 243.5 ps for the back reflection, coincide with the first and second artifacts of the
incident pulse. As a result, these reflections are obscured in the V1 data, preventing reliable
extraction of the actual TOF values. Consequently, these DCB specimens are not suitable for
evaluating the performance of the V1 sensor head.

Crack Length and Opening Displacement
The crack in the DCB specimen was opened with a 1.5 mm hex wrench inserted at y =

The crack

To account for uncertain edges caused by fiber bridging, all measurements were taken with a
tolerance of ±1 mm around each 10 mm marking. A third-order polynomial was then fit to the
average displacement at each point in Excel to estimate the displacement at the intermediate
points.
A third-order polynomial fit was chosen to mimic the deflection of a beam with a
point end-load. The polynomial coefficients are: A = 4.3822 × 10−7, B = −7.45075 × 10−5,
C = −0.01193, and D = 2.039 with an R2 value of 0.998. Figure 18, shows a comparison of

focus issue caused by the angled reflectance configuration of the V0 THz device, discussed in
subsection 4.1. The V0 sensor head captures the reflected pulse after a time delay corresponding
to the time it would take the pulse to travel from the transmitter to the sample surface and
back to the receiver in the default configuration. Because the refractive index of GFRP is much
higher than that of air, the THz pulse travels through it much more slowly. When the sensor
head is significantly closer to the part than the design configuration, the time it takes the pulse
to travel from the transmitter to the receiver increases, causing the device to capture a portion
of the pulse that is out of focus, meaning that the amplitude of any reflections present in the
time window are smaller and features are harder to identify. The V1 GLAZE device, discussed
in subsubsection 4.1.2, was designed utilizing a normal incidence configuration to address this
issue.

5.9
Summary of delamination crack detection
The V0 device detected open cracks with a minimum opening displacement of 0.07–0.05 mm,
extending up to 20 mm behind of the crack tip. In contrast, closed cracks were detectable up
to 23 mm, suggesting an even smaller actual detection limit. Delaminations were identifiable
to a depth of 9 mm, but the back surface at 17 mm was not clearly resolved.
The DCB sample was not suitable for scanning with the V1 sensor head because the TOF
and the crack and back reflection overlapped with artifacts 1 and 2, making them difficult or
impossible to distinguish. The V1 device was able to track an open crack to 17 mm before
the crack tip, but this is likely not indicative of the true capabilities of the V1 device for
delamination crack detection, and further experiments with more suitable samples are necessary.
The UT device was able to identify the delamination and back echoes. The speed of sound
within the sample was calculated to be V = 3118.5 m/s based on Equation 3. The depth of
the crack was calculated to be between 7.94-8.06 mm using the recorded TOF data from gate
2.
The UT system can complete a 100 × 100 mm C-scan at a 1 mm step size in 15 minutes
(0.09 s per point). In contrast, the THz-CCS V0 required 4.45 minutes for 160 points (1.7
s/point), while V1 needed 10.4 minutes for 146 points (4.8 s/point). The discrepancy between
the V0 and V1 Acquisition times arose from the XYZ scanning stages.

Wrinkle tracing
As a first step towards identifying wrinkle shapes within the specimen, the shape of the wrinkle
on the top surface of the part was traced, and the amplitude, wavelength, and critical angle
were determined. The spot size of this machine is significantly larger than that of the previous
machine, resulting in reduced lateral resolution. Using the V1 device to measure the surface

7.1
8-ply Laminate
The 8-ply unidirectional fiber laminate sample, shown in Figure 31c, was chosen to investigate
the effect of the individual laminate layers on the THz reflection data.
The peaks of the
background noise in the delamination crack scans with the v0 device were larger and more
regular than expected. This raised the question of whether the lamina of the composite could
be contributing to the noise amplitude. The sample was previously used for a shear test and
failed at the grip. It consists of 8 90-degree UD plies. The sample measures approximately 200
mm in length, 25 mm in width, and 4.5 mm in thickness, with an average ply thickness of 0.56
mm. A strain gauge is secured in the center of the sample, 50 mm from each of the grips. To
ensure that the metal strain gauge did not interfere with the B-scan data, it was covered with
electrical tape, leaving a 47 mm by 25 mm section of the sample available for scanning near
the undamaged grip. This area was deemed too small to be scanned with the V1 device.
Figure 31 shows a B-scan, an A-scan, and a picture of the 8-ply UD sample. The B-scan
was taken with the fiber direction parallel to the scanning direction and to the 6 mm dimension
of the spot size. This ensures that the spot overlaps with the maximum possible number of
fiber bundles for each scan, providing stronger reflections from each layer. The B-scan, shown
in Figure 31a, spans from v = 35 to 65 mm. The spot slightly overlaps with the grips from v =
35 to 40 mm, and with the tape from v = 62 to 65 mm. The slight waviness of the back echo,
dipping lower from v = 46 to 53 mm, appears to mirror the back surface of the part. There are
8 peaks, possibly corresponding to the 8 plys, between the peak of the front reflection and the
valley of the back reflection. Figure 31b Shows normalized A-scans and magnitude spectrum
of the pulse on the metal sample stage, shown in blue, and the 8-ply specimen at v = 49 mm,
shown in orange. The water absorption lines were not sufficiently developed to apply a time
shift correction. While the frequency is very similar to the incident pulse, the 8 peaks between
the front and back reflection have much higher relative amplitudes. Figure 31c shows a picture
of the side of the specimen in the scanning area.
The ply layer thickness, 0.56 mm, is very similar to the half wavelength of the THz pulse
in GFRP, 0.6 mm. While further analysis of this phenomenon was outside the scope of this
project, it suggests that THz could be used to observe ply-level details such as fiber waviness
and wrinkling. N. Palka et.al 2014 [47] and 2015 [31] found that they could accurately model
and measure the thickness and ply orientation of ultra-high molecular weight polyethylene
composites using THz-TDS measurements and the Fresnel equations. This avenue could be
worth pursuing further in future research.

for inspecting sandwich composites.
The relatively low refractive index of the foam allows
THz pulses to travel faster through the core, enabling deeper penetration and more reliable
detection of interfaces than in solid GFRP plates. This faster propagation, combined with
the dielectric contrast at interfaces, makes THz particularly well-suited for inspecting thick or
complex sandwich panels where UT may fail.

Discussion
To be competitive for wind turbine blade applications, THz-based NDT needs to be safe, cost-
effective, fast enough to allow inspections to be conducted quickly, light enough that it can be
carried out on a WTB by a technician or drone during inspections, and capable of accurately
determining defect location and depth in thick composite and sandwich structures. UT can
meet many of these criteria and has been specialized into many forms to fill different niches in
inspection and manufacturing quality control over the last decades [11]. However, UT methods
struggle to characterize layered damages, locate near-surface defects, or penetrate thick GFRP
composites and sandwich structures found in the near-root and maximum chord sections of
wind turbine blades [21].
THz may offer a valuable alternative technology to UT for NDT in wind turbine blades.
THz and UT NDT methods work based on very similar principles, especially pulse-echo UT and
reflection-mode THz NDT, but are based on different physical phenomena [4]. Because THz
operates based on much higher frequency electromagnetic radiation, compared to the lower
frequency elastic waves in UT, THz-based NDT methods have been demonstrated to have
higher axial resolution and penetration depth in non-conductive materials [24]. THz is also
able to overcome the shadowing affect and characterize layered damages in GFRP composites
[28].
THz-based NDT is particularly well suited to detecting damage that propagates in the
plane parallel to the surface of the structure and perpendicular to the beam path, such as
delamination and debonding cracks in WTB blade spar caps, adhesive joints, and between
the skin and core of sandwich panels. In this orientation, the full width of the beam will be
reflected off the damage, providing a much stronger signal. THz-based NDT also shows great
potential for quality control applications. THz-TDS has been successfully employed to detect
voids, determine the fiber volume fraction, and monitor curing in a laboratory setting [4], [49].
In this thesis, the results from both the V0 and V1 systems demonstrate that THz-CCS has
strong potential as a quality control and inspection technique for thick composite structures,
such as wind turbine blades. The V0 system, although limited in focus deeper within speci-
mens, was able to detect both open and closed cracks with nearly equal resolution, suggesting
sensitivity to subtle internal features, possibly even fiber bridging. These cracks were reliably
located by tracking the minimum peak value in the signal, suggesting strong compatibility with
future machine learning-based detection methods.
Additionally, the V0 system appeared to resolve individual plies in a thin 8-ply unidirec-
tional laminate, highlighting its potential for ply-level assessment and wrinkle detection. From
a theoretical standpoint, THz technology is particularly well suited for characterizing unidi-
rectional glass fiber composites. Typical ply thicknesses range from 0.125 to 0.30 mm, while
the axial resolution of the THz-CCS system is approximately 0.07 mm [8]. These capabilities
open a path to detailed, non-contact inspection of internal composite structure, with a level of
resolution and accessibility that traditional NDT techniques struggle to match.
The V1 system was developed to overcome the limited depth of focus observed in the V0
configuration, and it successfully demonstrated enhanced penetration and thickness measure-
ment capabilities. It was able to detect the back reflection of a 12.5 mm thick wrinkle specimen
and potentially resolve both top and bottom skin layers in 20 mm thick sandwich panels. De-
spite a reduction in lateral resolution due to the larger spot size of the collimated beam, the
V1 system still performed well in near-surface inspection and accurately reproduced the surface
geometry of the wrinkle specimen. These improvements suggest that, unless limited by exces-
sive absorption or signal noise, the V1 system should be capable of detecting more complex
subsurface features, such as layered delaminations, in thick composite structures.
THz has added potential for better penetration in sandwich structures due to the decreased
60
==PAGE:61==
TOF of lower refractive index core materials.
While the refractive index of common core
materials varies widely, with a value of 1.8 for balsa wood [29] and values between 1 and 1.5
for polymer foams [48][22], the refractive index of the core material is usually under 1.6, which
is significantly smaller than that of GFRP. This means that thicker sandwich structures can
be imaged faster than pure composite structures of the same thickness. Assuming that the
absorption coefficients of the core materials are not prohibitively high, the penetration depth
in sandwich structures should be higher than in classical composites. However, if the refractive
index of the core material is too low, it may be difficult to identify damage within the core
material itself.
Beyond composite laminate inspection, THz-based systems should be investigated in other
areas of wind turbine blade quality control, particularly in near-surface coating damage de-
tection and thickness measurement, areas where UT often struggles [21]. Because UT has a
longer wavelength and often multiple cycles in the incident pulse, some systems struggle to
locate near-surface defects. For example, if the 2.25 MHz transducer used in this thesis had
a two-cycle rather than a single-cycle pulse, it would likely not have been able to detect the
wrinkle fold. In contrast, considerable research has demonstrated THz’s capability to measure
coating thickness both during and after application [30], [32]. This thesis successfully detected
a fold-type wrinkle in a coupon sample, a subsurface defect that is hard for UT to detect.
The THz device not only captured the surface displacement caused by the wrinkle but also
extracted the maximum wrinkle angle to within 1° of a reference optical microscope measure-
ment. This validates the feasibility of using THz to trace geometric features, even in specimens
with complex internal fiber architectures such as iso-phase wrinkles. While current hardware
and software limitations still restrict full volumetric interpretation, the strong correlation be-
tween THz results and optical validation suggests that the methodology is scalable, providing
a compelling foundation for future system development.
Because they work off similar principles, both UT and THz are likely to struggle in identi-
fying cracks parallel to the beam path, as they do not present a clear surface for the pulse to
reflect off. In wind turbine blades, this type of crack often occurs at the trailing edge or near
the bolts in the root section [50]. The metal bolts that attach the blade to the rotor in the
root section also pose a potential challenge for THz-based NDT in this area, as THz radiation
is unable to penetrate conductive materials like metal, and the high-contrast reflections from
the bolts could potentially overpower and obscure smaller damage reflections. This could be
more or less of an issue depending on the relative size of the THz device’s spot size to the
gap between the bolts. Most severe cracks in the plane parallel to the beam path are visible
from the surface and do not require NDT techniques to detect [51]. Even in a purely glass
fiber-reinforced blade, any metal component, such as the lightning protection system or sensor
embedded within the blade, will cause high-contrast reflections and obscure the surrounding
data.
THz-CCS, while offering many of the theoretical advantages associated with THz-TDS sys-
tems, presents several added practical benefits that make it particularly attractive for field
deployment. Unlike TDS systems, THz-CCS devices do not rely on bulky and expensive fem-
tosecond lasers, making them significantly lighter, more compact, and cost-effective. Although
the exact weight of the GLAZE THz-CCS system is not specified, the Carmen control unit and
V1 sensor head were easily handled by a single researcher when detached from the scanning
stage, suggesting sufficient portability for use directly on wind turbine blades. However, sub-
stantial engineering refinement of the sensor housing and scanning mechanism is still needed
before practical on-blade inspections can be realized. In particular, maintaining normal align-
ment between the sensor head and the curved surface of the blade is critical for reliable mea-
surements. This alignment challenge is most pronounced in high-curvature regions—such as
near the blade root or maximum chord—where defects are most likely to occur. While this
61
==PAGE:62==
may present difficulties for small or mid-sized blades, larger blades tend to have gentler cur-
vature over a given inspection area, which could help mitigate alignment constraints in future
applications.
The biggest challenge for THz-based NDT of wind turbine blades, aside from the need for
continuing technological improvements, is the increasing use of carbon fiber or hybrid glass
carbon fiber composites in large wind turbine blades. Mishnaevsky et.al 2017 [5], reported
that many leading blade manufacturers, including Vestas and Siemens, have begun employing
carbon fiber in the spar caps of some of their longer blade models, and that LM Wind used
hybrid glass carbon composites in their 88.4 m long blade. This is particularly concerning for
the widespread adoption of THz-based NDT in the wind industry because carbon fibers are
used to increase the strength in high-load components of wind turbine blades, such as the spar
caps, where delamination cracks that would otherwise be good candidates for THz detection
are usually located.
8.1
Limitations
8.1.1
Measurement Precision
Thickness measurements from both the USB microscope and the THz system in their current
forms suffer from imprecision.
The USB microscope experienced calibration issues at high
magnification, and the THz measurements were limited by incomplete pulses and background
noise. More reliable calibration procedures and improved pulse fidelity would enhance overall
measurement accuracy.
8.1.2
Specimens
The DCB specimens used were more complex than typical delamination models. They featured
tapered crack openings and fiber bridging, in contrast to the thin, uniform Teflon inserts com-
monly used in the literature. Additionally, in the V1 system, the crack reflections unfortunately
coincided with pulse artifacts, obscuring meaningful data.
The core materials of both sandwich specimens are unknown, making it difficult to defini-
tively determine whether the far-side skins are actually being detected or if some unrelated
signal is masquerading as the far-side skin.
The wrinkle specimen used had a width approximately equal to the spot size of the V1 head,
which follows a parabolic intensity distribution. This led to edge effects that likely degraded
signal quality. The necessity for precise centering further increased variability and slowed setup.
However, it was the only available pre-made wrinkle specimen, and still yielded usable geometric
data.
8.1.3
THz Device Constraints
For most of the project, access was limited to only one system at a time. When the V1 system
was installed, the V0 device was decommissioned, preventing any repeated scans or direct
comparisons between systems on new samples.
The most significant shared limitation between both systems was the short acquisition time
window. Many advanced signal processing techniques assume the full reflected pulse is captured,
including the front reflection and clear zero signal boundaries. This was not the case. As a
result, multiple scans at different sensor head heights were stitched together to approximate
full pulses, introducing significant uncertainty. This made even basic measurements, such as
thickness, far more complex.
62
==PAGE:63==
Because front reflections were often missing, standard normalization procedures for A-scans
could not be applied. Spectral analysis was also compromised, and techniques like deconvolution
were rendered infeasible due to the incomplete data. To handle large components such as wind
turbine blades, future systems should allow acquisition windows up to 700 ps (or 50 mm in
GFRP).
In addition, neither V0 nor V1 had a beam targeting mechanism. Locating features required
placing and then removing metal reference markers, which slowed setup and reduced repeata-
bility. Integrating a simple laser-based targeting system would significantly improve usability
and consistency.
Finally, the V1 system lacks onboard background subtraction, making it impossible to
interpret A- or B-scan data directly. All signal cleaning had to be done externally. Adding this
functionality to the scanning software would enable real-time feedback and drastically improve
efficiency.
8.2
Next Steps
8.2.1
Device Improvements
A V2 system should prioritize the following: 1. A longer, adjustable acquisition window. 2.
Improved background subtraction built into the software. 3. A laser-based beam targeting
mechanism to aid alignment. 4. Enhanced sensor head resolution and pulse clarity to reduce
artifacts.
Together, these would eliminate many of the current barriers to advanced processing and
allow more detailed characterization of subsurface features.
8.2.2
Controlled Experiments on Crack Width
To better understand the limits of THz resolution for delamination thickness, a controlled study
varying air-gap sizes should be conducted. As demonstrated in other work [34], systematically
tracking how overlapping reflections evolve with decreasing TOF would help benchmark system
capabilities. This could also provide training data for future machine learning models.
8.2.3
Angular Tolerance Characterization
Another critical next step is to quantify the sensitivity of the normal incidence sensor head
to surface misalignment. By varying the incident angle between the sensor and the specimen,
it would be possible to measure the impact on amplitude and clarity, helping define angular
tolerances for real-world inspection scenarios.
8.2.4
Subsurface Wrinkle Tracking
Once signal artifacts are minimized, the surface-tracing methodology validated in this thesis
should be extended to subsurface wrinkles. Given that the extracted wrinkle angle matched
optical measurements within 1°, applying the same approach deeper into the laminate could
provide valuable geometric information.

9
Conclusion
This thesis set out to evaluate whether terahertz cross-correlation spectroscopy can serve as
a competitive alternative to current state-of-the-art nondestructive testing techniques, such as
ultrasonic testing, for detecting common wind turbine blade failure modes. The results in-
dicate that while the GLAZE THz-CCS device is not yet ready for industrial deployment, it
demonstrates significant potential to compete with current methods in the future. The tech-
nology remains in an early stage of development, with several critical limitations in speed,
data quality, depth penetration, and sensitivity to surface geometry. Nevertheless, its demon-
strated capabilities for detecting subsurface defects make it a promising candidate for further
refinement.
Performance for Delamination Cracks (Question a): The GLAZE THz-CCS device
showed the ability to detect delamination cracks under controlled laboratory conditions. Based
on experiments with double cantilever beam specimens, the smallest detectable crack length
was approximately 40 mm, corresponding to cracks being detectable up to 20 mm behind
of the physical crack tip.
While these specimens were highly complex and contained fiber
bridging, which complicates detection, they more closely resemble in-service cracks than the
simpler samples used in prior studies. This suggests that real-world cracks may present similar
challenges in accurately locating their edges.
The minimum detectable crack opening displacement for an open crack was between 0.07
mm and 0.05 mm, 20 mm behind of the crack tip. Closed cracks were traceable to nearly
the same location, 23 mm behind the crack tip, indicating that the true minimum detectable
displacement may be even smaller. In terms of depth penetration, the V0 prototype was able to
detect delaminations at a depth of approximately 9 mm, but was unable to clearly distinguish
the back of the specimen at a depth of 17 mm. In contrast, the V1 device showed no definitive
limit during testing on other samples but was unable to precisely identify the location of the
crack or the back reflection in the DCB samples because they overlapped with large artifacts
in the incident THz pulse and were partially obscured. The back surface of a 12.5 mm-thick
composite was identified, and observations suggest the system could potentially detect the back
surface of a 20 mm-thick sandwich panel, though this remains inconclusive. Depth accuracy
was within 1 mm when the front reflection was visible, but locating depth became significantly
more challenging when the front reflection was absent.
Surface curvature and uneven sensor alignment had a significant impact on data quality. In
the V1 device, any deviation from normal incidence causes beam misalignment, which reduces
or eliminates signal return. This highlights the necessity for precise positioning and sensor
design enhancements for real-world applications. Regarding visual identification, open cracks
were relatively easy to identify in the V0 system by restricting the B-scan amplitude range
to exclude the front reflection. Closed cracks resulted in lower-amplitude reflections that were
more difficult to detect visually. Visualization was also strongly influenced by the color map
used; rainbow color maps enhanced defect detection, while grayscale plots avoided misleading
artifacts. In comparison, commercial UT systems commonly use rainbow color mapping for
this reason.
Performance for Sandwich Panels (Question b): THz-CCS successfully measured the
thickness of the top skin of sandwich panels within 0.02 mm of the microscope value, demon-
strating high dimensional accuracy. While preliminary results suggest the system can penetrate
different core materials, these findings remain inconclusive because a critical experiment, scan-
ning with the specimen resting on a reflective metal plate, was not conducted due to time
constraints. Future work is needed to confirm this capability under controlled conditions.
Performance for Wrinkles (Question c): The GLAZE THz-CCS device effectively
identified the origin of a near-surface folded wrinkle and tracked the surface displacement. It was
64
==PAGE:65==
also able to measure the wrinkle angle at the surface within 1 degree of physical measurements.
Scans of an 8-ply UD laminate sample suggest the potential for extracting ply-level orientation
data from THz scans in the future. These capabilities are significant for detecting manufacturing
defects in composite laminates, where wrinkles can compromise structural performance.
Comparison with Other NDT Methods (Question d): When compared with UT and
microscopy, THz-CCS remains significantly slower and less practical for large-scale inspections.
The single-probe water immersion UT system is capable of completing a 100 × 100 mm C-scan
at a 1 mm step size in approximately 15 minutes, with an acquisition time of 0.09 seconds per
point, due to its high degree of technological maturity. In contrast, the THz-CCS V0 device
required 4.45 minutes for a 160-point B-scan, with each point taking 1.7 seconds, while the
V1 prototype took 10.4 minutes to complete a 146-point B-scan, with each point taking 4.8
seconds. The difference in the V0 and V1 scanning time was caused by a downgrade in scanning
stage quality, making it necessary to move more slowly between points. This stark difference
between the UT and THz acquisition times highlights the need for further improvement in the
scanning time of the GLAZE THz-CCS system to successfully transition from laboratory-based,
high-resolution inspections to rapid field assessments.
Overall Assessment and Future Outlook: THz-based NDT represents a highly promis-
ing and rapidly advancing technique, offering unique advantages over traditional methods like
ultrasonic testing (UT). With significantly higher theoretical spatial resolution and the ability
to image complex features such as wrinkles and multilayer delaminations—especially in sand-
wich structures—THz systems open new possibilities for detailed subsurface characterization
in composites. In particular, the V1 sensor demonstrated penetration depths comparable to,
and in some cases exceeding, UT performance, all while operating at higher frequencies that
offer superior resolution potential.
While current THz-CCS implementations, such as the GLAZE devices, are still under active
development and not yet fully competitive with established industrial NDT systems, they show
clear potential. The V0 sensor, optimized for angled reflection and thin coatings, performed well
in near-surface inspection, but the focused beam configuration severely limited the penetration
depth.
The V1 sensor, designed for deeper inspection with a collimated normal-incidence
beam, achieved greater penetration and revealed internal features previously inaccessible with
UT. However, it also introduced signal artifacts and required careful alignment, highlighting
areas for refinement. Moreover, the short time windows in both devices ( 8.5 mm in GFRP)
limited single-pass inspection depth, requiring multiple scans to fully resolve thicker specimens.
Despite these challenges, ongoing advancements in THz hardware and signal processing offer
a clear path forward. Improvements in scanning speed, penetration depth, artifact reduction,
and alignment robustness could make THz-CCS a highly valuable addition to the NDT toolkit.
With continued development, THz-CCS has the potential to become a powerful and comple-
mentary tool alongside UT, especially for inspecting thick, complex composite structures where
enhanced resolution and non-contact measurement are critical.
