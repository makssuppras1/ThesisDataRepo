Abstract 
In the automotive industry today the main challenges are the fuel consumption and the product cost; design 
and manufacturing, of vehicles. In recent years these issues have been addressed in a larger extent and 
several vehicle developers across the globe are today focusing on meeting the increasing demand for 
sustainable solutions. In current vehicle development more sustainable solutions are emerging by several 
measures such as designing more efficient and sustainable engines and reducing the weight of the vehicles 
by more efficient structural design. 
The current study was conducted at Volvo Bus Corporation in collaboration with FS Dynamics and the 
Technical University of Denmark and addresses the issue of reducing the weight of the load carrying 
structures of Volvo buses while securing the mechanical integrity, with the additional goal of streamlining 
the overall development process, by evaluating the use of optimization tools. A structural optimization tool 
using size optimization has been developed which uses ANSA [12] as pre-processor to set-up the finite 
element model and the optimization routine, MSC Nastran solution 200 [11] as optimizer and Meta Post 
[13] as post-processor. Support tools have been developed to support the set-up of the optimization 
routine such as ANSA script for selecting critical nodes/elements for node/element stress constraints and 
Matlab programs to generate stress responses and constraints for input to the optimization routine in MSC 
Nastran. The developed Size optimization tool is designed to minimize the weight of the structure by 
varying the thicknesses of the members using element or node stress, frequency and displacement 
constraints to limit the design space. The stress constraints are placed on individual element or node 
principal stresses, the frequency constraint is placed on the natural frequency of the structure and the 
displacement constraints are placed on node displacements. 
In the current paper the developed optimization tool is presented and the procedure for how to use it is 
described. The tool is tested by evaluating different design options on a test model and on full scale bus 
model. The following design options have been tested: element/node stress constraints, node selection, 
discrete variable optimization, sensitivity to initial design and optimization algorithm. Based on the results 
from the optimization analyses design option recommendations, when using the developed tool for 
optimization of vehicles, are outlined. 
During the testing it was found that the optimized design is sensitive to the initial design for large scale 
optimization problems which is assumed to relate to the non-convex original problem and the redundancy 
of the system. It was also found that the optimization routine is highly sensitive to the meshing near 
connections where the stress constraints are placed, the critical node selection and when discrete variable 
design cycles are applied. Due to these issues additional recommendations are outlined for the optimization 
routine set-up.  
The overall conclusion of the study is that the developed Size optimization tool provides a practical design 
tool for optimization of load carrying members of vehicle when a refined start guess is used as initial design 
and the provided recommendations and model considerations are considered in the optimization design. 
Keywords: Structural optimization, Size optimization, Bus body frame, Finite element analysis, Fatigue 
design, Dynamic design, Stress constraints, ANSA, MSC Nastran, Meta Post, Matlab.

1 Introduction 
In this chapter the current study is introduced and the aim and approach of the project are presented. The 
scope and limitations are also presented and finally the overall framework of the thesis is outlined. 
1.1 Background 
The main challenges for the automotive industry today are the fuel consumption and the product cost; 
design and manufacturing, of vehicles. In recent years these issues have been addressed in a larger extent 
and several vehicle developers across the globe are today focusing on meeting the increasing demand for 
sustainable solutions. In the transit industry these issues are critical due to the increase in public and 
political pressure for more efficient service with reduced environmental impact. 
In current vehicle development more sustainable solutions are emerging by several measures such as 
designing more efficient and sustainable engines and reducing the weight of the vehicles by more efficient 
structural design. 
The current study is executed in collaboration with Volvo Bus Corporation, one of the largest developers 
of buses worldwide and pioneers within vehicle sustainability with the vision of being “the world leader in 
sustainable transport systems” [1]. In the current study the issue of reducing weight of the vehicles is 
addressed, with the additional goal of streamlining the overall development process, by evaluating the use 
of optimization tools in the development process of Volvo buses. The weight reduction is focused on 
optimization of the load carrying structure of the vehicles. The streamlining of the overall development 
process is limited to the iterative development phase of the load carrying structure of the bus. The focus 
of the current project can be summarized as follows: 
 Weight reduction of load carrying structure 
 Streamline structural development process 
1.2 Structural Optimization Methods  
According to Christiensen and Klarbring [2] structural optimization can be described as follows. 
“Structural optimization is the subject of making an assemblage of materials sustain load in the best way” (Christiensen and 
Klarbring 2008) 
The concept of structural optimization could be related to designing a structure, e.g. a load carrying 
structure of a bus, in a way so that it sustains a given load in the best way. The “best” solution could be 
defined as the structure using the least possible amount of material, i.e. the lightest possible structure, but 
it could just as well be defined as designing a structure as stiff as possible, given a predefined amount of 
material and fixed design domain. 
As the current study aim at improving the sustainability of the buses by reducing the weight of the load 
carrying structure, the “best” solution in the context of this study corresponds to the lightest, given a 
series of predefined load cases with corresponding design criterions. 
In general the concept of structural optimization can be divided into the following classes [2]. 
 Topology optimization 
 Shape optimization 
 Size optimization

1.2.1 Topology optimization 
In topology optimization one seeks to find an optimal lay-out of a structure within a specified region by 
letting the connectivity of the structure be the variable. This can be achieved by e.g. letting the 
connectivity of nodes in a truss structure or the “density” of a set of elements, be the design variables 
which are allowed to take a value between a maximum limit and zero [3]. The optimal lay-out of the 
structure can be described by finding the optimal set of variables for sustaining the given loading. 
Topology optimization is usually performed according to the homogenization method developed by Bendsoe 
and Kikuchi [4]. 
1.2.2 Shape optimization 
In shape optimization the contour of the boundary of a specified region is the variable which is varied in 
order to find the optimal lay-out of the domain to withstand the given loading. In this optimization 
routine the topology (connectivity) of the structure is predefined while only the shape of the structure is 
varied. 
1.2.3 Size optimization 
In size optimization a given “size” e.g. the thickness or the cross-sectional area of a member is the 
variable which is varied in order to find the optimal lay-out of the structure to withstand the given 
loading. In this optimization both the connectivity of the structure and the contour of the boundaries are 
predefined. 
1.2.4 Evaluated optimization class 
The scope of the study has been limited to evaluate the optimization class; Size optimization. 
1.3 Aim and approach 
This project aims at evaluating the use of automatic optimization techniques in the development and 
design process of Volvo buses. These aims correspond to the focus of the current report presented in 
section 1.1 Background and are described as follows. 
 Aim 1: Reduce weight of load carrying structure 
 Aim 2: Streamline development process 
1.3.1 Aim 1: Reduce weight of load carrying structure 
In current development of load carrying structures of buses, weight reduction of components are mainly 
performed by the use of intuitive and experience based engineering knowledge. Even though this

approach has shown to be highly efficient and practical in the development of buses, it is also highly 
dependent on the knowledge of the individual designer/engineer. Several sources such as e.g. [5], [3] and 
[6] shows that the use of structural optimization tools in vehicle development can boost the structural 
design and lead to more efficient and sustainable solutions with improved product enhancements. 
In recent years several cases has also been presented where structural optimization tools have been used 
to improve the structural design within the Transit industry e.g. [7] and [8], as well as within the 
Automotive, Aero and Space industry e.g. [9] and [10]. 
1.3.2 Aim 2: Streamline development process 
In current bus development the preliminary bus design is produced by the design engineer. The design is 
then handed over to the strength analysis department for verification with respect to allowable stress, 
frequency, deflection criterions etc. If the current design does not fulfill the requirements, the design is 
sent back to the design engineer with suggestion on how to modify the design in order to meet the 
requirements. This intuitive iterative development process is often cumbersome, leading to increased cost 
due to the workload for the engineers, and does rarely lead to an optimal design. The current project aims at 
evaluating the usage of optimization tools in order to streamline this development process by adding the 
mathematical tool of optimization to the engineering toolbox of intuition. By implementing a practical 
and usable optimization tool some of the iterative loops could potentially be omitted by finding feasible 
solution which might be unintuitive or cumbersome to derive.

meant as supplemental design tool for the engineer and needs to be combined with practical and 
experienced based knowledge.

usability have been the main focus of the project. The theoretical foundation of the optimization tool has 
only been evaluated for design purposes.

In the current study fatigue design criterions for welded structures, based on an allowable stress range,

constraints have only been evaluated in the context of providing the additional options to the developed 
tool.

2.1 Fatigue design 
For structures that are exposed to dynamic loading, local time varying stresses and strains can cause 
cracks to form and propagate through the body of a structure [14]. This phenomenon is referred to as 
fatigue and can cause failure of the structure even for nominal stresses below the capacity of the material 
for static loading. The fatigue cracks are mainly formed at locations with high stress caused by geometric 
features. These locations are often referred to as “hot spots”.

3 Developed tool: Size Optimization 
In the current study a Size optimization tool has been developed. The purpose of the developed tool, 
how the tool works and the design options and limitations of the tool are described in the current 
chapter. 
The developed tool is designed to determine necessary thicknesses of components of an FE model which 
in optimization terms corresponds to a Size optimization tool. As the tool is meant to be used in the 
development process at Volvo buses, the options and limitations of the tool are tailored to suit current 
development procedures at Volvo buses. 
3.1 Example of usage 
In order to illustrate the capabilities of the Size optimization tool an example of usage of the given tool is 
presented as follows. 
Suppose an initial proposal of a frame model has been developed (see Figure 4, top figure). 
Design issue: Given the initial design, experience and static calculations indicate the need for improved 
mechanical integrity. In order to assure a suitable design, improvements needs to be made in order to 
meet the requirements. Using the developed tool, this can be done in two stages, presented as follows. 
Manual improvement: In the first step manual improvements is executed in order to improve the 
mechanical integrity of the structure. For the given frame additional crossbars and stiffeners in corners 
have been added (see Figure 4, mid figure). 
Automatic improvement (Size optimization tool): In the second stage the developed Size 
optimization tool can be used to calculate necessary thicknesses of added components and original parts 
(see Figure 4, bottom figure). 
 
Figure 4: Example of usage of developed Size optimization tool. 
As the developed tool only considers thicknesses of members as variables, the manual improvement stage 
is highly recommended before the optimization tool is applied in order to assure a good design.

3.2 Optimization Routine 
The optimization routine for the Size optimization tool is defined as finding the lightest structure, related 
to the thicknesses of the structural members, for which the node stresses at critical locations are lower 
than a predefined stress level. Natural frequency and displacement constraints are also added as additional 
constraint options. 
The optimization routine can in optimization terms be summarized as follows. 
Design objective: Minimize weight 
Design variables: Thicknesses of components (i.e. shell element thicknesses in the FE-model) 
Box constraints: Minimum and maximum thicknesses of components 
Constraints: 
 Maximum allowable stress 
 
Element stress 
 
Node stress: Compute-Average 
 
Node stress: Average-Compute 
 Minimum natural frequency  
 Maximum deflection. 
3.3 Model assumptions 
The optimization tool is developed with the following model assumptions: 
 Optimization solver: MSC Nastran, solution 200 [11] 
 Pre-processor: ANSA [12] 
 Post-processor: META [13] 
 Shell elements used in optimization routine: CQUADR and CTRIAR [27] 
 Analyzes: Linear static and modal analysis 
 Connections in structure are welded (related to stress constraints) 
Non-welded connections 
As the stress constraints in the developed optimization tool are based on design specifications for welded 
connections, connections between members which are not welded should be considered separately. For 
these connections alternative design criterions might be required.

 Closed section member (welded only on the outside e.g. a hollow rectangular beam): Node stress 
responses and constraints generated for top stresses only.

Once the FE-model from ANSA have been extracted, the stress constraints file generated and the 
Additional data file modified, the Template file need to be updated. In the Template file the ID‟s of the 
design objective and constraints should be set, the applied loading cases defined and the include files 
should be correctly referenced in the Bulk data. The Template file contains the overall information 
regarding the FE-analysis and includes the previously generated files in the Bulk data. 
A template for the Template file is provided in section D.2 Template file. How to modify the settings of the 
Template file is described in Annex 1: Tutorial – Size Optimization Tool. 
3.4.6 Run Optimization in MSC Nastran 
In order to run the optimization routine the Template file with the included files: FE-model, Stress 
constraints and Additional data should be sent to the MSC Nastran solver solution. 
3.4.6.1 No feasible solution 
In case the solver do not find a feasible optimal solution, but instead provides a best compromise infeasible 
design, a number of actions can be applied which potentially could solve this issue: 
 Visually validate the critical node/element selection in ANSA. This is always recommended in 
order to assure that the correct critical nodes/ elements have been selected. 
 Check if some of the design variables have reach their design constraint limits for the best 
compromise infeasible design. If so evaluate the box constraints placed on the given design variables 
and the constraint limits for the given members. 
 Divide structural members in separate PID‟s. If too many structural members are grouped in one 
PID, for which only one design variable defines the component thicknesses, problems have been 
==PAGE:45==
 
Structural Optimization of Volvo buses 
 
 
33 
 
experienced. A solution to this problem is to divide large group of structural members into 
separate PID‟s with individual design variables for respective thicknesses. 
 Check if any stress constraints are significantly violated in the best compromise infeasible design. If so 
the validity of the given nodes and the placed constraint limits should be evaluated. A script to 
ease the identification of violated (or active) constraints in the final design cycle has been 
developed and is presented in Appendix E: Script for reading Violated or Active constraints. 
3.4.7 Read results 
Once the optimization is performed the results can be read. 
In META the results can be imported according to standard procedures [13]. The stress states, 
eigenmodes, displacements and design variables (thicknesses) requested in the Output entries in the 
Template file can be viewed in the results. 
Additional results from the optimization analysis can be found in the .f06-file such as e.g. design variables 
for each cycle, objective and maximum constraint history, convergence criterions for each cycle, etc.. 
Additional results from the optimization analysis are available and can be printed on request [11]. How to 
request additional results and how to view, plot and interpret the results are thoroughly described in 
Annex 1: Tutorial – Size Optimization Tool. 
The execution time can found in the .f04 file. 
3.4.7.1 Validate results 
It is recommended that the design criterions are verified for the final design after the design optimization 
has been executed in accordance with standard design procedure. This is especially critical for sub-
optimization problems such as the current optimization analysis of the full floor structure in the full scale 
bus model. 
3.4.8 Import results back to ANSA 
In order to read the results back into ANSA the .pch file should be modified so that it only contains the 
results from the last design cycle. Once this is done the .pch file should be merged with the original 
ANSA model in ANSA with the property setting; keep-new. For more info see Annex 1: Tutorial – Size 
Optimization Tool.

6 Modeling considerations 
In this chapter modeling considerations based on the testing of the developed Size optimization tool on 
the test model and the full scale bus model are presented. 
6.1 Mesh dependency 
The stress criterions used in the developed design tool is based on either evaluating the element stress 
next to the intersections or the node stresses one element from the intersections. This means that the 
current stress criterions are highly mesh dependent and it is therefore crucial to verify the locations of the 
evaluated stress constraints in order to assure reliable results. 
6.1.1 Meshing near connections 
With respect to the mesh dependency of the stress design criterion it is crucial for the optimization to 
validate the mesh near connections in the model set-up. 
Considering the case of node stress constraints, using a standard free mixed meshing algorithm in ANSA 
[11] based on QUAD and TRIA elements. For this situation the corner nodes will be placed at an 
approximate distance from the intersections based on the desired element width. An example of critical 
nodes located one element from the intersections between members is presented in Figure 37. 
 
Figure 37: An example of critical nodes located one element from the intersections between members. 
As can be seen in Figure 37, the critical nodes (marked with red crosses) located on the corners of the 
diagonal bar and on the strait members are located approximately the distance;      , from the 
intersections based on the specified desired element width. The critical nodes on the diagonal bar which 
are not located on the corners of the member are not bounded by the algorithm to approximate the 
distance from the intersection nodes which means that the critical nodes might be placed with a large 
variety of distances from the intersection nodes. This could cause problems if the stress constraints are 
placed on critical nodes located closer or further away from the intersections than the design criterion 
specifies due to the high stress gradient in vicinity of the connections. If the critical node were to be 
placed closer to the intersection than the design criterion specifies this would cause the stress constraint 
to be too tough, while if the node were to be placed further away from to the intersection than the design 
criterion specifies it would be too loose.

6.2 Critical node selection 
Depending on the FE-modeling, the complexity of the structural system and the loading situation the 
final solution might be dependent on the critical node selection option and the critical nodes to use for a 
given design optimization problem should be considered for the individual case. 
6.2.1 Critical nodes on internal vs. external members 
If node stress constraints are placed on critical nodes located on external members one might experience 
that the solution do not converge due to the fact that node stresses on external members cannot be 
decreased to the desired limit as the thicknesses of the external members are not defined as a parametric 
variable. Based on current testing of the developed Size optimization tool the critical node selection 
options only considering critical nodes on internal members provided more robust alternatives which 
were better suited to find feasible designs. On the other hand the options lack the precision of evaluating 
external nodes in the vicinity of the design space which might be highly influenced by design changes and 
could potentially become critical during the optimization. Stress validation of node stresses at critical 
nodes on external members for the final design is crucial, especially when only considering critical nodes 
on internal members in the design optimization. 
6.2.2 Corner nodes 
Based on the FE-modeling and the loading situation, there are situations when the corner node selection 
alternative provides a more realistic estimate for the stress based design, compared with the node 
selection alternative of considering the full set of critical nodes.

6.3 Consider the full structural system 
In design optimization it is important to consider the full structural system in order to assure that the 
complete structure fulfills the design criterions. If only parts of a full structure are considered in the 
optimization (i.e. sub-optimization), reduction of thicknesses of components within the design space 
could potentially cause stresses in external parts, outside the design space, to increase and become critical 
for the design. For this reason the full structure needs to be considered either by including the full system 
in the optimization analysis or by verifying the external critical sections once the optimization has been 
performed in order to assure that all design criterions are met. 
6.4 Relaxation or addition of constraints 
In some cases engineering judgment need to be incorporated in the set-up of the optimization problem. 
If e.g. an intersection between PID‟s do not correspond to a welded connection or if sections, critical for 
the design, are not located in the vicinity of intersections between PID‟s, relaxation or addition of 
constraints might be needed. The two terms are described in the following. 
6.4.1 Relaxation 
Consider an intersection between PID‟s which do not correspond to a welded connection. For this case 
the optimization tool will strictly consider the connected members with different PID‟s as a welded 
connection and stress constraints will be placed on the critical nodes (or elements) based on design 
==PAGE:76==
 
Structural Optimization of Volvo buses 
 
64 
 
 
criterions for welded connections. In order to account for other types of connections, relaxation of the 
stress constraints can be applied either by adjusting the location of the critical nodes (or elements), 
modifying the stress limit criterion for the given stress constraints or by simply excluding the critical 
nodes from the analysis. 
6.4.2 Addition 
For large optimization problems, e.g. optimization of a full floor structure of a bus there might be 
sections in the structure which are critical for the design that are not located in the vicinity of the 
connections. This could e.g. be high stresses at door corners or deflection of floor members for which 
additional constraints might need to be added manually. The addition of constraints is here referred to as; 
addition. 
6.5 Failure modes 
When performing a full structure optimization it is crucial to ensure that all possible failure modes are 
adequately addressed by the design specifications. This means that additional design criterions should be 
considered in the optimization in order to assure that all applicable criterions are fulfilled in the final 
design. If e.g. one considers a long slender beam in a structure. If only stress constraint are used in the 
design optimization, this might not lead to an actual feasible design even though the stress criterions are 
met, if the physical design is also limited to design criterions on displacements. For this reason additional 
constraint options have been provided for the developed Size optimization tool such as displacement and 
natural frequency constraint options. 
6.6 Initial design 
Based on the test results from the optimization of the full floor structure of the bus model it was found 
that the final design is influenced by the initial design for large scale optimization problems. The 
optimizer tends to focus on the critical members, leaving the less critical members to a thickness closer to 
the initial design value and a good start guess is therefore vital to assure an optimal final design. The initial 
design should be based on either: design experience, a predicted mean value of the final values for the 
design variables or a more systematic approach using global optimization [24], [25]. 
6.7 Discrete variable optimization 
The discrete variable optimization options: Design of experiment, is recommended when using the 
developed optimization tool. The application of discrete design cycles (defined in the DISBEG parameter 
in the DOPTPRM entry) should be considered and evaluated with respect to the trade-off between 
weight reduction and execution time. For the full scale bus model it was found that an earlier application 
of discrete variable optimization cycles provided a noticeably more optimized design while the execution 
time was increased significantly.

7 Conclusion 
In the current study a structural optimization tool using size optimization has been developed. A series of 
tests were executed on a test model and on full scale bus model in order to evaluate a number of available 
design options and the capacities and limitations of the developed tool. The following design options 
were tested: element/node stress constraints, critical node selection, discrete variable optimization, 
sensitivity to initial design and optimization algorithm. The main results from the testing of the developed 
tool on the test model and on the full scale bus model, and model consideration and conclusions based 
on the derived results, are presented in this chapter.  
7.1 Test model 
The main results from the testing of the developed tool on the test model and the conclusions based on 
the derived results are presented in the following. 
Stress constraints: Based on the testing on the test model it was found that the stress constraint option; 
Node stress Compute-Average, provides an efficient constraint option for vehicle optimization using 
fatigue based design criterions. It was found that the element stress constraint option is more time 
consuming, compared with the node stress constraints, when the element stress constraints are placed on 
elements next to the intersections due to the high stress gradient in the vicinity of the connections. The 
stress constraint options; Node stress Compute-Average and Node stress Average-Compute was found to 
provide similar results for the test model when the critical nodes, decisive for the final design of the given 
component, were not located on corners (due to the limitations of the current implementation of 
Average-Compute3). It was also found that the Node stress Average-Compute option was significantly 
more tedious to set-up and time and data consuming compared with the stress constraint option; Node 
stress Compute-Average. 
Critical node selection: For the optimization problem considering the test model the final solution was 
not significantly influenced by the applied critical node selection option. 
Discrete variable optimization: For the considered test model and an allowable discrete space of: 1-15 
mm thick components with a discrete step of: 0.5 mm, it was found that the discrete variable 
optimization options provided similar results. It was also found that the two rounding options do not 
consider the constraint limitations in final design and are therefore not recommended for further usage. 
The two other options: DOE and CCD provided similar results. 
Optimization algorithm: It was found that for the test model the available optimization algorithms 
provided similar final designs. The only noteworthy difference between the analyses was the execution 
times. The MMFD (default option in MSC Nastran for size optimization) was found to be the slightly 
fastest to derive an optimal design for the given optimization problem compared with SLP and SQP. 
Sensitivity to initial design: Due to the simplicity of the optimization problem the final solution of the 
optimization problem for the considered test model was not significantly influenced by the initial design.

based on the derived results are presented in the following.

Critical node selection: During the testing it was found that if stress constraints are placed on critical 
nodes on external members a feasible design which fulfills the design criterions might not be found due 
to the limited influence on the stresses in the external members, as the thicknesses of the external 
members are not defined as parametric variables. The execution time of the Matlab script for generating 
stress constraints was found to be significantly larger for the critical node selection options considering 
the full set of critical nodes compared with the options of only considering corner nodes. It was also 
found that the final objective was influenced by the node selection option which relates to modeling 
assumptions and the fact that for some connections the corner nodes might not be decisive for the final 
design. Based on modeling assumptions and the complexity of the structure there might be cases when 
any of the four critical node selection options are preferred over the others. For the optimization of the 
full floor structure of the bus model the node selection option: Corner nodes on internal members, was 
found most applicable. For large scale problems the options of using corner nodes also has the advantage 
of the critical node selection being easier to validate and modify (if needed). 
Sensitivity to initial design: For large scale optimization problems, such as the considered full scale bus 
model, the final design was found to be influenced by the initial design. It was found that a more refined 
started guess (i.e. closer to the optimal design) resulted in a more optimized final design as the optimizer 
tends to focus on the critical members, leaving the less critical members to thicknesses closer to the initial 
design values. A rerun of one of the optimized design (initial thicknesses of 5mm) gave a further 
reduction of the weight which indicates that modification of constraint screening settings, convergence 
criterions or the relative size of the objective could potentially improve the final design. Testing with the 
alternative optimization algorithm SQP gave an opposite pattern i.e.: less refined started guesses resulted 
in more optimized final designs. The issue of the final design being influenced by the initial design is 
assumed to relate to the non-convex original problem and the redundancy of the structure. 
Discrete variable optimization: It was found that the discrete variable optimization option; Design of 
experiment, given an allowable discrete space of: 1-15 mm thick components with a discrete step of: 0.5 
mm, provided a final design with a minor increase in weight and a maximum constraint exceeding of 
approximately 2 percent. Given the complexity of the optimization problem this was considered 
acceptable. It was also found that an earlier application of discrete variable optimization cycles provided a 
more optimized design compared with the default setting of application only once after the continuous 
design optimization problem has been solved. On the other hand the earlier application resulted in a 
significant increase in execution time. 
Stress evaluation: The stress distribution in the internal members (design members) was seen to be 
redistributed during the optimization resulting in a final design which provided a more optimal design 
based on material utilization with respect to the given stress criterion, without exceeding the predefined 
stress limit. It was also seen that, given a reasonable initial design (t=5mm), the stresses in the external 
members were only slightly influenced (for the given case this roughly corresponded to stress changes of: 
±10 MPa) by the design optimization of the internal members. In some external members it was found 
that the stresses decreased slightly after the design optimization, even though thicknesses of the 
surrounding internal members had been reduced, due to redistribution of material within the design space 
creating a stiffer and lighter floor structure. 
7.3 Modeling considerations 
Modeling considerations which should be considered in the application of the developed optimization 
tool are presented in the following.

Mesh dependency: Due to the applied fatigue design model the optimization tool is highly mesh 
sensitive and to assure reliable results the mesh sizes near the intersections, where the stress constraints 
are placed, should be verified with respect to the given stress design criterion prior to the optimization. 
Critical node selection: Based on the given design optimization problem the critical node selection 
option should be considered. For some cases the option of only considering corner nodes might be most 
applicable while for other cases the full set of critical nodes might be needed. The critical nodes located 
on external members might result in an infeasible final design. 
Consider the full structural system: When using the developed optimizing tool it is important to 
consider the full structural system in order to assure that the complete structure fulfills the design 
criterions. This can be done either by including the full system in the optimization analysis or by adding 
additional constraints in order to cover all potential critical sections of the structure. If none of these 
options are applied, design criterions need to be verified for the full structural system once the 
optimization has been performed. 
Consider all possible failure modes: All possible failure modes should be adequately addressed by the 
design specifications in the optimization. This means that additional design specifications regarding e.g. 
deflection and natural frequency should be considered. In the current design tool additional constraints 
for deflection and natural frequency have been defined for this purpose. 
Initial design: For large scale optimization problems the final design is influenced by the initial design. 
The initial design should be based on either: design experience, a predicted mean value of the final values 
for the design variables or a more systematic approach using global optimization [24], [25]. 
Discrete variable optimization: The application of discrete variable optimization cycles (defined in the 
DISBEG parameter in the DOPTPRM entry) should be considered and evaluated with respect to the 
trade-off between weight reduction and execution time. 
7.4 Recommendations 
Based on the results from the optimization analyses the following design options are recommended to be 
applied when using the developed Size optimization tool for vehicle optimization: 
 Stress constraint options: Node stress Compute-Average 
 Node selection: Corner nodes on internal members 
 Discrete variable optimization: Design of Experiment (default) 
 Optimization algorithm: Method of feasible directions (default) 
 Initial design: A refined started guess (i.e. close to the optimal design) based on either: design 
experience, a predicted mean value of the final values for the design variables or a more 
systematic approach using global optimization [24], [25]. 
 Application of discrete variable design cycle (DISBEG): 




The overall conclusion of the study is that the developed Size optimization tool provides a practical 
design tool for optimization of load carrying structures of vehicles when the provided recommendations 
and model considerations are considered.

8 Further work 
For extended studies the following aspects would be interesting to pursue. 
8.1 Systematic approach for initial designs 
A more systematic approach using global optimization to derive a more refined initial design is presented 
in [24] and [25]. The current implementation of this tool is a plug-in version to MSC Nastran [26] and is 
limited to a certain version and operating system (i.e. it is not included in the current version of MSC 
Nastran: v. 2013.1.0). According to MSC the given tool will be implemented in an upcoming release. 
Creating a systematic scheme for deriving initial designs with the application of the given tool, when using 
the developed Size optimization tool, could potentially result in a more systematic and time efficient 
approach to derive initial designs which could results in more optimized designs for large scale 
optimization problems. 
8.2 Topology/shape optimization tool(s) 
Due to the limitation that the developed optimization tool only considers thicknesses of members as 
design variables, further design improvements could be achieved by evaluating the connectivity and shape 
of the structure. This could be done by implementing topology and/or shape optimization tool(s) to 
complement the developed size optimization tool. By creating additional design tools which could 
optimize the topology and shape of the structure, a more extensive and capable optimization tool box 
could be created where, not only thicknesses of members are evaluated, but also the location, shape and 
criticality of the individual members. The topology/shape optimization tool(s) could either be designed as 
a separate tool(s) or as an extension of the current design tool. 
8.3 Additional design criterions 
In the developed Size optimization tool constraint functions are defined for stress, deflection and natural 
frequency. Additional constraint options accounting for e.g. stability, noise and vibrations, etc. could be 
implemented to create a more refined design tool. 
8.4 Alternative fatigue design model 
Additional stress constraint options could be added to the developed optimization tool where the stress 
constraints are defined in accordance with alternative fatigue design models. One example of an 
alternative fatigue design model is the earlier mentioned IIW hot spot method [16]. For this method the 
node stresses one respectively two elements away from the intersection would need to be defined as 
functions of the connected element stresses and the hot spot stress as a function of the two node stresses 
according to the linear or quadratic extrapolation formula. As the mesh size in the vicinity of the 
connections is dependent on the thickness of the individual components in the original design model, 
evaluation of alternative solutions to the mesh design would be needed [16].

 Conservative Discrete Design (CCD) 
 Rounding up to the nearest discrete design variable 
 Rounding off to the nearest discrete design variable 
Default is that a discrete variable optimization cycle is performed after the “optimal” solution for the 
continuous problem has been derived (convergence is achieved) with the option; Design of Experiment 
(DOE). The discrete variable optimization option to use is specified in the DISCOD parameter in the 
DOPTPROM entry and the design cycle ID after which discrete variable processing analysis is performed 
after each design cycle is specified in the DISBEG parameter in the DOPTPROM entry. The Options are 
presented as follows. 
Design of Experiment (DISCOD = 1) 
The discrete variable optimization option: Design of experiment, uses a series of design samples to 
approximate the design space and base the discrete design on a best approximation for fulfilling the 
convergence criterions. 
Conservative Discrete Design (DISCOD = 2) 
An approximate analysis is executed for the nearest allowable discrete design values (larger and smaller) 
for each design variable and the discrete design which results in the lowest value for the maximum 
constraint is chosen. 
Rounding up the nearest discrete design variable (DISCOD = 3) 
As the name indicate the procedure corresponds to finds the nearest allowable discrete design value larger 
than the design value derived in the continuous optimization problem. 
Rounding off the nearest discrete design variable (DISCOD = 4) 
As the name indicate the procedure corresponds to finds the nearest allowable discrete value for the 
design values derived in the continuous optimization problem. 
Allowable discrete values 
The allowable discrete design values for a given design variable is defined by a DDVAL entry, for which 
the ID is referred to in the corresponding DESVAR entry.

Appendix C: Matlab script for generating stress constraints 
C.1 Stress constraint options 
Three implementations for stress responses and constraints have been developed and are presented in the 
following: 
 
Element stress 
 
Node stress: Compute-Average 
 
Node stress: Average-Compute 
C.1.1 Element stress 
For stress constraint option: Element stress, the constraints are placed directly on the response functions 
for the element centroid principal stresses (center values). In this option the principal stresses in the 
critical elements are considered as response functions defined in DRESP1 entries which are constraint by 
constraints defined in DCONSTR entries. 
C.1.2 Node stress 
For both node stress constraint options the node stress is defined as a function of the corner element 
stresses which means that a design response function for the node stress is defined as a functions 
(DRESP2‟s) of the design responses for the corresponding element corner stresses (DRESP1‟s). 
C.1.2.1 Node stress: Compute-Average 
In the stress constraint option: Node stress Compute-Average, the node stress is defined as the mean 
value of the extrapolated element corner values of the principal stresses for the element corners 
connected to the given nodes. 
C.1.2.2 Average-Compute 
In the stress constraint option: Node stress Average-Compute, the stress invariants are averaged before 
the principal node stresses are derived. The stress invariants are defined by the extrapolated element 
corner values of the stress tensor components for the element corners connected to the given nodes.

Limitations 
It should be noted that the current implementation has the following limitations: 
 Principal stress definition only works for nodes which are not located on corners. 
 Current version only considers nodes connected to four QUADR elements. 
C.5.4.4 Node stress constraint (DCONSTR) 
In the last step constraints are defined based on the user defined maximum stress level (max_stress) and 
linked to the given node stress response. 
C.5.4.5 Print data 
The node stress responses and constraints are printed together with the referenced element corner 
stresses, element coordinate vectors and equations during the execution of the script in a generated 
Nastran input file: Node_Stress_RC.nas.

Appendix E: Script for reading Violated or Active constraints 
A Matlab script to ease the identification of violated (or active) constraints in the final design cycle has 
been developed. The script reads the .f06 result file and prints the stress constraints which are violated, 
the maximum violation and the total number of violated constraints.  
The script is meant as a support tool when the solver is not able to find a feasible optimal solution, but 
instead provides a best compromise infeasible design. One of the recommended tests when this occurs is to 
check if any stress constraints are significantly violated in the best compromise infeasible design. If so the 
validity of the given nodes and the placed constraint limits should be evaluated.
