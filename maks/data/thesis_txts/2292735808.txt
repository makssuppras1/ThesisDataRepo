Summary (English)
Oil constitutes the worlds leading fuel with 32.6% of the worlds energy consump-
tion and while this consumption rises, the resources are depleted. Methods for
improved oil production have a large potential as a small increase in the average
oil recovery factor will result in a large increase in the absolute amount of oil.
The goal of this thesis is to increase the net-present-value of oil production by
means of PDE-constrained optimization using high-performance libraries, for in
silico oil reservoirs. The systems that arise in oil reservoirs management are of
very large scale and hence, computational eciency is crucial. For this reason,
the optimization is implemented in C/C++ using the libraries PETSc/TAO,
OPM and the Dune ISTL library.
The thesis considers waterooding in the secondary phase of oil recovery and
uses the slightly compressible two-phase immiscible ow model. The convection-
diusion-reaction is also considered in order to evaluate the parallel performance
of PETSc. The single-shooting method is used for the dynamic optimization.
The Fully Implicit method is used to simulate the two-phase ow, using Finite
Volume method for the spatial discretization. Finite Dierence methods are
used for the spatial discretization of the convection-diusion-reaction equation.
The implicit Euler method is used for temporal discretization in both cases.
The discrete adjoint method is used for computation of gradients when using
single shooting.
Oil reservoir simulation and production optimization of the top layer of the 2.
model of the tenth SPE project is presented together with examples of gradient
computations. The simulation is veried using the Matlab Reservoir Simula-
tion Toolbox (MRST). The computation time is compared to that of MRST
simulations and also to the commercial software Eclipse.

Examples of optimization of the convection-diusion-reaction equation are given
and scalability tests are carried out with respect to problem size and number of
processors. The collocation method is also compared to single shooting on con-
vergence for optimization of the diusion equation, using various initial guesses.

1.1
Introduction
Production of Oil Reservoirs
An oil reservoir is developed in three phases after the exploration and drilling
phase. In the rst phase, the pressure in the eld is suciently high to push
the oil through the production wells up to the surface.
In the second phase, water or gas is injected to maintain the high pressure.
Some of the produced gas that cannot be sold, may be recycled and injected
back into the reservoir.
A third phase, known as enhanced oil recovery, is implemented in some cases to
extract remaining oil. This could be polymer or CO2 injection.

Oil Reservoir Simulation
Simulations are a common tool in many industrial branches. In oil reservoir
production it is used together with a geological model of the reservoir to esti-
mate the oil production for a given set of injector and producer settings. The
simulation uses a model of the uid ow which often consists of partial dier-
ential equations (PDEs), and may take a number of physical phenomena into
account.
A single-phase ow model describes the motion of one uid and is thus not
relevant for modeling of the second phase of recovery, as it will not be possible
to estimate oil production. A two-phase ow is thus more applicable, as it allows
for dierentiating the injected phase from the phase that should be produced.
More advanced models may also used. For instance, the black-oil model which
assumes that water, oil and gas is present in the reservoir. In reality, the oil that
is present in reservoirs may consist of several thousand dierent hydrocarbons
such that three phases in the black-oil model may also be insucient to capture
the relevant dynamics of the uid motion. For this reason, compositional models
are used in some cases. This covers a family of models where four or more phases
are present.
Production Optimization of Oil Reservoirs
As the simulation of the uid motion of the oil reservoir is a useful tool for
assessing the quality of production strategies, computer-based optimization is a
natural extension of this procedure.
In its simplest form, this poses an optimal control problem (OCP) and sev-
eral methods are available for transforming such a problem into a conventional
optimization problem.
These cover the rst discretize, then optimize meth-
ods including single shooting, multiple shooting and collocation, and the rst
optimize, then discretize methods including calculus of variations, the Euler-
Lagrange dierential equation and Pontryagins Maximum Principle.
Furthermore, several methods are available for solving the arising optimization
problems. Gradient-based methods use local information about the objective
function to search for local minima. Gradient-free methods also exist which
generally searches for solutions with low values of the objective function, for
a predened amount of time and upon termination, returns the best found
solution.
Closed-Loop Reservoir Management
The solutions to optimal control problems constitute a complete strategy for
obtaining an optimal production. Real-life scenarios, however, are more complex
than what is tractable to include in the geological model and in the ow models.

For instance, the properties of the rock in which the oil resides vary on the scale
of micrometers, while the entire reservoir may be on the scale of hundreds of
meters. Furthermore, the knowledge of the properties of rock and uids may not
be known exactly as methods of measurements are limited to a certain precision.
Consequently, many of the parameters that have a high impact on the accuracy
of the simulation and hence the optimization may only be known to a certain
precision. This means that an optimal control strategy for a given geological
model or ow model may be signicantly suboptimal in the real scenario.
This gives rise to the concept of feedback. As the oil reservoir production is
carried out over several years, it is sensible to continuously remeasure properties
of the rock and uids, as well as use history matching to improve the quality of
future simulation.
In Closed-Loop Reservoir Management (CLRM) an optimal control strategy is
continuously updated when new information about the reservoir is available.
As such, an OCP is solved to obtain a strategy for producing the eld over a
certain amount of years. The strategy is then implemented and remeasurements
are carried out a few months later. With the updated parameter estimates, the
OCP is solved again and the new strategy is implemented.
The important part is that each time the parameters are updated, a long-term
control strategy is obtained even though only a short part of it is implemented
in reality.
The Specics of this Thesis
This thesis will be concerned with PDE-constrained optimization using high-
performance software libraries. The primary focus is production optimization
in oil reservoirs management. This topic has previously been treated in [7, 9,
10, 11, 12]. Optimization is also carried out for the heat equation.
The dynamic optimization method that will be used is the single-shooting
scheme, due to its advantages for problems with a low number of decision vari-
ables compared to the number of dynamic variables. The collocation method is
also considered for the convection-diusion-reaction equation.
The types of dierential equations considered in this thesis are the equations
for slightly compressible two-phase immiscible ow as well as the convection-
diusion-reaction equation. These are compactly written as

∂t (x(s, t)) = −∇· N(x(s, t)) + R(x(s, t)) + Q(x(s, t)).
The particular equations are obtained for appropriate choices of N, R and Q.

The convection-diusion-reaction equation is linear whereas the two-phase equa-
tions are nonlinear. Simplications of the convection-diusion-reaction equation
will also be considered, namely the diusion and the heat equation.
The two-phase ow equations will be solved using the Fully Implicit method.
The Finite Volume method will be used to discretize the equations together with
a Two-Point Flux Approximation scheme.
Low-order Finite Dierence schemes are used to discretize the convection-diusion-
reaction equations. These are a second-order central dierence scheme for dif-
fusion terms and a rst-order upwind scheme for convection terms.
The implicit Euler method will be used for temporal discretization in both cases,
due to its stability properties and its low computational demand.
The Toolkit for Advanced Optimization (TAO) is used for carrying out opti-
mization [21]. This is a part of the Portable Extensible Toolkit for Scientic
Computation library (PETSc), see [3], which is also used for carrying out sim-

equations, the Open Porous Media (OPM) library is used [1].
The OPM library for automatic dierentiation uses data structures from the
Eigen library [17]. The Eigen library does not feature parallel functionality for
solving linear system, so the Dune ISTL library is also used, see [4] and [5]. An

1.2
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to carry out production optimization of in silico
reservoirs by means of high-performance PDE constrained optimization using
the software libraries described in Section 1.1.
Specically, the following is
implemented
1. Single-shooting optimization of the two-phase ow equations using a layer
of the 2. model in the tenth SPE project
• Optimization using TAO
• Simulation using OPM and Dune ISTL and the Fully Implicit method
• Gradient computations using the adjoint method using OPM
2. Single-shooting optimization of the convection-diusion-reaction equation
• Optimization using TAO
• Simulation using PETSc
• Compute gradients with the adjoint method using PETSc
3. Collocation optimization of the convection-diusion-reaction equation
• Optimization using TAO
• Simulation using PETSc
• Construct Jacobian matrices using PETSc
The implementations feature a level of user exibility through command line
arguments and input decks.
For the two-phase ow equations, the industry
standard ECLIPSE deck format is used which allows for changing certain of the
parameters used in the simulation such as the permeability eld, the porosity
eld, the relative permeability curves and the densities of oil and water.
For the optimization of the convection-diusion-reaction equation, the number
of spatial grid points and time steps as well as weights for the optimization
algorithm, can be specied through the command line.
Furthermore, initial
condition, initial guess for the control function and the controls used for cre-
ating the tracking prole can also be chosen, provided that they have been
implemented in a le named Configuration.c in an appropriate manner.

Outline
This thesis is divided into eight chapters and four appendices. The models de-
scribing convection-diusion-reaction and two-phase ow are presented in Chap-

Chapter 3 describes the discretization of the convection-diusion-reaction equa-

The Finite Dierence discretization
is used for the convection-diusion-reaction equation and the Finite Volume
method is used to discretize the two-phase ow equations. This chapter also
describes the Fully Implicit method for simulation of the two-phase ow equa-
tions.
The discrete adjoint method for computation of the gradients for the single-
shooting method is described. Iterative methods for solution of linear systems
is discussed as well as preconditioning, which is important for eciency of the it-
erative methods. Automatic dierentiation is used in obtaining Jacobi matrices
when simulating the two-phase ow and is also discussed.
The dynamic optimization methods that will be used are described in Chapter
4. These are single shooting and the collocation method.
Chapter 5 describes how the discretized equations are solved to obtain the sim-
ulations and the gradients with respect to the controls. The implementation of
optimization is also described.
Results obtained for the convection-diusion-reaction equation are presented

the diusion and the convection-diusion-reaction equations. It also carries out
scalability tests with respect to spatial and temporal grid renement and with
respect to number of processors. The collocation method is also compared to
the single-shooting method on several parameters such as number of iterations
in the optimization algorithm, total number of iterations in the linear solver and
computation time.

SPE project which is compared to an MRST simulation. Gradients are com-
pared for a simple oil reservoir simulation and veried with Finite Dierence
approximations. Optimization is carried out for both this simple oil reservoir
and the top layer of the 2. model in the tenth SPE project.

and disadvantages of the software libraries that have been used. The conclusions

cluster located at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), and a smaller

256GB RAM.
The workstation has an 8 core Intel Core i7-4790 3.60 GHz processor with 15.6
GB RAM and 64-bit Ubuntu 14.04 LTS.

The General Flow Model
The general ow model can be used to describe diusion, transport and reaction
and may be used to describe several phenomena such as heat in matter or a
chemical contamination in a moving environment or solution. It has the form

R is the reaction term and can be used to model chemical reactions. Q mod-
els source/sink terms. In the setting of oil reservoirs management, Q models
production and injection wells.

2.5
Summary
This chapter has presented the convection-diusion-reaction equation, where it
is assumed that the velocity eld is incompressible. The two specializations, the
heat and the diusion equation have also been discussed.
The model of slightly compressible two-phase immiscible ow has been intro-
duced. This model consists of two PDEs of the general type (2.1). It mod-
els convective transport of mass which is conserved. The rock is assumed to
be incompressible and both gravity and capillary pressure is neglected. Both
porosity and permeability are heterogeneous and the permeability eld is as-
sumed to be isotropic. The Peaceman model is used for the production wells
while the injection wells are rate controlled. The producer are controlled by
bottom-hole-pressure. The primary variables are the pressure of the reservoir
and water saturation.

solution in each iteration. This gives exibility with regard to desired accuracy.

3.7
Automatic Dierentiation
This section is based on material from the Open Porous Media Initiative1
(OPM). Automatic Dierentiation or Algorithmic Dierentiation (AD), is based
on the fact that computing derivatives can be done by computing derivatives of
basic functions and combining them using dierentiation rules, such as the chain
rule. Thus dierentiation is very suitable for being carried out by computers.
There are a number of advantages from using AD rather than carrying out the
derivatives of complex functions by hand or by means of Finite Dierences. AD
is much less prone to human error and for complex or large expressions this
can be signicant. Moreover, AD does not produce any approximation errors as
compared to e.g. Finite Dierences. Given that AD is already implemented, it
also reduces the complexity of a given implementation, since the derivatives are
given by the AD implementation. It might, however, be computationally more
requiring to use AD to create derivatives. This is naturally true if the expression
is simple or trivial. In the case of solving the fully implicit two-phase immiscible
reservoir ow by means of Newton iterations, it can be advantageous to use AD,
due to the complexity. It is also useful in obtaining the adjoint equations.
There are two dierent ways of implementing AD and there are furthermore two
dierent types. Perhaps the most intuitive implementation of AD is operator
overloading.
In this case each variable is replaced by a class that holds the
variables value and its derivative. Operators for evaluation of arithmetic oper-
ations and basic functions are then overloaded such that when an AD variable
is passed, the operator returns the value together with its derivative.
The alternative implementation is source transformation where source code is
transformed or replaced by automatically generated code which evaluates both
the value and derivatives.
Both methods have advantages and disadvantages. Source transformation can
be implemented for almost any language whereas operator overloading, as the

4.3
Discussion of Dynamic Optimization Meth-
ods
As is discussed in [16], each of the two direct methods have advantages and
disadvantages. Several aspects of the problem to be solved should be taken into
account in the choice of method.
The shooting method can use state-of-the-art time integrators in the implemen-
tation as opposed to the collocation method, which may be crucial for compu-
tational eciency and exibility.
Furthermore, the collocation method has a large number of optimization vari-
ables. The single shooting method has only the parametrized controls whereas
the collocation method also has the discretized state variables. This dierence
is especially pronounced if the number of states is very large and the number of
controls is small.
The advantage of adding more states, on the other hand, is twofold. The Ja-
cobians exhibit more structure and convergence is expected to be faster. The
latter is partly because the collocation method allow for infeasible trajectories.

Solving the Two-Phase Immiscible Flow Equa-
tions
This section, describes how to implement the fully implicit method for the two-
phase immiscible ow equations. The implementation of the adjoint method for
computation of gradients is also described.
Since the two-phase ow equations are nonlinear, Newtons method is used to
solve for pressure P and water saturation S. The simulation and adjoint gradient
computations are implemented in an object named OPMSim. This means that
the object can be initialized once and precomputed quantities can be reused for
simulations using dierent well controls.
Three member functions are implemented, a constructor, a simulation function
and a gradient computation function. The simulation function will also provide
various injection and production statistics including computed net-present-value
for the various time steps. The interfaces of the member functions are shown in
Listing 5.1.
The constructor requires the number of time steps, the number of injectors and
producers and the method for solving linear systems. It also requires one toler-
ance for the iterative method used in solving the linear systems in the simulation
and one for solving the adjoint equations. The last two parameters are used to
parse information from an input deck in Eclipse format into internal variables.
Based on this information it allocates memory and initializes variables for use in
the simulation and gradient computation routines. It also precomputes certain
quantities such as matrices and the geometric part of the transmissibilities.

5.3.2
Box-Constrained Optimization
Using a single-shooting optimization scheme transforms the optimization prob-
lem to a box-constrained or unconstrained optimization problem as described
in Section 4.1. In this case, only the controls are decision variables in the op-
timization algorithm and the gradients are computed using the discrete adjoint
method as described in Section 3.4. This means that the gradient of the objec-
tive function is more complicated than was the case when using the collocation
method. This also means that it is desirable to avoid calculating the Hessian ma-
trix. For large-scale problems this will also be intractable both from a memory
and a computational point of view.
Most of the TAO solvers require the Hessian matrix, however, the Limited-
Memory Variable-Metric method approximates this matrix using gradient pro-
jections.
See the TAO user reference [21] for more details.
There are two
versions of this method. An unconstrained version and a bound-constrained
version.
The unconstrained version has run slower on preliminary tests and
thus the constrained version is used. If bounds are not necessary, they may be
left unspecied.
The function and gradient are specied in the same manner as for collocation,
hence the two rst functions in Listing 5.2 are also used in this case.

approximation of the solution to the single-shooting optimziation problem.

set, since the single-shooting scheme incorporates the PDE-constraints into the
objective function and gradient. The next dierence is that the initial guess

5.4
Summary
This chapter has described the implementation of simulation and optimization
of the convection-diusion-reaction and the slightly incompressible two-phase
immiscible ow equations. In both cases, the linear systems to be solved are
very sparse which is exploited in the implementation.
For the heat and the diusion equation, the linear systems are symmetric and
the Conjugate Gradient method is used. For the convection-diusion-reaction
equation and the two-phase immiscible ow equations, the linear systems are
not symmetric and either the GMRES or the BiCGStab methods are used.
For the convection-diusion-reaction equation and its specializations, a single
Gauss-Seidel sweep is used as a preconditioner. For the two-phase immiscible
ow equations, an incomplete LU factorization is used.
The Linearly-Constrained Augmented Lagrangian method and the Limited-
Memory Variable Metric method from the Toolbox for Advanced Optimization
(TAO) is used to carry out the optimization. The usage of the TAO library is
explained and example code has been given.

6.6
Summary
This chapter has presented examples of tracking-type optimization with minimal
controls. Examples have been given for both the heat and the diusion equation,
and the convection-diusion-reaction equation.
The examples show that the
optimization nds a balance between tracking the reference and minimizing the
controls.
The optimization has been carried out using various number of spatial grid
points, time steps and processors. The computation time of the optimization
scales faster than algebraically with the number of grid points and close to
linearly with the number of time steps.
The number of degrees of freedom is important for the parallel performance.
Using 256 grid points in each direction and either 64 or 128 time steps will not
produce signicant speedup, whereas 1024 grid points in each direction produces
speedup of up to 8. The DTU Compute cluster has been used for testing the
parallel speedup.
The architecture has a number of 2 socket, 8 core nodes.
This is reected in the scaling of the speedup, where the highest speedup per
processor is obtained using 8 or fewer processors.
The single shooting method and the collocation method has been compared on
convergence for optimization of the heat equation. The methods are tested us-
ing dierent initial guesses for the controls and in the case of the collocation
methods, also the dynamics. The single shooting method is found to be less
sensitive to the initial guess as it consistently produces good results. The com-
putation time of the single shooting scheme is generally higher when a step size
stopping criteria is used. If a minimum objective value tolerance is used, the
single shooting scheme is slightly faster.

Dierence in the order of magnitude for controls in dierent SI units: This
refers to a problem that arises when using dierent units for the controls. For
instance, the SI unit of injection rate is m3/s.
Injection rates given in this
unit are small compared to the bottom-hole-pressure, if this is given in the SI
unit Pa. Consequently the gradients will exhibit the same dierence in order of
magnitude and since the optimization algorithm is based on local information,
it appears as if the objective function is indierent to the choice of producer
bottom-hole-pressures. To exemplify, there is vast dierence from increasing
the injection rate by one m3/s and increasing the bottom-hole-pressure with
one Pa.
To overcome this problem, it is important to scale the units of the controls
such that their gradients in the situation of interest represent their respective
importance. If the gradients of one set of controls are scaled to be larger than
the other, the optimization algorithm is likely to use this set of controls more
actively. The scaling consists of the following change

8.1

In this section, the capabilities of the two programs that have been implemented

Distributed control of the convection-diusion-reaction equation: This
program optimizes a tracking objective while minimizing the controls.
The

8.3
Conclusion
The focus of this project has been to carry out production optimization of in sil-
ico oil reservoirs using PDE-constrained optimization. The convection-diusion-
reaction equation and the slightly compressible two-phase immiscible ow equa-
tions have also been considered. The high-performance libraries PETSc/TAO
and Dune ISTL together with OPM have been used in the implementation.
Oil production optimization is an essential part of Closed-Loop Reservoir Man-
agement. The program developed during this thesis constitute the very basic
code base of a useful tool for attaining an increase in oil production.
The program solves the optimal control problem that gives a long term strat-
egy for injection of water and production of oil and water. As discussed in the
introduction, several aspects have impact on the usability of the optimal con-
trols. The ow model may need to be extended to incorporate a gas phase or
even more phases, in order to capture the dynamics of the uid motion in the
reservoir to the necessary precision.
Furthermore, it is important to take the uncertainty of several reservoir and
uid parameters into account. The Ensemble Kalman lter may be used for
simulation under uncertainty. As this lter requires a number of simulations
to be carried out simultaneously, it may be well suited for parallelization as
each simulation may be carried out sequentially. In this case, the implemented
program that solves the optimal control problem is already suitable.
Uncertainty is not only present in the physical parameters. The objective func-
tion is crucial to the quality of the optimal solutions and two aspects are im-
portant. Oil prices are inherently volatile and certain production schemes may
increase the net-present-value only at the end of production while revenue is
necessary during the production as well.

8.4
Recommended Future Work
This section presents suggestions for increasing the computational eciency of
the programs that have been developed during this project, as well as suggestions
for expanding the functionality.
1. Sparsity exploitation of the simulation of the convection-diusion-reaction
equation.
In the implementation discussed in Section 5.1, nt linear systems are solved
separately. In order to obtain parallel speedup when using PETSc, the
linear systems should have no less than 10, 000 degrees of freedom and
preferably more. The degrees of freedom for each system is m2
s which is
the number of spatial nodes. A matrix-free shell has been used to exploit
sparsity.
In a similar and equally simple manner, the entire system may be solved
with a single call to the iterative method while exploiting the sparsity
to the same extent. This means that the desired number of degrees of
freedom is reached more easily, as the degrees of freedom of the entire
system is nt · m2
s.
2. Parallelization of the simulation of the slightly compressible two-phase im-
miscible ow equations.
By means of either the Dune ISTL library or
PETSc, the implementation may be parallelized eciently as to obtain
signicant speedup.
3. Sparsity exploitation of the simulation of the slightly compressible two-
phase immiscible ow equations. For the same reasons that parallel speedup
may be improved for the simulation of the convection-diusion-reaction
equation, the nonlinear equations for two-phase ow may also experience
an increase in eciency if solved simultaneously. This may, however, be
problematic with respect to adaptive step sizes.
4. Proling of the code to identify potential bottlenecks and improve compu-
tational eciency.
5. Assembly of Jacobian matrix without automatic dierentiation.
6. Comparison of libraries on computational performance of the iterative
solvers. The following options are available
- PETSc (parallel)
- Eigen (sequential)
- Dune ISTL (parallel)

This appendix describes the implementation of the Sequential Implicit Method
(SIM) of incompressible two-phase immiscible ow using OPM solver function-

the adjoint equations for the Fully Implicit method in conjunction with the

simulation is carried out with the Fully Implicit method.

This section presents a Fully Implicit simulations which will be used in Sec-
tion D.3 for verication of gradients. A homogeneous square reservoir with a
permeability of one Darcy is used. It has a rate-controlled injector with rate
500m3/day in the upper left corner and a producer in the lower left corner with
a bottom-hole-pressure of 104 Pa.
Since the simulations will be compared on both the simulation and approxima-
tion of gradients, it is important to note that the MRST simulation has been
carried out using an example in the 2013b distribution which treats the wells
explicitly. In the 2014b distribution, the same example has been modied to be
fully implicit, however, due to convergence problems when using the example in
the 2014b version, the example from 2013b is used for comparison.
Various parameters are shown in Table D.1. Important to notice is the length,
width and height of the reservoir which are all 10 times higher than in the
IMPES simulations as well as the initial saturation which is now 0.1 instead of
0. The simulation is run for 200 days using 1000 time steps. This Fully Implicit
simulation is computationally demanding, especially in MRST. Note that it is
generally necessary to take very small time steps for the Newton iterations to
converge.
Figure D.1 shows the simulation, where pressure is shown on the left and satura-
tion on the right. A slight change is seen in the pressure prole, but the general
assumption that saturation changes faster, also holds in this case. The water
suppresses the oil from the top left injector radially outwards until the water
breaks through to the producer. In this case, some oil is trapped in the corners
without wells. In a case like this, an optimal controller might lack actuation to
overcome this problem in a straightforward way, at least in the incompressible
case.
Figure D.2 shows the scaled dierence in pressure and saturation between the
Fully Implicit simulation and the MRST simulation. The dierence has been
calculated for each time step as the ratio between the 2-norm of the dierence
and the 2-norm of the MRST simulation. The dierence is small compared to
the scales of the saturation and the pressure, and especially compared to the
IMPES simulations in Sections C.3 and C.3.2. In absolute numbers, the largest
dierence in saturation is around 10−2 and for pressure it is on the scale of 104.
