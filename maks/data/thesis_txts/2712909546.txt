Abstract
This report attempts to answer whether the localization of a CAPRA mobile robot would
be significantly improved by implementing a system combining GIS-based previous knowl-
edge about the environment, with a state-of-the-art deep learning object detector.
Throughout the thesis is described the process followed to achieve the aforementioned
goal. The first step was the analysis of GIS data, demonstrating its versatility and the
potential of employing it in mobile robotics in urban environments.
Then, the study
and development of the visual system of the robot has returned great results in terms of
accuracy, and the applicability of the technology in this field.
Finally, after evaluating the performance of both technologies, their valuable contribution
to the whole system has been proved, whereas under the reach of this project, it has not
been possible to completely assess the performance of the combined system. Neverthe-
less, after analyzing the first measurements of the accuracy, the system has shown great
potential for further development and improvement.

Introduction
Robotics is an engineering field that involves the development of machines controlled by
computers that can help and assist humans by substituting them and cloning human
actions. Current research puts the main focus on the development of mobile robotics, a
subfield of robotics that gives robots the capability of moving around the environment,
not being fixed to one physical location.
While certain robots require user input to operate, other robots (Autonomous Mobile
Robots - AMR) have the capacity of functioning autonomously. AMR rely on a wide
variety of sensors to navigate unknown environments, define paths and operate without
the need for external guidance or human intervention.
The increased flexibility and the continuous advancements in robotics technology as well
as the emergence of Industry 4.0, are key factors in the major increase of mobile robots
market size. From a valuation of $12.63 billion in 2020 to a market projection of $72.5
billion by 2030, registering a compound annual growth rate of 21,4% from 2021 to 2030
according to [1].
1.1
Motivation
Capra Robotics [2] aims to provide one robot for multiple applications. With a versatile
design Capra’s autonomous mobile robot platform has endless potential use cases. There-
fore the AMR should be able to operate in a wide variety of environments, including rural
and urban areas.
However, successful navigation in unknown environments is a challenging problem for
AMR, and in order to face this challenge, the robot passes through the following phases
[3]:
1. Perception: The robot interprets its sensors to extract valuable data.
2. Localization: The robot uses its sensors to estimate its current location in the envi-
ronment.
3. Cognition: The robot calculates the steps needed to reach the target.
4. Motion control: The robot uses its motors to follow the planned trajectory.
One crucial step for autonomous navigation, is certainly the localization phase, i.e. the
robot’s awareness of its own relative position inside that environment. In order to obtain
accurate information about the position, the robotics community has come up with several
solutions.
Capra Robotics seeks to develop state-of-the-art navigation techniques for their platforms,
ensuring their correct localization in every environment.

1.2
Problem Statement
Localization forms the heart of mobile autonomous robots and in consequence, these robots
adopt effective strategies to successfully obtain their position.
In the case of outdoor scenarios, the first idea that comes to mind is the use of Global
Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) such as GPS (or Global Positioning System). In the
absence of obstacles, in open areas like parks, GNSS-based measurements can achieve
enough accuracy to act as the ground truth for the position of the robot and are reliable
enough to use as a basis for the navigation of the environment.
However, CAPRA platform is designed to perform in a multitude of environments, includ-
ing crowded urban areas where GNSS measurements are unreliable, due to the shadowing
and multipath produced by obstacles like buildings. In addition, in urban areas, even more
precise localization is required to perform tasks and preserve the integrity of the robot.
In these environments, measurement errors can reach tenths of meters, which makes it
completely unfeasible to differ, for instance, whether the robot is on the sidewalk or the
road.
1.3
Project Description
The thesis aims to perform a thorough study of the reliability of previously known land-
marks, recognizable in the environment, in a landmark-based graph-SLAM system in
urban environments.
Landmarks are obtained using GIS (Geographic Information System) data [4], filtered and
selected based on their singularity and their visibility from the robot’s perspective. The
thesis will focus on how recognizable are this previously known features from the robot’s
camera system.
The robot will be trained to recognize these landmarks and match them with the previous
knowledge of the surroundings provided by the analysis of GIS data. Here it is important
to be able to match the features that the robot is supposed to see, with the features that
the robot is currently seeing.
1.4
Previous work
This thesis continues the work of Jakob Yde-Madsen and Oliver Holck Vea [5] in their
approach to integrate GIS data into a VSLAM system. Their report attempts to answer
if graph-SLAM system would be improved by using GIS data. Jakob and Oliver discussed
the quality of several GIS sources while proposing a modular graph-SLAM solution.
Afterwards, Stefan Mathias Carius Larsen and Martin Jensen [6] address the method of
landmark-based graph-SLAM system and try to answer whether a bearing-only approach
is a viable method of landmark-based SLAM in an urban environment.
Both thesis carried out in collaboration with Capra Robotics, lead to the discussion pre-
sented in this project.

Robot kinematics
Versatility is a core feature of the Capra Hircus robot. The cutting-edge design of its wheel
frame is the differentiating factor from any other AMR. In Figure 2.2, the specifications
of Capra Hircus’ wheel frame are illustrated. It consists of two side wheels in combination

of Figure 2.2.

Figure 2.2: Capra Hircus’ Wheel Frame.
It combines two of the most used method for 4-wheeled robots, taking advantage of the
agility of Differential steering [7] and the stability of Ackermann steering [8] excluding
their disadvantages.
The principle behind differential-wheeled robots is the application of more torque to one
side of the wheel frame than the other. This wheel system increases the robot’s agility and
allows reducing the turning radius of the vehicle at the expense of reducing the stability
and the need for the tires to slip sideways under curvilinear motion.
On the other hand, Ackermann-wheeled robots turn their front wheels while maintaining
the back wheels fixed. Ackermann steering geometry avoids slippage in curves improv-
ing stability, but in contrast, the turning radius increases compared to the differential
geometry.

2.2
Navigation system
This section contains an overview of the navigation system. The first subsection discusses
different approaches to solve the localization challenge for mobile robots. The following
subsection, illustrates the structure of the system, with the present interconnections and
dependencies between modules. The last subsection aims to expose the problem that each
module tries to solve and provides fundamental knowledge of the theory behind it.
As mentioned in the introduction, this project seeks to contribute to the development of
a reliable localization system for mobile robots, such as Capra Hircus.
2.2.1
Navigation approaches in mobile robotics
Autonomous Mobile Robots use odometry measures to track and control their movement
in a known environment. Odometry is defined as the technique which estimates the robot’s
pose over time, using the initial pose and data from motion sensors [11].
However, odometry estimates are highly sensitive to errors due to the accumulation of data
from different sensors. Position uncertainty increases with each estimation and confuses
the robot about its actual position. Hence, the robot should localize itself to keep this
uncertainty from growing. To do so, exteroceptive sensors, such as laser, ultrasonic sensors,
or cameras, are used to provide an observation of the surroundings of the robot, that in
combination with the odometry, can be used to localize the robot.
This implies that exteroceptive sensors only improve the knowledge about the best estima-
tions of the robot’s location, but the robot’s precise position can not be acquired directly,
even counting with GPS (Global Positioning System) information.
This section will cover two among other techniques used to obtain the best estimation of
the position, these being the probabilistic approach and autonomous map building [3].
• Probabilistic approach: Markov localization and Particle or Kalman Filters are
popular examples of probabilistic map-based localization approaches, based on the
computation of the probabilities of the robot being in specific positions.
Kalman Filter is a special case of Markov localization. Instead of using an arbitrary
density function, it uses Gaussians to represent the robot’s localization assumption.
Both of them apply the Theorem of Total Probability in the prediction.
Figure 2.5: Kalman Filter Algorithm Schematic.

In these approaches, robot’s current state SBest(xt) is computed from the previous
estimated position SBest(xt−1) and inputs from motion sensors ut.
SBest(xt) =
∫
p(xt | ut, xt−1)SBest(xt−1)dxt−1
(2.1)
After prediction update phase, the robot corrects its position SBest(xt) using extero-
ceptive sensors data zt. Bayes rule is applied to compute robot’s new state SBest(xt)
SBest(xt) = ηp(zt | xt, M)SBest(xt)
(2.2)
where η has the function of a normalizing constant, M is the map, and p(zt | xt, M) is
the probabilistic measurement model, i.e. the change of coordinates from the world
frame to the sensor reference frame. It represents the probability of measuring zt,
given robot pose xt and the map M.
Kalman Filter expects the system to be linear and with Gaussian noise. Never-
theless, there is an evolved version of Kalman Filter approach for nonlinear robotic
systems, called Extended Kalman Filter or EKF. It first linearizes the system to
later apply Kalman Filter theory. It makes use of covariance matrix and mean to
represent the Gaussian probability distribution.
Suliman et al (2010) [12] addressed the comparison between EKF and KF, and EKF
proves significantly better performance in mobile robot position estimation than KF
approach.
Other probabilistic techniques have been developed for mobile robots like grid-based
localization and Monte-Carlo localization (MCL). These solutions use respectively
histogram filters and particle filters, to represent robot’s belief. These filters gener-
ally give better results at the price of increased computational cost [13]. However,
Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) provides a balance in performance and computa-
tional effort between particle and Kalman filter.
Particle Filters share some characteristics with UKF, like the utilisation of several
points, transform then and combine the results into an estimate of the mean and
covariance of the real state. Nonetheless, particle filters chooses the points randomly,
using a gaussian or uniform distribution. It also uses a much larger number of points
to be transformed, increasing the computational cost.
In addition, the estimation error in a UKF does not converge to zero, while it does
in particle filters, then, the computational effort as well as the number of particles
approaches infinity.
Kalman filters have much lower computational requirements
than particle filters, but are less flexible.
• Autonomous Map Building: While the probabilistic approaches discussed before
require previous knowledge of the environment map, in Autonomous Map Building
the robot makes use of available or artificial landmarks in the environment to in-
terpret the scene and reconstruct the environment, obtaining an accurate position
relative to the map.
Thanks to the latest advances in Computer Vision and the increased computational
power, the goal of autonomous exploration of unknown environments while comput-
ing the position of the robot and the map of the surroundings is nearly achieved, it
is known as SLAM.
==PAGE:18==
10
System Description
SLAM attempts to recover data from proprioceptive and exteroceptive sensors to
recover the environment map and the robot’s path. This time collected data comes
from the displacement of the robot, estimated by the odometry and features ex-
tracted with exteroceptive sensors.
SLAM technology allows mobile robots to build a map while navigating and oper-
ating in unknown environments. SLAM estimates robot’s current position and the
environment map as a probability distribution. Countless different versions of SLAM
have been developed in recent years, depending on the type of external sensors that
the robot uses and the probabilistic approach which is combined with. [14]
In general, SLAM applies a probabilistic approach, where the current position and
the robot map is estimated as a probability distribution.
EKF-SLAM has been
extensively formulated in several papers such as [15, 16, 17, 18]. EKF-SLAM formu-
lates an extended state vector machine containing the robot pose and the position
of all features in the map. Particle filter estimates has also been popular in SLAM
algorithms.
In this project, it is proposed a solution using particle filters. The pose and the
orientation of the robot and the visibility of specific features in the map are jointly
used as particles or estimates of the real pose of the robot. In the final section, it is
explained the proposed solution as well as some of the results.

Odometry
Odometry, defined in Section 2.2.1, is the use of onboard sensors to estimate the change in
the position and orientation of the robot over time, relative to an initial position. For short
short-term measurements, odometry provides good accuracy but as the displacements
increase, so does the error due to the accumulation of the uncertainty of each individual
measurement.
Recently, many studies have emerged on odometry methods, implementing and combining
different sensors. [20] contains a survey on odometry for Autonomous Navigation Systems
and it categorizes the existing odometry techniques by the sensors used by each method,
e.g. wheel encoders, radars, IMU, laser and even visual methods. Figure 2.7 illustrates
the mentioned categorization, including the methods that combine more than one sensor.
Figure 2.7: General categorization of odometry strategies for localization [20].
For robots such as Capra Hircus, wheel odometry is a very simple manner of estimating
the localization relying on internal sensors. Wheel odometry uses wheel encoders that
convert the analog angular position of the wheel into a digital signal that indicates the
number of wheel revolutions, or the distance traveled by each wheel. Nonetheless, wheel
odometry technique suffers from either systematic or non-systematic errors [21].
• Systematic Errors are caused by inaccuracies in the system. Such as inaccuracies in
the wheel diameter or the wheel frame. Even the finite resolution of the encoders
can create inaccuracies.
• Non-Systematic Errors are mostly introduced by the environment, such as uneven
terrain, unexpected obstacles, or wheel slippage.

GNSS
Global Navigation Satellite System or GNSS describes a network of satellites that trans-
mits timing and orbital information used to provide global positioning, navigation and
timing services (PNT) of a receiver GNSS antenna [22].
GNSS constellations orbit between 20000 and 37000 kilometers above the earth.
The
satellites forming these constellations broadcast signals to master control stations or mo-
bile receivers, that report the position of the receiver and the time of the measurement.
Using trilateration calculations, the receiver device computes its location, based on the
information received from at least 4 satellites [23].
Within the information transmitted by a satellite, is included identification of which satel-
lite is transmitting, the time, orbit and status of health. There are several constellations
working independently, each one managed by different countries o organizations.
The
main GNSS are GPS (US), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (EU) and BeiDou (China). In
addition, QZSS (Japan) and IRNSS (India) provide regional GNSS services.
Being GPS the most prevalent system, according to [24] it can reach an accuracy of 4.9
meters for GPS-enabled smartphones under open sky. However, accuracy worsens in the
presence of obstacles that interfere with the signal, like buildings, bridges, and trees.
High-end GPS equipment can improve accuracy by using dual-band receivers or augmen-
tation systems. It can achieve real-time localization within centimeters and in long-term
measurements, millimeters.
Improved techniques using GPS provide positional corrections when errors are introduced
due to delays in the signal because of the atmosphere or multipath produced by obstacles.
These techniques are Differential-GPS (DGPS) or Real-Time Kinetic (RTK).
• DGPS communicates with a base station with a known position to estimate atmo-
spheric delays due to e.g. weather conditions.
• RTK uses carrier wave phase to allow the receiver to interpret satellite’s signal with
higher precision.
Regarding the use case of this thesis, due to the inaccuracy in the GNSS measurements
for a mobile receiver in urban environments, it is only used to obtain a rough localization
of the robot, and using this measure, make query requests to GIS servers to obtain the
information of the surroundings of the robot.

Graph-SLAM
Simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM) often refers to the process of building a
map of an unknown environment while maintaining an estimation of the robots pose within
that environment over the time. In the recent years, robotics community has witness a
large increase on the proposed approaches to solve this problem. This solutions can be
classified either as filtering or smoothing approaches [36].
Filtering approaches are on-line estimators of the current state or pose of the robot and
the map. The estimate is repeatedly refined by including new available measurements.
Popular approaches includes Kalman or particle filters. On the other hand, smoothing
approaches, such as SLAM techniques, rely on least-square error minimization to estimate
the full trajectory of the robot from the full set of available measurements.
A very intuitive solution is graph-SLAM, which involves constructing a graph, where the
nodes encodes robot poses over time, or landmarks of the environment.
Edges of the
graph represent spatial constrains between nodes, result from observation, in the case of
landmarks, or from odometry measurements when referring to robot poses.
This means that an odometry measurement links two different poses of the robot, and
observations represent the links between robot poses and visual landmarks, available in the
environment. Since odometry measurements and visual observations are always affected
by noise, to deal with uncertainty, graph constrains consist in a probability distribution
over the relative transformation between two poses.
==PAGE:23==
System Description
15
A very important step in graph-SLAM is loop closure. It is the technique to recognize the
return to a previously visited region, or node in this case. Loop closure in graph SLAM
is crucial to restrict measure uncertainty to grow unbounded. When the robot estimates
that it is in a position where it has been before, due to the positional error this wont
be correct, and instead on that position the robot would be nearby. By correcting this
estimation, uncertainty is decreased, and the robot can provide better estimates of its
positions.
Figure 2.10: Representation of graph-based SLAM. Nodes represented as robot’s pose
(blue robot) or landmarks (orange star), are connected between them by edges or con-
strains, encoding odometry measurements (Solid lines) or visual observation (red dashed
lines). It illustrates loop closure, when one of the constrains, points back to a previously
visited node.
Once the graph is constructed, the goal is to minimize the uncertainty of each constrain
by finding the map distribution that best satisfies the constrains. Thus, graph-SLAM
technique can be decoupled in two tasks [37]:
• Graph construction (Front-End): Heavily sensor dependent task. It constructs
the graph as it has been described above, based on raw measurements.
• Graph optimization (Back-End): The data handled by the front-end is sent to
the back-end to be optimized. The back-end determines the most likely distribution
of the nodes given the edges.
As mentioned, one of the particular features of graph-based SLAM systems, is the uti-
lization of landmarks present in the environment. This landmarks should be recognizable
from different perspectives, and the robot must recall previously seen landmarks. This
project aims to analyze the robustness of landmarks available in the environment, that
can serve to the purpose of graph-SLAM.
In the subsequent chapters, first is studied GIS data in order to obtain previous knowledge
of the environment. Later it is developed an object detector able to recognize landmarks
provided by GIS, and it is designed a proof of concept that aims to validate the utilisation
of visual landmarks in the localisation task.

When working with geographic data, it is required to define a geodetic datum. A geodetic
datum defines the shape and size of the earth, and a reference coordinate system. In
addition, it provides a set of parameters, used to map the earth, such as origin and
orientation, related to some known landmarks like earth’s center of mass. Since the earth’s
surface is highly irregular and due to its ellipsoidal shape, a reference geoid is established.
The geoid is a mathematical model of earth measured with gravity, that corresponds to the
mean sea surface level on the earth. In addition, local geoids are calculated that describe
more accurately some regions on earth.

4.1
Ray casting
Due to the 2-dimensional nature of the maps used in this project, it is a simple problem of
computational geometry where given an origin, virtual rays are cast or traced from that
origin into a specific point in the map, in order to determine if another object (polygon)
stands between the origin and the end of the ray, obstructing the visibility of that final
point [48]. This technique is called ray casting or ray tracing.
Before ray casting, computer graphic algorithms used to project the surfaces or edges of
the map into the same visibility plane [49]. With the development of computer graphics,
ray casting and ray tracing appeared to simplify image rendering using the simple principle
of representing the path of light from an object to the observer point.
While ray tracing calculates if a secondary ray is emitted towards any light source once an
object is hit, in ray casting the algorithm is halted after the collision of the primary ray
with the first object. In the beginning ray tracing and ray casting were interchangeably
used, however, due to the improvement in computer graphics, ray tracing now refers to a
more sophisticated technique where light physical properties are taken into account, like
refraction or reflection.
During the first attempts of implementing ray casting on this project, a uniform distribu-
tion of a fixed number of rays was cast from a given point, to later compute the intersection
of that ray with the polygons in the scene, and save the closest intersection of each ray.

4.2
Graphics acceleration algorithms (Review)
The key to eﬀicient ray casting is the use of effective acceleration data structures. The
naive approach to determine which object is first hit by a ray is to simply test every
object in the scene against the ray, resulting in linear time complexity depending on the
number of objects in the scene, what makes it, as shown before, extremely expensive. The
application of acceleration structures reduces the complexity to usually logarithmic time
complexity [50].
Graphic acceleration techniques for high-performance ray casting usually require spatial
or hierarchical data structure for eﬀicient accessing the objects in the scene.
Modern
acceleration structures are grouped into the following classes: Regular grids [51], quadtrees
and octrees [52], binary space partitioning trees [53, 54], bounding volumes hierarchies [55,
56].
Regular Grids
Regular grids [57, 58, 59] acceleration overlays the entire scene with a uniform grid, with
equally sized cells. Each one of these cells contains several objects of the scene. To traverse
the structure, the ray is checked against each cell by order of proximity. Then, on each
cell that the ray intersects with, check the ray against all the objects that contain that
cell. If an intersection is found, the traversal through the grid can terminate since the
closest intersecting object has been found.
An example of traversing a regular grid is
shown below.
Figure 4.5: Regular grid traversing example.
Unfortunately, in most cases the objects in the scene are not uniformly distributed, usually
coming in clusters such as buildings, characters or vegetation. Therefore, usually many
cells of the grid will not have any geometries inside and on the contrary, most of the
objects will be concentrated in a few cells, as it is drawn in the left picture of Figure 4.6.
This results in an unnecessary computation wasted in empty cells, and high complexity
when the ray hits one of the crowded cells.
If we increase the resolution of the regular grid, like in the right plot of Figure 4.6, using
finer grids we will obtain a more decent distribution of the triangles into individual grid
cells, but in consequence, it implies that now the majority of our data structure memory
is wasted due to the emptiness of the majority of these cells.

maximum amount of objects per cell or the number of splits that would be used in the
creation of the structure.

Binary Space Partitioning Trees (BSP)
This structure recursively divides the scene using so-called hyperplanes such that the
space that lies at each side of the division, is known as a half-space. These data structures
subdivide the scene until certain termination criteria is reached. The partition can be
done at any point with any orientation that the working space allows. BSP Trees have a
very high degree of adaptability as every splitting plane is placed where it is most effective,
performing very well even in complex scenes.
With regard to this project, the hyperplane is simply a line. As the BSP arbitrarily divides
the scene, it creates a binary tree where the root node corresponds to the complete scene,
while each child nodes correspond to each half-space.
As well as in the octrees structure, it is needed to set either the parameter of maximum
number of splits, or maximum amount of objects on each leaf node. In the Figure 4.8 it
is illustrated the arbitrary position of the dividing hyperplanes.
Figure 4.8: Tree structure (left) and arbitrary hyperplane divisions in BSP (right).
BSP can also make use of early ray termination, as in the case of grid structures. This
means that once an intersection with an object is found, the remaining nodes do not need
to be checked. Hence, BSP Trees are simple, eﬀicient and flexible. However, arbitrarily
oriented hyperplanes are not frequently applied in practice. In contrast, ray tracers typi-
cally implement Kd-trees, a specific variation of BSP trees on which every hyperplane is
restricted to be aligned to the base axis of the work space.
Kd-trees are traversed extremely eﬀiciently and faster than general BSP trees, due to
the orientation restriction on the splitting planes. On the other hand, as a disadvantage,
Kd-trees are not as flexible as general BSP trees. An axis-aligned partition is not always

suitable to divide the objects on the scene cleanly, while in most cases an arbitrary parti-
tion would be available. This results in an increased amount of objects overlapping with
multiple cells.
In both cases, BSP trees and Kd-trees there is a significant amount of memory consump-
tion. Building time depends on the distribution of the scene and the algorithm used to split
the scene whereas traversal time is usually O(log n), assuming the tree is well-balanced,
it is believed that the fast traversal of Kd-trees makes up for the increased complexity of
building the structure.
Bounding Volumes
Bounding volumes are enclosing conservative volumes that provide easier test for in-
tersections than the actual objects inside them. Typical bounding volumes are bounding
spheres or bounding boxes. Bounding volumes can be axis-aligned (AABB), which orien-
tation matches the base axis of the space, or they can be oriented bounding boxes (OBB),
which usually allows a tighter fit around the object enclosed in the bounding volume.
Since the goal is to save computational effort, bounding volumes comes in handy especially
when they enclose a complex object that would be expensive to test against. If there is
no intersection with the bounding volume, neither it is with the complex object inside.
Figure 4.9: Bounding Volumes.
The most common choice is the axis-aligned bounding box (AABB) and this is partly
due to the fact that they are very simple, only defined by two extrema, meaning that in
order to encode and store them, it is only needed to maintain one vector for its minimum
coordinates and one vector for its maximum coordinates.
Also, the computation run time of these two vectors for a given figure is linear, since it is
only needed the minimum and maximum values of the vertices for each dimension.
Figure 4.10: Iterative Bounding Volume building.
==PAGE:39==
Feature Visibility
31
These volumes are also combined into a bigger bounding volume that encloses more than
one smaller bounding volume. In this process, it is only needed to consider the general
minimum and maximum vectors of the enclosed bounding volumes, and those will be the
vectors of the new bounding volume. This process is commutative and can be performed
in constant time.
Bounding Volumes cannot only be applied to individual objects, but they could also be
applied to clusters of complex geometries, and that would allow to reject the clusters if
the bounding volume is not hit. That would be a very effective way to save unnecessary
intersection tests with thousands of objects.
However, there are certain cases in which this solution does not help. For instance, if the
scene is not partitioned into individual objects, or if the object in our scene is extremely
large. Or objects are extremely detailed and once a bounding volume is hit, it will be
needed to iterate over a huge number of geometries to find whether there is an intersection.
In these cases, applying bounding volumes saves almost nothing. In consequence, the idea
of Bounding Volumes Hierarchy appears to solve these issues.
Bounding Volumes Hierarchy (BVH)
Using Bounding Volumes, BVH creates multiple layers structured as a tree. The root node
is a bounding volume that encloses the complete scene and the child nodes are bounding
volumes that encloses part of the scene. Each node’s bounding volume is a subset of its
parent’s bounding volume (i.e., child nodes are spatially contained by their parents).
Every node in the BVH can either be an inner node, meaning that it references multiple
child nodes (Node B in Figure 4.14) or it can be a leaf node, meaning that it references
the actual objects (Node C Figure 4.14).
Figure 4.11: Bounding Volumes Hierarchy.
A particular feature of Bounding Volume Hierarchies is that in contrast to the acceleration
structures that were reviewed earlier, the cells that the scene ends up with are allowed to
spatially overlap. As a consequence, there is no implied necessity to reference an object
multiple times or split it into multiple boxes.
That directly enhances the eﬀiciency with regards to the memory needed to construct
a BVH, by comparison to other acceleration structures. The feature of spatial overlap
is visible in Figure 4.14, where both child nodes of node B, are overlapping due to the
distribution of the inner geometries.

4.3
BVH Implementation
BVH can be implemented top-down fashion or bottom-up fashion, depending on the hard-
ware where it will be executed, CPUs or GPUs respectively. Since all the tests of this
project will be carried out using the CPU and for the sake of simplicity, it is developed
top-down using AABB (axis-aligned bounding boxes).
4.3.1
Build BVH
In order to create a tree hierarchy with the bounding boxes of each object, it is needed to
do the following.
1. Set the maximum number of objects that each bounding box will contain.
==PAGE:41==
Feature Visibility
33
2. Then, starting with the root node which contains all the objects in the scene, switch
to the general case.
3. Once a node is created, the general process is to create a bounding box with all
the objects on that node, and check if it contains more objects than the maximum
amount allowed per leaf node.
4. If it does, the objects are split in two child nodes. Go back to the general case.
5. If it does not, the current node is considered a leaf node.
This process is recursively repeated for the newly created child nodes until the condition
of the maximum objects per leaf node is fulfilled.
An high level overview of the algorithm is included here, introducing the main decision
statements. The complete code of this algorithm can be seen in the file called BVH_Tree.py,

Traverse BVH
To traverse a BVH it is required to follow the following steps:
1. Set the distance of the closest intersection to infinity.
tmin = ∞
If the ray does not intersect with the root bounding box, return. If it does, enter
the root node.
2. Once we entered the node we switch to the general case. Where a node is processed
if its closest intersection with the ray is closer than tmin
3. If it is an inner node, go back to step 2. Process the closest node first and keep the
others on the stack to process them later. In terms of programming, recursion does
all the work. There is no need to allocate a specific stack for that, recursion acts as
the program stack.
4. If it is a leaf node, check all the objects contained within and update the closest
intersection distance tmin in case of a closer hit.

4.3.3
Split nodes
The best way is to use only basis vectors only (1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0), (0, 0, 1) as already seen
before in the Kd-tree. Alternatively, it can be completely arbitrary, but considering any
potential axes for generating the bounding boxes and splitting becomes too much of an
overhead in terms of performance. Since the search space for an arbitrary best split is too
large that is not possible to eﬀiciently search for it in the amount of time that is worth
investing in building the spatial acceleration structure.
Regarding where to split, there are three main options:
Spatial median
Pick the longest axis of the current node and find the midpoint on that axis. Then simply
assign every single object inside the node, either to group A or to group B based on which
side of the midpoint the objects’ centroid lies on. Then groups A and B will be the child
nodes for the node that it is being split.
Figure 4.12: Spatial Median Division (left) and Object Median Division (right).
==PAGE:43==
Feature Visibility
35
Object median
This method involves an increase in computational effort. It is needed to pick an axis of
the node bounds, then sort the objects according to the centroids’ position on that axis.
Then take the sorted set and assign the first half of the objects to group A and the second
half to group B. This will leave two child groups that are odd in terms of the number of
triangles by at most one
Surface Area Heuristic
When creating a data structure like BSP tree, Kd-tree or BVH, the method used to divide
the objects into two child nodes, can have a great impact on the number of expected
traversal intersections. Both of the aforementioned methods, Spatial and Object Median
are outperformed by the greedy Surface Area Heuristic (SAH). It estimates the cost of a
split plane assuming that both of the resulting halves would become leaves.
It takes the following assumptions:
• The rays are uniformly distributed in space.
• The probability of a ray hitting a node is proportional to the node surface area.
• The cost of traversing a node depends on the number of objects that it has in its
leaves.
Therefore we would like to avoid large nodes with many triangles, since:
• Large nodes have a tendency of getting checked often.
• Having many triangles inside a node means that iterating over it will be expensive.
Then, the goal is to find the hyperplane b that splits a node and minimizes f(b)
f(b) = LSA(b) · L(b) + RSA(b) · (N −L(b))
where LSA(b)/RSA(b) are the left/right areas of the node split by plane b that enclose
the objects’ centroids. L(b) is the number of objects on the left side of b and N is the
total number of objects.
Applying SAH for every node split is undoubtedly much more expensive in terms of com-
putational effort than the other two methods that we saw before, so it is normal to wonder
if it is worth investing that much in the building time for the BVH.
This is where the trade-off comes into play between building time and traversal time.
Given the same tracing/traversal code, the quality of a BVH tree may have a significant
impact on performance and that could be as high as a factor of 2x compared to naive
splitting.

5.4.1
YOLO overview
YOLO stands for ”You Only Look Once” referring to its capacity of performing accurate
object detection with only one forward propagation through a neural network. This makes
the model extremely fast compared to two-stage detectors like R-CNN [31] or Fast R-CNN
[32].
In contrast to classifier-based systems that apply the model to an image at multiple loca-
tions and scales to later keep the highest scoring regions, YOLO applies a single neural
network to the full image, predicting bounding boxes and probabilities.
YOLO [34] was originally created in 2015 by Joseph Redmon based on the darknet neural
network and through the years have been developed improved versions of the algorithm.
On each new version, the number of that version has been added to the end of the name,
being YOLOv3 the last version backed by a scientific publication from the original devel-
oper.
After this newer versions of YOLO appeared like YOLOv4 [65] and YOLOv5 [66], claiming
state-of-the-art performance. For this reason, in this project, it is implemented the newest
version which is YOLOv5, developed by Glenn Jocher using the Pytorch framework.
==PAGE:54==
46
Robot’s Vision
There are three attributes that make YOLOv5 the perfect fit for the project:
• Speed: It can make predictions in real-time. Really needed in the field of mobile
robotics.
• High accuracy: There are minimal false positives in real-time object detection.
• Learning capabilities: It is very easy to train YOLOv5 in a custom dataset and
apply its speed and accuracy to new solutions.
YOLO combines the techniques of:
1. Residual blocks: The image is overlapped with a regular grid of S x S, then each
cell is in charge of detecting the pertinent objects that may appear within that cell.
2. Bounding box regression: YOLO implements bounding boxes with the attributes
width bw, height bh, class c and box center bx, by. It uses bounding box regression
to predict an object and a probability.
Figure 5.7: Example of YOLO bounding box.
3. IoU: It is used when bounding boxes overlap, in order to return a bounding box
that surrounds the object properly. It also serves to eliminate repeated predictions
that do not overlap with the real object.

A review was carried out to better understand the performance metrics used in computer
vision field for object detection, starting with one of the most visual metrics, the confusion
matrix. To create a confusion matrix the following ratios are needed:
• True Positives (TP): The predicted label matches the ground truth
• True Negatives (TN): When there is no ground truth and the model does not
predict any label on it.
• False Positives (FP): The model predicts a label that is not included in the ground
truth.
• False Negatives (FN): The model misses to predict a label that is inside the
ground truth
A confusion matrix includes these four metrics, comparing the predicted measure against

A precision-recall balance is important when constructing a model since:
• High recall but low precision means that most ground truth objects are detected but
many wrongful additional predictions are done too.
• High precision and low recall imply that all the predictions will match ground truth,
but the model misses many objects that go undetected
• An ideal detector has both, high precision and high recall, due to its ability to detect
precisely all the objects in the scene.

threshold. The confidence threshold is a tunable parameter that detections with a score

are considered false positives (FP).

Position Optimization
This section aims to put together the theoretical knowledge and the perception of the
robot. To do that it is needed to match both, theoretical and actual features, and then
jointly try to optimize the positioning of the robot. Since there is previous work [5, 6] in
collaboration with Capra around graph-SLAM system for their platform, this project is
focused on the contribution of using a particle filter to that SLAM system. To accomplish
that, the following solution is proposed.
After calculating the visibility given a certain point in the map, the robot knows: Which
features are visible from that position, which ones are closer, and the position that these
features regarding the field of view of the robot. This means that the robot knows the
direction of a vector that would match the robot’s position and the visible feature.
On the other hand, from the object detector the robot extracts information about the
number of features that it is seeing, and where are located in the image plane. Using the
position of the features in the frame, it is feasible to calculate the angle of view of that
features, with regard to the field of view of the robot’s camera.
Then, it is clear that the angles of visibility of the features can provide great information
to match in order to improve the localization of a robot, based on static features with a
known accurate location in the real world. To illustrate the idea, the following sections
contain an overview of the process of obtaining this knowledge by using the modules that
have been developed and explained throughout the project.

6.3
Joint System
Thus far, it has been talked about separate branches of the system, about theoretical and
real knowledge of the environment, but these two sources of information must be used
together to achieve something meaningful. The idea is to compare what the robot sees,
with what the robot expects to see.
From both of the subsystems it has been able to extract the position of the features, relative
to the orientation and position of the robot, then it is reasonable to use that information
from both subsystems and compare it to correct the estimation of the position of the
robot. To do that, one way is to assume a position, as has been explained in Section 6.1,
extract the values of the angles and compare them to the angles extracted from the vision,
as explained in Section 6.2.
With this comparison it is possible to obtain an error value or the difference value between
both measures, theoretical knowledge and vision. If the system count with different as-
sumptions about the position of the robot, it might be able to choose the best assumption,
the one that contains the most similar measures to the real ones.
This can be achieved with a particle filter. A particle filter creates a great number of
assumptions, each one of these assumptions is a pose of the robot, and in consequence, it
is possible to compute the visibility for that pose, as it has been shown in Section 6.1.
Particle filters do this process iteratively. Each step or iteration is associated with each
movement of the robot. On each step, all the particles are moved in the same way that the
real robot has moved. Then, the visibility of each one of these assumptions is compared to
the actual measures made by the robot, and the error value in the measures is calculated
for each assumption or particle. After this, the particles with a larger error are removed,
and the system resample particles based on the most probable particles, the ones with the
lower error in their measures. In other words, the particles that have a higher grade of
similitude with the real pose of the robot, are used to resample after each movement.

6.3.1
Particle filter implementation
To understand better the implementation of a particle filter in the project, here is an
explanation of the different steps carried out during the initialization of a particle filter
and each iteration carried out in order to improve the localization of the robot.
The
particle filter algorithm is completely developed in python and it is included in the file
called Simulation_Particle_Filter.py, on the GitHub repository of the project [42].
Particles and weights initialization
Taking the insight of the overview exposed at the beginning of the section, the process
starts by creating hundreds of particles. Each one of these particles represents a belief of
where the robot is in the scene and the orientation. Depending on if there is knowledge
of the location of the robot or not, either the particles are scattered using a Gaussian
distribution in some specific area where the robot is supposed to be or the particles are
scattered uniformly over the entire scene. In this project, it is assumed that it is known
the starting position of the robot, hence it is used a Gaussian or a normal distribution in
some specific area.
Each particle has an associated weight, meaning the probability that the particle represents
the true position of the robot. In the beginning, there is no reason to favor one particle
over another, hence all the weights are initialized to 1/N for N particles.
The functions used to create particles are called create_Gaussian_particles() and
create_uniform_particles() and takes as inputs the mean value of the required distri-
bution, standard deviation and number of particles. The mean value is a robot’s pose (x,
y, orientation), which in the initialization is the starting point of the robot.
Predict Step
The prediction step moves the particle according to the motion of the robot, i.e. the robot
moves 0.5 meters and rotates 0.02 radians, this step applies the same transformation to
every particle. Due to the intrinsic inaccuracy in the measurements of the movements of
the robot, it is reasonable to add some noise to the movement of each particle. Uncertainty
modeling is needed in a particle filter system in order to be able to model the probability
distribution of the particles.
The prediction step is performed by predict() function, taking as inputs the particles,
motion model u of the robot, and the standard deviation.
Update Step
In the algorithm used in the project, when the particle’s pose is updated or new particles
are created, it is needed to compute the visibility for that particle in the new predicted pose.
This is a new set of measurements that will be used to alter the probability distribution of
==PAGE:69==

the particles. For that, the function rayTracing() is used, after checking that the particle
does not overlap with any structure in the map.
In this step, the weight of each particle is updated based on the likelihood of being the real
pose of the robot. The weight of a particle prior to this step, i.e. the current probability, is
multiplied by the likelihood that the visibility measurement matches the actual location.
Then the weights are normalized.
This step is performed by the function update() which takes as input parameters the par-

deviation.

detected corner with the theoretical corner that is closest to it. With this, even if a corner
is predicted twice, or two corners are so close that it is diﬀicult to differentiate between
them, the system will deal with this issue by assigning them to the same theoretical corner.
To sum up, feature matching aims to match similar angles of view from the GIS map and
the visual detector together.

Particle Resampling
As the algorithm starts running, some particles tend to spread out with extremely low
weight. Only the particles that remain near the robot have meaningful weight, and this
could be even 3 particles among 1000 created. To prevent the algorithm from degenerating
and wasting computational effort on meaningless particles, it is needed to resample the
particles.
There are two steps inside the resampling stage. The first one is to discard the meaningless
particles with the lowest weight. Then, the second step is to replace those particles with
duplicates of the particles with the highest weight, that better represent the real state of
the robot. The result is a new set of particles that accurately represent the probability
distribution.
There are different ways of resampling the particles but in this project, only two are
covered.
• The first one is to resample a new set of particles from a small subset of most probable
particles. It is called multinomial resampling or simple random resampling, and in
it, the probability of selecting any given particle should be proportional to its weight.
To accomplish this resampling method, it is used the cumulative sum of the weights
and random numbers generated in the range from 0 to 1, then, the weight that most
closely matches the number is used to resample the particles.
• The other method is to resample using a probability distribution around the weighted
mean value along with the covariance of the most probable particles in the previous
distribution. This way, the mean of the best estimates of the actual pose of the
robot is used to generate a new distribution around it.
The resampling step is not executed for every iteration of the algorithm. In fact, in many
iterations, it won’t be needed due to the lack of movement of the robot or the scene.
In order to select when to resample, a parameter called Effective N is calculated which
indicates if resampling is needed or not.

6.3.2
Proof of concept
The implementation of a particle filter helps to visualize how the solution proposed in this
project can positively contribute to the localization of an AMR in urban environments.
This is the reason why its implementation was suggested and it was carried out, providing
the first interesting results that will be exposed in the next section.
However, the development of the particle filter is not the main focus of the project. To
test the solution provided in this project there is no need to simulate the whole process
of displacement since it is not the focus of the project. The project aims to validate a
solution that uses visual landmarks and previous knowledge of these landmarks, hence the
step of interest is the update step of a particle filter. Where the particles are weighted
based on the likelihood of being the correct pose of the robot.
Hence, the following proof of concept test was carried out for several poses of the robot,
including specific localization and orientation. For each pose, hundreds of particles are
initialized according to a uniform distribution around the actual position, with random
position and orientation based on the probability distribution. Then, using the update
method from the particle filter the particles are weighted based on the similarity of the
visual information to the real robot’s vision, giving greater weight to the most similar
particles.
Then, the estimate method of the particle filter is used to compute the weighted mean
and variance of the distribution, giving an idea of the state estimate. And using the mean
as the most representative robot, its visibility is plotted a compared to the ground truth.
In the following section, the results from a particle filter test, as well as the results provided
by this proof of concept test are included.

6.4.1
Particle filter results
First of all, the implementation of a particle filter has been carried out in a simulation
environment, the motion measures come from theoretical displacement created by sub-
tracting two consecutive positions. To create a more realistic measure, Gaussian noise
was added to the simulated inertial measures. Then, about features visual detection, it
has been simulated by extracting the visible corners from the position of the robot, then
among all the visible features, only three were randomly chosen for each measure, and
significant Gaussian noise was added.
This simulated visual detection of the features in the street was used for the shake of
simplicity and to speed up the tests. It is fair to say that the simulation of the detection
does not provide any advantage over the real object detection using cameras. It was made
a comparison between them and, the simulated version faithfully represents the accuracy
of the deep learning object detector.
All the simulated measures contribute to faster and more flexible testing, being able to
change the measures and include more measures into the simulation.

7.1
Conclusions
Throughout the report, each chapter has reviewed different parts of the project. After the
introduction, Chapter 2 explained a general overview of the system, including a high-level
explanation of some of the modules. Then, Chapters 3, 4 and 5 contain the greatest part
of the project and the sections that explained the specific tasks that needed to be solved
in order to work in the final solution.
Having in mind the purpose of improving the robot’s localization, GIS module (Chapter
3) provides information that can be used to calculate useful previous knowledge of the
environment, using Visibility module (Chapter 4). This theoretical information of the
surroundings is compared with the actual perception of the environment, provided by the
onboard camera and a deep learning model which is able to identify the target features in
the environment (Chapter 5).
Is in Chapter 6 where these different tools developed during previous sections are employed
in a final solution, which using a probability method aims to estimate the correct pose
of the robot. In this chapter is studied the particle filter, a concept already introduced
in Chapter 2. The particle filter attempts to estimate the correct position of the robot
by creating a high number of assumptions or estimates, and weight them based on the
visibility measure.
In order to discuss the final results of this project, it is important to start with the first
module studied, GIS-based map computation. GIS data has proven to be a very useful tool
in mobile robotics, it provides different knowledge of many features of the environment.
However, the quality of this data is not always the best.
During this project, it was
needed a lot of research at a very low level on the data provided by GIS. It is needed to
pay attention to different notations or terminology. In consequence, it was needed a lot of
work to filter this information, and while it can provide knowledge about many features
like trees, lampposts or structures, in many situations these are not available.
Therefore, there are two options, to filter with a very fine grain and use reliable features
only, such as buildings, or to create a system flexible enough that when some of these
features are not available, do not imply problems for the mobile robot.
Then, regarding the study of visibility, it is a very mathematical procedure. It is needed
to understand geometry and data structures, but it can provide very useful information
in combination with GIS. The method proposed to compute the visibility along with
the acceleration structure provides magnificent performance even though it is completely
executed in python and in the CPU. If specific software and hardware were used for this
task, the time performance would be drastically improved. Deeper insights into the graphic
acceleration method used in the project were given after the results
Concerning the vision of the robot, it is fair to say that the implementation of the state-of-
the-art model along with a custom dataset, has given great results. In its basic model, it
was able to detect 94% of the building corners available in the environment while running
in real-time at more than 5 frames per second. And once again, these results were obtained
==PAGE:80==

in a laptop CPU, and they would be greatly increased if the model was run on specific
hardware.
On the other hand, this model was trained in a custom dataset acquired around the
building of the Department of Electrical Engineering at DTU, where most of the structures
are very similar. In consequence, the system would struggle to generalize if it was applied
to footage from the center of the city. However, the great adaptability that YOLOv5
has shown is a clear indication that if it is trained in a greater dataset with a variety of
buildings, it will be able to perform as good as in this project.
Lastly, the solution proposed in Chapter6 attempted to prove the valuable contribution
of the previously discussed sources, to the goal of self-localization of a mobile robot. It
is clear that due to the reach of the project the evaluation is uncompleted but the first
results included in the chapter show great potential in the combination of these two tools.
When a particle filter is implemented, it defines the correct position of the robot, as it
moves around the scene. In other words, it needs several iterations and estimations to
produce a reliable state estimate that faithfully represents the actual pose of the robot.
However, the solution presented here was capable of in at least 68% of the initial poses,
without any movement or iteration needed, of estimating the orientation with up to 11
degrees of error. What is more, in only one estimation the system was able to obtain
the localization of the robot with a mean error of 3 meters, when all the particles were
initialized in an area of 100 square meters.
It is clear that these metrics are not definitive to say that these tools form a system capable
of accurately estimating the pose of a robot, however, it is fair to say that it is a great
step toward that goal.
To conclude, this project has shown that GIS information is a fantastic source of envi-
ronmental information and this project can reaﬀirm that using computer vision in mobile
robotics brings benefits further than improving the localization of a robot in urban en-
vironments.
The combination of these two tools can also be implemented in different
manners, for instance, the system can be prepared to recognize already visited places by
seeing features that it has seen before, and flagging these features in a previously computed
map, based on GIS.
7.2
Future work
If one thing is clear in the field of mobile robotics is that always there is work to do.
Systems are constantly evolving and being upgraded. This project shows a path in the
combination of GIS data with computer vision, but there is still much work to be done.
As a first line in the future work, it is the completion of the particle filter employing
the filtering technique based on the similarity of the angles of vision of the features, the
detected ones with the cameras and the features that the system expects to see.
Then, as was mentioned earlier, in order to develop a robust system, the deep learning
detector must be trained in a greater dataset. To do that, the first task is to prepare that
dataset. Gather a high amount of pictures of different buildings, a sample representative
enough of all the standard buildings that one can find in the city and annotate that dataset.
It is a tedious process but it is an unavoidable step to improve the corner detector.
As an additional note of the detector, it would be interesting to develop a model able to
match in real-time the building that it is currently seeing, with the same structure in the

enabling to recognize previously seen buildings or streets.
After enhancing the detector, and improving the system of estimating the actual position
using a particle filter, this system can be packed and tested in one of Capra’s robots.
This will be the definitive test of the reliability of a navigation system using graph-SLAM,
implementing this filtering technique to refine the current pose of the robot.
