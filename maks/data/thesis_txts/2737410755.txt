Abstract 
The main goal of this project was to create a resin for stereolithography 
(SLA). This includes the functionalization of cellulose with allyl chloride or 
methacrylic anhydride. It was shown that the functionalization of cellulose is 
possible, but more effective if methacrylic anhydride is used. The creation of 
a resin only with functionalized cellulose, water as a solvent and a photo ini-
tiator was not possible. Therefore, the focus was shifted to the creation of 
cellulose composites with a commercially available resin.  
The comparison of the tensile test result showed that the introduction of cel-
lulose particles can lead to an improvement in tensile stress at break and 
Young’s modulus. Although the composite CR95Cell05 0.05 lh, the cellulose 
resin composite, does not present the highest values for the samples for 0.05 
mm layer height. The Young’s modulus for CR95Cell05 with a layer height of 
0.1 mm is 1797.63 MPa. The Young’s of CR95Cell05 0.05 lh is 1814 MPa and 
is exceeded by the post cured commercial resin with a value of 1949.98 MPa. 
The tensile stresses at break show a similar trend. It is again the case that 
CR95Cell05 0.1 lh presents the highest value for tensile stress at break with 
27.12 MPa when compared to the other samples with a layer height of 0.1 mm. 
In the case of CR95Cell 0.05 lh the value for tensile stress at break is 33.08 
MPa. This is less than the value determined for the post cured commercial 
resin with 39.39 MPa.  
Through differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) the thermal properties of the 
commercial resin and the composite were determined. The results of all ther-
mograms show the presence of a curing peak, melting peak and a Tg at 80°C. 
The melting peak was detected at 125°C and the peak associated with curing 
was located at 106°C.   
Keywords  SLA, Stereolithography, Resin, Composite, Cellulose, Cellulose deriva-
tives, Cellulose modification, functionalized cellulose, AM, additive manufacturing

1 Introduction 
 
In today's rapidly changing world additive manufacturing (AM) is becoming 
more and more important, as it offers several advantages compared to tradi-
tional manufacturing techniques. For example, additive manufacturing ena-
bles faster product development. Besides this general improvement of prop-
erties in the resulting part, as well as possible weight reduction are further 
advantages when utilising additive manufacturing. Due to these advantages 
additive manufacturing is used in different industries like the automobile in-
dustry, aerospace industry or in the medical industry. [1]  
 
Even though Cellulose is such an abundant biopolymer found in nature. [2] 
Its use in additive manufacturing is still limited. [3] Cellulose has been used 
for melt-based 3D printing of composites. [4] 
To further increase the use of cellulose in additive manufacturing this project 
was designed. The goal of this project was to enable the use of cellulose in 
stereolithography by creating a resin based on modified cellulose. To do this 
the goal was also to successfully modify the cellulose with modifications that 
allow for curing and crosslinking when exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light. To 
reach this goal the following project formulation was drawn up.  
The first phase of the experiment series and the base of the whole project was 
the modification of methyl cellulose. This modification was done with allyl 
chloride to achieve an allyl functionalized cellulose. The functionalized cellu-
lose was afterwards purified via hot water centrifugation, dialysis or room 
temperature centrifugation. The produced allyl cellulose was dried in the vac-
uum oven overnight to determine the dry matter content and enable the anal-
ysis with NMR. 1H-NMR and FTIR were utilised to monitor the extent of the 
modification. The last step included choosing the solvent for the SLA print-
ing, as well as the selection of a suitable photo initiator.  
The optimization of the printing parameters followed the first phase of the 
experimental section. This second phase is considered the main phase of this 
project. The printing was optimized considering various variables such as the 
UV light source, the exposure time, the layer thickness and the general resin 
formulation. The obtained formulation was affected by different factors 
namely cellulose concentration, the solvent used in the process, the concen-
tration of photo initiator and the addition of a multifunctional crosslinker 
e.g. trimethylolpropane trimethacrylate. If necessary, the printed parts, were 
subjected to post curing, cleaned and dried. Mechanical characterization was 
carried out to evaluate the suitability of the printing parameters. This me-
chanical characterization consisted of the determination of tensile strength 
by tensile testing. Additionally, impact tests were performed. Finally, the var-
ious batches were compared to determine the optimal printing parameters.

2 Literature Review 
 
2.1 Additive Manufacturing 
The field of additive manufacturing is subdivided into different categories. 
One of them is additive manufacturing. This means that it is not a single pro-
duction technique, but rather a group of techniques.  As the name already 
suggests this category of manufacturing methods is characterized by the 
layer-wise addition of material, rather than removing material like in other 
techniques. [5] Although additive manufacturing is a broad term the com-
mon dominator of all techniques included is that they require a 3D/CAD 
(computer-aided design) model. [5, 6]  
Additive manufacturing can be further categorized into two groups, these are 
called rapid prototyping and rapid manufacturing. The main difference be-
tween these two subcategories is that rapid prototyping is mainly used to test 
isolated properties of the object made. Depending on if properties are to be 
demonstrated or tested or if the prototype is only for visual purposes the pro-
totype is either categorized as a functional prototype or just as a concept 
modelling prototype. This enables a more efficient design process, as no tools 
need to be made. [5] Furthermore, the geometrics that are possible are more 
complicated compared to classical manufacturing methods. [6] Although in 
some cases depending on the chosen part geometry support structures might 
be necessary, which are later removed. [5] The consequence of this is saving 
of money and time. Rapid manufacturing on the other hand is used to pro-
duce objects that are not just used as prototypes.  Even though additive man-
ufacturing is divided into these two subcategories, they do not use different 
machines. [5]  
The base of every additive manufacturing technique is a computer aided de-
sign (CAD) model of the object, which is to be produced. In the creation of 
this CAD model, it is important to pay attention to the layer height, as well as 
the z coordinates. This is due to the fact that too big layer heights can lead to 
objects that are not as smooth as they are supposed to be. This CAD model is 
then sliced into thin layers, to enable the machines to produce this model. 
The slicing can be done either by triangulation or direct cutting. This means 
the object is either covered by triangles and then sliced or directly cut at spe-
cific points. The results are either an STL or SLC file. These files are then 
imported into the corresponding machine. No further steps are needed, and 
the production can be started. [7]

2.2 Stereolithography 
In the field of additive manufacturing stereolithography or short SLA is a 
special technique that utilizes photopolymerization to create objects. For 
this, a liquid resin is needed. A laser is used to locally polymerize and cure 
the resin. [8] As the polymerization only occurs locally, the laser must be 
moved to achieve the geometry of the desired object. [9] Stereolithography 
like other additive manufacturing methods is done in a layer-by-layer wise 
manner. That means the cycle of adding resin and polymerizing it is repeated 
until enough layers are created for the object to be finished. [8]  
In general, there are two categories of stereolithography direct (laser) writing 
and mask-based writing. Therefore, the main difference between these two is 
whether the surface is free or not. To move the laser a system of mirrors is 
used to cure the respective area in each layer. In each layer, the smallest vol-
ume that is possible to polymerize is referred to as a voxel in the literature. 
Besides the mirror system, the most important components needed are a la-
ser, a build platform that must be moveable and a resin tank. [10]  
If an object is to be printed, many layers need to be formed. The cycle of cre-
ation of these layers is as follows. First, the printing platform is immersed in 
the liquid resin. The liquid on the build platform is then used to form the first 
polymerized layer. This layer is then immersed into the resin and after a short 
waiting period, the cycle continues. [10] A waiting period is required as with-
out it poor interlayer adhesion can be a consequence. The build process itself 
can be varied, as there are different hatch styles and therefore different build 
styles. These build styles are in direct correlation to the amount of uncured 
resin left in each layer. If the hatch spacing is big, the amount of uncured 
resin left is also big. Small spacing on the other hand leads to an overlap of 
solidifying zones and therefore the amount of uncured resin left is lower. [10] 
 
2.3 Photo Initiators 
As established earlier a laser is used for the curing in stereolithography. This 
laser serves as a light source which is needed to start the reaction, but light 
alone is not necessarily enough. For this reason, photo initiators are required, 
i.e. initiators that are photosensitive. In combination with light, these photo 
initiators produce the radicals or ions that start the curing reactions. [10]  
In order to get the radicals to start the reaction, light must be absorbed by 
the photo initiators. Light absorption is there the first step before the reaction 
can start. Because of the light absorption, the electrons of the absorbing func-
tional group go from the so-called ground state into the excited state. This 
means that as a result of the energy absorbed from the photon a valence elec-
tron is promoted. This promoted valence electron goes from its occupied or-
bit into a not occupied orbit. The orbit that is left by the valence electron is 
==PAGE:14==
11 
 
the highest fully occupied orbit. In order for the light absorption to happen 
two conditions must be met. The first one is, that the energy difference be-
tween the ground and excited state must match with the energy of the pho-
tons. Furthermore, the interaction with the light must cause a change in di-
pole moment. The formed excited singlet state is only of temporary duration 
and energy is dispersed again. This dispersion of energy can happen through 
radiative or non-radiative processes. The radiative processes that allow the 
electrons to go back to the ground state are fluorescence and phosphores-
cence. There are also non-radiative processes like internal conversion or in-
tersystem crossing. Besides these photophysical processes, the absorption of 
light can also lead to photochemical processes. These are cleavage of bonds 
in the molecule, an electron transfer reaction, hydrogen abstraction reaction 
or other types of reactions. It has to be mentioned that these reactions often 
require an excited triplet state. This is due to the fact that the excited singlet 
states lifespan is often not sufficient, as it is too short. [10]      
 
There are different classes of initiators, as different classes of polymerisation 
can be used.  However, only radical initiators are discussed in the following, 
as this was the chosen polymerisation method later on. As the name suggests 
are radical photo initiators responsible for releasing the radicals to start the 
reaction. How this happens was already discussed at an earlier point. Radical 
photo initiators can be divided into two subclasses Norrish type I and type II. 
Type I initiators are one component system, where radicals are formed by 
photo cleavage. Popular examples are benzyl ketal initiators or acyl phos-
phine oxides. Type II photo initiators on the other hand are two component 
systems. Here one component is responsible for the light absorption, 
whereas the other component acts as a coinitiator. The radical formation 
does not occur through cleavage but instead through hydrogen abstraction. 
An example is camphorquinone. [10, 11]   
 
2.4 Photopolymerization Reaction 
 
In the case of a radical photopolymerization reaction, there are 3 steps the 
reaction itself can be divided into. The first step is the initiation. Here the 
needed radicals are formed from the initiator. For this to happen the afore-
mentioned light absorption takes place resulting in fragmentation of the ini-
tiator and the production of radicals. These radicals then react with the mon-
omer units or reactive polymer units depending on the resin and reaction. 
This reaction can be between double bonds in the monomer or polymer and 
the radicals. In this case, a single electron is passed on. A reactive intermedi-
ate is formed, as the reacted monomer is now a radical. In the next step, the 
propagation, this reactive intermediate reacts with new functional groups of 
==PAGE:15==
12 
 
other monomers or polymers resulting in a chain reaction. As this chain re-
action is not infinite there must be some ways the chain reaction is termi-
nated. This termination, the third step, can happen through several mecha-
nisms like radical coupling, disproportionation, hydrogen transfer to a poly-
mer radical or chain transfer.  The irreversible combination of the radicals 
can happen between two polymer radicals, a polymer and initiator radical or 
two initiator radicals. [7, 11] This bimolecular termination is the main form 
the termination takes place, unless chain transfer agents are present. The rate 
coefficient of this bimolecular termination decreases when a network is 
formed, which results in a loss in mobility. A reversible way of termination is 
the trapping of radicals during the formation of a gel, as the reaction pro-
ceeds. Radical trapping is also already possible at low conversion rates in the 
microgels. If the network becomes more mobile again, the radicals can then 
react again. Contrary to the combination of radicals is this version of termi-
nation only monomolecular. [12] For there to be no new radicals formed the 
light source must be turned off. [7, 11]  
In the case that the reacting monomer or polymer has multiple polymerizable 
double bonds crosslinking can happen. One of the existing double bonds will 
react and will therefore no longer be present. The other double bonds are 
called pendant double bonds and remain unreacted at first. The radicals can 
react at different locations. The first is the monomer as mentioned earlier. 
The other option is that the radical will not react with the double bond of the 
monomer but instead with a pendant double bond. Here a distinction must 
be made. If the pendant double bond is of the same molecule as the reacting 
radical, then a loop is formed. Whereas when the pendant is not of the same 
molecule then a crosslink is formed. These crosslinks ultimately result in the 
formation of a network. This process is called gelation and is not reversible 
due to covalent crosslinks. First, the crosslinking leads to an initial microgel 
formation and then through a combination of these microgel molecules to a 
macrogel via macro gelation.  [12] The gel point marks the transition from 
branched molecules to crosslinked molecules and from liquid to solid. Right 
after the gel point the gel only displays weak mechanical properties. Further 
polymerization leads to more stability. [7] During the gelation there are two 
distinct phases present. These are the gel fraction, which is insoluble and the 
soluble fraction. The soluble fraction can be extracted from the gel. Depend-
ing on the conversion, the ratio between these two phases changes, as more 
conversion results in a higher amount of gel phase. [10, 12] Contrary to that 
the formation of loops delays the reaching of the gel point, when compared 
to gelations where loop formation does not occur. This loop formation is in-
fluenced by the distance of the double bonds. If they are far apart a loop for-
mation is less likely than in the case of very close double bonds. The first part 
of the gelation, the microgel formation, can cause inhomogeneities in the net-
work. This in turn leads to a reduction of the mechanical properties. In addi-
tion, the diffusion effect is particularly emphasised here due to the network 
==PAGE:16==
13 
 
formation. This means properties like temperature and the general proper-
ties of the monomers become very important, as they influence the reactivity 
of the radicals, as well as the mobility in the network formation. The same 
applies to the kinetics associated with the network formation. This means 
that the mobility of the polymers is highly restricted, and that the termination 
is heavily influenced by diffusion effects. From a kinetic standpoint, it has to 
be said that the translational diffusion is limited. This is the case even at the 
beginning of the reaction, as the radical ends are connected to a network. The 
result is a missing steady state. The steady state would require no limitations 
regarding the translational diffusion. Furthermore, it has to be noted that al-
ready early on in the polymerization there is an auto acceleration, the gela-
tion. [12]  
During the auto acceleration, there is an increase in temperature, due to the 
polymerization. This polymerization, a chain polymerization, is an exother-
mic process. In general, the reaction is driven by changes in the enthalpy. The 
reacting double bonds possess bond strain which is relieved in the reaction 
and heat is released.  Because of the polymerization and the resulting poly-
mers, the viscosity increases with increasing conversion. The high viscosity 
affects the segmental motion of the polymers in a negative way. Another con-
sequence is that it is hard for molecules to combine with each other, which 
means that termination reactions are suppressed. Therefore, kt is about 0. At 
the same time the small molecules, the monomers, are not affected. They are 
still able to diffuse, which means that contrary to the termination, propaga-
tion is still possible. This particular diffusion is called reaction diffusion. [13] 
It is possible to delay the gelation via the use of a high intensity UV light 
source. This works because a large number of radicals are generated, result-
ing in many propagating radicals with only short chains. However, as long 
chains are required for gelation, this delays the gelation process.  [12] 
As previously stated, there is a loss in mobility when due to the network for-
mation. This loss in mobility leads to an auto deceleration of the reaction. As 
the mobility of the molecules keeps sinking, the reactive sites are harder to 
access. This in turn leads to a slower propagation and therefore an auto de-
celeration. In combination with the vitrification which lowers the conversion, 
there are some pendent double bonds which will remain unreacted. Regard-
ing the reactivity, it can be said that it can vary depending on the place of the 
double bond, as well as the concentration and the accessibility. A higher con-
centration of double bonds typically results in higher reactivity, whereas 
shielding effects caused due to network formation diminish the reactivity. 
[12]  
Besides the gelation, there is another process that occurs, vitrification. Due 
to crosslinking and the therefore growing molecular weight of the molecules 
a glassy solid is formed. Contrary to the gelation this process is thermo re-
versible. As already established earlier the reaction rates are decreasing and 
==PAGE:17==
14 
 
are diffusion controlled. However, this control by diffusion is only present 
when the vitrification has set in. Before that, the reaction rates are controlled 
kinetically. Furthermore, there is once more the possibility of inhomogene-
ous structures, which in turn will have an effect on the properties and play a 
part in the creation of stresses. The reason behind the creation of stress lies 
in the changes in specific volume. Overall, the polymerization leads to a 
denser material, which leads to shrinkage and therefore to internal stresses. 
The extent of the shrinkage that occurs depends on the used monomer. 
Cracks or warping can appear due to this internal stress. [10] 
The materials that can be used in SLA are still limited. [14] This is due to the 
fact that the materials need to be photocurable. [15] To find more materials 
and asses there potential for SLA printing cellulose could be an interesting 
material.  
  
2.5 Cellulose 
Cellulose is a very abundant natural compound that is found in nature, which 
means it is also renewable. It can be for example found in woods. The growth 
of cellulose per year lies between 1010 and 1011 tones. From a chemical per-
spective cellulose is a linear homopolysaccharide. It is also classified as a syn-
diotactic polymer. More precisely it is made up of ß-1-4-D-glucopyranoside 
units. The physiological state cellulose exists in nature is in the form of mi-
crofibrils. These microfibrils are made up of cellulose chains, which display 
intra and also inter molecular forces between the parallel chains e.g. hydro-
gen bridging bonds between the chains. In general, the morphology of cellu-
lose is affected by these intra and inter molecular forces. The intra molecular 
forces. lead to a more linear arrangement. Furthermore, the acting forces also 
have an influence on the reactivity as the reactivity is influenced by the mor-
phology, which is affected by the intra and inter molecular forces. Depending 
on the location of the hydroxyl groups in the structure they are easier or 
harder to access. The ones which are harder to access are the ones in the crys-
talline parts, whereas the hydroxyl groups in the amorphous parts are easier 
to access. The cellulose microfibrils are also not fully crystalline but instead 
are semicrystalline. Therefore, they contain highly ordered crystalline re-
gions and amorphous regions, which are not tightly fit like the crystalline 
sections. Between 55 and 75% of the cellulose is in the crystalline parts, 
whereas only 45 to 25% is in the amorphous parts. Also, the cellulose chains 
are in a ribbon like structure. The strength and stiffness of the cellulose are 
attributed to the crystalline parts of the cellulose, but also the relatively low 
solubility. The natural not treated cellulose is called cellulose I. This kind of 
cellulose possesses a degree of polymerization up to 10000. [2, 16, 17, 18]   
==PAGE:18==

Figure 1 Structure of Cellulose repeating unit   
 
The hydroxyl groups of cellulose can be utilised in the chemical modification 
of cellulose. This means there are three possible reaction sites per cellulose 
molecule. These are located at the second, third and sixth carbon atoms. This 
also means that the maximum degree of substitution is three. Through these 
modifications e.g. the solubility and the plasticity are affected. Depending on 
the groups that are attached to the cellulose after the reaction, the plasticity 
could be improved if e.g. nonpolar groups were attached. One example of a 
class of cellulose derivatives are the cellulose ethers. [18] Cellulose ethers can 
be categorised into silyl ether, ionic alkyl ether and non-ionic alkyl ethers. 
This category of cellulose derivatives is produced in great quantities. A pop-
ular example is methyl cellulose, which belongs to the class of non-ionic alkyl 
ethers. It is used in various food related applications due to its gelation prop-
erties and good solubility e.g. in water. This means it can act as a thickener. 
Although it also finds application in other industries like the packaging in-
dustry or the pharma industry. To gain methyl cellulose, cellulose needs to 
react with either methyl chloride or dimethyl sulphate. The reaction takes 
place at alkaline pH values. Another popular class of cellulose derivatives are 
cellulose esters. Depending on the reaction partner the cellulose ester can be 
categorised into organic esters like cellulose acetate or inorganic like cellu-
lose sulphate esters. Cellulose acetate is made by reacting cellulose with ace-
tic acid, acetic anhydride and sulfuric acid. Sulfuric acid acts as a catalyst in 
this reaction. Like the etherification reaction, the hydroxyl groups are again 
substituted. Cellulose acetate possesses good mechanical properties and sim-
ultaneously good solubility. The production of cellulose sulphate on the other 
hand is a bit more complicated as two different synthetic approaches exist. 
The first one is the heterogenous synthesis. The second method is the homog-
enous route. Here dinitrogen tetroxide-dimethylformamide (N2O4-DMF) 
and sulfur trioxide (SO3) complexes are utilized. A third possibility for mod-
ifying cellulose is the tempo-mediated oxidation of cellulose. The abbrevia-
tion TEMPO stands for 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine-1-oxyl. It acts as a cat-
alyst and needs oxidants like sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) to work. Further-
more, sodium bromide (NaBr) and an alkaline pH value are required. This 
modification is done for cellulose fibres. After the oxidation, these fibres can 
be used to form films with good optical, barrier and mechanical properties.  
[19] 
==PAGE:19==
16 
 
Cellulose has also found its way into the field of additive manufacturing. It 
can for example be used as part of composites, which can be made into fibres. 
These fibres can then be 3D printed. [20] Besides the making of fibres the 
composites can also be made into granulate, which can then be 3D printed or 
injection moulded. [4] Another example is the use of modified cellulose fibres 
as reinforcement in polyurethane resins. These can be used for digital light 
processing 3D printing. [21] In addition to the use of cellulose composites in 
additive manufacturing, cellulose gels can also be utilised in the so-called 
screen additive manufacturing. [22]  
 
2.6 Composites 
Composites are materials consisting of a matrix as a continuous phase and a 
reinforcement, which is the discontinuous phase. Looking at the composite 
there is a clear interface between the two components, as they also have dif-
ferent physical properties. [23, 24] For the matrix polymers are often utilized 
like polyesters, vinyl esters or epoxides. For the reinforcement fibres are 
common. [23, 25] It is possible to categorize composites into two categories 
based on the used reinforcement. These categories are fibrous and particu-
late composites, and each of these categories can be further divided into sub-
categories. For fibrous composites, these subcategories are continuous and 
chopped fibres, also referred to as whiskers. Particular composites can be 
further divided into flake and skeletal composites, the latter one is also called 
filled composites. The difference between the two is that for flake composites 
particles with a high area to thickness ratio are used, whereas for skeletal 
composites a continuous skeletal matrix is required. This matrix is filled up 
with a different compound. The particles which are used for the particulate 
composites can take many shapes or sizes. [24] In the composite the matrix 
and the reinforcement have different responsibilities. The matrix is respon-
sible for keeping the reinforcement e.g. the fibres, incorporated and protect-
ing the reinforcement. Besides that, the matrix is also responsible for the load 
transfer from fibre to fibre. The reinforcing fibres on the other hand bring 
strength, as well as stiffness to the composite. Combining the matrix and re-
inforcement into composites often results in superior properties compared 
to classical materials. [25] The properties of composites are depending on the 
compound it is made from. Furthermore, also the geometry and the concen-
tration of the compounds are influencing factors. The reason why the con-
centration is important is because up to a certain point the increase in volume 
fraction of reinforcement results in better mechanical properties. If the point 
of maximum reinforcement is surpassed and the volume fraction of rein-
forcement is increased further the mechanical properties begin to get worse 
again. In general, composites are either considered anisotropic or ortho-
tropic. If a composite possesses anisotropic properties this means the prop-
erties vary in every direction. In addition, there is a connection between the 
==PAGE:20==
17 
 
shear deformation and extension, if the tension is applied in a uniaxial way. 
If the composites are orthotropic that means the properties vary in three per-
pendicular planes, also the connection between shear deformation and ex-
tension like in the anisotropic case is not there. [24]

3.2 Modification of Cellulose with Allyl Chloride  
2 g methyl cellulose was added to a beaker and soaked in 20 mL 80°C water. After 
carefully stirring with a spatula, 80 mL of cold water was added to the beaker. This 
mixture was then poured into a round bottom flask. In this flask, sodium hydroxide 
solution (5 mol/L) was added to raise the pH to 12. By the use of a water bath around 
the outside of the flask it was heated to 40°C. A magnetic stirrer was added to the 
flask and the mixture was stirred for 1 hour at 40°C. The flask was then covered in 
aluminium foil to prevent exposure to light and 3 mL allyl chloride was added into 
the flask by using a needle and a syringe. The water bath was also removed, and the 
heating was turned off. The stirrer was kept at 700 rpm. The flask was sealed with a 
glass stopper for 24 hours. After this 18 mL hydrochloric acid (0.1 mol/L) was added 
to stop the reaction and lower the pH to around 7. To precipitate the modified 
==PAGE:22==
19 
 
cellulose the reaction mixture was poured into 80°C hot water. The solids were sep-
arated by centrifugation for 1 minute at 6000 rpm. Before each round of centrifuga-
tion, the centrifugation tubes were heated in an 80°C water bath. The supernatant 
was collected in a separate beaker. After all the mixture was centrifuged and the 
cellulose was collected in the centrifuge tubes 10 mL of 80°C hot water was added, 
and they were again centrifuged. This process of adding hot water, centrifugation 
and pouring off the supernatant was repeated three times to purify the reaction prod-
uct. Part of the purified product was then weighed in the wet state and vacuum dried 
at 40°C and 80 mbar for at least 24 hours to determine the dry matter content.

3.4 Purification (Methacrylic Anhydride-route) 
The final method used for the purification of the reaction product is as follows. The 
reaction mixture was first precipitated in 500 mL of hot (80°C) water. The next step 
was the centrifugation. Here the centrifuge tubes were filled, balanced and then 
warmed up in a covered water bath. The top and sides of the water bath were covered 
in aluminium foil to protect the modified cellulose from light and help to achieve 
better heat distribution. When the centrifuge tubes were sufficiently heated, they 
were centrifuged at 6000 rpm for 1 minute. This short time was chosen to prevent 
the cellulose from going into solution again, because of the cooling down during the 
centrifugation. After centrifuging all the liquid from the reaction, the tubes were 
washed with hot water. Therefore, the tubes were filled with 10 to 20 mL 80°C hot 
water and then centrifugated. The excess liquid on top was poured out each time. 
This washing procedure was done three times before dissolving the cellulose again 
in cold water. Here the modified cellulose was added into about 150 mL water and 
stirred until no more solid/ gel like structures were visible. Following was again a 
precipitation step with hot water and centrifugation as described earlier.  
 
3.5 Characterization 
3.5.1 NMR 
For the 1H-NMR analysis, 10 mg of vacuum dried cellulose were dissolved in 0.5 mL 
dimethyl sulfoxide-d6 (DMSO-d6). To fully dissolve the cellulose the solution was 
stirred overnight at 24°C. The sample vial with the solution was covered in alumin-
ium foil to prevent exposure to light. After complete dissolution of the modified cel-
lulose, the solution was transferred into NMR test tubes with a 1 mL needle and a 
syringe. The analysis was done in the NMR Spectrometer AV III 400 by Bruker. The 
chosen method was the standard 1H-NMR method with spin. This method was 
slightly modified as the parameter D1 was set to 5 seconds and the number of scans 
was increased to 128 scans.  
 
3.5.2 FTIR-ATR 
All FTIR-ATR measurements were done in transmission mode between the wave-
number of 4000 and 500 cm-1. The numbers of scans were set to 16 with a resolution 
of 4 cm-1. The used measurement instrument was the FT-IR Spectrometer Spectrum 
Two by PerkinElmer, as well as the Bruker Alpha II Platinum-ATR. For this instru-
ment, the number of scans was set to 24 with a resolution of 4 cm-1.  
 
3.5.3 Grinding/ Milling of Cellulose 
Before the milling the cellulose, it was placed in an aluminium plate and liq-
uid nitrogen was poured over it, to cool it down. After no more steam was 
visible the cellulose was placed in the grinding vessel, which was filled with

25 grinding pearls. The vessel was then closed with an O-ring and the lid. The 
closed vessel was then placed in the Fritsch Pulverisette 6 Ball Mill. There it 
was secured in place and the machine was closed. The milling time was set to 
15 minutes and the cooling time to 20 minutes. As more than one cycle was 
needed the number of repetitions was set to 8. After finishing all the cycles, 
the ground material was retrieved from the machine and the machine was 
thoroughly cleaned. The ground material was transferred to a beaker where

3.5.4 Tensile testing 
Before the printed tensile specimens could be tested, they were placed in the 
test room for two days to acclimate. After this, they were measured and then 
one by one placed and fixated in the test machine, the Universal Tester In-
stron 4204. The respective measurements for each specimen were entered 
into the software. The following key parameters were applied in the used 
method. The length and the fixture separation were set to 32 mm and 30 mm 
respectively. Since the cross-section of the sample was rectangular the cho-

minute.

After acclimation of the samples in the test room for 48 hours, the samples 
were placed on the test machine and tested. The used impact head was the 4 
Joule one.  
 
3.5.6 DSC 
To analyse the respective sample a small piece was cut off using a scalpel. The 
weight of this piece was between 5 and 10 mg. This piece was then placed in 
a DSC pan and sealed with a lid which was pressed on. Tzero pans by TA 
instruments were used. If the sample was not post cured, a small hole was 
made in the lid using a very thin needle. The samples were analysed with a 
TA Instruments Discovery DSC 250 Auto.  
The method which was utilized during the analyses consisted of multiple 
steps. First, the samples were equilibrated at 0°C and holding this tempera-
ture for 2 minutes. Next, the first heating cycle was started. Therefore, the 
temperature was ramped up to 200°C at a speed of 10°C per minute. The 
maximum temperature was held for two minutes before cooling down to 0°C, 
again with a speed of 10°C per minute. The temperature of 0°C for two 
minutes before this cycle was started again.

Before printing the white resin from formlabs that was supposed to be used 
was mixed with the modified Cellulose. For this, the resin and 5 wt% cellulose

bands for the applied modification of cellulose can be detected. Both the me-

absorption bands. Starting at the high wavenumbers in Figure 11 the first 
absorption band that can be attributed to the methyl cellulose is at 3456 cm-
1. This absorption band resembles the stretching vibration of hydroxyl groups 
found in the literature at 3468 cm-1. Next, the absorption band at 2834 cm-1 
can be assigned to the symmetric stretching vibration between a carbon atom 
and an attached proton. The literature value in this case is 2838 cm-1. The 
deformation vibration in the  C-H plane can be seen in two absorption bands 
at 1447 cm-1 and 1375 cm-1. The corresponding literature values are 1459 cm-
1 and 1376 cm-1. Besides these absorbance bands, methyl cellulose also dis-

the stretching of ether groups connecting the glycoside units in the cellulose 
chain and the presence of ether groups attached. The respective literature

two more absorption bands can be seen at 1647 cm-1 and 922 cm-1. Both of

allyl group. [35] The absorbance band at 1645 cm-1 presents itself only as a 
small shoulder. Furthermore, the absorbance band at 922 cm-1 is only de-

4.3 Printability of Resins 
In principle, it was possible to modify cellulose with an allyl group. However, 
it was not possible to use this modified cellulose for SLA successfully. There-
fore, the change to the methacrylic anhydride modification route was made. 
Although this route resulted in higher degrees of substitution than the allyl 
modification route, it was again not possible to create three-dimensional ob-
jects when using cellulose for SLA printing. The reason behind that could be 
a too low crosslinking density in the cellulose water resin.  
 
To overcome this problem the addition of crosslinkers into the resin was per-
formed. For this purpose, PEGDA was used. The curing tests under a UV 
lamp showed that the mixture of cellulose functionalized with methacrylate, 
water and PEGDA with a molecular weight of 250 g/mol or 575 g/mol is cur-
able. Although the mixture containing the higher molecular PEGDA showed 
faster curing. Applying the same mixture to the SLA printing did not yield 
==PAGE:48==
45 
 
any three-dimensional structures and was therefore considered not success-
ful. Further trials utilizing HEMA and PEGDA in combination or just HEMA 
together with the cellulose functionalized with methacrylate, water as a sol-
vent and a photo initiator did not cure fast enough and were therefore not 
considered for SLA printing. Due to this, it was decided to change the ap-
proach of utilizing modified cellulose in SLA. The new way that was decided 
was to incorporate cellulose particles functionalized with methacrylate into 
commercial resin, creating cellulose resin composites.

The importance of the layer heights in regard to the tensile properties is also 
reported in the literature. Shergill et al. came to the same conclusion that 
layer height is of major importance and that thinner layer heights lead to im-
proved tensile properties. However, they were not able to spot a trend in the 
corresponding Young’s moduli. [42, 43] further confirmed the role of layer 
height and in addition, reported increased Young’s moduli when decreasing 
layer heights. [43] The reason why smaller layer heights result in better ten-
sile properties is that the smaller layer heights lead to better interlayer adhe-
sion. Due to the very close proximity of the layers. Which in turn has a posi-
tive effect on the tensile properties. The opposite can be said for bigger layer 
heights. Here the interlayer adhesion is not as good. [44] The close proximity 
of layers also leads to denser parts and therefore can display a higher cross-
linking density. [43] The higher crosslinking density then leads to increased 
Young’s moduli. [43, 45] The crosslinking density plays a big role as the me-
chanical properties of polymers depend on it. In addition to influencing the 
mechanical properties, the molecular weight can also change due to the 
crosslinking density. Besides increased Young’s moduli also an increase in 
the maximum tensile stress a material can handle is reported. This fits the 
experimental observation reported in Table 10 to Table 12 quite well. In 
contrast to the literature, no clear trend is visible in regards to the fracture 
strain which can be seen in Table 11 and Table 12.  [45] Another factor that 
influences the tensile properties is the presence of micro voids between the 
==PAGE:51==
48 
 
layers. The size of these micro voids is connected to the layer height, as for 
bigger layer heights the micro voids typically are also bigger. [44] Besides 
these micro voids, there is also the possibility of the presence of inhomoge-
neities due to uncured resin. [46]

4.5 Thermal Characterization 
From the DSC results in Figure 22 from “CR 0.1 lh not post cured”, so the 
commercial resin which was not post cured it can be seen that the glass tran-
sition temperature Tg is at about 80°C. After reaching the Tg there is a short 
stagnation in the heat flow. When the temperature is further increased the 
endothermic peak at 125°C is reached. This peak could be attributed to reach-
ing the melting temperature Tm. This indicates that the curing of the resin is 
not complete, as thermosetting resins do not melt when fully cured. [51] This 
theory is further supported by the fact that there is an exothermic peak at 
106°C. This peak can be assigned to the curing reaction of the left uncured 
resin. This curing reaction is possible as for thermosetting resins, it is possi-
ble to cure them either via UV light or thermally. [51, 52] The presence of 
residual not cured resin seems plausible in the case of this sample, as it was 
not subjected to any post treatment after the printing and cleaning process.

5 Conclusions 
This thesis showed that it was possible to modify cellulose using allyl chloride 
or methacrylic anhydride. However, it must be noted that the modification of 
cellulose with methacrylic anhydride is more effective, as higher degrees of 
substitution could be achieved.  
The primary goal of this thesis was the creation of a resin based on modified 
cellulose and water as a solvent was not achieved. Therefore, the focus was 
changed so that the cellulose was incorporated into a commercially available 
resin and creating composites thereof.  These composites display different 
properties depending on the used layer height.  
The determined Youngs modulus for CR95Cell05 0.1 lh and CR95Cell05 lh 
0.05 are 1797.63 MPa and 1814.06. compared to not post cured and post 
cured commercial resin CR95Cell05 0.1 lh has a higher modulus. For 
CR95Cell05 0.05 lh, the modulus does not exceed the Youngs’s modulus for 
the post cured resin, which has a value of 1949.98 MPa. Looking at the tensile 
stress at break a similar trend is visible as CR95Cell05 0.1 lh again displays 
the highest values with 27.12 MPa for the samples with a layer height of 0.1 
mm. The post cured commercial resin again exceeds CR95Cell05 0.05 lh with 
a tensile stress at break of 39.39 MPa compared to only 33.08 MPa for the 
composite.  
Besides the tensile testing also impact was performed. Contrary to the tensile 
testing post curing does not improve the properties of the resin or composite. 
This can be seen as the impact strength is diminished after post curing. These 
are further diminished when cellulose is incorporated into the resin. The 
trend that smaller layer heights lead to better properties continues in impact 
testing. This is most likely due to stress concentrations caused by the parti-
cles [50] or an increased crosslinking density leading to a more brittle mate-
rial. [43]  
The post curing and incorporation of cellulose particles change the mechan-
ical properties of the resin or the composite. The same cannot be said for the 
thermal properties of the resin samples and the composite samples. The pres-
ence of a melting peak and a peak associated with curing in the DSC indicate 
that the curing of the resin is not complete. Thus, the applied post curing 
seems not to be effective, as there should not be a curing or melting peak.  
 
Based on this thesis the development of a resin based on functionalized cel-
lulose and water should be continued. To achieve this additives like dyes 
could be used. Also, the scale-up of the reaction should be reevaluated be-
cause the degree of substitution was lower when scaled up. Besides that, also 
the post curing should be optimized, as the DSC should signs of uncured 
resin. If the idea of cellulose composites with commercial resin should be fur-
ther pursued, the grinding of the cellulose should be optimized to achieve a 
homogenous particle size.
