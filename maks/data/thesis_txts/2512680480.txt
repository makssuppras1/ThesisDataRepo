Abstract
Roskilde Festival is the biggest festival in Northern Europe. Roskilde Festival is currently
applying a strategy that focuses on human, environmental and economic sustainability
which contributes to the festivals sustainable development (“Bæredygtighedsstrategi 2019-
2020”, Roskilde Festival, 2019). During the 8-day long festival, festival guests will buy
⇡1.6 million dishes.
Our research shows that many festival guests bring long lasting food to the festival in
part because the price for dishes at food stalls can be expensive. This food is prepared in
poor conditions resulting in low quality meals. The festival guests have voiced a need for
improvement regarding the social and economic aspect of food experience at the festival
and Roskilde Festival expressed a desire to improve their current food o↵ering with an
alternative that also reduces environmental impact.
Our development process was conducted with an entrepreneurial and a Design Thinking
approach that especially focuses on Customer Empathy. Business Models have been used
as a tool to reach impactful and realistic results. Design methods have been utilised
to create an holistic solution with a foundation in economic, environmental and social
sustainability.
The outcome of the the development process is a customer involving food concept,
SelvSkab, an open kitchen run in collaboration with RF. SelvSkab enhances collaboration
and festival spirit, thereby increasing the overall well being of the festival. SelvSkab sells
organic and vegetarian meal kits and juice kits with an overall low environmental impact.
These initiatives have been validated and approved of potential customers and gives the
values of Roskilde Festivals sustainability strategy the pride of place.
We believe that SelvSkab has potential to radically change the way people eat at Roskilde
Festival by showing a new way of cooking with (new) friends and acquaintances. Decisions
in the concept has been based on customer input to ensure a proper product-market ﬁt
that improves the overall festival for the guests of SelvSkab. The concept embraces the
festivals value and increases RF’s incentive to implement SelvSkab at the next and future
festivals, and thereby drive the festival in a more sustainable direction.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation
Roskilde Festival (RF) is the largest music and arts festival in Northern Europe with over
130.000 participants (“About The Festival”, Roskilde Festival). The festival does not only
o↵er a variety of music with 180 di↵erent bands, but also several events and sales stalls
(“Fakta og tal”, Roskilde Festival). This includes 130 di↵erent food stalls that provide the
festival guests with everything their cravings desire (Madtiltag og drikkevarer, Roskilde
Festival, 2018).
The food stalls are not the only food source for the festival guests: home brought canned
and long-lasting food is consumed in the camps. The main reason for bringing food your-
self is to save money. Our observation from the festival in 2019 and an acknowledgement
from Henrik Bondo Nielsen, head of Area Resources, showed that the initiatives created
by RF performed poorly and were hardly used. Only a few people bring portable cook-
ing stoves and ingredients, and the community kitchen in Dream City that o↵ers basic
kitchen appliances is merely used to boil water for cup noodles.
These observations inspired us to improve the festivals current cooking options and enable
the RF guests to get a low price meal with a home-cooked meal quality. We arranged a
meeting with RF in August 2019 and presented our idea. Lene-Maria Toksværd, head
of Food and Drinks, and Henrik Bondo Nielsen agreed to collaborate on a concept that
enables festival guests to cook themselves by providing cooking facilities and meal kits
with ingredients. During the ﬁrst meeting expectations for the projects were matched
and high-level focus areas were codiﬁed and will be referred to as requirements from RF:
• The dish price should be under 55DKK, preferably 35DKK.
• The concept should beneﬁt all festival guests on RF
• The concept should be as power saving as possible as power on RF is generated by
diesel generators
• Setup should be: simple, cheap, saleable, robust, safe, modular, customer-friendly

1.2
Thesis objectives
A problem statement that includes both RF’s needs and our vision was formulated:
In an occupied food sector at Roskilde Festival, how do you create a food concept that is
economically, environmentally and socially sustainable?
To help break this problem statement into concrete investigation areas, the following
research questions were derived and investigated throughout the project:
1. Can Design Thinking be used to create a food innovation concept for RF?
2. Can sustainable and customer-involving cooking be a part of RF?
3. Can the price of a meal be reduced by involving customers in the cooking process?
4. Can low energy kitchens be created in a festival context?
5. Can a business model be used to design a sustainable food concept on RF?
6. Can public accessible and customer-driven kitchens make it convenient to make
home-cooked meals in public environments outside a festival setting?
1.3
SelvSkab
SelvSkab is the result of a development process in which it is explored if there is an en-
trepreneurial opportunity to create a social cooking concept for everybody who attends
RF.
SelvSkab is an open kitchen run in collaboration with RF. Next to the kitchen, SelvSkab
sells two meal kits with all the ingredients for a Morning Porridge and an Asian Noodle
Salad. Additionally, juice kits with ugly fruits and vegetables are o↵ered. Meal kits are
sold for 35DKK per person and juice kits are sold for 15DKK per person. The meal kits
and the kitchen facilities are designed for groups of four and two and the juice kit for one.
Festival guests can use the open kitchen to cook, with or without the kits. The customers
are responsible for the whole cooking process, including cleaning. Volunteers on-site help
to streamline this process.
SelvSkab is open 6 full days, Sunday to Saturday with opening hours from 8-20 each day.
The kitchen has a capacity of 8 groups/32 people while the seating area has a capacity
of 50. The physical space required for a sales booth, a kitchen and a seating area is
approximately 12x15 meters (180 m2). SelvSkab will be located in the camping area C,
where 12.000 festival guests camp throughout the festival. The total costs approximate
60.000DKK and the revenue will be approximately 30.000DKK. SelvSkab will sell 686
meal kits and 628 juice kits the ﬁrst year. These sales estimates are conservative and
utilise 17% of the kitchens maximum capacity. RF2020 will be SelvSkabs ﬁrst year in

expenses of the concept and is therefore dependent on economic funding from RF.
SelvSkab is designed to be a socially and environmentally sustainable food concept and
embraces these values by enhancing collaboration, social well being and reducing CO2

By enhancing RF’s Value Proposition to its festival guests

1.4
Process methodology
This project has followed Brown’s Design Thinking cycle, see Figure 1. The model in-
dicates an iterative approach, where the developer is never only in one phase at a time.
The outer arrows of the ﬁgure show that a development process is not necessarily leading
from one phase to the next i.e. a Design Thinking process is not linear. Following is a
description of the three phases in the context of this project. The ﬁndings and results of
each phase have been presented to relevant stakeholders at preset milestones to validate
and decide the next steps, see Appendix A.
Inspiration
The project started in the inspiration phase where the problem is identiﬁed by under-
standing the customer needs. We tried to understand the constraints on the festival, the
festival guest’s wants and needs and how they behave to know what we had to keep in
mind in the ideation phase. All discoveries were codiﬁed in a log which acted as our
virtual project space. The information gathered in this phase is represented by personas,
a list of requirements and the Customer Proﬁle in the Value Proposition Canvas.
3
==PAGE:13==
Ideation
The ideation phase is about testing and prototyping the ideas we generated through
brainstorming based on previously made discoveries. When testing prototypes in this
phase, it often led to discoveries which set us back in the process. This iterative process
eventually resulted in a better product-market ﬁt. The outcome of this phase was e.g.
proposals of the menu, physical layout, activities and the level of inclusion of customers.
Implementation
We have used the implementation phase to compile all ideas into one concept. In this
phase, we often made presentations about our solutions that we presented to stakeholders
along with a more or less detailed economic evaluation. Hereby we made collaboration
with partners that could help us make our concept a reality. Each time we had presented
our concept, we gained feedback and external evaluation of our concept. The feedback
guided us to the inspiration and ideation phase to further detail and develop the concept.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Brown (2008, p. 87) states that “the increasing complexity of products, services, and
experiences has replaced the myth of the lone creative genius with the reality of the
enthusiastic interdisciplinary collaborator”. Collaboration expands beyond an interdis-
ciplinary design team to collaboration between research and industry and collaboration
between industry and market (Olsen, 2015). According to Brown (2008, p. 90), the idea
is to “expand the innovation ecosystem by looking for opportunities to co-create with
customers and consumers” and collaboration in the whole ecosystem.
This literature review aims to elaborate on the process of developing a food concept by
using a Design Thinking approach and Sustainable Business Models. We will explore how
the methodology can be applied to create user-oriented innovation within the food sector
and how business models can create value for startups.
2.1
Design Thinking
Design Thinking was ﬁrst mentioned by Buchanan (1992) and has since been vastly
acknowledged by innovative ﬁrms, big and small. Design Thinking is a design methodol-
ogy that is deﬁned as “a human-centred innovation process that emphasises observation,
collaboration, fast learning, visualisation of ideas, rapid prototyping, and concurrent busi-
ness analysis” (Lockwood, 2010). Brown (2008) describes Design Thinking as a discipline
that uses the designer’s sensibility and methods to satisfy the need of a consumer in a
technologically viable and economically feasible way. Design Thinking is also described
as the sequence of diverging and converging solutions. Choices are generated in the di-
verging phase and in the converging phase choice are made, see Figure 2 (Brown & K¯atz,
2009).
In a Design Thinking process, needs are often identiﬁed by observing everyday activi-
7
==PAGE:17==
Figure 2: Divergent and convergent thinking. Picture: IDEO
ties and realising there might be a problem (Olsen, 2015). If the problem is collectively
acceptable it can be a starting point for a development process with a basis in Design
Thinking (Beckman & Barry, 2007, p. 26). This also emphasises that the players within a
given practice domain are essential in a Design Thinking process and according to Beck-
man and Barry (2007) a broad range of players are involved both in ﬁnding the problem
as well as the solution. The practical inputs from the players are then translated into ab-
stract ideas or theories and then converted back to practice. Olsen (2015, p. 182), argues
that 5 major practitioners (IDEO, Continuum, D-School at Stanford University, Rotman
Business School at University of Toronto, and Darden Business School at University of
Virginia) all have three stages of Design Thinking that can be divided into consumer
empathy, visualisation and rapid prototyping and collaboration.
The idea of involving users in the innovation process is not novel. von Hippel (1978)
was the ﬁrst to mention this concept as he described the two paradigms for an industrial
product: manufacturer-active paradigm (MAP) and customer-active paradigm (CAP).
The MAP process is characterised by the manufacturer having an active and controlling
role and the customer having a respondent role who only speaks “when spoken to” (von
Hippel, 1978, p. 40). In the CAP process, the customer takes the initiative and contacts
the manufacturer with his innovative idea who then has the role to screen the customer
ideas. These paradigms still hold and a review of customer involvement practices in the
food industry show that the MAP approach is most commonly used (Busse & Siebert,
2018).
Innovation in the food sector often relies on a top-down approach where evaluations from
panels of trained sensory experts replace the voice of the customers in the early ideation
and the most commonly used methods in the food sector are focus groups, surveys and the
study of demographic data (Menichelli et al., 2012; van Kleef et al., 2005). Olsen (2015,
p. 183), states: “Design Thinking perceives consumer insights as the point of departure for
the whole development process, the contemporary food science view perceives consumer’s
voice as a validation of the expert’s voice” which indicates that most food innovation
is not done with a Design Thinking process. Through listening, watching and collecting

stories, a Design Thinking team can discover inspiration and unexpected insights that can
create meaningful innovations (Olsen, 2015). By immersing the design team into the lives
of the consumers, they gain knowledge about how the consumer thinks and what they feel
about the problem that the team aims to solve (Olsen, 2015). According to Costa and
Jongen (2006, p. 426), one of the major obstacles in user-oriented innovation in compa-
nies is the lack of concrete guidelines for e↵ective implementation into everyday practices.
In a recent study on user-oriented innovation in the food industry, Grunert et al. (2008)
have identiﬁed that applicable user-oriented innovation methods depend on the market
size. When dealing with a mass-market the term arms-length describes the inability to
interact with all users in a qualitatively way which results in quantitative methods being
the primary approach to characterise a large population of users (Grunert et al., 2008,
p. 592). On the other hand, when dealing with a smaller market the iterative innovation
becomes possible due to the small number of potential buyers. Here it is possible to have
direct interaction with users and qualitative methods are easier to apply (Grunert et al.,
2008, p. 592).
Visualisation and Rapid Prototyping can drive a project forward as internal ideas are
being externalised in a comprehensible manner so stakeholders can discuss, comment and
improve upon (Kelley, 2007). By sketching an idea the sketcher can choose to leave out
the irrelevant information and focus on what is important about a speciﬁc idea. This
can both help to capture a ﬂeeting thought and make it more permanent whilst it also
has the quality of being able to erase the working memory of the sketcher (Tversky, 2002).
The goal of prototyping is to make mistakes as fast as possible to make surprising discov-
eries in the process (Olsen, 2015, p. 183). The discoveries made in the prototype process
will illuminate the strengths and weaknesses of the concepts and help to identify the
new direction of future development, as Brown (2008) puts it: Design Thinking perceives
rapid and simple prototyping to be a goal in itself. Especially simple and rapid prototypes
are worth considering. The more time and resources people spend on a prototype, the
less they are appreciating feedback and the more they will defend what they have made,
regardless of the goodness of the idea/prototype. The goal of making prototypes is the
process, as this is where the imagination thrives (Hargadon & Sutton, 1997). Another
reason to make simple and resource cheap prototypes is that it becomes less expensive
to make iterations of a food concept, and most food concepts could beneﬁt from making
iterations according to Grunert and Valli (2001).
Design Thinking methodology o↵ers several methods and techniques that can be applied
in various situations (d.school Stanford University, 2018). Using this as a foundation

Business models
“A business model describes the rationale of how an organisation creates, delivers, and
captures value“ (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). This is how Osterwalder and Pigneur
(2004), who made the initial Business Model Canvas (BMC), deﬁnes a business model.
The concept of business models gained traction in the early 90’s during the dot com
period and was used to explain complex business ideas quickly and eﬃciently (Zott et
al., 2011). Since then the concept has developed and is seen as a tool to analyse, plan
and communicate the business as well as a strategic asset to gain competitive advantage
(Lindgardt et al., 2015b). According to Casadesus-Masanell and Ricart (2010, p. 196), a
business model can also be seen as the logic behind how a ﬁrm operates and creates value
for its users. Most authors who write about business models are agreeing that value is es-
sential and that can be seen in Richardson (2011, p. 138), where he describes the business
model framework with value proposition, value creation, value delivery and value capture.
A BMC is divided into nine building blocks, see Figure 3 (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010).
The right side describes the customer segments, customer relationships, channels and
value propositions. The left side describes key partners, key activities, key resources, and
again the value propositions. The base is made up of cost structure and the revenue
streams.
Stenn (2017), recommends to use the following steps when using the BMC as a pre-
startup:
• Key partners: Who is going to help you? What assets do you have? What do
you need?
• Value proposition: What are you going to create? Where is that need?
• Customer segments: Who will be using this and how?
And then the logistics . . .
• Key activities: How will it be made?
• Key resources: By whom and with what?
And the ﬁnancials:
• Cost structure: How much does it cost?
How will you pay for it?
Are you
sure? Run the numbers and give hearty markups for marketing, distribution, and

• Revenue streams: Who is going to pay for it? How? When? Where? How often?
And ﬁnally the marketing . . .
• Channels: How will the customer know about it? Get it?
• Customer relationships: . . . and get it again? How will they tell their friends?
According to Stenn (2017), the canvas can be approached di↵erently depending on one’s
starting point. If in a pre-startup phase, the team should start with the key partner as “it
gives pre-start-ups a place to begin and identiﬁes readily available resources” from which
allies and support can be created (Stenn, 2017, p. 58). Key partners’ acceptance of an
idea can also work as proof of concept from which the entrepreneur can prototype, test
and prove that the idea has merit. The importance of partnerships is also described as
a cornerstone of many business models (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010, p. 38). If one, on
the other hand, has identiﬁed a customer need and wants to develop a product to satisfy
that speciﬁc need, it can make sense to start exploring the customer segment of the BMC
as the ﬁrst thing to ensure a great customer-product ﬁt (Stenn, 2017, p. 57).
Bocken et al. (2013, p. 483) deﬁne sustainable business models as: “Sustainable busi-
ness models seek to go beyond delivering economic value and include a consideration of
other forms of value for a broader range of stakeholders”. Today sustainable business

incorporating concepts, principles, or goals that aim at sustainability; or 2) integrating
sustainability into their value proposition, value creation and delivery activities, and/ or
value capture mechanisms”.

Chapter 3
Methods & tools
3.1
Introduction
Throughout the project, several methods and tools have been used to gain insight, develop
concepts and validate design choices. This chapter explains what each method or tool is,
why it was important in this project and how we have applied it in our context. Di↵erent
methods and tools were used throughout the inspiration, ideation and implementation
phases. Some of the methods and tools were used in multiple phases.
3.2
Inspiration
Following are the methods used to gain and codify insights about the festival guests,
competitors and relevant problems related to the food sector at RF.
3.2.1
Online research
Roskilde Festival is a national event and gets extensive media coverage through news
articles and RF also provides news articles and statistics about the festival, strategies
and new changes. Online research has been a valuable source of information about food
on RF and about the festival in general.
3.2.2
Survey
A survey is a research tool to collect empirical data through a series of questions in any
phase of a project (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 49). With an online survey, it is possible
to collect information about the target group. Such a survey was used in the early phases
of the project to hear the voice of the festival guests. We focused on designing a short
survey that uncovered the festival guests preferences and habits. Some of the questions
were formulated qualitatively to learn about the stories behind the answers and to avoid
14
==PAGE:25==
priming participants with our view of possible answers. The answers collected in the
surveys kick started the ideation phase and the results have been a guiding tool through
the early phases of the concept development.
3.2.3
Interviews
Interviews are face-to-face consultations that can be useful for understanding customer
perceptions, motivations and behaviour concerning product or services (van Boeijen et
al., 2014, p. 48). Semi-structured interviews follow an interview guide to ensure all topics
are covered but are open and allows the interview to be guided by the answers given by
the participants. We used semi-structured interviews to strengthen our understanding of
the festival guests’ perceptions, motivation and behaviours that we had already gotten
through the survey. We created questions at the end of our interview guide to receive
some feedback on our early ideas. Each interview took around 30-45 minutes. Later in
the project, interviews were used during prototype testing.
3.2.4
Personas
Personas are archetypal representations of intended customers, describing and visualising
their behaviour, values and needs (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 95). When the research
is ﬁnished, personas can be used to summarise and communicate your ﬁndings and the
customers’ real-life behaviours, values and needs. Personas of customers were derived
from the information and insights found in the survey and interviews. These personas
were validated by festival guests and used as a supporting evaluation tool throughout the
ideation phase.
3.2.5
The Five Whys
The Five Whys approach is a technique that helps you to derive the root problem and its
cause from one or more problems (Serrat, 2017). In any problem-solving process, it is of
utmost importance to be sure that the root problem is identiﬁed as you would otherwise
try to solve the symptoms instead of the actual problem. The Five Whys technique was
used to dig into the identiﬁed problems from our insight and made sure we solved the
root problems. These root problems were the foundation of the ideation process.
3.3
Ideation & Implementation
Stakeholders and potential customers have been in focus throughout the ideation phase.
Tools that involve the customers have been prioritised to get objective insights and val-
idations that could eliminate any bias that we might have as previous festival guests.

==PAGE:26==
Following is a description of each method used during the ideation, validation and imple-
mentation.
3.3.1
List of Requirements
A List of Requirements states the most important characteristics that your design must
meet to be successful (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 103). A structured List of Require-
ments is vital when designing complex products that involve many di↵erent aspects and
the list keeps evolving during the project as the concept becomes more concrete and de-
tailed. Based on RF’s input and the ﬁndings from the inspiration and ideation, a List
of Requirements was drawn up and continuously updated as the requirements could be
detailed and concept proposals could be tested on the requirements.
3.3.2
Brainstorm
Brainstorm prescribes a speciﬁc approach with rules and procedures for generating a large
number of ideas (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 117). Brainstorming can be useful during
each phase of the design process, but especially in the early phases after deﬁning the
design problem. Brainstorming was used in the early phases of the project to generate
a large number of ideas on speciﬁc food types. Throughout the project, unstructured
brainstorms were performed with festival guests and stakeholders and helped us expand
the solution space on di↵erent elements of a food concept at RF.
3.3.3
Business Model Canvas
The Business Model Canvas is a visual tool to discuss the aspects of a business idea,
e.g.
what value a given product gives to whom, how the product is supposed to be
marketed and where the revenue and costs are supposed to come from (Osterwalder &
Pigneur, 2010). The tool can be used to analyse the developing business and identify
strong and weak spots, threats and opportunities (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 105). We
continuously used this tool throughout the project to codify the collected information,
insights and design directions. It helped us grasp the holistic sense of the concept. We
followed the Business Model approach that Stenn (2017, p. 59) suggests pre-startups to
use, ﬁrst identifying the key partners, in this case, Roskilde Festival, and with this basis
our Business Model Canvas continuously explored. In the process of making the Business
Model, we experienced what Franceschelli and Santoro (2017) refer to as Business Model
Innovation where all elements are interconnected and a change in one of the categories
can a↵ect all the others directly or indirectly.
16
==PAGE:27==
3.3.4
Value Proposition Canvas
A Value Proposition Canvas is a systematic approach to understand what customers want
and how to create a value proposition that fulﬁls the customer’s needs (Osterwalder et al.,
2014). The canvas is a visual and structured way to create a product-market ﬁt for a
product or service. We used the Value Proposition Canvas to codify the insight from our
customer research in the Customer Proﬁle. Based on this, we created the Value Map to
deﬁne our concept and updated the canvas based on new insights. Hereby it helped to
understand and identify the potential gain and pains that customers could experience by
interacting with our concept.
3.3.5
Prototyping and Evaluation
Prototyping and Evaluation is a method that helps you to simulate and test how people
will experience future interactions with your design (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 131).
It helps you to evaluate concepts at an early stage of development, facilitating quick
learning cycles during concept development.
We have used low-ﬁdelity prototypes of
menus, concepts and services to gain continuous feedback throughout the project.
3.3.6
User Observations
User Observations help you to study what your intended users do in a speciﬁc situation
(van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 45). Watching people interacting with your prototype will
help you to improve your prototypes and understand phenomena, inﬂuential variables
or other elementary interrelations in real life.
We have used observation in our test,
especially in our menu test with eight customers in FoodLab’s kitchen. The observations
were combined with interviews to get a better understanding of what was going through
the participant’s mind and to provide knowledge that would otherwise have been tacit.
The collected knowledge was codiﬁed and could be discussed internally in the team after
the test was completed.
3.3.7
Customer Journey Mapping
Customer Journey Mapping is a graphical representation of the stages a customer goes
through while experiencing the use of a product or service (van Boeijen et al., 2014,
p. 53). It helps you consider these complex experiences and design products and services
that generate value for both the user and the organisations that provide these experi-
ences. We used this method to identify all processes in our concept and the mapping
helped highlight critical stages and thereby identify improvement areas to streamline the
customer experience. Hereby, we were able to match expectations internally in the group
17
==PAGE:28==
and communicate the concept and its context with stakeholders. This method was mod-
iﬁed and used to identify all the stages a customer goes through at RF. From this basis,
Written Scenarios were used to go into depth with each stage and identify touchpoints
that would improve the user experience.
3.3.8
Written Scenario
A Written Scenario tells a story about your intended users in a speciﬁc situation and the
story brings your design and intended users to life in a speciﬁc context (van Boeijen et al.,
2014, p. 99). In the early stages of the design process, this method can be used to develop
user criteria for interaction with a product/service and reﬂect on a developed concept.
We used Written Scenarios to describe our persona’s interaction with our concept and
hereby we could test out di↵erent scenarios. This method highlighted areas where our
concept had weak spots and did not perform well.
3.3.9
Strategy Canvas
The Strategy Canvas is a visual representation of what consumers think about products
or brands (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 89). The Strategy Canvas provides valuable input
for segmentation, di↵erentiation, positioning and can analyse existing, new products and
potential new ones. Relevant product attributes were identiﬁed and competitors were
compared based on the insight from the customer research. As our concept matured we
could place SelvSkab on the canvas to identify the competitive gaps.
3.3.10
Morphology Chart
The Morphology Chart helps you to generate principal solutions analytically and sys-
tematically (van Boeijen et al., 2014, p. 121). The starting point is a break down of
the overall product function into means and the chart enables you to describe possible
principal solutions by combining means for each function. We have used this approach
to explore possible combinations of functions to diverge and generate di↵erent setups for
our concept.
3.3.11
Weighted Objectives Method
The Weighted Objectives Method (WOM) is an evaluation method for comparing design
concepts based on the overall value of each design concept (van Boeijen et al., 2014,
p. 151). WOM assigns scores to the degree to which a design alternatives satisﬁes a
criterion and sum up the scores of all the criteria into numerical values for each design
alternative. WOM gave us a way to choose between setups generated from our morphology
chart based on the importance of each criterion we had selected. With this method, we
got an indication about which concepts performed well and which did not.

Interviews
The participants for the qualitative interviews had all volunteered through the survey.
We chose to exclude all personal acquaintances to remove priming to the highest degree
possible. In total 9 subjects were interviewed with an age span from 20-33 years
The interview guide was tested on three subjects and their reactions and feedback were

The insights
gathered from these interviews, along with supporting data from the survey, are seen
as an indication of general trends, tendencies and preferences on RF. Key insight and
interesting ﬁndings from the 9 individual interviews are described in the following.
Best aspect of the food at RF
Unanimously the participants valued the great diversity that RF food o↵ers and the fact
that almost all of the food stalls have a decent level of quality.
Diet
The majority of the respondent’s everyday diet was healthy with a focus on eating varied.
Some respondents also focused on a sustainability diet. All participants mentioned that
on the festival a similar diet was found diﬃcult to follow. It also seemed like keeping a
strict diet was not as important on RF, compared to eating at home. Daniel Frykman,
an interviewee, put it this way:
29
==PAGE:41==
“Roskilde Festival is the Christmas time of summer”
This indicates that it is not important to the festival guests if the food is healthy but
rather if it is unique, environmentally sustainable and delicious.
Meal purchases
The festival guests buy on average 1-2 meals from food stalls each day. When buying
meals, the interviewees prioritised ﬁlling, delicious meals with value for money.
This
does not apply when certain meals have become traditions, when food experiences are
too tempting or when the friend group all go to a speciﬁc food stall. They bring home
brought food and snacks to eat for the remaining daily meals. The characteristics of the
home brought food are that it has to be easy and durable to store in a tent and can be
turned into meals quickly.
RF guests buy many ready-to-eat meals compared to the average Danish citizen. It is
only 20% of the Danish population that buys fast food 1+ times a week. It is clear that
RF guests’ habits di↵er from those at home. Some of the main reasons for this di↵erence
is according to the interviews: level of e↵ort needed to cook, laziness and time dedication.
Economy
Approximately half of the interviewees said they were price sensitive.
This was not
necessarily reﬂected by the price they were willing to pay for a full meal in the following
situations: trying something new, peer pressure or not getting lost from friends. When
asked how much they wanted and expected to pay for a ﬁlling meal, their answers covered
the following range: 30-120DKK.
Food stalls - what made you choose or not choose a food stall?
Reasons for choosing or avoiding a food stall were manyfold and subjective. Emphasised
reasons in favour of the food stalls were; word of mouth from friends or strangers, online
forums, newspaper articles mentioning food stalls and explicit signage at the food stalls.
On the other hand, long queues, unhygienic eating areas and uninviting surroundings
were reasons for not choosing a food stall.
Preparation spectrum
We explained the premise of our concept, customer involving cooking with provided ingre-
dients and asked to what degree they wanted the ingredients to be prepared beforehand
in such a scenario. We gave them an example of the preparation spectrum of an onion:
• Pre-chopped: The onion is peeled, chopped and ready to use
• Semi-prepared: The onion is peeled
30
==PAGE:42==
• Untouched: The onion is completely untouched
There were arguments for all levels. Two common perceptions were important for all the
interviewees: they wanted to feel part of the cooking process and have some inﬂuence
on the meal; they wanted to reduce the cooking time. Some interviewees prioritised the
cooking experience, one neglected time and wanted to use 2+ hours cooking, while some
prioritised the reduced time consumption around 15-30 minutes.
The preparation level is important as this a↵ects several aspects of the concept. E.g. a
high preparation level of the ingredients can:
• Reduce cooking time
• Reduce customer tasks
• Increase the price of meal kits
• Increase storage requirements as prepared ingredients have to be cooled
• Increase food waste as all the prepared ingredients have to be sold or used

Regulations
We contacted the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (FVST) to validate if

• Smooth, washable table surfaces

4.2.1
Exploring the menu
This section spans from the initiation of the project to a meeting with RF where we
deﬁned a type of menu to be served at SelvSkab. We wanted to diverge the solution
space and explore which menu types that could satisfy the needs of the customer and still
comply with the requirements from RF.
37
==PAGE:49==
We prioritised the type of menu as the ﬁrst thing to be deﬁned, an aspect of our concept
that only had a price restriction. A ﬁxed menu type would narrow the solution space for
the other parts of SelvSkab, thus making it easier to make critical decisions when deﬁning
those. Therefore we deemed the type of menu to be the most important and prioritised

4.2.1.1
Exploring meal types
RF had not yet given any restrictions regarding a heating source except that it should
reduce electricity usage as much as possible, thus we explored menus suitable for BBQ,
stoves and sous vide. With a simple morphology chart, we had a systematic approach
to the menu type exploration. We deﬁned four menu types with di↵erent parameters,
e.g. cold, hot, minimum cooking time, a high workload for customers and conducted a
brainstorm to explore the dishes suited to the four menu types.
To go from types of menus to speciﬁc dishes we evaluated the dishes by their uniqueness
at the festival, their alignment with the needs of festival guests and the requirements of
RF. It was important for us to choose at least one dish from each category. The dishes
chosen to investigate are presented below along with our considerations.
• Heatless:
– Fresh Spring rolls: preparing a spring roll becomes an activity, does not require
much heating, requires much table space to prepare and eat (same for wraps
and pita), not ideal for a festival, can be perceived as unhygienic
• Fast serving:
– Burrito: low availability at the festival, easy and fun to create, to-go friendly,
does not require much heating, requires much table space to prepare and eat.
• Semi-prepared meal kits:
– Pizza: low availability on the festival, rapid cooking, heating source can use
ﬁrewood or gas instead of electricity, serves one person at a time
• Slow serving:
– Pasta dish: easy to cook, familiar to many festival guests, a high quantity for
money, can be made in one pot, good for groups
– Risotto: good quantity for money, it takes longer to cook, can be made in one
pot, good as a group meal
Recipes for the selected dishes were found online and the economic feasibility behind
the dishes was investigated to compare the cost of the ingredients with the target of 35-

margins are preferable. Below are the costs per dish per person, see Appendix H for cost

4.2.1.2
Lead Candidate - Little Italy
We quickly became fond of the idea to provide gourmet pizza kits, a concept we called

and modular which would be beneﬁcial for our Cost Structure. Powering the pizza ovens
by natural gas or wood pellets would make the concept independent from the power grid
at RF.
The Little Italy scenario:
A festival guest chooses between three pizza kits (one with meat and two without meat).
All the needed ingredients are prepared and chopped beforehand and are included in
the kit. The festival guest rolls out the pizza dough, add the tomato sauce, cheese and
toppings and puts the pizza in a pizza oven.
• Pros:
– Short cooking time - 1 pizza per minute per oven
– Universally liked dish
– High-quality pizza because of pizza ovens
– Can use gas or ﬁrewood instead an electricity
– Few kitchen utilities needed for preparation as all the ingredients are prepared
beforehand
– Di↵erent variations can be made from the same base
– High potential to be used outside a festival settings
• Cons:
39
==PAGE:51==
– Requires high turnover to cover high equipment costs
– High workload to prepare ingredient to support the required high turnover
– An open ﬁre can be dangerous in a festival setting
– Pizza ovens waste energy if not used
– No real kitchen is needed and therefore there is no kitchen available for non-
customers
This menu would fulﬁl the following requirements from RF, see to which degree below.
The high compliance requirements outweighed the low compliance requirements.
We
especially saw the opportunity to be independent of the power grid as a major beneﬁt.
This would also be beneﬁcial with regards to the location of the concepts at the festival.
Based on this, Little Italy became our main track.

4.2.2.7
Green energy
We have evaluated di↵erent green energy options that are commonly used in Denmark:
wind energy, solar energy and carbon o↵setting. Following are our conclusions on the
three options.
60
==PAGE:72==
Wind energy
A private windmill costs between 30.000DKK and 400.000DKK (Petersson, 2014). Small
private windmills can produce between 6-16.000 kWh depending on their location and
distance between trees and bushes providing a windbreak. The RF area has many wind-
breaks and therefore the location is not optimal for smaller windmills.
According to
the wind map, provided by Erhvervsstyrelsen, the wind in the RF area is low (Er-
hvervsstyrelsen, 2019)). We conclude that temporary windmills are not a suitable option
for RF mainly because of the high entry price, regulations and setup.
Solar energy
A 1kW solar cell system in Denmark produces approximately 1000 kWh in a year (Kli-
maEnergi, 2017). A 300 Watt solar cell panel costs approximately 3000DKK (Viva Energi,
n.d.) and can produce 300 kWh per year or 5.75 kWh during RF (6 operating days).
Dishwashing for 360 groups with a dishwasher requires ⇡42kWh, section 4.2.2.6. This
requires 41/5.75 ⇡7 solar panels which equal approximately 21.000DKK. This omits
cooking the menus, the energy storage, discharge equipment, wiring, storage outside
RF’s time frame or the setup of the equipment. These quick calculations showed us that
solar cells are not a viable solution when SelvSkab is only operating 6 days per year.
Carbon o↵setting
The investigated options are not economically feasible for the festival’s time frame and
would signiﬁcantly increase the cost and complexity of our facilities. Using the existing
power grid that is established each year at RF is the cheapest option and would not
directly impact our Cost Structure. We evaluate that carbon o↵setting would be the
recommended option to use for SelvSkab.

Chapter 5
Discussion & Reﬂection
With this thesis, we have laid the foundation for a new type of food o↵ering to take
place at RF 2020. Design thinking and business models have guided the development
process and especially the value-creating elements of these approaches have been in fo-
cus. By prioritising value over proﬁt SelvSkab will not be able to operate without eco-
nomic support, as-is. If the concept becomes a success at RF2020 it might indicate that
customer-involving food concepts can thrive beyond RF as well.
SelvSkab is not fully matured and lacks detailing, yet we have gained knowledge to answer
the problem statement and research questions presented in the introduction of this thesis.
5.1
Problem Statement
In an occupied food sector at Roskilde Festival, how do you create a food
concept that is economically, environmentally and socially sustainable?
We have created a food concept for Roskilde Festival that is environmentally and socially
sustainable. This was possible through collaboration with RF and COL which has given
SelvSkab advantages, e.g.
a location in the heart of the camping area.
It has been
possible to create a unique, sustainable food concept at RF even though there are many
well-regarded food stalls already. The social aspects set SelvSkab apart from other food
stalls. By embracing environmental sustainability as part of a food concept it becomes
a competitive advantage instead of a burden (Nidumolu et al., 2015).
Before testing
the food concept on RF2020 we are unable to determine if SelvSkab is economically
sustainable. The social and sustainable values align with RF’s future food and current
social strategies that strengthen their incentive to fund SelvSkab.

5.1.1
Research Questions [1-6]
1. Can Design Thinking be used to create a food innovation concept for RF?
Design Thinking is not a tool that only works for one kind of product or concept devel-
opment. In this project, we have followed Brown’s 2008 model of a Design Thinking, see
ﬁgure 1, to guide us through the development of SelvSkab. If we look at the model it is
clear that it encourages iterations which can be seen in the curvy path through the model.
The iterative process extends the development phase but makes sure that all decisions
are taken on a solid basis.
Brown (2008) emphasises the importance of making tangible prototypes, which can be
both for products and services. In this project, we have made many explorations that we
see as immaterial prototypes like the Written Scenarios, see section 4.2.3.2. Prototyping
has been essential to evaluate food-related ideas without using food ingredients.
We have certainly beneﬁted from collecting and using the customer input continuously
in the project as guidance and validation that we were headed the right way. To get
these inputs we found it necessary to sometimes have the top-down, expert approach
that Menichelli et al. (2012) mention is common in food innovation. Speciﬁcally when
Roberto Flore and ourselves chose the six dishes for the menu selection test, a fully
customer empathetic Design Thinking approach was not followed.
Olsen (2015, p. 183) states, “Design Thinking perceives consumer insights as the point of
departure for the whole development process, the contemporary food science view per-
ceives consumer’s voice as a validation of the expert’s voice”. It is hard to know if this
project was started by an expert’s or a consumer’s voice as we see ourselves as being both
product developers (experts) and festival guests (consumers).
Brown (2008) emphasises the importance of collaboration in the development process.
The collaboration with RF was extremely important but it would have been beneﬁcial
to have a more dynamic and close collaboration during the project as this would have
increased the pace of progression.
2. Can sustainable, customer involving cooking be a part of RF?
Our research and the festival guests interest in FoodJam shows that customer-involving
cooking has its place at RF. Roskilde Festival is pushing a sustainability agenda onto
the food stalls of the festival with requirements like 90% organic ingredients and Carbon-
Cloud evaluation of meals. This indicates that sustainable cooking is already happening
and its importance will increase in the future. SelvSkab is used as an example to answer
89
==PAGE:102==
how sustainability and customer-involving cooking can coexist in a food concept.
The three pillars of sustainability (social, environmental and economic) have no single
point of origin and it is not possible to ﬁnd a theoretically rigorous description in the
literature though it is commonly ubiquitous (Purvis et al., 2019). Our focus of the aspects
is listed in their respective sections below.
Social sustainability
Focus: enhancing communities and social well-being
SelvSkab contributes to social sustainability by including the customers in the cooking
process thereby encouraging teamwork and good festival spirit. By having a low price
point for the meals we also allow the community to eat great meals no matter if you are
a low or high-income festival guest.
Environmental sustainability
Focus: reduce carbon footprint.
By serving vegan and vegetarian dishes we reduce our carbon footprint (Larsen, 2014).
The selected dishes require little energy to cook. Meals are cooked for one group at a
time hereby no food is prepared or cooked without having a hungry customer demand
it. This ensures that minimal food waste is created. The dishes are designed for groups
to ensure that bigger batches are cooked to reduce the footprint of each serving, even
though not in portion sizes as big as Spaghetti 60. A dishwasher is also used instead of
conventional dishwashing methods to lower the amount of heated water.
Economic
Focus: long-term growth without negatively a↵ecting social and environmental aspects.
Testing our food concept on RF2020 will show if SelvSkab can operate without economic
funding, with the current sales estimates that were limited by RF, it can not. With
the current facilities, we believe that SelvSkab has the potential to sell more dishes and
reduce the ingredient costs, thereby increasing the proﬁt margin of our revenue. This can
lead to growth and long-term economic sustainability. If RF commits to run SelvSkab at
future festivals, it will be to enhance their Value Proposition to the festival guests rather
than have an extra source of income. In this scenario, SelvSkab becomes a piece of the
puzzle that makes Roskilde Festival economically sustainable.
3. Can the price of a meal be reduced by involving customers in the cooking
process?
Involving the festival guests in the cooking process does not per se reduce the price of
a meal.
As every meal is cooked for 2 or 4 persons at a time, SelvSkab’s potential
90
==PAGE:103==
output does not compare to a conventional food stall such as Spaghetti 60, who sold
approximately 28.000 dishes in 2018 (Soelberg, 2018). Due to the low turnover, the one-
time costs take up a larger part of the total costs, making it necessary to have a high
proﬁt margin on dishes sold. The reduced price of a meal at SelvSkab is possible because
RF is willing to risk an economic loss.
4. Can low energy kitchens be created in a festival context?
It is possible to reduce the energy consumed in the kitchen by choosing the right meals,
especially by serving ingredients that do not require to be heated or cooked. The best way
to reduce the energy consumed would be to cook large portions that require no heating
thus reducing the cooking energy and reducing the dirty dishes to servings ratio.
It is hard to make a festival kitchen that does not consume power as hot water for dirty
dishes is required in any food stall at the festival. By introducing a dishwasher we were
able to signiﬁcantly reduce the electric energy used per served meal.
Roskilde Festival is a temporary event which limits the implementation of green energy
solutions.
Solar cells and windmills are expensive to buy and set up, making these
options unfeasible for temporary events, and would have little to no use before and after
the festival. We suggest carbon o↵setting as an approach that would help to neutralise
the electric energy consumed by SelvSkab though this will a↵ect the Cost Structure.
5. Can a business model approach be used to design a sustainable food con-
cept on RF?
We followed the recommended steps for a pre-startup suggested by Stenn (2017). We
initiated the project by reaching out to RF that are our Key Partners, after which the
Value Proposition and Customer Segment was detailed. Our process di↵ered from Stenn’s
2017 suggested steps as our Value Proposition and Revenue Stream was described in the
innovation brief sent to RF. This shows that nothing is cut in stone when developing a
concept with a business model approach.
By using the BMC actively throughout the process with sustainability in mind we had
an SBMI process because it satisﬁes the two requirements that Geissdoerfer et al. (2018)
deﬁnes as an SBMI: 1) sustainable development or positive, respectively reduced, negative
impacts for the environment, society, and the long-term prosperity of the organisation
and its stakeholders or 2) adopting solutions or characteristics that foster sustainability
in its value proposition, creation, and capture elements or its value-network, see section
2.2.1.

6. Can public accessible and customer-driven kitchens make it convenient to
make home-cooked meals in public environments outside a festival setting?
It is hard to deﬁne what convenience is, as convenience means di↵erent things to di↵erent
people. Currently, RF o↵ers a barbeque where you can cook food yourself. This makes
it more convenient to cook your food than without the barbeque. We have designed a
concept that makes it even more convenient to make a meal at RF. If the baseline of con-
venience cooking is a meal cooked in a private home, then SelvSkab can easily compare
conveniency-wise. At home, you would have to ﬁnd the recipe, make sure that you have
all the ingredients and then cook everything whereas at SelvSkab you can go there, get a
kit and then cook the meal.
Let us consider a non-festival context. The relatively high time consumption when cooking
at SelvSkab would probably not be suitable in many public places as people’s mindset are
di↵erent than at RF. We believe that Little Italy would be more suitable for a non-festival
context. The time needed to prepare and cook a pizza is less than the Asian Noodle Salad
and thereby it can become a high-quality fast food meal that you can cook yourself. For
SelvSkab we want to deliver raw and uncut ingredients because the festival guests have
more time to cook a meal from scratch. If SelvSkab moved outside a festival context, the
time aspect would be important to reconsider.
5.2
How is this thesis important?
This project shows that there is a need for a supplement to the food o↵erings at Roskilde
Festival and that RF guests are interested in new concepts and activities on the festival.
SelvSkab is an opportunity for Roskilde Festival to re-establish great cooking options for
guests of the festival and o↵er the guests an even more unique festival experience than
before. SelvSkab is intended to become an integrated part of the festival and with this
thesis, the foundation for this to happen has been established. If the concept proves to
be a success at RF, the idea of involving the customer in cooking meals might also be
applicable outside of festival context.
Olsen (2015) argues that a Design Thinking process often leads to incremental innovation
because customers inputs, within a speciﬁc framework, are collected, i.e the customers
are not always able to think outside the box. On the other hand, she argues that Design
Thinking occasionally can lead to radical innovation, as developers can innovate with a
basis on surprising observations and ﬁndings obtained from customers. If SelvSkab be-
comes successful at RF2020 we hope to create a radical change in the way people eat and
drink at RF.

The customers have been an invaluable asset in the major decisions of the concept such as
the menu, the cooking, the seating areas, the location, the preparation level of ingredients
and the physical layout. By using relatively simple, rapid and inexpensive tests it has
been possible to get the customer’s feedback dynamically. This process shows that Design
Thinking can be an integral part of developing a food concept for a festival setting such
as RF.
5.3
Limitations of the thesis
The insights gained through the report are RF-speciﬁc, which limits the possibility to
apply the insights generally. Di↵erent preferences, pain points and desires than those
found in this thesis might be valid outside a festival context. Therefore SelvSkab has to
be reevaluated and in part redesigned before taken outside RF.
RF might not be interested in SelvSkab serving too many people as they could lose rev-
enue from other food stalls. If SelvSkab was independent of the festival it had to apply to
get a food stall and the unique location in COL would also be lost. Furthermore, SelvSkab
is dependent on the sta↵and the organisers to work voluntarily. To make SelvSkab a
more versatile food concept it needs to eliminate the current dependency on RF.
SelvSkab has been tailored to Roskilde Festival and has only been tested in a look-alike
setting. It is therefore hard to know how it will perform at the festival. It is hard to see if
the Design Thinking and business model approach used to create SelvSkab is successful
as it has not yet been tested at the festival. By the end of RF2020, the assumption that
the process has been successful will be validated or disproven.
5.4
Contribution to theory
This project is a practical example of how a Design Thinking approach can be used
in the process of developing a Customer Empathetic food venture. Applying a Design
Thinking methodology to create holistic, customer-involving concepts in the food sector
at temporary events, such as RF, can generate increased value for both the customers
and stakeholders.
Normally the food industry is guided by sensory experts who make decisions on behalf
of the customers. Our social and sustainable food business’ scope is disregarding the
sensory panels and the expertise of focus groups to develop a food business that goes
93
==PAGE:106==
beyond the taste and smell of a meal. By using a Sustainable Business Model approach
with a Design Thinking foundation a developer can assure a product-market ﬁt.
The importance of concept development with prototyping and testing is emphasised in
this thesis. Starting a venture in a temporary event setting is a process that presents the
developers with new challenges daily and requires them to make quick decisions based on
varying levels of information. Design Thinking’s iterative and customer-minded approach
keeps food startups agile, which enables them to handle changing scopes and helps them
develop and mature projects at a fast pace. These features cannot be achieved by an
expert based and linear development process. The fail fast-prototyping can be utilised by
a start-up to test assumptions and decisions inexpensively. By accepting that iterations
are part of a good and thorough development process, developers will be more willing to
kill their darlings. It also becomes easier to accept and approve of rapid changing inputs
that inﬂuences and drastically changes the direction of the concept.
Collaboration is a cornerstone of Design Thinking (Brown, 2008). This report shows how
the network in a design context can be used to improve the value a business creates.
In a development project, it is important to remember that you as a developer, should
delegate responsibilities and use the resources of stakeholders.
5.5
Process
Design Thinking appraises close collaboration between the customer and the developer.
We saw the festival guests as the customers and tried to include them in vital deci-
sions through the project.
Looking back, we should also have seen RF as customers
as they could have given a more thorough insight into the ideation phase. This could
have initiated more dynamic discussions about the direction of the concept and remove
any mismatching expectations. At times, we worked on something that could have been
approved or disapproved in a ﬁve minute face-to-face conversation. Little Italy is an
example of this, which was our lead candidate but did not suit RF’s expectations. The
most important learning from this is to have a dynamic and ongoing conversation with
essential stakeholders and customers. This can accelerate the progress of developing a
startup.
We experienced that having a Customer Empathetic approach can slow down a devel-
opment process. The input gained from customers are seldom streamlined and insights
gathered in the middle and end of the process can sometimes inﬂuence the projects start-
ing point. Diverging insights are part of the reason that a development process becomes
iterative.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The result of this thesis is SelvSkab, a customer-involving food concept developed in col-
laboration with Roskilde Festival (RF). The development process was conducted with a
Design Thinking and Business Model approach that especially focuses on value creation
based on customer insights.
Our research shows that the food prices at the current food stalls force many guests
to bring simple food and snacks to the festival. The brought food is often low quality
and is typically prepared in unhygienic conditions. A few designated cooking areas can
be found at the camping sites, but these require the festival guest to bring ingredients,
cooking stoves, kitchen utensils and seating equipment. Despite this inconvenience, the
guests still cook. Festival guests have voiced a need for better options to cook and get
ingredients during the festival.
SelvSkab is an open kitchen run in collaboration with RF. The kitchen enables festival
guests to cook food together in proper conditions. Meal kits are sold next to the kitchen
for 35DKK per person and juice-kits are sold for 15DKK per person. The open kitchen
allows everyone to use the facilities, with or without meal-kits.
RF is a festival that values social, environmental and economic sustainability. SelvSkab
embraces these values by enhancing collaboration, social well-being and reducing CO2
emissions to a minimum. The current sales estimates of SelvSkab show that it will not
generate proﬁt at RF2020 though it is hard to know before testing. If RF commits to
run SelvSkab in the future, it will be to enhance their Value Proposition rather than to
create an extra source of income. Hereby, SelvSkab will contribute to making RF itself
economically sustainable.
RF2020 will be SelvSkabs ﬁrst year in operation to test the concept and its feasibility. The
total costs approximate 60.000DKK and the revenue will be approximately 30.000DKK.
96
==PAGE:109==
SelvSkab will sell 686 meal kits and 628 juice kits the ﬁrst year. These sales estimates
are conservative and utilise 17% of the kitchens maximum capacity. At 41% capacity
utilisation, SelvSkab will have a return of investment within one festival, and at 100% it
will generate 64.000 DKK in proﬁts.
RF is currently reviewing the concept and its budget. We are waiting for their ﬁnal
approval to make SelvSkab a reality. We strongly believe that there is a potential for
SelvSkab and customer-involving cooking to thrive at RF and sustainably unite festival
guests.

Appendix B - Innovation Brief
Råkilden 
Normal madlavning i en unormal hverdag 
Vi er to Design & Innovations studerende fra DTU som skal påbegynde et speciale projekt til 
efteråret. Vores studieretning sætter i høj grad fokus på brugerinvolvering under udvikling af 
projekter. Derudover er vi også øvede i føre projekter ud i livet og gøre dem til en realitet. Vi er 
begge hyppige Roskilde Festivals deltager​ ​og nyder at komme år efter år. Igennem vores 
erfaring på Roskilde Festival har vi også fået en del ideer, til optimering af oplevelsen. 
 
Mad er en vigtig del af det daglige Roskilde liv, og mulighederne er næsten uendelige for 
festivalsdeltageren med en tung pengepung. For festivalsdeltageren med den lette pengepung, 
er realiteten en anden. Denne realitet kan være at fylde rejserygsækken til bristepunktet med 
mad- og råvarer eller at alt maden kan nedprioriteres, hvilket medfører at man som deltager ikke 
får nok at spise. 
 
Folkekøkkenet i Dream City giver adgang til køkkenfaciliteter som åbner op for potentielt store 
madoplevelser. ​Vi oplever desværre ikke at faciliteterne udnyttes til fulde og at det ofte er 
hurtige løsninger som instant nudler der tilberedes her. 
 
Derfor vil vi starte en kamp mod de dominerende instant nudler, ved at gøre friske råvarer og 
muligheden for tilberedning, tilgængelige i campingområdet i højere grad end de er nu. Vores 
vision er at lave steder for de almene festivalsdeltagere, hvor man kan udnytte gode faciliteter 
og samtidig have adgang til de råvarer, der er nødvendig for at lave et sundt og nærende måltid. 
Vi er blevet inspireret, af blandt andre, Aarstiderne som leverer en madplan med de nødvendige 
råvarer samt en opskrift og vil gerne benytte os af denne innovative form for madlavning i en 
festivalskontekst. 
  
Med vores koncept skal festivalsdeltageren ikke bekymre sig om at få plads til ris, 
spidskommen, tahin og solsikkeolie, da vi vil tilbyde måltidspakker. Således kan 
festivalsdeltagerne få en pause fra rusfesten og i fællesskab gå i gang med at lave mad til sig 
selv, vennerne i campen eller nogle helt nyfundne kammerater. 
 
Da specialet bliver afviklet over ca 5 måneder vil der ikke i løbet af selve specialeprojektet være 
mulighed for at teste konceptet under reelle omstændigheder, men det vil være muligt at teste 
et brugsscenarie, hvor interesserede vil blive inviteret for at afprøve og komme med feedback.  
 
Vi synes umiddelbart at denne form for fælles madlavning kunne være interessant at 
implementere på Roskilde Festival, da I sætter værdier som fællesskab, bæredygtighed og aktiv 
deltagelse højt. Derfor spørger vi jer om i kunne have interesse i at lave et samarbejde for at få 
sådan et projekt til at blive en realitet? Vi er meget åbne overfor at indgå i en dialog om hvordan 
selve projektet og projektrammerne kunne se ud og vil meget gerne have jeres feedback. 
Hvis i har nogle spørgsmål er i mere end velkomne til at kontakte os!

Appendix D - Survey Summary

A survey was conducted to learn about how the festival guests perceive food, cooking 
and habits on RF. The survey was shared through groups associated with RF on 
Facebook to reach a diversity of people; DreamCity Dreamers, Roskilde Festival, 
Vegetarer og veganere til Roskilde Festival and the post was shared through several 
people of different age groups. 111 people answered the survey and the data was 
cleaned and crunched. The survey gives some indication to which pain points the 
festival guests have, but also which areas are doing very well.  
 
The demographics of the respondents are not representing the average festival guest 
at RF. The average age of the respondents of the survey was 26,5 while the true 
average on Roskilde Festival is 24. The distribution of male/female is respectively 
65%/35% while the true distribution is 47%/53%. This is not ideal but can be a result of 
the network in which the survey has been shared  
 
The answers to the question​ “What could improve your general food experience at 
Roskilde festival?”​ was divided into six categorized: Cheaper prices, Shorter queues, 
Better quality, More Sustainability, Better seating facilities, More vegetarian options.  
 
The answers indicate that lowering the prices of meals could be a major improvement 
to the food. A reason for this pain point, seems to be the correlation between the 
portion size and the meal price. Some people see this as an issue while others do not. 
Therefore there is not always a consistency between price and portion:

The Executive Summary (a short business plan) 
Business idea  
Vision: Our vision is to provide quality ingredients and tools that empower anyone to cook in environments 
otherwise unsuited for cooking 
● A description of the problem your customers have 
In environments unsuited for cooking, people can be forced to buy unhealthy or expensive food as no options 
to prepare and cook meals are available. Case: At Roskilde Festival, the guests have no possibility to store 
ingredients and miss the facilities to prepare and cook their own meals in an easy and simple manner. 
Therefore, meals bought from the expensive food stalls becomes the easiest, but not the cheapest or 
healthiest option.  
● Your solution (your product or service ) 
Our solution to this problem is to facilitate a balance between home cooked and prepared meals. By designing 
the whole cooking experience we create a simple and easy process where anyone can participate. We take the 
responsibility of logistics out of our users hands but lets them have power over the execution of the meals they 
are preparing and eating. Thus, our solution only requires time and engagement from the customers and 
therefore our offer is a unique quick-service dining experience. Pizza Case: At Roskilde festival we will serve 3 
different kinds of pizzas. The only task of the customer will be to roll out the dough, add the ingredients and 
bake it on the grill, a process that takes less than 15 minutes.  
● Business model (how you make money) 
We are offering a package solution that will provide both facilities and ingredients. We will generate revenue 
by selling our ingredients that provide everything customers need to cook a meal. Furthermore, we could 
generate revenue by renting our facilities to events or festivals. 
Market 
Our target market are price sensitive people that attend events or festivals that also is concerned with food 
quality.  The first customers we will target are the festival guests on Roskilde Festival. 130.000 guests are 
attending Roskilde Festival each year. 80.000 have a full festival ticket and 30.000 are volunteers and these two 
groups are our main target. If we assume that each guest is eating twice a day during the week there is a total 
of 1,3 million meals served. We estimate that we will serve 1% of these meals which means that we will sell 
13.000 servings during Roskilde Festival 
● Competitive advantage 
Our competitors are food stalls at events or festivals. By including the customers in the cooking, we give them a 
unique sense of ownership, pride and inclusiveness of the food itself.  Furthermore, the process of preparing 
and cooking a meal create social interaction that are otherwise not present when queuing buying ready made 
food from a food stall. The throughput of the system will be restricted by how many meal kits can be sold over 
the counter at the shop. By using a pop-up kitchen principle or a food trucks, we can cut down expenses 
regarding rent. 
Roadmap (Deliverables): 
2 months: Concept, business model, exact menu to serve at the first generation facility are finalized.
