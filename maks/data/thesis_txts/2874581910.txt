Abstract 
 
This study investigates the performance of a two-blade Vertical Axis Wind Turbine (VAWT) by employing an 
actuator line model (ALM)  and diΤ·erent lattice Boltzmann solvers. Sensitivity tests are conducted to optimize 
the model, comparing the numerical outputs with experimental data from Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) 
obtained from TU Delft's Open Jet Wind Tunnel Facility. The results indicate that the SimpliΟΜed and Highly 
Stable Lattice Boltzmann Method (SHSLBM) outperforms the BGK and MRT collision operators in terms 
of accuracy and stability, particularly evident at high Reynolds numbers, albeit at the expense of increased 
computational time. 
The ALM facilitates performance and load predictions of the rotor without fully resolving the ΟΜow around the 
airfoil, thereby signiΟΜcantly reducing computational time. Further sensitivity tests reΟΜne the kernel width of the 
ALM, with speciΟΜc settings necessary due to grid resolution limitations. The model simulates the rotor under 
various operating conditions, adapting to diΤ·erent rotational speeds.  
While acknowledging the need for further enhancements, the model eΤ·ectively establishes correlations 
between the numerical solutions and observed values, especially for the normal force coeΤ·icient. Highlighting 
its advantages, including reduced computational time and parallelization capabilities, the lattice Boltzmann 
solver emerges as a prominent tool for VAWT simulations, presenting a competitive alternative to traditional 
CFD methods.

1. Introduction  
 
 
1.1. 
Vertical Axis Wind Turbines 
Although Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWTs) dominate the wind energy sector, Vertical Axis Wind Turbines 
(VAWTs) have emerged as an alternative, with potential advantages in speciο§¹c applications. Unlike HAWTs, 
VAWTs feature a vertically oriented rotor axis, allowing blades to capture wind from all directions without 
requiring yaw mechanisms, rendering VAWTs suitable for deployment in urban and decentralized settings. 
Small-scale vertical axis wind turbines are a valid choice for these applications due to their low sensitivity to 
turbulent ο§²ow and the lower acoustic impact than HAWTs [1]. Since these rotors do not need to align with the 
wind direction, a yaw mechanism is absent, thus limiting installation costs. However, VAWT aerodynamics 
presents technical obstacles for performance optimization due to unsteady phenomena such as dynamic stall 
and wake interference eΤ·ects. VAWTs are classiο§¹ed as drag-driven (e.g., Savonius) or lift-driven rotors (e.g., 
Darrieus, H-Darrieus, helical), based on their operational physical principle as depicted in Fig. 1. 
 
 
Fig. 1 Drag-driven and lift-driven VAWTs [1].  
 
VAWTs typically operate at lower tip speed ratios than HAWTs due to the diΤ·erent design and structural 
conο§¹guration. The orthogonal interference of the blades with the wind implies lower aerodynamic performance 
compared to HAWTs, in turn achieving higher lift-to-drag ratios with their twisted blade proο§¹les. Hence, the 
highest performance is achieved at a lower tip speed ratio, somehow limiting the thrust force on the rotor.  
 
 
 
 
Fig. 2 Siemens 4navitas VAWTs [2].  
 
Technical issues involve blade deο§²ection at a high tip speed ratio due to high centrifugal forces and torque 
ripple, typically related to dynamic stall at a low tip speed ratio. Speciο§¹cally, torque ripple accelerates fatigue 
failure [3],[4] and generates shaft vibrations and power oscillations [5]. A limitation in the torque ripple is 
feasible in the design phase by reducing the rotor solidity or using a helical rotor, which eliminates self-starting 
issues but increases blade costs.   
==PAGE:11==
2 
 
Concerning the VAWTs design, Rezaeiha et al. [6] illustrated how the increase in solidity determines a drop in 
the optimum tip speed ratio, with an enhancement in the maximum power converted and thrust force. The 
eΤ·ect of the chord-based Reynolds number was investigated using rotors with the same solidity. By reducing 
the number of blades, the rotor's performance improved due to an increase in the Reynolds number, achieved 
either by enlarging the chord or reducing the diameter. Zanforlin and DeLuca [7] indicated that the eΤ·ect of tip 
losses prevails on the eΤ·ect of chord-based Reynolds number in medium to large-diameter turbines. Since 
reducing the tip losses is more convenient than enhancing the chord-based Reynolds number, a design with 
longer blades is more suitable for improving the maximum power coeΤ·icient. Conversely, changing the turbine 
aspect ratio does not signiΟΜcantly aΤ·ect the performance of small rotors, which are more inΟΜuenced by the 
chord-based Reynolds number. After selecting the shape and solidity of a single rotor, the subsequent step 
involves its simulation using a suitable numerical tool.  
 
 
1.2. 
CFD and lattice Boltzmann method 
Computational ΟΜuid dynamics (CFD) uses numerical techniques and algorithms to solve mathematical 
problems related to ΟΜuid dynamics and predicts ΟΜow properties such as velocity, pressure, temperature, and 
turbulence in the ΟΜuid domain. A CFD code involves the discretization of the governing equations into a set of 
algebraic equations to be solved on a computational grid iteratively. The approach implemented to discretize 
the solution involves ΟΜnite diΤ·erence, ΟΜnite volume, or ΟΜnite element methods. A numerical solution represents 
an approximation of the true solution, with deviations depending on truncation error proportional to the discrete 
time step and spatial step. 
Conversely, particle-based solvers focus on the microscopic or mesoscopic environment and do not solve the 
macroscopic governing equations, but attempt to simulate atoms, molecules, or distributions of molecules. 
The lattice Boltzmann method (LBM) has emerged among particle-based solvers as a mesoscale tool for 
modeling complex ΟΜuid dynamics problems, including those involving incompressible ΟΜows. The lattice 
Boltzmann equation (LBE) originates from the kinetic theory of gases, where microscopic interactions between 
individual particles manifest as macroscopic ΟΜuid behavior. Thanks to the improvements in numerical 
algorithms and parallel computing architectures, the LBM became suitable for various domains such as 
multiphase ΟΜows, porous media transport, and non-Newtonian ΟΜuids. The LBE governs the evolution of a 
probability distribution function f across a discrete lattice, with each node representing a mesoscopic ΟΜuid 
particle. Through collision and streaming processes, f evolves yielding macroscopic ΟΜuid properties such as 
velocity, density, and pressure.  
The Chapman-Enskog analysis, which is not addressed in this study, connects the dynamics of the LBM to the 
macroscopic conservation equations of ΟΜuids, i.e., continuity and Navier-Stokes equations. The LBM 
represents a second-order accurate solver for the weakly compressible Navier-Stokes equation, whose errors 
become signiΟΜcant as the Mach number approaches unity.  
LBM has diΤ·erent advantages compared with CFD. Unlike a typical particle-based method, LBM tracks only the 
distribution of particles rather than the particles themselves. In conventional CFD, the complexity depends on 
the computing derivative approximations across neighboring nodes. In contrast, the complexity of LBM derives 
from the particle description within individual nodes, leading to linear interactions between nodes, while a non-
linearity appears in the collision process within each node. Since the heaviest computations are conΟΜned to 
within nodes, the LBM is suited for high-performance computing on parallel architectures [8], accelerating 
model development and run time. Like pseudo-compressible methods, the LBM achieves scalability by 
allowing artiΟΜcial compressibility [9]. In addition, the LBM oΤ·ers the advantage of simple implementation than 
conventional methods. The LBM is useful to simulate porous media, and, in general, complex mass-conserving 
ΟΜows [8], [9], [10], and moving boundaries [9].

A disadvantage is the higher computer memory required than in CFD  [11]. There are many variables 
(populations) propagating from/to each node, and this makes LBM a memory-intensive tool. In addition, since 
time-dependent, the LBM is more suitable to simulate transient ΟΜows than steady ΟΜows [12]. 
 
 
1.3. 
Linking VAWTs and LBM: Exploring the Actuator Line Model 
The algorithm solves the ΟΜuid dynamics with the LBM module and the Actuator line model (ALM) simulates the 
blade as a line interacting with the ΟΜow so that a force ΟΜeld is developed in the computational domain. The force 
ΟΜeld is generated from the lift and drag forces and momentum exchange and aΤ·ects the ΟΜow ΟΜeld computed by 
the LBM in a time loop. Asmuth et al. [13] validated their CLBM implementation with the actuator line model 
for a horizontal axis wind turbine, demonstrating comparable wake statistics in laminar and turbulent inΟΜow 
conditions. Additionally, the study examines the impact of third-order cumulant limiters, showing their potential 
to improve CLBM simulations with turbulent ΟΜows. Ribeiro and Muscari [14] simulate entire wind farms using 
an actuator line model within a lattice-Boltzmann ΟΜow solver combined with a sliding mesh technique. The 
research shows good agreement between the solutions by implementing this methodology and validating it 
against reference Navier-Stokes simulations. The sliding mesh approach allows for local reΟΜnement around the 
blades and tip vortices, enhancing computational eΤ·iciency and providing potential advantages over traditional 
methods. Rullaud et al. [15] introduced a solver based on the Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) for VAWTs. The 
study validates the LBM/ALM coupling against experimental data and other simulation methods, demonstrating 
promising results without a turbulence model.  
Based on Rullaud's work, three diΤ·erent lattice Boltzmann methods have been developed for simulating the 
behavior of a vertical axis rotor in this thesis. These include two conventional methods based on BGK and MRT 
collision factors, while the last model (SHSLBM), based on the work of Chen and Shu [16], demonstrates high 
stability and accuracy with turbulent ΟΜows. This thesis aims to address the challenge of investigating the 
capability of the Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) to perform reliable and eΤ·icient simulations of VAWT 
performance. Conventional LBM and SimpliΟΜed and Highly Stable (SHS) LBM are examined, with consideration 
given to appropriate forcing schemes and accurate boundary conditions. Through comprehensive testing and 
validation, this work provides schemes for accurately predicting the behavior of VAWTs under stable (high tip 
speed ratio) and unstable (low tip speed ratio) operating conditions. Section 2 explores conventional BGK 
and MRT Boltzmann methods, and Section 3 is dedicated entirely to the SHS lattice Boltzmann method. 
The actuator line model for VAWTs is brieΟΜy introduced in Section 4, while the simulation outputs for the tested 
rotor and conclusions are outlined in Sections 5 and 6, respectively.

2.4. 
Boundary Conditions 
In the bulk ΟΜow, the relationship between mesoscopic populations and macroscopic variables is allowed by 
the density and momentum conservation equations. At the boundaries, the closure condition to resolve the 
population fi with a second-order accurate scheme is fi = fieq+¬Ζfi(1)+O(Υïx2). A parabolic solution (allowed exactly 
in the bulk) is not ensured at the boundaries, because the term fi(1) (ΟΩu) would imply only linear solutions to be 
exact. To allow a second-order exact scheme, the boundary condition must be third-order accurate to include 
fi(2) (ΟΩ2u), i.e., fi = fieq+¬Ζfi(1)+ ¬Ζ2fi(2)+O(Υïx3), according to Ginzburg and d9HumiΟ®res [18].  
In the 2-D ΟΜow domain for VAWTs, inlet, and outlet boundaries are open boundaries. The inlet velocity proΟΜle 
is imposed, while the outlet velocities are extrapolated. The ΟΜow enters from left to right, while symmetry 
boundary conditions are used for the top and bottom boundaries. Additional information about this topic is 
presented in [11], [17].

2.7. 
MRT Collision Operator 
In simulations with the BGK collision operator, stability issues often occur at a large Reynolds number, i.e., 
when viscosity ¬Ω is reduced or the velocity magnitude u is increased to approach a large Re without increasing 
the grid resolution. However, small ¬Ω and large u may cause instability (especially when Ο΅ ΟΑ ¬Ϋ or when u ΟΑ cs). 
On the other hand, accuracy issues in BGK simulations are governed by the relaxation time Ο΅ which relationship 
with ¬Ω is investigated with the Chapman-Enskog analysis. Conversely, velocity and lattice size are connected 
directly to macroscopic quantities. 
With the multiple-relaxation-time (MRT) collision operator, free parameters can be tuned to overcome stability 
or accuracy issues, so that diΤ·erent collision rates are used for each population, i.e., ¬Ϊi = -Ο΄(fi-fieq). The 
collision phase is performed in moment space, which means that populations fi are mapped in moment space 
before the relaxation of moments toward equilibrium. The ΟΜnal step consists of mapping the relaxed moments 
back into population space. Hence, the MRT collision operator allows moments to be relaxed rather than 
populations. 
However, MRT has the drawback of computational overhead compared to the simulations with the BGK 
collision operator evaluated in 15-20% [22]. In addition, the need to test and tune the variables of the relaxation 
matrix may limit its usage, despite the advantage of independently tuning the shear and bulk viscosity. The 
works [23] and [24] demonstrate that an increase in the bulk viscosity (i.e., decreasing its relaxation rate) may 
often enhance numerical stability by damping under-resolved hydrodynamic artifacts.

Stability is checked in conventional CFD using the Courant number which compares the speed of propagation 
of the information in the domain Υïx/Υït with the macroscopic speed |u| of the ΟΜuid ΟΜow. Unstable solutions 
occur if the information does not propagate fast enough, i.e., if Υïx/Υït < |u|. Conversely, unstable solutions are 
due to supplementary parameters in LBM. Numerical instability may appear for solutions growing exponentially 
and leading to NaN values, like truncation errors.

3.4. 
Boundary Conditions 
In the conventional LBM, the proposed schemes introduce errors during the conversion from the distribution 
functions to the macroscopic variables. It does not occur in the SHS method, as the implemented boundary 
conditions directly update the macroscopic variables, such as velocity, momentum, and pressure, making the 
method more eΤ·icient. The boundary conditions for distribution functions in Section 2.4 are converted into 
boundary conditions for macroscopic variables. 
 
 
3.5.

for conventional LBM. Since SHSLBM represents a reconstruction of solutions to the macroscopic equations 
recovered by the conventional LBM, the method preserves the second-order spatial accuracy.

4. Actuator Line Model for VAWTs 
 
SΟΗrensen and Shen [31] introduced ALM as an alternative to both blade-resolved Computational Fluid 
Dynamics (CFD) and Blade Element Momentum (BEM) models for Horizontal Axis Wind Turbines (HAWTs). 
The ALM incorporates lift and drag forces as distributed body forces along a line representing the blade in a 2-
D domain, signiΟΜcantly reducing computational costs by not resolving the boundary layer.  
Shamsoddin and PortΟ©-Agel [32] investigated the wake of a VAWT in the atmospheric boundary layer using 
large-eddy simulation (LES) and an ALM. They analyzed the power coeΤ·icient variations for a 1-MW VAWT and 
identiΟΜed the optimal solidity and tip-speed ratio. The wake analysis revealed maximum velocity deΟΜcits and 
turbulence intensities at speciΟΜc downstream distances, with notable vertical asymmetries in turbulence 
intensity and momentum ΟΜux ΟΜelds. Research also shows the eΤ·ectiveness of coupling ALM with LES for 
advanced applications, such as analyzing near and far wakes [33].  
By applying the ALM model to VAWTs, Bachant et al. [34] noted that RANS ALM tends to overpredict power 
coeΤ·icients at high tip-speed ratios due to unmodeled elements like drag from spokes or limitations in 
aerodynamic data. Martinez-Ojeda et al. Aiming to optimize VAWT wind farms, [35] proposed a hybrid wake 
actuator-cylinder model within an OpenFOAM solver replacing turbines with volumetric forces, reducing the 
computational cost for detailed meshes around the blades. Using the steady-state actuator-cylinder model 
[36], this two-dimensional approach requires minimal computational time and oΤ·ers higher ΟΜdelity than simple 
models by resolving the viscous wake through RANS simulation. Mendoza and Goude [37] investigated the 
wake produced by a VAWT and its interaction with terrain surface roughness using an ALM implemented in 
OpenFOAM. The simulations, validated against experimental data, focus on diΤ·erent terrains and similar inΟΜow 
velocities. The results show the model's ability to reproduce the wake pattern, capturing key ΟΜow parameters, 
vorticity concentrations, and turbulence production.  
Based on the experiences described in the previous literature, the following Actuator Line Model is developed. 
It relies on the 2-D blade element theory, constructed on the interpolation of lift and drag coeΤ·icient data from 
static angles of attack, and on an isotropic Gaussian distribution of forces within the computational domain.

5.6. 
Simulations with BGKLBM and MRTLBM 
Data sampling is preferred after 20 rotor revolutions to allow the wake to develop within the 
computational domain [60]. For safety, 40 revolutions are computed to achieve a statistically steady ΟΜow 
ΟΜeld. It is worth noting that SHSLBM deals with a time step equal to the grid spacing. Hence, a change in the 
grid resolution modiΟΜes the corresponding time step, causing a steep increase in the computational time.  
In an actuator line model, the computational Reynolds number aΤ·ects slightly the wake behavior due to the 
unresolved boundary layers, as clariΟΜed by SΟΗrensen et al. [63] and Mikkelsen [64]. In contrast, real ΟΜow 
Reynolds numbers primarily aΤ·ect the smaller scales of turbulent structures, which decrease with increasing 
Reynolds numbers [46]. Therefore, the main parameters aΤ·ecting the simulation are the grid spacing Υïh and 
the kernel width ¬Ζ, rather than the Reynolds number.  
However, simulations at a diameter-based Reynolds number ReD of 1.6e5, BGK lattice Boltzmann simulations 
are not feasible due to a small relaxation time that would lead to unstable solutions. Using the MRT collision 
operator, high Reynolds number simulations are theoretically feasible. However, achieving a satisfactory 
tuning of the relaxation factors Ο΄i that are part of the symmetric matrix S is not straightforward.  
To simplify the process, free stream ΟΜow speed u0 and viscosity ¬Ω0 are set preventively to 0.15 and 0.015, 
leading to a stable simulation with a ReD of 400. The PIV data from the Open Jet wind tunnel Facility (OJF) of TU 
Delft are added to the charts.

By visual inspection, BGKLBM and MRTLBM fail to predict accurate normal and tangential force coeΤ·icients

6. Conclusions 
 
The performance of a vertical axis rotor was studied using an actuator line model (ALM) combined with 
diΤ·erent lattice Boltzmann solvers. The outputs of the simulations demonstrated that the SimpliΟΜed and Highly 
Stable Lattice Boltzmann Method (SHSLBM) surpassed the BGK and MRT collision operators in accuracy and 
stability, especially at high Reynolds numbers, despite requiring more computational time. The SHSLBM 
proved to reduce numerical diΤ·usion, leading to more precise simulations, and exhibited high robustness in the 
lid-driven cavity case study across all mesh sizes. 
Sensitivity tests reΟΜned the actuator line's kernel width, though grid resolution limitations necessitated setting 
it equal to twice the mesh resolution. The sensitivity of the solution to diΤ·erent boundary conditions indicated 
that using an extrapolation boundary condition is preferable over a symmetry boundary condition, as it avoids 
the artiΟΜcial acceleration of ΟΜow and more accurately represents the pressure gradient across the rotor. 
Therefore, for more realistic simulations, the extrapolation boundary condition is recommended. 
After correcting the actuator line model with ΟΜow curvature and dynamic stall sub-models, a two-bladed rotor 
was investigated under two diΤ·erent operating conditions, demonstrating its adaptability to varying rotational 
speeds. 
Contour plots of streamwise and cross-wise ΟΜow velocities revealed increased skewness in the wake and 
showed that vortices form and merge into continuous vortex sheets a few rotor radii downstream due to 
overlapping blade wakes. This indicated a stable spatial wake pattern, with wake instability arising further 
downstream, leading to a fully turbulent wake due to the early destabilization of root vortices. 
Although further improvements are needed, such as incorporating a shaft-generated wake model and a 
turbulent inlet velocity proΟΜle generator, the model eΤ·ectively correlates numerical solutions with observed 
values, particularly for the normal force coeΤ·icient. Given its advantages, including reduced computational 
time and parallelization capabilities, the lattice Boltzmann solver can be considered a leading tool for VAWT 
simulations, comparable to traditional CFD methods.
