Summary
The goal of this thesis is to present a general strategy for playing games with
incomplete information, where the underlying epistemic models are highly com-
plex. We introduce the language GDL-II to enable the formalization of games
and to approach strategies for general game playing. We introduce Epistemic
Logic in order to reason about the underlying models of games with incom-
plete information. We introduce Cluedo as an example of an epistemic game,
and discuss speciﬁc strategies for that game. Then we analyze an existing gen-
eral strategy for games with incomplete information, Hyperplay-II, which shows
great potential for succeeding as an epistemic game player. The strategy relies
on samples of states from information sets, but we show that the sampling
method is unfeasible when applied to a game like Cluedo. We then propose a
design for an alternative method, that can be used by Hyperplay-II, but also in
other contexts, to generate a sample of states from an information set.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Humans have been playing games since ancient times, as a way to challenge
and stimulate our intelligence, and as a way to demonstrate our creativity and
cognitive skills. It is only natural that games has traditionally been, and still is,
the most popular way to demonstrate the quality and abilities of systems with
artiﬁcial intelligence (AI). Games can represent complex problems that involve
multiple agents, where creativity and empathy is rewarded, and where the goals
and rules can be unambiguously formalized.
We think of human intelligence as general intelligence, because we are able
to solve a wide variety of tasks, by learning, and by reasoning about subjects
that we have not encountered before. General Game Playing (GGP) involves
the design of AI systems that are able to play games without knowing the rules
in advance. GGP represents a great domain of challenges that a system with
artiﬁcial general intelligence (AGI) must be able to overcome, since we believe
that almost any challenge for an AGI system, can be modeled as a game.
1.2
Goal
One aspect of game playing, which only appears in games with incomplete in-
formation, is the uncertainties that arise, when certain facts about the game
states are withheld from one or more agents. In such games, the knowledge
that a player has, or the lack hereof, can become a signiﬁcant factor. A loosely
deﬁned genre of games, that we label as epistemic games, include games that
are designed, such that knowledge plays a central role in the game.
An excellent example of an epistemic game is Cluedo, where players gener-
ate clues that can help them solve a murder case. We consider Cluedo a great
1
==PAGE:7==
challenge for general game players, to see if they are able to reason about un-
certainties, while also being able to make good assessments on the value of
gathering information, and on the contrary, the value of keeping information
hidden from opponents.
There are 21 cards in Cluedo, and when the game begins, three random cards
are hidden in an envelope. The remaining cards are randomly dealt out to six
players, so that each player has three private cards. Because the combinatorics
of this initial shuﬄing and dealing allows for so many possible worlds, it is
unfeasible for a game player to consider all possible combinations. Our goal is
to propose a general strategy for playing epistemic games that can successfully
be applied to a complex game like Cluedo, without having to consider all the
possible combinations.
1.3
Road map
We will ﬁrst take a look at games, how we can formally describe their attributes,
and how their underlying models look in general. Then we introduce a language,
called Game Descriptive Language, that can be used to formalizing games. Such
a language is a necessity when designing general game players, and it allows us
to reason about the internal structure of games in a general way. Then we in-
troduce Epistemic Logic, which is a logic that describes the underlying models
of knowledge and uncertainties, which can be applied to games with incomplete
information. We then introduce a variant of Cluedo, and examples of strategies
for the game, to give an intuition of some of the challenges epistemic games
present. Then we introduce an existing strategy for games with incomplete in-
formation, called Hyperplay-II, and discuss its ability to be applied to a game
like Cluedo. Then we present our own design of a technique that can be utilized
by epistemic game strategies, to sample information sets in epistemic models.
The design is presented through a series of formal deﬁnitions and proven theo-
rems, which are illustrated by examples. Finally we do a case study to evaluate
how the presented technique will perform when applied to Cluedo.

Chapter 2
Formalizing Games
2.1
Games as Search Problems
When designing an AI system to play a given game, the classic approach is to
formally deﬁne the game as a search problem with the following elements[1]:
• s0: The initial state.
• PLAY ER(s): A function that determines, which player has the move in
state s.
• ACTIONS(s): The set of legal moves in a state.
• RESULT(s, a): The transition model that deﬁnes the next state when
move a is executed in state s.
• TERMINAL-TEST(s): A test that checks whether state s is terminal
(the game has ended).
• UTILITY (s, p): A utility function (or payoﬀfunction) that deﬁnes a
numerical value for each player p in each terminal state s. In a two-player
game the utility/payoﬀcould be 1 for the winner and 0 for the loser or 1
2
for each if the game ended in a draw.
2.2
Game Trees
The initial state, and the ACTION and RESULT functions determine the
structure of the game tree for the game. Each node in the game tree is a game
state, where the root node is the initial state s0, and a node t is a leaf node
iﬀt is a terminal state (TERMINAL-TEST(t) is true). Each state s has an
outgoing edge for every legal move a ∈ACTIONS(s), that goes from s to the
state RESULT(s, a). To contain everything in the tree, every leaf node t can
be marked with its corresponding utility value UTILITY (s, p), for each player
p.

Even if the game tree is ﬁnite, performing an exhaustive search is not always
feasible. Games that are interesting to play, and not just useful as examples, typ-
ically have enormous game trees. The complexity of the game tree for chess[2],
for example, is estimated to be ∼10123 (when the 50-move drawing rule is ap-
plied), and for the board game, Go, it is ∼10360. These game trees are much
too large to exhaustively search, but they are still the underlying models of
the games. In Section 5 we introduce Monte Carlo Tree Search[3], which is a
technique that searches a small part of the game tree, while still achieving good
results.
2.3
Characteristics of Games
In the introduction we mentioned games with incomplete information. Before
we go any further, let us deﬁne the exact meaning of that, as well as a handful
of other attributes that we can use to label games. The essence of the following
deﬁnitions are widely agreed upon, but these exact formulations are our own.
4
==PAGE:10==
Deﬁnition 2.1. A game is discrete if it has a countable number of steps, and
if each participant knows what the current step is at all times.
Intuitively, we can think of discrete games as progressing through steps, and
non-discrete games as progressing through time.
Deﬁnition 2.2. A game is ﬁnite if it has a ﬁnite number of players, moves
and states.
Many games that are technically inﬁnite can be considered ﬁnite if we apply
some constraint on the number of steps or something else that ensures progress.
A good example of this is with chess, where the game is ﬁnite only when the
so-called 50-move drawing rule is included, that says: if no captures have been
made and no pawns have been moved in the last 50 moves, then the game ends
in a draw.
Deﬁnition 2.3. A game is deterministic if any given sequence of valid player
moves always leads to the same state.
Games with a random factor, like shuﬄing a deck of cards or rolling dice,
are non-deterministic. Tic-tac-toe or chess are both deterministic.
Deﬁnition 2.4. A game has complete information if all players know the exact
moves chosen by every player each round.
Games where some moves are fully or partially hidden from players, like the
deal of cards in poker or the placement of ships in battleship, are referred to as
games with incomplete information.
The label perfect/imperfect information is sometimes used instead of complete
information, but in some literature this attribute refers to knowledge of the rules
of the game, rather than knowledge of the game state. We assume that every
player has access to the complete set of rules, for any game we consider in this
thesis. Furthermore, any game is assumed to be discrete and ﬁnite if nothing
else is explicitly stated.
2.4
State Machines vs. Game Trees
It may seem redundant, that two nodes in a game tree can represent identical
states, or at least identical in terms of what facts that are true in them. Two
identical game states in chess, for example, can be treated in the exact same
way, regardless of the sequence of moves that lead to them. And when a game
has complete information, there is actually a more lean way to describe the
underlying model.
Then we can think of it as a state machine, which is a
directed graph that is not necessarily a tree. In such a system we can have
diﬀerent sequences of moves that lead to the same state, such that the size of
the model can be reduced. But when we talk about games with incomplete
information, then two states can be diﬀerent even if they appear to be identical.
5
==PAGE:11==
Two paths that lead from the initial state to two seemingly identical states
may produce two diﬀerent sequences of observations for one or more players.
This diﬀerence can often be very relevant in games with incomplete information,
and especially in knowledge games, where actions can be impactful without
changing the physical state of the world. In Cluedo, for example, one player may
be obliged to show another player one of her cards, while the remaining players
only get to know, that the shown card is one of three publicly suggested cards.
This action does not change the state of the world directly, but it may impact
the agents’ knowledge, and therefore the resulting state should be distinguished
from the original. All information about player knowledge is accessible when we
consider game trees as the underlying models, where we identify a game state
by the sequence of moves that leads to it.
2.5
General Game Playing
There are some limitations to the type of games that we can model, if we have to
formally deﬁne them as search problems in the same way as we saw above. One
is that the game must be turn-based, such that for every state there is exactly
one player that can make a move. To remove this limitation we can instead
assume that all moves are carried out as joint moves, where a joint move is an
array containing a move for each player. Games that are actually turn-based,
like chess, can be modeled with joint moves by restricting the remaining players
to only do noop moves (or null move) that have no eﬀect. Using joint moves
increases generality, and allows us to formalize games where players make moves
simultaneously. Rock-paper-scissors, for example, is a deterministic game with
complete information that cannot be modeled with one player making their
move before the other.
Game trees are underlying models of discrete games, but as illustrated, it is
often not feasible to use them to explicitly deﬁne the entirety of a game. But
a fundamental requirement, when developing general game players, is that they
can be presented with the rules of a game. To meet this requirement, a lean
and elegant solution was suggested by Love et.al.[4] at Stanford in 2006, when
they proposed the Game Description Language (GDL). The language has since
then been widely adopted within the general game playing community as the
standard way to formalize games. In this thesis we utilize a variation of GDL,
and in the next chapter we summarize the syntax and meaning of the language.

Chapter 3
Game Descriptive Language
3.1
Introduction
GDL was developed to provide a compact representation of ﬁnite, discrete,
deterministic multi-player games of complete information. The language allows
games with the mentioned characteristics to be formalized compactly, so that
they can be eﬃciently communicated to, and be unambiguously interpreted by,
a general game player to whom the game itself is unknown in advance.
GDL-II was introduced by Michael Thielscher in 2010 as an extension to
GDL, in order to allow formalization of ﬁnite, discrete, nondeterministic multi-
player games with incomplete information. We give a rather detailed summary
of GDL-II [5] and its mechanisms, because it plays an essential role in the design
we propose in Chapter 6.
The deﬁnitions and concepts we introduce in this chapter, is a merger of
content from Love et.al.[4] and Thielscher[5], where we occasionally change the
notation slightly, to accommodate our later use of the language.
3.2
Syntax and Semantics of Datalog
GDL is a variant of Datalog that extends the syntactic deﬁnitions by allowing
function constants:
Syntax
The vocabulary of a language is the set of building blocks from which
sentences are formed.
Deﬁnition 3.1 (Vocabulary). A vocabulary consists of
• A set of relation constants with associated arity.
• A set of object constants.
• (GDL addition) A set of function constants with associated arity.
7
==PAGE:13==
The vocabulary is the building blocks of a language from which terms and
sentences are composed.
Deﬁnition 3.2 (Term). A term is
• a variable, for example X, or Player;
• or a constant, for example 1, a, or bob;
• or a function constant (or functor) of arity n applied to n terms, for
example cell(X, 2), or card(5, hearts).
We describe variables and constants as simple terms, and functors applied
to terms as compound terms. We write variables with the ﬁrst letter uppercase,
and constants with the ﬁrst letter lowercase.
Deﬁnition 3.3 (Atomic Sentence). An atomic sentence is a relation constant
of arity n applied to n terms, e.g. cellIsFree(cell(1, 1)).
Notice that we use the same notation for relations and compound terms,
but that they are fundamentally diﬀerent in our language. An atomic sentence
always has exactly one relation constant.
Deﬁnition 3.4 (Literal). A literal is an atomic sentence or the negation of an
atomic sentence, e.g. cellIsFree(Cell) or ¬cellIsFree(Cell).
Deﬁnition 3.5 (Ground Expression). An expression is ground iﬀit contains
no variables.
For example, the compound term hasCard(bob, card(ace, spades)) is ground,
whereas the atomic sentence cellIsFree(cell(X, 3)) is not.
Deﬁnition 3.6 (Datalog Rule). A datalog rule, also just called a rule, is an
implication of the form
h ⇐b1 ∧. . . ∧bn
where
• The head, h, is an atomic sentence.
• The body, consists of zero or more literals, bi.
For example, cardIsDealt(Card) ←player(Player)∧true(hasCard(Player, Card)).
Semantics of Datalog
Deﬁnition 3.7 (Model). Given a language, a model is a set of ground atomic
sentences of that language.
Variables in Datalog range over all ground terms in the language. So when we
deﬁne satisfaction, we can assume that explicit universal quantiﬁers are added
for each variable in a rule, such that when we write p(X) ←q(X, Y ), then it is
treated as ∀X∀Y (p(X) ←q(X, Y )).
8
==PAGE:14==
Deﬁnition 3.8 (Satisfaction). Let M be a model, and let the sentence in ques-
tion be from an explicitly universally quantiﬁed Datalog rule. Then:
• |=M distinct(t1, t2) iﬀt1 and t2 are not the same term, syntactically.
• |=M p(t1, . . . , tn) iﬀp(t1, . . . , tn) ∈M.
• |=M ¬ϕ iﬀ̸|=M ϕ.
• |=M ϕ1 ∧. . . ∧ϕn iﬀ|=M ϕi for every i.
• |=M h ⇐b1 ∧. . . ∧bn iﬀeither ̸|=M b1 ∧. . . ∧bn or |=M h or both.
• |=M ∀X.ϕ(X) iﬀ|=M ϕ(t) for every ground term t.
KIF, the Oﬃcial Language of GDL
The standard format used for GDL
is Knowledge Interchange Format (KIF)[4]. Variables in KIF begin with a ?
(question mark) and operators are written in preﬁx notation. Terms and sen-
tences are Lisp S-expressions. Arity and type of constants (relation, function,
object) are determined by context.
Examples:
• The variable X is written ?x
• The compound term f(a, b) is written (f a b)
• The atomic sentence true(has(a, 1)) is written (true (has a 1))
• The literal ¬true(has(b, 1)) is written (not (true (has b 1)))
• The rule p(X) ⇐q(X)∧¬r(X) is written (<= (p ?x) (q ?x) (not (r ?x)))
When we want to write a rule with an empty body, we write (p anne) instead
of (<= (p anne)).
3.3
Predeﬁned Relations in GDL-II
GDL-II predeﬁnes a number of relations that have special meanings in the con-
text of a game, and with restrictions on how they can be used, in order to ensure
game descriptions that translate to consistent games.
Players – the role relation:
Each player in a game is deﬁned through the
role relation. For example:
(role anne)
(role bob)
describes that the game has two players, which are referred to by the constants
anne and bob.
9
==PAGE:15==
Game State – the true relation:
In the description we can refer to facts
that are true in the current state of the game by using the true relation.
(true (hasCard anne (card 2 hearts)))
(true (hasCard bob Card))
The true relation is only used in the body of rules.
Initial State – the init relation:
We use init to deﬁne the initial state of
the game. In a game of chess, for example, we could deﬁne that white goes ﬁrst,
and the position of pieces:
(init (hasTurn white))
(init (position (piece white queen) (square 1 D)))
Legal Moves – the legal relation:
The legal relation deﬁnes player-move
combinations that are allowed by the game rules.
(<= (legal ?player (move ?from ?to))
(true (hasTurn ?player))
(true (position ?piece ?from))
(validMove ?piece ?from ?to))
The legal relation has arity 2, and the ﬁrst term must represent a role (deﬁned
by the role relation). We can think of an implicit literal (role ?player) in the
body, when the ﬁrst term of the legal relation is the variable ?player.
Moves – the does relation:
The does relation indicates that a move was
actually made by a player. It only appears in the bodies of rules, and is best
showed in an example together with the next relation, next.
Transitions – the next relation:
The next relation is analogue to init, but
refers to facts that will hold true in a successor state. For example, we can
describe that a player keeps the cards they have been dealt throughout a card
game:
(<= (next (hasCard ?player ?card))
(true (hasCard ?player ?card)))
It is important to see that the next relation does not describe the changes from
one state to the next, but that it describes exactly which facts are true in a
successor state. If x is true in the current state, and there are no rules with
(next x) in the head (or a next relation with a variable that can be substituted
with x), then x will not be true in the next state.
Another example could be the description in chess, of where the pieces will
be positioned next:
10
==PAGE:16==
(<= (next (position ?piece ?to))
(does ?player (move ?from ?to))
(true (position ?piece ?from)))
(<= (next (position ?piece ?at))
(does ?player (move ?from ?to))
(true (position ?piece ?at))
(distinct ?at ?from)
(distinct ?at ?to))
Terminal States (part 1) – the terminal relation:
The terminal relation
deﬁnes the set of terminal states (states where the game is over). An example
could be:
(<= terminal
(isCheckMate ?player))
Terminal States (part 2) – the goal relation:
The goal relation describes
the goals of the players, by assigning an integer value between 0 and 100 for each
player in each state (even though it is only relevant in the terminal states). Zero-
sum games can be described this way, but also asymmetric or complementary
(cooperative) games. In chess we could have:
(<= (goal ?player 100)
(isCheckMate ?opponent)
(distinct ?player ?opponent))
(<= (goal ?player 0)
(isCheckMate ?player))
Randomness (GDL-II) – the special role, random:
GDL-II introduces
the special role random to allow non-deterministic games. No player can take
the role of random. It is the game manager’s responsibility that random, in any
given state, chooses a move uniformly at random from all its legal moves in the
given state.
We can randomize the deal in a card game:
(<= (legal random (dealCard ?player ?card))
(true (dealRound ?player))
(cardAvailable ?card))
Or the ﬂip of a coin:
(<= (legal random (flipCoin heads))
(true flipCoinRound))
(<= (legal random (flipCoin tails))
(true flipCoinRound))
11
==PAGE:17==
In a game description, if a variable is used in place of a player, then it must
explicitly be described that the player is distinct from random (if that is the
intention of course). Also, the random role, like any other role, must always
have at least one legal move. So if we do not intend to utilize randomness in
some round of the game, we must explicitly allow random to perform a noop
move.
Perceptions (GDL-II) – the sees relation:
GDL-II introduces the sees
relation to allow games of incomplete information. The relation describes what
the players will perceive when a transition to the next state occurs. We can
describe, for example, that players in a card game only see their own cards that
they have been dealt.
(<= (sees ?player (yourCard ?card))
(does random (dealCard ?player ?card)))
(<= (sees ?player1 (cardDealt ?player2))
(does random (dealCard ?player2 ?card)))
3.4
Safety Constraints
There are a number of safety constraints that a game description in GDL must
satisfy in order to be considered valid[4].
Some of these are inherited from
Datalog:
• If a variable appears in the head of a rule, then it must also appear in a
positive literal in the body of that rule.
• If a variable appears in a rule in a negative literal, then it must also appear
in a positive literal in the body of that rule.
• The rules of a game description in GDL must be stratiﬁed.
To summarize what the last constraint means, we say that a set of rules is
stratiﬁed, if starting from a relation p(t1, . . . , tn) it is not possible to reach
¬p(t1, . . . , tn). The set of rules {p(X) ←q(X), q(X) ←¬p(X) ∧r(X)}, for
example, is not stratiﬁed.
For a precise deﬁnition, see Love et.al.[4].
In order for a game description in GDL to unambiguously deﬁne a game tree,
there are some further constraints that must be satisﬁed. We will summarize
them informally here - see Love et.al.[4] and Thielscher[5] for precise deﬁnitions.
• When the role relation appears in the head of a rule, then the body must
be empty. This is to ensure a ﬁxed number of roles throughout any given
game.
• The true and does relations only appear in the bodies of rules.
12
==PAGE:18==
• The init relation only appears in the heads of rules, and those rules are
not dependent on a true or does relation.
• The legal, goal, and terminal relations only appear in the head of rules,
and those rules are not dependent on the does relation.
• The next and sees relations only appear in the heads of rules.
• The set of rules satisfy the recursion restriction, which hinders inﬁnite
recursions with compound terms. The following rule, for example, does
not satisfy it: p(f(X)) ←p(X). Again, see Love et.al.[4] for the detailed
deﬁnition.
We say that a GDL-game is valid (or well-formed) if it terminates, is mono-
tonic, is playable, and if it is weakly winnable. A game terminates if any inﬁnite
sequence of legal moves reaches a terminal state after a ﬁnite number of steps.
A game is playable if every role has at least one legal move in every state (that
is reachable from the initial state).
A game is monotonic if every state has
exactly one goal value for each role, and if the goal values never decrease when
transitioning to the next state. A game is weakly winnable if there exists a
sequence of joint moves for each role that results in a terminal state where that
role’s goal value is maximal.
3.5
Semantics of GDL-II
We present a slightly altered version of Thielscher’s deﬁnitions of the GDL-II
semantics[5]. The diﬀerence is mainly in notation, the original meaning is kept.
Any game description in GDL-II uses a domain-dependent set Σ of ground
symbolic expressions . Players, moves and percepts are represented by individual
ground terms like for example the role anne, or the move (deal anne (card ace spades)),
or the percept (cardDealt (card ace spades)).
States of the game are represented as subsets of Σ. A game is composed of:
• S ⊆2Σ, the states;
• R ⊆Σ, the roles;
• s0 ∈S, the initial state;
• T ⊆S, the terminal states;
• L : R × S →2Σ, the legality function, which deﬁnes the set of legal moves
for any given role in any given state (moves are also represented by ground
terms from Σ);
• u : Σ|R| × S →S, the update function, which deﬁnes the next state for
any given joint move applied to any given state;
13
==PAGE:19==
• I : R × Σ|R| × S →2Σ, the perception function, which deﬁnes the set of
percepts for any given role, when any given joint move is applied to any
given state (percepts are also represented by ground terms from Σ);
• g : R × S →N, the payoﬀfunction, which deﬁnes the payoﬀfor any given
role in any given state.
Any valid GDL-II description G contains a ﬁnite set of constants, which
implicitly determines a, possibly inﬁnite, set of ground terms Σ. But the syn-
tactic restrictions in GDL-II ensure ﬁnite derivability, so that the set of roles,
the reachable states, etc.
all are ﬁnite subsets of Σ.
Let s = {f1, . . . , fn}
be a state, then the set of game rules G is extended by the facts strue =
(true f1), . . . , (true fn).
Determining an update requires the encoding of the current state along with
facts representing the joint move. If m is the joint move, where the players
r1, . . . , rk ∈R make moves m(r1), . . . , m(rk) then G∪strue is further extended by
mdoes = (does r1 m(r1)), . . . , (does rk m(rk)). We then deﬁne the semantics
of GDL-II as follows, where G ∪strue ∪mdoes |= x means that x is derivable
from the rules, when they are extended by strue and mdoes (which are based on
the joint move m being executed in the state s).
Deﬁnition 3.9 (Semantics of a GDL-II speciﬁcation).
• R = {r ∈Σ : G |= (role r)};
• s0 = {f ∈Σ : G |= (init f)};
• T = {s ∈S : G ∪strue |= terminal};
• L(r, s) = {m ∈Σ : G ∪strue |= (legal r m)}, for all r ∈R and s ∈S;
• u(m, s) = {f ∈Σ : G ∪mdoes ∪strue |= (next f)}, for all m : Σ|R| and
s ∈S;
• I(r, m, s) = {f : G∪mdoes∪strue |= (sees r f)}, for all r ∈R\{random},
m : Σ|R|, s ∈S, and f ∈Σ;
• g(r, s) = n, where G ∪strue |= (goal r n), for all r ∈R \ {random},
s ∈S, and n ∈N.
There are many similarities to the formalization from Russell and Norvig[1]
that we saw in the beginning of Chapter 2. As discussed previously, we now have
joint moves instead, but we still deﬁne the game by an initial state, a function
that deﬁnes legal moves in a given state, a function that deﬁnes the result
when applying a move to a state, a set of terminal states, and a utility (payoﬀ)
function.
The only fundamentally new concept, is the perception function,
which is what allows games with incomplete information.

3.6
Execution Model
We have now established GDL-II as a formalism to unambiguously deﬁne games
with incomplete information. In order to actually play such a game, we need
a game manager (GM) to be responsible for enforcing the rules. The respon-
sibilities of the GM includes: maintaining the current state, collecting moves,
distributing percepts, and informing about the scores when a terminal state is
reached.
A GDL-II game goes as follows:
1. The GM sends the GDL-II description to each player and informs them
about each of their individually assigned roles. The current state is set to
the initial state.
2. The GM collects the individual moves from each player, and checks that
they are legal. If random ∈R then the GM chooses a move uniformly
at random from the legal moves that random can make, and if one or
more players have chosen illegal moves, then the GM choose legal moves
uniformly at random for them instead. Then the GM creates a joint move
from all the individual moves.
3. The GM informs each player about their chosen move (this is relevant
when the GM has to replace illegal moves), and sends to each player the
derivable percepts for their role. The GM updates the current state with
the joint move.
4. If the current state is a terminal state, then the GM determines the score
for each role and sends all scores to each player.
Otherwise step 2 is
repeated.
The communication between the GM and the players is minimal, because each
participant is able to derive everything they need to know, besides the percepts
they receive, from the game description.

does, sees, and goal.

facts are not carried automatically to the next state. For example, we need to
explicitly write a rule, with the next relation, to carry forth the win-counts, even
when they do not change. And simultaneously, we explicitly make sure in each
rule with the next relation, that we only carry exactly those terms over that we
want. The last three rules of the game deﬁnes when the game terminates, and
how the players are rewarded when it does.

by limiting the number of rounds, and then end the game in a draw if the limit
is surpassed.

4.1
Introduction
Games with incomplete information have a dimension to them, that games
with complete information do not – the underlying models of knowledge and
uncertainties. The lack of complete information often adds, what is commonly
referred to as, mind games, to a game. Situations where expert players will apply
advanced strategies to try and hinder their opponents from gaining valuable
information, while trying to gain as much knowledge themselves as possible.
In this section we introduce Epistemic Logic as deﬁned by Ditmarsch, van der
Hoek and Kooi[6], which can be used to model knowledge and uncertainty in
multi-agent situations.
The word epistemic is an adjective used to describe something that is related
to knowledge. And epistemic logic is exactly that – a logic for reasoning about
knowledge and uncertainties. Epistemic logic can be applied to so called epis-
temic models, which are used to describe the set of possible worlds, and how
one or more agents can distinguish these worlds from each other. An epistemic
model can be used to reason about a static situation in a game with incomplete
information.
Ditmarsch et.al[6] also introduce the extension, Dynamic Epistemic Logic,
which allows transitions from one static epistemic model to the next, using
event models.
The deﬁnitions we introduce in this chapter are reproduced from Ditmarsch
et.al, with very minor changes to notation, but the examples are original.
Example 4.1 (Sum Game). We start by introducing a relatively small example,
called The Sum Game that we will use throughout this section to illustrate the

mechanisms of epistemic logic. Three agents, Anne (a), Bob (b), and Cath (c)

4.2
Language
First we will look at the logic of static epistemic models, which is a modal logic.
The language of epistemic logic is an extension of propositional logic with the
modal operator(s) Ka.
Deﬁnition 4.1 (Basic epistemic language). Let P be a set of atomic propo-
sitions, and A a set of agents. A basic language of epistemic logic L(P, A) is
generated by the following Backus-Naur form:
ϕ ::= p | ¬ϕ | (ϕ ∧ϕ) | Kaϕ
For every agent a, Ka is a unary modal operator, and Kaϕ is interpreted as
”agent a knows ϕ”.
Additional standard operators ∨, →, ↔, ⊤and ⊥are not included in the
language by deﬁnition, since they are derivations of conjunction and negation,
but they are freely used as convenient abbreviations for their corresponding
formulas generated by ∧and ¬.
Example 4.2. In the scenario 201 we could for example translate the following
expressions into our formal language as follows:
• ”a knows that b or c chose 1” becomes Ka(1b ∨1c).
• ”a knows that b considers it possible that a chose 0” becomes Ka¬Kb¬0a.
• ”c knows that a does not know that c chose 1” becomes Kc¬Ka1c.

A convenient and meaningful extension of the epistemic logic language is to
include the common knowledge operator, CB, where B ⊆A, or simply written
C when B = A.
We say that a formula is considered common knowledge
amongst a group B if everybody in B knows that everybody in B knows that
everybody in B knows that... (and so on)... that the formula is true. This is
diﬀerent from every agent knowing it to be true, because every agent can know
something to be true, without every agent knowing that everyone knows.

Strategies for Epistemic
Games
In this chapter we will ﬁrst formalize the epistemic game Cluedo, and then
develop two speciﬁc strategies for that game. These will serve as inspiration
for general strategies, and eventually they can be implemented as benchmark
competitors for a general epistemic game player. This part of the chapter is

Hereafter we will brieﬂy summarize the Monte Carlo Tree Search[3], which is
a simple, yet eﬀective, algorithm for playing games with complete information.
The reason that we include it, even though it targets games with complete
information, is that it plays a role as an embedded strategy in the following
two strategies that we introduce.
The ﬁrst is called Hyperplay[8], and is a
strategy that utilizes a sample of states from the information set, along with
an embedded strategy for complete information games like Monte Carlo Tree
Search. The next strategy is called Hyperplay-II[9], and is an even more general
strategy that uses Hyperplay as an embedded strategy, in order to excel in
epistemic games.

5.1
Formalizing Cluedo
Cluedo is a classic detective game, where six players compete to be the ﬁrst to
correctly solve the details of a murder (the suspect, the murder weapon, and
the room where the crime took place). In the original version of the game, each
player is represented by a token, that they move around on the board. We have
chosen to omit this aspect of the game, and instead reduce it to solely rely on
24
==PAGE:30==
the epistemic (knowledge related) actions of the game. When we refer to Cluedo
in this report, it is our reduced version that we are referring to, which we deﬁne
as follows:
A Cluedo match has the following objects: six players, ordered from ﬁrst
to last; six unique suspect cards for example Miss Scarlett or Mrs White; six
unique weapon cards, for example Knife or Candlestick; and nine unique room
cards, for example Kitchen or Ball Room.
The ﬂow of the game is deﬁned as follows:
• All suspect cards are shuﬄed, and one is chosen uniformly at random,
without anyone seeing it. The same process is carried out for the weapon
cards and the room cards respectively. The three chosen cards are placed
in an envelope without any players seeing their values.
• All remaining cards are all shuﬄed together, and then each player is dealt
three private cards.
• Then the players take turns according to their order, and each turn goes
as follows:
– Suggestion phase: The player on the turn, the suggester, publicly
announces a suggestion, which consists of a suspect, a weapon, and
a room.
– Then the player that comes next in the order, the disprover, has to
disprove the suggestion if able. A player is able to disprove if they
have at least one of the suggested cards on hand. The disprover dis-
proves the suggestion by showing one of the suggested cards to the
suggester. The other players only see that a card is shown, they do
not know which of the three it is. If the disprover is unable to dis-
prove, then the next player after him/her becomes the disprover. If
all players, except the suggester, are unable to disprove the sugges-
tion, or if someone disproves it, then the next phase begins.
– Accusation phase: The suggester can either choose to make an accu-
sation, or to end their turn. To make an accusation, the player must
publicly accuse a suspect, a weapon, and a room. They then get to
privately look at the three cards in the envelope, and if their accusa-
tion is correct, then they can reveal the cards and they win the game.
If they it is incorrect however, then they must return the cards to
the envelope without revealing them, and then they lose the game.
When a player loses the game they must stay at the table to partic-
ipate in the disproving phases, but they cannot make suggestions or
accusations for the rest of the game.
• If all players, except one, have lost the game, then the remaining player
wins.

==PAGE:31==
We have modeled our version of Cluedo in GDL-II, and it can be seen in Ap-
pendix A.
Notice that no cards change hands throughout the game once they have been
dealt, the only changes are knowledge related. That is why we use the term
epistemic game to describe it. Before we progress to general strategies for such
knowledge related games, let us go over a couple of speciﬁc strategies for playing
Cluedo.
5.2
Cluedo Strategy 1
Our ﬁrst strategy utilizes only the cards that we directly see, when other players
disprove our suggestions. We consider three sets throughout the game Suspects,
Weapons, and Rooms, which are initialized so that they contain every card of
their respective category. The sets represent the cards that we consider possible
to be placed in the envelope.
When we are dealt cards at the beginning of the game, we remove each of
those from the set of their category. Whenever we have to make a suggestion,
we pick one card at random from each of the three sets, and make a suggestion
with the three that we have picked. If someone disproves our suggestion, then
we remove that card from the set that corresponds to its category. If nobody
disproves our suggestion, then we simply make an accusation with the suggested
cards. If all three sets contain exactly one card each at the end of our turn,
then we make an accusation with the three remaining cards, otherwise we pass
the turn.
This strategy will probably lose to most human players due to being slow,
but it will never lose due to an incorrect accusation, because it only makes an
accusation when it can eliminate all other possibilities. The strategy completely
wastes the opportunity to gain knowledge by examining the results of other
players’ accusations.
5.3
Cluedo Strategy 2

Overall the strategy seems pretty solid, but can we improve it? There are
probably additional deduction strategies that we have not included, and these
could of course be added. But besides such improvements, there lies a strategic
potential in restricting our opponents of information. In the strategy we do
not consider the knowledge of our opponents. Could we choose our suggestions
more wisely, so that we ﬁnd some optimal balance between us acquiring as
much information as possible, while restricting our opponents from learning
valuable information? These kinds of mind games would be essential if expert-
level human players were playing the game. Mastering such strategies would
not guarantee a victory every time, but we believe that it would surely give a
non-negligible edge over opponents using the strategy described in this section.
A simple improvement, to this Cluedo speciﬁc strategy, would be to remember
what cards we previously showed to our opponents, and then make sure to show
them the same ones again whenever possible. We should also not exclusively
suggest cards, when we consider it possible that they are in the envelope –
opponents could pick up on that, and learn a whole lot from our suggestions
about what we know.
But to deﬁne a strategy that takes these things into
account will probably become a diﬃcult task, because of the complexity of the
underlying epistemic models.
In the following sections we move towards a general strategy for games with
incomplete information, which we believe can potentially participate in these
so-called mind games.

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a randomized algorithm that can be used

The algorithm has four steps that are repeated either a ﬁxed number of times,
or as many times as possible within a given time frame.
In Figure 5.1 we
see the general ﬂow of MCTS. The algorithm starts with a tree that has only
one node, the root, which corresponds to the current state of the game. The
algorithm requires that states and state-transitions (legal moves) of the game
can be simulated, in order to generate sections of the game tree. Each node
in the tree that we generate will keep track of two values: i) the sum of the
results (payoﬀs) from all the simulations performed below the node, and ii) the
number of times the node has been visited. From these numbers we can derive
an average expected payoﬀfor the node.
The algorithm works as follows:
1. Selection. Starting in the root node, recursively select an optimal child
node until a leaf node is reached. Optimal child nodes should be chosen
with a good balance between exploration and exploitation, or in other
words, so that searching less explored nodes is encouraged, but at the
same time so that successful nodes are favored.

2. Expansion. If the reached node is not a terminal node, and if it has already
been visited, then expand the tree to include its child nodes and select any
one of them instead.
3. Simulation.
From the selected node run a simulated play out until a
terminal node is reached and then note the result (payoﬀ) of the reached
terminal node. The simulation is performed by choosing random moves
for every player at each state. Notice that the nodes, that we visit during
the simulation, are not added to the tree.
4. Back propagation. In the ﬁnal step, traverse from the selected node and
all the way up to the root node, and for each node on the way, increment

Monte Carlo Tree Search is an elegant algorithm, which is relatively easy to
implement. Another of its primary strengths is that it is domain independent.
It does not need to know anything about the heuristics of a game, it only needs
to know the legal moves in each state and how to valuate the terminal states.
Also, as previously mentioned, the search can be terminated after any number
of iterations, making it very ﬂexible.
5.5
Hyperplay
The HyperPlay technique presents a solution to general game playing of games
with incomplete information. It does so by maintaining a sample collection, or
bag, of states from the assigned role’s information set. The solution is designed
to elevate an existing strategy for complete information games, like MCTS, to
make it capable of playing games with incomplete information, by applying it
to each of the states in the bag.
We explain the overall mechanisms of the
technique here, but see Schoﬁeld, Cerexhe and Thielscher[8] for details.

5.5.5
Hyperplay’s bias against gathering information
There is however a fundamental weakness in the way Hyperplay approaches
games of incomplete information. The fact that each of the embedded evalu-
ations is based on an assumption of complete knowledge, and thus, the algo-
rithm completely neglects the value in gathering new information. Schoﬁeld and
Thielscher demonstrate this, with a great little example – a game that they call
exploding bomb[9]. The game is a simple one-player game that goes as follows:
1. The random player arms a bomb, either using a blue or a red wire.
2. The player can then choose to ask which wire the bomb is armed with. If
he asks, then he is told, but asking also slightly reduces the payoﬀ, should
he win the game.
3. The player chooses one wire to cut, and if it is the correct one, then he
disarms the bomb. Otherwise it explodes in his face. Disarming the bomb
without asking yields a score of 100, while disarming it after asking only
yields 90. If it explodes, then the player gets a score of 0.
This example highlights the weakness perfectly, since Hyperplay always turns
down the option to ask. To understand why, let us recall how the technique
works. It samples a bag of states that could each be the true state. It estimates
the payoﬀof a given move, by making a weighted average over the estimations of
that move in each of the sampled states. But it does so by utilizing an embedded
strategy for complete information games, and that embedded strategy will see
only a single sample state, and will consider the chosen wire as a known fact,
which makes the ask-move an irrelevant investment.

us after the ﬁnal card was dealt to a player. Then in every state h ∈H we were
dealt x1, y1, and z1. But for h1, h2 ∈H we almost certainly will have that the
remaining cards were dealt in diﬀerent ways. Let us say that h′ ∈H is such
that we were dealt x1, y1, and z1, and such that r2 was dealt x5, y3, and y4.
To evaluate a move mi, we execute n simulations from each state h ∈H,
where mi is the ﬁrst move for our role, and where all other moves are chosen with
the original Hyperplay strategy. In the simulation with h′, we assume that r2 has
37
==PAGE:43==
received the play messages ξr2(h′). To select moves with the Hyperplay strategy
for r2, we then create a bag of Hypergame models H2 based on those messages.
h′ may or may not be represented in that bag, since only the incomplete path
ξr2(h′) is used to ﬁnd sample states. Bags are created for each role this way,
using hp(ξr(h′)).
Then we play out the simulation until we reach a terminal state. The ﬁrst
move for our role in the simulation will be mi, but besides that, every move
is selected with the original Hyperplay technique. We then collect the result,
which is the payoﬀfor our role.
We carry out simulations like this for all
h ∈H, to produce an estimate of the payoﬀwhen selecting move mi. When
every m ∈M(st, r1) has been evaluated, then we select our next move as the
one with the highest expected payoﬀ.
Schoﬁeld and Thielscher ﬁnds that Hyperplay-II performs well in epistemic
games. Their experimental results show the value that HyperPlay-II places on
knowledge, and how it correctly values information-gathering moves. It is able to
collect information when appropriate, withhold information from its opponents,
and keep its agenda hidden.
These are exactly the qualities that we are looking for in a general epistemic
game player! We believe that this technique, in theory, would provide a strategy
that is superior to the one we described in Section 5.3. But there is however a
problem, that makes it unfeasible to implement Hyperplay-II as a Cluedo player
– the technique it uses to sample information sets (described in Algorithm 5.1).
In the next chapter we propose an alternative solution, that can be used to
sample information sets (or to be precise, to produce a valid complete path
when given an incomplete path).

6.1
Introduction
First, let’s describe the underlying epistemic model of a GDL-II game.
Deﬁnition 6.1 (Indistinguishability). Let G be a GDL-II game, let s1 and s2
be two states that are reachable from the initial state in G, and let r be some
role in G. Then r can distinguish s1 from s2 iﬀξr(s1) ̸= ξr(s2).
In other words, a player cannot distinguish two states from each other, iﬀ
they are the results of two sequences of play messages, where the player’s own
moves and received percepts are the same in both. We can use this deﬁnition
to create an epistemic model M = ⟨S, ∼, V ⟩, where the set of agents is the roles
in G, the atomic propositions are the reachable ground atomic sentences, S is
the reachable states in G, and V is deﬁned by the ground atomic sentences that
are true in each state.
When we are assigned to a role, and a state is indistinguishable from the
actual state for our role, then we say that the state is consistent (and if not,
then we say that it’s inconsistent).
In this section we propose a design for a technique that, given a GDL-II game
and one player’s play messages, can be used to collect sample states from that
player’s information set. That is, given a game and an incomplete path ξr(st),
our technique produces a complete path ξ(s), such that ξr(st) = ξr(s). Our
design is intended to be utilized by general strategies like Hyperplay-II, but the
design is a stand-alone solution that can potentially serve other purposes.

The goal of our technique, is to represent the information sets, in each level
of the tree, by upper and lower bounds, which are then continually tightened
throughout the game, as we receive additional percepts. We track the possible
causes of each known fact, including the received percepts, by examining the
game rules. When successful, the technique can conclude that an entire subtree
only contains inconsistent states, without having to exhaustively search them all.
Our model is inspired by techniques from program analysis[10] and intuitively it
can be compared to the Cluedo strategy presented in Section 5.3. It uses all the
gathered percepts to derive as many facts as possible about the actual states
and actual moves in each round of the game.

In the illustrated example above, the game tree is very small, which means
that using our technique is worse than just making an exhaustive search. If
we consider the same example, but with 21 cards being dealt out to 7 players,
which is similar to the complexity of Cluedo, then our technique will improve
the performance of information set sampling dramatically. With 21 cards there
are 21! possible sequences of moves that the dealer can make. If we try to make
an exhaustive search without using our technique, then it’s very unlikely that
we succeed.
Given some speciﬁc card game like this, the problem could of course be taken
care of with a simple heuristic, where, after seeing that we are dealt the ace of
spades, for example, we can discard any previous move where random deals the
ace of spades to another player. This heuristic is so simple, and yet so eﬃcient,
that we as humans might be tempted to take it for granted. But unfortunately,
it is not possible to implement such a heuristic in a general game player when
states are treated as anonymous nodes in a transition system. We need to reason
about the content of the states, in order to achieve something of this sort, which
is exactly what our technique does.
6.2
Interpreting GDL-II Rules
First, recall from Deﬁnition 3.2 that a term is a constant, a variable, or a functor
of arity n applied to n terms. An atomic sentence is a relation constant of arity
n applied to n terms, and a literal is an atomic sentence or the negation of an
atomic sentence. A rule is an implication h ←b1 ∧· · ·∧bn where h is an atomic
sentence, and each bi is a literal. We say that a term is ground iﬀit contains
no variables, and by extension, we say that a rule is ground iﬀit only contains
ground terms.
Given a GDL-II game description, we deﬁne the following interpretation of
its rules:
Deﬁnition 6.2 (Transitional Interpretation of GDL-II rules). Given a GDL-II
game description as a set of rules G, we deﬁne the transitional interpretation
of G as:
Ω(G) = (Grole, Ginit, Gnext, Glegal, Gsees, Gcustom)
where Grole, Ginit, Gnext, Glegal, Gsees, and Gcustom are mutually disjoint sets
of rules deﬁned as follows:
• Grole contains every rule in G with role as the relation constant in the
head.
• Ginit contains every rule with init in the head, but in Ginit the init
relation constant in the head is replaced by true.
• Gnext contains every rule with next in the head, but in Gnext the next
relation constant in the head is replaced by true.
42
==PAGE:48==
• Glegal contains every rule with legal in the head, but in Glegal the legal
relation constant in the head is replaced by does.
• Gsees contains every rule with sees or terminal in the head.
• Gcustom contains every rule with a relation constant in the head that is
not predeﬁned in GDL-II.
Notice that we ignore the rules with goal in the head in the transitional
interpretation of G. We only need the rules that can derive whether or not a
state is consistent with some incomplete path.
We write ΩG to denote the set of all ground atomic sentences that can
potentially be derived from Ω(G).
The reason why we replace the relation constants in Ginit, Gnext, and Glegal
with true and does respectively, is to achieve a smooth linkup between the
states when applying transitional rules. We avoid any confusion by separating
the rules in diﬀerent sets, which allow us to distinguish them correctly, so that
the rules in Ginit are not mixed up with those in Gnext.

Deﬁnition 6.6 (Application of rules). Given a set of states S, a lower bound
l of Ω(S), an upper bound u of Ω(S), and a rule γ = h ←b1 ∧· · · ∧bn, then if
there exists a substitution θ such that γθ is ground, and such that
• for every positive literal bi = reli(f1, . . . , fni):
– if bi = distinct(f1, f2), then f1θ ̸= f2θ,
– and otherwise, then reli(f1, . . . , fni)θ ∈u,
• and for every negative literal bi = ¬reli(f1, . . . , fni):
– if bi = ¬distinct(f1, f2), then f1θ = f2θ,
– and otherwise, then reli(f1, . . . , fni)θ /∈l,
then we say that γ is applicable between u and l, and we say that γ′θ is an
application of γ between u and l, where γ′ is γ, but without all literals with the
distinct relation.
Consider the example bounds l = {r(bob)} and u = {q(bob), r(anne), r(bob)},
and some rule γ = p(X) ←q(X) ∧¬r(Y ). Then γσ is not an application of
γ between u and l if σ = [X →anne, Y →bob], because we have r(bob) ∈l,
but if σ = [X →bob, Y →anne] then it is, because we have q(bob) ∈u and
r(anne) /∈l.
Intuitively, given a rule and some bounds, we can think of the applications of
that rule, as all the possible instantiations that could apply to at least one state
within the bounds. If for example, a rule has a negative literal ¬p, and p is in
the lower bound, then we know that p is true in all states within the bounds,
and that the rule therefore is not applicable between the bounds. Inversely,
when some q is not in the upper bound, then we know that q is false in all
states between the bounds.
6.4
Bounded Information Sets
In Deﬁnition 6.4 we saw how the interpretations of a set of states S can be
bounded by two sets u and l, where l ⊆Ω(s) ⊆u for all s ∈S.
Now let
us use the deﬁnition the other way around instead. If we are given an upper
bound u and a lower bound l, then those bounds deﬁne the set of states S, for
which l ⊆Ω(s) ⊆u for all s ∈S. This leads us to a new way of representing
information sets.
When we play a game with incomplete information, we may not know the
exact actual state, we only know our own moves and the percepts that we have
received so far (the play messages). But even though we may not know the
actual state, we can always deﬁne a set of states that could be the actual state,
that is, states that could be reached from the initial state and where the path
47
==PAGE:53==
would result in the same play messages as those we received. We say that these
states are consistent with our received play messages, or for short, just say that
they are consistent. These states make up our information set in the game, and
the crucial thing about it is, that we know it contains the actual state. So if
we are given upper and lower bounds, u and l, for an information set, then we
know that the actual state is contained within those bounds.
The following deﬁnitions lay the foundation for our technique.
Deﬁnition 6.7 (Boundary Model). Let G be an ongoing GDL-II game, where
S = ⟨s0, . . . , sn⟩is the sequence of actual states visited by the game so far, and
where sn is the current state, which is non-terminal. Let ξr⋆(sn) be the ordered
sequence of play messages that the assigned role, r⋆, has received so far.
Then BG : S →(P(ΩG) × P(ΩG)) is a boundary model for ξr⋆(sn) iﬀfor
any si ∈S we have that BG(si) = (u, l), such that l ⊆Ω(si) ⊆u.
So to summarize the deﬁnition, a boundary model provides upper and lower
bounds for each of the information sets in the game, seen from the perspective
of an assigned role.
As long as the actual states are contained within their
respective bounds, the boundary model is valid. The bounds do not need to
be least upper bounds and greatest lower bounds, but of course, the tighter the
bounds are, the more useful the model is.
We now deﬁne the reduced state model, which is a data structure from which
we can derive a boundary model.
Deﬁnition 6.8 (Reduced State Model). S = (D, M, N, ρ, ⊕, ⊖) is a reduced
state model for the assigned role r⋆, where:
• D is a set of ground rules that we call the deduction rules. By D(α) we
denote the rules in D where the head is α.
• M is a set of ground rules that we call the move rules. Every rule in M
has the relation constant does in the head. By M(r) we denote the rules
in M where r is the ﬁrst term in the does relation of the head. By M(r, α)
we denote the rules in M(r) where α is the head.
• N is a set of ground rules that we call the transition rules. Every rule
in N has the relation constant true in the head. By N(α) we denote the
rules in N where the head is α.
• ρ is a set of ground rules that we call the perception rules. Every rule in
ρ has relation constant sees or terminal in the head. By ρ(r) we denote
the rules in ρ where the head is a sees relation with r as the ﬁrst term. By
ρ(r, α) we denote the rules in ρ(r) where α is the head. By ρ(terminal)
we denote the rules in ρ where the head is terminal.
• ⊕and ⊖are sets of ground atomic sentences called the facts.
48
==PAGE:54==
We now deﬁne the reduced game model, which utilizes a sequence of reduced
state models, corresponding to the sequence of states visited in a game.
Deﬁnition 6.9 (Reduced Game Model). Let G be a GDL-II game, with Ω(G) =
(Grole, Ginit, Gnext, Glegal, Gsees, Gcustom), where S = ⟨s0, . . . , sn⟩is the se-
quence of actual states visited so far in an ongoing game of G, and where sn is
the current state, which is non-terminal. Let r⋆be an assigned role.
Then GG = (R, ⟨S0, . . . , Sn⟩) is a reduced game model for ξr⋆(sn) iﬀ
• GG is a boundary model for ξr⋆(sn), when GG(si) = (ui, li) is deﬁned as
follows, for any si ∈S:
– α ∈ui iﬀα is not in ⊖i and α is the head of any rule in R, Di,
Ni−1, or Mi.
– α ∈li iﬀα is in ⊕i.
• For any i ∈{1, . . . , n}, and any set of ground atomic sentences Ω(˜s),
where li ⊆Ω(˜s) ⊆ui, we have:
there exists a rule γ ∈Grole ∪Gnext ∪Glegal ∪Gsees ∪Gcustom that can
infer α given Ω(˜s) iﬀthere exists a rule γ′ ∈R ∪Di ∪Ni ∪Mi ∪ρi that
can infer α given Ω(˜s).
The same is true for i = 0, if we have γ ∈Grole ∪Ginit ∪Glegal ∪Gsees ∪
Gcustom instead.
So the reduced game model is a concrete way to represent a boundary model,
based on the sets contained in a sequence of reduced state models. The second
part of the deﬁnition ensures correctness and completeness when using the rules
in the model to derive facts, and is vital to some of the propositions we present
later in the chapter. What it says is basically that the model should be able to
derive exactly those facts that can be derived with the game rules, given some
state that within the bounds.

help us gain an intuition of the previous deﬁnitions.

6.5
Tightening the Bounds
We have now prepared a model that we can use to bound each actual state
visited by a game. In this section we show a series of propositions that we can
use to tighten these bounds, based on the received percepts and the knowledge
about the game rules. These are all original contributions.
Proposition 6.1 (Out of bounds).
Let GG = (R, ⟨S0, . . . , Sn⟩) be a reduced game model for (ξr⋆(sn)), and let α
be an atomic sentence.
• If α /∈uk for some Sk then α is false in Ω(sk).
• If α ∈lk for some Sk then α is true in Ω(sk).
Proof of Proposition 6.1. We know that uk is an upper bound for Ω(sk), so if
α /∈uk, then we know that α /∈Ω(sk), that is, α is false in Ω(sk). Inversely,
we know that lk is a lower bound for Ω(sk), so if α ∈lk, then we know that
α ∈Ω(sk), that is, α is true in Ω(sk).

the head of every rule in Mk(r) \ Mk(r, α) is false in Ω(sk).
This proposition says that if we know the actual move of some role in sk,
then we know that other moves cannot be the actual move for that role.
Proof of Proposition 6.6. We know that every role performs exactly one move
each turn, so if we know that r performed α in sk, then we can conclude that r
did not perform any other move.
Proposition 6.7 (Conﬁrm fact from legal moves).
Let GG = (R, ⟨S0, . . . , Sk, . . . , Sn⟩) be a reduced game model for ξr⋆(sn), with
Sk = (Dk, Nk, Mk, ρk, ⊕k, ⊖k).
Let L be the smallest set, that contains every rule from Mk(r), where the
head is not in ⊖k, for some role r ∈R. If some literal b appears in the body of
every rule in L, then
• if b is a positive literal of α, then α must be true in Ω(sk),
• and if b is a negative literal ¬α, then α must be false in Ω(sk).
This proposition says that if every rule, that is able to produce a legal move
for some role r in sk, depends on some literal, then that literal must be true in
Ω(sk).
Proof of Proposition 6.7. Assume that the literal is not true. Then that role
has no legal moves in sk, which gives a contradiction if we assume the game
description to be valid.
Proposition 6.8 (Conﬁrm fact from received percept).
Let GG = (R, ⟨S0, . . . , Sk, . . . , Sn⟩) be a reduced game model for ξr⋆(sn), with
Sk = (Dk, Nk, Mk, ρk, ⊕k, ⊖k).
If some literal b appears in the body of every rule in ρk(r, α), for some r ∈R
and some α ∈⊕k, then
• if b is a positive literal of β, then β must be true in Ω(s),
• and if b is a negative literal ¬β, then β must be false in Ω(s).
This proposition says that if every rule, that could produce a percept α for
some role r in sk, depends on some literal b, and if r actually received that
percept, then that literal must be true in Ω(sk).
Proof of Proposition 6.8. Assume that the literal is not true. Then the percept
α can not be derived from ρk and Ω(sk), which contradicts the fact that r
received the percept α in sk.

eﬃciency of information set sampling.
7.1
Case study: Cluedo
Assume that we participate in a game of our implementation of Cluedo (see
Appendix A). The game description itself is not of a high complexity – it has
91 rules in total, including all the simple facts. But the resulting game tree
has a high complexity as we will now show. In the ﬁrst round of the game, the
random role randomly picks the three cards that will go in the envelope. In each
of the subsequent eighteen rounds, the random role deals one of the remaining
cards to a player, such that all players end up with three cards.
There are
6 · 6 · 9 diﬀerent combinations for the cards in the envelope. And after those
cards have been dealt, there are 18! ways that the remaining cards can be dealt
to the players. This results in a gigantic game tree, where there are ∼2.1 · 1018
diﬀerent states that could be the true state, when all the cards have been dealt.
Assume that we are assigned to the role that is dealt cards last. In the ﬁrst
round three cards are placed in the envelope. In the following ﬁfteen rounds
the other ﬁve players are dealt their cards.
Then in the next three rounds
59
==PAGE:65==
we are dealt our Cluedo cards, which let us say is missScarlett, candlestick,
and mrsWhite. When we are dealt the ﬁrst of those three cards, we receive a
percept, which speciﬁes the value of the dealt card. This is the ﬁrst time we
receive a percept in this game.
We can imagine that we have maintained a bag of Hypergame models up until
this point. And let us assume that in that bag, there is a model, s, where the
cards missScarlett, knife, and kitchen were initially placed in the envelope.
Notice that missScarlett is the ﬁrst card that we are dealt. Then if we use
Algorithm 5.1, we will reach inconsistent states no matter which move we try
going forward with, since none of the legal moves are the one where we are dealt
missScarlett. The algorithm will backtrack one level, and then try to perform
some other moves from that level. This will of course result in inconsistencies
again, because the initial move, where the cards are placed in the envelope has
not been changed. The algorithm will then keep backtracking one level further
up, but only after clearing the entire subtree below, and following this pattern
it will search through essentially the whole game tree before backtracking all
the way back to the top, so that diﬀerent cards can be placed in the envelope.
Even then, it may place missScarlett in the envelope again, but in a diﬀerent
constellation, so that we have to repeat the whole process again. This simple
sampling technique is basically a variant of depth-ﬁrst search, and it cannot be
expected to eﬃciently return a result when applied to a game tree of this size.
Our sampling technique on the other hand, will be highly eﬃcient in re-
trieving samples in the given scenario. Assume that the current state of the
game is just before we are dealt our ﬁrst card. Assume that we have initialized
a reduced game model using Theorem 6.1, and that we have used Proposi-
tion 6.3 to reduce the rules by conﬁrming facts, like suspect(missScarlett), or
assignMurderItemsRound. In our model, random’s legal moves in the ﬁrst
round are represented by 6·6·9 = 324 instantiated rules, similar to the following:
does(random, assignMurderItems(missScarlett, knife, kitchen))
In this initial model, there are 3 · 324 transition rules similar to the following:
true(murderItem(missScarlett))
←does(random, assignMurderItems(missScarlett, knife, kitchen))
true(murderItem(knife))
←does(random, assignMurderItems(missScarlett, knife, kitchen))
true(murderItem(kitchen))
←does(random, assignMurderItems(missScarlett, knife, kitchen))
In each of the following ﬁfteen rounds, random’s legal moves are represented by
21 instantiated rules, similar to:
does(random, deal(player1, mrsWhite)) ←¬isAssigned(mrsWhite)

There will also be deduction rules, similar to
isAssigned(mrsWhite) ←true(murderItem(mrsWhite))
isAssigned(mrsWhite) ←true(hasCard(player1, mrsWhite))
isAssigned(mrsWhite) ←true(hasCard(player2, mrsWhite))
. . .
Even though it amounts to a seemingly high number of rules, the number of
rules does not grow exponentially by the number of rounds, so the total number
of rules in our model is still far lower than the size of the game tree.
Now let us update the model and try to ﬁnd a sample state from the infor-
mation set. First we receive the percept dealtCard(missScarlett), which can
only be inferred by the rule:
sees(player6, dealtCard(missScarlett)) ←does(random, deal(player6, missScarlett))
Then we know that does(random, deal(player6, missScarlett)) was the actual
move that random selected. There is only one rule that can infer this to be a
legal move, and that is:
does(random, deal(player6, missScarlett)) ←¬isAssigned(missScarlett)
From this, the model concludes that isAssigned(missScarlett) is false in the
current state. Looking at the rules that can infer isAssigned(missScarlett) to
be true, the model concludes that true(murderItem(missScarlett)) is false, and
that true(hasCard(player1, missScarlett)), and true(hasCard(player2, missScarlett))
etc. are all false.
These false sentences will trickle up in the model, because of transition rules
like:
true(murderItem(missScarlett)) ←true(murderItem(missScarlett))
When they have trickled up to the ﬁrst round, the model concludes that all the
moves where random puts missScarlett in the envelope must be false, since
they infer true(murderItem(missScarlett)).
Similarly, every move, where random deals missScarlett to another player,
is also concluded false.
To ﬁnd a sample, we can now use Algorithm 5.1, but where we only choose
moves that are within the bounds of our model.
At this point in the game we conservatively estimate that the model has
less than 10.000 instantiated rules, and that it will execute even less updates.
Building the model and executing all the reductions is way more feasible than
searching through a tree with a size of ∼6·6·9· 18!
3! . And remember, that when
our model has been constructed and reduced, then we can use it to collect as
many samples as needed.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
We suggest that Hyperplay-II is a good candidate for a general strategy that
can be used to play epistemic games.
We have discovered though, that the
technique is unfeasible when applied to games with a high complexity in the
underlying epistemic models, like Cluedo.
We have proposed a stand alone design, that can be used to represent the
actual states in a game with upper and lower bounds. We have showed how
these bounds can be structured in a model, so that they depend on the game
rules, in a way that allows us to make logical conclusions about the transitions
between states, and by extension, about which moves that are consistent with
our received percepts. We have proved a series of propositions that allow us
to systematically reduce the model, such that the bounds of the actual states
are tightened. We have showed that the technique will be highly eﬃcient in the
game Cluedo, where the complexity of the underlying epistemic model is very
high, which suggests that the technique can improve the eﬃciency of information
set sampling in similar types of games.
Our design can be utilized by Hyperplay-II but also by other general game
playing strategies for games with incomplete information, where the strategy
wish to utilize a sample of states from an information set.
8.1
Future Work
We intend to implement our sampling technique, and test its performance when
applied to various epistemic games. If successful, we intend to implement a
general game player for epistemic games, which is based on Hyperplay-II and
our sampling technique. This should hopefully lead to new insights and im-
provements on both our technique and on the subject of general game playing
with incomplete information.
