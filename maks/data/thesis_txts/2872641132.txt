Abstract
Biological biogas upgrading is an environmentally friendly way to convert the carbon diox­
ide (CO2), which is present in biogas, into methane (CH4), thereby increasing the use and
value of the product while lowering carbon dioxide emissions. Trickle bed reactors (TBRs)
are among the most widely used technologies for this upgrading method. A crucial phase
for this operation is, however, the start­up period, during which biofilm formation happens,
as the biofilm will impact methane production and the system’s stability. Therefore, this
work aims to investigate the most effective start­up method by comparing different se­
tups of trickle bed reactors. One is constantly trickled, while the other is flooded during a
start­up period of 26 days before being wetted through periodic filling.
The two reactors were inoculated with digestate and run at thermophilic conditions (52
°C ± 2) for 78 days. Gas measurements were performed three times a week during the
experiment, while liquid sampling was performed two times a week. The liquid samples
were used for pH and volatile fatty acid (VFA) measurements. Furthermore, samples
were taken from inside the reactors seven times throughout the experimental run time to
monitor biofilm formation and prepare for future DNA analysis.
The biofilm formation in the reactors varied depending on the setups. In the TBR, the
biofilm was thin and dense and reached a steady formation after 22 days, while the biofilm
in the flooded reactor was aqueous and contained less biomass. After the periodic filling
was applied, the biofilm formation changed and became denser. Throughout the experi­
ment, the TBR showed higher methane levels (up to 97.69%) in the outlet gas and reached
complete conversion more often than the flooded reactor. The reactor with the highest
methane production changed several times over the 78 days. The VFA and pH content
were found to be somewhat similar in both reactors and fluctuated at levels that did not
affect the systems. Overall, the results of this experiment showed that the TBR performed
slightly better than the flooded reactor, suggesting that this is the most effective start­up
method. However, further research is needed to investigate long­term stability and pos­
sible scalability.

Resume
Biologisk biogasopgradering er en miljøvenlig måde at omdanne kuldioxid (CO2), der er et
af hovedkomponenterne i biogas, om til metan (CH4), hvorved værdi og anvendelsesmu­
ligheder forøges, samtidig med at kuldioxidemissionerne mindskes. Trickle bed­reaktorer
(TBR’er) er blandt de mest anvendte teknologier brugt til biologisk biogasopgradering. En
af de helt afgørende faser for denne proces er opstartsfasen hvor biofilmen skal dannes.
Dannelsen af denne biofilm vil nemlig påvirke både metan produktion og systemets sta­
bilitet. Dette projekt har derfor til formål, at undersøge hvilken opstartsmetode der er mest
effektiv ved at sammenligne to forskellige opsætninger for trickle bed­reaktorer. Den ene
reaktor (R1) vædes gennem perkolering, mens den anden (R2) er fyldt med væske de
første 26 dage, hvorefter den vædes gennem periodisk fyldning.
De to reaktorer blev inokuleret med afgasset biomasse og opereret ved termofile forhold
(52 °C ± 2) i 78 dage. Gas målinger blev foretaget tre gange ugentligt gennem hele
forsøget, mens væskeprøver blev udtaget to gange ugentligt. Disse væskeprøver blev
brugt til måling af pH samt præparation af prøver til målinger af flygtige fedtsyrer. Deru­
dover, blev der udtaget prøver inde fra reaktorerne syv gange gennem forsøget, som
skulle bruges til monitorering af biofilmdannelse og til fremtidig DNA­analyse.
Resultaterne viste i at biofilmdannelsen i høj grad afhang at reaktor opsætningen. R1
viste sig at have dannet en tynd men tæt pakket biofilm og opnåede en stabil mængde
af biofilm efter 22 dage. Samtidig var biofilmen i R2 langt mere vandholdig og derfor ikke
særligt tæt pakket. Efter reaktoren blev tømt, og derefter ikke konstant stod under vand,
ændrede dannelsen af biofilm sig, så det mindede mere om biofilmen i R1. Gennem hele
forsøget opnåede R1 højere metan niveauer (op imod 97.69%) i den udgående gas. Hertil
opnåede den også fuld konvertering flere gange end R2. Hvilken reaktor der havde den
højeste metan produktion ændrede sig løbende gennem forsøget længde. Koncentratio­
nen af flygtige fedtsyrer og fluktuering i pH­værdi var nogenlunde ens i begge reaktorer
og generelt så lavt at det ikke havde en effekt på systemet. R1 klarede sig samlet set
bedre end R2, hvorfor denne opstartsmetode på anses som værende den mest effek­
tive. Yderligere forskning er dog nødvendig for at undersøge den langsigtede stabilitet og
hvorvidt resultaterne er skalerbare.

Introduction
Climate change is a global issue that requires urgent attention. Sea levels are rising due
to the melting of polar ice, and the weather is becoming more and more extreme. This
leads to flooding, wildfires, and food and water scarcity, which affects our everyday lives
[1]. People are losing their lives and homes due to natural disasters, and the price of food
has gone up drastically, affecting those of us living outside of the most critical areas.
When talking about climate change, global warming is often one of the first things that
comes to mind. This is happening due to the emission of greenhouse gases (GHG), pri­
marily consisting of carbon dioxide. Since the Industrial Revolution, carbon dioxide emis­
sions have been readily increasing as it is created as a by­product of our most widely
used energy source, fossil fuels [2, 3]. According to the Global Carbon Budget, these
unfavorable emissions from fossil fuels increased by 0.8% in 2024 compared to the pre­
vious year, emphasizing the need for alternative energy sources [4]. A transition from the
commonly used fossil fuels to a more sustainable energy source can potentially lead to a
significant decrease in GHG emissions, thereby significantly lowering the contribution to
global warming. Some sustainable energy sources can also be used for carbon capture,
further reducing global warming [5].
This chapter describes the basic concepts of anaerobic digestion, including biomass com­
position, pretreatment methods, biological production of methane, and biogas upgrading
methods, focusing on biological upgrading. Furthermore, the impact of anaerobic diges­
tion on the Sustainable Development Goals will be assessed.
1.1
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a biochemical process where different types of anaerobic
microorganisms, in the absence of oxygen, degrade complex organic matter, turning it
into biogas. The composition of this biogas is highly dependent on the source of the
biomass, but it is generally said to be composed of 50­70% methane (CH4) and 30­50%
carbon dioxide (CO2) [6].
The process of AD has been known for more than a hundred years. However, the in­
terest in AD has recently increased due to its ecological benefits, social and economic
advantages, and energy­saving possibilities [7]. All of which will be described further in
the following sections.
1.1.1
Biomass
AD can contribute positively to the bioeconomy by utilizing different types of renewable
biological resources to produce green energy while minimizing environmental impact [7].
Since AD can be run on a wide variety of organic materials, it is used as a waste treatment
method in a broad range of industries, which can be generally categorized into three
categories: agricultural, industrial, and municipal, each of which encompasses a diverse
range of different biomass types. Agricultural waste can take various forms, including
algae, agroindustrial waste, energy crops, and manure. In contrast, the industrial waste
includes biochemical waste, waste from food processing or slaughterhouses, and pulp
and paper waste. Municipal waste covers the organic part of municipal solid waste (the
biowaste) and sewage sludge [8, 9].
As mentioned earlier, the biogas composition is highly dependent on the composition
of the biomass used. For example, if high levels of carbohydrates are present, then the

Lignocellulosic biomass
Lignocellulose is one of the most abundant natural resources and has a high methane
potential. Therefore, it is most often what is used for AD. Lignocellulosic biomass consists
of three main components: Cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin, but will in many cases
also include other carbohydrates, lipids, and protein depending on the origin of the waste
[9, 10, 11].
Cellulose is the most abundant polymer found in organic material. It accounts for between
40­50% of the lignocellulosic biomass and is responsible for the mechanical strength of the
plant cell walls. It is a linear polymer consisting purely of D­glucose units linked through β­
(1,4)­glucoside bonds, as can be seen in Figure 1.1, using hydrogen and Van der Waals
bonds. As seen in Figure 1.4, cellulose contains different arrangements where part of
the molecules are amorphous, which is shown by a wigglier line, while other parts of the
molecule are crystalline, which is seen by the straight lines. The crystalline arrangement
is unfavorable because it is more difficult to degrade. The crystalline structure can be
decreased by pretreatment [9, 10, 12].
Figure 1.1: Molecular structure of cellulose showing connection via β­(1,4)­glucoside
bonds [9].
Hemicellulose is a complex branched polymer consisting of different monosaccharides
such as pentoses (xylose and arabinose) and hexoses (mannose, glucose, and galac­
tose), but also sugar/uronic acids such as galacturonic, glucuronic, and methyl galac­
turonic acid. The composition of hemicellulose varies depending on the origin of the
feedstock, but xylan is the dominant compound and can account for up to 90% of the
hemicellulosic polymer. In lignocellulosic biomass, hemicellulose can be found within the
range of 20­35%. Hemicellulose consists of a linear backbone, which, like cellulose, is
built up using β­(1,4)­glucoside bonds, as can be seen in Figure 1.2. The branching of
hemicellulose happens due to β­(1,4)­glucoside bonds, or occasionally β­(1,3)­glucoside
bonds, which are connected to the backbone of the polymer. The function of hemicellu­
lose is to connect cellulose and lignin by forming a linkage between the molecules, thereby
creating a more compact network [9, 10, 12, 13].
Figure 1.2: Molecular structure of hemicellulose showing the backbone of xylose units
connected via β­(1,4)­glucoside bonds [9].

Since hemicellulose has different sugars, an amorphous structure, low molecular weight,
and short lateral chains, it is easy to hydrolyze, which is why pretreatment of the biomass
is not essential for hemicellulose [9, 10, 12].
Lignin is a large, complex, and aromatic polymer consisting of cross­linked polymers of the
phenyl­propane­based units, namely p­coumaryl alcohol, coniferyl alcohol, and sinapyl
alcohol, as can be seen in Figure 1.3. Lignin is usually found in the range between 10­25%
depending on the origin of the biomass. The primary job of lignin is to provide resistance
against oxidative stress and microbial attacks, which is why lignin is significantly more
complex for the microorganisms in AD to digest [9, 10, 12].
Figure 1.3: Structural units of lignin [9].
Pretreatment
Pretreatment of the biomass is commonly used to increase its biodegradability, thereby
enhancing product yield. The primary purpose is to partially or completely break down the
structure of the biomass into fermentable sugars so that the microorganisms can break it
down further, as can be seen simplified in Figure 1.4. Due to especially lignin and crys­
talline cellulose, this pretreatment is needed for the degradation of lignocellulosic biomass
to happen efficiently, leading to an overall more effective process [9].
Figure 1.4: Simplified structure of lignocellulosic biomass and how it is affected by
pretreatment [14].
Generally, pretreatment methods can be divided into five categories: Mechanical, physi­
cal, chemical, biological, and combinative technology. All of them with the goal to disin­

tegrate the structure of the biomass in different ways, leading to increased surface area
and reduction of the crystallinity of cellulose [12].
Mechanical pretreatment includes milling or grinding, which is one of the most commonly
used forms of pretreatment. It is an easy way to significantly reduce the particle size of
the biomass, thereby increasing the hydrolysis rate since it is easier to degrade. These
methods are often the first choice because no toxic compounds or inhibitors are generated
using this method. Furthermore, it is easy to scale up, relatively cheap to operate, and
has a limited capital cost. Several other methods can be found within the category of
mechanical pretreatment, such as extrusion, dispersion, and ultrasonication [12].
Physical pretreatment includes thermal treatment and microwaving. The high temper­
atures of the thermal pretreatment process decrease the viscosity of the biomass and
cause the organic matter to become solubilized and thereby released by breaking down
the hydrogen bonds. Microwaving the biomass will also cleave the hydrogen bonds of
the molecules, leading to changes in the liquid phase. When using microwave irradiation,
both thermal and non­thermal effects work as pretreatment. Due to the bipolar com­
ponents of the molecules, which contain an electric field, a rotating effect occurs when
microwave irradiation is added, leading to an increase in temperature. Furthermore, the
changes in dipole directions, which are also caused by microwave irradiation, will lead
to the breakage of bonds and protein deconformation, further increasing the biogas yield
[12].
Chemical pretreatment is one of the largest categories, encompassing various pretreat­
ment methods that utilize acids, alkaline solutions, wet oxidation, green solvents, sur­
factants, and organic solvents. The most widely used chemical pretreatment method for
lignocellulosic biomass is alkaline pretreatment since it can highly disintegrate the pheno­
lic nature and lignin content. This helps the enzymes reach cellulose and hemicellulose,
thereby increasing the methane yield. For many years, sodium hydroxide has been the
most used alkali to disrupt the lignin structure of the lignocellulosic biomass. However,
calcium hydroxide and lime have also shown potential, with lime being one of the cheaper
options. Since sustainability is one of the main focuses when talking about AD, green sol­
vents must be mentioned. These green solvents, such as ionic liquids, can be used for
chemical pretreatment as they can be used to disintegrate lignocellulosic biomass while
being recyclable and not producing toxic products. However, this method is still in its early
phase, as solvents and solvent recovery methods are costly and need more attention be­
fore green solvents can be used on an industrial scale [12].
Biological pretreatment can be categorized into three main types: fungal pretreatment,
bacterial consortium, and enzymatic pretreatment. Common for all categories, their pri­
mary role is to reduce the use of carbohydrates and remove as much lignin as possi­
ble. The fungal pretreatment used two separate steps to disintegrate the lignocellulosic
biomass. First, the oxidative lignolytic system ruins the phenyl bonds of lignin, before the

1.1.2
Microbial consortia and the biochemical stages of AD
Methane production through anaerobic digestion (AD) occurs through a series of bio­
chemical stages performed by a diverse community of microorganisms. The production of
methane consists of four stages: hydrolysis, acidogenesis, acetogenesis, and methano­
genesis, all of which are interconnected, with the product of the previous step serving as
the substrate for the next. In this way, all stages are dependent on each other, which
can lead to process failure due to kinetic stress [10, 15]. In Figure 1.5, the different steps
and their products can be seen. In the following, the different biochemical stages will be
described.
Figure 1.5: Biological production of methane through the four steps: Hydrolysis,
Acidogenesis, Acetogenesis, and Methanogenesis.
Hydrolysis
The first step of AD is hydrolysis. Here, the biomass, which consists of large and insoluble
organic materials such as carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids, is broken down into soluble
monomers such as amino acids, monosaccharides, and long­chain fatty acids. This is
done by extracellular hydrolase enzymes synthesized and secreted by the surrounding
microorganisms. During hydrolysis, the enzymes cleave the large polymeric molecules
before adding water to the cleaved site, as can be seen in Figure 1.6.
Figure 1.6: Example of hydrolysis of carbohydrates.

Type­specific enzymes hydrolyze each of the different materials of the biomass. This
means that the carbohydrates cellulose and hemicellulose are hydrolyzed into glucose
and xylose by the enzymes cellulase and hemicellulase, which have been secreted from
saccharolytic microorganisms. Proteins are split into amino acids by proteases, which are
secreted by proteolytic microorganisms, while lipids are hydrolyzed into long­chain fatty
acids by the lipase­enzyme. Since the products from hydrolysis are highly dependent on
the origin of the biomass, only the most common products have been mentioned. After
the hydrolysis, the molecules are small enough to enter the bacterial cell using active
transport.
Research has shown that in the case of complex substrates, hydrolysis is most often the
rate­limiting step of AD. This means that the process tends to fail due to the formation of
toxic byproducts and undesired volatile fatty acids (VFAs) [10, 15, 16].
Acidogenesis
Acidogenesis, which is the second step of AD, can also be referred to as the fermentation
step. This step is linked to the previous step since it further converts the products of the
hydrolysis. The simple sugars, including glucose and xylose, are converted into pyruvate
(C3H4O3), ATP, and NADH. For glucose, this conversion happens through glycolysis,
which can be seen in Figure 1.7. Xylose is phosphorylated into xylose­5­phosphate before
entering the pentose phosphate pathway. In the pentose phosphate pathway, xylose­
5­phosphate is converted into Fructose 6­phosphate and Glyceraldehyde 3­phosphate,
which are then converted into pyruvate through glycolysis, as seen in Figure 1.8 [10, 17,
18].

Figure 1.8: Conversion of xylose to pyruvate.
Furthermore, the fermentative bacteria turn amino acids and simple sugars into VFAs
(e.g., acetic acid, butyric acid, and propionic acid), alcohols, hydrogen, and carbon diox­
ide. Since acidogenesis follows a branched metabolism, an increased concentration of
hydrogen, which is formed as an intermediate product, will drive the reaction away from
the desired metabolism, thereby decreasing the number of reduced compounds [10, 15,
19].
Acetogenesis
The third step of the anaerobic process is acetogenesis. In this step, the fatty acids from
hydrolysis and the alcohol and VFAs from the acidogenesis are further fermented into
6

==PAGE:17==
acetic acid, H2, and CO2 by acetogenic bacteria. This reaction is endergonic, meaning
that it requires energy to be driven. This is why the reaction acts syntrophic together
with methanogenesis. By coupling the two reactions, the overall reaction becomes exer­
gonic and thereby thermodynamically favorable. During this step, inter­species hydrogen
transfer happens since the acetogenesis step produces H2, while the methanogenesis
step removes H2 by using it for methane production together with CO2. If the partial pres­
sure of hydrogen becomes too high, anaerobic oxidation will no longer happen, meaning
that H2 will no longer be produced. Therefore, the collaboration and complex stability
between acetogenesis and methanogenesis is crucial for effective biogas production [10,
15, 19].
Methanogenesis
Methanogenesis is the final step of the four steps of AD. Here, methane is produced by
two different microbial groups, namely, hydrogenotrophic and acetoclastic methanogens.
As seen in Equation 1.1, the hydrogenotrophic methanogens use hydrogen as an electron
donor, reducing carbon dioxide, thereby turning it into methane and water [10].
4H2 + CO2 →CH4 + 2H2O
(1.1)
Meanwhile, acetoclastic methanogens ferment acetate, converting it into methane and
carbon dioxide, as seen in Equation 1.2. This is the primary reaction for methane pro­
duction and accounts for approximately two­thirds of the methane production, while the
hydrogenotrophic reaction accounts for the rest [10].
CH3COOH →CH4 + CO2
(1.2)
The constant removal of acetate and hydrogen leads to continuous growth of the syn­
trophic bacteria that convert the VFAs propionate and butyrate into acetate, thereby in­
creasing methane production. An accumulation of hydrogen and acetate can thereby lead
to inhibition of the AD process. Furthermore, an accumulation of short­chain organic acids
can decrease pH, creating an unfavorable environment for the microorganisms [10]. Es­
pecially, the methanogenic microorganisms are affected by the change in pH since these
require a higher pH compared to microorganisms of the other stages [20].
1.1.3
Biogas and digestate
The conversion of organic material using AD produces two types of valuable products:
biogas and digestate. In short, biogas is a renewable fuel that can be used to generate
green electricity, heat, or as vehicle fuel, while digestate can be utilized as fertilizer [21].
Biogas
Biogas is the end­product of AD. It consists of organic carbon which has been converted
into a combination of its most oxidized state, carbon dioxide (CO2), and its most reduced
form, methane (CH4). The composition of the biogas varies based on different param­
eters such as the origin of the biomass and operational parameters. As mentioned ear­
lier, biogas consists of approximately 50%­70% methane and 30%­50% carbon dioxide.
Furthermore, the biogas consists of minor concentrations of other compounds such as
nitrogen (N2), vaporized water (H2O), and the toxic and corrosive ammonia (NH3) and
hydrogen sulfide (H2S). These impurities also depend on the composition of the biomass
used [6].
The biogas can, in theory, be used directly for heat, electricity, and combined heat and
power (CHP) production, however in many cases the hydrogen sulfide (H2S) impurities

must be reduced since it can lead to damage to the energy conversion technologies if it
exceeds the limit of 300­500 ppm [21].
Digestate
The digestate from the AD process has the potential to be used as an organic fertilizer, if
handled correctly, thereby serving as a sustainable alternative to synthetic fertilizers used
in agriculture. Studies have shown that the fertilizer value of digestate lies between the
value of livestock manure and mineral fertilizers, sometimes even equal to that of mineral
fertilizers, depending on the nature of the feedstock [22].
If the digestate can be used as fertilizer, it would significantly ease waste management,
while producing a product that can be sold, thereby earning more money on the process.
However, before applying the digestate as fertilizer, more studies are needed to evaluate
the potential impact on the environment and human health. It has been found that due to
the high pH and NH3/NH4 contents, the digestate presents a higher potential of emitting
ammonia and nitrous oxide into the atmosphere compared to livestock manure, leading
to a possible environmental risk [22]. Furthermore, a risk of sulfur volatilization is present
[23], which can lead to the loss of a vital nutrient [24], thereby lowering the fertilizer value
of the digestate.
It has been found that post­treatment of the digestate is needed to reach optimal fertiliza­
tion properties. The preferred treatment is composting since it produces a stable, safe,
and nutrient­rich fertilizer, but processing technologies such as drying, filtration, ammonia
stripping, etc., have also been proposed for the nutrient recovery of the digestate [24].

1.2
Biogas upgrading
As described earlier, biogas is composed of a mixture of different components, primarily
methane and carbon dioxide. All components that are not methane are unwanted impuri­
ties, as they limit the use of the gas. Methane has a relatively high energy content, which
can be described by its Lower Calorific Value (LCV) of 50.4 MJ/kg CH4. The LCV defines
the energy that can be released when burned, meaning the energy that would be avail­
able when using methane as a fuel. By having other gases present within the biogas, the
LCV is significantly lowered, thereby lowering its values as a fuel source. Furthermore,
possible contaminants such as hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and ammonia (NH3) are highly
corrosive, toxic, and can damage a heat­to­power unit if used as fuel. Therefore, the
biogas needs to be upgraded before it can be utilized as an effective energy source [6].
When upgrading biogas to a methane content above 95%, thereby having created bio­
methane, it can be directly used as a vehicle fuel source or added to the already existing
natural gas network [27]. Especially, the possibility of adding the upgraded biogas to the
natural gas network increases the value of the fuel source since transport costs can be
lowered if the production site is close to the network. The existing infrastructure is already
designed to handle the upgraded biogas, as long as they abide by the regulations [28, 29].
1.2.1
Conventional biogas upgrading methods
Biogas can be upgraded using a wide array of chemical, physical, biological approaches,
or a combination of these.
The chemical biogas upgrading uses H2 to reduce the unwanted CO2 to CH4 via the
Sabatier reaction [30]. One of the most commonly used chemical technologies for con­
Investigating different start­up methods for trickle bed bioreactors for biogas upgrading
9
==PAGE:20==
ventional biogas upgrading is amine scrubbing [31]. This chemical scrubber uses an
aqueous amine solution to bind the CO2, thereby removing it from the biogas [6]. Af­
terwards, the CO2 is, as briefly described, converted into methane. There are several
advantages to using this method for biogas upgrading, one of them being the fact that the
CO2 emissions are reduced since the captured CO2 is utilized for methane production.
Furthermore, this chemical reaction is highly selective, leading to a minimum of methane
getting lost. On the downside, expensive catalysts are needed to drive the reaction, while
trace gases present in the biogas can easily affect the process [30].
Physical biogas upgrading technologies work by simply removing CO2 from the biogas
[30]. Some of the most common methods in this category are water scrubbing and pres­
sure swing absorption [31]. The water scrubbing method uses the different solubility of the
gases to separate CH4, with a low solubility, from the CO2, which has a much higher sol­
ubility. This simple method can create methane of high purity while only applying water,
which can be regenerated. However, this method has both high operating and investment
costs, and requires vast amounts of water unless it is regenerated, which will further add to
the operating costs [30]. Pressure swing absorption uses a combination of the molecular
characteristics and affinity for the absorbent material to separate the different gases in the
biogas. As the name suggests, pressure differences retain the different gases. A specific
gas will be absorbed and separated from the rest at high pressure. When the pressure
is decreased, the gas will be released [6]. Using this method required limited capital and
energy costs while producing high­purity methane. The disadvantages here are the fact
that pre­purification and pre­drying are needed, and that methane loss is higher compared
to the other technologies [30].
Common for many chemical and physical upgrading technologies are the high investment
costs and energy demand. Furthermore, the CO2, which is separated using physical
upgrading, will often be released into the air, contributing to global warming and wasting
a valuable carbon source. Therefore, the interest in biological upgrading methods has
increased due to the low energy and carbon footprints [30].
1.2.2
Biological biogas upgrading
In biological biogas upgrading methods, microorganisms are used as catalysts to drive the
conversion of CO2 into CH4 or other valuable bio­based products. Unlike several other
biogas upgrading techniques, this conversion can be driven at moderate conditions, such
as atmospheric pressure and room temperature, which leads to a lower energy footprint.
This method can be divided into two categories: photosynthetic and chemoautotrophic
[30].
The photosynthetic method uses phototropic organisms like algae to convert CO2 into
CH4 through photosynthesis. Solar radiation, water, and nutrients are thereby being uti­
lized to produce a CH4­rich gas, oxygen, and biomass [30]. This method can reach high
methane levels of up to approximately 97%, while additionally removing H2S, which is
toxic and corrosive, and therefore highly unwanted in the upgraded gas [6]. The down­
side is, however, that it requires high investment cost, high energy demand, and high
natural resources requirements, while having a low photosynthetic CO2 uptake [30].
The chemoautotrophic methods include a wide array of different mechanisms for the con­
version of carbon dioxide, such as methanogens binding CO2 into CH4 or dicarboxylic
acid­producing bacteria binding CO2 into dicarboxylic acid, e.g., biosuccinic acid [30].
The CO2 to CH4 bioconversion can happen through 3 different pathways, namely through
direct hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis, indirect through homoacetogenic acetate for­

==PAGE:21==
mation, which is followed by acetoclastic methanogenesis, or through direct electron
transfer (DET). As described in the case of AD, the hydrogenotrophic methanogens con­
vert CO2 to CH4 by using H2 as an external electron source following the Wolf Cycle.
The homoacetogenic bacteria, on the other hand, convert CO2 into acetate through the
Wood­Ljungdahl pathway. Afterwards, the acetoclastic methanogens will convert the ac­
etate into methane [30].
Digestate is often used as the inoculum when starting a new bioreactor for upgrading
biogas since hydrogenotrophic methanogens are difficult to cultivate as pure cultures.
Because of that, the reactor is filled with a mixed culture of microorganisms specialized
in the breakdown of biomass and conversion of biogas [32].
Over time, the community within the microbial consortia will be affected by the change in
operational parameters, thereby creating a community specialized in converting CO2 into
CH4 [33].
To begin with, the hydrolytic and fermentative bacteria, which used to be abundant in the
digestate due to their crucial role in breaking down the incoming biomass, would decrease
significantly over time. This would happen since the reactor for upgrading is only fed gas
in the form of biogas and H2, leading to a selective pressure on the different microbial
taxa [34].
This means that the hydrogenotrophic methanogens, whose primary role is converting
CO2 into CH4 using H2, will become the dominant species within the consortia since an
excess of H2 will be present within the reactor. CH4 can also be produced from acetate by
acetoclastic methanogens. The relative abundance of these microorganisms within the
reactor is highly dependent on the availability of acetate and, thereby, on the amount of
acetogenic bacteria. As hydrogenotrophic methanogens are often faster than the aceto­
genic bacteria, they will usually outcompete them when excess hydrogen is supplied to
the reactor [33].
To make this method environmentally friendly, the H2 utilized by the hydrogenotrophic
methanogens should be derived from a renewable source. This could be the use of re­
newable electricity, from wind energy or solar panels, to drive water electrolysis, thereby
splitting water into H2 and O2. Furthermore, water electrolysis can be a way to store
surplus energy generated from renewable electricity sources as a power­to­gas (P2G)
technology. Hydrogen is, however, challenging to store, which is why utilizing it for bio­
gas upgrading is a promising solution, since it can then be added to the natural gas grid
[6].

When working with biological biogas upgrading, there are generally 3 design configura­

In­situ biogas upgrading happens within the biogas reactor itself [35]. Hydrogen is added
directly to the biogas reactor, thereby making it possible to convert the endogenous car­
bon dioxide into methane using the methanogenic archaea that are already present [36].

monitored and controlled to achieve optimal methane recovery. When utilizing CO2 to

While hydrogen ions and bicarbonate, which act as a buffer in the biogas process, are

When using ex­situ biogas upgrading, the biogas is transferred from the biogas reactor

of approximately 80­90%. Afterward, the partially upgraded biogas is transferred to an
external reactor, where it is further upgraded up to above 98% methane. By using this
method, the pH enhancements, which were required for the in­situ processes, are signifi­
cantly lower. At the same time, a smaller external reactor is needed for ex­situ upgrading,
since the biogas is already partially converted when it reaches the reactor [36].

2.2
Inoculum
The substrate used in this project has been retrieved from SSF Energi: Snertinge, Særslev,
Føllenslev Energiselskab A.m.b.a., which is a primarily cattle and pig manure­fed biogas
reactor. The digestate will act as the inoculum, providing the microorganisms responsible
for the biogas upgrading.
Following the retrieval, the inoculum was incubated at thermophilic conditions [55 °C ± 1)
for 21 days until most of the solids had been digested and the production of biogas had
been reduced. After the incubation period had ended, the biomass was sieved through
a range of sieves from 2 mm to 700µm to minimize the chances of clogging of the tubes
during trickling while constantly flushing with nitrogen to ensure anaerobic conditions.
After sieving, a sample was taken, and the inoculum was placed back in the incubator for
another day to let it rest before use.
The sample was centrifuged for 10 minutes at 12,000 RPM, and the supernatant collected.

measurements were made. Sulfate (LCK353, Hach, US), nitrate (LCK342, Hach, US),
ammonia (LCK302, Hach, US), and phosphate (LCK350, Hach, US) were measured ac­

2.2.1
Setup
The experimental setup for this project, which can be seen in Figure 2.4 includes two
laboratory­scale TBRs, which will be operated at thermophilic conditions (52 °C ± 2 °C
) and atmospheric pressure. Heating for the reactors was provided using a hot water
insulating system. The reactors are run in the lower end of the thermophilic range since
the biogas plant, from which the inoculum was retrieved, ran at approximately 48 °C.
Since the focus is on the different start­up strategies, the first reactor (R1) will be fed
only through trickling while the second reactor (R2) will be flooded throughout the start­up
phase before being only periodically filled.
Both reactors had a volume of 1 L before the addition of the packing material. After
the addition of Evolution Aqua K1 media packing material, the working volume of the two
reactors decreased to 820 mL and 790 mL, respectively. Three sampling ports are placed

throughout the length of the reactor, making it possible to perform DNA sampling and gas

Initially, four peristaltic pumps were used for this setup. The first pump (P1) is used to
trickle the inoculum, delivering microorganisms and nutrients, from the liquid reservoir
(LR1) through the 6 trickling ports (2 mm in diameter) at the top of the reactor. The pump
runs at a flow rate of 19.2 mL/min (10 RPM). The second pump (P2) is used for the addition
of carbon dioxide to both reactors. From the gas bag, the tubing was split into two before
going through the pump as two different streams, ensuring that both reactors are provided
with an equal amount of gas. Thereby, possible issues due to different resistance in the
reactors can be avoided while still sharing the gas supply. The third pump (P3) is used to
feed hydrogen to both reactors. Like with the carbon dioxide pump, both pump heads are
used to provide an equal amount of gas. Initially, the gas pumps are run at a stoichiometric
ratio of (1:4 CO2:H2), so the carbon dioxide pump has an initial flow rate of 0.4 mL/min
(0.2 RPM) while the hydrogen pump runs at 1.6 mL/min (0.8 RPM). This gas flow leads
to an initial gas retention time (GRT) of 6.9 h. After passing pumps 2 and 3, the gas inlets
are connected and passed through a gas sampling port. This makes it possible to monitor
the composition of the incoming gas before it reaches the reactors. The fourth pump (P4)
is used for manually filling and emptying the flooded reactor. This function is needed to
provide a representative sampling of the liquid fraction by emptying the reactor into the
liquid reservoir before sampling, and to be able to do DNA extractions.
Due to the difference in the start­up methods, the gas is fed differently to the two reactors.
In the trickling reactor, R1, the gas is fed from the top, pushed through the reactor, and
leaves the reactor at the bottom together with the liquid fraction, thereby following the
standard setup for a TBR. In the flooded reactor, R2, the gas is added from the bottom
due to its flooded nature. The gas will thereby travel through the liquid and exit the reactor
from the top. By adding the gas at the bottom of the reactor, contact time and surface area
between the gas and liquid phases are increased, leading to better gas­liquid transfer.

2.2.2
Feeding of reactors
Throughout the experiment, the reactors were fed 6 times, through the liquid sampling
port, to maintain the liquid fraction. For the first feeding, some of the remaining inoculum
was used, whereafter reject water from BIOFOS wastewater treatment plant was used
for the remaining feedings. Alongside maintaining the liquid levels in the liquid reservoir,
the reject water provided nutrients for the microorganisms in the form of ammonia and
phosphates. While feeding, the feed was poured into a beaker and constantly flushed
with nitrogen. The syringes used for the feeding were likewise flushed with nitrogen to

Sodium Tungstate and Sodium Sulfide were furthermore added to the reactors, as these
micro­nutrients were not present in the reject water but are vital for the microorganisms.

2.3.1
Analysis of the gas
Calibration curve
To be able to quantify samples using gas chromatography (GC), a calibration curve must
be made, using different calibration standards, of which the concentrations are known.
This calibration curve creates a relationship between the concentrations of the different
components and the response of the machine, making it possible to estimate concentra­
tions of an unknown sample by looking at the response. For this experiment, two calibra­
tion curves were made since two different inlets were used to measure the different gas
types.
To create the calibration standards, 4 glass bottles were found together with butyl rubber
stoppers and aluminum crimps for gas sampling. The bottles were then weighed together
with the matching rubber stopper and aluminum crimps. The weight was then noted down
for all bottles. Then, the bottles were filled with water. Before adding the rubber stopper,
a syringe needle was placed in the rubber stopper, thereby letting out possible air bubbles
that would be present inside the bottle. After closing the bottle, the needle was removed,
and the new weight of the bottle was noted down. This way, the exact working volume
of the bottle could be determined. After determining the working volume of all bottles,
they were placed inside an incubator at 55 °C ± 1 to dry. After being completely dry, the
bottles were cooled by placing them in cold water. The bottles were then flushed with the
main gas used for the different concentrations, e.g. pure nitrogen for level 1. After being
flushed for a while, the butyl rubber stoppers were inserted and fastened with aluminum
crimps. The remaining gases were then added to the bottle leading to the concentrations
seen in Table 2.2.

2.3.2
Analysis of the liquid fraction
Sampling
For R1, the liquid fraction sampling happens through the liquid reservoir’s sampling port
using a 100 mL syringe, since the liquid is constantly mixed due to trickling. On the other
hand, since R2 is flooded, it must be drained into the liquid reservoir to mix the liquid
fraction before samples can be taken. Afterward, the liquids are pumped back into the
reactor. On average, two liquid samples are taken each week. Generally, 12 mL samples
are taken for the determination of pH and VFA concentrations, while an additional 10 mL is
taken when samples are needed for DNA sequencing. The liquid reservoirs are regularly
topped up with nutrients matching the amount removed since the last feed.
VFA measurements
For the VFA measurements, 1.5 mL of sample was mixed with 100 µL of 34% o­phosphoric
acid. Duplicates were prepared for each reactor ensuring that at least on esample was
available in case something went wrong while performing the analysis. The sample was
thereafter gently shaken to encourage the removal of CO2. When the samples were no
longer bubbling, CO2 had successfully been removed, and the samples were then trans­
ferred to Eppendorf tubes. The samples were then centrifuged for 7 minutes at 12,000
RPM. Since the first centrifugation did not remove a satisfying amount of particles, the
supernatant was moved to another Eppendorf tube before being centrifuged for another 7
minutes at 12,000 RPM. When the sample was clear, 1 mL of supernatant was transferred
into a CG­vial before adding 100 µL of internal standard. Afterwards, the vials were put
in the freezer at ­18 °C until further analysis.
The Agilent 7890A Gas Chromatograph (Agilent Technologies, US) equipped with a flame
ionization detector (FID) and a SGE capillary column (length 30 m, diameter 0.53 mm, film
1.00 µm), which is used for the VFA measurements, has a capacity for 100 samples [40].

2.4
Biofilm
2.4.1
Sampling
To get samples of the biofilm, pellets of the packing material were retrieved from inside the
reactor columns through the 3 sampling ports (top, middle, and bottom). Before retrieval,
trickling is turned off and the reactors are emptied, leaving all of the process liquid inside
the liquid reservoirs. The gas flow was kept low enough for the remaining liquid not to be
pushed out of the sampling port, but also turned on to keep oxygen out of the anaerobic
system. To ensure that oxygen could not enter the reactor, nitrogen was flushed over
the sampling port while retrieving the pellets. All samples for DNA determination were
taken as duplicates from each of the sampling ports, while a single sample for measuring
biofilm accumulation were taken from the top of R1 and the bottom of R2. Upon retrieval,
the pellets were placed in 5 mL of Phosphate­buffered saline (PBS) solution in a 15 mL
centrifuge tube. Samples taken for biofilm analysis were placed in the fridge at 5 °C, while
the samples for the DNA were centrifuged for 20 minutes to ensure that the biofilm got off
the pellet. Then the pellet was removed and the samples are centrifuged for another 20
minutes. Lastly, the liquid fraction was removed and the samples stored in the freezer at
­80 °C until needed for further analysis.
2.4.2
Determination of biofilm accumulation
Before filtering the samples to determine the accumulation of biofilm over time in each re­
actor, the biofilm was separated from the pellet, first using a vortex to remove the majority
and then using sonication to remove the remaining biofilm. After separating the biofilm
from the pellet, the pellet was removed, and the biofilm was filtered using vacuum filtra­
tion. Before the filtration, each filter was weighed together with the weighing boat. After
filtration, the samples were left to dry before finally being weighed, thereby determining
the weight of the biofilm.

Results and Discussion
The data presented within the following sections have been obtained from the TBR and
the flooded reactor during an experimental run time of 78 days from the day of inoculation.
The overall run time has been divided into 4 periods: Start­up (day 0­32), disturbances
(day 33­55), recovery (day 56­70), and low GRT (day 71­78).
3.1
Trickle Bed Reactor
3.1.1
Start­up
As can be seen in Figure 3.1, the microorganisms quickly adapted to the conditions within
the TBR. Already, on day 6, the hydrogenotrophic methanogens are producing a signif­
icant amount of methane. Furthermore, acetate is slowly accumulating since the aceto­
genic bacteria, which were also present in the inoculum, are converting the excess CO2
into acetate.

When looking at the pH, which can be seen in Figure 3.2, a minor drop happens as soon

no longer be solubilized in the liquid phase, resulting in an increase in pH to above 8.5.

3.1.2
Disturbances
By day 33, the disturbance period was initiated. Here, the septum on the carbon dioxide
gas bag was loosened slightly so that low amounts of air could enter the bag, which would
then be pumped into the reactor to simulate a scenario where the community is exposed
to oxygen. This was left for 24 h, giving it a short exposure time, before gas samples were
taken. The days on which gas samples were taken after the addition of air are marked
with a darker border on the graphs. The addition of air, and thereby the addition of oxy­
gen, led to an aerobic environment, which is toxic to the microorganisms. This resulted
in a decrease in both methane production and methane levels. The gas bag was then
sealed off, and the gas was replaced with pure carbon dioxide. By day 35, the septum
on the gas bag had loosened once again, but this time even more, allowing air to enter
the system. The next day, methane production had decreased significantly, along with a
drop in methane levels of more than 60%, clearly indicating that the microorganisms were
affected by the aerobic environment. Furthermore, it can be seen from the pH measure­
ments that CO2 was accumulating in the liquid phase, leading to a pH below the optimal
range, which has been marked out on Figure 3.2.
By the end of day 36, the hydrogen pump was turned off, thereby starting the next step of
the disruption period. Within the first 5 days, the reactor was operating with a GRT of 34.1
h, as the only gas added was from the carbon dioxide pump, which was running at 0.2
rpm. At this time, the only hydrogen source for the reaction within the next days was the
hydrogen that was already present inside the reactor. Initially, conversion increased by
around 9% before decreasing again, due to the hydrogen being depleted. At day 41, hy­
drogen was reintroduced, thereby changing the GRT back to the original 6.9 h. The large
concentration of CO2, which had not been utilized due to the missing hydrogen, meant
that a lot of it is solubilized in the liquid phase, leading to the pH moving down towards
7.39, which is the lowest value found for this reactor. At the same time, the VFAs, mainly
acetate, are accumulating due to a combination of the sudden drop in methane production
at day 36 and the fact that hydrogen had been missing, why the acetogenic bacteria were
converting the excess CO2 into acetate while the hydrogenotrophic methanogens were
missing their key component to produce methane.
After leaving the reactor alone for 5 days, air was reintroduced via the carbon dioxide
gas bag. This time, it allowed air to enter for 48 h, thereby increasing the exposure time,
before gas measurements were taken. These measurements showed an increase in
both methane production and methane levels of the outlet stream. Based on data from
the earlier days of the experiment, it has been assumed that methane production and
methane levels increased significantly within the first 5 days of working at a GRT of 6.9 h.
After introducing air to the system, thereby creating an aerobic environment, methane
production is expected to decrease. Ultimately, these two different cases would lead
to an increase in methane production, despite the decline in production at the time of
measurement. To further investigate the robustness of the microbial consortium within
the reactor, the supply of oxygen was stopped, the reactor was fed with reject water, and
the additional micronutrients, Sodium Tungstate and Sodium Sulfide, were added. This
was done to determine if the microorganisms could overcome the toxic environment, after

same time, carbon dioxide had accumulated to above 80% in the outlet gas. After refilling
the hydrogen gas bag and ensuring that the septum was fastened tightly, thereby cutting
off the oxygen supply, the flows were changed to obtain a GRT of 3.3 h with a CO2 pump
running at 0.1 rpm, which is significantly lower than the stoichiometric ratio. This way, the
low levels of hydrogen, which had been happening since day 50, would be compensated
for while leaving the system with a GRT close to a time that had proved to work earlier in
the experiment.
3.1.3
Recovery
Day 57 marks the first day of the recovery period after the disturbance. No DNA samples
were taken to ensure the best possible situation for the microorganisms, as this could
further stress the system. Instead, reject water was fed, providing nutrients, and the inlet
flow was lowered to reach a GRT of 6.9 h. At this point, acetate levels had started to
increase as a delayed response to the decrease in methane production that began after
day 50.
By day 60, the reactor appeared to be recovering with an increase in methane production
and methane levels above 80%. Therefore, the inlet gas flow was increased to give a
GRT of 3.4 h, which had earlier been proved to be at the higher end of what the reactor
had time to convert. Two days later, both the methane production and the methane levels
had increased even further. Therefore, the gas inlet flow rate was increased until a GRT
of 2.5 h was reached.

3.1.4
Low gas retention time
After completing the samplings on day 69, the inlet gas flow was increased until a GRT of
1 h was achieved with a 1:4 stoichiometric ratio between carbon dioxide and hydrogen.

the two reactors combined, two extra gas bags were connected to the system to ensure
that the reactor would not run out of gas. At day 71, both the methane production and
the methane levels in the outlet stream had increased even further. Together with the
increasing methane levels, the pH kept going up due to the low levels of excess CO2.
During the weekend, the flow was reduced to a GRT of 6.9 h to ensure that the gas bags
would not run out, thereby allowing oxygen to enter the system. On day 74, the decreased
gas flow had led to a significant decrease in methane production, a slight decrease in the
methane level, and a slight accumulation of carbon dioxide compared to day 71. Since
the first push led to almost complete conversion, the second push would be with a GRT
of 20 minutes. This time, 89.5 L of hydrogen would be needed per day, so another gas
bag was connected to the setup.
At day 75, it was found that the methane production had increased. However, the methane
levels had dropped rapidly because the GRT was too short for the microorganisms to
react with the large amounts of gas provided. Since the point of lowering the GRT was to
determine if it would lead to system failure, the flow was left for another day to observe

3.2.2
Disturbances
The disturbance period was initiated at day 33 by adding air to the system. This was
achieved by loosening the septum on the carbon dioxide gas bag, allowing air to mix
with the gas inside. The mixed gas was then pumped into the reactor for the next 24 h,
creating a short exposure to an aerobic environment. This led to a rapid decrease in both
the methane production and the methane level in the outlet stream. The gas bag was
then sealed, and the gas inside was replaced with pure carbon dioxide.
At day 35, air was again being added to the system for 24 h. By the time of the next gas
measurements, the methane production had decreased even further, leaving the methane
levels in the outlet stream below 5%. Furthermore, carbon dioxide has accumulated sig­
nificantly inside the reactor due to the low conversion rates. These high amounts of carbon
dioxide had decreased the pH so that it was no longer within the optimal pH range.
By the end of day 36, the hydrogen pump was turned off. By turning off the pump, only
the CO2 pump running at 0.2 rpm was left, giving the system a GRT of 34.1 h. This high
GRT allowed the microorganisms sufficient time to react with the gas inside the system,
which is why the methane level increased by day 39. However, this also meant that the
system would eventually run out of hydrogen, as no new hydrogen was added, which
would lead to a drop in both methane production and methane levels. During the period
when hydrogen was no longer supplied, CO2 accumulated within the system, leading to
a further decrease in pH. Ultimately, the system reached a pH of 7.70, which is the lowest
pH value recorded for the flooded reactor during this experiment. By day 41, the hydrogen
pump was turned back on, changing the GRT back to the initial 6.9 h.

Conclusions
Since biological biogas upgrading is highly dependent on microbial communities, the start­
up period and the establishment of the community will significantly impact the biogas
upgrading process. Based on these experiments, the TBR was found to be performing
slightly better compared to the flooded reactor, suggesting that this may be the most
efficient start­up method.
Focusing on the biofilm formation, which is essential for microbial communities, the en­
vironment within the TBR made it possible to create a thin but dense biofilm due to the
intermittent wetting. On the other hand, in the constantly flooded reactor, a thick aqueous
biofilm was created, which was not nearly as dense and had mass transfer limitations
due to gas­liquid transfer. As soon as the reactor was partially flooded, the microbial
communities began creating a denser and more active biofilm.
In general, the outlet gas containing the highest levels of methane came from the TBR.
Within the experimental run time, this reactor reached what can be defined as complete
conversion, where either hydrogen or carbon dioxide levels are below 1%, 10 times, while
also reaching methane levels above 95% several times. On the other hand, complete
conversion only happened twice in the flooded reactor, while the maximum methane level
found was 89.4%. Over time, the TBR produced more methane than the flooded reactor,
even though the methane production would sometimes be better within the flooded reactor
based on methane levels and the amount of outgoing gas.
When making changes in the GRT, the TBR generally needed a shorter time to adapt to
the new environment. Furthermore, while pushing the reactors at a GRT as low as 20 min,
it was further found that the TBR continued to adapt while the flooded reactor could not
keep up with the flow, which led to the gas being diluted, resulting in even lower methane
levels.
The pH was slightly more stable in the flooded reactor, compared to the TBR. Throughout
the experiment, barely any VFAs were produced within the reactors; therefore, it can
be concluded that these did not significantly affect the pH. This means that the primary
contributor to changes in pH was the carbon dioxide that was solubilized in the liquid
phase. However, in general, the pH was relatively stable and remained within the range
of 7 to 9.5.
Since the reactors in general had reasonably high methane levels, this may suggest that
the system was more or less in balance. When comparing methane levels with the acetate
concentration, which was by far the most significant contributor to the VFAs, it can be seen
that when sudden decreases in methane levels happen in the TBR, it affects the acetate
concentrations more than in the flooded reactor. Since the increase in acetate generally
happened at the same time as a decrease in methane, it suggests that it happened due
to the acetoclastic methanogens becoming less active.
It has been found that periods without hydrogen only affect the current conversion rate,
and that the reactors will increase the methane production as soon as hydrogen is avail­
able again. On the other hand, the addition of air, thereby creating a toxic aerobic envi­
ronment within the reactor, has a more significant negative effect on the microorganisms,
as they require more time to recover.

Future Perspectives
Running this experiment for extended periods would give more representative data. First
and foremost, the start­up time must be long enough for the microbes to acclimate and for
the biofilm to be formed, which was prioritized in this process. Hereafter, a steady state
should have been found with a reasonable GRT, not too high or too low. This step was
skipped in this experiment due to time limitations; therefore, the reactors had not reached
a steady state completely before the disruptions were introduced. Had the disruptions
been performed over a longer time span, the reactors would have had time to completely
recover between different disruptions, thereby making the various data points stand out.
The long­term stability of the reactors would furthermore be interesting to monitor due to
their possible industrial use.
Furthermore, running the DNA sequencing and thereby determining the composition of
the microbial community throughout the reactors would make it possible to optimize the
reactors, creating higher methane production and process stability. Furthermore, it would
be possible to determine exactly how the microbes were affected by the aerobic environ­
ment, which was created in the disturbance period.
In this experiment, it was found that starting up the reactor only using trickling was more
efficient than flooding the reactor with the inoculum. However, it is known that liquid will
always take the easiest route; therefore, it can be assumed that routes will be created
inside the TBR, making areas in which limited nutrients are present and other areas that
were getting nutrients all the time. By using periodic filling instead of trickling, an even
distribution of nutrients can be expected, which may be an even better start­up method. In
order to investigate whether periodically filling is more effective within the start­up process,
further research is needed.
Focusing on the potential use in the industry, further research is needed regarding the
scalability of the reactors. In this experiment, the TBR was generally outperformed the
flooded reactor. Therefore, if a pilot­scale TBR shows that these results are in fact scal­
able, it would mean that significantly lower amounts of inoculum would be needed to start
up the reactor using a TBR compared to the flooded design.
