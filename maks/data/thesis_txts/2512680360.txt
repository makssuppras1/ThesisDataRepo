ABSTRACT
The power system is undergoing a transition from Conventional power generation to
Renewable Energy based generation to counteract the climate change, however, this
transition is causing the system inertia to reduce as rotating machines are being replaced
by converter-interfaced power generating units like wind turbines, solar PV, etc and this
reduction in inertia is posing a threat to the power system stability, which in turn gives
rise to the requirement of providing inertia service from wind turbines and other renewable
sources of energy.
Inertia emulation service for individual wind turbines and wind parks has been investigated.
For providing inertia emulation service that can comply with grid-code requirements, a
state-based inertia control was developed. At the wind park level, two Coordinated inertia
control schemes were developed i.e. Distributed and Centralized inertia control. Both
of the schemes were used to study the impact of park level response and to investigate
possible beneﬁts of such an approach.
The report is divided into four main parts. The ﬁrst part of the report focuses on the
fundamentals of inertia response and involves a study of diﬀerent inertia control schemes
proposed in the literature and on the development of a new and ﬂexible control scheme
to provide inertia response sustained inertia response based on speciﬁed limits by the
TSO. The second part of the report is development, test and improvement of the new
inertia control scheme, in which the impact of the newly developed scheme is studied
on the turbine behavior and the focus is on coordinating the local wind turbine control
and the newly developed state-based inertia control for a smooth operation of the wind
turbines and largely the wind park. The last part of the report involves transfer of inertia
control model and coordinated control model developed in Simulink to RTDS, setting-up of
communication between RTDS and Controller hardware using TCP/IP and Modbus-TCP
and validation of the control schemes on a test network using Hardware-in-the-loop test.
It has been observed that when considering local inertia control, the interaction between
existing wind turbine controls has been found to be necessary. Local control has been
observed to be forcing wind turbines, even with lower kinetic energy to inject a ﬁxed amount
of power, this as a consequence slows down the rotor too much and the recovery period
becomes too long. There is also a possibility of rotor stall in extreme cases. Thus, the
state of the kinetic energy of the wind turbine becomes an important variable for providing
inertia response. In coordinated control, this factor of kinetic energy was observed to be
working well in preventing the turbines to be slowed down too much. More so, the impact
of bus voltages on the inertia capability of the wind farm has also been observed.
In addition to this, the model of the wind turbine drive-train has been found to be of
importance when considering the study of the recovery period of the wind turbine after
inertial power injection. The shaft model is wind turbine models is very stiﬀand this can
hide the recovery dynamics of the wind turbine. Thus, a two-mass model for the wind

Based on the tests conducted, it was observed that the addition of inertia emulation control

grid support services like inertia emulation.

INTRODUCTION
The United Nations Climate Action summit saw a huge debate on the urgent need for
reducing greenhouse gas emission to keep global temperatures below the +1.5degC above
pre-industrial levels [1]. In order to push for supportive policies towards managing climate
change, large scale demonstrations were held in September 2019 under the banner of
‘Global Climate Strike’ [2] in diﬀerent parts of the world. These recent development have
brought much attention of global leaders and have brought Renewable Energy Sources to
the forefront of politics. In the Paris climate agreement [3], the participating nations have
made promises to reduce green house gas emissions and have pledged to reduce respective
CO2 emission as much as possible. The EU and China, one of largest contributors to
greenhouse gas emissions have taken up substantial goals towards the reduction of carbon
emissions. This is the reason why EU member nations have further accelerated development
of renewable energy. Few countries like Denmark are being considered as a benchmark for
sustainable energy development and shifting from fossil fuels and for good reason, since
Denmark has targeted 39.2% of its gross energy demand from renewable energy by 2020
and by today, it has already crossed the 30% mark. Additionally, on September 15th, the
country’s renewable energy production exceeded its demand for the ﬁrst time and Denmark
produced 130% of its Electricity from wind farms alone [4].
According to the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) [5], the total wind energy contributed over
the last 12 months has been 46% (from September 2018 to August 2019) and in the ﬁgure
1.1, it has been compared against 2017-2018 data for the same period and 5 year average.

1. Introduction
To understand the trend of increasing wind power, it is imperative to see how wind energy
generation in the Danish power system has increased over the years (ﬁgure 1.2). It is clear
that there has been a steep rise in energy generation from wind and the rate of increase
of wind energy has slowed down. This may be due to the fact that newer projects are
larger and more ambitious and take long time to complete, an example of such project is
the Kriegers Flak 600MW Oﬀshore wind farm for which construction started in 2016 and
is expected to be in operation by 2021 [6]. The wind farm will have 72 Siemens-Gamesa
wind turbines of rating 8.4MW and will supply 604.8 MW of power from the Baltic sea.
Figure 1.2. Wind Energy Generation in Denmark over the years
Large wind farm projects like these are common and the latest project in spotlight is a
tender won by Orsted to install a wind farm on Long Beach in New York where they
have planned to be 100% renewable by 2040 [7]. All these point towards an increasing
wind power percentage in the power systems all over the world. While this is a good news
from the perspective of the environment, the technical problems associated with replacing
synchronous generators with converter interfaced generation like wind farms has only
started to surface. Converter based power generating units are relatively new technology
as compared to conventional generation that has been evolving for many decades and

1.1
Eﬀects of the changes in the Energy system
The increase of Wind farms, PV farms and HVDC interconnections in the power system
is posing a challenge of rotational inertia in the power system (a design characteristic of
synchronous generator based conventional power plants). This inertia which is a stored
form of energy in large generators is inherently available in the events of drops in frequency
and protects the power system from causing frequency instability due to faults. One of
the main problems seen with converter interfaced generating units like Type 3 and Type 4
wind turbines is the lack of inertial response which is inherent property of synchronous
machines used in conventional power plants. This property is critical in preventing system
frequency to fall below a certain point where the loads have to be disconnected to keep
the power system operating, in other words, prevent a complete black out.
1.1.1
South Australia Blackout - 2016
On 28 September 2016, South Australia saw a massive blackout, triggered by rough weather
and incorrect setting of Low-voltage Ride Through of multiple wind farms [8]. The weather
conditions caused multiple power lines to trip over a short period of time and this caused
wind farms to completely shut down as most of them were not set to handle 9 ride-through
events in a short span of 120 seconds. The wind farms that did rode-through the faults
were eventually brought down by a system wide blackout as the other wind farms deloaded
and shut down, bringing the system frequency below critical limits with rate of change of
frequency of 6.25Hz/sec thus causing a blackout.
1.1.2
UK Blackout - 2019
A similar blackout happened in the in the UK [9] in August 09, 2019 which aﬀected
approximately a million customers in diﬀerent parts of the UK. The underlying reason of
the instability was frequency, caused by incorrect trigger of Low Voltage Ride Through of
the Hornsea 1 Wind farm after the system was stabilised following a fault on a transmission
line due to a lightning strike. The active and reactive power oscillations after the fault
and clearance event is what aﬀected the intra-wind-farm voltage, which caused local wind
turbine controllers to trigger LVRT. For the unexpected deloading behavior of the wind
farm, Orsted, the wind farm operator, have been ﬁned a sum of 5.9 Million USD [10]. The
system frequency, which dropped due to Hornsea 1 deloading and Little Barford Gas Fired
Power Station trip, reached below 48.8Hz and at this value, a controlled load shedding
(termed as LFDD - Load Frequency Demand Disconnection) had to be done to prevent a
complete system blackout. The railway transit system and many parts of London were also
aﬀected. The power reserves of 1000MW were rendered insuﬃcient to arrest the system
frequency as the combined reduction of power from Hornsea 1 and Little Barford Power
Station was about 1131MW. Additionally, embedded generation from small wind farms
etc. of about 350MW was also disconnected due to some parts of system experiencing
higher rate of change of frequency of 0.125Hz/sec and vector shift of more than 6.

BACKGROUND
In this chapter, the fundamentals of inertia response, its impact on the power system
and the perspective of the Transmission System Operator are discussed. The TSO is
responsible for the safe and steady operation of the power system network and thus their
point of view on inertia reserves and requirements from the renewable energy sources
become an important factor while designing inertia control services from renewable energy
based power plants.
2.1
Power System Stability
Power system stability can be divided into three main parts [13], Rotor Angle Stability,
Voltage and Frequency stability. Depending on the parameter, magnitude of the disturbance
and time of disturbance, the system stability has been classiﬁed as shown in ﬁgure 2.1. In
this thesis, the focus will be on frequency stability, speciﬁcally on short-term stability.
Figure 2.1. Power system stability classiﬁcation
Frequency instability is caused by imbalance in generation and load. Frequency stability is
maintained by keeping reserves in the power system that can be activated in deﬁnite time
intervals, either automatically or based on demand from AGC (Automatic Generation
Controller) or directly by the TSO (Transmission System Operator). The sizing of the
reserves is based on worst case scenarios, for example, largest generator shut down due to
internal fault etc. and that removes a lot of power from the system thereby reducing the
frequency as the load is the same. There is a contingency plan to mitigate such a situation.
Any under-frequency restoration has three main parts:
• Frequency Containment Reserves (FCR) - contain the frequency deviation within
speciﬁed limit and arrest rate of change of frequency.

• Frequency Restoration Reserves (FRR) - takes over control from containment reserves
and tries to bring frequency back to nominal value
• Replacement Reserves (RR) - long term reserves, take longer time to activate and
handle control on frequency by replacing restoration reserves.
Figure 2.2. Frequency regulation timeline
Short term frequency control, as understood from ﬁgures 2.1 and 2.2, is also called as
Frequency containment. A small part of this is inertia response which is critical as it
decreases the rate of change of frequency and allows primary reserves to start acting in time.
Due to the inherent property of inertia of the synchronous machines, primary reserves were
built to activate in a few seconds, for example, they must start acting within 15 seconds
and should reach full power within 30 seconds. But for the ﬁrst 15 seconds, the system is
on its own and the stored inertia is the saving grace in this case. As synchronous machines
are replaced by wind turbines and solar power plants etc., this inherent inertia will no
longer remain in the system and thus needs to be replaced with something else. One of
the solutions is synthetic inertia or virtual inertia, which is a active power response from
converter interfaced generators to mitigate rate of change of frequency. In the coming
chapters, this concept will studies in detail.
2.2
Inertia, Frequency and Rate of change
The concept of synthetic inertia comes from Wind turbines interfaced with power electronic
converters either partially or fully, providing synchronous generator like response to
frequency events for a short period of time. This has been deﬁned in [14], where synthetic
inertia has been termed as “The controlled contribution of electrical torque from a unit
that is proportional to the Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF) at the terminals of the
unit.” One must note that, unlike synchronous machines in conventional power plants,
wind turbine converters are restricted to their design limits and thus scope of providing
support services like synchronous machines is quite limited and depends on wind conditions,
operating points and duration of support function.
NREL [15] has explained the inertia and frequency response of all wind turbine types
and it has been mentioned that type 1 and 2 turbines, being directly connected to the
grid have inertia response inherently however, type 3 (DFIG) and type 4 (Full Converter)
turbines can provide this response by means of special controls. As per the report of the
Nordic TSOs [16][17] on the challenges and opportunities for the Nordic Power System by
2025, the frequency stability is one of the three main concerns regarding network system

Various control systems have been proposed for providing synthetic inertia [22][23][24] from

and demerits, the most common being, that the individual control would be aﬀect by

turbines but the current grid code requiring synthetic inertia response from Hydro Quebec

specify the recovery period as discussed in [29] which is also an important parameter.
2.3
Inertia support services from non-conventional energy
sources
Typically, the power system has some designated set of conventional generators that are
kept on stand-by so as to provide power quickly during system faults to maintain system
stability, such units are collectively called Spinning Reserve. Since, the is shift from
conventional power generation to Renewable energy has accelerated, the need to replace
conventional spinning reserves is also rising. There are multiple approaches to solve the
problem of reduction of system inertia which is a result of addition of large amounts of
wind power and other converter interfaced generation in the power system. Some require
large scale changes in the generating units like adding additional hardware, for example,
installing grid-level storage batteries along with RES while others make use of the physical
characteristics of the wind turbines, solar panels, electric vehicles etc. and make the
changes in the controllers to provide short-term additional power when needed. Various
methods of providing inertia from non-conventional sources of energy are listed below:
• Battery Energy Storage System (BESS): Inertia emulation service can be
provided by adding BESS at the wind farm terminals that can inject power when
frequency dips below the setpoint and the wind farm continues normal operation. This
approach is promising and one such wind farm is functional in Australia that provides

frequency regulation services [30] apart from other functions like load levelling, peak
shaving, voltage support, capacity ﬁrming etc.
• Electric Vehicles: Inertia emulation service can also be provided from batteries of
electric vehicles [31] and this is under investigation in Bornholm [32] [32].
• Wind Turbines: Use the kinetic energy stored in the wind turbines and modify
the turbine control system to adapt to the additional control scheme [25], [26].
• Solar panels: Solar panel inverter systems also have similar capability to provide
inertia service by utilizing the DC link voltage and creating a proportional DC voltage
reference to injection a little extra power into the grid for a short time and then
slowly recover the DC link voltage to the original value [33].
• Wind farm with HVDC: Wind farm connected via High Voltage DC (HVDC)
lines can also provide inertia service by coordinating the controllers of wind turbines
with that of the HVDC converters. Wind turbines can provide inertia normally, as
discussed earlier and a proportional DC link voltage change can be forced by the
HVDC converters thus injecting some additional power into the grid and use the
injected power from wind farm to recharge the DC line back to nominal voltage. [34].
• Coordinated inertia from Type 4 Wind Turbines: [35] proposes a coordinated
converter control system that utilizes the energy stored in the wind turbine rotating
mass as well as the energy stored in the DC link capacitor to provide additional
energy for inertia emulation service.
• Flywheel: Flywheel energy storage is an old technology but is gaining popularity
with advances in technology.
However, the cost of such system is still high as
compared to chemical batteries like Lithium ion. Flywheel store energy in rotating
masses suspended in a closed environment using magnets thus causing the rotating
mass to levitate, which reduces friction losses. These containers are vacuum sealed
to remove air friction and provide a robust energy storage service.
Apart from non-conventional and largely converter-interfaced power generation providing
inertia reserves to the power system, synchronous condensers is another approach which
is being considered. The coal plants being decommissioned can be retroﬁtted to work as
synchronous condensers and can provide both inertia and reactive power services. This
approach, however, may keep the power system reaching the clean energy goals as these
plants will constantly require some energy from coal.
From the perspective of a Generating COmpany (GENCO), the provision to provide inertia
and other support services to the power system are a matter of grid-code requirements, cost
and proﬁt. Renewable Energy Sources (RES) have recently been center of many discussions
in relation to power system stability and slowly demands from TSOs (Transmission System
Operators) are coming up to mandate certain services that RES are required to provide
to ensure compliance. Wind turbine are no exception to that. In view of this, if inertia
emulation is made mandatory, in any shape or form, one case is Hydro-Quebec, then a
detailed analysis of inertia capability and application of these schemes become critical.

Doubly-fed Induction Generator Wind Turbine model
The Doubly-Fed Induction Generator based wind turbine also classiﬁed as a Type-III
turbine, until recently, was one of the most common type of wind turbine installed and still

systems with inertia emulation capability.
3.2.1
Fundamentals of DFIG operation
A doubly-fed induction generator based wind turbine has a wound-rotor based generator, a
partial converter (converter size is approx. 1/3rd of the actual turbine rating), and power
ﬂows from both stator and rotor as seen in ﬁgure 3.6 as mentioned in [46]. The rotor side is
connected via a back-to-back converter and the stator is directly connected to the grid. A
direct connection of stator does not, however, imply that the generator is interfaced directly
with the grid. This is because the Rotor Side Converter, which is directly connected to the
generator rotor via slip rings manages the rotor ﬂux, which in turn aﬀects the stator ﬂux.
Thus, the stator ﬂux is independent of the grid and requires proper synchronization during
grid connection. This is why the stator side three-phase connection has a contactor in
between the generator stator terminals and the grid connection terminals and the contactor

power setpoint. All these sub-controllers combined together to control the drive train of
the wind turbine and allow optimum power extraction from the wind.

DEVELOPMENT OF INERTIA CONTROL
This Chapter focuses on the requirements of the inertia control model like the rate of change
of frequency. A comparison of results obtained from various inertia schemes discussed in
Chapter 2 is also used to establish a benchmark and a basis to develop a new state machine
based control scheme and a comparison of the new scheme to the benchmark methods.
4.1
Requirements for Inertia control from DFIG based
wind turbines
As discussed in Chapter 2, the synthetic inertia control models have an additional power
command as the output. This value is also be used to calculate additional torque command.
For the integration of these additional control signals to the wind turbine control system,
a detailed understanding of pitch, speed/torque, and converter control is required. The
coordination of both the Pitch system and Converter is critical so that these systems do
not counteract each other when an additional control signal is received, in this case, ∆P
from the inertia controller.
To better understand the response of the turbine under such additional control signals, the
machine model i.e. generator model and turbine model are needed. The generator model
of the DFIG in Simulink is 6th order model and the same has been used. The wind turbine
drive train model is a two-mass model and the aerodynamic part of the turbine is modeled
based on the Cp −λ curve, as already mentioned in Chapter 3.
Additionally, the turbine operational limits must be known. For instance, the minimum
speed of operation of the DFIG chosen for this study is 0.7pu and the maximum speed
is 1.2pu. Below the 0.7pu speed, the turbine will stall and will not produce any power
and above 1.2pu, the protection system will halt the turbine to prevent it from damage.
Thus, it is necessary to consider such operational constraints during the control design.
The same applied to the torque of the generator and the power delivered by it. Generally,
generators of wind turbines are produced with a safety factor of 1.1 i.e. for a short time,
the generator is capable of producing 10% more power than rated. Since the generator is
capable of such operation, the power converter design is also inﬂuenced by the generator
size and safety factor. It is then safe to assume that wind turbines can, in fact, operate
max. 10% above their rated value, however, this is not a universal rule that applies to all
turbines and mechanical and electrical design constraints must be evaluated carefully.
Lastly, a triggering mechanism is necessary to ensure that the inertia controller starts
to work at the desired setpoint. For the decision of inertia injection, both the rate of
change of frequency and the measured frequency itself were used. Both the rate of change
and frequency are considered for the trigger mechanism to ensure that there is indeed a

COORDINATED INERTIA CONTROL
This chapter focuses on the inertia response from wind turbines at the wind park level
and the control schemes that have been developed and evaluated are centralized inertia
emulation and distributed inertia emulation. The Centralized scheme is a stand-alone
scheme installed at the wind park controller and sends control signals to wind turbines
based on measurements made at the point of common coupling. On the other hand,
the distributed scheme builds on top of the local state machine based inertia control as
discussed in the previous chapter and adds a top layer of supervision at the wind park
level to it. A comparison between the two schemes has also been established at the wind
park level to see the diﬀerences in performance.
5.1
Wind farm, Inertia response and the need for
coordination
For wind parks, it is important to consider inertia response with respect to measurements
from the PCC (Point of Common Coupling) as there might be a possibility that individual
turbines together inject more power than needed or are individual turbines that are at
a lower power, thus have a lower rotational kinetic energy also get similar inertia power
injection responsibility due to lack of intra-wind-farm coordination. Such a situation can
be seen in ﬁgure 4.20, where turbine 1 can barely provide any additional power and the
recovery period, is too long and the rotor speed reaches quite close to the stop point of
0.8pu generator speed. Such situations can be prevented with coordination at the park
level by using some metric using which the inertia capability of the turbines can be judged
before the event and the same value can be used to provide dispatch instructions which
can then be taken up by local inertia emulation controller as set points. This can also help
with adaptive inertia power contribution from the wind farm that can be calculated based
on the amount of drop in frequency and deciding the amount of wind farm contribution
accordingly. Owing to the above problems and possible opportunities, a coordinated control
scheme to handle inertia response from wind farm was developed.

CONCLUSION
The design, simulation and validation study has been presented in this thesis work on
Inertia emulation control and wind park inertia coordination. A new state-based inertia
control scheme was developed and tested in Simulink and validation on RTDS. Wind park
inertia coordination was achieved with newly developed Distributed and Centralized inertia
coordination control. The local inertia control was transferred to RTDS and validation
in a similar power system network with similar wind farm dynamics to that of Simulink.
A coordinated distributed inertia control was programmed into Controller hardware, the
communication system was set-up between RTDS and controller hardware and a CHIL
test was conducted to validation the Distributed inertia coordination control.
7.1
Results
The study of local inertia emulation control response in Chapter 4 established that there is a
need for coordination of the inertia controller to the turbine pitch control and speed/torque
control. Without the controlled interaction between various control loops of the wind
turbine, the inertia response of the wind turbine was not smooth.
A comparison between Distributed and Centralized inertia coordination control suggested
that the distributed control seems better as there is lower dependency on communication
and both inertia response and recovery period seem to be better in case of the distributed
scheme.
A comparison between the Coordinated control and local control suggested that although
the local control may be faster to act as Coordinated control suﬀers from communication
delays, control system trigger was updated for contingencies in case of communication
failures or unwanted delays.For inertia emulation, the wind turbines are expected to deliver
full inertia power as per setpoint within 1 second, this means that a delay of activation
signal from the wind farm control cannot be more than 100-200ms as the wind turbine
power ramp rate would have to be very high otherwise. This may cause the wind farm to
not comply with the requirements as the turbines have a ramp rate limit based on the wind
turbine physical characteristics. The trigger mechanism was modiﬁed and the detection of
frequency events was done both locally and at the park level and the inertia emulation
activation was based on whichever signal came earlier. This modiﬁcation helped in solving
the problem of communication delays in case of Coordinated inertia control. Analysis of
the results on a more realistic power system network i.e. Two-area system showed that
voltage also plays a part in inertia emulation from wind farms as bus voltages close to 1
p.u. means that all the extra kinetic energy can be exported to the grid without hindrance,
however, voltages lower than 1 p.u. restrict the wind farm power injection capability.
Finally, the same inertia control scheme along with a locally coordinated pitch and

7.2
Future work
The control scheme should be modiﬁed for adaptive speed recovery using Kalman state
estimator approach. This will allow the wind farm to reach nominal state after providing
inertia service in the shortest possible time even with changing wind speeds.
The grid codes in various countries are being revised for inertia service from wind farms.
One such requirement is being developed by Nordic TSOs and the requirements are similar
to that of Hydro-Quebec. The state machine overall control was developed by taking a
reference from Hydro-Quebecrequirements and is ﬂexible enough to ﬁt the requirements of
Nordic TSOs, as mentioned in chapter 2, with little modiﬁcations. There is a need for just
one more state in the state machine to handle the ‘buﬀer time before recovery’.
The Coordinated inertia controller for wind farm can also be extended to handle Battery
Energy Storage System (BESS) to provide an even better inertia response among services.
The size of the battery can then be based on the TSO inertia requirements and other
services that the wind farm owner intends to provide.
During the inertia emulation, voltage drops during high wind can cause overspeed as
the turbine control system will pitch into the wind in response to the additional power
reference but the turbine will not be able inject all the power thus causing the generator
to accelerate and go into overspeed. This needs to be further investigated. Voltage drops
can be supported by using Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS) devices like Static
Compensator (STATCOM) at the wind farm substation to maintain the bus voltages or
by using a Battery Energy Storage System.

A. Appendix
A simple illustration in the ﬁgure A.2 shows how the wind farm model, individual wind
turbine physical model and wind turbine control models are connected to a power system
test network.
There are three layers in which the wind farm model has been divided:
1. Top layer - This layer contains the grid model, wind farm reference model and wind
farm control model (3.3.2). This layer communicates grid information to the reference
model block and works as an I/O interface with the lower layers.
2. Middle layer - This contains the wind turbine electrical and mechanical design models
like the generator, wind turbine two-mass model, VSC average model, ﬁlters etc.
This layer has a control reference model that refers to a complete control system of
the wind turbine.
3. Bottom layer - This is the control system layer of the model. This is a single control
subsystem that is solved for all the turbines in the same way. This allows single
point of tuning for all the wind turbines. This is why this layer was chosen for the
placement of local inertia emulation state machine control.
NOTE: The diﬀerent layers of the model are diﬀerent Simulink model ﬁles (in the format
*.slx) and the top layer calls the lower layer and so on.

B.3
Simulink to RSCAD conversion
Before the conversion process is initiated, the control model has to be made standalone i.e.
it should not have any dependency on the other parts of the Simulink model or on the
MATLAB workspace. Any data must be shared via ports so that RSCAD draft connections
can supply that information. For this reason, the inertia emulation control model was
modiﬁed to compile and run as a standalone model as shown in ﬁgure B.4.
Figure B.4. Inertia emulation state machine in Simulink prepared for conversion
The process of conversion and usage of Simulink blocks for use in RSCAD is as follows:
• Modify the Simulink model to compile stand-alone
• Set the C code generation settings as per the C Builder User Manual. There are
some speciﬁc settings without which the C Builder will not be able to build the
model for RSCAD.
• Convert the Simulink .slx or .mdl ﬁle to C code using Simulink Embedded Coder.
• Import converted model ﬁle ‘model.c’ into RSCAD C Builder application.
• Check inputs and outputs and their data types.
• Compile the block in C Builder.
• Import user deﬁned block into RSCAD, connect the inputs and outputs and simulate.
There are few nuances of RSCAD C Builder that must be considered while converter
Simulink control models to RSCAD:
• C Builder supports Simulink control blocks only.
• Simulink Embedded Coder must be set to ANSI C code.
• Simulink model must be set to a ﬁxed step solver.
• Simulink model must not use large memory blocks like long delays or moving average
ﬁlters with large time window, otherwise the model might not work during simulation
92 of 100
