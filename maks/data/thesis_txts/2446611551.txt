Abstract
Climate change has in recent years increased the interest in urban water systems. Both
urban sewer systems and urban fresh water bodies are affected by higher temperatures,
longer dry periods, and more extreme rain events. The consequences of climate change
include more frequent overflows during which untreated water from urban sewer systems
is emitted to fresh water bodies. The addition of untreated waste water from combined
sewer overflow (CSO) or separated sewer overflow (SSO) to urban freshwater bodies can
have negative effects on the water quality in receiving waters. To address these future
challenges, scientists have expressed the need for Integrated Urban Water Management
(IUWM) tools, which can be used for the exploration of different future scenarios. An
ongoing challenge is the identification of a computationally efficient stream water quality
model that can be used as a sub-model in IUWM frameworks and exploratory modelling.
The objective of this project is to understand and predict critical events for stream
water quality. The response of receiving waters to the addition of overflows can lead to
critical events. To predict these critical events, an emulator is developed based on an
existing WEST model representing the water system in Odense. The applied approach
is divided into three parts. In the first part, overflow events in the data simulated by the
Odense WEST model are identified. In the second part, variables representing climate,
rainfall and overflow volumes and loads are defined. The variables are subsequently
used to characterise the identified events by performing a correlation analysis, a princi-
pal component analysis and a cluster analysis. In the third part, a regression analysis
for model emulation is conducted. In the regression analysis, the statistical learning
methods lasso and principal component regression (PCR) are applied. This analysis is
performed on the most upstream and the most downstream section in the stream Stavis
Å.
The identified methods lead to a successful development, of simple and computationally
efficient emulator models. The lasso and the PCR enables the development of different
regression models with a low mean squared error (MSE). The lasso models obtain a
MSE between 0.16-0.26 and the PCR models between 0.17-0.33. The highest critical
success index is 0.6 for the best lasso downstream model. In all applied performance
indicators, the obtained models for the downstream section outperform the models for
the upstream section. The identified reasons for low performance of the upstream mod-
els are: (1) The regression analysis for the upstream stream section is lacking critical
explanatory variables, which leads to a low prediction accuracy. (2) A low number of
critical events in the data makes it difficult for the regression to identify the patterns of

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Climate change has in recent years increased the interest in urban water systems. Both
urban sewer systems and urban fresh water bodies are affected by higher temperatures,
longer dry periods, and more extreme rain events (Olesen et al., 2014). The combination
of extreme rain events, a constant increase of impervious areas in cities, and population
growth lead to an increasing pressure on urban sewer system (Montserrat et al., 2013).
If the urban sewer system cannot accommodate the inflow of water during sewer rain
events and the drainage capacity is exceeded, the excess water will be released to the ur-
ban water bodies as combined sewer overflow (CSO) or separated sewer overflow (SSO).
The addition of untreated waste water from CSO or SSO to urban fresh water bodies
can have negative effects on the water quality in receiving waters (Bach et al., 2013).
Hvitved-Jacobsen (1982) has shown that CSO has both a short-term and a long-term
effect on dissolved oxygen (DO) levels in streams. Sufficiently high DO concentrations
are vital for the species living in streams. A high deficit in DO can lead to low ecological
status in the receiving water body (Harremoës and Malmgren-Hansen, 1989). Research
has also shown that CSOs and SSOs lead to an increase in nutrients, metals, organic
compounds, pathogenic microorganism, and pharmaceuticals in streams (Montserrat
et al., 2013). The release of waste water poses a special risk for streams with low flow
patterns where the dilution of pollutants is insufficient (Barceló and Sabater, 2010). In
addition to more frequent overflows, it is expected that higher temperatures are likely
to increase the algae growth in streams, which increases the possibility of critical DO
levels (SCHLESINGER, 1991).
To address future challenges posed by climate change and urban development, it is
crucial to understand the complexity of the urban water system (Bach et al., 2013). Ac-
cording to Rauch et al. (2017) it is widely accepted that urban water management needs
to be approached from an integrated perspective that combines the various urban water
components. This concept is known as Integrated Urban Water Management (IUWM)
where cities and their water infrastructure are understood as interlinked systems (Bach
et al., 2014). The development of integrated modelling tools that recognize the dynam-
ics of future climate variation, socio-economic change, and land-use change remains a
challenge. For recognizing those dynamics in integrated modelling, frameworks coupling
the biophysical and societal system are required without the need of applying various
complex modelling tools. Integrated modelling tools enable the exploration of different
future scenarios, which is needed to address the major future challenges in urban water
systems (Bach et al., 2013; Rauch et al., 2017; Urich and Rauch, 2014). In addition
to planning and decision making processes IUWM tools are also used for integrated

pollution-based control (Erbe and Schütze, 2005). The pollution-based control focuses
on receiving water criteria in oppose to real time control, which is based on overflow
volumes (Schütze et al., 1999). In IUWM models, it is fundamental to recognize stream
water quality as a core component. Nevertheless, only a few IUWM modelling tools con-
tain a sub-model representing the fresh water bodies in the system (Bach et al., 2014).
An ongoing challenge is the identification of a suitable stream water quality model that
can be used as a sub-model in IUWM frameworks. Different attempts to model stream
water quality are found in the literature. The models range from simple models (e.g.
Streeter-Phelps model) to complex ecosystem models (Rauch et al., 1998; Blumensaat
et al., 2014). Complex stream water quality models are computationally heavy and have
high data requirements for calibration procedures (Jakeman et al., 2019). These limi-
tations make many stream water quality frameworks inadequate for the integration to
existing IUWM tools and the application for exploratory modelling. Hence, a simplified
input-output tool simulating river water quality is required. This model should have a
high computational efficiency, while still considering the effects from urban development
and climate change.
The aim of this project is to model and understand the response of water quality to
the impact from overflow originating from the urban sewer system.
To achieve this
objective, an event-based emulator predicting critical events for stream water quality
events is developed. The emulator is based on an existing model developed in the con-
ceptual modelling framework WEST and tested on the urban catchment in Odense.
The overall aim of this project is divided into a number of tasks.
• Event identification: Analysis of the simulation output from the existing WEST
model and identification of events, where an overflow leads to change in water
quality in the receiving water body.
• Event characterisation: Exploratory analysis on pre-selected variables using
different data analysis techniques. The purpose of the exploratory analysis is to
gain a deeper understanding of the dynamics related to the events leading to critical
water quality. The analysis is also performed to identify the variables controlling
the water quality.
• Prediction of critical events: Development of regression model emulating the
DO concentration simulated by the Odense WEST model. The intention with the
emulator development is the prediction of critical events in a selected stream in
Odense.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
This chapter provides background knowledge required to answer the research objective of
this project. The chapter is divided into three parts: (1) Section 2.1 outlines the existing
theory on stream water quality and explains the background theory on water quality,
biochemical and physical, related processes. Furthermore, the existing literature on river
water quality modelling and fresh water quality regulations is reviewed and presented
in Section 2.2. (2) Section 2.3 gives an overview of the statistical methods used for
performing an exploratory analysis. (3) Section 2.4 presents model emulation methods.
2.1
Stream water quality
In this project, the response of the receiving waters to the urban impact is analysed. The
major interlink between the urban surface water system and the urban water bodies are
the overflows from the sewer system, during which untreated storm water from a SSO
and waste water from a CSO is emitted to natural waters (Figure 2.1 and Figure 2.2).
Figure 2.1: Combined sewer system during for wet weather and dry weather situations. The waste
water and storm water are discharged to the same sewer system. The water is discharged directly to
surface water bodies, when the capacity of the combined sewer system is exceeded. This is referred to
as CSO. Illustration adapted from EPA (2004).

SSO and CSO carries a number of pollutants, including oxygen consuming organic mat-
ter and nutrient salts, which can lead to oxygen depletion and eutrophication (Schütze
et al., 2002). Overflows can also cause a decrease in water quality through xenobiotic
compounds, which are compounds not naturally present in the respective environment or
normally are present in much lower concentrations (Harremoës and Malmgren-Hansen,
1989).
The emission of organic matter to surface water leads to oxygen depletion in the polluted
water, as many removal processes consume oxygen. The removal processes of organic
matter in water includes degradation, sedimentation and extraction (Harremoës and
Malmgren-Hansen, 1989). During degradation, bacteria consume both organic matter
and oxygen, which leads to a depletion of DO in the water phase. Depending on the
form of organic matter (soluble or particulate), the organic matter can also be removed
by fixation or sedimentation to the bottom of the water phase. The degradation and
sedimentation mechanisms depend on factors such as the bacteria cultures present in the
water phase, bio-availability of organic matter or other pollutants emitted by overflows
or other pollution sources (Harremoës and Malmgren-Hansen, 1989).
It is important to distinguish between the mechanism causing an immediate oxygen
consumption and the processes leading to a delayed removal of oxygen. The immediate
oxygen consuming processes are degradation, which takes places in the polluted water
phase itself. The delayed oxygen depletion can be caused by degradation of the fixed
organic matter or by re-suspension of the organic matter. The delayed oxygen depletion
is exerted to the water passing by after the polluted water. Due to a delayed oxygen
depletion, the minimum DO concentration can occur after the polluted water originating
from the overflow has passed (Harremoës and Malmgren-Hansen, 1989).
The commonly used measures for oxygen consuming compounds are the chemical oxygen
demand (COD) and the biological oxygen demand (BOD) (Harremoës and Malmgren-

The water quality indicator applied in this project is DO, which is widely accepted as
an important water quality parameter (Schütze et al., 2002). A sufficiently high DO
concentration is vital to most fish species and short periods with low oxygen concentra-
tions can have long-term effects on certain fish species (Butler and Davies, 2003). A
definition of acceptable DO concentrations is found in Section 2.2.
The DO concentration in surface waters changes naturally with a diurnal and a sea-
sonal variation. This variation is caused by the photosynthesis and respiration processes
of macrophytes and microphates in the water phase. During photosynthesis carbon diox-
ide (CO2) is consumed and oxygen is produces. In respiration oxygen is consumed and
CO2 is produces (Harremoës and Malmgren-Hansen, 1989). The oxygen production by
photosynthesis is not evenly distributed over the day, as it is driven by solar radiation.
The solar radiation dependency of photosynthesis leads to a diurnal variation of the DO
level. Photosynthesis and respiration are occurring at a higher rate during summer, as
higher temperatures lead to a higher macrophyte and microphyte biomass. The temper-
ature dependency of the processes results in a seasonal variation with generally lower
oxygen levels during summer. The increased photosynthesis and respiration rate also
increases the magnitude of the diurnal variation in the summer months (Harremoës and
Malmgren-Hansen, 1989).
A key process for the DO level in streams is reaeration. Reareation describes the constant
exchange of air between the water phase and the air phase. This process is responsible for
the replenishment of oxygen in the water phase after DO depletion. The reareation rate
is controlled by the velocity of the water in streams and increases with higher velocities
(Harremoës, 1989).

2.1.1
River water quality modelling
Due to the complexity of the mechanisms occurring in polluted water, it is a challenging
task to model the impact of overflows on the DO level in receiving waters. A variety of
models aim to simulate flow, transport of pollutants and biochemical conversion. Sev-
eral models are developed to only handle steady-state flow and pollution from a point
source (Rauch et al., 1998). Other models are established for the modelling of detailed
sediment processes for situations, where sediment-bound contaminants are of interest
(Massoudieh et al., 2010). In resent publications, there has been a focus on develop-
ing river water quality models that can be used in combination with integrated urban
water management models. Especially reoccurring issues with extreme rain events lead-
ing to CSOs require an integrated approach towards river water quality (Spiller et al.,
2015).
Many river water quality models have in common that they consist of a hy-
draulic model, simulating flow and transport of pollutants. The models also include a
conversion model, modelling biological, chemical, biochemical and physical conversion
processes (Rauch et al., 1998).
The modelling approach also varies for the different
models. Some models consist of a number of PDEs and other models are conceptual,
using a couple of completely mixed or plug flow tanks (Rauch et al., 1998).
The complexity of water quality models vary largely and mainly depends on processes
considered in the conversion model. The most well-known model is the streeter-phelps
model that only recognizes the degradation of BOD and has two state variables. More
complex models describe the cycle of O2, N2, and P. Other models even include a compre-
hensive ecosystem with different classes of algae, zoo-plankton, and invertebrates (Rauch
et al., 1998). The most recent approach to develop a comprehensive water quality model
is the River Water Quality Model no. 1 (RWQM1) by the IWA Task Group on River Wa-
ter Quality Modelling (Shanahan et al., 2001; Reichert et al., 2001; Vanrolleghem et al.,
2001). The river water quality model implemented in the Odense WEST model, which
is applied in this project, is the DUFLOW model (Lijklema et al., 1996). The processes
included in the DUFLOW model can be found in Table 2.2, where examples of exist-
ing river water quality models and the corresponding conversion processes are presented.
According to Shanahan et al. (1998) many models presented in Table 2.2 are not suitable
for non-point sources pollution and transient stream flow. In this context an open mass
balance, in the models, when BOD is used as state variable can lead to wrong results
when water volumes change due to unsteady inflow of CSO. Furthermore, sessile bacte-
ria and benthic processes are not represented. The described issues can, in river water
quality models, lead to systematic uncertainty and challenging model calibration. Due
to model uncertainties decision makers have employed dilution factors for the regulation
of water pollution instead of actual concentrations (Shanahan et al., 1998). For the
dilution factors the hydraulic model is of greater importance than the conversion model.
However, dilution factors are inappropriate when working with non-steady-state events,
high diurnal fluctuations, and non-point source pollution (Shanahan et al., 1998).

Fresh water quality regulations
Fresh water quality standards depend on what the respective water body is used for.
Different national and international standards exist for bathing water, waste water, and
groundwater among others. For this project standards applying to fresh water are used
as the receiving waters in Odense are fresh water bodies. The standards and guidelines
for fresh water quality consider different biological, chemical, or physical parameters to
assess the biological or chemical water quality. In Denmark, the Danish Vandløbsfaunain-
deks has been used to assess the biological water quality by analyzing the quantity and
variety of macro-invertebrates in surface waters (Wiberg-Larsen and FDC for Ferskvand
Bioscience AU, 2013). The chemical water quality can be assessed by using chemical in-
dicators, such as O2 consuming substances, nutrients such as NO3
–, and P or xenobiotic
compounds (Miljøstyrelsen, 1983). Because the water quality indicator applied in this
project is DO, it is important to understand the fresh water standards for this parameter.
As a member of the EU, the EU directive 2006/44/EC applies to Denmark. The di-
rective 2006/44/EC from 2006 concerns the quality of fresh waters needing protection
or improvement to support fish life (European Parliament and Council, 2006).
The
member states are obligated to implement measures to ensure that the water quality in
surface water bodies conforms with the regulations. The member states can set more
stringent values, but not lower than the threshold values in the directive. The directive
regulates different parameters, including DO (Table 2.3).
In addition to the threshold values, methods for inspection and a sampling frequency is
specified for each parameter. For DO, a monthly sampling frequency is required by the
EU directive (European Parliament and Council, 2006). For DO, it is further specified
that ”[...] where major daily variations are suspected, a minimum of two day samples
in one day shall be taken.” (European Parliament and Council, 2006). The challenges
concerning short-term pollution events during wet weather is discussed in Foundation
for Water Research (2019), which is the Urban Pollution Management (UPM) manual
by the Foundation for Water Research (FWR).
Short periods of low DO can appear during wet weather, when CSO is released to
surface waters. In the UPM manual, an alternative approach for wet weather standards
are proposed. In this approach, the standards are expressed in terms of concentration-
duration thresholds in relation to a return period. The UPM manual considers inter-
mittent concentration-duration thresholds for sustainable salmonid and cyprinid fishery
(Table 2.4).

2.3.1
Principle component analysis
High dimensional data containing highly correlated variables often lead to challenges
in statistical analyses (Bellman et al., 1957). It is therefore desirable to find a low di-
mensional and uncorrelated representations of the original data. The aim is to identify
a representation that explains most of the variability in the original set (James et al.,
2013). In PCA, the original data is represented by principle components (PCs), which
are linear combinations of the variables in the original data set (James et al., 2013). The
first PC is the normalized linear combination of the features in the data set that has
the largest variance. The second PC is the linear combination with the second largest
variance. The subsequent PCs are obtained in the described order. The maximum num-
ber of possible PCs is the number of variables in the original data set (Conradsen, 1984).

The lasso
When performing regression analyses with high dimensional multivariate data, a num-
ber of challenges can occur (Bellman et al., 1957). Often, not all variables in multiple
regression are relevant for the response in the model. It is therefore desirable to identify
the subset of relevant variables. Testing all possible combinations of variables yields
2p different models, which is usually infeasible for a larger number of predictors. An-
other challenge related to high dimensional data is the bias-variance trade-off. Training
a model on a high number of predictors can lead to over-fitting the model and a low
prediction accuracy when using independent test data. The prediction accuracy of high
dimensional linear regression models can often be improved by constraining the number
of variables used in a model (James et al., 2013).

Multivariate principle component regression
The definition of a multivariate analysis is that the response in a multivariate regression
model contains more than one variable. Multivariate PCR uses several response variables
and thereby utilizes the correlation of the response variables. In multivariate PCR, not
only the predictors, but also the response variables are transformed to PCs. The least
squares regression is then performed on the PCs for the response variables and the PCs
for the predictors (Sun, 1996). The PCs for the predictors and responses are computed
using Equation 2.10.
If the number of response PCs is one, the multivariate PCR
becomes a univariate least squares optimization. If the number of PC’s is above 1, a
multivariate least squares optimization is performed (Sun, 1996).

3.1
Urban water system in Odense
The city of Odense has a couple of minor streams originating from the rural upstream
catchments that are located around the city. The stream system in the city is connected
to Odense harbour and several streams flow directly into the harbour (Figure 3.1). The
sewer system in Odense is a combination of a combined and separated sewer system.
There are a number of overflow structures connected to the separate and combined
system, which leads to both SSO and CSO being emitted to the streams during severe
rain events.
3.2
Odense WEST model
The conceptual WEST model of Odense consists of the urban water system including
sewer system, water treatment plant, and several streams. In this project, the focus lies
on the representation of the streams/river water quality module in the model (Table 3.1).
In the model, most streams are connected to an upstream catchment, which represents
a rural area outside Odense. A conceptual representation of the Odense WEST model
is found in the appendix (Figure A.8). The streams are split into several sections. Each
section represents a tank, which is a completely stirred tank reactor (CSTR), that has
a number of inflows and one outflow. The input to each tank are the river water com-
ing from the upstream catchment or the upstream river section and runoff from the
surrounding area. The inflow from the upstream catchment and the surrounding area
can be both dry weather flow and wet weather flow. Many stream sections are also con-
nected to a CSO and/or a SSO, which means they have one or more additional inputs.
Furthermore, each river section has a rain input.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
The objective of this project is to predict the response of the water quality in the receiv-
ing waters to the addition of overflow from the sewer system. For achieving the objective,
it is substantial to identify and understand critical events in the water quality simula-
tions performed with the Odense WEST model. The approach applied in this project is
divided into three main parts. The first part aims to identify events in the WEST sim-
ulation output (Section 4.1). In the second part, the identified events are characterised
with the intention to understand the variables leading to critical events (Section 4.2).
In the third part of the approach, the purpose is to identify a regression model that can
be used for emulating simulated DO concentration and predicting critical events for the
stream water quality (Section 4.3).
4.1
Identification of events
4.1.1
Definition of event
To identify events for the stream water quality, it is important to define an event. In this
project, an event is defined by the occurrence of an overflow event that possibly leads to
a reduced water quality. The event is critical, if the water quality goes below a specified
threshold at any location in the entire stream. An event is not critical, if the water
quality does not go below the specified threshold. The water quality indicator used in
this project is the DO concentration. The term event is used throughout the report.
If not stated otherwise, the definition specified here applies. The threshold for critical
events applied in this thesis are based on the UPM standards for salmonid waters and a
1 month return period (Table 2.4). Consequently, the threshold for the 1-hour minimum
DO concentration is 5mg/L, for a 6-hour minimum DO concentration 5.5mg/L, and
6mg/L for a duration of 24 hours.
4.1.2
Event identification algorithm
As explained in Section 4.1.1, an event is defined by the occurrence of an overflow from
the sewer system to the stream. Depending on the magnitude of the overflow events the
addition of CSO and SSO leads to an detectable reduction in DO concentration. In this
approach, it is important that all events, also the ones that do not lead to a reduced
==PAGE:44==
32
4 Methods
DO concentration, are considered. If only the events with a considerably reduced DO
concentration are taken into consideration, only the pattern of the extreme events can be
analysed. For this project, it is desirable to represent a wide range of events to achieve
a broader description of the impact of overflows on the stream water quality.
The event identification algorithm applied in this project consists of two steps. In the
first step, the events in the individual CSOs or SSOs connected to Stavis Å are identified
from the corresponding CSO and SSO time series. In the second step, the events for
the individual overflows are merged and events for the entire river are identified. The
outcome of this algorithm is an event table for Stavis Å with information about the
starting and ending time for each event within the simulation period. This algorithm is
applied to the data from the 16 different simulations performed with the Odense WEST
model (Table 3.5).
As part of this thesis an additional approach for event identification is developed. In
this approach the events are identified based on the pattern in the simulated DO con-
centration. This event identification approach is developed with a test data set from a
different WEST model (Appendix A.1). This method appeared not to be suitable for
the data from the Odense WEST model and is therefore not used.
Event identification in individual overflow
As described in Section 3.2.1, Stavis Å is divided into 8 stream sections with different
overflow facilities connected to each of the sections. In this algorithm the CSO and SSO
at every time step is analysed and a binary event vector with TRUE/FALSE is produced.
If no overflow is occurring the logical expression FALSE is stored in the event vector. If
the flow in the respective CSO or SSO exceeds 0.01m3/s an event starts and the logical
expression TRUE is stored in the vector at this time step. The algorithm continues to
store TRUE in the event vector until the event ends. An event ends if the flow has not
exceeded Q > 0.01mg/L for six hours. Once the event has ended the logical expression
stores in the vector becomes FALSE again.
Event identification in entire stream
In the second step of the event identification the event vectors for the individual over-
flows are merged in one matrix. Based on this matrix, an event table with information
about the beginning and end of an event in the entire stream is produced. An event in
the stream starts whenever there is an overflow event occuring at minimum one over-
flow facility. The events end if no event in any overflow is occurring. Eventually, this
algorithm clears the event table for overlapping events which means that all events with
less than six hours in between are merged. The event table is used in the event char-
acterisation for identifying the variable values corresponding to each event (Appendix
A.6).

4.2
Characterisation of events
In this section the approach for characterising the identified events is explained. This ap-
proach includes an initial selection and computation of variables that are to be analysed.
Once the variables are identified, a correlation analysis is performed for the selected vari-
ables. Eventually, a PCA and cluster analysis is conducted to understand the different
types of events that can be found in the simulated data. The PCA and cluster analysis
is performed on the critical events only, whereas the correlation analysis is performed
on all events. In all analyses events occurring in December, January, and February are
removed.
4.2.1
Selection of variables
The selected variables fulfill two purposes: (1) to use variables to characterise events and
to understand the importance of different factors for the water quality in the stream.
(2) to use the variables in the development of a regression model for model emulation
in the further course of this project. It is therefore important to include variables that
are expected to be associated with the DO concentration in the stream: independent
variables and dependent variables (Section 4.2.1). Dependent variables are variables
which variation is of interest. Independent variables control the dependent variables
and represent the causes for the variation in the dependent variables.
Dependent variables
Two indicators are used to characterise the stream: the minimum DO concentration
in an event for different durations and the maximum amplitude during an event (Ta-
ble 4.1). The variables representing the minimum DO concentration are expressed as
concentration-duration values which is in accordance with the UPM guidelines (Ta-
ble 2.4). The minimum DO concentrations are computed based on the DO concentration
time series simulated by the WEST model. The maximum amplitude variable represents
the magnitude of the change in the DO concentration level in an event (Section 4.2.1).
Note that the variables in Table 4.1 are per section. Hence, eight sets of these variables
have to be computed, one set for each section in Stavis Å.

4.2.3
Principal component analysis
As explained in Section 2.3.1 the PCA is applied to obtain a lower dimensional and
uncorrelated set of PCs, that can be used for a further analysis. The analysis supports
understanding the patterns in the data as it highlights variables that contribute the
most to the overall variability in the data. The method also identifies the variables that
vary in the same direction. The PCA is only performed on the independent variables
and is based on the following six steps.
1. Standardising the data: All variables are centered and normalised by subtract-
ing the mean and dividing by the standard deviation.
==PAGE:50==
38
4 Methods
2. Selection of events for PCA: For the PCA the events from the month De-
cember, January, and February are removed. This is done as the simulated DO
concentration in winter has a different pattern than the rest of the year. Further-
more, the critical events are expected to occur during high temperature periods.
In addition to the removal of winter events, only critical are included in the PCA.
The critical events are events with an 1-hour-minimum DO concentration below 5
mg/L, 6-hour-minimum DO concentration below 5.5 mg/L or a 24-hour-minimum
DO concentration below 6 mg/L at any section in the stream. This is done because
the aim with the PCA is to understand the patterns in the independent variables
leading to the critical events.
3. Selection of variables for PCA: The PCA is exclusively performed on indepen-
dent variables. Dependent variables are only used for the selection of observations.
4. Remove observations with undefined (NA) values: Because the PCA cannot
handle undefined values they are removed.
5. Performing PCA: For the performance of the PCA the R function princomp is
used. In this function the method explained in Section 2.3.1 is implemented.
6. Defining the no. PC’s used in further analyses: The number of PC’s used
in further analyses is based on the PVE (Equation 2.3) with a threshold of 90 %.
4.2.4
Cluster analysis
The cluster analysis is performed to group different observations with similar character-
istics. For this purpose the PCs obtained in the PCA are used instead of the original
variables as the PCs are uncorrelated. Consequently, the cluster analysis is only per-
formed on the variables and events selected for the PCA (Section 4.2.3). The applied
method for clustering is k-means. This method divides the observations into k clusters
according to the least squared euclidean distance (Equation 2.4). As k-means is sensitive
to the initial values of the cluster centroid selection 1000 different initialisations are used.
The criteria to determine the number of clusters (k) are the CH index (Equation 2.6)
and the Silhouette index (Equation 2.5). The number of clusters with the highest CH
index or highest Silhouette index is selected. As explained in Section 2.3.2 both tech-
niques have limitations. Hence, both methods are applied to avoid misinterpretation in
case one of the techniques fails. The outcome of the cluster analysis is a vector with a
length n, containing the cluster number for each event. This vector is merged with the
entire data set, which enables the extraction of the events in the individual clusters.

The main objective of this project is the development of an emulator on the basis of the
Odense WEST model. The emulator is to predict critical events for the stream water

quality in Stavis Å. The model emulation is done by developing a regression model that
reproduces the minimum DO concentration during an event, which is used for the predic-
tion of critical events. Different methods for linear regression analysis are tested to find
the most suitable regression technique for this purpose (Section 4.3.1). The tested re-

The developed regression models are evaluated using a number of performance indica-

4.3.1
Regression analysis for model emulation
The aim of the regression analysis is to find a regression technique that can fit a multiple
regression model with a good prediction accuracy and model interpretability. There are
three main challenges related to specific set of dependent (Table 4.2) and independent
variables(Table 4.1) used in this project:
• High number of independent variables: Regression analysis with a large num-
ber of predictors is linked to some challenges. In many regression analyses not all
predictors are strongly associated with the response. It may occur that two vari-
ables explain the same phenomena and, therefore, one can be discarded. Removing
the variables that are not relevant for the response can improve the interpretability
of the model. At the same time a large number of independent variables can lead
to overfitting a model and thereby reducing the prediction accuracy. It is desirable
to find the number of dimensions that can reduce the variability originating from
high dimensional data, without adding large bias to the model (James et al., 2013).
• Multicollinearity of the independent variables:
Multicollinearity occurs
when two predictors are correlated to each other or when one predictor is cor-
related to a linear combination of two other predictors. It is assumed that there
are a couple of different correlation patterns in the independent variables as many
of them are describing similar phenomena. Strong multicollinerarity can lead to
problems when fitting a regression model as it increases the variance of the esti-
mated coefficients and makes them unstable and difficult to interpret.
• Highly correlated dependent variables: The dependent variables in the model
are the DO minimum concentrations for different durations and locations. The DO
concentrations for different durations at the same location will always be highly
correlated. It can also be expected that the DO concentrations in the different
sections of a stream are correlated, but the magnitude of correlation depends on the
specific stream and section. Instead of developing a couple of univariate models, the
correlation among dependent variables makes it possibles to perform multivariate
regression analysis. In a multivariate regression analysis, all dependent variables
are analysed simultaneously. Hence, the correlation among the dependent variables
can be employed to develop a multivariate regression model.

Univariate lasso
The lasso is a regularization method where the regression coefficients for the predictors
are shrunken to zero which enables discarding the irrelevant variables. The benefits from
applying the lasso is that it enables balancing variance and bias in high dimensional data.
At the same time the lasso performs variable selection, which can improve the model
interpretability. Performing a regression with the lasso consists of a number of steps:
1. Selection of variables: When performing the unvariate lasso regression for the
most upstream section (8301_5664) only the variables related to that stream sec-
tion are included. Therefore, the variables for the overflow further down the stream
are removed. Furthermore, the variables related to the tributaries are removed, as
they enter the stream further downstream. The same applies to the rain gauge
NV, which is removed, because it is not connected to the most upstream section.
In the lasso regression for the most downstream stream section all variables are
included, as they are all connected to that section.
2. Coefficient estimation and performance evaluation The following steps for
coefficient estimation and performance valuation are performed 1000 times. This

implies that 1000 regression models are obtained and 1000 performance scores
determined. This step is performed several times to investigate the impact of the
specific training set used for coefficient estimation on the model performance.
a) Dividing data into a training and test set: When estimating the predic-
tion accuracy of a regression model it is important to use an independent data
set that has not been part of training the regression model. If data used for
training and testing the model is the same, the prediction accuracy is highly
overestimated. In this project, the training set contains a random sample of
2/3 of the events. Accordingly, the test set consists of 1/3 of the events.
b) Estimating regression coefficients: In the lasso, the regression coefficients
are estimated for a range of λ values (Section 2.9). The coefficient estimation
is performed for λ from 0-100, which implies that 101 regression models are
generated. The MSE (Equation 2.13) is computed for each of the 101 re-
gression models using a 10-fold cross-validation. Thereafter, the best model
is selected for predictions. To select the model, the model for the largest
λ within one standard error of the the minimum MSE is identified. The λ
identification and the coefficient estimation is performed using the R function
cv.glmnet, which is part of the R package glmnet (Friedman et al., 2018).
c) Assessing model performance: The obtained regression model is assessed
by testing the accuracy of the predictions. The prediction accuracy is tested
by predicting the response using the test data set. Predictions are then com-
pared to the observations and different indicators are applied to estimate the
prediction accuracy of the model (Section 4.3.2).
Univariate PCR
The PCR is a dimension reduction technique that transforms data to an uncorrelated
set of PCs prior to performing a linear regression. The benefit from using the uncor-
related PCs for the regression is that it removes multicollinearity in the data, which
may improve the estimation of regression coefficients. The steps in the development of
a PCR regression model are a combination of performing a PCA and a multiple linear
regression. The first three steps and the last step are the same as in the univariate lasso.
1. Selection of variables: The univariate model for the most upstream stream
section (8301_5664) does not require variables related to mechanisms occurring
downstream. The variables are thus not used in the PCR for the upstream section.
2. Coefficient estimation and performance evaluation The PCR and perfor-
mance evaluation is performed 1000 times for 1000 random divisions of the training
and test data set.
a) Dividing data into a training and test set: The data set is separated
into a training data set and a test data set. The training data set contains a
random sample of 2/3 of the events and the test data set 1/3 of the events.

b) Transforming predictors using PCA: All considered independent vari-
ables are transformed to PCs with PCA (Section 4.2.3). The number of PCs
obtained (p) is equal to the number of predictors in the model.
c) Performing multiple linear regression on PCs: A multiple linear regres-
sion using least squares (Section 2.4.1) is performed for all sets of PCs. The
first set of PCs includes only the first PC, the second set the first and second
PC, etc. In total, p regression models are generated.
d) Selecting no. PCs representing predictors: The most suitable number

the number of PCs the critical success index (CSI) is used as the performance

default method in the R function pcr from the package pls for dimension

rather than MSE, as the main objective is the prediction of critical events.

comparing model predictions and observation and estimating the prediction
accuracy (Section 4.3.2).

4.3.2
Assessment of model performance
The performance of the developed regression models is assessed with binary threshold
indicators, which are used to investigate the performance of binary predictions. In this
project, the regression models predict a critical or a non-critical event based on the min-
imum DO concentration. A minimum DO concentration below the specified threshold
indicates the occurrence of a critical event. The threshold applied in the analysis depends
on response variable used in the specific regression model. For the 1 hour minimum DO
concentration, the threshold is 5mg/L. The threshold for a 6 and 24 hour duration
is 5.5mg/L and 6mg/L, respectively. For the binary threshold analysis, a contingency
table explaining hits (H), misses (M), false alarms (FA) and correct rejection (CR) is
generated (Table 4.6). In the contingency table, pairs of simulated and observed events
are compared and assigned to one of the four categories.
The selected indicators are the CSI, bias score, hit rate and false alarm rate (Table 4.7).
The CSI can be used for measuring the fraction of events that are correctly predicted.
It penalizes for both misses and false alarms. The bias score indicates whether a model
has a tendency to overestimate (bias score > 0) or underestimate (bias score < 0).
The bias score cannot be used by itself without another indicator, as the optimal value
(bias score = 1) can be obtained with a low or now number of hits. The hit rate is the
ration between hits and misses and the false alarm rate the ration between false alarms
and hits. The performance is evaluated based on all events in the test data set.
In addition to the binary threshold indicators, the test MSE is used to assess the pre-
diction accuracy of the developed regression model. The test MSE is computed on all
events in the test data set (Equation 2.13) and compares the error between predictions
and observations. Note that in the performance evaluation of the regression model, the
term observations is used. In this thesis, this term referes to the data simulated by
the Odense WEST model. The term observations is used to distinguish between the
data simulated by the Odense WEST model, and the simulated data by the developed
regression models.

Regression analysis with lasso
The lasso regression is a regularisation method that is used to reduce the number of
variables by discarding the variables that are irrelevant for the response.
The lasso
method is applied to develop two univariate regression model predicting the 1-hour DO
minimum concentration at the sections StavisAa_8301_5664 and StavisAa_1405_0.
Analysing the test MSE, it is found that the overall prediction accuracy is on a sim-
ilar level for both models (Table 5.3). However, the CSI indicates that the model for
the downstream section performs better in predicting critical events (Table 5.3). To
get a deeper understanding of the regression models and the differences in performance,
the predictions are plotted against the observations (Figure 5.13, Figure 5.12). The up-
stream and the downstream model have a tendency to overestimate the 1-hour minimum
DO concentration a low DO concentrations and to underestimate the response at high
DO concentrations. It is found that most events in the data are clustered between a
minimum DO concentration of 6mg/L to 12mg/L. This distribution of events forces the
least squares coefficient estimation to assign more weight to the errors of the events with
a minimum DO concentration between 6mg/L and 12mg/L. The uneven weighting of
the errors leads to a reduction in prediction accuracy at low and high DO concentrations.
The described patterns in the predictions are observed for the upstream and downstream
model, but are more evident in the upstream model.
Two main reasons for this difference in prediction accuracy of the models are identified.
The first reason is that there is a high number of critical events with a 1-hour minimum
DO concentration below 5mg/L at the downstream section (173 critical events).
A
higher availability of more events with low DO concentrations for training the regression
model will improve the models prediction accuracy at low DO concentrations. The data
from the upstream section only contains 84 critical events, which reduces the fit of the
model at low DO concentration (Table 5.4).
The second possible reason for a better prediction performance of the downstream model
is that the model contains more information than the upstream model. The number of
predictors used in the upstream model is 40, whereas the downstream model has 107 pre-
dictors. It is tested, if adding the downstream variables to the upstream model would
improve the prediction accuracy of that model. It is found to reduce the prediction
accuracy instead of improving it. This analysis disclose that the behavior of the up-
stream model is not described sufficiently with the identified predictors. The missing
information in the upstream model lead to the bias observed at low DO concentrations
(Figure 5.12).

To gain a better understanding of the developed lasso regression models, a residual
analysis is performed for individual variables. As similar patterns are expected for the
upstream and the downstream model, only the residuals for the upstream model are anal-
ysed. The residuals are the differences between predicted values and the actual values,
where a positive value indicates an overestimation of the model and a negative value an
underestimation. Ideally, the residuals for a regression model would be symmetrically
distributed around the x-axis, without any pattern.
The residuals for the rain variables (Korup_6hour_rural and Korup_168hour_urban)
have a general tendency to be located above the x-axis (Figure 5.14). Especially at
higher rain volumes, most of the predictions are overestimating the actual minimum DO
concentration. A similar pattern can be seen for the solar radiation (sol_rad_current),
where the residuals are located above the x-axis at high solar radiations. The pattern
of the residuals for the current solar radiation suggest a piece-wise linear relationship.
Analysing the temperature variable (temp_air_current), a linear patter with a ten-
dency from the upper left to towards the lower right can be observed. This pattern
indicates that the model is overestimating the DO minimum concentrations at low
temperatures and underestimating the DO concentration at high temperatures. The
variables representing the overflow volumes and COD loads (CSO_load_8301_5664,
SSO_load_8301_5664, SSO_volume_8301_5664, CSO_SSO_volume_24hour_8301_
5664) have a less distinct pattern (Figure 5.14). The residuals for these variables are more
symmetrically distributed around the x-axis. The plot of variable SSO_load_8301_5664
suggests an increase of variance of the residuals at higher values, whereas the opposite
can be seen in the plot for the variable CSO_load_8301_5664.
The analysis of the estimated model coefficients and the residuals highlight a couple of
challenges related to the regression analysis performed with lasso. The main issue is the
high multicollinearity, caused by correlated independent variables. The multicollinearity
leads to unstable regression coefficients, and may also be the cause for biased residuals.
To accommodate this problem, one can pre-select the independent variables by remov-
ing the highly correlated ones. It is tested, if a regression with only one temperature
and solar radiation variable instead of five highly correlated variables can improve the
performance of the model. It is found that removing any of the temperature and solar
radiation variables does not increase the prediction accuracy.

residuals. It can be expected that there are interactions between the temperature and
the solar radiation, which are not represented by either one of the variables. This could
be resolved by introducting a variable which represents this interaction by multiplying
the temperature and solar radiation. In addition to that, a variable representing the

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The approach identified to answer the objective of this thesis is linked to a number
of uncertainties and limitations. In this chapter, the major limitations of the analysis
carried out in this work are presented together with a collection of recommendations for
improvement.
6.1
Event characterisation approach
The approach applied in this project is divided into three parts: (1) the event iden-
tification, (2) the event characterisation, (3) and the event prediction. The intention
behind the event characterisation is to get a deeper understanding of the data, to use
this knowledge for the emulator development. For the characterisation of events, a cor-
relation analysis, a PCA and a cluster analysis is performed.
The intention behind
applying a combination of PCA and cluster analysis is to identify the explanatory vari-
ables. However, it is found that it is difficult to identify the specific parameter that is
controlling the response, as it is often groups of variables that vary together. Never-
theless, the cluster analysis provides information about the types of events in the data.
This information could be used to identify the type of event that is overestimated in the
regression analyses for the upstream stream section in Stavis Å. Knowledge about the
characteristics of poorly predicted events can support the identification of the missing
explanatory variables in the upstream regression models.
6.2
Development of emulator
The development of the emulator is based on two techniques that address the challenges
related to high dimensional data: the PCR and the lasso. It is found that the methods
perform similar and both techniques have advantages and disadvantages (Section 5.3.3).
The performance of the lasso is reduced by the presence of multicolinnearity patterns,
which decrease the model interpretability and make the regression coefficients unstable.
In the PCR the challenge related to multicollinearity is addressed by using a set of
uncorrelated PCs for the regression. However, in PCR it is assumed that the linear
combination of the predictors with the highest variance equals to the linear combination
that is associated with the response. There may be cases where this assumption is not
true. The emulator development may therefore be improved by applying a canonical
==PAGE:86==
74
6 Discussion
correlation analysis (CCA). A CCA identifies collinearity patters between predictors and
response. The method is used for determining the linear combination in the predictors
that has the highest correlation with the dependent variables. Hence, testing a CCA is
recommended for the improvement of the emulator development.
6.2.1
Uncertainties and limitations of emulator
The model used as a basis for the emulator is a representation of the water system in
the city Odense, implemented in the modelling famework WEST. The Odense WEST
model is used, as it is currently one of the only available comprehensive IUWM models.
However, the model has a number of limitations. The Odense WEST model is calibrated
to the streams in Odense, but no information about the uncertainty and performance
accuracy is available.
Without information about the uncertainty, it challenging to
interpret the predictions made by this model and correspondingly the predictions by the
emulator. The uncertainties in the Odense WEST model originate among others from
the river water quality module in the model. Shanahan et al. (1998) discusses a number
of limitations related to the currently available river water quality models. Many of
the limitations discussed in Shanahan et al. (1998) also apply to the DUFLOW model
applied in the Odense WEST model.
• In the DUFLOW model BOD is used as a state variable. Due to the definition
of BOD, which does not include all types of biodegradable organic matter, the
mass balance in the conversion model cannot be closed. An open mass balance
is particularly challenging for non-point sources with inconstant waste load and
emission sources that lead to a change in volume (Shanahan et al., 1998).
• Shanahan et al. (1998) points out that many river water quality models fail in
representing the bacteria cultures sufficiently. A lack in representation of dynam-
ics in bacteria culture may also lead to an oversimplification of the degradation
processes. In DUFLOW the bacteria are represented by a variable for biomass
quantity in the water phase. The biomass quantity in the model change according
to one temperature threshold. Above and below that threshold, the amount of
biomass is constant.
• According to Shanahan et al. (1998), an adequate representation of the benthic
degradation processes are particularly relevant in small rivers, where the ratio of
wetted surface area to volume of water is high and the contribution of benthic
processes to the overall transformation may be significant. The benthic processes
in DUFLOW are only represented by one variable, which is the sediment oxygen
demand (SOD).
• In the WEST modelling framework the streams are modelled by a number of
CSTRs. The implicit assumption behind this formulation is that any emission to
the river are instantaneously mixed across the entire stream section. The actual
observation is that transverse mixing is a slow process and that effluents discharged
==PAGE:87==
6.2 Development of emulator
75
from a shoreline outfall distinguishable within the river flow for considerable dis-
tances downstream (Shanahan et al., 1998). Assuming complete mixing may lead
to underestimations of pollution concentrations at different points in the stream.
The highlighted simplifications in DUFLOW and the WEST modelling framework can
strongly reduce the predictivity, particularly when there are significant changes in the
bacterial ecosystem, the morphometry, or other basic characteristics of the stream. It
is therefore crucial to include an estimate of the predictions accuracy and the model un-
certainties when interpreting the Odense WEST model. Unfortunately, no sufficiently
comprehensive data with actual observations are available for estimating the uncertainty.
The emulator developed in this thesis can only be as good as the Odense WEST model
and the performance of the emulator is measured in relation to that model. When using
the emulator in an IUWM context, it is substantial to define the actual productiveness.
The availability of actual observations or an estimate of the uncertainty of the Odense
WEST model would clearly improve the applicability of the emulator for IUWM pur-
poses.
Another limitation of the emulator is that it only considers DO as an indicator for
water quality. In literature, DO is often used in combination with NH3, because high
ammonia concentrations have acute toxic effects on fish (Foundation for Water Research,
2019). Furthermore, low DO and high NH3 concentrations can have synergistic toxic
effects. Considering the effects of NH3 in the emulator could improve the quality of the
predictions by the emulator.
6.2.2
Applicability of emulator for IUWM tools
The motivation behind this thesis is the need of a sub-model representing the water
quality in surface waters for the integration in IUWM tools. There are a number of
challenges related to the technical applicability and data availability of the emulator in
IUWM tools. Depending on the type of regression model that is used as an emulator,

If the model is used for scenario

determination of the variables becomes more challenging. In pollution-based control the
variables need to be predicted, which adds an additional uncertainty to the emulator

by analysing the relevance of the uncertain variables and removing them, if possible.
Despite the number of uncertainties and challenges mentioned, the emulator also has
several benefits. The simulation time and computational budget is significantly lower
than running the entire Odense WEST model for the same simulation period. It is also
straightforward to understand the simulation process in the emulator, as the code is writ-
ten in a widely know programming language. In addition to that, the input variables do

6.3
Outlook
Throughout this thesis a number of recommendations for improving the event char-
acterisation and emulator development are mentioned.
These recommendations are
summarised in this section, which gives an overview of the possibilities for further im-
provement of the results found in this project.
==PAGE:89==
6.3 Outlook
77
• Stratification of events used for regression analysis by using an equal
number of events for all minimum DO concentration levels. As explained
in Section 5.3.1 most of the events have a DO minimum between 6mg/L-12mg/L.
Only a few events have a very low or a very high minimum DO concentration,
which leads to an uneven weighting of the error when performing the least squares
optimisation. An initial test, where only 60 observations from each 1mg/L interval
(i.e.
60 events from 4 mg/L-5mg/L, 60 events from 5mg/L-6mg/L etc.)
are
used for the coefficient estimation. It is found that this stratification reduces the
performance of the model. It is assumed that the reduction of the performance
occurs because the stratified number of events is too low to represent all patterns
in the data. It is therefore recommended to perform different tests with more
appropriate stratification methods.
• Including additional variables. The residual analyses disclose different biases
and tendencies in the temperature and solar radiation data. Based on this anal-
ysis, it is suggested to include a variable representing the interaction between
temperature and solar radiation. This variable can be generated by multiplying
temperature and solar radiation. An additional variable that should be tested is an
new solar radiation variable that represents the piece-wise pattern of that variable.
• Including information from cluster analysis in regression. In the cluster
analysis, the critical events are divided into three different clusters. However, the
obtained information is not applied in the regression analysis so far. Color coding
the events in the prediction vs. observation plots according to the clusters may
offer new insights and help understanding which type of phenomena the variables
fail to explain.
A better understanding of the regression analysis can help to
improve the models.
• Pre-selecting variables for lasso. It is found that high multicollinearity in the
data makes the coefficient estimation in the lasso unstable. Manually pre-selecting
different sets of variables where highly correlated variables are removed can be
tested.
• Application CCA for emulator development. CCA can be applied for the
regression analysis as an additional method to lasso and PCR. CCA is used to
find linear combinations in the predictors that have a high correlation with the
response.
• Analysing predictors with high uncertainties. The independent variables
that are difficult to obtain or linked to a high uncertainty should be identified and
tested for their importance in the emulator. If the identified variables have a low
impact on the predictions, they should be discarded from the emulator to improve
applicability and reduce uncertainty.
• Applying the emulator to a different stream. It is important to test the
emulator on data from different streams to find challenges that have not been
identified yet.

• Adding NH3 to analysis. A combination of the water quality parameters DO
and NH3 should be used for the definition and prediction of critical water quality
events. A wider range of critical events can be identified if NH3 is considered,
which would make the emulator more comprehensive.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The objective of this project is to understand and predict critical events for stream water
quality as a consequence of CSO and SSO. The identified approach for achieving this
objective is to develop an emulator for binary predictions on the basis of the DO con-
centration simulated by the Odense WEST mode. The emulator development is divided
into three steps: (1) the events in the data simulated by the Odense WEST are identi-
fied, (2) the identified events are characterised using different data analysis techniques,
and (3) a regression analysis for model emulation is applied and regression models for
two stream sections, in Stavis Å are generated.
It is found that the approach enables a successful development of an emulator for binary
predictions. The performance of the regression models highly depends on the specific
section. The obtained regression models for section Stavis_1405_0 yield a high predic-
tion accuracy with a MSE of 0.16 and a CSI of 0.6 for the best regression model. It
is concluded that the essential explanatory variables for explaining the DO variation at
Stavis_1405_0 are identified.
In the regression analysis for section StavisAa_8301_5664, the MSE is 0.24 and the
CSI is 0.26 for the best regression model. The low prediction accuracy discloses that
the regression model for StavisAa_8301_5664 is lacking critical explanatory variables.
An additional challenge in the regression analysis for StavisAa_8301_5664 is the low
number of critical events present in the data for that section. The low number of critical
events in the data make it difficult for the regression analysis to identify the dynamics
of the critical events.
It is furthermore found that a multivariate regression analysis can improve the model
performance of an univariate regression analysis. The multivariate PCR model for the
StavisAa_8301_5664 section leads to a maximum CSI of 0.41 and the univariate PCR
model for the same section results in maximum CSI of 0.27.

A.1.1
Implementation of event identification
The event identification approach is based on the pattern in the simulated DO concen-
tration and can be divided into two tasks: (1) obtain a time series where the daily and
seasonal variation is removed by applying smoothing methods. (2) identify the begin-
ning and the end of each event, based on the smoothed data. The smoothing procedure
can be divided into two steps:
1. The diurnal variation is removed by computing the moving average over a 24-hour
window, equal to 100 time steps in the data from the artificial WEST model. The
obtained time series is in this context referred to as DO_ndv.
2. The low DO peaks are removed from DO_ndv by computing the moving average
of the time series without diurnal variation over a 30-day window. This time series
is referred to as DO_sv. By subtracting the time series DO_sv from DO_ndv
a time series without daily and seasonal variation is obtained. This time series,
called DO_ndv_nsv, is approximately zero when no event is occurring and below
zero if an event is occurring.

Applying the smoothing approach on the river section 750m result in the graphs pre-

time step in DO_ndv_nsv for the occurrence of an event is developed. This algorithm
starts by finding the first derivative of DO_ndv_nsv, which is equal to the slope of
DO_ndv_nsv. The conditions for an event occurring are: (1) if the first derivative of
DO_ndv_nsv is below -0.001 or (2) if DO_ndv_nsv is below -0.1. The conditions for an
event to end is: (1) if the derivative of DO_ndv_nsv is between -0.000001 and 0.000001.
The developed event identification algorithm contains a loop that checks for points in
the time series that indicate no event, while the point before and after indicate and
event. These false negatives can occur when the slope is low at the bottom of a negative
peak.
