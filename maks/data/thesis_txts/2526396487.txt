Abstract
The rise of cementituos mortars in masonry walls has lead to constructions with settlement
cracks through the bricks and mortar that cannot be removed from the bricks hindering
their reuse. As each reused brick may reduce CO2 emission by half a kilo this thesis aims to
investigate whether it is possible to design a mortar with sufficient strength while allowing
for the reuse of bricks.
The strength is tested using the torsional experiment described in SBi 2006:13. Two series
of experiments were conducted. One testing for varying cement content in the mortar un-
der constant normal force and one testing the effects of brick treatment prior to fabrication
and under varying normal force but keeping the mortar recipe constant.
All experiments were conducted using bricks with high minute suction values and a non-
reactive lime filler to emulate a worst case scenario.
The elastic strength of specimens with full adhesion indicates that all mortar types allow
for a wall tie column horizontal distance of 1 meter while maintaining flexural strength to
withstand the wind pressure on a 12 meter wall. All tests done at a normal force equalling
5 meters of wall pressure.
The amount of data is minimal and the above conclusion is to be considered a premature
indication and may be investigated further.
The plastic strength has found to originate solely from friction post failure and does not
relate to adhesion type nor brick treatment.
The strength of the wall increases with an increase in cement content, but further studies
are needed to determine the causality as an increase in cement content is followed by a
decrease in sand content leading to a more favorable binder-to-sand ratio.
The plastic strength was also found to increase with an increase in normal pressure.
An analysis of varying normal stresses indicate that a tie binder column spacing of 0.7
meters is sufficient at two meters from the top of the wall and down. This results solely
reflects the strength of a KC 50/50/700 mortar.
Ultimately it is found that at a normal stress equalling 5 meters of wall pressure allows the
use of a KC 90/10 mortar and a binder distance of 0.8 meters. When using a KC 50/50
mortar a binder distance of 0.7 meters is sufficient at a wall depth of 2 meters.
Above this level one can reduce binder distance, increase cement content of use water-
dipped bricks to ensure full adhesion.

1
Introduction
Construction is a global economy responsible for 40% of the world’s CO2-emissions and
30% of the produced trash in Denmark. The production of construction materials alone is
responsible for up to 15% of emissions[1, 2].
As society at large moves towards more climate friendly solutions so has the construction
industry. The move towards sustainable construction is clearly visible throughout the in-
dustry. Universities are publishing research in more climate friendly options [3], there are
conventions and exhibitions on sustainable construction [4] and DTU itself has recently
seen enough interest in construction with wood to be forced to include it in the course
program1.
A traditional type of construction that has only recently started the transition into sustain-
ability and circularity is that of masonry walls.
1.1
A Brief History of Masonry
Masonry construction has existed for thousands of years around the world, and has been
used in Denmark for approximately 800 years[5]. Up until the mid 1900’s lime mortar
was used in all but every masonry construction. During the 1950’s construction methods
were altered, demands for insulation increased and requirements of structural elements
changed[6]. With this followed greater demands for the strength of mortar and the rise
of cementitous mortars began, as seen when Murfagets Oplysningsråd in 1987 specifically
stated that veneer walls must be made with a KC 60/40 mortar or stronger[7].
While the addition of cement to mortar increases the strength it is not without side effects.
As the strength of the mortar increases it may surpass the strength of the bricks. As the
wall settles cracks may form, and when the mortar is stronger than the brick the cracks will
pass through the bricks rather than the mortar. This is illustrated on the front page, and a
closeup is presented in figure 1.1

Following the trend towards sustainable and circular construction companies are currently

”It’s pretty black and white. If you use a cement mortar the bricks cannot be reused”[8] a

Theory
2.1
Mortar
In a masonry wall mortar is the glue holding everything together. It bonds with and adheres
to bricks emulating a single element from the individual parts.
In general two types of mortars are used in Denmark; designed mortar and prescribed
mortar.
Prescribed mortars are defined by their recipe. They consist of lime, cement and sand in
varying ratios, and the ratios between them define their characteristics. On the far end
you have a lime-mortar with no cement content, opposed by a cement-mortar with no
lime content.
A designed mortar consists of largely the same components as a prescribed mortar but may
have various additives to achieve predefined functionality and properties [10, 11, 12].
The majority of mortars used in Denmark are however lime-cement mortar - known as
KC-mortars in Denmark - and will be described in the following.
2.1.1
Lime Mortar
A lime mortar consists of only lime, sand and water, with lime being the binding agent.
Lime is acquired from a quarry where limestone is dug up. The limestone, CaCO3, is
burned at 800◦to 1200◦causing the limestone to dissipate CO2 with calcium oxide, CaO,
or quicklime remaining. This process is known as calcination[13, 10]. The process is shown
in equation (2.1).
CaCO3(s) →CaO(s) + CO2(g)
(2.1)
The lime is then slaked. Slaking is the addition of water to the calcium oxide. This is either
done as dry slaking, where a carefully calculated amount of water is added for CaO to react
and create the powder Ca(OH)2 known as hydrated lime, or by wet slaking, where a surplus
of water is added to the quicklime. See (2.2)[10, 14].
CaO(s) + H2O(l) →Ca(OH)2(s)
(2.2)

acid, which in turns reacts with the calcium hydroxide to form calcium carbonate. This is
shown in (2.3a) and (2.3b) and simplified in (2.4). The entire process is illustrated in figure

2.1.2
Cement Mortar
A cement mortar has cement as the single binding agent with the cement reacting with the
water in the mortar. Cement mortar quickly reaches a high strength[5].
Cement reacts with water in a hydration process. Portland cement is the most common
type of cement[17], and the hydration process is very complex as it consists of various
complex compounds[18, 19]. Figure 2.2 lists some main components.

The higher and earlier strength of a cement mortar does not come without drawbacks.
Cement mortar has a higher risk of drying out in contact with bricks. This reduces the

flexibility of the mortar and makes it stiff and difficult to work with. The mortar is also
at risk of losing moisture to the bricks due to the water absorbing abilities of the bricks.
This puts the mortar at risk of having insufficient water for the hydration to complete and
studies have found water/cement-ratios as low as 0.17 in the contact area[5].
2.1.3
Lime Cement Mortar
A lime cement mortar consists of both lime and cement as binding agents. The hardening
of a lime cement mortar is a dual process of both the carbonation of the lime and the
hydration of the cement. While one is slow and the other fast they can have a symbiotic
relationship. The cement will harden even when the water content is too high for the lime
to carbonate, and as the water content drops the cement can benefit from the emission of
water during the carbonation of the lime. The hygroscopic nature of lime may also aid the
risk of premature drying in a cement mortar[5, 16].
The strength of a KC-mortar will, within limitations, increase with an increase in cement
content. At too low levels of cement content, where the majority of strength comes from
the lime content, the hydrated cement may limit air access to the mortar and inhibit the
carbonation of the lime. As such it is argued, that no less than 35% of the binder should be
cement in a KC-mortar. At lower levels the cement may affect strength negatively[5, 16].

Bricks
The majority of bricks used in Denmark are made from burned clay. Clay is a desirable ma-
terial due to the plastic nature prior to burning and compressive strength post burning[5].
Two main types of clay are used; blue clay and red clay. Blue clay becomes yellow bricks
after burning while red clay becomes red bricks. The color is dependent on the chemical
composition of the clay[5].
A burnt clay brick is a porous material. This is central to mortar and brick adhering to each
other. The bricks ability to absorb moisture is broken into two different categories; minute
suction, that denotes the amount of water the brick can absorb in one minute while water
absorption is the total amount the brick can hold.
Minute suction is key to the strength of the final wall and standard values are within 1.5-2.0
kg/m2/minute. Yellow bricks typically have higher suction values than red bricks [5].
In Denmarkbricks follow standarddimensionsof length 228mm, width 108mm and height
54mm as per figure 2.5.

2.1.5
Properties of Fresh Mortar
The properties of a mortar are of great importance. Most important to the mason is the
workability or malleability of the mortar. That is the sole dictator of water content of the
mortar when mixed by a mason. Malleability can be broken down into stiffness and con-
sistence or flexibility[5].
9
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 2 - Theory
Consistence
The consistence, or stiffness, of the mortar is directly linked to the water content. Different
areas of masonry utilize mortars at different stiffnesses, and as such it is not a property per
se but a condition.
Different methods of testing the consistence exist, i.e. the jolting table. If one asks different
masons to mix a proper mortar one will often find they create a mortar with the same
stiffness[5].
Flexibility
Flexibility is the most important property of the two. It can be further broken down into
plasticity and cohesion. Plasticity indicates the ease at which the mortar can be shaped and
by extension is an expression of the internal friction in the mortar. Cohesion describes the
extend to which the mortar will stay in one piece when the shape is changed.
No procedure exists for testing the flexibility of a mortar[5].
The flexibility of the mortar will solely be assessed by the mason and can be effected by the
ratio of cement to lime, the purity of the lime and the grading curve of the sand[5].
Workable Life
The workable life of a mortar is a descriptor of the duration after mixing in which one can
use the mortar without risk that the chemical processes have progresses too far[5].
A lime mortar has a borderline indefinite workable life while a cementitous mortar has a
limited workable life dependent on the choice of binding agent, temperature of the mortar,
potential additives and more[5].

ronment, requires low maintenance and is only required to carry its own load and be able
to transfer the vertical load into the main load carrying structure via steel ties, see figure

3.3
Choice of Materials
3.3.1
Sand
The sand used throughout this thesis is Kronhøj Mørtelsand 0-5mm. Despite the name
the sand has a grain distribution of 0 to 4 mm. See appendix B. Kronhøj Mørtelsand 0-
5mm was chosen as it was the only off-the-shelves product available where either retailer
or manufacturer were willing to confirm adherence to grading curve requirements.
3.3.2
Lime
The carbonation of lime is a slow process. Given the time constraints of this thesis it is
not feasible to use lime as a binding agent as it will not have time to develop significant
strength.
To err on the side of caution a non-reactive lime filler has been used. The filler consist of
broken down limestone, CaCO3, branded as Rollovit by Faxe Kalk under Lhoist.
3.3.3
Cement
Portland cement is the most used cement in Denmark and will be used in this thesis.
Specifically Aalborg Portland Basis cement is used. Aalborg Portland lists this cement
as applicable in masonry construction[34]. Alternatively one could use Aalborg Portland
Rapid cement but this may put the mortar at risk of having too short a workable life.

Mortar Consistence - 4.2
4.2
Mortar Consistence
As the water content of mortar is dictated solely on the workability or malleability it is not
possible to keep water content a constant when changing the recipe, as found in section
3.2.
The DS/EN 1015-3 standard[31] dictates a method to measure the consistence of a fresh
mortar. Consistence is a measure of viscocity, fluidity and wetness and gives a measure
of the deformability. It does however not give a direct assessment of the workability nor
malleability that defines a finished mortar when used by a craftsman[31].
Thus a baseline is set from a standardized 50/50/700 mortar mixed by a professionally
trained mason, employed by the institute of civil engineering. The mortar is mixed to
a workability and malleability approved by the professional and the baseline viscocity is
measured using the below method. A tolerance of ±10% is accepted.
4.2.1
Materials and Apparatus
• Sand that fulfils the requirements in DS 414[38]
• Cement, here Aalborg Portland Basis.
• Lime, here Rollovit from Faxe Kalk.
• Jolting table
• Truncated conical mold from stainless steel or brass of height 60mm, internal di-
ameter of 100mm at the bottom and 70mm at the top1.
• Tamper with a circular cross section of 40mm in diameter and a mass of 250g.
• Caliper
4.2.2
Procedure
Mix mortar according as prescribed in the standard. See also 4.2.3. Notes.
Place mold at the center of the jolting table.
Fill the mold halfway with the fresh mortar and compress the mortar with ten strokes of
the tamper, by letting it drop on to the mortar.
Fully fill the mold and compress the mortar with an additional ten strokes.
Skim off the excess mortar and wipe any excess mortar and/or water from the mold and
the clean surface of the jolting table.
After 15 seconds lift the mold.
Jolt the table 15 times at approximately one jolt per second.

increase cohesion and thereby better adhesion[16].

for fabrication and storage. Despite the bricks being Danish normal bricks they did not
always meet requirements for size and were not always even on all sides. It is believed the
rigidity of the pallet may have led to worse conditions during storage than the previously
used solution of a metal guide and F-clamps.

5.4.2
Data Processing of Test-Machine Output
The raw data from the testing software does not lend itself to easy analysis. The force-
displacement diagrams varies substantially with adhesion type and within each adhesion
group and data analysis rests on an individual manual assessment of data. This section
aims to clarify the ground principles of the data analysis.
52
==PAGE:69==
Torsional Experiment - 5.4
Two-Sided Adhesion
When constructing a masonry wall it is assumed that the bricks adhere to the mortar on
all possible sides. A two-sided adhesion in this experiment is thus considered the base line
in regards to real world applications. It is also the simplest force-displacement diagram.
The specimen will deform elastically with a linear relationship between force and displace-
ment until failure. Failure can occur in the mortar, on the surface between mortar and
brick or in the brick itself. This failure is found at the peak of the linear section of the
force-displacement diagram. In figure 5.7 this area is emphasized with a red circle.
After the initial failure the resistance of the specimen is lowered and the force decreases.
The displacement following the initial failure is denoted the plastic displacement. The re-
sistance found in this area relies solely on friction between the two sides of the failure - in
this thesis all failures were surface failures or partial surface failures, why the friction lies
between brick and mortar. As mortar is a brittle material the plastic strength is seen as a
series of increases and decreases in force over the displacement, as the two sides interlock
and resist the force until the force is too great, another small failure happens and the sides
interlock anew. This area is emphasized with a red line in figure 5.7.

One-Sided Adhesion
In the following adhesion between mortar and bottom brick and adhesion between mortar
and top brick have been considered identical.
Specimens with one-sided adhesion do not show a clear initial failure, as one side is already

displacement.
Ones does observe a ”kink” in the initial state of the force-displacement diagram. This
is believed to originate from the mortar and brick ”interlocking” to one another during
construction. As the mortar is a flexible material it is able to conform to the contours of
the brick and fill in holes that may be present. As such an initial increase in friction is to
be overcome which leads to increased strength in the starting stages of the test procedure.
This is considered the elastic strength of the one-sided specimens, and is highlighted with

6
Conclusion
This project aims to investigate the possibilities of designing mortars for a circular economy
where the reuse of bricks is possible.
This was done using the torsional experiment promoted by SBi as it allows for small scale
testing allowing for more experiments to be conducted. The test returns both shear and
flexural strength of the specimens while also indicating whether the failure mechanism will
occur in the mortar, the brick or the interface of the two.
Two series of tests were conducted: • Mortars of varying lime and cement content in the
spectre of using lime as the only binding agent to using cement as the only binding agent.
This test series allows for analysis of the strength of the mortar as a function of the cement
content. All specimens during this test series will be exposed to a normal force equalling
5 meters of overlaying wall.
Due to the slow strength development of lime a non-reactive lime filler was used. This is
erring on the side of caution as the filler will not add any strength to the specimen. It also
allows for more certainty in the results as the complex reactions between lime and cement
are removed ensuring that the cement can harden freely. • Mortars with a set recipe of
KC 50/50/700 were used with bricks of various treatment and tested with varying normal
pressure. Bricks were either oven dried, dipped in water for 5 seconds or used with no
treatment. This allows the authors to test whether the pre-treatment of the bricks affects test
results. Varying the normal pressure indicates whether strength is related to the position
in the wall.
A series of sub-experiments were carried out to ensure the quality of the materials, the
quality of the mortar and uniformity of the mortar across specimens:
• The grading curve of sand must follow strict requirements. The sand used for this thesis
was purchased as it had a listed grading curve within these requirements. Upon testing it
was found that the sand did not meet regulations as shown in figure 5.1. How this effects
the results from the following tests is uncertain, but some prior research has indicated
that altering the grading curve may lead to mortars with better properties and as such it is
expected to have some effect on the following tests.
• The viscosity of mortar directly relates to the consistence of mortar. A baseline was set
using a mortar made by a professional mason using a jolting table. All subsequent mortars
made by the authors are withing ± 2.5% of this consistence.
• The bending and flexural strength of mortar prisms were tested following DS/EN 196-1.
67
==PAGE:84==
Chapter 6 - Conclusion
Upon comparison with requirements in DS/INF 167 the compressive strength was found
sufficient while the flexural strength was insufficient for specimens of low cement content.
The reason to the lack of strength is unknown but it may be explained by the non-reactive
lime not adding strength or that the specimens were not allowed to cure for more than 16
days as opposed to the listed 28.
The results may be reflected in the strength of the specimens in the torsional test.
During the torsional testing no specimens experienced failure of the brick. As such it is not
possible to specify at which level of cement content the strength of the mortar surpasses
that of the brick.
Only a minority of specimens had two-sided adhesion at the end of the curing period. This
may be caused by the high minute suction of the bricks used, as this may lead to a lack of
water in the brick and mortar interface.
An analysis of the elastic strength of specimens with two-sided adhesion showed that in-
creased cement content in the mortar lead to increases strength of the specimen. The same
results indicate that for all levels of normal pressure a steel tie binder distance of 0.8 meters
is sufficient. The amount of data is however insufficient to present any clear findings of the
correlations.
Examining the adhesion across mortar type and brick treatment it is found that the water-
dipped specimens showed a higher rate of two-sided adhesion opposed to the non-water-
dipped specimens.
In plastic deformation stage no difference in bearing strength could be found for initial
adhesion type nor brick treatment. The plastic strength originates from friction and as this
occurs post-failure the adhesion has no effect. The plastic strength does show a correlation
between cement content and flexural strength as shown in figure 5.24a. It is however not
possible to specify whether the strength development follows the increased cement con-
tent, the improved binder-to-sand ratio of a low-lime mortar or a combination of the two.
Figure 5.24b shows that at a binder distance of 0.8 meters or less a mortar with 10% cement
or more grants sufficient strength. This strength is however only valid at a normal stress of
0.1 MPa equalling 5 meters of overlaying wall.
An analysis of varying normal stresses indicate that a tie binder column spacing of 0.7
meters is sufficient at two meters from the top of the wall and down. This results solely
reflects the strength of a KC 50/50/700 mortar.
Ultimately it is found that at a normal stress equalling 5 meters of wall pressure allows the
use of a KC 90/10 mortar and a binder distance of 0.8 meters. When using a KC 50/50
mortar a binder distance of 0.7 meters is sufficient at a wall depth of 2 meters.
Above this level one can reduce binder distance, increase cement content of use water-
dipped bricks to ensure full adhesion.
As the plastic strength has proven sufficient in all but the top of a masonry wall a con-
struction with no adhesion between bricks and mortar may be used to experience a ductile
failure in stead of the brittle failure following a strong adhesion.

Further Work
• As stated in section 5.4.1. Adhesion Types Across Specimens one possible explanation
for the low number of two-sided adhesion specimens is the pallet used for fabrication and
storage. The former method using a ”murelære” and applying pressure with an F-clamp
does not appear to have this issue, and it may be of interest to investigate whether the
pallet was at fault. This could be done using identical mortars and bricks and observing
the difference in adhesion across methods.
While the pallet design may be faulty it does show improvements over the existing method
of using the ”murelære” as it negates the requirement of large numbers of F-clamps and
allows the user to apply a known force to the specimen while in storage.
• Figure 5.6 indicates that water-dipped specimens have a higher ratio of double-adhesion
specimens. This may indicate that bricks dipped in water can lead to better adhesion even
with a lower cement content. Bricks used in this thesis was submerged for 5 seconds but
it may be of interest to investigate whether other durations leads to better results. It is
expected that such experiments must also include different types of bricks with different
minute suction.
• As stated multiple times it is uncertain whether a lowered strength following a decrease in
cement content is actually caused by the lowered cement content or the change in binder-
to-sand ratio. In order to test this experiments can be conducted where one alters the lime
cement ratio but keep the sand at a constant level.
• Due to the low number of two-sided adhesion specimens the elastic analysis is superficial
and no clear conclusions can be made on correlation or causality. More data is needed to
properly analyse the elastic spectrum.
• The choice of using the SBi designed torsional experiment relies on the claim that it will
result in failure in the brick if that same mortar and brick combination was used in a small
scale wall test with brick failure.
No single specimen during these tests experienced a failure in the brick and upon re-
inspection of the literature it is not made expressively clear that brick failures were present
in prior tests. SBi 2006:13 experiences brick failure in the small scale experiment but no
brick failure in the torsional experiment[26]. The authors question the validity of the claim
and suggests tests with high strength cement based mortar to ensure brick failure is possi-
ble in the given test setup.

Grading Curve, Sand
The measured weights for the grading curve can be found in table D.1 and illustrated in
figure D.1.
The weighted average is calculating using
¯S0.250 = S10.250 · S1T + S20.250 · S2T + S30.250 · S3T
S1T + S2T + S3T
¯S0.250 = 34 % · 1131.03 g + 33 % · 1111.26 g + 39 & · 1326.41 g
1131.04 g + 1111.26 g + 1326.41 g
= 36 %
where ¯Si denotes the average for a given sieve size, S1i, S2i and S3i is the measured weight
for each sample for a given sieve size. S1T, S2T and S3T denote the total amount for each
sample. This calculation has been done for all sieve sizes.
It should be noted that the sieve size of 0.080 mm does not figure in DS/EN933-1[32] but
does comfort to the addition of ISO 565[32] and has been included for added data resolu-
tion as suggested by the laboratory technician.
It should also be noted that while an 8.00 mm sieve should be included as per the reference
material this has not been possible as this size was not an available option in the lab. As-
sessing the data it is however apparent, that only approximately 1 % of sand is withheld at
4.00 mm why the lacking 8.00 mm sieve is of no crucial importance, as the reference ma-
terial allows for approximately 5 % of sand being withheld at this level as per DS 414[38].
Lastly it should be noted that the reference material consists only of graphs, and even more
so graphs of questionable quality, why the upper and lower limits of the grading curve are
based on best estimates.
