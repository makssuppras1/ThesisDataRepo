Abstract
Sewage contains harmful microorganisms, and when discharged into the ocean, it can
have a negative impact on Arctic ecosystems and local communities which rely on ma-
rine resources for food and recreation. In Arctic regions, such as Sisimiut, Greenland,
conventional wastewater treatment is a challenge due to the cold climate, high costs
and remote population. The aim of this study was to assess the health risks posed by
wastewater discharges in Sisimiut, focusing on microbial contamination in both seawater
and greywater. In this work, samples were collected from seven seawater sites and nine
greywater sites in the town and tested for contaminants such as Escherichia coli, total
coliforms and heterotrophic bacteria. These were measured using 3M Petrifilm and digi-
tal PCR with the uidA and HF183 gene markers to detect the presence of faecal matter.
The results showed that in some areas the levels of coliforms and E. coli exceeded the
European Commission’s water quality standards, indicating potential health risks at areas
where there’s daily activity. The risk of infection by E.coli was high in swimming, fishing,
and boating areas, while the presence of the gene markers at these locations showed
a lower risk of infection by faecal contamination. The greywater locations also showed
elevated risk of infection in cases where exposure to contaminated water occurs. Further
research and data collection is needed to fully understand the human health risks posed
by wastewater discharges to local populations, particularly in relation to traditional fishing
and other activities.
Keywords: Arctic ecosystems, wastewater, pathogens, public health, microbial risk as-
sessment

Resumé
Spildevand indeholder mange sygdomsfremkaldende mikroorganismer, som ved udled-
ning i havmiljøet kan have en negativ indvirkning på de arktiske økosystemer og den
lokale befolkning, der er afhængige af havets ressourcer til mad og rekreation. I arktiske
regioner, så som Sisimiut, Grønland, er konventionel spildevandsrensning vanskeligt på
grund af det kolde klima, de høje omkostninger og den afsides befolkning. Formålet med
dette studie var at vurdere sundhedsrisikoen for den lokale befolkning i Sisimiut, med
fokus på mikrobiel forurening i både havvand og gråvand. Til dette formål blev der indsam-
let spildevandsprøver fra syv havvands områder og ni gråvandsområder i Sisimiut, som
alle blev undersøgt for mikrobiel forurening, herunder Escherichia coli, totale coliforme og
heterotrofe bakterier. Tilstedeværelsen af disse blev målt ved hjælp af 3M Petrifilm og
digital PCR med uidA- og HF183-genmarkører for at vurdere tilstedeværelsen af fækal
forurening. Resultaterne af undersøgelsen viste at niveauerne af koliforme bakterier og
E. coli i nogle områder overskred Europa-Kommissionens etablerede grænseværdier for
vandkvalitet, ensbetydende med en potentiel sundhedsrisiko. Risikoen for smitte med E.
coli var høj i de områder som bliver anvendt til svømning, fiskeri og sejlads, mens tilstede-
værelsen af gen markører i disse områder indikerede en lavere risiko for smitte gennem
fækal fourening. Gråvandslokationerne indikerede også en forhøjet risiko for smitte i de
tilfælde hvor eksponering til forurenet vand forekommer. Yderligere undersøgelser og
dataindsamling er nødvendig for at forstå den komplette menneskelige sundhedsrisiko
af spildevandsudledning til lokal befolkninger, særligt i områder som har relation til tradi-
tionelt fiskeri og andre aktiviteter.
Nøgleord: Arktiske økosystemer, spildevand, patogener, folkesundhed, mikrobiel risikovur-
dering

1
Introduction
1.1
Wastewater Systems in the Arctic
Wastewater refers to any water that has been affected by human activities, encompassing
domestic, industrial, and storm water sources (Naidoo et al. 2014). Domestic wastewater
includes blackwater from toilets, which contains human waste and pathogens, and grey-
water from sinks, laundry, and showers, which contains organic matter and chemicals.
Industrial wastewater is produced by manufacturing and other industrial processes and
often contains heavy metals and hazardous chemicals (Manasa et al. 2020). Storm water
is runoff from precipitation that collects pollutants from surfaces such as roads and roofs.
Wastewater typically contains a mixture of organic matter, pathogens, nutrients, chemi-
cals, solids, and heavy metals. Treatment processes, including preliminary, primary, sec-
ondary, and tertiary stages, are used to remove contaminants and ensure safe discharge
to the environment or for reuse, thereby protecting public health and the environment from
pollution and disease.
In many Arctic communities, such as Sisimiut in Greenland, wastewater treatment is often
rudimentary or non-existent. In towns like Sisimiut, flush toilets are connected to a sewer
system and blackwater is discharged directly into the ocean, while greywater is discharged
directly onto the land (Gunnarsdóttir et al. 2013). In small settlements, bucket toilets are
handled individually or organized by the municipality.
1.2
Background and Context
1.2.1
Geographical and Climatic Context
Sisimiut is a town located on the west-central coast of Greenland, near Amerloq Fjord
(Britannica 2022), and is the second largest town in Greenland with a population of 5412
(Greenland 2024) who are engaged in a variety of activities, including fishing, hunting
and, increasingly, tourism. It lies just north of the Arctic Circle, making it the northernmost
town in Greenland with an ice-free harbour in the winter (Hendriksen 2022). The area
is characterised by rocky, mountainous terrain, which affects water flow patterns and the
potential spread of pollutants from water discharges. Sisimiut’s fjord provides a natural
conduit for water flows from inland areas to the sea, thus playing a crucial role in the
dispersion of untreated wastewater.
Moreover, Sisimiut has a polar tundra climate (NASA 2024) with cold winters and cool
summers, which affects biological activity in wastewater and the efficiency of microbial
degradation processes (Gonzalez-Martinez et al. 2018). In addition, it experiences sig-
nificant seasonal variation in monthly rainfall, which affects the volume and dilution of
wastewater which enters the natural water bodies.
The ocean currents around Sisimiut can also have an impact on the dispersion and dilution
of wastewater discharges into the ocean. The West Greenland Current flows northwards
along the west coast of Greenland and it is formed by the mixing of the East Greenland
Current, which brings cold polar water, and the warmer Irminger Current from the Atlantic
Ocean (Britannica 2018) (Fig.1.1). This current has a major influence on the temperature
and ice conditions along Greenland’s west coast (Gotfredsen et al. 2004), often keeping it
free of ice even in the colder months and thus supporting human settlements and maritime
activities, as is the case in Sisimiut. The northward flow of the West Greenland Current can
help to disperse the effluent, reducing the concentration of pollutants near the discharge

higher concentration, potentially affecting local marine ecosystems and human health.

1.2.2
Current Wastewater Management Practices
In Sisimiut, sanitation is the responsibility of the municipality and is done through different

• Wastewater to the ground: greywater is drained directly to the ground in approxi-

• Honey buckets/Night soil: bagged toilets are emptied via the night soil collection

1.2.3
Environmental and Health Impacts
According to Qeqqata Kommunia (Kommunia 2021), the environmental impacts of dis-
charging untreated wastewater into the sea are minimised due to the favorable dilution
conditions at ocean sewage outlets and the low population density (app. 5600 habitants)
in Sisimiut (Statistikbanken 2022). However, direct discharge of untreated wastewater in-
troduces contaminants into the Arctic environment (Gunnarsdottir 2012), which may pose
public health risks (Daley et al. 2019).
These contaminants can disrupt marine ecosystems by causing disease in marine life,
promoting harmful algae blooms, and altering water quality (Kreissig et al. 2023).
In
addition, pathogens such as bacteria and viruses can pose direct health risks to local
populations through waterborne transmission (Neudorf et al. 2017). Residents who rely
on marine resources for food and recreation are particularly vulnerable (Dorevitch et al.
2011). Contaminants can accumulate in seafood, potentially leading to long-term health
problems when consumed (Canada 2001). Therefore, effective wastewater treatment

1.3
Microbial Contamination of Wastewater
1.3.1
Types of Microbial Contaminants
Wastewater is a complex mixture of water and various contaminants, including various
microorganisms. These microorganisms are pathogens and can be grouped into different
types (Leclerc et al. 2002):
• Bacteria: waterborne bacterial pathogens can be enteric and aquatic bacteria.
Common enteric bacterial pathogens found in wastewater are Escherichia coli, Salmonella
spp., and Shigella spp., while indigenous aquatic bacteria can include Legionella
spp., Aeromonas spp., Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Mycobacterium avium.
• Virus: released into the aquatic environment but have a lower infective dose com-
pared to bacterial pathogens because they can’t multiply and grow outside the host
cell.
• Protozoan parasites: Giardia lamblia and Cryptosporidium parvum are enteric pro-
tozoa resistant to standard disinfection processes in wastewater treatment and like
virus they do not multiply outside the host.
• Helminth: is a type of parasitic worm that can live in humans and other animals
and cause various diseases. It includes the platyhelminthes or flatworms (flukes
and tapeworms) and the nematodes or roundworms (Wakelin 1996).
1.3.2
Pathways of Exposure
Understanding the pathways by which individuals can be exposed to contaminants in
wastewater is essential to mitigate health risks and protect public health. These path-
ways include skin contact, ingestion, and inhalation, each with specific scenarios and
associated risks (Mbanga et al. 2020). In Sisimiut, the pathways by which residents can
be exposed to wastewater contaminants are diverse and depend on the interactions be-
tween the environment, infrastructure and human activities.
Skin contact
Skin contact with contaminated wastewater can occur in a number of ways, particularly
through recreational activities, occupational exposure or handling of contaminated mate-
rials. Many Sisimiut residents enjoy recreational activities such as swimming, fishing, and
boating in nearby waters. If untreated or inadequately treated wastewater is discharged
into these waters, people may come into direct contact with pathogen contaminants. Cuts
or abrasions on the skin can provide an entry point for infection, increasing the risk of
illness. Workers that handle wastewater are at higher risk of direct contact with such
contaminants. To minimise this risk, appropriate protective equipment and strict hygiene
practices are essential (Mbanga et al. 2020).
Ingestion
Ingestion of contaminated water or food is one of the most common and important path-
ways of exposure to wastewater contaminants.
Although Sisimiut relies primarily on
treated water for drinking, there is a risk that untreated wastewater could contaminate
local water sources, particularly if there are failures in the water disposal infrastructure
or during extreme weather events. Residents who consume contaminated drinking wa-
ter may ingest pathogens, heavy metals, and other hazardous chemicals. In addition,
4

contaminants from wastewater can bioaccumulate in marine life, including fish, shellfish
and seaweed, which are an integral part of the local diet. The consumption of seafood
harvested from contaminated waters can therefore lead to the ingestion of pathogens
and toxic substances, posing significant health risks such as gastrointestinal illness and
chronic conditions such as heavy metal poisoning (Karri et al. 2021).
Inhalation
Inhalation of aerosols and bioaerosols containing effluent contaminants is another impor-
tant exposure pathway. Wastewater treatment processes and natural processes such as
wave breaking can aerosolize pollutants (Sharma et al. 2020), creating tiny droplets that
can be inhaled by residents. This pathway is of particular concern for volatile organic com-
pounds (VOCs) and pathogens that can survive in airborne particles, potentially leading
to respiratory infections and other health problems. In homes with poor sanitation, such
as bucket toilets, improper waste management can lead to the build-up of harmful gases
such as methane and ammonia indoors. Residents can inhale these gases, causing res-
piratory irritation and other adverse health effects.
Environmental pathways
Another exposure pathway can be environmental. In Greenland, and particularly in Sisim-
iut, weather conditions can contribute to the spread of pollutants from untreated sewage.
During heavy rainfall or snowmelt, surface runoff can carry contaminants from poorly man-
aged wastewater into local water bodies. This can exacerbate the spread of pathogens
and contaminants, increasing the risk of exposure to residents through the multiple routes
described above.
The pathways of exposure to wastewater contaminants in Sisimiut are diverse and inter-
connected, highlighting the need for comprehensive wastewater management strategies
to reduce these risks.
1.3.3
Risk of Infection from Exposure
According to Gerba et al. 1975, the primary factors determining the extent of microbial
health risk are:
• Pathogens’ ability to survive and grow in the environment
• Concentration of pathogen needed to cause an infection
• The need for intermediate hosts and whether they are present or not
• The vulnerability of the individual at risk, influenced by prior exposure leading to
immunity
The risk of infection from wastewater pathogens is determined by both the likelihood of
exposure to the contaminant and the effect of the pathogen on the exposed person (WHO
2018). To reduce the risk of infection, it is therefore important to reduce the concentration
of the pathogen load and to minimise human exposure to the contaminant (Robb et al.
2017; Mills et al. 2018). Fig.1.3 shows how the increasing exposure to a contaminant and
an increasing concentration of that contaminant creates a higher risk of infection.

1.4
Health Risks Associated with Wastewater Contamination
1.4.1
Potential Health Impacts
Wastewater can have serious health impacts due to the presence of harmful pathogens
and pollutants.
Untreated wastewater, as previously shown, contains bacteria, virus,
protozoa and helminths, which can cause diseases such as gastrointestinal infections,
cholera and hepatitis (Xu et al. 2018). Hepatitis A can be transmitted by the virus present
in the water when combined with poor sanitation (Divizia et al. 2008). The presence of
protozoa in sewage can lead to waterborne diseases such as giardiasis and cryptosporid-
iosis. There is also a risk of tetanus if the bacteria Cloistridium tetani is present in the water
(Edlich et al. 2003). Below are some examples of the most common pathogens found in
wastewater and the diseases associated with them.
A. Salmonella
Typhoid fever is caused by Salmonella Typhi bacteria, which multiply and spread through
the bloodstream and intestinal tract. It can be treated with antibiotics, although the bac-
teria can remain in a person’s body and be spread through their faeces. This disease
is common in communities with poor sanitation and lack of safe drinking water (WHO
2023b). Sisimiut has safe drinking water, so typhoid would only be a problem if contami-
nated water came into contact with natural drinking water reservoirs (lakes).
B. Vibrio cholerae
Cholera is caused by Vibrio cholerae bacteria and leads to severe diarrhoea and dehydra-
tion, which can be fatal if untreated (WHO 2023a). It is a major health problem in countries
where there is severe poverty and poor sanitation and hygiene such as some places in
Africa and Southest Asia. As these bacteria naturally live in brackish or salt water, where
they easily attach themselves to the chitinous shells of crabs, shrimps and other shellfish,
their infection can be spread by ingesting contaminated food.
C. Shigella
Shigella infection (shigellosis) is an intestinal infection caused by a family of bacteria
known as Shigella. It causes dysentery and the main sign of infection is diarrhoea, which

is often bloody (CDC 2024). The bacteria are easily spread through contaminated food or
by drinking or swimming in unsafe water and are found only in humans. Infected people
excrete the bacteria in their faeces, so exposure to sewage-contaminated water could
pose a risk of infection.
D. Escherichia coli
E. coli O157 is known as the most common cause of verotoxin-producing E. coli (VTEC)
disease in humans. It causes bloody diarrhoea (haemorrhagic colitis) and kidney failure
(haemolytic uremic syndrome) in humans. This bacterium is adapted to survive in aquatic
environments, so swimming in contaminated areas can be a way of exposure.
E. Legionella
Legionella can cause legionellosis or Legionnaires’ disease, an acute purulent pneumo-
nia, and Pontiac fever. It is found in aquatic environments such as safe drinking water
supplies. Its growth is favoured at higher temperatures, allowing it to colonise in hot show-
ers, sinks, etc., exposing susceptible patients to infection (Institut 2024).
F. Aeromonas
Some studies have suggested that the pathogenicity of Aeromonas may be strain related,
with only a few strains causing disease in humans (Morgan et al. 1985). Aeromonas spp.
are commonly found in aquatic environments and readily multiply in wastewater (Leclerc et
al. 2002). They are psychrotrophic bacteria and can grow coliforms in sewage. Although
these bacteria are frequently isolated from water, no major outbreak from Aeromonas
related disease has been reported.
G. Pseudomonas aeruginosa
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is common in wastewater and surface waters that receive pol-
luted wastewater (Leclerc et al. 2002). Even though the risk to the general population is
not significant (Hardalo et al. 1997), there’s enough evidence that some hosts are at risk
of an infection from P. aeruginosa.
H. Mycobacterium avium
According to some reports (Collins et al. 1984; Moulin et al. 1986; Jenkins 1991; Falkin-
ham 1996), Mycobacterium avium uses the aquatic environment to infect and colonise.
Nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) are a group of bacteria that can cause infections in
people, especially if their immune system is weak. Some types of NTM, such as those
in the Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC), can cause serious lung infections. These
infections can be very difficult to treat and can be life-threatening if not treated properly
(Falkinham 2018).
I. Giardia lamblia
Giardia lamblia is a protozoan that causes giardiasis, an intestinal infection. It is transmit-
ted by ingesting contaminated water, so infection can occur in water environments such
as lakes and rivers (Caetano et al. 2013). Even without symptoms, a contaminated person
can spread it through faecal matter, which is then present in sewage that is discharged
into seawater.
J. Cryptosporidium
Cryptosporidiosis, commonly known as crypto, is a diarrhoea-causing infection caused
by Cryptosporidium found in faeces. This infection can occur after eating food or drinking
water that has been contaminated with faeces. This includes swallowing contaminated
water while swimming or drinking from contaminated streams and other water sources.
It is also possible to become infected by touching objects or surfaces that have been
contaminated with faeces, or by direct contact with an infected person (Gururajan et al.

In addition to these pathogenic microorganisms, wastewater can also contain antibiotic-
resistant bacteria, which can cause significant health problems. When wastewater con-
tains these resistant bacteria, they can spread to natural water bodies (Jensen et al. 2013).
Communities can then be exposed to these bacteria through drinking water, recreational
activities or by eating contaminated food. Infections caused by antibiotic-resistant bac-

can lead to higher healthcare costs and increased morbidity and mortality. In Sisimiut,
wastewater from the hospital is discharged into the ocean and a few antibiotic-resistant
bacteria have been found in Sisimiut’s waters (Jensen et al. 2013).
1.4.2
Vulnerable Populations
In Greenland, vulnerable groups who are particularly susceptible to the health effects of
wastewater contamination include children, the elderly and people with weakened im-
mune systems. Indigenous communities often depend on local natural resources for their
livelihoods and are therefore at high risk. In addition, people living in the remote set-
tlements may be more vulnerable due to inadequate sanitation infrastructure. Workers
who handle wastewater, fishermen, and local people living near wastewater discharges
are groups at higher risk of infection. These groups are more likely to suffer from infec-
tions and other health problems caused by untreated wastewater, highlighting the need
for improved wastewater management and sanitation practices in Greenland.
1.5
Previous Studies on Microbial Risk Assessment in Arctic
Communities
1.5.1
Case Studies
Several case studies from coastal and Arctic regions have evaluated microbial contam-
ination in seawater, providing comparisons for this research. For example, studies con-
ducted in Alaska and Northern Canada have examined the presence of E. coli, coliforms,
and other microbial contaminants in communities that discharge untreated or minimally
treated wastewater into the marine environment.
In Nunavut, Canada, there have been studies of wastewater treatment systems and re-
ceiving waters where water quality measurements, including E. coli and total coliforms,
have been made. In Neudorf et al. 2017, faecal indicator bacteria were counted in wastew-
ater and receiving waters in Iqaluit and it was concluded that the number of total coliforms
and E. coli was significantly (P<0.05) higher at low tide than at high tide. Although the
estimation of actual disease risks from wastewater exposure in Arctic communities has
not been well studied due to the challenging conditions, waterborne diseases are believed
to be comparatively higher than in other regions. Other studies such as Daley et al. 2018
have attempted to develop a microbial risk assessment framework for Northern Canada to
evaluate wastewater treatment and public health. In this study, a screening-level QMRA
(quantitative microbial risk assessment) of wastewater management in Canadian Arctic
communities was proposed. However, information on the levels of pathogens associated
with wastewater in the Arctic environment is still very limited and needs to be further inves-
tigated. Nevertheless, the study concluded that the available data are sufficiently robust
to be used in a predictive manner to assess the nature and magnitude of associated public
health risks.
In another study (Daley et al. 2019), QMRA was used to provide the first estimates of AGI
attributable to wastewater treatment systems in the Arctic territory of Nunavut, Canada.
Scenarios were developed to assess the number of exposure pathways and microbial
8

==PAGE:27==
pathogens, and it was concluded that there is a high incidence of disease when wastewa-
ter is discharged directly into the seawater at low tide. Even with some primary treatment,
there was considered to be a moderate risk of AGI incidence. In Daley et al. 2022, QMRA
was also used to estimate microbial health risks associated with wastewater exposure of
the Arctic Canada communities and to further evaluate mitigation options for wastewater
treatment. Other studies, such as Storesund et al. 2017, conducted in coastal Norway,
have focused on microbial transport due to tides and currents and shown how bacterial
communities in surface waters can be influenced by geographic distance.
Some of these studies apply methodologies such as culture-based microbial counts and
molecular techniques like PCR but also mathematical models such as QMRA to evaluate
microbial contamination and potential health risks. The findings highlight the persistence
of microbial contaminants in cold marine environments, influencing local ecosystems and
posing health risks to populations reliant on marine resources.
1.5.2
Comparative Analysis
Studies conducted in Greenland and similar Arctic environments have provided insights
into wastewater contamination and its impact on human and environmental health. A
study (Kreissig et al. 2023) was carried out in Sisimiut, where faecal indicator bacteria such
as coliforms and E. coli were detected in Sisimiut seawater. Another study (Jensen et al.
2013), investigated the presence of coliforms and E. coli in the waters around Sisimiut.
In contrast, the study conducted in this thesis focuses on both seawater and greywater
contamination in Sisimiut and uses laboratory methods such as culture-based and molec-
ular methods to determine concentrations of faecal indicators at different locations and
tidal conditions. In addition, it aims to propose a risk assessment evaluation of the micro-
bial impact on human health. It also attempts to provide mitigation strategies, taking into
account the conclusions of previous studies and its own conclusions on microbial concen-
trations in Sisimiut. By building on these previous efforts, the current study contributes to
a more comprehensive understanding of microbial risks from wastewater in Sisimiut.
1.6
Scope of the Study
The aim of this thesis is to answer the following central research question: What are the
health risks posed to the population of Sisimiut by microbial contamination in untreated
wastewater, discharged into the marine environment, and what mitigation strategies can
be implemented to reduce these risks?
The objective of this thesis is to conduct a comprehensive microbial risk assessment of
wastewater in Sisimiut, Greenland, and to evaluate the associated human health risks and
potential mitigation strategies. The aim is to analyse the health effects on the population
by investigating the potential exposure to microbial levels and the types of microbiological
contaminants present in the untreated wastewater discharged into the marine and terres-
trial environment. The objective is to conduct a qualitative risk assessment of the public
health impact of microbial contamination in the exposure areas in Sisimiut and to identify
potential mitigation options to reduce this risk.
During the project the levels and distribution of microbial contaminants such as E. coli,
coliforms and heterotrophic bacteria in seawater and greywater, will be determmined,
areas of high contamination will be identified and insights into patterns related to tides
and location will be provided. Establishing a baseline with this information is essential to
assess the health risks of the population. For this reason, fieldwork has been carried out
in Greenland, sampling various water sources in the Sisimiut area which pose a risk of

Materials and Methods
2.1
Study Area and Sample Collection
2.1.1
Study Location
Sisimiut (66°56′20″N 53°40′20″W) was selected as the area for this study. It is part of
Qeqqata Kommunia and faces challenges in terms of wastewater management, and the
health of the population may be at risk from exposure to microbial contaminants from
wastewater.
Various areas were selected for sampling, including areas used for fishing and underwater
hunting, kayaking and swimming/other recreational activities. All these areas are sources
of seawater for blackwater discharge analysis. Greywater samples were collected from
discharge areas around Sisimiut where children play and people pass by. The sites were

2.2
Microbial Analysis
Microbial analysis is a critical component of the wastewater assessment process, aimed at
detecting and quantifying pathogenic microorganisms present at various stages of treat-
ment. This analysis helps to determine the efficiency of treatment processes and to as-
sess the potential health risks associated with wastewater discharge in Sisimiut.
2.2.1
Pathogen Selection
Heterotrophic bacteria are analysed in the water samples as the number of heterotrophic
bacteria is a broad indicator of the general bacterial population in the water. These bacte-
ria are a diverse group that includes many naturally occurring microorganisms in aquatic
environments. While not all heterotrophs are harmful, a high HPC can indicate elevated
levels of organic matter and nutrients, which are often found in wastewater. This can
indicate potential contamination and provide an overall picture of microbial activity in the
water.
In this study the microbial analysis focuses on pathogens commonly found in wastewater,
such as E. coli, because of their prevalence, potential health impact and ease of detec-
tion. In addition to specific pathogens, indicator organisms such as total coliforms are
analysed to provide a general indication of microbial contamination. Their presence in
water indicates that faecal matter and potentially harmful pathogens may also be present.
2.2.2
3M Petrifilm Method
At the laboratory, the wastewater samples collected were prepared for analysis. This
involved homogenising the sample to ensure uniformity and, if necessary, diluting it to
fall within the detection range of the analytical methods. In cases where concentration of
microorganisms was expected to be low, the sample was concentrated by filtering 100 ml
of the sample through a membrane filter water sample using a 47 mm, 0.45 micron pore
size Mixed Cellulose Ester (MCE) filter. In general, greywater samples were used directly
without dilution or concentration as they were expected to be more concentrated, while
seawater samples were concentrated as these samples were expected to be diluted by
the ocean.
Heterotrophic Plate Count (HPC) using 3M Petrifilm
Heterotrophic plate count (HPC) measures the concentration of heterotrophic bacteria,
which are microorganisms that require organic carbon for growth. The Neogen 3M Petri-
film Aqua Heterotrophic Count (AQHC) Plate used in this study is a sample-ready culture
medium system containing standard method nutrients, a cold water soluble gelling agent
and a tetrazolium indicator to facilitate colony enumeration.
For this analysis, 1 ml of the sample was placed directly in the centre of a Neogen 3M Pet-
rifilm Aqua Heterotrophic Count Plate. A plastic spreader was used to spread the sample
evenly over the surface of the plate, which helps to ensure uniform bacterial growth. The
inoculated plates were incubated at 35 °C ± 1 °C for 24 ± 2 hours. After incubation, the
colonies formed on the Petrifilm plate can be easily observed as red dots representing
heterotrophic bacteria. These colonies are then counted and the total is expressed as
colony forming units (CFU) per plate.
Coliform and E. coli Detection Using 3M Petrifilm
The Neogen 3M Petrifilm E.coli/Coliform Count Plate used is a sample-ready culture
medium system which contains modified Violet Red Bile (VRB) nutrients, a cold-water-
soluble gelling agent, an indicator of glucuronidase activity, 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolylD-
glucuronide (BCIG), and a tetrazolium indicator that facilitates colony enumeration.

The 3M Petrifilm method for detecting E. coli and coliforms in wastewater involves several
key steps. For seawater areas 100 ml of the sample was filtered as described before and
the filter was placed on the plate, adding 1ml of sample. For greywater samples, a 1 mL
portion of the sample was placed on a Neogen 3M Petrifilm E. coli/Coliform Count Plate.
A plastic spreader was used to spread the sample evenly across the plate to ensure even
distribution of bacteria. The plates were then incubated at 35 °C ± 1 °C for 48 ± 2 hours.
After 24 ± 2 hours of incubation, the coliform bacteria were identified by the formation of
red colonies accompanied by gas bubbles trapped in the gel due to lactose fermentation.
E. coli colonies also appear after 48 ± 2 hours as red colonies but can be distinguished
by a blue precipitate surrounding the colony, indicating the presence of the enzyme β-
glucuronidase, a specific marker for E. coli.
2.2.3
Data Analysis
The microbial analysis data were quantified to determine the concentration of pathogens
per unit volume of wastewater. This quantification helps to compare the exposure to
wastewater contamination of different areas in Sisimiut.
Interpretation and reporting
• Heterotrophic bacteria: The total number of red colonies on the HPC Petrifilm was
manually counted and reported as CFU/plate. The results provide a measure of the
overall bacterial load in measured portion of the sample.
• Coliforms: The number of red colonies with gas bubbles was manually counted
and reported as CFU/plate. This indicates the level of coliform contamination in the
sample.
• E. coli: Blue colonies with gas bubbles were specifically counted and reported as
CFU/plate, indicating the presence of E. coli and therefore faecal contamination in
the sample.
2.2.4
Quality Control
To ensure the accuracy and reliability of the microbial analysis, controls and duplicates
were included in each test batch. To check that the procedure was accurate and there was
no cross contamination, clear water (negative control) was filtered and tested for bacteria
after each sample filtration. No bacteria were detected in any of the tests, providing further
assurance that there was no cross-contamination between samples. A positive control
was not used in this analysis as there was no sample available with accurately known
levels of pathogens. Duplicates were made of each sample site to verify the reproducibility
of the results.
2.3
Digital PCR Analysis
Molecular methods such as Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) are used for rapid and spe-
cific detection of pathogens. These methods amplify the genetic material of microorgan-
isms, allowing identification and quantification of even low concentrations of pathogens.
Digital PCR (dPCR) is a highly sensitive technique for quantifying DNA or RNA molecules.
It is an advanced form of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) that allows absolute quantifi-
cation of target nucleic acid sequences without the need for standard curves or reference
samples as required in traditional quantitative PCR (qPCR).
2.3.1
Target Markers
In this study, two markers were selected to quantify the presence of faecal contamina-
tion in the water samples. A good marker for this purpose is uidA which encodes β-
14
Microbial risk assessment of wastewater in Sisimiut, Greenland: Health risks and mitigation options
==PAGE:33==
glucuronidase enzyme, a characteristic of E. coli and other similar bacteria. This gene
was therefore chosen to detect and quantify the presence of E. coli in the seawater and
greywater samples from Sisimiut because it is a highly specific marker and is univer-
sally present in this species. In addition, the uidA gene marker has a low probability of
cross-reactivity with non-E. coli species, which reduces the chance of false negatives and
ensures that the detected E. coli concentrations are a true reflection of the faecal contami-
nation in the water samples. HF183 is another marker chosen for these studies because it
is a human-associated genetic marker derived from Bacteroides, a genus of bacteria that
is abundant in the human gut but rare in the gut flora of other animals. HF183 is therefore
a highly specific indicator of human faecal contamination of environmental waters. This
specificity is essential for this study as it helps to understand the source of faecal contam-
ination in order to assess human health risks. Furthermore, this marker is able to detect
low levels of human faecal contamination even in diluted or highly mixed water samples,
which is the case in seawater samples.
2.3.2
DNA Extraction
For sample preparation, 100 ml of each sample was filtered and then preserved by freez-
ing in sterile sample tubes. These samples were then selected according to the results
obtained from the 3M Petrifilm method and used for DNA extraction. Note that a reduced
number of samples were used due to laboratory/time availability.
DNA was extracted from the samples using the QIAGEN DNeasy PowerWater Kit ac-
cording to the protocol provided. The filter was placed in a 5 ml PowerWater DNA Bead
Tube and 1 ml of PW1 solution, previously warmed in a warm water bath (to dissolve
precipitates), was added to the bead tube. This was repeated for each selected sample.
The tubes were secured horizontally in a vortex adapter and vortexed at maximum speed
for 10 minutes. The resulting supernatant was then transferred to a clean 2 ml collec-
tion tube using a 1 ml pipette tip and added to the beads. This was repeated until all
supernatant was removed. The 2 ml tubes were centrifuged at 13,000 x g for 2 minutes
at room temperature. After 2 minutes, the supernatant was transferred to another clean
2 ml collection tube, avoiding the pellet. 200 µl of IRS solution was added to each tube
and briefly mixed by vortexing. All tubes were incubated at 4 °C for 5 minutes. The tubes
were centrifuged again at 13,000 x g for 2 minutes. Avoiding the pellet, the supernatant
was transferred to a clean 2 ml collection tube and 650 µl of solution PW3 (previously
heated in a warm water bath to dissolve precipitates) was added. All tubes were vortexed
briefly to mix. 650 µl of supernatant was loaded onto an MB Spin Column and centrifuged
at 13,000 x g for 2 minutes. The flow was discarded and the procedure repeated until
all the supernatant had been processed. Still using the same MB Spin Column, 650 µl
of Solution PW4 (shaken) was added and the tubes were centrifuged at 13,000 x g for 2
minutes. The flow-through was again discarded and 650 µl of ethanol were added and
the tubes were centrifuged at 13,000 x g for 2 minutes. The flwo-through was discarded
and the tube were again centrifuged at 13,000 x g for 3 minutes. The MB Spin Column
Filter were placed into a clean 2 ml collection tube and 100 µl of Solution EB were added
to the center of the white filter membrane. The tubes were centrifuged at 13,000 x g for
2 minutes and the MB Spin Column was now discarded. The DNA in the collection tube
was finally ready for the next procedures.

2.3.4
Data Analysis
dPCR results are expressed in absolute concentrations. During PCR, the DNA sample is
divided into individual partitions, each containing a single DNA molecule. An independent
PCR reaction takes place in each of these partitions and if the target DNA (markers used)
is present in a partition, it will be amplified and a fluorescent signal will be generated. The
partitions that emit a fluorescent signal are called positive partitions, while the negative
partitions and the one without the target DNA are called negative partitions. dPCR there-
fore gives results in terms of the number of positive partitions, which is proportional to the
number of target DNA molecules present in the original sample. Because this partitioning
is a random process, some partitions may contain more than one DNA molecule and oth-
ers may contain no molecules at all. To account for this randomness of DNA distribution
in the partitions, the Poisson distribution estimates the probability of a partition containing
a given number of target DNA molecules. In equation 2.1 there’s represented the Poisson

2.4.2
Statistical Tests
In this study, statistical tests were used to identify the significance of our results and to
compare variations across locations, conditions, and wastewater sources. In addition,
the results were also analysed against a threshold to determine if there is a significant
difference between the observed data and the water quality limits, and to assess whether
the observed difference is likely to be due to chance or not. Before selecting the tests to
run with our data we had to evaluate whether our data followed a normal distribution or
not. To do so, we performed a Shapiro-Wilk test on all groups of data (high tide vs low
tide, seawater vs greywater, seawater across locations, greywater across locations). After
evaluating the distribution of the data, a homogeneity of variance test (Leven’s test) was
performed to assess whether the variances of the two groups are equal. Non-parametric
tests were then chosen to perform a statistical analysis on our data since they don’t follow
a normal distribution nor have equal variances.
A Mann-Whitney U test was selected to perform on seawater and greywater samples,
regardless of location or tidal conditions, as well as on high tide and low tide samples.
The input data was the bacterial concentration in log10 CFU/ml. A Krustal-Wallis test was

performed in order to compare bacterial concentrations at different locations of seawater
samples. This test compares the medians of the locations to determine if there is a statis-
tically significant difference between values. A One-Sample Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test
was performed on seawater and greywater samples to compare the bacterial concentra-
tions against a threshold defined by the Danish bathing water quality standard regulation
(COWI 2001). The threshold was log transformed to be able to be comparative to our
log transformed data. For the greywater samples we performed a bootstraping analysis
where the results allow to infer properties about the population, such as the mean of bac-
terial concentration, based on our small sample. The mean from the bootstrap samples
gives an estimate of the true population mean based on the few data available. This value
therefore represents what we would expect the average bacterial concentration to be if
we had access to a larger representative dataset. The confidence interval (CI) provides a
range of values within which the true population mean is likely to lie with a certain level of
confidence. In this case, a 95 % confidence interval means that if we repeated the sam-
pling process many times, 95 % of the confidence intervals we calculated would contain
the true population mean. A Spearman correlation was performed to assess the rela-
tionship between coliform and E. coli concentrations in seawater and greywater samples.
We also applied this correlation to determine the relationship between E. coli counts and
coliforms (CFU/ml) with dPCR gene markers (copies of uidA and HF183/ml).

Interpretation
For the Shapiro-Wilk test if the p-value < 0.05 the distribution of the data is not normal,
while if the p-value > 0.05 the data is normally distributed. For statistical tests such as
Mann-Whitney U, Krustal-Wallis and Wilcoxon Signed-Rank, if the p-value < 0.05 (signifi-
cance level), we reject the null hypothesis, i.e. there is a statistically significant difference
between the compared groups. If the p-value > 0.05 , we do not reject the null hypothe-
sis and there is no significant difference. For the Spearman correlation, a p-value < 0.05
means there’s a significant monotonic relationship between variables.

3.2.3
Coliform and E. coli Correlation with uidA and HF183
To assess the relationship between bacterial counts and the gene markers indicating fae-
cal contamination, we performed a Spearman correlation between coliforms, E. coli and
the gene markers uidA and HF183. This is important because coliform and E. coli counts
are traditional indicators of faecal contamination. E. coli is a subset of coliforms and is a
more specific indicator of faecal contamination, so we want to evaluate the relationship
between the two. In addition, uidA is specific for E. coli and was used in dPCR to directly
detect E. coli. In this way, a correlation between E. coli counts and uidA copies would
confirm that the gene marker reflects actual E. coli contamination. The HF183 marker
is a human-specific gene marker from Bacteroides species and is used to detect human
faecal contamination. A correlation between HF183 and E. coli and coliforms would there-
fore indicate whether traditional bacterial indicators reflect human-specific contamination.
By performing a Spearman correlation, we can assess whether there is a statistically
significant association between traditional bacterial indicators (coliforms and E. coli) and
molecular markers (uidA and HF183), thus improving the interpretation of faecal contami-
34
Microbial risk assessment of wastewater in Sisimiut, Greenland: Health risks and mitigation options
==PAGE:53==
nation sources in this study. We use a Spearman correlation because it is non-parametric
and assesses monotonic relationships between variables, i.e. non-linear relationships. It
is therefore suitable for data that are not normally distributed, as is the case for our water
samples with bacterial concentrations.
First, we evaluated the correlation between coliform and E. coli counts for both seawater
and greywater samples. The p-value was less than 0.05, so we can conclude that there
is a significant monotonic relationship between both variables. When we performed the
Spearman correlation to compare E. coli with uidA and with HF183 and did the same for
coliforms, we obtained a p-value > 0.05, which means that there is no significant mono-
tonic relationship between the variables. This can likely be explained by the small sample
size, as we only used 22 samples for dPCR.

4
Discussion
4.1
Limitations
Sampling from multiple sites (seawater and greywater sites) in Sisimiut provided a broad
geographical understanding of contamination patterns. It also helped to identify point and
non-point sources of contamination, which is essential for understanding the distribution
of microbes. In addition, the distinction between high and low tide samples allowed an
assessment of the tidal influence on microbial concentrations. However, a major limitation
of this study is the lack of long-term monitoring data to assess trends in microbial con-
tamination over time. This study provides a snapshot of microbial risk based on samples
collected during specific seasons and conditions, which may not fully represent annual
variability. Furthermore, while qualitative risk assessment may be useful, it may not cap-
ture the full complexity of health risks posed by untreated wastewater, especially in the
context of traditional Arctic diets and activities.
Another limitation of this study is the lack of comprehensive data on pathogen survival in
cold marine environments, particularly in the Arctic. Seasonal variations, such as freeze-
thaw cycles, can affect microbial survival and contamination levels, but the exact effects
are not considered in this work. In addition, logistical constraints in Sisimiut, such as lim-
ited access to advanced laboratory facilities, have affected the depth of microbial analysis.
While digital PCR provided accurate quantification of microbial contaminants, the sample
size and scope were limited and may not have captured the full extent of contamination,
particularly during different weather or tidal conditions.
4.2
Methodological Evaluation
The methodological approach taken in this study to analyse the samples provides a bal-
anced and detailed discussion of the results, presenting a qualitative health risk assess-
ment based on two methods. The 3M Petrifilm method was useful for an initial screening
of contamination levels, while dPCR provides more targeted risk assessments when con-
sidering human health. Both methods have shown a discrepancy in results, which may
be due to their difference in sensitivity. The 3M Petrifilm method may overestimate risk by
detecting both viable and non-viable E. coli and other coliforms, resulting in a higher CFU
count. On the other hand, dPCR detects specific DNA sequences that provide a more
accurate measure of viable contamination, but may miss bacteria present at low levels.
While the 3M Petrifilm results showed high CFU/ml of E. coli at most sampling sites,
indicating that the water quality exceeded safe levels for bathing and posed an increased
risk of infection for people engaged in activities such as swimming, fishing and other
recreational activities, the dPCR results showed fewer problematic sites. This is due to
the higher sensitivity and specificity of the dPCR method to detect faecal contamination.
In addition, fewer sites were sampled using this method, further limiting the statistical
power to detect trends. In addition to identifying the presence of E. coli at the DNA level,
dPCR also adds a qualitative dimension to our results, providing additional confidence that
contamination at some sites is of human origin by using a specific faecal contamination
marker.

4.3
Spatial and Temporal Variations
As we observed in the results, the differences in microbial contamination between high
and low tide were not significant (P>0.05) between sites, although some sites had higher
concentrations at low tide as expected. As mentioned above, the collection of snapshot
samples at these tides did not allow for a proper evaluation of the influence of tidal changes
on microbial contamination. There is also no clear difference between sites (P>0.05),
although one would expect to see a difference between sample sites collected close to
the outlets and those further away. It was not possible to assess a trend in spatial and
temporal variation due to lack of data and information on other environmental factors.

In the results we observed that there was a significant difference between the threshold
values from COWI 2001 and the bacterial concentrations from the analysed samples. This
showed that the mean of total coliforms in seawater and greywater samples was higher
than 10 000 CFU/100ml and the mean E. coli concentration in seawater and greywater
samples was higher than 1000 CFU/100ml. When we analysed the samples with the uidA
and HF183 gene markers, the mean value was lower than standard values for seawater
and greywater samples, indicating that, according to dPCR methods, the water quality in
evaluated areas is not as critical as initially thought with the 3M Petrifilm method.
The results of the qualitative microbial risk assessment were used to produce maps of
Sisimiut showing locations where the risk of bacterial contamination exceeds human health

exposure to pathogens that could cause gastrointestinal and other waterborne illnesses
in local people using the water for recreation. These observations suggest that where
local people fish, swim, use boats, kayak, and dive, there are human health concerns due
to high exposure to pathogens.

E. coli can cause extraintestinal illness in humans, including urinary tract infections, ab-
dominal and pelvic infections, pneumonia, bacteraemia and meningitis. The most com-
mon symptoms of exposure are general gastrointestinal discomfort, skin rashes, and
eye and ear infections. However, some strains can cause other serious and even life-
threatening complications such as bloody diarrhoea, kidney failure and (in rare cases)
death. Therefore, exposure to high levels of microbial contamination through swimming,
fishing or harvesting shellfish from contaminated areas increases the likelihood of infec-
tion. For example, exposure to water with high levels of E. coli can lead to infection in
swimmers through ingestion or skin contact. Similarly, consumption of seafood harvested
from contaminated waters can lead to foodborne illness, posing a significant threat to
people who rely on these marine resources for food. In addition, prolonged exposure to
contaminated water (such as boating, fishing and hunting), particularly in the high expo-
sure areas identified in this study, could lead to repeated or chronic health problems in
vulnerable populations such as children, the elderly and those with weakened immune
systems.
Although the 3M Petrifilm results suggest widespread high risk of infection, while the dPCR
analysis suggests that the actual health risk to the population may be more limited, all
results show the need for improved wastewater management to protect public health in
Sisimiut. In the long term, if wastewater management practices remain unaddressed,
these health risks could lead to a public health burden in Sisimiut, affecting both the local
population and visitors. Immediate mitigation strategies, including enhanced wastewater
treatment, public health advisories, and continuous monitoring, are necessary to minimize
these health impacts.
4.5
Mitigation Strategies
The findings from our microbial assessment of the coastal waters in Sisimiut indicate that
the ecological and human health is at risk from microbial contamination. These impacts
highlight the need for immediate action to reduce sources of contamination such as im-
proving wastewater management.
4.5.1
Monitoring
Monitoring wastewater discharges is an important step in identifying, controlling and min-
imising microbial contamination. By consistently monitoring wastewater discharges in
Sisimiut, we are able to detect elevated levels of pathogens such as coliforms and E. coli
before they reach critical thresholds, allowing early intervention to prevent contamination
of recreational waters. Monitoring can help identify the largest sources of pollutants and
direct efforts to minimise their impact on human and environmental health. In addition,
regular monitoring helps to track fluctuations in microbial contamination due to seasonal
changes, such as increased runoff during snowmelt and rainfall, or changes in contami-
nation during tidal cycles, as observed in this study. This data could enable public health
authorities to issue water quality reports and warn the public when water is unsafe for
bathing or other activities, minimising direct exposure to harmful pathogens, and to time
the release of sewage into the sea to coincide with the high tide. In Sisimiut, effluent mon-
itoring could be critical in assessing the effectiveness of any proposed treatment systems
to ensure that treated water meets regulatory standards for microbial levels before being
discharged into the environment. By monitoring effluent discharges, long-term water qual-
ity data could be collected and used to conduct quantitative microbial risk assessments
and ultimately inform decisions on infrastructure improvements and public health inter-
ventions. Furthermore, this data can be valuable in identifying trends in contamination,
which can be used to develop risk exposure models and develop wastewater manage-

==PAGE:66==
ment policies aimed at reducing health risks to the population.
4.5.2
Wastewater Management Treatment
Sisimiut’s Arctic climate presents unique challenges for wastewater treatment, requir-
ing solutions that perform well in cold weather while remaining energy efficient and cost
effective (Yates et al. 2012). Effective treatment processes can remove or neutralise
pathogens, reducing the likelihood of waterborne disease and protecting the health of
community members. Furthermore, wastewater treatment helps to preserve the fragile
Arctic ecosystem (Daley et al. 2018). The community of Sisimiut thrives on activities
deeply intertwined with its coastal environment, such as fishing and seaside recreation.
In order to maintain these cherished traditions while prioritising public health, effective
wastewater treatment systems are essential. By maintaining clean waterways through
robust treatment processes, Sisimiut can ensure the viability of its fisheries and provide
safe havens for recreational activities, promoting both community well-being and environ-
mental stewardship. Although the proposal to install wastewater treatment technologies
appears to be viable options for Sisimiut, further research and detailed analysis will be
required to select the most appropriate wastewater treatment option for Sisimiut and its
conditions and constraints.
4.5.3
Public Awareness
Community involvement in wastewater treatment can be an essential mitigation strategy
for long-term success. By educating locals about the impact that improper wastewater dis-
posal can have, not only on human health, but also on the environment, we can change
the patterns of human activity in the treatment areas and the receiving environment. How-
ever, behaviour change should be chosen carefully, taking into account what is accept-
able, appropriate and culturally relevant to the local population (Nguyen-Viet et al. 2009).
Public campaigns on sanitation and the dangers of exposure to untreated water can help
reduce health risks by encouraging personal hygiene practices. Informing the population
about the location of wastewater discharge points can ultimately help to prevent the pop-
ulation from engaging in water-related activities in these areas. It is likely that some of
the exposed population will change their behaviour and move their activities away from
the outlet areas.
4.6
Future Research
In order to improve the microbial risk assessment in Sisimiut, some suggestions for further
research can be made.
A long-term monitoring programme to assess seasonal variations in microbial contamina-
tion levels would be required to collect extended data on different pathogen levels present
in wastewater discharges. By using both 3M Petrifilm and dPCR on the data collected,
it is possible to get a more accurate assessment of microbial risk over time. For dPCR,
other gene markers specific to antibiotic-resistant bacteria or other indicators of faecal
contamination could be used to better understand the microbial concentrations of differ-
ent locations in Sisimiut. More extensive sampling should be carried out by selecting more
and different areas of potential exposure of the population. In addition, other factors such
as temperature, nitrogen and phosphorus levels, dissolved oxygen and chemicals present
in the water should be measured at the time of sampling. These could influence the levels
of microbes and therefore mitigation options could be different depending on this informa-
tion. A better data set should be collected for the water bodies in Sisimiut as a whole so
that the risk assessment models can be more complete and better conclusions can be
drawn about the risks to human health and the ecological impact. Other environmental
factors such as tidal changes and currents should be taken into account in these models.

In addition, more research should be done on dose-response models for Arctic commu-
nities, where cold temperatures, unique diets and different exposure pathways may alter
the health risks associated with microbial contamination. Disease outbreaks should also
be studied in the community to gather data on infection rates associated with exposure to
contaminated waters, thereby strengthening local dose-response models. Moreover, the
identification of other hazards in the water sources besides E. coli could help to build a
better microbial risk assessment and to better understand the impact of poor wastewater
management on health risks.
Studies of the effects of climate change on microbial contamination should also be un-
dertaken to assess the impact on the transport and persistence of pathogens in the envi-
ronment. Warmer temperatures, changes in ice cover and shifts in precipitation patterns
could affect microbial survival and lead to increased human exposure. In addition, further
research on the ecological impacts of wastewater contamination may point to changes in
ecosystems that may affect not only the Arctic environment but also the communities that
depend on these ecosystems.
Further research is also needed on more appropriate wastewater treatment systems, tak-
ing into account Arctic conditions. Experimental trials of new treatment technologies could
be carried out to assess their potential for reducing microbial risks in local water bodies.

Conclusions
The microbial risk assessment carried out in Sisimiut, Greenland, reveals significant con-
cerns about the public health risks associated with the discharge of untreated wastewater
into the marine and terrestrial environment. The elevated levels of E. coli, coliforms and
heterotrophic bacteria in both seawater and greywater samples, particularly at sites close
to human activities, indicate an increased risk of infection through recreational exposure
and consumption of marine resources. Analysis using digital PCR to detect the uidA and
HF183 gene markers further confirmed the presence of faecal contamination, adding to
public health concerns.
This study highlights the urgent need for improved wastewater management practices
in Sisimiut. Without mitigation, continued discharge of untreated wastewater is likely to
result in ongoing microbial contamination of coastal waters, threatening not only local
ecosystems but also the health of residents and visitors. Risk characterisation and dose-
response modelling indicate that even low levels of exposure can lead to adverse health
effects, highlighting the need for intervention. The results of this study provide a valuable
data base for future research focused on developing a comprehensive risk assessment
for the indigenous population that hunts and fishes in the receiving waters, and aimed at
improving water quality and protecting health in Sisimiut and similar Arctic communities.
