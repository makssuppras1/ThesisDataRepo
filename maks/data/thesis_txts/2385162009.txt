The main objective of the study is to develop the methodology for energy 
planning for country or state. Geographic information system is used for 
analyzing global wind atlas dataset with the purpose of energy planning for 
country or city. The methodology is based on the removal of area first which 
are not considered for wind energy planning and then remaining area is 
utilized for making the wind energy planning for country and city. First the 
constraints are selected based on their physical limitation, environment 
issues and legal framework. After that, exclusion map is prepared which 
shows the areas to be excluded from the analysis. Consequently, useful 
area is obtained which is used in analysis for wind energy datasets. The 
wind resources datasets are provided as capacity factor maps of turbines 
V119 and V90. Now these capacity factor values on the useful area are 
analyzed to obtain the results which can be used for making sensible 
decision in wind energy planning. 
The developed methodology is applied over three countries (Denmark, 
Kenya and Uruguay). First, the study is focused on evaluation of wind 
resources over the areas where datasets are uncertain and then it is 
focused on evaluation of wind resources near populated area and 
transmission line. After that, the coverage of area of wind turbines that 
produces the energy equal to annual energy demand is estimated and then 
at last, the sensitivity analysis of buffer zone is performed in terms of energy 
output.  
It is observed that wind resources near the cities of Denmark have wind 
resources greater than 30% average capacity factor of V112 whereas the 
cities of Uruguay have around 20 % but the wind resource is quite poor near 
the cities of Kenya. Moreover, it is also found that the optimum energy in 
Kenya does not have access of transmission line network whereas Uruguay 
can exploit some of its optimum wind resources near their transmission line 
network. The area coverage of turbines is higher in Denmark than other two 
countries because of higher energy consumption in Denmark.  
At last, it is found that buffering distance is quite sensitive for water bodies 
and coastline. Wind resources are higher near the water bodies and 
coastline and lower away from these locations. This information can be 
used by the country which does not have any wind energy policy and it 
enables the planner to capture the maximum energy available around these 
areas.

1 Introduction 
1.1 
Motivation 
Country preparing for harnessing wind energy need to make a planning before implementing the 
project. In the planning, any wind project, availability of wind resource is examined so that potential 
site is identified. During the planning phase, planner requires resource data as accurate as possible 
because they are going to identify the installation sites which has a possibility of capturing highest 
energy as possible with minimum cost. That means, higher the accuracy of resource data more 
chances of selecting the good site for the project. If the resource data possesses less accuracy then 
there is a greater chance of selecting a poor site, this means it will produce less amount of energy 
with larger project area and consequently increasing the overall cost of projects. This risk can be 
eliminated by using higher accurate resource data.  This signifies pressing need of the wind resource 
dataset over the larger area for planner. 
There is increasing demand of massive open global wind dataset which can be easily accessible 
through the internet1. Technical university of Denmark together with international collaboration has 
produced the global wind atlas which provides wind resource datasets globally. This dataset gives 
the wind resource information in resolution of 250 m. So that this map could be very useful for 
planner because of higher resolution than other dataset like reanalysis dataset which have 
resolution in scale of kilometers because the coarse resolution reanalysis datasets underestimate 
the wind resources as it fails to capture the small-scale fluctuation of wind so that larger section of 
wind resources will be missing from calculation, in gentle terrain one fifth of wind is not captured 
whereas more than half of wind resources will be missed in complex terrain2.  
Thus, global wind atlas can work as tool that policy maker and energy planner can use for sensible 
wind energy planning because of its high resolution. Energy planner can use the wind atlas as per 
their need. Planner can use this atlas to find best wind resource around the county, around the city 
and around the transmission line or around the anyplace the planner wants within the country 
boundary. In this way, global wind atlas can be used for making energy policy and planning as per 
their country need. Now it is desired to use the global wind atlas data for energy planning purpose. 
The way it can be done by the geographic information system (GIS) tool because atlas dataset gives 
the information of wind resource information in the form of spatial datasets. To handle any spatial

This shows importance of developing the GIS technique for analysis of global wind atlas datasets. 
1.2 
Aim and objectives 
The aim of the study is to develop the GIS based methodology for analysis of global wind atlas 
datasets and use this methodology for different countries. This study is focused on analysis of wind 
resources datasets in such a way that result could provide the valuable information for making policy 
and planning of wind energy for their respective countries. 
In this study, wind resources datasets are obtained as capacity factors map of turbines derived from 
global wind atlas. These datasets are provided for three countries which are Denmark, Kenya and 
Uruguay. They are chosen to compare their results so that some of findings could be generalized. 
The capacity factor maps of two turbine V112 and V90 are provided. These two turbines have the 
same rated power of 3MW with the rotor diameter of 112 m and 90 m for V112 and V90 turbines 
respectively. In this study, it is desired to compare the energy output between these turbines but 
not recommending which turbine is better to use because they are compared based on their energy 
output only.  
This study aims on evaluating the wind resources not only for whole country but also near urban 
area, transmission line and over the locations where the datasets are uncertain. Evaluating the wind 
resources on these areas has its own benefits in planning phase of wind energy. 
Most of these activities centers in and around the cities. The development of city not only increase 
the gross domestic product (GDP) but also boast the overall economy of the country. So, for the 
cities to thrive constantly, there should be constant supply of energy without interruption so that 
cities activities could run smoothly. Thus, cities planner always looking for energy resources near 
the urban area so that they could harness the energy with ease. This shows the importance of 
knowing the wind resources near population area.

Similarly, there are also benefits of knowing the wind resources near the transmission line too. The 
transmission line is needed for delivering the power form the wind turbine to end users. The site 
which normally have good wind resource are far away from the city at where the electricity is 
consumed mostly. Therefore, wind energy planner always seeks the good wind resources near the 
existing transmission line. This is because they don’t have to spend more on building new 
infrastructure for delivering the power if the wind turbine is near the existing transmission line. So, 
it is always desirable for the planner to know status of wind resources near transmission line. 
This study also aims on evaluating the wind resources over the areas where datasets are uncertain. 
This evaluation helps the country to identify the uncertain resources exist in their country and help 
the country to take next step that could be more beneficial to their wind energy planning. 
Now, optimum wind resources location is identified and area is calculated. The calculated area is 
based on position of turbine place on highest capacity factor pixel with minimum distance between 
the turbine of 7*Diameter of turbine. This is important for the country because once the optimum 
positions are known, they can install the turbines so that they can harness the optimum wind energy 
available in their country. Thus, they can produce maximum energy with minimum installation of 
turbines. In this study, we are more concerned on the putting the turbine at highest capacity factor 
values and counting of these turbines till they produce energy equal to the yearly energy need of 
2018-2019. The energy consumption of these countries is projected based upon their previous year 
consumption and annual growth rate. In 2018-2019, the energy consumption of Denmark is 
projected as 31.37 billion kWh (based on 31 billion kWh3 in 2014 with annual growth of 0.3 %4). 
Similarly, the energy consumption of Uruguay is projected as 11.29 billion kWh (based on 10 billion 
kWh3 in 2014 with annual growth of 3.08 %5) and the energy consumption of Kenya as 10.35 billion 
kWh (based on 9.2 billion kWh3 in 2014 with annual growth of 3 %6). 
At last, study is focused on the buffer zones. Buffer are the areas described by the bounding region 
which is determined by specified distance from the feature (feature is representation of a real-world 
object). Countries having wind energy policy has its own buffer rules. On these buffer zones, 
installation of wind turbines is prohibited. These buffer distances are varied from country to 
country. They are set according to their countries need and perception. Thus, this study performs 
analysis of buffer distances in terms of energy capture near the features so that general idea of

buffering could be formed. This information of general ideas of buffering could become useful to 
the countries looking to make the wind energy policy. 
Therefore, objectives of this study are set as 
1. Comparing the energy output from two turbines (V112 and V90). 
2. Calculating the wind resource on over the area where Global wind atlas datasets are 
uncertain.    
3. Estimating the wind resource near populated areas and transmission lines 
4. Determining the areas of a country that are suitable for harnessing the wind energy so that 
energy produced from the suggested area would be enough to meet the country annual 
energy demand. 
5. Suggesting the buffer distance for countries that do not have any wind energy policy. 
One of the main reason for selecting three countries is that comparing the results from different 
buffer distances between the countries in order to get general idea of buffer zone that could able 
to capture the maximum wind resources. 
To achieve those objectives stated above, GIS based analysis of capacity factors map of three 
turbines are performed. First, relevant GIS datasets are collected which consists of different features 
that are considered to be excluded from analysis. On this excluded area planning of turbines are 
prohibited. These areas are identified based on the physical constraints, environment constraints 
and legal framework exist in country. The places where the turbine is not physically installed are 
categorized in physical constraints like forest, lake etc. and similarly, places where wind turbines are 
prohibited to conserve its nature and environments like national park, cultural heritage places etc. 
are categorized in environment constraints. Then legal frameworks include the buffering of 
features. Now identifying the constraints where wind resources are uncertain to evaluate the 
uncertain wind resources present in the datasets. Then all the constraints are combined together to 
make exclusion map. And then these exclusion map is omitted from the total area to get the useful 
area (useful area is termed as area that would be under the investigation during wind farm planning) 
which can be considered in evaluation of wind farm siting. With the help of useful area, useful 
capacity factor map can be derived. And then, at last, different GIS operations are performed to get 
the desired result.

Literature Review  
Many of literature are reviewed that are based on the use of GIS in the field of wind energy. Some 
of important literatures relating to this study are summarized below.  
Beata Sliz-Szkliniarz and Joachim Vogt (2010)7: The evaluation of wind energy potential is carried 
out using GIS in Kujawsko-Pomorskie, Poland. In their study, first they have identified the location 
available for wind farm siting. To carry out that, they prepared the exclusion map which contains 
the areas along with buffer zones that are excluded from the wind farm siting, which are later 
omitted from the total area to get areas available for wind farm siting. These excluded areas are 
urban, road, rails, protected area, water bodies, forest, land above 2000m and slope above 250. 
They estimated the area of 7500 sq. km is available for wind farm siting. 
Now, available location is then gridded with the area of 6 D*6 D assuming area in rectangle which 
is occupied the single turbine to encounter the wake effect.  And then annual energy production of 
each gridded is calculated for different turbines.

Amol et al. (2012)8 carried out research to evaluate the techno-economic onshore wind potential in 
India. In this research, they removed the constraints such as slopes (>20 degrees), elevation (> 
1,500m), forests, snow areas, water bodies, urban areas and low-quality wind area (WPD<200 
W/m2) from total study area and then remaining map is used as good wind resource sites. Assuming 
the turbine density of 9 MW/km2 with turbine of 2.5 MW and diameter of 100 m. They estimated 
543 GW  of wind potential at 80 m with the capacity factor greater than 25 % with the land coverage 
of approximately 1629 km2 which is 0.05 % of total study area. 
Suzie Cave (2013)9: The research is focused on availability of area for wind farm siting after excluding 
the residential areas with buffer distances in Republic of Ireland. The minimum separation distance 
between the wind turbine and residential area was set according to the turbine sizes. This was 
introduced as The Environment and Public health bill (wind turbine) in 2012. They set the distances 
of 500 m when the turbines height is 50 m. Similarly, for the turbines heights of 100 m, 150 m and 
height greater than 150 m the minimum distances set as 1000 m, 1500m and 2000 m respectively. 
But this bill did not pass as they felt this bill might hinder on achieving the EU renewable energy and 
climate change commitments.  
This decision is bolstered by the result of this research. They found out 23.75 % of total area of the 
country is available for the buffer of 500 m. However, area is dropped sharply as the buffer distance 
is increased. Total percentage of area is dropped to 9.4 % for 1000 m buffer, 5.2 % for buffer of 1500 
m and 3 % for buffer 2000 m. In this research, they have not considered the other factor like 
availability of wind resources, buffer for water courses, protected area etc. Therefore, the area 
calculated might become smaller if they have considered all the factors. They showed the 
importance of appropriate buffer distance for finding the area for wind farm siting. Increasing the 
buffer minimizes the area of the country for wind farm siting.

2 Tools and Datasets 
This study is based on the data analysis of the spatial datasets. The tool used for analysis of 
these datasets is geographic information system(GIS). In the geographic information system, 
there is a key word geography. This word itself means location and datasets used in this 
system are spatial or, in other words, datasets are referenced to the positions or locations on 
the world. The analysis of these dataset is called spatial analysis in GIS environment. The 
spatial analysis is the bunch of technique that analyses the spatial datasets. The output of 
spatial analysis is based upon the position or location of these analyzed datasets.  
2.1 
Geographic information system 
Geographic information system (GIS) is the computer based application which carries out the 
wide range of operation based on the geographic information. Geographic information 
denotes the information of location in the surface of globe. So, the purpose of GIS is to store, 
manipulate, visualization and analyze the geospatial data. Some of GIS tools used nowadays 
are ArcGIS, MapInfo, QGIS etc. In this study, ArcGIS desktop 10.1 package is used for analysis. 
This package is one of widely used GIS software today. The brief description core of every GIS 
is explained below. 
Spatial data represented in GIS are vector or raster data. Vector data are in the form of points, 
line and polygon to represent the earth surface 
and these shapes are used to define centers and 
edges of real world features like boundary, land 
parcel, streets etc. whereas raster data use the 
pixel to define the feature location. Vector data 
are useful for representing discrete datasets 
while raster data are useful storing continuous 
datasets like values that are varied form 
location to location like elevation, temperature 
of the earth surface.  
Figure 1 GIS data representing the real-world features11 
To understand GIS and its working principle, first it is necessary to understand coordinate 
system. Each dataset has its own coordinate system and this system helps the dataset to

system allows the datasets to be same location for integration. There are two coordinate 
systems used in GIS. 
Three-dimensional spherical coordinate system which is latitude and longitude is used to

All of the GIS operations are performed in ArcGIS 10.1. Datasets provided as tiles of raster 
maps (capacity factor maps and RIX maps). The capacity factor map consists of no data pixel 
on the border of each tiles, which are indicated by black lines. These black lines of each tiles 
overlap with each other.  Now, these unnecessary black lines are removed before they are 
combined. After omitting black lines, datasets are combined into single raster map because 
single maps are easy for GIS operation. Raster datasets (capacity factor maps or RIX maps) are 
combined by using tool mosaic to new raster under data management toolbox. In ArcGIS, there 
are six ways to handle these overlapping areas by using mosaic operator in Mosaic to New 
Raster tool. In this study, Mean is used to get the output value of overlapping raster, mosaic 
operator returns the output cell value of the overlapping areas from mean value of the 
overlapping cells. The output cell size is 250 m which is same as tiles raster map and projection 
of output raster is same as their country projection system.

3.2 
Exclusion map and useful area 
First, exclusion map is prepared through GIS analysis with the purpose of finding suitable area 
which will be used for locating wind turbine on the optimum wind resource locations. Now, 
the restricted areas are identified which are not considered for wind turbine installation. 
These areas are selected based on its physical constraint, environment constraint and legal 
framework.  
For physical constraints, areas such as forest and built up areas are considered because there 
is physical challenge of installation of wind turbine in those areas. Also, the global wind atlas 
dataset is not certain in these areas because these datasets are calculated based on the WAsP 
engine and WAsP does the poor prediction upon these areas because it does not have any 
specific model or procedure to model the wind flow on these areas and inside WAsP, these 
areas are simply indicated by roughness change line in vector map. Other physical constraints 
areas such as wetland, water bodies, highway, railway and transmission line are considered 
for this study.  
Another constraint is related to terrain. The WAsP software is developed for gentle terrain 
where wind flow is attached i.e wind follow along the terrain. But, in case of steep terrain, 
separation of boundary layer occurs and flow modelling will be biased. Therefore, WAsP result 
is uncertain in steep terrain. When the terrain slope is about 30% on a downwind slope and 
40% on an upwind slope, separation of flow occurs (Wood, 1995)15. So, the slope area greater 
==PAGE:28==
Geographical Information System (GIS) analysis of Global Wind Atlas Data      
28 
 
than 30% is one of the constraints for exclusion map. Also, RIX map is available for the study 
and the RIX greater than 5% is also used as constraint for exclusion map because spatial 
extrapolation using WAsP gives higher accuracy when RIX between measured and predicted 
sites lies 0-5% (Mortensen et al., 200616, 200817). 
Natural environment should not be disturbed and is protected as much as possible. Thus, 
wind turbine should not be located in these protected areas such as national park, natural 
monument, protected areas for birds etc to conserve the natural environment condition. 
Buffer zones are also the part of exclusion map and always taken into consideration for spatial 
planning of wind farm. Assigning the buffer is defined by the legal authority. If the country 
has the rule of specific value of buffer for certain areas then it must be included in final 
exclusion map because design wind farm cannot be approved unless all the legal legislation is 
met. Different country has different amount of buffer. In this study, different buffer distances 
are used for analysis to find its sensitiveness towards the energy output so the appropriate 
buffer distance policy can be suggested for the country which does not have any wind energy 
policy.  
In GIS, buffer is a zone around a map feature measured in units of distance which is used for 
proximity analysis. The main aim for locating wind farm with buffer is related to its 
performance, safety and preservation. Baban and Parry (2001)18 conducted a research to 
determine the relevant criteria for location of a wind farm where the study states that 
vegetation in vicinity highly effects wind speed and its flow direction and these factors directly 
interrupts in performance of wind turbine, that is why buffer is done near the forest. 
Furthermore, the study also expresses the shadow flicker effect and noise pollution generated 
by the wind energy plant which are extremely critical issues in health and safety, this is reason 
is turbine is kept away from household areas. Similarly, the study conveys that visual intrusion 
created by the wind farm possibly forward to accidents in the road, this justifies buffering in 
road and transmission line. Finally, the study says that encroachment of any development 
near protected areas such as cultural heritage exploits its existence, this justifies buffering in 
protected area. Therefore, considering these factors, buffers for the following constraints

3.3 
Spacing of Turbines 
Another restriction is turbine spacing which is needed when finding the total number of 
turbines that gives the energy equal to certain energy demands. This helps to reduce wake 
losses between the turbines. So, in this study, spacing is kept minimum of 7*rotor 
diameter(D). Spacing distance is taken based on the reduction of wind speed in downstream 
and it is found that above the 7*rotor diameter (in the direction of prevailing wind) decrease 
of wind speed is minimum25. And along the lateral direction wake grows slowly for 4-5D 
spacing25. But, in this study wind direction is not considered and taking the account of fact 
that wind can come from any direction, so, the spacing of 7*D is kept both longitudinal and 
lateral direction. 
To kept minimum spacing between the turbine, final raster map is converted into point 
feature followed by addition of x and y coordinate of each point in attribute table. Each point 
has the value of raster pixel and positioned in middle of pixel. Then attribute table is exported 
into MATLAB for analysis. 
In MATLAB, tables are arranged in descending order of capacity factor because it is essential 
to pick the minimum number of turbine so that it produces higher amount of energy because 
of higher capacity factor. Now the distance between two points in calculated by using x and 
y coordinate of points in distance formula. Finally, the condition is given in MATLAB that it 
picks only the points which have the higher value of capacity factor with minimum spacing of 
7*D. The highest capacity factor turbine is kept first and then searched another turbine which 
have highest capacity factor and not fall on 7D region. Then search is made for next turbine 
turbines which have highest value and not within the 7D distance from first and second 
turbines. Following this algorithm, result will be the point (x and y coordinates) along with its 
capacity factor arranged in descending order having minimum of 7D distance between these 
points. The MATLAB script for this is attached in appendix. 
The minimum number of number of turbines counted on the optimum wind resource position 
till the energy produced equal the energy need each country in the year of 2018-2019. The 
optimum number of turbines is evaluated for two conditions. First, it is determined within the 
10-km buffer of transmission line and second, it is determined within the boundary of whole 
countries.

The result from spacing between the turbine through MATLAB is the turbine position which 
is ordered in the descending order of capacity factor of turbines and these turbines have the 
minimum spacing of 7D between them. It is desired to find minimum turbine now. For this 
MATLAB script is created again in which while loop created for the energy demand. In the 
script, annual energy production in MWh is first calculated from the capacity factor rated 
power (3MW) and total annual hour (8760 hours). The turbine producing energy is counted 
till the energy demand it met through the while loop. The script is attached in appendix. In 
this way, minimum turbines are counted for both scenarios, around the transmission line and 
within the country boundary.  
3.4 
Wind resource near the populated area 
In this study, populated area datasets are collected from LANDSCAN dataset which refers the 
urban area as minimum of population density of 200 per sq. km. Now it is wished to find the 
wind resource around the 20 km of urban area of each of country. Two kilometers of buffer 
around the urban area is considered and then wind resources beyond 20 km from this buffer 
distance is calculated. 
Buffer tool is used for buffering the 2 km from the urban area along with dissolve tool which 
dissolves all area and make single solid polygon. If this solid polygon contains any of free 
polygon that might be created by present of hole in urban polygon is removed manually. Then 
buffer tool is applied again for the buffering of 20 km from the solid polygon and this time 
buffering is performed on the outside only. 
At last, thus created buffered polygon is used to extract the capacity factor pixel that lies 
within the boundary of polygon. For this purpose, clip tool is used for the capacity factor pixels 
map under useful area only. Finally, the zonal statistic under spatial analyst tool is used to 
evaluate the statistic information of capacity factor pixels within the boundary of 20 km buffer 
of city area.

4.6 
Suitable site location and coverage of area calculation 
Sometime energy planner need to know the minimum area needed for harnessing certain 
amount of energy so that they can identify the good wind resource location that can be used 
for harnessing energy in future. It is important to find the turbines as minimum as possible so 
that investment cost could be much lower. This can be done by identifying best possible site 
for harnessing wind energy. Thus, in this section, the number of the turbine is located on the 
best possible location so that minimum area coverage will be evaluated. The number of 
turbine is counted till the energy produced by them equal the yearly energy consumption of 
2018-2019. In this section area is considered within the 10-km buffer zone of transmission 
line and area within the country boundary. By doing this, all the high resources area is utilized 
for energy calculation.

turbine and then based on area coverage of total height of turbine on the ground.  
Amount of energy is evaluated for the different sets of buffer distance. The sets of buffer 
distances are listed on the Table 2 above. The reason for doing so is to observe the 
sensitiveness on the number of turbine with respect of different sets of buffer distance.

resources that is remained after removing of resources lying on the exclusion zones and

turbines of each country is determined so that they will produce the energy equal to energy

5 Discussion  
The one of main objective of this study to find out optimum area of country that can harness 
the energy equal to country energy future need. This will address the issue that most of 
planner come across that they want to know exact position of highest wind potential and as 
well as the total area of coverage of wind turbines on those locations because planner wants 
to achieve the highest wind energy as possible by putting up few turbines so that they can 
save some money and land which can be later used for other purposes. 
5.1 
Uncertain wind resources 
When the planner has wind resource map of country from atlas datasets then they can find 
uncertain wind resources present in the atlas dataset and locate these areas. After that, these 
resources can be excluded right away so that planner can consider wind energy planning to 
the other places or find other means of reducing the uncertainty. This information can be 
used as first screening tool for planner. In the case of Denmark, uncertain resources are 712 
TWh/year over forest and 0.4 TWhr/year over steep terrain even though area coverage of 
forest (4875.25 sq. km) and steep terrain (2.6875 sq.km) is small when compared with Kenya 
and Uruguay. This is because of higher wind resources present around these areas. For Kenya, 
land coverage of forest and steep terrain are higher compared to areas in Denmark and 
Uruguay, but the present of wind resource is quite low in these areas this is reason why Kenya 
has less percentage of uncertain wind resource despite having large areas. Similarly, for the 
Uruguay atlas dataset presents the average capacity factor 0.19 and 0.22 for steep terrain and 
forest respectively. The area of forest is significantly larger than steep terrain so that 
uncertain wind resources over forest is larger than steep terrain as 362.34 TWhr/year and 
53.30 TWhr/year respectively.  
Thus, the uncertain wind resource in atlas dataset in the Denmark is mainly due to the forest 
whereas Kenya has significant uncertain wind resources by both forest and steep terrain and 
Uruguay has its uncertain wind resources mainly due to forest. This information can be used 
as first screening tool for planner like, once they find complex terrain they can perform more 
detail assessment of wind resources (like by using CFD models) on the steep terrain to find 
accurate wind resource and use these resources when they require or they can look for gentle 
==PAGE:72==

terrain where presence of wind resource is optimum. Therefore, the country with uncertain 
wind resource in the dataset can invest for more accurate modelling of wind resources to 
reduce the uncertainty in wind resources.

5.4 
Sensitiveness of buffer zones 
It is found that buffering of the protected are quite sensitive when there is presence of large 
number of protected area, like in Denmark. Denmark consist of lots of number of protected 
area (178299), which are attached in the appendix, even the small buffer distance to the 
protected area covers lots of area of the country because of their large number. If the 
protected area of a Denmark is buffered by 1000m to each area then there will be no more 
area available in the country. This is reason why buffering of protected in Denmark is kept 
null when the analysis is performed in this study. Therefore, it is wise decision to buffer only 
to the areas which are very important to the country or communities and less importance 
protected area should not be buffered if the number of protected area is too high in the 
country. 
Another major objective of this study is to suggest the buffer zone for the country which does 
not have any rules and regulation related to wind energy. It is found that Denmark has its own 
rules for buffer for the most of constrains where as other two countries do not have any rules. 
So, the different sets of the buffer are used for all three counties to see variation in number 
of turbines that produces the same amount of their annual energy need. All the sets of the 
buffers distance are listed in Table 2, first set of buffer distances follow Danish rules for most 
of the constraint and another set of buffers is taken from the literature which is the increase 
of distance from the previous set. And the last, buffer is increased beyond the values from 
the literature. Now number of turbine is calculated based on the projected energy demand 
of each respective countries for the year of 2018-2019.

5.5 
Limitations of this study 
The accuracy of result depends on the accuracy of Global wind atlas data, accuracy of GIS data 
adopted, and limitation of GIS modelling. If the any of the uncertainty exist in either Global 
wind datasets or GIS data then this uncertainty inherent in the result.  
Some of buffer distance is less than pixel value of global wind atlas dataset that means when 
removing this buffer from capacity factor map then it might not clip the same distance as it 
should have been because pixel of dataset is larger than the buffering distance. So higher 
resolution of datasets is preferred. Some of the of GIS data sets do not represent feature 
correctly as it deviates by some distances in real world especially the land cover dataset which 
sometime does not show actual shape of the feature. Many of the times, in this study, many 
of land cover feature is manually digitized to represent actual area coverage. So, the accurate 
GIS data gives accurate buffering distance in the real world. Thus, the use of most accurate 
GIS data is encouraged to use, if available. 
In this study, the minimum spacing between turbines are created but these spacing are not 
the optimized position of the turbines because when listing down the turbine position in 
MATLAB, the highest capacity factor turbine is put first and then find next turbines which are 
not in minimum spacing (7D), so the positions are not optimized in this study because set of 
==PAGE:78==
Geographical Information System (GIS) analysis of Global Wind Atlas Data      
78 
 
turbines starting from second highest capacity factor points might be able give more energy 
in total. Therefore, result could be more accurate if the optimized position of turbines could 
be used. 
Improvement of GIS methodology gives better result. GIS process can be improved by taking 
the more constraints as possible. The purpose of exclusion map is to exclude area which is 
not considered for wind farm planning, which are based on physical constraint, social 
acceptance, environment concern and legal framework. Sometimes the countries have 
different sets of rules for different state or region. Thus, it better to put all state rule and 
criteria in the model. Thus, while doing planning for individual country, it is better to put as 
many constraints as possible that fits that country well. So that result obtained will be more 
accurate for country.

6 Conclusion  
The wind resource datasets are provided as capacity factor map of V112 and V90 wind 
turbines for three countries. The comparisons are made between the energy output of these 
turbines. It is found that energy output given by the V112 3MW turbines is greater than V90 
3MW. This is because of low rated wind speed of V112 turbines than V90. 
In Denmark, wind resources dataset reports the energy of 712.71 TWhr/year (V112 3MW) 
over the forest area and 0.4 TWhr/year from the steep terrain whereas datasets provides 
energy of 1138.53 TWhr/year (over forest) and 884.21 TWhr/year (over steep terrain) in 
Kenya. This shows the dominant presence of forest in Denmark than steep terrain whereas 
present of both forest and steep terrain accounts the larger portion of energy in datasets of 
Uruguay. Similarly, wind resources dataset of Uruguay presents the energy of 362.34 
TWhr/year over the forest and 53.2 TWhr over the steep terrain. 
Wind resources around the cities of Denmark, Kenya and Uruguay are studied. It is found the 
wind resources around the cities of Denmark are higher than cities of other countries. Wind 
resources around seven cities of Denmark are studied. Among them, Frederikshavn city 
possesses the highest mean capacity factor of 38.1 % (V112) on its 20-km surrounding and 
lowest of 31.4 % capacity factor on the surrounding of Roskilde. In the case of Kenya, eight 
different urban area are studied and found that none of city has good wind resource on their 
surroundings. The highest capacity factor of 13.4 % exists on the periphery of Mombasa. Now, 
for Uruguay, twenty-one different cities are studied. Among them, Maldonado have the 
highest capacity factor of 25.7% as highest and 14.8 % as lowest for Vergara. This information 
can be used by the planner to make sensible decision for wind energy planning like they can 
identify the highest resources location around the city and utilize them for wind energy.  
It is found that Denmark has transmission system in every corner of the countries where as 
Kenya lacks the transmission line facility on the most part of their counties and the Uruguay 
has good network of transmission line system. Therefore, almost all the location in Denmark 
is within the reach of 10 km buffer of transmission line. Denmark offers the good wind 
resources around its coastal area especially on the upper part of the county where as Uruguay 
which has its optimum wind resources also along the coastline and dam (water bodies), which 
==PAGE:80==
Geographical Information System (GIS) analysis of Global Wind Atlas Data

is in middle of country. Similarly, Kenya possess the optimum wind resources near the big lake 
on the upper part of the country. 
Wind resource around the transmission line is found as 0.11 for Kenya and 0.21 for Uruguay. 
This shows that Kenya has poor wind resource quality near their transmission line but Kenya 
possesses the optimum wind resource on the upper part of country but they lack the 
transmission line facility there. In the case for Uruguay, they have reach of optimum wind 
resource partially within the 10 km of the transmission line. Thus, Uruguay can utilize some 
of their optimum with resources without constructing the new transmission line system 
whereas Kenya does not have their transmission line system on their optimum wind resources 
location. 
The total number of turbines are counted till it produces the energy equal to energy demand 
in the year of 2018-2019 of each country. Considering the buffering distance that follows most 
of Danish rules, the area occupied by these turbines are 3251.18 sq.km, 827.98 sq.km 
,1805.11 sq.km in Denmark, Kenya and Uruguay respectively when considering area coverage 
of single turbine as 7D. Similarly, when considering area coverage as area of height of turbine 
on ground, the area occupied by these turbines are 252.92 sq.km, 50.77 sq.km ,130.40 sq.km 
in Denmark, Kenya and Uruguay respectively. The higher area coverage in Denmark is  due its 
high energy need and lower area coverage in Kenya is due to presence of high energy 
resources. 
Another main objective is to suggest the buffer area for the countries which do not have any 
wind farm planning framework. It is observed that country having the larger number of 
protected area like in Denmark is sensitive to buffer distance because buffering of 1000 m 
results the coverage of all the area in Denmark so that none of area is left available for wind 
farm siting. Therefore, it is concluded that area having larger number of protected area should 
be categorized according to their importance to the country or society. Then buffer distance 
is provided according to their importance. Higher importance area can be provided with 
higher buffer and lower importance can be provided with lower buffer distance so that 
enough area will be available for wind farm siting. But it is up to the planner of the country to 
give the buffer according to their perception and need but it is advised to them to put sensible 
buffer area so that protected area is conserved and enough area is left for wind farm siting.

From this study, it is also found that wind resources are usually high near coastline and water 
bodies. So, it is suggested that countries should put the buffer distance as small as possible in

buffering more would not bring any meaningful change in terms of finding the optimum wind 
resource in the country or state. Thus, again, it is up to energy planner to choose the buffer 
that fits their county’s perception and need but in the case of water bodies and coastline it is 
recommended that buffer distance must be kept as minimum as possible so that optimum 
wind energy can be utilized.
