Abstract
As processing power in embedded devices continues its unstoppable increase in speed
and eﬀiciency while managing to maintain or even decrease their size, the Internet
of Things is becoming more and more pervasive in different applications and easier
to being applied. Most traditional scenarios include embedded systems and wireless
sensor networks, but especially in the consumer market, IoT devices are used in
household appliances such as temperature monitoring, home security systems, and
cameras. Similarly, in the industry sector, wireless sensor networks are widely used.
Often sensor devices are battery-operated and deployed in any kind of industrial
setting such as manfacturing, agriculture, and infrastructure to last for years. The
importance to save energy of these devices is crucial due to it being very costly to
maintain a large-scale network. It is highly important that energy consumption of
such devices is kept low while still being able to operate without interference.
This thesis has studied the effects of jamming attacks in Wireless Sensor Net-
works applied in an industrial setting. In particular, for low-rate wireless personal
area networks (LR-WPAN) the 802.15.4 standard describes the physical and media
access control operation of these lower network layers. This long range wireless com-
munication enables devices to communicate between each other using a low date rate
while focusing on saving power consumption. Various specifications exist of this pro-
tocol such as LoRa, Zigbee, ISA100.11a, WirelessHART but Time Slotted Channel
Hopping (TSCH) operating mode was chosen.
Results show that it is possible for an attacker to perform various jamming at-
tacks towards particular nodes of a Wireless Sensor Network (WSN) but it is very
challenging to do so. The detrimental effects include, reduced availability, reliability
and a bigger power consumption from these devices to operate.
Keywords
IoT ; WSN; Wireless Sensor Networks; 802.15.4; Jamming; TSCH

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the Technical University of Denmark in fulfillment of the
requirements for acquiring a M.Sc.in Engineering degree in Computer Science.
I have always taken interest in the radio world, but my experimentation with
hardware based devices has always been somewhat limited with few experiences us-
ing WiFi dongles to perform Wi-Fi attacks. After finding a Software Defined Radio
in the DTU Compute HackLab I approached my supervisor, Xenofon Fafoutis, and
expressed my interest in using it for my thesis.
The thesis focuses on jamming attacks in Industrial IoT Wireless Sensor Networks.
In particular, it focuses on observing how sensor devices behave under particular jam-
ming attacks. The jamming attacks have been performed through a Software Defined
Radio and the chosen communication protocol between sensor devices relies on the
IEEE 802.5.4 standard and TSCH mode of operation.
Kongens Lyngby, 24th June 2022

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
With the constant increase of sensor devices utilized in industrial environments,
Wireless Sensor Networks (WSN) are facing increased challenges with communica-
tion and monitoring of real-time data due to interference from other devices. It is
essential that the interconnection of these sensor devices utilize appropriate technol-
ogy to mitigate interference and in the case of the 802.15.4 standard used for Low
Rate Wireless Personal Area Networks (LR-WPAN) utilize the least amount of energy
possible to communicate.
A specific mode of operation which will be analyzed in this thesis is a specific
MAC mode of operation introduced in the 802.15.4e standard. Time Slotted Chan-
nel Hopping (TSCH) handles possible interference and path-loss fading by constantly
changing frequency (also known as channel hopping) to communicate with other sen-
sor nodes and utilizes a shared schedule amongst devices to save energy, so that
devices can switch on when they are expected to receive a packet and switch off when
they are not expected to communicate or receive communication. Channel hopping
and Low Power Mode or Deep Power Mode are often used in industrial settings since
they enable to have reliability with low power consumption.
The reliability of TSCH to constrast interference is well known, but a potential
adversary can try to disrupt the reliability of the network by launching intentional
interference, also known as jamming, in the environment and prevent packets from
reaching their destination.
This thesis will implement different types of jamming attacks using a hardware
device called Software Defined Radio, and observe how low power sensor devices of
a WSN utilizing TSCH react by monitoring their power consumption and packet
delivery ratio.

1 Introduction
1.1.1
Objectives
To define the objectives it is important to do background research of the domain to
understand concepts, technologies, and methods already existing. These include, the
sensor devices and their communication standard in WSN, jamming concepts, and
software defined radio concepts.
A crucial goal of the thesis is to understand if jamming attacks affect sensor de-
vices in terms of energy dispensation. In particular, if these sensor devices that use
a particular form of channel hopping technique created exclusively to mitigate inter-
ference are altered.
To simulate intentional interference, jamming techniques will be implemented us-
ing the SDR and and the jammer which affects the sensor devices will be chosen as
the most performant.
The thesis goals have been divided into smaller milestones to achieve the desired
results:
1. Research on Jamming techniques in WSN and jamming using Software Defined
Radios
2. Implementation of the software in GNU Radio for Signal Processing for Software
Defined Radios
3. Implementation of the jamming attacks in a controlled environment
4. Analysis of the results and discussion
1.1.2
Contribution
While conducting the literature review, it has been noted that it is particularly dif-
ficult to find research in jamming attacks conducted using physical devices. Rather
the preferred way of obtaining results is through simulation. Few works analyze and
implement real scenarios in the presence of attackers in the physical layer using the
standard IEEE 802.15.4. Furthermore, to the best of the authors knowledge, jam-
ming attacks have not yet been applied to TSCH networks using a Software Defined
Radio to implement the attacks.
This thesis aims at providing additional contributions to the work conducted so
far by the community by providing real results and important metrics such as PDR
and power consumption, which is arguably one of the most important metrics for
low power low data networks. The results obtained from this attack add value in
understanding TSCH and how it behaves in interference environments using hard-
ware devices. Although channel hopping mitigates interference, limited research has
been studied under the perspective of intentional interference which can deviate from
normal high interference scenarios.
Furthermore, the approach to utilize a Software Defined Radio enables to eas-
ily share SDR-hardware-independent schematics of the jamming block to another

Background
Widely adopted scenarios in Internet Of Things (IoT) applications are in the Wireless
Personal Area Network (WPAN). The Institute of Electrical Electronics Engineers
Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) supports and maintains dif-
ferent working groups that work to create and write wireless and wired communication
standards. For example, the 802.15 group of standards, specifies a variety of WPANs
for different applications.
Applications of Wireless Sensor Networks are endless. Any application scenario
where a collection of sensor devices are deployed to collect sensory information (tem-
perature, wind, humidity) of an environment. Applications include environmental
monitoring, agriculture, industrial applications, healthcare, smart cities, transporta-
tion and many more. [11].
1.3.1
Security
Once sensor devices are deployed, they are often expected to have a long lifetime and
forgotten in the environment. Nevertheless, if security flaws in the firmware of the
sensor nodes are found and sensor nodes need a firmware update, it can be diﬀicult
to update each sensor nodes singularly. Secure over-the-air (OTA) can be a solution
to update firmware for large-scale wireless sensor networks. Although, most often
these sensor devices need to have a shared key which can add overheard to energy
consumption of the devices. [12]
The 802.15.4 is a proposed standard known for its simplicity and concern with low
data rate and low power communication for WPANs. The 802.15.4 architecture con-
sists of only two layers: Physical Layer (PHY) and Medium Access Control (MAC).
It is often used by manufacturers because it enables development of customized pro-
tocols by allowing to develop the upper layers of the Open Systems Interconnection
Model (OSI) model. Examples include Zigbee, IPv6 over Low-Power Wireless Per-
sonal Area Network (6LowPAN), ISA100.11a, WirelssHART.
The following sections will describe the PHY and MAC layers of the 802.15.4
standard in detail, starting by its history and introduction of channel hopping in the
IEEE 802.15.4e revision, how the frequency bandwidth is divided, TSCH, Routing
Protocols in WSN, a selection of jamming techniques and finally a brief description
of Software Defined Radio Software Defined Radio (SDR).

1.4.1
PHY Layer
The physical layer was created to be easily compatible with other wireless networks
standards like 802.11 and 802.15.1 (Bluetooth) particularly because they transmit at
the same band. Devices can operate at the 2.4GHz and 868/915MHz frequency bands.
In the 2.4GHz band, a total of 16 channels can be used. Channels are numbered from
11 to 26 and each has a bandwidth of 2MHz with 5Mhz distance between each other.
Figure 1.3. Channels in the 2.4GHz band for 802.15.4 are divided into 16 channels starting
from channel 11 up to channel 26 [1]
As previously explained, The PHY layer handles the activation and deactivation of
the radio transceiver and executes instructions from the MAC sub-layer to transmit,
receive, sleep. Furthermore, the PHY layer also performs some measurements and
estimations on packets and channels. Most importantly, the PHY layer provides a
function to perform Clear Channel Assessment (CCA). This function allows to see
if the channel is busy by performing Energy Detection (ED) of the received signal
power within the bandwidth and Carrier Sensing which reports a busy medium if it
detects a signal with the 802.15.4 characteristics (modulation, direct sequence spread
spectrum).

1.4.2
MAC Layer
As explained in section 1.3.2 the MAC layer is contained in the DataLink Layer
and provides support to the PHY layer to send packets along the physical medium.
The MAC layer is involved also in everything to do with nodes joining and leaving
a network and providing informational packets to keep synchronized and guarantee
time slots communication. It also has some additional functions for security purposes.
1.4.2.1
Device Classes
The 802.15.4 standard defines two types of devices: reduced function devices (RFDs)
and full function devices (FFD). As the name implies, the RFD are devices with
reduced functions of the MAC layer and capabilities. They are able to only join an
existing network because they do not need to route traﬀic which allows them to sleep
and reduce communication to reduce power consumption.
FFDs, on the other hand, have the full MAC layer functions and can act as a
single coordinator called Personal Area network (PAN) coordinator which sets up
and manages the network or a general coordinator of a subgroup of RFD nodes. The
coordinator of the network is in charge of nodes wanting to join the network and is
also responsible of assigning an address of the node that joins the network.
Fig. 1.3 shows different typologies that can be formed at the network layer depending
on the two types of nodes used: Star, Cluster-Tree, Peer-to-Peer (Mesh).
In the Star Network topology the FFD is at the center acting as the PAN co-
ordinator, while in the other two typologies, FFD (in blue) act as coordinators of
subgroups of RFD which never communicate between each other since these are end
devices commonly a sensor or switch, but always through an FFD node. Typically,
star topologies are chosen for limited range and small networks which allows for bet-
ter energy eﬀiciency, although the PAN coordinator may be mains powered due it
having to handle more traﬀic, while the connected sensor devices battery powered.
In Peer-to-Peer topologies, there still exists a PAN coordinator, but it is a decen-
tralized topology. Devices can communicate between each other freely in their range
and take different routes to reach parts of the network, albeit increasing complexity
to maintain the network.
In the tree topology, which is a specific type of peer-to-peer topology, every FFD
that joins the network still adds the PAN coordinator as parent. The main difference
between the peer-to-peer topology is FFD cannot communicate between each. This
leads to clusters forming in large networks and no loops forming. For example, in
Fig.1.6 an example of a large multicluster network can be observed. The starting
coordinator can ask another FFD to become coordinator of a cluster.

Beacon-Enabled Mode
In Beacon-Enabled Mode beacon frames are sent periodically to enable synchroniza-

If it is a coordinator sending out frames to
synchronize with sensor devices this is defined as a beacon-enabled PAN. The coor-

Contention Based. On the other hand, if devices require a guaranteed bandwidth
(e.g. for applications that have low-latency) the coordinator can reserve slots which
are called Guaranteed Time Slots (GTSs) to a particular device (CFP). In 1.8 we
can see that channel access is performed by using superframes. The superframes is
divided into 16 slots, where the first one is reserved for the Beacon and the remaining
slots are reserved for CAP, CFP and GTS.

1.5
802.15.4e
In the 802.15.4e [13] extends the previous standard and enhances and extends the
functionalities of the 802.15.4 standard. Firstly, the IEEE 802.15.4e standard intro-
duces five new mac behaviours: Time-Slotted Channel Hopping (TSCH); Determin-
istic and Synchronous Multi-Channel Extension (DSME); Low Latency Determinis-
tic Network (LLDN); Radio Frequency Identification Blink (BLINK); Asynchronous
Multi-Channel Adaptation (AMCA). A very notable difference is the utilization of
multiple channels in respect to the previous 802.15.4-2011 which supported only one
channel.
With the introduction of DSME and TSCH, nodes have the possibility
to access the channel through channel hopping or channel adaptation mechanisms.
LLDN offers very low latency thanks to the introduction of a Low Energy mecha-
nism called Coordinated Sampled Listening (CSL) and is designed for single-hop and
single-channel networks, while BLINK allows nodes to communicate their ID to each
other (useful for tracking, location).
To conclude, AMCA relies on asynchronous multi-channel adaptation and is al-
lowed only in non-beacon mode [14]. Only the TSCH mode will be analyzed in the
following sections.
1.5.1
TSCH
TSCH is a synchronous standardized MAC protocol which particular properties that
make it an ideal candidate for deployment in industrial setting scenarios, particularly
in process automation applications with a particular focus on equipment and process
monitoring [14].
TSCH allows nodes of the network to conserve their energy by
switching on and off and communicate between each other at particular points in
time. It also has a very high reliability as shown in [15] compared to an asnychronous
standardized MAC protocol such as coordinated sampled listening (CSL) at a cost
of a higher latency.
The high reliability and channel hopping properties mitigate
interference and multipath fading (signal bouncing off of physical objects) providing
real-time transmissions in harsh environments [16] and increased robustness of the
network.
Overall, TSCH manages to maintain a low energy eﬀiciency thanks to its low duty
cycle and provide an increased network capacity due to more nodes now being able
to communicate at different channel frequencies and in the same timeslot.
1.5.1.1
Characteristics of TSCH
TSCH divides time into timeslot amd a group of timeslots is called a slotframe. TSCH
does not use the superframe structure used in DSME and LLDN for beacon-enabled
mode but instead adopts slotframes.
During a timeslot a node in the network can be in three states: transmit a frame,
receive a frame or sleep to save energy.

1.6
Routing over Low Power and Lossy Networks (RPL)
Routing over Low Power and Lossy Networks (RPL) is a standard routing protocol
for wireless networks with low power consumption.
It is built on top of 802.15.4
and nodes update the network by routing messages containing various information
between each other periodically (distance vector routing). In particular, the routing
algorithm creates a directed acyclic graph from the root node. The other nodes of
the graph calculate their position in the group from the root. The Graph is thus
called Destination Oriented Directed Acyclic Graph (DODAG). Performance of RPL
has been studied extensively in the literature and many advantages, disadvantages,
improvements have been made. Advantages include: the use of a trickle timer [18] to
decrease energy eﬀiciency by reducing control messages; fast network setup [19]; good
scalability [19]. On the other hand, RPL can introduce network negative performance
due to load imbalance [20]. Load imbalance introduces problems such as bottle neck
energy hole, thundering herd, early node death and poor network performance [21].
Furthermore load imbalance can cause frequent parent change. Arguably, each node
of the network has an objective function that instructs on the preferred parent based
on certain metrics, but not a lot of load balancing objective functions exist.

1.7
Jamming
Jamming prevents devices from communicating between each other via radio.
It
can take place unintentionally as form of interference like noise or collisions or it
can be intentional. Most of the time, jamming is referred to as intentional malevolent
behaviour to degrade the performance of a network and prevent devices from receiving
signals. This interference is caused by jammers, devices which intentionally introduce
interference in the environment.
Jamming can happen at different layers of the OSI model such as the PHY or MAC
layer. At the PHY layer a high transmission power signal is sent into the environment
medium, while at the MAC layer, a jammer can occupy the whole frequency band
making it look very busy preventing devices to access the channel.
Arguably, as we will see in the coming sections [s. 1.7.1.2 1.7.1.3] jamming at this
layer is more energy-eﬀicient against devices, due to jammers being more aware of
the MAC protocol and being able to adjust accordingly.
1.7.1
Jamming Techniques
There are many types of jamming present in the literature. A useful way to categorize
jamming techniques is how authors did in [5] shown in [s. 1.13]. The authors divided
jammers into two types: elementary and advanced. In the prospect of the jamming
attacks conducted in this thesis, only the constant, random and channel-hopping
attacks will be explained further.
1.7.1.1
Constant Jamming
As the name suggests, constant jamming is the act of continously emitting random
bits in the air to simulate the wireless channels to be busy. In CSMA the devices will
have to perform channel sensing before transmitting so this type of jamming will not
allow devices to transmit because of them seeing the channel as busy. This type of
jammer is easily detected and very energy ineﬀicient.
1.7.1.2
Random Jammer
A random jammer can be very stealthy as it can be very diﬀicult to differentiate
a random jammer from normal interference.
Furthermore, the jamming won’t be
continous and will switch off when not performing the attack to save energy and
avoid detection.
1.7.1.3
Reactive Jammer
Reactive jammers are particularly interesting and extremely stealthy due to them
activating only when sensing that the victim device is transmitting. In particular, a

Firstly, different types of jamming attacks are investigated in the general radio

Subsequently, a brief description of papers regarding WSN and 802.15.4 is made to

2.1
Jamming in the Radio World
In cognitive radio networks, channel hopping designs have been proposed to combat
jamming and several proposed mitigation methods have been introduced. For exam-
ple, in [22] propose an anti-jamming channel hopping algorithm, Tri-CH for Cognitive
Radio Networks. Tri-CH uses a similar idea of improving the determination of the
TSCH schedule by generating random channel patterns, and having a a fixed channel
for receiving packets only. In [23] authors present a simulation of a multi-channel
802.11 jammer and examine the impact under different radio setings. In the experi-
ment, it is hypothesized that the attacker with the use of a cognitive radio, can use
advanced channel sensing and fast channel switching to perform the attack. A similar
approach to channel switching has been implemented in chapter 4, 5 and future work
can include advanced sensing also for 802.15.4 networks.

2.2.1
Dealing with interference
In general and traditional settings, PHY layer implementations such as Direct Se-
quence Spread Spectrum (DSSS) can be used to combat interference, noise and jam-
ming. These PHY layer technologies are often costly, and transceivers used in WSN
do not have the capacity to spread the frequency enough to avoid noise or jamming.
Alternatively, sensor nodes using the 802.15.4 standard perform carrier sensing
for medium access [s.1.4.2] and a very common approach in WSN is to mitigate
interference through Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS).
For example a popular technique called channel surfing switches channel once
interference is detected (e.g. a jammed node can change to another orthogonal chan-
nel to mitigate interference completely). In [28] Xu et al. explore two approaches
to channels surfing: coordinated channel switching, where the whole wireless sensor
network switches channel frequency; and spectral multiplexing, where only the nodes
in the jamming region switch channel.
In TSCH networks, authors in [29] compare advantages of several adaptive channel
selection based on RSSI or PRR and conclude that the PRR based channel quality
assessment improves reliability the most. Another interesting algorithm addressed
in [30] introduces the concept of blacklists, transmitter and receivers share their
”blacklist” of chanels based on an estimation of their relative PDR metrics.
2.3
Jamming attacks and mitigations in WSN
A taxonomy of DoS attacks in WSN is described by Wood et al in [31], and jamming
is identified as a subset of DoS attacks. At the PHY layer, jamming, node tampering
and droplet attacks are. Droplet is slightly different from jamming, where the goal
is consume a receiver’s bandwidth by not enabling it to not go to sleep by sending
short data frames to enable header synchronization and fram decoding. A synonym
jamming term is also used for MAC layer jamming attacks. As a survey of intelligent
MAC layer jamming attacks describes [32] the main difference is the increased level of
attack sophistication due to the jammer having the possibility to interpret data link
layer protocols. Examples include, power exhaustion, collision attacks, and denial of
sleep.

the 802.15.4 standard by introducing a MAC protocol called DEEJAM and analyze
it against progressively more complex jamming attacks. The solution proposed is

Regarding denial of sleep attacks to consume energy an example is described
in [34]. Authors observe that in the asynchronous MAC protocol: Coordinated Sam-
ple Listening (CSL), has no built-in denial-of-sleep defenses. Nodes wake up after

Other jamming mitigations are based on the concept of proactive or reactive sen-
sor networks. Proactive approaches involve sensors to proactively send periodic report
on their status or other solutions involve status of their neighbours. If they do not
reply in time, it can be derived an adversary is performing an attack. This solution
is very energy consuming and can be prone to false alarms. Reactive mitigations, can
be more energy eﬀicient due to communicating an adversary attack only if detected.
Authors in [35] propose very interesting simulation mitigations and mathematical
probabilities of how sensor nodes can ”wormhole” their way out of a jammed region.
A standard is not mentioned, but mitigations include introducing in a fixed channel
sensor network, wired sensor devices or sensor devices that use frequency hopping.
Equally as important is also having a method to locate the jammer once it has been
established it is in use, and several jamming localization algorithms have been pro-
posed [36].
2.3.1
Selective Jamming Attacks
As we have seen, several approaches to deal with interference exist, a proposed mit-
igation is based on channel switching according to sub-optimal data link metrics.
However, in case of intelligent attacks these metrics can be misleading in case of
stealthy type of attacks such as selective jamming.
This type of attack is hard to detect due to the attacker selectively choosing which
packet to jam. An attacker can decide to corrupt the packet which will still reach the
destination, but unable to be read by the receiver. The receiver node can attribute
this corruption to interference and not switch the channel due to the correct reception
of the packet. The aim of the attacker in this case is not to ruin the reliability of the
network, but force devices to communicate more than strictly necessary and consume
energy.
Wireless Sensor Network that utilizes a central coordinator node can utilize a
time-slotted form of communication called Guaranteed Time Slot (GTS) [section
1.4.4] which is vulnerable to selective jamming attacks as identified [37]. In the pa-
per, Sokullu et al.go on to explain there are two types of attacks the adversary can
perform: random or intelligent attack. The latter performs the attack on knowing
when the allocated longest slot will be. [38] Tiloca et al. propose a selective jamming
resistant GTS solution and evaluate its implementation in Tmote Sky motes.
Xia et al. [39] focused on devising a stealthy smart selective jamming attack on the

2.3.2
Jamming in TSCH
Although, performance of TSCH under interference can be found only a few authors
have applied jamming attacks in TSCH networks.
As discussed sophisticated jamming attacks in TSCH can be performed due to
the periodic channel hopping sequence that can be derived. Authors in [41] prove
it is possible to reverse engineer the channel hopping sequence by eavesdropping the
environment but only suggest this can be useful to conduct smart selective jamming
attacks.
Tiloca et al. are major contributors to jamming in TSCH and different proposals
regarding selective jamming attacks are proposed. [42]. Furthermore, they have pro-
posed DISH - Distributed SHuffling Against Selective Jamming atacks where the idea
is to change communication pattern of every node at every slot frame. The describe
it is implemented in Contiki OS in Telos Motes but do not provide information on
how the selective jamming was performed. [43]
R-TISCH [44], similarly to Tri-CH [s.2.1] tries to create pseudorandom channel
sequences which cannot be derived from an eavesdropping attacker. One main dif-
ference between Tri-CH does not use any pre-shared secret keys. Authors, propose
an algorithm which is independent from the type key-exchange mechanism chosen:
pre-deployed or dynamically generated and evaluate it in a simulation. It can be ar-
gued, that in open environments, an attacker could have physical access to the sensor
device and derive the key.

3.9 Threat Modelling considerations

3.9
Threat Modelling considerations
A brief Threat Modelling must be made, as the feasibility of the attack is substan-
tially dependent on many assumptions of prior knowledge of how the Wireless Sensor
Network functions and environment the jammer is in.
3.9.1
Assumptions
It is assumed that the attacker is capable in multi-channel communications to not be
able to jam all channels at the same time and that the channel sequence of TSCH is
known as described in [41].
The placement of the jammer is crucial to perform a successful jam. The location
of the jammer is dependent on the intentions of the malicious actor. If their intention
is to jam a large area of sensor nodes, the placement might be different if it is more
important to go unnoticed for a long time. Strategies for the optimal placement of
the jamming are highly increased if the attacker has knowledge about the network
and placement of its nodes.
Obviously, if network information is known and an attacker has access to the lo-
cation site and wants to perform a limited-range jamming attack which is diﬀicult to
detect, a strategic location would be to place the jammer close to nodes that act as
coordinator nodes or have high traﬀic flow (in/out).
We must also assume what is the goal of the adversary. If the intention is to re-
main unnoticed or not. Jamming all 16 channels is very easily detected, so we assume
the attacker wants to remain stealthy and avoid detection by launching attacks which
are similar to interference and focus on increasing energy-consumption by the sensor
nodes.

4.2.3
Discussion
From the power consumption tables derived, we observe that the most effective jam-
ming attack between the two is the constant jamming. The PDR rate drops down
to 0 making transmission beween devices impossible. The activation of the jammer
in single channel communication increases power consumption of the client device
to about 7.58 mW and 6.578 mW for the server. This is quite a significant result
since a lifespan with a CR2032 coin cell battery that has about 250mAh (which not
all energy can be used) the CC2650 can last about 16h in high performance mode
(which consumes 6.17 mA).
In the multichannel communication. the multichannel hopping jamming affects the
communication marginally. Just an increase in total average power consumption by
1.31mW in the client and 0.12 mW for the server. It is not surprising to see a mi-
nor effect of the multichannel hopping onto the multichannel communication. The
selected channel by the jammer must be the same which is selected in the moment of
the communication of the devices.
As we have seen from Fig. 4.5 the association phase is the most power intensive
for the client. Investigation into desynchronizing attacks is an interesting option to
consider in future work.
We have found out that the SDR is not a reliable source in the current setup due
to it having such a difference in PDR even in baseline configurations. Further investi-
gation is needed to understand this difference in PDR due to it to be very beneficial
to have a cognitive radio placed near the Sink of the PAN coordinator, which is an
area of high traﬀic inside the network.
A consideration regarding the packet delivery ratio is that it can be argued it
does not indicate specifically if the decrease in PDR is due to normal interference,
congestion or jamming.

4.2.4
Challenges and limitations
In regards to the project there have been significant roadblocks to enable the devices
and software to work together. The major problem lied in the outdated bladeRF
x40 which did not integrate well with the new major changes of GNU Radio [57].
The FPGA of the BladeRF was downgraded to an older version to solve the issue.
Additional challenges included also the installation of the out of tree gr-802-15-4
module in the new version 3.9 of GNU-Radio.
There were some time cost factors in installing GNU Radio software from source
which took around +4h, added to this that multiple installations had to be redone
because of integration issues. Initially, GNU Radio was also tried to be installed on
Windows, but due to requiring to build code from source Linux is recommended.
Setting up environment and preparing log management is long and tedious work
even for just two sensor devices, a jamming device, and a sniffer. To acquire the
Energest logs, resetting of the device was done to restart the Energest period at 0.
This process required waiting also for the client to join the network, which sometimes
took several minutes. Furthermore, connecting to the serial devices was not always
straightforward.
Interference environment can also change from day to day as Fig. 4.9 shows. This
change in interference can affect results and that is why is necessary a repoducible
testbed is necessary when experimenting with interference. A solution that can be
used that provides a low cost infrastructure to replicate interference patterns onto
sensor devices [40].
Finally, jamming in real world scenarios is severely limited due to range and power
needed to perform the attack.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
and Future Work
The goal of the thesis was to investigate jamming attacks in industrial IoT wireless sen-
sor networks. Jamming attacks have been studied extensively in WSN, but because
of the tediousness of the hardware implementations, they are often not researched
and simulation based approaches are prevalent. Hardware implementation is vital,
due to very nature of the deployment of sensor devices in real world environments.
The chosen mode of operation in the 802.15.4 standard presents many features to
combat interference by nature in its implementation. Nevertheless, the performance
of the network also depends on the TSCH implementation. If real-time adaptation
of channels is not dependent on link quality metrics, TSCH will continue to have
it’s statically set channel hopping sequence since the initial deployment of the sensor
devices in the field.
indent This predetermined channel hopping sequence has many advantages and dis-
advantages. As any radio device, sensor nodes are particularly vulnerable to jamming
attacks and TSCH is no exception to that. As observed in the literature review several
mitigations address this problem and solutions vary from case to case.
Once deployed into an environment, they are usually expected to have a long
lifetime, due to changing their battery can be very costly.
Collective results, have demonstrated sensor nodes to be affected by jamming at-
tacks under performance and energy consumption metrics.
Future work can address more specific metrics in relation to the jammer. For
example, regarding changing the jammer’s power level to create different signal to
interference and noise ratios (SINR). Further, the placement of the jammer can be in-
vestigated by analyzing Received Signal Strength Indication (RSSI). More advanced
jamming attacks can also be considered such as Reactive or Selective jamming. Partic-
ularly towards the weakness of the predetermined TSCH schedule, selective jamming
should be the next step to consider.
Overall, jamming attacks have notable range limitations which hinder the implemen-
tation in real world scenarios.
