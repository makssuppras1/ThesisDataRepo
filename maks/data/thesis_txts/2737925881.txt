Abstract
The continued increase of CO2-emissions into the atmosphere is expected with fossil fuels as the
main source for energy. Carbon capture technology oﬀers a promising solution by directly capturing
CO2 from the emission source. One common method involves using an absorber/desorber process.
The reaction kinetics of both aqueous and non-aqueous liquid solvents were determined by mea-
surement of absorption of CO2 into the solvents. Experiments were conducted using four solvent
compositions: 30 wt% MEA in water, 35 wt% CESAR-1 in water, CESAR-1 in 15 wt% methanol
(MeOH), and CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH. The absorption measurements were carried out using a
wetted wall column (WWC) set-up operated at 20, 40 and 60 °C.
Due to limited literature on the physical properties of CESAR-1 solvents in methanol, assumptions
were made regarding their density and viscosity.
These assumptions were based on the proper-
ties of aqueous CESAR-1 containing 2-Amino-2-methyl-1-propanol (AMP) and Piperazine (PZ) at
23.10/6.88 wt%. A sensitivity analysis highlighted signiﬁcant variations in results based on diﬀerent
density and viscosity assumptions, emphasising the need for accurate physical parameter estimation.
The experimental run with 30 wt% MEA solvent served as a validation for the experimental and
calculation procedures. While the graphical results aligned with expectations, the numerical results
diﬀered from other similar works. Despite this, the consistency of calculated values across parameters
suggested the experimental and calculation methods were reliable.
The remaining results indicated that CESAR-1 in 15 wt% MeOH had the highest rate constants
with k2-values of 6.36, 10.95, and 20.83 m3/mol·s at 20, 40, and 60 °C. The addition of methanol was
observed to improve the absorption of CO2 into the liquid solvent to an extent; however increasing
the methanol to 30 wt% generally worsened the rate constants, with k2-values of 4.65, 17.14, and
11.44 m3/mol·s at 20, 40, and 60 °C. These values were comparable to the aqueous 35 wt% CESAR-1
solvent, which was unexpected and attributed to MeOH volatility.
Overall, the results of this work showed that adding methanol to aqueous CESAR-1 solvents can
enhance CO2 absorption rates. The limited understanding of the interactions between methanol,
CO2 and amine in solution make it necessary to conduct more research to fully understand how
alcohol additions to amine-based solvents can improve CO2 absorption. Nonetheless, the ﬁndings
indicate the potential for incorporating alcohols in amine-based liquid solvents.

Problem Statement
Carbon capture technology, which selectively removes CO2 from various emission sources, is gaining
traction. As this technology advances, there is a growing need to optimize its processes to enhance
eﬃciency, save costs, and reduce overall energy consumption.
However, the solvents currently used for carbon capture present several challenges. These include
high energy demands, the risk of thermal degradation, and the potential to corrode process equip-
ment. With this, the desire for alternate solvent formulations which may address some of these
issues is increasing.
The objective of this project is to determine the reaction kinetics of CO2 absorption into innovative
solvents, speciﬁcally blended amines mixed with alcohol. The addition of alcohol is said to enhance
the reaction between CO2 and amines, potentially improving the overall absorption process. This
study aims to demonstrate that incorporating alcohol can signiﬁcantly improve the kinetics of CO2
absorption, contributing continued research in this area.
Learning Objectives:
1. Obtain and review relevant literature regarding carbon capture, solvents used for that as well
as their reaction kinetics
2. Formulate an experimental plan for testing new solvents
3. Execute experiments using wetted wall column apparatus with innovative solvent mixtures
4. Evaluate the kinetics of solvents related to mass transfer coeﬃcients and enhancement factor
5. Analyse, summarise and discuss the obtained experimental results
6. Discuss and consider the eﬀects of new solvents and any implications these may have on a
carbon capture process
7. Disseminate the results in a report and oral presentation

Methodology
The methods applied to meet the objectives given in the problem statement are stated in this section.
The total duration of this masters thesis was 5.5 months.
The master thesis was initiated in January 2024 with the ﬁrst month of the project used conduct
a literature review. This consisted of research on revelant carbon capture technologies, properties
of solvents used for carbon capture (primarily amines), reaction mechanisms and kinetics of these.
Additionally, the theoretical background for the the two-ﬁlm model for gas-liquid interactions, mass
transfer and process engineering regarding chemical substances and wetted wall column was studied
based on works by V.G. Danckwerts, O. Levenspiel and the Ph.D thesis of Arne Gladis.
The
literature search was done primarily using "Research Gate", "DTU Findit" and "Google Scholar"
to methodically obtain relevant material.
The second and third month (February - April 2024) were spent getting familiar with the experi-
mental set-up, understanding the procedures and executing the experiments. Before any experiment
could be initiated, a chemical risk assessment was submitted outlining the safety precautions in-
volved with the speciﬁc chemicals. The solvents with alcohol were initially tested for their solubility
to ensure that no precipitate would occur that could damage the the apparatus. The experiments
in the wetted wall column consisted of measuring the change in CO2 content in gas while running
a given solvent through the column. This was done for four solvent compositions: 30 wt% MEA in
water, 35 wt% CESAR-1 in water, CESAR-1 in 15 wt% MeOH and CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH.
The experiments were done by guidance of an SOP with initial supervision by Meng Shi and Randi
Neerup. The experimental procedures and methods sections were also written in this time frame.
The fourth and ﬁfth month (April - June 2024) were used to conduct the data analysis, write the
ﬁnal sections of the report and draw conclusions from the results.
The data analysis was done
based on the experimental data combined with the obtained theoretical background on the gas-
liquid interactions, mass transfer and reaction kinetics. Values for the mass transfer coeﬃcients and
reaction rate constants were calculated and discussed. All calculations were done using Microsoft
Excel which have been attached separately.
Overleaf, the online LaTeX editor was used to write this master thesis which was submitted on the
24th June 2024 at CERE, Department of Chemical and Biochemical Engineering at DTU Denmark.

Introduction
The increasing global energy demand and continued dependence on fossil fuels has shown to have
a negative impact on the climate. This has resulted in the surge of greenhouse gas emissions, CO2
being the largest contributor. As of 2022, energy-related and industrial process emissions reached a
record of 36.8 Gt, the development from 1900-2022 seen in Figure 1.1 [1]. The main culprits causing
the increase being the transport and building industries.
Figure 1.1: Global CO2 emissions from energy combustion and industrial processes and their annual
change, 1900-2022 [1].
In recent years, is has become evident that a signiﬁcant global eﬀort is necessary to reduce these
emissions. Multiple technologies have been explored to tackle this issue and more recently, another
method to actively reduce emissions is gaining traction: carbon capture.
Carbon capture is an innovative approach for minimising the environmental consequences of indus-
trial processes that rely heavily on fossil fuels and/or biomass, this could be for the production of
cement or iron and steel. This method involves the capture of CO2 directly from the emission source.
It allows for the eﬀective reduction of greenhouse gases that are released into the atmosphere. The
captured CO2 can either be redirected to be utilised in other industries, like in food and beverage
production. Otherwise it can be compressed to be transported and stored at a geological site - often
nestled within depleted gas or oil reservoirs [2].
The appeal of carbon capture is not only limited to the reduction of atmospheric CO2 concentra-
tions. There are additional advantages to using carbon capture technology in industry. A major
one being that, the use of carbon capture technologies allows for the possibility of integration into
existing production facilities. Additionally, it does not necessarily require any structural changes to
the existing production facility; allowing for the capture process to be a direct extension of the point
source [3].

2.1
Carbon Capture Processes
The following processes focus on the carbon capture that has been seen in power generation plants.
This has, until recently, been the primary application for carbon capture technology. Although,
now, more industrial applications are exploring the use of carbon capture to reduce CO2 emissions.
This will be discussed in further detail later on.
2.1.a
Pre-combustion
In a pre-combustion capturing process, the CO2 is separated before the fuel has been burned to
completion. The fuel undergoes a gasiﬁcation process, producing a synthetic gas (syngas). Following
the formation of syngas is a water-gas shift reaction, producing a mixture of H2 and CO2-rich gases.
Figure 2.1 shows a depiction of the pre-combustion process. This occurs under high temperature and
pressure conditions. This results in a high CO2 concentration, making the pre-combustion capturing
process highly eﬃcient, although it has a high capital cost [4].
Figure 2.1: Pre-Combustion Carbon Capture Process [5].
2.1.b
Post-combustion
During the post-combustion capturing process, the fuel is completely burned and CO2 capture
occurs afterwards.
The resulting ﬂue gas consists of a mixture of CO2, N2 as well as SOx and
NOx compositions. The gas mixture is treated to remove any contaminants and the SOx and NOx
compounds. Thereafter, it is necessary to separate the CO2 and N2 which is the major challenge
within this process. The challenge arises as there is a relatively low concentration of CO2 in the
gas stream (⇠16%) compared to a high concentration of N2 (⇠73 −77%). Additionally, the low

Amongst the industrial sector, heavy production of cement, iron and steel and petrochemicals are

increase the CO2 tax in order to justify the costs of a carbon capture and storage unit [11].

coming years to encourage industries to reduce emissions. As an example, the Danish coalition
government is introducing a corporate carbon tax from 2025, that will progressively increase until

players to introduce carbon capture technologies to reduce emissions and come one step closer to
meeting the climate goals for 2050.
Second, the public opinion is considered. There is an increased awareness of the negative impacts of
greenhouse gas emissions amongst potential collaborators and/or consumers. With this, corporations
are being held accountable for their actions to a higher degree. As a company, being able to state
that active eﬀorts are being made to reduce emissions with quantiﬁable goals is a great way to
attract positive public relations.
It has been established that there is a growing interest in the use of carbon capture as a technology to
reduce CO2 emissions, some even arguing that it will be a necessity in order to meet the 2050 goals.
This speciﬁcally regards the use of carbon capture in a wide variety of industries with high CO2
output. The largest drawback preventing the expansion of carbon capture technology in industrial
processes is the associated cost. With the likely increase in emission taxes, the use of carbon capture
could become cost eﬀective in the future.

2.2.a
Adsorption
One technique that can be used for carbon capture is a ﬁxed bed adsorption column. With adsorp-
tion, the target gas molecules adhere onto the surface of a solid adsorbent material. Thereafter,
conditions can be altered so the CO2-rich adsorbent can be regenerated. Figure 2.6 shows an illus-
tration of the adsorption process.
Figure 2.6: Schematic of an adsorption process for CO2 capture [16].

With a ﬁxed bed adsorption column, the ﬂue gas mixture is injected directly from the bottom
moving upwards through a bed of the speciﬁed solid adsorbent material from seen on the left side
of the ﬁgure in blue.
This material should have a high aﬃnity for the target gas, CO2 in this
case.
Typically, materials such as activated carbon, metal organic frameworks (MOFs) or high
surface zeolites are used. Once the adsorbent is saturated with CO2, it is regenerated by a pressure
reduction or application of heat to the desorber, shown on the right side of the ﬁgure in orange.
With that, the pure CO2 is released and let out of the desorber [16] [17].
Using adsorption technology oﬀers some advantages but not without some challenges attached as
well. Generally, adsorption processes have lower energy requirements in the regeneration of the CO2-
rich adsorbent. Additionally, an adsorption process can be modiﬁed to cater to speciﬁc industrial
requirements with the added beneﬁt of tailored adsorbent materials to speciﬁc ﬂue gas compositions.
However, the challenges following the use of adsorption technology include the diﬃculty in selecting
an adsorbent material with optimal CO2 selectivity, capacity and stability whilst taking potential
impurities into account. This will typically require careful optimization which pose high costs for
development of these as well as a high capital cost for installment of the adsorption unit. Despite this,
research is being done to overcome these challenges in the hopes of improving the use of adsorption
technology for carbon capture [18].
2.2.b
Absorption
Another method used for carbon capture is the use of an absorption column coupled with a stripper.
The intent with this is to use the absorber to selectively absorb the target gas into a liquid solvent
and thereafter separate these in a stripper [18].
The gas mixture is fed at the bottom of the absorber while the liquid solvent (typically amine-based),
enters from the top of the absorber (shown in blue in Figure 2.7). The gas mixture containing CO2
and solvent come in contact in a countercurrent ﬂow. The solvent selectively absorbs the CO2 from
the gas mixture, forming a CO2-rich mixture. The CO2-rich mixture is led to stripper. Within
the stripper, the mixture is heated and the CO2 and solvent are separated (shown in orange in
Figure 2.7).
The released CO2 is collected and condensed, either to be utilised or stored.
The
remaining solvent is regenerated and recircurlated to the absorber through a reboiler system [18].
Using a cyclic absorption-desorption process allows for a continuous and eﬃcient capture of CO2
that can be implemented for a variety of gas mixture compositions. Although, the main challenge
with the use of this process is the high energy demand. This is especially considering the energy
used to maintain the conditions needed in the desorber and the energy needed for the regeneration
of the solvent. Additionally, the potential toxicity of the solvent as well as the high capital costs for
absorption- and desorption columns should also be mentioned [18].
When considering the use of an absorption-desorption process, it should be evaluated whether the
high energy consumption could counteract the beneﬁt of CO2 capture.

3.1
Wetted Wall Column (WWC)
To conduct measurements on various parameters relating to absorption processes, a Wetted Wall
Column (WWC) can be used. With this it may be possible to obtain measurements related to reac-
tion kinetics and mass transfer coeﬃcients of substances in the system. These parameters play an
important role in estimating the ideal equipment size for increased eﬃciency as well as maintaining
decent capital- and operational costs. The wetted wall column may be considered a small-scale ab-
sorber in which various absorbents can be studied to be used for carbon capture. These absorbents
could be amine-based or an inorganic compound. The absorbent chosen is essential in ensuring the
most eﬀective capture with the possibility to separate the captures CO2 with the same eﬃciency.
Figure 3.1 shows a scheme of the WWC apparatus to be used in experiments.
The WWC consists of three systems. One, being the ﬂow of gas (shown as orange ﬂow in Figure 3.1).
Two, the ﬂow of liquid solvent (shown as blue ﬂow in Figure 3.1) and third is the ﬂow of cooling
water (shown as black ﬂow in Figure 3.1). The gas stream is fed into the reaction chamber from the
bottom moving upward. The outlet gas is lead to a condenser and thereafter leaves the system.
The liquid solvent is fed from the top and trickles downward on the outer surface of the chamber as
a thin homogeneous ﬁlm. The outlet liquid is recirculated to a liquid tank, creating a closed loop
for the liquid solvent [22].

For commercialised post-combustion carbon capture, primary and secondary amines are the most

Here, R1 and R2 represent the radical groups that may be attached to the amine group and B

The main limiting factor for the reaction is the reactivity of the amine and subsequently, the zwit-

the reaction toward forming the intermediate and thereafter the carbamate [26].
The main hindrance following the use of primary and secondary amines is the energy penalty caused
by the regeneration step for the aqueous absorbent.
Mentioned earlier, primary and secondary
amines are well-suited as solvents because of the strong amine-carbon bonds that are formed. This
bond in turn makes it increasingly diﬃcult to separate the carbon dioxide in the desorption process,
resulting in higher energy demands for the solvent regeneration. Multiple studies estimated that
the reboiler alone occupies approximately 80% of the entire energy consumption for CO2 capture [25].
Tertiary amines
A potential substitute to the primary and secondary amines may be the use of tertiary or sterically
hindered amine compounds. This opportunity arises from studies which have shown them to be
more energy eﬃcient than their primary and secondary counterparts [27].
Using tertiary or sterically hindered amines will inﬂuence the reaction mechanism. The steric hin-
drance may aﬀect the reactivity and selectivity of the amine toward the carbon atom. Equation 2
shows the reactions that occur with the use of tertiary amines also accounting for the presence of
water in the system [28].
R1R2R3NH + CO2 + H2O −−*
)−−R1R2R3NH+ + HCO3−
(2a)
R1R2R3NH+ + HCO3−−−*
)−−R1R2R3NCOO−+ H2O
(2b)
Equation 2a shows the protonation of the amine by the carbon atom, forming a bicarbonate ion
(HCO3 –). The following reaction, seen in 2b, shows the deprotonation of the amine to form the
recognisable carbamate and water.
This mechanism can be described as a hindered nucleophilic attack. This hindrance favours the
formation of bicarbonate ions, as seen above. Additionally, the amine-carbon bond is weakened
resulting in slowed reaction kinetics. Although, this eases the separation in the desorber and min-
imises the energy demand in comparison [28] .

to increased operational expenses [27].
CESAR-1: A blended amine
Formulating a blended amine solution is also an option for a potential solvent. These consist of
a mixture of amines, seemingly taking the "best" properties for each sub-category and combining
them to formulate an optimal solvent with a high absorption rate and lower energy penalty. The
main beneﬁt of using a blended amine would be the possibility of tailoring the ratios to speciﬁed
processes, ideally providing the desired balance in reactivity, energy usage and stability [29]. An
example of a blended amine which could beneﬁt the CO2 capture process is a CESAR-1 solvent.
It is a blend of 2-amino-2-methyl-1-propanol (AMP) and piperazine (PZ). This blend combines the
high absorption and stability of AMP and high reactivity of PZ [30].
Using conventional amine absorbents presents a tedious regeneration process, being energy intensive
and requiring a high temperature. By using a CESAR-1 absorbent it may be possible to reduce
temperature and energy consumption whilst maintaining a high CO2 absorption eﬃciency [30].
Patil et al. did a ’Bench-Scale Study for CO2 Capture Using AMP/PZ/Water Mixtures’ in which
they experimented on the ’eﬃcacy of CO2 capture by aqueous AMP/PZ mixtures in a closed loop
absorber-desorber system.’.
Their results showed a CO2 removal eﬃciency at 85% and a 3.4%
reduction in energy requirements [30]. Additionally, a techno-economic assessment concluded that
using a CESAR-1 blended solution could reduce capital- and operational costs by 29%, respectively
when compared to using conventional amine solutions like MEA [31].
The largest setback to using a blended-amine solution would be the complexity of formulating and
optimising the absorbent. This could in turn limit the wide-spread applicability of the absorbent
solution. Although, the presented studies show promise in the future use of this absorbent type.
Multiple other studies using a CESAR-1 blend as an absorbent in varying solvents have been made,
all coming to similar conclusions; that the application of a CESAR-1 absorbent could improve
operations and reduce costs of the capturing process but it should be studied further to solidify its
potential commercial application [32, 33].
Some of the mentioned studies attempt to use the CESAR-1 absorbent dissolved in a non-aqueous
solvent. The purpose and added beneﬁt to doing this is presented in the following section.
Non-aqueous solvents
Until now, the mentioned absorbents have been assumed to be dissolved in water. This is because
amines are more easily soluble in water and water is widely available to use as a solute for industrial
applications. Having an aqeuous alkanolamine system results in issues regarding higher desorption

This can be assorted to the high heat capacity of water, increasing the overall energy requirements

A new proposal which preserves the high absorption eﬃciency of the amine absorbent whilst avoiding
the associated issues involves the use of a non-aqueous solvent. Speciﬁcally, using an organic alcohol
solvent as an alternative. Alcohols generally have lower heat capacity and enthalphy of vaporisation
compared to water so that the energy requirements and temperature/pressure conditions may be
reduced. This parameter makes alcohols an attractive alternative to water as a solvent [32].
Two studies testing an organic alcohol as a solvent for a CO2 capture process are presented here.
The ﬁrst one by Barzagli et al. tried to see whether eﬃcient CO2 capture could be achieved by
improving solvent formulations. They dissolved a CESAR-1 blend in ethylene glycol monoethyl
ether (EGMEE) containing approximately 15 wt% water. Their results showed that a lower stripper
temperature of 80-90 °C could be achieved at atmospheric pressure with an absorption eﬃciency at
84-96% [32].
The second study by Barbarossa et al. in collaboration with Barzagli did similar by dissolving AMP
in diﬀerent alcohols (1,2-ethandiol, ethanol and 1-propanol) and comparing their ability. Especially
AMP blended with ethylene glycol and either ethanol or propanol combined gave a "high eﬃciency
(>90%) with a relatively low regeneration temperature (80-90 °C)." [34]. Both studies showed that
dissolving an amine or amine blend in a non-aqueous solvent could be a promising alternative to
traditional aqueous amine solvents. Both studies did elaborate the need for more studies to be done
to support this.
A possible explanation as to why the use of non-aqueous solvents is not wide-spread could be the
solubility of amines in alcohol. Amines are less soluble in alcohol meaning that less CO2 may be
captured per unit volume of solvent compared to water [35]. Another explanation may be found in
the cost. The increased cost with the industrial application of an organic alcohol solvent should be
considered before implementing it into a capturing facility.
No study comparing the costs of introducing an organic solvent versus the saved capital and oper-
ational costs was found, although it would be an important calculation to evaluate the economic
feasibility.

gas is led directly to the WWC reaction chamber to interact with the liquid solvent or the gas is

there are three limiting factors. These are the availability of CO2, availability of solvent and the

limitation of reaction kinetics. Typically, an absorber will operate balancing the availability of CO2
and availability of solvent.
The mass transfer acts as a measure for the transferred amount of CO2 within a given area. Equa-
tion 47 shows how the overall mass transfer coeﬃcient, area and mole fraction can be used to
determine an amount of CO2 that can be transferred.
mCO2 = Kg · A · ∆ylm
(47)
From Equation 47, it can be seen that the the amount of CO2 depends on the overall mass transfer
coeﬃcient and area. This equation can be used to evaluate the values for Kg and A which would
maximise the amount of transferred CO2. This relation is often used in the design of an absorber.
The area and mass transfer coeﬃcient will determine the column size and absorption capacity. These
are the driving factors in determining the capital and operation costs.
The enhancement factor relates the physical and chemical driving forces and will inﬂuence the overall
mass transfer. Therefore, it is an important parameter in the design of an absorber. The values in
Table 22 indicate that the solvent is not reactive and the driving force for the transfer is primarily
physical. In that case, it is likely that the priority would be to maximise the contact area so that
the CO2 gas and liquid solvent can interact for as long as possible, enhancing the physical transfer
of CO2 gas into the liquid.
Although, there is also the limitation of reaction kinetics which may also have an inﬂuence. By
understanding the kinetics, conditions could be optimised to improve reaction rates. The MeOH
showed that the driving force could be shifted to be more chemically dependant, which could also
be of valuable information in the process design. By having eﬀective reaction kinetics, it would
be possible to maintain a higher absorption capacity while decreasing the contact area within the
absorber - hence, decreasing the cost.

From these results, the suggested assumptions regarding the physical properties of the solvent were
insuﬃcient. The values were not comparable to that of the bass case, with variations being quite
signiﬁcant. A better result may have come from using values with more similarities to the CESAR-
1 liquid solvent. Although, in the instance of a blended amine dissolved in alcohol, these values
were not easily available in literature. So, in any instance it would be necessary with some kind of
assumption(s). In this work, it was assumed there was no methanol in solution and that the blended
amine only contained AMP. It may be discussed whether this assumption is viable since this would
likely impact the results. Since the solvents primarily consisted of water, it was deemed acceptable.
Overall, the results showed how inaccurate assumptions for the physical parameters could have a
signiﬁcant impact on the results. This could potentially lead to inaccurate conclusions and thereof,
inaccurate recommendations for processing or design of equipment.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
In this thesis, kinetic measurements for CO2 absorption into non-aqueous CESAR-1 solvent was
studied. The objective was to unveil the potential beneﬁts to using alternative amine based solvents
for CO2 capture, with hopes of mitigating the issues associated with well-known amine-based aqueous
solvents. Through experimental runs using a wetted wall column (WWC) set-up, it was possible to
determine reaction kinetics for the absorption of CO2 into an aqueous 35 wt% CESAR-1 solvent, a
CESAR-1 in 15 wt% and 30 wt% methanol solvent. Prior to that, the experiment was done with
an aqueous 30 wt% MEA solvent to validate the experimental procedure as well as the calculations.
For the 30 wt% MEA solvent, the overall mass transfer was determined to be 6.272·10−6 mol/Pa·m2·s.
The gas-side mass transfer was found to be 7.67·10−6 mol/Pa·m2·s and the liquid-side mass transfer
being 3.44 · 10−5 mol/Pa·m2·s These values were compared those found by other students. The
values found varied signiﬁcantly and were not easily comparable. The values associated with this
work were all notably higher than those in the other works. Despite this, the experimental methods
and calculations yielded consistent results. The experimental and calculation procedures were used
for the analysis of the subsequent CESAR-1 solvents.
The same parameters were found for the aqueous 35 wt% CESAR-1, CESAR-1 in 15 wt% MeOH
and CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH. The lowest value for overall mass transfer coeﬃcient was found for
aqueous 35 wt% CESAR-1 at 9.32 · 10−7mol/Pa·m2·s. The highest value was found for the CESAR-
1 in 30 wt% MeOH at 1.96 · 10−6mol/Pa·m2·s.
The obtained results hinted that the addition
of MeOH impacted the mass transfer coeﬃcient. The overall tendency showed the overall mass
transfer increasing with temperature and MeOH concentration. Although, an exception to this was
observed. For CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH, the overall mass transfer for shown to decrease above 40
°C. It was discussed what could have caused this change. It was deduced that an amount of MeOH
would have evaporated from solution so that the solvent contained less MeOH than at 40 °C.
The gas- and liquid-side mass transfer coeﬃcients as well as the enhancement factor were also cal-
culated. The purpose for calculating these values was to determine the second order rate constants.
Additionally, it was possible to gain an understanding in the gas-liquid interactions occurring in the
absorption of CO2 into the solvent.
For all CESAR-1 solvent the values for the gas-side mass transfer showed to decrease with increasing
temperature. On the contrary, the liquid-side mass transfer was seen to increase with temperature
and MeOH concentration. In general, the values for the liquid-side were less than those for the
related gas-side. This meant that there was a tendency for a higher resistance to the transfer into
the liquid. The added MeOH was seen to decrease the resistance into the liquid. This would make
the transfer from the gas into the liquid easier. Although for the CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH, this
was not the case at 60 °C. The gas- and liquid-side mass transfer was shown to decrease compared
at 40 °C. Again, it was most likely due to the unintended evaporation of MeOH which lead to this
result.
To be able to determine the second order rate constant, it was assumed that the reaction was of
pseudo-ﬁrst order. With that assumption, the enhancement factor was said to be equal to the hatta

number. For all the liquid solvents used in the experiments, this was the case. With that it could be
concluded that the reactions were of pseudo ﬁrst order. As expected, the second order rate constant
increased with increasing temperature. The addition of MeOH also increased the rate constant. The
exception being for CESAR-1 in 30 wt% MeOH at 60 °C. The solvent with the fastest rate constant
was the CESAR-1 in 15wt% MeOH at 20.83 m3/mol·s at 60 °C.
The results from these experiments showed that the addition of MeOH to a blended-amine liquid
solvent improved the kinetics of the reactions. Although, increasing the MeOH content altered the
results as the MeOH volatility was said to compromise the solvent composition and in turn played
a larger part in the gas-liquid interaction.
Even with the useful insights from these results, the limitations to these methods should be con-
sidered. There was limited knowledge or literature available on the use of amine-based solvents in
an alcohol mixture for CO2 absorption. Due to this, it was necessary to make assumptions for the
physical properties of the liquid solvents containing methanol. The measurements and calculations
were done using these assumptions. Additionally, the results may be up for reconsideration as an un-
stable liquid ﬂow, occurrence of air bubbles in the WWC and/or inconsistent temperature/pressure
sensors were noted and could have skewed the measurements.

FUTURE PROSPECTS
9
Future Prospects
This section will brieﬂy introduce areas which could be relevant for future work within this topic.
These are presented as suggestions for the wetted wall column experiment and the liquid solvent.
Wetted wall column experimental set-up:
• For the experiments, there were multiple occasions in which the liquid ﬂow rate was unstable
and air bubbles would appear. For future experiments, the tubing may be expanded so that
more liquid can be run through the column. This would aid in ensuring a continuous stable
liquid ﬂow as well as avoiding air bubbles or ﬂuctuations in the measurements of the solvent.
• The experiments in this work only considered a CESAR-1 amine blend in methanol. It would
be of interest to do similar absorption studies using other types of amines (secondary or
tertiary) like DEA or MDEA in similar alcohol compositions to compare the results and make
any additional observations.
• For these experiments, no measurement of CO2-loading was done. It could be of interest to
conduct similar absorption studies considering the impact of CO2-loading. The results showed
that the possibility of CO2-loading would alter the absorption rate and following kinetics.
Additionally, these types of measurements would reﬂect an actual absorption process more
accurately.
Liquid solvent:
• For the solvent compositions including methanol, no physical properties were readily available.
It is recommended to conduct experiments to determine values for the physical properties of
the amine-based solvents in alcohol. The sensitivity analysis showed that making assumptions
regarding density and viscosity greatly inﬂuenced the outcome of the results.
• For the CESAR-1 solvent in 30 wt% MeOH, precipitation was observed during the experiments.
In this work, the precipitate was not identiﬁed, although it was discussed what the precipitate
may be.
Diﬀerent analyses should be done on the precipitate to identify the compounds
present. This would contribute in understanding how the the solvent reacts and identify the
inﬂuence on the capability of the solvent in an absorption process.
• The solvent compositions tested were limited to 15- and 30 wt% methanol. A suggestion could
be to formulate amine-based liquid solvents with diﬀerent weight percentages of methanol
between 15-30 wt% and/or above 30 wt% alcohol to investigate whether there is an ideal
fraction of alcohol which would beneﬁt the absorption process.
• The calculations done in this work assume the reaction to be pseudo-ﬁrst order. The role
of methanol in the reaction is neglected although the results indicate that the addition of
methanol impacted the kinetics. It would be of interest to work on a kinetic model which
considers the role of methanol in mixture with regards to the absorption of CO2 into liquid.
