Abstract 
Many water distribution agencies have been forced to apply various measures in order to address 
water scarcity including the implementation of Intermittent Water Supply (IWS) policies. IWS can 
be defined as piped water supply services delivering water to consumers for less than 24 hours per 
day. This service is used as a measure for reducing water consumption without, however, 
considering the effects on the condition and the performance of the water distribution networks 
(WDN). This research aims to investigate the vulnerability of WDN under IWS operations based 
on hydraulic and mathematical analyses. The case study refers to the WDN of Nicosia, Cyprus.  
The EPANET software was utilized for the hydraulic analysis. Several models of a specific district 
metered area (DMA 1) in Nicosia were constructed and the hydraulic performance of the network 
was analysed under continuous water supply (CWS) and IWS conditions and under three 
scenarios: fire breakage, contamination breakage and disinfection through chlorination. Moreover, 
a large database including leakage incidents and the volume of water channelled, consumed or lost 
in the Nicosia’s WDN during the period 2001-2017 was examined with statistical and survival 
analysis tools. The non-parametric Kaplan-Meier estimator was used for the survival analysis 
through the XLSTAT EXCEL package. 
The results of the hydraulic analysis showed that the normal daily operational cycle for which the 
WDN is designed for, is disrupted during IWS periods. Extreme variations of water pressure and 
flow were detected while the hydraulic conditions of the WDN including the water quality were 
degraded under fire and contamination breakages during the IWS period. Furthermore, it was 
found that the subdivision of a DMA to sub-DMAs as a measure of the IWS policy is detrimental 
for the network. The importance of applying demand patterns in such simulations is also 
highlighted in this paper. During high water demand periods the efficiency of the WDN was 
decreased.  
The negative effect of the IWS policy was confirmed by the statistical and survival analyses. The 
number of pipe leakage incidents was increased by 81% and 62% during the second year of the 
IWS period (2-year period) and the year followed, respectively. Water losses were also increased 
the years following the IWS period. In addition, the results showed that seasonality affects the 
distribution of pipe leakage incidents and water losses. Although, most of the incidents were 
related to house connections (HC), survival analysis showed that water mains (WM) had a lower 
survival probability during and before the IWS period. The failure rate of WM, however, dropped 
several years after that period and reached the corresponding rate of HC which was increased.  
This abnormal behaviour was attributed to a change of the operational policy of the local water 
board regarding the way pipe incidents were confronted.  
The comparison between the hydraulic and mathematical analysis results showed that the extreme 
changes of water pressure and flow were among the parameters which contribute to the 
deterioration of the WDN.  A correlation between the number of pipe incidents with the headloss 
and the maximum velocity was identified. Pipes with high water velocity and large pressure drop 
were more vulnerable to breakages.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
The impacts of climate change and global warming are increasing day by day, leading to additional 
strain on the availability of water resources in many countries. Meanwhile, the increasing water 
consumption due to population growth and the excessive urban development are creating an 
unbalanced situation between water demand and supply.    
Located in the southeast corner of Europe, Cyprus is faced with extended periods of water scarcity 
and short supply of fresh water. Therefore, water distribution agencies have developed and applied 
various measures in order to address water shortage while being able to maintain sufficient water 
in all the residential areas. Such a measure, was the introduction of Intermittent Water Supply 
(IWS) policies.  IWS can be defined as piped water supply services delivering water to consumers 
for less than 24 hours per day (The International Water Association, 2017). The intermittent water 
supply can be characterized by expected and unexpected interruptions, on a regular basis, of water 
delivery and by reducing pressure and filling/emptying the pipes aiming for a successive water 
supply between different parts of the water distribution network (WDN).  
This IWS service is mainly addressing the water scarcity issue by reducing water consumption 
without, however, considering the effects on the condition and the performance of the piping 
networks which were designed for a continuous water supply. Continuous operation systems are 
designed based on conditions with sufficient water resources and a fixed water demand thus when 
they operate as intermittent systems, inadequate and heterogeneous pressures are detected 
(Fontanazza, Freni, & La Loggia, 2007).  
1.1  
State of Knowledge 
Although a significant volume of research on WDNs has been undertaken so far, studies 
concerning the impact of IWS on a WDN’s behavior have been limited. Thus, the vulnerability of 
WDNs should be examined before, during and after the implementation of IWS through the use 
of statistical and hydraulic tools which can track pipe failures and water losses and which can 
simulate the hydraulic performance of the network. 
Water pipe failures can be considered as the most important cause of WDN water losses and 
financial damages, thus various mathematical models have been developed for the prediction of 
such failures. These models are categorized into physically and statistical based models. Physical 
- based models can be addressed as micro models since they investigate only the physical causes 
leading to pipe breakage while statistical models are characterized as macro models since they 
access long- term data in order to identify pipe break patterns (Kleiner & Rajani, 2001). Statistical 
models can be considered then more suitable for the prediction of pipe failures as they can evaluate 
and fit historical data to observed conditions and they are cost – effective (Kleiner & Rajani, 2001). 
Furthermore, statistical models can be multi-objective and multi-factored. 
Thomas (1981) used approximation strategies based on the geometric measures of size and shape 
to estimate the failure probability at the leakage and breakage incident. Other mathematical 
approaches use historical data of pipe breaks to forecast the frequency of failures if a pipe is not

replaced and to express the time between pipe installation and break with cost data consideration 
(Clark et al., 1982; Shamir & Howard, 1979). In a study carried out by Andreou et al. (1987), 
proportional hazard models were used in the prediction of pipe failures at an early stage while 
Poisson model was used for the later stages. Later on, a non-homogeneous Poisson distribution 
model was proposed by Goulter & Kazemi (1988)  which predicts the probability of subsequent 
breaks when the first failure has already occurred. Kleiner et al. (1998) presented a procedure that 
identifies the optimal rehabilitation strategies for water distribution networks in order to reduce 
the rehabilitation investment and maintenance costs based on both partial and implicit enumeration 
schemes. In the same manner, Kleiner & Rajani (1999) developed a methodology that process 
incomplete data about pipe failures to assess future need and Rezaei et al. (2015) investigated the 
impact of dynamic hydraulic conditions on pipe failures through a correlation between the dynamic 
pressure and breakage of pipes.  
Following the above, Xu et al. (2013) developed a prediction model of pipe break using genetic 
programming and an economically optimal pipe replacement model which were used in the 
determination of the optimal pipe replacement time. The outcome of these models was used to 
deploy cost – effective pipe maintenance plans for the piping system of WDNs. In addition, Kim 
et al. (2015) proposed a framework for optimal scheduling and maintenance of WDS based on the 
Markov decision process and Monte Carlo simulation. More recently, Farmani et al. (2017) used 
the Evolutionary Polynomial Regression (EPR) as the prediction model for pipe failures and also 
considered the K-means clustering approach in order to improve its performance. Both static and 
dynamic factors were considered in this study with temperature to be the most important factor in 
the mid-term prediction model for pipe failures.   
Analytical and numerical modelling techniques coupled with geographical distribution systems 
(GIS) were also used to develop a framework for WDN management. These approaches assess the 
probability of risk failure using data patterns and neurofuzzy logic systems (S. Christodoulou et 
al., 2006; S. Christodoulou et al., 2009; S. Christodoulou & Deligianni, 2010; S. E. Christodoulou, 
2010). In a more specific study, the software tool IRA-WDS was used to predict pipe failure risks  
(Vairavamoorthy et al., 2007). Moreover, the use of multi-factored models resulted in more 
accurate and realistic predictions (S. Christodoulou et al., 2008; Symeon Christodoulou et al., 
2006, 2009; Symeon Christodoulou & Deligianni, 2010). 
Besides statistical tools, hydraulic models were used to investigate the causes leading to pipe 
failures. An initiative named “Battle of Background Leakage Assessment for Water Networks 
BBLAWN” aimed to combine the best management practices of pressure control in order to 
achieve fewer leakages and lower operational costs (Iglesias-Rey et al., 2014). EPANET was the 
main software tool used to assist these efforts through the construction of pressure - dependent 
models and leakage modelling (Morley & Tricarico, 2014; Muranhos et al., 2014; Roshani & 
Filion, 2014).    
Water quality problems associated with IWS have also been reported. Kumpel & Nelson (2014) 
detected high concentrations of total coliform and E. coli bacteria during the initial flushing of 
water through pipes and within periods with low pressure in a study on an IWS case in India. 
Moreover, these indicator bacteria were found at higher concentrations in samples taken from taps 
==PAGE:23==
Introduction 
3 
 
in periods when IWS was performed than periods with continuous water supply (CWS) (Kumpel 
& Nelson, 2013).  Studies carried out in Lebanon revealed that biofilm detachment and microbial 
re-growth were occurred during static conditions while bacterial re-growth had great possibilities 
on storage tanks during intermittent supplies (Ayoub & Malaeb, 2006; Tokajian & Hashwa, 2003).  
Similar studies by Andey & Kelkar (2007) and  Elala et al. (2011) enhance the above-mentioned 
findings stating that IWS operations are negatively affecting public health. 
As mentioned before, to date only a small volume of work has been reported in literature 
considering the effects of IWS on the vulnerability of WDNs. Andey & Kelkar (2009) who 
investigated the influence of IWS and CWS in four Indian cities, cited that domestic water 
consumption is not sensibly affected under IWS if the consumer’s water demand is satisfied during 
this period. Similarly, Fan et al. (2014) through a comparison between the influence of IWS and 
CWS on the water consumption in a case in China reported that domestic water consumption was 
reduced during IWS depending on the restrictions. It was found that outdoor water consumption 
is reduced during slight supply restrictions (6-12 h d-1) while indoor during moderate restrictions 
(1.5 – 6 h d-1). Concerning the WDN reliability, Fragiadakis, et al. (2016) proposed a methodology 
to assess the risk of pipe failure by extending the American Lifelines Association’s (ALA) 
guidelines on the seismic performance of WDNs with the introduction in the analysis of the studied 
network’s non-seismic behavior and its hydraulic characteristics. This method was based on Graph 
Theory and Monte Carlo simulations coupled with hydraulic analysis using EPANET models 
With regard to Cyprus, a study carried by S. Christodoulou & Agathokleous (2012) revealed that 
during the IWS period the number of water leakage incidents was increased by 28% and among 
the 12000 incidents most of them were related to house connections and pipes with small diameter. 
In addition, Agathokleous & Christodoulou (2016) using a mathematical approach that simulates 
the effects of IWS on the urban water distribution system found that during and right after the 
introduction of a two-year period of IWS the number of pipe leakages was increased. Specifically, 
the amount of water loss incidents was increased to 70-80 per day during the first year.  
Most of the work reported so far, was focused on pipe leakage prediction and water losses 
management techniques with no emphasis to be given on the hydraulic behavior of a WDN under 
IWS operations. Although several papers have been published regarding pressure and leakage 
modelling of WDNs through EPANET software, few of them were focused on the comparison 
between the behavior of a WDN during a CWS and IWS period. 
1.2  
Objectives 
This thesis aims at investigating the vulnerability of urban water distributions networks under IWS 
operations based on a spatio-temporal analysis. The hydraulic performance of a specific WDN is 
evaluated with the findings of this research to shed light whether the implementation of IWS is 
helpful in saving water during drought periods or results in negative effects on the hydraulic 
conditions and behavior of the WDN. This research is divided into two parts. In the first part, the 
analysis is carried out through hydraulic models and in the second based on statistical and survival 
analysis tools. Furthermore, this study aims to identify any potential correlations between the 
hydraulic characteristics of the WDN under IWS operation and the corresponding number and 
==PAGE:24==
Introduction 
4 
 
location of pipe leakage incidents. The case study is located in Nicosia, Cyprus where the behavior 
of a specific District Metered Area (DMA) was examined during two different situations; one 
under IWS and one under CWS.   
The hydraulic analysis is based on a demand driven model in EPANET. Two models, one for each 
situation, were constructed using the EPANET software based on an already existing skeletonized 
model of the case study WDN. Pipe hydraulic characteristics, elevation and water demand data 
were utilized for the conversion of this model.  
Then, both IWS and CWS operations were simulated and the behavior of the WDN in each case 
was examined. Pressure and flow characteristics for each period were reported and compared. 
Following the initial evaluation of the results, the two models were tested under fire and 
contamination breakage scenarios while critical zones and demand situations were identified. 
For the second analysis, a large database including leakage incidents, the percentage of unbilled 
water and the total volume of water channeled to network during the period 2001-2017 was 
analyzed with statistical and survival analysis tools using the EXCEL-XLSTAT package. The data 
were separated into IWS and CWS periods and compared. All the data were obtained from the 
Water Board of Nicosia (NWB).

2 Background 
A WDN is complex system which comprises of several hydraulic elements such as pipes, water 
tanks, reservoirs and pumps developed to ensure sufficient water supply from various sources to 
consumers.  It can also be characterized as a ‘living organism’ with all its components to be 
essential for its survival and it is placed among the top important and primary construction 
networks of modern and developing urban societies. Thus, a WDN vulnerability assessment is of 
high importance. For this reason, the International Water Association (IWA) has published through 
the Water Loss Task Force (WLTF) a manual including the best possible management strategies 
for WDNs (Morrison et al., 2007) and a manual referring to the detection and repair of pipe 
leakages (Pilcher, 2010).  
In this chapter a short presentation about the WDNs and the water quality in Cyprus is given. 
Moreover, a brief description of the main principles governing the pressure, flow and the 
interactions of water through a WDN and how they are simulated in EPANET is presented.  
2.1  
Water Distribution Networks in Cyprus 
In Cyprus, the water supply networks are managed through Water Boards, the local municipal 
councils and the communities. The WDNs of the three biggest cities in Cyprus; Nicosia, Limassol 
and Larnaca are managed by the corresponding Water Boards while the rest areas are supervised 
by the local communities such as the municipality of Paphos (4th largest city). Water Boards are 
semi-government organizations which operate on the basis of Article 188 of Chapter 350 of the 
Water Supply Act, belong to the Ministry of Interior and audited by the General Auditor of the 
Republic of Cyprus (Ministry of Interior, 2018). The local municipal councils act base on Articles 
84(b) and 87(1)(c) of the Municipalities Law and Regulations while the community councils act 
according to Articles 82(a) and 83(g) of the Communities Law and Regulations (Ministry of 
Interior, 2018). 
The responsibilities of the Water Boards include  
 The provision of satisfactory drinking water quality and quantity for domestic and 
commercial purposes.  
 The operation, maintenance and extension of the WDN. 
 The planning and execution of technical works and the installation of domestic facilities. 
 The monitoring of pipes, house connections and water meters to confront waterlosses. 
 The recording of consumption, collection of fees and billing. 
The areas covered by the WDNs are divided to large and small Water Supply Zones (WSZ). The 
large WSZs cover approximately 75% of the total resident population in Cyprus while almost 21% 
is covered by the small WSZs. Based on the latest report from the Drinking Water Directive 
98/83/EC (EC, 2013a) and the Cypriot authorities, there are 25 large and 292 small WSZ with 
surface water to be the main water source which covers 58% of the total volume of water (Figure 
2.1). In Table 2.1 and Table 2.2, information concerning the WSZ of Cyprus are presented (EC, 
2013a).

Water quality in Cyprus

Organisation, 2011). Sodium hypochlorite is the main disinfectant agent used in Cyprus.

2.4  
EPANET modelling 
2.4.1  EPANET 
EPANET is a software package developed and published by the Water Supply and Water 
Resources Division of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) of the United States. It is a 
public software and free to use. It can be used as tool for modelling drinking water distribution 
networks and for simulating the hydraulic and water quality behavior of pressurized pipe networks 
for long-term periods (Rossman, 2000). Various tasks such water flow in pipes, pressure at each 
node, water elevation in water tanks, concentration of different chemicals, water age and source 
tracing can be simulated in EPANET. Moreover, EPANET can be used for several other 
applications and strategies dealing with the behavior of the WDN. Such applications are hydraulic 
model calibration, sampling program design and water quality improvement. The corresponding 
strategies include replacement and cleaning of pipes, altering source utilization within multiple 
source systems, use of satellite treatment and optimizing pumping and tank filling/emptying 
schedules (Rossman, 2000). 
With all these specific features, EPANET was chosen as the main modelling tool for the simulation 
of the case study areas. What is more, EPANET can be used with convenience and requires only 
a few input data in order to simulate a WDN. In addition to this, pipe, fire and contamination 
breakage scenarios can be easily performed making this software appropriate for achieving the 
aims of this study.

2.4.2  Hydraulic analysis 
The hydraulic head loss and the flow continuity equations are solved in EPANET using the 
“Gradient Method” developed by Todini & Pilati (1987). This method used a hybrid node-loop 
approach to simulate the hydraulic state of the pipe network.  
The head loss is computed through one of the following formulas: 
 Hazen-Williams formula 
 Darcy-Weisbach formula 
 Chezy-Manning formula 
In the case of the Darcy-Weisbach formula, the friction factor is computed depending on the flow 
regime with one of the following formulas (Rossman, 2000): 
 Re < 2000: The Hagen-Poiseuille formula.  
 Re > 4000: The Swamee and Jain approximation to the Colebrook-White equation. 
 2000 < Re < 4000: A cubic interpolation from the Moody Diagram.

2.5  
Survival analysis 
Survival analysis is defined as a set of statistics dealing with the expected duration of time until an 
event occurs by estimating the survival time between  an initiating and a final event (John P. Klein, 
2003; Kleinbaum & Klein, 2011). The event can be disease occurrence, death or any kind of event 
of interest and the time is divided in three steps; the start of the study, the end of the study and 
whether an event has occurred or not (Kleinbaum & Klein, 2011).   
In the current study, an initiating event is represented by the date of pipe installation, the start of a 
pipe treatment or a pipe leakage observation. A near-terminal event is represented by the date of 
pipe repair and a terminal event is represented by the date of a pipe replacement.  
Survival analysis was mainly preferred among other types of analysis since it does not require a 
complete dataset. In contrast with the linear regression model which can be considered as another 
potential model tool, survival analysis can model the survival time using databases with both 
complete and censored observations by forecasting and filling in the missing data thus resulting in 
accurate predictions. Censored are called the incomplete observations with partial survival time 
since the initiating or terminal event time is unknown (Hosmer, Lemeshow, & May, 2008). The 
aforementioned, makes the survival analysis the most suitable tool for the processing of the data 
since the online recording of the leakage incidents of the WDN was only started in 2003 in Cyprus 
while the WDN of the case study was deployed in 1960. Furthermore, the behavior of the WDN 
which can be considered as a failure of mechanical systems is in line with the simulation of death 
or occurrence of different diseases in biological organisms which was the initial application of the 
survival analysis.  
In survival analysis, three types of censoring schemes are possible:  right censoring, where the 
only known information is that  the event hasn’t occurred in the specific time,  left censoring, 
where the event has occurred prior to the beginning of the observation and interval censoring 
where, the event occurs within some interval (John P. Klein, 2003). In addition, truncated 
observations are included in these datasets. The two truncation schemes are the left truncation 
where failures below a specific time are not included in the sample (only individuals who survived 
for a specific time) and right truncation where only events which have already occurred by a 
specified time are included in the study (John P. Klein, 2003). When data are censored or truncated 
likelihood functions must be constructed assuming that the observation times are independent from 
the life times.  
The main mathematical equations used in survival analysis are presented below (2.5.1 – 2.5.6) 
with T describing the elapsed time until the occurrence of a specified event, F(t) being the 
cumulative distribution function which describes the probability that an event occurs at time t, S(t) 
the survival function which gives the probability of surviving of an individual until time t, h(t) the 
hazard or failure rate  that gives the probability an individual at time T to experience the event in 
the next instance, H(t) the cumulative hazard function which is the integral of h(t) from 0 to T and 
f(t) the probability density function denoting the chance that an event is occurring at time t (S. E. 
Christodoulou, 2010; John P. Klein, 2003).

3 Case study 
3.1  
Introduction 
In 2008, following a 4-year period of drought, the Cypriot authorities decided to take several 
measures in order to address the water scarcity the island was facing, including the introduction of 
an IWS policy in the capital city of Nicosia. The IWS policy was implemented for approximately 
2 years, lasting from March 2008 to January 2010. During this period, water supply was regulated 
through the opening/closing of several flow valves on a daily basis. A total of 17-19 million m3 of 
water were imported to the network each year of the IWS period while under CWS conditions the 
amount of water channeled is usually 19-24 million m3 of water (Nicosia WB, 2018). According 
to the IWS plan, the WDN was separated into 4 groups of District Metered Areas (DMAs) where 
water was supplied consecutively for a time period of twelve hours. However, due to altitude 
differences the water supply was not sufficient in some areas thus the supply period was extended 
(Nicosia WB, 2018).  
In addition, a similar IWS policy was also implemented for a period of 5 days in April 2015 due 
to a major breakage on one of the main pipes which supply Nicosia with water. The period 2014 -
2016 was also characterized by water scarcity due to drought but no further IWS policies were 
introduced.  
The comparison between the water consumption during the CWS period of 2007 and the IWS 
periods of 2008 and 2009 revealed that 1,235,503 and 608,386 m3 of water were saved 
respectively. On the other hand, the number of incidents such as damage to the valves and pipe 
failures was increased (Nicosia WB, 2018). According to Agathokleous (2015), who investigated 
the IWS dataset, the condition of the piping network was worsened during that period and the 
water savings were due to water losses than reduction of the water consumption.  
3.2  
Network and Dataset 
As mentioned above, the data utilized in this research work were acquired from the NWB and are 
related to the Nicosia’s WDN. Based on the data, the WDN is divided in 24 main DMAs which 
are subdivided into 65 smaller areas (Figure 3.1).  The network covers an area of approximately 
50 km2 with the altitude differences ranging from 120 m to 350 m above sea level. Water is mainly 
supplied with gravity to 120 000 consumers with more than 112,000 water meters operate under 
the supervision of the NWB.  
For the operation of the WDN, 19.5 million m3 of water per year in continuous flow are needed. 
The maximum daily demand in the summer is around 59,200 m3 and the minimum and average

4 Hydraulic Analysis  
4.1  
Methodology 
The hydraulic analysis was performed by utilizing the EPANET software. Firstly, two hydraulic 
models representing the examined area and its WDN under CWS and IWS operations were 
designed. The hydraulic performance of each model was then evaluated under three different 
scenarios; fire breakage, contamination breakage and disinfection with free chlorine.   
The initial model was developed based on an already existing skeletonized network in EPANET. 
The model was improved, redesigned and optimized in order to be identical with the real DMA 1 
of the WDN. An AUTOCAD model consisting of all the pipes including the length, diameter and 
material was used to access all the necessary data for the modification of the existing EPANET 
model.  Finally, the network consist of 2151 nodes, 2298 pipes, 1 tank, 1 reservoir and 1 pump.  
Each node represents a single house or building.  
The flow units and the Headloss Formula were set to LPS (Liter per Second) and D-W (Darcy-
Weisbach), respectively.  The elevation of each node was edited using the Google Earth software 
and the water demand based on data retrieved from the NWB. As stated before, water consumption 
was estimated to be 0.27 m3/inhabitant/day or 0.003 l/inhabitant/second. With this in mind, the 
average demand of each node ranges from 0.001 to 0.1 l/s (Figure 4.1).  The total average demand 
of DMA 1 was calculated to be approximately 20 l/s. The elevation of this area range from 155 m 
to 186 m above sea level (Figure 4.1). The pipe diameter and roughness were imported from the 
AUTOCAD model and range from 100 to 300 mm and 0.1 to 0.3 mm, respectively. Among the 
2298 pipes included in the model, 24 of them were used as additional pipes which were necessary 
only for the IWS case in order to control the flow to specific directions.

4.1.1  Demand pattern 
In order to make the model as realistic as could be, demand multipliers and demand patterns were 
introduced.  According to De Moel et al. (2006), the extreme maximum and minimum daily

decided to evaluate the behavior of the WDN during the critical situation thus an average value of 
the extreme maximum multipliers was used. The daily and hourly average maximum demand 
multipliers were set to 1.5 and 2.16.

4.1.4  Scenarios 
Different scenarios which include fire and contamination breakages in various nodes during the 
intermittent and the continuous water supply periods were investigated. The hydraulic 
consequences on the WDN were analysed for each period and compared. Furthermore, a 
disinfection scenario with free chlorine throughout the network for both water supply cases was 
also simulated and analysed.  
4.1.4.1  Fire Breakage Scenario 
The fire breakage scenarios were performed using the average design model under a single analysis 
simulation. Since the purpose of these analyses was to compare the hydraulic consequences on the 
two water supply models with IWS and CWS, the use of demand patterns or multipliers was not 
necessary. The addition of the demand patterns would be useful to identify the hydraulic reaction 
of the network during a day based on the hourly water demand of each node or sub-area.  
The simulations were carried out using 1 CWS model and 3 IWS models, one for each sub-DMA. 
Thus different scenarios were examined with fire breakages in each sub-DMA. The simple rules 
which were initially used for the simulation of the IWS situation were modified and separated in 
order to create 3 single models with water provision to the corresponding sub-DMA. A fire 
breakage scenario was simulated by adding an extra water demand on a selected node which 
operate as fire hydrant according to Figure 3.3. A fire hydrant is estimated to supply water with a 
==PAGE:43==
Hydraulic Analysis 
23 
 
rate of 10 to 25 l/s (Nicosia WB, 2018). The same water demand was added to the same node for 
both the CWS and the IWS models and the behaviour of the network in each case was examined. 
4.1.4.2  Contamination Breakage Scenario 
Contamination breakage scenarios were performed also without using any demand patterns or 
multipliers since the aim was to investigate how a contaminant is spread in the network during 
IWS and CWS operations.  For that purpose, seven models were constructed by modifying the 
initial conditions applied in EPANET. Two models were designed with water provision for 4 and 
12 hours for each sub-DMA and one model was designed with CWS conditions to the entire WDN. 
The IWS plan of 2008-2009 was designed for a 12 hour water provision to each sub-DMA thus 
the purpose of designing two types of IWS models was to check how a sub-DMA is affected during 
specific periods after the contaminant leakage whether there is water supply or not in the network 
at that time. 
It was decided to evaluate the fate of the contaminant based on the worst case scenario which is a 
contamination leakage at the main tank. Besides of identifying which areas are the most vulnerable 
in case a contaminant leaks at a critical point such as the water tank, this scenario can be used also 
as a tracking tool which can trace the location of the contaminant or any other chemical in the 
WDN at any time. 
By utilizing the EPANET software, the initial concentration of the contaminant was set to 20 mg/L 
using the Setpoint Booster command and the contamination was applied for 1 hour at the beginning 
of the simulation (Time: 01:00 hours). It was assumed that the contaminant is not degraded thus 
no decay coefficients were applied to the model. 
4.1.4.3  Disinfection - Chlorination 
A disinfection scenario was also simulated in order to compare the reaction of the disinfectant 
under IWS and CWS conditions. As stated before the main disinfectant agent used in Cyprus is 
the sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) which forms hypochlorous acid (HOCl) when it is added to 
water according to the following reaction (Fukuzaki, 2006): 
𝑁𝑎𝑂𝐶𝑙+ 𝐻2𝑂 ⟶𝐻𝑂𝐶𝑙+ 𝑁𝑎𝑂𝐻− 
Free chlorine is thus presented in the water as HOCl(aq). The 72-hour IWS and CWS models were 
used for this simulation with some modifications. The tank and pump were removed and the 
reservoir was set at an attitude of 230 m (water level of tank in full capacity) and connected directly 
to the piping system. The purpose of this was to ensure the constant supply of the disinfectant in 
the network. It was assumed that the free chlorine residual follows a first order reaction rate and 
decays with a rate of 2 d-1 (bulk coefficient) with no wall reactions. These changes were applied to 
EPANET using the Reactions Options while the residual concentration at the outlet of the reservoir 
was set at 0.5 mg/l. According to data retrieved from the NWB the chlorine concentration in the 
outlet of the water tank supplying DMA 1 is ranging from 0.35 to 0.60 mg/l (Nicosia WB, 2018).

4.3  
Key Findings 
4.3.1  Pressure/velocity standards 
No general pressure standards are established globally although some guidelines have been 
published by the Water Security Agency (Water Security Agency, 2015). The agency states that 
water pressure should range from 35 m (50 psi) to 56 m (80 psi) under normal conditions with the 
minimum and maximum acceptable values being 14 m (20 psi) and 70 m (100 psi), respectively. 
Any pressures outside this range could probably damage the pipe system. Many countries have 
introduced national regulations with maximum and minimum pressure limits varying depending 
on the conditions of the WDN. According to Ghorbanian et al. (2016), pressure standards set by 
many countries range from 10 m to 70 m with Friedman et al., 2010) to recommend that the 
pressure limits should be above 0 m during extreme conditions and emergencies, above 14 m 
during the maximum hourly demand and fire breakage cases and between 25 m and 70 m under 
normal conditions. According to NWB, the normal pressure limits for the Nicosia WDN should 
be approximately between 30 m to 60 m. Based on data measured through the SCADA system, 
the average pressure of DMA 1 is approximately 38 m (Nicosia WB, 2018). 
The specific guidelines were fulfilled during CWS conditions as shown in Figure 4.3 with the 
simulate pressures ranging from  18 m to 63 m and  32 m to 64 m under the maximum and the 
average hourly demand cases. During the fire breakage simulations on the CWS model, some 
pressures below 14 m were detected (Figure 4.12:c, Figure 4.12:d and Figure 4.13:c). However, 
the pressures were always above 5 m thus it can be said that the pressure criteria are fulfilled. On 
the other hand, the pressure criteria were not met when IWS policies were applied to the network 
with the situation to be worsen when fire breakage was simulated. 
With regards to velocity, the Water Security Agency (2015) suggests that the optimal range should 
be between 0.6 – 1.5 m/s while a velocity above 0.3 m/s is necessary in order to avoid clogging in 
the pipes (De Moel et al., 2006). Such guidelines are not established in Cyprus thus the velocity 
variations were not investigated in this research. In addition, the EPANET model was designed 
including a large number of nodes which represent the buildings of DMA 1. Thus, for simulation 
purposes most of the pipes were simulated by connecting small links instead of one single link 
with the actual length of the pipe. Therefore, only the simulated velocity or flow of some pipes, 
such the ones presented in Figure 6.1 and Figure 6.2, was comparable to real data in order to 
examine whether guidelines are exceeded or not.  
4.3.2  Effects of IWS on pressure and flow 
The main goal of the EPANET model was not to evaluate if pressure or velocities standards are 
met rather to investigate how the behaviour of a WDN is changed between an IWS and a CWS 
period. The effects of the IWS policy were clearly illustrated in Figure 4.8 and Figure 4.10. Low 
pressures and extreme variations of water flow were detected in most of the nodes and pipes across 
the network. As system simulations have shown, due to these variations, the normal daily 
==PAGE:59==
Hydraulic Analysis 
39 
 
operational cycle for which the WDN is constructed, is disrupted under IWS conditions forcing 
the system to operate under extreme conditions.  
The current DMA (as well as the corresponding EPANET model) consist of many pipes and nodes, 
thus the results presented in chapter 4.2  are just a small sample of how the network reacts under 
IWS. Pressure and water flow vary from pipe to pipe and node to node depending on the 
corresponding water demand, elevation and hydraulic characteristics of the area and its hydraulic 
structural components.  
4.3.3  Demand patterns difference 
The importance of using demand patterns is highlighted in this research. The variations of the 
pressure and flow depending on the hourly water demand were useful to identify how a WDN 
actually operates under real conditions. Since water consumption varies during the day, an average 
designed model with no demand multipliers would not be representative of a real WDN.  
Furthermore, the WDN behaviour varies during an IWS period. For example, as shown in Figure 
4.4 which illustrates the first period of the IWS operation with water supply to sub-DMA A, 
pressure is below the acceptable limits at 6:00 PM but it complies with the guidelines at 9:00 PM. 
This change of is attributed to the water consumption rate which is higher at 6:00 PM. If no diurnal 
pattern was applied in the model, network pressure would be constantly the same, something not 
feasible in real conditions. It is important to note that, when comparing the water consumption 
pattern (Figure 4.2) with the IWS schedule (Table 4.1), the water supply starting time of each sub-
DMA falls during the periods with the higher water demand. Water provision to sub-DMA A was 
set to begin at 6:00 PM whereas for sub-DMAs B and C at 6:00 AM.  Higher water demand periods 
according to the applied pattern which was based on real statistics were between 6:00 – 8:00 PM 
and 6:00 – 8:00 AM. This fact probably imposed an extra burden on the already overwhelmed 
network. A smoother transition of water between the sub-DMAs could probably be achieved 
during times with low water demand rates. 
4.3.4  Scenarios 
The vulnerability of the WDN under IWS conditions was also verified when fire and 
contamination scenarios were applied to the model.  
Results show that the network cannot operate efficiently when fires break out during IWS periods. 
The main reason is the way the IWS policy was designed which was based on the division of DMA 
1 to three sub-DMAs. As proved by this research, at least two of the three sub-DMAs were not 
able to withstand additional water demand, which caused large pressure drop (< 0) mostly to the 
areas surrounding the corresponding fire hydrant. Generally, when the water demand of a specific 
node is increased then the water flow of the pipes connected to that node is also increased in order 
to cover that extra demand. This phenomenon causes a reduction of flow in the rest of the piping 
system leading to pressure drop. This situation is more intense in smaller networks when the size 
of water imports or water flow in the system is not large enough to cover extra water demands. 
This case is presented during IWS periods when the active network shrinks from an entire DMA 
==PAGE:60==
Hydraulic Analysis 
40 
 
to a smaller sub-DMA. Thus the hydraulic performance of a sub-DMA is low since it is not 
designed to operate independently.  
Water quality issues were also detected when contamination breakage scenarios were applied to 
the IWS model. According to the results presented in chapter 4.1.4.2 , the main contamination 
problem developed when water supply was terminated while the contaminant was still in network. 
This action lead to accumulation of the contaminant in several parts of the sub-DMA in higher 
concentrations. Furthermore, the residence time of the contaminant was increased during the 
period of no water supply. 
As discussed in chapter 2.2 , the highest percentage of bacteria in drinking water exceeding 
guidelines during the 2011-2013 period was that of coliforms, with WDN problems being the main 
cause for low drinking water quality. According to the World Health Organization (2003), 
coliforms grow faster with low disinfectant residual, high temperatures, long residence times and 
stagnation of drinking water. Moreover, low pressures enhance the growth of indicator bacteria 
such as total coliform and E. coli (Kumpel & Nelson, 2014). Such conditions occur during an IWS 
period, when water is not supplied for hours thus the water flow is low (Figure 4.10), pressure 
drops (Figure 4.8), temperature rises and chlorine residual is dissolved (Figure 4.19). Although the 
effects of the temperature factor were not investigated in this study, all other factors affecting 
coliform growth were modelled and the results confirmed the aforementioned.  Unfortunately, no 
data were found regarding the water quality in Nicosia for the IWS period of 2008-2009, thus a 
comparison of the water quality during the IWS and CWS period could not be performed.  
On the other hand, some results show a potential positive effect of IWS policy in terms of water 
quality. In the IWS case with water supply to sub-DMA A for the entire time of the observation 
period (IWS scenario 2), contamination was isolated in just one area and was not spread to the 
entire network (Figure 4.14:b). In addition, the concentration and residence time of the 
contaminant was at the same levels as during the CWS scenario (Figure 4.15, Figure 4.16 and 
Figure 4.17).  
The behaviour of a WDN is unpredictable during an IWS period. The effects of various incidents 
such as fire or contamination breakages vary depending on the location and time each of event. 
The division of a WDN or just a DMA into smaller areas during IWS periods in order to control 
water supply makes the prediction of these effects more difficult and complex than during a CWS 
period. Especially a contamination breakage during an IWS period can lead to degradation of the 
water quality if it is immediately followed by termination of water supply while it can constrain 
the contaminant if water is provided continually just to one sub-DMA. The results of this scenario 
were evaluated based on a contaminant leakage at the water tank which is considered the worst 
possible case of contamination since potential chemical or bacteria can be distributed across the 
WDN. Other cases with contamination breakage on several pipes across the network under IWS 
conditions revealed that the spreading of the contaminant will be limited since it will dissolve quite 
fast.  However, the presence of a contaminant in the network depends on its initial concentration 
thus higher contaminant concentrations, prolong its existence in the network.

5.2.1  Statistical analysis 
The statistical analysis was applied to data pertaining to the volume of water channelled, consumed

Figure 5.1 and Figure 5.2 present the volume of water channelled to the network, water

and consumed in the network was generally followed by a corresponding increase in the next years. 
The same applies to unbilled water which was almost double the years after it was reduced (Figure

the strict polices taken by the government for confronting water scarcity. The second reduction is 
observed in 2014 probably because of low water reserves due to drought. Although the reduction 
of water channelled to the network does not affect the behaviour of the network in the same manner

5.3  
Key Findings 
5.3.1  House Connections vs Water Mains  
The majority of pipe leakage incidents was related to HC’s bearing the vulnerability of these parts 
of the network (Figure 5.6). As explained by Wood & Lence (2009), pipes with small diameters 
such as HC have a reduced wall thickness which results to reduced resistance and increases the 
potentiality of leakages. Furthermore, the high number of HC incidents is attributed also to the 
high number of HC in the WDN compared to WM. However, according to the survival analysis 
(Figure 5.8), HC have a higher survival probability than WM despite the fact that more HC 
incidents were recorded. This is confirmed by the results in Table 5.8 where the percentage of 
failed and censored events is presented. Approximately 79% of the WM were replaced when 
leakages occurred with the corresponding percentage for HC to be 16%. The above facts, suggest 
that both HC and WM are important parts of the WDN and their performance should be 
investigated differently.  
Statistical (Table 5.7) and survival analyses (Table 5.10 and Table 5.9), which were carried out 
differently for HC and WM, revealed that pipe leakage incidents were not always dealt in the same 
manner. According to the findings, HC were gradually replaced instead of repaired, whereas most 
WM were repaired after the IWS years and specifically during the 2016-2017 period. This was 
attributed to a change of the NWB policy. Thus, the percentage of failed events in the WDN 
depends on the evaluation of the condition of each pipe incident by the NWB and the decision 
whether it is critical and needs to be replaced or not.

Mathematical Analysis

5.3.2  Correlation between mathematical and hydraulic analysis 
The statistical analysis which was performed considering the number of incidents and the unbilled 
water confirmed the negative effects of the IWS policy on the WDN. Although water losses were 
reduced during that period, the following years (2010-2011) the percentage of unbilled water was 
increased from 12% to 24% which was a consequence of the high number of pipe leakage incidents 
occurring. During the years 2009 and 2010 the number of incidents increased dramatically by 81% 
and 62%, respectively. The seasonal analysis revealed that the worst situation of the WDN 
occurred during the spring and summer seasons, when water loss and the number of pipe leakages 
reached the highest levels. This is in line with the EPANET results (Figure 4.4 - Figure 4.10) which 
showed that during high water demand periods the piping system is operating under extreme 
conditions with pressure and flow decreasing and increasing dramatically with short periods of 
time. Even though the simulations performed were based on daily instead of a seasonal pattern it 
can be said that the periods of high water demand (6:00 – 8:00 PM and 6:00 – 8:00 AM) correspond 
to the summer period. 
In a research carried out by the Federation of Canadian Municipalities and National Research 
Council (2003), extreme changes of water pressure, water quality, flow velocity and backflow 
potential were identified as the main operational factors which cause pipe deterioration and 
leakages. The variation of water pressure and the high pressures result in additional stresses on the 
pipe and increase the possibilities of pipe bursting, with low water quality and variation of the flow 
velocity enhancing corrosion (Vasyliev, 2015). The results of the hydraulic and mathematical 
analyses of this research agree with findings above and confirm the correlation between WDN 
deterioration and the increase of the pipe leakage incidents due to the implementation of IWS 
policies.

6.2  
Uncertainties in Survival and Statistical Analysis 
The results of the statistical and survival analyses showed that the IWS policy is negatively 
affecting the WDN. However, it must be taken into consideration that the outcome of the 
mathematical analysis is influenced by the following parameters as explained below:  
 Missing data 
Although the survival analysis and the Kaplan-Meier estimator do not require a complete 
database, the absence of data regarding pipe failure incidents for long periods affected the 
shape of the survival curves. Such is the case with the survival curves of HC for the periods 
2009-2010 (Figure 5.10) and 2003-2017(Figure 5.9). Probably both survival curves would 
had been steeper if more pipe failure incidents classified as “Replaced” had been 
incorporated in the analysis.  
 
The dataset which include the location (house number) of each event was also incomplete 
thus only street names were known. This was a drawback in the design of the pipe leakage 
risk maps (Figure 5.12) since in cases with big streets and avenues the most vulnerable 
areas in terms of pipe failure were difficult to be identified. Moreover, because of the 
absence of this kind of data, only street level analysis was possible for the stratification of 
the data which compromised the accuracy of survival analysis (see 6.2 /Level of analysis). 
 
 Data Quality  
Besides the limitation of the missing data, the quality of the data was another important 
parameter affecting the outcome of the analysis. As described in chapter 2.5 , survival 
analysis depends on the number of censored and failed events with the latter to control the 
shape of the survival curves. Thus the classification of the pipe leakage events as censored 
or failed was critical for the analysis followed. The initial database was lacking in detailed 
classification regarding the type of action taken for each event thus for many incidents the 
categorization  as “Repaired” or “Replaced”, which corresponds to censored or failed event 
was based on approximations. The classification of the incidents into “HC”, “WM” and 
“Not related” was also partly based on assumptions since it was not always clearly stated 
if the incident was associated to a HC or a WM. 
 Level of analysis 
As already mentioned, the data were evaluated based on a street level analysis. This 
method, however, has an important drawback. Many incidents which occurred in different 
HC, due to the lack of information about their specific location, were grouped into the same 
category based on their street name and thus it was assumed that they were referring to the 
same HC. Due to this limitation, some of the results may be biased, magnifying the negative 
image of the consequences of the IWS policy.  
 
==PAGE:88==
Hydraulic and mathematical analysis considerations 
68 
 
 Left and Right Censored Data 
For all survival analyses carried out, data were left and right censored in order to isolate only 
the events with known birthdate and failure date. The problem with this approach is that the 
installation date of each pipe was considered to be the date when the corresponding DMA was 
constructed (Table 5.4). Such reliance on estimated than real values, lead to the exclusion of 
many pipe incidents whose installation date was probably more recent than the DMA 
construction day.  Thus when the data were left censored the analysis was performed with 
much fewer data which may not be representative for the entire DMA.

Conclusions

7 Conclusions 
This research was divided into two parts which investigated the behaviour of DMA 1 of Nicosia’s 
WDN under IWS operations.  
The first part included the construction and analysis of several models in EPANET which 
simulated the hydraulic performance of the WDN under CWS and IWS conditions and in three 
scenarios; fire breakage, contamination breakage and disinfection through chlorination.  The main 
conclusions derived are the followed: 
 Extreme variations of pressure and water flow were detected in most of the nodes and pipes 
across the network under IWS conditions. The normal daily operational cycle of the WDN 
is disrupted, forcing the system to operate under extreme conditions with pressures and 
flows below or above the recommended limits. 
 
 The behaviour of a WDN is unpredictable during an IWS period. The effects of various 
incidents such as fire or contamination breakages differ aggravated depending on the 
location and the time each event happened.  
 
 The hydraulic behaviour of specific areas of the WND is worsen under fire breakage during 
the IWS period. 
 
 Contamination breakage during the IWS period can lead to degradation of the water quality 
if it is immediately followed by termination of water supply. 
 
 Disinfection with chlorination during the IWS period is not as effective as during CWS 
periods. 
 
 The separation of a WDN or just a DMA to sub-DMAs during IWS periods in order to 
control water supply is detrimental to the performance of the network. Furthermore, it 
makes the prediction of IWS effects more difficult and complex than during a CWS period.  
 
 The importance of using demand patterns in hydraulic modelling is highlighted. During 
high water demand periods (6:00 – 8:00 PM and 6:00 – 8:00 AM) and with the 
implementation of the IWS operation, the WDN was more vulnerable.  
 
In the second part, a statistical analysis and a survival analysis using the non-parametric Kaplan-
Meier estimator through the XLSTAT EXCEL package were performed. The main conclusions 
from the statistical and survival analysis are presented below: 
 The IWS policy is not an effective measure for confronting the problem of water scarcity. 
Water losses were increased the years following the IWS period. The same behaviour was 
also detected in similar situations when water supply reduction had been applied. The 
==PAGE:90==
Conclusions 
70 
 
percentage of unbilled water was estimated to be 14-18% during the IWS supply period of 
2008-2009 and the drought period of 2013-2014 while it reached 26% the following years 
of 2010 and 2017, respectively. 
 
 Pipe leakage incidents increased during the second year of the IWS period (2009) and the 
year following (2010).  Totally, an 81% and 62% percentage increase was observed in 2009 
and 2010, respectively.  
 
 The majority of the incidents were related to HC. Generally,  the number of WM incidents 
was not increased during the IWS period 
 
 The distribution of pipe leakage incidents and water losses is also affected from 
seasonality. The highest percentage of incidents and water losses is presented in summer 
(July) and spring (March), respectively.  
 
 The survival analysis confirms the negative affect of the IWS policy.  
 
 Although most of the incidents were related to HC, the survival curves show that the WM 
had a lower survival probability and affected negatively the WDN condition.  
 
 The handling of pipe leakage incidents gradually changed after the IWS period. HC were 
replaced instead of being repaired while most of the WM were repaired. The change of 
incidents handling was decided by policy changes decided by NWB. 
The IWS policy is not an efficient method for saving water and confronting the water scarcity 
since it appears to affect negatively the hydraulic conditions of the WDN. The comparison between 
the results of the hydraulic and mathematical analysis indicate that the extreme changes of the 
water pressure and  flow and the degradation of the water quality as a result of the IWS are among 
the factors which contribute to the damage of the WDN. Furthermore, pipes with large flow 
velocity and headloss were found to be more vulnerable to breakages. 
EPANET proved to be an important tool which provided adequate information concerning the 
behaviour of the WDN under IWS conditions and several different scenarios. A more detailed 
model which would had included both HC and WM as different parts with their own parameters 
would provide more accurate and thorough results. In addition, the implementation of various 
demand patterns which correspond to different periods of the year would have improved the 
reliability of the models. Concerning the mathematical analysis, a completed database which will 
allow a more specific analysis based on incidents reported per house number or per each pipe 
independently instead of the current street level analysis would give more sufficient results.
