Abstract
DTU Management
MSc in Business Analytics
AI-Based Portfolio Analysis and Risk Management of Index Funds and
Cryptocurrencies
by Alexandros Giannakakis and Rasmus Blirup Jensen
The main goal of this project is to implement an algorithm capable of creating port-
folios based on the investment preferences of the user while also achieving high levels
of diversiﬁcation without diluting returns. To achieve this task, the assets used in the
ﬁnal portfolios are a combination of Exchange-Trade Funds and Cryptocurrencies. These
assets were later fed into the ﬁrst part of the funnel, consisting of different machine learn-
ing algorithms, creating a smaller customised asset universe composed of a mix of those
assets. A naive optimisation algorithm operating with the Portfolio Diversiﬁcation In-
dex method used the ﬁnal universe to create hybrid and non-hybrid portfolios of the
aforementioned assets. The ﬁrst portfolio type has been compared with the second type
for three different strategies in terms of risk and return performance. The output showed
that the two strategies were more prominent for the non-hybrid portfolios while the third,
responsible for maximising diversiﬁcation, had very interesting results for hybrid port-
folios.

Executive Summary
In this thesis, assets such as Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) and Cryptocurrencies
were used to create hybrid and non-hybrid portfolios of ETFs, based on a Robo-Advisor
that was implemented for the scope of the project. The goal of this thesis was to differ-
entiate from conventional Robo-Advisers, by implementing a funnel framework capable
of constructing a diversiﬁed investment strategy based on investor’s preferences. This
framework could motivate and help individuals to invest their money as an alternative
to the already existing funds, or investment packages offered by banks.
The strategy followed in this thesis starts with a series of clustering techniques on
metadata from ETFs, which the user could ﬁlter upon to create an individualised asset
universe. Secondly, a diversiﬁed subset of that universe was created with the use of min-
imum spanning trees, which pick assets based on their highest diversiﬁcation potential.
Finally, Bootstrapping was used for portfolio generation, and the Portfolio Diversiﬁcation
Index was utilized for portfolio selection and asset allocation.
The Robo-Advisor was able to handle 1126 ETFs and 9 Cryptocurrencies, to form
optimal hybrid (ETFs + Cryptos) and non-hybrid (only ETFs) portfolios for 3 different
strategies, which are the maximum diversiﬁcation, maximum Sharpe Ratio, and 1/N
strategy. Moreover, two Robo-Advisers were tested in this project, one with short-term
memory and one with long-term.
For the non-hybrid case, the maximum Sharpe ratio strategy is performing the best,
followed by 1/N and, ﬁnally, the maximum diversiﬁcation. This phenomenon is ex-
pected because the maximum PDI strategy is trying to further diversify already diversi-
ﬁed assets. For the hybrid portfolios, the diversiﬁcation model stands out. The max-
imum PDI model can keep volatility lower, also hindering returns from reaching ex-
tremes. While the other strategies suffer from the high volatility of Cryptocurrencies,
the maximum diversiﬁcation is controlling and penalising the allocation of volatile as-
sets, because they yield lower PDI. Lastly, according to the results, by allowing more
diversiﬁcation in Cryptocurrencies, the risk-adjusted return is almost doubled compared
to non-hybrid portfolios.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the last decades, ﬁnancial markets are continuously evolving because they are al-
ways subject to new technologies. The market is becoming more complex and difﬁcult
to understand since new ﬁnancial instruments are continuously created and multiplex
algorithmic models are replacing traditional exchange members. Based on these facts,
the scope of this master thesis is to provide an easier and more personalised approach,
with the use of machine learning, for asset selection and portfolio diversiﬁcation.
The concept of investing in the stock market has been around since 1653, and in the
beginning, it was not easy for everyone to invest. For years, only a minority of people
ventured into the world of investing, simply because it can be intimidating and compli-
cated to be involved. For example, in the past, a physical arena was used to exchange
securities, called pit trading, where traders and market makers were exchanging securi-
ties using the open outcry system. Of course, in an environment such as this one, it is
not easy for everyone to participate and invest. Because of that complexity, many people
have turned to investment solutions provided by banks and other institutions. While
this can be a good idea, these institutions also take a percentage of the ﬁnancial gains as
interest, which, in some cases, cancels out the proﬁt generated by the investment.
As years went by, technological progress digitised the whole market and the ﬁnancial
instruments that are exchanged there. Consequently, a wide variety of trading platforms
showed up, which made it easier for retail investors to buy and sell many different assets.
Moreover, in March of 2020, the whole stock market crashed because of the COVID-19
pandemic. That combination of the broad availability of trading platforms and the recent
crash led a lot of people to become investors in the stock market.
Since the above environment increased the number of retail investors, the goal of
this thesis is to build an algorithm that guides the individual interacting with it. In this
way, the investor will have the ability to create a portfolio based on assets related to
his investment desire while also be optimised in terms of diversiﬁcation. Based on that,
non-professional investors will have a more deﬁned picture of what they are investing in

2.4
Risk
Risk in ﬁnancial term is seen as the chance that an outcome of an investments actual gains
will differ from the expected output. It includes the risk of losing some or all of the initial
investment.
Commonly risk is quantiﬁed by considering the historical ﬂuctuations in an assets
price movement. This is also known as measuring the standard deviation, which com-
pared the price movement according to the average price movement within a certain
time frame. This has also commented on in eq. 2.7 and 2.5, where the variance is σ2. A
high standard deviation implies high ﬂuctuations in an assets price, which reﬂect a risky
investment due to the increased uncertainty of the price movement. While a low stan-
dard deviation deﬁnes a less risky or stable investment as the price dues not have large
ﬂuctuation from the average or expected return of an assets.
When talking about risk, there are different types of risk. There is the systemic risk
also referred to as the market risk which affect the entire market, regardless of assets type
or category. Such an event could be as the Covid-19 pandemic, which infected entire
markets for a brief period. Other types of systemic risk can be, political decisions, wars
or inﬂation risks. Furthermore systemic risk is hard to mitigate through diversiﬁcation,
because it affects a large proportion of the market. On the other side there is unsystematic
risk which is a more speciﬁc risk or idiosyncratic risk which only affect an investment
category, industry or company. Examples of unsystematic risk could be a product recall
from a company, the world having a ill view on oil production or Elon Musk citing that
they will not accept bitcoin at Tesla anymore. It can also be that a new technology such

fails or a company fails to reach anticipated sales and revenue decline.
While there will always be risk in the market, the idiosyncratic or unsystematic risk

giving them immunity to manipulation and interference by them.

Blockchain
All Cryptocurrencies mentioned above have a common element and that is the distributed
public ledger known as Blockchain (Swan, 2015). While Blockchain is a type of database,
one key difference to the standard database is the structure of data. In this case, the collec-
tion of information is done together in groups, called blocks, which are holding these sets
of information. Those blocks have a speciﬁed capacity, storage, and they are chained to
the previously ﬁlled block, once they are ﬁlled as well, ﬁnally forming a chain of blocks.
Any new information will be added to the block next to the previously ﬁlled one, thus
repeating the process. The body of each block consists of transactions and transaction
counters. An asymmetric cryptographic mechanism is used in Blockchain, in order to
validate the authentication of transactions (NRI, 2015).

Blockchain Characteristics
Blockchain has several key characteristics that are listed down below:
1. Decentralisation
In a standard non-decentralized transaction system, a central trusted agency is re-
sponsible to authenticate each transaction, which can lead central servers to performance
congestions. On the other side, in Blockchain networks transactions can be authenti-
cated via peer-to-peer interaction and without having a central agency to monitor the
process. In this direction, Blockchain network can reduce considerably the server costs
(both operation and development cost) and, at the same time, alleviate existing perfor-
mance bottlenecks in central agency’s servers.
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 3. Cryptocurrencies and Blockchain
18
2. Persistency
The fact that transactions, who are spread across the Blockchain network, require to be
veriﬁed and recorded in blocks throughout the whole grid, makes it impossible to med-
dle with them. Furthermore, each dispersed block needs to be veriﬁed by other nodes, as
well as the transactions that are included within that block. This process makes it easy to
detect and identify forgeries.
3. Anonymity
A user can interact with the Blockchain by using a generated address. In addition, the
user has the option to generate more than one addresses to avoid exposing his identity,
since the central agency, that keeps all the private information of the user, no longer ex-
ists. Having established such a mechanism, the transactions of a Blockchain user are, in a
way, private. Of course, there is no absolute guarantee that a perfect privacy framework
can be achieved through Blockchain network.
4. Auditability
One of the main functions within Blockchain is the ability to record and validate
all transactions on the network, since all of them have a timestamp, making it easy to
trace and verify previously recorded transactions by accessing all of the nodes in the dis-
tributed network. For example, in Bitcoin Blockchain, any transaction is possible to be
traced by former ones iteratively. This implementation enhances the transparency and
traceability of all data available in the distributed network.
Taxonomy of Blockchain Systems
There are three types of Blockchain systems that have been identiﬁed: public, private
and consortium Blockchain (Buterin, 2015). A comparison of those three types of those
Blockchain types has been made from different angles (Zheng et al., 2018).
1. Consensus Determination
Each node of a public Blockchain could participate in a consensus process, while in
consortium Blockchains only a chosen set of nodes could participate in this type of pro-
cess. As for the private Blockchain, since it is controlled by a private organization, they
are the ones responsible for the consensus.
2. Read permission
Based on what was mentioned before, it should be clear that in a public Blockchain,
transactions are visible to the public while this might not be the case in a consortium or
public blockchain, where the read permissions might differ. It is up to consortium or or-
ganization to decide whether the saved information will be available to the public or not.
3. Immutability
==PAGE:31==
Chapter 3. Cryptocurrencies and Blockchain
19
Due to the fact that all transactions are reserved in many different nodes within the
Blockchain Network, the interference and alternation of information is nearly impossi-
ble to happen in public systems. Although, this is not the case in the other two types of
Blockchain, since it is up to the majority of the consortium or the presiding organization
to interfere to the network and make modiﬁcations.
4. Efﬁciency
Efﬁciency. It takes plenty of time to propagate transactions and blocks as there are a
large number of nodes on public blockchain network. Taking network safety into con-
sideration, restrictions on public blockchain would be much more strict. As a result,
transaction throughput is limited and the latency is high. With fewer validators, consor-
tium blockchain and private blockchain could be more efﬁcient.
5. Centralized
The main difference among the three types of blockchains is that public blockchain
is decentralized, consortium blockchain is partially centralized and private blockchain is
fully centralized as it is controlled by a single group.
6. Consensus Process
Everyone in the world could join the consensus process of the public Blockchain. Dif-
ferent from public Blockchain, both consortium Blockchain and private Blockchain are
permissioned. One node needs to be certiﬁcated to join the consensus process in consor-
tium or private blockchain.
Since public Blockchain is open to the world, it can attract many users. Commu-
nities are also very active. Many public Blockchains emerge day by day. As for con-
sortium Blockchain, it could be applied to many business applications. Ethereum also
has provided tools for building consortium blockchains (ethereum, n.d.). As for private
Blockchain, there are still many companies implementing it for efﬁciency and auditabil-
ity.

Bitcoin Blockchain
As mentioned before, each block has a ﬁxed capacity and storage speciﬁed by the respec-
tive Cryptocurrency system, meaning that there is an upper bound to the total number of
transaction information that are included in each block. For example, Bitcoin blocks have
a maximum size of 1MB (Nakamoto, 2008). Figure below shows a simpliﬁed portrayal of
a bitcoin blockchain.
FIGURE 3.5: Bitcoin Blockchain (Nakamoto, 2008)
Bitcoin blocks are consisted of ﬁve ﬁelds:
• Magic Number (ﬁxed).
• Block Size.
• Block header.
This ﬁeld contains the timestamp, the hash of the previous block, the version num-
ber of the block, the nonce, and the hash that includes all transactions happened in
this block.
• Transaction counter (number of transactions included in the block).
• Transactions (the set of veriﬁed transactions added from the block).
The ﬁrst or “genesis” block holds the very ﬁrst transaction made by that cryptocur-
rency. The hash of the genesis block is then passed to a miner, who uses that block to
create a nonce for creating a hash for the new block added to the chain. Moving forward,
the hash of the 3rd block contains the hashes included in the previous two blocks and
the process is repeated for the next blocks added. Consequently, this process, through
the incorporation of hashes, is creating a chronological link starting from the ﬁrst block
until the latest block connected to the chain, meaning there is only a single path from that
==PAGE:33==
Chapter 3. Cryptocurrencies and Blockchain
21
very ﬁrst block until the most recent one. Having established this connection, makes the
attack, and more speciﬁcally the alternation of information, from an individual or group
to the system extremely difﬁcult. That is because any interference would have been de-
tected, since all upcoming blocks would have to be generated leading to mismatches in
the ﬁnal hash.
There are cases where two blocks are created approximately at the same time, this is
called fork. By using the timestamp of the block header, it can be identiﬁed which block
was created ﬁrst and connect that block to the chain, while the subsequent blocks are
linked to the accepted one. Forking is linked with the fact the history of Blockchain is pre-
served by distinct parties obligated to follow common rules. There are times when parties
are not coming to an agreement, leading to the creation of alternative chains. While the
majority of those forks are temporary some of those are perpetual. In a distributed sys-
tem, such as Blockchain, when fast conformity is not achieved from the members, this
creates temporary forks. On the other hand, permanent forks are used to add new char-
acteristics to the Blockchain (protocol changes), to prevent bugs in the Blockchain that
could be catastrophic, or to reverse hacking effects.
Based on all above, forks can be divided into two categories intentional or acciden-
tal. The latter category happens, as mentioned before, when two miners discover a block
at the same or almost identical times. In this type of fork, one of the chains is becom-
ing longer than the alternative ones since in only one of them the subsequent blocks
are added. When that happens, the network deserts blocks that are not included in the
longest chain (orphaned blocks).
In contrast, intentional forks are modifying the rules of a Blockchain and they can be
divided into two sub-categories:
1. Soft Fork:
This type of fork is a modiﬁcation to the software protocol to make all previously
valid blocks into invalid. The new blocks will be recognised as valid from the old nodes,
making this set of forking backwards compatible. In terms of the miner’s community, soft
forks need only a majority vote to enforce the new rules, which means that the Blockchain
will accommodate network nodes that do not follow the newly applied rules, named old
nodes, and others that accept those rules (Antonopoulos, 2017). Bitcoin and Ethereum
Blockchains have used soft forks in order to implement new backwards compatible func-
tionalities.

newest versions are valid, meaning that a indeﬁnite divergence is created for that new

Dimensionality Reduction
Throughout the last decades, numerous techniques were implemented and proposed for
visualising high-dimensional data, a number of those are examined by Oliveira and Lev-
kowitz (2003). Some of those techniques are graph representation of data dimensions as
vertices from (Battista et al., 1994), techniques based on pixels (Keim, 2000), and displays
using iconography by Chernoff (1973). The majority of the techniques mentioned above
deliver tools that can only exhibit data in more than two dimensions, leaving the analy-
sis of those data to the end-user. This attribute, of course, limits the application of these
kinds of techniques signiﬁcantly due to the fact that real-word data are quite complex
and high dimensional.
In contrast, dimensionality reduction methods converts data with many dimensions
X = (x1,x2...,xn) which transforms high - dimensional data into a lower dimensional
space, mainly into two or three dimensional data, which can are easier to be visualised in
a scatter plot. Dimensionality reduction in its core essence, is about reducing the amount
of data, while still keeping data integrity. The purpose of reducing the the dimension of
the data can be for various reasons. High-dimensional data can be computational expen-
sive to compute. High-dimensional data is represented by the large amount of variables
explaining a data point. This can often be difﬁcult to work with, because large compu-
tational power is often needed. Furthermore, it can be hard to visualise the overall data,
==PAGE:39==
Chapter 4. Applied Algorithms
27
as data can at max be visualised by three components, also referred to as 3-dimensional
plots. The lower-dimensional data can ease the computing process, but also help inter-
pret the data, as it is easier to visualise but also overcome fewer dimensions.
Performing dimensionality reduction does not come without its sacriﬁce. Reduc-
ing the dimensions of high-dimensional data introduces the loss of some information.
Lower-dimensional data will not explain 100% if the high-dimensional data. Depend-
ing on the reduction method used, the amount of information explained be the lower-
dimensional space can vary. This has to be taken into consideration, when choosing to
perform dimensionalty reduction and the desired output.
Dimensionality methods are often divided into linear and non-linear methods. Di-
mension reductions can be used for noise reduction, data visualisation, cluster analysis
and other analysis steps.
The usage of dimensionality reduction has different use cases in this thesis. It is used
to perform clustering upon, but also to visualise the effect of the clustering in a two-
dimensional space.
t-distributed stochastic neighbour embedding (t-SNE) - Missing
While PCA, one of the most well-known techniques, belongs to the linear dimensionality
reduction framework, there are a number of nonlinear approaches aiming to maintain
the local structure of the data. Some of those approaches are: stochastic neighbour em-
bedding (G.E. Hinton and Roweis, 2002), curvilinear components analysis (Demartines
and Herault, 1997), Maximum Variance Unfolding (Weinberger et al., 2004), e.t.c. Regard-
less of the robust performance of the above mentioned techniques, it is still difﬁcult for
them to visualise high-dimensional data successfully. According to Maaten and G. Hin-
ton (2008) it is not possible, for most of those techniques, to maintain the structure of the
data in a single map, from a local and global perspective.
T-distrubuted stochastic neighbour embedding (t-SNE; Maaten and G. Hinton, 2008)
is capable of transforming high-dimensional data into a pairwise similarities matrix and
then visualise the similarity data. From a local perspective, it is efﬁcient in capturing the
local structure of data with multiple dimensions, while also divulging the global struc-
ture, like the presence of clusters. The main objective of t-SNE is the minimisation of
divergence between the distribution measuring pairwise similarities of the input data
and the distribution that calculates pairwise similarities in the embedding for the low-
dimensional points.
In this way, t-Distributed stochastic neighbour embedding is able to map to a lower
dimensional level the multi-dimensional data, while also attempting to discover data
patterns by locating observed clusters of multiple-featured data based on similarity. Al-
though, after performing the t-SNE process, it is no longer possible to identify the input
features, and it is not recommended to make any speculation based only on the output
of this method. For that reason, this method is used for visualisation, exploration and, in
this thesis, it is paired together with the clustering techniques.

Clustering
Clustering analysis is the structuring of a pattern arrangement into clusters depending
on similarity. These pattern arrangements are most commonly presented as a multidi-
mensional point in space, or a vector of measurements. The clustering problem arises
normally in a a wide variety of ﬁelds such as ﬁnance, statistics, machine learning, and
==PAGE:46==
Chapter 4. Applied Algorithms
34
pattern recognition (Murty et al., 1999). The problem of ﬁnding groups that are having
similar data points, within a given dataset, is the basis of clustering. These kind of groups
are called clusters and they can be identiﬁed as the regions in a two dimensional space
where a local increase in density of data points is observed compared to other regions. Of
course, data or patterns within a well-created cluster are more similar indistinguishable
compared to data assigned to a different cluster.
Clustering belongs to the unsupervised techniques of machine learning, also known
as unsupervised classiﬁcation, and it has some differences compared to it’s counterpart,
classiﬁcation, in supervised machine learning. The latter technique uses a collection of
labeled or pre-classiﬁed data to classify a new pattern that was unlabeled or new found-
ing. Normally, the already existing patterns are used to train the algorithm into learn the
classes descriptions, which are then used to create labels for the newly identiﬁed pattern.
On the other hand, clustering is an alogrithm that groups into meaningful clusters a col-
lection of unlabeled patterns. This technique is able to discover hidden patterns to the
data set without human intervention, hence the unsupervised machine learning category
it belongs to. This means that category labels are data-driven and they are acquired only
from the data.
Cluster analysis is better described as a task rather than an execution of a single algo-
rithm. This means that clustering can be executed from numerous algorithms that might
diverge signiﬁcantly on their perceptive of what deﬁnes a cluster, as well as, the ways of
identifying them. Popular conceptions of this technique include clusters that their data
are having small distances between them, areas packed with data, or statistical distribu-
tions. For that reason, cluster analysis becomes a multi-objective optimisation problem.
This means that, depending on the deliberated use of the results and data sets, an appro-
priate selection of the algorithm and its parameters has to be executed before performing
cluster analysis. Even though, when executed, the algorithm works without human inter-
vention, clustering still needs a number of iterations, or trial and failure tests to produce
reliable results. Normally, data pre-processing and modiﬁcation of model parameters are
two things that have to be done before performing this type of analysis.
Based on all above, the essence of a cluster cannot be determined precisely, which
leads to the fact that, currently, there are numerous clustering techniques (Estivill-Castro,
2002). Different cluster models are employed from different researchers, leading to dif-
ferent algorithms. The table bellow shows all these cluster models and their conception
of what deﬁnes a cluster. It should be mentioned that in this project 3 of those clustering

Minimum Spanning Tree
In the following section, the graph theory method minimum spanning tree will be dis-
cussed. The minimum spanning tree is graph optimisation problem, which seek to con-
nect all nodes in a network, and where a cycle between nodes are not allowed. The opti-
misation seeks to ﬁnd the shortest path that to connects all nodes. The usage of minimum
spanning tree is to create an initial investment universe that consists of lower correlated
asset, allowing for greater diversiﬁcation and a alternative to the more stochastic diver-
siﬁcation bootstrapping.
A graph is a non-liner data structure that consists of nodes and edges. The minimum
spanning tree is a set of edges in the undirected graph that connects all nodes with no
cycles and has the minimum sum of weights compared to other spanning trees in the
graph. A spanning tree or a minimum spanning tree will always consist of (E-1), where
E is the number of edges in the completed graph.
In the following case a undirected graph is built, using the correlation between assets.
The correlation between asset make up the weight or the cost of using an edge between
==PAGE:50==

two nodes. With this terminology, the shortest distance creating a minimum spanning
tree of a graph constructed from assets an their correlation consists of the least correlated
assets. From the minimum spanning tree, the least correlated asset are the assets that
only have one neighbour or a degree of one, meaning that that node is only connected
to one other node. These one degree nodes make up the least correlated asset potentially
yielding the highest diversiﬁcation, and are used for the optimisation problem.

Chapter 6
Performance Analysis of Portfolio
Diversiﬁcation Trading Strategy
In the previous chapter, the whole asset universe was used to create random portfolios,
which made it possible to investigate the probability of using the Portfolio Diversiﬁca-
tion Index as a risk management strategy. Although, in this chapter, only a subset of that
universe will be utilised for creating portfolios and analyse their performance. All port-
folios created in this section, are subjected to different allocation strategies and, ﬁnally,
they are compared from a performance perspective to a benchmark, which, in this case,
is the MSCI world index.
First of all, the asset universe was reduced, by performing a due diligence analysis.
The reason for that was to have enough price data to carry out the performance analysis.
The output of this analysis reduced signiﬁcantly the asset universe, but the latter was still
quite large, making the calculation time immense.
To further decrease the complexity of the algorithm, the asset universe had to be re-
duced even more. To achieve such a task, a funnel was implemented, to reduce the size
of the universe, by using clustering, an unsupervised machine learning technique, fol-
lowed by a Minimum Spanning Tree algorithm. Funnelling down to a smaller asset uni-
verse helped the algorithm to run more efﬁciently, and, at the same time, produce better
results, while also converging much faster.
The output of the investment funnel was used as the input to a bootstrapping al-
gorithm, responsible to create portfolios for the funneled asset universe. Several tests
were executed to analyse the performance of those portfolios. Furthermore, the time
frame used for the performance tests is six years of data, starting from 01/01/2015 until
01/01/2021. From those six years, the ﬁrst year was used for training and the rest of the
years were used for testing. Finally, it should be noted that those tests were executed for
only ETFs and combinations of ETFs-Cryptos, while at the same time they were divided
into two big test categories: Fixed periods back-testing and Rolling periods back-testing.
6.1
Funnel Implementation
The starting point of the performance analysis is the ETF funnel, which is used to re-
duce the large asset universe of ETFs to match speciﬁed investment requirements. In this

65

be applied to the data types of the ETFs, in order to create the cluster areas mentioned in

whole model. The output variable, that was used to test the results, was the correlation

area tried to explain.

studied variable. DBSCAN was rejected immediately because, in order to show a high
correlation, and therefore explain the dataset in the respective area, it had to discard
many ETFs as outliers, which was not acceptable for the investment funnel in this project.

with each respective studied variable, but Kmeans clustering was able to explain better
all studied areas compared to Hierarchical clustering. For that reason, Kmeans paired
with TSNE was used to explain all clustering areas.

at relative easy to navigate, and guides the user through the funnel. First the user is intro-

an risk. One a portfolio is picked, the user can back-test the portfolio to see how the
performance of the portfolio has performed.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this thesis project, the investment funnel, involving several different algorithms, cre-
ated optimal portfolios based on the strategies followed in Section 6.2. One important
aspect of the funnel is the ability to cluster ETFs based on different meta-data, leading
to a variety of new features that the user could use for ﬁltering. The goal of this thesis
was to implement a funnel framework that allows retail investors to isolate ETFs to suit
their preferences, pick the most diversiﬁed assets from that new universe, and, ﬁnally,
merge that universe with the Cryptocurrencies, forming hybrid and non-hybrid portfo-
lios. This framework could motivate and help individuals to invest their money by using
this alternative rather than the already existing funds or investment packages offered by
banks.
Taking things from the beginning (model selection step), there are several different
approaches for creating a Robo-Advisor similar to this one. There are many different
Robo-Advisers using methods such as MPT, CVaR, etc. In this thesis, the PDI was selected
to assist in identifying a diversiﬁed strategy for managing risk. Moreover, compared to
the conventional Robo-Advisers, this solution provides the possibility for investors to
personalise their investments to match speciﬁc criteria.
As a next step (model veriﬁcation step), the Portfolio Diversiﬁcation Index method
was examined. This analysis helped to understand its effects, a necessary process before
constructing the Robo-Advisor’s algorithm and explaining the output. The results of
Chapter 5 deﬁned the input parameters for the model, such as the number of assets in
the optimal portfolio, number of Cryptocurrencies used, etc. Of course, apart from the
input, understanding the effect of PDI was also important for explaining the performance
results in terms of risk and return. Furthermore, this Chapter also showed that by having
a higher PDI value, the uncertainty of all ﬁnancial measures of a portfolio is reduced.
This means that when a portfolio with a high PDI value is selected, there is a much higher
chance that all its ﬁnancial metrics will be around the market average, reducing the risk
of selecting an outlier portfolio, which could be highly volatile and inefﬁcient.
After having deﬁned the model used for the Robo-Advisor, the third step (person-
alisation step) required the reduction of the asset universe, which would help the algo-
rithm to improve statistical performance and reduce stochasticity. This step is where the
model is differentiating from conventional models because the individualised approach
is modeled. With the use of ETFs metadata, the assets were clustered in many different
==PAGE:101==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
89
categories. These new features would help the user to ﬁlter the universe for matching his
investment criteria.
The task assigned to the fourth step (diversiﬁcation step) was to further reduce this
ﬁltered universe by isolating assets that have the maximum diversiﬁcation potential. Two
approaches were used for this task, Minimum Spanning Tree and PDI-Bootstrap. The
ﬁrst approach created the MST universe, and the second approach the PDI- Bootstrap
universe. These two universes are the input to the optimisation algorithm. It has to
be mentioned that the latter method has been implemented in this thesis. The main idea
behind PDI-Bootstrap was to provide the user with more control over the subset selection
process and, at the same time, combat the fact that MST is not necessarily selecting the
most diversiﬁed assets. A more controlled subset selection gives the ability to the user
to deﬁne the PDI and the ﬁnancial metrics the output universe should have. In contrast,
MST is performing the asset reduction process exclusively based on correlation. Since it
is still in a testing stage, results from the PDI-Bootstrap universe are in Appendix B and
results from MST are in section 6.2 of the main text.
The ﬁnal step of the process (performance analysis step) took the output of the diver-
siﬁcation step, used Bootstrap to generate portfolios, and the PDI model to select optimal
portfolios for each investment period. More speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst 4 steps of the Robo-
Advisor are applied to the initial ETF universe, forming the ﬁnal ETF universe, which
is then combined with Cryptocurrencies. This means that the Robo-Advisor was able to
handle 1126 ETFs and 9 Cryptocurrencies, to form optimal hybrid (ETFs + Cryptos) and
non-hybrid (only ETFs) portfolios for 3 different strategies, which are maximum diver-
siﬁcation, maximum Sharpe Ratio, and 1/N strategy. Lastly, two Robo-Advisers were
tested in this project, one with short-term memory and one with long-term.
Concluding, the strategies implemented for this thesis are showing some interesting
results for both non-hybrid and hybrid portfolios.
For the non-hybrid case, the maxi-
mum Sharpe ratio strategy is performing the best, followed by 1/N, and the last place
is for the maximum diversiﬁcation. This phenomenon is expected since the maximum
PDI strategy is trying to diversify already diversiﬁed and correlated assets. This has al-
ready been argued in Modern Portfolio Theory. Furthermore, all of the strategies are
outperforming MSCI World in terms of return, but at the cost of slightly higher volatility,
while they are similar in Sharpe Ratios.Regarding the hybrid portfolios, the diversiﬁca-
tion model stands out. The maximum PDI model is able to keep volatility lower, also
hindering returns from reaching extremes. While the other models suffer from the high
volatility of Cryptocurrencies, the maximum diversiﬁcation is controlling and penalis-
ing the allocation to volatile assets, because they yield lower PDI. Also, according to the
results, by allowing for more diversiﬁcation in Cryptocurrencies, the risk-adjusted re-
turn is almost doubled compared to non-hybrid portfolios. Finally, the results of this
thesis project provide the necessary motivation to further improve the framework. Be-
cause on limited research on PDI, the results of the model should not be considered as
indisputable, but rather used for further research.

8.1
Limitation and Future Work
It cannot be forgotten, that many startup and large investment banks have large data-
science teams and quants modelling algorithms and Robo-Advisers in order to capture
market share and grow the customer base. These institutions also have large funds for
this, and lately Saxo Bank has made even greater pushes into this area to grow the cus-
tomers base. By this, it is also necessary to acknowledge, that similar solution or models
exists, using similar algorithms and techniques to enrich the customer experience.
One of the areas that needs more research is the minimum spanning tree. That is be-
cause when a minimum spanning tree is constructed, it measures the minimum distance
between the assets, and takes assets that have one degree. The potential error that needs
to be researched further, is the fact that the minimum spanning tree does not punish the
correlation between the chosen assets, meaning that these assets could still be correlated,
even tough the minimum spanning tree has identiﬁed the interconnection between assets
with the lowest correlation. Alternative graph methods can also be introduced, to select
a sub-graph based on speciﬁc correlation criteria.
Moreover, the Portfolio Diversiﬁcation Bootstrapping can be enhanced or optimised,
so all possible combination of portfolios can be searched, ﬁnding the assets that yield
the highest diversiﬁcation, for the optimisation process. Here, genetic algorithms can
be explored as a possible solutions to this, enabling optimised searching, allowing to
capture the full area of combinations, by solving the Knapsack problem. Furthermore, the
naive optimisation can be greatly increased to increase performance and stability of the
model. Also longer training periods can be utilised to give the model more data to work
with, for better market explanation over time. The asset allocation can also be further
developed allowing for more iteration to optimise for several parameters, insuring both
diversiﬁcation and further decrease of volatility. Moreover, scenario generation could
be implemented to test the portfolio diversiﬁcation strategy in a different context, to see
how that would perform.
A signiﬁcant limitation of the thesis is the length of the data-set. While the only lim-
itation of the data was the chosen time period from 2015-2020 which limited the amount
of Cryptocurrencies and ETFs. The time period was kept, as years of data was need for
the analysis part. This reduced the amount of new ETFs and Cryptocurrencies a lot as
the market seen in Table 5.1 has more then tripled throughout the period. The length of
the data-set need to have some length, as the training period is 52 weeks. Longer train-
ing periods can be tested to see if there is an increase in performance. While the train
period does not consist of any downturns, the test period experiences the ﬁrst Cryp-
tocurrency bubble and both asset universes experience the COVID-19 crash. Further, as
the results show, the portfolio diversiﬁcation index strategy shows signs of controlling
highly volatile assets, while the market back-testing has been performed in has been in
uptrend all the time, it could be interesting to see how the strategy performs in different
markets, such as 2000’s. Another drawback that can be addressed is the method of posi-
tioning in Cryptocurrecies, as the current model is static to the initial picks. While that is
==PAGE:103==
Chapter 8. Conclusion
91
enough for ETFs which cover large segments and tracks different indexes, that is not the
same for Cryptocurrencies. A method for re-picking Cryptocurries can be implemented,
as this would also be able to pick up newly arrived Cryptocurrenices.
As mentioned in the beginning the naive strategy and the simpliﬁed diversiﬁcation
framework is not without compromises. However, the goal was not build a state of the
art Robo-Advisor, but explore the possibilities to enable retail investors to make more
data driven investments, and there is room for improvements. While the intention was
to build a simple framework allowing for a more individual and personalised portfolio
construction. A positive outcome from our naive algorithm is that it is able to compete
with MSCI World in terms of Sharpe Ratio and returns, giving some validity to a diver-
siﬁcation strategy that can be easily interpreted by investors.

PDI-Bootstrap
In this Appendix, as mentioned in the main text, a presentation of the universe created by
PDI-Bootstrapping is going to be done. This PDI-Bootstrapping is an alternative method
to the MST model, so instead of using MST to the funnelled down universe, we are us-
ing this approach. This method gives the ﬂexibility to the user to decide the number of
assets that will be responsible for portfolio creation, as well as the total PDI that the asset
universe will have.
All in all, in this Appendix a study of the output of the algorithm is performed, but
in this case it is for a new asset universe. The results are again divided into two sections,
one for Fixed and one for Rolling periods. Both subsections contain all three strategies
that were studied in this thesis, for all kind of portfolios, such as hybrid or non hybrid.
B.1
Performance Analysis of Fixed Periods
In this section, again, the back-testing performance for ﬁxed periods is going to be anal-
ysed. The model is using the same quarterly periods to allocate assets for a time horizon
starting from 2016 until end of 2020. Using ﬁxed periods means again that the algorithm
has a short-term memory, since it is trained only on last the last period data.
This section starts with a high-level comparison between the three 3 optimal portfo-
lios (ETFs only, ETFs with one cryptocurrency allowed, and ETFs with two cryptocurren-
cies allowed) and the MSCI World. This comparison is executed for all 3 strategies that
were studied in this thesis. Following, all three strategies results for performance and
asset allocation are presented and discussed. The section concludes with a ﬁgure that
shows all portfolios in the risk-return graph, where the reader can compare the strategies
with ETFs.
The Table B.1 bellow contains all different strategies and portfolios for ﬁxed periods.
This table follows the same format as the one in the main text and it presents the statistical
performance for all cases including the benchmark.

ETFs only
By looking at the case where only ETFs are included in the portfolio, it is clear that this
universe follows the same patterns as the MST universe, in terms of performance. This
is happening for all 3 strategies listed in the table. Although, all cases in this universe
are having better statistical performance results than the MST universe. This behaviour
might be due to the fact that the PDI-Bootstrap algorithm is seeking to ﬁnd the asset
universe with the highest Sharpe Ratio within a speciﬁed diversiﬁcation range. This is
not the case for MST, which is only trying to ﬁnd the most distant assets in the universe.
Regarding the comparison of the three strategies, of course, the maximum diversiﬁ-
cation case has the lowest Sharpe ratio compared to the other two. The reason behind
that is that ETFs are already diversiﬁed assets and further diversiﬁcation is actually di-
luting returns. Another interesting observation is the difference between PDI and 1/N
allocation. While in longer periods of training these two have identical Sharpe Ratios,
when the training period is shortened, this behaviour changes. This can also be seen by
Figure B.1, where it is clear that the max PDI strategy is not following the same alloca-
tion as 1/N. Furthermore, the best Sharpe Ratio is coming from the strategy of maximum
Sharpe Ratio. Looking at the allocation graph of that strategy, as well as the average PDI
on Table B.1 it is clear that, in most periods, this strategy is investing in only two assets.
