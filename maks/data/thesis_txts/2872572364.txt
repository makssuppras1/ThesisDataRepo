Abstract
This thesis investigates how public, private, and hybrid funding pathways influence the
success of sustainable startups. Given that these ventures face unique challenges, such
as long development horizons and market failures, the source of their funding is a critical
strategic choice. This study examines success across three dimensions: the ability to
secure follow­on funding, the likelihood and speed of a commercial exit, and long­term
operational survival.
Drawing on a dataset of 1,638 ventures from Crunchbase, a quantitative analysis was per­
formed using logistic regression and Cox proportional hazards models to test the influence
of each funding archetype. The findings reveal that the presence of a market­oriented in­
vestor, in either a purely private or a hybrid syndicate, is the strongest predictor of achiev­
ing market­based success, including securing larger amounts of follow­on funding and
accelerating the path to an exit. Conversely, a reliance on public funding is associated
with a longer, non­exit­focused trajectory. A critical distinction emerged within public fund­
ing: non­dilutive grants act as a powerful certification of quality for future investors, while
dilutive public equity can send a negative signal.
The research concludes that a startup’s funding choice is a foundational decision that
aligns it with a distinct path toward either rapid commercialization or long­term, policy­
supported endurance, offering key strategic insights for entrepreneurs and policymakers.

Introduction
The global community faces unprecedented environmental and societal challenges, from
anthropogenic climate change and species extinction to resource depletion and social
inequality (Calvin et al. 2023). In response, there has been a growing recognition of
the urgent need for sustainable development ­ a concept famously defined by the World
Commission on Environment and Development (1987) as ”development that meets the
needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their
own needs.” At its core, this definition presents a twofold challenge: meeting the essential
needs of the world’s poor while operating within the limitations that current technology and
social organization pose on the environment’s capacity to provide for present and future
generations.
To address these pressing issues, ambitious global goals have been established, in­
cluding the Sustainable Development Goals (UN General Assembly 2015) and the Paris
Agreement (2016), which set clear timeframes and far­reaching targets for sustainable
development. Achieving these goals necessitates a profound shift in both consumer and
producer behavior (Thøgersen 2021), a societal pivot that is already leading to a greater
demand for environmentally friendly products and services, as well as a stronger dedica­
tion to sustainable practices (Gast et al. 2017).
While this momentum towards sustainability creates opportunities for established firms to
minimize their environmental impact (Gast et al. 2017), the scale and urgency of today’s
sustainability challenges call for more than incremental change — they demand radical
innovation. Consequently, a new generation of sustainable startups is emerging as pivotal
actors, devising inventive solutions to address crucial issues like pollution, the depletion of
resources, and the propagation of disease (Tiba et al. 2021). These ventures embody the
process of sustainable entrepreneurship, defined by Greco and De Jong (2017, p.14) as
”the discovery, creation, and exploitation of entrepreneurial opportunities that contribute
to sustainability by generating social and environmental gains for others in society.”
These ventures exemplify this entrepreneurial model by tying their commercial success
directly to positive results for the environment and humanity, which in turn creates value
for many different stakeholders (Nunes et al. 2022). Spanning diverse sectors, they often
achieve this by translating scientific research into scalable solutions that challenge exist­
ing systems and tackle the most pressing societal and environmental issues of our time
(Tiba et al. 2021). This distinct focus on impact, amplified by shifting consumer trends
and national commitments to a greener economy (Peng and Pan 2024), has fueled a sig­
nificant increase in the formation and popularity of sustainable startups in recent years
(Karani and Mshenga 2021).

However, their crucial role in the sustainable transition (Horne and Fichter 2022) does not
shield them from significant and unique hurdles that distinguish their journey from main­
stream innovation. Their technologies are often disruptive, requiring expensive, long­
horizon development before they can generate revenue. This creates a challenging fi­
nancial profile characterized by high capital costs, uncertain revenue streams, significant
information gaps between founders and financiers, and shifting policy and regulatory bar­
riers (Mukherjee et al. 2024).
These heightened risks and market failures create a difficult funding environment for con­
ventional private investors, who naturally allocate capital where it can most efficiently
maximize financial returns. As a result, private funding has historically been insufficient
to meet the financing demands of sustainable innovation (Clark et al. 2018). For instance,
a 2014 analysis of climate finance revealed that private investment constituted only a frac­
tion of the total capital deployed, even as forecasts predicted trillions of dollars in future
climate investment opportunities. This shortfall created a clear and persistent ”funding
gap” that threatened to stifle the growth of many promising ventures (Clark et al. 2018).
To address this market failure, government intervention became a necessary response.
Public funding programs were created with the express purpose of addressing the pri­
vate market’s limitations, investing in underfunded technologies and creating a fairer en­
vironment for innovative startups (Berger, Criscuolo, et al. 2025). Through early­stage
investment, these public entities send a powerful signal to the private sector; by reducing
significant technical risks and offering a credible ”technology certification,” they actively
encourage subsequent private investment (NREL 2003).
This intervention, however, is not always direct. Governments have developed more nu­
anced, hybrid strategies with the express purpose of ”crowding in” private capital (NREL
2003). The core principle is to use public development finance to guide and attract much
larger sums of private investment toward areas of public good, such as sustainable tech­
nologies (Cull et al. 2024). This strategy has given rise to hybrid funding models, where
public entities and private investors co­fund startups. In these arrangements, the gov­
ernment can de­risk projects for private partners by offering subsidized equity, thereby
inducing their participation in ventures with high societal benefits (Bai et al. 2021).
The scale of this public­private entanglement is significant. Recent statistics from the
European venture capital market have highlighted that government bodies have become
a substantial source of capital for Venture Capitalists, at times representing the single
largest source of funds raised (Bai et al. 2021; Bertoni and Tykvová 2015).
This deep entanglement of public and private capital has created a complex funding land­
scape for sustainable startups, which can now follow distinct public, private, or hybrid
pathways, each with its own underlying logic and goals
While the need for governmental intervention is clear, its implementation and ultimate effi­
ciency have not escaped scrutiny, sparking a long­standing academic debate most promi­

nently introduced by Lerner 1999. Critics of these programs raise several valid concerns:
despite their potential to build innovation ecosystems, they risk displacing private invest­
ment, funding projects with limited economic value, or encouraging inefficient practices
within the ventures they support (Berger, Criscuolo, et al. 2025; Schulte and Birkenmeier

However, while much valuable research has assessed this dynamic, it has largely fo­
cused on the performance of singular instruments or specific programs. Studies have
examined the role of government in venture capital finance (Brander, Du, et al. 2015), the
success of public­private syndication deals (Zhang 2018), and the historical performance
of state­owned development banks (Gutierrez et al. 2011). This instrument­by­instrument
analysis, while essential, can produce a fragmented view, making it difficult to understand
the broader strategic implications of a startup’s complete funding pathway.
This thesis, therefore, adopts a more holistic and nuanced perspective. First, instead of
evaluating isolated instruments, it compares the influence of broad funding archetypes—
private, public, and hybrid—to understand how these entire pathways shape startup tra­
jectories. Second, it moves beyond a single definition of success. Acknowledging that a
rapid exit may be a triumph for a private investor but a failure for a public agency seek­
ing long­term impact, this research assesses performance across multiple dimensions,
including follow­on funding, exit, and long­term survival.
This integrated approach, which respects the distinct mission of each funding model, leads
directly to the central research question guiding this thesis:
How do private, public, and hybrid funding archetypes distinctly influence the success of
sustainable startups, when success is measured by the likelihood of securing follow­on
funding, achieving an exit, and ensuring long­term survival?
To answer this question, the remainder of this thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2
builds the theoretical foundation. It applies relevant theories to discuss in depth why fund­
ing is so critical for sustainable startups, defines the underlying private, public, and hybrid
archetypes based on their motivations and the non­financial support they provide, and de­
rives three testable hypotheses from this framework. Chapter 3 outlines the quantitative
methodology, introducing the underlying Crunchbase dataset, explaining the extensive
measures taken to clean and prepare the data for analysis, and detailing the statistical
models used to test each hypothesis. Chapter 4 presents the empirical findings, begin­
ning with a descriptive overview of the sample and then sequentially reporting the model
results for each of the three hypotheses on follow­on funding, exit, and long­term survival.
Finally, Chapter 5 provides a comprehensive discussion that interprets these findings and
links them back to the theoretical framework, and Chapter 6 concludes by summarizing
the contributions, implications and limitations of the research.

2
The Logics of Funding: A Theoretical
Framework for Sustainable Startups
To understand why different funding models might lead to different outcomes, it is essen­
tial to first build a robust theoretical framework. This chapter establishes that foundation.
It begins by exploring the unique operational and financial challenges inherent to sus­
tainable ventures, using Resource Dependence Theory to explain their profound reliance
on external funding. Subsequently, it introduces Institutional Logics to deconstruct the
funding landscape into three distinct archetypes — private, public, and hybrid — each
governed by different motivations. By analyzing the interplay between startup needs and
investor logics, this chapter culminates in a conceptual framework and a set of testable
hypotheses that guide the empirical analysis to follow.
2.1
The Unique Challenges of Sustainable Startups
The pursuit of widespread societal benefits exposes sustainable startups to a unique set
of hurdles, which can be broadly understood as either external market barriers or internal
operational constraints. At the external level, these ventures must often contend with sig­
nificant market inefficiencies. Many sustainable innovations present a systemic challenge
to market economies because they exhibit the characteristics of public goods. Their ben­
efits, which often manifest as improvements to the collective welfare, are non­excludable
and non­depletable, placing them in direct contrast with traditional private goods. This is
especially true for innovations concerning the environmental commons, where property
rights are ill­defined. As a result, market forces alone cannot efficiently allocate resources
for their development, leading to their systemic under­provision and a failure to capture
their full societal value (Cumming, Henriques, et al. 2016).
Compounding the challenge of market inefficiencies, a limited public understanding of the
broader benefits of sustainable products and services can further devalue sustainable
business practices. Consumers often struggle to fully appreciate the environmental and
societal gains of sustainable offerings compared to traditional alternatives. This necessi­
tates that sustainable businesses frequently invest in educating potential customers about
the long­term benefits of their innovations (Gast et al. 2017).
This situation gives rise to a policy paradox for sustainable startups. On one hand, they
require an enabling policy environment (streamlined permitting processes or stable car­
bon pricing), created through targeted interventions, to overcome market inefficiencies
and translate their societal benefits into market viability (Temmes et al. 2021). On the
other hand, instead of this needed stability, many startups contend with unstable regu­

latory frameworks and frequent policy shifts, which significantly impact financial viability
and deter long­term investment (Mukherjee et al. 2024).
Shifting from the external market barriers, the second category of hurdles is rooted in
the internal operational and financial profile of sustainable startups themselves. Their
ambitious pursuit of disruptive solutions often relies on emerging technologies demand­
ing extensive, long­term investments (Mukherjee et al. 2024). This creates a challenging
financial position defined by lengthy development horizons, high upfront capital require­
ments, and uncertain revenue streams (Kennedy et al. 2024).
These combined factors place startups in the precarious ”valley of death” — a critical
phase between inception and early commercialization where risk is exceptionally high
(Kennedy et al. 2024). In this phase, a knowledge gap between founders and investors
creates major hurdles for securing funding (Mukherjee et al. 2024). As a consequence,
capital is often allocated to more established, lower­risk infrastructure projects, while
transformative green technologies from small and medium­sized businesses remain un­
derfunded (Mukherjee et al. 2024).
Taken together, the external market barriers and internal operational constraints discussed
above underscore a profound reliance on outside support for sustainable startups. To nav­
igate this complex operating environment and achieve their dual objectives of commercial
viability and societal impact, these ventures depend on a variety of external actors and
resources, with funding being particularly critical. This heightened dependency sets the
stage for a deeper examination of their strategic interactions with resource providers.
2.2
Theoretical Lens 1: Resource Dependence and the
Criticality of Funding
The pronounced challenges faced by sustainable startups highlight their critical need for
external resources. This section introduces Resource Dependence Theory (RDT) as a
lens to understand these dependencies, with a particular focus on funding as a pivotal
resource and investors as key suppliers of this resource.
2.2.1
Strategic Dependencies and the Role of Funding
Resource Dependence Theory, introduced by Pfeffer and Salancik (1978), provides the
theoretical lens to understand this dynamic. The theory’s core premise is that organi­
zations are dependent on their environment for critical resources, which influences their
behavior and forces them to strategically manage these dependencies to ensure survival
and success (Nienhüser 2008; Hillman et al. 2009). This dependence is particularly per­
tinent for nascent organizations like startups, which must actively acquire resources from
their environment to operate and survive (Roundy and Bayer 2019). Their success hinges
on effectively managing these external relationships to secure support — which encom­
passes not only capital but also the specialized knowledge, mentorship, and partnerships

Viewing sustainable startups through the lens of RDT reveals a network of critical exter­
nal dependencies that extend far beyond simple financing needs. Their dual mission—
balancing commercial viability with socio­environmental objectives—intensifies this re­

ventures becomes contingent on their ability to manage a specific set of strategic depen­
dencies:
• Dependency on the Policy Environment. The public­good nature of many sustain­
able innovations creates market failures where their full societal value is not cap­
tured by private actors (Cumming, Henriques, et al. 2016). Observed through the
lens of RDT, this inherent market limitation, often compounded by unstable regu­
latory frameworks, translates into a direct dependency on policy actors (Temmes
et al. 2021). Navigating this fluctuating policy landscape thus becomes a critical
capability for survival and growth (Mukherjee et al. 2024).
• Dependency on Patient Capital. The financial profile of sustainable startups, typi­
cally defined by long development horizons and high upfront costs, places them in
the precarious ”valley of death” (Kennedy et al. 2024; Mukherjee et al. 2024). In
RDT terms, this is not just a generic need for capital, but a critical dependency on a
specific and scarce resource: ”patient” investors who are willing to supply financial
support under conditions of high risk and delayed returns.
• Dependency on Legitimacy and Validation. The significant information asymme­
try between founders and financiers presents a major hurdle to securing invest­
ment (Karsai 2018). RDT provides a framework for understanding this challenge
not merely as a communication problem, but as a critical dependency on acquiring
intangible resources. From this viewpoint, gaining legitimacy and third­party valida­
tion becomes a strategic imperative to signal the venture’s quality and potential to
a skeptical market (Islam et al. 2018; Karsai 2018).
RDT thus frames the journey of a sustainable startup as an exercise in strategically man­
aging the heightened dependencies—on policy, patient capital, and legitimacy—that arise
from its dual mission. While startups must secure various resources to navigate this en­
vironment, funding stands out as the most pivotal, offering the potential to address these
specific dependencies. Therefore, to understand how sustainable ventures can satisfy
their complex needs, we must first unpack the nature of funding itself. The following
section will do just that, defining funding not merely as capital, but as a critical, multi­
dimensional resource.

2.2.2
The Role of Funding as a Critical, Multi­Dimensional Resource
To understand how funding addresses the complex dependencies of sustainable startups,
it must be viewed as a multi­dimensional resource package. This package encompasses
two primary dimensions. The most evident remains the tangible component: capital. The
amount of financial capital a startup secures directly dictates its operational runway and
ability to pursue growth, making it a significant predictor of survival for new firms (Coleman
et al. 2013). However, this tangible capital is almost always accompanied by a second,
intangible dimension comprising non­monetary support mechanisms.
The nature of this intangible support is intrinsically linked to an investor’s underlying moti­
vations — be it purely financial gain, a specific policy objective, or a blend of both. These
resources are not passive benefits; they are strategic tools actively deployed by investors
to steer a venture toward their desired outcomes. This active involvement can be under­
stood through two key facets identified by Hellmann and Puri (2002): first, support, where
investors take actions that benefit the company (e.g., aiding in recruitment or strategy),
and second, control, where they can influence decisions to maximize the firm’s value,
even if it conflicts with a founder’s preferences.
Viewing this through the lens of RDT highlights why this distinction is so important. Given
their heightened dependencies on specific external factors — such as policy stability, pa­
tient capital, and market legitimacy — the intangible support a sustainable startup receives
is arguably as crucial as the capital itself. This suggests that a venture’s success may
hinge on its ability to align with funding providers whose strategic support mechanisms
are equipped to address these unique challenges. It is therefore essential to examine
how different funding institutions vary in the tangible and intangible resources they offer,
and consequently, how they are suited to foster the growth of sustainable ventures.
2.3
Theoretical Lens 2: Institutional Logics and Funding
Archetypes
The previous section established why sustainable startups have distinct needs and framed
funding as the critical, multi­dimensional resource for addressing them. However, the
world of funding is not uniform as it encompasses a broad scope of providers with dif­
ferent backgrounds, goals, and investing mechanisms. To understand how these distinct
modalities influence startup performance across varied notions of ’success’, it is first cru­
cial to map the spectrum of available funding types and the institutional logics that govern
them.
To understand why different types of investors operate with such divergent goals, this the­
sis draws on the concept of institutional logics, which was first introduced by Alford and
Friedland in 1985. In this context, an institutional logic can be thought of as the ”unwrit­
ten rulebook” — the set of socially constructed practices, assumptions, and beliefs—that
guides the behavior of actors in a field (Friedland and Alford 1991). In essence, these
THE INFLUENCE OF PUBLIC, PRIVATE, AND HYBRID FUNDING ON SUSTAINABILITY STARTUP
SUCCESS

==PAGE:18==
frameworks shape what is considered rational and legitimate action for a group (Thornton
and Ocasio 2008), whether that group is a government agency or a venture capital fund.
While its origins lie in describing broad societal contradictions, institutional logics are now
frequently used in funding and investment research to explain the distinct behaviors of
market actors (e.g. Pahnke et al. 2015; Xiao and Au 2020). Consequently, the theory
provides a fitting framework for analyzing the fundamental differences among the funding
archetypes in this study.
Applying the theory of institutional logics to the investment landscape reveals two domi­
nant frameworks that govern the funders central to this thesis: a market logic and a state
logic.
The market logic, which primarily guides private investors, is rooted in competition and fi­
nancial return. Under this framework, relationships are shaped by the competitive pursuit
of the most promising ventures, and power is determined by market dynamics and cor­
porate control. Legitimacy is conferred almost exclusively through profitability or a clear
trajectory towards a lucrative exit; in short, success is defined by maximizing financial
value (Thornton and Ocasio 2008).
In contrast, the state logic primarily guides public entities and is oriented towards collec­
tive benefit and policy achievement. Under this democratic philosophy, the primary con­
cern is contributing to the common good, often by correcting market failures or advancing
specific policy goals. Legitimacy is therefore conferred not by profit, but by adherence to
public rules and a venture’s contribution to broader societal objectives, such as regional
development or technological advancement (Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury 2012).
These distinct market and state logics give rise to the three funding archetypes central
to this thesis: private, public, and hybrid funding. Each archetype operates with different
motivations and, as a result, provides a unique combination of financial capital and the
critical non­monetary support mechanisms discussed previously. They can be defined as
follows:
• Private funding. Guided primarily by a market logic, this category comprises actors
whose main motivation is financial return, although an expanding segment pursues
explicit impact objectives. It includes venture capital and private equity funds, angel
investors, corporations, and dedicated impact­investment vehicles.
• Public funding. Guided by a state logic, these investors are typically mission­
oriented and deploy resources in line with governmental policy goals. Exemplars in­
clude government agencies, national or multilateral development banks, government­
owned venture­capital entities, and sovereign­wealth funds with developmental man­
dates.
• Hybrid funding. This archetype represents a formal blend of the two logics, com­
bining public and private resources—through co­investment syndicates or other
blended­finance structures—to mobilize private capital toward projects that advance

2.3.1
Private Funding: Profit­Driven Investment Dynamics
Operating according to the principles of a market logic, private funding represents one
of the most significant and diverse sources of external financing available to startups. It
refers to investments and support sourced from private­sector entities, including individual
investors, private companies, institutional investors, and investment firms (Cumming and
Hammer 2025).
Consistent with this logic’s focus on competition and profit, investment decisions within
private funding are typically mainly driven by profit­oriented objectives, risk­return profiles,
and potential strategic alignment. Startups, being inherently uncertain and risky ventures,
rely heavily on these external funds to get off the ground. To reward the risk investors,
undertake in financing such high­risk projects, they expect a return that appropriately
compensates for the uncertainties (Andrieu 2013).
While the primary motivation is financial return, an expanding segment of private capital
specifically targets sustainable startups. These so­called impact investors actively seek
ventures that can deliver measurable environmental and social gains alongside finan­
cial ones. This reflects a more complex motivation where the traditional market logic is
blended with principles of social or environmental value creation, aligning more closely
with the dual objectives of sustainable entrepreneurs (Trelstad 2016). This shift is not
solely fueled by the aim “to do good”, but also based on the believe that sustainability
can be a vital strategic benefit and can contribute to maximizing the total value of the
investments (Bugg­Levine and Emerson 2011).
Investment Instruments of Private Funding
An investment instrument is the specific legal and financial vehicle through which the
tangible component of funding — the capital — is provided to a startup. These instru­
ments fundamentally define the startup’s obligations and the investor’s relationship to the
venture. The primary distinction is between equity­based (dilutive) financing, where cap­
ital is exchanged for ownership, and debt­based (non­dilutive) financing, where capital is
loaned and must be repaid. While early­stage ventures can also rely on less institutional­
ized sources like personal funds, angel investors, or crowdfunding platforms, this analysis
focuses on the more formalized instruments typically employed by institutional investors.
Equity Financing
Equity financing is the most dominant instrument for high­growth startups and the purest

expression of the market logic. It involves exchanging capital for an ownership share
(Nazara et al. 2023), a process that directly aligns the interests of investors and founders
toward a common goal: increasing the company’s value for a lucrative exit event such
as an acquisition or initial public offering (IPO) (Metrick and Yasuda 2021). The primary
institutional actors employing this model are:
• Venture Capital (VC) Firms: Formal investment institutions that manage pooled cap­
ital to invest in a portfolio of high­growth startups, acquiring a significant equity stake
with the objective of generating high­multiple financial returns for their fund (Metrick
and Yasuda 2021).
• Corporate Venture Capital (CVC): A specialized arm of a large corporation that
makes equity investments to generate financial returns while also gaining strate­
gic advantages for the parent company, such as insight into new technologies or
potential acquisitions (Dushnitsky and Lenox 2006).
• Private Equity (PE) Firms: Investment firms that typically acquire stakes in more
mature companies but may provide ”growth equity” to later­stage, proven startups
to finance expansion (Gilligan 2021).
Debt Financing
As a form of non­dilutive funding, debt financing involves a loan that must be repaid with
interest, meaning the startup does not give up ownership (Nazara et al. 2023). While
still driven by a market logic of seeking returns, this instrument is more common for com­
panies at later stages with predictable cash flows, as it is less flexible and exposes the
investor to less upside than equity (Oranburg 2020). Unlike equity investors, traditional
debt providers like banks do not typically share in the venture’s growth.
An increasingly prominent instrument that bridges the gap between debt and equity is
convertible debt. This starts as a non­dilutive loan but is designed to convert into equity
at a later funding stage, providing flexibility for both startups and investors in valuing an
early­stage company (Chaplinsky and Becker 2020).
Finally, it is important to note that an investor’s motivation is distinct from their choice
of instrument. For instance, impact investments, which specifically target ventures with
measurable social and environmental goals, can be deployed through any of these vehi­
cles — equity, debt, or composite finance — with varying expectations for financial returns
(Ormiston et al. 2015).

2.3.2
Public Funding: Policy­driven Investment and Social Objectives
Alongside the successful private funding industry, public funding avenues have emerged
as an essential source for early­stage startups in recent years. Guided by a state logic,
public funding refers to capital provided by government entities to support entrepreneurial
ventures, particularly those that may not attract sufficient private investment due to higher
risks or slower financial returns (Schulte and Birkenmeier 2024). The primary goal of this
logic is not to capture immediate financial gains, but to achieve broader policy objectives
and generate significant positive externalities—societal spillover benefits like cleaner en­
ergy or improved public health (Bai et al. 2021).
This focus on societal benefits makes public funding particularly critical for sustainable
startups. As established in Section 2.1, the unique challenges these ventures face ­ such
as long investment horizons and high technical risk ­ often deter private capital (Cumming
and Johan 2008; Berger and Hottenrott 2021). These obstacles can create a significant fi­
nancing gap where private investment alone is insufficient to meet demand. Public funding
therefore steps in as a corrective measure, providing the crucial bridge financing needed
to help startups achieve initial viability and attract subsequent private investment (NREL
2003). By filling this gap, governments can stimulate innovation, foster regional devel­
opment, and advance strategic policy goals like decarbonization (Cumming, Knill, et al.
2025; Pierrakis and Saridakis 2019), ultimately facilitating the transition of these startups
into scalable businesses that drive long­term economic and technological advancement

Investment Instruments of Public Funding
As with private funding, the investment instruments used by public funders govern the
tangible transfer of capital. However, the nature of these instruments is fundamentally
different, reflecting the distinct goals of the state logic. In direct alignment with this logic’s
focus on fostering long­term innovation over seeking immediate financial returns, public
funding places a notable emphasis on non­dilutive mechanisms. By providing capital
without requiring founders to give up equity, this approach reduces the financial burden
on early­stage startups, allowing them to focus on high­risk, long­term R&D. The most
common forms are outlined below.
Grants
Grants are arguably the most common form of public support. As a non­dilutive instru­
ment, this funding allows startups to develop and implement their ideas without the pres­
sure of repayment or equity exchange, thus reducing their financial burden (Fini et al.
2023). They are crucial for technology startups facing prohibitively high costs for R&D
and prototyping (Okunnuga et al. 2024), allowing them to focus on innovation and long­
term vision without worrying about cash flow in the critical early stages (Mueller 2023).
This support is substantiated by empirical evidence showing that public grants increase
R&D activity and lead to higher innovation output. Importantly, these funds do not ”crowd
out” private R&D investment but rather complement it, enhancing a firm’s overall capacity
to conduct research and bring new technologies to market (Errico et al. 2024).
These grants are typically administered by specialized government funding agencies. Op­
erating at federal, state, or local levels, these agencies are an essential pillar for policy­
makers in almost every developed country, though they vary in size and structure (Ross
2000; Smits and Denis 2014). Their core function is to execute policy by funding research
and innovation, and they have increasingly oriented their support towards strategic goals
such as advancing sustainable development (Mobjörk and Linnér 2006) and supporting
the direct implementation of science into practice (Smits and Denis 2014)
Loans, Subsidies and Tax Credits
These are other forms of primarily non­dilutive support designed to ease the financial bur­
den on startups in capital­intensive or high­risk industries (Mueller 2023) and are often
tied to specific goals, such as the aforementioned boost of sustainable technologies or
advancement of research and development. Subsidized loans, often offered at below­
market interest rates, provide access to capital at a lower cost, while tax credits can sig­
nificantly reduce R&D expenses (Cull et al. 2024).
Central to the deployment of these instruments is the development bank, a government­
backed institution with a mandate to advance economic growth. They often fulfill this
mission by providing credit in situations local capital markets prove inadequate. Beyond

Equity Financing
In recent years, some public funders have begun to adopt instruments that mirror those
of the private sector, most notably through equity financing. However, even when taking
an ownership stake—a hallmark of the market logic—these public entities continue to
operate under the principles of the state logic.
Most commonly referred to as Government­backed venture capital, this has become an

an ownership stake in the company through these investments, they are not governed
by the same pressures as private VCs: Unlike conventional venture capital, they are
less constrained by aggressive profit targets, often operate without a specific timeline for
divestment, and enjoy freedom from the need to generate rapid returns (Alperovych et

growth startups in strategic sectors like clean energy, healthcare, or technology (Berger,
Dechezleprêtre, et al. 2024).

can deter private investment. To achieve this, government­backed funding offers more

network access and mentorship, all of which are essential for their long­term development
and success.

Public funding is uniquely positioned to combat the information asymmetry that deters

motives, their vetting process is often perceived as impartial and rigorous. Consequently,

it receives a powerful signal of credibility. This “stamp of approval” validates the venture’s
quality and potential for the entire market, including partners and customers (Okunnuga

2.3.3
Hybrid Funding: Synthesizing Goals and Mitigating Constraints
Theoretically, hybrid funding represents a formal arena where the previously discussed
market and state logics are intentionally blended. It is a structured collaboration between
public and private entities that seeks to create a powerful synergy by combining the ”best
of both worlds”: the market logic’s discipline and profit­driven efficiency with the state
logic’s patient, mission­oriented, and de­risking capacity. The rationale for its emergence
is best understood by examining the inherent constraints each funding type presents when
operating in isolation.
Strict adherence to a market logic provides crucial expertise but can place startups on
a ’tight leash’ through its intense focus on short­term milestones and financial returns

SUCCESS
15
==PAGE:26==
(Gompers and Lerner 2001). The use of staged capital infusions, while protecting the
investor, creates a dependency that can limit a founder’s autonomy and strategic flexi­
bility (Sahlman 1990). Furthermore, a startup’s reliance on an investor’s knowledge and
network can lead to conflicts over control or vision (Hallen et al. 2014).
On the other hand, a pure state logic carries its own challenges. Wholly government­
backed programs are often hampered by bureaucratic inefficiency, political pressures,
and administrative red tape that can significantly delay and complicate the allocation of
capital (Brander, Du, et al. 2015; Karsai 2018). Moreover, the public fund managers
dispensing this capital are often civil servants whose compensation is not tied to a ven­
ture’s success, which can lead to a lack of performance­based incentives and urgency
compared to their private­sector counterparts (Leleux and Surlemont 2003). These chal­
lenges are well­documented in academic literature, which has often questioned the value
of state ownership and cited the historically poor performance of some government­led
financial institutions (Gutierrez et al. 2011; Alperovych et al. 2015).
By combining these two models, hybrid funding aims to mitigate these respective weak­
nesses. The public partner’s stable, long­term capital can loosen the ”tight leash” of purely
private financing, giving ventures more breathing room. In turn, the private partner’s profit
motive and market expertise inject a level of commercial discipline and efficiency that can
be lacking in purely public initiatives, addressing the criticism that governments some­
times fail in the task of allocating capital effectively (Alperovych et al. 2015).
This synergistic structure is particularly well­suited for sustainable startups, as their own
dual mission—achieving both commercial viability and positive societal impact—is per­
fectly mirrored by the blended logic of the hybrid model. This synergy is further reinforced
by findings that actors in sustainable innovation are themselves increasingly moving to­
ward such hybrid logics (Siefkes et al. 2024). In this ideal arrangement, the private partner
validates the business case while the public partner validates the venture’s environmental
or social mission.
Investment Models in Hybrid Funding
The blending of the market and state logics is realized through several distinct hybrid mod­
els. These structures are often more complex than the discrete investment instruments
previously discussed, ranging from direct co­investment in a single startup to the insti­
tutional design of entire funds and the development of collaborative ecosystems. While
diverse in form, these models all share a common strategic goal: using public resources
to de­risk opportunities and “crowd in” private­sector capital and expertise
Direct Co­Investment and Syndication
The most direct form of hybrid funding is the co­investment syndicate, where public and
private investors invest in the same funding round of a startup. This model is common
in both syndicated loans (Fotak and Lee 2020) and venture capital (Bai et al. 2021). A
prominent example, particularly in the VC space, is the matching fund model. In this

structure, a public entity, such as a government­backed fund, commits to matching the
capital a startup raises from private investors, thereby sharing the risks and due diligence
of the investment (Kim et al. 2015).
Hybrid Investment Vehicles
A more structural approach involves creating hybrid investment vehicles, where public and
private capital are blended at the fund level. The most prominent example is government­
supported venture capital, where a government acts as a Limited Partner in a privately
managed VC fund. This model is distinct from fully state­run or government­owned ven­
ture capital and is designed to attract further private investment by reducing the fundrais­
ing risk for private fund managers (Brander, Du, et al. 2015). The key principle of this
hybrid approach remains that while the capital may be partially public ­ fulfilling the gov­
ernment’s aim to complement voids in the private market and foster economic develop­
ment (Sohn et al. 2012) ­ the fund’s investment decisions and management are left to
private professionals, thereby leveraging their market expertise and incentive structures.
Innovation Ecosystems and Networks
Finally, hybrid funding can be fostered at an infrastructural level through collaborative
innovation ecosystems. These are often described by the ”Triple Helix” model, which em­
phasizes the interwoven relationships between Academia (universities as sources of re­
search and talent), Industry (private firms and investors as engines of commercialization),
and Government (as a provider of funding, stable policy, and regulatory guidance) (Et­
zkowitz and Leydesdorff 2000). University­affiliated accelerators, public­private research
parks, and industry­specific innovation hubs are concrete examples of these ecosystems
(Etzkowitz 2008). Within these networks, public agencies, corporations, VCs, and angel
investors collaborate to sponsor, mentor, and fund new ventures, creating a fertile ground
where syndications and partnerships emerge organically.
Support Mechanisms of Hybrid Funding
The support mechanisms offered by hybrid funding are a direct result of its blended in­
stitutional logic, which combines the market­driven discipline of private actors with the
policy­oriented resources of public entities. Rather than creating entirely distinct forms of
support, hybrid funding’s strength lies in the synergy this creates. These synergistic mech­
anisms can be broadly understood through two complementary dimensions: the structural
and financial synergy of the investment itself, and the operational and knowledge synergy
that follows
Structural Synergy
The structural synergy of hybrid funding stems from the powerful signal created when
public and private entities co­invest. This arrangement creates a form of amplified val­
idation, as the startup has simultaneously earned the confidence of both market­ and
policy­oriented actors. By passing the due diligence of both a private investor seeking

SUCCESS

commercial viability and a public body vetting for technical merit or policy alignment, the
startup is significantly de­risked.
This de­risking occurs through tangible structural mechanisms.
Public capital, for in­
stance, can be deployed as subsidized equity or through loan guarantees to lower the
financial risk for private co­investors (Bai et al. 2021). Critically, the involvement of a
private partner simultaneously introduces a strong, market­based incentive structure, en­
suring a focus on commercial milestones alongside public policy goals (Brander, Du, et al.
2015). This combination of public de­risking and private incentive alignment results in a
venture with a uniquely credible and robust investment profile.
Operational Synergy
Beyond the structural benefits, the operational synergy of hybrid funding comes from
blending the distinct expertise and networks of its public and private partners (Block et
al. 2018). This gives the startup comprehensive guidance from two critical perspectives.
• From the private partner, the venture gains essential business acumen in areas like
go­to­market strategy and team building, which is known to improve its commercial­
ization trajectory (Block et al. 2018; Hallen et al. 2014).
• From the public partner, the venture gains access to invaluable institutional re­
sources, such as regulatory sandboxes, technology validation environments, or
preferential access to public procurement contracts (Okunnuga et al. 2024; Temmes
et al. 2021). Securing a government as a first customer, for instance, provides not
only early revenue but also powerful market validation (Uyarra et al. 2014).
This synergistic transfer of knowledge—blending market intelligence with policy expertise—
creates a uniquely capable and mature organization. A startup guided by these dual per­
spectives is better equipped to navigate uncertainty and scale its operations. By being
both commercially savvy and institutionally connected, the venture develops a compelling
profile, strongly positioning it for future growth and investment.
The Ultimate Sustainability Synergy
Addressing complex challenges like environmental sustainability requires deep collabo­
ration, as single institutions often cannot deliver the necessary multidimensional commit­
ments alone (Farinha 2020). Hybrid funding provides this integrated framework, where its
combined structural and operational synergies create a model uniquely aligned with the
dual mission of sustainable startups. In this blended arrangement, the public partner, by
providing institutional access and policy expertise, validates the venture’s environmental
or social impact claims. Simultaneously, the private partner, by contributing commercial
discipline and market­based incentives, ensures the sustainable solution is also scalable
and profitable. This creates a venture where impact and profit are not competing goals but
integrated components, providing the holistic support necessary for dual­mission compa­
nies to achieve lasting success.

Conceptual Framework and Hypothesis Development
The central goal of this research is to holistically compare how different funding archetypes
— private, public, and hybrid — influence the success of sustainable startups. The pre­

Selected Success Metrics and Hypothesis Derivation
To account for the varied objectives of the funding archetypes, this research adopts a
multi­dimensional view of success. While the literature presents numerous success di­
mensions, this study selects three that are well­suited to capture and measure the distinct
goals of the funding archetypes: exit, survival (operationality), and follow­on funding. This
approach allows for a nuanced analysis where ”success” is defined in terms that align with

the core logic of each funding model.
• An Exit (via acquisition or IPO) is the quintessential measure of success for tradi­
tional private investors, as it represents the terminal event where they realize their
financial returns (Deias and Magrini 2023).
• Survival is a particularly relevant metric for public investors. For a startup with a
long­term societal mission, remaining operational to continue its R&D and impact­
generating activities is a primary indicator of success, aligning perfectly with policy
goals that prioritize innovation spillovers and regional development over immedi­

are often used as a key macro­economic metric to gauge economic dynamism and
compare national economies (Gonzalez 2017).
• Securing Follow­on Funding is a fitting measure for the effectiveness of hybrid mod­

hypothesis that blending public de­risking with private commercial validation makes
a venture more attractive to subsequent investors.
This strategic alignment of metrics to archetypes is the foundation of this study’s multidi­
mensional approach to success. However, this framework does not presume that these
goals are exclusive. While an exit is the quintessential measure of success for a private
fund, continued survival is an obvious prerequisite. Conversely, while public capital is
patient, a successful exit can also signify mission accomplishment by launching a self­
sustaining, impactful enterprise. Therefore, the hypotheses that follow are constructed to
test the relative strength of each funding pathway in achieving the outcome that is most
aligned with its core institutional logic.
Based on this framework, the following hypotheses are proposed:
H1 (Follow­on Funding): Sustainable startups that initially receive hybrid funding
are more likely to secure subsequent rounds of follow­on funding.
The reasoning for this hypothesis is that from the perspective of a later­stage investor,
an initial hybrid round makes a sustainable startup a uniquely compelling and de­risked
opportunity. The presence of a public co­investor signals that the venture’s technology
and societal impact claims have been rigorously vetted, mitigating the high technical and
policy risks characteristic of sustainable innovation. Simultaneously, the involvement of
a private co­investor provides crucial market validation, confirming the startup possesses
a commercially viable business model. This dual validation directly addresses the core
challenge of assessing a sustainable startup’s dual mission, making it an attractive can­
didate for follow­on funding.
H2 (Exit): The greater the prevalence of private funding in a sustainable startup’s
financing history, the more likely it is to achieve a successful exit, and to do so
more quickly.

The reasoning for this hypothesis is that private investors, motivated by the primary goal
of realizing financial returns, provide targeted non­monetary support to actively accel­
erate the path to an exit. For sustainable startups, this support—such as active board
participation for strategic scaling and providing network access to potential acquirers—is
particularly crucial for navigating long development horizons and shaping the venture into
a commercially attractive acquisition target.
H3 (Survival): The greater the prevalence of public funding in a sustainable startup’s
financing history, the more likely it is to exhibit long­term survival.
The reasoning for this hypothesis is that public investors prioritize long­term societal im­
pact and regional development over rapid financial returns. This patient capital approach
is particularly vital for sustainable startups, which often face long R&D horizons and ad­

foster innovation that aligns with long­term policy objectives. This insulates the venture
from short­term market pressures, allowing it to focus on its core mission and thereby
enhancing its prospects for long­term operational survival

Consistency and Logic Checks for Funding Rounds
The initial phase of data cleaning commenced with a assessment of the funding rounds

• Post­Exit Funding Removal: Similarly, funding rounds occurring after a startup’s

degree of data integrity for the remaining observations.

• Investor Name and URL: The presence of both an investor’s name and a unique URL
was mandated. This ensured sufficient identifying information to consistently and
accurately classify investor types (e.g., public, private, or hybrid), a central variable

• Funding Amount per Round: The monetary value of each funding round was re­

served as a proxy for data quality, operating on the premise that funding rounds with
explicitly listed amounts generally exhibit a higher degree of accuracy and formal­

• Crowdfunding Rounds: Funding rounds identified as crowdfunding events were sys­

tional investor behavior, as crowdfunding mechanisms typically involve a different
investor base and distinct motivations that are outside the scope of this study.

Dependent Variables
As established in the theoretical framework, this study measures success across multiple

Follow­On Funding
To investigate the influence of the initial investor type on a startup’s subsequent growth
trajectory, the concept of ”follow­on funding” was operationalized using two distinct vari­
ables, capturing both the occurrence and the financial scale of the event.
• First, to measure the likelihood of attracting further investment, the binary variable
Follow­On Funding (bin_follow_on_funding) was constructed. Coded as ’1’ if a
startup received any funding after its initial round and ’0’ otherwise, it serves as an
indicator of early perceived success and continued investor confidence.
• Second, to capture the financial magnitude of this success, the variable Logarithmic
Follow­On Amount (log_follow_on_amount) was created. This was derived by
taking the total U.S. dollar amount of all follow­on funding, converting it to millions,
and applying a log­plus­one transformation (y = log(x+1)) to normalize the skewed
data and accommodate cases with zero follow­on funding.
Exit
Hypothesis H2 investigates the influence of funding on both the likelihood and speed
of a startup exit. To test this dual­natured hypothesis, two dependent variables were
constructed.
• First, to measure the likelihood of an exit, the binary variable Exit Status (bin_exit_
status) was created. Consistent with established metrics in the literature, an exit
is defined as either an acquisition or a successful IPO (Potanin et al. 2023). Ac­
cordingly, the variable is coded as ’1’ (Yes) if a startup experienced such an event
and ’0’ (No) otherwise. For companies with multiple liquidity events, the date of the
chronologically earlier event was used as the definitive exit date.
• Second, to analyze the speed of an exit using survival analysis, the variable Time to
Exit (time_to_exit_years) was engineered. This variable measures the duration
in years from a startup’s founding date to an outcome. For startups that exited, this
is the time until their recorded exit date. For those that had not exited by the end of
the observation period (April 20, 2025), the duration is the time until this cut­off date.
This approach correctly handles the right­censored data required for time­to­event
modeling.
Together, these variables enable a comprehensive test of a startup’s path to a liquidity
event.

constrained to assess operational status at a fixed point in time (May 2025) using logistic
regression. This necessary trade­off introduces limitations, particularly regarding time­
dependent biases, which are addressed in section 3.6.
The second challenge was the reliability of Crunchbase’s native ”Operating Status” label
itself. Initial robustness checks revealed that many startups listed as ”active” were, in fact,
defunct. Therefore, a manual double­verification process was implemented to create a
more accurate classification. This involved assessing each startup’s webpage activity, an
approach supported by research that considers an inactive homepage a strong indicator of
market failure (Gautam and Wattanapongsakorn 2024). In ambiguous cases, professional
networking sites and public company registries were also consulted.
The resulting variable, indicates a startup’s verified operational status, coded as ’1’ for
operational and ’0’ for inactive or closed.
3.4.2
Independent Variables
The explanatory power of the analytical models hinges on the independent variables con­
structed for this study. To test the core hypotheses and isolate their effects, these vari­
ables were separated into two categories: the primary independent variables, which op­
erationalize the funding archetypes, and a comprehensive set of control variables that
account for other known influences on startup success. The construction of both variable
types, drawing upon Crunchbase’s rich data and tailored feature engineering, is detailed
below.
Funding Type Classification
A central component of this study was to classify each funding round as ’public’, ’private’,
or ’hybrid’. This round­level classification is essential for constructing the primary inde­
pendent variables that measure the influence of each funding archetype on a startup. To
achieve this in a systematic and replicable manner, a two­step process was employed:
1. Each individual investor was classified into one of the three archetypes.
2. This investor­level data was then aggregated to assign a final type to each funding
round.
Step 1: Classifying Individual Investors
The classification process began at the individual investor level, a task that presented
two primary challenges. The first, as outlined in the theoretical chapter, pertains to the
inherent fluidity in the boundaries between investor types. This is compounded by the
often incomplete disclosure of an investor’s precise ’composition’ or funding sources.
To ensure a rigorous and consistent classification despite these challenges, the following
rules were applied:
THE INFLUENCE OF PUBLIC, PRIVATE, AND HYBRID FUNDING ON SUSTAINABILITY STARTUP
SUCCESS
31
==PAGE:42==
• Public Investors: Classified as such if unequivocally owned and governed by pub­
lic bodies. This category includes national and international agencies, nationally
owned development banks, and entirely government­funded VC funds.
• Private Investors: Assigned to this category if no discernible public involvement was
apparent. This encompasses conventional VC funds, PE firms, CVC, and individual
or angel investor groups.
• Hybrid Investors: Designated as hybrid if any indication of public­private interplay
was present. This primarily includes venture capitalists with governmental backing,
university­affiliated programs, or organizations structured as public­private partner­
ships.
While the aforementioned logic guided the individual investor classification, the methodol­
ogy for achieving this required a multi­source approach. The native Crunchbase ”Investor
Type” label provided an initial indication of an investor’s nature; however, its predefined
labels were deemed overly broad and, upon a robustness check, demonstrated insuffi­
cient accuracy for the granularity required by this study. Therefore, additional sources
were consulted: (1) the expanded Crunchbase profile, specifically its ’About’ section; (2)
the investor’s LinkedIn profile, focusing on their brief company description; and (3) the
investor’s official website, with particular attention to ’About Us’ and ’Partners’ sections,
as well as website headers or footers often indicating affiliations. This largely manual
classification process aligns with approaches adopted in prior research, such as Bran­
der, Du, et al. (2015), who conducted extensive website analysis of VC firms to identify
indicators of government support. This method, however, relies on the critical assump­
tion that investors transparently disclose their public affiliations, particularly in cases of
co­funding or when public institutions serve as Limited Partners. While this assumption
is supported by the general benefits of voluntary disclosure in enhancing credibility (She­
hata 2014) and the strategic advantages associated with public affiliations, the possibility
of some misclassifications due to incomplete or non­transparent disclosure cannot be en­
tirely eliminated. This inherent limitation will be further discussed in Section 3.6
Step 2: Assigning a Type to Funding Rounds
Following the comprehensive classification of individual investors, their respective types
were systematically assigned to the funding rounds they participated in. This presented a
final challenge in the context of syndicated rounds involving multiple investor types. For
rounds with a single investor or a homogeneous group, the classification was straightfor­
ward. However, for mixed­syndicate rounds, a nuanced ”dominance logic” was applied.
A specific logic was introduced to classify such mixed funding rounds as ’hybrid’, even if a
’hybrid’ investor was not explicitly present in the syndicate. A funding round was deemed
’hybrid’ if it featured a significant blend of public, private, or classified hybrid investors.
To avoid an over­representation of ’hybrid’ rounds and maintain a ’dominance logic’, a
conservative threshold of 0.67 (two­thirds) was established. This meant if a funding round
THE INFLUENCE OF PUBLIC, PRIVATE, AND HYBRID FUNDING ON SUSTAINABILITY STARTUP
SUCCESS
32
==PAGE:43==
featured more than two­thirds of investors belonging to a single type (public, private, or
hybrid), the round retained that specific type; otherwise, it was classified as a ’hybrid’
round. While this specific logic is novel in the existing literature, it was deemed necessary
to enhance the robustness of the dataset and ensure that the classification reflected a
clear dominant type or a deliberate mixed composition. This sensitive approach was
favored over simply classifying any mixed appearance as a ’hybrid’ round, which might
have been overly susceptible to errors arising from the initial, somewhat manual, investor
labeling process.
The determined classification for each funding round was then utilized to construct several
startup­level variables. To capture the ”prevalence” of each funding archetype in different
ways, three sets of metrics were engineered:
• Count: The variables num_public_rounds, num_private_rounds, and num_hybrid_
rounds represent the simple cumulative count of each funding round type through­
out a startup’s lifecycle.
• Proportion: To measure relative influence, ratio variables (e.g., ratio_private_
rounds) were created by dividing the count of each round type by a startup’s total
number of funding rounds.
• Financial Magnitude: To capture the financial contribution of each archetype, a
third set of variables (e.g., amount_private_funding_m) was calculated by summing
the funding amount from all rounds of a given type, measured in millions of U.S.
dollars.
Furthermore, the classified type of a startup’s first and last tracked funding rounds (first
_round_funding_type and last_round_funding_type) were assigned to the startup’s
profile, reflecting the investor composition at these critical junctures.
Moderating and Control Variables
In addition to the primary independent variables that operationalize the funding archetypes,
two further sets of variables were constructed. The first is a key moderating variable used
to explore the structural characteristics of investments. The second is a comprehensive
set of control variables included in the analytical models to account for confounding factors
and ensure the robustness of the findings.
Moderating Variable: Funding Nature
To gain deeper insights into the structural characteristics of investments, the Funding Na­
ture was constructed as a binary variable (dilutive vs. non­dilutive). This was created by
mapping Crunchbase’s native funding types (Note: the platforms use of the terminology
type is different to this thesis) to the appropriate classification (the specific logic is de­
tailed in Appendix A.2). Given its strong theoretical relationship with the primary funding
archetypes, this variable was specifically used as a moderator to test whether the influ­
ence of a funding type (e.g., public) on startup success differs based on whether it was

provided as equity or not.
Control Variables
The following control variables were included in the analytical models based on academic
standards and data availability to account for other factors that could influence startup
outcomes:
• Startup Maturity: As a firm’s age can independently influence its success, startup
age (e.g. age_at_exit, age_at_first_round) was calculated as the time elapsed
between founding and a specific event date.
• Startup Size: A startup’s size was measured by its total funding amount. This was
included as a control variable because it is a key determinant of a venture’s capacity
to scale and absorb shocks. Depending on the analysis, this metric was used in two
ways: either as the cumulative lifetime funding or as the amount from a specific
round (e.g., the first or last).
• Macroeconomic Context: The date of various events (e.g., funding rounds, found­
ing, exit) was used to implicitly account for broader temporal trends and economic
conditions.
• Geographic Region: The geographical region of a startup, derived from its HQ loca­
tion (following an approach similar to Gautam and Wattanapongsakorn (2024), was
merged into broader clusters. This clustering adapted, but did not strictly adhere to,
the logic proposed by the United States Geoscheme (Divisio 2025) and the World
by Region classifications (Group 2017).
• Startup Industry Cluster: To address Crunchbase’s often loose and inconsistent in­
dustry labels (which can appear as comma­separated lists), individual industries
were grouped into higher­level clusters. This process created a more robust cate­
gorical variable for use in the analytical models.
A comprehensive list of all independent variables, including their brief definitions, is pro­
vided in Appendix A.3. All categorical independent variables were subsequently con­
verted into dummy (binary) variables through one­hot encoding for inclusion in the sta­
tistical models. It should be noted that the specific set of variables used was tailored for
each of the three analytical models, as detailed in the upcoming section.

3.5.1
Analytical Model for H1: Follow­on Funding
To test Hypothesis 1, a multi­stage analytical approach was employed to investigate the
influence of the initial investor type on a startup’s ability to secure subsequent financing.
The primary analysis utilized a binary logistic regression model to predict the likelihood
of receiving any follow­on funding using the binary follow­on status variable. The key

SUCCESS

independent variable was the operationalized first round funding type. To isolate its effect,
the model controlled for several factors, including: the size of the initial investment, the
startup’s age at the time of the round, macroeconomic timing (via the funding year), and
fixed effects for geographic region and industry cluster.
To delve deeper into the nuances of this relationship, two post­hoc analyses were con­
ducted:
1. Linear Regression on Funding Amount: To assess whether the initial investor
type also influenced the financial magnitude of subsequent investment, a sepa­
rate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression was performed using the logarithmic
follow­on amount as the continuous dependent variable.
2. Interaction Effect Analysis: To explore the interplay between investor type and
investment structure, the primary logistic regression model was extended to include
an interaction term. This tested whether the effect of the first round investor type
was moderated by the first round funding nature (i.e., dilutive vs. non­dilutive).
For all analyses, a consistent procedure was followed: a baseline model containing only
control variables was compared to the full model using a Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT)
to confirm the statistical significance of the added variables. Furthermore, a Variance
Inflation Factor (VIF) check was conducted prior to final estimations to ensure the stability
and reliability of the model coefficients (All VIF results can be found in Appendix A.4, for
the follow­on model see Table A.4).

Analytical Model for H2: Likelihood and Speed of Exit

First, a preliminary analysis of exit events by startup founding year was conducted. As
shown in Figure 3.2, this did not reveal a clear time­correlated trend, why the decision

proportional ratios). The final models presented are those that yielded the best fit, as

data, such as the appearance of a single funding round (Brander, Du, et al. 2015).

3.6
Methodological Limitations
Already touched upon repeatedly throughout this chapter, the primary limitations stem
from the nature of the secondary data source, Crunchbase. As discussed in section 3.1,
while Crunchbase is a comprehensive resource, it has known biases. Its coverage tends
to favor more successful, venture­backed, and U.S.­based technology firms. Although
some of this study’s filtering processes mitigate some of these issues, a potential bias
toward more visible startups may remain.
Beyond these known sampling biases, the analysis fundamentally assumes that the un­
derlying event data is both complete and accurate. The validity of the findings is contingent
on Crunchbase having comprehensively tracked the full funding history and correctly re­
ported the exit status for each startup in the sample. Any unobserved missing funding
rounds or inaccurately recorded exit data represents a critical limitation that could influ­
ence the results.
Furthermore, the operationalization of key variables presented specific challenges:
• Investor Type Classification: The classification of investors into public, private, and
hybrid archetypes, while based on a systematic multi­source verification process,
relied on publicly available information. The potential for incomplete disclosure by in­
vestors regarding their Limited Partners or governance structures means that some
misclassification is unavoidable. The difficulty was compounded by the need to in­
terpret websites in various languages and to consolidate the nature of an investment
entity when multiple individual investors were involved in a single round.
• Operational Status and Survivor Bias: As detailed above, the significant number of
missing closure dates for failed startups made a traditional time­to­event analysis
for survival unfeasible. The decision to instead analyze long­term operationality by
filtering the sample to firms aged 10 years or older was a necessary methodological
trade­off. However, this introduces a significant survivor bias for the analysis of H3.
The findings on survival are therefore only applicable to the population of firms that
have already survived for a decade and cannot be generalized to predict the survival
probability of a startup from its inception.
Finally, the diagnostic checks for multicollinearity revealed a moderate level of collinearity
among the regional control variables. The VIF scores for some regions approached 6.5, a
likely result of the high concentration of startups in North America and Western Europe. As
these values remain below the common critical threshold of 10, the main model results
are considered robust. Nevertheless, this moderate collinearity is acknowledged as a
limitation that could slightly affect the precision of the coefficients for the regional variables
themselves.

Causality and Unobserved Factors
Beyond the data and measurement challenges discussed above, a more fundamental
limitation arises from the study’s observational research design, which means the findings
represent strong associations rather than strict causal relationships. While the models
include a comprehensive set of controls, it is impossible to account for all unobserved
heterogeneity that could influence a startup’s trajectory.
For instance, factors such as the intrinsic skills and network of the founding team, or
the specific competitive intensity within an industry sub­sector, are notoriously difficult
to measure yet are known to influence success (Żbikowski and Antosiuk 2021). These
unobserved variables could create an omitted variable bias; a highly skilled founding team,
for example, might be more likely to attract private funding and be more likely to achieve
an exit, independent of the funding’s direct effect. Consequently, the results should be
interpreted as demonstrating the powerful and distinct pathways associated with each
funding archetype, rather than as definitive proof of a causal link.
3.7
Ethical Considerations
Lastly, the research presented in this thesis was conducted with strict adherence to ethi­
cal principles and academic integrity. The study relies exclusively on available secondary
data sourced from Crunchbase and publicly accessible web data for enrichment. All data
pertains to organizations and their institutional funding activities; no sensitive personal
data of individuals was collected, used, or stored during the research process. The meth­
ods of data collection and analysis were designed to be objective and transparent, ensur­
ing the replicability and reliability of the findings.

5
Discussion
The empirical findings have established that different funding archetypes lead sustainable
startups along distinct pathways to success. This chapter now addresses the question of
why these divergent outcomes occur. To do so, it will interpret the results for each of
the three hypotheses through the theoretical lenses of Institutional Logics and Resource
Dependence Theory. The discussion will then culminate in a holistic synthesis, weaving
the individual findings into a broader narrative about the strategic trade­offs inherent in
the funding pathways available to sustainable startups.
5.1
Discussion of Findings for H1: The Power of Investor
Signals
The investigation into Hypothesis 1 — which posited that an initial hybrid round would
be the most effective pathway to subsequent financing— yielded a more complex reality
than anticipated. The findings only partially support this hypothesis, revealing instead
that the presence of a market­oriented investor is the most critical signal for attracting
future capital. This discussion will first interpret this primary finding, before dissecting
the nuanced and contradictory role of public funding that was uncovered in the post­hoc
analyses.
The Prevailing Power of Market Logic
The regression analysis showed no statistically significant difference between a private
and a hybrid start in securing follow­on funding. Both pathways proved significantly more
effective than a solely public round. This suggests that for later­stage investors, the key
signal is the presence of a private counterpart who has already performed commercial due
diligence and validated the venture’s business model. While the ’dual validation’ of hybrid
funding is theoretically powerful, these results indicate it is the market logic component
that prevails in the eyes of future funders. The public partner’s role appears to be getting
the startup ‘ready for market,’ but it is the private partner’s stamp of approval that makes
it truly ‘market­ready’.
This interpretation is further bolstered by the first post­hoc analysis, which examined the
amount of follow­on funding raised. In that model, a purely private start was not just equal
but statistically superior to a hybrid start, leading to significantly larger subsequent funding
rounds. This reinforces the conclusion that a strong, undiluted signal of market logic is
the most powerful catalyst for attracting future private capital.
THE INFLUENCE OF PUBLIC, PRIVATE, AND HYBRID FUNDING ON SUSTAINABILITY STARTUP
SUCCESS

==PAGE:79==
The Two Faces of Public Capital
The initial finding that public funding underperforms seems to contradict its core policy
objective of bridging funding gaps and catalyzing private investment. This apparent puzzle
necessitated the deeper dive in the second post­hoc analysis, which revealed that public
funding is not monolithic; its effect depends entirely on its structure.
A non­dilutive public investments acts as a pure and powerful certification signal. In line
with Resource Dependence Theory, it provides critical early­stage capital without compli­
cating the ownership structure or governance. This ’clean’ signal seemingly makes the
venture attractive, elevating its chances of securing future funding to a level statistically
indistinguishable from its private­ or hybrid­funded peers.
Conversely, a dilutive public investment appears to send a potent negative signal. The
market may interpret this as a sign that the startup was unable to attract private capital
on its own terms, suggesting a lack of commercial validation. Furthermore, this finding
aligns with prior research, which suggests that state­backed ventures can lack market­
efficiency. The poor performance may therefore not only stem from a negative market
signal, but also from the internal bureaucratic inefficiencies and political pressures often
associated with public funding initiatives (Brander, Egan, et al. 2008; Karsai 2018). This
pathway proved to be the least effective of all, performing significantly worse than every
other funding combination, including its non­dilutive public counterpart. It is important to
note, however, that this subgroup was the smallest in the analysis (N=77). While the effect
was statistically significant, future research with a larger sample is needed to confirm the
robustness of this specific finding.
5.2
Discussion of Findings for H2: The Market­Driven Path to
Exit
While the findings for follow­on funding revealed a complex interplay of investor signals,
the analysis of Hypothesis 2 provides a clearer, though still nuanced, illustration of how
funding pathways are associated with the likelihood and speed of a commercial exit. The
results suggest that while a prevalence of private funding is indeed a strong predictor of an
exit, the equally strong performance of hybrid­funded ventures points to a more complex
mechanism centered on the influence of market logic.
The Expressway to Exit: The Dominance of Market Logic
The analysis indicates a strong positive association between a funding history dominated
by market­oriented investors and a faster, more probable exit. As the purely private path­
way was the reference category in the model, its strong performance is inferred from the
significantly weaker performance of the public funding path relative to it.
Notably, startups with a high prevalence of hybrid funding were statistically indistinguish­
able from their privately­funded peers in both their likelihood and speed of exiting. This
THE INFLUENCE OF PUBLIC, PRIVATE, AND HYBRID FUNDING ON SUSTAINABILITY STARTUP
SUCCESS
69
==PAGE:80==
finding offers only partial support for the original hypothesis, which singled out private
funding. Instead, it suggests that the crucial driver is the presence of market logic within
the funding syndicate. For purely private investors, the entire model of engagement is
optimized to achieve a liquidity event. For hybrid investors, it appears this market­driven
demand for an exit often overrides the state partner’s preference for patience. Therefore,
the strong correlation between these two pathways and successful exits likely reflects the
targeted support provided by investors whose primary goal is a profitable sale or IPO.
The Patient Path: Public Funding’s Alignment with State Logic
Conversely, a high proportion of public funding was the strongest statistical predictor
against achieving a commercial exit. This association, however, should not necessar­
ily be interpreted as a failure of public funding. It is more likely a direct manifestation of
the state logic that guides these investors. With primary goals centered on long­term in­
novation, regional development, or correcting market failures, a rapid exit can be contrary
to the mission. The ”patient capital” provided by public entities appears to successfully
insulate ventures from market pressures, allowing them to pursue longer­term R&D and
impact generation—a different, but equally valid, strategic path.
Finally, beyond the central role of funding composition, the models highlighted other sig­
nificant predictors of a startup’s exit. The finding that the ’age at last round’ negatively
impacts the likelihood of an exit suggests that momentum is critical; ventures that go
longer without securing fresh capital may be perceived as stagnating, reducing their at­
tractiveness for acquisition. Similarly, the positive association between the ’total number
of funding rounds’ and the hazard of an exit aligns with expectations, as each successful
round acts as a step of validation and provides resources that move a startup closer to the
scale required for an IPO or a strategic sale. While not the primary focus of this study’s
theory, these factors provide crucial context, illustrating that the path to an exit is shaped
by both the type of funding and the rhythm of the financing process itself.
5.3
Discussion of Findings for H3: The Complexities of
Long­Term Survival
Shifting from the clear­cut pathways to an exit, the investigation into Hypothesis 3 — the
link between public funding and long­term survival — yielded the most complex results
of this study. This complexity stems largely from a critical, overarching methodological
limitation established earlier: survivor bias. Because the analysis focuses exclusively on
mature firms that have already survived for a decade, it cannot measure the role funding
plays in helping a venture reach that milestone in the first place. All subsequent findings
must be interpreted through this lens. With this key caveat in mind, the analysis did
not support the simple hypothesis that a higher prevalence of public funding increases
survival; instead, it points towards the structure of capital being more influential.

The Fading Signal of Patient Capital
The initial regression model showed results contrary to the ’patient capital’ hypothesis.
It was the amount of private and hybrid funding—not public—that showed a significant
positive correlation with endurance past the ten­year mark. This finding may be explained
by the ’washed­out’ effect: after a decade of operations, the specific advantages of early­
stage public funding may fade, becoming less critical than more contemporary factors
or other unobserved complexities like team quality and market adaptation, which likely
become the dominant drivers of success for mature firms.
The Nuances of Funding Structure
Even within this cohort of survivors, a more granular analysis revealed that the structure
of funding can be more important than its source. Public non­dilutive funding (i.e., grants)
did emerge as a marginally significant positive predictor of survival, suggesting that for
mature firms, ”clean” capital that provides stability without complicating governance is still
beneficial. The faintness of this statistical signal, however, may itself be a consequence
of the survivor bias—a lingering echo of a much stronger, but un­measurable, early­stage
survival effect that is obscured by the study’s design.

Having dissected the findings for each hypothesis individually, this final section of the
discussion adopts a more holistic perspective. By weaving together the key threads from
the analyses of follow­on funding, exits, and long­term survival, it is possible to derive
several overarching conclusions about the complex funding landscape for sustainable
startups.
Private Funding: The Effectiveness of Market­Driven Support
Across all three success metrics, private funding emerged as a consistently effective path­
way. It was associated with securing the largest amounts of follow­on funding, a high
likelihood of a swift and profitable exit, and was also a significant predictor of long­term
survival. This suggests a strong compatibility between the ’market logic’ of private in­
vestors and the needs of scalable sustainable startups. The active, hands­on support
and commercial discipline provided by these investors appear to be crucial for navigating
the path to scale. Furthermore, this strong performance may also reflect a maturation of
the investment landscape itself; the growing trend of impact investing has likely equipped
many private funds with specialized expertise in evaluating and nurturing ventures that
blend profit with purpose.

In stark contrast, public funding in its general form was not associated with strong per­
formance on the observed market­based metrics, a finding that raises questions about

Hybrid funding consistently performed ’toe­to­toe’ with purely private funding on all market­

powerful endorsement of the public policy goal behind such models. The findings suggest
that hybrid structures are working as intended: public capital is successfully leveraged to
de­risk opportunities and ”crowd­in” the efficiency, expertise, and capital of the private
sector, guiding sustainable innovations towards commercial scale.

Finally, across all archetypes, the analysis revealed several factors that act as universal

These factors provide crucial context, reminding us that while the choice of investor sets
the strategic direction, a startup must still obey the fundamental rules of growth and market
confidence to succeed.

Conclusion
The journey of a sustainable startup is profoundly shaped by the institutional logic of its
financial backers. This thesis has demonstrated that the choice between private, pub­
lic, and hybrid funding is not merely a financial transaction but a foundational strategic
decision that sets a venture on a distinct path toward either rapid commercialization or
long­term operational endurance. This concluding chapter summarizes the key findings
that support this conclusion and outlines their theoretical contributions, practical implica­
tions, and directions for future research.
The key findings of this study are:
• Private Funding: A Strong Enabler of Commercial Success. The findings con­
sistently showed that private funding is a highly effective pathway for sustainable
startups aiming for scale. Once initial funding was secured, a high prevalence of
private capital was associated with securing the largest amounts of follow­on fund­
ing, achieving a faster and more probable commercial exit, and was also a positive
predictor of long­term survival. This suggests a strong compatibility between the
market logic and hands­on support of private investors and the needs of growth­
oriented sustainable ventures.
• Public Funding: The Critical Distinction of Structure. The most striking find­
ing regarding public funding is that its influence is not monolithic but is critically
dependent on its structure. The analysis revealed a dramatic split: while dilutive
public equity was associated with a significantly lower likelihood of attracting follow­
on funding, non­dilutive public grants acted as a powerful, positive certification of
quality, performing on par with private and hybrid funding. This suggests that the
way public support is delivered is more important than its source alone.
• Hybrid Funding: A Successful Synthesis Dominated by Market Logic. Hybrid
funding consistently performed on par with purely private funding across all market­
oriented metrics, including likelihood of follow­on funding and the probability and
speed of an exit. This success offers a dual insight: it strongly indicates that the
market logic of the private co­investor tends to dominate the venture’s strategic tra­
jectory, while also confirming the effectiveness of the public policy design. Hybrid
models appear to be working as intended, successfully using public funds to de­risk
opportunities and leverage private sector efficiency to guide sustainable innovations
toward commercial scale.
These core findings give rise to several important contributions to both academic theory
and professional practice.

6.1
Theoretical Contributions
This thesis contributes to the literature on startup finance by adopting a more holistic
theoretical lens than is common in studies focused on singular funding instruments. By
applying the theory of Institutional Logics, this research moves beyond a purely finan­
cial assessment to differentiate public, private, and hybrid funding archetypes based on
their underlying motivations and goals. While this high­level approach takes an aggre­
gated view, its value is demonstrated by the significant and plausible results it delivered
regarding the divergent pathways of sustainable startups.
Within this framework, two specific contributions should be highlighted. First, the ex­
plicit operationalization of hybrid funding provides a clear model for understanding it as
a synthesis of market and state logics, a category often under­examined in the literature.
Second, the adoption of a multi­dimensional view of success—assessing follow­on fund­
ing, exit, and survival—allowed for a more complete and nuanced analysis by aligning
performance metrics with the competing objectives of each funding archetype.
6.2
Practical Implications
Alongside the theoretical contributions, this thesis offers several practical implications for
the key actors within the sustainable innovation ecosystem:
For Entrepreneurs
The results provide a clear strategic guide for founders. The hands­on support and com­
mercial discipline from investors with a private background—whether in purely private or
hybrid arrangements—appear invaluable for achieving growth and a successful exit. At
the same time, entrepreneurs should actively pursue public grants, as this form of non­
dilutive funding provides crucial capital with few conditions. Founders should, however,
approach dilutive public equity with caution, as the findings suggest this pathway may lack
the market­oriented drive of its private counterparts and could send a negative signal to
the investment community.
For Policymakers
The same results, when interpreted from a policy perspective, offer clear directives for
program design. Non­dilutive grants are generally shown to be an effective instrument for
achieving the public good of regional development. However, the findings also suggest
that governments may be less effective when running their own direct equity programs,
a role perhaps better left to private, incentive­based investors. Instead, the most potent
strategy for public intervention appears to be the creation and support of hybrid models.
Their strong performance in this study confirms they are an efficient mechanism for using
public funds to de­risk opportunities and leverage private sector expertise.

For Investors
For conventional investors, the direct implications are more nuanced, given the study’s
focus on sustainable startups without a non­sustainable comparison group. However, the
findings clearly demonstrate that sustainable ventures are capable of achieving strong,
market­based outcomes when backed by the right capital structure. In the broader context
of global challenges like climate change, the evidence that these dual­mission startups

their expertise and engagement in this critical sector.
6.3
Limitations and Directions for Future Research
Before concluding this thesis, it is essential to acknowledge its limitations, which in turn
highlight promising directions for future research.
On a theoretical level, this study applied broad concepts like Institutional Logics in a ”dif­
fused” manner to create its high­level archetypes. Future research could adopt a more
granular approach, conducting qualitative case studies to explore the specific nuances of
investor motivations in greater depth.
Thoroughly discussed in section 3.6, methodologically, the analysis relies on second­
hand data from Crunchbase which, while extensive, cannot be fully validated and led to
operationalization challenges like the significant survivor bias in the H3 analysis. As also
detailed in that section, the study’s observational design means the findings represent
strong associations rather than strict causal relationships and cannot rule out the influence
of unobserved variables like founder quality. A clear avenue for future work would be to
validate these quantitative findings using first­hand data from interviews or surveys with
founders and investors.
Furthermore, while the thesis revolves around ”sustainable” startups, a significant limi­
tation is its reliance on market­based success metrics due to data availability. A crucial
next step for this research agenda would be to re­evaluate these funding pathways using
direct measures of sustainability performance, such as alignment with the Sustainable
Development Goals or quantified environmental impact. This would reveal whether the
pathways to commercial success are also the pathways to the greatest societal impact.
Finally, for the sake of focus, this analysis used geography and industry primarily as con­
trol variables. A deeper investigation into the differences between specific regions (e.g.,
the policy and investment environments in Europe vs. North America) or within specific
sustainable industry sub­sectors would be a valuable and logical extension of this work.

This research has focused on the funding pathways of sustainable startups, recognizing
their critical role in addressing the immense environmental and societal challenges ahead.

A.4
Diagnostic Checks: Multicollinearity
This section details the multicollinearity diagnostics performed for the primary regression
models using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). A common rule of thumb is that a VIF
value exceeding 5 or 10 indicates potentially problematic multicollinearity. As shown in
the tables below, while some regional variables exhibit moderate collinearity (VIF > 5),
all predictor variables remain below the conservative threshold of 10, suggesting that
multicollinearity is not a significant issue for the model interpretations. The VIF for each
model’s intercept term was excluded from the tables as it is not used to assess collinearity
among predictors.
