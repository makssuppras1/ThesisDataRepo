DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark 
with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and public/private 
sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new 
opportunities and technology for the global and Danish exploitation of wind 
energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central 
for the development, innovation and use of wind energy and provides the 
basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD 
students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes organized 
into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and 
Basics for wind energy.

Abstract
Wind ﬂow inside a boundary-layer wind tunnel at Politecnico di Milano was mea-
sured using two synchronized short-range WindScanners in a joint measurement
campaign by Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Carl von Ossietzky Univer-
sit¨at Oldenburg (UniOL), Politecnico di Milano and Technical University of Munich
(TUM). The empty chamber of the wind tunnel was scanned in several patterns
in order to investigate the mean ﬂow, turbulence properties and changing surface
characteristics. The horizontal and vertical 2-D mean ﬂow and turbulence ﬁelds are
determined from plane scans. Also, it has been observed that, for a ﬂow which is
dominantly in one direction, the 2-D reconstructed streamwise velocity ﬁeld matches
with the one lidar evaluation of the same wind ﬁeld. In addition, surface character-
istics (roughness length and friction velocity) at ten positions along the tunnel are
determined by using the logarithmic law on mean vertical proﬁles. It is observed
that, for rough to smooth transition, the surface characteristic parameters decrease
and the turbulence intensity along the ﬂow also reduces. Next, instantaneous tur-
bulence spectra at several adjacent positions are determined from fast line scans. A
well known feature of lidars is that, the probe volume ﬁlters out turbulent ﬂuctua-
tions smaller than the probe volume. The standard deviation in radial wind speed
is investigated by using the method of averaged spectra [1, 2] to neglect the eﬀect
of attenuation of ﬂuctuations by the probe volume. It is found that, the variation
determined from averaged spectra is higher compared to the measured line-of-sight
velocities. Moreover, the numerical uncertainties are calculated for the 2-D velocity
reconstruction to investigate the accuracy of the method.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Light detection and ranging (Lidar) is a remote sensing technique, which is mostly
used in ﬁeld measurement of wind speed and direction. In this current research
project, measurements taken within a boundary layer wind tunnel using two syn-
chronized short range lidars, WindScanners are analyzed. These measurements were
taken in the wind tunnel at Politecnico di Milano [3], in a joint measurement cam-
paign by Technical University of Denmark (DTU), University of Oldenburg (UniOL),
Politecnico di Milano and Technical University of Munich (TUM).
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, lidars have become a popular measurement device for wind energy
applications. Nowadays, lidars can be used for resource assessment [4], power curve
estimation [5, 6], characterization of wakes [7, 8] and also turbulence etc. Moreover,
focusing three lidars at the same point the 3-D wind vector and turbulence can be
determined [9]. Also, using pulsed wave lidars, measurements can be done at very
high altitudes, where it is not possible to install cup or sonic anemometers. On the
other hand, continuous wave (CW) lidars have smaller probe volume (see subsection
2.1.2) at short focal distance. Because of this better resolution at close range, short
range CW lidars can be used to do measurements within a boundary layer wind
tunnel (BLWT).
The BLWTs are used to simulate the ﬂow close to earth’s surface, and used
for several experimental application: wind loading and wind induced response of
building structures, diﬀusion of air pollutants, wind ﬁelds in complex terrains [10]
etc. Generally, spires and roughness stones are used in the wind tunnel to generate
atmospheric boundary layer ﬂow.
These eddy generators also contribute to the
height of simulated boundary layer [11]. Again, using these turbulence generators,
the characteristics of wind ﬂow over a terrain can be produced within a tunnel [12].
Researchers aim to simulate diﬀerent terrain conditions within the tunnel to perform
analysis on wind loading on structures and also to better understand the eﬀect of
transition of roughness.
The measurements within a tunnel are mostly done with hot wire anemometers
[3, 12]. This device can only measure at a single point, and multiple devices can-
not be placed along the ﬂow due the disturbance generated by the device at the
2
==PAGE:16==
upstream. On the contrary, lidars can measure several places in a very short time
diﬀerence without disturbing the ﬂow. Also plane scans can be done to evaluate
ﬂow ﬁelds. The development of 3-D velocity and turbulence ﬁeld determination by
scanning lidars, known as WindScanners were ﬁrst presented in 2008 by DTU Wind
Energy [13]. These scanners have been used in several appilcations. Two synchro-

2016, three synchronized WindScanners were used to characterize the wind velocities

have been used to evaluate the coherence of turbulence on the Lysefjord Bridge
[17], where a good agreement between the data obtained from sonic and lidar has
been observed. Now in this current research project, the WindScanners are used
to characterize the ﬂow inside a wind tunnel. It has to be mentioned that in the
same campaign, measurements were taken on three scaled wind turbines within the
tunnel [8]. The following results were obtained in that analysis.
• The 2-D measurement capability of the lidar was validated by comparing the
2-D measurements of the lidar with measurements of a hot wire.
• The two components of the wind velocity, u and v, were evaluated from the
line of sight measurements of both lidars of the transverse wake proﬁle at hub
height of downstream of a wind turbine.
• Wake wind ﬁeld of the horizontal plane at turbine hub height, containing
the wake of all three wind turbines, which demonstrates the capability of
synchronized lidars to characterize the wake ﬁeld.
The objective of this current study is to investigate the ﬂow along an empty
wind tunnel by measurements taken by two short-range WindScanners. Here it is
aimed to evaluate the 2-D wind and turbulence ﬁelds, and investigate the changing
surface characteristics and turbulent conditions along the ﬂow.
1.2
Research Scope
The ability of the WindScanners to scan in several user deﬁned patterns, fast sam-

line-of-sight velocities stream wise component u and component in the transverse
direction v can be calculated, see subsection 2.1.3.
The disturbance in the ﬂow generated by the spires and roughness stones at the

are mostly responsible for turbulence near the ﬂoor. After the array of roughness

2.1.2
Spatial Averaging
Lidars are divided into two categories, continuous wave (CW) lidar and pulsed lidar.
A CW lidar can only measure at a single height whereas the pulsed lidar can measure
many heights simultaneously. In a CW lidar the beam is focused at a distance and
the backscatter from this position is used to determine the line-of-sight velocity, Vlos.
On the other hand, a pulsed lidar emits a very short pulse of light and the arrival
time of the reﬂected light can be used to determine the distance of Vlos measurement.
Again, the spatial measuring resolution of lidars are responsible for determining the
measurement range. This resolution is diﬀerent for CW lidars and pulsed lidars. In
a CW lidar system the probe volume depends on the focus distance from the lens.
The probe length increases quadratically with the focus distance [13]. In contrast,
pulsed lidars have a constant spatial resolution for all distances [22], which depends
on the pulse length and gate open time [13]. Thus at shorter ranges CW lidars have
better range resolution compared to pulsed lidars.

is given by a Lorentzian function, which has highest spatial sensitivity at the beam
waist and falls symmetrically on either side [23]. The probe length is deﬁned as

In a wind tunnel, a boundary layer develops due to wall surfaces in the roof and
ﬂoor of the wind tunnel. But in the atmosphere, the boundary layer is developed
due to the earth’s surface. Within this layer, wind velocity near the wall or surface
changes from zero to free stream value. Again, the boundary layer thickness is also
deﬁned as the distance from the surface, where the velocity reaches free stream
velocity.
u(δBL) = 0.99uo,
(2.17)
here δBL is the boundary layer thickness and uo is the free stream velocity.
The atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) is the lowest part of the atmosphere
and the ﬂow in the ABL is turbulent [27]. Two factors mainly contribute to the
turbulence generation in the atmosphere: convection and wind shear, these are
discussed below.
• The convection in the atmosphere is mainly generated due to thermal eﬀects.
Because of the heating of the ground during day time, the warmer surface
heats the relatively colder atmosphere. Consequently, heated air near the sur-
face moves upwards. This causes strong convection in the atmosphere, and
this condition is known as unstable condition.
On the other hand, during
night, due to cooling of the surface, stratiﬁcation in the atmosphere eliminates
turbulence. This is known as stable condition. In stable conditions, the rising
air parcel moves down as it’s surrounding atmosphere is warmer than it. As
the cool air parcel is heavier than the warmer atmosphere it moves back to
it’s original position. On the other hand, when the air parcel is moved down-
wards in stable conditions, it ﬁnds colder atmosphere than it and moves up to
it’s original position. In case of unstable condition the opposite phenomenon
occurs. This movement of air parcels for two diﬀerent conditions are well un-
derstood with the vertical temperature proﬁles of the atmosphere [27]. These
two conditions also determine the height of the ABL. Again, as the movement
of air parcels are suppressed in stable conditions, wind speed reaches to the free
stream velocity at lower height compared to unstable conditions, this makes
the vertical gradient of wind speed in stable conditions more steeper than the
unstable condition. In the atmosphere, for stable and unstable conditions the
height can be 100 m and 1000 m, respectively. Again, a third scenario neutral
condition can be described, when thermal eﬀects are not relevant.
• Again, within the ABL, due to friction between surface and layer of ﬂow, a
vertical gradient of velocity is observed. This causes the wind velocity to be
zero at the surface and it gradually increases to free stream velocity at δBL.
The ﬂow closer to the surface is mostly aﬀected by surface friction, as height
increases, eﬀect of surface friction decreases and wind velocity gradually equals
to free stream velocity.
The surface layer within the ABL is deﬁned as the region near the surface, where
the vertical momentum and heat ﬂuxes are almost constant or the variation is less
than 10% [27]. The depiction of ABL, also showing the surface layer is given in
ﬁgure 2.4.

2.2.4
Spectral Analysis
Turbulent ﬂows contain a broad range of time or length scales. The turbulence is
created at large scales due to application of some external force, this added energy
is inserted into the ﬂuid, and then transferred to smaller eddies from large eddies,
until molecular forces become important and the kinetic energy is converted to heat
energy [29]. This broad range of scales of turbulence can be studied by spectrum
analysis. This is done by calculating power spectrum of wind speed signal u(t). This
power spectrum describes the energy contained in the signal over the frequency or
wave number domain.
In the turbulence spectrum, a certain frequency/wave number is reached, af-
ter which the turbulence do not remember the inﬂuence of initial large scale (low
frequency). Beyond this point in the spectrum is known as universal equilibrium
range. Within this equilibrium range there is an inertial sub-range. According to
Kolmogorov, within this sub-range the energy spectrum can be given as

apart. Within the two dominating wave number range there is a range where there
is no signiﬁcant energy or dissipation, here the turbulent energy transfer is mostly
dominated by inertia terms of Navier Stokes equations [30], and this sub-range is
known as the inertial sub-range.

5.3
Uncertainty
In this section, the measurement uncertainty associated with dual-Doppler recon-
struction of u and v-components for the plane scans is discussed. Here a similar
approach as [8] is used, the same analysis is also done in [18] and [19]. The uncer-
tainty is calculated by the standard uncertainty propagation method described in
[24], which is also described brieﬂy in section 2.1.4.
The uncertainty associated with the reconstruction of the u and v components
are given in equation 2.13 and 2.14, respectively.
In these two equations, eVlosn
is the uncertainty of the measured line-of-sight velocities of the both lidars. This
uncertainty is assumed conservatively to be 1% of the free stream velocity [33]. Here
1% of mean wind speed at hub height (uhub = 5.67 m/s) 0.0567 is taken and a worst
case bias of sin(3◦)w ≈0.0523 is added on the measured Vlos [8]. Again, the error is
assumed to be 0.5 mrad in both azimuth eχn and elevation eδn angle. The azimuth,
χ and elevation, δ angle for grid center points and corresponding averaged Vlos for
all the scans of two lidars are used to determine combined uncertainty.
The two wind components u and v are determined from equation 2.9. According
to [16], to reduce the error in the u and v components the individual elements in the
inverse of the detection matrix (the 2×2 matrix with χ and δ in equation 2.9) should
be kept small, and the sensitivity to Vlos errors can be assessed using each row of
the inverse matrix. It is also stated, when the lidar beams intersects at 90◦it has
norms of 1 in each row of the inverse detection matrix, which minimizes the error in
this inverse detection process. But in the plane scans the measurement point moves
within a large area and the intersection angle is not always 90◦. The maximum
norm values of each rows of the inverse detection matrix and the intersection angle
range of the three plane scans are given in table 5.1. It can be seen from the table
that the sensitivity of the inverse detection process for the u-component is close to
the optimal value and for v it is higher in all the plane scans. Thus, it is expected
to have higher estimation error in the v-component than the u-component.

turbulent ﬂuctuations are attenuated due to the weighted average within the probe

related to the probability density function of the radial velocities in the vicinity of
the focus point. Here, similar analysis is done on the single point measurement and
the horizontal scan at scan head height (1.3 m) for the line-of-sight velocities.
To attain the statistical properties from the averaged spectrum, each of the
spectra has to be made noise ﬂattened and scaled. This is done according to one of
the processes described in [1, 2]. The method is described brieﬂy in appendix A.12.

5.4.4.3
Discussion
It can be seen that, instantaneous spectra at several positions along a line can be
evaluated by the line scan measurements from the lidars. But these line scans are
extremely fast. For instance, the line scan along the ﬂow in section 5.4.4.2, a 2
m line is measured back and forth within 0.10 s. It is mentioned before, during
these measurements there were vibrations in the scan head, and the motors within
the lidars started making loud noises. Thus, these measurements have errors and
higher uncertainty. The high power spectral density at higher frequency might be
due to the noise. It is to be noted, for the two cases discussed here, the line across
the ﬂow can be resolved to higher frequency compared to the line along the ﬂow,
because of faster scan in the former one. Also the weighting functions eﬀects the
variance of the measured velocity, this can be predicted theoretically [9, 35], but this
is beyond the scope of this thesis. Comparison with other measurement techniques
is required to verify the accuracy of these analyses. Measurements from line scans
of WindScanners are validated with sonic anemometers in a campaign at Lysefjord
bridge [17], but there the scanning speed was not very fast, spectrum for the 2
wind components were evaluated up to Nyquist frequency close to 0.25 Hz. On
the contrary, line scans within the wind tunnel in this measurement campaign are
extremely fast, power spectral density can be resolved up to 18.52 Hz for the line scan
across the ﬂow (see ﬁgure 5.23a and 5.23b). When the WindScanners are scanning
extremely fast there are some technical issues which might lead to erroneous data.
However, the determination of accuracy of these measurements are not possible as
there are neither measurements from the hot-wire nor simulated ﬂow is available.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this research project, measurements taken by two short-range WindScanners in
diﬀerent scanning patterns at two diﬀerent roughness conditions, in the boundary
layer wind tunnel at Politecnico di Milano are analyzed. Within the tunnel the ﬂow
is mostly dominant in one direction, thus an assumption has been made that, the
vertical component w = 0. With this assumption, the two other components, u and
v are evaluated using two line-of-sight velocities measurements. From diﬀerent scan-
ning patterns mean wind ﬁelds, mean vertical proﬁles, surface characteristics and
turbulence properties are determined and discussed. Here, the changing turbulent
condition along the tunnel is well described by the surface characteristic parameters.
Flow ﬁelds are determined at horizontal and vertical planes. From these mea-
surements, it has been seen that, by using lidars, the homogeneity of the ﬂow within
a tunnel can be observed. In this case, the wake generated by the spires and the
higher turbulence due to roughness stones can be observed in the evaluated ﬂow
ﬁelds. Again, if the ﬂow is only in one direction one single WindScanner can be
used to evaluate the mean wind ﬁeld within a tunnel.
Next, with roughness elements placed at the upstream of the ﬂow, mean vertical
proﬁles of the streamwise direction (u-component) at ten diﬀerent positions along
the ﬂow have been obtained by using the vertical plane measurements along the
ﬂow. Subsequently, roughness length, z0 and friction velocity u∗are obtained at
these positions by using the log law.
A decreasing trend has been observed for
these surface characteristics along the tunnel due to roughness change, which was
expected due to the rough to smooth transition. Also from the turbulence intensity
proﬁles it is observed that, TI in the ﬂow direction also decreases due to roughness
change, but the TI in cross-ﬂow direction remains almost constant along the tunnel.
This is because the ﬂow is mainly in the longitudinal direction. But, the vertical
velocity contamination aﬀect the measurements where the elevation angle is high.
Thus, comparison with other measurements or simulation is recommended to have
better understanding of the eﬀect of vertical velocity contamination.
The numerical uncertainties associated with the reconstructed u and v compo-
nents are determined for diﬀerent plane scanning patterns. The uncertainty in v is
found to be higher than u in all cases, and these uncertainties depend on the in-
tersection angle between the two beams of the two lidars at a certain measurement
position.
50
==PAGE:64==
Furthermore, The comparison between statistics calculated from measured Vlos
and averaged spectrum is done on single point measurement and horizontal plane
measurement. It is found that the variation based on averaged spectra is higher
compared to the variation based on Vlos distribution. For plane scans the method
of averaged spectra gives variations that are more detailed while it is more averaged
for the method based on Vlos distribution.
In addition, 2 m fast line scans along and across the ﬂow are used to determine
turbulence spectrum at diﬀerent positions along the lines. It has been demonstrated
that, with fast line scans the inertial sub-range (-5/3 slope) of the turbulent spectrum
can be determined by the lidars simultaneously in diﬀerent positions, keeping in
mind that the lidars have weighting eﬀect on the calculated spectra.

A.10
Floor and Ceiling as Seen by the Lidars
In section 5.2 it has been discussed that in the reconstruction of u-component a
global oﬀset of 45 mm has been observed. This happens due to the error in real
and recorded measurement point in the system. If there is a tilt in the scan head,
there will be an oﬀset in these two points, and it will increase with the measurement
distance. Also the probe volume has eﬀect on the measurement of Vlos close to ﬂoor
and ceiling. One way to better understand about this oﬀset is to look into the signal
power of the Doppler frequency spectrum along the height.
The ﬂattened Doppler spectrum (ﬁgure A.22a) for each of the line-of-sight veloc-
ity measurement are given in the data set. When a spectrum has a signiﬁcant peak
it measures a Vlos, this happens when the backscatterd signal is returning from a
moving particle. When the signal returns from a non-moving object, there is no sig-
niﬁcant peak. Thus, when the lidar probe volume do not interact with any moving
particle, there is no peak. The total power within a spectrum can be calculated by
adding all the power spectral density within a spectrum. If pi is the power spectral
density corresponding to velocity vi, the total power in the spectrum is calculated
by,

In the vertical plane measurement along the ﬂow, there are ten vertical lines
for 371 scans. The spectra found in the same vertical coordinates are averaged at

calculated using equation A.2. Now, the position where the velocity is measured
within the ﬂoor and roof is estimated by taking the position where the gradient of
the power is high and low, respectively along the height. Several plots regarding
this analysis are given in appendix A.11.
In ﬁgure A.18, the ﬂoor and ceiling positions as seen by the lidars determined

volume and the misalignment of the lidar scan head are contributing. Firstly, here
the tilt in the beam is positive, because both at the ceiling and the roof the lidars
see a decreasing trend. For example, when it is recorded a height within the ﬂoor
in the system due to the positive tilt the lidar is actually measuring above the ﬂoor,
and this diﬀerence increases as the measurement position moves farther. In, case of
the ceiling, it is also understood that due to positive misalignment of the tilt angle,
when the measurement position is recorded just beneath the ceiling it is actually
measuring at the ceiling. The increasing probe volume also have contribution in this
case. But looking at the decreasing trend at the ceiling it can be understood that,
the tilting eﬀect is more signiﬁcant. The error in tilt is higher in R2D3, because the
diﬀerence of height from the ceiling and ﬂoor is greater for this lidar. Furthermore,
there are two trends for upward and downward scan can be observed. It indicates
that, there is also an oﬀset between the measurement position and the recorded
