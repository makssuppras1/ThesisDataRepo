Abstract  
 
Oral drug administration is the most conventional method for drug delivery due to its convenience 
and patient compliance. However, administration of such therapeutics faces biological barriers, 
which cause poor drug solubility, poor permeability, and poor stability in the gastrointestinal (GI) 
environment.  
 
To address these limitations, this project designed a colon-targeted delivery system utilizing 
reduced graphene oxide (rGO) as a nanocarrier, integrated within a protective, pH-responsive 
electrospun fiber matrix composed of Eudragit S100. The model compound, Rhodamine B, was 
first loaded onto rGO to improve its payload and modulate release kinetics. This rGO-drug complex 
was then successfully incorporated into Eudragit S100 (ES100) fibers via electrospinning.  
 
Comprehensive characterization techniques, such as SEM, FTIR, DSC, contact angle 
measurements, and confocal microscopy, verified the formation of uniform fibers, successful drug 
encapsulation, and interactions between the polymer and nanocarrier. 
 
In vitro release profiling under simulated gastrointestinal conditions (FaSSGF and FaSSIF) 
demonstrated that the rGO-based fibers exhibited a significantly attenuated burst release and a more 
sustained, controlled release profile than control fibers without rGO. The ES100 matrix effectively 
served as a gastric shield, preventing release in acidic media while facilitating targeted payload 
delivery at intestinal pH. 
 
Overall, the findings demonstrate that combining rGO as a drug carrier with a pH-responsive 
polymeric shield offers a promising strategy for developing oral drug delivery systems capable of 
bypassing gastric degradation and achieve localized, sustained release in the colon.

1. Introduction  
Oral drug administration is the most conventional method for drug delivery due to its convenience 
and patient compliance. However, administration of such therapeutics faces biological barriers, 
which cause poor drug solubility, poor permeability, and poor stability in the gastrointestinal (GI) 
environment. The GI tract consists of barriers such as digestive enzymes, variable pH values, and 
the mucus layer [1,2].  
The intestinal epithelium is composed of villi, which are covered by enterocytes (absorptive), goblet 
cells (mucus secreting), and M cells. These specialized epithelial cells reside in the Peyer’s patches, 
and lymphoid organs. The M cells are significant for drug delivery, as they are less covered by 
mucus and consist of high transcytotic capacity. Therefore, comprehending such barriers is crucial 
for designing effective drug carriers [2].  
 
Several different nanocarriers have been designed for drug delivery systems to overcome such 
barriers. Using such drug carriers provide advantages such as regulating pharmacokinetics and 
enhancing delivery of agents to target sites. Furthermore, the nanoparticle formulations encapsulate 
and protect drugs and can release them in a controlled manner [1]. Additionally, the nanoparticle 
surface can be modified to enhance or reduce adhesion to target specific cells [2].  
 
1.1 Electrospinning  
Electrospinning is a widely used technique for fabricating nanofibers with diameters ranging to the 
nanometer (nm) scale. The process consists of applying high voltage to extract or draw the charged 
polymer solution from the syringe, which forms a thin fiber reaching the collector. As the applied 
electric field overcomes the surface tension of the droplet at the tip of the needle, a taylor cone is 
formed. From its apex, a jet emerges, which undergoes elongation and thinning, and it dries into a 
continuous nanofibrous mat. The fiber diameters can range from micrometers (µm) to nanometers 
(nm) [3,4].  
 
The instrument setup consists of a syringe filled with the polymer solution, a high voltage power 
supply, and a collector, which is generally a conductive material. There are several factors that may 
influence the outcome of electrospinning process. These parameters include the flow rate, the 
applied voltage, the distance between the spinneret and the collector, surface tension, as well as 
viscosity, and conductivity. Additionally, environmental factors including temperature and 
humidity affect the entire procedure. Therefore, adjustments in these parameters lead to significant 
changes regarding fiber morphology, which determine the structure of the formed fibers [5].  
 
Since the 1990’s, electrospinning has attracted attention across numerous scientific fields. This 
technique seemed appealing as it contains the ability to produce fibers with high surface to volume 
ratios, and tunable porosity in a cost-effective manner [4]. In the course of time, several variations 
of this technique, such as coaxial, triaxial, and needleless electrospinning procedures have been 
developed. These advances have improved its capabilities, and have enabled the creation of 
complex fibers, while broadening the range of materials that can be utilized [3]. Presently, 
electrospinning plays a vital role in biomedical applications such as tissue engineering, wound 
healing, and drug delivery [6].

1.1.1 Electrospun Nanofibers in Drug Delivery  
Electrospun nanofibers have emerged as promising carriers in drug delivery systems (DDS), due to 
their distinctive structural and functional characteristics. Their fibrous architecture mimics the 
extracellular matrix, creating a favourable environment for cell adhesion and tissue integration. 
Furthermore, their high surface area, interconnected porosity, and ease of chemical modification 
make them suitable for the incorporation and controlled release of therapeutic agents [6,7]. Drugs 
can be integrated into these fibers through various methods, including blending with the polymer 
solution prior to electrospinning, encapsulating within the core of coaxial fibers, or functionalizing 
the fiber surface after fabrication. Each approach affects the drug release profile, enabling outcomes 
from immediate release to sustained, or stimuli-responsive delivery [7]. 
 
Among the various strategies, coaxial electrospinning has gained significant interest due to its 
ability to produce core–shell structured fibers. This configuration offers protection for sensitive 
bioactive compounds such as proteins, nucleic acids, and growth factors against harsh external 
conditions, while facilitating controlled and sustained release. Core–shell matrixes also minimize 
the initial burst release commonly observed with simple blend electrospun fibers, making them 
especially advantageous for prolonged therapeutic applications [7]. In addition to coaxial systems, 
multilayered mats and composite fiber constructs further expand the potential to fine-tune drug 
release profiles and enhance stability [6]. 
 
Electrospun fibers have been explored across a broad spectrum of therapeutic applications. These 
include oral and transdermal delivery systems, antimicrobial and regenerative wound dressings, and 
implantable scaffolds for localized treatment [6]. Their adaptability and versatility further support 
combination therapies, allowing for the simultaneous delivery of multiple drugs with synergistic or 
complementary actions. In comparison to traditional dosage forms, electrospun nanofibers offer 
several advantages, such as high drug encapsulation efficiency, compatibility with both hydrophilic 
and hydrophobic agents, and the ability to incorporate thermally sensitive biomolecules due to their 
gentle fabrication conditions [7]. 
 
In general, electrospun nanofibers represent a highly versatile and promising platform for next-
generation DDSs. By combining the biomimetic sturcture of biolgical tissue with engineered drug 
release profiles, these materials are increasingly recognized as a valuable tool in pharmaceutical and 
biomedical innovation [4,6]. 
 
1.1.2 pH-Responsive polymers (Eudragit S100) 
pH-responsive polymers have gained wide attention in oral drug delivery because they can exploit 
the natural pH variations along the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. The stomach typically maintains a 
highly acidic environment (pH 1–3), while the small intestine and colon are near-neutral to slightly 
alkaline (pH 6–7.5) [2,8]. These conditions provide opportunities to design drug carriers that remain 
intact in the stomach but dissolve or swell further along the tract, enabling targeted release. 
 
Eudragit polymers, a family of methacrylate-based copolymers, are among the most widely used 
pH-responsive materials. Eudragit S100 dissolves at a pH value above 7, making it suitable for 
colon-specific delivery [2,8]. This selective solubility helps protect drugs from the acidic gastric 
environment and enzymatic degradation in the upper intestine, while releasing them in the distal 
ileum or colon. Studies have shown that drugs encapsulated in Eudragit-based nanoparticles, 
==PAGE:7==
 
7 
microspheres, or nanofibers demonstrate enhanced stability, bioavailability, and site-specific release 
profiles [9,10]. 
 
Recent developments include combining Eudragit polymers with other excipients and 
nanomaterials to create multifunctional systems for site-specific release. For example, Eudragit 
S100 has been widely employed to coat formulations of anti-inflammatory drugs such as 
mesalamine and budesonide, enabling protection from gastric acidity and selective release in the 
colon where inflammation occurs [10,11]. Such pH-sensitive systems have demonstrated improved 
local drug availability and reduced systemic side effects, underlining their therapeutic relevance in 
managing inflammatory bowel diseases. 
 
1.3 Role of Nanocarriers (rGO) in Drug Delivery  
Reduced graphene oxide (rGO) has recently emerged as a promising nanocarrier in drug delivery 
systems because of its unique physicochemical features. rGO sheets provide a large surface area, 
abundant functional groups, and strong π–π interactions, which enable efficient drug loading, 
surface modification, and controlled release [12]. Moreover, the incorporation of rGO into 
polymeric scaffolds enhances mechanical strength, conductivity, and responsiveness to external 
stimuli such as light or heat. 
 
One of the most attractive features of rGO is its ability to convert near-infrared (NIR) light into 
localized heat, a property that has been exploited in photothermal therapy. This effect has been 
combined with drug delivery functions, enabling on-demand release of therapeutic agents under 
external stimulation. For example, electrospun nanofibrous mats incorporating rGO have been 
designed to release antibiotics when irradiated, with the photothermal response simultaneously 
enhancing bactericidal activity [12,13]. Such dual functional systems are particularly promising for 
treating infections associated with chronic wounds and inflammatory diseases, where both localized 
antimicrobial action and controlled drug delivery are required. 
 
In addition to its photothermal properties, rGO also improves aqueous stability of drug-loaded 
scaffolds, preventing premature release under physiological conditions while allowing stimulus-
triggered release when required [6]. These features make rGO an attractive component in advanced 
nanocarrier systems for personalized and responsive drug delivery. Its integration into hydrogels, 
nanofibers, and composite nanoparticles demonstrates strong potential for applications in 
antimicrobial therapy, oncology, and regenerative medicine. 
 
1.4 Colon targeted drug delivery system  
Targeting drugs to the colon is particularly valuable for the treatment of local diseases such as 
ulcerative colitis, Crohn’s disease, and colorectal cancer, as well as for systemic delivery of 
molecules that benefit from colonic absorption (e.g., proteins and peptides). The colon offers a 
relatively stable environment, longer transit time, and lower enzymatic activity compared to the 
stomach and small intestine [2]. However, physiological barriers such as mucus layers, variable pH, 
and limited fluid volume make efficient delivery challenging. 
 
Several strategies have been developed to achieve colon targeting. pH-sensitive polymers, such as 
Eudragit S100, remain the most widely adopted, releasing drugs when exposed to the higher pH of 
the distal gut [9,10]. Time-dependent systems, pressure-sensitive coatings, and biodegradable 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
carriers that respond to colonic microbiota have also been investigated. For example, thioketal 
nanoparticles that degrade in response to reactive oxygen species have shown promise for treating 
inflammatory bowel disease by releasing drugs selectively at inflamed sites [14]. 
 
In general, colon-targeted delivery is a critical advancement in oral medicine. By combining smart 
materials like pH-sensitive polymers, advanced nanocarriers like rGO, and disease-specific 
targeting strategies, researchers are moving closer to achieving highly precise, effective, and 
patient-friendly treatments for chronic gut disorders and cancers. 
 
2. Hypothesis & Aim of Research  
This project investigates the hypothesis that the delivery of therapeutics using the nano-carrier 
reduced graphene oxide (rGO) together with pH-dependent Eudragit S100 (ES100) will ensure 
sustained drug release and prevent gastric degradation. This specific Drug Delivery System (DDS) 
utilizes electrospun nanofibers composed of ES100, a polymer that dissolves at the higher pH of the 
intestines, to protect its payload from the harsh acidic environment of the stomach. 
 
This project focuses on developing a novel drug delivery system targeting inflammation in the 
colon. Rhodamine B presents a model drug, which is encapsulated in reduced graphene oxide 
(rGO), chosen as a drug carrier to shield the drug from rapid release. Furthermore, the formed 
complex is combined with ES100 to establish a protective outer layer. The polymeric nanofibrous 
sheets are fabricated by electrospinning. Consequently, the efficacy of this approach is tested by 
comparing fiber mats with and without rGO, as well as their micro-cut particle formulations, 
through a series of in vitro release studies and material characterizations including FTIR, DSC, and 
contact angle measurements. 
 
 
2. Materials and methods  
2.1 Materials and solvents  
 
Rhodamine B (Rhod. B), reduced graphene oxide (rGO), N, N-Dimethylacetamide (DMAc), 
absolute Ethanol (EtOH), Sodium chloride (NaCl), Hydrochloric acid (HCl), MilliQ water, Sodium 
hydroxide (NaOH), sodium phosphate (Na3PO4), Eudragit S100, FaSSIF/FeSSIF/FaSSGF powder.  
 
2.2 Preparation of polymeric solutions  
The 16 wt.% polymeric solutions of Eudragit S100 (ES100) were prepared using solvents 
EtOH/DMAc with a ratio of 80:20 v/v. The solutions were stirred overnight at 25℃ on a magnet 
stirrer set to 110 rpm until the solutes were dissolved to obtain homogenous and transparent 
solutions.  
The preparation of Eudragit S100-Rhodamine B polymeric solutions consisted of adding the 
weighed drug (Rodamine B) to the prepared polymeric solution approximately 30 minutes prior to 
electrospinning. The amount of Rhodamine B corresponded to 1 % w/w (weight of Rhod. B to the 
weight of the polymer) of the total polymer mass. Eventually, the solution was subjected to 
continued stirring on a magnet stirrer to ensure uniform mixing for at least 30 minutes.  
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
 
The Eudragit S100- rGO-Rhodamine B polymer solutions were prepared by adding 1 ml of the 
polymer solution to the pellet (collected from rGO-Rhodamine B complex suspension) with a 
pipette to form a mixture. The 1 ml polymer suspension was vortexed for approximately 2 minutes 
to ensure a homogenous mixture with the pellet, where it was subsequently added to the rest of the 
polymer solution. The polymer suspensions were left on a magnet stirrer for 15 minutes at 110 rpm. 
To achieve a uniform and complete integration of the polymer solution with the complex 
suspension, the mixture was subjected to ultrasonication for 30 minutes. This step further promoted 
degassing of the mixture to avoid bubbles prior to electrospinning, as it can negatively affect the 
quality of the electrospun fiber sheets.  
 
 
2.4 Drug Loading  
 
Rhodamine B (Rhod. B) was used as a model drug in this project. Complex suspensions of 
Rhodamine B: rGO were prepared by dissolving weighed Rhodamine B and rGO in pure ethanol. 
The suspensions were prepared by a 2:1 ratio, where mostly Rhodamine B was used. The solutions 
were left on a magnet stirrer for 24 hours at 25℃. Subsequently, the pellet (encapsulated 
Rhodamine B onto rGO) was collected by washing it with Ethanol for 5-6 times to remove free 
Rhodamine B from the complex suspension. The washing step was employed by centrifugation for 
5 minutes at 14.000 rpm for each round. After washing the pellet, 1 ml of the polymer solution was 
added to the pellet with a pipette to form a mixture. The 1 ml polymer suspension was vortexed for 
approximately 2 minutes to ensure a homogenous mixture with the pellet, where it was 
subsequently added to the rest of the polymer solution. The polymer suspensions were left on a 
magnet stirrer for 15 minutes at 110 rpm. To achieve a uniform and complete integration of the 
polymer solution with the complex suspension, the mixture was subjected to ultrasonication for 30 
minutes. This step further promoted degassing of the mixture to avoid bubbles prior to 
electrospinning, as it can negatively affect the quality of the electrospun fiber sheets.  
 
 
2.3 Electrospinning of Nanofibrous polymer mats  
The solutions were electrospun (using a home-made electrospinning setup) with a 21-gauge needle 
using the parameters, including 15 kV for voltage, 15 cm for needle-collector distance, and 1 ml/hr 
for flow rate.  
 
2.2.3 Preparation of Micro-cut polymeric fiber sheets  
Electrospun polymeric fiber sheets were subjected to micro-cutting, using an industrial hot 
embosser (Collin Press P300 SV, Collin Lab & Pilot Solutions GmbH, Maitenbeth, Germany). The 
micro-cutting process consisted of cutting the fiber sheets utilising square-shaped ultra-sharp micro-
cutters by applying pressure (30 bars) for 5 minutes to the micro-cutter placed on top of the 
polymeric sheet. The cut fibers were carefully removed from the micro-cutter, while being attached 
to an obducat foil. The size of the micro-cutter used for cutting all the fiber sheets was 130 µm, 
consisting of a depth of 70 µm.  
The micro-cut sheets were collected on the obducat foils and stored for in vitro release studies.

2.6 In Vitro Drug Release Studies  
The in vitro drug release studies were conducted on three different types of samples: Eudragit S100-
rGO-Rhod. B, Eudragit S100-Rhod. B, and micro-cut polymeric sheets of ES100-rGO-Rhod. B, 
and ES100-Rho. B.  
 
For the release studies FaSSIF and FaSSGF buffer solutions were prepared to simulate the pH of 
the gastrointestinal environment. 
 
The FaSSIF medium was prepared according to protocol by weighing the buffer components: 0.105 
g of sodium hydroxide (NaOH), 0.8595 g sodium phosphate (Na3PO4), 1.5465 g sodium chloride 
(NaCl), and mixing them with 225 ml MilliQ water. The solution was left on a magnet stirrer until 
the components were dissolved. Subsequently, the pH of the solution was adjusted by adding 
hydrochloric acid (HCl) to reach a pH value of 7.4 by using a pH meter. Then 25 ml of MilliQ 
water was added to adjust the volume of the solution to 250 ml in a beaker. Afterwards, 0.560 g of 
the FaSSIF/FeSSIF/FaSSGF or F3 powder was weighed and added to a glass jar, where the 
prepared buffer solution was added and stirred until dissolved. Finally, the solution was left on a 
magnet stirrer for 2 hours to equilibrate the solution.  
 
The FaSSGF solution was prepared by weighing 0.9995 g of sodium chloride (NaCl), 14.55 g 
hydrochloric acid (HCl), and mixing them with 450 ml MilliQ water in a beaker. The buffer 
solution was left on a magnet stirrer until the components were dissolved. The pH of the solution 
was adjusted using a pH meter, where sodium hydroxide (NaOH) was added to reach a pH value of 
1.6. Subsequently, 50 ml of MilliQ water was added to the solution to adjust the volume to 500 ml. 
Then 0.03 g of the FaSSIF/FeSSIF/FaSSGF or F3 powder was weighed, and the buffer solution was 
added. The solution was left on a magnet stirrer until dissolved.  
 
2.6.1 Dissolution Studies (Dialysis bag method) 
The in vitro drug release study was employed utilising the ‘’dissolution’’ method, where the 
realeased Rhodamine B from fiber sheets loaded into dialysis bags, was collected at different time 
intervals from the FaSSGF and FaSSIF media.  
The samples consisted of 20 mg polymer fiber sheets either consisting of Rhodamine B and rGO 
complex (sample A), or only Rhodamine B (sample B). The samples were placed in snakeskin, 
where the ends were knotted or twisted. Then the dialysis bags were inserted in 20 ml of FaSSGF 
medium in falcon tubes, respectively. The falcon tubes were installed in a water bath sonicator at 37 
°C and 60 rpm. 200 uL of samples were taken after 0, 5, 15, 30, 45, 60 minutes, and the 
corresponding volume was replaced each time with fresh FaSSGF solution.  
Subsequently, the dialysis bags were removed from falcon tubes containing FaSSGF, and then were 
inserted in falcon tubes containing 20 ml fresh FaSSIF solutions. Then 200 uL of samples were 
collected from the falcon tubes at different time intervals: 0, 5, 10, 20, 30, 45, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180, 
240, 300, 360 minutes. The corresponding volume was replaced each time with fresh FaSSIF 
solution.  
All samples were collected in eppendorf tubes prior addition to a 96-well-plate for further analysis 
with the plate-reader instrument.  
The dissolution studies were replicated several times to achieve reliable and reproducible data.

2.6.2 Micro-cut release 
The micro-cut samples were employed in a similar procedure for in vitro release studies described 
in section 2.6.1. The fiber sheets that had been micro-cut and adhered to the surface of an obducat 
foil were excised and removed using a scalpel. The retrieved micro-cut samples were subsequently 
loaded into dialysis bags. The dialysis bags were further inserted in falcon tubes containing FaSSGF 
and FaSSIF solutions, respectively. The falcon tubes were installed in a water bath sonicator at 37 
°C and 60 rpm. 200 uL of samples were taken after 0, 5, 15, 30, 45, 60 minutes, and the 
corresponding volume was replaced each time with fresh FaSSGF solution.  
A similar approach was applied as the fiber sheets release studies, where the dialysis bags were 
removed and inserted in falcon tubes containing FaSSIF solution. Samples were collected and 
stored in eppendorf tubes at different time intervals: 0, 5, 10, 20, 30, 45, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180, 240, 
300, 360 minutes, where the corresponding volume was replaced with fresh FaSSIF solution.  
 
2.6.3 pH-dependent release  
The pH values of FaSSGF and FaSSIF solutions were altered to test the release profile of 
Rhodamine B at different pH values. The pH changed from 1.6 to 2.5 for FaSSGF solution. The pH 
changed from 7.4 to 6.8 for FaSSIF solution.  
The pH changes applied to all samples, including fiber sheets of Eudragit S100-rGO-Rhod. B, 
Eudragit S100-Rhod. B, and micro-cut fiber sheets.  
 
2.7 Plate-Reader  
The multimode microplate reader (Thermo ScientificTM VarioskanTM LUX Multimode Microplate 
Reader) is an instrument designed to detect Fluorescence Intensity (FI), and Absorbance (UV-Vis). 
It has the capability to measure multiple samples simultaneously, which offers efficiency and 
consistency in terms of data collection [15].  
As mentioned in the earlier sections, the samples collected from dissolution studies were inserted 
into black polystyrene 96-well microplates for measuring fluorescence assays. The emitted 
fluorescence intensities from the samples were measured by the microplate reader to calculate the 
released percentages of Rhodamine B versus time.  
Prior to measurements of fluorescence intensities from the samples, stock solutions of Rhodamine 
B (2 mg) dissolved in FaSSGF (8 ml) and FaSSIF (8 ml) were prepared, respectively. Furthermore, 
standard dilutions were formulated from the stock solutions to obtain a standard curve for the 
microplate reader. The standard dilutions and the samples were transferred into black polystyrene 
96-well microplates for quantifying fluorescence intensities. The results were compiled in Excel 
spreadsheets.

3.1.2 Fiber Thickness  
The thickness of electrospun fiber mats were measured prior to in vitro release studies, using a 
digital caliper. This step was crucial to ensure accurate and coherent drug release from the 
electrospun fiber sheets. The variation in thickness of nanofibrous mats affects the available surface 
area, and the rate and extent of drug release. Therefore, the digital caliper was utilised to quantify 
the thickness at multiple points across each sample to minimize variability, hence improving the 
reliability of the release data. As a result, more precise comparisons could be employed between 
samples, confirming that any observed differences in drug release profiles were not due such 
inconsistencies. 
The fiber sheets weighed for the in vitro release studies consisted of matching thicknesses. Figures

3.1.3 Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC)  
 
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) is an analytical technique, which measures the heat 
capacity of samples as a function of temperature. DSC profiles provide information about thermal 
stability, and physical and chemical transitions of polymers, such as glass transition temperature 
(Tg), melting point, and degradation behaviour. The applied heat flow can be monitored as a 
function of temperature, where the structural state of the polymer can be characterized. 
Furthermore, the processing of the polymeric fibers during electrospinning could influence the 
polymer’s thermal stability. DSC is a valuable tool for detecting amorphous transitions, residual 
solvent evaporation, and degradation, which are important parameters for assessing the performance 
of polymers in drug delivery systems (DDSs) [19,20].  
DSC thermograms for polymers such as ES100, reveal a Tg value of 170-180°C, where there is not 
sharp melting points detected, due the amorphous nature of such a polymer [21].  
 
The thermal characteristics of Eudragit S100 (ES100), ES100-rGO-Rhod. B, and ES100-Rhod. B 
samples were determined using a DSC equipment. The samples were compressed in standard 
aluminium pans and heated from 40 to 250°C at a heating rate of 10°C/min under constant purging 
of nitrogen at 30 ml/min.  
Figure 19 presents the obtained thermogram of ES100 sample. As shown in the thermogram, the 
detected smaller peaks below 100°C region, indicate moisture release or residual solvent 
evaporation. In the intermediate region from 100-150°C indicate endothermic or exothermic 
fluctuations in the polymer matrix. Furthermore, a noticeable transition is observed within the 
region of 170-185°C of the thermogram, which corresponds to Tg of ES100. Additionally, the 
thermal degradation of the polymer is observed at the broad endothermic signal shift in the region 
above 220°C.

due to the Prescence of rGO and Rhod. B.  
 
These DSC analyses reveal that incorporation of Rhodamine B and rGO into ES100 electrospun 
fibers progressively modifies the polymer’s thermal transitions, mainly through enhanced solvent 
retention and restricted chain mobility. While the glass transition of ES100 remains within the 
expected range, the damped transitions in the composite systems highlight polymer–additive 
interactions that can promote a more uniform amorphous structure. Such interactions are 
advantageous for stabilizing the drug in its amorphous form and may contribute to more consistent 
release profiles, although attention must be given to managing solvent retention.

3.1.4 FTIR  
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) is a versatile technique that identifies functional 
groups in molecules by measuring how chemical bonds absorb infrared radiation at characteristic 
frequencies. The resulting spectra act as a molecular fingerprint, providing valuable information 
about the structural composition of polymers and potential interactions with incorporated 
compounds [21,22]. 
 
In pharmaceutical research, FTIR has proven especially useful for investigating potential 
interactions between drugs, excipients, and nanocarriers. Shifts or changes in peak intensities can 
indicate hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interactions, or van der Waals forces between polymer 
matrices and incorporated molecules [20]. For enteric polymers such as ES100, FTIR can be 
employed to confirm the presence of characteristic ester and carboxylic acid groups, while also 
providing evidence of interactions with encapsulated agents, such as model dyes or bioactive 
compounds [21]. When combined with complementary techniques such as DSC, FTIR strengthens 
the interpretation of both the chemical structure and the physical state of electrospun drug delivery 
systems. 
 
Figure 22 shows the FTIR chromatograms of pure ES100 in powder form, and ES100 polymer 
sheet samples, respectively. The FTIR spectra of pure ES100 powder and the processed polymer 
(electrospun form) showed highly comparable absorption profiles, confirming that the chemical 
structure of the polymer remained unchanged during processing. Both spectra displayed 
characteristic peaks of the methacrylic acid–methyl methacrylate copolymer, including a broad O–

prominent ester/carboxyl C=O stretching peak at approximately 1720 cm⁻¹, and distinct C–O

Minor differences in peak intensity were observed, particularly in the C=O and C–O bands, which

3.1.5 Contact angle measurements  
 
Contact angle measurement is a quick way to judge how a surface “likes” a liquid. We place a tiny 
droplet on the material and record the angle where the droplet edge meets the surface (sessile-drop 
method). A small angle means the liquid spreads (the surface is hydrophilic); a large angle means it 
beads up (hydrophobic). This number reflects the balance of interfacial tensions described by 
Young’s equation and it changes with surface chemistry and roughness. On rough or porous sheets, 
such as electrospun Eudragit mats, the roughness can amplify the intrinsic wettability [23,24], or 
trap air under the drop [25], so reporting texture and preparation is important. In practice, we 
typically report a static contact angle (one image) and, when needed, advancing/receding angles (to 
capture hysteresis). These measurements help predict how the fibers will interact with water or 
biological fluids, and they complement FTIR/SEM results by linking surface chemistry and texture 
to wetting behavior. 
 
Figure 25 shows the measured angles of a water droplet on the surface of ES100 polymer sheet. The 
sessile-drop image on the electrospun ES100 mat shows a beaded droplet with a static contact angle 
of 137-138°, indicating a hydrophobic surface (contact angle > 90°). Because the substrate is 
fibrous and porous, this is an apparent contact angle influenced by texture: roughness and trapped 
air pockets can raise the measured angle [25] compared with a smooth ES100 polymeric sheet. In 
practical terms, this hydrophobicity suggests slower initial wetting in water; documenting drop 
volume, imaging time, and whether the value is static, or advancing/receding makes the result 
reproducible. These interpretations follow standard contact-angle theory and reporting practices.

3.3 Interpretation of Loading Efficiencies (LE) 
 
To determine the optimal drug loading within the nanocarrier reduced graphene oxide (rGO), 
appropriate amounts of Rhodamine B had to be incorporated into the polymer solutions prior to 
electrospinning. Therefore, calculations of loading efficiencies were conducted to evaluate the 
amount of Rhodamine B encapsulated in rGO, which is a critical factor for ensuring effective drug 
delivery. To optimize this parameter, Rhod. B:rGO complexes were prepared at varying weight 
ratios; 3:1, 2:1, and 1:1. The 3:1 ratio was prepared by adding 9.13 mg of Rhod. B to 3.04 mg of 
rGO. For the preparation of 1:1 ratio, 9.13 mg of Rhod. B was added to 9.13 mg of rGO.  
These different formulations were designed to assess which ratio would yield the highest drug 
loading efficiency. All sample preparations were performed in duplicates to minimize experimental 
variability and validate the consistency of the results. The results of these analyses are presented in 
Figures 33 and 34. As illustrated in the graphs, the highest loading efficiencies were observed at the 
2:1 Rhodamine B:rGO ratio, with values of 17.09 and 13.42, respectively, surpassing those 
obtained at the 3:1 and 1:1 ratios. Based on these findings, all subsequent ES100-rGO-Rhod. B 
complex suspensions were prepared using the 2:1 ratio, wherein a higher proportion of Rhodamine 
B was incorporated relative to rGO.

3.4 Release mechanism 
 
Rhodamine B (Rhod. B) binds to reduced graphene oxide (rGO) through p-p interactions, where the 
aromatic rings of Rhod. B stack on the sp2 graphene domains of rGO, forming non-covalent 
bindings [33,34].  
Another interaction between Rhod. B and rGO is through electrostatic attractions. Rhod. B is a 
cationic dye, and rGO is a less oxidised form of graphene oxide (GO), which has oxygen groups 
[35]. As a result, the molecules are electrostatically attracted. Rhod. B’s amine and carboxylic 
groups can form hydrogen bonds with the oxygen groups of rGO [36,37,38].  
As mentioned in the earlier sections, Rhod. B is a pH-dependent fluorescent dye, where its release 
is highly affected by this parameter [29,39]. Rhod. B is proven to be more stable at pH < 4 or acidic 
pH [40]. However, it is released at pH > 6, as the hydrogen bonds weaken [29].  
 
 
3.5 Impact of pH on Eudragit S100 and Rhodamine B  
 
ES100 is a pH-responsive polymer, and as such, its drug release profile is significantly influenced 
by changes in environmental pH [41,42]. Similarly, Rhod. B is a pH-sensitive fluorescent dye, 
whose optical properties are affected by pH fluctuations. Under acidic conditions (low pH), the 
lactone ring in Rhodamine B remains closed, leading to reduced fluorescence emission [43]. In 
contrast, at higher pH values, the ring opens, resulting in enhanced fluorescence [44]. Due to this 
behaviour, variations in the fluorescence observed during in vitro release studies can be attributed to 
the differing pH conditions of simulated gastric and intestinal fluids [42]. This makes Rhodamine B 
a suitable model compound for investigating pH-dependent drug delivery systems. Additionally, its 
distinct colour facilitates visual tracking of drug release behaviour in response to pH changes [45]. 
 
3.6 Effect of rGO as a carrier  
 
The reason reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is so effective at carrying large amounts of drug 
molecules comes down to its unique chemistry. Essentially, the aromatic rings found in many drugs 
are powerfully attracted to the sp² carbon network of rGO through what's known as π–π stacking 
[32,33,34]. This is backed up by additional hydrophobic and hydrogen-bonding forces. A great 
example is the dye Rhodamine B (Rhod. B); its large, conjugated structure locks in tightly with the 
foundational graphene sheet, while its amine and carboxylic acid groups can form hydrogen bonds 
with any leftover oxygen groups on the rGO [37,46]. To make the bond even stronger, there's often 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
an electrostatic attraction at play, as the positively charged Rhod. B is drawn to the negatively 
charged oxygen sites on the rGO, which further boosts how much can be loaded [36]. 
 
Beyond its impressive capacity for binding, rGO is also known for being biocompatible and 
dispersing well, particularly when it's blended with polymers [35]. This makes it much easier to 
work with and allows it to be integrated smoothly into materials like electrospun nanofibers without 
clumping up. That said, there is a catch: if you add too much rGO, the nanosheets can start to re-
stack on top of each other [27]. This self-stacking hides a lot of the valuable surface area and 
ultimately hurts its ability to interact with and hold onto drug molecules. Because of this, finding 
the perfect balance between the amount of rGO and the drug is a critical step to maximize loading 
without sacrificing the material's stability. In the end, it's this powerful combination of strong 
molecular interactions, useful physical properties, and an ability to play nice with polymers that 
makes rGO such a promising candidate for controlled drug delivery systems. 
 
4. Conclusion  
Based on the obtained results from in vitro release studies, and characterization techniques (FTIR, 
DSC, contact angle measurement, and confocal microscopy), it can be verified that the integration 
of reduced graphene oxide (rGO) markedly enhanced both the loading efficiency and the sustained 
release profile of Rhodamine B within electrospun Eudragit S100 fibers. These findings substantiate 
the promise of ES100 as an effective pH-responsive matrix for targeting therapeutic release to the 
colon. It is important to note, however, that this research was confined to a model drug and small-
scale in vitro testing; consequently, subsequent work must focus on optimizing the fiber 
composition and release methodologies. Ultimately, this study establishes a foundational proof-of-
concept, demonstrating that combining rGO with pH-sensitive polymers is a viable strategy for 
developing oral delivery systems that can bypass gastric degradation and facilitate localized 
intestinal release. 
 
5. Future Perspectives  
 
Considering the current results, it would be more precise to try other in vitro release studies, such as 
MicroDiss profiler instrument (Pion µDISS Profiler). In this project, several in vitro release studies 
were performed using the MicroDiss equipment. However, the obtained data displayed 
inconsistencies that could not be interpreted within the scope of this Thesis. Therefore, these data 
are not presented in this project. Future studies shall further optimize the application of this 
instrument, as it may provide more precise comprehension of the release kinetics under biorelevant 
conditions.  
 
Furthermore, different polymer concentrations (w/v) could be prepared in future studies to identify 
the optimal formulation in terms of structural stability, drug loading capacity, and release 
performance.  
 
Additionally, varying the ratios of the rGO-Rhod. B complex suspensions could be explored to 
enhance the loading efficiency of the drug molecule.
