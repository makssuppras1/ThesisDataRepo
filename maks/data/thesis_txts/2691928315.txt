Abstract
Hearing loss is a condition most people are familiar with. However, few outside
professional ﬁelds know the actual consequences on acoustical scene awareness
and speech perception. In order to give the general population interactive expe-
riences with hearing loss, it has previously been suggested to employ a simulator
of hearing loss. This would enable a normal hearing listener to show results com-
monly linked to hearing loss, when subject to psychoacoustic tests. To facilitate
the use of such a simulator in everyday settings, a system was developed with
real-time digital audio processing and low latency. The simulator is based on the
dynamic compressive gammachirp auditory model, which was fully adapted for
real-time, low latency processing. By using delay lines for phase compensation,
a system latency of 27 ms was achieved. Digital ﬁltering was optimised using
vectorisation, enabling processing of up to 28 channels at 22 050 Hz. Three types
of hearing loss were simulated: frequency dependent loss of sensitivity, loud-
ness recruitment, and reduced frequency selectivity. Threshold detection tasks
for three subjects show the ability of the simulator to approximate audiograms
associated with hearing loss. An inversely compressive ﬁlterbank cancels the nat-
ural compression of normal hearing listeners. This was conﬁrmed by a loudness
scaling experiment, which showed results consistent with changes in loudness
growth associated with hearing loss. Using a spectral smearing algorithm the
eﬀect of broadened auditory ﬁlters on frequency selectivity and speech percep-
tion was simulated. The platform for the simulator is bela, enabling integration
in a fully wearable device. In combination with some supporting systems, the
proposed simulator makes it possible to experience the eﬀects of hearing loss with
few restrictions on usability and setting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Motivation
Hearing loss aﬀects millions of people worldwide and an ever-growing part of
the human population relies on hearing aids for work, safety, and social activities.
Many normal hearing people have interacted with hearing impaired and wit-
nessed its consequences on speech perception. However, few have experienced
what hearing impairment actually feels like.
Since there is a growing trend of hearing impaired it is vital to keep policymakers,
administrators of funding, and the general population aware of the signiﬁcance
of hearing loss.
Increases in investments in auditory research, instructions in
prophylactic measures, and communication procedures with hearing impaired
can beneﬁt many people. This education of groups outside audiology and related
ﬁelds could be supplemented by an interactive experience, where normal hearing
subjects would hear everyday situations as if they were hearing impaired.
The idea of simulating hearing impairment is not new.
However, many such
studies are limited in their usability for realistic scenarios by either relying on
pre-computed audio ﬁles or by requiring substantial processing power. In this
project, an attempt is made at creating a simulator ﬁt for usage at social gatherings
or in restaurants1, at conventions or other forms of exhibitions.
It is the hope of the project’s author and the aﬃliated supervisor that the developed
simulator will be straightforward to install and not require expensive and delicate
clinical equipment. Given this, any institution, science fair, or artist could make
use of the simulator, and hopefully raise awareness of a condition, which will
likely impact many of us in some form.
Previous Work
Simulating various eﬀects of hearing loss has been done for many years. Notable
contributions to the ﬁeld that have inﬂuenced this project date back to the mid
1990s where Baer & Moore[1] developed spectral smearing, Nejime & Moore[2]
simulated threshold elevation in combination with reduced frequency selectivity
and ter Keurs et al.[3] studied spectral envelope smearing.
The framework of the system developed in this project is the gammachirp ﬁl-
terbank of Irino & Patterson[4] which has recently been expanded to simulate
loudness recruitment[5]. This framework has also been utilized in Hu et al.[6]
and by Grimault et al.[7] for hearing loss simulation. The latter project claims to
run in real-time on a personal computer and its software is published under an
open-source license.

Other notable hearing loss simulations are the gammawarp ﬁlterbank of Kates &
Shashidhar[8] and the 3D TUNE-In[9] project.
To the best of the author’s knowledge, no published attempts at creating a wear-
able, low-latency and real-time hearing loss simulator exist.
Application and Scope
The project presented in this report aims a building a lightweight, real-time sim-
ulation of hearing loss. This system is developed for operation on bela, a small,
embedded system for audio. This platform can readily be a foundation for a
wearable device allowing for experiencing hearing loss in any situation.
It is not the intention to create a full auditory model from the outer ear to nerve
ﬁbers that emulates true physiological behavior. Instead, the objective is to create
hearing loss eﬀects on sound environment and speech perception in a normal
hearing subject. The project thus has a phenomenological, rather than a physio-
logical approach.
The auditory models and hearing loss eﬀects implemented have a strong scientiﬁc
foundation. The audiological setup for testing and prototyping, however, was
built using consumer-grade equipment.
This was largely due to the covid-19
pandemic restricting access to laboratory facilities. On the other hand, it is also
the intention of the project to construct a simulator that does not need a high level
of expertise and high-quality equipment for installation and operation.
This (partially intentional) limitation on equipment brings a general loss of ﬁdelity
and audiological precision. However, this is not strictly necessary as the intended
application of the simulator is in a variety of acoustical environments and without
detailed subject-speciﬁc calibration.
Structure
This report has three parts. Chapter 2 will introduce the auditory model laying
the foundation of the simulator and acts as a framework for implementing hear-
ing loss eﬀects. Chapter 3 establishes the hearing loss procedures and presents
experimental validation. Lastly, chapter 4 concerns itself with the real-time im-
plementation on bela of methods described in the ﬁrst two parts.
Additionally, this report is accompanied by a public source code repository[10]
alongside a project website[11] containing code documentation and collection of
sample audio ﬁles.

Auditory Model
2.1 Background
2.1.1
Gammachirp Filter
A widely used model of frequency selectivity is the Gammachirp (GC) ﬁlterbank,
by Irino and Patterson[4], which is an extension of the well-known Gamma-
tone (GT) ﬁlterbank. More speciﬁcally, the Dynamic Compressive Gammachirp
(dcGC) ﬁlterbank is a collection of time-varying and intensity-dependent non-
linear ﬁlters with bandpass characteristics. This model has gone through multiple
iterations and has a strong and well-documented mathematical foundation[12].
Methods for implementing this model in near real-time using block-based pro-
cessing have been proposed[12], while Grimault et al.[7] have built a true real-time
implementation in python by leveraging the parallel processing power of GPUs.

2.1.2
Analysis/Synthesis Framework
By processing an array of ﬁlters in parallel a broad frequency range can be mod-
eled with each ﬁlter representing a frequency channel of the model. Each channel
is treated independently and the collection of ﬁlters belonging to the same pro-
cessing stage at diﬀerent center frequencies will be referred to as Filterbank (FB).
The center frequencies are spaced equidistantly on an ERB scale[1]. On a linear
frequency axis, this results in a high density of low bandwidth GT ﬁlters for
lower frequencies, with decreasing sharpness and ﬁlter density as the frequency
increases.
The full dcGC-FB system is depicted in Figure 2.2. The pGC and HPAF ﬁlterbanks
make up the analysis system after which any signal processing strategy can be
employed. In this project, this is where some of the Hearing Loss (HL) eﬀects
are implemented. The synthesis system then combines the collection of channels
into a single audio channel for playback. The active part of the dcGC model and
some HL eﬀects depend on signal level. This is estimated for each channel at the
output of the pGC and subsequently fed to each subsystem.

2.1.3
Synthesis
The fundamental challenge of the synthesis system is compensating for the highly
non-linear phase response of GC ﬁlters, thus making the overall system approxi-
mate a linear phase all-pass system. As Figure 2.3a depicts, the dispersion results
in a smeared impulse response, resulting in a chirpy eﬀect. Additionally, the
overall magnitude response of the system shows strong destructive interference
resulting in cancellations. This is depicted in Figure 2.3b, where the system re-
sponse can be seen to be far from ﬂat. Ideally, if no processing is done between
analysis and synthesis, the output should not audibly diﬀer from the input.
Most instances of GC ﬁlters in literature are used in oﬀ-line computations and
non-causal time-reversed ﬁltering is a straightforward method for phase compen-
sation. However, in Real-Time (RT) applications this is not possible and other
methods have been suggested, including overlap-add zero-phase ﬁltering[13],
FIR ﬁltering[13], delay lines[14] or inverse cepstrum of inverse system magnitude
response[5].

2.2 Methodology
The following section will describe how the dcGC system was implemented for
real-time usage on a small digital computer.
2.2.1
Filter Implementation
All ﬁlters of the dcGC system are comprised of four cascaded Inﬁnite Impulse
Response (IIR) biquad ﬁlters in transposed Direct Form II. The time-domain GT
ﬁlter realization is based on coeﬃcient calculations in Slaney[15] and is a fourth-
order GT ﬁlter. The HPAF and LPAF realizations are based on Unoki & Irino[16],
also four cascaded biquad ﬁlters.
GT ﬁlters and LPAF ﬁlters do not necessarily share the same peak frequency and
there is no guarantee for the pGC gain when combining them. The coeﬃcients
of the ﬁrst GT biquad are therefore normalized in order to give the full pGC
series unity gain. The ﬁlter coeﬃcients of the dynamic HPAF system are likewise
normalized to give the pGC and HPAF (which again do not share peak frequency)
series unity gain when the maximum level is reached. All other levels will result
in ampliﬁcation at the peak frequency.

2.4 Discussion
It is vital to stress the inherent limitations of modeling a psychophysical system
such as the human auditory system. The majority of data backing even the most
widely accepted paradigms in this ﬁeld is based on psychoacoustical experiments.
The presented model is little more than a black box, rendering it near impossible
to separate the eﬀects of diﬀerent stages in the signal chain. It is an important
distinction to make, that the auditory models presented and implemented in this
report do not claim to simulate true physiological mechanisms, but rather some
of their perceptual eﬀects.
The response shown in Figure 2.10a is ﬂatter for low levels but also has a reduced
bandwidth. This is likely due to the HPAF peak gain region moving too far out of
the pGC peak region. This could be alleviated by restricting the absolute shifting
of HPAF for high frequencies in the active system.
Distributing ﬁlter center peaks on an ERB scale has a strong foundation in liter-
ature, but arguments for the scale’s over-emphasis on low-frequencies could be
made. If the density of ﬁlters was more even, the full system magnitude response
could be made be ﬂatter at all frequencies.

CHAPTER 3
Hearing Loss Simulation
3.1 Background
Hearing loss aﬀects an estimated 250 million people worldwide[18] and can man-
ifest itself in a multitude of impairments. It can have many causes, ranging from
aging and medication to genetic predispositions, physical trauma, or overexpo-
sure to noise. There are many stages in the auditory system signal chain where
performance degradations can manifest themselves. Examples include damages
to the mechanics of the outer and middle ear, resulting in conductive hearing
loss[18], loss of sensitivity due to inner hair cells "dead regions"[19] or lineariza-
tion due to loss of outer hair cells active feedback[20].
The most apparent and simplest forms of hearing impairment are changes in
the subject’s audiogram, here referred to as Passive Hearing Loss (pHL). Passive,
because it takes the form of a static audiogram alteration and is often associated
with a frequency-dependent loss of sensitivity[20]. While this eﬀect can come in
varying degrees and shapes it is most often of a lowpass characteristic, with a ﬂat
loss for low frequencies and an increasing loss for higher frequencies.
The Outer Hair Cells (OHC) are generally linked to active compression in auditory
ﬁlters by acting as feedback mechanisms. Damages to OHC are thought to reduce
their compressive ability and, in severe cases, leading to a linearized system. This
will result in a general expansion of the dynamic range, reducing sensitivity to
low levels and providing no dampening for very high levels. Additionally, it is
suggested that the frequency selectivity at low levels is decreased, due to the loss
of sharp tuning[20]. Simulating the eﬀect of OHC loss will be referred to as inverse
compression or loudness recruitment and aims at negating the natural compression
of the Normal Hearing (NH) subject[5]. This inverse compression is not how the
auditory system of Hearing Impaired (HI) subjects is believed to behave. Rather,
if it is put in series with a healthy cochlea, such as a NH subject or dcGC model,
the full system will approximate the linear behavior found in subjects with severe
OHC loss.
Another widely studied eﬀect of hearing impairment is that of reduced frequency
selectivity, resulting in a subject’s reduced ability to discern between stimuli of
similar frequencies. This is thought to be caused by the broadening of auditory
ﬁlters and manifests itself in reduced speech perception, especially in the presence
of competing speakers or masking noise[21].
Together, these methodologies will be employed to simulate hearing loss for a
NH subject in real-time. In literature, these procedures have previously only been

3.2.3
Spectral Smearing
A widely used way of simulating auditory ﬁlter broadening is that of Spectral
Smearing. By convolving the signal’s short-term power spectra with a smoothen-
ing kernel the power contained in distinct frequency peaks is mixed into adjacent
channels, distorting the signal’s frequency content.
The technique for this is adopted from [21] and a schematic of it can be seen
in Figure 3.4. Here a short term Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) is used to gener-
ate the complex Fourier Spectrum, and in order to preserve time structure, the
phase spectrum is separated and not further processed. The squared magnitude
(power) spectrum is then smeared using a convolution matrix. Instead of a simple
one-dimensional convolution, this allows for frequency-dependent smearing by
increasing smearing kernel width for higher frequencies. Resynthesis is achieved
by recombining the square root of the smeared power spectrum with the orig-
inal phase spectrum and computing the Inverse Fast Fourier Transform (IFFT).
To ensure continuous RT processing the system operates using the overlap-add
algorithm with two parallel and time-shifted smearing systems.

3.4 Discussion
An alternate methodology for creating loudness recruitment is presented in [2],
which also uses an auditory ﬁlterbank framework. This procedure simulates loss
of compression by expanding the time-domain envelope non-linearly. The liter-
ature accompanying the two distinct methods for loudness recruitment does not
allow for direct comparison, since their experimental methods vary signiﬁcantly.
However, the method of inverse compression using the dcGC framework by [5]
and presented in Figure 3.2 and Figure 3.3 was found to be more suitable for
RT purposes. No frame-based approaches are necessary and the processing is
performed on a sample-to-sample basis through the already established digital
biquad ﬁlter of the dcGC. Additionally, the computational burden was judged
to be signiﬁcantly lower by running and updating low order digital IIR ﬁlters
compared to computing a Hilbert transform and performing heavy ﬂoating-point
operations on each sample.
In [5], where inverse compression via HPAF shifting is introduced, the authors
mention a necessary alteration of ﬁlter parameters. However, these adjusted pa-
rameters are not given and no method for obtaining them is presented. Therefore
the parameters of the inverse dcGC and dcGC are in the present project chosen
to be identical. Due to the asymmetric pGC ﬁlter shapes, this does not lead to
perfect cancellation. Though the compressive model is based on psychoacoustical
data and has been employed to model compressive phenomena, it is far from a
numerically exact recreation of individual subjects’ data. Perfect cancellation of
the inverse compressive system is therefore not strictly necessary and the inverse
compression system yielded satisfactory results.
The "digital" texture of the spectral smearing algorithm is likely caused by the
limited spectral resolution of the method due to short audio frame sizes. The
kernels sampling interval is
𝑓𝑠
𝐿= 172 Hz and is thus not able to produce an
"organic" sounding eﬀect. Increasing the length of the FFT could counteract this
unnatural element of the eﬀect, however, this would bring a substantial latency
penalty in a real-time system. Tests of doubling or quadrupling the FFT length
did not yield noticeable improvements, indicating the necessity of an impractical
FFT length.
The three HL eﬀects are able to run simultaneously, but this is not guaranteed to
give desirable results. This is especially true for pHL and inverse compression
since both decrease general sensitivity and act as low-pass ﬁlters. Employing both
at the same time would lead to extreme attenuation at low levels, where inverse
compression would decrease sensitivity with up to 40 dB in addition to pHL. One
solution could be to normalize all pHL audiograms to account for the combined
eﬀect. Spectral smearing possibly decreases sensitivity as well by distributing
pure tone power over a broader range, however, this is much less severe than
the pHL-inverse compression combination. Spectral smearing can therefore be
combined with either pHL or inverse compression. The dcGC system and its

CHAPTER 4
Implementation
Previous chapters have not been concerned with how processing procedures are
realized and have thus not been strongly application-speciﬁc.
The following
chapter will present how the various auditory modeling and HLS eﬀects are
implemented in real-time on a lightweight computer. Some of the details are
speciﬁc to the chosen platform while others are more general signal processing
strategies.
Retaining suﬃcient audio ﬁdelity with the available computational power for
a realistic acoustic environment is vital. Latency was identiﬁed as the crucial
performance measure in order to make the system usable in natural conversations.
The source code for usage on bela is published on a public git repository. Full
source documentation is hosted on the website.
4.1 Bela
The platform laying the foundation for the real-time hearing loss simulator is
bela, a system built on top of BeagleBone Black embedded computers; the central
processing unit is an AM3358 1 GHz ARM Cortex-A8 running a linux kernel. Bela
uses Xenomai as an operating system and it can be considered hard Real-Time
Operating System (RTOS) with an audio pass-through latency of around 1 ms.
The bela can be programmed in C++ 14 with clang as the compiler and many I/O
functionalities for audio are built-in.
4.2 Frame Based Processing
The dcGC and synthesis systems presented in chapter 2 fundamentally operate on
a sample-by-sample basis. However, just like most other RT audio processors, the
audio infrastructure on bela operates in a frame-based manner. This aﬀects the
rate at which supporting systems such as level estimation, coeﬃcient updating,
and run-time parameter changes are performed.
The actual IIR ﬁlters of the
system, however, are not aﬀected by the frame-based approach so while the bela
system operates in audio frames, the actual audio signal is processed on a sample-
by-sample basis.
Filter coeﬃcients associated with the active part of the system are updated once
per audio frame.
When updating HPAF ﬁlter coeﬃcients, the delay registers
are not altered or reset, but simply kept unaﬀected. Rapid changes in level and
thus signiﬁcant numerical changes to coeﬃcients can cause undesired clicking.
To accommodate this issue an alternating updating scheme is developed which
processes two identical dcGC systems in parallel and only updates one of them

each frame. By using a half-length hann window, the output of the most recently
updated system is attenuated and the system acting in steady state is emphasized.

4.3 SIMD Instructions
The ARM Cortex-A8 includes a NEON co-processor, implementing the Advanced
Single-Instruction-Multiple-Data (SIMD) Architecture[27]. This processor allows
for vectorization, making the processing of multiple signal paths in parallel pos-
sible. NEON intrinsic SIMD instructions can perform ﬂoating-point operations
on 128-bit quadword registers, eﬀectively performing four 32-bit single-precision
ﬂoating-point operations in parallel[28].
Using SIMDinstructions forprocessingbiquadﬁlters, assuggestedbyG.Moro[29],
resulted in a major performance boost. Optimizing the implementation of the bi-
quad ﬁlters was given a high priority since they are the fundamental building
block of the time-domain implementation of the dcGC auditory model. In a full
system consisting of tens of channels with multiple ﬁltering stages, each con-
sisting of four serial biquads, the computational burden lies heavily on these
fundamental ﬁlters.
The idea is to store the coeﬃcients and delay blocks of four parallel biquad ﬁlters
in quadword registers and then use vectorized multiply-accumulate instructions
to process the ﬁlters simultaneously. The memory used by these ﬁlters must be
properly aligned.

Implementing this meant a breach of the modular design paradigm at the lowest
level since channels had to be clustered in sections of four and the fundamental
C++ processing class was no longer four biquad ﬁlters in series, but in parallel,
belonging to diﬀerent channels. Channels are, at the lowest code level, no longer
independent. This brought some limitations, e.g. number of channels has to
be an integer multiple of four and more complex methods for changing ﬁlter
coeﬃcients. Still, the optimization proved fruitful and a major speedup factor of
≈2.4 was measured in average execution time.
4.4 Latency, Frame Length and Sampling Rate
Bela runs at a native 44.1 kHz sampling rate, however, considering the employ-
ment of sloping hearing loss, this broad bandwidth is not necessary. Therefore,
the audio signal processing is performed at a rate downsampled to 22.050 kHz,
greatly reducing CPU load. This is in accordance with earlier HLS studies, which
used even lower sampling rates. Downsampling to 11.025 kHz is a possibility (and
done at similar rates in [21], [30] and [3]) and would free even more CPU usage.
However, as this would not give a realistic high-frequency sound environment for
mild HL, it was deemed unsatisfactory.
The human auditory system is naturally an analog and therefore continuous
system and it is thus desirable to choose a large number of channels to closely
approximate this, often over 100[4][14]. However, in constrained environments
much lower model sizes have also been used, down to 23[31], 25[13], 26[6] and
32[7] channels.
Another parameter on ﬁlterbank level is that of overall frequency range. Proposals
for frequency ranges include 100 Hz−8000 Hz[6], 100 Hz−15 000 Hz[4] and 40 Hz−
7500 Hz[14].
Audio frame lengths were found to have a signiﬁcant and non-monotonic impact
on CPU load, see Table 4.1 for an example for a full system with eight channels.
While the processing cost of individual audio samples does not change, both
very short and very long audio frames showed a signiﬁcant drop in performance.
Short frames are likely burdened by the increased rate at which ﬁlter coeﬃcients
are updated and the level is estimated, while overhead due to RTOS scheduling
(Xenomai mode switches) and buﬀering procedures increased. Longer frames
mitigate these drawbacks, however, very long frames suﬀer from poor perfor-
mance as well. Some methods scale poorly with frame length, such as frequency
smearing. This could be counteracted by using a smearing algorithm with better
scaling properties, but the latency issue, addressed shortly, would remain.

CPU load.

4.5 Other Details
• Math NEON library:
Performinghighprecision ﬂoatingpointoperationsusingstandard-template-
library (STL) functions in C++ can be costly. Therefore the math_neon li-
brary[32] was used, since it oﬀers substantial relative speedups (between 2
and 8) at the cost of RMSE typically below 1−6[33].
• Ne10 library:
The Ne10 library[34] was used for its FFT implementation which makes use
of NEON SIMD instructions. A bela speciﬁc wrapper object for this library
exists but lacked some IFFT and phase calculation functionalities. After
developing these for this project they have since been released on the oﬃcial
bela development branch.
• Eigen library:
For linear algebra operations, mainly related to frequency smearing, the
Eigen library[35] was chosen, since it uses NEON SIMD vectorized instruc-
tions, which were found to have execution times suitable for RT purposes.
• Unity build:
The compiler was found to better optimize the SIMD biquad implementation
when using a unity build of the biquad object. Since this is merely a breach
of coding conventions with no actual drawbacks, this was not investigated
further.
• Lookup Tables:
Filter coeﬃcients associated with the dynamic system are pre-computed
for a pre-deﬁned range of levels and stored in lookup tables in order to
reduce expensive, precise mathematical operations at run-time. The level
estimation system therefore also performs a search in a one-dimensional,
sorted array for the pre-deﬁned level closest to the estimated level. Smearing
kernels and HL audiograms for varying degrees of HL are likewise pre-
computed and accessed at run-time.
• Precision:
All processing signals are 32-bit single-precision ﬂoating-point numbers.
This allows for automatic compiler optimizations using the NEON co-
processor and the ﬁdelity loss compared to 64-bit double-precision is judged
to be of little signiﬁcance.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
General Remarks
In order for the HLS to fully come to life, it is vital to design a wearable and
easily operable system. This includes high-quality hearing protection to cancel
the NH listeners’ natural hearing as much as possible, in-ear headphones playing
the HLS output, and a power supply. Recording could be done with small electret
microphones mounted on the hearing protection, possibly binaurally. This step
may also beneﬁt from a small microphone pre-ampliﬁer circuit. Operating the
device can be done via an on/oﬀbutton and a few switches controlling HL eﬀects
at run-time.
Mounting the microphone and speakers close to each other necessitates a feedback
cancellation system. This should be analogous to feedback cancellation in hearing
aids, a widely studied topic, see [36][37] for examples. Using hearing protection
and in-ear headphones may reduce the risk of unstable feedback.
Calibration of the system between listeners could be performed via a simple
threshold detection task, possibly only at a single frequency.
This would be
a very fast procedure to roughly calibrate the playback strength and align the
subjects’ hearing level and the bela systems level estimation.
While this is a
broad simpliﬁcation of a listeners audiogram, it can serve as a straightforward
method and, as described before, clinical-grade calibration is neither necessary
nor possible for the intended use-case.
In the ﬁnal released version of the HLS, the full dcGC system and its synthesis
counterparts are processed as seen in Figure 4.1. However, the dynamic HPAF
ﬁlters are not necessary since the intermediate signal is not used for HL eﬀects
and is directly followed by the inverse dynamic HPAF. This consumes processing
power without contributing to the HLS and omitting these steps would free
the system to process more channels. For the sake of completeness, however,
these stages are preserved since the HLS then contains a full dcGC system. The
published code can therefore facilitate further development in HL eﬀects, or act
as a standalone real-time dcGC analysis/synthesis framework on bela for other
uses.
Limitations
No phase compensation of the inverse compression HPAF is implemented. This
results in a slight increase in chirpyness. Since the HPAF ﬁlter coeﬃcients are
updated each frame, their group delay is not constant. Therefore static delay lines
are judged to be less eﬀective than for GT ﬁlters, but possibly still suﬃcient.
The inverse compression brings a slight unnatural distortion to dynamic stimuli
such as speech. Harsh sounds, e.g. sharp vocalizations, are ampliﬁed, like speak-
Simulating Hearing Impairment in Real-Time
35
==PAGE:44==
ing too close to a microphone without a windscreen. Softer sounds do not share
this eﬀect. To a certain extent, this is intentional and part of the HL simulation.
However, it can be unpleasant. As addressed previously, this is even more so the
case when employing spectral smearing. This simulation introduces the intended
distortion, but it is not a pleasant experience and sounds very unnatural.
Considering the use-case with a wearable simulator in noisy environments, the
ability of the HLS to simulate HL is strongly dependent on the hearing protection
to cancel as much of the natural hearing of the listener. This is especially true
when employing reduced sensitivity through pHL or inverse compression. If the
hearing protection is not of a certain grade or does not ﬁt well, the employed
hearing loss will be much less eﬀective, as the natural hearing system will assist
the listener.
The ﬁdelity of the auditory model is also limited by the microphone used in the
audio setup. While the bela board does oﬀer a low-noise voltage supply specif-
ically for microphones, capturing a realistic soundscape with small, mountable
microphones might not be possible. A careful design considering the available
power, component size, and necessity of external ampliﬁers should be made.
Otherwise, the signal supplied to the digital system might be too poor and have
intrinsic quality degradation even before an HLS.
Outlook
All processing in this project is done monaurally.
This simpliﬁcation brings
a loss of loudness and timing cues, important for localization. Processing the
HLS binaurally via two fully parallel and independent systems is possible and
a straightforward extension of the developed HLS. It would, however, double
the processing load on the CPU. This should be possible to overcome by previ-
ously described measures such as omitting the redundant dynamic HPAF and
downscaling the number of channels.
In a similar vein, head-related transfer functions (HRTFs) have not been addressed
in this project. This is another area where a suﬃciently lightweight model could
increase soundscape realism. This is especially true when considering the pro-
posed microphone mounting very close to the listeners’ ears and in combination
with binaural processing. HRTFs and Binaural-Room-Impulse-Responses are im-
plemented in the 3DTI HLS[9].
An interesting addition to the HLS system would be the inclusion of a virtual hear-
ing aid on top of hearing loss. The already established dcGC framework would
lend itself naturally to this. This could include a multiband compressor or the
addition of quantization noise, both also implemented in the 3DTI HLS[9]. The lis-
tener could then choose between three main system conﬁgurations: passthrough
(NH), HL and aided HL to see the positive eﬀects of hearing aids, but also where
their limitations lie.
Another method of simulating hearing loss not addressed in this project is that
of temporal smearing, as proposed by Hou & Pavlovic[31]. This could simulate a
decrease in temporal resolution sometimes observed in hearing impaired.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In this project, a lightweight and easy-to-use simulator of hearing loss was devel-
oped. This implementation was speciﬁcally designed for minimal delay, real-time
processing on the bela platform.
The processing framework is the dynamic compressive gammachirp ﬁlterbank
and an appropriate synthesis system for signal reconstruction was designed. It
was shown how this was adapted and optimized to run in real-time and with
minimal latency using an environment with limited processing power. The ﬁnal
system encompasses a full dcGC analysis/synthesis system capable of processing
28 channels at 22 050 Hz with a latency of 27 ms.
Using this platform three types of hearing loss eﬀects were simulated. Audiogram
changes associated with hearing loss were approximated via the gammachirp
framework. Threshold detection tasks for a set of subjects veriﬁed the validity for
diﬀerent degrees of audiogram alterations. Loudness recruitment was simulated
by an inversely compressive ﬁlterbank. A loudness scaling experiment showed
an elevated threshold and an increase in loudness growth, consistent with loss of
compression. The third eﬀect is spectral smearing, simulating broadened auditory
ﬁlters. All eﬀects are supported by sample audio ﬁles.
With the software being openly available for download and only requiring a
simple audio setup it should be straightforward to use the simulator in a variety
of settings. It does not require expensive equipment and is possible to operate
without previous expertise.
While is not ready to use wirelessly out-of-the-
box, only few additions such as power supply, physical buttons, and feedback
cancellation are needed. Hopefully, the simulator will be used as an interactive
method for raising awareness about hearing loss.

This method of using a time-reversed ﬁlterbank is non-causal since the last input
sample is to be processed before the ﬁrst and the entire input sequence must thus
be known a the time of computation. This poses no issue in oﬀ-line models.
For the purpose of RT processing, attention is therefore directed towards more
general methods for RT zero-phase ﬁltering using non-linear phase IIR prototype
ﬁlters[38][39]. These methods still use time-reversed non-causal ﬁltering, but do
so in a block-based manner by truncating the IIR ﬁlter response. By using a Last-In
First-Out (LIFO) buﬀer on data blocks the time-reversal is done locally and the
system, while non-causal, is realizable and can run in real-time. This approach
has two drawbacks:
1) It introduces a delay since the time-reversal necessitates processing in audio
blocks and not on a sample-by-sample basis. This latency can be substantial since
it must be longer than the slowest impulse response of the system in order to
approximate inﬁnite length non-causal processing.
2) Unresolved boundary conditions for time-reversed block can give large tran-
sient responses.
Since the "last" sample in the LIFO buﬀer is the ﬁrst to be
processed in the time-reversed system, no "previous" samples are known and the
ﬁlter is not in a steady-state. Methods for initial condition estimation in forward-
backward ﬁltering are given in [40] and [39]. Alternatively, the latency could be
increased even further and thus use the newest samples for time-reversed onset
response, but not as actual output to be synthesized.
The signiﬁcant portion (95%) of the energy in the GT impulse response of a
low frequency (100 Hz) GT ﬁlter is contained within the ﬁrst ≈20 ms.
