4     ABSTRACT 
 
ABSTRACT 
Humans emanate several biological compounds through skin from different body parts, which 
are referred to as bio-effluents. Bio-effluents are pollutants produced by people through 
biological processes and they exist as gases and vapors [1]. Too high concentrations and too 
high exposure time to individual chemicals may cause adverse short-term, distinct and acute 
or long-term effects such as allergy, asthma or cancer [2]. Initiatives should therefore be 
made in order to reduce the concentration of contaminants in indoor air.  
Studies have shown that the use of advanced local ventilation systems have the potential to 
increase the indoor air quality compared to conventional total volume ventilation systems [2], 
[3]. Advanced local ventilation, named Ventilated Cushion (VC) has been developed [2]. VC 
covers the seat cushion and backrest. It sucks air and thus removes human body bio-effluents 
locally before they are mixed with room air. In this way the background room ventilation is 
reduced. A recent study [2] concluded that exhausting 1.5 L/s from the VC combined with 1 
ACH background room ventilation to reduce the bio-effluents’ concentration at the breathing 
zone, was up to 12% more efficient than increasing the background room ventilation to 6 
ACH. Thus, such system has a potential to provide cleaner inhaled air and better perceived air 
quality for the occupants. Furthermore, several studies [4]–[6] have suggested that there is an 
energy saving potential by using advanced local ventilation systems. 
The present study investigates the energy performance of the ventilated cushion when 
applied in office environments. Energy simulations were carried out with the software IDA 
ICE. Four air conditioning scenarios were simulated: 1) Only background mixing ventilation 2) 
Reduced background mixing ventilation and chilled ceiling 3) Reduced background mixing 
ventilation, chilled ceiling and ventilated cushion, and 4) Reduced background mixing 
ventilation, chilled ceiling, ventilated cushion and local heating. The simulations were 
performed for a call-center where 14 occupants work seated during relatively long period of 
time. Full scale measurements were carried out to study the cooling effect of the ventilated 
cushion and to investigate the thermal effect of applying local heating. In total 56 full-scale 
measurement scenarios were studied in the process of improving the seat design. 
The results of the simulations showed that combining mixing ventilation and chilled ceiling in 
a call-center located in Copenhagen reduces the energy use compared to a system with only 
mixing ventilation. The results of the full scale experiments showed that sucking 1.5 L/s from 
the VC has very little or no cooling effect on the body parts in contact with the VC and the 
body as a whole. Sucking 3.0 and 5.0 L/s from the VC provides cooling of the body parts in 
contact with the VC and the body as a whole. This applied for the three room temperatures 
20, 24 and 28 oC without local heating. Local heating with a power between 1.6 and 17.2 W 
may be used to compensate for the cooling provided from the VC.

10     INTRODUCTION 
 
1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 INDOOR AIR QUALITY IN NON-RESIDENTIAL BUILDINGS 
Non-residential buildings should provide comfortable indoor environments for working, 
learning, recreation, etc. The indoor air quality (IAQ) is the part of the indoor environment, 
which is associated with the occupants’ subjective perception of the indoor air, and should 
meet appropriately set quality criteria. Negligence or failing to take proper actions to comply 
with common quality criteria for the IAQ, can result in undesirable financial costs and 
negative consequences for health and comfort of the occupants [7]. Health effects of certain 
air pollutants have been evaluated by the World Health Organization (WHO): tiredness, 
headache, exhaustion, sleepiness, lack of concentration. These symptoms are known as Sick 
Building Syndrome (SBS) symptoms [8]. 
The indoor air can be polluted by contaminants emitted from building materials, furnishing 
and humans, which can cause complaints with poor IAQ [9]. Humans have two senses to 
perceive the air: 1) the olfactory sense which is situated in the nasal cavity and is sensitive to 
several hundred thousand odorants in the air and 2) The general chemical sense which is 
situated in the mucous membranes of the nose and the eyes. The chemical sense is also 
sensitive to a large number of pollutants. These two senses determine whether air is 
perceived fresh and pleasant or stale, stuffy and irritating. Too high concentrations and too 
high exposure time to individual chemicals may cause adverse short-term, distinct and acute 
or long-term effects such as allergy, asthma or cancer [8]. Initiatives should therefore be 
made in order to reduce the concentration of contaminants in indoor air. 
The desire of designers, researchers and HVAC Engineers is above all to provide clean air, in 
order to avoid negative effects such as the SBS symptoms listed above. In workspaces such as 
offices and call-centers a high IAQ can promote the effectiveness of the work, and in schools 
the learning and productivity can be increased. Studies have shown that improvement of IAQ 
by a factor of 2–7 compared to present practice and to standards, increased office 
productivity and school learning significantly [10]. Another study [11] conducted in a call-
center, showed that increasing outdoor air supply rate and replacing an old filter, reduced the 
talk-time by 6%, suggesting that a higher supply rate of clean air would increase the 
productivity of the operators. 
In practice standards such as DS/EN 15251 [12] and DS/CEN/CR 1752 [13] are used to 
determine the required ventilation rate in order to reduce the concentration of pollutants to 
achieve acceptable IAQ in buildings. DS/EN 15251 [12] defines four different IAQ categories (I, 
II, III and IV), based on the expected percentage of dissatisfied. It relates to perceived air 
quality (PAQ) which means the predicted percent of people to perceive the air as being 
unacceptable just after entering a space. The expected percentage dissatisfied are 15%, 20%, 
==PAGE:12==
11     INTRODUCTION 
 
30% and >30% for category I, II, III, IV respectively. A high IAQ is therefore obtained if there is 
a low percentage of dissatisfied [2]. Beside pollutants, temperature and humidity are also 
factors which should be considered when evaluating PAQ. Studies have shown that people 
perceive inhaled air as better when the air temperature and humidity are low [14]. Also air 
movement affects the PAQ. Elevated air movement towards the face improves perceived air 
quality and diminishes the negative impact from increased air temperature, relative humidity 
and pollution levels [15]. Especially at warm and humid conditions and high background 
pollution the air improvements were evident. 
 
1.2 AIR POLLUTION GENERATED BY OCCUPANTS 
As mentioned humans are sources of pollution; hence a high person density in an indoor 
environment can reduce the IAQ significantly. An inactive human releases approximately 
100.000 particles per minute [16], shed from human skin. The emission from the body, the 
properties of the released particles as well as gaseous pollutants and their ways of 
distribution are discussed in the following. 
1.2.1 POLLUTANTS GENERATED FROM THE BODY 
Humans emanate several biological compounds through skin from different body parts, which 
are referred to as bio-effluents. Bio-effluents are pollutants produced by people through 
biological processes and they exist as gases and vapors [1]. Bio-effluents released from the 
body include volatile organic compounds (VOCs), sulfur compounds, inorganic compounds 
such as ammonia gas, etc. [17]. Other pollutants that are emitted from the human body are 
bio-aerosols and skin flakes. [2].  
VOCs are organic chemicals which have a low boiling point (less or equal to 250 oC at 101,3 
kPa) [16], and they therefore easily evaporate into the surrounding air. VOCs emanated from 
the human body are governed mainly by three types of glands on the human body: 1) eccrine, 
2) sebaceous and 3) apocrine. Distinct body parts emit odors which are partly reflected by the 
distribution of gland types on the skin surface. Eccrine glands (producing odorless sweat) are 
found in large numbers on the entire skin surface, but are especially located on hands and 
feet. Sebaceous glands (producing sebum and lipids) are also distributed all over the body, 
but are found in large numbers on the head. Apocrine glands (producing lipids, proteins and 
steroids) are found especially in the armpits and genital (groin) regions. However, odors are 
likely to show high variability from one human to another, as the physical composition of 
every human body varies greatly and can moreover be affected by environmental factors such 
as diet. The secretions from these glands provide favorable environments for numerous types 
of microorganisms, which combined with oxidation, can cause odorous compounds [18].  
==PAGE:13==
12     INTRODUCTION 
 
Bio-aerosols are airborne biological materials released from living organisms such as humans, 
but bio-aerosols also include living organisms like viruses, bacteria, fungal spores, fungi and 
pollen and their fragments in sizes of 0.5 – 100 μm in diameter. Bio-aerosols may result in 
adverse health effects as they can be pathogenic or cause allergic reactions after inhalation. 
In particular, an increase of bio-aerosol levels has been related to an increase in asthma 
attacks and bronchial hyper-reactivity. Human activities like talking, sneezing, coughing, 
walking, washing and toilet flushing are related to emanation of biological particulate matter. 
The maximum limit of bio-aerosol particles set by the National Institute of Occupational 
Safety and Health (NIOSH) and American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists 
(ACGIH) is 1000 CFUs/m3 [19].  
Small skin flake fragments released from humans can be categorized as bio-aerosols [2]. 
Approximately 10 million skin fragments are released from an active human body (running up 
and down stairs) per minute as a natural process [16], as high activity and specifically abrasive 
actions such as bedding and clothing increase the liberation from skin. The skin flakes can be 
carriers of other bio-aerosols such as bacteria (e.g. staphylococci), which can cause infections 
in case of inhalation [20]. 
The distribution and transportation of bio-effluents and bio-aerosols in indoor air is affected 
by several factors such as human activity, exhaled air jets, air movement caused by 
mechanical ventilation systems, air movement caused by natural ventilation, etc. However, 
the natural convective flow around humans caused by the temperature difference between 
the room air and the body surface should be considered when studying pollution exposure 
and IAQ, as the convective flow may transport bio-effluents [21] and particles greater than 50 
μm in diameter upwards [20]. Consequently, a sitting person may be exposed to bio-effluents 
and airborne particles located in the lower part of the space, as free natural convection 
around the body transports airborne contaminants upwards to the breathing zone [14].  
1.2.2 POLLUTANTS GENERATED THROUGH EXHALATION 
The respiration process includes supply of oxygen to the body and exhaling the CO2-rich air. 
CO2 is the most important bio-effluent in terms of quantity [2]. The indoor CO2 concentration 
should be limited, as moderately elevated concentrations have a negative effect on human 
performance, PAQ and prevalence of distinct health symptoms such as irritation of the 
mucous membranes, headaches or tiredness [22]. In practice, CO2 is often used as an 
indicator for the general IAQ in indoor air, as various other pollutants’ concentration rise 
along the concentration of CO2 as a result of insufficient ventilation [22]. Several simple 
measuring methods and equipment can be used to monitor the CO2 concentration in air, 
which makes it an effortless operation to assess the state of the IAQ in a specific space. 
Besides CO2, exhaled air may contain contagious pathogens such as bacteria and viruses if a 
person is ill [2]. 
==PAGE:14==
13     INTRODUCTION 
 
According to DS/EN 15251 [12], the following permitted CO2 concentrations above outdoors 
should be obtained due to IAQ: Categories I, II, III, IV which have the corresponding 
concentrations 350, 500, 800, and >800 ppm respectively.  
The exhalation causes jets originating from mouth and nose, and primarily nose at low activity 
levels. The direction of the jet through the nose is 45 degrees below horizontal direction in a 
normal upright position. The jet is further divided into two jets from the two nostrils which 
penetrates the room air in an angle of 30 degrees [16].  
 
1.3 THERMAL COMFORT 
From the review in the previous section 1.2, one might have the perception that an unlimited 
amount of air supply will unambiguously increase the comfort of the occupants in indoor 
environments, which is not the case. Air flow rates, air temperatures, and air terminal devices 
should be chosen carefully, in order to provide an airflow distribution that is optimal for 
occupants’ thermal comfort. Thermal comfort is the condition of mind which expresses 
satisfaction with the thermal environment based on an individual and subjective assessment 
[23]. Humans have their individual preferences, which make it practically impossible to design 
a thermal environment with 100% satisfied occupants. In practice, standards such as DS/EN 
15251 [12] and DS/CEN/CR 1752 [13] are used to provide guidelines on how to achieve 
thermal environments assessed as “acceptable” for a high percentage of occupants. Thermal 
comfort is affected by several factors which affect the thermal balance between the human 
body and the surrounding environment. These factors can be divided into two groups [24]: 
 Personal factors: Activity level (met) and thermal insulation of clothing (clo) 
 Environmental factors: Air temperature (oC), average radiant temperature (oC), air 
velocity (m/s) and air humidity (%) 
The standard DS/EN ISO 7730 [25] provides the term “Predicted Mean Vote” (PMV), which is 
an index used to predict the mean value of votes from a large group of people. The index is 
based on a 7-point thermal sensation scale (cold, cool, slightly cool, neutral, slightly warm, 
warm and hot) which refers to the heat balance of the body. From the PMV, the Predicted 
Percentage of Dissatisfied (PPD) can be calculated. PPD is an index that is used to 
quantitatively predict the percentage of thermally dissatisfied people who feel too cold or too 
warm in a specific environment [25].  
Discomfort may also be caused by local thermal discomfort, where a certain body part feels 
discomfort, which can be caused by [24]: 
 Draught (unwanted local cooling of the body caused by air movement)

 Vertical temperature difference between head and ankles 
 Hot or cold floor 
 Radiant asymmetry

Dissatisfied (PD) can be used to describe this. The standard DS/EN ISO 7730 [25] divides

The previous two sections (1.2 and 1.3) suggest that several factors should be included when 
designing indoor environments. Both IAQ and thermal environment are important factors 
which should have great attendance, in order to increase performance and avoid health 
problems. Several different ventilation and air conditioning strategies can be applied to 
achieve high quality indoor environments. The most used strategies are described in the 
following.  
 
1.4 TOTAL VOLUME VENTILATION (TVV) 
Total volume ventilation is the most commonly used ventilation principle nowadays. The 
principle handles the air throughout the whole room, in order to supply clean air to the 
occupants and create a uniform occupied zone in terms of temperature and pollution by 
means of mechanical and/or natural driving forces. When applying mechanical ventilation in 
practice, several air distribution methods can be used: Mixing Ventilation (MV), Displacement 
ventilation (DV), Stratum Ventilation, Piston Flow, etc. However, the most commonly applied 
air distribution methods at present are MV and DV, which are described in the following. 
 
==PAGE:16==
15     INTRODUCTION 
 
 
1.4.1 MIXING VENTILATION 
By MV air distribution, air is supplied at high initial mean velocity outside the occupied zone. 
The high initial mean velocity generates high turbulence intensity which promotes good 
mixing, so a uniform temperature and pollution distribution is obtained in the occupied zone 
[26]. In principle air supply openings (and extraction openings) can be placed anywhere 
outside the occupied zone, as long as too high air velocities (which may cause unpleasent 
draught) is avoided in the occupied zone. In practice, air supply openings – or air terminal 
devices (ATDs) – are often placed in the ceiling, which combined with proper design of the 
remaining design parameters (ATD type, room geometry, air temperatures, etc.), ensures that 
the supplied air reaches suitable velocities in the space between ceiling and occupied space 
(typically 1.7 m above the floor for a standing person and 1.1 m for a sitting person [27]) (see 
Figure 1).  
 
Figure 1: Principle of Mixing Ventilation [28] 
 
1.4.2 DISPLACEMENT VENTILATION  
DV aims to supply clean and cool air directly into the occupied zone through low-velocity 
ATDs, and extract the contaminated, heated air at ceiling level (see Figure 2). The aim is to 
attain layer stratification in the room, so clean air (in the occupied zone) and polluted air 
(above the occupied zone) are separated. DV, therefore, has the potential to improve the 
inhaled air quality compared to MV [2]. 
==PAGE:17==
16     INTRODUCTION 
 
In case of DV, it is of great importance that the supply air temperature is lower than the room 
air temperature, else a short-circuit may occur between inlet and exhaust. Heating of air is 
therefore needed in order to displace the polluted air from the occupied zone, which makes 
displacement more suitable for the following types of spaces: restaurants, meeting rooms, 
classrooms, theatres, conference rooms, etc. In general, DV is suitable in rooms with high 
occupant density, and electronical equipment since they provide natural heating of the air 
and thus force the air to move upwards. However, the DV principle has the disadvantage that 
occupants may feel draught on lower body parts (e.g. ankles) if they stay in the near zone 
close to the air supply diffusers. Moreover, practical negative implications may occur in case 
of improper placement of furniture, plants etc. in front of the supply diffusers [29]. 
 
Figure 2: Principle of Displacement Ventilation [28] 
 
1.4.3 DISADVANTAGES OF TOTAL VOLUME VENTILATION  
TVV results in high amounts of air delivered to and extracted from the space, which causes 1) 
Relatively high operation costs [16], 2) Substantial spacial needs for ducting and Air Handling 
Units (AHU). Furthermore, the clean air is supplied far from the occupants and thus the air is 
usually already polluted and warm when it reaches the breathing zone.  
Every individual has different perceptions of what characterizes a “good indoor environment”. 
The principle of TVV does not take into account the individual preferences of the occupants. 
Individual control has an effect on humans, as their satisfaction increase with an increasing 
self-control of the micro-environment [24]. Innovative and more advanced local ventilation 
systems which may be controlled by the individual occupants are therefore needed in order 
==PAGE:18==
17     INTRODUCTION 
 
to satisfy every individual. In the following, advanced and innovative ventilation systems are 
studied. Such systems have the potential to achieve higher air quality in the breathing zone 
and save energy compared to a conventional ventilation system. 
 
1.5 ADVANCED LOCAL VENTILATION SYSTEMS 
With advanced local ventilation systems the microenvironment of each person is considered, 
therefore these systems are often referred to as “personalized ventilation” (PV) systems. Such 
systems can supply clean air directly into the breathing zone of each occupant, or extract 
polluted air from the local zone around each occupant before it is mixed with the room air. 
The air supplied to the local zone may have another temperature than the body and 
extracting air close to the body may cause higher air velocities and cool the body. This 
provides the possibility for indoor environment designers and engineers to accommodate 
individual thermal preferences and improve IAQ in order to increase their satisfaction, 
decrease SBS symptoms and increase work performance.  
Several studies [4]–[6] have suggested that there is an energy saving potential by using 
advanced local ventilation systems.  
1.5.1 PV IN SEDENTARY ENVIRONMENTS 
Employees in sedentary office environments are typically seated by their desk for 76% of the 
working hours (5 hours and 41 minutes out of 7 hours and 30 minutes) [30], therefore 
sedentary environments have a great potential of exploiting the properties of PV. This high 
ratio of seated time suggests that rooms with one or more seated occupants may be divided 
into one “background zone” with reduced demands to thermal comfort and IAQ, and several 
“personal zones” with higher thermal comfort and IAQ compared to current standards. 
A study [6] investigated the effect of a chair-based personalized ventilation system and the 
human response to it (see Figure 3). Fresh air was supplied directly into the breathing zone 
using eight different ATDs, aiming to find the most optimal flow rate and ATD geometry.

The most efficient solution was capable to compose 80 % of the inhaled air of fresh,

Another study [31] developed a Personalized Ventilation system combined with Personalized

1.5.2 LOCAL COOLING 
Another advanced local ventilation and body supporting device was invented and patented in 
1990 by Wyon et al. [34], with the purpose to provide local cooling of the body. The inventor 
had observed sweating problems in cars during summer periods especially from the body 
parts in contact with the car seat. He observed that windows were opened to cool down the 
body, which only had very little cooling effect and resulted in uncomfortable draught. In first 
case, a device was invented where cold air was circulated in seat incorporated ducts, with the 
purpose of increasing the heat radiation of the body. However, it was found that this solution 
was unusable as too much cooling of the body was achieved. Consequently, the body 
supporting device was invented as a second proposal.

The body supporting device, like the first proposal, had the purpose of generating cooling 
close to the body in order to avoid sweating caused by thermal insulation of the chair, 
specifically in warm environments. The device uses the principle of extracting air through 
small openings close to back and thigh regions (see Figure 7) which provide cooling, due to 
higher air velocities close to the body surface, hence the device is able to keep users dry [34]. 
Performance tests of the body supporting device have shown that the air suction increase the 
heat loss from the body parts in contact with the chair, corresponding to a level as if the 
person was freely standing up. Also, the seat surfaces in contact with back and thighs are 
capable of absorbing sweat which also causes the support surfaces to be cool and dry [34]. 
The body supporting device was only studied with regards to cooling of the body. Hence, the 
potential for local exhaust with the purpose to improve air quality was not investigated. 
A study by Rezgals [2] investigated the performance of a ventilation cushion (VC) with regard 
to IAQ and thermal comfort. The cushion was applied to exhaust polluted air around a seated 
person in order to effectively remove bio-effluents from the body before the polluted air 
reached the breathing zone and the surrounding environment. The operating principle of the 
cushion was similar to the body supporting device [34] discussed previously, but the design is 
different.

an individual control of temperature at each workstation in the range of ±2.3 K around 
average neutral temperature would be necessary for 95 % of the occupants in their preferred 
clothing to achieve a neutral thermal environment.

1.5.4 ENERGY SAVING POTENTIAL IN CASE OF PERSONALIZED VENTILATION 
As mentioned, the most frequently used ventilation strategy nowadays is TVV. Indoor 
environment designers and engineers choose this system for air conditioning, because they 
are familiar with the processes of dimensioning, maintaining etc. As long as clients do not 
demand higher IAQ, thermal comfort and more sustainable solutions, personalized ventilation 
systems will not be applied in practice.  
The financial incentive of applying more advanced personalized ventilation has not been 
substantially proven. Hence, there is a need to conduct energy saving potential of PV systems 
to convince clients that such systems are beneficial investments and should be applied in the 
future.  
There are three main strategies to reduce energy use with personalized ventilation [4]: 
1) Reducing the outdoor air supply rate due to higher ventilation effectiveness of PV 
2) Expanding comfort limits, by creating microenvironments using PV, and accepting a 
lower IAQ and thermal comfort outside the microenvironments 
3) Personalized ventilation is turned off when the occupant leaves the desk 
==PAGE:27==
26     INTRODUCTION 
 
Ventilation effectiveness can be defined in several ways. In this paper the ventilation 
effectiveness is defined as the ratio of the concentration of the pollution in the exhaust air 
divided by the concentration of the pollution in the air inhaled by the occupants.  
A study by Bolashikov et al. [38] achieved a ventilation effectiveness of 20 times or more 
compared to MV, by using a highly efficient air terminal devices which supplied almost 100% 
clean and cool personalized air. Air was supplied from a Round Movable Panel (RMP) 
(mounted on a desk) and from a headset-incorporated supply. The airflow from the RMP 
ranged from 5 to 15 L/s and the airflow from the headset from 0.18 to 0.5 L/s. This suggests 
that high air quality can be achieved in the breathing zone by means of relatively low airflow 
from PV. Therefore the background ventilation may be considerably decreased which leads to 
energy savings. 
Bauman et al. [39] carried out a study where expanded comfort limits of room air 
temperature were applied. High room temperature was kept between 25-27 oC and a local 
desktop system was applied to supply fresh and cool air to the microenvironment zone. The 
local desktop system was able to maintain average temperatures in the occupied zone from 
0.5 to 1.5 oC lower than the temperatures in the zone of another desktop without the local 
desktop system. 
In office buildings, the time a person spends at the desk vary with the type of work. Energy 
savings may be achieved if the PV is automatically turned off when occupants are absent from 
the desk, and energy can be saved. However, this method is highly dependent on the 
presence of sustainable heat removal methods, as switching off the PV will reduce the 
amount of free cooling effect supplied from the outdoor air supply. Due to this issue, the 
study of Schiavon et al. [4] reported that reducing airflows due to absent occupants is an 
inefficient way of saving energy. Furthermore, it is very difficult to theoretically predict the 
amount of saved energy by means of calculations and simulations, as the amount of reduced 
energy highly depends on the individual habits of each occupant in practice.

1.6 SUSTAINABLE HEAT REMOVAL IN NON-RESIDENTIAL BUILDINGS  
Buildings which comply with modern standards have a low heating demand due to high air 
tightness and low heat transmission through the building envelope. However, buildings with 
high heat loads generated by occupants, equipment, lightning and solar radiation may be 
subject to overheating, which result in high energy demand of the cooling systems. This calls 
for energy efficient cooling systems, which can maintain an acceptable thermal indoor 
environment by means of low energy use. The most used cooling systems in practice are 
presented in the following. 
1.6.1 AIR- AND WATER BASED COOLING SYSTEMS 
Both air and water can be used as media for cooling. Cooling with air as medium is referred to 
as convective cooling. Cooling with water as medium is referred to as radiant cooling. The 
most common method for cooling is based on MV only, which often results in poor indoor 
environment characterized by draught, temperature asymmetry, poor air quality, etc. It has 
been proven that combining convective and radiant cooling can result in improved 
performance of the indoor climate systems which ultimately provides more comfortable 
indoor environments [40].

water a better energy conductor than air. Therefore the supply air rate can be reduced when 
combining radiant cooling and MV, as the radiant cooling system copes with surplus heat and 
the MV should only supply air in order to fulfill the air quality requirements stated in DS/EN 
15251 [12]. Consequently, the size of ducts, fans, ATDs and other components in the 
ventilation system can be downscaled. This will allow the space above the required extended 
ceiling to be saved in the design phase. This strategy leads to reduced economical expenses 
for both operation and construction [24]. 
Water based radiant cooling are gaining popularity as an energy efficient approach for 
conditioning non-residential buildings. The increased interest for these systems in the recent 
years is due to strong evidence of their efficiency compared to TVV distribution systems [41].

1.6.2 CHILLED CEILING 
Water based cooling can be supplied using a chilled ceiling, where the radiant panels are 
suspended above the ceiling with heat carrier temperature relatively close to the room 
temperature [42]. The pipes carrying chilled water are typically distributed in a uniform 
pattern over the entire ceiling (Figure 10). The characteristic pattern ensures that cooling is 
distributed evenly through the entire room, whereas air cooling is mostly concentrated in the 
zones where the ATDs are placed in the ceiling (Figure 11). The application of chilled ceiling 
therefore has the potential to eliminate thermal discomfort, if the requirements for radiant 
asymmetry are met [24]. 
If not designed properly, water based cooling systems might have disadvantages and cause 
problems with the indoor environment. Too low temperatures can lead to condensation of 
water on the ceiling panels or radiant asymmetry can occur. Furthermore, the water based 
cooling has a slower response time for humans compared to convective cooling [24]. 
Chilled ceilings have a maximum cooling capacity of 100 W/m2 for sensible cooling [42]. 
1.6.3 CHILLED BEAM 
A chilled beam is another advanced and energy efficient cooling solution applied in buildings, 
which utilizes the advantageous properties of water for cooling. The beam is situated in the 
ceiling and cools convectively using natural or forced air flows. Several models are available 
on the commercial market, but they can be divided into two main groups: 1) Active chilled 
beams 2) Passive chilled beams (Figure 12).  
==PAGE:30==
29     INTRODUCTION 
 
 
Figure 12: Operation principles of three chilled beam concepts [43] 
Active chilled beams are connected to ventilation supply ductwork and chilled water system. 
Primary air is supplied to the ventilated space from a main air-handling unit through the 
chilled beam. A heat exchanger is located in the chilled beam, where hot room air is 
circulated and cooled and re-circulated into the space. This way allows the room temperature 
to be controlled by the amount of water flowing in the heat exchanger. The most optimal 
operating range for active chilled beams is when the sensible cooling requirement is between 
60-80 W/m2 floor area, and the maximum cooling effect should be below 120 W/m2 floor 
area [43]. 
Passive chilled beams are only connected to a chilled water system, and exploit the properties 
of natural convection in the space. A heat exchanger with chilled water controls the room 
temperature. With a passive chilled beam, primary air is supplied from a separate ventilation 
system. The most optimal operating range for passive chilled beams is when the sensible 
cooling requirement is between 40-80 W/m2 floor area [43]. 
Cooling demands higher than 120 W/m2 floor area can be met in rooms with no requirements 
for thermal comfort or non-sedentary environments e.g. copying rooms, computer rooms etc. 
Both types of chilled beams are suitable for rooms with moderate internal humidity loads, 
since too high humidity may cause condensation [43].

1.8 JUSTIFICATION OF PRESENT RESEARCH 
The present literature review indicates that a lot of research has been conducted in order to 
improve indoor environment conditions by using less energy consuming methods. The review 
comprises several strategies on how to improve indoor environment in sedentary 
environments, as people are sedentary in a large percentage of the working hours. At 
present, the most applied ventilation systems comprise extremely complicated control 
systems of VAV systems which often turn out to be non-efficient. Simpler CAV systems with 
local zones of individual control of air supply, heating and cooling may provide higher comfort 
for the occupants. 
The study by Rezgals [2] showed that exhausting low flow rates reduced the concentration of 
bio-effluents in the breathing zone very effectively. Exhausting 1.5 L/s from the VC combined 
with 1 ACH background room ventilation to reduce the bio-effluents’ concentration at the 
breathing zone, was up to 12% more efficient than increasing the background room 
ventilation to 6 ACH. Thus, such system has a potential to provide cleaner inhaled air and 
better perceived air quality for the occupants. A recent study by Tsushima et al. (2016) 
showed that people judge room air quality to be lower when it is polluted with body bio-
effluents compared to air polluted with orally exhaled bio-effluents. Thus, the localized 
exhaust method has the potential to greatly improve the IAQ.

The study by Rezgals [2] also showed that there is a cooling effect from exhausting air close to 
the body, which can be advantageous in warm environments, but may also have a negative 
effect on the user’s thermal comfort in colder environments. 
The study suggested that substantial energy savings could be achieved by using the VC, but it 
was not conducted. In order to reduce the total energy use of the entire HVAC system, 
sustainable heat removal methods should be applied in conjunction with VC. BPS software 
can be used to conduct the energy saving potential of such system. The present research

Some of the literature reviewed in the Introduction suggested that local exhaust systems are 
applicable in highly occupied spaces with seated occupants [3], [32], [33]. Such spaces are 
typically subject to a high pollution load generated from the body of humans and due to 
respiratory activities. The local exhaust applied in such spaces have the potential to effectively 
remove human-generated bio-effluents, which may be beneficial in terms of used energy, as 
the background ventilation rate may be reduced considerably. Local exhaust systems are 
especially suitable for seated environments (aircraft cabins, trains, theaters, etc.) as the 
sources of bio-effluents (humans) are relatively fixed in the same location during longer 
periods of time. Therefore a larger amount of bio-effluents are removed compared to spaces 
where people are more active and moving around.

3.1 SCIENTIFIC APPROACH 
The primary scientific method of the present research was the hypothetico-deductive 
method. This method aims to confirm or falsify scientific hypotheses by observing 
experimentally based consequences and outcomes. If a hypothesis has a specific number of 
consequences which are confirmed, the hypothesis can be confirmed. If one consequence of 
a hypothesis is disproved, the hypothesis is falsified. The hypotheses tested in this research 
were the following: 
- 
Substantial energy savings can be achieved when applying VC combined with 
sustainable heat removal, due to reduced background ventilation airflow rate 
- 
Cooling of the body provided by the VC may improve the thermal comfort in hot 
environments, but the VC may cool the body too much in cold environments which 
leads to dissatisfaction with the thermal environment.  
- 
Passive heating (thermal insulation) of the body parts in contact with the VC may be 
used to compensate for undesired cooling when applying the VC in cold environments 
- 
Active heating (local heating of the seat) of the body parts in contact with the VC may 
be used to compensate for undesired cooling when applying the VC in cold 
environments 
- 
Substantial energy savings can be achieved when applying the VC combined with 
sustainable heat removal and active local heating, due to reduced background 
ventilation airflow rate 
The opposite of hypothetico-deductive method, the hypothetico-inductive method, was to 
some extend applied in the current research. The hypothetico-inductive method comprises 
observing a number of individual statements or experimental results which are then 
formulated into a universally applicable theory. An example of appliance of the hypothetico-
inductive in the present study: The present research comprised finding the needed power 
output of a heated seat at different room air temperatures and VC airflow rates, for the 
purpose of the energy saving calculations. Four different seat power outputs were tested at 
all temperatures and airflow scenarios, from which it was concluded which seat power output 
was needed to achieve thermal comfort for each case.

3.3 ENERGY SIMULATIONS 
Indoor environment- and energy simulations were carried out using the software IDA Indoor 
Climate and Energy (IDA ICE) 4.6.1. The purpose was to determine the energy saving potential 
by using the VC to remove bio-effluents released from the occupants, and thereby reduce the 
airflow and energy use of the background ventilation. The simulations also comprised energy 
saving potential calculations by combining the VC with local active heating. 
The premises of the simulations were chosen to replicate a real non-residential environment 
where several people are seated for longer periods. A call-center was considered a suitable 
environment to be studied, as call-center operators are typically carrying out seated work for 
longer periods of time. The number of operators on duty, room size and time of operation 
(time by the desk) were adopted by the study of Wargocki et al. [11], who studied the IAQ 
and work-performance in a call-center located in Denmark.  
Several simulations with varying parameters were carried out, to conduct the energy 
performance under different circumstances. The varied parameters were: 1) Cooling methods 
2) Background room ventilation flow rate 3) Ventilation flow rate of the VC 4) Local heating 
methods.  
The heat load in the office was significant, due to presence of several occupants and 
electronic equipment such as computer screens, desk lamps, etc. Removal of heat by means 
of ventilation solely would need high background ventilation flow rate even in the case with 
VC appliance. The study therefore included simulations of sustainable heat removal by means 
of a chilled ceiling, which made it possible to lower the background ventilation. The simulated 
cases were: 
1) Only TVV Mixing Ventilation, denoted as “MV” 
2) TVV Mixing Ventilation and chilled ceiling, denoted as “MV+CC” 
3) TVV Mixing Ventilation, chilled ceiling and VC, denoted as “MV+CC+VC” 
4) TVV Mixing Ventilation, chilled ceiling and VC and active heating, denoted as 
“MV+CC+VC+AH”

3.5 THERMAL FULL SCALE MEASUREMENTS: EXPERIMENTAL FACILITIES 
The purpose of the thermal full scale measurements was to study the cooling effect of the VC 
at different airflow rates at room temperatures 20, 24 and 28 oC. The results of the 
measurements were used to identify the effect of a passive heating method (thermal 
insulation) and an active heating method (local heating) for compensating the body cooling 
effect provided by the ventilated cushion at the low range of comfortable room temperature 
to identify the energy used in the case of local heating. Eight different seat designs were 
tested during the seat optimization process. 
3.5.1 CLIMATE CHAMBER 
All thermal measurements were carried out in a full-scale climate chamber called “Chamber 
3” at the International Centre of Indoor Climate and Energy, Technical University of Denmark. 
The chamber had the dimensions W x L x H = 4.7 x 6.0 x 2.5 m.  
The chamber is specially designed for research on thermal comfort in indoor environments. 
Air was supplied using upward piston flow through a textile membrane in the floor and was 
exhausted through a rectangular exhaust (400 x 400 mm) located in the ceiling. The floor area 
was covered by a metal grid (located above the textile membrane) which made it possible to 
walk in the room and re-arrange instruments without damaging the textile membrane. The 
ceiling was composed of perforated aluminum panels supported by metal frames. The 
perforated panels allowed air to pass through the entire ceiling to the exhaust. However, for 
the purpose of this study four metal panels were removed above the location of the thermal 
mannequin, in order to effectively remove additional heat produced by the mannequin. Four 
fluorescent lighting panels (containing 4 x 18 W fluorescent tubes each) covered by 
translucent plastic panels were installed in the ceiling to provide lighting in the chamber. 
The software “Building Operation Workstation (1.8.1.79)” was used as control system 
software, and was used to regulate the room air temperature within accuracy of 0.1 oC. The 
temperature was changed by regulating the room temperature set point, which could be set 
in the range of 10 oC to 40 oC. The temperature sensor for the control system was placed on 
the left side wall in the room at 1.7 m above the floor (see Figure 22). A heating coil and a 
cooling coil were installed in the HVAC system of the chamber to achieve a suitable supply air 
temperature. To speed up the process of changing the room air temperature a “speed up” 
button was available in the control system software. However, during the measurements it 
was found that activating this button would make the room air temperature fluctuate 
approximately ±4 oC around the set point temperature, making the temperature changing 
process even more time consuming. Hence, the “speed up” feature was not utilized in order 
to achieve the desired room air temperature in this study. 
==PAGE:54==
53     METHODOLOGY 
 
The airflow rates from the VC studied in the present research were the same as the airflow 
rates in the study by Rezgals [2]: 1.5, 3.0 and 5.0 L/s. These airflow rates were chosen, so it 
would be possible to use and compare results in the present study with the results from the 
study by Rezgals [2]. 
3.5.2 VENTILATION SYSTEMS 
The room was equipped with two separate ventilation systems; a total volume ventilation 
system (piston flow) working as background ventilation, and the local exhaust system 
implemented in a chair cushion - the ventilated cushion (VC) (see section 3.5.3 for description 
of the cushion). 
The background ventilation system did not utilize recirculation, consequently 100% outdoor 
air was supplied. The air distribution method was upward piston flow which ensured very low 
air velocities (less than 0.06 m/s) and low vertical temperature gradient [51]. A wooden plate 
was placed below the mannequin to ensure a very calm and quiescent environment (see 
Figure 20). 25% of the air was supplied between the inner wall and a thin textile layer on the 
walls. This ensured that the walls had the same temperature as the room air and 
consequently that the air temperature was equal to the mean radiant temperature. Therefore 
it was assumed that no radiant asymmetry occurred in the chamber.

3 and 5 L/s from the VC. The fan was located outside the climate chamber during the 
measurements. 
The exact airflow rate from the cushion was determined by measuring the pressure drop in 
the exhaust duct using a MICATRONE MFS-C-0080. However, the uncertainty of the 
MICATRONE MFS-C-0080 at airflow rates below 11 L/s was too large and would not be 
reliable. Therefore, in order accurately measure the airflow rate from the VC (1.5, 3 and 5 
L/s), the three-way branch system was established. By using this setup, relatively high flow 
rates in branches 1 and 3 (approximately 30-40 L/s) was measured accurately, which allowed 
the operator to adjust an exact low airflow rate from the VC by means of a damper (see 
position 5 on Figure 21).  
The duct exhausting air from the VC inside the chamber to the space outside the chamber 
was lead through a small perforation in the wall (located behind the mannequin) of the

TEMPERATURE CALIBRATION OF THERMAL MANNEQUIN 
Prior to the full scale measurements the thermal mannequin was calibrated to ensure that 
the mannequin surface temperature and heat loss were measured accurately. The 
mannequin was placed in climate chamber 3 in upright sitting position without any clothing. 
The device which supported the body of the mannequin was made from an office chair, but 
only consisted of the metal supporting parts (see Figure 30). The metal parts in contact with 
the mannequin were covered with rubber tubes, to minimize the heat conduction between 
the metal and the mannequin body. 
 
Figure 30: Body supporting device for mannequin calibration 
The legs of the mannequin were placed on a metal frame and the arms of the mannequin 
were placed on a metal bar in front of the mannequin (see Figure 31). The special positioning 
of the mannequin was arranged in order to minimize the contact surface between the 
mannequin and other surfaces. This ensured that the body surface of the mannequin was not 
heated or cooled through conduction from other surfaces, so all body parts of the mannequin 
had the same temperature. Three fans were circulating the air around the mannequin, in 
order to increase the convectional heat loss from the body surface of the mannequin. 
 
==PAGE:64==
63     METHODOLOGY 
 
 
Figure 31: Setup for the mannequin temperature calibration 
The software was set to “No heat” mode during the calibration procedure, meaning that the 
electrical wires were not activated during calibration. The mannequin was calibrated for two 
different temperature ranges: temperature A (20 oC) and temperature B (30 oC). Initially, the 
mannequin was calibrated for temperature A with the following procedure [54]: 
1. Set the temperature of the chamber to 20 oC 
2. Select “No heat” mode of the mannequin (in the software) 
3. Wait until the stability criteria is satisfied. At that point the sub window screen will 
turn green. 
4. Wait until the temperature in the room reaches the set point temperature. The 
temperature in the room was measured with a separate thermometer. 
5. Set the temperature A in the software to be equal to the room air temperature. This is 
done from: Body segment/Calibrate/Temperature A – 20 oC 
6. Wait at least 10 minutes and change the temperature condition of the room 
Secondly, the following procedure was followed to calibrate for temperature B [54]: 
1. Set the temperature of the chamber to 30 oC 
2. Repeat steps 2 to 5 from the temperature A calibration procedure 
3. Set the temperature B in the software to be equal to the room air temperature. This is 
done from: Body segment/Calibrate/Temperature B – 30 oC 
During the calibration procedure, the chamber was only ventilated with TVV piston flow 
functioning as described in the section 3.5.2.

3.5.7 THERMAL MEASUREMENTS 
During the thermal measurements the “Comfort mode” was activated in the software. To 
retrieve data, the logging option in the “Manikin” software was utilized. Data was only logged 
when the stability criterion of the software was met. The stability criterion was calculated as 
the difference between the average body temperature of the mannequin 10 minutes ago and 
the running mean value of the average body temperature of the mannequin during the past 5 
minutes. The stability criterion was met if the value was equal to 0.05 oC or below. At that 
point the sub window screen turned green. If the value of the stability criterion did not 
change immediately when changing any experimental settings (VC airflow rate, room air 
temperature, heating mode, etc.), a break of at least 10 minutes was held before logging 
again. This ensured that the temperatures were steady during the data logging procedure and 
the stability criterion did not suddenly change while logging.  
Data logging for every thermal case was carried out for 10 minutes with a logging interval of 
10 seconds resulting in approximately 60 data sets per log file. The resulting heat losses from 
the mannequin presented in the “Results” chapter were calculated as average values of the 
data logged for 10 minutes. This method ensured that small fluctuations in the heat loss and 
mannequin body temperatures would not affect the results significantly.  
During all thermal measurements the temperature in the exhaust duct of the VC was logged. 
This was done to get an impression of the amount of heat which was removed from the local 
zone around the mannequin. Furthermore, the relative humidity was recorded for all thermal 
measurements from the control system software.

3.6 EXPERIMENTAL CONDITIONS 
For the purpose of the thermal comfort measurements the VC performance was tested at 
three different room air temperatures: 20, 24 and 28 oC ± 0.1 oC. At 20 oC the overall thermal 
insulation of the clothing was 1.0 Clo and at 24 and 28 oC the thermal insulation was 0.5 Clo. 
The setup did not include any table or laptop in front of the mannequin. See Table 10 to see 
experimental conditions for each case. 
The VC thermal comfort performance was studied at airflow rates of 0.0, 1.5, 3.0 and 5 L/s ± 
0.1 L/s. In the process of improving the design to obtain thermal comfort, eight different seat 
designs were tested. All cases had in common that the performance was measured at all four 
VC airflow rates. The eight different designs and the motivation of the designs are listed in the 
following in chronological order:  
1) Standard design without active or passive heating of the body 
This experimental condition included the VC without active or passive heating (see Figure 36). 
The body heat loss found at room temperature of 24 oC with no VC was assumed to represent 
the case where the mannequin was in a state of thermal comfort. This case was denoted as 
“The thermal comfort case”.  
The thermal comfort assumption was based on the design value of the indoor temperature 
provided from the standard DS/EN 15251 [12]. This standard recommends a minimum 
temperature for heating (winter season) of 21.0 oC and a maximum temperature for cooling 
(summer season) of 25.5 oC for landscaped- and single offices with category I thermal indoor 
environment. Hence 24 oC was considered as an optimal comfortable temperature for both a 
winter and summer scenario.  
This design was tested at three room air temperatures (20, 24 and 28 oC).

4. RESULTS 
4.1 INDOOR ENVIRONMENT- AND ENERGY SIMULATIONS 
In the following the energy saving potential of applying sustainable heat removal method and 
the VC combined with active heating on 14 workstations in a call-center located in Denmark is 
presented. The hypotheses were: 
1) Substantial energy savings could be achieved when applying the VC combined with 
sustainable heat removal, due to reduced background ventilation airflow rate; 
2) Substantial energy savings could be achieved when applying the VC combined with 
sustainable heat removal and active local heating, due to reduced background ventilation 
airflow rate.  
As mentioned in the “Methodology” chapter, four cases were simulated. In the following 
presentation of the results, the four cases will therefore be referred to as: 
- 
“MV”: Total volume mixing air distribution  
- 
“MV+CC”: Total volume mixing air distribution and chilled ceiling 
- 
“MV+CC+VC”: Total volume mixing air distribution, chilled ceiling and VC  
- 
“MV+CC+VC+AH”: Total volume mixing air distribution, chilled ceiling and VC with 
active heating to compensate for cooling provided by the VC 
The IDA ICE result sheets (“Delivered energy” and “Systems energy”) from all 16 simulations 
can be found in Appendix 6.

4.1.1 ENERGY SAVING POTENTIAL OF APPLYING VC IN A CALL-CENTER LOCATED IN 
COPENHAGEN  
The yearly primary energy use (referred to as ”energy use” in the following) for the four 
simulation cases are shown in Figure 45.  
 
Figure 45: Yearly primary energy use for the four simulation cases, 20-26 °C 
As expected, the results of the energy simulations show that energy savings can be achieved 
when applying sustainable heat removal, i.e. chilled ceiling (CC) combined with MV. In the 
MV+CC case, appliance of the chilled ceiling (without VC) decreased the energy use with 3.6 
W/m2 floor area compared to the case with only MV. Figure 45 also shows that applying the 
VC at the four different airflow rates kept the energy use more or less constant around 52 
W/m2. This result indicates that the decrease in background ventilation airflow does not make 
up for the increased airflow from the VC applied on the 14 workstations, in terms of energy 
saving potential. However, the results for the MV+CC+VC case also indicate that removal of 
human bio-effluents by means of VC does not increase the energy use compared to the case 
MV+CC; consequently a (much) better indoor air quality can be achieved for the same 
amount of used energy. The results for the MV+CC+VC+AH case show that the energy use 
increases with increasing the VC airflow rate, when additional local heating was provided to 
the VC to compensate for the cooling effect of the VC at 20 and 24 °C (winter, spring and fall 
in the simulations). When sucking 5 L/s of air from the cushion, the energy use was the same

5. DISCUSSION 
In this chapter the results obtained from the indoor environment- and energy simulations and 
the full scale measurements with thermal manikin are discussed in separate sections. 
5.1 INDOOR ENVIRONMENT- AND ENERGY SIMULATIONS 
5.1.1 SIMULATION RESULTS 
From the results presented in the previous “Results” chapter it was concluded that combining 
mixing ventilation (MV) and chilled celing (CC) for sustainable heat removal in a call-center 
located in Copenhagen reduces the energy use compared to a system with only mixing 
ventilation. Applying the VC with reduced background ventilation combined with MV and CC 
does not lead to further energy savings compared to the case with MV and CC. However, 
neither the energy use increases when applying the VC with reduced background ventilation, 
consequently the energy saving potential of VC+MV+CC was identical to the case with 
MV+CC. Furthermore, applying active heating (AH) in order to remove cooling from the VC 
and to obtain thermal comfort at room temperatures of 20 and 24 oC combined with 
VC+MV+CC increased the energy use stepwise as the VC airflow rate increased. Energy 
savings were still obtained at VC airflow rates 1.5 and 3.0 L/s, whereas VC airflow rate of 5.0 
L/s led to very small or no energy savings. Finally it was found that expanding setpoints for 
operative room temperature from 20- 26 oC to 20-28 oC led to further energy saving 
potential. The difference in energy use was approximately 300 kWh for all the cases where CC 
was applied at 20-28C temperature setpoints. The expanded operative temperature led to 
substantial energy savings also in the case with AH when sucking 5.0 L/s from the VC. 
5.1.2 SCOPE OF THE SIMULATION RESULTS 
The results found from the simulations in the present study have a narrow scope, as they are 
specifically valid for the current case comprising a specific room type, room temperature 
setpoints, cooling and heating methods, etc. The resulting energy saving potential or the 
economical incitement may be significantly different for other rooms or buildings with 
different occupants’ activity, heat loads or located in other climate zones. In the present 
study, a COP value of 4.0 was used for district cooling and a PEF of 1.8 for electricity, making 
district cooling a preferable source of energy. However, another building may have 
photovoltaic panels installed which may make it economically beneficial to decrease the 
consumed amount of district cooling and increase the airflow and hence the electricity 
consumed from the fans. The present study, however, suggests a methodology for energy 
saving calculations when applying VC combined with chilled ceiling, a methodology which may 
be applied in other contexts than the one presented in the present thesis. 
 
==PAGE:112==
111     DISCUSSION 
 
5.1.3 ASSUMPTIONS AND RELIABILITY OF THE SIMULATION RESULTS 
The results gained from the indoor environment- and energy simulations are based on several 
assumptions regarding user behavior of the occupants, amount of equipment, setpoints for 
temperature and CO2 levels for control of the HVAC system, etc. The assumptions are a 
necessary prerequisite in the process of determining how non-existing buildings and building 
systems perform (e.g. in terms of energy), which automatically will cause diversions between 
the theoretically calculated and the actual performance of the building systems when they 
are constructed in real life. Hence, standalone results from simulations should always be 
analyzed with precaution. However, indoor environment- and energy simulations are an 
exceptional tool when conducting analyses on how design alternatives perform compared to 
a reference case. The indoor environment- and energy simulations carried out in the present 
study primarily applied as a comparative analysis tool. 
In the present study the simulated premises were chosen to replicate a real non-residential 
environment (presented in the study by Wargocki et al. [11]). This was done in order to give 
the simulations a dimension of reality when choosing number of occupants in the call-center, 
size of the room and duration of occupancy. However, even these realistic input data are to 
some extent modified for the purpose of the simulations. These modifications and 
assumptions are presented and discussed in the following. 
It was assumed that all 14 occupants are present during the occupied hours which implies 
that breaks are spend in the same room, which may not reflect the realistic conditions of the 
real call-center. This affects the amount of heat emitted from the occupants but also how 
much heat is emitted from the equipment as the equipment has the same operation schedule 
as the occupants. Further studies may comprise adding a simultaneity factor which takes into 
account the fact that the room is not fully loaded during all occupied hours. The simulations 
carried out are estimated to be more conservative compared to a simulation where a 
simultaneity factor is applied, as more heat is emitted and more energy is consumed to 
remove the heat. 
The fact that the background ventilation, the chilled ceiling and the local VC is controlled by 
the same room-based setpoints for CO2 and temperature is a simplified prerequisite. In 
reality, the background ventilation and the chilled ceiling may be controlled by a room-level 
CO2 and temperature sensor, but the VC airflow rate is more likely to be controlled by the 
occupant which the VC serves or a local CO2 and temperature sensor monitoring the 
temperature of the body surface in contact with the VC. The simplification was necessary to 
be applied in the present study as the used simulation software (IDA ICE) does not have the 
option to simulate a separate exhaust fan which is connected to the heat exchanger. More 
complicated HVAC systems can be simulated with another software (such as IES/Virtual 
environment ApacheHVAC). It is estimated that the present model is of conservative 
==PAGE:113==
112     DISCUSSION 
 
character, in the sense that the VC consumes more energy compared to a more detailed 
simulation model, where the VC is controlled separately. The estimation is based on the fact 
that the VC may be turned off automatically when the operator leaves the workstation for 
lunch or break, which may be implemented in a more detailed simulation model.  
The fact that the solar shading devices applied on all windows are activated at incident 
sunlight power of 100 W/m2 on the inside of the glass is an assumption which may cause 
variations compared to real conditions. The automatic control of the shading may in practice 
be over-modulated by the occupants in the room, e.g. due to presence of glare (closed earlier 
compared to 100 W/m2) or due to an increased demand of daylight (closed later compared to 
100 W/m2). It is complicated to estimate whether the assumption is conservative, as it highly 
depends on the subjective preferences of the occupants of the room how the shading is 
controlled. However, as the room is oriented towards east and west, the sun is likely to be 
lower in the sky compared to a southern oriented window. This may more easily cause glare 
which goes deeper within the room. Thus, it is estimated that the shadings may be drawn 
earlier compared to 100 W/m2, which causes a decreased solar heat gain and less cooling 
compared to the simulated model. From these considerations, the solar shading model is 
slightly none-conservative. 
It is assumed that the windows in the call-center cannot be opened and used for venting. This 
is done as the IAQ measurements carried out by Rezgals [2], from which the background 
ventilation rate in the present simulations are selected, are based on solely mechanical 
ventilation combined with VC. In reality the frequency of opening and closing the windows is 
more likely to be controlled by the wish of the occupants due to desire of natural ventilation 
or a noisy external environment. 
The active heating applied in the simulation case MV+CC+VC+AH is controlled by a simple 
scheme (presented in the chapter “Methodology“) according to the different seasons of the 
year. Further studies may include human subject experiments, where the occupants should 
be allowed to increase and decrease active heating according to the room temperature. The 
power should then be recorded for the purpose of the yearly energy simulations. 
Furthermore, the assumption that the room temperature in the call-center is 20, 24 and 28 oC 
(for appliance of the active eating) for the winter, summer and fall/spring, respectively, is also 
robust as the room temperatures vary at all times of the year. However, this assumption was 
necessary, as the used simulation software does not allow implementing a scheme for the 
power output of electronic heating equipment according to room temperature. 
In general, more realistic results might be obtained on the energy performance of the VC by 
conducting a full scale measurement with human subjects who could regulate the VC airflow 
rate, leave for breaks, etc., which would form a different energy use pattern. This would be 
more preferable compared to a simulation model as it gives a human dimension to the 
==PAGE:114==
113     DISCUSSION 
 
measurements, thus make it more realistic. Even more realistic results could be obtained if a 
field study where the VC solution was applied on real office environment workstations was 
carried out. However, this would be a highly extensive task which is beyond the workload of a 
master’s thesis. 
5.1.4 SIZE OF ENERGY SAVING POTENTIAL 
It is evident that energy savings can be achieved when applying the VC in combination with 
chilled ceiling, which forms an incentive to install these systems in a call-center. Sucking air 
from the VC increases the IAQ, which may lead to a more satisfactory office environment 
which eventually may increase the productivity of the phone-operators. If active heating is 
used to remove the cooling from the VC and obtain thermal comfort at indoor temperatures 
20 and 24 oC, the energy saving potential decreases but is still evident at  up to 3.0 L/s VC 
exhaust flow rate. When sucking 5.0 L/s from the VC combined with active heating the energy 
saving potential is very small or none-existing. 
Using the calculation method for decreasing the background ventilation airflow for the 
purpose of the simulation presented in this study suggested that the appliance of VC 
combined with sustainable heat removal does not pose larger energy saving potential 
compared to the case with only sustainable heat removal (without VC). The energy use was 
the same for both cases despite the exhaust flow rate of the VC (no matter it was 1.5, 3 or 5 
L/s). This result may be explained by the fact that the airflow was VAV controlled, meaning 
that the airflow rate was automatically reduced when qtot (the maximum airflow rate) was not 
required to maintain the CO2- and the temperature setpoints. If the ventilation system had 
been designed as a CAV system, the energy saving potential would have followed the size of 
the value qtot more coherently. A CAV system in a general perspective is more energy 
consuming compared to a VAV system, consequently the yearly use would be considerably 
higher for all four simulation cases which would not be preferable. Further studies on energy 
saving potential may therefore comprise other control setpoints of the HVAC systems for CO2, 
in order to sharpen the demands for maximum airflow rates. This may result in higher energy 
saving potential of applying the VC with reduced background ventilation. 
Even though the case of MV+CC+VC does not decrease the energy use compared to the case 
with MV+CC, the MV+CC+VC case is still preferable, as applying the VC improves the IAQ since 
bio-effluents emitted from the body of the occupants are effectively removed. The study by 
Rezgals [2] showed that sucking 3.0 L/s from the VC (combined with 1 ACH from MV) 
corresponds to increasing the room airflow to 6 ACH (instead of 1 ACH). These results were 
obtained when the room was ventilated using MV air distribution, the room air temperature 
was 26 oC and the simulated bio effluents were released from the occupant’s groin region 
(see Table 15). The IAQ is considered to be improved when bio-effluents emitted from the 
==PAGE:115==
114     DISCUSSION 
 
body of occupants are removed as a recent study [48] have shown that people are more 
sensitive to bio-effluents emitted from the body compared to pollutants from exhaled air. 
The present study only comprises airflow rates according to category II in DS/EN 15251 [12] 
which is normal level of expectation with the indoor environment [12]. Category II demands 
airflow of 7 L/s per person. Furthermore, airflow due to building pollution was calculated 
according to very low polluting buildings. Further research may include studies on energy 
saving potential for category II buildings and low polluting buildings, as it is not included in the 
present study. There may be a higher energy saving potential when applying VC for a category 
I environment, as this category demands 10 L/s per person, and the qred (reduction of the 
total flow rate) therefore will be approximately 30% higher. Nevertheless, the higher 
minimum airflow due to building emissions (0.5 L/s m2 for category I compared to 0.35 L/s m2 
for category II) may increase the general energy use in all four cases. 
The reduction of the airflow rate from the background ventilation was based on the 
assumption that the qocc can be reduced equivalently according to the results found by 
Rezgals [2]. As mentioned already, these results were obtained when the room was ventilated 
using MV air distribution, the room air temperature was 26 oC and the simulated bio effluents 
were released from the occupant’s groin region (see Table 15). These results were used for 
the purpose of the present study, as they were not the highest nor the lowest reductions 
compared to the three other VC cases in Table 15, with the aim to use realistic reduction 
values.

at 26 oC at all VC airflow rates (see Table 15). Using the results at 20 oC may therefore 
increase the energy saving potential of applying the VC at the three airflow rates. 
The concentration reduction found in the study by Rezgals [2] was measured in a test room 
replicating a single office room. For the purpose of the present study, it was assumed that the 
concentration reduction is similar for the landscaped call-center which has a different 
geometry, occupant density, etc. This may be a rough assumption as the general 
experimental setup varies between the simulation setup and the test room setup which has 
an impact on the concentration reduction when applying VC and higher background 
ventilation rate. However, the test room setup by Rezgals [2] implied a typical single office 
working environment with a desk and a laptop. This was estimated to be sufficiently similar to 
the setup of one workstation in a typical call-center. 
5.1.5 ENERGY USE FROM BUILDING SERVICE SYSTEMS

conditioning non-residential buildings. The increased interest for these systems in the recent 
years is due to strong evidence of their efficiency compared to total volume ventilation 
distribution systems [41]. From the results it is evident that the inclusion of chilled ceiling 
panels for radiant heat removal combined with reduced background ventilation airflow rate is 
an effective strategy to obtain energy savings. As mentioned, a COP value of 4.0 was used for 
district cooling and a PEF of 1.8 for electricity, which enhance the chilled ceiling to be an 
effective energy saving strategy. However, for other scenarios where different COP values and 
PEF applies, distinctively different results may be obtained.

5.2 THERMAL MEASUREMENTS 
5.2.1 COOLING EFFECT OF VC 
From the results of the design without passive or active heating it is clear that the VC provides 
unwanted cooling for left and back thigh, the back and the body as a whole when sucking 3.0 
and 5.0 L/s at room temperature of 20 oC but also at 24 oC. It is also evident that the studied 
VC airflow rates generally are not sufficient to provide thermal comfort of the body parts in 
contact with the VC or the body as a whole at room temperature 28 oC. However, sucking 5.0 
L/s from the VC almost provides thermal comfort of the back region at room temperature 28 
oC. This suggests that a bit higher airflow from the VC may provide the needed cooling for the 
back region. However, the remaining body parts in contact with the VC need further cooling 
to obtain thermal comfort. This may call for improvement or modifications of the VC device. A 
modification may provide the option to suck more air from the thighs and pelvis regions.  
However, if allowing higher room temperature such as 28 oC the VC solution may be 
combined with (local) radiant cooling panels to provide thermal comfort of the body parts 
which are not in contact with the VC and the body as a whole. The study by Bolashikov et al. 
[40] found that during high heat load conditions chilled ceiling combined with mixing 
ventilation was able to maintain lower operative temperature in  a room compared to three 
other systems with 1) mixing ventilation only 2) chilled beam 3) chilled beam with radiant 
panels. The performance of the VC solution should be investigated in conjunction with the 
mentioned systems to study the effect of applying the VC in combination with other 
convective and radiant cooling systems. 
The cooling provided from the VC increases with an increased VC airflow rate, especially at 
3.0 and 5.0 L/s the cooling effect is evident at all room temperatures. Especially the results for 
the back and the thighs showed high cooling effects, whereas the cooling effect of the body 
as a whole was cooled less compared to the individual body parts in contact with the VC. The 
mannequin body is therefore subject to thermal asymmetry which may cause discomfort.

5.2.3 WHY IS PASSIVE HEATING NOT SUFFICIENT TO REMOVE COOLING FROM THE VC? 
The results from the cooling effect of the three seat designs comprising the passive heating 
methods indicate that passive heating is not sufficient to compensate for the cooling effect of 
the VC (Figure 58-Figure 60). In fact, there is not any clear trend of the heat loss of the body 
parts in contact with the VC when applying passive heating. The changes in heat loss may 
simply be due to small changes in placement of the mannequin on the VC when re-arranging 
the experimental setup.  
The results of the design with passive heating and tape on edges indicate that there is a small 
reduction in cooling effect on the right thigh (for both room temperatures 20 and 24 oC)(see 
Figure 50). However, this is not the tendency for the left thigh which seems to be exposed to 
more cooling compared to the first design without passive heating. The pelvis is cooled 
significantly more at room temperature of 20 oC compared to the first design at all VC airflow 
rates. The reason for this may be that the placement of the mannequin on the chair for this 
design was slightly different compared to the first design and the second design at 24 oC, in a 
way that the pelvis was more exposed to air velocities. The exact clothing positioning might 
also affect the heat loss from the pelvis region, as this is where the trousers and shirt meet, 
which unintentionally might have left the pelvis a bit bare. 
The results of the design with passive heating with open edges (see Figure 51) at 24 oC are 
very similar to the results gained from the design with closed edges (see Figure 50), thus 
removing the tape from the edges of the chair had no effect. 
The design with passive heating narrow insulation (see Figure 52) seems to remove the 
cooling effect, but when looking further into the results, it turns out that this design in 
general poses a higher heat loss (especially for left back thigh and right back thigh) compared 
to the previous passive heating designs. Consequently, the design with narrow insulation has 
a larger heat loss compared to a state of thermal comfort (see Figure 60). 
But why is the effect of applying passive heating not any larger and why is the effect this 
indistinct? Several reasons may explain this phenomenon. Firstly, even with a large effort in 
positioning the passive heating correctly on the VC, small dislocation may still occur, which 
may partially block the openings on the VC and affect how much air is sucked through them. 
This may affect the heat loss of distinct body parts in contact with the VC. Secondly, the 
additional insulation introduced by the passive heating may not insulate significantly more 
than the office chair do in the first case, and therefore it does not provide any heating. Thus 
the passive heating is not enough to remove the increased air movement from the VC which 
forces the additional convectional heat loss from the body parts in contact with the VC.

5.2.4 ACTIVE HEATING COMBINED WITH VC – THERMAL COMFORT, CONTROL AND 
IMPROVEMENTS 
From the thermal comfort results it is evident that the active heating has a substantial impact 
on the thermal balance of the mannequin body. The active heating can be used to remove the 
cooling effect from the VC at lower room temperatures and to obtain thermal comfort of the 
body parts in contact with the seat. The results showed that a seat power output in the range 
between 1.6 and 17.2 W is sufficient to remove cooling effect from the VC and to obtain 
thermal comfort. These are rather low values compared to the study [36] reviewed in the 
Introduction chapterwhere a maximum active heating power of 42 W and an average power 
for heating of 27 W was used. The reviewed study was carried out with human subjects, 
whereas the present study was calculated according to results from a thermal mannequin 
measurement. This may indicate that humans appreciate local heating or cooling of specific 
body parts to a higher extent than assumed in the present study. The phenomenon also 
confirms that local discomfort is felt and assessed subjectively by each individual person. This 
topic is further discussed in the section 5.2.5. 
From the results in this study it is evident that the different body parts in contact with the VC 
pose different heat losses compared to thermal comfort. E.g. when observing the results for 
the 8th design at 20 oC (see Figure 64), the two thighs are cooled too much, and the pelvis and 
the back are heated too much, except when sucking 5.0 L/s from the VC where the back is in a 
state of thermal comfort. This phenomenon suggests that the active heating should be able to 
differ distinctly between body parts to which it supplies heat. This calls for modifications of 
the design of the current active heating design, which supplies the same heat on the entire 
seat surface. 
The results also suggest that more sophisticated power regulation of the active heating could 
be applied to obtain thermal comfort of the body parts in contact with the seat at different 
VC airflow rates, as the VC cools differently at different airflow rates and room temperatures. 
The VC may also be controlled by more sophisticated controlling procedures, so the occupant 
should not be concerned about regulating airflows. A study by Gagge et al. [61] based on 
human subject experiments proved that the evaporative heat loss due to sweating of a sitting 
unclothed person increases rapidly with the increase of ambient temperature at room 
temperatures above 28 oC. The amount of sweat released is directly linked to the pollutants 
emitted from the human body as presence of microorganisms and oxidation reactions allows 
sweat components to convert into odorous compounds [18]. In the study by Rezgals [2], it is 
therefore suggested that high room temperatures should cause higher exhaust airflow rates 
from the VC to rapidly remove the bio-effluents emitted due to sweating, which also provides 
cooling. Accordingly, the VC airflow rate could be reduced at lower room air temperatures 
since less pollutants are emitted and cooling is not needed. The study by Rezgals [2] also 
suggests that the VC may be automatically controlled according to the amount of sweat 
==PAGE:123==
122     DISCUSSION 
 
released from the occupant. However, the active heating studied in the present study allows 
sucking air at lower room air temperatures and still obtaining thermal comfort (which 
however increases the energy use). The VC airflow may therefore be controlled manually by 
the occupant, and according to the room air temperature and the VC airflow rate, the active 
heating should supply a certain amount of heat according to thermal preferences of the 
individual person. Further work should be carried out on this topic and the energy saving 
potential should be conducted accordingly. 
Furthermore, tracer gas measurements should reveal if the appliance of the active heating 
has a negative effect on the air pollution removal compared to the results in the study by 
Rezgals [2]. 
5.2.5 THERMAL COMFORT OF THE BODY PARTS IN CONTACT WITH THE VC AND THE BODY 
AS A WHOLE 
In the present study, a reference case (“main reference”: room temperature 24 oC and VC 
OFF, 1. design) was used to calculate the heat loss compared to a “state of thermal comfort” 
of each body part in contact with the seat and the body as a whole. This assumption was 
based on the standard DS/EN 15251, category II [12], which demands operative temperatures 
between 20 and 26 oC. However, as described in the Introduction chapter, humans have their 
individual preferences which make it practically impossible to design a thermal environment 
with 100% satisfied occupants. Hence, it is a robust assumption that people are in a state off 
thermal comfort at room temperature of 24 oC when the VC is off. The reference case 
however helps giving a good impression of the thermal balance of the mannequin at different 
seat designs in order to evaluate the specific performance of one seat design compared to 
another. Human subject experiments should be carried out in order to find out the optimal 
controlling methods of the VC and the heated seat as described in the section above. 
5.2.6 POTENTIAL EXPERIMENTAL SETUP MODIFICATIONS 
It has already been mentioned how the deformed constructional parts within the VC may 
cause the airflow from the VC to be affected, as the small openings in the VC may be blocked 
due to the weight of the mannequin. This may also be the reason why the back in general is 
the body part in contact with the VC which is the largest subject of cooling. The present 
condition of the VC may depict the condition after a certain period of appliance in reality, as 
weight from humans sitting on the VC obviously affects the constructional parts in the VC. 
Further studies may include improvement of the VC design in order to avoid the 
constructional parts within the VC to deform after a period of appliance. Tracer gas 
measurements of the present VC condition may show a reduction in pollution removal. This 
should be investigated further. 
==PAGE:124==
123     DISCUSSION 
 
When changing the clothing value from 0.5 to 1.0 clo, the clothing is only changed for the 
torso and the feet. As a consequent of this, the cooling effect of the thighs increase relatively 
more at room temperature 20 oC compared to room temperature 24 oC than the remaining 
body parts in contact with the VC (for instance for the first design). It is possible that dressing 
the mannequin differently to obtain a clo value of 1.0 clo will cause less cooling of the thighs 
and they will therefore be closer to a state of thermal comfort. A suggestion may be to apply 
thicker trousers or long underwear on the legs and apply slightly less clothing on the torso. 
5.2.7 FURTHER WORK 
When discussing energy saving potential in the present study, a precondition for energy 
savings was to apply sustainable heat removal, as the dimensioning airflow rate is often (also 
in the present study) due to heat removal. Sustainable heat removal is therefore needed in 
order to decrease the background ventilation airflow rate when applying the VC solution. 
From the literature review it was concluded that comfortable indoor environments can be 
achieved by combining convective and radiant cooling due to improved performance of the 
indoor climate systems. The thermal full scale experiments carried out in the present study do 
not comprise a chilled ceiling for heat removal. This may be applied in further full scale 
mannequin- or human subject experiments to evaluate the effect of cooling provided from 
the VC combined with radiant cooling panels. 
It may be considered to apply the VC without connecting it to a main ventilation system, by 
cleaning the air locally and recirculate it instead of exhausting. A study by Bivolarova et al. 
[62] studied this method with a ventilated mattress combined with air cleaning fabric. In one 
case air was cleaned locally by a special fabric and recirculated back into the room. This 
reduced the generated bio-effluents in the room with approximately 70%. A solution with air 
cleaning fabric incorporated in the VC may therefore be investigated, as ducting to a main 
ventilation system in this case may be excluded which will make the installation of the VC 
solution easier in practice.  
The present study does not present solutions for problems of more pragmatic character such 
as ducting for VC solution for office workstations or other practical applications, general 
design of VC solution, how to cope with clogging of VC ducts, etc. Such challenges should be 
addressed in further studies to evaluate how realistic it is to imply the VC solution in practice. 
In relation to this it should be studied how active heating and VC can be integrated in one 
design solution, instead of several solutions applied on each other. The VC and active heating 
solution may even be integrated into the office chair to make a more functional and aesthetic 
seat solution for practical use.

6. CONCLUSION 
The present study investigates the use of a ventilated cushion (VC) with regards to energy 
performance and thermal comfort. Energy simulations were carried out to clarify the energy-
saving potential of the VC with reduced background ventilation and sustainable heat removal 
(chilled ceiling). Furthermore full scale measurements with a thermal mannequin were carried 
out to clarify the thermal performance of the VC combined with passive and active heating. 
The main conclusions of the study are presented in the following: 
 Combining mixing ventilation (MV) and chilled ceiling (CC) for sustainable heat 
removal in a call-center located in Copenhagen reduces the energy use compared to a 
system with only mixing ventilation. 
 Applying the VC with reduced background ventilation combined with MV and CC does 
not lead to further energy savings compared to the case with MV and CC. However, 
neither the energy use increases when applying VC with reduced background 
ventilation, consequently the energy saving potential of VC+MV+CC was identical to 
the case with MV+CC. 
 Applying active heating (AH) in order to remove cooling from VC and to obtain thermal 
comfort at room temperatures of 20 and 24 oC combined with VC+MV+CC increased 
the energy use stepwise as the VC airflow rate increased. Energy savings were still 
obtained at VC airflow rates 1.5 and 3.0 L/s, whereas VC airflow rate of 5.0 L/s led to 
very small or no energy savings. This applied for operative room temperature setpoint 
of 20- 26 oC. 
 Expanding setpoints for operative room temperature from 20- 26 oC to 20-28 oC led to 
further energy saving potential. The difference in energy use was approximately 300 
kWh for all the cases where CC was applied. The expanded operative temperature led 
to substantial energy savings also in the case with AH when sucking 5.0 L/s from the 
VC. 
 Sucking 1.5 L/s from the VC has very little or no cooling effect on the body parts in 
contact with the VC and the body as a whole. Sucking 3.0 and 5.0 L/s from the VC 
provides cooling of the body parts in contact with the VC and the body as a whole. 
This applied for the three room temperatures 20, 24 and 28 oC without active- or 
passive heating. 
 The VC provided unwanted cooling for left and back thigh, the back and the body as a 
whole when sucking 3.0 and 5.0 L/s at room temperatures of 20 and 24 oC. The VC 
provided cooling of all the body parts in contact with the VC and the body as a whole 
at room temperature of 28 oC primarily when sucking 3.0 and 5.0 L/s. This took all 
body parts in contact with the VC and the body as a whole closer to a state of thermal 
comfort. When sucking 5.0 L/s from the VC, the back region was considerably close to 
a state of thermal comfort. 
==PAGE:128==
127     CONCLUSION 
 
 Passive heating had no clear effect on the cooling effect of the VC or the thermal 
comfort of the body parts in contact with the VC or the body as a whole at room 
temperatures of 20 and 24 oC. Hence, passive heating was not sufficient to provide 
thermal comfort in cold environments. 
 Active heating has a significant impact on the thermal balance of the mannequin body, 
which can provide thermal comfort in cold environments. Calculations showed that an 
active heating power between 1.6 and 17.2 W was sufficient to compensate for the 
cooling from the VC and to obtain thermal comfort of the body parts in contact with 
the VC. 
 Further work may comprise full scale mannequin or human subject experiments to 
reveal the effect of combining the VC and radiant cooling to obtain thermal comfort in 
warm environments.
