Abstract
This thesis evaluates the in-door and underground performance of four wireless technologies—ZigBee,
LoRaWAN, SigFox and NB-IOT. The last three are from low-power wide area network (LP-
WAN) domain. The technologies were evaluated based on six criteria—Link quality, Through-
put, Battery life, Security, Ease of integration and Cost. The thesis starts with introduction,
requirements for the tests, delimitations, limitations, and methodology of the conducted tests.
For each technology there is a section introducing it, a speciﬁcations section which introduces
the reader with the most important aspects of it, devices and test procedure, test results and
related work. The thesis concludes with overall discussion in which all of the technologies are
compared; conclusion and recommendations; and future work. The main goal behind the the-
sis is to introduce the reader with the current wireless LP-WAN technologies, examine their
in-building performance and compare it based on multiple criteria above-mentioned.

Introduction
The world of IoT (Internet of Things) is expanding rapidly nowadays. IoT devices can be seen
everywhere around daily life—smart washing machines and white appliances, smart home au-
tomation even smart cities where waste bins can monitor their remaining volume, et cetera. By
2022 there will be between 25 and 30 billion connected devices, according to the 2017 Ericsson
Mobility Report [1]. Seventeen billion of these devices will be part of the IoT domain. They will
be in all aspects of industries—manufacturing, high tech production, telecom, entertainment,
health-care, education, security, et cetera. All of these devices will need a communication tech-
nology to be used in order to interact with each other and exchange information, apart from
their connection to the Internet.
Currently multiple wireless communication technologies exist—Wi-Fi, BLE, cellular M2M (LTE-
MTC), ZigBee et cetera. LPWAN’s are used where all other technologies are not suitable whether
because of costly devices, inadequate coverage or power utilization. Low power wide area net-
works are an excellent choice for connecting multiple devices which need long range, low power
and low bandwidth communication. These are the cases of temperature measurements inside
a building or parking sensors, where data is sent every now and then. Combining low costs
for installation and operation, long battery life and long range communication backed up by
robust modulation makes LPWAN technologies a great solution for the speciﬁc niche—Internet
of Things.
This thesis focuses on performance analysis of four wireless communication technologies—ZigBee,
LoRaWAN, SigFox and NB-IOT, where the last three are from the LPWAN domain. The per-
formance analysis is aimed at a speciﬁc need—the in-building communications. This type of
information exchange hides multiple pitfalls—wave reﬂections, wave propagation, speed and
power limitations, et cetera. The thesis discusses the speciﬁcation and architecture for each of
these technologies, giving the reader the most valuable information before proceeding to the
tests. In doing so, comparison between technology speciﬁcation and real life tests could be pro-
duced. Continuing with the tests, each technology is tested in multiple domains—wireless signal
quality, data throughput, battery life, security, ease of integration, and cost estimation. Some
of these tests are based on simulations; battery life and cost estimation are calculated. Others,
such as ease of integration, are theory-based. Also papers of similar tests and possible drawbacks
are reviewed and compared to the results.
The thesis concludes with overall discussion where all of the four technologies are compared
with their respective positives and negatives in the context of the requirements. It is followed
by conclusion and recommendations and ﬁnished with future work.
2
Requirements
The main requirement is to evaluate wireless communication technologies including low-power
ones for a smart energy management system used in multi-family buildings. This system should
use multiple sensors, collect data from them, and store it in a cloud solution for easier man-
agement. Implement communication techniques such as ZigBee, LoRaWAN, Narrow-band IOT,
Bluetooth Low Energy, SigFox, et cetera, sensor nodes are to be battery operated.

3
Delimitations
In order to narrow down the vast list of possible wireless communication technologies, the thesis
focuses on four main cases:
• ZigBee
• LoRaWAN
• SigFox
• NB-IOT
Details in regards of operation, functioning, et cetera—which are not related with the
tests—are omitted. Security is not evaluated but given a glimpse over the positive and neg-
ative sides in general. This delimitation is important in the last NB-IOT section, where only a
general description of the mobile network is given. Details in regards to the diﬀerent parts of
the mobile network (MME, backhaul, RAN, et cetera) are omitted. The focus of the thesis is
the performance evaluation of the given technology. Cost estimation parts of each technology is
only for general value comparison. It does not go into details in regards to the costs of design-
ing, production, et cetera. The detailed information for the physical structure of the buildings
where tests took place is sourced from Google Earth and it could be not precise. The calcu-
lations of battery life for LoRaWAN, SigFox and NB-IOT show performance for the modem
parts only without including microcontroller, sensors and additional components’ power con-

6.1
Introduction
Wireless technologies nowadays are evolving with lightning-fast pace. Standards such as Wi-Fi,
Bluetooth, Cellular, Wi-Max etc, are great examples of this process (Fig.6). Most of them are
pursuing faster speeds, adding features to suit the needs of content and multimedia consumption.
However, ZigBee has a diﬀerent goal to achieve. It is aiming at simple, low power and high range
communication for wireless sensor networking and control. It is designed to be used in sensor
networks where a temperature reading needs to be sent, a thermostat or motor controlled, lights
activated, et cetera. Such actions need to be done in a speciﬁed time interval, for example, every
5, 10, or 20 seconds. The XBee modules used in the tests incorporate a microcontroller and con-
nection pins where all diﬀerent sensors/data collecting devices can be connected using Analog
and Digital output. Another great advantage of ZigBee technology against its competition is in
the power consumption domain— it is designed to run years on batteries. It is highly reliable
because of the use of O-QPSK and DSSS with addition with CSMA/CA, mesh networking and
end-to-end acknowledgments. It is important to mention that this is an open standard, which
makes the adoption easier and in addition is very cost eﬀective, due to the low prices of the
modules. Last but not least, it is very secure—AES-128 could be used to encrypt the payload
data.

6.2.2
ZigBee Architecture
The ZigBee network stack has some similarities with the 7 Layer OSI model like the Physical
(PHY), MAC (Link layer) and NWK (Network). However, the rest, APS and ZDO, represent
the OSI’s transport, session, presentation and application layers.
The ﬁrst two layers, Physical and MAC, are deﬁned by IEEE 802.15.4, where the rest—NWK,
APS and ZDO—are deﬁned by the ZigBee standard. Between the diﬀerent layers as shown in
Fig.8, the SAP’s (Service Access Points) clearly can be seen. They act as data and management
channels between the layers while isolating the inner structure of the one above or below. As in
the OSI model, here the upper layer relies on the lower ones.

vice-versa. These bits are then sent

transmission speeds. 802.15.4 phys-

The NWK or Network layer is in charge of mesh networking, route determination for uni-
cast packets and making sure communication is reliable from node to node. It is important to
mention that ZigBee’s Network layer is secured where the payload is encrypted.
The APS or Application Support Sub-layer serves to the end-points (Application Objects)
and ZDO (ZigBee Objects) in order to provide the needed interface to the lower layers. Such
are data transfer requests, conﬁrmations and responses.
The Application Objects (endpoint) are responsible for the input and output to the APS.
ZigBee Device Objects are controlling and managing the application objects, performing device
and service discoveries, et cetera.
6.2.3
Device Types
There are three main device types in the ZigBee deﬁnition: coordinator, router, and end device.
Coordinator
There should be only one coordinator in a network (PAN). It initiates and creates the net-
work using selected channel and PAN ID. One of the responsibilities of the coordinator is to
allow routers and end devices into the network. It assists in routing, for example: when a node
drops, the coordinator utilizes a routing protocol depending on the topology. It should be mains
powered and cannot sleep. This is important for end devices as it buﬀers the data for currently-
sleeping end devices. It can incorporate the Trust center for authentication and encryption to
be used.
Router
Multiple routers could be in a given network; however, they need to be joined to a PAN.
They also allow end devices and other routers to join the network. They cannot sleep and need
a mains power. As in the case of the coordinator, the router could buﬀer data for sleeping nodes.
The two main roles of routers are to expand the network coverage and route packets.
End Device
In order to transmit or receive, the node needs to join PAN. It is not responsible and cannot
allow other devices to access the network. It should transmit through its parent node, can be
battery operated, and is able to enter in low-powered ”sleep” mode.

6.2.5
Reliability
It is assumed that the wireless communication is not so reliable, compared to a wired one. How-
ever, this is not a barrier for the technologies in this domain to exist and improve. The slight
imperfections in focus are based on the physical properties of wireless communication—using the
thin air as the communication medium. Firstly and perhaps most importantly are the obstacles
which could lead to bad or even impossible communication. As these technologies are used in
everyday life, obstacles are not hard to ﬁnd – metal in the building walls, water, concrete, et
cetera. There are multiple scenarios in which some obstacles could be bigger factors than others.
However, this is dependent on diﬀerent properties—design of the antenna, wireless operation
spectrum, power ampliﬁcation, et cetera.
ZigBee is a reliable technology because of its advanced properties ﬁghting the above-mentioned
issues which are both seen in the IEEE 802.15.4 part (PHY and MAC layers) as well in the
ZigBee Alliance speciﬁcation above. This includes Oﬀset-Quadruple Phase Shift Keying (O-
QPSK), DSSS, CSMA-CA, acknowledgments at each hop, mesh networking topology and end-
to-end acknowledgments. It utilizes O-QPSK, where four diﬀerent values of the phase are used
to modulate the signal. When constructing the QPSK symbol by using 2 bits (4 values) at
a time, it could lead to a ”jump” of 180 degrees, which is undesirable due to the large am-
plitude ﬂuctuation of the signal. What the oﬀsetting does is simple—it changes the timing of
the even and odd bits by one bit-period or half symbol; this way, the quadrature components
will never change at the same time. This produces lower amplitude ﬂuctuation, compared to
the non-oﬀset QPSK. Ultimately, this leads to better communication in ”noisy” environments
by lowering the interference. Another interesting technology is the DSSS or Direct Sequence
Spread Spectrum where the spreading of the signal produces wide-band channel which is more
noisy, but allows better resistance to interference. By ”multiplying” the data with a ”noise”
signal, an enhanced signal-to-noise ratio is enhanced. By using Carrier Sense Multiple Access
with Collision Detection, every time there is transmission, the sending device is ”listening” for
ongoing transmission. When the channel is clear then the device is able to send its data. In
18
==PAGE:20==
order to ensure the data is intact while transferred, ZigBee uses FCS - Frame Check Sum of 16
bits. It also includes acknowledgments at two places: the MAC and the APS layers. At the MAC
layer, the acknowledgments are between hops, and at the Application layer are between source
and destination. Last but not least, the mesh networking topology provides ways for dynamic
routing - self-healing.
6.2.6
Security
ZigBee has a ﬂexible security policy - depending on the application it can be absent or full
ﬂedged. This includes options for 128-bit AES encryption, two security keys, trust center as
separate device or coordinator integrated, message integrity and conﬁdentiality, et cetera. Se-
curity in ZigBee can be seen in two diﬀerent layers—in the Network and in the APS. In the
Network , the key used is for device communication, where the second is used to encrypt the
data. The encryption of packets is done using 128-bit AES encryption. Only devices sharing the
same key in the same network are able to communicate between.
Network Layer Security
Network Layer Security is per-hop based. This means that when the packets arrive at a hop,
they are decrypted, authorized, frame-counter incremented, and then encrypted and sent to the
next hop. This security is applied to the route request and reply messages as well, but the goal
is to encrypt the APS layer (Header, Data, Integrity) and application data. All packets in a
secured network are encrypted and authenticated with the same network key using 128-bit AES.
APS Layer Security
As additional layer of security, APS security is optional. Compared with Network Layer
Security, in APS the encryption is applied end-to-end where only the source and destination
nodes are aware of the key. It is not possible to be applied on broadcast transmissions.

Battery Life
Battery life is an essential part of the general performance in communication systems. Such is
the case with the ZigBee end-nodes, which are battery operated. Three important factors form
the battery life: the polling interval, the size of the data to be transmitted and the power level
at the antenna. Depending on the use case, the size of the battery varies. In the reviewed case,
the used battery for the calculation is 1000 mAh. The battery life of an end-node is calculated
based on an oﬃcial calculator provided by Digi international and implemented by Max Stream,
Inc. in the form of a MS Excel spreadsheet. In this tool, the sleep, transmission and idle times
are set, as well as the size of the packet. Figure 20 shows the summarized battery life data for
diﬀerent size of packets as well as polling intervals when 1000 mAh battery is used. It is im-
portant to note that the polling interval plays a crucial role in the overall battery performance
as indicated by the graph. For example, polling data every 30 seconds with 20 bytes of data,
the battery will last for half of year. However, as expected, the battery life increases with the
increase in the period between data polling. Depending on time sensitivity of the measurements
the battery life could be insuﬃcient. Taking, as an example, measurement transmission every 60
seconds, the battery life could last around one year. Looking into the diﬀerence in the payload,
battery life decreases with very small steps. Keeping in mind that a sensor data is around or
below 20 bytes, polling approximately every two hours results in two years of battery life across
the payloads (20-100 bytes). Assuming the end devices are situated at customer premises, the
longer the battery period, the better. This could result in lowering the maintenance costs and
possible related issues. Depending on the scenario and use case, if possible, doubling or tripling
the battery capacity will result in better operating periods.

Related Work
In [8], the authors review the issues related with the co-existence of ZigBee and 802.11 (Wi-Fi)
networks. Their experiments take place in a building, where multiple Wi-Fi networks are de-
ployed. They ﬁnd out that wireless routers or access points located closer than 8 meters from
ZigBee modules will cause signiﬁcant interference. This degrades the ZigBee signal and intro-
duces packet errors. This could also reﬂect negatively on the self-healing mesh network, making
routes unavailable because of ZigBee nodes installed closer to Wi-Fi router or access point.
Authors suggest three main mitigation techniques in order to combat the interference caused
by Wi-Fi networks. The ﬁrst is to execute regular energy detection at each ZigBee router on all
16 channels. This way, each router could choose the most suitable channel on which to commu-

suggest adding packet sequence number and number of retransmissions occurred. Judging by

In [9], the authors investigate the RF behavior of ZigBee devices in a real-life, in-building

Case 2: LoRaWAN
7.1
Introduction
Before continuing with LoRa and LoRaWAN, an important distinction needs to be made be-
tween the two. LoRa is physical (PHY) layer modulation, which is proprietary and owned by
Semtech Corporation, allowing devices incorporating it to communicate over the air. On the
other hand, LoRaWAN is a MAC layer providing networking options running on top of LoRa.
Three distinct classes of devices are described as Class A,B and C—each of them with a diﬀerent
purpose. LoRaWAN is deﬁned by LoRa Alliance where multiple hardware and software vendors
take part. Also LoRaWAN is fully open standard and part of LPWAN’s, where it is one of the
most successive members. It accents on long communication range (2-5 km in urban area, 15
km in suburban areas and up to 45 km in rural areas), low power consumption on such as a
device could last 10 years, and has low data rate between 0.3 to 5.4 kbps (at 125kHz bandwidth)
and up to 27 kbps at higher bandwidths [10]. It uses star-of-stars topology where multiple end-
devices are communicating to a one or multiple gateways. These gateways are connected to the
IP network (IP over Ethernet for instance) and relay information between the end-nodes and
a cloud network location. Communication is focused mainly at the direction of end-devices to
the network server (up-link) with the ability of sending information the other way (down-link).
In Europe it uses 868 mHz unlicensed band providing three 125 kHz channels and three more
when using a pseudo-random hopping mechanism[10]. As a limitation, the time-on-air regula-
tion (duty cycle) of 1 percent for end devices in EU can be pointed out. In the following sections
only European-related speciﬁcations will be discussed.
7.2

7.2.1
LoRa Physical Layer
As mentioned earlier LoRa is in the core of LoRaWAN and is responsible for the modulation

LoRa modulation is both bandwidth and frequency scalable. It can be modiﬁed for operation
in narrow-band frequency hopping and wide-band direct sequence applications. It also oﬀers

Using orthogonal spreading factors enables multiple spreading signals to be transmitted at the
same time on the same channel. Thanks to the ADR (adaptive data rate), the gateway could
regulate the spreading factor and bandwidth dynamically.
7.2.2
LoRaWAN
LoRaWAN is an asynchronous ALOHA-based MAC protocol running on top of the LoRa phys-
ical layer. It enables sensor networks to communicate and exchange packets to a network server
at long-time intervals transmitting a very low volume of data at low speeds. Figure 23 shows

7.2.3
LoRaWAN architecture
Build-up parts of a LoRaWAN network:
• End-nodes - Communicating Using LoRa and LoRaWAN Technologies to the Gateways.
• Gateways - Exchanging LoRaWAN frames between end devices and network server/s.
Usually backhaul communication is Ethernet, Wi-Fi or cellular. There can be multiple
LoRaWAN gateways operating in the same network, so one frame could be received and
dispatched by more than one gateway.
• Network server - Main task of it is decoding the packets sent by end-devices as well security
checks. Also it makes sure duplicate packets do not exist. It generates packets to be sent
to the end devices.
• Application server/s - Each application or application server decodes the security packets
and decides the actions to be taken based on the information.
Figure 24 illustrates a typical LoRaWAN implementation and communication.
Figure 24 - LoRaWAN network architecture

It is important to note that all of the devices need to implement LoRaWAN MAC protocol
in order to communicate successfully. Figure 25 shows an example communication through the
diﬀerent layers between a LoRaWAN sensor node, gateway and Network server. What happens
is as follows: the sensor node encapsulates the application data (readings, et cetera) into Lo-
RaWAN frame. Then it is encoded and sent over the radio interface to the gateway using LoRa
modulation. The gateway receives the data over the radio interface and decodes it. Furthermore
it encapsulates it into IP packet and then into an Ethernet frame (In case Ethernet is used, for
instance) so it is possible to ”travel” over IP networks. When received, decapsulation is con-
ducted until LoRaWAN frame, which in turn is decrypted and data sent to the application logic.
It is possible that the application logic is to be on a separate server or in one with the Network
one. In the case that the Application server is used further, TCP/IP encapsulation/decapsula-
tion is performed. Communication between devices is not possible—gateway to end-device and
vice versa are the only options.

7.2.4
Classes of devices
Three device classes are deﬁned in LoRaWAN, each serving a diﬀerent purpose:
• Class A - Battery-operated devices, mostly sensors. These type of devices are the most
power eﬃcient, as they schedule uplink communication based on their needs (random)
ALOHA style. However downlink communication is possible only in two short downlink
windows after the initial uplink message.
• Class B - They are power eﬃcient on a medium level and have periodic wake-up. Here
the communication is slotted with beacon synchronization. Using the synchronization, the
gateway is aware when the next available communication window is.
• Class C - Always on devices which are least power optimized and not suitable to be battery
operated. At the same time they have the lowest latency in downlink communication. They
use similar algorithm to the sliding window for communication. Are able to continuously
receive and stop listening only when transmitting.
7.2.5
Security in LoRaWAN
All end-devices must be activated in order to join a LoRaWAN network. Two ways of acti-
vation are deﬁned in LoRaWAN OTAA Over The Air Activation and ABP Activation By

Personalization. During activation, the end-device obtains the following information:
• Device address - Unique device identiﬁer with 32 bit size. Seven bits are used for network
identiﬁcation and the remaining 25 for device network address.
• Application identiﬁer - Used to identify the owner of the end device using global Applica-

• Network session key - In order to ensure data integrity, this network key is used by both

Another paper [26] researches the interference on LoRaWAN and SigFox. The tests were
conducted in urban area in the Northern part of Denmark covering 150 square kilometers. The
study compares the two technologies coverage based on two scenarios: with and without inter-
ference from external sources. External and internal measurements were also conducted. They
conclude that under perfect conditions without any interference introduced, the SigFox has deep
indoor coverage of 96 percent compared to 90 percent for LoRaWAN. According to the test re-
sults, SigFox is more sensitive to interference than LoRaWAN. The fact that SigFox sends a
message three times makes the situation worse—the chances of collision are higher. With the
introduction of interference the picture takes diﬀerent shape: LoRaWAN has 78 percent deep in-
door coverage, whereas SigFox shows less than 50 percent. This is partially because of the spread
spectrum techniques. Regarding capacity the authors conclude that under no interference both
SigFox and LoRaWAN have less than 1 percent failure based on the 95 percentile for indoor
and outdoor. However when interference is introduced, the deep indoor performance shows the
negative impact. The 95 percentile failure rate for LoRaWAN is around 50 percent where SigFox
shows worse results with value exceeding 60 percent. When designing the LoRaWAN network,
the interference needs to be measured in advance. Especially indoors, in which the requirements
for the scenario of the thesis fall. Using better antennas, higher spreading factor and placing
multiple gateways are some of the techniques for mitigation of external and inter-network inter-
ference. In addition external phenomena such as fading, attenuation, wave reﬂection, multipath
fading et cetera should be taken in consideration an possibly examined. As mentioned in the
ZigBee section, multipath fading is common issue in the case of in-door communications [4].
Yet another paper [10] titled ”Understanding the Limits of LoRaWAN”, discusses the issues
with the capacity of LoRaWAN. ”The eﬀect of the duty-cycle stated in Section IV jeopardizes the
actual capacity of large-scale deployments. This has been initially addressed by TheThingsNet-
work [15], an interesting global, open, crowd-sourced initiative to create an Internet of Things
data network over LoRaWAN technology. ” [10]. Unfortunately this case could be applied to
all technologies operating in the ETSI regulated ISM bands. This should be taken in consider-
ation when dimensioning a network based on LoRaWAN for example. It reveals the need for
deployment of multiple gateways or ”nano-gateways” in order meet the requirements. The paper
suggests the use of multi-hop communication instead of high SF which impact the time on air
for the price of longer distance.

Case 3: SigFox
8.1
Introduction
SigFox is a proprietary LPWAN wireless technology promising long range communication—at a
very low battery consumption—aimed at the emerging IoT sector. It uses UNB (Ultra Narrow
Band), which provides higher capacity and higher utilization of the communication channel.
Operating in the unlicensed ISM band gives ﬂexibility as well as concerns for interference. It
provides two-way communication service in which a connected node can send and receive data.
SigFox is works in cooperation with telecom operators to provide country wide coverage. As with
the mobile subscription, in SigFox one also must have a subscription in order to have access
to the network. More than 36 countries worldwide provide SigFox coverage [27]. A connected
device communicates with the nearby cell tower where the data is sent to a SigFox partner
back-end. From there the data is accessed by applications and services. The SigFox cloud can
be seen playing two important roles - BSS (Business Support System) for the end clients and
OSS (Operations Support System). SigFox enables IoT devices to send small data at longer
periods of time with low speed but at the price of longer battery life. It claims great reduction
in protocol overhead compared to IPv6 when transferring small amounts of data.
8.2
Speciﬁcations
8.2.1
SigFox Network Architecture
Due to the proprietary nature of the SigFox technology, not all details are revealed in this sec-
tion. SigFox provides two-way communication to devices utilizing integrated SigFox certiﬁed
modem—in most cases sensor nodes. The network transceivers pickup the data and route it to
the SigFox back-end. Later this information is gathered from the applications’ back-end servers
related with the given end-devices. Figure 37 shows the SigFox network architecture.
Figure 37 - SigFox network architecture
The messages are sized as follows: 12 bytes uplink and 8 bytes downlink. Except these size
limitations, in Europe ETSI organization regulates the duty cycle of the ISM band—meaning
1 percent at 868 mHz. On the other hand, this limitation is not of concern because of the
subscription models provided by SigFox. The following list shows the allowed message amount
per 24 hours:
• Platinum 101 to 140 messages + 4 downlink
• Gold 51 to 100 messages + 2 downlink
• Silver 3 to 50 messages + 1 downlink
• One 1 to 2 messages + no downlink
In the best case scenario, using the Platinum subscription, maximum messages sent per hour
will be between 5 and 6. This equates to one message every 10 minutes. Depending on the ap-
44
==PAGE:46==
plication of a given IoT device, this could be enough or not. A gas leakage sensor in a residential
building will not be able to provide adequate alarm in the case of an accident. However a tem-
perature or humidity level sensor used to regulate/report will be able to leverage the technology.
In order to make sure messages are received, SigFox sends each message three times at dif-
ferent sub-band channels. In addition, it uses ultra narrow band channels of 100 Hz. The base
stations are at all times listening over the 192 kHz band; this way the data could be picked up
from more than one base station, reducing the risk of undelivered data. The power of the radio
transceivers is limited to 14 dBm in Europe which roughly corresponds to 25 mW.
Figure 38 - SigFox Network Stack
Figure 38 shows the SigFox network stack layers. It should be added that the network stack
blueprints are given to the chip manufacturers without any royalty fees. At the top is the Ap-
plication layer containing the custom application of the user. This could be sensor or motors
operation and the like. Then follows the Frame layer: this is where the application payload is en-
capsulated into radio frame. A sequence number is added to each frame. Additional information
is added into the next layer - MAC (Medium Access Control) - which is the authentication ﬁeld,
error detecting code (CRC), et cetera. It should be mention that SigFox removed the signaling
in the MAC layer so devices can randomly access the medium. The devices use patented forced
statistical radio medium multiple access scheme. Lastly the PHY (Physical) layer is respon-
sible for modulation of the signal over the air. Two modulations used—DBPSK (Diﬀerential
Phase-Shift Keying) for uplink and 2GFSK (Gaussian frequency-shift keying) for downlink. Also
operational frequencies are managed by the layer. The bit-rate is 100 bps (EU) and 600 bps
(US).
8.2.2
SigFox Transceiver Cycle
Figure 39 shows the default SigFox transmission cycle. In order to make sure that the data
is received correctly, 3 subsequent messages containing the same data at diﬀerent frequencies
are sent out. A message could contain a maximum of 12 bytes payload, taking 2.08 seconds of
airtime. Later in this subsection the diﬀerent payload sizes and corresponding times for trans-
mission are described. There is a one second delay between each transmission. This is done so
the modem is able to change the frequency for the next transmission—Figure 41 visualizes the
frequency hopping. This technique of frequency hopping is implemented with the idea of signal
robustness and it will make it less vulnerable to interference.

8.2.3
Device Classes
In Europe according to regulations of ETSI, the maximum allowed power output is up to 14dBm
or 25mW. SigFox characterizes four classes of devices listed below:
• 0u – 14dB +/- 2dB
• 1u – between 12dBm and 7dBm
• 2u – between 7dBm and 0dBm
• 3u – below 0dBm
In most cases the only diﬀerence is the antenna size. In this way, SigFox is able to predict
the service level for the users.
SigFox was created with the idea of unleashing IoT and Sensor networks providing a reliable
and suﬃcient wireless communication technology. The fact that the radio access network is built
and maintained by an organization could be positive as well negative. It is positive because of
the uniﬁed management, control, security and reliability. However, it could be negative in the
capacity and coverage domains. SigFox has a coverage map of the countries and Denmark is
covered 100 percent, while claiming that capacity is over-provisioned [28]. Also the back-end
servers could present a potential bottleneck in the communication to the application side. While
assuring that there is an end-to-end security, VPN with SSL, et cetera, there is no guarantee
of where exactly the traﬃc passes, nor as how well the sensitive data is handled. Basically—if
a company wants to utilize SigFox, it should rely only on the expertise of SigFox. The closed
ecosystem could be beneﬁcial, however, it hides the above-mentioned risks. One of the strong
points of this technology is the use of ultra-narrow band channels (100 Hz), which help with the
interference mitigation. Combined with the triple data sending, the data transfer is made more
robust. As a negative, there is no acknowledgement by default and if enabled, it is limited by
the subscription models. A possible technique could be acknowledging only the most important
information or multiple messages. Everything is up to the application and developer; however,
some level of ﬂexibility is present. As in the case of LoRaWAN as well in SigFox, the hard
limit of the ISM band in the European Union is determined by ETSI. However this is overruled
by SigFox’s own limitation. In the end the ISM band limitation should not be discarded and
perhaps SigFox will be able to loosen its own limitations in the future.

Case 4: NB-IOT
9.1
Introduction
Narrow Band Internet of Things is also known as LTE category NB1. It is a low-power wide-
area wireless communication technology aimed at the emerging IoT niche. It provides eﬃcient,
reliable and secure connection to the Internet for the IoT devices as it uses the already-built
mobile networks infrastructure. The technology promises very low power consumption and most
importantly, greater signal range and strength, both in-building and underground, compared to
other LPWAN technologies [31]. The adoption by the network operators is easy to implement
and cost eﬃcient. The end-device low-cost components, combined with public standardization,
great battery life, secure communication and eﬃcient integration, make this technology a pos-
sible favorite for a wide spectre of implementations.
NB-IOT has the potential to become the most widespread technology in the IOT niche. This
is because of the global standardization from bodies such as 3GPP. This standardization and
public availability gives the technology strength and ease of integration. This results in peace of
mind to the adopting companies or users. The fact that the network operators need to do only a
software update in order to accommodate the NB-IOT support, plays a big role. This could lead
to a short time needed for the network operators to start providing these services to the users
or companies. This standard could emerge and improve with each new release from 3GPP. The
vast number of devices supported by a signal cell-sector has a positive impact on the adoption
rate among the end users and companies trying to provide services using IoT devices. With
the emerging of IoT devices in all aspects of our life, NB1 could ﬁll the gaps in deployments,
where for example, the conditions are bad. These could be underground basements with sensors
for gas, water, et cetera. With its fast transmission speed and relatevely low latency, alarming
a potentially harmful situation could be done in a timely manner. Of course many diﬀerent
situations could be used as a reference. Such a case is the underground parking lot in Groningen
(NL) where the tests took place. NB1 should not be mistaken for LTE-Category M1 also part
of the 3GPP Release 13 or LTE-Category 1 (Rel. 8).
9.2
Speciﬁcations
NB-IOT was introduced in the 3GPP release 13 as a new type of UE (User Equipment) part
of LTE. It is categorized as cat. NB1. It operates in the licensed radio frequencies and has a
maximum range of around 35 km, with a maximum theoretical speed of 250 Kbps and 180 kHz
bandwidth. It aims to support ultra-low complexity devices used in diﬀerent IoT scenarios. One
of the main claims is the improved indoor coverage compared to the legacy GPRS. It supports
up to 52000 devices within a single cell site. Another positive feature is that the NB-IOT de-
ployment will not disturb any already-deployed technologies. One of the most important factors
is that NB-IOT is purely based on LTE and reuses almost all of the functionalities with opti-
mizations for low-power and low-cost data transfers.
9.2.1
Overview
NB-IOT occupies 180 kHz of spectrum, which is rather small compared to the LTE bandwidths.
Twelve sub-carriers ﬁll up the bandwidth with 15 kHz spacing between. As another option in the
deployment a 3.75 kHz carrier spacing is supported with 48 carriers. The UE could be assigned
with 3, 6, or 12 tones. In cases where 3.75 kHz spacing is used, a single tone could be utilized.
56
==PAGE:58==
In Release 13 only FDD (Frequency Division Duplex) is supported.
One of the main goals of the NB-IOT is the use of ultra-low complexity devices. This includes
narrow-band transmission with a single antenna and half-duplex operation. BPSK or QPSK is
supported as well. In addition, the huge amount of devices’ support is of great importance. The
improved power eﬃciency with technologies used in LTE such as power saving mode, connection
suspend/resume, et cetera allows for overall great battery life.
The reuse of the already-deployed-and-built mobile networks gives the mobile operators,
companies and end users the ﬂexibility of maintaining and using NB-IOT devices. The fact that
only a software update is needed to accommodate the NB1 functionality could play a great
role on the adoption rate by companies and users. The huge number of devices per cell sector
(around 52000) means that there will be enough devices to satisfy the needs of the customers in
the upcoming few years. The MCL (Maximum Coupling Loss) of NB-IOT is rated at 164 dB.
MCL represents the total channel loss including antenna gain, path loss, shadowing, et cetera,
between the UE and the base station (in this case eNodeB).
9.2.2
NB-IOT improvements in 3GPP LTE Release 14
LTE Release 14 adds important features in the NB-IOT domain. Some of them are positioning,
multicast capabilities, new power UE class, capacity and speed improvements.
• Positioning - This feature will add positioning capabilities to the IoT devices. It has
great impact on the mobile devices - asset trackers, ﬁtness bands et cetera.
• Multicast - Sending data to multiple receivers in single transmission will improve IoT
communications in general. For example ﬁrmware upgrades to multiple smart metering
devices. This way energy and resource utilization will be minimal while serving a group
of devices compared to sending individually the same data to each device.
• New low power UE class - The main aim is to achieve smaller form factor with low
power consumption and cheaper to produce. Allowance of up to 14 dBm power output in
order to achieve system on a chip design with integrated power ampliﬁer. This way a power
optimized device will utilize battery capacity better and allow use of smaller batteries and
overall dimensions.
• Multi-carier enchansments - The design target for Release 13 was 6×104 devices/km2
compared to 6 × 106 devices/km2 in Release 14. Another improvement related to paging
and random access in Rel. 14 is the ability of IoT devices to monitor paging and perform
random access on some carriers.

9.4.4
Security
NB-IOT as per 3GPP release 13 is based on the LTE technology. It inherits all of the security
measures found in LTE. This includes the public key cryptography and the X.509 certiﬁcates.
The PKI (Public Key Infrastructure) combines the certiﬁcate usage with the procedures among
the devices in the network. The CA (Certiﬁcate Authority) plays a key role, which makes sure
the user identity and public key are genuine by signing it. IPsec tunneling is used in the part
where the traﬃc ﬂows in the backhaul network.
62
==PAGE:64==
In some cases the protection mechanisms can be more general, so multiple protocols are
protected by a single framework. Such is the case with IPsec, which assists in securing the
unprotected IP traﬃc. As IPsec lies at the Network Layer (IP), it can provide protection to
any higher level application or protocol without additional methods, which results in a great
advantage. In the standard covered by 3GPP, IPsec is chosen as the security mechanism for the
traﬃc between eNB and core network (3GPP 33.210). IPsec can be used in diﬀerent scenarios
for various traﬃc and can be implemented on the edge of the network or directly incorporated
into the network elements. It can also be combined with TLS (Transport Layer Security) for
securing the management traﬃc as additional step. In this way, the management traﬃc secured
with TLS can be transported over IPsec tunnel, giving it stronger security.
In the end the NB-IOT end devices are secured edge-to-edge. This is because of the heavily
integrated security on multiple levels in LTE. As in LTE, all connections are IP-based, which
has its positives and negatives. The latter being related with the security: now virtually all
possible attacks in IP networks are possible in the mobile as well. This should raise the aware-
ness and security concerns in the face of the mobile operators and standardization bodies. New
mechanisms for protection should be considered for implementation.
Layer 2 security in NB1 provides authentication between UE and the core network. NAS
(Non-Access Stratum) and AS (Access Stratum) are secured by encryption and integrity pro-
tection. The data plane between the UE and radio network is encrypted as well.
9.4.5
Ease of Integration
The SARA-N2 modems from U-blox are with very slim and have a small footprint 16 x 26 x
2.3 mm. This allows for the opportunity to make small devices as an end-product. Operating
temperature range is between -40 to +80 degrees Celsius.
The low cost and ultra-low complexity of end devices is another great example of how NB-
IOT is satisfying the IoT domain needs. Single antenna operation without any limitations for
frequency utilization as in LoRaWAN or Sigfox. On the other hand it depends on the mobile
operators to provide pricing and subscription models. This could have a negative impact and
make users and companies turn to other alternatives. Even though the only action by the
operators is a software update, at the time of the writing, only a few countries have fully-
functional NB-IOT networks. The future of NB-IOT lies in the hands of the mobile operators
and their technology strategies and targeted market segments.

Related Work
In [34], the authors review the Release 13 by 3GPP in relation to the NB-IOT. Also performance
test for coverage, capacity, battery life and report latency were carried out. They concluded that
the advertised 164 dB MCL can be achieved. The measured speeds at this rate are as follows:
0.40 Kbps down-link and 0.27 Kbps uplink. Based on a series of simulation, the authors found
that 250 thousand devices could be supported per cell-site sector per carrier. This measurement

==PAGE:65==
is related to capacity only. For battery life the authors use 5Wh battery with MCL 164 dB (max-
imum coverage level) and goal of ten years. They found that with daily report of 200 bytes the
device will last 10.5 years and with daily report of 50 bytes battery life will yield around 16 years.
In an extract from the Ericsson mobility report for 2017 named ”Massive IOT Coverage in
the City” [35] a simulation for outdoor and indoor performance of NB-IOT is described. The
simulation takes realistic large-scale IOT scenario evaluating the performance of in-door and
deep in-door located devices. The simulation took in consideration the two possible deployment
bands of 800 MHz and 2.6 GHz. Close to 1000 buildings per square kilometer with an average of
5 ﬂoors were considered in the simulation. Uniformly distributed IoT devices were stated to be
20 thousand per square kilometer. For 800 MHz NB-IOT devices have 100 percent reachability
for indoors case such as apartments and partially underground buildings. Fully underground,
such as a basement, yields a 99 percent. At 2.6 GHz the situation is similar, however with
small diﬀerences: 100 hundred percent for an apartment, 98 percent for a partially underground
building, and 92 percent of devices reached fully underground. In comparison with a typical
LTE device (UE) at 800 Mhz, the underground performance showed 77 percent of the devices
to be reachable. At 2.6 GHz the same UE showed only 32 percent of devices to be reachable
underground. The great results of the NB-IOT in the 800 MHz band are based on the reduced
data rate in order to provide better coverage. The report concludes that the targets were met
and even exceeded. Based on this massive number of NB-IOT, devices could be deployed in
cities with up to 99 percent coverage using the cellular networks.
In [36], the authors compare diﬀerent LPWAN technologies based on indoor/outdoor capac-
ity and coverage. The compared technologies are LoRaWAN, SigFox, GPRS, NB-IOT. They
conclude that NB-IOT has the best performance overall compared to the other technologies at
the price of the longest time on air. ”NB-IoT outperforms the other technologies, having an 95
%-tile uplink failure probability of less than 4 % even for ten devices. The reasons include the
best coverage and the use of link adaptation, while a drawback is the longest time on air.” [36]
This shows the potential of the NB1 technology for use in IoT niche.

Overall discussion
The four technologies reviewed in the thesis show how fast the progress is within the IoT wireless
communications. Starting with ZigBee as a not so modern technology which provides wireless
connectivity in sensor networks. It operates in short ranges 10-20 meters indoors (advertised up
to 90m [3]) and at a high power cost, compared to the newer technologies such as LoRaWAN,
SigFox, et cetera. It advertises speeds up to 31.5 Kbps; however, the tests show speeds in the
range of 2 to 4 Kbps. Even though the speeds are far from those advertised, they are still suﬃ-
cient for sensor network, having in mind the size of sensors data. The range could be expanded
with the use of router devices, which could be categorized as a positive as well as negative.
Positive on the side where the deployment topology could be customized and re-sized in case
of need. This could be interference, capacity, coverage, et cetera. For example, in the cases of
SigFox and NB-IOT, this is not possible due to the nature of the systems—they are operated
by telecom structures, so no changes are allowed by the user in terms of coverage or capacity.
However, it could be negative as the routers need to be mains-powered. Another important
factor is the battery life, which was examined during the tests. ZigBee shows one of the lowest
battery life performances, along with SigFox. The fact that ZigBee operates on an airtime un-
restricted band makes it perfect for cases where the ISM bands used by other technologies such
as LoRaWAN and SigFox are occupied by noise originating from diﬀerent wireless technologies
such as RFID or medical devices. Possible are use cases with occupied 868 MHz ISM band and
the potentially ”free” 2.4 GHz band. In addition to these, there are no limitations for air-time
and no subscription or access payments. Unfortunately the 2.4 Ghz spectrum from the ISM
band is used by Wi-Fi and as mentioned in the tests, it could result in wireless performance
penalty. Security-wise, ZigBee unfortunately provides not such a strong encryption at the price
of a performance penalty. As mentioned, Last but not least, the possibility of building own
back-end system provides ﬂexibility in terms of privacy and system management.
LoRaWAN is the second technology reviewed in the thesis which stands up above ZigBee
and SigFox in multiple domains: link quality, battery life, topology ﬂexibility, and performance.
This strength over the other two technologies was observed during the performed tests. At the
time of writing LoRaWAN is widely adopted and popular among the users with 42 publicly
announced operators and more than 460 alliance members. On of the main strengths of the
technology is the robust signal and range of spreading factors. This range allows for ﬂexibility
and allows adjusting the technology to the speciﬁc needs of the use case. The balance between
speed and coverage could play a signiﬁcant role in scenarios where an end-node needs to be
positioned further away from the gateway, compared to the other end-nodes. Another possible
scenario is the need of using it in underground or basements. Using a higher spreading factor,
the communication could be made possible. As a negative, the use of a higher spreading factor
results in longer air-time, which limits the messages per hour. The great wireless performance
is because of the modulation techniques used in LoRa/LoRaWAN. Regarding the reviewed pa-
pers in the LoRaWAN discussion section, observations of the performed test show better indoor
radio signal quality compared to SigFox. Battery life is exceptional with up to 21 years with
messages every ten minutes or 11 years every 5 minutes at SF7 and 1000mAh battery. The
most interesting result is the one where a message per hour at SF7 results in more than 116
years with 1000mAh battery. Of course battery shelf life in higher-end batteries is between 10
to 15 years. The ﬂexibility of using self-hosted services is noteworthy. In turn these services will
support the operations of the applications. Cases where the data is sensitive or it is preferred to
make data private are excellent examples. Security is well-deﬁned and maintained in LoRaWAN
making the technology robust against malicious threats. A great feature of LoRaWAN is the
absence of any kind of subscriptions or monthly fees. The only limitation is the ETSI-regulated
65
==PAGE:67==
ISM bands in the EU with its 1 to 10 percent access to the wireless stratum. The result is that
everything combined makes LoRaWAN the potential candidate for satisfying the IoT needs and
speciﬁcally the sensor networks in-doors.
SigFox was designed with the idea of robust signal communications between nodes and ac-
cess points. This is achieved by using UNB (ultra-narrow band) frequencies and repetition when
sending messages. In the LoRaWAN discussion section a paper is reviewed where the SigFox
and LoRaWAN indoor performance is evaluated in an urban area in-doors and outdoors. Even
though the UNB and 3 time sending of data, the performance indoors was far better in the
case of LoRaWAN than SigFox when interference is present in the ISM bands. The low-speed
throughput (300 bps), subscription model of use, and not so great battery life make SigFox just
an acceptable candidate for sensor networks. Via the tests conducted, the battery life showed
in the range of 2 years to 4 months, depending on the messages per hour. One message per
hour yields 2 years of battery life with 1000mAh battery, where compared to LoRaWAN, this
scenario yields between 5.6 to 116 years depending on the spreading factor. Partial role for the
battery life is the fact that SigFox sends three times each frame in order to make sure data was
received by one of the access points. On the positive side, it can be mentioned the uniformity
of the SigFox ecosystem. The operators manage the whole network including from device to
back-end. This gives great overview and control over all planes of the network. This is why se-
curity is so deeply integrated and maintained in SigFox. Also buying a device and subscription
enables the user to start using the service. SigFox could be a potentially great example of rapid
deployments while having no need for external support. This uniformity has a negative side as
well, which can be seen in the lack of topology and adjustment ﬂexibility. This includes capacity
and coverage where the user is solely dependent on the network provider. A simple example is
a low-coverage of a given building—the user is unable to adjust the technology to its needs—to
add another access point or move the already installed one. Of course small adjustments like
adding a better antenna could be a solution, but this is not the case. The closed ecosystem
could be an issue for users or service integrators where conﬁdentiality is top priority.
One of the most anticipated wireless technologies in the IoT domain was NB-IOT. One of
its key features is the public standardization. The model of re-using the already deployed in-
frastructure makes the telecom operators adoption fast, as only a software update is needed.
The NB-IOT case is similar to SigFox in the way that it lacks ﬂexibility and adjustments of
topology, capacity and coverage. NB-IOT requires a SIM-card with subscription for each device,
which need to be connected to the network. However NB-IOT provides greater throughput, as
per design it could reach up to 170 Kbps downlink and 250 Kbps uplink. This makes it great
in cases where further information in IoT needs to be transferred: regular ﬁrmware updates,
complex measurements, et cetera. In the ”Massive IOT coverage in the city” by Ericsson, the
performed simulation shows 99 percent coverage for indoor and basement located devices. The
battery life as calculated (1 message every 10 minutes or 6 messages per hour) varies depending
on the size of the payload. The calculated battery life is between 16 and 83 years. Compared to
SigFox NB-IOT has a lead, where SigFox results in 0.3 years with 6 messages per hour. When
comparing NB-IOT with LoRaWAN, diﬀerences are hard to be judged. This is because of the
spreading factor of LoRaWAN. At SF7 and SF8 LoRaWAN shows better or similar battery life
as NB-IOT. Last but not least when compared to ZigBee, NB-IOT takes the lead as well. ZigBee
shows a battery life of nearly two years when sending data every 600 seconds. Combination of
rapid deployment, fast transfer speeds, great indoor coverage and great battery life could make
NB-IOT attractive to many users and companies. In addition, the service level is monitored
and maintained by the network operator, meaning rapid deployment of user equipment. On
the negative side, the subscription model and lack of ﬂexibility for capacity/coverage could be

mentioned. When set aside, NB-IOT has the greatest potential for becoming the standard for
sensor networks, especially in-doors.

Conclusion and Recommendations
The four technologies in focus of this thesis—ZigBee, LoRaWAN, SigFox, and NB-IOT—were
evaluated indoors and deep underground. This evaluation was based on six main points: link
quality, throughput, battery life, security, ease of integration and cost. All of the carried out tests
were aligned between technologies as much as possible. The tests were conducted in building 343
on DTU’s campus; at the premises of the author (Soendervej 58, 5672, DK) and in Groningen,
The Netherlands. As a result of the tests’ performance, the technologies can be divided into two
main groups: ZigBee and LoRaWAN and as a second group, NB-IOT and SigFox. This division
is based on their topology ﬂexibility—the ﬁrst group can be adapted to virtually any case, where
the second could have diﬃculties. These diﬃculties are mostly related to capacity and coverage
where in the ﬁrst group this could be solved by simply adding additional corresponding nodes
(router nodes or gateway). This should be taken into consideration in the planning of services
and roll-out. The second important feature of the ﬁrst group is the security, especially in the
case of LoRaWAN. The administrator of the system is responsible from edge-to-edge: back-end
to end device. No third party is involved as in the case of the second group. Figure 50 could
be a guide for choosing a technology based on these speciﬁc needs. As a third point, the ﬁrst
group is more cost eﬀective due to the lack of subscription models. Battery-wise, NB-IOT and
LoRaWAN show the strongest performance with NB-IOT being on the top. All of the technolo-
gies are equal in terms of integration. The small size of modems and microcontrollers makes
this possible. In terms of link quality and interference immunity, NB-IOT is the top performer,
followed by LoRaWAN.
The future of connected IoT devices is still unknown, however the technologies are starting
to adapt to the speciﬁc requirements of this niche. ZigBee is relatively old technology which does
not share the low power trend and is almost obsolete for the needs of modern IoT. LoRaWAN is
a strong competitor thanks to its good link quality, modulation, coverage and cheap integration.
Battery life is great but related to the use pattern. SigFox is aiming at fast deployment and
secure connection; however, the small speeds and interference vulnerability makes it a less-
desirable option. In addition the battery life is poor because of the ”three time sending of
data” pattern, and costs are increased due to the subscription plans. Last but not least, NB-
IOT has the potential to become the standard and most common technology, thanks to the
public standardization and continuous improvement. Good link quality and great throughput,
combined with the best performing battery life, could lead to rapid adoption. This of course
depends as well on the telecom operators for support and subscription models.

Future Work
Focus of the future work should be performing more extensive tests. In the near future (sometime
in 2018) the NB-IOT network should be available in Denmark which will give opportunity for
extensive evaluations. This includes indoor and underground locations. Evaluation of purely
physical aspect of wireless communications—wave propagation, reﬂections, fading, et cetera,
could be of interest. Furthermore battery tests on speciﬁc devices for speciﬁc scenarios and
use cases could be carried out. Interference and interference mitigation should be studied and
evaluated for each of the above-mentioned technologies. Additionally security could be tested
and evaluated including performing test attacks and ﬁnding vulnerabilities.
