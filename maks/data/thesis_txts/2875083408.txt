1 Introduction 
This chapter introduces the context, relevance, and scope of the thesis. It raises the question of 
whether and how climate benefits in early-stage Climate Tech startups are translated into 
financial metrics. The chapter defines the research problem, formulates the research questions 
and scope, and presents the thesis's structure. 
1.1 Trend of Climate Tech Startups 
One of the most pressing and complex problem the world faces in the 21st century is climate 
change, primarily caused by human activities (Bandh et al., 2021, p. 24880). At the heart of this 
crisis lies the accelerating concentration of greenhouse gases, particularly carbon dioxide 
(CO₂), which is the result of the combustion of fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial activity. 
Although there are many other threats to the global environment, such as chemical pollution 
and biodiversity loss, the rising levels of CO₂ and the related increase in global temperatures 
require immediate and specific action (Etukudoh et al., 2024, p. 2).  
As awareness of and concern about this climate crisis continue to grow, so does the search for 
effective solutions to mitigate climate change. One promising approach is the development and 
promotion of new technologies, often through startup companies. Since the early 2000s, a 
growing number of such companies have emerged to reduce industrial carbon emissions, 
mitigate environmental risks, and foster societal resilience (Tang, 2023). Those startups 
combining technological innovation with climate impact are referred to as Climate Tech 
startups (Net Zero Insights, 2024).  
The need for climate solutions is also evident in the investment landscape. In the early 2020s, 
a growing number of venture capital funds have targeted green and climate technology 
investments specifically, channelling capital into startups that support the transition to net zero. 
According to Dawe (2022), global Clean Tech investments have grown significantly since 
2018. There are larger deals and a broader reach across different sectors and regions. He also 
notes that venture funding hit a record with $66.3 billion in 2021, marking the peak of the 
Climate Tech trend.  
1.2 Problem Statement 
Although Climate Tech investment reached a historic peak in 2021, momentum has slowed 
significantly since then. Venture capital funding in the sector has declined by 37.6% from that 
peak to 2025, dropping to $37.8 billion (MacDonagh, 2025, Page 1). This reduction in available 
==PAGE:9==
2 
capital poses a serious challenge for Climate Tech startups, many of which are developing 
investment-intensive technologies that require long timelines and substantial upfront funding 
to reach market readiness.  
Furthermore, this sector is now facing even more challenges. Recent policy changes in the 
United States have weakened political and financial support for clean energy solutions. This 
has resulted in delayed investment and significant job losses in climate-related sectors (St. John 
& O’Malley, 2025). In addition, Trump’s rollback of US climate policies and subsidies has sent 
shockwaves around the world, contributing to a decline in investment in climate technology 
and prompting regulatory easing regarding sustainability not only in Southeast Asia, but also 
in parts of the European Union (Tsu-lin, 2025). 
In this context, it is becoming increasingly clear that Climate Tech startups cannot rely solely 
on their impact-oriented missions to secure capital or convince customers. In order to remain 
competitive and resilient, they must demonstrate clear financial viability and market 
competitivness. While their solutions may improve operational efficiency, they also create 
value by reducing emissions, generating both climate and customer benefits. This presents an 
opportunity to articulate and quantify the financial value of climate action. 
By translating climate impact into concrete, monetary terms, startups could strengthen their 
value propositions for investors and customers alike. This leads to the following research 
questions: 
• RQ1: How do Climate Tech startups currently approach the financial evaluation of 
climate action? 
 
• RQ2: How do investors and B2B customers assess the financial relevance of climate 
action in startup evaluation and procurement processes? 
 
• RQ3: What elements should a financial framework include to help Climate Tech 
startups quantify and communicate the financial value of climate action? 
1.3 Scope and Limitations 
This thesis focuses on Climate Tech startups in the pre-seed to seed stage. At this stage, founders 
are actively building their business models, pitching to investors, and refining their value 
propositions. Such ventures typically have limited financial and human resources, are in the 
early stages of interacting with customers, and face high levels of strategic uncertainty (Ries, 
2011, p. 8). The geographic focus is primarily on European Startups, although the insights may 
also be relevant to similar ventures in other regions. 
==PAGE:10==
3 
The research explores how Climate Tech startups in the early stages approach the financial 
evaluation of climate action, particularly in the context of investor communication and 
customer engagement. The empirical foundation consists of qualitative semi-structured 
interviews with startup founders and investors, complemented by a review of relevant academic 
and practitioner-oriented theory. 
It does not examine established corporations, scale-ups beyond the seed stage, or companies 
for which climate impact is incidental rather than core to their mission. Additionally, the B2B 
customers' views were taken by interviewing startup founders, as they were seen as key 
informants. This restricts the depth of insight into procurement-side decision-making, meaning 
that the findings in this thesis reflects founder and investor perspectives. Further validation by 
other stakeholder groups is recommended. 
Finally, this thesis does not seek to quantify the actual climate impact of the startups studied. 
Instead, it focuses on how climate impact is currently represented, or could be represented, in 
financial narratives and valuation logic.

2 Literature Review 
This chapter prepares the ground for the discussion and reviews the literature on early-stage 
entrepreneurship and models that integrate environmental, economic, and social value. It also 
covers how venture capital works, as well as the motivations and challenges faced by B2B 
customers in adopting Climate Tech solutions, the foundation of climate value. 
2.1 Climate Tech Startups  
The fundamentals of startups will be outlined, and the specific characteristics of Climate Tech 
ventures will be highlighted in this chapter. The aim is to provide a clearer understanding of 
startups and their challenges in relation to their business model and climate impact.  
2.1.1 Definition 
To understand the main point of this thesis, it is important to know what sets startups apart from 
other businesses. Startups are a distinct form of entrepreneurial venture that seeks to create a 
scalable business based on a new idea, technology, or product. Unlike traditional companies, 
which usually work within established markets and use tested business models, startups often 
chase innovations that bring something completely new to the market (Freeman & Engel, 2007, 
p. 101). This approach places startups in a position of high uncertainty, particularly in early 
stages when product-market fit, customer acquisition, and financial sustainability have not yet 
been validated (Ries, 2011, p. 8).  
As outlined in the introduction, many startups are developing innovative technologies to 
address the climate crisis. These ventures are commonly referred to as Climate Tech startups. 
Their solutions typically aim to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by offering low- or zero-
carbon alternatives to conventional technologies. They minimise emissions during production 
and use, or capture and store carbon dioxide directly from the atmosphere and therefore add a 
value to the climate (Net Zero Insights, 2024). 
2.1.2 Success Rate and Lifecycle 
Approximately 10 percent of startups fail within their first year. Between the second and fifth 
years, this figure rises to around 70 per cent, with nearly 90 percent of startups not surviving in 
the long term (Kumar, 2025). Even 30 percent of venture-backed startups tend to fail, with poor 
product–market fit cited as the most common reason (Howarth, 2025). This challenge is also

an impact on the climate.

Throughout the lifecycle of a startup, outcomes can differ. Early exits or termination are among 
the possibilities. The process is not always straightforward and can vary significantly depending 
on the sector in which the startup operates or the type of product it develops (Derindere and 
Köseoğlu, 2023, P.10).  
Startups can pursue various financing strategies to support their development. One common 
approach is bootstrapping, where the founders fund the company themselves and sustain it with 
revenue generated from sales, rather than relying on outside funding (Kenton, 2024). Startups 
can also obtain non-equity financing, such as loans from banks or government programs. 
Another path is equity financing, where external investors provide capital in exchange for 
ownership stakes. These investors typically include business angels, venture capital (VC) firms 
or corporate venture capital (CVC) units (compare Chapter 2.2). 
Whether they raise money or bootstrap, startups often receive financial support in the form of 
grants, particularly Climate Tech startups, as they have an impact on climate change. Grants are 
financial contributions that do not require repayment. They come from governments, non-profit 
organizations, or private companies. Unlike loans or equity funding, grants do not require 
repayment or transfer of ownership in the company. This makes them a great choice for startups 
aiming to grow, innovate, or stabilize their operations without incurring debt or losing 
ownership (Jawad, 2024). 
This thesis focuses on startups that have raised or intend to raise external capital. While only a 
minority of new ventures seek funding from external investors in general, this path is 
significantly more common among technology-oriented startups and especially Climate Tech 
startups in all industry sectors (OECD, 2025, p. 18 f.). 
2.1.3 Industry Sectors  
Startups can develop a wide range of solutions, including software and hardware products, as 
well as technically complex innovations that are not yet available on the market. The time it 
takes to develop and bring a product or technology to market varies significantly depending on 
its nature (Nedayvoda, Mockel, & Graf, 2020, p. 1). While software-based startups often grow 
and iterate quickly, hardware or deep-tech companies typically require longer development 
periods and more resources before they can enter the market (Karpenko & Schmitz, 2024, p. 
13). Climate Tech startups can develop a technology in any of this areas, but are usually found 
in the hardware or deep tech sector, where scaling is more complex but can have a significant 
impact on the climate (Tham et al., 2024, p. 7).

While industry characteristics shape the technical and regulatory environment in which Climate 
Tech startups operate, they do not fully explain how climate value is approached or financially 
evaluated. These choices are equally, if not more, shaped by the founders themselves: their 
motivations, underlying assumptions, and strategic priorities. 
2.1.4 Founders 
The founder is one of the most critical factors influencing the success and real-world impact of 
a Climate Tech startup. At the heart of a founder's journey lies their motivation, which is often 
the key driver behind their persistence, strategic decisions and long-term vision. In the context 
of Climate Tech, founders are motivated by a range of different factors that shape how they 
Source: (MacDonagh, 2025, p. 2) and (Cox et al. 2024) 
==PAGE:15==
8 
approach both business and climate action. Kaesehage et al. (2019) have talked to Climate Tech 
founders about their motivation and clustered them into following groups:  
• Climate Opportunists are financially motivated entrepreneurs who view climate 
change primarily as a business opportunity. Incentives, such as feed-in tariffs or market 
trends, influence their decisions. Their main goals are personal success and rapid 
growth. Although their products can help mitigate environmental impacts, they view 
sustainability as a by-product rather than a primary mission (Kaesehage et al., 2019, p. 
9 ff.). This group is unlikely to view climate action as a driver of financial value, since 
impact is not a core concern. 
• Traditional Entrepreneurs are motivated by deep socio-environmental values, often 
rooted in long-standing ties to place or community. They act out of personal conviction 
and long-term responsibility, regardless of short-term market signals. Their focus is on 
societal benefit and systemic change, not personal gain (Kaesehage et al., 2019, p. 11 
ff.). While climate action is central to their motivation, they may not translate this 
impact into financial terms, as profit is not their main focus. 
• Integrative Entrepreneurs seek to combine profit with climate impact, aiming to 
create value for both business and society. They balance short- and long-term goals and 
treat sustainability as part of their operational logic (Kaesehage et al., 2019, p. 12 f.). 
This group seems most likely to view climate action as a strategic asset and integrate it 
into their financial narrative.  
These founder types will be used to categorize interview participants and explore whether their 
motivations influence how they communicate the financial value of climate action to customers 
or investors.  
2.1.5 Challenges 
As mentioned in Chapter 2.1.2, the long-term survival rate of startups is low, with only about 
10 percent managing to last. To be successful, startups must tackle various challenges, such as 
assembling the right team, finding a good product-market fit, and securing funding. Climate 
Tech startups face even more difficulties.  
In this thesis, two significant challenges are identified: first, creating a strong and financially 
viable value proposition that incorporates climate impact is difficult. Secondly, there are the 
technical and methodological issues in measuring the carbon savings that their solutions 
provide. The following sections will explore these two areas in more detail.

2.1.5.1 Buidling Values and Business Models 
A business model describes how a company creates and captures value by defining what it 
offers, who it serves, and how it earns revenue (Kopp, Kindness, & Perez, 2025). For startups 
finding the right problem-solution fit and building a financially viable business model is already 
a major challenge. Designing one that also embeds climate impact adds a further layer of 
complexity.  
One framework for managing early-stage uncertainty to find the right problem-solution fit is 
the Lean Startup approach, created by Steve Blank and Eric Ries (Blank & Eckhardt, 2024, p. 
1). It focuses on fast experimentation, customer feedback, and step-by-step product 
development using minimum viable products (MVPs) (Blank & Eckhardt, 2024, p. 28 ff.). 
However, a software engineer originally developed the framework. As a result, it may not work 
well for hardware-heavy or deep-tech startups, like those in Climate Tech, which often deal 
with longer development cycles and require more capital. Additionally, while the Lean Startup 
model helps with survival and finding market fit, it does not include climate benefits. As a 
result, early-stage ventures using this approach may delay or deprioritize the financial 
evaluation of climate impact. 
In contrast, the Sustainable Business Model Archetypes proposed by Bocken et al. (2014, p. 42 
ff.) offer a set of strategic design patterns for embedding environmental and social sustainability 
into business model innovation. These archetypes include environmentally focused strategies, 
like maximizing material and energy efficiency, using renewable resources, and creating value 
from waste. They also cover socially oriented strategies, such as inclusive governance and 
promoting sufficiency. They are useful because they offer a way to understand how to 
incorporate sustainability into a company's value creation and capture methods. 
However, while the framework is useful for describing and organizing sustainability-focused 
business models, it was originally designed for established companies or innovation units going 
through sustainability changes (Bocken et al. 2014, p. 43). So, this framework is not directly 
useable for early-stage Climate Tech startups, as their business model development focuses on 
surviving and adapting in uncertain markets, rather than matching archetypes. 
Moreover, while the archetypes highlight what types of sustainability value can be proposed, 
created, and captured, they do not offer guidance on how to quantify or communicate this value 
financially (Bocken et al., 2014, p. 48). This is a key issue for Climate Tech startups aiming to 
integrate climate impact into investor pitches and customer-facing value propositions.  
==PAGE:17==
10 
Therefore, while the archetypes provide valuable inspiration and structure for long-term 
sustainable business design, they are not sufficient to guide early-stage ventures trying to 
monetize climate action or integrate it into financial modeling. This points to a broader gap in 
the literature: the need for startup-relevant frameworks that bridge sustainability theory with 
financial practicality. 
2.1.5.2 Qunatifying Carbon Emissions 
Many companies and entrepreneurs use the term 'sustainability' to communicate their climate 
impact, which can often lead to vague interpretations. Sustainability is generally understood to 
mean the integration of economic, environmental and social factors, also known as the 'Triple 
Bottom Line' concept popularised by Elkington (Slaper & Hall, 2011, p. 4). However, this 
multidimensional view is incomplete without a temporal perspective. The Brundtland 
Comissiom (1987, p. 17) defines sustainable development as one ”[…] that meets the needs of 
the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.” 
Together, these perspectives highlight that sustainability is not only about balancing competing 
priorities in the present, but also about ensuring the long-term resilience and fairness of our 
systems across generations. In addition, the United Nations (n.d) has introduced the 17 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to provide a more structured and globally recognized 
understanding of sustainability across social, economic, and environmental domains.  
It is important to align with these three domains. However, there is an urgent need to reduce 
current and future carbon emissions in order to prevent disasters such as rising sea levels, the 
spread of disease and deadly heatwaves (Etukudoh et al., 2024, p. 2). Therefore this thesis will 
focus on SDG Goal 13: Climate Action. As defined by the United Nations (n.d), this goal calls 
on all stakeholders to “take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts”. Rather 
than investigating the societal impacts of climate change, this thesis will focuses on combatting 
climate change, especially Climate Tech startups preventing and reducing CO₂ emissions.  
However, in order to tackle the problem of CO₂ emissions, it is necessary to identify their 
sources. Every firm or person is emitting CO₂ and this can be tracked across three stages. 
• Scope 1: Direct Emissions: These are greenhouse gas emissions that come directly 
from sources owned or controlled by the company. This includes emissions from 
company-owned vehicles, boilers, furnaces, or industrial processes. (Ranganathan et al., 
2005, p. 25). 
• Scope 2: Indirect Emissions from Purchased Energy: Covers emissions from the 
generation of electricity that the company purchases and uses. Although the emissions 
==PAGE:18==
11 
occur at the power plant, they are accounted for by the company that consumes the 
electricity (Ranganathan et al., 2005, p. 25). 
• Scope 3: Other Indirect Emissions: This includes all other indirect emissions resulting 
from the company’s activities, but which are produced by sources not owned or 
controlled by the company. These include emissions from supply chains, product use, 
business travel and waste disposal. This provides a more comprehensive overview of a 
company’s carbon footprint (Ranganathan et al., 2005, p. 25). 
Climate Tech startups can help their customers reduce their carbon footprint, thereby addressing 
all three emission scopes. However, it is important to be able to track emissions in order to 
define whether a change in production or a new, more climate-friendly technology is necessary 
for a company. This is especially difficult for companies, and particularly startups, to measure. 
To monetise their climate impact, a startup needs to be able to demonstrate the potential carbon 
savings of their solution for a customer. Current methods can help identify the amount of 
emissions and their negative impact. In the following a framework is being described, which 
helps to assess a company's emissions or the emissions that a startup solution could help to 
avoid. 
Life Cycle Assessment 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is a standardized framework used to evaluate the environmental 
impacts of a product or service across its entire life cycle, from raw material extraction to 
disposal (Ranundeniya et al., 2025, p. 2). This approach is based on the principles set out in the 
ISO 14040/44 series, which define the methodology and its application. The output of the Life 
Cycle Assessment is the environmental impact of the product, but in the process of developing 
it the carbon emission over its lifetime are also being calculated (Diekel, Bach, & Finkbeiner, 
2023, p. 217; Ranundeniya et al., 2025, p. 2).  
However, more studies using the LCA framework mentioned common limitations and 
challenges. The result is influenced by the chosen functional unit at the outset, the depth and 
quality of the data and the exclusion of smaller processes in the development. (Ranundeniya, et 
al., 2025, p. 15; Akbarian-Saravi, et al. 2025, p. 15; Niu & Fink, 2025, p. 8). This highlights 
that within the internal use of the LCA tool there can be different outcomes, especially if you 
do not have very detailed data. Furthermore, calculating the impact through LCA is very time-
consuming. 
Overall, there is limited literature exploring how early-stage startups with limited data and 
resources can implement or adapt LCA methodologies. While initiatives like the CPP carbon 
==PAGE:19==
12 
footprint framework developed by World Fund1 attempt to create new insights by assessing 
emissions reduction potential of the startups in the future, they still rely on LCA-based 
assumptions and require specialist teams (Diekel, Bach & Finkenbeiner. 2023, p. 213 ff.). This 
highlights a question: how do startups quantify their impact that maintain credibility without 
requiring full-scale LCA implementation. Only with the information on the potential carbon 
savings can a startup assess how much impact it might have, which can be important for 
discussions with investors.  
2.2 Investments in Climate Tech Startups 
Building a company is challenging enough but scaling it up requires an even greater range of 
resources. This is often impossible without substantial financial backing. Therefore, founders 
rely on investors to provide the necessary funding to realise their vision. This is particularly 
true of Climate Tech startups, as they often develop hardware or deep-tech technologies that 
require substantial investment and can only achieve a real global impact if they can scale their 
product effectively (compare Chapter 2.1.3). 
2.2.1 Definition and Kind of Investors 
An investor is a person or organization that puts money into something to make a profit. They 
can invest in various options like stocks, bonds, real estate, or funds. Investors have different 
strategies, risk levels, and timeframes. Some aim for long-term growth, while others look for 
quick profits with higher-risk investments. Generally, they invest either by purchasing shares 
(equity) or by lending money or buying bonds (debt). Their goal is to increase their wealth or 
achieve certain financial goals (Hussain, 2024). 
There are different kinds of investor, but only some of them play an essential role of a startup’s 
lifecycle:  
• Personal Investors: Individuals who invest their own savings in various financial 
instruments like stocks or funds. They are not professional investors and usually invest 
to build personal wealth over time (Hussain, 2024). However, some of them invest in 
early-stage startups if they know the founder personally or professionally and trust them.

• Angel Investors: Individuals, who are wealthy and invest their own money in early-

(Bernstein, Korteweg, & Laws, 2017, p. 209). 
• Venture Capitalists (VCs): Professional investment firms provide funding to startups 
at various stages that have already shown some progress and growth potential. They 
offer both money and strategic support in exchange for equity, aiming to sell their shares 
later for a profit (Hussain, 2024). 
• Corporate Venture Capitalists (CVC): Is a venture arm from established companies, 
who are investing in startups to support either strategic goals or financial returns. These 
investments can help the parent company access new technologies or markets, while 
also offering startups valuable resources such as branding, infrastructure, or distribution 
channels (Chesbrough, 2002). 
Given the financial lens of this thesis, the primary focus is on Venture Capital (VC) investors. 
Compared to Business Angels or personal investors, VCs typically operate with more 
formalized investment processes and dedicated analytical teams, allowing for a deeper 
evaluation of a startup’s financial potential. Unlike Corporate Venture Capital (CVC) funds, 
which often invest for strategic alignment, traditional VC funds are more likely to engage at the 
early stages of startup development and place a stronger emphasis on scalable financial returns. 
As such, they represent a critical stakeholder group for understanding how climate impact is, 
or is not, integrated into early-stage financial assessments. 
2.2.2 Structure of Venture Capital Funds 
The goal of venture capital funds is to generate high returns, typically aiming for a gross 
multiple of around 3× invested capital, over a typical fund lifetime of 10 years (StartupYard, 
n.d.). Venture capital funds are structured around specific investment hypotheses. This indicates 
that each fund identifies a distinct focus or industry within which it seeks to invest, and 
articulates this strategy to its limited partners, who “[…] invests money in exchange for shares 
in a partnership but has restricted voting power on […]”(Kenton, 2022) the fund. With the 
investment thesis they can only invest in specific sectors, like SaaS or Climate Tech. This means 
they have a different view on how to evaluate Climate Tech startups and their impact (Astoor, 
2023).  
==PAGE:21==
14 
In the European investment landscape, the integration of sustainability considerations into 
venture capital funds is increasingly shaped by regulatory frameworks such as the Sustainable 
Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR). Introduced by the European Union, the SFDR aims 
“[…] to strengthen the response to climate change by, inter alia, making finance flows 
consistent with a pathway towards low greenhouse gas emissions and climate-resilient 
development” (European Parliament, 2019, p. 1).  
As part of this effort, Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria have emerged as 
a key tool to help financial market participants assess how non-financial factors may influence 
long-term risk and return (Friede, Busch, & Bassen, 2015, p. 210 f.). Within the SFDR 
framework, ESG factors aim to improve transparency by requiring venture capital funds and 
other financial actors to reveal how they consider and manage sustainability risks (Columba et 
al., 2025, p. 9). While ESG has increased accountability, especially for larger and later-stage 
companies, it also has significant limitations. The metrics used are often qualitative, 
fragmented, and inconsistent across rating systems (Berg, Kölbel, & Rigobon, 2022, p. 1341). 
Therefore not very useable for the early-stage investment.   
Under SFDR, VC funds can be classified into three categories (Columba et al., 2025, p. 2 f.): 
• Article 6 (“brown”) fund does not integrate sustainability into their investment process. 
• Article 8 (“light green”) fund promote environmental or social characteristics. 
• Article 9 (“dark green”) fund have a sustainability-related investment objective. 
Funds classified as Article 8 or 9 are structurally required to assess climate impact factors, when 
screening and selecting investments. As a result, Climate Tech startups that can demonstrate 
measurable contributions to emissions reduction, regulatory alignment, or ESG compliance are 
more likely to align with the mandates of these funds. Throughout the thesis the Venture Capital 
Funds will be clustered into those three segments.  
While regulatory frameworks such as the SFDR increasingly require VCs to consider climate 
impact, the literature provides limited insights into how early-stage investors evaluate climate 
action in practice and if they combine it with the financial evaluation or look at it separately.  
2.2.3 Challenges of Investing into Climate Tech 
At first glance, one might think that investment would mainly go to Climate Tech startups in 
areas with the most potential for reducing emissions. This assumption is based on the idea that 
climate impact is valued as much as financial returns. However, Cox et al. (2024) show a 
notable gap between the industries that produce the most greenhouse gas emissions and those

agriculture, food, and construction contribute greatly to global emissions, venture capital is still 
largely focused on the energy and transportation sectors (see Figure 2). This funding imbalance

While there is no definitive explanation for this investment-emissions mismatch, it merits

Beyond assessing environmental impact, VCs conduct rigorous due diligence to determine 
whether a startup has the potential to scale, compete, and succeed in the long term (Chen,

business model. At the early stage, where limited data is available, founder competence

the central driver for most VC funds remains the potential for a high financial return on

methodologies are more accessible to early-stage investors, they can lead to misjudgement or 
even greenwashing if they are not technically sound enough (Diekel, Bach & Finkenbeiner 
2023, p. 219 f.).  
This lack of standardisation creates a structural barrier for both startups and investors. Startups 
lack a clear idea of how their climate impact will be judged, while investors risk making 
inconsistent or overly positive impact statements. This situation weakens credibility and trust 
in the Climate Tech ecosystem. 
2.3 Framing Climate and Financial Value 
In recent years, the climate impact of companies’ activities has become an increasingly 
important concern for regulators, investors, and customers alike. While quantifying monetary 
value, for example, with traditional financial evaluation methods, remains central to decision-
making for customers (Kirchberger, Wouters, & Anderson, 2020, p. 370), these methods 
typically fail to account for the climate value that many Climate Tech startups generate. 
Therefore, this chapter explores the financial models that exist to communicate value and the 
theories that already assess the idea of combining climate value and financial value.

2.3.1 Standard Financial Metrics Used to Communicate Value  
Every startup wants to offer its customers a solution to a problem - value. For B2B business 
models, this value usually means a measurable benefit, like lower costs, improved operational 
efficiency, or replacing old processes and technologies. According to Kotler & Keller (2015, 
Page 151), customer-perceived value results from the trade-off between total expected benefits 
(including economic, functional and psychological) and the total perceived costs across 
available alternatives. Startups can use financial models to demonstrate and quantify this value 
in economic terms. These models are essential for communicating with customers, investor 
relations, and internal decision-making (Kirchberger, Wouters, & Anderson, 2020, p. 370). 
There are different financial metrics used, like IRR2, Payback Period3, Break Even Point4 and 
ROI (Gallo, 2016). However, the master's thesis emphasizes ROI, due to its simplicity, 
universality and adaptability across Climate Tech business models. 
Return on Investment (ROI) is a financial metric used to evaluate the profitability of an 
investment. It expresses the net gain or loss relative to the initial cost, allowing for a 
straightforward comparison of investment opportunities. ROI is typically presented as a 
percentage and is widely used in both corporate finance and startup decision-making. (Beattie,

2.3.2 Theoretical Foundations of Climate Value 
Over the past twenty years, several theoretical frameworks have developed to tackle the gap 
between financial value and the environmental or social value created by businesses. Two of 
the most influential are Emerson’s Blended Value Theory and Elkington’s Triple Bottom Line 
(TBL).  
Instead of seeing social and environmental returns as less important than financial results, 
Emerson (2003, P. 37 f.) presents the Blended Value theory. He claims that all businesses create 
a combination of economic, social, and environmental value, regardless of whether they 
measure or recognize it. According to this perspective, investment and return should not be seen 
as a trade-off between financial, environmental, and social priorities but rather as components 
of a unified value proposition. 
However, Emerson also acknowledges the practical limitations of this theory. While financial 
performance is easy to quantify, social and environmental value are often excluded, not because 
they lack importance, but because we lack the tools and language to measure them. As he writes: 
“[…]we have not committed ourselves to the creation of new words and numbers […] and as a 
result lack the constructs to adequately describe and track that which we ultimately value” 
(Emerson, 2003, p. 41). In other words, he calls for a new framework that not only reflects the 
complexity of blended value but also makes it communicable and understandable. He questions 
the foundational assumptions of how we currently define and measure value in business. 
The idea of combining environmental, social and economic values into one model is also 
reflected in Elkington’s Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework, which proposes assessing 
==PAGE:26==
19 
business performance across three dimensions: people, planet, and profit. This model aims to 
expand traditional financial metrics by including social and environmental performance as core 
dimensions of success (Slaper & Hall, 2011, p. 1).  
However, as with Emerson’s concept, the main challenge in the TBL framework lies in 
implementation. Slaper & Hall (2011, p. 1). note that the difficulty is not in defining the model, 
but in measuring it. Like Emerson, they identify the lack of a shared unit of measurement across 
the three dimensions as a central barrier. Several solutions have been proposed, such as 
monetizing non-financial impacts or creating new indices, but each comes with trade-offs. 
Monetization raises ethical and methodological questions about how to assign prices to 
emissions or biodiversity, while creating indices face the challenge of determining how to 
weigh different variables against each other (Slaper & Hall 2011, p. 1).  
One notable attempt to operationalize the TBL framework is the Genuine Progress Indicator 
(GPI). The GPI aims to measure sustainable economic welfare by including environmental and 
social factors along with GDP figures (Slaper & Hall 2011, p. 6; Talberth, Cobb, & Slattery, 
2006, p. 2). However, the GPI is designed for macroeconomic or governmental decision-
making, for example used by governments like the State of Maryland (Maryland.gov, 2025) 
and is not suitable for early-stage startups, particularly in the Climate Tech sector.  
Despite these limitations, both frameworks point to a shared insight: environmental and social 
value can, and arguably should, be part of how we define business success. In fact, growing 
empirical evidence suggests that integrating sustainability into core operations can improve 
long-term profitability by reducing resource use, increasing efficiency, or improving customer 
retention (Slaper & Hall 2011, p. 6). 
Building on the ideas of Emerson and Elkington, this thesis explores how especially climate 
value can be translated into practical financial terms that are meaningful within the context of 
startup business models. Emerson’s vision for a complete rethinking of valuation is compelling, 
but startups cannot afford to wait for systemic change. Instead, they must operate within current 
financial paradigms and develop ways to integrate climate-related value into their existing 
business logic. 
A key part of this process involves examining the monetization of climate value, as suggested 
by Slaper & Hall (2011, p. 1). This means looking into how businesses currently price and 
communicate their climate contributions, and how these methods can be changed or improved. 
The next chapter will tackle this issue from the viewpoint of B2B customers: What does 
‘climate value’ mean for them? How can it be framed in financial terms that they can act on 
==PAGE:27==
20 
today? To address this, the chapter will explore the motivations and obstacles B2B customers 
encounter when adopting Climate Tech solutions.  
2.4 B2B Customers of Climate Tech Solutions 
Startups aim to solve a customer's problem by providing a product or service that offers enough 
value to justify the cost. In this thesis, the focus is on business-to-business (B2B) customers, 
which means the startup's customers are other companies (Chen, 2025a). These customers have 
different reasons for purchasing products from Climate Tech startups, or for not doing so. This 
will be elaborated on further in the next chapters.  
2.4.1 Drivers of B2B Climate Tech Adoption as Potential Climate Value 
B2B customers adopt Climate Tech solutions for a range of internal and external reasons. In 
doing so, they often capture value for their own company, while simultaneously contributing to 
broader environmental goals, most notably carbon reduction. This thesis refers to the 
combination of these private and planetary benefits as ‘climate value’. 
This chapter outlines the key drivers behind B2B adoption of Climate Tech and examines 
whether, these drivers can be translated into financial terms. By exploring both motivation and 
monetization potential, this section provides the theoretical foundation for assessing how 
startups might incorporate climate action into their financial models.  
2.4.1.1 Internal Motivation 
One key internal factor is the attitude and commitment of the company’s management team. 
The decision-makers' focus on sustainability greatly affects the choice to adopt a green solution 
(Sajjad, Eweje, & Tappin, 2015, p. 652). This aligns with the Theory of Planned Behaviour, 
which is “[…] a theory designed to predict and explain human behavior in specific contexts” 
(Ajzen, 1991, p. 181). Hinterhuber & Khan (2024, p. 43) emphasizes that individual attitudes 
are a primary driver of behavioural intentions and therefore state that positive attitudes towards 
sustainability are critical in the willingness to pay for Climate Tech products. However, even 
though it greatly affects purchasing decisions for their products, as Gigerenzer & Gaissmaier 
(2011, p. 452 f.) argue, most real-world decisions depend on shortcuts and happen under 
uncertainty. This makes it nearly impossible to consistently measure subjective decision-
making. Thus, it is hard for startups to quantify and present it in terms of climate value. 
==PAGE:28==
21 
2.4.1.2 Operational Efficiency  
While not necessarily rooted in sustainability goals, one of the strongest external drivers for 
adopting Climate Tech solutions is the potential to increase operational efficiency. Green 
technologies often help companies reduce energy consumption, minimize waste, or streamline 
resource use, leading to measurable cost savings, which was also highlighted in the archetypes 
of the sustainable business model (compare Chapter 2.1.5.1) and within the usage of TBL-
Framework from Emerson (compare Chapter 2.3.2). 
Molla & Abareshi (2012, P.100) using IT-focused examples, show that investing in green 
technology can reduce energy use and waste, thereby lowering long-term operational costs. 
This is echoed by Attah, Garba, & Ogunsola, (2023) that with solution for energy efficiency 
companies try to lower their operational costs, and enhance productivity.  
This type of climate value, here referred to as ‘Operational Efficiency’, translates directly into 
financial benefits. In many sectors, especially energy, manufacturing, and circular economy 
applications, the financial gains from efficiency improvements can be quantified and clearly 
communicated to customers (Ali & Evans, 2025; Rosário, Lopes, & Rosário, 2024). 
2.4.1.3 Regulatory Pressure  
Regulatory pressure has been seen as an important external factor for adopting green 
technologies. Hofmann, Theyel, & Wood (2012) say that companies facing tighter 
environmental rules, along with those that are good at following rules and innovating, are more 
likely to put climate-friendly solutions into practice. Similarly, Ervin et al. (2013,p. 403) 
highlight that even when the implementation costs of green technologies are high, regulatory 
pressure can serve as a decisive motivator for action. From this perspective, regulatory 
environments can create a form of climate value by making sustainable alternatives more 
attractive or even necessary. 
However, the challenge for Climate Tech startups lies in making this value tangible. As 
emphasized by Emerson and Elkington, many environmental benefits, remain difficult to 
express using traditional financial metrics (compare Chapter 2.3.2). While regulatory 
compliance may not generate direct revenues or cost savings, it can prevent significant future 
losses, particularly as regulatory, reputational, and physical climate risks intensify (Krueger, 
Sautner, & Starks, 2020, p. 34; Lash & Wellington, 2007, p. 8). 
Within the regulatory risk, there are two different type of risk: 
==PAGE:29==
22 
• Climate transition risk: Financial risks related to policy changes, carbon pricing, or 
technological shifts can devalue carbon-intensive assets or need quick adjustments. This 
is the most common risk for companies. (Monasterolo, 2019, p. 5 f.).  
• Climate liability risk: These are “[…]risks arising from those affected by climate 
change seeking compensation for losses”(Woetzel et al., 2020, p. 1). This risk could 
also turn into a real financial issue in the future. The International Court of Justice 
mentioned that countries might break international law if they do not address climate 
change. This could make them responsible for paying reparations to those impacted 
(Quell & Corder, 2025). 
While frameworks like CLIMAFIN attempt to quantify such risks, especially for financial 
institutions, they remain largely inaccessible to early-stage startups due to their complexity and 
data requirements (Monasterolo, 2019, p. 11). Therefore, the financial quantification of those 
risks may challenge an early-stage Climate Tech startups. 
2.4.1.4 Brand Positioning  
In markets where sustainability is becoming more important, companies that share strong 
environmental values can gain an advantage. This is not just about being more efficient in their 
operations; it's also about improving their brand image (Oberhofer & Fürst, 2013, p. 385; Ervin 
et al., 2013, p. 402). This is especially true in B2B situations, where customers feel increasing 
pressure to reduce carbon emissions in their supply chains. For example Sajjad, Eweje, & 
Tappin (2015, p. 653) highlight that B2B suppliers may adopt green technologies in response 
to client expectations, fearing the loss of contracts if sustainability standards are not met. 
This brand-oriented climate value can manifest in tangible outcomes such as improved sales 
conversion, customer loyalty, or price premiums (Agu et al., 2024, p. 2472). Quantitative 
support for this comes from a Capgemini report, which finds that 79% of consumers are more 
likely to purchase from companies with strong sustainability credentials, and 57% are willing 
to pay a premium (Jacobs et al., 2022, pp. 2 & 20). Kulova & Nikolova-Alexieva (2023, p. 7) 
supports this with data showing that ESG-branded firms can build brand trust, which reduces 
churn and increases customer lifetime value. 
However, for Climate Tech startups, the financial climate value of brand positioning is difficult 
to measure directly, as it is often diffused across customer perception, media visibility and long-
term reputation (Joseph, 2009, p. 2). One potential option could be a green premium, which the 
B2B customer could use to increase their revenue, making it measurable.

2.4.1.5 Carbon Markets  
One of the core promises, and opportunities, of many Climate Tech solutions lies in their 
potential to reduce greenhouse gas emissions compared to conventional technologies. If a 
startup can credibly measure the volume of avoided CO₂ emissions enabled by its product or 
service, this creates a form of climate value for B2B customers. That value becomes more 
tangible in the context of carbon markets, where a defined price per ton of CO₂ offers a 
monetary benchmark for emissions reductions. 
Carbon markets fall into two broad categories: compliance markets and voluntary markets. 
Compliance markets, such as the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)5, are created by 
regulation and mainly affect high-emission sectors such as energy, aviation, and heavy industry. 
Participants need to have allowances that match their emissions, or they will face penalties. 
This regulatory pressure creates a clear financial incentive for companies to adopt low-emission 
technologies, as outlined in section 2.3.1.3 and due to the regulation pressure be categorized 
into that defined motivator (Montel, 2024). 
By contrast, voluntary carbon markets are not driven by legal obligations; they are driven by 
proactive climate commitments. Companies use them to pursue carbon neutrality or net-zero 
targets. They often buy credits from projects like renewable energy, reforestation, or methane 
capture. These markets support goals such as improving brand reputation, meeting stakeholder 
expectations, or preparing for future regulatory changes (Montel, 2024). 
The market price for carbon credits in voluntary markets usually ranges from €25 to €80 per 
ton in recent years (European Commision, 2024, p. 27). This pricing gives Climate Tech 
startups a guide for assigning financial value to their emissions reductions. For B2B customers, 
there are two possible benefits. If they adopt these technologies and achieve carbon negativity, 
they can create an extra source of income by selling credits. Additionally, for companies with 
climate goals, it provides a way to measure the financial value of reducing emissions, even if 
the solution has higher initial costs.  
However, accessing the financial benefits of carbon through credit monetization presents 
challenges. Startups have to deal with verification protocols, registry systems, and additionality

2.4.1.6 Subsidies  
Another important reason for companies to adopt Climate Tech solutions is the strategic 
advantage gained from access to public subsidies. These subsidies are financial incentives given 
by governments to help individuals, businesses, or organizations achieve specific policy goals. 
They can take the form of direct payments, grants, or indirect measures such as tax relief and 
are typically aimed at reducing financial barriers while promoting social welfare and climate 
action (Gordon, 2025). 
In many regions, such financial support is available for the adoption of green technologies. If a 
startup’s solution helps its B2B customer qualify for these subsidies, it can lower the upfront 
investment and significantly improve the return on investment (ROI) (Li, Tong, Ye, & Song, 
2020, p. 4932). For example, the EU Innovation Fund provides non-dilutive grants that cover 
up to 60% of the additional costs for low-carbon industrial projects, such as renewable process 
heat or sustainable agriculture (UN trade & development, 2023). Similarly, Germany’s 
“Bundesförderung für effiziente Gebäude” (BEG) program provides up to 15% in funding for 
energy-efficient retrofits and green technology upgrades (Bundesamt für Wirtschaft und 
Ausfuhrkontrolle, n.d.). 
By quantifying this climate value and actively supporting customers in accessing funding 
opportunities, Climate Tech startups could significantly strengthen their value proposition. This 
not only improves the financial attractiveness of their solution but also creates strategic 
alignment with public climate goals. 
2.4.2 Barriers to B2B Climate Tech Adoption 
Despite increasing regulatory pressure and public awareness, B2B customers still encounter 
several obstacles when adopting Climate Tech solutions. One major challenge is the lack of 
reliable green suppliers, which makes it hard for companies to consistently find 
environmentally friendly materials (Runtuk, Ng, & Ooi, 2024, p. 8). Even when such products 
==PAGE:32==
25 
are available, ensuring quality becomes a critical concern. Many companies are concerned that 
green alternatives may not meet performance standards, which could result in delays, defects 
or increased operational costs (Runtuk, Ng, & Ooi, 2024, p. 9).  
A further complication is the lack of transparency and standardization when it comes to 
verifying green practices. Companies find it difficult to confirm whether suppliers adhere to 
sustainable operations, which increases the risk of greenwashing and complicates supply chain 
shifts (Runtuk, Ng, & Ooi, 2024, p. 9).  
Customers encounter another major issue: the higher cost of buying green products, as noted 
by Runtuk, Ng, & Ooi (2024, p. 7). For customers of Climate Tech startups, one of the most 
frequently cited challenges is the high upfront investment cost associated with green 
technologies, which are often characterised by long return-on-investment (ROI) periods and 
uncertain payback timelines (Bhaskar & Kishore, 2025, p. 21 f.; Selgas-Cors, 2025, p. 22). 
These capital-intensive solutions can be especially difficult to justify internally, particularly for 
SMEs or businesses operating with narrow profit margins. In addition, global market dynamics 
place further strain on adoption. Many companies operate under pressure to maintain short-
term cost competitiveness, and green investments may temporarily increase operational 
expenses or reduce pricing flexibility (Aziz et al., 2023, p. 157; Mehejabin et al., 2024, p. 10).  
As a result, even firms with strong sustainability intentions may postpone or limit their adoption 
of Climate Tech. For startups entering these markets, this presents a dual challenge. They need 
to show technological credibility and measurable climate impact. They also have to clearly 
communicate the financial value of that impact. If startups can present climate action in terms 
of customer ROI, risk reduction, or cost savings, they might overcome resistance and speed up 
adoption.  
2.5 Identified Gaps in Literature 
Despite the growing interest in Climate Tech as both a financial and environmental frontier, 
several important gaps remain in the academic literature. 
First, there is no unified or widely accepted framework for how early-stage Climate Tech 
startups should quantify their potential in preventing carbon emission for their B2B customers. 
The proposed LCA framework in chapter 2.1.5.2 is a working tool, however with the need for 
accurate data might it not be very suitable for early stage startups .  
Second, there is limited empirical research on how startups present their value propositions. 
While they are advised to quantify monetary value (Kirchberger, Wouters, & Anderson, 2020, 
==PAGE:33==
26 
p. 370), guidance on which financial metrics, such as ROI to use when communicating with 
customers or investors is lacking. 
Third, there is limited research on how investors assess the climate-related performance of 
early-stage startups. While institutional investors are increasingly using ESG criteria in their 
due diligence, these evaluations usually happen at the portfolio or corporate level, not at the 
startup level. In venture capital, systematic methods for assessing climate impact in early 
investments are still lacking. A significant exception is the Climate Performance Potential 
(CPP) framework introduced by the World Fund (see Chapter 2.1.5.2), but this remains a unique 
case. Voß et al. (2024, p. 2675) also point out this issue, stating that early-stage funding often 
depends on qualitative assessments because standardized environmental data is not readily 
available. 
Fourth, while ideas like Blended Value and the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) have added to the 
discussion on creating value in multiple ways, there is still a clear lack of practical tools. These 
tools would help translate climate performance into financial metrics that are useful for startups. 
Currently, no widely accepted model exists that allows early-stage ventures to systematically 
incorporate factors such as carbon reduction, risk mitigation, or brand positioning into a simple 
and actionable financial evaluation framework. This gap is further explored in this thesis 
through qualitative interviews with key stakeholders across the Climate Tech ecosystem. 
3 Methodology  
This chapter outlines the methodological approach adopted for this study, which explores how 
and if Climate Tech startups communicate the financial value of climate action and how 
investors and B2B customers understand and value it. The research aims to investigate whether 
climate value should be quantified in monetary terms. 
In this study, a qualitative, exploratory research design based on primary data is applied. This 
approach is well-suited for addressing research questions that have not been widely studied and 
for generating rich, contextual insights directly from participants (Tegan, 2021; Saunders et al., 
2016, p. 168). It aligns with the aim of this thesis: to understand real-world practices and 
perceptions among early-stage Climate Tech actors regarding the evaluation of monetizing 
climate value and how it improves the attractiveness of the Climate Tech startup. 
The following sections describe the research design and process. They include a detailed 
explanation of the data collection method, which mainly involves semi-structured interviews. 
The chapter also covers the sampling logic, the analytical strategy, and the ethical 
==PAGE:34==
27 
considerations guiding the study. The author recognizes a personal connection to the topic from 
his role in co-founding a Climate Tech startup. This experience gave him early insight into the 
difficulties of measuring climate value in the early stages of venture building. While this insider 
perspective helps inform the study’s relevance and practical framing, care has been taken to 
ensure a diverse range of perspectives through interviews with multiple founders and investors 
to avoid the risk of bias. 
3.1 Research Design and Process 
The research design is a qualitative, exploratory approach to investigate how climate action is 
perceived and communicated by actors within the Climate Tech ecosystem. The objective is to 
gather practitioner insights into the potential monetisation of climate value, including how such 
value is understood, measured, and whether it is seen as relevant in communication with B2B 
customers and investors. 
3.1.1 Exploratory Interview-Based Approach 
Given the emerging and practice-oriented nature of the topic, a semi-structured interview 
format was chosen. This approach allowed for guided discussions around key themes such as 
financial metrics, climate impact, and communication practices. It also stayed open to 
unexpected insights and the possibility of uncovering new findings (Bryman, 2016, p. 10; Gioia, 
Corley, & Hamilton, 2012, p. 20). This method fits with an interpretive research perspective. It 
emphasizes that reality is socially constructed and seeks to understand how participants 
interpret their experiences in context (Merriam & Tisdell, 2016, p. 2). This acknowledges that 
value, especially in the climate field, is often subjectively shaped and context dependent. 
3.1.2 Interview Process 
The research process unfolded in the following stages: 
• Desk Research and Pilot Discussions (March–June 2025): 
A detailed review of academic and industry literature was done to examine how Climate 
Tech startups, investors, and customers deal with climate action. The aim was to 
understand the ecosystem and find current frameworks for linking climate and economic 
value. It also looked for gaps in the literature that needed further research. 
• Interview Rounds (May–August 2025):  
A total of twenty semi-structured interviews were conducted with founders of Climate 
Tech startups and investors from early-stage venture capital funds. The main goal was 
to evaluate the practical importance and challenges of monetising and explaining 
==PAGE:35==
28 
climate value in discussions with investors and customers. Interview questions looked 
at how people currently see climate value, if it is measured or discussed financially, and 
if this adds credibility or makes a startup's value proposition more complicated. 
3.2 Data Collection Methods 
This study is based on semi-structured interviews with Climate Tech startup founders and early-
stage investors. Semi-structured interviews were chosen because they provide both structure 
and flexibility. This approach helps examine planned themes while being open to new insights 
that come up during the conversation (Saunders et al., 2016, p. 391 & 727). 
3.2.1 Participant Selection and Sampling 
The aim was to gather insights from both sides of the early-stage climate tech ecosystem: 
• Startup founders developing or selling climate-impact-driven technologies have a 
good overview of their solutions, customers and value propositions, as well as their 
communication strategies. 
• Venture capital (VC) investors who assess and fund these startups, including those 
working within funds classified under SFDR Articles 6, 8 and 9. They check many value 
propositions and view the startups from a broader perspective, making their insights 
highly valuable. 
Although B2B customers are the main target group for monetised climate value propositions, 
they were not interviewed directly in this study. This decision was based on two considerations. 
Firstly, customer perspectives in Climate Tech vary widely across industries, use cases, and 
company profiles, making it unfeasible to obtain a representative sample within the scope of a 
master's thesis. Interviewing enough B2B clients to account for this diversity would have 
required a much larger qualitative effort, potentially introducing more noise than clarity at this 
exploratory stage. 
Secondly, startup founders were considered reliable proxy respondents, given their continuous 
and direct engagement with B2B customers throughout the sales process. In qualitative 
research, this approach, known as key informant interviewing, is well established. When 
informants have detailed knowledge of customer interactions and decision-making processes, 
their input serves as a valid substitute for direct customer voices (Kumar, Stern, & Anderson, 
1993, p. 2; Heide & Weiss, 1995, p. 35). Founders, particularly those in early-stage ventures, 
are often highly customer-focused and continuously adapt their business models in response to 
feedback and evolving customer needs (Blank & Eckhardt, 2024, p. 28 ff.). Their perspectives 
==PAGE:36==
29 
therefore offer valuable insights into day-to-day issues and broader behavioural patterns across 
different client types. For this study, CEOs were selected as interview partners specifically 
because they typically have direct contact with customers and a solid understanding of market 
dynamics. 
Furthermore, including early-stage investors added a valuable second-order perspective. 
Investors evaluate startup models not only based on current traction but also on the potential 
for long-term market adoption and customer scalability. In doing so, they form hypotheses 
about customer willingness to pay, value perception, and product-market fit across multiple 
B2B segments. As such, they indirectly capture customer expectations at scale and offer a 
complementary, market-level perspective.

3.3 Data Analysis 
This study applied the Gioia Methodology (Gioia, Corley, & Hamilton, 2012), a structured and 
widely recognized approach for conducting qualitative, inductive research. The method is 
particularly suited to investigating emerging or under-theorized phenomena, such as how 
Climate Tech startups and investors perceive and translate climate value, because it enables the 
development of theoretical insights grounded in participants’ own experiences and language. 
The Gioia approach proceeds through three core stages (Gioia et al., 2012, p. 20 f.): 
• First-Order Concepts (informant-centric codes) 
• Second-Order Themes (researcher-centric concepts) 
• Aggregate Dimensions (theoretical constructs) 
First-Order Coding: “Getting Lost in the Data” 
Following Gioia et al. (2012, p. 20) advice to “get lost before you get found,” the process began 
with a detailed review of all 20 interview transcripts. Interview responses from startup founders 
and investors were separated to maintain analytical clarity. Using open coding, verbatim quotes 
that reflected concrete experiences, challenges, and opinions related to financial logic, climate 
value, investors perspective, and customer engagement were extracted. 
During the open coding phase, over 50 meaningful quotes were extracted from the interview 
transcripts (compare Appendix 6). As many of these displayed thematic overlap, they were 
grouped into initial first-order concepts based on common language and shared underlying 
meaning. In order to maintain empirical grounding, theoretical interpretation was kept to a 
minimum at this stage, ensuring that the voices of the participants remained central. All quotes 
and codes were compiled in a structured spreadsheet to enable systematic comparison across 
cases and support the identification of emerging patterns. 
Second-Order Themes: From Description to Explanation 
Once a critical mass of first-order categories was established, the analysis progressed toward 
abstraction. We began clustering the codes into broader conceptual themes by asking: 
• What core idea underlies each group of first oder concepts? 
• Are there patterns across different stakeholders? 
• What are the participants really trying to express? 
==PAGE:42==
35 
This interpretive step involved several iterations of refining and collapsing codes, resulting in 
a set of second-order themes that reflected deeper explanatory concepts. 
Aggregate Dimensions: Structuring Insight 
In the final stage of the analysis, we grouped second-order themes into higher-level aggregate 
dimensions. These dimensions form the theoretical framework for the findings chapter. These 
dimensions do not correspond directly to the research questions but instead reflect broader 
patterns that emerged from the data through an inductive process. 
This layered coding approach allowed the findings chapter to present a structured narrative 
based on participant perspectives, while also facilitating cross-case comparison. 
3.4 Ethical Consideration 
Ethical integrity was maintained throughout the research process in accordance with the DTU 
Code of Conduct for Good Scientific Practice (DTU, 2014). This code highlights honesty, 
transparency, and accountability at every stage of research, including planning, data collection, 
analysis, and sharing results. These principles informed the approach to informed consent, data 
protection, and participant anonymity in this study. 
Prior to each interview, participants were informed about the purpose of the study, the intended 
use of their data, and their right to withdraw participation at any time without consequence. 
Verbal consent was obtained before each interview began, and explicit permission to record the 
conversation was requested and documented. Recordings were only made when consent was 
clearly granted. 
All interview materials, including recordings, transcripts, and summaries, were stored 
temporarily on secure cloud platforms like Turboscribe.AI and Google Drive for data analysis. 
After finishing the thesis, these files will be permanently deleted. Anonymized transcripts and 
recordings will be kept securely on a local device to protect the data long-term and allow for 
possible future validation of the research. To protect participant privacy, all identifying details, 
such as names, company affiliations, and contextual references, have been removed from the 
thesis and related outputs. Participants could review their anonymized interview summaries and 
request the deletion of their data at any time. In these cases, all related material was permanently 
deleted without exception. Access to full transcripts or audio recordings may be granted for 
research validation purposes, but only with explicit prior consent from the respective 
participant.

4.1 Customer Value Complexity 
This section explores the two dimensions that influence the way Climate Tech startups approach 
the value proposition for customers. Firstly, it explores how startups deal with the perceived 
trade-off between climate impact and price sensitivity in B2B decision-making. It shows how 
value communication is shaped by client expectations. Secondly, it considers how startups 
attempt to estimate and communicate value to customers, often focusing on operational savings 
while struggling to quantify or monetise broader climate benefits. 
Together, these findings illustrate the discrepancy between the missions of climate-oriented 
startups and the financial criteria that drive customer decisions, emphasising the difficulty of 
incorporating climate value into business-to-business purchasing logic. 
4.1.1 Price vs. Climate Action Trade-Offs 
One of the most recurring patterns among startup participants was that B2B customers primarily 
prioritize cost savings over climate benefits. Startups reported that in conversations with 
potential buyers, the discussion almost always centres around financial performance, such as 
operational savings or return on investment, rather than sustainability outcomes.  
“No one cares about sustainability, only whether you save money.” (Trans.) – St. 7  
Several startups pointed out that while climate benefits might be part of the solution, they 
seldom serve as the main reason for engaging customers. Often, the climate impact is seen as a 
secondary benefit and is only discussed if the financial arguments are already strong. 
“Of course, you're interested in the climate, but you're always mostly interested in money. 
[…] I would say leave the sustainability out and you would still talk to them.” – St. 2 
However, exceptions exist. One startup reported that its customer prioritized carbon reduction 
over price, especially in the context of green construction: 
“He said [...] price is not the biggest factor right now, because […] it's the sustainability." – 
St. 4 
This highlights the fact that customer priorities are far from uniform. They vary not only across 
industries and use cases, but also within individual organisations. The founder of preevious 
quote noted that them and their customers prioritised climate impact over financial return, a 
notable exception within the sample. Furthermore, a founder reported that different 
stakeholders within the same client organisation frequently prioritise distinct decision criteria, 
which makes it challenging to communicate value consistently and compellingly: 
==PAGE:47==
40 
“It depends on who you're talking to. I don't discuss risk minimization in customer meetings 
with the production manager – these discussions are strategic and are conducted with C-level 
executives.” (Trans.)  – St. 7 
Yet, even with these outliers, the dominant market dynamic remains financial. Both founders 
and investors expressed scepticism about the existence of a “green premium” or widespread 
willingness to pay more for climate friendly solutions. 
“Right now, there's no green consumer. If the data was different and we actually had a huge 
group of people around the world who would pay green premium prices, a product would 
exist. But that's not the case. That's not the reality. There's no such thing as a green 
consumer.”– Inv. 1 
“Our vision was that companies would be willing to pay a green premium. And now I think 
we've realized that that is not existing in the industry.” – Inv. 10 
Overall, the findings suggest that climate impact rarely provides a compelling reason to buy, 
particularly at an early stage. Financial terms, such as cost savings or ROI, are the important 
drivers. 
4.1.2 Customer-Facing Value Estimation 
Given the strong emphasis B2B customers place on cost, many startups actively try to estimate 
the financial benefits their solutions offer. This often includes calculations around potential 
savings.  
“We’ve tried to put a value on it within [saving] water, energy, culture, medium [and] 
especially time.” – St. 4 
However, founders acknowledge the uncertainty inherent in these calculations, especially in 
dynamic of early-stage markets. 
“We already have a forecast of what prices we can achieve. But there is an extremely high 
uncertainty in the market […].” (Trans.) – St. 9 
“[We have] some kind of [..] estimates and magic numbers. But we need to confirm it with 
who we're collaborating." – St. 5 
When it comes to incorporating climate value into these calculations, most startups hesitate. 
Although climate action is considered important, the challenge lies in translating climate value 
into concrete financial terms, especially for the founders in the early stage (until Seed Stage), 
where many more uncertainties exist in the value proposition. 
==PAGE:48==
41 
“So it is very important for my value proposition. But I think it's hard to put sometimes into 
numbers exactly how it's going to affect the customer.” – St. 3 
Some founders in the later stage (Lean + Early Stage) have even moved away from trying to 
include monetised climate values in customer conversations, arguing that such estimates can 
overcomplicate the discussion or even harm the deal. 
“All these add-ons, I know we did that at the very beginning, but they're just so abstract, and 
then suddenly you're having these crazy discussions, [...] it always ends up being a bit needy 
and annoying.” – St. 7 
In sum, while startups strive to quantify the core financial benefits of their offering, most avoid 
monetising climate value. This is due to technical difficulties, limits in measurement, and the 
worry of damaging their credibility during sales talks. 
4.2 Impact Measurement & Communication 
This section examines the two key factors that influence how Climate Tech startups measure 
and communicate their climate value during early development stages. First, it looks at the 
technical and operational challenges of measuring climate impact, especially carbon savings, 
while dealing with limited resources and early data. Second, it points out the scepticism among 
founders and investors about the credibility and practical value of early climate metrics, 
especially when they are used to make financial claims.  
Overall, these findings show the gap between the significance given to climate impact and the 
existing challenges in tracking and using it as part of a startup's value proposition. 
4.2.1 Measurement Challenges of Climate Impact 
A key requirement for turning climate action into financial value is measuring the actual carbon 
savings. Interviews showed that this quantification is still a big challenge for many Climate 
Tech startups, especially the early-stage ones.  
Several founders mentioned technical limitations that make it hard to estimate the carbon 
footprint reductions of their solutions. This issue is especially noticeable among startups that 
are creating foundational technologies or platforms, where the impact is indirect:  
“It's hard for us to assign a number to like how much carbon emission we save, because we 
are a foundational layer” – St. 2 
“No not yet. I mean to measure the CO2 footprint; it’s something that also needs time.” – St. 
4 
==PAGE:49==
42 
From the investor side, this measurement gap was also highlighted as a barrier to assessing 
climate impact potential. One early-stage investor expressed concern over the superficial or 
inconsistent nature of climate metrics provided by pre-seed startups:  
“Most of the pre-seeds have done only impact assessment, you know where they only go on 
the internet. It's a big issue.” – Inv. 1 
Beyond technical limits, some founders also raised concerns about the credibility and 
objectivity of self-reported carbon metrics. One entrepreneur suggested that carbon savings 
calculations are often adjusted to fit narrative goals. This raises the risk of greenwashing:  
“As soon as you start calculating CO₂, you always tend to adjust the figures to suit your own 
agenda” (Trans.) – St. 7 
In summary, the findings show that most early-stage Climate Tech startups lack the tools, 
data, and resources needed to measure their climate impact effectively. This leads to 
difficulties in communicating verified climate values to customers and investors. 
Furthermore, even when metrics are provided, they may be perceived as unreliable or biased, 
which makes it more challenging to incorporate climate action into financial discussions. 
4.2.2 Skepticism of Climate Value Projections 
One of the consistent themes that came up in the investor interviews was a general doubt about 
including financial projections of climate value in early-stage startup business models. While 
many see climate value as important, most investors stressed that startups need to concentrate 
on creating a solid core business with a clear and dependable revenue model. Attempts to 
quantify the financial benefits of climate impact, such as avoided emissions through carbon 
credits, were generally viewed as too uncertain to be meaningfully integrated into early-stage 
investment decisions.  
“I don't think I would include them in the analysis because they are too uncertain. Firstly, the 
revenue is uncertain, and secondly, these added values are even more uncertain than the 
revenue anyway.” – Inv. 3 
“It's a nice to have in the early stages. The focus should be on the core business, what you 
really want to develop over the next five years. I can perhaps list the other thing. But it's not 
that […] I'm just going to rely on that now. I assume that if you really want to have a startup 
that can scale up significantly, in the long or short term it will have to focus on one revenue 
stream.” (Trans.)  – Inv. 6 
==PAGE:50==
43 
This scepticism was not limited to investors. One founder similarly acknowledged that trying 
to quantify climate-related value can actually backfire. In attempting to be precise, startups risk 
being challenged on the validity of their numbers, particularly because climate metrics are often 
based on assumptions or projections that differ across contexts: 
“You could write a doctoral thesis on it, of course. The problem is that the more precise you 
are, the easier it is to challenge, because in the end, everyone's predispositions are different.” 
(Trans.)  – St. 7 
Overall, the findings point out a conflict between the aim to express climate impact in financial 
terms and the real challenges of building a startup in its early stages. For both investors and 
founders, adding monetised climate value to a startup's value proposition often brings more 
complexity, uncertainty, and risk than it solves, especially when it distracts from creating a 
scalable business model. 
4.3 Market & Revenue Models 
This section looks at two factors that greatly affect how climate value fits into startup revenue 
models. First, it reviews the potential and challenges of the carbon credit market. This market 
could provide a direct way to earn money from carbon savings, but many view it as too 
complicated and underdeveloped to depend on. Second, it examines the wider effects of 
structural and regulatory uncertainty. This includes changing climate policies and public 
subsidy systems, which can both support and limit the growth of business models.  
Together, these insights reveal the struggle between climate-focused goals and actual market 
conditions. They highlight the challenge of creating scalable and strong business models in a 
policy-driven and fast-changing environment. 
4.3.1 Carbon Credit Market Complexity 
Among the various dimensions of climate value, carbon credits stand out as one of the few 
mechanisms that directly link climate impact to financial return. In theory, startups that reduce 
or avoid emissions could monetize these benefits for the customer by generating carbon credits. 
However, the interviews revealed widespread uncertainty, unfamiliarity, and scepticism 
surrounding the carbon credit market, both among founders and investors.  
Several early-stage startups reported that they had not seriously explored carbon credits, largely 
because customers had not expressed interest and the topic felt too distant from core business 
discussions: 
==PAGE:51==
44 
“I haven't looked into the carbon credit market, because it's not something that I've been 
asked about by the customers. Like I try to ask a lot of questions just to get to know them […]. 
But it's not something they have inquired me about or like asked about.” – St. 3 
“We haven't looked into it. I'm actually not that familiar with it." – St. 5 
Even among those founders who had considered carbon credits more deeply, the market was 
seen as unreliable and impractical, especially for early-stage companies without the resources 
to navigate the bureaucratic requirements or wait for long-term returns: 
“The timeline, i.e. when the revenue from carbon credits would come in, is at the earliest 
three years after the project.” (Trans.)  – St. 10 
“But because carbon credits don't generate that much money, it just wasn't attractive.” 
(Trans.) – St. 10 
“So there is demand, but of course trust and transparency are issues, and in this era of 
market regulation, there has naturally been a lot of greenwashing.” (Trans.) – St. 10 
As a result, none of the startups interviewed were actively pursuing carbon credits as a revenue 
stream, even when their technologies clearly saved emissions. 
This cautious approach was echoed by investors, who described the carbon market as too 
volatile, opaque and misunderstood to form part of a viable early-stage business model: 
“Carbon credits are a bit of a difficult market, so we don't have any investments there. The 
market is so crowded and also somewhat difficult. I think it's still a bit too risky for us at the 
moment. To be honest, no one really understands it.” (Trans.) – Inv. 4 
“In general we're very sceptical of carbon credits as a business model. And so I don't think 
we are going to invest in any business that has carbon credits as part of their business 
model.” – Inv. 5 
In summary, carbon credits provide a way to earn money from climate action, but many see 
them as overly complex, uncertain, and disconnected from immediate business needs. For 
startups and investors, the carbon credit market seems appealing in theory, but it is often 
impractical and risky in reality. 
4.3.2 Structural and Regulatory Uncertainty 
In addition, two challenges regarding the monetisation of climate value, the associated 
regulatory and structural uncertainties, were frequently mentioned in the interviews. Both 
startups and investors pointed out that changing policies, especially regarding carbon pricing 
==PAGE:52==
45 
and public subsidies, create a lot of unpredictability in business planning and customer 
behaviour. 
From the founder perspective, several startups noted that their customers struggle to make long-
term strategic decisions due to unclear or evolving regulations. The lack of clarity around future 
carbon taxation, in particular, creates hesitation and anxiety: 
“If carbon tax regulations are introduced, customers would have to pay approximately 35% 
more per tone. It's like a Damascus sword hanging over your head, but you don't know 
exactly when it will fall or how sharp it is. […] Everyone is preparing for it and everyone is a 
little nervous because they know it could happen overnight.” (Trans.) – St. 7 
“And there is also a great deal of uncertainty. Like what will happen with the regulations.” 
(Trans.)  –St. 10 
At the same time, public subsidies were seen as a double-edged sword. On one hand, some 
investors and founders agreed that subsidies can be essential for launching pilot projects and 
supporting early validation, especially when a new technology isn't cost-competitive yet: 
“The product was initially too expensive for the market, but with subsidies, a pilot project 
was able to be launched. This allows the start-up to learn and then scale up.” – Inv. 3 
On the other hand others raised concerns about the long-term implications of relying on public 
funding. For some investors, heavy dependence on subsidies signals an unsustainable business 
model, one that cannot scale independently or compete on its own pricing: 
“[…] You don't have a sustainable business and then it's not interesting for us. If you get 
subsidized by the government, then you are not a sustainable business anymore. Sustainable 
means that it's capable of running on its own, basically. And if you are subsidized by 
governments, it's not a sustainable business in the long run.” – Inv. 10 
In summary, the findings suggest that structural and regulatory uncertainty complicates 
customer decision-making processes and startup go-to-market strategies. Participants noted 
that, although instruments such as subsidies and carbon pricing can accelerate climate action, 
inconsistent implementation often causes problems. Consequently, the importance of balancing 
short-term policy incentives with the objective of developing more resilient, self-sustaining 
business models.

4.4 VC Investment Logics  
This section explores the two main factors that influence how early-stage investors evaluate 
Climate Tech startups. Firstly, it examines the key criteria used to determine investment 
readiness, focusing on traction, team and business model rather than climate impact. Secondly, 
it considers how funds categorised under SFDR Articles 6, 8 and 9 differ in their treatment of 
climate-related performance. It reveals that, while sustainability framing may vary, financial 
return remains the dominant logic across all fund types. 
These findings show the gap between climate impact stories and how investment decisions are 
made. They suggest that impact is often seen as a secondary concern instead of a key part of 
financial checks. 
4.4.1 Criteria for Investment Readiness 
Investors tend to focus on conventional startup evaluation criteria, especially those that reduce 
perceived risk and signal the potential for scalable, competitive ventures, then directly on the 
climate impact.  
Across the interviews, the most consistently cited factor in early-stage assessments was the 
strength of the founding team. Investors emphasized that a capable, committed, and visionary 
team is the foundation upon which all other aspects of a startup are built: 
“In principle, the team is extremely important in the early stages. [...] So I think that's almost 
always the first criterion you look at.” (Trans.) – Inv. 6 
“The focus is definitely on the team and business model at the beginning, on the initial 
analysis.” (Trans.) – Inv. 2 
Unsurprisingly, financial return remains the central concern for most investors. Even those who 
consider themselves “impact-aligned” or focused on sustainability stressed that returns to 
limited partners must be prioritized:  
“From an analysis perspective, nothing changes for us because our main goal is still to 
return the fund from a monetary standpoint.” – Inv. 9 
However, an investor pointed out that responsibility and focus on climate could act as strategic 
filters. They may improve long-term survival and lower reputational risk: 
“Financial return is the top priority, but responsibility and impact are strategic filters. They 
believe responsible companies are more likely to succeed and scale sustainably.” – Inv. 7 
==PAGE:54==
47 
Another exception came from an evergreen fund, where impact was the first reason to check. 
“First and foremost, we'll look at the impact if it doesn't work from an impact perspective, if it 
doesn't help drive us towards the systemic change that we need, we will not go into that 
investment.” – Inv. 5 
In essence, the potential financial return of an investment is paramount for investors. In the 
early stages, they try to mitigate the risks by examining the founding team more closely.  
4.4.2 Investment Logic and Impact Standards 
While impact-driven capital is often seen as a major factor for Climate Tech innovation, the 
interviews showed that investor expectations about impact differ greatly, even within the EU’s 
Article 6, 8, and 9 fund frameworks. Even with official sustainability guidelines, most 
investment choices still depend on traditional financial metrics. 
Funds registered under Article 9 of the EU Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) 
are required to demonstrate measurable environmental impact. For some, this translated into 
clear CO₂ reduction targets that startups must fulfil to be eligible for investment: 
“That is precisely the approach where we say that a certain number of tones of CO2 must be 
saved. Because, of course, we also have to meet certain targets due to our structure as an 
Article 9 fund.” – Inv. 3. 
Other funds were more flexible, accepting startups with limited or indirect impact, as long as 
they did not contribute negatively to climate goals. This highlights a wide spectrum of 
interpretations within the same regulatory category. 
“It's not necessarily pure climate tech. There can also be other areas, but at least it's not 
affecting climate and environment negatively. We're not investing in oil and gas.” – Inv. 10 
Interestingly, none of the interviewed funds described a truly integrated due diligence process 
that balances financial and impact factors in a combined model. Instead, impact and financial 
evaluations were conducted simultaneously, but assessed independently. A failure in either 
dimension was a deal-breaker: 
“The focus is definitely on the team and business model at the beginning, on the initial 
analysis. Only once the hard facts have been established do we go into depth and make a 
direct comparison of the impact.” (Trans.) – Inv. 2 
“We have our impact partners [...]who is primarily responsible to look at the impact model at 
the same time as we do the other due diligence. So it runs simultaneously, [...] because 
==PAGE:55==
48 
otherwise, if you encounter a deal breaker in one of the two areas, then it's a deal breaker for 
the entire investment.” (Trans.) –  Inv. 4 
A key insight was the belief that Climate Tech startups need to reach price parity or have a cost 
advantage, no matter their climate benefits. Investors were sceptical about models that depend 
on “green premiums.” They argued that scalable impact is only achievable if a solution is 
competitive in traditional market terms:  
“But before they can have impact at scale, they really need to get the price right, because 
otherwise, they will not be able to sell their product to customers. […] It doesn't matter if 
you're green. You still need to be more financial attractive than your competitors. […] We 
need impact to scale. Otherwise, it's not an impact case.“– Inv. 1. 
“So if the company is not at price parity with fossil-based solutions, that is a bit of a negative 
sign. In the way that it's difficult for us to see that they'll actually be able to build a 
sustainable business in the long run. Because they need to compete with the market.” – Inv. 
10 
In summary, while Article 9 funds have stricter requirements for demonstrating climate impact, 
the investment logic underlying all fund types is still primarily driven by financial return. 
Climate impact is often considered an additional criterion rather than being fully integrated into 
the financial case. Regardless of their sustainability orientation, most investors still expect price 
parity or a clear cost advantage, regardless of any climate benefits. 
5 Discussion 
The previous chapter presented the empirical findings of this study, which were based on 
interviews with Climate Tech founders and early-stage investors. This chapter shifts the focus 
from reporting to interpretation. Building on the theoretical frameworks outlined in the 
literature review, the findings are discussed in relation to the three guiding research questions. 
The discussion then explores the broader implications for theory and practice. 
5.1 Answering the Research Questions 
5.1.1 RQ1 
How do Climate Tech startups currently approach the financial evaluation of climate action? 
This study found that early-stage Climate Tech startups rarely perform a formal financial 
evaluation of their climate impact. Instead, operational efficiency is usually the only climate 
value incorporated into financial calculations, but it is then considered to be the core value 
==PAGE:56==
49 
proposition. Although climate action is central to the missions of many of these ventures, 
translating that impact into financial terms is uncommon, particularly in the earliest phases of 
development. However, closer examination of the startups’ maturity levels reveals meaningful 
differences in how and why this gap persists. 
Idea and Seed Stage 
As shown in Table 1 of the literature review, startups in the idea and seed stages usually focus 
on finding their target market and creating a minimum viable product (MVP). Founders at this 
stage mainly worry about achieving a problem-solution fit and validating their main value 
proposition. In this context, financial modelling of climate impact is generally considered 
premature. Founders reported that they were still trying to 'see the bigger picture' and that the 
financial quantification of climate action had not yet become a priority.  
A key barrier at this stage is the lack of accessible data and tools for quantifying carbon 
emissions. As discussed in Chapter 2.1.5.2, emissions can occur across multiple scopes (direct, 
indirect and supply chain-related), which makes quantification extremely difficult for many 
early-stage start-ups, particularly those whose solutions create indirect environmental benefits. 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is widely seen as the gold standard for environmental impact 
measurement, but its application requires granular data, time, and expertise as highlighted in 
Chapter 2.1.5.2, which small ventures do not have. These limitations were strongly echoed in 
the interviews, where founders described impact metrics as either inaccessible or too time 
consuming. 
Lean and Early Stage 
Startups in the early and lean stages, such as those from prototype to pre-Series A, generally 
engaged more deeply with customers and refined their value propositions. Although these 
founders thought about including climate impact in their financial models, most decided against 
it. Their reasoning included a mix of market doubts, strategic focus, and risk management. 
Consistent with Lean Startup principles, these ventures prioritized rapid iteration, customer 
feedback, and ROI-focused messaging (compare Chapter 2.1.5.1). Founders reported that 
customers were far more responsive to discussions about cost savings, efficiency, or operational 
ROI than to sustainability metrics. Several described climate value apart from the operational 
efficiency from chapter 2.4.1 as “too abstract,” “hard to quantify,” or “likely to raise more 
questions than it answers.” 
==PAGE:57==
50 
Carbon-related revenue mechanisms, such as voluntary offsets or regulated carbon credits, were 
also considered unattractive. As confirmed by Battocletti, Enriques, & Romano, (2023, p. 8), 
startups reported that long certification timelines, complicated verification processes, and low 
revenue potential were significant barriers. Some interviewees said they had stopped trying to 
obtain carbon credits because the administrative burden was greater than the benefits. 
Overall 
According to Kaesehage et al. (2019), founders can be categorised based on how they 
incorporate climate goals into their business logic. In this study, nine of the ten founders could 
be described as 'Integrative Entrepreneurs' - those who embed climate values personally but 
separate them from economic strategy. Only one founder could be classified as a ‘Traditional 
Entrepreneur’ – St. 4, where the customer’s sustainability values aligned so closely that climate 
impact became the economic logic. While this offers interesting nuance, the sample size is too 
small to draw firm conclusions. 
This tension between mission-driven intent and market-driven pragmatism reflects a core 
dilemma in sustainable entrepreneurship. Bocken et al. (2014, p. 43) suggest that sustainable 
business model innovation can take different forms, such as efficiency, function, or systemic 
solutions, but for early-stage ventures, climate value often remains under-articulated in 
financial terms. The startups in this study largely fit within the “efficiency” or “product 
substitution” archetypes, which can be categorised under the operational efficiency, but without 
a clear mechanism for monetizing their other climate values  
In theory, early-stage Climate Tech startups have an opportunity to integrate the other climate 
values into their financial models apart from operational efficiency. In practice, however, this 
study finds that most do not. Technical limitations, customer resistance, strategic prioritization, 
and immature carbon markets all contribute to a widespread hesitation. For now, climate action 
remains part of the narrative, but not yet part of the numbers, unless the startup’s core product 
revolves around carbon removal or offsetting. 
5.1.2 RQ2 
How do investors and B2B customers assess the financial relevance of climate action in startup 
evaluation and procurement processes? 
The findings reveal that while both investors and B2B customers express interest in 
sustainability, they rarely treat climate action as a financially material component in decision-
==PAGE:58==
51 
making. Instead, climate value is typically considered a non-essential, supported in principle, 
but sidelined in practice unless it directly reinforces cost, risk, or growth logic. 
Investors 
This disconnect reflects the gap between theory and practice in sustainable innovation. For 
example, Blended Value Theory from Emerson suggests that organizations and capital markets 
should aim for value creation that combines financial, social, and environmental returns. 
However, the interviews reveal that most investors and customers still follow two separate 
approaches: profit first and planet second. Climate action is valued only when it fits traditional 
business measures, not when it involves trade-offs (see Chapter 2.3.2). 
Similarly, while the Triple Bottom Line framework from Elkington advocates for 
simultaneously balancing people, planet, and profit, the venture capital logic observed in this 
study tends to decouple environmental impact from financial analysis. Even among Article 9 
funds, climate metrics are applied as gatekeeping filters, not as integrated components of 
valuation. Impact and financial due diligence are conducted in parallel, but a failure in the 
financial case remains the most common deal-breaker (compare Chapter 2.3.2). 
This dynamic is even clearer in early-stage investing. At the seed or pre-Series A level, 
investors focus on the quality of the founder, market size, and scalability, which fits with 
traditional VC thinking. Climate impact projections, particularly when presented as monetary 
values, such as carbon credits or green premiums, were often seen as speculative and 
distracting. This does not align with the statements form the chapter 2.4.1.4, where for example 
Agu et al. (2024, p. 2472) sees that brand-oriented climate value can manifest in tangible 
outcomes such as improved sales conversion, customer loyalty, or price premiums, which will 
be further discussed in the next chapter.  
B2B - Customer 
In parallel, the demand-side behaviour of B2B customers follows a similar pattern. Most 
startups reported that their buyers prioritize price and operational savings. As a result, 
sustainability messaging is often used as a secondary narrative, not as a value driver. This is 
consistent with Bhaskar & Kishore ( 2025, p. 21 f.) &  Selgas-Cors (2025, p. 22), who identify 
high upfront costs and long payback times as barriers to green tech adoption in B2B markets, 
but it contradicts with the motivators and climate values from chapter 2.4.1, which will be 
further discussed in the next chapter.  
==PAGE:59==
52 
However, there are different customer views from the theory and through interviews with 
startups. The Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen, 1991) outlined in chapter 2.4.1.1, provides 
additional insight into why only a few customers actively pursue climate-oriented procurement. 
According to the theory, a main decision driver is individual attitude towards a topic. In some 
cases, when individual buyers (e.g. ESG-aligned managers) have strong pro-climate attitudes, 
sustainability becomes a purchase driver, as found with one interviewee. However, as some 
investors were mentioning this more the exception then a common given and therefore not 
scalable. 
Moreover, founders noted that attempts to introduce detailed climate impact calculations often 
backfired. These narratives invited scepticism, prolonged sales conversations, and introduced 
complexity that customers were unwilling or unable to process.  
Overall 
In both investment and procurement processes, climate action remains a secondary concern 
unless it strengthens traditional financial logic. For investors, ROI and scalability take 
precedence; for customers, price and payback time drive decisions, which is shown with the 
operational efficiency. While frameworks like Blended Value and Triple Bottom Line provide 
useful theoretical bases, their use is still limited in early-stage Climate Tech. This is mainly due 
to market structure, risk aversion, and the lack of strong regulatory or pricing signals. 
5.1.3 RQ3  
What elements should a financial framework include to help Climate Tech startups quantify 
and communicate the financial value of climate action? 
While climate action lies at the heart of most Climate Tech startups’ missions, the findings 
suggest that integrating this impact into a credible financial narrative remains difficult in 
practice. 
As outlined in chapter 2.2.1 an efficient framework to communicate and financial communicate 
the value proposition is the return of investment (ROI). However, the findings showed that in 
the practical world startups use different measurements, like cost saving to communicate their 
value and did not include climate values.  
Chapter 2.3.1 introduced five external drivers of B2B Climate Tech adoption that hold potential 
to generate both customer and climate value. These include: operational efficiency, regulatory 
pressure, brand positioning, carbon markets, and subsidies. In the following, these drivers are 
==PAGE:60==
53 
discussed in relation to the empirical findings. Their potential integration into a practical 
financial framework is explored. 
Operational Efficiency 
As discussed in Chapter 2.4.1.2, one of the primary motivations for adopting Climate Tech 
solutions is the potential to improve operational efficiency and reduce costs. These 
improvements not only create direct financial benefits but also contribute to climate impact, an 
alignment emphasized by the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework and reflected in sustainable 
business model archetypes. For many startups, this is the main way they communicate their 
financial value proposition, typically by highlighting how much money their solution can save 
the customer. These direct cost savings should serve as the core metric around which other 
forms of climate value can be layered. 
Regulatory Pressure  
A second potential value lever is risk mitigation. While it may not bring in immediate revenue 
or directly reduce costs, it can help avoid future financial losses. This is especially important as 
regulatory risks related to climate policy increase (Krueger, Sautner, & Starks, 2020, p. 34; 
Lash & Wellington, 2007, p. 8). In theory, this makes regulatory preparedness a significant 
form of climate value. 
In practice, startups rarely try to quantify these benefits. Several founders observed that their 
customers are becoming more aware of possible carbon taxes or changes in regulations. 
However, these concerns rarely influence purchasing decisions. The lack of clear policy 
frameworks or pricing methods, like binding CO₂ taxes or required carbon disclosure, makes 
these risks too vague to include in financial calculations confidently. Additionally, founders 
mentioned that while top executives may recognize the importance of compliance and long-
term resilience, these factors rarely impact decision-making. As a result, risk mitigation often 
stays an unspoken benefit rather than a key part of the financial argument. 
Nevertheless, if regulatory risks become more concrete or standardized, for example, through 
predictable carbon pricing, startups could integrate risk-adjusted savings into their ROI models. 
For now, the value of regulatory preparedness should be treated as a strategic layer in the 
financial framework: meaningful but not monetised unless the risk can be credibly measured. 
Simplicity in communication is essential to avoid overcomplicating the customer discussion. 
 
 
==PAGE:61==
54 
Brand Positioning  
Contrary to the literature discussed in chapter 2.4.1.4, which suggests that integrating Climate 
Tech solutions can enhance brand equity, increase customer loyalty, or even justify price 
premiums (e.g., Agu et al., 2024; Capgemini, 2022), the empirical findings of this study reveal 
a different picture. Both founders and investors expressed scepticism about the existence of a 
scalable B2B customer base willing to pay a green premium. 
Investors argued that sustainability-driven brand value, does not translate into long-term 
possible increased prices. One investor explicitly stated that startups relying on green premiums 
do not constitute a sustainable business model, particularly if they aim to scale in competitive 
markets. Founders reinforced this view, noting that customer conversations were 
overwhelmingly focused on cost competitiveness rather than environmental differentiation. 
Even if brand positioning does provide marginal value, startups face the challenge of 
quantifying it in a verifiable way. As highlighted by Joseph (2009, p. 1), brand-based value is 
often difficult to express in financial terms. Consequently, while brand positioning may 
contribute to the broader climate value narrative, it should only be included in early-stage 
financial frameworks, if it can simply be quantified and validated. 
Voluntary Carbon Markets 
One climate value that can be directly linked to measurable carbon reduction is the potential to 
monetise impact through voluntary carbon markets (see Chapter 2.4.1.5). While theoretical 
price ranges for carbon credits exist, typically between €25 and €80 per ton (European 
Commision, 2024, p. 27), the findings reveal that early-stage startups often view this market as 
too complex, bureaucratic, and time-consuming to pursue. These concerns align with 
Battocletti, Enriques, & Romano (2023, p. 8), who note the administrative burden and long 
timelines required to access carbon revenue. 
Moreover, investors interviewed in this study shared similar reservations. They described 
carbon credits as unreliable, difficult to verify, and distracting from the core business model, 
especially when pursued as an additional, rather than primary, revenue stream. As a result, while 
the climate value of carbon removal remains theoretically significant, it should be treated as an 
internal metric rather than a financial driver in early-stage projections. Only when carbon 
revenue constitutes a stable and major part of the business model should it be included in 
financial terms. 
 
==PAGE:62==
55 
Subsidies  
Government subsidies for sustainable products can improve the financial case for adoption by 
significantly reducing the upfront investment costs for B2B customers (Li, Tong, Ye, & Song, 
2020, p. 4932). However, none of the interviewed startups said they were actively using 
subsidies in their business model. Additionally, several investors showed doubt about startups 
that rely on public funding. They argued that this dependence can limit growth. One investor 
even said that it is not a sustainable business model if a product can't support itself in the market 
without outside help.  
That said, subsidies were acknowledged as useful in the early stages of startup development, 
particularly for funding pilot projects with customers and validating technical feasibility. As 
such, while this form of climate value can support short-term financial viability, it should be 
treated as a temporary enabler, not as a core component of financial projections in scaling 
scenarios. 
Conclusion 
The outlined elements, which could be included in a financial framework, could help startups 
better communicate their value to B2B customers and investors, which would increase the 
likelihood of adoption and climate impact. To support this, climate-related value should be 
quantified in a way that strengthens the financial proposition for the customer. However, a 
practical financial framework must balance ambition with realism. Instead of pushing for quick 
monetisation of climate value, the framework should help founders present impact as a layered 
value proposition. This proposition should enhance, not replace, the main financial case, like 
operational efficiency.  
Additional value components, such as carbon credits, risk mitigation, brand positioning, and 
subsidies, may be relevant in certain situations. However, they are not universally applicable 
and cannot stand alone. Each startup must identify which value elements are credible and 
important for their specific case. Additionally, the framework should steer clear of unnecessary 
complexity. As some founders mentioned, overly detailed financial models that include 
speculative climate value can raise doubts and disrupt discussions with investors and customers. 
Simplicity, clarity, and context-driven application are essential for making climate value an 
asset, not a liability, in early-stage communication.

5.2 Implication of Theory 
This study makes a valuable contribution to several well-established theoretical frameworks at 
the intersection of climate action, finance, and early-stage entrepreneurship. Although most 
Climate Tech startups are motivated by climate action, the empirical findings suggest a 
significant discrepancy between theoretical ideals and practical implementation. This section 
outlines some of the key theoretical implications.  
5.2.1 Life Cycle Assesment and Climate Performance Potential Method 
One of the core challenges identified in this study is the difficulty that early-stage Climate Tech 
startups face in quantifying their potential carbon savings. As supported by both literature and 
interview findings, most founders lack the time, expertise, and data quality to perform robust 
environmental impact assessments (compare Chapter 2.1.5.2). 
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), as introduced in the literature review, is the established method 
for measuring a product’s environmental footprint across its whole life cycle (ISO 14040/44). 
While LCA is based on science and is commonly used in larger firms, it has essential limits for 
startups. It takes a lot of time, needs specific input data, and can lead to varying results based 
on the methods used (Ranundeniya et al., 2025, p. 15; Akbarian-Saravi et al. 2025, p. 15; Niu 
& Fink 2025, p. 8). Founders shared these concerns. They also pointed out that the risk of 
greenwashing rises when impact numbers are not independently verified or are selectively 
presented. 
Some players in the startup ecosystem have begun adapting the LCA to suit their specific needs. 
One example is the Climate Performance Potential (CPP), a forward-looking tool developed by 
the World Fund to estimate the future carbon reduction potential of Climate Tech solutions. 
However, the CPP is mainly meant for investors and is not easily used by startups. It needs 
technical resources and expert knowledge that most early-stage ventures do not have (Diekel, 
Bach & Finkenbeiner 2023, p. 219 f.). 
This highlights an essential discrepancy between theory and practice: current environmental 
assessment tools, such as LCA, are not adapted to the realities of early-stage startups. There is 
a growing need for simplified frameworks that combine scientific credibility with usability, 
tools that can work with limited data, support early-stage storytelling, and evolve. 
Such frameworks could help startups estimate their climate impact per unit sold, which in turn 
could strengthen customer conversations, improve investor credibility, and enable early 
==PAGE:64==
57 
validation. As ventures grow and gather more reliable data, these preliminary estimates could 
be verified or replaced by full-scale assessments conducted by experts. 
In short, climate measurement tools that were originally designed for mature, data-rich 
environments must be redesigned for use by startups. This would not only lower the barriers 
for impact quantification but also enable a better integration of climate metrics into financial 
narratives, supporting the broader vision of blended value and triple bottom line accounting. 
5.2.2 Integrated Value Theories: Blended Value and Triple Bottom Line 
Both Blended Value Theory (Emerson, 2003) and the Triple Bottom Line (TBL) framework 
(Elkington) advocate a holistic view of organisational performance, in which economic, 
environmental, and social outcomes are not in conflict but are co-generated (see Chapter 2.3.2). 
Climate Tech startups, with their mission-driven orientation and potential for impact, would 
appear to be ideal candidates for realising such integrated value creation. 
However, the findings of this study suggest that, in the context of early-stage startups and 
investments, these integrated value frameworks are not operationalised in practice. Financial 
metrics dominate both investment decision-making and B2B procurement. Climate-related 
value is either treated as a secondary consideration (as seen in Article 9 fund mandates) or as 
an additional narrative that is only considered once financial viability has been proven. 
Startups rarely quantify their climate impact in financial terms, nor do investors or customers 
expect them to. Instead, climate and financial logics tend to be pursued separately, with climate 
value often being acknowledged but not strategically integrated into pricing, valuation, or 
business models. This reveals a gap between the theoretical ideal of integrated value and the 
realities of early-stage market logic, in which uncertainty, risk aversion, and financial pressure 
take precedence over blended or triple-bottom-line considerations. 
This suggests that blended value and TBL may require contextual refinement to remain relevant 
in early venture settings. Future research and theory-building could focus on identifying 
preconditions (e.g. maturity, policy pressure, investor type) that enable the integration of 
climate and financial value. 
5.2.3 Return of Investment (ROI) and the Case for an Augmented Model 
As shown in the previous sections, there is currently no widely accepted framework that helps 
early-stage Climate Tech startups systematically quantify and communicate the combined 
financial and climate value of their solutions.  
==PAGE:65==
58 
While traditional financial metrics like Return on Investment (ROI) are often used to justify 
purchasing decisions, they frequently miss climate-related benefits that are harder to measure 
or monetise.  
The classic ROI formula after Beattie (2025):

This model works well for measuring direct cost savings or profit increases. However, it falls 
short when applied to climate-driven business models, which often generate indirect, long-term, 
or risk-based value that may not be reflected in traditional return calculations. 
Based on the findings from RQ3, a more suitable framework for Climate Tech startups would 
be an Augmented ROI model that incorporates climate-linked value components into the 
financial logic of the business case. However, as revealed in the findings, the inclusion of these 
elements is highly context-dependent and must be handled with caution: 
• Operational Efficiency: Already included in many ROI calculations via cost savings, 
but rarely framed as a form of climate value. Startups may overlook the climate 
relevance of efficiency gains. 
• Risk Mitigation: Viewed by founders and investors as strategically important, but 
difficult to quantify due to policy uncertainty and long time horizons.  
• Brand Positioning: Seen as useful in niche markets or for early adopters, but generally 
not scalable or monetizable in most B2B contexts. Its financial impact remains vague 
and difficult to validate. 
• Carbon Markets: Offer a theoretically strong link between climate action and 
monetary return. In practice, however, they are often dismissed by startups due to 
bureaucratic hurdles, long lead times, and reputational risks. That said, for technologies 
with substantial carbon removal potential, carbon monetisation may become more 
viable in the future.  
• Subsidies: Viewed as a valuable enabler for pilot projects or early-stage validation. 
However, heavy reliance on subsidies is seen as unsustainable and often discouraged by 
investors. 
Taken together, these findings highlight that while traditional ROI metrics fall short, a carefully 
designed Augmented ROI framework could help translate abstract climate benefits into tangible 
business value, if done transparently and contextually. Founders must avoid overstating 
==PAGE:66==
59 
uncertain or marginal values, as these can provoke scepticism and potentially harm deal-making 
outcomes.  
The broader theoretical implication is clear. Conventional financial frameworks need to change 
to reflect blended value and long-term impact, especially in the uncertain environment of early-
stage Climate Tech. An Augmented ROI approach could work next as a communication tool 
for founders, as help for investors and customers make decisions that connect climate impact 
with financial reasoning. 
5.3 Practical Implications 
The findings of this study have several practical implications for key stakeholders in the Climate 
Tech ecosystem, namely startups, investors, and policymakers. While climate impact remains 
central to the mission of many Climate Tech ventures, the translation of that impact into 
financial logic is still limited and inconsistent. These insights can inform how stakeholders 
adjust their strategies, tools, and support mechanisms to align climate and economic goals more 
effectively in early-stage contexts. 
5.3.1 Climate Tech Startups 
One of the clearest takeaways from this research is that early-stage Climate Tech startups face 
significant barriers when attempting to measure their impact and put it into viable financial 
terms. However, these barriers also present an opportunity: rather than viewing climate 
monetisation solely as a financial task, startups should begin using it as a tool for deeper 
customer insight. 
The reasons customers choose Climate Tech solutions provide a clear way to look at various 
types of value, like reducing risk or improving brand image, even if these benefits don’t 
translate directly into profit. This shift helps move from just calculating profit to grasping how 
climate action fits with customer thinking. For example, exploring how a product reduces long-
term exposure to CO₂ taxation or enhances ESG positioning may not lead to a direct price 
premium today, but can reveal the strategic motivations behind customer interest. 
To implement this approach effectively, startups should use the climate value elements as a 
climate-informed customer discovery tool. Applying it requires engaging more deeply with 
customer use cases, internal decision dynamics, such as procurement and C-level decisions, and 
the external policy environment. This not only improves the founder’s ability to tell a 
compelling climate story but also helps in designing resilient, value-driven business models 
that can change as climate regulations and carbon pricing evolve. 
==PAGE:67==
60 
Importantly, startups should not feel pressured to force climate value into early-stage financial 
models. Instead, they should be encouraged to track and document climate value separately, 
building internal literacy, readiness, and optionality for future fundraising or B2B discussions 
where climate performance may become more central. 
5.3.2 Investors 
This research highlights the need for a more balanced and connected way to assess early-stage 
Climate Tech startups for venture capital investors. This is especially important for those in or 
closely tied to the sustainability sector. While making a financial return is still the main goal, 
for traditional funds focused on exits, the findings show that impact is often viewed separately 
instead of being seen as an important part of investment choices. 
To address this, impact investors (including those in Article 8 and 9 funds) could benefit from 
developing lightweight, integrated screening tools that treat climate impact as a core 
consideration rather than a secondary filter. Instead of conducting impact and financial due 
diligence separately, funds could consider simple models that link climate value to long-term 
market differentiation, regulatory resilience, or shifts in customer preferences. This would 
enable them to assess climate risk and opportunity more effectively as part of the commercial 
case rather than as an external criterion. 
Additionally, the findings suggest that evergreen or long-duration fund models may be better 
suited to the unique challenges of Climate Tech. This is especially true in hardware or 
infrastructure-focused areas, where the timelines for scaling and getting returns on investment 
are generally longer. These funds can offer startups the flexibility and patient capital needed to 
refine their business model and climate strategy simultaneously. 
Finally, investors should take a more constructive role in helping founders navigate climate 
monetisation. Rather than dismissing carbon credits, green premiums, or risk mitigation as 
"soft" or distracting, investors could support startups by helping them frame these elements 
credibly, even if they do not play a central role in near-term financial models. When done well, 
this creates more resilient and mission-aligned portfolio companies, without compromising 
return potential. 
5.3.3 Policymakers 
The findings highlight that policy frameworks are crucial in determining how and whether 
early-stage ventures can monetise climate impact. However, for many startups and customers, 
==PAGE:68==
61 
regulatory environments are often fragmented, unstable, or too complicated. This makes it hard 
to include climate considerations in procurement choices, pricing, or startup strategies.  
To tackle this problem, policymakers should focus on making climate-related regulations clear, 
consistent, and accessible. More straightforward and predictable carbon pricing systems, with 
well-defined timelines, eligibility criteria, and enforcement, would help startups incorporate 
CO₂-related metrics into their business decisions more confidently. Similarly, ESG and 
reporting standards should be transparent and user-friendly to help early-stage companies 
engage without the need for large compliance teams. 
Subsidies are vital for encouraging innovation in sectors that are particularly difficult to 
decarbonise. However, support schemes must be designed with long-term independence in 
mind.  
Moreover, governments and innovation agencies could offer open-access tools and templates 
for measuring climate impact. This includes basic CO₂ calculators, scenario-planning 
frameworks, and simplified LCAs. This would lighten the load for startups, which currently 
have to find their way in this area alone. 
Ultimately, policymakers have a critical role to play in making climate value visible and 
actionable in markets. This can be achieved not only through mandates, but also by building 
the measurement and incentive infrastructure that allows climate value to influence financial 
decision-making. 
6 Conclusion 
6.1 Summary of Key Findings 
As outlined in the introduction, Climate Tech startups are finding it harder to secure funding 
and survive in today's markets, as the overall momentum around sustainability appears to be 
slowing down. This reality became particularly evident during the thesis period, when one of 
Denmark's most promising early-stage Climate Tech accelerators, Earthbound, was 
unexpectedly shut down (Earthbound, 2025). In response to these pressures, this thesis had two 
aims: to understand how startups, investors and customers currently perceive the financial 
importance of climate action; and to identify which elements of climate value, such as cost 
savings, carbon credits, risk mitigation and brand positioning, could be incorporated into 
financial frameworks to enable startups to communicate their economic and climate value more 
effectively. 
==PAGE:69==
62 
Drawing on qualitative, semi-structured interviews with founders and early-stage investors, the 
study provides an in-depth view of how climate impact is integrated into financial decision-
making at the startup stage. 
The findings reveal that the majority of startups, particularly those in the pre-lean and early 
stages, do not actively monetise their climate impact. Instead, their financial narratives tend to 
focus on traditional operational metrics, such as ROI, payback time, and cost savings. Although 
climate-related value components such as carbon markets, risk mitigation, brand positioning, 
and subsidies are acknowledged as conceptually important, they are rarely quantified or 
emphasized in business models. Founders cite several reasons for this, including a lack of 
accessible tools and data, fear of being challenged on vague metrics, market uncertainty, and 
weak customer demand for climate-centric arguments. 
Nevertheless, this study suggests that exploring these 'hidden' climate values could help startups 
to understand customer needs better and refine their value propositions. While this remains a 
hypothesis, it presents an opportunity for the development of deeper, more customer-centric 
strategies. 
From an investor's perspective, climate action is generally considered a secondary issue rather 
than a core value driver. Even in Article 9 funds, investor often see climate impact as a basic 
requirement or a screening tool. It is not typically a part of financial valuation models. 
Traditional criteria like team quality, market potential, and price competitiveness mainly 
influence early-stage investment choices. The same logic is reflected in most B2B customer 
decisions, where cost efficiency tends to outweigh climate action, unless there is already a 
strong internal ESG mandate in place. 
Overall, the findings suggest that, despite a growing awareness of and interest in climate action, 
the financial logic that drives early-stage startup ecosystems is still based on traditional 
economics. As a result, the theoretical potential of blended value, which refers to combining 
climate impact with financial return, is still mostly unrealized and underdeveloped in practice. 
More research and experimentation are needed to see if this alignment can be better reached 
through combined frameworks and metrics. 
6.2 Limitations of the Study 
While this study provides valuable insights into the financial evaluation of climate action by 
Climate Tech startups and investors, it has limitations. 
==PAGE:70==
63 
Firstly, although the sample is relatively diverse, it is geographically concentrated in Northern 
and Central Europe. This regional focus may restrict the applicability of the findings to other 
regulatory or market environments, for example in the Global South or North America. 
Secondly, the research relies on self-reported data from founders and investors, which may be 
subject to social desirability bias. Given the ethical and political sensitivity of climate-related 
topics, participants may have portrayed their practices as being more climate-friendly or 
progressive than they actually are. 
Thirdly, the study does not include direct interviews with B2B customers. Customer 
perspectives are indirectly drawn from startup narratives, which may not fully represent the 
motivations, barriers, or purchasing logic of those making purchases. This is a significant 
limitation, given that customer decisions are central to monetising climate value. 
Fourthly, while the semi-structured interview design allowed for rich and adaptive 
conversations, it also introduced inconsistency in data depth across interviews. As the guide 
was open-ended, some interviews explored specific themes, such as carbon quantification, 
policy risk, or pricing strategies, in much greater detail than others. While this flexibility 
generated valuable qualitative insights, it limited the uniformity of cross-case comparisons. 
Fifthly, due to the novelty of the research topic and the lack of well-established theoretical 
frameworks, the study remains exploratory mainly. Concepts such as the financial relevance of 
climate impact had to be examined through a combination of adapted theories and emergent 
themes rather than through field-tested, grounded models. Consequently, while the findings 
propose some hypotheses and conceptual ideas, they do not provide specific frameworks 
tailored to particular sectors. So further quantitative or comparative research would be 
necessary to build on these insights. 
6.3 Suggestions for Future Research 
Future research could extend this work in several directions. First, quantitative studies could 
measure how widespread certain attitudes and behaviours are across different geographies and 
stages of venture development. A larger dataset could also allow for segmentation by sector or 
technology type. 
Secondly, future experiments could empirically test tools such as the Augmented ROI model 
in order to evaluate the effectiveness of integrating climate metrics into startup financial models 
in terms of improving investor understanding or customer engagement. Such interventions 
==PAGE:71==
64 
would be valuable in validating the practicality and limitations of integrating climate and 
finance in real-world settings. 
Thirdly, further research is required to gain a deeper understanding of the demand side. 
Interviewing corporate procurement officers or sustainability managers could offer valuable 
insights into how B2B customers evaluate climate value, as well as identifying the most 
effective types of messaging and metrics. 
Finally, future research could examine how regulatory changes, such as mandatory climate 
disclosures or carbon pricing, might affect investor and customer expectations. This would help 
to establish whether these changes will lead to climate impact being increasingly integrated into 
financial decision-making over time.

2. 
IRR: 
The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) represents the annualized rate of return at which the net 
present value (NPV) of an investment becomes zero. It captures the profitability of an 
investment over time, taking into account the time value of money. In essence, it shows the 
break-even discount rate where future cash inflows equal the initial investment outlay. IRR is 
typically presented as a percentage and is widely used in both corporate finance and startup 
investment analysis, particularly for long-term projects or capital-intensive ventures. (Vipon, 
2019)

3.  
Payback Period  
The payback period is a financial metric that estimates how long it will take for a company to 
get its investment through cash flow back. It is commonly used in capital budgeting and startup 
evaluation to assess the risk and liquidity of an investment. A shorter payback period is 
generally considered more attractive as it suggests quicker capital recovery and reduced 
exposure to uncertainty. Although the payback period is simple to calculate and communicate, 
it does not account for the time value of money. (Kagan, 2025)

4.  
Break Even Point 
The break-even point (BEP) is the financial threshold at which the total revenue from product 
or service sales is exactly equal to the total costs. At this point, the business has covered all its 
fixed and variable costs but has not yet generated a profit. Break-even analysis helps to 
determine the minimum sales volume required before a business becomes profitable, and it is 
widely used in start-up planning, pricing strategy and investment assessment. (Hayes, 2025)

Guide Interview Investors 
1. Investment Focus 
• 
Can you briefly describe your investment focus and thesis? 
• 
Do you only invest in ClimaTech or impact-driven ventures? 
• 
What stage do you typically invest in? 
• 
Do you have ESG or climate-related screening criteria? 
2. Perception of Climate Action 
• 
How important is climate impact in your investment decision-making? 
• 
Do you see climate action as a risk mitigator, value driver, or compliance factor? 
• 
Can climate impact make a deal more attractive to you or your LPs? 
• 
Have you ever passed on a deal due to lack of sustainability commitment? 
3.  Financial Evaluation 
• 
How do you evaluate the business case of a ClimaTech startup? 
• 
Should climate impact be quantified financially (e.g., carbon savings, avoided risk)? 
• 
Would a startup that integrates climate action into ROI modeling stand out to you? 
• 
How do you balance financial return with environmental or social impact? 
4.  Communication & Pitching 
• 
What are the most compelling ways startups communicate their sustainability efforts? 
• 
What kind of sustainability evidence or storytelling do you look for in a pitch? 
• 
Is it more effective when startups frame climate action in financial terms? 
5. Reflections & Outlook 
• 
Do you expect sustainability or ESG to become more central to venture investing? 
• 
What trends are you seeing in how ClimaTech startups frame financial value? 
• 
What would help you evaluate climate action more consistently in future deals?
