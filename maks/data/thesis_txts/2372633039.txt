Abstract
The share of renewable energy resources (RES) in the power grid is increasing, and it makes the power
balance harder to maintain because the production is more ﬂuctuating as it depends more on intermittent
resources such as wind and sun. At the same time, an increasing share of the energy consumption is
transitioning to electricity, which among others covers the electriﬁcation of domestic heating and the
transportation sector. The electriﬁcation of the transportation sector is caused by an increasing use of electric
vehicles (EVs), which are seen as an additional load by the grid operators, as each EV doubles an average
household’s electricity consumption. However, EVs can become an integral part of the future power system
because instead of passively consuming power they can provide valuable services to the power system as
they are continuously manufactured with larger storage capacity and with the right power converter can
provide a fast power response. With the right control, EVs can participate in the electricity markets in order
to decrease their charging costs by consuming energy when prices are lower.
The use of EVs for providing ancillary services to the power system can be both an additional revenue
for EV owners and assist the integration of a larger share of RES. The use of bidirectional power converters
can substantially increase the capabilities and proﬁtability of EVs, since they are able to offer power to the
system, a concept referred to as Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G). In order to accommodate a signiﬁcantly larger share
of RES in the power system, it is necessary to ﬁnd new and faster sources of balancing power. EVs can
provide fast primary frequency control (PFC) by either modulating their charging or with V2G.
PFC reserve is traded the day before so it is necessary to schedule the service provision day ahead. The
schedule for charging and service provision can be made with respect to the energy and reserve prices to
achieve the maximum revenue for the EV owner. The purpose of this thesis is to demonstrate how to make a
schedule that optimises the revenue of each EV while respecting all constraining aspects, such as physical
storage capacity and user requirements. The following can cause an EV to not be able to provide accurate
and reliable PFC: ﬁrstly, the system-frequency deviation can be non zero-mean over the hour, which can lead
storage units performing PFC to become fully charged or depleted; secondly, due to the charger efﬁciency,
losses will affect the state of charge over time, if not taken into account in the scheduling phase. In order to
quantify the effect of biased system frequency deviations and charger losses for an aggregation of EVs to
provide reliable PFC, a data set consisting of one year of frequency measurements of the Nordic synchronous
zone is analysed.
This thesis presents statistical bounds on how variable the average system frequency can be on different
time scales. Additionally, a method for calculating the expected energy loss caused by continuous charging
and discharging is presented, together with efﬁciency measurements of a commercial bidirectional EV
charger. It is found that during a year, the energy balance of the service provider, relative to the grid, is
within the calculated bounds. The efﬁciency losses are calculated and validated to have a linear relationship

with the reserve capacity and the provision time. The analysis is used to make an optimal schedule that is
validated by simulations with real frequency measurements. The revenue is calculated based on historical
market data, and compared to the operational costs in different scenarios.
In order to investigate the effect of large EV penetration in the Bornholm distribution grid, a part of
it is subject to a preliminary analysis to see whether the calculated schedule could cause local congestion
problems. Finally, two real-time control methods are presented and used for simulation with the measured
frequency with the purpose of optimising the efﬁciency of the power converter. The real-time control
guarantees that the state of charge of a group of EVs will converge to the same value and was found to
reduce the efﬁciency losses by 63%.

1.1.1
Relationship between global warming and greenhouse gases
The global temperature is increasing. Records of the temperature have been made since 1880 and the 8
warmest years ever recorded were all in the last 20 years; 2016, 2015, 2014, 2010, 2013, 2005, 2009, and
1998. Fig. 1.1.1 shows the global temperature of each of the months of each of the years relative to the
average global temperature of all the 20th century and comes from the global climate report made by the
National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) under the US Department of Commerce [1].
Figure 1.1.1: 2017 year-to-date temperatures versus 8 hottest years ever recorded [1]
The increase in temperature is causing a wide pallet of changes to the environment, such as weakening
the ﬂow of the Gulf Stream and causing large masses of ice to melt at the poles, resulting in increasing water
levels.
1
==PAGE:16==

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
There is a very wide consensus within the research community that these temperature increases are
caused by the emissions of CO2, to a large part due to burning fossil fuels. An increasing layer of CO2 is
covering the earth, reducing the heat emission, referred to as the greenhouse effect. In Appendix 1 a page
from a climate status report made in 2014 by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) shows
the correlation between the global temperature, the averaged sea level and the global CO2 emissions and
concentration [2]. There is a political will to reduce CO2 emissions.
1.1.2
Sources of greenhouse gases
The IPCC report also presents a calculation of the CO2 emissions by economic sector, as seen in Fig.
1.1.2.
Figure 1.1.2: Global greenhouse gas emissions by economic sector [2]
A large share of the global emissions occurs from the production of heat and electricity, which might
lead to the idea that electricity as a source of heat or power is as bad as fossil fuels. While this might be true
in some areas where electricity is produced from coal, electricity however, can be produced from RES which
are close to CO2 neutral. Electricity can be produced with a very low emission per energy unit, measured as
gr. of CO2 per kWh. The only way to reduce the global emissions without reducing the energy consumption
is to produce electricity with lower emissions per kWh. Most of the emissions come from other sectors, but
their emissions could also be reduced by converting to electricity.
In the transportation sector, the transition from fossil fuelled internal combustion engines (ICE) vehicles
to EVs is ongoing. Domestic heating of air and water is being produced more from electricity as heat pumps
are becoming more reliable and efﬁcient. It is technically possible to produce almost CO2 neutral electricity
and at some point use electricity as the main energy source in all of the economic sectors and in that way
achieve an overall reduction of the emissions.
The Danish Transmission System Operator (TSO), Energinet.dk (ENDK) predicts that the Danish
electricity consumption will increase signiﬁcantly because other sectors such as transportation and building
heating will convert to electricity. ENDK’s prediction is shown in Fig. 1.1.3 which shows the future demand,

1.1.3
Electricity production from renewable energy sources
The electriﬁcation of the overall energy consumption is ongoing, but it requires a transition of the
electricity production from fossil fuels to renewable sources before it results to signiﬁcantly lower CO2
emissions. In Europe, the share of electricity production from renewable sources is increasing; in Fig. 1.1.4
the various renewable sources used to produce electricity in Europe are shown [4]. By 2014 the share of the
electricity production from renewable sources were above 27% of the European consumption. The different
Figure 1.1.4: Electricity generated from renewable energy sources, Eurostat , EU-28, 2004–14 [4]
RES of electricity production have different pros and cons. hydropower is an ideal power source because it is

reliable and very controllable. The disadvantage is that there are limited locations with large water reservoirs
in high altitudes where the conditions for hydropower are available, which is the reason that the amount is
not increasing. The amount of biomass is increasing but also has a maximum as the available space to grow
biomass is limited and is competing with the space to grow food.
The shares of energy from wind power (WP) plants and photovoltaics (PV) are increasing as technological
advancements are continuously making them more efﬁcient, reliable and cheaper. The production from PV
and WP are very far from any theoretical maximum as WP can be made offshore and PV can be made on
virtually any open surface. Hydropower and biomass are very ﬂexible power sources and can be controlled
based on the demand, which is unlike PV and WP that are based on intermittent energy sources and depends
on the availability of wind and sun. In the following section it is discussed, whether it can be problematic to
have a large share of intermittent energy resources in the power system.

1.1.5
Renewable electricity production in Denmark
The share of renewable sources in electricity production is larger in Denmark than in the rest of Europe,
but is mainly based on wind power. Fig. 1.1.5 shows the yearly production of different sources, such as
wind, sun, large central thermal power plants and smaller decentralized thermal power plants. ENDK makes
a distinction between central and decentral combined heat and power (CHP) plants. Biomass is included
in the central and decentralized power plants. In 2015, the Danish wind production was 42 % percent of
the Danish consumption and the share is expected to increase to 60 % by 2025 [6]. Denmark is one of the
countries with the lowest outage likelihood and the Danish consumers have on average only experienced
21.6 minutes of outage in the last 5 years [3]. Despite the large share of intermittent energy resources, the
Danish grid is very reliable.
Fig. 1.1.6 shows the Danish production of WP, PV, central and decentralised power plants together with
the consumption for one week in April 2017. In this short period, the intermittent sources at some periods
cover from more than 100 % to less than 10 % of the consumption.

1.2.1
Power balance effect on the grid frequency
The electrical grid is a number of interconnected networks with the purpose of transporting electrical
power from the producers to the consumers. It consists of a high voltage transmission network with the
purpose of transmitting the power over large distances with low losses. Close to the different consumers
the voltage is reduced by transformers to the medium or low voltage which are the voltage levels on the
distribution grid that distributes the power to the end consumers. The TSO is responsible for the voltage
level on the transmission level and the overall power balance. The power balance can be determined from a
measurement of the grid frequency, which is the same as the speed of the various synchronous machines.

1.2.2
Power system operation overview and market rules
As the share of RES is increasing it is also transforming the power system from being based on a small
number of large centralised thermal power plants to being based on a large number of distributed energy
resources (DERs). When power production becomes more distributed, the losses related to transmission are
reduced. Today, the overall losses in the transmission and distribution grid are 2.9% and 5% respectively
[13]. The disadvantage is that the centralised control become more challenging as the number of controlled
units increases dramatically.
That problem was in focus when Nord Pool Spot was founded shortly after the energy market was
liberalised [18]. The liberalisation separated the utility who sells the power to the consumers from the
energy producers who now sells to the utilities. In the new market based system, the utilities who are best at
predicting the consumption will be able to deliver the energy cheapest and the cheapest producers will at all
times produce it. The actors on the market are called balance responsible parties (BRPs) as they maintain the
overall balance by using the amount of energy they have bought or by producing the energy they have sold

and they will be ﬁned by the TSO if they do not. The BRPs will all try to optimise their revenue within the
TSO determined requirements which in the end should give the consumers the cheapest electricity.
RES are less controllable than conventional thermal power plants as they depend on intermittent energy
resources such as wind and sun. It is necessary to balance the uncontrollable and ﬂuctuating production with
the also ﬂuctuating but much more predictable demand. This balancing takes place on three different time
scales.
1. The BRP who manages the RES predicts the production a day ahead and sell the electrical energy on
the Nord Pool Spot electricity market. If the utilities have predicted a larger consumption, they will
buy the rest from conventional thermal power plants. RES is sold ﬁrst as it is the cheapest.
2. One hour before the energy is to be delivered a more reliable estimate can be made. The difference
between the ﬁrst bid and the new estimate can then be bought or sold at the intra-day market which is
open until one hour before.
3. In both of the above, energy is sold per hour but the system needs a power balance every second.
To achieve this balance, the TSO buys ancillary services, which among others consist of PFC and
secondary frequency control (SFC), which is stand by power that the various power plants will change
their output in case of an imbalance.
The ﬁrst two points do not become problematic with an increasing share of RES as the free market means
that the necessary amount of thermal units will stay in operation as long as it is economically feasible. The
disadvantage of using thermal units for long term balancing is that it is non-renewable energy that contributes
to the emission of green house gases. In Denmark the largest source of installed thermal power capacity is
hard fossil coal which has the highest emission per kWh compared to other conventional fossil fuels as seen
in table 1.1.1.
As the share of RES increases, the required size of the PFC reserve will increase in order to achieve the
necessary stability during both normal and fault operation. This is caused by the decreasing inertia in the
power system when RES replaces thermal plants which often are synchronous machines with large spinning
generators. It is also caused by the RES being less capable of providing exactly what they have sold [19].
There are four possible solutions:
1. The PFC reserve is today mainly provided by thermal plants and more reserve could be bought from
these but it is unwanted as it is non-renewable. Additionally lower system inertia requires a faster
responding PFC reserve in the future as is explained in section 1.2.1.
2. Large storage units containing e.g. Li-ion batteries could be used as a source of balancing power. A
suitable ancillary service for storage units is PFC, because it is compensated per power capacity no
matter the degree of activation, and the energy requirements are relatively small, which is beneﬁcial as
batteries with the right power converter can have a large power capacity and very fast response [20].
It is becoming more cost-efﬁcient to provide PFC with electrical storage units in Europe, but Li-ion
batteries are expected to remain an expensive source of storage in the near future [21–23].
3. Control of a part of the consumption to ﬁt the production is called demand response (DR). Potential
devices that could participate in DR are ones with thermal inertia such as heat pumps or refrigerators.
Modern refrigerators (A++ rating), however only have a consumption of approximately 200 kWh per
year [24]. The monetary beneﬁt and the contribution to the DR per unit is therefore limited and does
not cover the expenses of installed equipment[25, 26].

4. Worldwide, the number of EVs and plug-in hybrid EV are increasing exponentially. Their primary
goal is transportation but when they are parked and connected to the grid, they can serve an alternative

1.2.3
Primary frequency control
The power system does not need to have a system frequency of 50 Hz to be stable, but as described in the
previous section, 50 Hz is a set-point and deviations are a symptom of a power imbalance. PFC is therefore
not about regulating the system frequency but uses it as a measure for imbalances in order to restore the
balance again. When the frequency deviates from 50 Hz, it is the role of the PFC reserve to restore the
balance between the production and consumption, limiting the deviation.
The TSOs of the Regional Group Nordic (RG-N) synchronous zone have a shared set of grid codes
describing the different ancillary services with the purpose of maintaining system stability. PFC is divided
into two services, frequency normal-operation reserve (FNR), activated for all system frequency deviations
up to ±100 mHz and frequency disturbance-operation reserve (FDR), only activated when frequency goes
below 49.9 Hz.
DK-2 is a part of RG-N and ENDK, as the Danish TSO is responsible for the supply of the Danish
share of the Nordic FNR reserve. The TSOs in RG-N are jointly responsible for procuring 600 MW of
FNR reserve, proportional to each TSO’s share of the production. ENDK buys the reserve together with the
Swedish TSO, Svenska Kraftnät, at a shared auction and DK2’s share is 23 MW [30]. The service is bought
on market terms 1 or 2 days ahead but can only be provided by a BRP with a minimum bid size of 0.3 MW.
The grid code allows for combined delivery of multiple units that collectively deliver the required response.
In the case of a frequency deviation, the purpose of FNR is to re-establish an equilibrium between
production and consumption. FNR is a symmetrical service, which requires the provider to offer the same
power capacity for upwards and downwards regulation. The supplier therefore has to be able to reduce and
increase its production or consumption with an equal amount of power. The FNR reserve must be provided
linearly, with full activation for deviations of ±100 mHz. As the service historically has been provided
by thermal power plants, the maximum response time is relatively large, and equal to 150 s [30]. For a
frequency value ft at time t, the normalised response yt is calculated by (1.8).

1.3
Project description
This project investigates to which degree each EV can assist balancing of the power grid by providing
PFC. This involves economic analysis of the reserve and spot market prices, the technical capabilities of
series produced EVs and statistical analysis on the system frequency deviations and efﬁciency losses during
service provision. An optimal schedule that maximises the revenue for the EV without violating the various
constraints is calculated, based on the above mentioned analysis.
1.4
Research questions
This project will aim at investigating and answering the following research questions.
• What is the effect of the grid frequency characteristics and the charger efﬁciency on the frequency
reserve provision with EVs?
• How can an aggregator offer primary frequency reserves in a reliable manner while respecting the
user requirements?
• Can centralised real-time control achieve a better efﬁciency and less variance in the ﬁnal SOC of the
individual EVs?
1.5
Outline
Chapter 2 Contains a technical analysis of EVs capabilities and constraints regarding PFC, as well of a
description of two Danish research project where it is provided.
Chapter 3 In this chapter is calculated statistical bounds on how variable the system frequency can be
on different time scales. Additionally, a method for calculating the expected energy loss caused
by continuous charging and discharging is presented together with efﬁciency measurements of a
commercial bidirectional EV charger.
Chapter 4 Optimisation theory is used to schedule charging and reserve provision to maximise the revenue
without violating the physical constraints. The schedule is validated by simulation with the measured
frequency.
Chapter 5 An overview of Bornholm power system is given. It is then investigated if a certain substation
would experience congestion problems from the proposed EV schedule.
Chapter 6 Two methods for differentiating the charging of a group of EVs with centralised control, are
presented in order to maximise the charger efﬁciency and minimise the variance of the SOC of different
EVs.
Chapter 7 Contains the conclusion and further work.

CHAPTER 2
The electrical vehicle and its capabilities
regarding grid services
The number of EVs is increasing and will continue to do so in the future [31]. To a power grid with
intermittent energy resources that brings new opportunities and new challenges. From the distribution system
operator (DSO), perspective each EV represents a large movable load that can cause congestion in the
distribution grid [32]. Buying an EV would most likely double a household’s peak power consumption as
it often would be charging in the evening after the owner comes home and at the same time coincide with
the peak consumption from cooking. This is the worst time to charge the EV and is often referred to as
dumb charging. On the other hand, an EV can also be considered a ﬂexible load, as it is a large storage
unit of 16 −100 kWh that often does not have to be charged immediately. Charging the EV at speciﬁc
times could have a value to the TSO or the DSO [33]. Equipping the EV with a bidirectional charger gives
the EV a signiﬁcantly higher value to the grid as it also could perform V2G. This section will describe
the capabilities of EVs with different charging equipment and how it can generate value for the owner by
performing ancillary services.
2.1
Relevant standards involved in EV charging
Safe charging of an EV is described in an international standard by the International Electrotechnical
Commission (IEC), called IEC 62196. Between the outlet and the EV there has to be an Electrical Vehicle
Supply Equipment (EVSE), that informs the EV of the maximum allowed current. The limiting factor can
both be the fuse in the outlet and the size of the cable, which via an internal resistor in the plug informs the
EVSE of the capacity. The EVSE will inform the EV of the maximum current by adjusting a PWM signal
to a duty cycle corresponding to a maximum current. In Fig. 2.1.1 which duty cycle corresponds to which
charging current is shown, as described by the standard. The standard does not allow for currents below
6 A or above 80; between 6 A and 80 A the step size is 1 A. The wire carrying the PWM signal is called
the control pilot. The EV controls the voltage amplitude of the PWM signal by setting an internal resistor,
which informs the EVSE that the EV is ready to charge and it can close the internal relay.
Most EVs are sold with a simple version of the EVSE built into the cable, called IEC 62196 Mode 1.
It only allows for charging with 10 A on one phase and can be supplied from domestic outlets. Using a
permanently installed EVSE, most EVs can charge with 16 A on one phase but some models such as Renault
Zoe can charge with 32 A on 3 phases resulting in 22 kW. The limiting factor is the capacity of the power
equipment inside the EV, used to convert 230 V AC to DC with the battery voltage. An EVSE installed
at home, usually has a maximum of 16 A on one phase as that is the domestic power level in Denmark.

2.2
Frequency control with EVs
FNR is the most ﬁtting ancillary service for EVs, as the EV both would charge and discharge, depending
on the frequency, and thereby use the battery as a buffer rather than a source of production. FNR has high
potential for storage units as it does not provide payment per energy in kWh but per availability power
capacity [20].
When an EV is charging according to IEC 62196 Mode 2 it charges with the maximum allowed current up
to its own maximum charger capacity. It is therefore possible to control the charging current by modulating
the PWM signal, adjusting the maximum set-point from the EVSE. The power consumption ﬂexibility is
usually 6-16 A, as the minimum current allowed by the standard is 6 A and the maximum for most EVs is
16 A. FNR is a symmetrical service that requires an equal power availability for upwards and downwards
regulation. To accommodate this with an EV, a set-point of 11 A can be made with an FNR capacity of ±5
A, giving a droop curve as the bottom ﬁgure in Fig. 2.2.1. It has been experimentally demonstrated that
series produced EVs can be used to perform FNR, according to the grid code in DK-2, by modulating the
PWM signal [28, 29].
All series produced EVs that have the CHAdeMO plug, with the right external charger can have a
bidirectional power ﬂow. As there is no minimum power, this allows for providing FNR with the full power
capacity. Assuming the same domestic power level as in the unidirectional case, FNR could be provided
with up to ±16 A, resulting in a droop curve like the top ﬁgure in Fig. 2.2.1. Even at the same domestic
power level of 16 A, it has been found that an EV with V2G can provide up to 13 times more FNR in a year
resulting in an equally higher capacity payment [29]. The added value of V2G-capable EVs compared to
unidirectional EVs, as a resource to the power grid, has been a topic of research the last 20 years [20]. Using
the CHAdeMO plug, the state of charge (SOC) of the EV is accessible for the charger, which is necessary in
order to guarantee robust delivery of FNR, so that it is known whether it has capacity to charge or discharge
[33]. A series produced EV with the CHAdeMO connector and a V2G charger is fully capable of performing
FNR.

large power capacity could be fully charged or depleted during periods with a biased grid frequency. The
magnitude of this problem will be discussed later. The maximum of potential revenue per set of EV and
V2G charger is 8511 DKK, without counting any costs for losses or equipment. It is important to keep the
costs low as the revenue is limited unless the FNR prices increases in the future.
2.3
The impact of EVs on the system stability
To put the contribution of each EV into perspective, it is related to the total PFC reserve in Denmark.
ENDK continuously procurs ±27 MW of PFC reserve for DK-1 and ±23 MW FNR reserve in DK-2 [30].
The PFC service in DK-1 is slightly different from the FNR service in DK-2. Assuming that each EV is
connected to a 10 kW V2G charger and delivering FNR constantly ±9 kW, the national PFC reserve could
be covered by 50MW/9kW = 5555 EVs. For DK-1 it would be 27MW/9kW = 3000 EVs and for DK-2
it would be 23MW/9kW = 2555 EVs. There are 9111 registered EVs in Denmark by March 2017, so the
number of EVs are already considerable [36]. As the EVs only are connected to the grid a fraction of the
time, the real requirement would be more than twice as much, unless they also were providing the service
when parked at the owners work place. The EVs capable of V2G are only the ones with the CHAdeMO plug,
which are only a share of the total number of EVs. The EV, Nissan Leaf is the most sold EV worldwide with
250.000 sold models, all have the CHAdeMO plug [37].
As the number of EVs are expected to increase in the future and each of them potentially can contribute
with 9 kW of regulation capacity, it is expected that the market will be saturated. This will cause the prices
to decrease and then increase as less EVs perform the service until it reaches a new equilibrium [25]. ENDK
and the Swedish TSO, Svenska Kraftnät, are buying the FNR reserve at a shared auction and the Swedish
TSO buys ±230 MW, independent of the provider’s location, so the market potential is quite large.
2.3.1
Relationship between energy and power capacity
EV batteries are built up of small lithium-ion batteries that could be cylindrical battery cells of the type
ICR J18650, fabricated by rolling a stack of layered sheets of some lithium based cathode, a separation layer
and a graphite anode [38]. The name refers to the size 18mm in diameter, a length of 65 mm and 0 refers to
the cylindrical shape. The 18650 from Panasonic are used in the Model S from Tesla Motors. They have
a capacity of 9 −13 Wh per cell. Instead of cylindrical batteries some, OEMs are using square batteries,
known from mobile phones. In all cases the EV battery consists of a number of smaller battery cells that
each has an energy capacity and a maximum power output. A large EV battery consisting of many battery
cells therefore have a larger maximum input/output power as each of the cells can contribute. The maximum
output depends both on the voltage as a function of the SOC and the duration of the power ﬂow because
it generates heat and there is a thermal constraint. By taking both thermal and electrical constraints into
consideration, 18650 battery cells with 9,6 Wh has been found to be able to deliver a maximum of 18 W
for half an hour, giving an energy to power ratio of 9, 6Wh/18W= 0.53 h [38]. Using a commercial V2G
charger with a power capacity of 10 kW with a Nissan Leaf with a battery capacity of 30 kWh only gives a
ratio of 30 kWh/10 kW= 3 h, which means each cell only would need to output one sixth of their maximum
capacity[35, 39]. The maximum power output of the battery is a function of the SOC, but it does not have an

The payment for FNR provision is settled per kW, so the goal would seem to provide FNR with as high

The degradation of the battery is one of the main concerns, regarding grid service provision with EVs.

drive train with a power capacity of 80 kW (107 hp), which means that driving and breaking gives power

battery, regardless if it is being used for FNR or not. It is therefore reasonable to assume that the degradation
of the battery is of limited importance, but FNR provision would have to generate enough revenue for a
satisfying compensation of the EV owner.
2.4
Controllability of EV charging with respect to SOC
2.4.1
AC charging
The battery and the AC/DC converter have a maximum input/output power but there is an additional
factor which is the battery management system (BMS). The BMS manages the battery pack such that it is
not operated in zones with very low or high voltages to minimise the degradation. It measures temperature
and voltage to calculate the battery’s SOC and state of health (SOH). In the end, to fully charge the battery to
100 % of the capacity, the BMS levels the different cells by only charging the ones with the lowest voltage.
The levelling can only be made at low power so the BMS reduces the charging power as it goes from the
phase called constant current mode to the phase called constant voltage mode. During the constant voltage
mode, the EV is uncontrollable with respect to FNR provision. When charging with AC, the BMS usually
starts at the constant voltage mode when the SOC is above 90 % [41]. Fig. 2.4.1 shows the AC charging of
Nissan Leaf with 16 A. The SOC is logged from the EVs CAN-bus via the OBD-port during the Nikola
project[42]. The maximum SOC is 92.6% because the battery capacity has decreased since it was produced.
2.4.2
DC charging
ABB markets their DC chargers based on how fast they can charge the EV up to 80 % of the SOC,
because the maximum power also is reduced by the BMS for the highest SOC when using DC charging [43].
This could limit the range of the battery SOC usable for V2G FNR provision. Fig. 2.4.2 shows a test of the
charging power at different SOC for the newest and the previous Nissan Leaf. The ﬁgure shows that the
charging power of the new Leaf decreases above 70%, but that it can be charged with at least 10 kW up to
95%. That means that 10 kW V2G chargers could be used for FNR up to a SOC of at least 95%.

3.2.3
Conclusion and economic considerations
FNR could be provided with EVs of the type Nissan LEAF when they are connected to a V2G charger
via a plug such as CHAdeMO. If it is connected to the grid constantly in the period 16:00-07:00, it could
provide FNR for 15 hours. From the analysis it was found that Et|t=end ∈[−4.2, 2] kWh for 1 kW of
capacity, which results in a necessary capacity range of 6.2 kWh per 1 kW of Pcap. The Nissan LEAF has a
capacity of 30 kWh but it is often recommended that it is only operated between 20 −90% of the SOC to
minimise the wear of the battery, leaving 70% of the capacity for FNR [53]. The maximum capacity Pcap
that can be sold in the 15 hours period can be calculated as
Pcap = 70% · 30kWh
6.2kWh/kW = 3.4kW
(3.6)
3.4 kW is the largest capacity of FNR the Nissan LEAF can be used to deliver in 15 hours, if no actions
to reduce the variance of the energy bias are taken. Providing FNR with 3.4 kW for these 15 hours every day
in a year would amount to a capacity payment of 3157.5 DKK , based on an average of the market prices
2013 −2015, as already shown in [29]. However, compared to the previous analysis where losses were
not taken into account, the expected daily losses are found to be 0.086 · 15h · 3.4kW= 4.4 kWh. With the
average Danish retail electricity price of 2.2 DKK/kWh, including taxes and tariffs, this would amount to
a cost of 3607.5 DKK per year, therefore nullifying the revenue. Additionally the EV would have a SOC,
normally distributed between 20-90 % at the end of the period which could be an inconvenience for the EV
owner.
There are multiple reasons why the business model of performing FNR with EVs could make sense after
all. By increasing the efﬁciency with 10 %, the energy losses is reduced with 50 % as seen in table 3.2.2. The
cost of energy losses would then only be 1803.8 DKK per year. In a following section, it is demonstrated
how the losses can be reduced. By changing the charging set-point during operation could the variance of
the energy content in the end, Ebat,end, be reduced. This could potentially remove the limitation of Pcap and
the capacity payment. Larger Pcap would still give higher losses. How the variance can be reduced and while
the BRP still is living up to the obligation to the TSO, will be discussed and tested by simulation.

3.4.3
Conclusion
The approach with the POP and the bias is problematic for 2 reasons. When the bias is increased to
e.g. 8 kW, the EVs are only able to deliver 2 kW of downwards FNR regulation (load increase). If more
than 2 kW (e.g 0.25 kW) of FNR capacity has been sold, the aggregator is not living up to the requirements,
however for a short period of time. It is only in short periods that the bias is that high but the bias is
often quite signiﬁcant, which should force the aggregator to bid very conservatively. The aggregator is
also becoming imbalanced when increasing the set-point without buying the energy at the spot market,
which causes a ﬁne from ENDK. The fees are however relatively low on this scale. The imbalance-fee for
over-consumption is 1.31 DKK/MWh and 0.59 DK/MWh for over-production [54]. If the aggregator was
controlling a signiﬁcantly larger amount of EVs, the imbalance fees would increase but could potentially also
be unacceptable from the TSO perspective. It must be noted however, that the bias of the aggregator must be
informed to the TSO before it is applied, so that service veriﬁcation can be conducted by considering the
proper operating point.

CHAPTER 4
Optimal scheduling strategy
The MATLAB toolbox Yalmip is used for constructing the optimisation problems [55]. Yalmip is
designed for prototyping optimisation problems so it handles all the matrix calculations. It is not fast enough
for real time control but since every simulation only is run once, it is very useful.
Every EV has a limited power and energy capacity and is connected to the grid for a limited period of
time every day, so there are simple bounds calling for a constrained optimisation. The electricity price and
the reserve capacity payment vary during the day, so it makes sense to co-optimise the schedule for when
the EV is charging and providing FNR, such that it charges when the energy is cheapest and provides FNR
when the payment is highest.
First, the parameters of the EVs, the considered assumptions, and the physical constraints are presented.
The optimisation is then made in 4 increasingly complex versions, each of which solves a shortcoming of
the previous version. The ﬁrst optimisation will not take the FNR losses and the energy bias of the frequency
into account. It would work if the efﬁciency of the V2G charger is equal to one, and the frequency deviation
is zero-mean on the hour scale. It is found that it is necessary to estimate these losses to predict the expected
value of the SOC at the end of the period, which is done in the second optimisation. The third optimisation
will also take the frequency bias into account to make sure that the storage capacity limits are not violated.
This will, as shown in the analysis section, reduce the provided reserve dramatically, which is why the fourth
optimisation method will reserve power for recourse actions.
4.1
Description of scenario
The objective is to optimise the revenue from existing EVs and V2G chargers. The cost of investment in
the necessary hardware is not considered as well as the problem of dividing the revenue between the different
stakeholders, i.e. the aggregator and the EV owners. The different parameters are chosen to be similar to
the case of the Parker project such that they are close to a real application. The rated power converter is
Pmax = 10 kW. The battery capacity is Qn = 30 kWh, equal to the newest model of Nissan LEAF.
It was found in the previous section that an average efﬁciency of η = 0.8 in both directions corresponds
to a loss of 0.081 kWh per hour per kW of Pcap. This corresponded to the average loss calculated from the
the real V2G chargers and to the bias chosen by Nuvve, so an overall efﬁciency of η = 0.8 is a realistic
scenario. The efﬁciency of the V2G chargers depends a lot on the power level which can be simulated with a
look-up table but it appears that an average efﬁciency of ηc = ηd = 0.8 is accurate enough.
FNR is traded one or two days before the service provision. The prices are better two days ahead, but the
FNR reserves must bought by the TSO at least the day before the service is to be provided. In the case of
buying or selling electrical energy this is done at the day-ahead spot market. Accurate predictions of the

4.4
Optimisation considering losses and frequency bias
In order to make a schedule where the energy constraints are not violated during the service provision, it
it not enough to model the expected SOC. It is necessary to use the previously found conﬁdence interval
and implement it in a model of the uncertainty of the SOC. The expected SOC with the uncertainty must be
within the energy constraints. This is expected to result in more conservative bidding and therefore smaller
revenue.
4.4.1
Modelling the frequency energy content
To model where the SOC could be conﬁned, a new random variable is introduced as the uncertainty. Ew
represents the energy that the EV could receive in an hour because of the bias of the frequency. A random
Gaussian distributed variable is introduced and can is bounded with a conﬁdence interval, within certain
limits Ew ∈[-w, w]. A schedule that does not violate the constraints when the random variable is at its worst
case, is considered robust with the same conﬁdence as the variable was bounded with. Robust optimisation
therefore refers to worst-case optimisation [60].
It was found in section 3.1 that the frequency bias per hour, E1
bias,k, is Gaussian distributed, and in 99%
of the cases will be within ±0.79 kWh per Pcap for one hour. This can be used to model the uncertainty, Ew,
so that in every hour could obtain a value Ew ∈[−0.79, 0.79] kWh. The optimisation should then make sure
there is capacity in the battery for the worst case which is when the bias is at its maximum of Ew = 0.79
kWh in every hour and the case where it is at its minimum of Ew = −0.79 kWh in every hour.
Choosing w= 0.79 would guarantee robustness no matter how long the service is provided. It was,
however, found in section 3.1 that the rate of change of the conﬁdence interval is negative with respect to
the number of hours. That means that w= 0.79 would be overly conservative when the service provision is
longer than one hour. The appropriate choice of w, with respect to the duration of the service provision can
be seen in Fig. 4.4.1.

4.5

Recourse actions refer to actions that are determined during operation and are not ﬁxed in the scheduling
phase. The objective is to reduce the uncertainty of the SOC by additional charging or discharging. If the
full power capacity is not used for FNR, the set-point can be shifted from zero to a positive or negative
value, and thereby adding or subtracting an amount of energy. If a BRP consumes or produces more or
less energy than what is traded at the spot market, they will have an imbalance relative to their schedule
and will receive a ﬁne from the TSO. In order to avoid this imbalance, the BRP can buy or sell additional
energy at the intra-day market. If the SOC of the EV is outside a certain range, the BRP can buy additional
energy for charging, starting one hour later. The imbalance fee is very low in this energy scale and is only
1.31 DKK/MWh for over-consumption and 0.59 DK/MWh for under-consumption [54]. In case of a very
problematic SOC, using the correcting power as soon as the problem is detected instead of waiting for the
intra-day market will not make a big economic difference.
The uncertainty is modelled in a different way, in order to make the optimisation choose how much
power to reserve for recourse actions. The uncertainty is modelled as an accumulating energy capacity that
the EV would need to deliver or receive to or from the grid in the worst case. The uncertainty, Ew, therefore
is not an interval but is equal to the worst case Ew = 0.4 every hour. Ebias

distribution grid
The transmission grid is designed to carry power over signiﬁcant distances to consumers spread over
a large geographical area. The power is distributed via comprising subsystems operating at decreasing
voltage levels, called the distribution grid. The distribution grid is the last stage of the transfer of power to
the consumers. PFC is traditionally provided by conventional power plants connected at the transmission
grid. When the service is provided by distributed EVs, it will be on the low voltage 0.4 kV distribution grid,
where the capacity is signiﬁcantly lower as it is not designed for that purpose [62]. If a large group of EVs
provides V2G FNR and the frequency is very high, they could overload the local feeder by all charging at
the maximum power. If the frequency is very low, they would all discharge at the same time and the power
could ﬂow in the other direction and cause a voltage increase along the distribution lines.
In the case an over-frequency occurs, and all the participating EVs increase their charging power at a time
of peak consumption of a heavily loaded transformer, the EVs could cause the transformer to overload. The
aggregator of the EVs therefore would be in a situation where FNR delivery would need to be compromised
in order to guarantee the safe operation of the distribution grid. The transmission grid is operated by the
TSO who is responsible for the power balance and the distribution grid is operated by the DSO. This conﬂict
of interest is therefore also a conﬂict between providing a service for the TSO or the DSO. The EVs are in
a situation where they, as a storage unit, are connected to the low voltage feeders and can deliver services
to both the TSO and DSO. To the DSO the services could be voltage control or avoid charging at times of
peak consumption, also referred to as peak shaving. Transfer of power close to the capacity limit results in
increased temperatures, which shortens the life of transformers [63].
Simulations on a Danish power grid have shown that by modulating the EVs charging current they can
balance the voltage of individual phases in a low voltage system [64]. It has also been shown that peak
shaving can be added in a multi-objective optimisation control schedule in order to reduce losses and increase
transformer lifetime [65].

Optimising efﬁciency with real time control
of EVs performing FNR
The previous analysis and simulations have been made per EV, but because of the minimum bid size
of 0.3 MW, EVs performing FNR will always be a part of an aggregated group. Controlling the EVs as
a pooled resource gives the aggregator another degree of freedom to differentiate the power consumption
between the individual EVs. This is an implementation of centralised control because the response of the
individual EV is not decided locally with a droop curve but by a central entity with knowledge of the SOC of
the whole ﬂeet.
It is difﬁcult to predict the initial SOC of a single EV, but the expected SOC of a group of EVs with a
certain distribution can be predicted. An aggregator with knowledge of the SOC of all the vehicles can use
centralised control to introduce differentiated response to align the SOC of the individual EVs. Centralised
control can also be used to reduce the efﬁciency losses from FNR provision by only transferring power with
high loading where the efﬁciency is signiﬁcantly higher. The purpose of this chapter is to demonstrate how
the freedom introduced by centralised control can be used to solve the mentioned problems.

6.4
Heuristical power distribution method
A different method for distributing the charging with centralised control is proposed. In this more
heuristical method, it is ﬁrst calculated how few EVs it would require to deliver the response with 10 kW
each. This is done by dividing the total response with 10 kW and rounding up to the nearest integer. The
found number of EVs are chosen as the ones with the lowest or highest SOC depending if the EVs are
charging or discharging. The EVs SOC should then converge. All of the chosen EVs will charge equally.
The participating EVs are only updated if the required number is changing. The computation time is a very
insigniﬁcant 0.000017 seconds.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions and future work
7.1
Conclusions
In section 1.4, these three research questions are outlined for this project. In the following, the results are
concluded:
1. What is the effect of the grid frequency characteristics and the charger efﬁciency on the fre-
quency reserve provision with Electric Vehicles?
If the energy content of the system frequency is not taken into account, it can lead the EV’s SOC
outside its allowed range, thereby hindering its ability to provide PFC. The energy content, Eh
bias,k,
is found to be Gaussian distributed for periods of 1 to 15 hours, with a decreasing variance with
respect to the number of hours. It is therefore possible to estimate the need for storage capacity with a
certain conﬁdence for several hours of provision without being overly conservative. These ﬁndings
are used to the describe the uncertainty of the SOC during the service provision to guarantee that it
only violate the capacity limits in extreme cases. While the energy content of the frequency deﬁnes
the uncertainty interval, the efﬁciency losses affects the expected value of the SOC. The losses in the
charger were found to be a linear function of the reserve capacity and the provision time. Despite the
losses depending on the energy content, the expected value gave very accurate results in all simulations
and comparisons with measurements of the ENEL chargers.
To predict the losses it is essential to model and control the expected SOC by sufﬁcient charging. In
section 3.1 it was found that the main concern when providing FNR with EVs is the losses, because
the cost almost or completely nulliﬁes the revenue. The business model can be improved by increasing
the efﬁciency. If the efﬁciency of the converter is increased to 90 %, the losses would be reduced by
50 %.
The losses are, from the TSO point of view, resulting in less offered capacity from this kind of
storage-based FNR providers, which is against their intention. It could therefore be argued that the
losses should be exempted from taxes which are about 64 % of the electricity cost. On the other hand
there should be a large incentive for the service provider to reduce the losses; either via different
control strategies or by buying different charger equipment.
In chapter 4 it is found that the efﬁciency losses can be predicted and thereby covered by adjusting
the set-point. It is therefore a cost problem rather than an operational problem. The variance of the
energy content in each hour is however very problematic as it reduces the power capacity used for
FNR, which is proportional to the payment. A way to reduce the energy imbalance ratio is to allow
for changing the set-point during operation depending on the energy balance at the current time. If

the V2G charger has a capacity of 10 kW, a share of this must be reserved for set-point changes, by
buying or selling the appropriate amount of energy on the intra-day market. It is potentially costly to
trade at the spot market right before the energy is needed so it is unclear if it is cheaper than paying

Both the efﬁciency losses and the evolution of the energy bias uncertainty are very useful when
scheduling how much of the power capacity, should be used for FNR, charging or for buying intra-day
power, and at which time periods.
2. How can an aggregator offer primary frequency reserves in a reliable manner while respecting
the user requirements?
The basic user constraint is that the EV should be ready for transportation in the morning, when the
service provision is over. This is achieved by allowing a low uncertainty of the SOC in the end of
the service provision. Therefore, to respect the user constraints, the aggregator is relying on correct
modelling of the SOC uncertainty. An accurate uncertainty interval makes it possible to allocate the
right amount of power for correcting intra-day actions, such that it is possible to provide the service in
a reliable manner.
From the TSO point of view, it could be in its interest to not require robustness in 100% of the
cases but to rather specify a certain conﬁdence interval like the one presented in section 3.1. The
veriﬁcation would then be done in a statistical manner, with the service providers being required to
offer the services with predeﬁned statistical bounds. Such bounds can be on the availability to provide
the service, or based on total errors throughout the service provision (e.g. daily). In this case, the
aggregator would be able to modify the EVs set-points to counteract the effect of the frequency biases,
but it would compromise service delivery.
3. Can centralised real-time control achieve a better efﬁciency and less variance in the ﬁnal SOC
of the individual EVs?
The measurements of the ENEL charger showed that power efﬁciency is unacceptably low at low
loading but acceptable at high loading. The large efﬁciency losses are therefore caused by the working
point of the charger and it is reasonable to assume that the efﬁciency can be increased by operating the
charger at a higher loading. The power response depends on the magnitude of the frequency deviations
but since the required response most of the time is relatively small compared to the maximum value, it
could be provided by fewer EVs operating at higher loading levels (in absolute terms).
In chapter 6 it was demonstrated that the response of a group of EVs could be met in a way that
optimises the efﬁciency by some application of centralised control. A simple heuristic method for
allocating power is presented to demonstrate how high the average absolute power can be. It was
increased from 2.56 kW to 8.35 kW, but could be closer to 10 kW if a group of more than 10 EVs
were controlled. By applying such a control method, it was shown that it is possible to increase the
average efﬁciency from 80% to 90% and reduce the losses by 63%.
There are large variations in the FNR price, which means that it makes a large difference in which period
the service is provided. It is very convenient that the best paid period is generally from midnight to 7 in the
morning, where the EV is available for the service. However, it also means that if the EV is only available
until 7, using the last hour for correcting the SOC and not provide FNR implies a missed revenue. The
period that was chosen to demonstrate the scheduling had small variations on the SPOT price, and it would
therefore not have made a big difference to use a ﬁxed cost of the charging. When adding the tariffs and
taxes, the SPOT price variations become insigniﬁcant, which is why the calculations of the actual costs were

A group of 10 or more EVs that can consume 10 kW each is a very signiﬁcant load on the distribution

It was found that the distribution grid is only monitored down to the 10 kV level, which was a limiting factor

7.2
Future work
In this thesis, the analysis of system frequency on purpose has been made with different starting times
such that the results represent what happens when an EV is providing FNR at random periods. It is however
shown in [29] that there are systemic frequency deviations that are correlated with the sunset times. Future
work could involve analysing how systemic imbalances of the system frequency at speciﬁc times of the day
can be used to make more accurate estimates of the energy content.
There is large potential for developing a better algorithm for distributing the power, because it is not
ideal that each EV only delivers power in 10 seconds. This could involve using the same EVs for 10 minutes
or so, before choosing a new set of EVs to contribute. Regarding the optimal scheduling two aspects were
not modelled due to the introduction of nonlinearities, even though they could result in a different schedule.
The ﬁrst is the effect that two hours with Pcap = 5 kW gives a smaller conﬁdence interval than one hour
with Pcap = 10 kW in an optimal schedule. The second is that it is not modelled that the intra-hourly losses
are depending on the charging set-point. This could cause the charging to be spread out over more hours.
A further analysis of the distribution grid would make it possible to determine if there are any congestion
problems. This could involve installing meters at the 10/0.4 kV substations, to determine if this lower part
of the grid is as over-dimensioned as the 10 kV grid.
