Understanding the Impact of Discount Rates on H2 Modeling in Europe
Max FAVROT
Abstract
This thesis investigates the impact of discount rate on the deployment of hydrogen infras-
tructure in Europe. While most large-scale energy system models apply uniform discount rates,
this approach overlooks regional investment risks and may bias system outcomes.
Using the
Balmorel model, three scenario types are analysed: a uniform 4% baseline reflecting standard
practice, uniform rates spanning from 1% to 7%, and a scenario with country-specific discount
rates derived from private sector risk assessments. Results show that discount rate assumptions
strongly influence hydrogen deployment, technology mix, emissions, and infrastructure. Green
hydrogen supply in 2050, for instance, is more than three times higher under a 1% rate than under
a 7% rate. Introducing country-specific rates reveals spatial reallocations that challenge existing
policy expectations. For instance, some regions shift from hydrogen exporters to importers due
to higher perceived risk. More generally, the findings highlight the need to understand the im-
pact of varying discount rate assumptions on energy system modelling results, which are used in
real-world decision making.

Resumé
Denne afhandling undersøger diskonteringssatsens indvirkning på udbredelsen af brintin-
frastruktur i Europa. Mens de fleste store energisystemmodeller anvender ensartede diskonter-
ingssatser, overser denne tilgang regionale investeringsrisici og kan forvride systemets resultater.
Ved hjælp af Balmorel-modellen analyseres tre scenarietyper: en ensartet baseline på 4%, der
afspejler standardpraksis, ensartede satser, der spænder fra 1% til 7%, og et scenarie med lan-
despecifikke diskonteringssatser, der stammer fra risikovurderinger i den private sektor. Resul-
taterne viser, at antagelser om diskonteringssatser har stor indflydelse på udbredelsen af brint,
teknologimix, emissioner og infrastruktur. Den grønne brintforsyning i 2050 er f.eks. mere end
tre gange højere med en sats på 1% end med en sats på 7%. Indførelse af landespecifikke satser
afslører rumlige omfordelinger, der udfordrer eksisterende politiske forventninger. For eksem-
pel skifter nogle regioner fra brinteksportører til importører på grund af højere opfattet risiko.
Mere generelt fremhæver resultaterne behovet for at forstå virkningen af forskellige antagelser
om diskonteringssatser på resultaterne af energisystemmodellering, som bruges i den virkelige
verdens beslutningstagning.

Introduction
1.1
Background
In 2021, the European Union (EU) committed to ambitious climate goals.
Using 1990
levels as comparative base, the EU aims to reduce its Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions by at
least 55% by 2030, and to reach carbon neutrality by 2050 [1]. These objectives are supported
by comprehensive policy frameworks, including the "Fit for 55" package [2] and the revised
Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) [3] for instance. Collectively, these initiatives emphasise
the expansion of Renewable Energy Sources (RES) and the integration of innovative technologies
to decarbonise various sectors of the economy.
In that regard, green hydrogen has emerged as a key enabler for decarbonisation, particularly
in hard-to-abate sectors such as heavy industry, aviation and long-distance transport according
to the International Energy Agency (IEA) [4]. Notably, the EU Hydrogen Strategy aims to install
at least 40 GW of renewable hydrogen electrolysers by 2030 [5] and to cover 10% of the EU’s
energy needs by 2050, as stated in the REPowerEU plan [6].
Although hydrogen has been positioned as a promising decarbonisation solution, its scala-
bility has proven more difficult than anticipated. According to BloombergNEF’s (BNEF) 2024
Hydrogen Supply Outlook [7], the public and policy enthusiasm for hydrogen has gradually
shifted from early-stage optimism ("Peak of inflated expectations") towards a more grounded
and realistic appraisal ("Trough of Disillusionment"), following the Gartner Hype Cycle [8].
Figure 1: Gartner Hype Cycle for New Technologies [8]
In this context, energy system models are widely used to inform policy and investment
decisions amidst this uncertainty. These models simulate the evolution of energy systems over
time, accounting for various factors such as technology costs, resource availability, and policy
measures. They play a critical role in shaping the quantitative aspects of frameworks like the
Fit-for-55 package and national hydrogen roadmaps for EU Member States [9].
A fundamental aspect of these models is the consideration of the time value of money,
typically incorporated through the application of a Discount Rate (DR). This parameter is used to
convert future costs and benefits into present values, reflecting the principle that money available
now is worth more than the same amount in the future due to inflation, risk, and opportunity
cost [10]. The DR essentially represents the required return or cost of capital for an investment
and is therefore critical in influencing the economic viability of long-term infrastructure projects
[11].
DR is particularly relevant for hydrogen and green hydrogen specifically, which is an emerging

and capital-intensive technology [12]. Unlike electricity, hydrogen currently lacks a pan-European
transmission network and must rely on new or repurposed infrastructure [13]. For instance, the
European Hydrogen Backbone (EHB) combines repurposed natural gas pipelines with newly built
infrastructure, positioning Germany as a central European hub for hydrogen [14]. As a result,
deploying a hydrogen economy involves large up-front Capital Expenditures (CAPEX), and many
of these projects depend on the private sector for financing [12].
Therefore, understanding the impact of DR assumptions in energy system models is crucial
for accurately assessing the economic feasibility of green hydrogen projects. Such understanding
can inform investment decisions and policy formulation aimed at facilitating the growth of a more
sustainable and tangible hydrogen economy in Europe.
1.2
Hydrogen in the European Energy Transition
Key role
Hydrogen is increasingly recognised as a key component of Europe’s strategy to decarbonise
its energy system. As stated by the European Commission [5], green hydrogen could play a major
role in reducing carbon emissions. This applies in particularly to hard-to-abate sectors where
direct electrification is limited, due to the need for high-temperature heat or chemical feedstock
for instance. The versatility of hydrogen allows it to serve as an energy carrier for industries
such as steel, cement, and chemicals, as well as for long-distance transport and aviation [4].
Moreover, hydrogen enables sector coupling by linking renewable electricity generation with end-
use applications, enhancing both system flexibility and energy security. This integration potential
is one of the major reasons why hydrogen is considered a keystone in the EU’s long-term energy
strategy[5].
Targets
The EU has progressively built a comprehensive strategy to integrate hydrogen into its
climate and energy policy framework. This began in July 2020 with the publication of the EU
Hydrogen Strategy [5], which sets the goal of installing at least 40 GW of renewable hydrogen
electrolysers within the EU and producing up to 10 Million tonnes (Mt) of green hydrogen per
year by 2030. In 2021, the EU introduced the "Fit for 55" package [2], a broader legislative
framework aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared to
1990 levels. Hydrogen is considered in this package as a critical tool for decarbonising energy-
intensive sectors. In response to the energy security concerns triggered by the 2022 energy crisis,
the REPowerEU plan [6] raised ambition further by reinforcing the 10 Mt domestic production
target and adding an additional 10 Mt of renewable hydrogen imports by 2030. Then, in 2023,
the EU adopted RED III [3], which sets binding targets to achieve at least 42.5% renewable
energy in gross final energy consumption by 2030. For hydrogen specifically, RED III mandates
that by 2030, at least 42% of hydrogen used in industry must be renewable, and 60% of hydrogen
used in transport must come from Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBOs) [15].
Challenges
Despite its strong potential, the deployment of hydrogen technologies faces significant bar-
riers. First, producing green hydrogen via electrolysis is highly capital-intensive [12]. It requires
substantial upfront investment in both renewable electricity capacity and electrolysis infrastruc-
ture [12]. Additional investments are often needed for storage and transport, depending on the
project’s scope and location. Second, hydrogen storage remains technically complex and costly
[16]. As a small molecule composed of two of the lightest atoms in the periodic table, hydrogen
has low density and high diffusivity, making it difficult to contain, compress, or liquefy effec-
tively [16]. In some configurations, the cost of storage may even exceed the cost of production

itself [17]. Third, transport costs remain a key barrier. While repurposing existing natural gas
infrastructure could reduce transport costs by up to 70% [18], dedicated hydrogen infrastructure
will still be necessary in many regions. These infrastructure investments are highly exposed to
uncertainty such as market dynamics, policy shifts, and technology evolution, which may reduce
their long-term value. A further risk arises when early infrastructure is optimised for technologies
that later prove suboptimal, potentially locking in inefficient solutions—a phenomenon known as
“technology Lock-In” [19].
Current Outlook
Despite this policy momentum, market outlooks suggest that the EU may fall short of its
hydrogen targets. BNEF forecasts that low-carbon hydrogen production in the EU will reach only
4 Mt per year by 2030, well below the 20 Mt combined domestic and import target set under
REPowerEU [7]. Furthermore, less than one-third of announced hydrogen projects are expected to
reach Final Investment Decision (FID) on schedule. A key barrier to scaling hydrogen deployment
is the Levelised Cost of Hydrogen (LCoH), which remains high, typically ranging between €4 and
€8/kg depending on location and input costs [20]. Market Willingness To Pay (WTP) for green
hydrogen remains below this threshold in most sectors, reflecting a gap between economic viability
and policy ambition [21]. In this context, funding mechanisms such as the EU’s Hydrogen Bank
[22], Germany’s H2Global scheme [23], and other national subsidies play a crucial role in bridging
this cost gap and enabling market uptake [24].
Main Actors
Even though overall expectations for hydrogen have decreased, energy system models enable
to project the geographic distribution of supply and demand, linked through transport infras-
tructure.
Germany is expected to become a major hydrogen demand hub in Europe, driven
by its industrial base and coal phase-out plan [25]. Thyssenkrupp Steel alone could require up
to 720,000 tonnes of hydrogen annually by 2050 for green steel production [26]. On the supply
side, the Iberian Peninsula, especially Spain, is well-positioned to lead green hydrogen production
thanks to abundant solar resources and lower renewable costs [27][28]. Transport to northern de-
mand centres will rely on infrastructure like the European Hydrogen Backbone [14]. The Baltic
Sea region is also emerging: Finland may export up to 70 TWh of hydrogen by 2050 [29], while
Lithuania plans 8.5 GW of electrolysis capacity to meet 32% of its final energy demand [30].
While hydrogen presents significant potential for decarbonising Europe’s energy system,
it requires energy to be produced—unlike fossil fuels such as natural gas—and this conversion
necessarily brings additional energy losses and costs. As a result, investment decisions in hydrogen
are, and will likely remain, highly sensitive to financing conditions. Understanding and addressing
economic uncertainties, especially regarding the DR, will be essential to unlock the full potential
of green hydrogen in Europe.
1.3
Role of Discount Rates in Investment Decisions
Definition
The DR is a financial metric used to convert future costs and benefits into their present
value. It reflects the time value of money, i.e. the principle that a euro today is worth more than
a euro tomorrow due to inflation, risk, and opportunity cost [31]. In project finance, tools such
as Net Present Value (NPV) are used to assess the profitability of future cash flows. For private
investors, the DR often corresponds to the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), which
combines the cost of equity and debt based on a project’s capital structure [32]. Closely related is
the concept of a hurdle rate, which is the minimum rate of return required by an investor before
committing capital to a project. Hurdle rates typically include a premium over the WACC to

==PAGE:16==

account for additional project-specific risks or strategic considerations [33]. In the public sector,
DRs are used in cost-benefit analyses for infrastructure, climate policy, and long-term regulatory
planning [32].
Social vs. Private
As introduced above, two main perspectives guide the application of DRs: social and private.
The Social Discount Rate (SDR) is used in public policy to evaluate long-term projects with
societal implications, such as climate mitigation. Typically between 1% and 4% [34], it tends
to be low to give more weight to long-term and inter-generational societal benefits. The Private
Discount Rate (PDR), on the other hand, reflects the opportunity cost and risk tolerance of
private capital [32]. It is generally higher, ranging from 5% to 10% or more depending on the
sector, country, and perceived risk [34]. The distinction is crucial, as using one or the other
implies different priorities: public-good optimisation versus risk-adjusted returns. Selecting the
appropriate DR thus depends not only on methodology, but also on the intended perspective and
audience of the analysis.
Influencing factors
The value of the DR depends on a wide set of financial, economic, and policy-related factors
[35]. In public evaluations, governments may use lower rates to reflect ethical considerations or
long-term sustainability goals [31]. Policy instruments such as subsidies, government guarantees,
or long-term purchase agreements can reduce perceived investment risk and justify the use of lower
DRs [32]. In private investment, DRs are typically derived from market expectations. These are
influenced by macroeconomic indicators such as inflation, interest rates and sovereign risk, and
are often approximated by the WACC [32]. In practice, DRs are closely linked to long-term
government bond yields and equity risk premiums, which serve as approximations for the cost of
capital and investment risk in a given country [36]. As illustrated in Figure 2, these yields exhibit
significant variability both temporally and geographically. For instance, the European Union’s
average 10-year government bond yield has fluctuated markedly over the past decades, reaching
peaks of over 15% in the early 1980s and dropping below 0% in recent years [37]. Disparities can
also be seen amongst European countries: Germany and the Nordic countries typically benefit
from lower equity risk premiums due to their perceived economic stability, while countries in the
Balkans or those neighboring Russia often face higher premiums, reflecting greater investment
risks. [38]. In this context, DRs are dynamic and vary significantly across time, sectors, and
countries. In particular, emerging technologies such as green hydrogen are often assigned higher
DRs due to uncertainties in regulation, infrastructure, and market maturity [39].

Typical use in models
Most large-scale energy system models such as PRIMES, TIMES, or Balmorel usually apply
a uniform, fixed DR across all countries, sectors, and technologies [40] [41]. This may reflect
limited familiarity with financial concepts, particularly among modelers without finance-heavy
backgrounds [40]. As a result, a 4% default value is widely used, although the rationale behind
this figure is not always well-documented [42]. This practice resembles the adoption of the 2%
inflation target by central banks, which began in New Zealand in 1990 and spread globally, more
by convention than empirical grounding [43]. In the case of Balmorel, a 4% DR is used by default
[44], based on recommendations from the Danish Energy Agency (DEA) for techno-economic
analyses [45], even though the model is applied to the broader European energy network. As a
result, a mature coal plant in Germany, with relatively low financial risk (e.g., 4.33% country
Equity Risk Premium (ERP) for Germany [38] and 5% hurdle rate for the technology [33]), is
treated the same as a first-of-a-kind ocean tidal project in Lithuania, where perceived investment
risk is much higher (6.91% premium [38] and 12-17% hurdle rate for the technology [33]). While
this simplification ensures internal consistency, it overlooks important economic heterogeneity
within the EU. The European Commission’s Economic Appraisal Vademecum [32] recommends
a 3% SDR for public infrastructure evaluations. However, national planning documents reveal
a much broader range in practice, going from 1% in Denmark and Estonia to 8.5% in Spain,
reflecting differences in methodology, risk profile, and policy priorities [34]. Using a single DR
in pan-European models can mask country-specific investment strategies, potentially leading to
inaccurate projections of hydrogen deployment across different regions. While technology-specific
hurdle rates are also relevant, this thesis focuses on country-specific DRs.
Calculation Methods for country-specific DRs
The determination of an appropriate DR per country involves various methodologies. For
public-sector evaluations, the European Commission recommends the approach proposed by Cata-
lano et al. [46], which defines the SDR based on the Ramsey formula:
SDR = ρ + ε · g
(1.1)
where ρ is the pure rate of time preference, approximated by the annual crude death rate; ε is
the elasticity of marginal utility of consumption; and g is the per-capita growth rate of consump-
tion. This method reflects long-term societal preferences, intergenerational equity, and national
economic dynamics.
For private-sector perspectives, the DR typically corresponds to the Weighted Average Cost
of Capital (WACC), which blends the cost of equity and debt according to a project’s financial
structure [32]. The cost of equity is frequently estimated using the Capital Asset Pricing Model
(CAPM) [47], defined as:
re = rf + β · ERP
(1.2)
where re is the cost of equity, rf the risk-free rate (typically based on German or Euro area
10-year bond yields), β the equity beta, and ERP the equity risk premium.
The DR is not just a technical input; rather, it is a reflection of risk, priorities, and time
preferences. Whether used in private investment or public planning, its value can influence which
technologies get built, which policies get funded, and which futures are seen as viable.
For
robust and transparent analysis, its use must be well-justified, clearly communicated, and ideally
stress-tested across a range of values.

1.4
Literature review
While most energy system models rely on a uniform DR across countries, technologies, and
sectors, a growing body of literature has begun to explore the implications of changing this
assumption. The following section provides an overview of this emerging research, highlighting
key findings that inform the methodological choices of this thesis.
Bias in energy system models with uniform cost of capital assumption
A growing body of research has begun to challenge the conventional use of uniform DRs
in energy system modelling.
A foundational contribution is the 2019 Matters Arising article
published in Nature Communications by Florian Egli et al. [48], which critiques the assumptions
used in a high-profile study by Bogdanov et al. [49]. The original study proposed a feasible
global pathway toward a 100% renewable electricity system by 2050, using a techno-economic
energy system model. While it concluded that such a transition is technically and economically
viable, it applied a uniform cost of capital across all regions and technologies. Egli responds
by highlighting that this modelling choice introduces a systemic bias in favor of low-CAPEX
technologies and low-risk regions, thereby distorting technology and location choices in the model
outcomes. He argues that DRs are inherently context-specific, reflecting country-level economic
risk, financial maturity, and institutional stability. Ignoring this variability can lead to misleading
policy conclusions and suboptimal investment strategies. This early exchange in the literature set
the stage for a more critical examination of discounting practices in energy models, particularly
in the context of capital-intensive technologies such as hydrogen.
Combining Social and Private
This critique of uniform assumptions has motivated new approaches that differentiate be-
tween DR, particularly between public and private perspectives. These DRs are not necessarily
mutually exclusive; they can be applied in parallel to better capture the dual nature of energy
policy investments. Maselli and Nesticò [50] propose a dual-discounting approach in which a
financial DR is applied to monetised economic flows, while an ecological DR is used for envi-
ronmental externalities. Applying this framework to a photovoltaic plant and a wind farm, they
show that the ecological DR significantly increases the relative weight of long-term environmental
benefits in project evaluation. As a result, projects previously deemed marginal under a purely
financial lens appear more favorable when environmental dimensions are accounted for. This
demonstrates that integrating both perspectives can shift investment priorities toward more sus-
tainable outcomes. The authors argue that this distinction should become standard in public
planning, echoing calls from the European Council for an Energy Efficient Economy to clearly
separate the modelling of private investment behavior from societal cost assessments [34].
Impact on the Electricity System
If both social and private DRs deserve consideration in theory, what does this mean for actual
energy system planning?
Nandi Moksnes and Usher [51] address this question by examining
how uncertainty in DR assumptions affects model outcomes in the context of electricity system
planning for Kenya and Benin. Using a detailed techno-economic model, the authors conducted
a global sensitivity analysis across 16 input parameters and found that the DR was consistently
one of the top three drivers of variability in key outputs such as total system cost, electricity mix,
and renewable energy deployment. For example, in Benin, changing the DR from 5% to 10% led
to a shift from 59% to 28% solar PV share in the generation mix by 2050. Conversely, higher
DRs favored fossil-based generation due to their lower capital intensity. These results suggest
that even within lower-income contexts with different investment profiles, DR assumptions have
a structural influence on the modeled outcomes. The study concludes that overly simplistic or
static discounting can obscure key trade-offs, and that uncertainty in financial parameters should
be explicitly accounted for when using models to inform long-term energy policy.

Before turning to hydrogen specifically, it is worth considering how capital-intensive tech-

many economic characteristics with hydrogen, such as high upfront investment, long lifetimes,
and uncertain future cash flows, offers a useful point of comparison.
Floating Offshore Wind
The DR plays a particularly significant role in the economic viability of capital-intensive
renewable technologies such as offshore wind. Fernández Chozas et al. [52] investigate this in
the context of a floating offshore wind farm off the Galician coast in the European Atlantic area.
Using a techno-economic model, the authors assess the Levelised Cost of Electricity (LCoE) under
various DR assumptions, ranging from 3% to 10%. The study demonstrates that the choice of
DR has a major influence on the estimated LCoE, with values rising from approximately 130
€/MWh at a 3% rate to over 200 €/MWh at a 10% rate. This nearly 55% increase in cost solely
due to financial assumptions highlights the sensitivity of emerging technologies to investment
risk. The authors conclude that DR selection is critical in determining project feasibility and
competitiveness, particularly for nascent technologies where high upfront costs dominate the
economic profile.
This finding reinforces the importance of using context-specific DRs when
evaluating renewable energy technologies, especially in investment planning and policy analysis.
LCoH Competitiveness
Like offshore wind, green hydrogen is a capital-intensive technology whose competitiveness
is highly sensitive to financing assumptions. While offshore wind has seen a steady reduction
in risk through market maturity and infrastructure build-out, hydrogen remains an emerging
sector with considerable uncertainty.
In this context, Kigle et al.
[53] assess how country-
specific investment risk affects the LCoH. Using a global 50 km × 50 km spatial resolution, the
authors combine renewable resource potential with more realistic financing assumptions to map
the global LCoH landscape. Their findings show that the impact of country-specific DRs on
hydrogen economics is substantial. For instance, in North Africa, applying a 10% DR instead
of 5% increases the LCoH by over 30%, from €2.70/kg to €3.60/kg. In Sub-Saharan Africa,
some locations experience LCoH increases of more than €1.00/kg purely due to financing risk.
Even in the EU, internal variation exists: countries like Portugal and Spain exhibit lower LCoHs
due to more favorable DRs and high solar capacity, whereas regions in Eastern Europe end with
higher values despite similar technical conditions. The study illustrates that relying on uniform
DRs may systematically overestimate the attractiveness of high-risk but resource-rich regions, or
underestimate the viability of lower-risk locations with moderate resources.
Revisiting the Debate: Green Hydrogen
These findings on hydrogen competitiveness further echo a broader methodological debate
on discounting in energy models. Following the 2019 Matters Arising critique of uniform cost
of capital assumptions in global energy transition models [48], Bogdanov et al. responded in
Nature Communications with a formal reply [54], defending the methodological choices of their
original study and highlighting the difficulty of combining regional data uncertainties with model
transparency and replicability. This exchange highlighted the intrinsic complexity of DR selection,
and particularly the trade-off between global comparability and regional realism.
Building on this dialogue, Egli and colleagues recently returned to the issue with new em-
pirical depth. In a June 2025 Nature Energy article titled "Mapping the cost competitiveness
of African green hydrogen imports to Europe" [55], the authors assess the viability of hydrogen
imports by combining renewable resource availability, infrastructure requirements, and country-
specific investment risk into a techno-economic model. Using granular data on financing condi-
tions, including country-specific WACC values derived from real market risk premia, they simu-
late LCoH for green hydrogen exported from North and West Africa to Europe. The study finds

1.5
Research Gap
Despite growing recognition of the importance of DR assumptions, most large-scale European
energy system models like PRIMES [56], TIMES [57], and Balmorel [41] continue to rely on
uniform, static values across technologies and countries [40].
As shown in Section 1.4, this
practice simplifies modelling but ignores the reality that financing conditions vary widely across
Europe, particularly between mature markets and regions with elevated investment risk [48, 53].
While studies have examined how DRs affect individual technologies like wind, solar, or
hydrogen at the asset level [52, 53, 58], little research has been done from a full system per-
spective, where DRs vary spatially across countries and influence broader investment decisions.
In particular, no study has systematically assessed how country-specific DRs affect the modeled
development of green hydrogen in Europe, including technology choice, spatial deployment, or
integration into the wider energy system.
This gap is especially relevant given the current misalignment between public and private
perspectives: whereas public planners often assume simplified financing (e.g., 4% social DR),
private investors assess risks with greater granularity, adjusting DRs based on political stability,
regulatory risk, or macroeconomic indicators.
As a result, there is a growing disconnection
between the “modelled” rollout of hydrogen and what the private sector may actually be willing
to finance.
This thesis addresses this gap by integrating spatially varying DRs into the Balmorel energy
system model, exploring how such differentiation reshapes the trajectory of hydrogen deployment
across Europe. It offers a more realistic lens for investment planning and helps reconcile public
roadmaps with private sector feasibility.
1.6
Thesis Objective
This thesis aims to explore how DR assumptions influence the development of green hydrogen
in Europe as projected by energy system models. It begins by evaluating whether the base case
of the Balmorel model, which uses a uniform 4% DR, aligns with the hydrogen production and
import targets set by the European Union for 2030 and beyond. This serves as a reference point
to assess the realism of standard modelling outputs.
Building on this baseline, the second objective is to assess how sensitive model outcomes
are to uniform variations of the level of the DR. In particular, it investigates how different
DR assumptions influence key system outcomes such as hydrogen deployment, infrastructure
investments, technology selection, and emissions.
Finally, the thesis seeks to better understand the divergence between the assumptions com-
monly used in public or academic modelling studies—the baseline—and those used by private
investors, who apply differentiated risk premiums depending on location and technology.
By
comparing these results, the analysis provides insights into where and under what conditions

1.7
Relevance for Stakeholders
Energy system modelers are often the first to set the assumptions that shape how policies are
evaluated and how investments are forecasted. This thesis demonstrates that the commonly used
uniform discount rate, typically 4% in European models, can strongly influence both technology
and spatial outcomes in hydrogen development. Yet discount rates are still frequently treated as
fixed technical inputs rather than strategic variables. For emerging, capital-intensive technologies
like hydrogen, this is a missed opportunity. By showing how results shift under different DR
assumptions, this work encourages modelers to integrate DR sensitivity into standard scenario
planning. Models should also evolve to allow easier implementation of region- or technology-
specific rates, supporting more realistic outputs.
For investors and developers, particularly those planning infrastructure in uncertain regula-
tory or financial environments, this research offers guidance on risk exposure. Green and blue
hydrogen production react differently to changes in financing assumptions, and this dynamic plays
out unevenly across regions. The analysis helps identify more resilient locations where investment
viability is less sensitive to the cost of capital, and provides a clearer picture of how new suppliers
or technologies may enter the market. This is especially relevant for private equity firms, infras-
tructure funds, and hydrogen technology providers (e.g., electrolyser or reformer manufacturers)
who must align asset strategies with systemic developments. While many private actors already
adjust for these financial variables internally, greater alignment with public assumptions would
improve credibility and collaboration.
For policymakers, the findings highlight the urgent need to bridge the gap between public
targets and private risk assessments. Most EU-level hydrogen strategies, including Fit-for-55,
REPowerEU, and the Hydrogen Bank, assume uniform financial conditions that do not reflect
real investor constraints. This can lead to misaligned support schemes, delayed projects, and
ineffective planning. By identifying where small shifts in perceived investment risk could unlock
large-scale hydrogen deployment, this thesis supports the smarter allocation of CfDs, guarantees,
and other state-backed mechanisms. A more open dialogue between public and private actors is
essential to ensure that energy strategies are both ambitious and bankable.

2.1
Balmorel Model and Data
Model Overview
Balmorel is a partial equilibrium model for the electricity and district heating sectors, de-
signed to simulate energy systems with high spatial and temporal resolution. It was developed to
support decision-making on energy planning, policy design, and investment analysis in liberalised
energy markets [41]. The model is particularly well-suited for this study, as it is widely used for
academic and policy applications across Europe [41]. It solves for the least-cost dispatch and in-
vestment configuration in an interconnected power system using linear programming techniques,
coded in GAMS [59].
Model Inputs and Variables
Balmorel requires a wide range of exogenous inputs such as energy demand, technology-
specific costs and efficiencies, fuel prices. Endogenous variables include electricity and hydrogen
production, investment decisions, technology selection, capacity expansion, and dispatch. In this
study, hydrogen demand is modeled as both exogenous and endogenous. It is treated as a fixed
input in sectors such as transport, liquid fuel and chemical production, and direct industrial use
for high-value heat. Conversely, it is considered endogenous when hydrogen is used for power
generation or biomethane production [44].
Technology and Demand Data
The technological parameters used in Balmorel, such as CAPEX, Operational Expenditures
(OPEX), fuel efficiency, and lifetimes, are sourced from the DEA Technology Catalogue [60],
which is widely accepted in European policy analysis and modelling exercises [44]. The exogenous
hydrogen demand projections are based on the hydrogen backbone report [61], and then down-
scaled based on geographical information mapping of European industrial and information for
long-haul truck activities [44]. A visual representation of the 2050 demand volumes is shown in
Figure 3. A comparison with the demand assumptions used in other studies is provided in the

2.2
Scope of the Model
Temporal
The Balmorel model used in this study operates with a multi-level temporal resolution. The
simulations are run across three yearly time steps (2030, 2040, 2050), subdivided into five seasonal
periods (weeks 2, 15, 28, 41 and 50) and further detailed into twenty-four representative hourly
time slices (spaced every 3 hours from Thursday 1:00 AM to Saturday 10:00 PM, representing
two week-days and a day in the week-end) per season. This setup enables the model to capture
seasonal and intraday variations in demand and supply, which are particularly relevant when
assessing the integration of variable renewable energy and hydrogen technologies.
Geographic
The model includes the EU-27 countries, the United Kingdom, Norway, Switzerland, and
most Western Balkan countries, with the exception of Kosovo. Ukraine and Türkiye are excluded
from this analysis. The spatial resolution is based on electricity bidding zones, allowing the model
to reflect real market dynamics. Each zone represents a node in the electricity market, enabling
the study of both national and cross-border effects.
Sectoral
Balmorel includes multiple sectors. Electricity demand is treated exogenously and is not
assumed to increase significantly after 2030. However, the model endogenously determines sec-
toral electrification, such as in transport and synthetic fuels. Heat demand is covered for both
industrial (e.g. cement production) and residential applications (e.g. electric boilers and heat
pumps). Hydrogen is included as a dedicated energy vector, with supply and infrastructure deci-
sions determined endogenously, although demand in transport and industrial sectors is provided
exogenously. Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) technologies are also included in the model.
Technological
New investments in nuclear power are excluded for political and public acceptance reasons,
reflecting standard modelling practices adopted in prior studies. However, existing nuclear capac-
ity is allowed to operate until its technical lifetime expires. While this conservative assumption
has been made to maintain comparability with other model-based assessments, it may be revised
in future analyses.

2.4
Implementation of Country-Specific Discount Rates in the Model
In its standard configuration, Balmorel uses a single, uniform DR defined as a global pa-
rameter across all regions, sectors, and technologies. This makes it straightforward to adjust the
model for uniform sensitivity scenarios, such as comparing 1%, 4%, or 7% DR cases by simply
modifying the relevant input parameter. However, the model does not natively support country-
specific DRs, reflecting how embedded the conventional 4% assumption is in typical modelling
practice.
To address this limitation, a structural adaptation of the model was necessary. The first
step was to understand precisely how the DR is used within Balmorel’s cost calculations. As
explained in a Balmorel analysis from I. Kountouris et al. [44], the DR is used to annualise both
investment and fixed operating costs by means of an annuity factor:
f = 1 −(1 + τ)−n
τ
(2.1)
where τ is the discount rate and n is the lifetime of the asset. Since the model architecture
only allows for a single τ value, implementing a regional differentiation required bypassing the
internal annuity calculation mechanism.
To enable country-specific discounting without rewriting the entire model structure, the
annuity values were computed externally in a dedicated Excel tool. A snippet of this tool is
shown in Appendix A.2. For each asset class—generation technologies, electricity grids, hydrogen
pipelines, and heating networks—annuity factors were calculated using the appropriate national
DR and technology-specific lifetime. These externally computed values were then entered into
Balmorel’s data input sheets, effectively overriding the model’s internal cost annuity calculation.
This method preserved the model’s optimisation behavior while incorporating differentiated fi-
nancial conditions at the country level.
While this workaround enabled the required functionality for the purpose of this thesis, it
introduces a number of manual steps and lacks scalability. A more permanent solution would
involve modifying the model code to accept a DR parameter indexed by country and technol-
ogy. Nonetheless, this implementation demonstrates that Balmorel can accommodate national
financial differentiation with modest adjustments, highlighting its flexibility. Even if this research
focuses on country-specific DR, the same methodology could be used for future research using
technology-specific DRs, which may be especially relevant for heterogeneous asset classes like
renewables, storage, or early-stage technologies.

2.6
Why is Balmorel a Suitable Tool for This Study?
Balmorel provides an appropriate platform to explore how financing assumptions, and par-
ticularly DRs, affect hydrogen deployment in a cross-country European context. Its transparency,
adaptability, and credibility make it a strong fit for the research objectives of this thesis.
Strengths
Balmorel is an open-source, modular energy system model written in GAMS, allowing users
to easily modify components and test unconventional assumptions [41].
For this study, this
flexibility enabled the implementation of custom annuity structures to simulate country-specific
DRs, a feature not available in the default model. Balmorel has also demonstrated high credibility:
a recent study in Nature Communications (2024) used it to assess EU transition pathways and
showed strong alignment with other leading models [63]. Furthermore, Balmorel supports high-
resolution spatial and temporal detail and is optimised for High-Performance Computing (HPC)
[64], which is essential for modelling infrastructure-heavy technologies like hydrogen.
Limitations
Despite its advantages, Balmorel has limited financial modelling capabilities.
It applies
a single global DR across all technologies and regions, and lacks built-in support for hurdle
rates, investor heterogeneity, or other financing gaps that had to be addressed externally for this
research.
Model Comparison
Compared to other prominent European models, Balmorel offers greater modularity and
flexibility. PRIMES, widely used by the European Commission, is closed-source and difficult
to adapt externally [56]. TIMES, developed under the IEA’s ETSAP initiative, offers detailed
technology representation but requires high calibration effort and is less agile for methodological
experimentation [57]. Balmorel’s structure makes it more accessible for academic work focused

Scenario Framework
To explore the impact of financing assumptions on hydrogen deployment in Europe, a set of
scenarios was designed based on variations in DRs. Each scenario reflects a different economic
environment, ranging from simplified uniform rates to more detailed country-specific investor
perspectives. Apart from the DR, all other inputs like demand profiles, technical assumptions or
fuel prices are held constant. This isolates the influence of the DR while preserving comparability
across cases. The scenario framework aligns with the analytical structure of the Chapter 4 and
is illustrated in Figure 5, which presents colour maps of the country-level DRs. The specific DR
values used in each scenario are provided in Appendix B.1 Table 6.

3.1
Basecase Scenario
The basecase scenario represents the standard financial assumption used in most techno-
economic modelling studies: a uniform DR of 4% [42]. This value is consistent with the default
used in the Balmorel model [44] and originates from recommendations issued by the DEA for
socio-economic assessments [45]. The basecase rate serves both as a point of validation to ensuring
model behavior aligns with known outputs, and as a reference case against which sensitivity
scenarios will be compared.
3.2
Uniform Discount Rate Sensitivities
To evaluate the sensitivity of model outputs to the DR parameter, a series of scenarios were
developed using uniform DRs applied consistently across all regions and technologies.
These
include DR values of 1%, 3%, 4% (basecase), 5%, and 7%. This range reflects the spectrum
commonly found in European energy modelling and infrastructure appraisal [34].
Each scenario allows for a comparison of system outcomes, such as hydrogen production,
technology mix, emissions, and infrastructure buildout, under different financing conditions. This

Validating the Model with Baseline Scenario
To begin the analysis, this section investigates the model’s baseline scenario using a uniform

This Basecase reflects the
most common assumption in techno-economic modelling [40] and aligns with guidelines from the

4.1.1
System Outlook: Fossil Phase-Out, Electrolyser Ramp-up
In terms of system-wide electricity generation capacity, solar and wind expand significantly
over time, reaching approximately 1250 GW and 950 GW, respectively, by 2050. Over the same
period, natural gas capacity declines steadily from around 100 GW in 2030 to 40 GW in 2050,
indicating a gradual phase-out of fossil assets (see Appendix C.1, Figure 29).
However, due
to lower average Full Load Hours (FLH), solar generators contribute less to overall electricity
production. As shown in Figure 7, solar generation reaches nearly 1800 TWh in 2050, accounting
for just over 50% of the total wind output, although solar capacity is 30% higher. Hydro and
nuclear remain important but are exogenous inputs in the model and thus excluded from this
investment-driven analysis.
Figure 7: Electricity Production - Basecase - 2030-2050
Turning to hydrogen, the model predicts significant growth in electrolyser capacity—from
approximately 2 GW today in Europe [4] to 35 GW in 2030 and 220 GW by 2050 (see Ap-
pendix C.1, Figure 30). While this suggests strong deployment momentum, the 2030 figure still
falls short of the EU Hydrogen Strategy’s 40 GW target [5].
Hydrogen production in the model comes from three sources: Electrolysis (ELZ), Steam
Methane Reforming with Carbon Capture and Storage (SMR-CCS), and Imports. ELZ gains
share over time but does not reach full dominance as more than 40% of hydrogen is still produced
from natural gas by 2050 (Figure 8). That said, total production in 2030 reaches 10.7 Mt, meeting
the REPowerEU target [6] for domestic hydrogen supply volume.

policy implementation. The model could thus be viewed as an upper-bound scenario for hydrogen
deployment.

tion and does not specifically aim to meet policy targets. It excludes mechanisms like penalties for
non-compliance or direct subsidies, which helps explain why the model may underdeliver relative
to EU hydrogen ambitions.

Conclusion of the Analysis
The analysis highlights the profound effect that DR assumptions have on hydrogen deploy-
ment and broader energy system configurations. In the Basecase, modeled under a uniform 4%
rate, hydrogen deployment fails to meet EU targets, with a strong reliance on SMR-CCS and
limited imports for outside Europe. Lowering the DR significantly boosts ELZ production, re-
duces emissions, and increases cross-border energy flows, indicating that investment risk is a key
barrier to clean energy expansion.
The shift becomes more nuanced in the country-specific scenario. While global trends still
align with the higher DR scenarios, regional impacts are far more diverse and non-linear. Coun-
tries like Spain or Italy lose their status of hydrogen exporter, while others like Germany or
France strengthen their domestic production. These results challenge the use of uniform financial
assumptions as an approximation for real-world investment conditions.
Overall, the findings emphasise that DR assumptions are not a modelling detail. They are
central to shaping energy pathways, technology choices, and infrastructure development. As such,
they should be explicitly tested and transparently communicated in any policy-relevant scenario
work.

5
Discussion
This chapter interprets the major findings of the thesis in context, reflects on their method-
ological and policy implications, and outlines strategic directions for future work.
5.1
Comparison to Literature
This thesis closely aligns with the recent study by Egli et al. (2025) in Nature Energy [55],
reinforcing its relevance in current academic discourse. Both works examine how country-specific
investment risks, captured through differentiated discount rates, affect hydrogen development.
However, while Egli et al. focus on export competitiveness in African regions, this thesis evaluates
hydrogen deployment outcomes within a pan-European energy system.
Key convergence: Both studies find that discount rates significantly influence spatial
investment patterns. Egli et al. show that when applying country-specific rates, regions like Mo-
rocco or Mauritania become less attractive than under uniform assumptions. Similarly, this thesis
demonstrates that applying differentiated rates across Europe shifts green hydrogen deployment
away from higher-risk areas such as the Mediterranean or Eastern EU and toward regions with
more favourable investment environments, like Central Europe and the Nordics (see Figure 38).
This reinforces the view that perceived financial risk has clear spatial implications.
Key difference: Methodologically, the studies diverge.
Egli et al.
use a project-level
LCOH analysis on a 50 km × 50 km grid, while this thesis adopts a system-level optimisation
model that accounts for cross-country energy flows and infrastructure interactions. As a result,
this thesis offers broader insights into how financial conditions affect system-wide feasibility and
coordination, whereas Egli et al. provide detailed analysis useful for local investment decisions,
but without modelling system interdependencies.
Interpretation: The two approaches are complementary. Egli et al. highlight that financing
conditions are as critical as technical potential in shaping hydrogen economics.
This thesis
confirms that conclusion from a system perspective and adds a European policy angle: public
models using uniform discount rates may overlook key investor realities, potentially distorting
infrastructure and policy recommendations. Together, the studies underscore the need for more
realistic financial assumptions in both system planning and project-level assessments.
5.2
Practical use of the Thesis
While this thesis provides a broad set of quantitative results, the most policy-relevant insight
lies in the varying responsiveness of regions to changes in financing assumptions, as summarised
through the elasticity maps in Figure 18. These maps illustrate how sensitive regional hydrogen
deployment is to discount rate variations, and thus to perceived investment risk. In practical
terms, this means that targeted financial support, such as fundings or offtake agreements, can
yield disproportionately large impacts in some regions, while producing only limited change in
others.
For instance, the analysis shows that green hydrogen production in the Nordic countries
and Denmark is highly sensitive to discount rate changes.
This implies that relatively small
interventions from public entities could unlock significant volumes of green hydrogen in these
regions. Although the general economic environment appears relatively stable in these regions,
the perceived risk for hydrogen is high due to limited local demand. Enabling offtake through
Page 40 of 44
==PAGE:53==

Max FAVROT
projects like the Baltic Sea Hydrogen Collector [66] could significantly reduce this risk by allowing
direct offtake from Germany. Even if the project itself has a poor standalone business case, its
broader system value, by unlocking hydrogen deployment in high-potential RES regions, should
not be overlooked. Notably, the project has been designated a Project of Common Interest (PCI)
by the EU and received funding from the Connecting Europe Facility (CEF) at the beginning of
2025.
Conversely, areas such as the UK or Iberia display lower elasticity, suggesting that even
substantial public support may produce only modest shifts in investment behaviour. As a result,
the prioritisation of infrastructure such as the Iberian Hydrogen Corridor [66], which is also a
PCI, could be revisited in light of this analysis. Incentives aimed at scaling SMR-CCS or enabling
hydrogen exports in low-sensitivity regions may prove less effective and could potentially be
reallocated to areas where investment risk is more binding.
These findings underscore the importance of context-specific policy design. A uniform sup-
port mechanism risks inefficient resource allocation. Instead, elasticity-informed strategies can
help policymakers better identify where and how to intervene to reduce investment risk. Al-
though this analysis focuses on hydrogen, the framework could be extended to other technologies
and applied at finer spatial or temporal scales to evaluate the strategic effectiveness of various
support mechanisms.
5.3
Limitations
Realism of hydrogen prices
Despite incorporating more realistic financing assumptions, the model remains optimistic
regarding future hydrogen prices. As shown in Table 2, Balmorel estimates hydrogen prices in
2050 to range from 1.3 to 1.6 e2025/kg depending on the discount rate scenario. In contrast,
current industrial expectations are significantly higher, typically between 2 and 5 e2025/kg based
on private-sector forecasts and recent market data [67, 68], and can be even higher according to
personal experience in private Power-to-X development. This discrepancy remains a key barrier to
trust between public modelling efforts and private-sector decision-making, as highlighted during
interviews with the industrial partner Uniper.
It is worth noting, however, that electricity price projections, ranging from 35 to 53 e2025/MWh
in 2050 across scenarios, are more consistent with industry benchmarks and private investment
analyses, which generally forecast around 50 e2025/MWh [69]. This suggests that while hydrogen-
specific modelling assumptions require further refinement, Balmorel’s broader power system out-
puts are more in line with market expectations.
Simplified assumptions in key model parameters
As with many techno-economic studies, this work relies on a set of simplifications to re-
duce model complexity and computation time. For instance, no additional nuclear capacity is
allowed beyond the existing fleet, despite ongoing debates in Europe around new nuclear invest-
ments. Similarly, gas prices, hydrogen import costs, CO2 capture rates, and hydrogen demand
projections are assumed exogenous and static across scenarios. These parameters are subject
to significant uncertainty and can substantially affect optimal system configurations if varied
dynamically according to research [44].
Gas price inelasticity despite high SMR usage
Although the model shows increased reliance on SMR-CCS under high DR scenarios, it
assumes fixed natural gas prices over time and across scenarios. In reality, higher gas demand

==PAGE:54==

would likely put upward pressure on prices, especially in regions with limited supply flexibility.
This may result in an underestimation of costs associated with fossil-based hydrogen production,
particularly in high-SMR pathways, and could bias results in favor of this technology.
Discount rate implementation in Balmorel
While this thesis successfully introduced country-specific DRs through external annuity cal-
culations, the core objective function of Balmorel still uses a fixed global DR for system-wide
present value calculations. Due to structural constraints in the model, it was not possible to ap-
ply country-indexed DRs within the objective function. This partial implementation may slightly
affect the results.
Country-level approximations do not capture asset-specific investor risks
The “private” DR scenario used in this study is based on country-level equity risk premiums
derived from the NYU Stern dataset [38], representing a credible approximation for sovereign-level
financing risk. However, private developers in reality evaluate projects using asset-specific hurdle
rates. As highlighted by the industrial partner Uniper, these incorporate a range of premiums,
including those related to technology maturity, regulatory frameworks, infrastructure access,
and project-specific features such as the presence of reliable offtakers or competitive pressures.
Therefore, while the private scenario brings the model closer to investor logic, it still simplifies
important dimensions of financial risk.
Omission of tax effects and capital structure detail in WACC
The private DR used in this thesis reflects a simplified equity premium-based view. For
greater realism, future work could use a Vanilla-WACC formulation—including both equity and
debt financing costs, but excluding the taxes—as suggested by Egli et al. [55]. This could be
further extended to a post-tax WACC incorporating country-specific corporate tax rates pro-
vided in the NYU Stern dataset [38], improving alignment with how private actors assess project
economics in real-world financial environments.
5.4
Recommendations for Stakeholders
1. Incorporate Discount Rate Sensitivity into Model Design: For energy modelers, the
findings of this thesis underline that DRs are not trivial numerical inputs but fundamental
economic parameters with system-wide consequences. Small changes in the DR can dramat-
ically alter modeled investment outcomes, technology preferences, and emissions pathways.
Modelers should therefore incorporate sensitivity analyses around DRs and clearly justify
their choices. To facilitate this, additional effort may be required to design models that can
easily accommodate differentiated financing assumptions within their structure.
2. Use Discount Rate Sensitivities to Evaluate the Impact of Support Schemes: For
policymakers, a more nuanced understanding of how private investors perceive financial risk
is essential for designing effective support schemes. Investors are, in practice, the ones who
will ultimately realise the energy transition. Instruments such as government guarantees,
contracts for difference, or secured offtake agreements can help reduce perceived investment
risk. However, they are most effective when targeted at regions where small reductions in
the DR can significantly change deployment outcomes. This is precisely the type of insight
provided by DR sensitivities.
3. Increase Transparency on Private Investment Risk Metrics: For private investors
and transmission system operators, increased transparency around investment risk metrics,
particularly the WACC, could significantly improve the quality and credibility of public
energy modelling. As highlighted during discussions with Uniper, WACC is often treated
Page 42 of 44
==PAGE:55==

as a confidential input, closely guarded by private actors. This lack of transparency creates
a disconnect between public model-based forecasts and the internal financial analyses used
by investors, especially for emerging technologies like hydrogen where the LCoH is highly
sensitive to financing assumptions. In this thesis, efforts to represent private-sector perspec-
tives were hindered by the limited availability of public data on WACC, ultimately relying
on a single, non-industrial academic source [38].
Broader sharing of risk-related inputs
and methodologies, even in aggregated or anonymised forms, would support more realistic
academic research and help the alignment between public and private sector planning.
5.5
Further Research
Several directions emerge for extending this work and improving the modelling of financing
assumptions in energy system analysis.
Improved treatment of discount rates
One of the most immediate opportunities lies in further refining the representation of DRs.
As suggested by Maselli and Nesticò [50], future models could differentiate between public and
private perspectives within the same system, applying dual discounting logic to better reflect both
societal benefits and investor risk. A declining DR over time, which is a common assumption
in long-term public project evaluation, could also be considered, especially for intergenerational
infrastructure such as hydrogen networks. Additionally, incorporating more granular risk fac-
tors such as technology-specific hurdle rates or equity premiums could provide a more accurate
reflection of real-world investment criteria.
Modelling financial support mechanisms
As suggested for policymakers, another promising avenue involves simulating the impact of
policy instruments designed to reduce perceived investment risk. Programs such as H2Global or
the European Hydrogen Bank could be represented in the model by lowering the DR for specific
technologies or regions, thereby capturing the effect of risk-reduction tools like long-term offtake
agreements, guarantees, or subsidies. This approach would allow researchers and policymakers to
evaluate where such instruments are most effective and whether they unlock meaningful hydrogen
deployment in otherwise non-viable areas. Ultimately, it can support more precise targeting,
sizing, and allocation of financial support across Europe.
Stochastic modelling
Given the inherent uncertainty around future financing conditions, further research could
explore stochastic approaches. For instance, future DRs could be varied across scenarios to reflect
potential geopolitical shifts, global interest rate trends, or macroeconomic instability. This could
involve Monte Carlo simulations or other probabilistic methods to understand the robustness
of hydrogen development under different risk profiles.
For public planners, this would allow
for stress-testing policy mechanisms; for private actors, it could inform risk-averse investment
decision-making under uncertain future market conditions. Hydrogen demand assumptions could
also be stress-tested by using this methodology.
Overall, integrating a richer and more realistic view of financing into system modelling
remains an important challenge for both academic and policy-oriented research. Doing so will
help bridge the persistent gap between public planning models and private investment logic.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to examine how discount rate assumptions influence the deployment of
hydrogen in Europe, both in terms of timing, spatial allocation, and technology choice. Using
the Balmorel energy system model, a series of scenarios were constructed to assess the impact of
financial assumptions on system-wide outcomes. The objective was not to predict future discount
rates, but to evaluate how different rates alter the pathways suggested by cost-optimal models,
and whether current modelling conventions are aligned with real-world investment perspectives.
The analysis reveals several notable insights. First, the baseline scenario using a uniform 4%
discount rate does not meet the stated hydrogen targets of the EU, highlighting the challenges
of scaling green hydrogen and the relevance of this topic.
Second, sensitivity analyses show
that hydrogen deployment, electricity generation mix, emissions, infrastructure investments, and
energy prices are all highly responsive to discount rate levels. Even modest changes in financing
assumptions can materially shift the trajectory of the modeled energy system.
Third, when
comparing the standard public-sector modelling approach with a private investor perspective
based on country-specific discount rates, a misalignment becomes evident: regions identified as
cost-optimal by public models may not be as economically attractive under actual investment
conditions.
These findings highlight a central recommendation: energy system models must incorporate
more realistic financing assumptions if they are to produce insights that resonate with both
policymakers and market actors. Bridging the gap between public targets and private feasibility
is not just a technical improvement, but it is essential for enabling actionable planning, allocating
funding efficiently, and increasing investor confidence in Europe’s hydrogen future.
On a deeper level, this work raises fundamental questions about the role and design of energy
system models. Should these models describe the world as it currently operates, marked by frag-
mented national policies, uneven economic development, and diverse investment risks? Or should
they instead project an idealised version of Europe, where capital costs are harmonised, financial
disparities are assumed to dissolve, and market integration is fully realised? The widespread use
of uniform discount rates across models may reflect this aspirational vision of European cohesion.
However, while politically convenient, such simplification risks obscuring the financial realities
faced by investors on the ground. Ignoring structural disparities in cost of capital can lead to mis-
leading investment signals, misallocated subsidies, and planning misalignments between public
institutions and private developers.
In a time of growing global uncertainty, closing the gap between high-level energy plans and
real-world investor decisions is both important and possible. Future energy models should not
only highlight optimal energy solutions, but also consider the financial realities that determine
whether those solutions can actually be implemented. Only then it can be ensured that what
models suggest turns into real progress on the ground.
