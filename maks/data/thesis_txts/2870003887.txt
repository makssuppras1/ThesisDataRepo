1 Introduction
Recent breakthroughs in ancient DNA research have transformed our understanding of
human evolution, unveiling complex patterns of migration, population interactions, and
admixture events between modern humans and archaic hominins like Neanderthals and
Denisovans. These advances were possible due to the ability to extract and sequence
genetic material from skeletal remains, offering insight into humanity’s past. However, the
analysis of ancient DNA presents unique challenges due to its fragmented and degraded
nature, often yielding sparse genetic information compared to modern samples.
To address these challenges, genotype imputation – a computational technique origin-
ally developed for modern genomes – has become a frequently used tool to infer missing
genetic information in ancient DNA by drawing on inheritance patterns deduced from
modern reference genomes. Relying on reference panels consisting of modern individuals
carries the risk of not accurately capturing the genetic diversity and haplotype structure of
archaic populations. While imputation has been successfully applied and tested in many
studies, its effectiveness in reconstructing ancestry tracts introgressed from Neanderthals
has not yet been adequately evaluated, leaving its reliability in regard to capturing
archaic segments insufficiently explored.
This thesis investigates the accuracy of genotype imputation when applied to reconstruct
archaic tracts in ancient human genomes. Through a controlled simulation framework, the
admixture between Neanderthal and early European populations was modelled, enabling
a systematic assessment of the accuracy under various scenarios of DNA fragmentation,
ancestry proportion and sample ages. By comparing the imputed genotypes to the ground
truth, performance metrics were evaluated in order to determine the influence of ancestry
proportions on the obtained results.
The aim of this research is to inform on the effects of archaic segments in geno-
type imputation, providing insight into the accuracy of this method for reconstructing
Neanderthal ancestry in ancient human genomes and the effects caused by high rates
of fragmentation. The findings highlight the strengths and limitations of genotype im-
putation when applied to ancient DNA, underlining the importance of representative
reference panels for research in both ancient and modern DNA.

2 Background
Grasping the interaction between genetic inheritance, DNA analysis and imputation
methodology is essential in order to understand the work that went into this project.
This chapter aims to establish the fundamental concepts and methods that are crucial
for the comprehension of the following chapters.
2.1 Population genetics and human evolution
Recent advances in ancient DNA research have reshaped and revolutionized the under-
standing of human evolution. These advances also affect the research regarding the
timing and geographic dynamics of the ancient populations, revealing a far more intricate
history than previously recognized.
Genetic evidence indicates that around 60 000 to 70 000 years ago, a small subset
of an ancestral African population migrated outwards, laying the groundwork for the
subsequent spread of anatomically modern humans (Homo sapiens) across Eurasia. This
migration to Eurasia consists not only of this single event, but various distinct waves
following through different routes. The divergence, begun by geographical separation,
led to genetic differentiation - the accumulation of distinct genetic variants in separated
populations due to adaptation to different environments, genetic drift, and varying
selective pressures. These adaptive processes have left clear genetic signatures, making it
possible to trace the genetic history of human populations and to infer the timing and
extent of ancient admixture events through the analysis of genetic markers and their
distribution patterns across populations.
In Eurasia, Homo sapiens encountered multiple archaic human species, including
Neanderthals and Denisovans, who had been established there for hundreds of thousands
of years. Not only did Homo sapiens and these archaic humans live in the same regions,
genetic studies have revealed that interbreeding did occur. These encounters left a
lasting genetic legacy, with modern non-African populations carrying approximately 1-4%
Neanderthal DNA, while some populations, particularly in Oceania, can have up to 6%
Denisovan ancestry, as visualized in Figure 1 [1], [2].

The distribution of archaic segments is not uniform across the genome. Some regions
are showing evidence of adaptive introgression where certain variants provided selective
advantages to modern humans while other regions lack archaic ancestry, suggesting
negative selection. [4]
2.2 Ancient DNA
Ancient DNA (aDNA) samples can mostly be found through skeletal remains of ancient
individuals, such as teeth and bones. Like the physical remains of these ancient individuals,
the DNA also undergoes ageing and degradation over time, beginning immediately after
an organism’s death. The genetic material suffers from chemical modifications as well as
fragmentation due to environmental exposure, such as hydrolytic and oxidative damage,
and biochemical processes like cytosine deamination. aDNA can therefore only be found in
short, damaged fragments, typically 30 to 100 base pairs in length, rather than complete
genomes like in modern individuals. The fragmentation follows a predictable exponential
decay pattern, with the majority of recovered fragments being closer to the 30 base pairs
in length. [5]
This leads to multiple challenges. Due to the fragmentation, only small pieces of the
original genome remain, making it difficult to reconstruct the genetic information of
ancient individuals. Additionally, contamination with modern DNA is a persistent issue,
as fragments from the environment, personnel or even microbial sources can easily mix
with the ancient material, falsifying the results.
To address these challenges, new methods have been tailored to the specialized needs
in the analysis of aDNA. The fairly recent advances in sequencing technologies, combined
5
==PAGE:6==
with strict authentication criteria such as characteristic damage patterns and fragment
length distributions, are allowing for better reconstruction and validation of fragmented
genomes, unlocking more details of the genetic history.
2.3 Genetic variation
All humans share about 99.9% of their genetic material, leaving only 0.1% of the genome to
genetic variation [6]. This genetic variation represents the cumulative result of mutations
acquired throughout human evolutionary history and the complex patterns of inheritance
that distribute these variants. Variants can occur in different structures, as visualized in
Figure 2, but the majority of genetic variation in humans consists of single nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs). These are single base pair differences in the DNA sequence,
occurring due to point mutations in the DNA substituting one nucleotide for another,
which can then be passed on to the offspring.
Figure 2: Types of structural variants [7]
SNPs serve as markers for genetic diversity and are the main focus for the analysis
of genomes. In modern human genomes, approximately one SNP occurs every 300 base
pairs, resulting in roughly 10 million SNPs per genome [8]. The density of SNPs varies
across the genome, with some regions containing more mutations due to recombination
activity or selective constraints. Ancient DNA typically yields far fewer observable SNPs
due to degradation and fragmentation, often covering only 10 to 50% of known variable
positions [9].
The entirety of SNPs that are inherited together on one chromosome are called a
6
==PAGE:7==
haplotype, which is a segment of DNA containing multiple genetic variants that tend to
be inherited as a unit. Each individual has two haplotypes, representing the maternal
and paternal chromosomes.
The length and structure of haplotypes are influenced
by recombination – the process of genetic material exchange during reproduction [10].
Recombination breaks up haplotypes over generations, but nearby or linked variants tend
to remain together, a phenomenon known as linkage disequilibrium (LD), which can be
influenced by demographic events, natural selection, population structure and the age
of the affected variants. LD patterns vary between populations and can extend from a
few thousand to hundreds of thousands of base pairs, providing crucial information for
tracking genetic ancestry and enabling accurate genotype imputation. [1], [11]
The persistence of specific haplotypes, especially those containing archaic hominin
variants, allows researchers to identify and track introgressed genetic material from
Neanderthals and Denisovans in modern human populations. These archaic tracts are
detectable as distinctive haplotypes that show greater similarity to Neanderthal or
Denisovan reference genomes than to other modern human haplotypes. The length
distribution of these introgressed haplotypes provides information about the timing of
admixture events, as older admixture results in shorter archaic segments due to more
generations of recombination breaking down the original haplotypes. [12]
2.4 Phasing and heterozygosity
Combinations of variants can either be present on the same physical molecule of DNA
(in cis) or on a pair of chromosomes with similar genes called homologous chromosomes
(in trans). The set of cis and trans relationships between variants are known as the phase
of the variants. Phasing or haplotyping describes the process of determining which of
the two haplotypes the genomic data originates from. Accurate phasing is essential for
understanding the relationship between genetic variants, as well as for imputing missing
information. Phasing can be achieved through computational (utilizing statistical models
and population data) or experimental methods (physically separating chromosomes). [13]
Heterozygosity refers to the presence of different alleles at a specific locus, meaning
an individual is heterozygote if it inherited two different alleles from its parents at this
position. High levels of heterozygosity indicate a diverse gene pool, while low levels may
suggest inbreeding or a population bottleneck, leading to a reduced number of genetic
variants being inherited. [14]

2.5 Variant Call Format
The Variant Call Format (VCF) is a widely used file format in genetics research. Instead
of storing the entire genome of each individual, the VCF format makes use of the fact
that most of the genome is identical between individuals and only stores the differences
of each sample in relation to a reference genome. As seen in Figure 3, each line contains
the position of the variation site along the genome (POS), a reference and alternative
alleles (REF, ALT), and the genotypes of each sample at that position. At each position
of each sample, the two numbers represent the genotype of the diploid chromosome, with
0 representing the presence of the reference allele and any other number the respective
alternative allele. The separator between the two numbers indicates the phase of the
genotype, with “ | ” standing for a phased genotype (where it is known which haplotype
each allele originates from) and “ / ” for an unphased genotype (where the haplotype
assignment is uncertain). [15], [16]
Figure 3: Example of a VCF file
2.6 Genotype imputation
Genotype imputation is a computational method to predict missing genetic information
in incompletely sequenced or genotyped samples based on known haplotype patterns from
complete genomes. It utilizes the fact that genetic variance is not randomly distributed,
but inherited in blocks or haplotypes, meaning that if certain variants are present in the
fragmented sample, the presence of other variants can be predicted with high accuracy
due to the linkage disequilibrium. Most modern imputation methods are built upon
variations of the Li and Stephens haplotype copying model, which provides a probabilistic
framework for representing an unobserved haplotype as an imperfect mosaic of observed
haplotypes from a reference panel. [11]
The imputation process typically begins with phasing to determine which variants are
inherited together on the same chromosome, after which the imputation process compares
8
==PAGE:9==
the incomplete phased data to an extensive reference panel, identifying segments of high
similarity between the target sample and reference haplotypes. Modern reference panels,
such as the 1000 Genomes Project, contain multiple thousands of individuals representing
diverse populations and capturing a wide range of genetic diversity [8]. The composition
and size of these panels can significantly impact the imputation performance. Larger
reference panels generally improve imputation accuracy, especially when they include
populations genetically similar to the target sample, as they better capture the variants
present in the target population. Several specialized algorithms, such as the ones used in
Beagle or Impute, have been developed, each with distinct approaches and specialized
applications. [17]
Imputing aDNA presents additional challenges due to the sparse and damaged nature
of the source material, and the potential genetic differences between ancient populations
and modern reference panels. However, when successful, imputation can significantly
increase the amount of usable genetic information from ancient samples, enabling more
detailed analyses of ancient population histories and admixture events. [18]

3 State of the Art
This chapter introduces the current state of the aDNA research by reviewing recent
advancements and their implications for the field, as well as presenting publications
related to the objective of this thesis.
3.1 Sequencing technologies
The analysis of aDNA has greatly benefited from the rapid evolution of sequencing
technologies, which can be divided into three generations. The first nucleic acid molecule
was sequenced in 1964, marking the beginning of the field of DNA sequencing, but the
first generation only started with the development of the Sanger sequencing method
multiple years later. In this generation, the methods were generally limited by their low
throughput and short reads. The second generation introduced simultaneous sequencing
of up to millions of DNA fragments, which significantly increased the throughput and
speed, making it possible to sequence whole genomes. The current generation is the
third generation, with the most prominent examples of platforms using third-generation
sequencing technologies being PacBio and Oxford Nanopore. [19]
The Oxford Nanopore MinION is a portable technology that allows single-molecule
sequencing of DNA and RNA, enabling the detection of modifications on individual nuc-
leotides [20]. PacBio combines the single-molecule real-time approach with fluorescently
labelled nucleotides, enabling long-read sequencing of DNA fragments with a length of up
to several tens of kilobases [21]. This all helps in improving the accuracy by eliminating
the amplification bias.
While short-read sequencing is still in use, e.g.
to determine the abundance of
specific sequences, long-read sequencing provides better genome coverage, enabling the
identification of more complex variants, caused by for example the ageing of DNA samples.
[19]
3.2 Authentication methods
Because contamination of the genetic material poses a great risk to the results of aDNA
analysis, robust authentication protocols have been developed to distinguish between
authentic aDNA and modern contamination. In a widely used method to determine
if a sample is authentically ancient, post-mortem damage patterns, such as cytosine
deamination, are analyzed [22]. Cytosine deamination results in C-to-T substitutions
in the DNA, which happen proportionally to the age of the sample and can be used to
identify and date ancient DNA. Using the empirical distributions of post-mortem damage
10
==PAGE:11==
(PMD), some authors define a PMD-score based on quality scores and polymorphism
levels relative to a reference genome to help distinguish true ancient sequences. [23]
A newer strategy to identify and remove human contamination is competitive mapping.
This method involves mapping sequenced data to multiple reference sequences of different
sources and comparing the reads to them. Reads that align exclusively to the target
reference genome are considered authentic, while those aligning exclusively to the human
reference genome represent human contamination and are removed from the data. This
method is currently only used on ancient faunal DNA datasets, but is a promising
approach for human aDNA as well. To make this complex process more accessible,
process pipelines have been developed and published to automatize the mapping [23],
[24].
More recently, tools such as PyDamage have been developed to automate the identific-
ation and estimation of aDNA damage. By modelling aDNA damage from deamination
data, PyDamage uses a likelihood ratio test to distinguish truly ancient data from modern
contaminants, increasing speed as well as the confidence in the data. [25]
3.3 Demographic models
Recent advances in aDNA analysis have refined the understanding of ancient human
populations, particularly regarding admixture events and population structures. By
analyzing high-coverage genomes from 45 000 year old remains, scientists were able to
reveal a Neanderthal introgression event that occurred approximately 45 000 to 49 000
years ago, dating the event to more recent times than previously estimated. This also
has implications on other aspects of the demographic model, suggesting that ancestors of
all non-Africans sequenced so far resided in a common population at this time and older
samples from outside Africa represent different non-African populations. Additionally,
the findings indicate a remarkably small effective population size of approximately 160
individuals. [26]
Other research shows that the oldest known Neanderthals inhabited southern Siberia
approximately 80 000 to 140 000 years ago, and notes that these Neanderthals likely
interbred at least once with the later arriving population of modern humans. Regarding
Denisovan populations, it was found that at least four lineages interbred with different
Asian populations, as well as evidence that Denisovans may have persisted longer than
Neanderthals. [27]
All these findings have caused revisions in the demographic models of human evolution,
providing more precise dating of Neanderthal admixture, establishing a clear temporal
framework for the early history of non-African populations and highlighting the need to
11
==PAGE:12==
account for the complex history of admixture between multiple hominin lineages.
3.4 Advanced Imputation Methodologies
Genotype imputation has improved drastically in recent years. The start of this devel-
opment can be attributed to genome-wide association studies (GWAS), which are able
to identify associations of genotypes and variants throughout the genome. Although
traditional imputation methods use statistical tools (as described in Section 2.6) or even
machine learning, new methods have been developed using deep learning based approaches
to infer the most probable genotypes. Because of this, the models are able to better
extract patterns and learn complex relationships from large amounts of data. While
deep learning-based tools only lead to modest accuracy improvements over statistical
and conventional machine learning-based methods, the developers have high hopes for
better results and usability in the future. [28]
Because a larger reference panel improves the imputation quality significantly, multiple
groups have started projects, e.g. the 1000 Genome Project, to collect diverse datasets,
in order to provide a collection of common human genetic variation and thereby achieve
more reliable results. [8]
3.5 Related works
The research of aDNA has been subject to a number of studies and developments in
recent years. While the imputation of aDNA has been improved and evaluated in multiple
studies, the performance specifically in regard to archaic segments of Neanderthal ancestry
has not yet been explored.
3.5.1 An empirical evaluation of genotype imputation of ancient DNA
Ausmees et al. (2022) conducted a systematic evaluation of genotype imputation of
ancient DNA, stating that the performance of imputation methods in the context of the
challenges of ancient DNA has not yet been fully explored. The only option to impute
aDNA is by using a reference panel from present-day individuals, possibly introducing
bias. To evaluate the performance, the authors used ancient genome data consisting
of high-, moderate- and low-coverage samples, and modern reference material from the
1000 Genomes phase 3 v5a panel, which they filtered to only include biallelic SNPs,
resulting in 27 904 756 markers over chromosomes 1 to 22. They used this reference in
two configurations: once using the entire data set of 2504 samples and once filtering for
European populations only, resulting in 254 samples. The imputation was performed
12
==PAGE:13==
using Beagle v4.0, in order to evaluate based on probabilistic input with regard to very
low-coverage data.
Overall, the results showed a high imputation accuracy, with genotype concordance
levels exceeding 99% for data with 1x coverage, and similar levels of accuracy at coverages
as low as 0.75x. The study indicated an improved performance with a larger reference
panel as well as the inclusion of ancient individuals in the study sample. The authors
found that the performance for heterozygous sites was consistently lower than the overall
concordance, with concordance levels reaching 95% at 1x coverage and as low as 82.5%
for the lowest coverage level of 0.1x. [29]
3.5.2 Achieving improved accuracy for imputation of ancient DNA
In another publication, Ausmees et al. (2022) presented a new implementation called
prophaser to improve accuracy and capturing of rare variation in comparison to previous
imputation pipelines. This tool is based on the full probabilistic Li and Stephens model
of haplotype frequencies and is designed for low-coverage data, particularly ancient
DNA. Where previous implementations prioritized on increasing computational efficiency,
prophaser is designed to instead focus on a more comprehensive and expensive algorithm,
while maintaining practical usability through parallel execution.
The authors evaluated prophaser against both single-step and two-step methodologies
based on the Beagle software, using both ancient samples and simulated data from
present-day individuals. The advantages of prophaser can be seen for coverages below
0.5x, where the concordance and fraction of evaluated SNPs are higher. For extremely
low coverages (0.1x), prophaser outperformed the other methodologies consistently across
the Minor Allele Frequency (MAF) spectrum, while showing very little improvement at
higher coverages. In regard to the fraction of evaluated SNPs, prophaser is not able to
outperform the Beagle pipelines for moderate to high coverage. While not improving the
overall performance, prophaser proves to yield better results in the low-coverage range,
showing promising relevance for the field of aDNA research where high-coverage data is
sparse. [30]
3.5.3 Evaluation of ancient DNA imputation: a simulation study
The effect that the degree of divergence of the ancient sample from the reference panel
has on the imputation accuracy has not yet been robustly quantified.
To explore
this, Escobar-Rodríguez et al. (2024) used a coalescent simulation approach to show
the effects of variation in genome coverage on imputation performance as a function
13
==PAGE:14==
of divergence from the reference population. The study systematically compared two
popular imputation software packages, Beagle and Glimpse, across multiple coverage
levels, divergence times and reference panel sizes.
The maximum imputation accuracy was reached by using the largest reference panel,
the lowest divergence between target and reference population and the highest coverage.
While more recently diverged target samples were overall better imputed, the greatest
impact of increasing divergence on the accuracy was at heterozygous sites with low MAF.
For the most diverged target samples (d = 1500 and d = 2000 generations), imputation
accuracies were in the same range, suggesting a possible divergence threshold beyond
which the performance will not deteriorate further. For samples with extremely low
coverage, the accuracy was consistently at 40% for heterozygous sites and 60% overall,
regardless of the imputation strategy or the divergence between target and reference.
Additionally, the study found smaller reference panels more closely related to the target
population to yield better results than larger panels of highly diverged samples. [31]

4 Methodology
This project aims to evaluate the accuracy and reliability of genotype imputation in
detecting and reconstructing archaic segments.
For a systematic assessment under
controlled conditions, the experimental setup combines a population simulation, creating
genomic data with known archaic introgression patterns, an artificial DNA fragmenting
process, simulating the degradation characteristic for ancient DNA samples, and state-of-
the-art imputation methods, applied to reconstruct the degraded sequences.
4.1 Population simulation
Figure 4: Schematic representation of population model

the ground truth regarding genotypes and the ancestry of genetic sequences is known
and reliable, eliminating possible biases caused by sequencing errors, contamination, or
misinterpreted ancestry. The simulation was set up to model a simple scenario of human
evolution (as seen in Figure 4), with an ancestral African population from which the

From the simulated populations, samples were drawn to create a reference panel and
test samples for the imputation. Additionally, the positions of the Neanderthal ancestry
tracts along the genome, as well as sample ages, were extracted and stored for later use
in the evaluation of the imputation.
In order to simulate the degraded nature of aDNA, the samples were intentionally
fragmented using a site masking approach. This approach simulates the fragmentary
nature of ancient DNA by systematically removing genetic variation sites from the
samples. Because the degree of degradation of aDNA can vary, the effects of different
masking rates on the imputation quality were observed.
4.2 Genotype imputation
The genotype imputation was performed via an external software, taking the test samples
and reference panel as input and delivering the imputed genotypes as output.

4.3 Evaluation of imputation accuracy
The imputation accuracy was determined by comparing the imputed genotypes with the
true genotypes from the simulation. Even though the imputation accuracy is generally
measured through the allele accuracy, the pair accuracy and the rate of heterozygosity
were additionally measured to provide a comprehensive assessment. In order to answer
the objective about the effectiveness of imputation in inferring Neanderthal tracts, the
metrics were compared between segments of Neanderthal and European ancestry.
Correctly imputed alleles
The proportion of correctly imputed alleles provides a fundamental measure of the
accuracy, showing how well the imputation captures individual genetic variants. Especially
for aDNA this metric is crucial, as it captures degradation and mutations that are affecting
singular bases.
Correctly imputed pairs
The rate of correctly imputed pairs evaluates the accuracy of complete genotypes, as it
considers both alleles at each position, testing whether complete pairs tend to be imputed
correctly. By comparing the correctly imputed pairs with the probabilistic distribution
of the alleles over the genotypes, it can be determined whether the imputation correctly
captures the patterns of alleles across the two chromosomes.
Rate of heterozygosity
In comparison to the previous metrics, the rate of heterozygosity is not affected by the
phase of genotypes. It is a measure often used to determine the genetic diversity of
a population and can be utilized to test whether both parent pass on the same genes,
showing if and how the inheritance of variation is captured by the imputation.

5 Implementation
The implemented pipeline follows the methodology outlined in Chapter 4, consisting of
generating population samples, imputing missing genotypes, and analyzing the results.
The pipeline spans multiple scripts and programming languages and is designed with
flexibility and extensibility in mind, to provide a foundation for future research.
5.1 Population simulation and sample selection
The first step, simulating the population history, utilized the population genetic simulator
msprime [38]. msprime is specifically designed for simulating coalescent ancestral histories
for sample individuals, making it an ideal engine for this application. The simulation was
set up and configured using slendr [39], a flexible and ergonomic R-based interface for
msprime simulation scripts. slendr was chosen for its ability to schedule sampling events
along the population simulation timeline and directly extract population genetics data.
The model was set up with the parameters from Table 1, simulating each individual
with a genome length of 108 base pairs. The genetic ancestry was simulated with a
recombination rate of 10−8 and a mutation rate of 1.2 × 10−8 [40]. For the analysis, three
groups were selected:
1. 990 broad test samples, from evenly distributed time points between the present
and the end of the gene flow event. These samples reflect how the imputation
quality changes over time.
2. 990 focused test samples, from the 1000 years directly following the gene flow event.
As the amount of Neanderthal ancestry varies more early after the admixture, these
additional samples allow for better observation of imputation quality in relation to
Neanderthal ancestry.
3. The reference panel, consisting of 1000 random individuals from the European
population at the present time.
To prepare the data for further processing, test samples and the reference panel were
stored in compressed VCF format. Since slendr’s VCF export function does not allow for
a direct application of a site mask to the data, a custom export function was implemented
to mask a certain rate of variation sites before exporting, in order to create fragmented
aDNA samples. Additionally, the Neanderthal ancestry tracts and age for each test
sample were stored in separate parquet files for later use in the statistical evaluation.

5.2 Genotype imputation
For the imputation, Beagle 5.5 [41] was used. Beagle, along with Impute and Glimpse,
is one of the most widely used imputation tools in population genetics and is the most
common choice for imputation in aDNA studies [29]–[31]. The software accepts input
data in the form of phased genotypes stored in VCF, though the reference panel can
alternatively be supplied in the binary bref3 format, which was used in this study for its
faster read and processing speed.
While VCF files record divergence from the reference genome, samples frequently
exhibit sites displaying “0|0”, indicating concordance with the reference genome, such as
the genotypes at positions 143, 618, and 629 for samples EUR_2 and EUR_3 in Figure
3. This phenomenon occurs because multiple samples have been stored in the same file
and VCF explicitly denotes the reference genome for positions where some samples have
variations and others do not. However, Beagle’s imputation algorithm only operates on
positions where no genotype is defined for the given sample, thus establishing a functional
distinction between 0|0 and no entry, despite both ultimately representing the reference
genome. To address this issue, all instances of 0|0 in the input file were replaced with
“./.”, denoting a missing or undefined genotype, enabling Beagle to impute the genotype
at these positions. This approach is equivalent to exporting and imputing each sample in
a separate VCF file, but allows for parallel processing across all samples.
After defining file paths and downloading Beagle, the imputation script (shown in
Listing 1) converts the reference panel to bref3 format. For each masking rate, the masked
test samples are cleaned of 0|0 entries as mentioned above, and then imputed with Beagle.
Beagle sets the IMP flag in the INFO field of a position to true if the genotype was
imputed. The script creates an index file with tabix, needed for the subsequent filter
command, before filtering for the imputed positions.

5.3 Data analysis
With raw file sizes of multiple gigabytes, loading all genotype data into memory using
standard R or Python tools quickly surpasses memory capacity. To reduce the memory
footprint and increase throughput DuckDB [42] was employed. DuckDB is an embedded
analytical database system that runs in the process of the host application. It is specifically
designed for analytical workloads and excels at executing complex queries on larger than
memory datasets.
Preprocessing with Polars
The evaluation was rerun many times during development. To save time and increase
iteration speed, the genetic data was preprocessed once, frontloading many computation-

later on.
The preprocessing transformed only one file at a time, enabling loading the whole file
into memory for faster processing. This allowed the use of Polars [43], a DataFrame
library for Python that offers a much faster CSV parser compared to DuckDB.

6 Results
This project assessed the accuracy and reliability of genotype imputation in reconstructing
archaic segments. The simulation was validated on the heterozygosity and Neanderthal
ancestry, in order to ensure that the achieved results are conclusive and of relevance for
research on real-world samples.

To validate that the simulation parameters lie within the expected range of real-world
samples, the heterozygosity ratio and the Neanderthal ancestry proportion were calculated
for all samples.

This trend is visualized in the 25 %, 50 %, and 75 % quantile regressions in Figure 6.
They clearly show the stable population median of Neanderthal ancestry over time, with
the 25 % and 75 % quantiles converging to the median after the initial rapid decrease in
variation.
While the trend of Neanderthal ancestry proportion closely resembles what would
be expected from real data, the numerical values might look unrealistic at first glance.
Modern European populations are estimated to have between 1 % to 2 % Neanderthal
ancestry, while Figure 6 shows just over 10%.
But the metrics might not be directly comparable to real-world samples: A part of
the discrepancy is caused by the different method used here for the determination of
Neanderthal ancestry, which works by definition of descent, where Neanderthal sequences
in ancient samples are estimated by resemblance. When DNA from ancient individuals is
found, sequences are compared to DNA sequences known to be from either Neanderthal
or Homo sapiens. Sequences strongly resembling Neanderthal are declared to be of
Neanderthal ancestry, whereas all others are assigned as human. Because of this, sequences
originally of Neanderthal descent but evenly resembling Neanderthal and Homo sapiens
are defined as human. Another contributing factor is the fact that the simulation is a
very simplified toy model with static population sizes and no migrations or interaction
with other populations besides the initial admixture. For more complex and realistic
models, more accurate ancestry proportions can be expected.
Considering that the concrete values do not influence the trend of Neanderthal ancestry,
the obtained results remain comparable to those achieved with ancient samples.

7 Conclusion
This thesis presented a simulation framework to evaluate the accuracy and reliability of
genotype imputation in reconstructing archaic segments in ancient human genomes.
The simulation was designed to model a simplified scenario of admixture between
Neanderthal and early European populations.
While using a simplified population
model, the simulation was validated to ensure the key parameters, such as heterozygosity
ratio and Neanderthal ancestry proportions, were realistic enough to provide conclusive
and relevant results.
The imputation accuracy was assessed using multiple metrics, including allele accuracy,
pair accuracy, and heterozygosity ratio. As expected, the results showed that imputation
accuracy decreases with increasing sample age and higher masking rates.
Notably,
the accuracy was significantly lower for Neanderthal segments compared to segments
of European ancestry. The divergence between allele and pair accuracy indicated an
imbalance where too many alleles were imputed to be homozygous, meaning a loss of
genetic variation. Neanderthal ancestry proportion was found to be the dominant factor
influencing imputation quality for samples of similar age, accounting for around 80 % of
the variance in allele accuracy in this simulation.
These findings provide new insights into the factors affecting the accuracy of genotype
imputation when applied to ancient DNA with archaic ancestry. The lower accuracy for
Neanderthal segments highlights the challenges in reconstructing archaic tracts using
modern reference panels that may not fully capture the haplotype diversity of archaic
populations, also underlining the relevance of representative reference panels in the
imputation of modern samples. The results emphasize the importance of considering
sample age, degree of DNA fragmentation, and the genetic divergence between the ancient
target population and modern reference panel when interpreting imputed genotypes and
estimating genotype probability.
In order to achieve even more conclusive results in future iterations, a more realistic
simulation could be designed by considering various factors. Implementing these changes
would enable future researchers to draw conclusions about the imputation accuracy in
real-world settings, which are not only trend-accurate but also numerically correct.
1. Longer sample genomes lead to a more meaningful number of variation sites,
providing more data points that contribute to more conclusive results.
2. A larger reference panel improves imputation accuracy through a better representa-
tion of diversity and rare variants.
35
==PAGE:36==
3. A model with a more complex demographic history portrays the effects of changing
population sizes and more realistic interactions between ancestry of different sources.
4. By using a more precise ageing method than masking and including empirically
derived patterns of DNA damage and fragmentation the imputation quality of
Beagle could be assessed more robustly.
Finally, estimating the extent of Neanderthal ancestry directly from the imputed data
and comparing it to the ground truth from simulation would provide further insights into
the reliability of imputation-based archaic ancestry inference.
In conclusion, this thesis contributes to the understanding of genotype imputation
performance in the presence of archaic admixture, highlighting areas for improvement and
future research directions. The simulation framework developed here lays the foundation
for more comprehensive evaluations and method development to advance the field of
ancient DNA analysis.
