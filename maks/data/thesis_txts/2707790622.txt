Abstract
With the IPCC 2021 report once again emphasising the need for climate action, European
legislators and the society-at-large are increasingly demanding more environmentally sustain-
able products. Circular business models have recently gained traction due to their eco-effective
strategies but manufacturing companies are struggling with implementing circular practices.
80% of a product’s environmental impact is determined in the early design stages which comprise
product architecture development and modularization (PADM). Large manufacturing compa-
nies utilise modularisation to increase product variety while keeping internal variety and com-
plexity low and this entails standardisations limiting freedom of design for whole product fam-
ilies. Thus, the impact of environmental decisions in PADM are leveraged, and they affect
multiple departments. Hence, PADM is a complex practice with multiple conflicting objectives.
Despite many benefits of modularization on circular economy many companies fail to redeem
them due to insufficient trade-off management (TOM) and lack of knowledge and skills.
This empirical design research proposes a qualitative, visual TOM approach in three overarching
steps to improve the quality of environmental considerations in the PADM. A central element
of the Circular Modularization Framework (CMF) is the Modularity Impact Model, which is
the first to map and visualise causal relationships between modularity properties and circular
strategies.
The CMF is evaluated in two case study workshops. This thesis contributes with a prescriptive
approach supporting PADM practitioners with cooperative TOM to improve the environmental
performance of product portfolios.

Learning Objectives
The Master’s Thesis should let students plan a project and complete it while achieving certain
learnings. The students of this report have set up the learning objectives below.
A student who has met the objectives of the course will be able to:
1. Identify and reflect on measures when proposing architectures for new products.
2. Consolidate key drivers, goals, and strategies for incorporation of environmental aspects
in the product architecture development and modularization practices at a case company.
3. Identify and reflect on the key synergies and contradictions between Circular Economy
and modularization.
4. Reflect on how environmental aspects can work in conjunction with other aspects.
5. Provide argumentations for incorporating environmental sustainability into the product
architecture development and modularization process, highlighting potential benefits and
disadvantages.
6. Compare and critically analyze the case company’s environmental- and architecture strat-
egy.
7. Identify and reflect on the key success fac tors and barriers for trade-off management
within product architecture development and modularization.
8. Identify and systematize best practices for trade-off management and decision-making
during the product architecture development and modularization process.
9. Analyse and compare different trade-off management strategies to understand their ap-
plication opportunities.
10. Develop and test a framework for incorporating environmental sustainability and manag-
ing induced trade-offs in the product architecture development and modularization pro-
cess.
11. Identify and reflect on limitations and improvement opportunities for the developed frame-
work.

1.1.
Introduction
Since the 1980s, many groups in society have expressed their concerns about environmen-
tal risks and climate change, due to the unsustainable consumption of resources and emission
of greenhouse gasses (GHGs) (Grin et al., 2010). These concerns have within recent years
grown into a shared sense of urgency, as it is clear that the increase of GHGs in the atmosphere
is caused by human activities and the global warming is a direct consequence (IPCC, 2021).
The increasing temperatures are contributing to higher water levels, more frequent floods, and
shifting weather patterns which is threatening the global water supply, food production and bio-
diversity (IPCC, 2021). Climate change becomes more extreme concurrently with the global
warming, and it is therefore critical to decelerate the global temperature growth (UNFCCC,
2015). Global warming will exceed fatal levels in the 21st century unless significant reductions
in carbon dioxide and other GHGs occur in the coming decades (IPCC, 2021). For this reason
Climate Action is one of the UN’s 17 Sustainable Development Goals and policy-makers are
forced to take action (Potting2017; EC, 2015, 2020) which pushes the sustainable development
forward as companies must find ways to incorporate and fulfill new legislative requirements and
deliver value propositions that can accelerate the transition towards a regenerative growth model.
Fortunately, new development models and principles have emerged within the past decades to
enable a greener production, consumption and environmental sustainability such as ecodesign
and circular economy (CE).
Ecodesign is an enabler of environmental sustainability, and involves consideration of envi-
ronmental issues within the product development of manufacturing companies, as a means to
identify and minimise the environmental impacts throughout the products’ life cycle (McAloone
& Pigosso, 2021). Ecodesign consists of a range of ecodesign strategies with a corresponding set
of guidelines for the development of products with better environmental performance (Vezzoli
& Manzini, 2008). The strategies and guidelines are supported by a wide range of methods and
tools that can be applied to realize the desired strategies (D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014). Despite
the growing recognition of ecodesign, the approach has not been adopted widely due to lack of
knowledge to execute the theories in practice (D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014).
A model enabling environmental sustainability that has gained significant traction in recent years
is CE. It is breaking up with the non-sustainable, linear, cradle-to-grave consumption, which
have dominated the entire social system since the beginning of the modern industrial society
(Grin et al., 2010). CE proposes a circular cradle-to-cradle model designing out waste, circu-
lating products and materials for as long as possible and regeneration of nature (EMF, 2013).
The circular model builds on three overarching strategies concerning resource flows: slowing-,
narrowing- and closing loops (Bocken et al., 2016). The slowing strategy is addressing resource
deceleration and seeks to extent the product’s use-phase. The narrowing strategy addresses re-
source efficiency by using fewer resources per product, and the closing strategy entails the closing
of resource cycles. Manufacturing companies should aim for adapting their products to fit into
the circular strategies by design (Blomsma et al., 2019).
It is commonly estimated that 80% of a product’s environmental impact is determined in the
early design stages where conceptual design decisions are made (Ramani et al., 2010; Tan et al.,
2017). It is therefore essential to integrate ecodesign and CE into the early development deci-
sions, to make substantial environmental impact reduction of a product. Product architecture

development and modularization (PADM) is by definition a part of the early product develop-
ment stages, as it lays the foundation for the subsequent product design stages (M. Meyer &
Lehnerd, 1997; Sanchez, 1999; Ulrich, 1995). As product architectures and modularization
are used to achieve product variety while maintaining low internal variety and complexity the
discipline involves standardizing product platforms. These standardizations limit the freedom
of design at later stages of entire product families making the importance of incorporating en-
vironmental sustainability into PADM practices even more evident (Kim & Moon, 2017).
Recently, the role of modularization in the CE and ecodesign has been researched pertinaciously
highlighting several benefits (Machado & Morioka, 2021; Mignacca et al., 2020; Sonego et al.,
2018). These include enabling repair, maintenance, upgrade, reuse, remanufacturing etc.
Despite encouragement of manufacturing companies to adopt ecodesign and CE to become
environmentally sustainable many are still struggling utilising PADM as an enabler for imple-
menting CE and ecodesign. A central barrier preventing the application is the lack of knowledge
and skills among practitioners. (Machado & Morioka, 2021; Mignacca et al., 2020).
There is a need for understanding how early high-level architectural decision affect the envi-
ronmental sustainability of a product family, as a means to understand how trade-offs can be
managed and exploited to implement CE and ecodesign from the very beginning of the prod-
uct development. Involved stakeholders with different responsibilities often reveal conflicting
objectives, and hence, trade-off problems to be settled. Managing these trade-offs and making
decisions in such a multi-stakeholder environment is challenging and does not become easier
with the inclusion of environmental aspects.

1.2.
Research Goals and Questions
The academia indicates a lack of knowledge on how to use PADM as a means to enable environ-
mental sustainability within product family architectures and modularisation. PADM involves
complicated trade-off decisions, as PADM contains numerous parameters affecting strategies
and drivers differently. Thus, incorporating environmental aspects into PADM will require the
ability to navigate within these trade-offs to develop the most advantageous products architec-
tures and modules. This thesis aims to support PADM practitioners by using TOM and PADM
to improve the environmental performance of product portfolios. The research question to be
answered is question RQ0:
How to support trade-off management in product architecture development and
modularization to improve environmental performance of product portfolios?
To support answering the main research question RQ0 the following sub-research questions
(SRQ) will be attended to:
• SRQ1: Which practices exist in academia that support incorporation of environmental aspects into
the product architecture development and modularization practices?
• SRQ2: What barriers are companies facing in relation to incorporating environmental aspects in
product architecture development and modularization practices?
• SRQ3: What is the support required for the practitioners to successfully incorporate trade-off man-
agement into the product architecture development and modularization process?

2.1.
Design Research Methodology
The goal of this thesis is to develop a support tool for design practitioners based on consolidated
or new research, methods, and tools. Therefore, the methodological approach and structure of
the thesis is based on the Design Research Methodology (DRM). The DRM is developed by
Blessing and Chakrabarti (2009) with four main phases; Research Clarification (RC), Descrip-
tive Study I (DS-I), Prescriptive Study (PS), and Descriptive Study II (DS-II). The methodology
aims at supporting an integrated and systematic way of conducting design research to increase
the opportunity at obtaining valid and useful results (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Utilizing
DRM will in addition provide guidelines for a more systematic planning of research, context
for positioning research projects relative to other design research, encourage reflection on the
applied approach, allow a variety of research methods and approaches (Blessing & Chakrabarti,
2009).
The subsequent subsections provide a detailed understanding of the objectives described for
each phase of DRM in the context of this thesis. The objectives for each phase was supported
by a set of questions which either directly answered the SRQs defined in Section 1.2 or assisted
in providing insights.
2.1.1.
Research Clarification (RC)
The RC is review-based and aimed to identify and define targets for the design research project
including answering the SRQ1. This consisted of a preliminary literature review to explore
and understand essential theory and practices to establish a knowledge-base for the remaining
research. This included creating an overview of the current research landscape and contributions
from new design research. The main outcome was a description of current state-of-the-art
theories and practices within environmental development, PADM and trade-off management.
This knowledge was used as a baseline for the remaining thesis. To support achieving this
outcome the following questions were defined:
• Which theory and practices on environmental improvement, PADM, and TOM exist in literature?
• How can modularity affect environmental impact of product families?
• What are the barriers in academia for incorporating environmental aspects within PADM?
2.1.2.
Descriptive Study I (DS-I)
DS-I was mainly supported by empirical studies and a systematic literature review. The main
objectives were to understand and address barriers that companies are facing when incorporating
environmental aspects in the PADM process including key factors, requirements, and criteria
that is crucial to achieve for improving environmental performance of product portfolios. This
resulted in clarification of the support type and where it was needed, hence contributing with
insights for answering SRQ2 and SRQ3.
Therefore, this lead to determination of existing
problems and opportunities in the context of real case scenarios through empirical studies. The
empirical studies were conducted with a case company which will be described further in 2.2.
Through analysis and synthesis of insights from the empirical studies, the outputs contributed to
defining goals for the subsequent literature review. A systematic literature review was conducted
to examine the state-of-the-art PADM and TOM methods and tools to improve environmental

2.1.3.

The aim of the PS was to define and develop the type of PADM and TOM support necessary

3.1.1.
Ecodesign
3.1.1.1.
Introduction to Ecodesign
The practice of ecodesign has grown over the last decades. Due to recognition of the effects of
industrial pollution on human health and the environment, governments worldwide started to
launch environmental legislation in the 1980s (McAloone & Pigosso, 2021). To accommodate
the legislation companies started to adapt a preventive design approach with the aim of design-
ing out environmental problems of the product, even before the product reached production
(McAloone & Pigosso, 2021). This is considered as a favourable strategy as 80% of the environ-
mental burdens of a product is determined during the early design stages (Tischner et al., 2000).
Being able to prevent waste, minimize costs associated with waste-treatment, and improve re-
source efficiency were not only good for the environment but also a good business, which gave
birth to the ecodesign practices (McAloone & Pigosso, 2021).
Ecodesign is described as the integration of environmental aspects in the product design pro-
cess (Union, 2009). Ecodesign is defined as practices oriented to make environmental friendly
products and processes, by making impact reductions in the product’s life cycles consisting of
the material-, manufacturing-, use-, transport- and End-of-Life (EoL) stage (Borchardt et al.,
2011). The practices can be applied with different targets depending on which product life cycle
stage that is critical to improve (Sanyé-Menguala et al., 2014).
3.1.1.2.
Ecodesign implementation
Ecodesign practices can be classified into three main groups according to their characteristics in
regard to the subject of interest including ecodesign management practices, ecodesign opera-
tional practices and ecodesign techniques and tools (D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014).
The management practices are those related to the activities of product development, they are
generic and can be applied by any kind of company, regardless of the type of products developed
(D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014). Multiple ecodesign methodologies have been developed over the
recent decade, to support the selection of management practices. Some have been developed
with focus on specific products and product sectors, while others are generic ((Borchardt et al.,
2011); (McAloone & Pigosso, 2021); (Sanyé-Menguala et al., 2014)). However, the frameworks
often also include a variety of different ecodesign operational practices and ecodesign techniques
and tools.
The ecodesign operational practices, also known as ecodesign strategies and ecodesign guide-
lines (Vezzoli & Manzini, 2008), deals with the technical issues of product design, as it provides
strategies and guidelines for the development of products with a better environmental perfor-
mance across life cycles from raw material extraction to end of life. (D. C. Pigosso et al., 2013);
(Vezzoli & Manzini, 2008).
Many different ecodesign operational practices can be found within the literature. They are
often referred to as ”Design for X” where X can be replaced by any product life cycle step or
virtue e.g. design for manufacturing, design for disassembly or design for material recycling
(McAloone & Pigosso, 2021); (Rossi et al., 2016). Six ecodesign strategies with correspond-
ing guidelines are presented in Table 3.1. The strategies are elaborated briefly in the following
paragraphs based on the nomenclature presented by (Vezzoli & Manzini, 2008).

Minimise resource consumption means to reduce the energy- and material consumption. This
can be implemented within all life cycle stages of a product or system. Minimising the material
consumption concerns the input flow of materials, which should be minimised as much as pos-
sible to save natural resources and avoid emissions into the nature. However, minimising the
energy consumption is also important, especially within the use phase of electrical products, as
the continues use of energy will accumulate over the years.
Selecting Low Impact Resources and Processes means to select non-toxic & harmless resources
and renewable & bio-compatible energy resources. Certain materials and energy sources have
a bigger impact than others. Thus, it is important as a practitioner to consider avoiding use of
toxic and harmful materials and -energy sources. However, this strategy also stresses the impor-
tance of choosing materials that are renewable, to ensure resources for the future generations,
and bio-compatible resources with the capacity to return to natural conditions without excessive
accumulation.
Product lifetime optimisation means to design a product with a close fit between the product’s
actual lifetime and the product’s relevance on the market. However, products with long life-
time will generally secure lesser environmental impact, as products with short lifespans creates
un-timely waste and an indirect impact caused by the demand of product replacement. Thus,
to minimise the environmental impact of a product, the practitioner should strive for designing
products with a long market relevance by e.g using the guideline “Facilitate upgrade and adapt-
ability” and long product lifetime.
Extending the lifespan of materials means making materials last longer than the product they
are part of. This can in general be performed through two different processes. The materials
can be recycled or cascaded to become an input for a new product-system as a secondary raw
material. The use of secondary raw materials are beneficial as this prevents the use of virgin
materials which often will have a larger environmental impact. When the characteristics of the
material no longer satisfy any requirements, the material can be combusted to recover the en-
ergy potential.
Facilitating disassembly focus on how to design easily disassembled products in which parts can
be easily and economically separated from each other. Thus, facilitating disassembly is an ex-
tension to Product lifetime optimisation and Extending the lifespan of materials, as easy disassembly
enables i.a. the ecodesign guidelines facilitating upgrade, textitfacilitating repair, facilitating main-
tenance, textitfacilitating remanufacturing, textitfacilitating recycling, textitfacilitating cascading,
textitfacilitating energy recover, etc.
System Design for Eco-efficiency means to design systems that can stop the industry’s economic
incentives for selling as many products as possible. This can be accomplished by shifting from
a business which only produces and sell products, to a business of selling products and services
jointly to fulfil the customer’s needs. This dematerialise the value-chain, as the companies don’t
earn their money on selling as many products as possible, but by providing service(s) jointly with
a product for as long as possible. However, this often requires a reconstruction of the business
model and surrounding system of the product.
Numerous ecodesign techniques and tools are proposed within the literature for supporting the
implementation of ecodesign management practices and ecodesign strategies. However, it is
important to emphasise that ecodesign is not only about developing an environmental product,
as the traditional product requirements have to be fulfilled aswell. Thus, the ecodesing practices
should be conducted alongside conventional design approaches (Vezzoli & Manzini, 2008).

3.1.1.3.
Benefits and Barriers of Ecodesign Guidelines
By utilizing a holistic life cycle perspective through the product development, ecodesign appli-
cation creates environmental value in addition to the traditional product requirements such as
functionality, performance and function(D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014)). Furthermore, ecodesign
offers economic and social benefits and opportunities as well (Sanyé-Menguala et al., 2014).
Environment-related product responsibility is an enabler for long term success, as it promotes
product innovation, improved product quality, compliance with environmental legislation, and
reduction of risks and costs (D. C. Pigosso et al., 2013). It can also improve the company’s im-
age, which can allow the company to differentiate themselves from their competitors and enter
new markets (Sanyé-Menguala et al., 2014).
Despite the growth of ecodesign development practices and the recognition of potential ben-
efits, its application has not yet reached companies worldwide (D. C. a. Pigosso et al., 2014).
The companies do not possess the required technological knowledge, the required know-how
to change and manage the design procedures, or does not have the time and the finances to
perform these activities (Borchardt et al., 2011); (Van Hemel & Cramer, 2002); (Rossi et al.,
2016). The large number of tools present are making it difficult to select the most suitable tools
for the companies’ specific needs. The lack of a systematization of existing ecodesign practices
and the continuous generation of new ecodesign techniques and tools are preventing studies and
improvement of the excising ones. Additionally, it prevents their integration into the product
development process which also is a barrier for a widespread implementation (Bey et al., 2013).
Many of the ecodesign strategies e.g. minimise material consumption and product lifetime optimization
are eco-efficeint meaning during more with less as a means to reduce the impact on the world’s
carrying capacity and slow depletion of natural resources (G. Wang & Côté, 2011). Thus, eco-
efficiency is not a long term solution, as it is only about being less bad. However, some suggest
that eco-effective solutions are the answer, where there not only is a focus on minimising waste,
but also a focus on closing material flows and circulate in cycles (G. Wang & Côté, 2011).
3.1.2.
Eco-efficiency and eco-effectiveness
Along with the increased focus on sustainability since the 1980s, eco-efficient activities have
emerged (Young & Tilley, 2006). These activities aimed to support corporate sustainability
and competitive advantages in a so-called win-win situation. The mantra was: doing more
with less - spending fewer resources without compromising or even increasing value in order to
reduce environmental impact. This strategy, however, is not sustainable on the long term as eco-
efficiency only is about being less harmful (G. Wang & Côté, 2011). For this reason Young and
Tilley (2006) argued that businesses instead should strive towards becoming eco-effective. The
idea of eco-effectiveness is to mimic nature’s ability to restore and regenerate resources to restore
and enhance the environment. In eco-effectiveness waste is considered to be a commodity and
this view entails designing products in such a way that makes it easier to restore and regenerate
them. The research area of Circular Economy (discussed in Section 3.1.3) embraces this view
and is focused on researching viable business models around it (EMF, 2013).
3.1.3.
Circular Economy
3.1.3.1.
Introduction to Circular Economy
In recent years, Circular Economy (CE) has increasingly been gaining traction in academia, in-
dustry, and among policy makers (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017). The concept of Circular Economy

has been introduced by a multitude of authors according to Ghisellini et al. (2016) e.g., Ander-
sen (2007), Bocken et al. (2016), and Su et al. (2013). The concept of CE already emerged in
the late 1970s in the form of ”regenerative design” (EMF, 2013). More recently, contributions
from The Ellen MacArthur Foundation and European Commission have particularly acceler-
ated the transition from a linear economy towards a circular economy (EMF, 2013). Political
and legislative agendas contribute to setting the cornerstones e.g. the ’Circular Economy Action
Plan’ by EC (2020) which pushes industry to accelerate their transitions .
EMF (2013) describes CE as an industrial economic model ’restorative by intention; that aims
to rely on renewable energy; minimises, tracks, and eliminates the use of toxic chemicals; and
eradicates waste through careful design’ which is in agreement with Geissdoerfer et al. (2017)’s
definition. Figure 3.2 developed by EMF (2013) showcases the Butterfly Diagram as a new
systems perspective. The system contains hierarchical flows recirculating both biological- and
technological products, materials and nutrients through the economic system. Key principles of
the system are design out waste and keep maximum value of resources (EMF, 2013). If designed prop-
erly, waste can be ’eliminated’ if the biological and technical products are designed to adapt to
the material flows e.g. through disassembly or refurbishment. Building resilience through diversity
is another principle focusing on accommodation of the uncertain and fast-paced developments
through modularity, versatility, and adaptivity of products and components. To rely on energy
from renewable sources is another principle encouraging an efficient shift away from fossil energy
sources to renewable ones. To introduce and think in ’systems’ is a key principle of understanding
complexity and interdependencies of infrastructure, environment and social contexts to ensure
system development around products to enable them to stay in the loops and not dissipate.
Waste is food highlights the potential of reintroducing flows of non-toxic biological nutrients into
the biosphere with restorative loops. For the technical nutrients this can be done through e.g.
recirculation of materials.

CE does not only provide a restorative industrial ecological system but moreover it contributes
to generate profits from flows of material and products over time (Bocken et al., 2016). Imple-
mentation and innovations of circular business models plays a significant role in enabling eco-
nomic viability necessary for successful restorative systems in a circular economy. For instance,
there are high potential profits in recycling certain materials instead of using virgin materials
(McKinsey, 2017). The economic opportunities are worth billions and support substantial net
material savings, mitigation of volatility and supply risk, drivers for innovation and job creation,
improved land productivity and soil health, and long-term resilience of the economy (EMF,
2013). These benefits provide drivers for organizations to adapting more CE-oriented business
models for transitioning towards a more circular economy. To enable these ’CE-oriented’ busi-
ness models, various circular principles or practices can be incorporated (Pieroni et al., 2019).
Particularly, manufacturing companies are interesting to support as they are significant users
of materials and energy, produce large amount of byproducts or waste, and contributes im-
mensely to employment sectors and GDP (Jaeger & Upadhyay, 2020; Rashid et al., 2013).
This provides an opportunity for these companies to create new value to their customers and
decouple value provision from linear resource consumption (Blomsma et al., 2019). Further-
more, manufacturing companies can gain reductions of cost through sustainable supply chain
and end-of-life management, open markets for recycling and remanufacturing, environmen-
tally friendly products that save energy and reduce pollution generation, environmentally sound
management practices for organizational and supply chain resilience, etc. (Kumar et al., 2019).
EMF (2013) mentions that CE benefits can be achieved through applying circular economy
principles through circular design allowing improvements in ’materials selection and product de-
sign e.g. standardisation/modularization of components, purer materials flows, and design for
easier disassembly’.
3.1.3.2.
Strategies of Circular Economy
To support the constant emergence of new CE terminologies Bocken et al. (2016) saw the
necessity to specify concepts and tools for businesses transitioning towards a circular model. In
one conceptual framework, the distinction between linear and circular models can be expanded
into anticipating resource cycles as three-dimensional strategies; slowing, closing, and narrowing
(Bocken et al., 2016). By slowing resource loops use-cycles of products, it can be extended
and/or intensified e.g. by designing long-life products and servitization. On the other hand,
’closing resource loops’ focuses on recycling and creating circular flow of resources to close the
loop between post-use and production. Lastly, through narrowing resource flows it aims for
utilization of fewer resources per product. The three-dimensional approach is illustrated in
Figure 3.3.
In addition, contributions from Blomsma et al. (2019) further support the incorporation of CE
principles and strategies in early stage developments of Circularity-Oriented Innovation (COI).
The work builds on research by Potting2017 proposing a circular strategies framework called
’Circular Strategy Scanner’ illustrated in Figure 3.4. It includes a variety of circular strategies
categorised into CE paradigms providing an overview and visualisation ’to support visioning in
COI processes’ especially for manufacturing companies. The definitions of the strategies can
be found in Table 3.2. Among the recirculation strategies the model in Figure 3.4 highlights
a hierarchical relationship indicating that some strategies should be selected over others due to
reduced value.
With a multitude of circular strategies it can be difficult to navigate and select strategies. How-
ever, is supported by Hildenbrand et al. (2020) who presents the ’Market and Product Decision
Tree’. This aids in strategy selection by providing users questions about market demands and
feasibility of recirculation based on product properties to guide them towards the various strate-
gies.

3.1.3.3.
Mitigating Barriers for Circular Economy
When implementing principles and strategies towards barriers can occur as significant changes
to the entire organization including stakeholders is often required (Jaeger & Upadhyay, 2020).
“Barriers to the Circular Economy – Integration of Perspectives and Domains” (2017) presents
multiple barriers for manufacturing companies through qualitative research including a shallow
understanding of the CE concepts and principles preventing evolutionary change towards a cir-
cular economy. An indication of risk aversion highlighted the difficulty for disruptive changes in
the organization. The need for integrating functions and aligning views also posed as a barrier
as the representatives lacked an understanding of collaboration and shared action towards CE.
Expectations of rapid financial gains and cost reductions hindered participants in understanding
benefits of long-term system changes. Kumar et al. (2019) agrees with barriers such as lack of
understanding of CE principles and qualified personnel on CE while economic barriers are posed
as high upfront investment, high cost of eco-industrial establishment, etc. Environmental barri-
ers hindering CE implementation the most are considering as inadequate resource management
systems, as well as a lack of available green incentives to promote ’greener’ activities to save water,
energy, and materials (Kumar et al., 2019). In addition, Jaeger and Upadhyay (2020) proposed
that some other barriers for manufacturing companies are the lack of less resource-intensive
models, lacking information on circular product design hindering strategies as reuse, disassem-
bly, remanufacturing. Sihvonen and Partanen (2016) presents that implementing practices for
improved environmental performance in early phases of new product development influences
attitude and enjoyment for the company practitioners, which is supported by Pinheiro et al.
(2019) developing a practical framework for integration of CE practices in new product devel-
opment. Development of new product families poses another opportunity as it is a key strategy
in developing cost-effective solutions and can provide significant improvement to sustainability
performance (Mesa et al., 2018). Thus, it leads to the necessity of exploring and understanding
early-stage design.

3.2.1.
Product architecture and platform strategies
The product architecture phenomenon emerged in the nineties and was among other authors
studied by Ulrich (1995). He defined the product architecture as: (1) the arrangement of functional
elements; (2) the mapping of functional elements to physical components; (3) the specifications of the inter-
faces among interacting physical components. The mapping of from functional elements to physical
components is illustrated with an example of a trailer on Figure 3.5. The contribution of defin-
ing the concept product architecture as consisting functions, physical components and interfaces
seem to be recognized well within academia. Ulrich (1995) also points out that product ar-
chitecture design can be utilized not only to design single products smarter but to manipulate
portfolios of products - product families.

3.2.2.
From Functional Structures to Modularization
Mapping functional structures are fundamental for design of product architectures and platforms
as functions are what constitute a product (Agus & Otto, 2001). Their relations, in terms of what
energy, information and material flows the different functions are part of, is what is mapped in
a functional structure. The map can be used to identify common flows that can be shared across
different product families. An example is provided on Figure 3.8 where a functional structure
of drilling machines is shown. The blue highlighted functions are common and standardized
for all the different product families while the shaded blocks are special functions.
Another approach to identify designs to be standardized is the Design Structure Matrix (DSM).
Also known as Dependency and Structuring Modelling it is a technique that can be used to var-
ious purposes besides product structure including project planning, organisation and processes
(“dsmweb.org”, n.d.). In designing product architectures component-based DSM can be used
to group components based on their interactions and inter-dependencies as a means for mod-
ularization. Rottke et al. (2012) defines modularization as breaking a complex system down into
an assortment of easily manageable components with well-defined interconnections. In application of

3.2.4.
The Effect of Modularity on Environmental Impact of Product Families
In Section 3.2 key contributions to the theory of product architecture design was presented. M.
Meyer and Lehnerd (1997) and Kristjansson and Hildre (2004) contributed with frameworks
and tools for developing market strategies for product families and Greve et al. (2020) and
Hackl et al. (2020) map effects of modularity properties on economic target values. In terms of
sustainability there seems to be a significantly stronger focus on the economic dimension than
on the environmental dimension when it comes to designing product architectures. However,
with the increasing focus of the society on the environmental impact of companies, knowledge
about the effects of product architecture design have become more relevant. This subsection
seeks to answer the question: How can modularity affect environmental impact of product families?
Firstly, it is important to keep in mind that the scope here is focused on modularity. Thus, it is
concerned with separating and grouping components and it is not about e.g. material selection
and component optimization. This distinction is important as some eco-design guidelines do
not apply to the design of product architectures.
As mentioned in Section 3.2.3 Erixon (1998) presented 12 module drivers in different life
phases of products. Some of the module drivers - service and maintenance and upgrading and
recycle - are reasons to modularize that harmonize well with circular economy (CE). These are
central strategies of the CE indicating that modularization can enable CE. The latter is a strategy
for closing resource loops while the others are slowing the resource loops by maintaining the

on how exactly to modularize to realize the benefits are rarely provided. An exception is Qian
and Zhang (2009) who contributed to environmentally conscious modular design by presenting
eight criteria as part of a design for environment (DfE) analysis model for modularity analysis of
electromechanical products. These modularity criteria and their associated indicators are similar
to Salvador (2007)’s modularity properties in that way that they can be used to analyse modular-
ity but they are specifically focused on capturing most of potential environmental impact. Three
of the criteria are describing the compatibility of the components grouped into module. That is
compatibility in terms of usage life, technology life, and materials. Usage Life Compatibility is rel-
evant as it is a big waste of resources when a module is replaced and sent to recycling even if only
one of its components fails. In order to reduce waste of residual life components with similar
usage lifetimes should be grouped. The same principle counts for Technology Life Compatibility
but here it is matter of obsolescence and fashion that should be taken into account. Components
of technology that is expected to become obsolete fast or out of fashion should be grouped into
same modules to minimize waste of residual life. Material Compatibility is about grouping com-
ponents of similar materials to ease recycling processes. Maintainability is the difficulty degree of
maintenance of a module/component. The last four criteria are concerned with the properties of
interfaces between modules. Geometric Connection is about enabling easy-to-disassemble which
is a key aspect of DfE (Qian & Zhang, 2009; Vezzoli & Manzini, 2008). In modular designs
geometric connections can allow modules to be easily broken without damaging the modules.
Disassembly Time and Disassembly Energy are also criteria describing the difficulty degree of sepa-
rating modules. Easy-to-Assemble describe the difficulty degree of assembling modules which can
be different from easy-to-disassemble. For example a snap fit connection is easy to assemble but
can be difficult to disassemble. These criteria require different amounts of expert estimates and
subjective judgements. Furthermore, material compatibility, maintainability, geometric con-
nection, and easy-to-assemble are not directly quantitative criteria and Qian and Zhang (2009)
recommend applying fuzzy logic to deal with the uncertain nature of expert estimates.

Barriers of Environmentally Conscious Product Architecture Design
In Section 3.2.4 several examples were presented of how modularity can be done in an environ-
mentally conscious way and contribute to the circular economy. So, why are more companies

not redeeming all these benefits that modularity brings? What are the barriers inhibiting compa-

Adopting modular design brings the risk of being copied (Ericsson & Erixon, 1999). Modular
products are easier to dismantle and understand for copycats making them subjects to reverse
engineering. This risk demotivates investing in modular innovations of products. Furthermore,
increased modularity of products entails higher internal complexity (Mignacca et al., 2020) . The

barrier. Obsolescence is a major challenge when developing a long lasting product architec-
ture. Several aspects of product architecture can become subject to obsolescence: functional
performance, product and manufacturing technology, logistics, economic, regulatory and social
obsolescence (Mignacca et al., 2020; Schischke et al., 2019). Schischke et al. (2019) argue that
modularity was developed in academia and still emerging in industry. However, on the political
level modularity is almost absent. This entails that there is a lack of policies aimed at promoting
modularity. Regulatory actions could for example support interface standardisation. In the en-
ergy sector there are still difficulties in standardising interfaces making it impossible to upgrade
and reuse. For example when the gearbox inside a windmill tower reaches end-of-life standard
interfaces would make it easier to reuse the tower (Schischke et al., 2019). An important barrier
is general lack of knowledge and skills. Knowledge about the link between modularity and CE is
still sparse. The skills needed to modularize for circularity are thus also lacking (Schischke et al.,
2019). The challenges within this discipline entails dealing with the ambiguities of modularity
and sustainability. This barrier is fundamental for the advancement of environmental practices
in product architecture development and modularization (PADM) as policies, for instance, do
not matter if the knowledge and skills to comply are missing. As this is an internal barrier it is also
a barrier that manufacturing companies can act on and try to overcome. Machado and Morioka
(2021) pinpoint that more research exploring conflicts and synergies between modularity and
sustainability is needed along with training of practitioners.
3.3.
Trade-off Management
This section seeks to provide an overview of knowledge and practices used for managing conflict-
ing objectives - trade-offs - in the context of manufacturing companies and product architecture
design and modularization (PADM). Trade-off management research is a source of relevant
knowledge that can be incorporated in a support for environmental sustainability in PADM
practices as the environmental aspect increases the number of objectives.
3.3.1.
The Sand Cone Model vs. Conventional Trade-off Management
To excel in different disciplines necessary to drive a sustainable business conflicting objectives
often leads to dilemmas, or trade-offs, where optimizing one parameter worsens another. This is
the conventional notion of trade-offs which is true unless there are some slack in the system. An
alternative to disturbing the balance of the parameters is to cumulatively increase capabilities as
suggested in the Sand Cone Model for manufacturing companies (Ferdows & De Meyer, 1990).
It suggests that the conventional trade-off principle always applies on the short term between the
key factors quality, dependability, speed, and cost efficiency, e.g., increasing speed will happen
on the expense of cost efficiency and quality. However, in the long term the parameters can be
increased by cumulatively increasing the manufacturing capabilities. The Sand Cone model on
Figure 3.11 suggests that this must happen in a hierarchical order where management’s attention
first should be on increasing quality. Dependability can then be increased subsequently. Next,
focus can be on speed and lastly on cost efficiency. Approaching trade-offs in this manner

Solutions for trade-off problems with conflicting objectives are called Pareto optimal (Mardle

of Pareto optimal solutions. These are placed along the Pareto Frontier as visualized with an

of discrete decision spaces with a predefined list of decision alternatives. These alternatives
are screened, prioritised, and ranked according to the stated criteria decisions and goals. The
MADM methods are categorized into different subgroups as seen on Figure 3.13. These consist
of the ’Out ranking’ methods e.g. ELECTRE and PROMETHEE, the Distance based methods,

3.3.2.1.
TOPSIS for Identification of Best Solutions
One commonly used method is ’Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solu-
tion’ (TOPSIS) which based on normalized weighting of criteria defines the positive and negative
ideal solutions and then calculate the distance of possible alternative solutions to the positive and
negative ideal solutions (Hwang et al., 1993). The alternative closest to the positive ideal so-
lution and farthest from the negative ideal solution is the solution decision-makers applying
this method should choose. As there can be an infinite number of Pareto optimal solutions a
prerequisite for applying TOPSIS is the subjective weighting of criteria.
3.3.2.2.
Weighting of Criteria with AHP
There are several methods that can be used for this task. One of the most widely used of
these is the ’Analytical Hierarchy Process’ (AHP) as this is particularly useful in group decision-
making situations (Forman & Gass, 2001). The process involves breaking the main problem or
objective into sub-objectives. These sub-objectives can differ in terms of being discrete attributes
or continuous; being carefully measured or roughly; being tangible and intangible depending
on the decision at hand. Subsequently, pairwise comparison of the sub-objectives is conducted
by the decision-makers ranking one sub-objective over the other. This is most often done by
human judgements while data can also be used. Lastly, prioritization or numerical weights of
the objectives can be derived and normalized in order for the TOPSIS to determine the best
solution.
3.3.2.3.
Managing uncertainty with Fuzzy Logic
Decision-making in early stages of product design is often dominated by incomplete informa-
tion and uncertainty making the human judgments necessary but often inaccurate and unreli-
able (Nhu & Tollestrup, 2017). Fuzzy logic is a concept that has emerged to accommodate that
humans make decisions based on non-numerical and imprecise information (Pelletier, 2000).
Unlike Boolean logic where things are either true or false, fuzzy logic is used to describe that
the truth may be somewhere in between 0 and 1. The use of fuzzy logic in decision-making
entails allowing human decision-makers to input judgements as linguistic variables. A fuzzy set
containing the uncertainty corresponding to the linguistic variable is then obtained from a mem-
bership function. An example of how the membership function converts the linguistic variable
into a quantified fuzzy set is shown on Figure 3.14. It shows that the term hot most often refers
to the highest temperatures but it takes into account that it in less likely cases refers to a lower
temperature which other decision-makers sometimes refer to as warm. As fuzzy logic takes the
uncertainty into account it can be used in AHP to make the weighting of criteria more accurate.
Fuzzy logic, AHP, and TOPSIS are applied in combination in several of the approaches iden-
tified in the systematic literature review in Section 4.2.2.

Visual Trade-Off Management for Cooperative Decision-Making
While mathematical models can provide performance indexes for decision support in choosing
the most optimal of alternative solutions, another approach that can help decision-makers is by
visualisations. In recent years, researchers have contributed with different kinds of visualisations
making it easier and appealing to analyse multi-objective problems (Tang et al., 2019). However,

directed at qualitative identifications, descriptions, and conclusions about conflicting objectives
(Hurford et al., 2014). It can be a measure to inform decision-makers about which two objectives
conflict or where trade-offs are weak and obvious. In the light of lack of knowledge being a barrier

useful. Windheim (2020) argues that visualisations are important tools in cooperative decision-

as it requires alignment between multiple disciplines and departments. Visualisations enable
efficient human information processing in particular in PADM (Gebhardt et al., 2014). It works
as a boundary object fostering the applicability and comprehensibility of PADM practices and
provides an objective base for discussion between different stakeholders (Cannon-Bowers et al.,
1993). Visual techniques provide shared mental pictures establishing alignment for decision-
making which supports justification and acceptability of decisions (J.-A. Meyer, 1996).

Recently, realisation of the need for eco-effectiveness and not just eco-efficiency has fostered sub-
stantial research on the circular economy (CE). In CE, eco-effectiveness is promoted through
managing resource flows in a restorative manner and viable circular business models. Three fun-
damental strategies for resource flow management constitute CE: narrowing, slowing and closing.
Several tangible strategies complying with the aforementioned strategies have been consolidated
in the Circular Strategy Scanner. Examples are designing efficient products to have low con-
sumption during use to narrow resource flow; repair and upgrade of products to prolong the
use-phase and slow the flow of resources; reuse, remanufacture and recycle products, parts and
materials to close the loop and avoid dissipation and depletion. The idea of CE is not merely to
benefit the environment but also to drive viable businesses by harvesting the benefits of ecode-
sign, decrease costs of materials and innovative circular business models.
For manufacturing companies, adopting circular business models entails designing products that
fit into the fundamental strategies of CE. Particularly in large companies multiple products builds
on the same product architecture meaning the design of these architectures can have leveraged
impact on the environment. Hence, the practice of designing product architectures was in-

and sustainability. 13 benefits of modularity for the circular economy were identified in all

facilitation of repair and maintenance, and enabling recirculation of products parts and materi-

guidance for which modularity properties to increase to improve environmental performance.
However, despite their research concrete guidelines largely lack and companies are struggling
with becoming more circular and sustainable. Several barriers have been identified in literature
to explain why.
In particular the internal barrier and lack of knowledge and skills is relevant for this research as
it indicate a demand for supporting guidelines and practices in architecture developers’ tool-
boxes. As PADM is a highly complex practice comprising multiple objectives, lack of know-how
on how to improve environmental performance can induce low prioritisation and sub-optimal
decision-making when competing with conventional objectives of PADM.

4.2.2.
Identifying state-of-the-art PADM and TOM Literature
The systematic literature review were conducted based on the approach presented by Biolchini
et al. (2007). The process of the systematic literature review was divided into three consec-
utive stages; Planning, Execution, and Result Analysis (Biolchini et al., 2007). The structure of
the study and review was intended to provide a scientific background for the project giving the
possibility for other researchers to reproduce similar results using the literature review approach.
In the Planning stage the aim was to identify objectives for the review. This included stating
the problem and relevant review questions. The definition of the problem, questions, and effect
was supported by the outcomes from both RC and the empirical study of DS-I. By specifying
the scope of the systematic literature review the Execution stage was initiated. One of the main
tasks prior to the Execution stage was defining the keywords and search string to use within the
literature search. An initial literature review was conducted to find articles with relevant content
and keywords that could contribute to defining a final search string (Appendix A7). The outcome
of the initial literature review contributed to developing the search string through a number of
iterations. The search string allowed for a focused literature search towards the defined problem
and question in the Planning stage. Furthermore, in the iterative approach different filtration
functions in the Scopus search engine has been inspected to help precise the search string results e.g.
’Source type’, ’Year’, etc. To systematically process the literature results, three filters with focus on
(1) reading title, keywords, and abstract, (2) reading introduction, conclusion, and figures, and (3)
reading full paper were created to exclude irrelevant literature. The general criteria for inclusion
were ’must contain tools for early-stage PADM with focus on knowledge in areas of criteria
processing, TOM, and decision’ which served as an overall consideration during each filtering
and used as qualitative guidelines for selecting relevant literature. To avoid a comprehensive
review in the initial execution and devoting time on irrelevant literature only a full review of the
papers was performed in Filter 3. To process the data it was necessary to classify and synthesise
the results both qualitatively and quantitatively. This lead to the Result Analysis of the systematic
literature review and a classification of the PADM and TOM methods and tools.

Trade-off Navigation Framework
Kravchenko et al. (2021)’s Trade-off Navigation Framework (TONF) can be used in multiple
phases of the product development process to transparently and explicitly examine the sustain-
ability trade-offs among different alternatives. The main goal of this matrix tool is comparative
in the TOM knowledge area. It can process both qualitative and quantitative inputs and the
nature of the output is qualitative. It consists of two phases: (1) determination of relevant
criteria/indicators (e.g. lifetime), acceptable ranges (e.g. 5-7 years) and the negotiability/non-
negotiability of each criteria/indicator; (2) support for the analysis of the input data from phase
(1) in a three step guide to (2.1) analyse the performance on non-negotiable criteria, (2.2) eval-
uate the performance on the negotiable criteria, and (2.3) a qualitative analysis of whether the
product performance on non-negotiable criteria can compensate for the trade-offs that are ac-
cepted in the negotiable criteria. The method used in this framework is generic and can even
be applied in different product development stages. It is fairly simple to use so it can be applied
by non-expert practitioners and can help document decision-making. It relies on input data
to be available for alternative proposals to be evaluated. Dependent on the difficulty of data
preparation the demanded time for use is assessed to be relatively low.
Value and Sustainability Assessment in Design Space Exploration by Machine Learning
A significantly different tool with a more comprehensive and technical approach is the one pre-
sented by Bertoni et al. (2020). They propose a detailed and overarching approach to support
decision-making by design space exploration including modeled environmental performance in
the early-stage of sustainable product development. It is an analytic visualization software in the
knowledge area of sustainability assessment and decision support. It considers the company’s
strategic long term criteria development, leading criteria identification and sustainability KPIs as
well as value models to explore value-driven design. This is all enabled by a machine learning
model which takes a combination of quantitative and qualitative data. It would take an expert
practitioner to utilize the model. The decision support is realized in a resulting visualization
as shown on Figure 4.7. Lines represent different designs based on design parameter variation
and the modeled performance in terms of value and sustainability. Designers can then analyse
the designs and consider aiming for the combinations of design parameters that have the best
modeled performances. The limitations to this approach is that it requires a substantial amount
of input data in the form of design variations with associated sustainability and value data to
establish a reliable machine learning model. While the authors argue that the machine learning
model is designed to be fast the substantial data preparation has high demanded time for use.
Furthermore, it is only able to optimize based on a limited number of parameters and it does
not explore radically different design concepts consisting of different parts and number of parts.
Sustainable platform Identification for Product Family Design

family design. This tool apply guidelines and DSM matrix to prescribe possible module struc-
tures and comparatively assess them for decision support. In their methodology a sustainable
platform has three characteristics. High sustainability, low risk to product redesign, and high
commonality. For degree of sustainability, the values of sustainability indicators such as envi-
ronmental, economic, and social indicators are aggregated to a single sustainability value of a

EoL Strategy Selection by Fuzzy Logic
Ma et al. (2018) proposes a model that identifies the most appropriate end-of-life strategy for
each component based on subjective criteria. It is a comparative tool in the knowledge areas of
criteria processing and decision making. It functions by incorporating uncertain expert estimates
by using fuzzy logic. A number of characteristics were pre-determined to possibly have an im-
pact on which end-of-life (EOL) strategy would result in the most optimal reuse of resources.
Using component A as an example, the evaluation criterion was component durability, and the EOL strategy
was reuse. The corresponding question for the expert was, “How do you evaluate component A’s component
durability performance with respect to EOL strategy of reuse?” The expert’s answer to this question follows:
“If component A’s duration is long, it is good for the reuse strategy”; thus, the component duration is “very
high” and the corresponding fuzzy set is (8, 9, 10) (Ma et al., 2018). As the criteria ’durability’ is very
good for reuse that criterion is weighted accordingly and the Technique for Order of Preference
by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) will likely select reuse as the appropriate EOL strategy
for the durable component. Several of the identified tools apply a combination of fuzzy logic
for weighting criteria and TOPSIS for determining the most optimal option. Again, the limi-
tation is that the computation of a quantitative decision support result is the lack of transparency.

5.2.

competences and validate that the developing framework was solving the tasks for which is was
intended. The iterations were following a lean startup approach where an MVP was created

development practices, which contributed to a systematic and scientific development of the MVP
within each cycle. An overview of the three cycles and the methods used within each cycle are

environmental sustainability considerations within the Vision and Strategy stage. Through a
morphology chart tools were selected and consolidated, qualitatively by the researchers on the
basis of the performance specification, to fit the Vision and Strategy workflow. Ultimately the
framework was tested with the CC, which provided insights that was utilised within Cycle 2.
The framework was reformed accordingly to provide a non-expert tool, as the initial framework

The results enabled an identification of dimensions and aspects for change both in the support

the workshop are summarized shortly in the bullets below.
• The mathematical complexity through e.g., use of machine learning was deemed too com-

5.2.3.3.
Conceptualization of Step 2 - Modularity Impact Model
As more clarity was wished upon how different modular designs impact the criteria and strate-
gies of interest, it was decided to construct a Modularity Impact Model. The purpose of this
model was to link modularity properties to the circular strategies they influence. For exam-
ple, increasing material compatibility of modules can affect recirculating strategies positively
and oversizing can have a negative impact on the minimize material content strategy (explained
in Section 3.1.1.2). This would support practitioners as they become aware of how the prop-
erties of their module designs influence their criteria and strategies positively and negatively.
It can support architecture design teams in getting aligned on which modularity properties are
relevant to increase or decrease in module designs. Knowing the cause and effect relationship
between modularity properties and target values is fundamental for taking informed decisions
in the PADM process. The usefulness of such a model was supported by the notion that the
CC’s architecture department currently use similar impact models made by Greve et al. (2020)
and Hackl et al. (2020) to achieve awareness and team alignment on how modularity properties
can affect economic target values.

The Study Design for Constructing the Impact Model
The modularity impact model should consist of modularity properties, the effects of increasing
or decreasing them, and ultimately the impact of the effects on the circular strategies. The cir-
cular strategies to be included was determined in Section 5.2.3.2. The modularity properties
and their effects needed to be researched. Specifically it first needed to be researched which
modularity properties exist and, second, what are their effects. The existing modularity proper-
ties were found by means of literature review. A list of acknowledged modularity properties was
derived from the research of Salvador (2007) who systematically defined the most recognized
modularity properties in literature as mentioned in Section 3.2.3 and Qian and Zhang (2009)
who proposed eight environmentally conscious criteria for modular designs as mentioned in
Section 3.2.4. Among Salvador (2007)’s modularity properties, only the ones in the bottom of
the hierarchy (oversizing, interface standardisation and function binding) shown on Figure 3.10 in
Section 3.2.3 were included. Combinability and commonality were included as induced effects of
them. Decoupling is excluded from the model as it is fundamental for modular structures (Greve
et al., 2020).
Regarding the effects of the modularity properties and their impact on circular strategies, only
very few links exist in literature. This stands in contrast to the amount of literature concerned
with the benefits modularity provides for the circular economy (Machado & Morioka, 2021).
Due to diverse impacts the effects of the properties of modular design often occur across de-
partments and are difficult to measure (Greve & Krause, 2019; Schwede et al., 2020). For this
reason, an interview study was conducted where effects of modularity properties on the iden-
tified strategies were recorded by means of expert interviews. This was necessary, because the
knowledge mostly is implicit and had to be extracted (Schwede et al., 2020).
Four experts with extensive experience and/or specialization within PADM and/or sustainability
in manufacturing companies participated in the study. Table 5.1 shows information about the
experts. They have worked on diverse modularization projects with various production strategies
which means they have different perspectives on modularity properties. This should increase
the validity of the statements. Before the interviews the modularity properties and the circular
strategies were clearly defined and explained to make sure the interviewer and the interviewee
were on the same page. A matrix chart with the modularity properties on the vertical axis and the
circular strategies on the horizontal axis were then used to systematically go through the effects.
The interviews had durations between one and two hours and were carried out over the course
of four weeks. As the interview format was iteratively improved, some experts were interviewed
twice. Additionally, the literature was assessed selectively to identify and map relations and
effects to identify the links between the identified modularity properties.

of the modularity properties are effecting multiple circular strategies positively and negatively,
which results in complex trade-offs that are hard to navigate which compromises the decision
process.
Due to the complexity it was deemed useful to include multiple stakeholders from various de-
partments because of their buy-in to the architecture design strategy was crucial for the further
development described with the ecosystem map in Section 4.3.1.2. To enable inclusion of mul-
tiple stakeholders, Step 3 was - like Step 1 and 2 - also required to be of a qualitative nature.
It should be able to include diverse perspectives coming from different departments to result in
the most suitable output for the company. Previous experience from cycle 1 and 2 also showed
that quantitative trade-off management tools wasn’t ideal, as the tools are difficult to customize
and the results are non-transparent.
Based on the requirements a filtering for relevant tools was conducted within the classification
library. The filter searched for tools classified as ”Trade-off management”, ”Decision support”,
”Qualitative Output”, ”Architecture application level” and ”non-expert practitioners”. The only
tool that accommodated the requirements was the Trade-off Navigation Framework (TONF),
which was used as primary inspiration for the Modularity Property Selection Tool (Kravchenko
et al., 2021). This tool was relevant as it navigates trade-offs in an iterative manner and includes
qualitative judgements. Its goal of helping to make trade-offs explicit by structured guidance is
very relevant for companies for whom it is new to incorporate sustainability in existing processes.
Modifications, however, was required to make it fit into Step 3. TONF rely on quantitative input
data which is not available at the stage of Vision and Strategy for architecture development.
Thus, the quantitative input was substituted by qualitative assessments which provided basis for
a subjective weighting of each modularity property. Furthermore, TONF analyses acceptability
of trade-offs which are not relevant as it is often not possible to speak about acceptable ranges for
the modularity properties’ associated indexes at this initial stage. Finally, the structured approach
to encourage a dynamic decision process and reinforce the knowledge of decision-makers about
potential risks and opportunities behind their choices was adopted into the Modularity Property
Selection. Besides TONF the Prioritising Opportunity Clusters tool were used to facilitate a
qualitative assessment of the most impact full modularity properties (Blomsma et al., 2020).
The initial framework was tested with the CC to identify necessary improvements of the concept,
introduction and realisation. The test was constructed as a real use scenario, where the CC should
assess a set of modularity properties enabling a circular strategy for a specific product range.
The test of the trade-off management tool provided valuable feedback and observations for the
further iterations. The main points are shortly summarised within the bullets below.

5.3.1.
The Circular Modularization Framework
The resulting ’Circular Modularization Framework (CMF)’ was developed on the basis of the
findings of the previous research phases, extensive conceptualizations of parallel steps, and eval-
uations of a coherent support approach. The approach for the CMF is showcased in Figure
5.2.
The CMF consists of three overarching steps with consecutive inputs and outputs; (Step 1) Iden-
tification of Circular Strategies, (Step 2) Modularity Property Identification, and (Step 3) Modularity
Property Selection. In the context of the CMF, a product range is defined as several products in
the organizations portfolio which can be categorized together. It is recommended to the user(s)
that the product range is ideally decided preliminary to Step 1. The categorization can be based
on, but not limited to (1) design, (2) component functionality, (3) similar product family, and
(4) customer requirements. The product range is decided by the organization and user(s) of the
CMF and will vary between framework iteration depending on which focus on at the specific
point of assessment. In the context of the CC, this depends on which part of the One-Plan
architecture they want to develop.
With the product range, identification and selection of relevant circular strategies suitable for
the product range is performed to retrieve most promising circular strategies as input for Step
2. A Modularity Impact Model provides linkage between modularity properties and the cir-
cular strategies. This visualisation of causes and effects of modularity properties and strategies
intends to align development teams on how to modularize for achieving one or more circular
strategies. Finally, to select the most relevant modularity properties for a given product range,
this framework contains a structured approach to assess impact of modularity properties on cir-
cular strategies. The intention is to make trade-offs between modularity properties transparent
and explicit which enables informed decision-making for finding the most favorable prioriti-
sation of modularity properties for the subsequent PADM. The three overarching steps of the
framework are further described in the following subsections 5.3.3-5.3.5.
The CMF intends to support manufacturing companies seeking the overall goal of improving the
environmental performance of their product portfolio by managing trade-offs to develop circular
product architectures and modules. In addition, the specific expected outcomes of utilizing the
CMF are:

Modularity Impact Model
40 effects of 11 modularity properties on 13 circular strategies have been mapped in the Mod-

the collective of the interview study with four experts and literature review. Several aspects are
worth noticing in terms of the impacts. All of the environmentally conscious modularity proper-
ties from Qian and Zhang (2009) tend to have positive effects. The only negative impact from
these modularity properties comes from material compatibility. Increasing this property can
lead to more material used hence conflicting with the circular strategy minimize material content.

eral negative impacts were identified to be caused by oversizing and function binding. While they
do have positive impacts as well, it is especially important to be aware of these. The reason for
this is that you can use it to enable one circular strategy but you risk diminishing another. This
information is crucial for companies who are ambitious about improving their environmental
performance.

Concluding notes on Prescriptive Study
To understand the support required for the practitioners to successfully incorporate circular
trade-off management into the product architecture development and modularization (PADM)
process cf. SRQ3, the following two research questions have been answered:
• How can the methods and tools identified in academia contribute to support PADM and TOM
processes for improving the environmental performance of product portfolios?
• How can the methods and tools be synthesised to provide effective support for the identified key factor?
The Prescriptive Study has suggested a support framework for practitioners of the Vision and
Strategy stage to incorporate circular trade-off management into the PADM process. The sup-
port named Circular Modularization Framework (CMF) consists of: Step 1 - Identification of
Circular Strategies; Step 2 - Identification of Modularity Properties; and Step 3 - Selection of
Modularity Properties.
It was developed throughout three cycles following a lean startup approach in close collaboration
with the case company (CC). This method has induced a user-oriented development process and
result. Testing concepts iteratively with the CC ensured a tailored level of comprehensibility and
emphasized the need for transparency and understanding of modular design decisions on envi-
ronmental performance. This has been accommodated by the development of the Modularity
Impact Model. It is a visual support tool linking effects of modularity properties to circular
strategies. While not claiming to be exhaustive it serves as a tool for team alignment and cir-
cular modularization guidance. The content is built on a foundation of expert interviews and
literature review ensuring content credibility.

created as the result of design thinking activities. The Strategy Compass, Cards and Selector

The identification of strategies and the Modularity Impact Model are coherent steps as the means
of enabling and disabling the strategies are given in the model.
The Modularity Property Perspective Matrix was developed to structure a discussion of the

7.1.
Discussion
7.1.1.
Key Findings
The goal of this research project was to investigate ”How to support trade-off management in
product architecture development and modularization to improve environmental performance
of product portfolios?”
The research conducted through literature and empirical studies identified a need for manag-
ing environmental sustainability within early architecture development stages. The empirical
study of DS-I defined the quality of environmental considerations in Vision and Strategy as the
key factor, as knowledge about how to incorporate environmental sustainability and trade-off
management within Vision and Strategy was absent. The proposed solution is the developed
Circular Modularization Framework (CMF) consisting of (1) Circular Strategies Selection, (2)
Modularity Property Identification, and (3) Modularity Property Selection.
7.1.2.
The Modularity Impact Model
The backbone of the CMF is the Modularity Impact Model in Step 2 linking properties of
modular design decisions to circular strategies. In contrast to previous studies identifying effects
of modularization on CE, this study the benefits and drawbacks have been coupled directly to
the modularity properties. As modularity properties describe the design of modules (Salvador,
2007), this linkage enables practitioners to analyse designs and make informed design decisions.
The validity of this type of model is supported by recent studies highlighting the importance
of visual decision support in product architecture development and modularisation (PADM)
practices (Greve et al., 2020; Hackl et al., 2020; Windheim, 2020). The authors of this thesis
also realised this after initial efforts in cycle 1 and 2 of utilising methods of multiple criteria
decision-making (MCDM) (Sections 5.2.1 and 5.2.2). This set of methods seems as an obvious
fit for trade-off management in PADM at first glance as the process entails numerous objectives to
be considered. Moreover, MCDM methods and tools were highly represented in the systematic
literature review of sustainable PADM trade-off management practices (Section 4.2.2. However,
the case company (CC), did not find the tools useful as they often relied on unavailable data,
were complicated to use and furthermore the results were difficult to trust and communicate to
stakeholders (Section 5.2.2). The strength of MCDM methods is that they are able to determine
the optimal alternative based on subjective preferences (Hwang et al., 1993; Pearman & Phillips,
2014; Tang et al., 2019). But, as knowledge and skills on how to modularize are generally
lacking, the challenge at hand is not to make the trade-off management a tiny bit more optimal.
The research indicated that it is rather to qualitatively identify and describe potential conflicts
and point out parts of objectives that are obviously weak. This is exactly the focus of visual
analytics in decision-making according to Hurford et al. (2014) and Tang et al. (2019) and
Windheim (2020). The latter argues that visual decision support is especially crucial in multi-
stakeholder decision-making as in the context PADM. During the case study workshops DS-
II the participants often returned to the Modularity Impact Model as a boundary object when
making a point (Section 6.3. This is because visual techniques establish common, mental pictures
of decision-making problems (Cannon-Bowers et al., 1993) supporting the rationalisation and
thus the acceptance of the decision (J.-A. Meyer, 1996). Moreover, visualisations are powerful
tools in human information processing, and especially in PADM (Gebhardt et al., 2014), which
makes the Modularity Impact Model practical and useful on a daily basis.

7.1.3.
Circular Strategy Selection
The Step 1 - Circular Strategy Selection works well in coherence with the Modularity Im-
pact Model as it raises awareness of the circular strategies and facilitates selection, which has a
synergistic effect on the model. It makes use of the model more targeted and allows it to re-
veal relevant trade-offs. In contrast to other circular strategy frameworks, such as the Circular
Strategy Scanner (Blomsma et al., 2019), the Strategy Compass does not seek to encompass all
circular strategies. It is merely comprised of the strategies which can be enabled or diminished
by modularisation according to the Modularity Impact Model. Researchers and practitioners fa-
miliar with circular strategies may say that some strategies are missing but they are purposefully
excluded to prevent redundant discussions. Another aspect that makes facilitation of discussions
and decision-making easier is that the Strategy Compass, like the Circular Strategy Scanner,
serves as a boundary object (Star & Griesemer, 1989) structuring the order of discussion.
7.1.4.
Modularity Property Selection
The CC’s One-Plan architecture is already balancing many sets of requirements e.g., product,
manufacturing, market and economic etc. Thus, to simplify the task of balancing trade-offs dur-
ing the implementation of environmental aspects, it was, in consultation with the CC, chosen
not to focus on the product, production and market trade-offs. However, the financial element
was included as Circular Economy (CE) often requires considerable upfront investments(Kumar
et al., 2019). On the other hand CE can also enable a long-term economic return, which is an
important element to consider when choosing modularity properties. Since the CC already has
an economic lens, it was redundant to guide these economic deliberations within the CMF. In a
wider perspective, inclusion of a guideline supporting the assessment of the economic perspec-
tive, within Step 3, would be beneficial for utilisation of the framework outside the context of
the CC. Eventually, the final architecture and modularization decisions need to involve, market,
product and production considerations as well, which is not supported by CMF. However, it con-
tributes with an assessment of which modularity properties to aim for to improve environmental
sustainability, which can be used in the overall decision analysis.
7.1.5.
Implications
In the Descriptive Study 2 (Section 6.3) it was found that the participants of the case study
workshops managed to select circular strategies and modularity properties using the CMF. The
associated interview sessions with stakeholders indicated that the implementation of the frame-
work would support the considerations of environmental aspects in their PADM Vision and
Strategy practices. Hence, the quality of environmental sustainability considerations in PADM
is improved inducing more environmentally sustainable product families and portfolio. Thus,
implementing the CMF as an environmental sustainability practice in large manufacturing com-
panies can potentially reduce environmental impacts and support transition to the CE. A model
of the impact is shown on Appendix A16.
7.1.5.1.
Limitations & Recommendations for the further work
While the potential of the CMF has been initially validated in this work, more research is needed
to further validate and improve the framework.
The development of the One-Plan iterations are conducted over several months, where numer-
ous module and architectures are developed and evaluated until a fitting product architecture is
defined. The CMF is facilitating the identification of the circular strategies with the biggest po-
tential and the modularity properties that should be prioritised to enable the strategy. However,
98
7 Discussion
==PAGE:107==
it does not propose any method for evaluating if a successful implementation of the modularity
properties have been accomplished. This compromise the usability of the CMF in two differ-
ent ways. Firstly, the practitioners rely on their own reasoning to assess whether the identified
modularity properties have been implemented to a level in which it actually will enable the
identified strategy. Secondly, the practitioners also rely on themselves when analysing whether
one module variant has more of the chosen modularity properties than alternatives. Guidelines
for specifically assessing the implementation level of a modularity property should be explored
to overcome this issue. However, one solution might already exist in literature. Besides the
eight environmental modularity properties, Qian and Zhang (2009) also proposed one indica-
tor for each modularity property. These can be used to analyse the level of implementation of
a modularity property to make a quantitative comparison of two defined modules or identify
improvement areas for a module within the conceptualisation phase. However, due to the time-
frame of this research this has not yet been explored further.
It is important to emphasise that the Circular Strategy Selection only involves CE and ecodesign
strategies that are directly affected by modularisation. Hence, it might be relevant to implement
other strategies besides the chosen strategies from the Circular Strategy Selection within other
parts of the organisation to maximise the environmental sustainability. E.g., it will for many
companies be beneficial to select low impact resources and processes within their product devel-
opment despite that it cannot be enabled by modularization.
Despite efforts to analyse vast amounts of data from existing literature and expert interviews,
the Modularity Impact Model is not an exhaustive source of causes and effects. The participants
of the case study workshops pointed out a missing effect which was important for their product
family (6.3. Thus, further research on the causal relationships between modularity properties
on circular strategies are needed to continuously update the model.
The approach does not provide systematic or prescriptive steps supporting synergistic combi-
nations of multiple circular strategies during Step 1. The overarching Step 1 merely focuses on
providing support to the key factor by providing know-how on environmental sustainability in
the PADM process enabling identification and selection of relevant circular strategies. However,
by mapping the connection between the circular strategies can allow the practitioners to find
opportunities to implement multiple strategies in favorable conjunction. This requires further
exploration of literature to focus on mapping relationships between strategies including bene-
fits or potential downsides when strategies are implemented jointly within PADM. The studies
should in addition focus on the feasibility and implications for companies to implement multiple
circular strategies. These additional steps may increase the complexity and comprehensiveness
for a support.
CMF is designed to suit the practice of Vision and Strategy in PADM, however, when mod-
ule design begins, practitioners are facing trade-off problems on a daily basis. Hence, research
on how the CMF, or parts of it, can be used routinely is needed. Particularly, it could interesting
to consolidate the Modularity Impact Model with the economic impact models developed by
Greve et al. (2020) and Hackl et al. (2020) which are currently used by the CC to make eco-
nomic decisions. The Modularity Impact Model could also be integrated in Windheim (2020)’s
Modularity Decision Dashboard which comprises 9 visualisations of important areas to consider
in PADM. The model would contribute by enabling application of the dashboard for circular
considerations as well.

8.1.
Conclusion
Manufacturing companies are largely struggling with redeeming the benefits of becoming more
environmentally sustainable and circular. Particularly manufacturing companies offering wide
varieties of products are challenged as the environmental sustainability must be incorporated
early in the product architecture development and modularisation (PADM) stage. PADM lays
the foundation for several subsequent development activities and it affects multiple stakehold-
ers within the companies. The practice of PADM involves trade-off management of numerous
objectives and cooperative decision-making is necessary. Choosing between Pareto optimal so-
lutions is difficult in multi-stakeholder environments and it does not become any easier when
incorporating environmental criteria. As general barriers of implementing environmental sus-
tainability practices are the lack of knowledge and skills, these criteria are often not considered
to nearly the same extent as other more established, conventional criteria. For this reason, the
main research question of this work has been:
How to support trade-off management in product architecture development and
modularization to improve environmental performance of product portfolios?
This design research contributes with a solution to this challenge by both systematic literature
and empirical research.
The first descriptive study made it clear that the case company (CC) struggled with improving
the environmental performance of their product portfolio because sustainability practices were
included at a late development stage. At that stage the freedom of design was limited hence
the opportunities for designing substantially more sustainable products were restricted. The key
factor was identified to be the Vision and Strategy stage of the PADM. This was the process of
setting a strategy and prioritising the ways of designing the modular product architectures for
product families. The multiple stakeholders participating in this practice, however, had difficul-
ties on settling and accepting environmental strategies due to lack of the know-how necessary to
achieve the strategies. Effective design handles remained largely unidentified and shared under-
standings of the effects of modular design decisions failed to appear. The result was uninformed
decision-making and sub-optimal trade-off management not improving environmental sustain-
ability.
To address these challenges efforts were made to consolidate existing trade-off management
and decision support methods and tools in the context of PADM. A systematic literature study
identified 50 tools but over the course of two prescriptive study cycles it became clear that none
of the tools were without major modifications suitable for the CC’s Vision and Strategy prac-
tice. The nature of the decision support needed were less of an optimisation problem-solver but
rather it was an understanding of the causes and effects of modular design decisions on strategies
(Section 5.2.2). The existing methods and tools concerned with PADM and sustainability, focus
on quantitative optimisation of designs rather than cooperative decision-making. No support
for cooperative, environmental decision-making in the context of PADM has been identified
indicating a gap. As a result, a framework supporting these challenges is developed.
This thesis is the first to combine fundamental theories of modular design for product families,
sustainability-oriented practices and cooperative trade-off management. This enables a novel

approach to encounter the challenges of sustainable PADM decision-making. A circular, qual-
itative, visual decision support framework is developed to avoid the the drawbacks of existing
methods. For the described target, the Circular Modularisation Framework consists of 3 over-
arching steps: Step 1 - Circular Strategies Selection; Step 2 - Modularity Property Identification; and

A central element of the framework, is the extensive Modularity Impact Model mapping out

the specific product family to enable informed and aligned decision-making. The elements are
the results of development cycles in close collaboration with the CC.
Implications
In a second descriptive study, two case study workshops with the CC was carried out along
with an interview study to evaluate a situation where the framework was implemented. The
practitioners who were stakeholders from various departments applied the framework to make
PADM decisions for one of their existing product families. The qualitative-naturalistic validation
method led to the following findings:
• The Strategy Compass and Selector enabled the practitioners to get familiar with the
strategies and they were able to utilise them to settle on three circular strategies for the
product family.
• The questions designed to spark considerations about suitability of the circular strategies
made it easy for the facilitator and the participants to structure the discussion.
• The participants were able to fairly quickly understand the Modularity Impact Model and
recognise the many modularity properties and their effects. IT allowed them to create a
common mental picture of the design options and their potential impact and consequences
for the circular strategies.
• The participants often referred back to Modularity Impact Model when discussing the
implications of increasing or decreasing modularity properties. Hence, it functioned as an
shared, objective base for decision-making.
• The macro-enabled automation ensured that no modularity properties were overlooked
nor incorrectly associated with strategies and it saved tedious double-checking.
• The Modularity Property Perspective Matrix in Step 3 efficiently structured the discussions
of impact and costs.
• The Effect/Cost Graph visualised the results of the deliberations for the participants allow-
ing them to continuously evaluate their choices. Moreover, the aggregation of modularity
properties of different strategies eased the final decision-making on modularity properties
to be prioritised in the subsequent PADM process.
• The participants estimated that the framework would indeed raise the quality of environ-
mental considerations in the PADM process.
102
8 Conclusion
==PAGE:111==
The evaluation research highlighted the following limitations:
• Unproductive discussions about definitions of circular strategies took place despite readily
available Strategy Cards with explanations. The participants did not use them for support.
• The participants pointed out some effects of modularity properties that were not mapped
on the Modularity Impact Model. These effects were important in this case suggesting
further research on modularity properties’ effects on circular economy.
• Some of the discussions, particularly, in Step 3 were cumbersome and time-consuming.
More efficient ways of facilitating the discussions are needed.
• Some of the participants pointed out that many important trade-off problems are not
automatically addressed using this tool. Objectives that they missed more focus on in
the discussions included manufacturing, economics, customer requirements, and material
selection.
• The CMF in its comprehensive form is not designed to support the subsequent stages
where module variants are detailed and assessed.
Next steps
With the initial overall validation of the Circular Modularisation Framework as an enabler for
cooperative, circular decision-making in the early PADM stages the fundamental potential has
been shown. The current version still has many areas to be improved. In the short term research
activities should focus on enhanced automation to make use even more efficient and comfort-
able for the stakeholders. As this is the first research to connect modularity properties to circular
strategies the mapped impacts are not exhaustive, and more research need to be conducted to
update the Modularity Impact Model.
Furthermore, research on how to favorably combine the circular strategies to redeem synergistic
effects is suggested. This could optimise the circular strategy selection of Step 1 and ultimately
improve the quality of environmental considerations.
Through the continuous refinements of the framework, the support can become even more
applicable for different manufacturing companies as the effects on the Modularity Impact Model
become more universal through further research. The authors see great opportunities within
researching how the Modularity Impact Model can be used in conjunction with existing visual
decision-making tools for the economic perspectives of PADM.

9.1.
Reflection
Throughout this project the team has aimed to complete the learning objectives listed in Section
iv. An assessment and reflection on the extent to which they have been fulfilled follows below.
Through literature and empirical studies several measures needed when proposing a new ar-
chitecture were identified. As the practice contains multiple objectives, such as the 12 module
drivers and sustainability aspects, it affects many disciplines and stakeholders. For this rea-
son visual trade-off management was identified as the most important measure. This includes
clarifying cause and effect relationships between modularity properties and circular strategies.
The empirical study indicated that the key drivers for the case company (CC) to improve en-
vironmental performance included customers demanding more sustainable products and future
legislative regulations. The CC did not have clearly defined sustainability goals. They did, how-
ever, have existing practices implemented to promote sustainable product solutions, but it was
at a late stages of the product development process.
The literature study in RC defined clear synergies and contradictions between modularization
and circular economy. Several strategies narrowing, slowing and closing resource flows were
identified to be enabled directly by modularisation. This thesis has contributed to linking them
to modularity properties in the Modularity Impact Model. Ecodesign and, in particular, Cir-
cular economy are examples of research suggesting that environmental aspects can work well
in conjunction with other aspects, such as economy. Products must be designed to fit into the
circular loops in order to make circular businesses viable. It is commonly estimated that 80%
of the environmental impact of products are determined at early design stages. As PADM per
definition is an early development stage laying the foundation for whole product families it is
highly relevant to incorporate environmental practices. The only formally implemented envi-
ronmental practice of the company was the SPS Toolbox on late development stages. In regards
to the PADM process, they did not have implemented strategies in place for environmental
PADM. In contrast, they did have ambitions of take-back. What makes trade-off management
and decision-making in PADM challenging is the mix of uncertainty, lack of data and multiple
objectives and stakeholders. Furthermore, lack of knowledge and know-how about how mod-
ularity properties impact strategies, leads to uninformed and sub-optimal decision-making. To
successfully achieve higher quality of environmental considerations in the PADM Vision and
Strategy stage, the barrier to overcome is highly communicative. A systematic literature review
identified and analysed 50 methods and tools for trade-off management in PADM. Quantita-
tive MCDM methods are useful for identifying the most optimal trade-off solution based on
subjective preferences. However, for the CC a qualitative, visual tool was needed to reveal and
communicate trade-offs and decision-making in the cooperative multi-stakeholder environment.
To address the challenges and encounter the drawbacks of existing tools the Circular Modular-
ization Framework was developed, tested and preliminary validated in descriptive case study
workshops. Limitations entail cumbersome discussions, and that not all objectives of PADM
are automatically discussed. Moreover, the Modularity Impact Model should be continuously
updated with new research on modularity properties’ impacts on circularity.
Throughout this project the team has aimed to complete the learning objectives listed in Section
iv. An assessment and reflection on the extent to which they have been fulfilled follows below.
