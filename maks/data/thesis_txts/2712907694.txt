Abstract
Carbon-carbon bonds and their formation are an essential part of organic chemistry. Many tradi-
tional methods generate or require the use of toxic and hazardous substances or produce stoichio-
metric amounts of waste. Alcohols are therefore an attractive and alternative reagent. They are
generally unreactive, but can be activated through methods such as acceptorless alcohol dehydro-
genation and the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction/Oppenauer oxidation.
This project describes the reaction of benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene under basic condi-
tions to form 1,3-diphenylpropan-1-one (Scheme 1).
Scheme 1: The formation of dihydrochalcone from benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene.
Based on the work performed by Kumar et al.,[1] and Azizi and Madsen,[2] previous Mas-
ter’s student Christian Falck Jørgensen proposed a radical mechanism for the reaction.[3] However,
through mechanistic studies a radical pathway could not be confirmed, and instead a polar mech-
anism with three diverging pathways was suggested. Acetophenone and phenyl acetylene were
found as intermediates. The optimised conditions for the reaction were a ratio of 1:1.5 for benzyl
alcohol and β-bromostyrene, 2 equivalents of KOtBu in refluxing toluene for 1 hour as concluded
from the experiments performed by bachelor students, Hans Forum Møller and Sarah Gyldendal
Melberg.

1
Introduction
An essential part of organic chemistry is the formation of carbon-carbon (C−C) bonds. The C−C
backbone is the foundation of all organic compounds. There is, and has, always been a great
interest to study the current techniques and to find new methods for forming these bonds, making
it possible for chemists to synthesise larger and more complex molecules.[4]
As the world advances further into the 21st century and the importance of sustainable devel-
opment rises, it has become more obvious than ever that the use and generation of undesirable
toxic and hazardous substances should be limited for chemical processes as part of the concept
of ”green and sustainable chemistry”.[5] Prioritising the more abundant 3-d transition metals as
catalysts and focusing on alcohols instead of alkyl halides as reagents are just some of the methods
that can be implemented to avoid the use of toxic metal reagents and generation of stoichiometric
amounts of harmful waste for a better atom efficiency.[6,7]
In 2020, Azizi and Madsen reported the α-alkylation of acetamide with benzylic alcohols fol-
lowing a radical condensation.[2] The group has previously also reported the acceptorless alcohol
dehydrogenation (AAD) of benzyl alcohols with a large variety of different first-row transition
metals.[8–13] This indicates that alcohols are a viable reagent regarding α-alkylation for C−C bond
formation and that chemists are not limited to the more reactive, yet also toxic, organohalides.[4]
One of the questions that therefore arises is, which other functionalities can be employed. In
2021, Christian Falck Jørgensen discovered the reaction shown in Scheme 2 while working on his
Master’s project in the Madsen group. It was observed that benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene
react to give dihydrochalcone, a ketone.
Scheme 2: The formation of dihydrochalcone from benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene.
The focus of this project was therefore to map out the reaction, optimise the reaction conditions
and determine the reaction mechanism to better understand the reaction itself. Mechanistically it
was of great interest to investigate if the proposed radical mechanism could be validated or if other
processes are involved in the formation of the product.
1.1
Carbon-carbon bond formation
Many methods of forming C−C bonds exist, including but not limited to: aldol reactions, α-
alkylations, Diels-Alder reactions, Grignard reactions, the Heck reactions, Michael additions and
Wittig reactions.[4,14] Most of these reactions are well known, the aldol reaction was discovered
by Borodin and Wurtz in the latter half of the 1800s[15] and in 2010, Heck was awarded the Nobel

alkene.[16] As with all chemistry, there is great interest to investigate in which ways the above
methods may be improved to become more green and sustainable.[4]
1.1.1
Aldol reactions
The advantage of aldehydes and ketones is their reactivity and versatility. Due to the difference in
electronegativity between the carbon and the oxygen in the carbonyl group, the carbon can act as
an electrophile. In the presence of a base, deprotonation on the α-carbon of the carbonyl occurs
to form an enolate ion, which can act as a nucleophile. If two carbonyls react with each other, the
nucleophilic enolate ion attacks the electrophilic carbon of the other to give the aldol product as
seen in the first half of Scheme 3.[14]
Scheme 3: An aldol self-condensation under basic conditions with acetone as the only carbonyl compound.
If large amounts of base are used, the aldol product may react further by condensation through
an E1cB mechanism, resulting in an α, β-unsaturated carbonyl as the final product (Scheme 3).
These compounds can then be used in e.g. Michael additions, Diels-Alder reactions, polymerisa-
tions or be hydrogenated.[14]
A mixture of aldol self- and cross-condensation products may be obtained if the starting mate-
rials consist of two different carbonyls with free α-proton, thus resulting in a low regioselectivity
and a lowered atom efficiency in terms of green chemistry.[17] Between two aldehydes, the side
products may be limited as seen in Scheme 4, but with unsymmetrical ketones the products are
almost endless. The reactivity may be limited if one side of the ketone is blocked by a tert-butyl or
aryl substituent or the carbonyl just does not have any α-proton available as in the case of benzalde-
hyde. The strong base, lithium diisopropylamide (LDA), can be used to irreversibly deprotonate
one carbonyl compound followed by the slow addition of the second in order to lower the risk of
self-coupling, which leads to only one cross-product being produced.[14]

Lewis acid metal catalysts may also be utilised to obtain stereoselective aldol products between
aldehydes and ketones, although these may suffer from chemoselectivity issues. Fortunately, in
the case of the Mukaiyama aldol reaction, this limitation is overcome by the preformed group IV
enolates. Silicone and tin enolates are relatively stable, are not formed in situ, and are able to
prevent aldol self-condensations.[17,18]
In the presence of water and either acid or base, retro aldol reactions can be observed on both
the aldol product and the α, β-unsaturated carbonyl. The mechanism is the reverse of the aldol
condensation as seen in Scheme 3. The retro aldol reaction of cinnamaldehyde (Scheme 5) has
been studied by Guthrie et al. to understand the kinetics of the aldol reaction in the ”forward”
direction without the same risk of cross-products. They found the equilibrium constant for the
addition of water to cinnamaldehyde to be 4.42 · 10−3.[19]
Scheme 5: The products obtained from a retro aldol reaction on cinnamaldehyde.[19]
1.1.2
α-Alkylation
The reactions of enolates from aldehydes and ketones are not just limited to aldol reactions as they
are able to react with many different electrophiles through SN2 type reactions. These electrophiles
could for example be halides, alkyl halides and α, β-unsaturated carbonyls as seen in Scheme 6.
Likewise, α-deprotonation can also occur in compounds containing esters, nitriles and nitroalka-
nes as the α-proton is sufficiently acidic enough to be removed by the base.[20] There are however
certain limitations, such as tertiary alkyl halides not being able to undergo SN2 reactions, and the
main drawback is the use of organohalides which contribute to harmful waste products.[4]

α-Alkylation on the enolate of a carbonyl requires the use of a strong base such as LDA to
avoid self-aldol reactions. Furthermore, the base also needs to be non-nucleophilic to ensure
that it does not react with the electrophile of the carbonyl. Variants of this base can be seen in
Figure 1. However, for aldehydes the rate of deprotonation is still not fast enough compared to
the rate of aldol self-condensation and it is often more useful to use an enolate equivalent such as
enamines, silyl enol ethers and aza-enolates to achieve α-alkylation instead of the aldol reaction.[14]
Figure 1: Lithium diisopropylamine (LDA), lithium tetramethylpiperidine (LTMP) and lithium hexamethyl-
disilazide (LHMDS).
Enamines can be synthesised from a reaction between an aldehyde or ketone and a secondary
amine in an acidic aqueous solution. The enamine is then able to react with an organohalide via
an SN2 type reaction, yielding an iminium ion. The iminium ion can then be converted back to the
carbonyl by mild acidic hydrolysis. If the starting material is an unsymmetrical ketone, the less
substituted enamine is typically formed because enamine formation is reversible. Thus, formation
of the less hindered enamine will dominate.[14]
Silyl enol ethers are able to react with tertiary alkyl halides via an SN1 type reaction as they
are less reactive and require the strong electrophilicity of a carbocation. Stable carbocations can
be formed from tertiary alkyl halides in the presence of Lewis acids such as TiX4 and SnX4.[14]
Primary amines can be used to make aza-enolates which are able to convert an aldehyde or
a ketone into an imine. Subsequent deprotonation with a strong base such as LDA produces the
aza-enolate which is highly nucleophilic and will react with alkyl halides that are available for
SN2 type reactions. Acidic work-up of the alkylated imine results in the alkylated carbonyl.[14]

1.2
Dehydrogenation of alcohols
The hydroxyl group in alcohols is a poor leaving group and often requires the conversion into e.g.
a halide to take part in substitution reactions. This may lead to additional steps in the synthesis
and unwanted side products. Therefore, dehydrogenation of alcohols into aldehydes and ketones is
an attractive method to functionalise the otherwise rather unreactive group.[21] Traditionally, car-
bonyls are prepared from alcohols with oxidation agents such as Dess-Martin periodinane (DMP)
or the harsher pyridium chlorochromate (PCC), chromium and manganese oxides.[14,22] However,
disadvantages such as the requirement for stoichiometric or excess amounts of reagents and the
generation of undesirable byproducts make alternative methods much more attractive. Two main
methods for this conversion exist in literature: the acceptorless alcohol dehydrogenation (AAD)
and the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction/Oppenauer oxidation (MPV-O).[23,24]
1.2.1
Acceptorless alcohol dehydrogenation
Acceptorless alcohol dehydrogenation is a method to dehydrogenate alcohols so they may be
utilised in e.g. the alkylation of carbonyl compounds to form new C−C bonds. Instead of the
oxidation agents mentioned in Section 1.2, transition metal-based catalysts are employed. The
reactions based on these catalysts have a high atom efficiency and only produce water as the by-
product.[24]
Early reports of transition metal-based catalysts use precious and costly metals such as Ru,[25,26]
Rh,[27] Os,[25] and Ir.[28] Their high cost however demands cheaper alternatives like the first-row
transition metals. The Madsen group has worked with many such catalysts[13] and especially Fe
is of great interest due to its abundance in the earth’s crust.[29,30] A difficulty that may arise is
the tendency for first-row transition metals to participate in single-electron reactions compared
to the second- and third-row transion metals’ two-electron reactions. However, by designing the

1.2.2
Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction/Oppenauer oxidation
The MPV-O reaction describes the forward and reverse directions of the reaction seen in
Scheme 9. From left to right, a carbonyl compound is reduced to an alcohol by hydrogen do-
nation from an alcohol which in exchange gets oxidised.
Scheme 9: Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction and Oppenauer oxidation.
In 1925, Meerwein and Schmidt performed the reduction of an aldehyde with ethanol using
aluminium ethoxide as catalyst. Aluminium catalysts were used due to their easy preparation.
Aluminium derivatives such as aluminium isopropoxide, tert-butoxide and phenoxide were used.
A drawback to the reaction is the need for stoichiometric amounts of aluminium catalysts which
lead to the development of transition metal catalysts for the conversion.[32] It has been reported
that the reaction may very well proceed without the need for transition metal catalysts as it has
been observed that catalytic amounts of simple alkali metal bases also promote the H transfer.
The ”transition metal-free” process is therefore an alternative to the method requiring aluminium
alkoxides and prevents the alcohols from being contaminated by transition metals, making it at-
tractive for the pharmaceutical industry.[23,33,34]
In general, the MPV-O reaction will proceed through a six-membered transition state where
both reducing alcohol and carbonyl compound are bound to the metal ion and the hydride. The
metal also provides a template to hold the reactants in the proper orientations as the hydride is
transferred. The mechanism is illustrated in Scheme 10.

1.2.3
Guerbet reaction
Guerbet alcohols is a material first synthesised by Marcel Guerbet in 1890. The reaction is related
to that of aldol reactions and produces β-alkylated alcohols and water under heat and catalytic con-
ditions. The starting materials are both alcohols and Scheme 11 summarises the reaction forming
the dimer alcohol.[35] The reaction can be performed using only a base in some instances, although
a co-catalyst is usually added to speed up the overall reaction. Various catalysts including Raney
Ni,[36] Universal Oil Products Co. (U.O.P.) nickel,[37] and the metal chromites of copper, man-
ganese or zinc have been employed in the Guerbet reaction.[38]
Scheme 11: The general mechanism of the Guerbet reaction. The two alcohol reactants do not need to be
identical.
During the reaction, the alcohol is first oxidised to an aldehyde, followed by an aldol conden-
sation where water is produced as the byproduct. The allylic aldehyde is hydrogenated to obtain
the β-alkylated alcohol. The disproportionation of two molecules of the aldehyde is possible as
a side reaction, giving the corresponding alcohol and carboxylic acid. This is also known as the
Cannizzaro reaction.[35] Guerbet alcohols and their derivatives such as Guerbet esters, acids and
sulfates can be found commercially in hair, skin, and other personal care applications,[39–41] and the
reaction itself has now additionally been applied in synthesising second-generation biofuels.[42]

1.3.1
Radical reactions
The reactions described until this point involve the movement of two electrons. If a covalent
bond breaks, it often leaves two charged intermediate species: a cation and an anion. Thus, a
heterolytic bond cleavage is ionic in nature and further reactions are dominated by electrophilic
and nucleophilic species. However, the shift of one electron or the homolytic cleavage of a bond
is also possible. Atoms and molecules with an unpaired electron can be found as free radicals and
are usually generated with the help from thermal or light energy.[20,43]
Radical chain reactions include three distinct steps: initiation, propagation and termination.
Molecules containing weak bonds undergo homolysis at low temperatures, making them suitable
radical initiators. Azo-compounds are good initiators for forming radicals due to their high decom-
position and low stability, and 2,2’-azobisisobutyronitrile (AIBN) is a traditional azo-compound
that requires elevated temperatures to generate the radicals.[44] This however means that AIBN is
unsuitable for use with heat-sensitive compounds,
thus other azo-compounds such as
2,2’-azobis(2,4-dimethyl-4-methoxyvalero-nitrile) (V-70) have been taken advantage of due to
their activation at room temperature or below.[45] Peroxides also undergo homolytic cleavage, pro-
ducing acyloxy and alkoxy radicals upon heating, making them another class of suitable initiators.
Available peroxide initiators include benzoyl peroxide, acetyl peroxide, tert-butyl perbenzoate,
and di-tert-butyl peroxide.[44] Organoboranes such as triethylborane have also been reported as
radical initiators.[46,47]
The initiators are not expected to take part in the reaction. Instead, they are required to initiate
the reaction before the propagation step. In Scheme 12 the homolytic cleavage of AIBN and
benzoyl peroxide are illustrated.
Scheme 12: The initiation of AIBN and benzoyl peroxide.
Propagation occurs once the initial radical is formed and may proceed by abstraction, addition
or elimination as seen in Scheme 13. During abstraction, a new bond is formed as the former radi-
cal abstracts an electron from a single bond. Single electrons can also add to a double bond, effec-
tively reducing it. This mechanism is often seen in the formation of polymers through free-radical
polymerisation. A radical elimination reaction is the reverse of a radical addition reaction.[14]

1.3.2
Radical stability
The ease of forming a radical is linked to its stability; a more stable radical is more easily formed.
A tertiary alkyl radical is more stable than a methyl radical because the bond dissociation energy
for the C−H bond in the methyl case is 439.3 kJ/mol which is larger than that of the tertiary alkyl
(400.4 kJ/mol). For carbon-centred radicals it is therefore convenient to express the stability using
the isodesmic H-transfer reaction with CH4 being the reference system.[48,49]
Another way to determine the stability of a radical is to analyse a molecular orbital (MO)
diagram of the system. The singly occupied molecular orbital (SOMO) will hold the unpaired
electron. The more stable radical will either have a SOMO with lower energy or an overall system
with a lower energy.
Like for cations and anions, electron-withdrawing groups (EWG), electron-donating groups
(EDG), and conjugation will stabilise radicals. Thus allyl and benzyl radicals are more stable
than alkyl radicals, and vinyl, alkynyl and phenyl radicals are less stable than alkyl radicals. The
orientation of the SOMO in the benzyl radical allows for overlap with the π-system of the ring,
whereas it is perpendicular to the π-system in the phenyl radical.[14]
EWGs such as carbonyls and nitro groups will stabilise the radical by overlapping an empty
antibonding π∗orbital with the SOMO. Two new orbitals are produced, and the only available
electron is placed in a new SOMO with a lower energy. EDGs such as alkoxy groups will instead

1.3.3
Radical trapping
Identifying a radical reaction pathway for a new reaction is not always straightforward. Sometimes
the mechanism may come as a surprise, especially if a reaction was believed to proceed via a
polar reaction and unexpected radicals suddenly emerge. Dimethylformamide (DMF) radicals
were observed in the presence of NaH[50] despite earlier statements of radicals not forming in
alkaline DMF. Hence, further research has found that the two solvents, dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)
and DMF, form radicals at room temperature when a few drops of potassium methylate (MeOK)
solution (25% in MeOH) were added.[51]
Radical trapping is an attractive method to search for radical intermediates. Radical scavengers
and radical clocks are molecules that are able to react with any radicals present in a reaction, thus
halting the rate of the reaction.[52]
Many different types of radical scavengers exist such as (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-yl)oxyl
(TEMPO), cyclohexa-1,4-diene, and 4-methyl-2,4-diphenylpent-1-ene (MSD) (Scheme 15). The
radical scavenger, TEMPO, is stabilised by four methyl groups adjacent to the amioxyl group and
will readily react with radicals, resulting in a stable adduct.[53] Meanwhile, Cyclohexa-1,4-diene
will either aromatise to benzene with the loss of two hydrogen radicals or combine with a radical
upon the loss of one hydrogen.[54] MSD is able to fragment upon the addition of a radical species
and produce cumyl radicals. These can then either combine into the major product, dicumene, or
disproportionate into isopropylbenzene and isopropenylbenzene.[55] Out of the three, cyclohexa-
1,4-diene has the disadvantage of a low boiling point, often limiting its application to reactions at
less elevated temperatures.

rates can be approximated. Should the reaction however not follow a radical pathway, the radical
clock scaffold will instead be found as part of the intermolecular product. Scheme 16 exemplifies

Kumar et al.: C−C bond formation of benzyl alcohols and alkynes
In 2019, Milstein’s group reported a C−C bond formation between benzyl alcohols and alkynes in
toluene using catalytic amounts of potassium tert-butoxide as seen in Scheme 17. The proposed
mechanism follows a radical pathway.[1]
Scheme 17: The reaction described by Kumar et al.[1]
The product of the reaction is an α-alkylated ketone, dihydrochalcone, which can be found
in natural products and can be synthesised through α-alkylation of enolates with alkyl halides,
transition metal-catalysed hydrogenation of chalcones and other methods that require a ketone
as a starting material. However, utilising other substrates instead of ketones is attractive for a
wider synthetic application. Thus, methods that utilise alcohols as the alkylating agent have been
explored.[1]
Compared to a previously reported reaction of alkynes with alcohols that requires a transition
metal-based catalyst (Scheme 18),[56,57] Milstein found that the ketone obtained sits on the side
derived from the alcohol instead of the side derived from the alkyne. This indicates an unusual
regioselectivity that has not been observed before.[1]
Scheme 18: Transition metal-based catalysis of alcohols and alkynes.[56,57]
Milstein’s group found that the optimised reaction proceeds at thermal conditions (125°C) for
24 hours in the presence of KOtBu (5 mol%) with a 1:1 ratio of benzyl alcohol to phenyl acetylene.
No formation of the ketone was observed upon the addition of aliphatic alcohols but product could
be found when using a variety of substituted benzyl alcohols and substituted phenyl acetylenes.
Upon investigating the mechanism, the influence of trace metals or light was dismissed by
control experiments and ICP-MS analysis, thus excluding a transition metal-based pathway. The
potassium ion was found to play an important role as a lower yield was obtained with both the cor-
responding sodium bases and addition of 18-crown-6. A hydrogenation/dehydrogenation pathway
could be dismissed as they did not detect H2 gas. A Markovnikov addition of water to the alkyne
was ruled out as there was no effect from molecular sieves and because of the placement of the

Upon optimisation, it was found that the major product was N,N-dimethyl 3-phenyl propan-

conditions are a 1:2 ratio of benzyl alcohol to acetamide, 2 equivalents of KOtBu in refluxing

Scheme 20: The reaction observed by Azizi and Madsen.[2]
Deuterium labelled experiments with PhCD2OH and PhCH2OD were carried out to investi-

rate-determining step. Deuterium was found incorporated in either α- and β-positions or exclu-

Solvent and reaction temperature
The high boiling and apolar solvent mesitylene was used in the original procedure. Such properties
are needed to ensure that the solvent does not decompose under very basic conditions. However,
mesitylene requires a very high boiling point for reflux, so toluene, o-xylene and dimethylfor-
mamide (DMF) were investigated as well.
Due to previous reports of radicals being formed in DMF under basic conditions,[51] it was
of interest to see if DMF would perform well as a solvent. One could expect DMF radicals to
promote a radical reaction. However, after 5 h, no product was formed and conversion of BnOH
and BBS was not observed (Table 1, entry 1). This indicates that the reaction does not follow a
radical pathway. The same yield of 32% was obtained for both o-xylene and toluene (entries 3 and

two are correlated. This could indicate that acetophenone is a degradation product as it would
make sense for there to be more degradation at elevated temperatures. One could therefore expect
a higher yield of product 7 if less BBS is converted into compound 26.

to determine the optimal solvent. The best result from the reactions performed in Table 1 were

Influence of additives
The reaction mixture is heterogeneous due the use of apolar solvents and a base which is not
soluble. There is a low concentration of KOtBu in the reaction mixture as it remains undissolved,
which makes it difficult for it to participate in the reaction. In an attempt to make the base more

and 18-crown-6 are able to complex with alkali metal ions, thus separating a cation and anion from
each other and increasing the solubility of the tert-butoxide anion. The advantage of 18-crown-6
over diglyme is its affinity for potassium cations.[60]

The conversion of BBS (84%) was similar compared to the earlier experiments, and likewise the
yield of 26 (16%). The boiling point of diglyme is 162°C which might explain that no significant
increase in yield was observed since a majority of the additive may be in the gas phase above the
reaction mixture.

2.2.1
The conversion of β-bromostyrene into phenyl acetylene
For the conversion of BBS into phenyl acetylene to proceed, KOtBu needs to be a very strong base.
In an acid-base reaction, the equilibrium will be pushed in the direction of the weaker acid and
the proton will move to the stronger base. In water, the pKa value of HOtBu is 16.[62] However, to
obtain a more precise picture of the reaction, it would make sense to use the pKa value of HOtBu
in an organic solvent. No experimental values can be found for compounds in toluene, but an
approximation can be made with DMSO. The pKa value of HOtBu in DMSO is 32.[63] The apolar
nature of the solvent makes the base stronger due to the lack of solvation from hydrogen bonds.
The elimination reaction of BBS to phenyl acetylene can therefore be expected in the presence of
KOtBu at elevated temperatures (Scheme 25). These types of reactions have also previously been
reported under similar conditions.[64]
Scheme 25: The conversion of BBS into phenyl acetylene.
With the conversion of BBS to phenyl acetylene (27) taking place in the reaction mixture, the
reaction resembles the coupling described by David Milstein’s group. Thus it could proceed by
the radical mechanism they proposed.[1] Furthermore, the conversion of BBS to phenyl acetylene
opens up for the possibility for even more side products such as the dimerisation of the alkyne.
Møller and Melberg performed a reaction with 1 equiv. of phenyl acetylene and 1 equiv. of KOtBu
but did not detect any dimerisation products.[61]
2.2.2
Stability of dihydrochalcone
Product stability may also explain why the conversion of BBS is so high compared to the low
yield of dihydrochalcone. Previously, the Madsen group studied the hydroxide-mediated cleavage
of C−C bonds.[65] If there are trace amounts of water present in the reaction setup, it may easily be
deprotonated and thus introduce hydroxide ions to the reaction mixture. Two reaction pathways are
possible, either through a monooxy anion or a dioxyanion. It was found that the former pathway
is favoured in poorly solvating solvents such as o-xylene while the latter pathway is favoured in
aqueous solutions.[65] The C−C bond cleavage of dihydrochalcone through both reaction pathways
is illustrated in Scheme 26 where pathway A goes through the monooxy anion and pathway B
goes through the dioxyanion. Unfortunately the presence of the carboxylate cannot be confirmed
by GC-MS. Benzene could not be identified either.

2.7
Future work
First, BnOH is oxidised to benzaldehyde due to trace amounts of oxygen, and BBS eliminates
to the phenyl acetylide ion in the presence of KOtBu. The acetylide anion can be obtained due to
the strength of the base in organic solvents since the pKa values of phenyl acetylene and HOtBu
in DMSO are 28.8 and 32, respectively.[63,66] Thus, the reaction will favour the formation of the
deprotonated phenyl acetylide ion. The two react to give intermediate 35 through a nucleophilic
addition. Intermediate 35 is then converted to an α, β-unsaturated ketone (36) with the help of
KOtBu. Here, two pathways become available: ketone 36 can be converted into species 38B
through an MPV-O pathway or it may undergo an addition of water to form β-hydroxyketone 37.
The ketone (36) can under strong basic conditions be converted into the mono-substituted product,
7B through the unsaturated alcohol 38B.
However, if hydroxyketone 37 is formed due to residual water, it may undergo a retro aldol
reaction to give a substituted acetophenone and an unsubstituted benzaldehyde. Once again, two
different pathways become available.
Pathway A finds the unsubstituted benzaldehyde reacting with the acetylide anion (27) to give
compound 35A. Similar to pathway B, the compound is first converted to α, β-unsaturated ketone
36A, followed by an MPV-O reaction with BnOH to yield 38A, and last of all converted to product
7A under basic conditions.
In pathway C, the enolate species 26C can react through an aldol reaction with the substituted
benzaldehyde formed through pathway B. Hydroxyketone 37C is formed and will under basic
conditions give an α, β-unsaturated ketone (36C) upon condensation. Like for pathways A and
B, ketone 36C can undergo an MPV-O reaction with BnOH to form 38C and subsequently the
di-substituted product 7C.
If none of the starting materials were substituted, dihydrochalcone would be expected as the
product from all three pathways. Should BBS instead be substituted and BnOH unsubstituted, the
same non-substituted and di-substituted products would be formed. Pathway B should however
form the product with the ketone by the non-substituted ring. Furthermore, in this mechanism
acetophenone is an intermediate and not a side product. This can explain its low yield as it is both
formed and consumed in the reaction.
The three reaction pathways can explain the many products observed by GC-MS and may also
explain why the [M+] of product 7A could be found on LC-MS from the reaction with o-allylbenzyl
alcohol, since pathway A would result in the non-substituted product.

3
Conclusion
The reaction of benzyl alcohol with β-bromostyrene yielding 1,3-diphenylpropan-1-one was stud-
ied for this project. The standard reaction of 1 equivalent of BnOH, 2 equivalents of BBS, and
2 equivalents of KOtBu in refluxing mesitylene over 5 h could be optimised. The reaction could
instead proceed with a starting material ratio of 1 equivalent BnOH to 1.5 equivalents of BBS, and
2 equivalents of KOtBu in refluxing toluene for 1 h. Bachelor students Møller and Melberg found
the yields comparable even under the milder conditions; 50% were reported for the optimised
reaction and 54% for the standard reaction.[61]
Addition of diglyme and crown ether saw a decrease in product yield, indicating that the potas-
sium ion may play a role in stabilising anionic intermediates. No trapped radicals were detected
from the radical scavenger experiments, thus it is very likely that the mechanism does not follow
a radical pathway. Findings of non-, mono- and di-substituted products during the substrate scope
with only one para-substituted starting material further suggest that three different pathways are
occurring simultaneously in the reaction mechanism.
Investigation of the reaction performed by Kumar et al. also raises a question on whether or
not their reaction follows a radical pathway, since it has not been possible to isolate any products
expected from a radical mechanism.
The proposed mechanism for the reaction of benzyl alcohol and β-bromostyrene follows a
polar reaction pathway with known reactions such as the Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verly reduction/Op-
penheimer oxidation and aldol condensation reactions. Notable intermediates, acetophenone and
phenyl acetylene, were also discovered during the reaction and their presence can be explained
by the proposed reaction mechanism. Three pathways diverge from the main one: pathway A,
pathway B, and pathway C, each leading to either the non-substituted, mono-substituted, and di-
substituted products, respectively. If both BnOH and BBS are substituted in the same fashion, the
three final products will be the same.

4.3.1
General reaction procedure
All reactions were performed in two-necked Schlenk flasks fitted with PTFE-stopcocks and equip-

GC-MS samples were prepared at different times to monitor the reaction.
Silica plug filtration (EtOAc) was performed to remove KOtBu and the solvent was removed

The corresponding substituted cinnamic acid (15 mmol), N-bromosuccinimide (2.67 g,
15 mmol) and Mn(OAc)2 (0.26 g, 1.5 mmol) were added to a 100 mL round bottomed flask fol-
lowed by a 1:1 mixture of water and acetonitrile (50 mL). A watch glass was placed over the
opening of the flask, and the reaction mixture was stirred overnight at room temperature. The
crude mixture was extracted with DCM (3 x 40 mL) and the combined organic phases were dried
with Na2SO4, filtered and concentrated in vacuo.
The residue was purified by flash column chromatography (9:1 n-heptane: EtOAc) and con-
centrated in vacuo to obtain the pure product. A yield of 885 mg (27% yield) was obtained for
the p-chloro-β-bromostyrene, and a yield of 3.07 g (96% yield) was obtained for the p-methoxy-
β-bromostyrene. The products were stored at −5°C.
4.3.7
Synthesis of o-allylbenzyl alcohol
5 mL abs. EtOH was added to a Schlenk flask equipped with a septum, glass stopper and stirring
bar. The flask was lowered into an ice bath at 0°C and evacuated and filled with nitrogen three
times. o-Allylbenzaldehyde (0.68 mL, 4.70 mmol) was added via syringe and NaBH4 was added
(266.7 mg, 7.05 mmol) under N2 overpressure. The reaction was stirred for 1.5-2 hours at room
temperature and monitored by TLC (95:5 n-hexane:EtOAc). The reaction mixture was cooled to
0°C, quenched with water (10 mL) and extracted with DCM (3 x 10 mL). The combined organic
layers were dried over Na2SO4, filtered and concentrated in vacuo. This gave a colourless oil which
was stored at 4°C and used without further purification (592 mg, 85% yield).
4.3.8
Synthesis of 1,3-diphenylpropan-1-one following Kumar et al.
The reactions were performed in two-necked Schlenk flasks following the procedure described in
Section 4.3.1. The flask was then charged with the solid reagents KOtBu (11.2 mg, 0.1 mmol) and
1,3,5-trimethoxybenzene (84.1 mg, 0.5 mmol) as an internal standard. The flask was evacuated
and filled with nitrogen five times before degassed toluene (2 mL) was added, the reaction mixture
heated to reflux (125 °C) and then followed by the addition of BnOH (0.10 mL, 1 mmol) and phenyl
acetylene (0.11 mL, 1 mmol). After 24 hours, a GC-MS sample was prepared to determine the
yield.

C.1
Introduction
Following the discovery by Azizi and Madsen,[2] several reactions were performed with alternative
reagents. A 1:1 mixture of styrene and DMA in refluxing dimethylformamide (DMF) gave a 30-
40% yield of N,N-dimethyl-4-phenylbutanamide (39). The reaction mechanism was suspected to
be a radical mechanism with an unknown initiation. The propagation steps could be something
akin to the cycle in Scheme 33.
Scheme 33: A proposal for the propagation step for the coupling of styrene and DMA.
With DMF radicals able to form under basic conditions, a radical mechanism is therefore not
unlikely. Øpstad et al. proposed that the base abstracts a proton from DMF to form an anion which
then loses a single electron, resulting in the active radical (Scheme 34).[51]

ies report transition metal-free coupling reactions that require little more than the base for the
cross-coupling of haloarenes, as described by Murphy.[76] Drapeau et al. found that KOtBu and
DMF alone could promote an α-phenylation between propiophenone and iodobenzene. A 2:1 ra-
tio of propiophenone and iodobenzene at 60°C gave a quantitative yield of the product. A solvent

Barham et al.: C-alkylation of N-alkylamides with styrenes
Barham et al. describe the coupling of styrene and DMA with 1.5 equiv. of KOtBu. The reaction
yielded two products, a mono-adduct and bis-adduct. The selectivity for the mono-adduct was
promoted to 77% yield by decreasing the concentration of styrene to 0.5 M in DMA, and the

towards the left) but the potassium-amide enolate is able to react with styrene through a metal-ene
reaction. Protonation of this species yields product 39. The mono-adduct, 39 is able to react with
another molecule of styrene to afford the bis-adduct, 41.[75]
