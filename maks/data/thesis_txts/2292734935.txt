ABSTRACT 
 
 
Fossil fuels are widely used as energy sources; however, they cause considerable 
environmental pollution, and their widespread use means that they will be exhausted in the 
near future.  
To cope with these problems a lot of countries have set objectives to reduce the fossil fuels 
dependence and to develop the use of sustainable energies. 
The increasing percentage of renewable energy sources in the electric production has been 
leading in the last years the interest in developing new energy storage technologies to balance 
the fluctuating behaviour of sustainable energies. 
In this work it will be studied a new technology under investigation and patented in the last 
years by ABB corporate research: a Thermo-Electric-Energy-Storage system (TEES). 
In the first part, different configurations right now investigated will be shown presenting their 
topology and their theoretical performance. 
Then, a base case model will be carried out, achieving a round trip efficiency at 44,04%; 
starting from this point, different scenarios will be investigated by modifying the cycle or by 
adding external sources in order to increase the round trip efficiency. 
Firstly, an expander substituted to the throttling valve during the charging, and a heat 
exchanger high pressure divided in 3 steps in order to achieve a better connection between the 
CO2 and the water curves will be carried out; the round trip efficiency achieved is 57,47%. 
Afterwards, the same topology will be connected with water underground to exploit its yearly 
average temperature. A maximum round trip efficiency of 64,46% has been achieved. 
Subsequently, a pre-heating and sub-cooling between the turbomachinery will be carried out 
in order to be able to operate with a minimum cycle temperature lower, achieving 63,16% as 
round trip efficiency. 
Moreover, two new configurations exploiting a LNG terminal as cold source will be 
investigated achieving interesting results. 
Finally an economic analysis will show a comparison between the solution analyzed with 
other energy storage systems. 
 
Key Words: Thermo-Electric-Energy-Storage (TEES), Energy Storage System (ESS)

1.  INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
The human history has been for a lot of time characterized by the small use of energy to meet 
the requirement leaving to the human and animal force the main duty to carry out the work. 
At half of the 18th century, the invention of the vapor machine by James Watt gave the 
impulse to start the period known as industrial revolution. 
Thus were born the first factories that produced more and more assets employing less people. 
The main motor of this development was the conversion of the thermal energy obtained by 
the coal combustion into mechanical energy. 
Then, thanks to the oil discovery at half of the 19th century, it began a quick technology 
development. 
 
Nowadays fossil fuels are widely used as energy sources; however, they cause considerable 
environmental pollution, and their widespread use means that they will be exhausted in the 
near future.  To cope with these problems a lot of countries have set objectives to reduce the 
fossil fuels dependence and to develop the use of sustainable energies. After the Kyoto 
protocol, the UE has set the 20-20-20 plan to achieve by 2020. This plan requires to reduce 
the CO2 emissions by 20% compared with 1990 emissions, to use the renewable sources for 
the 20% of the needs, and to improve the energy efficiency consuming the 20% less.  
 
Nowadays Denmark is the country in the world with the most ambitious energy targets for the 
next 35 years:  
- 50 % of electricity demands met by wind by 2020  
- 40% reduction of domestic greenhouse gas emissions from 1990 by 2020   
- A complete phase-out of coal by 2030   
- 100% renewable energy in electricity and heating by 2035         
- 100% renewable energy by 2050 (Denmark is the only country in the world that has a 
concrete plan to become fossil fuels free) [IEA-Denmark].    
In this light, new and renewable energy sources such as wind and photovoltaic are witnessing 
steady developments. However renewable energy sources (RES) have many disadvantages: 
they have low energy density so it is necessary to use plant with big areas, they use expensive 
technologies (this is true, but thanks to the research in the last years the price of the kWh is

1.1 Problem statement 
 
The production of renewable energy is to a large extent variable and difficult to control. In 
contrast to fossil energy, renewable energy must therefore be harvested when it is available, 
and to maintain a balance between production and consumption, some sort of energy storage 
is needed or at least of value. 
The biggest challenges are in the electricity sector, where the real-time balance between 
production and consumption is closely linked to grid stability. However, energy storage 
technology can also facilitate higher levels of renewable energy in the heating, cooling and 
transport sectors. 
Since both energy consumption and renewable energy supplies fluctuate over time, energy 
management is needed to ensure that energy supply balances demand at all times – this is true 
for heat, electricity, and energy for transport. Energy storage can facilitate this management 
and has the technical potential to be a key system component as the proportion of renewable 
energy increases. 
 
 
1.2 Energy storage plants 
 
Energy storage is not a new technology. Also in the last decades it was used to store 
electricity during the night when the demand was lower than the production. 
Two kinds of ESS are distinguished depending on their store capacity: large capacity for 
those systems able to store GWh or small capacity for those systems able to store less than 
1MWh.

1.2.2 
 CAES System 
 
CAES is an English acronym that means Compressed Air Energy Storage. Basically in this 
kind of ESS, the electric power is converted into thermo-mechanical energy by compressing 
the air in a reservoir such as a salt cavern underground. 
During the charge phase, the air is compressed around at 70 bar reaching more than 600 °C.  
The heat can be stored in a thermo energy storage (TES) tank (Adiabatic CAES) or wasted 
(Diabatic CAES) and the 
compressed air is stored 
[5]. 
When power is required, 
the compressed air flows 
on surface, gets heat from 
the 
TES 
or 
a 
gas 
combustor and expands in 
a turbine generating electric power. 
Considering specially Adiabatic CAES, this kind of technology is really good because it 
achieves  high round trip efficiency (more than 70%) with a high energy density and high 
capacity (more than 1 GWh). 
Nevertheless also this technology relies on the topology of the site: to obtain a cheap plant it 
is required an underground cavern air-tight.

1.2.3 
 Batteries 
 
Already in the 250 b.C the Egyptians knew a rudimentary system of batteries used for magic 
purpose. 
The first real battery was invented by the Italian Alessandro Volta in 1800. A current 
generator was made available for the first time. 
Battery energy storage systems, comprising lead-acid batteries, power conversion systems, 
and control systems, are in commercial operation around the world. They are used by three 
main groups: power generating utilities; power distributing utilities; and major power 
consumers (such as electric furnace foundries). The principal advantages of battery energy 
storage systems to generating utilities include: load leveling: frequency control; spinning re- 
serve; modular construction; convenient siting; no emissions; and investment deferral for new 
generation and transmission equipment. Power distributing utilities and major power 
consumers can avoid costly demand changes by discharging their batteries Lit peak periods 
and then recharging with lower cost off-peak power (say, at night). Battery energy storage 
systems are most cost effective when designed for discharge periods of less than 5 h; other 
systems (for example, pumped water storage) are better suited for longer discharges. 
Nowadays batteries are used a lot for portable devices, rarely also big batteries are used to 
store the grid power, and in the future it is expected they will play a big role as tank for the 
electric cars. [6] 
The most used kind of batteries are Na-S, Vanadio Redox, and Lition.

1.2.4 Flywheels 
 
The “Flywheel Energy Storage” or “Flywheel batteries” describes a system, which consist of 
three parts: - flywheel, - motor/generator, - power electronic system. The electric energy from 
the source, store as a kinetic energy of rotation, and delivers it to the load (as electric energy). 
The machine (working as motor) spins up the flywheel and stores energy mechanically. When 
the machine works as generator, the electrical energy is delivered to the load. Modern high-
speed flywheels differ from their forebears in being lighter and spinning much faster. Today’s 
system operate somewhere between atmospheric pressure (100 kPa) and 0,1 Pa, with the

1.3.1   State of the Art 
 
Thermo Electric Energy Storage with a heat pump is not a new concept to store the energy,

1.3.2 Base case cycle 
 
The working principle of a TEES system is that in charging mode, shown in Fig. 1.6, electric 
power is used to compress a working ﬂuid, and the heat from the compressed working fluid is 
transferred to a hot storage tank and the working fluid is cooled to ambient temperature.  
 
 
        Figure 1.6: Working principle of a thermo-electric energy storage system (charging mode) [9] 
 
Then, the compressed working fluid is expanded producing power that can be used to reduce 
compression power, and the cold expanded working fluid is used to remove the heat from a 
cold storage tank and heated to ambient temperature. In discharging mode, shown in fig. 1.7, 
the process of the TEES system is the reverse of that in the charging mode. 
==PAGE:32==
__________________________________________Introduction 
  
 
11
 
        Figure 1.7: Working principles of a thermo-electric energy storage system (discharging mode) [9] 
  
The working fluid that was cooled by the cold storage, is compressed and heated to ambient 
temperature (if TL<Tenv). Then, the compressed working fluid is further heated by the hot 
storage and expanded through an expander producing power, a portion of which is used for 
compression power, and cooled to ambient temperature. Two datum heat exchangers 
maintaining ambient temperature are used to correct the temperature drift caused by the 
irreversibility of the system in both modes. 
The working ﬂuid in the TEES system can undergo a phase change as in the Rankine cycle, or 
it may not undergo a phase change as in the Brayton cycle [9].

small compression ratios are required which results to higher efficiencies for the 
machinery[13].

5  ECONOMIC ANALYSIS 
 
 
A ESS is not a system that produces energy. The quantity of energy produced is always less 
than the quantity of energy absorbed (except for a TEES combined with a LNG terminal), so 
the question that the reader can make is: how is it possible to make business with a plant of 
this kind? 
The business for a ESS consists in absorbing electric power during the period when the 
demand is lower than the production and consequently when the kWh price is low, and 
generating electricity during the pick period when the price is high. 
This chapter will carry out an economic analysis for a TEES plant, size 50 MW considering 
25 years (n) as operating life. 
It will be define the pay-back time (PBT) and also the net present value (NPV) so that it will 
be possible to compare the income actualizing them through a discount rate. 
To be able to carry out a good analysis it is important to define more precisely as possible the 
investment cost for each part of the system.

6  DISCUSSION 
 
6.1  Technical results 
 
This work started considering a base TEES cycle found in the literature and from this point 
the goal has been to operate some improvement to increase as much as possible the 
performance of the system in particular the round trip efficiency. 
Five new configurations have been investigated, and for each one of them there has been 
some discovered advantages and disadvantages aspects. 
The biggest difference  in operating the improvements occured at the beginning: the base 
cycle from which has been started, has shown a round trip efficiency at 44,04 % as result. 
Substituting the throttling valve with an expander in the charge phase to recover the power, 
and operating a more efficient discretization of the heat exchanger high pressure setting 
different steps temperature in the thermal storage process, has let to increase the round trip 
efficiency from 44,04% to 57,47%. 
Then, several parameters have been varied in order to verify the sensitivity of the system. 
The isoentropic efficiency of the compressor and the expander has resulted to be the variable 
most sensitive in order to influence the value of the round trip efficiency. 
It has been seen for the base cycle improved that, varying the isoentropic efficiency from 0,72 
to 0,92, the round trip efficiency value increases drastically form 46,26 % to 73,3%. 
This means a raise of 58,45%, and the same growing has been noticed also for the other 
configurations. 
Afterwards, the variation of the lowest temperature TL has been taken under investigation: the 
results are impressive. 
Decreasing the storing temperature for evaporation/condensation TL, it has been achieved a 
significantly increasing in the round trip efficiency (65,83% - 43,63% if -20<TL<12 °C for the 
improved base case; 63,87% - 54,48% if  -20<TL<10 °C for the pre-heating before the 
compression topology) and at the same time a decreasing in the CO2 mass flow rate; this 
means a decreasing in the TES volume sizes and therefore an increasing in the energy density. 
A problem found during the heat exchanger LP discretization is the impossibility to store 
enough thermal energy at low temperature in the HP stage to run the TE phase for the same 
==PAGE:95==
____________________________________________Discussion 
  
 
74
time in the base cycle improved, so the TL value must be equal to the environmental 
temperature. 
This limitation has been solved by modifying the cycle creating the pre-heating before the 
compression configuration that lets to sub-cool the refrigerant liquid after the high pressure 
heat exchanger, so that to obtain a fluid after the charge expansion with really low quality 
(this is the big advantage in using this topology). 
Nevertheless, it has also been noticed that decreasing the TL, it increases dramatically the 
Tmax after the compression at constant pressure (see fig. 4.25 – 4.29). This raise is more 
emphatic in the pre-heating configuration where the Tmax overtakes the 200 °C (see fig. 4.29). 
The water cannot be in liquid phase at this temperature if not increasing dramatically the 
pressure inside the tank and consequently the cost. 
The solution here proposed for further investigations is to substitute the water with diathermic 
oil in the highest TES temperature step. 
The result expected is a lower value in the energy density because of the lower thermal 
capacity value of the diathermic oil. 
In the improved base case it has been investigated that the influence in changing the value to 
the CO2 temperature outlet the heat exchanger high pressure Tstor,L. 
The results reached are shown in fig. 4.26: the influence for the round trip efficiency is lower 
compared with the other parameters previously seen, but it has been seen that a decreasing in 
Tstor,L, involves a little decreasing in the round trip efficiency probable because it is decreased 
the work available for the charge phase expander. 
About the “pre-heating” configuration, it has been investigated the variation in the value of 
the difference of temperature generated by the pre-heating heat exchange between the point 
outlet the evaporator and the point after the condenser. 
Fig. 4.30 displays the sensitivity variation: the increasing of ΔT12  generates an increasing of 
some point percentage in the round trip efficiency but the difference is not so marked. 
A modification to the base cycle improved has carried out considering to use water 
underground as source during the winter as hot source for the evaporator and during the 
summer as cold source for the condenser. 
It has been chosen to use this configurations during the cold and hot season because the 
temperature difference between the water underground and the environment is pretty marked 
(10°C or also more). 
The result achieved for the winter topology are a really good round trip efficiency of 64,46 % 
and an energy density quite low 6,038 kWh/m3.

An innovative idea designed in this thesis is to combine a TEES plant with a LNG terminal 
for regasification to exploit it as cold source because of its low temperature status. 
Since the first model, it has appeared the big opportunity that gives this operating with its 
132,9 % in round trip efficiency. 
Essentially, the configuration for a TEES combined with a LNG in the HP stage is the same 
previously seen for the base cycle improved. 
The difference is the cold source used in the TE phase (the LNG) that lets to run an expansion 
of the CO2 much higher than the normal arriving till - 40 °C. 
The problem verified is the low value of quality achieved after the TE expansion (0,7964) that 
could create corrosion problems to the turbine blades. 
To cope with this problem it has been investigated a way to increase the Tmax after the 
compression: operating a pre-heating in the HP phase. 
The value of the quality after the TE expansion as desired increases inside an acceptable 
range (0,9), but dramatically decreases the round trip efficiency (from 132,9% to 122,5%) . 
Hereby, it has been carried out an investigation to understand how does the round trip 
efficiency vary changing the ΔT12. 
The analysis displayed in fig. 4.34 shows that increasing the pre-heating i.e. ΔT12, decreases 
the round trip efficiency. 
 
It can be concluded that for a TEES combined with a LNG terminal for regasification, the 
pre-heating before the compression is harmful for the round trip efficiency. 
On the other hand the value of the quality after the TE expansion is really low, so the solution 
here proposed for further investigations is to divide the TE expansion in 2 steps. The first one 
ends some degrees under the environmental temperature (10 - 20 °C less), and exploits this

The economic analysis has shown that right now TEES is not a great business. The pay-back 
time is in all the cases quite high, more than 15 years except for the configurations that 
exploit the LNG terminal. 
The base case does not have a NPV positive, the base case improved has a NPV even worsen 
because of the higher investment cost. 
The pre-heating before the compression has 15 years of PBT, NPV at around +3 M$, but  
considering an investment of 28 M$, the ratio NPV/I0  is a little bit more than 10%. 
The most important parameter found in order to achieve good economic results is the round 
trip efficiency since it is the most important factor that influence the variable incomes. 
Considering the electric market right now in the Nordpool, where the yearly average price is 
around 25 $/MWh [19], it is important for the future to develop better ESS, but in particular 
to develop sustainable energy systems able to produce the electricity at low price such as 
30$/MWh.

7.  CONCLUSION 
 
 
 
The main target of this thesis is to give a contribute to one of the biggest research energy 
sector: the energy storage. 
The present project focuses on Thermo-Electric-Energy-Storage system, a new technology for 
storing electrical energy converting it into thermal energy through a heat pump, and several 
scenarios have been taken under investigation. 
 
At the beginning a base model has been carried out and a maximum round trip efficiency of 
44,04% has been achieved. 
Subsequently, different scenarios have been taken in consideration in order to improve the 
round trip efficiency of the system. 
Firstly, an expander has been introduced substituting the throttling valve, and the heat 
exchanger high pressure has been divided in 3/4 intervals in order to achieve a better 
connection between the CO2 and the water profile. 
The round trip efficiency improvement is substantial, since it increases from 44,04% till 
57,47%. 
Then, it has been investigated the possibility to use water underground or sea or lakes as 
source because the yearly average temperature. 
Two models have been carried out considering to be in summer conditions (a maximum 
round trip efficiency of 60,81% has been achieved) and in winter conditions (a maximiìum 
round trip efficiency of 64,46% has been achieved). 
Alternative system configurations were introduced by modifying the so called basic 
configuration of the base case scenario. 
In particular, the base case configuration was modified in 3 steps: by operating a pre-heating 
and a sub-cooling between the turbomachinery stages, by exploiting a LNG terminal for 
regasification as thermal source, by exploiting a LNG terminal as cold source operating a pre-
heating before the compression. 
In the first case a maximum round trip efficiency value of 63,16% has been achieved using 
ice slurry as cold thermal storage material since the system achieves a TL of  - 20 °C. 
In the 2nd and the 3rd case, the exploitation of the cold temperature made available by the 
LNG that requires heat to be gasified, lets possible to achieve incredible round trip efficiency 
values ( respectively 132,9 % and 122,5%). 
==PAGE:100==
____________________________________________Conclusion 
  
 
79
It cannot be said that LNG scenarios are the best since they require methane to gasify and of 
course this is not always a condition available. 
Finally, an economic analysis has been carried out to verify if the scenarios anlyzed in this 
work can generate incomes or losses for a hypotetical investor. 
The results achieved are not amazing for sure, since the base scenario, and the improved base 
scenarios have a NPV value negative and a pay back time around 20 years. 
As expected, good results are achieved from the LNG scenarios since the NPV achieves 
values more or less equal to the initial investment and the PBT is around 10 years. 
Of course, this results are valid only if a country provides to satisfy the gas necessity by using 
big quantities of LNG, so it can be used a TEES to work in symbioses.    
Differently from the expectation, the “pre-heating before the compression” topology has 
better economic results. 
The PBT is lower compared with the base scenarios, 15 years, and the NPV has a positive 
value of 3M$. 
It is necessary to keep in consideration that the initial investment is much higher 28M$, so 
considering that the ratio NPV / I0   is little bit more than 10%, it is not a great business for 
sure. 
 
Nevertheless, it is good to consider that ESS presence is essential in an electric grid that 
generates energy only by renewable energies. 
To have designed a system able to store big quantity of energy with high energy density, low 
footprint, that does not rely on geographical topology, and that does not generate economic 
losses, I think it is a good solution to let to a country to become carbon fossil fuel free. 
 
 
Future works 
 
The work here presented, wanted to develop some improvement for a TEES starting from a 
base scenario and to carry out an economic analysis in order to estimate the cost of the energy 
stored. 
However, in order to achieve more accurate results, the following proposal are recommended: 
• Dynamic simulation 
 
==PAGE:101==

80
The models here displayed do not keep in consideration the transitory time, that is more 
important when it is working for short periods. 
 
• Better discretization of the heat exchanger high pressure with the water profile 
 
It has been seen that dividing the TES tank in N steps even if 3-4 as it has been carried out in 
this work, the round trip efficiency can increase 1-2 point %. 
This means that the connection carried out in this work can be improved, maybe keeping also 
the same number of tanks. 
 
• Thermo-economic optimization 
 
It has been seen that the round trip efficiency plays a big role in making a TEES competitive 
from the economic point of view. 
Nevertheless, this is not the only factor; in this work it has been seen that a scenario with a 
round trip efficiency of 64,46% (base scenario improved in winter condition using 
underground water) is worsen from the economic point of view compared with a “pre-heating 
before the compression” configuration (63,16% of round trip efficiency). 
For this reason it will be appropriate to carry out a model that keep in consideration from a 
hand to achieve the best round trip efficiency as possible, and to the other hand to design a 
system with the lowest fixed cost as possible.
