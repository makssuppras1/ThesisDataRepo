Abstract 
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most frequent neurodegenerative disease amongst the 
elderly. It is defined as neuronal degradation and the formation of plaques in the brain. 
However, the precise pathomechanisms are still unknown.  Genome wide association 
studies have however uncovered a primary risk factor associated with AD, which is a certain 
genotype of the Apolipoprotein E (ApoE) gene. It is known that three distinct alleles of ApoE 
exist, commonly referred to as ApoE2, ApoE3 and ApoE4. ApoE3 as the most common is the 
baseline, while ApoE2 reduces the risk of developing AD, and carriers of ApoE4 show 
significantly increased chance of developing AD. The precise function in association with AD 
has yet to be uncovered.  
To uncover the pathomechanisms of these alleles, CRISPR-cas9 technology was employed 
on induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) to obtain isogenic lines with an ApoE2/2, 3/3, 4/4 
and knockout (KO) genotypes. These lines were then differentiated into cortical neurons as 
confirmed by immune staining against specific cortical markers.  
As impairment of mitochondria is a commonly observed cellular phenotype in 
neurodegenerative diseases, the oxygen consumption rate (OCR) of these lines was 
investigated by a Seahorse experiment. Furthermore, oxidative stress, and formation of 
amyloid-β40/42 were assessed. Findings by Seahorse measurement showed significantly 
higher OCRs for ApoE2/2 as compared to ApoE4/4. To enforce AD related phenotypes, an 
additional AD mutation, the L150P mutation, was inserted into the PSEN1 locus of the ApoE 
lines by gene editing technology. The hope for these lines is to find synergistic effects of 
L150P and the high risk ApoE 4/4 genotype.

1.  Introduction and aim of the study 
Alzheimer’s disease is the most frequent neurodegenerative disease in the elderly 
population. As the human population starts increasing the average age due to extermination 
of diseases such as smallpox1, polio2 or infectious diseases in general3 we will see an 
increasing elderly population. Estimates are that 47.5 million people suffer from dementia 
of which AD is the common worldwide and that number is expected to grow with 7.7 million 
new cases every year, with some estimates setting numbers even higher4-5. AD is defined as 
neuronal degradation and the formation of plaques in the brain6. However, the precise 
pathomechanisms are still unknown7-8. Genome wide association studies have however, 
uncovered a primary risk factor associated with AD, which is a certain genotype of the 
Apolipoprotein E (ApoE) gene9. It is known that three distinct alleles of ApoE exist, 
commonly referred to as ApoE2, ApoE3 and ApoE4. ApoE3 as the most common is the 
baseline, while ApoE2 reduces the risk of developing AD, and carriers of ApoE4 show 
significantly increased chance of developing AD. The precise function in association with AD 
has yet to be uncovered.  
The aim of this study is to build disease models and uncover the pathomechanisms of these 
alleles, advancing basic research and understanding of AD. The induced pluripotent stem 
cells (iPSCs) technology developed in 2006 allows for ideal building of diseases models by 
use of gene editing. CRISPR-cas-9 gene editing technique is employed on iPSCs to obtain 
isogenic lines with an ApoE 2/2, 3/3 and 4/4 genotypes as well as a KO line. These lines are 
then differentiated into cortical neurons as confirmed by immune staining against specific 
cortical markers. As impairment of mitochondria is a commonly observed cellular 
phenotype in neurodegenerative diseases, the OCR of these lines is investigated by a 
Seahorse experiment. Furthermore, oxidative stress, and formation of amyloid-β40/42 is 
assessed.

2.  Theory 
2.1.  Dementia 
Dementia is a collective term for a group of diseases affecting the brain, which significantly 
decrease mental capabilities. These symptoms include but are not limited to deterioration 
of memory, thinking, the ability to perform daily tasks or a significant changes in behaviour 
or personality4. At present the World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that 47.5 million 
people suffer from dementia worldwide and that number is expected to grow with 7.7 
million new cases every year, with some estimates setting numbers even higher4-5. 
Age is the primary risk factor associated with dementia which  constitutes a significant 
health risk and is considered by the WHO to be an important focus point to improve 
health10. As the average age of the population increases several diseases causing dementia 
start becoming more prevalent, such as AD which is found in 1% of the population between 
the ages of 65-69, but in 40-50% of the population above the age of 955. Though the 
different diseases attributed to causing dementia are numerous, they are often grouped 
together. One such grouping is vascular dementia, which causes dementia due to reduced 
blood flow to the brain11. However the most prevalent form of dementia by far is not a 
grouping but a single diseases, AD which contributes approximately 60-70% of all dementia 
cases4.

Though AD has been defined as three types - no clinical or phenotypical distinguishing have 
been identified. FAD is therefore often studied as a basis for general application of 
phenotypes and pharmaceutical products, as it is well defined. PSEN1 and PSEN2 form 
subcomponents of the proteolytic complex processing APP, and mutations in their genes 
have been linked with increased production of Aβ42, which has a clear line of cause and 
effect20. The two more common types of AD however lack this clear cause/effect system, 
and only risk factors have been identified in the course of research12. The most predominant 
risk factor is ApoE and while other risk factors have been identified, ApoE is believed to 
constitute the majority of the risk of developing AD12,21. As of yet an effective treatment 
method for AD has not been developed. Several treatment methods slowing AD progression 
have however been developed and employed successfully, but they involve several side 
effects for the patients22. 
2.3.  Apolipoprotein E 
ApoE is a 299 amino acids long protein that is essential in the catabolism of triglyceride-rich 
lipoproteins23 in the liver and transport of cholesterol in the brain12. It has been connected 
with glucose metabolism, neuronal-inflammation, neuronal signalling, and mitochondrial 
functions24. This protein comes in three different isoforms, ApoE2, ApoE3 and ApoE4 (Figure 
3)25. Each of these variations has strong ties to AD and interacts differently. ApoE2 is found 
in approximately 5% of the population and has been found to have a protective function 
against developing AD25. ApoE3 is found in ~75% of the population and is neither positive 
nor detrimental to the odds of developing AD25. ApoE4 has repeatedly been found to 
increase the risk of AD in several large scale genetic studies26,27, with results showing a three 
times increase in risk of developing AD as a heterozygous and a 15 times increase with a 
homozygous ApoE4 genotype12,26.

Studies have shown that ~50% of all cases of AD involve an ApoE4 allele, while only 20% of 
the population are carriers of an ApoE4 allele25. However, this still leaves a significant 
number of cases where ApoE4 is not involved, and people carrying ApoE4 can end up never 
developing AD. This leads to ApoE4 being a risk factor and not a clear indicator as the above 
mentioned APP, PSEN1 and PSEN2 mutations, where the penetrance is a 100%. Though the 
ApoE variants have been studied extensively, these studies had significant weaknesses in 
that they were often mice studies23 or based on cancer cell lines28 overexpressing ApoE. 
These studies did not find a clear pathomechanism and a clear phenotypic difference of the 
variant forms has not been established - only a multitude of theories.  
Among these theories is that ApoE4 is worse at reusing and utilizing the membrane lipids 
and thereby affecting neuronal repair12. Others have shown that ApoE is present in the Aβ 
plaques found in AD patients and could therefore be involved in the aggregation by binding 
to Aβ or by impairing it’s clearance24. However these theories need to be tested in a more 
robust model, without the potential interference of overexpressing ApoE or the variation 
present in mice or cancer cells.  
 
2.4.  Studying Alzheimer’s disease 
The human brain is an immensely complex structure composed of several subdivided parts 
known as lobes, and many different cell types. The fact that the brain is essential to survive 
and the sensitive nature of the brain has made it extremely difficult to study diseases like 
AD in living humans, and has largely limited investigations to autopsies, cell culture systems 
and animal models29.  
Recently, new techniques have allowed for human cellular models via cellular 
reprogramming that show great promise30. These models are of particular interest as they 
most closely reproduce the conditions associated with AD. The new models allows the study 
of pathomechanisms in AD and hold great promise for easy and reliable testing of potential 
treatment methods30. They can also allow for tailor made AD progression models and 
treatment testing, based on producing cell models derived from patient cells31. These cells 
are known as induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC)32 and are derived from fibroblast cells

2.4.1.  Induced pluripotent stem cells 
Stem cells have been a target of research since an article in Nature in 196334. Stem cells are 
at their core defined as cell types that can divide indefinitely, meaning it’s self-renewing and 
that they can differentiate into any other cell type present in the body known as 
pluripotency35.  The field has greatly advanced since then and gained momentum due to the 
discovery of the expansive possible applications of stem cells in a wide variety of disease 
treatments and research. Over the course of developing this field, three distinct avenues of 
research have been established based on the source of the stem cells, embryonic35,36, 
adult35 and induced pluripotent31. Embryonic stem cells are harvested from embryos at a 
stage before differentiation into specific cell types takes place and was first discovered and 
derived in 198137. Adult stem cells on the other hand are harvested from adults most 
commonly from the bone marrow though other harvest locations are possible31,35. Adult 
stem cells however do not live up to one of the core principles of stem cells as they are not 
pluripotent, meaning they can’t differentiate into every cell type38. iPSCs are significantly 
different from the two other groups in that they are not naturally occurring cells, but are 
rather artificially induced to revert somatic cells into stem cells31.  
In that regard, the initial development of the iPSC-based systems allow for new avenues of 
research unavailable with the previous two. This includes patient specific cell models and 
treatments with cells developed from the patient in question, significantly lowering the 
chance of rejection31. It also allows for samples to be taken and used for research after 
having shown disease markers which might develop late in life, such as AD32. It also 
eliminates the significant ethical debate and considerations that have been associated with 
embryonic stem cells38, which when harvested is taken from the inner cell mass from human 
embryos destroying it in the process36. 
The technology to produce iPSCs was pioneered by K. Takahashi and S. Yamanaka with their 
ground-breaking article in 2006 showing that it is possible to reprogram fibroblasts from 
mice to a pluripotent stage39. One year later, they showed the same in human cells32. This 
was achieved by transducing the fibroblasts with the transcription factors Oct3/4, Sox2, Klf4, 
and c-Myc40.

However, significant issues were associated with this new technique, as a retrovirus inserts 
the transcription factors into the genome. This is associated with a great risk of variation in 
the procedure, as the insertion site is random and can therefore cause significant 
disruptions in the cell functions, if vital parts of the genome is affected41. Though still in use 
as an effective method42, several alternatives have been developed, such as the use of 
recombinant proteins43, micro RNAs44, synthetic mRNA45, small molecular compounds46 or 
episomal vectors47. Concerns have also been raised over the original cell type, from which 
the iPSCs are derived. It was shown, that iPSCs tend to differentiate more easily to the 
original cell type such as fibroblasts48, which can raise concerns related to purity and 
stability of any cell models that are later established based on the iPSCs. Each of the 
mentioned different methods provides strengths and weaknesses to the systematic use of 
iPSCs and alternatives will in all probability be continuously developed as the field holds 
incredible promise. 
However, having produced iPSCs is only a part of the way to producing viable cell models for 
further work. The cells need to be differentiated into the cell type of interest, in order to 
produce useful data (Figure 4). This is the primary point of current development as different 
cell types require different protocols, and is also one of the limits for current disease 
models. A major obstacle on the way to generating a functional and useful disease model 
for AD is the differentiation of the iPSCs to neurons and astrocytes to investigate AD related 
pathomechanisms, as these are the primary cell types found to be involved in the disease.  
 
2.4.2.  Neurons and Astrocytes 
Following the establishment of an iPSC culture, there is a need to produce the cell types of 
actual interest in the cell model or treatment. In regards to AD, the cell types of interest are 
neurons and astrocytes, which both have established differentiation protocols49,50. The 
protocols however undergo continuous testing and improvement in order to approach as 
close to natural states as possible49.  
The first step to produce mature neurons and astrocytes is the differentiation into neural 
progenitor cells (NPC), which can then be further matured into neurons or astrocytes51. A 
commonly used method to produce NPCs is employing the two SMAD protein signalling

The successful induction of NPCs results in the cells showing several phenotypes that can be 
tested for, such as a clear change in morphology (Figure 5) and the expression of several

Having produced multipotent NPCs, further differentiation into fully matured cell types are

Though a multitude of differentiation protocols have been established for neurons, work is 
still ongoing to produce the matured neurons faster or in more mature forms53. Neurons 
and astrocytes can likewise be tested for expression of genes associated with their specific 
cell types such as Tuj1 which is a stabilizing protein found in microtubules of neurons or 
CTIP254, TBR155 and Satb256 which are transcription factors for neurons. GFAP is a filament 
playing an essential role for astrocytes, and is therefore also a target of testing57.  
However, even following maturation, several differences have been found in the expression 
of neurons isolated from human brains and those produced from stem cells, which requires 
additional studies58. Another significant issue found in the differentiation of cell lines to 
build viable cell models for diseases studies, is that several different cell types might 
perform interactions to produce the disease effect or in general change the phenotype of 
the system. For instance, astrocytes have recently been found to have significant impact 
upon differentiation of neurons found in the brain, instead of the rather passive role they 
have been assigned before59. This issue is especially relevant in regard to AD as the total 
amount of the ApoE found in the two cell types can differ a lot. Studies have shown that the 
production in neurons is limited and astrocytes produce significantly increased amounts60,61. 
Studies have however found that under neuropathological conditions, neurons show 
increasing production of ApoE but it is most commonly produced by astrocytes9,60,61.

2.4.3.  Clustered Regularly-Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats-Cas9 
Clustered Regularly-Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeat – Cas9 (CRISPR-Cas9) is a recently 
developed form of gene editing that has proven a vital tool by virtue of its precise nature. It 
is based on discoveries made in 1987 which found specialized base pair repeats and 
spacings in Escherichia coli’s genome that were too systematic to be random62. This was 
later discovered to be insert locations for viral infections memory in the bacteria present at 
multiple locations in the genome63. 
This set of a 29 base pair repeat and a 32 base pair 
spacer found in Escherichia coli, have led to the 
search for and discovery of similar setups in 
bacteria species and archaea with a theorized 
similar nature63. These findings have formed the 
consensus that CRISPR are storage areas for 
immunity-assisting DNA sequences, which help the 
cell with combating previously encountered viral 
threats much like a human immune system64,65.

2.4.4.  Seahorse - Functional Metabolic Analysis in live Cells 
The Seahorse experimental method is a new way of measuring metabolic flux in cells, 
providing an excellent supplement to previous methods. Previous methods were often 
complicated and arduous tasks often requiring extensive replicates, expansive work hours, 
expensive components and killing the cells74. Advances in metabolic flux analysis have 
resulted in the development of a device to measure metabolic flux in real time, the XF 
Extracellular Flux Analyzer by Seahorse Bioscience75. This new device allows real time 
analysis of metabolic flux, more precisely oxygen and proton flux. The measurement is 
performed with live cells and allows for injection of metabolic changing components to 
measure cellular responses, to for instance stress factors or to get base readings on cellular 
states. An application is measuring the oxygen consumption rate (OCR) of different cell lines 
to establish if changes occur between cell lines (Figure 10)76. This system is highly useful as 
mitochondrial function is a common point of investigation for neurological disorders. 
Several mitochondrial phenotypes like impaired mitophagy, mitochondrial morphology, 
branching, fusion/fission ability and membrane potential have been associated with 
neurodegeneration. As such, the overall mitochondrial function, the respiration, is a 
promising feature to look at for characterization of a disease model for neurodegeneration 
like AD.

3.  Experimental strategy 
The aim of this study is to uncover phenotypes associated to different ApoE genotypes in iPSC 
derived neurons. The study is part of a collaboration where this study will cover aspects of a larger 
international project. An outline of the experimental procedures can be seen in Figure 11. 
Initially, five different gene edited isogenic cell lines with the following ApoE genotypes; ApoE3/4 
(Original) (WT), ApoE2/2, ApoE3/3, ApoE4/4 and ApoE KO were derived from a healthy male 19 year 
old. Initial iPSCs of each line will be differentiated into NPCs in triplicates to produce biological 
replicates. The cells will then be frozen to produce banks for further work, and thawed again to 
confirm the frozen cells viability and to perform further maturation and experiments. 
Following thawing, Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) with NESTIN and SOX1 antibody 
staining will be performed in order to control for biological markers of the differentiated NPC and 
the purity of the differentiations. NPCs showing acceptable FACS purity will then be matured into 
cortical neurons, to produce AD relevant cells. The mature neurons will be tested for ApoE 
expression, using western blots and qRT-PCR. Furthermore, typical markers for cortical neurons will 
be investigated by antibody stainings for Tuj1, CTIP2, TBR1 and Satb2, to check for maturity in the 
differentiated neurons. Following establishment of above information the phenotypes of cell lines 
are then determined by performing Seahorse analysis, LDH-analysis, Cell-Rox and ELISA Aβ40/42. 
In parallel to the neuronal differentiated lines, two different CRISPR-Cas9 gene editings are 
performed alongside the previously mentioned procedures in order to produce cell lines of interest 
to studying AD. One could potentially enhance phenotypic effects and the second will make for an 
easier differentiation and better maturation of the ApoE lines. The first gene editing will result in the 
L150P PSEN1 mutation associated with FAD, which would allow for studying whether this mutations 
would enhance the phenotypes seen in the ApoE lines. The second gene editing will be performed by 
inserting the Ngn2 gene into the AAVS1 locus of the cell lines in order to take advantage of a newly 
developed differentiation protocol that allows for faster and more efficient neuronal differentiation. 
Astrocytes differentiation will be established and performed in order to produce cell types with 
potentially higher ApoE production and for experimental procedures to study interactions between 
neurons and astrocytes in regards to ApoE phenotypes.

4.  Materials and methods 
4.1.  Neuronal differentiation 
Neuronal differentiation was based on the publication of Chambers et al., 2009. The five 
different iPSC cell lines 3/4, 2/2, 3/3, 4/4 and KO were grown to a confluency of 
approximately 50% in Matrigel (MG) (Corning Bioscience, cat no. 354277) coated 6 well 
plates with E8 growth media and daily media change. The cells are then split as outlined 
below. 
The media is removed and the wells are washed with DPBS to remove any remaining media 
and Accutase (Gibco, cat no. A11105-01) is added to disassociate the cells from the well. 
After adding Accutase, the plate is incubated for 5-10 minutes at 37°C and the cells are 
washed off with E8 media to inactivate the Accutase and move them to 2 ml Eppendorf 
tubes. The cells are then spun down for 5 minutes at 120 g, and supernatant is discarded. 
Cells are resuspended in E8 media, two droplets are added to a prepared MG coated 
differentiation plate and a single droplet to a maintenance plate. Each well of the prepared 
plates contains E8 media with a 1:200 RevitaCell™ Supplement (100X) (cat no. A2644501) 
dilution. Maintenance plate is kept to produce biological replicates and enters into the start 
of the Neuronal Differentiation. After differentiation plate has grown to a cell density of 90-
100% the media is switched to Neural Induction Media (NIM) (Appendix 10.1.4) and media 
is changed every second day. After three days the cells are split as outlined above and 
resuspended in NIM and transferred onto a new MG coated 6 well plate with NIM and 1:200 
diluted RevitaCell. After growing for 3 days the cells are then split onto a MG coated 10 cm 
plate, and grown for another 3-6 days, until an appropriate cell densities of ~90% have been 
reached. The cells are then split, and before being spun down they are counted using a 
NucleoCounter from which total cell counts are estimated, they are then resuspended in 
freezing media, E8 with a 10% DMSO dilution, with 1 mL per ~1 million cells. 1 mL of 
resuspended cells are then added to freezing tubes and frozen at -80°C, the following day 
they are transferred to nitrogen tank at -182°C for long term storage.

The frozen NPCs are thawed in a hot water bath and then transferred to a 2 mL Eppendorf 
tube. They are then centrifuged at 120 g for 5 minutes and subsequently the supernatant is

4.2.  Fluorescence-activated cell sorting of NPCs 
NPCs are thawed and grown 1 day with NEM on a PLO/Laminin-coated 6 well plate. Cells are 
then washed with DPBS followed by disassociation with Accustase, they are then washed 
down with 1% Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) in DPBS and cells are counted using a 
NucleoCounter. Cell suspension resulting in a total of 4x105 cells are then transferred to a 
1.5 mL Eppendorf tube, spun down at 120 g for 5 minutes and subsequently the 
supernatant is discarded. Cells are resuspended in 0.5 mL of Foxp3 / Transcription Factor 
Fixation/Permeabilization (cat no. 00-5521-00) working solution (1:3) and left at 4°C in the 
dark for 30 minutes. Following incubation, 1 mL of Permeabilization buffer is added and the 
cells are spun down at 120 g for 5 minutes and supernatant is discarded. Cells are then 
resuspended in 400 µL of Permeabilization buffer and 200 µL is transferred to two wells in a 
96 well plate, and spun down at 400 RCF for 5 minutes. Supernatant is discarded and cells 
are resuspended in 100 µL of Permeabilization buffer with Nestin and Sox1 antibodies at 
manufacturer recommended concentrations (Appendix 10.2.2) for one well and 100 µL of 
Permeabilization buffer for another well. The plate is then incubated for 45 minutes at room 
temperature in the dark, and then spun down at 400 RCF for 5 minutes. Supernatant is 
discarded and the cells are resuspended in 200 µL of Permeabilization buffer followed by 
another centrifugation at 400 RCF for 5 minutes. This step is repeated again and cells are 
then resuspended in 200 µL of 1% BSA in DPBS. Samples are then run on AccuriTM FACS 
machine analysing 50.000 cells at high speed.

5.3.  Oxygen consumption rate in cortical neurons measured by Seahorse  
Seahorse analysis was performed in order to compare the OCR of the five different ApoE 
lines, as impairment of mitochondria is a common cellular phenotype for neurodegenerative 
diseases. Measurements were performed in 8-16 technical replicates for each 
differentiation, with Diff J5, Diff J9 and Diff J10 serving as biological replicates. In addition 
the Seahorse analysis was repeated twice, to ensure reproducibility and data from the two 
experiments were fused. Measurements constitute an initial three readings to establish 
basal OCR levels, t(0) representing start of measurements. This is followed by the addition 
of oligomycin which is an inhibitor of ATP synthase, which shows the mitochondrial 
respiration 
levels. 
Following 
this 
the 
addition 
of 
Carbonyl 
cyanide-4-
(trifluoromethoxy)phenylhydrazone (FCCP) will disrupt the ATP synthesis by transporting 
protons across mitochondrial inner membranes. This allows the measurement of the highest 
possible respiratory capabilities of the cells. The addition of Rotenone/Antimycin A will 
completely prevent the electron transport chain lowering OCR levels to the zero reaction 
point (Figure 23). All measurements were performed under normal growth conditions 
except the addition of mentioned components, and measuring points were adjusted to 
allow for reactants to take full effect. The measurements showed significant differences in 
the maximal OCR with ApoE2/2 having a significantly higher basal and spare capacity OCR as 
compared to ApoE4/4. The results were analysed for statistical significance by t-testing, 
which showed that the comparison of ApoE2/2 and ApoE4/4 falls within the statistically 
significant 5% cut-off for all measuring points. ApoE KO shows a different profile as 
compared to other cell lines, with overall higher values for all possible time points. T-testing 
however shows that KO is only statistically significant when compared to WT and only show 
sporadic significance when compared to remaining cell lines.

6.  Discussion 
6.1.  Neuronal differentiation 
The differentiation into cortical neurons is an essential step in the process of building a cell 
model for studying AD pathomechanisms and for potential drug testing. The 
characterization of the neuronal differentiation is therefore essential for the validity of any 
of the following results. After differentiation of iPSCs to NPCs, several aliquots of the cells 
were banked at -182°C, and one vial was then used to check for cell viability after thawing. 
Thawed cells were run on FACS, which revealed that typical NPC markers were expressed 
with consistently high purity levels of NESTIN and SOX1. In addition all three differentiations 
showed morphology consistent with NPCs.  
After the maturation into neurons, the antibody stainings showed consistent results in 
expression of the neuronal marker TUJ1 in the processes as well as the cortical markers and 
transcription factors CTIP2, TBR1 and Satb2. GFAP staining revealed that astrocytes were 
also present in the neural culture system. This is highly desirable as a study by Hongjun Song 
et Al. in 2002 shows that astrocytes play an active and important role in differentiation and 
maturation of neurons and Boyles et al. in 1985 found that they are the primary source of 
ApoE in the brain. It also allows for a closer approximation of the human brain, which is 
primarily composed of neurons and astrocytes. TBR1 stainings do not show any clear nuclei 
stainings, but very large unspecific stainings, indicating that TBR1 was not present in our 
culture system. The result is consistent with the data from the qRT-PCR analysis, where 
TBR1 was almost undetectable. This could be attributed to TBR1 being predominantly 
associated with early development of neurons as found by Robert F Hevner et al. from 2001, 
which might indicate that antibody stainings were not performed early enough in the 
differentiation process to detect it. Robert F Hevner et al. found that TBR1 expression 
should still be present in minimal amounts indicating that antibody staining might not be 
sensitive enough for TBR1 detection. qRT-PCR showed very limited mRNA which is in line 
with what was found by the antibody stainings. A valuable tool would have been a positive 
control for stainings. However, neuronal cells expressing TBR1 are difficult to access and 
could therefore not be included. CTIP2 was detected in the nuclei, which is also in line with 
the literature, where it’s claimed that CTIP2 in contrast to TBR1 is expressed in mature 
neurons. Satb2 as an indicator of highly matured neurons showed only unspecific antibody

6.2.  ApoE expression in neural cultures 
ApoE expression in neural cultures was investigated by western blot and qRT-PCR. This was 
done in order to ensure that results detected in the following experiments, could be 
connected with a difference in the ApoE allele. The western blots did not detect any ApoE 
expression in the cells, which indicates that no ApoE was expressed or was present at so low 
levels that it was undetectable by western blotting. However, qRT-PCR detected ApoE 
expression in the neuronal cultures - though at significantly lower levels than those found in 
iPSC cultures. The lower levels of ApoE can be attributed to the presence of a neuron 
population that contributes GAPDH but no ApoE, which primarily stems from astrocytes. 
The function of ApoE in iPSCs is unknown.

6.3.  Oxygen consumption rate 
Mitochondrial impairment is a commonly described characteristic of neurodegenerative 
diseases, among them AD. Seahorse analysis was therefore performed to investigate 
whether the different ApoE genotypes showed altered OCRs.  
The results show a clear difference in the OCR of the five cell lines, with statistical 
significance between ApoE2/2 and ApoE4/4. The results are composed of 8 – 16 technical 
replicates and 3 biological replicates per run in 2 independent experiments. Interestingly, a 
mouse model system shows similar results in a previous study by Hung-Kai Chen et al. in 
2011 (Figure 44). As such, the data in the mouse experiment confirms what we found in our 
human cell model.  
Hung-Kai Chen et al. additionally identified, in what way ApoE interfered with the 
mitochondrial process, showing that ApoE altered the expression of mitochondrial genes. 
Hung-Kai Chen et al. suggests that the change in gene expression is due to the binding 
domain of ApoE4, which they illustrated by showing increased mitochondrial expression by 
introducing a small molecule that blocks ApoE binding. 
The findings of this study matches up very closely to what would be expected in regards to 
the risk of developing AD, as it shows that ApoE4/4 makes the cells less able to utilize 
oxygen. This will put the neurons in an overall weaker position, which over time might stack 
up to significant defects. Additional experiments will need to be performed in order to form 
a statistical significance for comparison of all lines.

An outlier in the process is however, the ApoE KO line, which shows an extreme difference 
compared to the remaining four lines. The KO line shows statistical significance when 
compared to the WT line and sporadic significance across the different time points for the 
other lines. This could be explained by the theory proposed by Hung-Kai Chen et al. that 
ApoE binding modulates the mitochondrial expression, ApoE3 and ApoE2 might also reduce 
expression but with a lower binding affinity. The removal of ApoE allows the cell to work at 
optimal levels. However, further studies will need to be performed to investigate how ApoE 
deficiency optimises mitochondrial respiration.  
 
6.4.  Oxidative stress sensitivity 
Results of the oxidative stress sensitivity analysis are unusable due to the failure of the two 
experiments performed.  
Experimental conditions for optimal H2O2 concentrations to measure the sensitivity of the 
neurons towards oxidative stress were based on the procedures of Zhang et al. 2017. The 
LDH activity in the media from cell death due to exposure to H2O2 was measured 
successfully. However, LDH activity in the media could not be normalized to total LDH 
activity as the cell lysis produced inconsistent results. Several different lysis buffers and 
treatment conditions have been attempted, to produce conditions that would consistently 
show similar results. Small subsets of date points could be extracted from the experiments 
that fell within the expected spectrum of results. However, the discrepancies of the lysis 
made these results too unreliable to put any significance on the data.  
The CellROX analysis was performed, at a late stage in the project. The aim was to measure 
the concentration of mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS) while under oxidative 
stress, as a compensation for the non-working LDH release assay. The results could have 
been integrated with the Seahorse experiment, where differences in basal and maximal 
respiration were found. 
However, the FACS analysis showed primarily cellular fragments instead of whole intact cells 
and therefore did not provide meaningful data. Due to the time constraints of the study, the 
experiment was performed only once.  
==PAGE:59==
Kennie R. Prehn – s112831 
 
Page 59 of 75 
 
 
01/02/2017 
As outlined in the results section, two gatings were performed, which was isolation of cells 
based on size (forward scatter) and complexity (sideward scatter) and then separation of 
stained and unstained cells based on gatings of negative and unstained control samples. 
Normally the initial gating covers a much smaller area with a more intense cellular 
concentration. This gating would then allow for a second gating that isolates the single cells 
to ensure that signals are actually significant for a single cell and not a cell lump.  Cut-off 
values for unstained, negative and positive controls would then allow for identification of 
stained cells. Four distinct cell populations were expected with this experimental setup, 
ROS-/Dead-, ROS+/Dead-, ROS+/Dead+ and ROS-/Dead+ but these did not appear due to the 
cell fragments present in the media and the limited gating performed.  Cut-off values could 
therefore not be established properly resulting in measurements showing highly variable 
results. 
The two primary reasons for the cellular fragments have been identified for improvement of 
the experimental procedure. First, resuspension following spin-down of the cells as outlined 
in the method section could have been too harsh, resulting in breakdown of the neurons as 
they are highly sensitive. It is also possible that resuspension was inadequate resulting in 
cells being stuck at the bottom of the wells while only cell fragments were present in the 
media. A possible way to alleviate both these problems would be to adjust the 
centrifugation of the cells so that it was performed at a lower speed or for a shorter time. 
This would prevent the cells from settling too firmly at the bottom of the well, thereby 
lowering the required mechanical force to resuspend cells and preventing both possible 
breakage and lack of cells in suspension.

establish a work flow. A secondary high sensitivity kit was employed but results were largely 
inconclusive due to a large response detected in the negative control, which means that 
measurements could not be trusted. The measured response in the negative control can

However, ground-breaking new research into the role of 
ApoE in Aβ production has been recently released from 
the lab of former Nobel prize laureate Thomas Suedhof. 
Huang et al. published an article in January of 2017 that 
has found a direct link between ApoE and the 
production of APP. This study has found that the binding 
of ApoE to an ApoE receptor prevents the degradation 
of the dual leucine-zipper kinase protein, which in turn 
starts a phosphorylation chain of MKK7 and ERK1/2 
MAP kinases (Figure 45). The phosphorylation of ERK1/2 
induces the phosphorylation of cFos which induces the 
transcription factor AP-1. This results in an increased 
transcription of APP.  
The increased APP transcription results in a large production of the Aβ by-product, which 
leads to more plaque formation and could be a reasonable explanation for ApoE being a risk 
factor for AD. The study also shows a clear difference between the three possible ApoE 
alleles. ApoE4 shows a larger potency for inducing APP compared to ApoE3, while ApoE2 
shows a lower potency (Figure 46). This difference can lead to a cumulative build-up over a 
whole life resulting in the increased AD risk for ApoE4 carriers.

6.7.  Astrocyte differentiation 
As ApoE was only poorly expressed in our neuronal culture system, a commercially available 
kit from Stem cell technologies was used to differentiate our NPCs to astrocytes. Results of 
the astrocyte differentiation did not look promising, in regards to ApoE isolation and 
potential co-cultures with larger astrocyte to neurons ratios which were the primary targets. 
Results of the GFAP and TUJ1 antibody stainings indicate that the ratio of neurons and 
astrocytes are similar to original neuronal differentiation protocol. The qRT-PCR performed 
showed comparable but lower levels of ApoE mRNA present in the astrocyte differentiation 
as compared to original differentiation protocol. Differentiation would have to be repeated 
to confirm the date, but results indicate that a lower ratio of astrocytes was present in the 
new differentiation.  
However, it is also possible that ApoE expression is increased in neurons of the original 
neuronal differentiation. Yadong Huang et al. published a study in 2004 showing neurons 
can express ApoE if under neuropathological conditions, which makes it possible that the 
difference in expression between the two differentiations is due to different handling of 
cells from which samples were taken. Experiments should be repeated at same time points 
in differentiation with same treatments of cells to ensure this is not a cause of variation.

6.8.  Alternate neuronal differentiation using the Ngn2 system 
Insertion of the Ngn2 system with CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing was performed successfully. A 
total of 242 cell colonies distributed evenly between the cell lines were picked after 
selection with neomycin and puromycin. The following controls by enzyme digestion PCR 
and DOX induction resulted in three out of five viable cell lines, ApoE2/2, ApoE4/4 and ApoE 
KO. Several nucleofections were required before producing the successful lines, which made 
the process very time consuming. Further nucleofections will be required to complete the 
set of ApoE lines but experiments were not performed due to time constraints. 
The results of the alternate Ngn2 differentiation system using DOX induction to produce 
mature neurons showed promising results. Antibody stainings of TUJ1 show similar results 
to established neuron differentiation. However, the transcription factors show very 
dissimilar expression when looking at antibody stainings and results are inconsistent with 
known neuronal progression.  As mentioned previously TBR1 is considered an early 
transcription factor in the neuron differentiation, while CTIP2 appears later in the process. 
Satb2 is a transcription factor found in very mature neurons but has been shown to have 
transient expression in early neurons. Brn2 is also a factor indicating very mature neurons. 
The Ngn2 neurons show early expression of TBR1 which is to be expected but CTIP2 is 
minimally expressed while Satb2 appears much earlier according to the daily qRT-PCR 
experiments. This indicates that more mature neurons are produced on a much shorter 
timescale, as the original neuronal differentiation had not begun showing clear signs of 
Satb2 and Brn1 until ~2-3 months into the differentiation. Antibody stainings and the 
second qRT-PCR however throw the Satb2 and Brn2 results into question. The antibody 
stainings do not indicate nuclei staining for Satb2 but rather unspecific stainings and the 
second qRT-PCR results for Satb2 and Brn2 indicate much lower levels compared to the first 
run performed.  
The Ngn2 based system shows great potential as an alternative method to establish 
neuronal models. Additionally the system has also been employed successfully in the study 
by Huang et al. showing its prospects. Further studies are required to investigate the effects 
of the lack of CTIP2 expression and to produce repeatable results for the qRT-PCR for Satb2 
and Brn1.

7.  Conclusion 
In this study, neuron based cell models have been generated from isogenic gene edited iPSCs for 
phenotypic determination of ApoE3/4, ApoE2/2, ApoE3/3, ApoE4/4 and ApoE KO. Characterization 
of the morphology, qRT-PCR of CTIP2, Brn1 and Satb2 as well as antibody stainings with TUJ1 and 
CTIP2, have shown the iPSCs to have successfully been differentiated. Structures of astrocytes have 
also been shown with GFAP stainings. ApoE production was assessed by western blot and qRT-PCR 
to establish ApoE production, which was determined to be relatively low. To alleviate the low ApoE 
expression an alternative differentiation method was employed to increase the amount of 
astrocytes for higher ApoE production. This was met with limited success as determined by antibody 
staining against GFAP and qRT-PCR of ApoE showing similar or worse results for the desired 
parameters. 
OCR was assessed for the five lines with the ApoE2/2 line showing statistically significant 
improvement of OCR when compared to ApoE4/4. This provides clear signs that ApoE has influence 
on the mitochondrial respiration, which is commonly affected in neurodegenerative diseases like AD. 
As such, the results strengthen the hope that the system has the potential to capture 
pathomechanistic effects related to AD and might be a valuable tool for research and drug 
development. Experiments in the form of a LDH-analysis and CellROX were performed investigate 
the sensitivity towards oxidative stress. Results were initially promising but could not be replicated. 
The sources of failures were thoroughly analysed.  
ELISA assay of Aβ40/42 concentration and ratio was attempted and should investigate a potential 
link between the ApoE genotypes and APP production, but failed due to errors with kit used for 
determination. New research in connection between Aβ40/42 and ApoE was reviewed and its 
relevance for ApoE phenotypes for AD was investigated.  
CRISPR-Cas9 gene edited lines for the L150P PSEN1 mutation were generated successfully for the 
ApoE3/4, ApoE2/2 and ApoE4/4 lines for the purpose of creating cell lines with increased AD 
phenotypes. Success was determined by enzyme digestion PCR and genetic sequencing, generating 
multiple lines for further studies into AD phenotypes.  
Insertion of the Ngn2 induction system was successfully performed by CRISPR-Cas9 and insertion 
was checked by enzyme digestion PCR and morphology studies following induction by DOX. The 
differentiated lines were characterized by antibody stainings with TUJ1 and TBR1 as well as by qRT-
PCR of TBR1, Brn1 and Satb2. Ngn2 based induction show significant potential as an easier to handle, 
faster and less expensive differentiation system.

8.  Future perspectives 
The measured changes in OCR found by this study concur with the mouse study of Hung-Kai Chen et 
al. showing changes consistent with the ApoE alleles. This makes performing several of the 
experiments conducted by Hung-Kai Chen et al., in regards to mitochondrial transcription with the 
new cell models produced in this study, very interesting. Repeating the experiments ensures that the 
data found in the mouse study, translates into human conditions and to ensure removal of variability 
due to the study not using isogenic lines.  Repeating the Seahorse experiment to determine whether 
the OCR change is only present in neurons would also be of interest, such as repeating the Seahorse 
experiment on original undifferentiated iPSCs for comparison.  
Replicating the experiments of Huang et al. with the isogenic ApoE cell lines would also provide 
interesting insight, by eliminating possible genetic variations. If similar data is gathered from the 
isogenic ApoE lines, it would strongly collaborate the Huang et al. study and would provide increased 
certainty for going forward with the research. 
In extension to the study of Huang et al. it would be interesting to determine whether ApoE 
expression is increased during times of neuropathological conditions for the neuronal cells as found 
by Yadong Huang et al. This might show how oxidative stress could have an influence on AD 
progression, by increasing ApoE expression in neurons resulting in more Aβ being produced. 
Finishing the L150P PSEN1 mutated lines and repeating experiments with the new lines would 
determine whether they show similar or enhanced phenotypes as the non-edited lines. This could 
help by enhancing effects that might otherwise get lost in the “noise” associated with the various 
experiments performed.  
The Ngn2 line also provides a helpful method to produce neurons that show maturity markers at 
much earlier time points. This technology would have the potential to significantly reduce the costs 
associated with researching and producing neurons, as the time frame would be significantly 
shortened. This would allow for the iPSCs derived neurons to be applied as a more general 
technique. The gene editing process would first have to be streamlined to reduce processing time 
and costs associated with the process.

10.1.  Media recipe’s 
10.1.1.  
Preparation of NBM 
1. Introduction 
a. This SOP describes the procedure for the preparation of Neurobasal medium 
(NBM). NBM is used for mature neurons after neuronal maturation. It is also 
used for the preparation of Neural induction medium (NIM), Neural 
expansion medium (NEM), and Neural maturation medium (NMM).  
2. Scope 
a. This procedure is intended as a ‘Benchmark protocol’ for use by EBiSC 
derivation centers providing iPSC lines intended for the EBiSC Foundational 
Collection. 
b. This SOP applies to preparation and use of NB medium within Bioneer A/S 
3. Keywords 
a. Coating, PLO, Laminin, Neural progenitors, Neurons 
4. Keywords 
a. Coating, Laminin, Poly-L-Ornithin, PLO, neurons 
5. Consumables 
a. N2 Supplement, Gibco, 17502-048 5 mL 
b. B27 Supplement, Gibco, 12587-010 10 mL 
c. DMEM/F12 (+ L-glut and Hepes), Gibco, 11330-057 
d. Neurobasal medium, Gibco, 21103049 
e. Glutamax, Gibco, 21103049 
f. 1 sterile glass bottle, 500 mL 
g. Penicillin/Streptomycin; Lonza; DE17-602F 
6. Procedure 
a. All medium preparation procedures must be performed under aseptic 
conditions within in a class 2 Microbiological Safety Cabinet 
b. Mix 250 mL of DMEM/F12 with 250 mL Neurobasal medium in a sterile glass 
bottle 
c. Add 5 mL of N2 Supplement 
d. Add 10 mL of B27 Supplement 
e. Add 5 mL of Glutamax 
f. Add 500 µL of Penicillin/Streptomycin 
g. Keep the medium in the fridge and protect from light 
h. Warm up to room temperature prior to use

10.2.  Various 
10.2.1.  
Coating with Poly-L-Ornithine and Laminin 
1. Introduction 
a. This SOP describes the procedure for the use and preparation of Poly-L-
Ornithine (PLO) and Laminin for the maintenance of neural progenitors 
2. Scope 
a. This procedure is intended as a ‘Benchmark protocol’ for use by EBiSC 
derivation centers providing iPSC lines intended for the EBiSC Foundational 
Collection. 
b. This SOP applies to preparation and use of PLO and Laminin within Bioneer 
A/S 
3. Keywords 
a. Coating, Poly-L-Ornithine (PLO), Laminin, Neural progenitors, Neurons 
4. Consumables 
a. PLO, Sigma, P4957, diluted 1:10 in PBS for short term culture (<30 days) and 
1:5 for long term culture (>30days) 
b. Laminin (1 – 2 mg/mL), Sigma, L2020, diluted 1:200 in PBS (10 – 20 µg/mL) for 
short term culture (<30 days) and 1:100 for long term culture (>30days) 
c. PBS, Lonza, cat. no. BE17-512F 
d. 6 well cell culture plate 
5. Procedure 
a. Add 1 mL of 1:10 or 1:5 diluted PLO to one well of a 6 well plate and incubate 
either for 4 hours at 37°C or overnight at 4° C  plates with PLO can be 
sealed with parafilm and stored in the fridge for later use (one month works, 
longer storage not tested) 
b. Remove the PLO and wash 1X with PBS 
c. Add 1 mL of 1:200 or 1:100 diluted Laminin to the well and incubate either 
for 4 hours at 37° C or overnight at 4° C  plates with PLO/Laminin can be 
sealed with parafilm and stored in the fridge for later use (10 days works, 
longer storage not tested) 
d. Remove the Laminin and wash 1X with PBS. Plates are ready to use.
