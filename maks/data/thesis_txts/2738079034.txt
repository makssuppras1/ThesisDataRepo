Abstract
The high demand for renewables results in a rapid acceleration of offshore wind development,
including floating offshore wind, which necessitates a robust evaluation of key performance
indicators to ensure healthy growth. One key indicator is wind farm availability. This thesis
focuses on developing an O&M model tailored for floating wind farms, emphasizing the assess-
ment of availability and providing methods to optimize it by understanding the factors that
most influence availability. A key finding is that moving from three turbines per intervention to
two turbines per intervention results in a 2.8% increase in availability but also a 41% increase
in costs. This highlighting the importance of selecting an appropriate maintenance strategy.
Another finding is the impact of failure rates, where a 30% reduction in failure rates increases
availability by 2.9% and reduces costs by 27%. This underlines the importance of enhancing
turbine reliability through a comprehensive maintenance plan.

Introduction
In this section, background information about the current situation related to wind energy,
which results in the interest in wind farm availability, is presented. This is followed by the
research question, objectives, novelty of the work, and thesis structure.
1.1
Background
In 2009, the EU established the Renewable Energy Directive to promote the use of energy
from renewable sources (including wind energy). Since its introduction, the share of renewable
energy sources in EU energy consumption has increased from 12.5% in 2010 to 23% in 2022
[47].
In 2023, the Renewable Energy Directive was revised and the EU targets at least 42.5% of
renewables by 2030 in order to achieve the long-term target of net-zero emissions by 2050.
According to the latest data, the current share of renewables in Europe’s energy production is
23%, which means the share needs to be nearly double in six years to achieve the 2030 targets.
Wind energy plays a crucial role in reaching the 2030 and 2050 targets where according to
Eurostat, wind accounted for over one-third (37.5%) of the total electricity generated from
renewable sources in the EU in 2022 [17],
These ambitious targets, the energy crisis that followed the Russian invasion of Ukraine, the
diﬀiculties faced European wind industry in operating its business and with a total of 16GW of
wind projects installed in 2022 (18.2GW in 2023). This number is nowhere near the 37GW/year
needed to achieve the EU 2030 targets. This situation calls for immediate action, leading to
the announcement of the European Wind Power Action Plan based on six pillars to accelerate
wind energy manufacturing and deployment.
As a result, offshore wind development is accelerating rapidly, and floating wind turbines have
started to gain popularity over conventional offshore wind turbines due to their numerous
advantages and are considered one of the most promising ways to achieve 2050 targets of net-
zero emissions.
With the growth of the floating wind industry, there is a need to use different key performance
indicators to ensure a healthy growth of this industry. One main key performance indicator is
availability.
1.2
Research question
Availability of wind turbines and wind farms is not only an important factor for energy genera-
tion and warranty but it is an important parameter in operation and maintenance (O&M). Due
to the early stages of the floating wind industry, there is a lack of data and research about float-
ing offshore wind turbines, unlike fixed-bottom offshore wind turbines. The following question
will be investigated in this thesis:
• How can algorithms and methodologies be developed and integrated into an Operations
and Maintenance (O&M) tool to effectively manage the availability and maintenance
challenges of floating wind farms, considering the highly uncertain nature of reliability
and maintenance data in the offshore wind sector?

1.3
Objectives
The aim of this thesis can be divided into primary and secondary.
Primary objectives:
• Develop an Operations and Maintenance (O&M) tool specifically designed for floating
wind farms and focusing on evaluating the availability of these farms.
• Enhance the planning and execution of O&M activities, integrating these improvements
within the newly developed tool.
Secondary objectives:
• Understand the impact of different inputs on the overall availability of floating wind
farms.
• Develop a cost module to assess the cost associated with the operation and maintenance
of floating wind farms.
1.4
Novelty of the work
The novelty of this work lies in the conclusions drawn from developing a versatile O&M tool
designed to evaluate and optimize the availability of floating wind farms. The developed o&m
tool designed for the early planning stages of floating wind farms, where accuracy is not the
primary concern. Unlike existing tools, which are often highly detailed and complex, this pro-
posed tool emphasizes simplicity, enabling planners to make informed decisions with limited
initial information. By focusing on the early stages, this tool aids in the allocation and priori-
tization of resources related to labor, spare parts, and vessels, ultimately improving wind farm
availability and reducing maintenance costs.
1.5
Thesis structure
This thesis is structured into five different sections. It begins with an introduction including
a background, research question, objectives, novelty and structure. In Section Two, a litera-
ture review includes theoretical background about floating wind turbine technology, offshore
O&M modelling , availability and cost. Section Three details the methodologies used to de-
velop various modules of the O&M tool. In Section Four, the results of the simulation are

2.1
Floating wind energy
Floating wind energy technology is considered to be one of the most promising sources of wind
energy in the future. Notably, 80% of the world’s offshore wind resources potential lies in waters
deeper than 60m [24], where floating wind turbines become more feasible compared to fixed-
bottom ones. Since the installation of Hywind, the world’s first floating wind farm, the floating
wind industry has been expanding annually. According to the global offshore wind report [24],
it is expected that the commercialisation of floating offshore wind to be reached after 2026.
Based on the same report, the period 2021-2025 is considered to be a pre-commercial phase.
Europe has 4 small floating wind farms today – totalling 176 MW of capacity [22]. Europe
can be confident it will have 3-4 GW of floating wind in operation by 2030. And it is not
unreasonable to think it will have 10 GW by then if Governments back up their expansion
targets with the right policies [22].
Globally, is expected to exceed 5GW by 2030 and 25GW by 2035 [18].

Among the advantages of floating offshore wind are the potentially low environmental impact
and the ease of manufacture and installation, as the floating turbines and platforms can be
built and assembled on land and then towed to the offshore installation site. In addition, as

2.1.1
Floating platform
The floating platform also called a floater or floating substructure, is the concrete, steel or
hybrid substructure on which the wind turbine is installed, providing it with buoyancy and
stability [20].
There are different concepts for floating platforms, the four main types are
shown in Figure 2.
Figure 2: The different types of floating platforms. Source: Ørsted.
• Tension-leg platform (TLP):
Pontoon-based structure anchored with a tensioned
mooring system that goes straight down to the seabed [19]. This type of platform is
stabilized by taut mooring lines. However, this foundation is not stable in itself and
remains stable only when the mooring line is under tension Losing one leg could result
in the loss of the entire unit [29]. It is has simple stuctural design and low fatigue loads
[40].
• Semi-submersible: Also known as spar-submersible. It is a column and pontoon-based
structure anchored to the seabed with a spread mooring system [19]. The platform utilises
buoyancy far from the centre (similar to the barge) and ballast at the base of each column
(similar to the spar) [23]. It is consider to be one of the most feasible floating platform
[10].
• Barge: A hull structure with a large surface area in contact with the water – the principle
is like that of ship design. Anchored to the seabed with a spread mooring system [19]. In
short, buoyancy is provided by the geometry of the cylinder, while stability is provided
by the weight at the lowest point [21].
• Spar buoy: A vertical cylindrical structure with ballast at the bottom for stability.
Anchored to the seabed with a spread mooring system [19]. This type can extend 100m
or more underwater, they can not be deployed in normal docks which are not deep enough
[23]. This concept was adopted in Hywind project, the first full-scale FOWT prototype
[49].

2.1.2
Mooring system
A mooring system is used to keep a floating structure in place when the entire turbine system
is subjected to loads and contributes to the stability of the turbine and floater [4]. There are
three main types of mooring systems: Catenary, taut leg and tendon (tension leg).
Figure 4: Main types of mooring systems. Source: Vryhof Anchors B.V. 2015.
• Catenary: This is usually made as steel chains or wires. The weight of these mooring
lines is responsible for generating most of the restoring forces. The weight also becomes a
limited factor for the motion of the floaters. One of the advantages in this type is the low
cost of the anchor but the large footprint is a main disadvantage where a large footprint
would disturb the seabed and affect the marine habitat [41].
• Taut leg: This type is usually made from synthetic fibres or ropes which means less
weight than catenary, so the taut leg mooring overcomes the problem of the weight. The
main difference between the catenary and taut leg can be seen at the seabed where the
catenary arrives horizontally while the taut leg arrives at an angle. Taut leg mooring has
a smaller footprint, which helps in reducing costs and lower impact on marine habitats
[12].
• Tendon (Tension leg): This type is made from steel cables. This type has a very small
footprint and the platform becomes more stable but it requires costly anchors [12].

==PAGE:14==
The mooring system consists of three main components: mooring lines, connectors and an-
chors. Each of these components has different types and shapes based on the application. A
comprehensive guide to each type of component can be found in [60].
2.1.3
Cables
The purpose of the cables is to connect the floating wind farm to the electrical grid. There
are two main types of cables involved: inter-array cables and export cables. Inter-array cables
transmit power from the turbines to the floating offshore substation (FOSS), while export cables
carry power from the FOSS to an onshore substation [6].
Figure 5: Inter-array and export cables in floating wind turbine farm layout. Source: WFO,2024
• Inter-array cables: Besides the main purpose of inter-array cables, they also provide
auxiliary power to the turbines when they are not generating and provide fibre commu-
nications [6]. These cables are designed with dynamic lengths and utilize a lazy wave
configuration to accommodate the movement of the floating platforms. At the seabed,
the cable is either buried or sits on the seabed anchored. Inter-array cables are relatively
short in length, this is the reason that these cables are AC (3 phase generally). The
voltage rating of the inter-array cables available in today’s floating offshore wind farms is
36kV or 66kV. These ratings are expected to increase in the future wind farms to 132kV
to accommodate higher power turbines [62].
• Export cables: These cables are static, as of today, there are no dynamic export cables
available in the market [6][62]. For long distances, typically further than 80 to 100Km, a
High-Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) is used as these types have less transmission loss.
In contrast, a High-voltage Alternating Current (HVAC) is a more eﬀicient choice for
small and medium distances. The voltage rating of the current static HVDC is typically
220kV and 320kV for larger and more distant offshore wind farms.
2.2
O&M modelling of offshore wind energy
The Operations and Maintenance (O&M) phase of offshore wind projects constitutes approx-
imately 30% of the total life-cycle cost [63]. Consequently, there is increasing focus on this
7
==PAGE:15==
phase as a key area for potential cost reduction. This has led to increased interest in O&M
simulation tools that can be used to estimate costs, assess availability, and evaluate various
maintenance strategies. There are various tools designed for different purposes within offshore
wind projects. The available O&M tools can support three types of decisions [3]. The first is
operational decisions, which are made on a daily basis. The second type is tactical decisions,
typically made annually. Finally, there are strategic decisions, which are made before a farm
begins operation or every 10-20 years [3]. These tools can be found both commercially and in
academic settings.
The development of an O&M tool raises several challenges that require extensive collaboration
between industry, academia, and tool developers [26]. Some of these challenges include:
• Validation: Ensuring the reliability of these tools necessitates rigorous validation against
real projects. This is challenging because it requires long-term access to and tracking of
the project data. Additionally, validating new technologies, such as towing-to-shore, is
diﬀicult due to limited operational experience with these technologies.
• Accurate Maintenance Parameters: Obtaining precise data on maintenance parame-
ters is crucial. This includes failure rates of components, repair times, vessel mobilization,
and cost estimates.
• Understanding Sources of Uncertainty: Identifying and understanding the sources
of uncertainty is essential, as these uncertainties impact decision-making at all stages of
the project.
Several tools have been developed with significant methodological differences between them. A
comprehensive list of academic and industry models can be found in [32] and [25]. Below is a
review of some of these tools:
• CONTOFOAX: Developed by TU Delft, CONTOFOAX is a simulation tool designed
to assess the availability of wind farms at specific locations. It employs a Monte Carlo
approach to realistically model maintenance actions under varying conditions of wind and
waves, random failures, predefined maintenance crew schedules, and equipment availabil-
ity. Additionally, it incorporates stochastic weather simulation [5].
• O2M Created by Garrad Hassan, O2M is a tool designed to optimize operation and
maintenance strategies by balancing the costs of lost production and resource utilization
[56]. The model performs a time-domain Monte Carlo simulation for wind farms, assigning
each turbine a reliability profile based on Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF). It also
accounts for wave height [13].
• NOWIcob: Primarily developed by NOWITECH, the Research Council of Norway,
NOWIcob analyzes operational and maintenance strategies using a discrete-event Monte
Carlo simulation. This model simulates maintenance operations over the operational life
of a wind farm. Its weather module utilizes time series data of wind speed, wind direction,
wave height, wave period, and wave direction. The tool is implemented in Matlab, with
input entry and result viewing managed in Excel [58].
• ECN: Is the only tool validated by Germanischer Lloyd [44]. It is designed for use in the
planning phase of offshore wind farms, calculating costs and downtime from unplanned
corrective maintenance while also considering preventive maintenance. Programmed in
MS-Excel with Visual Basic, ECN is not a simulation tool; instead, it uses long-term
average data as input [43].

==PAGE:16==
When exploring operational and maintenance (O&M) tools for floating offshore wind, it be-
comes evident that several of these tools are adaptations of existing models. For instance,
Dewan & Asgarour [15] utilized a modified version of the ECN O&M Access tool, incorporating
tow-to-shore considerations tailored for floating offshore applications. Other approaches em-
ploy models specifically developed for offshore wind. For example, Martini et al. [37] employed
three distinct models to assess the impact of downtime on the long-term energy production of a
floating wind farm. The first model is a discrete event model that simulates failures and repair
durations. The second model focuses on the dynamics of floating wind turbines, accounting for
platform movements and operational halts. Lastly, the third model addresses the wind farm
dynamics, capturing wake interactions among turbines[37].
The existing models and tools for offshore wind are based on a combination of risk analysis,
uncertainty analysis, and reliability engineering [34]. These methodologies are used to model
the relationship between availability, cost, and maintenance strategies, taking into account
weather variation. The main model inputs are met-ocean data, reliability, maintenance and
logistics. Figure shows a flow chart of the common components of O&M tool.

2.2.1
Met-ocean data
Met-ocean conditions significantly influence the accessibility, total downtime and availability of
a wind farm. Typically, met-ocean conditions are assessed through two key weather parameters:
wind speed and significant wave height (Hs).
While these metrics suﬀice for fixed-bottom
offshore wind farms, floating offshore maintenance activities necessitate the consideration of an
additional parameter: peak wave period (Tp) [51]. This parameter is crucial for floating turbines
as it characterizes the movement between the turbine and maintenance vessels, ensuring safe
and eﬀicient operations [51].
Met-ocean data is typically used to assess accessibility and identify weather windows. Accessi-
bility refers to the amount of time that vessels can access a site safely [50]. Weather windows
are a discrete time periods where access is possible [50] . In general, there are two approaches
for determining accessibility and weather windows. One approach involves analyzing historical
9
==PAGE:17==
time series to identify weather patterns and trends. While simple, this method is limited to
the conditions present in the historical data. The other approach employs stochastic methods,
such as Monte Carlo or Markov chain simulations, to model and predict weather conditions.
Time series approaches are advantageous when historical data is available, as they can effec-
tively capture trends, patterns, and seasonality over time. Conversely, statistical methods are
more beneficial when data is limited, as they rely on underlying probability distributions and
can make inferences or predictions based on smaller datasets [50]. Recently, a third approach
has started to gain popularity. This approach is based on data-driven models using artificial
intelligence (AI)[57].
The met-ocean conditions for FOW are expected to be more severe compared to FBW due to
the remote nature of FOW installations. Therefore, incorporating an accurate weather module
into O&M modeling is crucial to enhance accessibility, safety, and availability. Various models
have been developed to forecast weather conditions, each with strengths suited to different
timeframes. Below is a review of some models used in O&M modeling:
• Exponential smoothing: Is one of the most basic models widely used in the industry
due to its simplicity and accuracy [8]. This model assigns weights to past data to predict
future values.
However, it can sometimes be inaccurate, requiring adjustments.
To
estimate predictive error, methods such as Mean Squared Error (MSE) might be employed
[9].
• Markov Chain: Is a widely accepted tool for modeling and has been used to solve
various problems in wind energy literature [33] [8]. This model assumes that future states
depend only on the current state [42]. In [42], which compared LSTM and Markov Chain
methods, it was found that the Markov Chain model is effective and fast for forecasting
long-term weather conditions.
However, it has limitations when used for wind speed
prediction, particularly for short intervals.
• Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM): Is an artificial recurrent neural network capable
of learning long-term dependencies by incorporating memory units. These memory units
allow the network to learn, forget previous hidden states, and update hidden states [28].
In [42], which compared LSTM and Markov Chain methods, it was found that while
LSTM was less effective in terms of accuracy and uncertainty for wind speed forecasting,
it performed relatively better at wave height forecasting. A limitation of the LSTM model
is the substantial amount of time and data needed to train it.
2.2.2
Reliability
Reliability is defined as the ability of an item to perform a required function under specified
environmental and operational conditions for a given period of time [45]. This general definition
can be applied to wind turbine systems and subsystems. Reliability can be measured in various
ways [45]:
• Mean Time To Failure (MTTF)
• Failure rate.
• Probability that the item does not fail in a time interval (0,t].
• Probability that the item can function at time t.
10
==PAGE:18==
The first two ways to measure reliability are commonly used as input modules when developing
an O&M tool. The reliability data and failure rates are used to estimate the number of transfers
to the turbine per annum. Over time, as wind turbine components age, their failure rates change
accordingly. This can be represented by the so-called ”bathtub curve”, where failure rates are
high during the early life of the turbine, decrease during its mid-life indicating a high reliability,
and increase again towards the end of the turbine’s lifespan.

With the lack of data about failure rates of offshore wind turbines, there are some studies that
tried to analyse the few published data. One widely accepted paper by Carrol [7] is provides

4MW, and a rotor diameter between 80m and 120m. The wind turbines included in the analysis

Maintenance
Maintenance is another crucial input for O&M modeling.
The European standard defines
maintenance as a ”combination of all technical, administrative, and managerial actions during
the life cycle of an item intended to retain it in, or restore it to, a state in which it can perform
the required function” (DIN EN 13306). Maintenance strategies for offshore wind farms can
be divided into two main classes: failure-based (reactive response), and proactive maintenance.
The main difference is when the failure is carried out [55]. These two classes then is divided
into different types as shown in Figure 8.

Figure 8: Maintenance Classification [46]
Corrective maintenance:
This type of maintenance is performed to repair or replace a component or system that no
longer fulfils its designed purpose. It is divided into [44]:
• Planned maintenance: Based on observing the degradation of a component or system,
this type of maintenance is scheduled to occur before the actual failure happens.
• Unplanned maintenance: This is conducted after a component or system has already
failed.
Proactive maintenance:
This type is more advance, where the inspection and replacement is carried out before failure
occurs to prevent minor failures from developing into a major failures [46]. It is divided into:
• Preventive Maintenance: This type of maintenance refers to scheduled maintenance
that takes place at a predetermined period or at a given level of power generation [46].
• Condition-based maintenance: Sometimes called predictive maintenance, it is based
on the actual health of the system. This approach typically relies on real-time monitoring
using various types of sensors to identify any signs of potential failure [44].
Opportunistic maintenance:
This maintenance type involves a series of activities performed on a component whenever the
opportunity arises during the maintenance of other components within a multi-component
system [64].
Another aspect of maintenance as an input to O&M modelling is the location of carrying out the
repair. Maintenance of floating wind farms, especially for large components, can be either in-
situ or ex-situ depending on whether the operation is conducted offshore or port side. Deciding
a maintenance strategy depends on many factors such as type of floating substructure, distance
from shore, depth of water, type of repair or failure, port capabilities and cost [51]. Below is
a review of some common maintenance strategies proposed for floating turbines that used in

2.3
Availability
The term availability, as used in the wind industry, is a measure of the potential for a wind tur-
bine or wind farm to generate power [16]. Therefore, if the turbine is available, grid-connected,
and the wind and other conditions are within the operating limits, then the turbine should
produce power. Availability greatly influences wind turbine energy production and revenue.
It is a key performance indicator for wind turbines and is used for many purposes, including
design performance evaluation and warranties. Because of its many uses, various methods for
calculating availability have been developed. Therefore, it is very important to understand the
various definitions of availability and how to calculate it.
The International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) standard, IEC 61400-26-1, provides a
structured and standardized method for recording the operating states of wind plants, but it
does not seek to standardise the availability definitions themselves [16]. In contrast, DNV,
a leading independent engineer for wind energy projects worldwide, provide a more detailed
framework to record these operating states. They suggest that some states, which are often
contentious or diﬀicult to distinguish based on available operating data, should be broken
down into more specific categories. This detailed breakdown helps in better capturing the true
performance and availability of the wind plants.
Availability can be measured for both a single wind turbine and an entire wind farm. When
measured for a single turbine, it’s referred to as turbine availability, which is useful for eval-
uating the turbine’s technology. In this context, downtime due to non-turbine causes, such as
grid outages, is typically disregarded. Conversely, when measured for a wind farm comprising
multiple turbines, it is known as system availability. This measure helps assess the overall
power production of the wind farm, accounting for all downtimes [16]. Contractual availability
and technical availability are two distinct metrics used to evaluate the performance of turbines
in wind energy projects. Contractual availability, defined under a turbine supply agreement
(TSA) or an operations and maintenance (O&M) service agreement, includes allowances for
specific conditions such as scheduled maintenance periods when the turbine is not generating
power but is still considered available by the original equipment manufacturer (OEM). In con-
trast, technical availability, also known as effective turbine availability, measures the turbine’s
performance without such allowances. It focuses on the turbine’s actual operational time or
the energy produced when site conditions are within the specified operating ranges, providing
a more accurate measure of turbine performance.
There are different methods to calculate availability given a set of operational data.
2.3.1
Time-based availability
Time-based availability is calculated based on the ratio between times. According to DNV
GL, there are two distinct definitions of time: full-period and Wind-in-limits. This method is
straightforward to compute and does not take into account the variations in wind speed over
time [16]. The general formula for calculating time-based availability is:
15
==PAGE:23==
Time-based availability =
Time available (in hours)
Total time in consideration (in hours)
(1)
Due to the two definitions of time, the denominator in Equation 1 can vary. When calculating
annual availability, it might be set to a full year, such as 8760 hours. However, if the wind-in-
limit definition is used, the denominator changes to include only the time when the wind speed
and temperature are within the turbine’s operating limits [16].
2.3.2
Production-based availability
Production-based availability (PBA), also known as energy-based availability, is calculated
based on the fraction between energy produced and energy expected based on actual wind
speeds. Unlike time-based availability, PBA accounts for variations in wind speed over time.
This method is more complex to calculate because it requires additional data, such as the
turbine’s power curve and actual wind speeds. The general formula for calculating production-
based availability is:
Production-based availability =
Energy actually produced (in kWh)
Energy potentially expected (in kWh)
(2)
According to DNV, the wind-in-limit time-based availability results are very close to the
production-based availability results.

are non-exclusive and may occur simultaneously. For example, if a turbine is not producing
power due to a fault state, but is also experiencing calm conditions (below cut-in speed) and is
inaccessible for repair due to a snowstorm (suspended state), prioritizing these states becomes
necessary. From the owner’s perspective, the fault state would take precedence over the calm
and suspended states.

Methodology
In this section, the methodologies used in the modeling of the proposed O&M model of floating
wind farms are discussed. Starting with an overview of the model and the interaction of the
different modules. Following this, each module within the tool is presented and described in
detail, including the assumptions made. At the end, a reference wind farm is selected to perform
the analysis.
3.1
Model overview
The proposed operation and maintenance (O&M) tool focuses on finding the availability and
direct maintenance costs of floating wind farms. Unlike most existing O&M tools, which typi-
cally adopt a probabilistic approach to account for uncertainty, this tool employs a deterministic
approach based on statistical methods. This approach offers an eﬀicient and straightforward
means of achieving the tool’s objectives. To address uncertainty, the tool includes sensitivity
analysis and the examination of different maintenance cases.
The tool is developed mainly on Excel, with a use of Python in modelling some modules
like weather module. The model is only calculating the wind farm availability based on the
availability of wind turbines only, as the availability of substations are not included in this
study. Furthermore, the tool calculates direct maintenance costs.
The model consists of four main modules: weather, reliability, maintenance, and cost. These
modules interact with each other to compute the availability and maintenance costs for floating
wind farms. The interaction of these modules is illustrated in Figure 15.
Figure 15: Tool overview modules interaction
The model requires specific inputs related to weather conditions, failure rates, and wind farm
characteristics. These inputs are processed within the different modules, resulting in various
outputs such as weather windows, expected repair times, and costs associated with labor, ma-
terials, and vessels. These outputs are then utilized to evaluate different maintenance scenarios
and strategies, ultimately determining the availability of the wind farm and the direct mainte-
nance costs.
The core of the model is based on calculating the downtimes from different sources such as
weather, repair, travel, vessel mobilization, spare parts .. etc. The total downtime can be
calculated as follows:

After understanding the failure, the operator checks the availability of the necessary

will consider the item that takes the longest to arrive.

Once the vessel and spare parts are ready, the operator ensures the availability of tech-

overall downtime calculation.

Even if the vessel, spare parts, and technicians are ready, adverse weather conditions

once the repair is completed.

3.2
Weather Module
The objective of this module is to assess the accessibility of the wind farm and determine the
downtime caused by weather conditions. Wind farm accessibility can be influenced by various
factors, including [50]:
• Wind speed
• Significant wave height
• Peak wave period
• Distance to port
• Visibility
• Fog
For this study, the focus is on the first two factors: wind speed and significant wave height. Wind
speed is critical for ensuring technician safety during operations and is especially important
when helicopters are used for crew transfers or heavy lifting tasks. Significant wave height
affects vessel motion and the safety of the crew [50].
24
==PAGE:32==
When discussing floating wind turbines, it is crucial to consider the peak wave period due to
its significant impact on workability. Workability refers to the ability of technicians to perform
their tasks without their comfort being compromised by adverse conditions [53]. Closely re-
lated to workability is transportability, which is defined as the ability to transport technicians
without their comfort being negatively affected by external factors. Both workabaility and
transportability are not considered in the weather module modelling, as this study using a very
similar vessel limits used in [53], where it was found that the workability and transportability
restriction on the weather windows are lower than the restriction of the vessel limits used. It is
worth mentioning that the workability is not necessarily becoming worse at higher sea states.
The response of the floater, which leads to high accelerations, may even be more significant in
low wave height and low frequency wave states [52].
The weather data used in this study is the ERA5 reanalysis data, provided by the European
Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). ERA5 uses historical real-world data
along with advanced modeling to generate detailed weather data. The data used covers the
period from 1990-2019 with a resolution of one hour and was downloaded from the ESOC
LAUTEC website [35].
To determine accessibility, a Python code was developed to compute weather windows based on
wind speed and wave height. This calculation helps find accessibility and the average waiting
time between consecutive weather windows, which is then used to calculate the total downtime
due to weather conditions. An outline of the steps followed in weather module can be seen in
Figure 17.
Figure 17: Outline of the steps followed in weather module
The code begins by importing the weather data. It requests the user to enter desired values
for wind speed limit, wave height limit, and the length of the weather window. Based on these
inputs, the code filters all the weather data between 06:00 and 18:00, which corresponds to the
working shift. These filtered data points represent weather windows that meet the wind speed
and wave height criteria set by the user. The code then identifies the start and end points
of each weather window to calculate its length and compare it with the desired value entered
by the user. The code subsequently identifies all accessible weather windows that meet the
specified criteria. Accessibility is calculated by dividing the sum of accessible weather windows
by the total time.

Next, the code calculates the waiting time between two consecutive accessible weather windows.

which may differ from daily or monthly averages. While this does not help in planning main-

are needed, it is assumed that the weather remains accessible. This initial assumption will be
revised when using different maintenance strategies, such as ”two to shore.”
3.3
Failure analysis
The aim of this module is to utilize the available data on failure rates and repair times to
determine the expected time to repair. The turbine is modeled as a combination of different
subsystems, each with a specific failure rate per year. The definition of failure used in this
study is adopted from [7], where failure is defined as an instance requiring a visit to the turbine
26
==PAGE:34==
and the consumption of materials, which includes anything used or replaced in the turbine.
The failures are classified into three categories: replacements, major repairs, and minor repairs.
The definitions of each failure type as used in this study are as follows:
• Replacement: The turbine shuts down immediately once the failure occurs and remains
non-operational until the faulty part is replaced. This typically involves material costs
above €10,000 and has a long lead time.
• Major repair: Similar to replacement failures, the turbine remains non-operational until
the repair is completed. This usually involves replacing materials costing between €1,000
and €10,000, but with a shorter lead time than replacement failures, resulting in shorter
downtimes.
• Minor repair: The turbine remains operational when the failure occurs and during the
repair process. This includes materials costing less than €1,000.
These definitions are incorporated into the modeling process, indicating that only replacement
and major repair failures impact the availability calculations, whereas minor repairs do not
affect availability but do influence costs.
The turbine is modeled as a combination of 24 subsystems, including the floating structure,
inter-array, and export cables. The failure rates are assumed to be constant, and data provided
by sources [7] and [48] are used for the failure rates and repair times.

3.4
Maintenance Module
This study focuses exclusively on unplanned corrective maintenance, where repairs are per-
formed after a failure occurs. Preventive maintenance is not included in this study because
it assumes constant failure rates, rendering preventive measures unnecessary.
Additionally,
preventive maintenance is typically performed during periods of low wind speed and high ac-
cessibility to minimize its impact on availability and energy production. The model identifies
three types of failures: minor, major, and replacement. Each type of failure is assigned a spe-
cific vessel based on the maintenance strategy employed. The tool can implements two main
strategies:
3.4.1
In-Situ Strategy
In this strategy, all maintenance activities, including those requiring the replacement of large
turbine components such as blades, are conducted offshore at the wind farm site. A Crew
Transfer Vessel (CTV) is used for transporting the crew in the case of minor or major failures,
while a Heavy Lift Vessel (HLV) is used for replacement failures. It is important to note that
there are currently no HLVs on the market capable of lifting the large components of 15MW
wind turbines [53]. Additionally, there is a need for floating HLVs, as existing HLVs operate
in water depths of up to 60 meters only [1]. For the purposes of this study, it is assumed that
HLVs can withstand the heavy weight of large components and are suitable for use with floating
wind turbines.
28
==PAGE:36==
3.4.2
Ex-Situ Strategy
In this strategy, the maintenance activities for replacement failures are carried out at the port,
with the floating wind turbine towed to port for repairs. In this study, the towing process is
performed using an Anchor Handling Tug Supply Vessel (AHTSV) and tugboats, while minor
and major failures are still repaired at the turbine site using a CTV. This strategy, also known
as ”Tow-to-Port,” requires consideration of various factors, including the type of floater. For
instance, if the turbine has a spar floater, the port must be a deep-water port [11]. To simplify
the analysis, it is assumed that the port is suitable for all types of floaters.
3.5
Cost of direct maintenance
This module aims to calculate maintenance costs based on different maintenance strategies,
emphasizing how cost considerations influence strategy selection. The study focuses on direct
maintenance costs, encompassing materials, technicians, and transport. Operational costs such
as rental, insurance, and transmission are excluded due to variability across countries.
A
deterministic approach is employed, using parameters from available data to compute costs
per failure and then aggregating these to determine overall costs based on failure rates. This
method is eﬀicient and straightforward for cost calculation, and it is applied to assess the total
costs of materials and vessels.
For material cost calculations, the study uses data provided by Carroll and modified by industry

4.3
Limitations
This study acknowledges several assumptions and limitations, some of which are highlighted
throughout the text. Here, some of the key limitation of the model will be discussed.
One of the main limitations is the model’s ability to handle uncertainty. Due to its determin-
istic nature, the model cannot account for variability and uncertainty, which are inherent in
operations and maintenance processes. For instance, the model can not handle the uncertainty
in failure rates, weather conditions and vessel costs.
Another limitation is the use of failure rates provided by Carroll [7]. These failure rates were
gathered from turbines with capacities ranging from 2-4 MW, which may not be directly ap-
plicable to 15 MW wind turbines.
Another important limitation concerns weather modeling. The deterministic approach used in
this model relies on historical weather data, utilizing statistical methods to identify and analyze
trends and patterns. However, this approach cannot capture future weather trends, limiting
the model’s predictive capability regarding future weather conditions.
Furthermore, the model’s accuracy is heavily dependent on the quality of its inputs, making it
sensitive to input data variations.
Power module was not considered, yet it is an important module to understand the revenue
losses due to downtime. This module would enhance the process of selection a proper mainte-
nance strategy.
Lastly, more strategies such as tow-to-port should be evaluated by the model to understand
how flexible is the model to different maintenance strategies.

4.5
Practical Implication
The results of this study highlight how certain inputs have a greater impact on availability and
cost than others. This information can help planners prioritize and allocate resources in the
early stages to optimize the availability of the wind farm once it is operational. Based on these
results, an action plan can be implemented focusing on the following:
• Maintenance Strategy: The study shows that changing the strategy from servicing
one turbine per intervention to three turbines per intervention significantly impacts both
37
==PAGE:45==
availability and costs. Therefore, it is crucial to allocate resources during early planning
to evaluate and select strategies that optimize availability and reduce costs.
• Mobilization Time and Weather: The impact of mobilization time and weather on
availability is notable. Planners should work on vessel chartering strategies to ensure the
best vessel availability and shorter mobilization times. While weather cannot be con-
trolled once the wind farm is operational, one can mitigate the impact of weather delays
by using an accurate weather forecasting during maintenance planning. This underscores
the importance of weather modeling for accurate forecasts.
• Spare Parts Lead Time: The lead time for spare parts is the third most influential
factor on availability, following mobilization time and weather delays. This highlights the
importance of effective stock management. Maintaining suﬀicient stock levels will result
in shorter downtimes, thereby enhancing wind farm availability.
• Distance from Shore: The distance from shore directly affects travel time and accessi-
bility, as weather conditions tend to be more severe farther from shore. The distance also
influences maintenance strategy selection. For instance, using a floating accommodation
base close to the farm might be more suitable for farms located far from shore that using
a CTV for normal maintenance activities.
• Failure rates: It was found the decreasing the failure rates increases the availability
and reduces the cost. This highlights the importance of turbine reliability in reducing
the failure rates. Turbine reliability can be increased by implementing a comprehensive
maintenance plans that includes preventive, proactive and opportunistic maintenance
strategies.
By considering these factors and allocating resources appropriately, with a focus on maintenance
strategy and vessel chartering strategy, planners can optimize wind farm availability.

5
Conclusion
To conclude, this thesis presents an O&M model developed through the implementation of
various methodologies to evaluate the availability and maintenance costs of floating wind farms.
The model consists of four main modules: weather, failure, maintenance, and cost, all of which
interact with each other.
A case study was conducted using the model, and the results were compared with similar
models for validation. The case study demonstrated the impact of different factors such as
maintenance strategy, vessel mobilization time, and spare parts availability on both availability
and cost. The findings provide insights into optimizing wind farm availability during the early
planning stages by implementing an action plan focused on resource allocation, which helps
outline a future maintenance plan to optimize availability.
The model has some limitations that can be addressed in future work to improve the accuracy
of certain modules, such as the weather module.
