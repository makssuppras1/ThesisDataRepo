Abstract
In the civil engineering world, it is essential to understand the effect that service loads
and extreme events impose on a load-bearing structure. Today concrete is the most used
construction material worldwide, and specially reinforced concrete is commonly used
as a structural material in the main load-carrying structural elements. The combination
of the concretes load-carrying capacity in compression and the reinforcements superior
properties in tension utilizes the material properties in an optimized way to achieve a
strong and durable material. To predict a structure’s behavior, it is often helpful to under-
stand a smaller element and its behavior. This can be achieved experimentally by setting
proper boundary conditions. If proper boundary conditions are set, any combination of
stresses can be investigated, and phenomenons like cracks and crushing of the concrete
can be observed. This study concerns reinforced concrete disk elements exposed to vari-
ous combinations of in-plane stresses.
Numerous research has been conducted trying to predict the load-deformation response
of elements subjected to in-plane stresses in different combinations. However, only a few
experimental investigations have been conducted in this ﬁeld due to its costliness and the
high complexity of designing a set-up with the proper boundary conditions. In this master
project, the aim is to investigate and predict the response of RC disk elements of a limited
size subjected to different combinations of in-plane stresses while utilizing different ex-
perimental tools to obtain data and analyze the test specimen. The application of realistic
boundary conditions on a limited geometry is achieved by designing an interface to a con-
trolled transfer of forces to the disk element of interest and generating the desired stress
state by combinations of shear and normal stresses transferred on the boundary of the disk
element’s desired stress state. The interface is designed with an indented ﬁber-reinforced
mortar interface surrounded by cast concrete blocks. The intention is to introduce both
normal and shear stresses to the core of the specimens. A total of 11 test elements were
cast with orthogonal reinforcement of Y8 and Y6 rebars, and the elements were subjected
to in-plane biaxial loading.
The unique interface transfers the stresses in the element, and analyzing the displacement
across the interface, an expansion is observed due to elongations of the reinforcement

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
For decades, concrete has been the most used construction material in the world. It can
be dated back to the Ancient Egyptians, and the idea of constructing structures with iron-
reinforced concrete was conceived in 1850s France (Moussard et al., 2017). Besides
being economical and durable compared to other construction materials, different rea-
sons exist as to why concrete is the most popular construction material. First, the mix
has high workability, and with the right mold, nearly any design can be formed. Second,
it is strong in compression, and when reinforced, the tensile strength of steel is acquired
and overcomes the inherent lack of strength in tension. When reinforced concrete (RC)
structures are subjected to tension, cracks may occur in the concrete, exposing the rein-
forcement. Thus it is essential to predict the crack widths at the serviceability limit state
(SLS). If these exceed the requirements, it can reduce the service life of the structure.
Today, when engineers design RC structures, the whole structure can often be divided
into smaller and simpler elements. Understanding one small element and its behavior can
help understand the structure as a whole and predict the structure’s behavior. Different
structures can be subjected to different stresses, as they carry the load through various
actions, e.g., a shear wall or a box girder of a bridge. When looking at a small rectangular
element of these structures, the element will primarily be subjected to in-plane stresses.
Tensile stresses are carried by the reinforcement, while the concrete carries compressive
stresses. Shear stresses in RC are often modeled as being resisted only by the concrete
since dowel actions in the reinforcement are neglected. This demonstrates the importance
of understanding and analyzing the element, especially in certain stress scenarios such as
pure shear and shear with normal stresses.
Numerous research has been conducted trying to predict the load-deformation response
of elements subjected to in-plane stresses in different combinations. However, only a few
experimental investigations have been conducted in this ﬁeld due to its costliness and the
high complexity of designing a setup with the proper boundary conditions. One such
research project was undertaken by Frank Vecchio and Michael P. Collins at the Univer-
sity of Toronto (Vecchio and Collins, 1982) where several orthogonal RC elements were

RC subjected to in-plane shear and normal stresses. Another research project from The

The test setup in the project is less resource-intensive by trying to investigate the stress

The test specimens have been designed with a unique indented ﬁber-reinforced mortar
interface surrounded by cast concrete blocks. The intention of this was to introduce both
normal and shear stresses to the core of the specimens.
This master thesis will be analyzing 11 orthogonal reinforced elements with different re-
inforcement conﬁgurations of either Y8 or Y6 rebars. The elements were loaded in-plane
to achieve pure shear stresses and combined shear and normal stresses. All the elements
were cast by the authors in the concrete laboratory at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU). The test setup subjected the test elements to biaxial monotonic loading while col-
lecting displacement measurements over the interface with a device called Linear Variable
Differential Transformer (LVDT). To analyze the crack behavior in the concrete, the 2D
digital image correlation (DIC) technique with the software GOM Correlate 2020 will be
adopted.
The maximum stresses at failure will be compared to the expected concrete strength at
the ultimate limit state (ULS) utilizing the theory of plasticity. The crack widths will be
estimated by a revised version of Eurocode 2 (R-EC2) (CEN, prEN-1992-1-1, 2020).

CHAPTER 2
Theory
This chapter will describe the theory and models utilized within this project. The theory of
plasticity will be described concurrently with the deﬁnition of the strengths and the yield
conditions (YC). A description of stress-strain behavior for the RC disk element subjected
to in-plane stresses will be given. The response and behavior of cracked concrete will be
described to understand the calculations of the crack widths further and crack distances.
2.1
The Theory of Plasticity and Limit Analysis
The theory of plasticity fundamentally describes a rigid-plastic material deﬁned as a ma-
terial that deforms plastically subjected to constant loading when the load reaches the
yielding limit. The limit analysis is undertaken using this theory, and with the yield con-
ditions and the normality conditions the load-carrying capacity can be determined. One
of the ﬁrst to formulate this theory generally was Gvozdev in 1936 (Gvozdev, 1960). A
Western analog to Gvozdev’s theory was developed in the 1950s, but traces of the plastic-
ity theory can already be traced back to the 1908 Danish code for RC beams (Nielsen and
Hoang, 2011). Almost coincidentally, the Prager school at Brown University developed a
similar theory, (Drucker et al., 1952).
The yield condition states an envelope that includes all the combinations of stresses the
member can carry. As stated in Nielsen and Hoang, 2011 if the yield condition is formu-
lated as
f(Q1, Q2, ..., Qn) = 0
(2.1)
if Q is a set of stresses, then yielding will occur when the Eq. (2.1) is fulﬁlled. Stresses
giving f > 0 are not possible to occur since it is beyond the yield condition, and stresses
giving f < 0 are carried by the material. The relation between the stress ﬁeld and the
strain-ﬁeld is found by applying the normality condition, which states that the strains
found are outward-directed strains normal to the surface. If the yield surface has an apex
or an edge, the strains occur at an arbitrary angle on the apex. These conditions are es-
sential when the load-carrying capacity is to be determined with estimations of material
behavior.

2.4
Cracks
When RC structures are subjected to tension, cracks may occur in the concrete, exposing
the reinforcement. Wide crack widths can reduce the service life of the structure. Thus it
is essential to predict the crack widths at SLS. The stress-strain relation in a cracked mem-
brane is complex, and not many models yield information on crack widths and distances.
The basic concepts of cracks will be described in this section.
2.4.1
Cracking Stages
Cracks appear when the tensile stress perpendicular to the concrete compression ﬁeld ex-
ceeds the tensile strength. After the ﬁrst crack is introduced in the disk element, the stress
distribution becomes more complex. When the ﬁrst crack appears, a crack formation
stage is introduced in the element, after which a crack stabilizing stage takes over until
the reinforcement reaches the yielding limit, as illustrated in Figure 2.7.
Figure 2.7: Simpliﬁed load-strain relation (ﬁb, 2013).

across the element carried as tensile stresses in the reinforcement and as compression in
the concrete’s diagonal compression ﬁeld, as seen in Figure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: Compressive stress-ﬁeld in membrane model (CEN, prEN-1992-1-1, 2020)
The ﬁrst crack appears when the principal tensile stress reaches the tensile strength of the
concrete, ft. The concrete has no tensile strength at the crack, and the reinforcement has
to carry the force. With a distance on each side of the crack, some of the tensile forces will
be transferred to the concrete, as the bond reduces the stresses in the reinforcement. This
phenomenon is called tension stiffening (Pimentel et al., 2010). The determination of the
local stresses and strains across the element becomes complex, as the stress-distribution

The test elements were subjected to in-plane stresses to obtain predetermined stress states

Pure shear was

programmed to pull with the same force. Shear with normal tensile stresses was obtained

compressive stresses, actuator 2 exerted double the forces compared to actuator 1.

CHAPTER 5
Digital Image Correlation Analysis
This project has utilized the DIC technology to determine the strains in the element. This
technology was originally explained by Sutton et al., 1983 and described the basics of the
technology. In basic terms, the technology measures deformations by collecting different
levels of gray, reﬂected by the surface of the test specimen. The surface of the specimen
was covered with a random black and white speckle pattern to obtain different reﬂection
intensities across the surface. The quality of the pattern is crucial to take useful pictures
for the DIC-software, GOM Correlate 2020. To capture high-quality photos, the right
light setting and camera shutter was needed. Further reading of this technology can be
found in Schreier et al., 2009.
This chapter will undergo the different 2D DIC analyses undertaken in the software GOM
Correlate 2020. A Nikon D800 60 mm lens was used during the DIC analysis on a
specimen subjected to in-plane biaxial loading. The speckle pattern was generated by
creating a ﬁne speckle pattern of white and black dots. The facet points used during DIC
analysis were of size 19 and standard computation. The color scale was set to cover 0-5%
in all the projects created in GOM. The following will describe how the different strain
and displacement analysis of the test elements was conducted with GOM Correlate 2020.

6.8
Sources of Error
This section aims to discuss the sources of error observed during the data processing and
the possible effects and implications on the data.
During the trial of the test setup with T0, two load cells were placed on the opposite
side of actuator 2. A difference was observed in the applied load by the actuator and the
recorded loads by the load cells. Additionally, a difference between the two load cells was
observed. The difference of measured forces amongst the two load cells, which should
measure more or less the same, could be due to several things: The sides of the blocks
were not even, the whole element was rotating, and thereby one side of the element ex-
perienced more load than the other, or the anchor bolts used to impose the test element to
tensile stresses, were not pulling equally, leading to a rotation and thus more stresses on
one side of the blocks. This was further avoided by changing the load cells to a bearing to
achieve a more distributed loading. Furthermore, due to uneven sides of the blocks, loads
could be concentrated more on one side, which could further lead to rotation in the plane.
By having roller supports on the passive sides of the element, rotation was not restrained.
When the rotation occurs, the loads will not be distributed evenly. E.g., one anchor bolt
at actuator 1 might pull more than the other and thereby result in unexpected stress states.
By only having one active actuator for each direction, the stresses might not be distributed
==PAGE:83==
6.8. SOURCES OF ERROR
65
evenly, and one part could be subjected to more tension or compression. This could be
avoided by implementing 2 additional actuators on each side. Another obstacle was dis-
covered later in the process. The forces applied to the core were, in fact, not centered on
the interface. This could lead to a deviation of the stress states assumed.
It was important to be attentive during the installation of the test elements. If the top
and bottom steel plates were not placed centered, as shown in Figure 4.5, the test element
could be placed skewed in the setup. If the element was placed skewed, the loading would
be eccentric, leading to a greater pressure at one side, causing crushing of the blocks as
seen in Figure 6.33. Furthermore, the bearing seen in Figure 6.33 had a smaller diameter
compared to actuator 2.
Figure 6.33: A picture of the blocks failing due to compressive stresses in T3.
Even if the test element was placed in alignment with the test setup, the out-of-plane rota-
tion could occur later on during the testing if the forces acting upon the test element were
eccentric. This problem was also found in the literature where it is stated that out-of-plane
movements are identiﬁed as the most signiﬁcant source of error leading to pseudo strain
when measured by DIC (Hoult et al., 2013), (Halding et al., 2019). Different suggestions
are proposed by Hoult et al., 2013 to correct the error in the strains when out-of-plane
movements are observed. However, the suggestions were not applicable for this project
as it would make the testing more time-consuming and expensive by using expensive
lenses or multiple cameras.
The sources of errors stated in this section were most likely occurring for all elements, but
with different intensities which can either counteract each other or the opposite making it
highly complex.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
The project aimed to design and construct a representative volume disk element and an-
alyze its behavior when subjected to in-plane stresses in terms of pure shear, combined
shear, and tension or compression. This setup was intended to be less resource-intensive
and more economical and thereby achieving signiﬁcant results without the necessity of
conducting a full-scale test.
Firstly, the response of the elements during testing was analyzed both for shear stress-
strain behaviors and for shear stress-shear strain behaviors. This was achieved by utiliz-
ing digital image correlation (DIC) technology. The behaviors of the test elements did
show an expected trend. The elements with 8x8 reinforcement in both directions were
acting stiffer than the elements reinforced with 8x6 rebars. Moreover, the elements sub-
jected to shear stresses and normal compressive stresses were showing showed a higher
capacity. Elements subjected to shear and normal tensile stresses showed a more fragile
behavior. However, these trends are based on the fact that only one type of each test was
conducted, and some deviations were observed. Furthermore, the DIC analysis might
generate pseudo-strains when out-of-plane movements are occurring.
Upon monotonic loading of the test elements, the crack widths were affected by the load
combinations, and the distances increased at lower reinforcement diameters. The crack
parameters were compared to theoretical estimates determined by the recommendations
from the revised version of EC2. In general, it was established that the EC2 overestimates
both the crack widths and distances. In real life, structures are subjected to loading and
unloading; thus, an interest in crack width behavior during cyclic loading was sparked.
Test element T6 was subjected to cyclic loading, which increased the crack widths at the
cyclic levels.
Another measurement device utilized was the ﬁber optic sensors applied to the reinforce-
ment bars. These measurements by FOS did show a local increase in deformation in the
rebars around the same place as cracks in the concrete were appearing. Hence, this de-
67
==PAGE:86==
68
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSIONS
vice is a tool to measure the behavior inside the element. However, the sensors were very
fragile. Therefore one should be attentive during installment.
Both LVDT’s and DIC were used and compared to investigate whether the interface
worked as intended. It was found that the LVDT’s were highly affected by the cracks
appearing in the blocks at high load steps. Thus before cracking, both measuring devices
can be used to determine the deformation of the interface, but when cracks appear in the
blocks, the DIC is preferred to measure the deformations in the interface.
The purpose of the design of the test elements was to create a stiff surrounded concrete
block that would transfer the load from the actuators to the core without any deforma-
tion. The interface is intended to create the proper boundary conditions enabling the core
to crack. This was possible due to the interface being independent and unrestrained at
its ends. The interface seemed to function as intended since neither crushing nor fail-
ure during loading was observed. Furthermore, a resembling crack pattern was seen in
the elements with a distributed crack pattern in the interface, implying that the interface
worked. However, cracks appeared in the blocks during loading affecting the crack pat-
tern. Furthermore, due to the cracks in the blocks and limitations of the displacement of
the actuator in tension, ULS was not reached.
Generally, observing the behavior of the test elements, some tendencies were observed.
The uniquely designed interface does transfer stresses to the elements. This can be seen
when observing the stress-strain relations and the crack patterns and widths. It should,
however, be noted that signiﬁcant sources of error were detected during testing, which is
mainly related to the test setup.
7.1
Further work
The further work lies within reducing the sources of errors and uncertainties around the
experimental set-up. This could be done using two cameras for the DIC measurements,
enabling correction of out-of-plane movements. Furthermore, if more resources are avail-
able, it would be beneﬁcial to use four actuators instead of two to prevent the rotation of
the element and secure the right loading conditions of the element. Lastly, it is crucial to
stiffen the blocks to prevent the occurrence of cracks in the blocks, as these cracks might
affect the crack pattern on the core.

APPENDIX B
Materials
The materials used for casting of the elements are described in this section.
B.1
Cement and Aggregates
The cement used is Aalborg Portland rapid cement marked CEM | 52.2 N (LA). Rapid
cement is a Portland cement with a two-day strength of approx. 34 MPa and a seven-day
strength of approx. 53 MPa (Rapid Aalborg Cement 2021).
It can be used for all purposes and in all environmental classes (Portland, 2014).
Aggregates and sand from the sea are used in the concrete mixture for the core and blocks.
The sand is a 0-4mm fraction in class E with an absorbency of 0.2 %.
The aggregate is a 4-8mm fraction in class M with an absorbency of 0.8 %.
B.2
Mortar
A mortar mixture was used to cast the interface. The mortar mixture used is called Nonset
50 and is based on Portland cement, with plasticizing and stabilizing additives and Dmax
0.2 mm expanding. The mortar mixture is from the manufacturer Mapei, and the data-
sheet is found at (NONSET 50 2021).
B.3
Superplasticizer
The superplasticizer admixture used in the casting of the blocks of the elements is Dy-
namon XTend 200 from the manufacturer Mapei. It is developed especially for Aalborg
Portland cement. For declarations or other data, go to the website of the manufacturer,
(Dynamon Xtend 200 2021).
