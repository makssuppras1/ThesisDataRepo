Preface
This thesis, Raman Analysis of Cosmeceutical Components Through the Epidermis, was
carried out from August to December 2025 at the Department of Chemical and Biochem­
ical Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), in collaboration with Gly­
sious. The thesis was conducted as part of the fulfilment of the requirements for the
Master of Science in Engineering degree.
The project was conducted due to the growing need for reliable, non­invasive analytical
techniques that are able to monitor the penetration of unstable cosmetic actives into hu­
man skin, such as L­Ascorbic Acid. Mini Raman spectroscopy can be considered as a
promising approach for non­invasive, real­time analysis.
This thesis explores its potential in detecting Vitamin C penetration in the skin after appli­
cation of glycerol–silicone formulations.
The project consisted in several experimental activities starting from identification of refer­
ence and pure compound peaks to in vivo Raman measurements and biophysical analy­
sis. Completing this work has been an extremely valuble learning experience, combining
formulation science, skincare technology and analytical chemistry all together.
I hope that the obtained results of this thesis can contribute to the development of im­
proved analytical methods and more effective cosmetic delivery systems.

Abstract
Vitamin C (L­ascorbic acid) is a very known and widely used cosmetic active ingredient
known for its antioxidant, anti­pigmentation, and collagen­stimulating properties. How­
ever, limitations for its use are the high instability and low skin permeability that make its
topical delivery and analytical detection particularly challenging. The aim of this thesis
is to evaluate whether the mini Raman technology can serve as a reliable, non­invasive
technique for detecting intact L­AA penetration through the epidermis in vivo.
To fulfill the project objective a stepwise experimental strategy was implemented. The first
step was to gather Raman spectra of pure L­AA, glycerol, and silicone matrices in order
to identify characteristic vibrational markers in the 1600–1750 cm−1 region. Glycerol–
Vitamin C mixtures were then analyzed both in liquid form and on the skin to validate
peak detectability in more simple systems without the presence of silicones. Further tests
focused on two glycerol–silicone delivery systems developed by Glysious, a cream and
patch formulation. The products were tested on the skin to see their ability to deliver
Vitamin C into the epidermis. In vivo measurements on the volar forearm, performed
using an 80–120 µm probe depth targeting the viable epidermis, confirmed the detection
of intact L­AA for all formulations. The 15% cream showed a rapid penetration peak at
approximately 2–3 h, while the patch showed a slower, sustained release over ~4 h.
Inter­subject tests of eight volunteers demonstrated that MiniRaman can consistently de­
tect Vitamin C penetration despite differences in sex and age. Complementary biophys­
ical assessments using the Corneometer and DermaLab ultrasound were taken. These
tests showed that the glycerol–silicone cream provided more durable hydration than a
commercial Vitamin C cream, while the patch produced a milder but controlled hydration
effect.
Overall, the findings demonstrate that mini Raman spectroscopy is a sensitive and practi­
cal analytical tool for non­invasive, in vivo monitoring of Vitamin C. Together with biophysi­
cal measurements, it provides a robust framework for evaluating formulation performance
and guiding the development of enhanced cosmeceutical delivery systems.

1.1
Vitamin C in Topical Applications
1.1.1
Introduction to L­Ascorbic Acid
Vitamin C (VC) is the most abundant non­enzymatic antioxidant present in human skin,
it is a component of the endogenous defense system against oxidative stress [1]. Unlike
plants and some animals, humans are not able to synthesize Vitamin C due to the absence
of L­gulonolactone oxidase, the final enzyme in the biosynthetic pathway [2]. In fact, even
if high doses of Vitamin C are ingested orally, only a small fraction becomes bioavailable in
the skin because intestinal uptake is limited by a saturable transport mechanism. Because
of this, VC must be obtained from external sources and , in the context of cosmetology
and dermatology, topical application represents the most effective strategy to increase its
concentration in the epidermis [1].
Figure 1.1: Molecular structure of L­ascorbic acid (VC), showing the lactone ring, the
enediol system and the carbonyl group associated with the Raman peak in the 1600–
1750 cm−1 region. Image adapted from Wikipedia [3].
The chemically active form of Vitamin C is L­ascorbic acid (L­AA), which is a small, hy­
drophilic, weak organic acid. Thanks to its molecular structure it has exceptional antioxi­
dant properties but it also has several formulation limitations.
In fact, L­AA is highly unstable and undergoes rapid oxidative degradation when exposed
to light, oxygen, elevated temperature, alkaline pH, or traces of metal ions [4]. These
degradation processes can lead to loss of biological activity and yellowing of formulations,
making stability one of the major limitations in product development.
The absorption of L­AA in the intestine is limited by a saturable transport mechanism,
meaning that even high oral dosages of VC bring to only modest increases in circulat­
ing and dermal levels [4]. Furthermore, oral intake does not efficiently increase levels of
the active compound in the skin, since the epidermis is one of the last tissues to receive
systemic Vitamin C. For these reasons, topical administration of L­AA is very important.
When applied topically, ascorbic acid can exert localized antioxidant activity, stimulate col­
lagen synthesis, and contribute to tissue repair and regeneration, effects that are difficult
to achieve through only systemic supplementation [4].
The importance of VC for the body and the skin, combined with the limitations of oral
delivery and the instability of L­AA highlight the need for optimized topical formulations
that are able to stabilize the molecule and ensure its penetration into the viable layers of
the skin.

1.1.2
VC benefits for the skin
The skin is a complex organ and its main function is to act as a barrier against external
agents[4]. It consists of two main layers: the epidermis, which provides the primary barrier
against environmental insults, and the dermis, which confers mechanical strength and
elasticity. Vitamin C concentrations are naturally higher in the epidermis (6–64 mg/100 g
tissue) than in the dermis (3–13 mg/100 g tissue), reflecting its central role in counteracting
the oxidative stress generated by UV radiation and pollution [4].
The fact that high levels of concentration of VC are present in the skin means that it has
several important biological functions for overall skin health.
One of its most important functions is its involvement in collagen biosynthesis. In fact,
Vitamin C acts as a co­factor for the proline and lysine hydroxylases that stabilise the
collagen molecule tertiary structure, and it also promotes collagen gene expression[5].
This makes Vitamin C crucial for maintaining dermal structure, elasticity, and overall skin
integrity.
Several studies also show how ascorbic acid is a stabilizing collagen and is fundamental
in wound healing [4], eventhough its assumption for wound healing remains controversial.
In addition, Vitamin C is a powerful antioxidant that can neutralise and remove oxidants,
such as those found in environmental pollutants and after exposure to ultraviolet radiation
[5]. However, Vitamin C acts as an antioxidant in the body when used together with
Vitamin E, a lipid­soluble non­enzimatic antioxadant that protects cell membranes from
oxidative damage [5]. When Vitamin E neutralises a free radical, it becomes oxidised
and loses its protective ability. Vitamin C can regenerate Vitamin E, restoring it to its
active, antioxidant form. This recycling mechanism makes the combination of Vitamin
C and Vitamin E significantly more effective at preventing oxidative damage than either
antioxidant alone.

Ascorbic Acid is also an anti­pigmentary agent, in fact a clinical study investigating the
effect of a topical formulation containing 25% ascorbic acid and a chemical penetration
enhancer reported a significant decrease in pigmentation caused by melasma after 16
weeks [4][6].
Altogether, these functions that VC plays in the skin such as promotion of collagen for­
mation, neutralization and removal of oxidants and pigmentation control explain why the

1.1.3
Topical formulations of L­Ascorbic Acid
The cosmetic market offers numerous VC formulations, including serums, creams, emul­
sions, and transdermal patches. For biological significance, topical products generally
require L­AA concentrations above 8%, while concentrations exceeding 20% offer no ad­
ditional benefit and may increase irritation [1]. The formulation must overcome two main
challenges: the extreme instability of L­AA and its poor permeability through the lipid­rich
stratum corneum.
Oxidation represents the first obstacle to delevoping an effective formulation. When ex­
posed to light or air, L­AA rapidly converts to DHAA, an oxidant byproduct of ascorbic
acid [4], leading to loss of efficacy and yellowing of the formulation. Maintaining a pH
below 3.5 significantly increases stability by reducing ionization, thus improving skin per­
meability[1]. Several commercial products solve instability issues by combining L­AA with
other antioxidants such as ferulic acid, which doubles photoprotection and stabilizes the
molecule [1].
The second issue is that L­AA is a hydrophilic, unstable, charged molecule which can so
struggle to penetrate through the layers of the skin (lipophilic). Strategies to solve these
problems can be combining the use of L­AA with other active ingredients, as well as its
encapsulation for optimal delivery.
Formulation strategies to improve L­AA delivery have also been introduced; in fact, many
studies have shown that Vit C has been incorporated successfully into different delivery
systems, in order to improve the treatment outcomes, targeting or protecting the molecule
from chemical degradation[7]. These systems include: nanoparticles (which have low
toxicity, are biodegradable and biocompatible), liposomes (interesting because they can
carry both hydrophilic and hydrophobic molecules) as well as microemulsions and mi­
celles [7].
These technologies favour permeation but at the same time they add complexity and cost
to products, highlighting the need for simpler, more stable systems capable of protecting
L­AA until it reaches its target site.
1.1.4
Rationale for studying penetration of intact L­Ascorbic Acid
As previously stated, most topical formulations fail to deliver intact L­ascorbic acid into
the viable epidermis due to its instability. L­Ascorbic Acid is rapidly oxidized on the skin
surface when exposed to air, light, or physiological pH, often converting to dehydroascor­
bic acid before meaningful penetration can occur. This is why the majority of commer­
cial products use more stable derivatives (such as ascorbyl tetraisopalmitate, magnesium
ascorbyl phosphate, or 3­O­ethyl ascorbic acid) rather than free L­AA [4].
These derivatives are more easy to formulate and more stable but their conversion back
into biologically active L­AA inside the skin is variable and sometimes inefficient [8]. For
these reasons, the real biological efficacy of many products that contain VC depends on
whether L­AA can actually penetrate through the skin and reach the active sites of the
epidermis and dermis.
Studies have tried to quantify VC penetration in the skin, for example a study based on
percutation absorption studies with a more traditional method (HPLC) is more labour­
intensive and fail to distinguish between intact and oxidized forms of the active principle
[9].
For this reason, a method that allows real­time, non­invasive, in­vivo detection of intact L­

in vivo penetration of L­AA from glycerol–silicone formulations, presenting so a new ap­
proach for studying real­time Vitamin C delivery in human skin.
1.2
Glycerol–Silicone Technology
The technology considered in this work is based on the two­phase glycerol–silicone sys­
tems developed by Glysious, which are delivery systems specifically engineered to sta­
bilise hydrophilic active ingredients while enabling their controlled release into the skin.
The technology consists in the dispersion of discrete glycerol droplets within a cross­linked
silicone network. These materials are produced by mixing pharmaceutical­grade glycerol
with a two­part silicone elastomer under high shear forces, followed by curing of the sili­
cone phase. The result is a a stable adhesive or cream matrix containing micron­sized,
immiscible glycerol domains [10].
The droplets typically fall within the 2–10 µm range.
This two­phase system creates a unique delivery platform with several advantages when
compared to water­silicone systems. First, the hydrophilic glycerol droplets act like reser­
voirs for water­soluble actives like VC, stabilizing them at the same time by reducing the
presence of free water. Second, the silicone matrix forms a film that increases skin hy­
dration, improves penetration of the active ingredient, and protects the active compound
from environmental degradation.
Overall, the glycerol–silicone technology represents the best delivery system for the de­
livery of unstable hydrophilic active ingredients, such as VC.
1.2.1
Mode of Action
Glycerol is a humectant, meaning it attracts water form the surrounding environment
through absorption and adsorption mechanisms at defined conditions [11]. When incorpo­
rated into a two­phase glycerol–silicone system, glycerol exists as micron­sized droplets
that are dispersed in a silicone elastomer matrix.
Figure 1.3 shows a schematic representation of the droplet­based mode of action of the
glycerol­silicone patches during skin contact: the stratum corneum (SC) contains intrinsic
moisture in the form of tightly bound water droplets. When the glycerol–silicone patch is
placed on the skin, due to its high affinity for water, glycerol begins to absorb moisture
from the superficial layers of the SC, leading to progressive swelling and coalescence of
the droplets[11]. The droplets start to grow progressively and the may come into closer
proximity and undergo partial coalescence, forming larger hydrophilic reservoirs. These
bigger droplets contain the VC dissolved particles in a stable environment that minimizes
the presence of free water. The increase in internal water content allows the movement
of VC within the droplet phase and so for it to travel towards the surface of the skin.
Advantages in using the glycerol–silicone technology are firstly that the silicone phase
controls the rate at which the active compound is released.

In fact, GSEs have recently been reported to allow the controlled delivery of active sub­
stances dissolved in glycerol droplets, which are released upon contact with an aqueous
environment [12]. This process ensures that the active ingredient reaches the viable epi­
dermis in a controlled and sustained way.
Another very important aspect of the technology is that the silicone matrix forms an adhe­
sive film that remains strictly attached to the surface of the skin. This not only improves the
transfer of the active compound but also minimizes evaporation of water droplets present
in the skin, reducing so the transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and increasing hydration
of the skin.
Lastly, the resistance and integrity of the cured silicone network prevents premature rup­

application.
Overall, the combined advantages that are guaranteed by the technology create a delivery
system capable of stabilizing VC and releasing the compound gradually into the skin.

1.2.2
Comparison Between Glycerol–Silicone and Water–Silicone
Systems for Vitamin C Delivery
Selecting an appropriate hydrophilic phase is crucial when it comes to designing silicone­
based delivery systems for such an unstable molecule like Vitamin C. Many cosmetic
formulations are based on water–silicone emulsions where the water droplets are dis­
persed in a hydrophobic polymer matrix (silicone).
While these systems are widely used in cosmetics, they are fundamentally incompati­
ble with L­ascorbic acid due to the molecule’s extreme sensitivity to free water. In an
acqueous environment, VC undergoes rapid oxidation to dehydroascorbic acid (DHAA),
followed by hydrolytic ring­opening to 2,3­diketogulonic acid, leading to complete loss of
biological activity and visible yellowing of formulations [7]. These degradation pathways
are strongly accelerated by free water, high pH, oxygen, heat, and metal ions [13].
Consequently, conventional water–silicone emulsions are unsuitable for stabilizing Vita­
min C due to the continuous presence of an aqueous phase that promotes oxidative and
hydrolytic breakdown.
In contrast, glycerol–silicone systems provide a very different microenvironment.
Glycerol is a humectant capable of absorbing and retaining water through hydrogen bond­
ing, reducing the amount of free water which can initiate Vitamin C degradation. This
water­binding capacity has been shown to enhance the shelf life of water­sensitive actives
by lowering their exposure to hydrolysis and oxidation [14]. In fact, the silicone network
protects the glycerol droplets from oxygen and environmental triggers and the absence
of free water significantly slows the degradation of the active compound. As a result, VC

1.3.1
Fundamentals of Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy, a powerful analytical technique, has found extensive application in
cosmetics and dermatology due to its non­invasive, molecular­focused, and high­resolution
capabilities [15]. It analyzes how the molecules vibrate when illuminated by a laser. The
laser hits the sample and the light interacts with the molecules inside of it; the majority of
the photons bounce off without losing energy (Raileigh Scattering), a very small fraction
gain or lose energy due to the interactions with the vibrations of the molecules. This en­
ergy shift is called Raman Scattering. The shift provides unique fingerprints owing to the
vibrational signature of the respective molecules [15].
In the obtained spectrum (Figure 1.5), the horizontal axis represents the Raman shift (in
cm−1), while the vertical axis corresponds to the intensity of the scattered light. Different

(a.u) on y­axis [16].
materials have different vibrational modes, and therefore their own characteristic Raman
spectra.
This makes Raman spectroscopy a useful technique for material identification[16].
The technique is non­destructive and requires minimal sample preparation. Because the
Raman signal originates from vibrations of molecules, it can also be applied to the analysis
of complex materials such as polymers.

1.3.2
Raman Spectroscopy for Skin and Cosmetic Applications
Raman Spectroscopy has been used in cosmetic applications, in particular in the study
of facial cream products where the technology is used for the chemometric investigation
of cream samples [15].
A study of the analysis show how different cream samples presented different additive
and contaminant levels despite having uniform chemical compositions because of differ­
ent manufacturing techniques [15].
In their work, the authors further demonstrated several advantages of Raman spectroscopy
for cosmetic analysis. The reproducibility test for 40 different samples showed no major
difference between the creams of the different brands, showing the excellent reproducibil­
ity of Raman Spectroscopy as the peaks overlap exactly [17]. Overall, the cream samples
presented similar characteristic Raman peaks, indicating so that the formulations had the
same chemicals but had different ratios of additives and contaminants due probably to the
different manufacturing processes. These differences between the creams can be seen
by visual inspections of the Raman spectra [17].
Together, these findings highlight how Raman spectroscopy can sensitively capture sub­
tle formulation differences while maintaining high repeatability and reliability, making it a
powerful tool for analyzing cosmetic products and evaluating how their molecular compo­
sition may influence skin performance.
1.3.3
Miniaturized Raman Technology for In Vivo Penetration Studies
Recent advances in technology have transformed the Raman technology from a labo­
ratory analytical tool into a compact, portable and user­friendly technique (mini Raman)
which can be used for in­vivo applications. The mini Raman technology allows to perform
high quality measurements directly on human skin without the need for invasive sampling.
These innovations have been essential especially in the cosmetic and dermatologic field
where a real­time and non­destructive testing of the penetration of active compounds
though the skin is crucial.
The Mini Raman device used in this project is specifically designed for in vivo skin mea­
surements, focusing on the use of the 80–120 µm laser. Reason for this choice is that
this depth corresponds to the viable epidermis which, as previously stated, is the layer
where VC is present in most concentration. Thickness of the epidermis fall well within the
probing capability of the Mini Raman system.
For the volar forearm, which is the anatomical site used for all in vivo measurements in
this thesis, a study reported a value of mean epidermal thickness of 75.5 µm, with lower
and upper limits of 61.7 µm and 89.3 µm, respectively [18].
Targeting this skin depth is also important because VC is known to be unstable on the
surface of the skin, due to it’s susceptibility to light and oxygen. By probing deeper layers
the mini Raman increases the probability of detecting intact and non­degraded VC.
This device allows for the identification of L­ascorbic acid based on its characteristic
peaks, associated with its lactone carbonyl and enediol groups. Because these features
are unique and specific to this molecule, the Mini Raman provides direct chemical con­
firmation of whether the vitamin has successfully penetrated the epidermis. The ability
of the technology to collect the spectra in a non­invasive way, without disturbing the skin
barrier makes it particularly suited for monitoring penetration dynamics over time.

1.4
Project Objective
The main objective of this thesis is to evaluate whether mini Raman spectroscopy repre­
sents a reliable and non­invasive analytical technique to monitor and detect the penetra­
tion of Vitamin C through the epidermis with in vivo testing.
L­ascorbic acid (L­AA) is a chemically unstable and highly reactive molecule, and its
topical delivery remains a major challenge in cosmetic science. Therefore, establishing
whether mini Raman spectroscopy is capable of identifying the characteristic vibrational
features of intact L­AA in the human skin is a very important step for formulation develop­
ment and real­time skin analysis.
To satisfy this goal, the project has been structured around a series of progressive exper­
imental steps that move from testing of pure compounds to increasingly complex fomula­
tions.
The first phase involves measuring the Raman spectra of pure reference compounds,
including powdered Vitamin C and neat glycerol, to identify the characteristic peaks that
will serve as markers during in vivo testing. Determining the characteristic peaks for the
pure compounds is very important as it allows to verify that there aren’t any potential
overlaps with other formulation components, allowing so to monitor the VC peak during
penetration studies.
The second phase focuses on monitoring VC peaks in a simplified binary system that
consists only of glycerol and L­AA. Measurements are performed both in liquid vials and
directly on the skin after topical application of the mixture. This phase is important as
this system doesn’t contain any silicones and so it provides a clearer assessment of the
Raman detectability of L­AA and serves as a reference against which more complex for­
mulations can be compared.
The third and central phase of the project investigates Vitamin C detection present in
two advanced delivery systems developed by Glysious: glycerol–silicone based creams
and glycerol–silicone based patches. These formulations are designed to stabilise L­AA
and enhance its penetration into the epidermis through a controlled release mechanism.
By tracking the characteristic Raman peak of L­AA at a probe depth corresponding to
the viable epidermis (approximately 80–120 µm), the study aims to determine whether
the Mini Raman device can detect intact Vitamin C in the pure formulations and also
following topical application of the products. Complementary analysis is to compare the
performance and penetration profiles of the two delivery systems.
Another objective is to assess factors that influence Raman detection quality. In fact,
Raman spectroscopy can be sensitive to environmental and biological factors such as
hydration state, skin heterogeneity and inter­individual variability. Becsause of this, an
additional component of the project is to investigate how the Mini Raman signal intensities
vary across the different subjects by evaluating the penetration of Vitamin C through patch
application on volunteers of different ages and sexes. These tests aim to see how these
factors can affect signal interpretation, reproducibility, and measurement reliability.
In addition to penetration analysis, the thesis also incorporates secondary objectives re­
lated to the biophysical performance of the formulations. Both the cream and patch formu­
lations are characterized by the presence of glycerol which is a very powerful humectant,
which provides hydration to the skin. Because of this, complementary measurements are
performed using two non­invasive instruments to assess biophysical parameters.
The Courage + Khazaka Corneometer provides quantitative information about surface hy­
dration of the stratum corneum, while the DermaLab SkinLab Single ultrasound probe

measures parameters such as the Lower Echogenic Band (LEB), skin thickness, and
dermal echogenic intensity. These analyses allow comparison of the moisturising effects

landscape.
Overall, the objectives of this thesis extend beyond the validation of the Mini Raman sys­

Methods
2.3.1
Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectra were acquired with the MiniRaman spectrometer controlled by Miraspec
(v. 0.7.2–105). A 785 nm laser was used for all measurements. The set exposure time
was 1000 ms with 10 accumulations for all tests, except for in vivo skin measurements
where it was increased to 4000 ms (10 accumulations).
An 80–120 µm contact probe was used to detect the penetration of VC through the deeper
layers of the epidermis and upper dermis. The choice of using this probe is due to the
instability of VC on the surface of the skin, hence why the investigation was carried out
for deeper layers. For the testing, the probe was gently placed on the volar forearm by
applying light pressure in order to ensure a stable acquisition of the Raman signal.
Sample handling
Pure L­ascorbic acid powder was placed inside the screw holder,
compressed with the Allen key, and mounted in the axial focusing accessory. Glycerol–
vitamin C solutions were measured in glass vials using the same accessory together with
the light­protection cover.
Cream films were peeled from their PET support and transferred onto a Raman­inactive
metal plate to avoid PET contributions. The contact probe was then placed directly on the
film. Silicone patches were similarly removed from the PET substrate and were directly
attached on the probe lenses.
Spectral processing and quantitative analysis
All spectra were processed in Mi­
raspec. A rolling­circle baseline correction (radius = 1000, Y = 0.1) was applied, followed
by Min–Max normalization in order to compare the different measurements. Spectra were
cropped to 400–2000 cm−1.
Vitamin C was quantified by extracting the maximum intensity in the 1600–1750 cm−1
region. This intensity was used to construct:
1. concentration–intensity calibration curves for glycerol–VC solutions, cream films
and patch films, and
2. time–intensity curves describing the evolution of the VC signal in vivo for cream and
patch applications.
For the in vivo experiments, the time­dependent intensity profiles were fitted with a third­
degree polynomial to better describe the behavior.
2.3.2
FT­IR Spectroscopy
FT­IR measurements were performed on the Nicolet iS50 using a diamond ATR crystal.
Spectra of solids, liquids and films were collected in the range 4000–400 cm−1.
For film analysis, silicone cream films and patch matrices were removed from their PET
substrates and placed directly onto the ATR crystal to avoid interference from PET.
The Qufora FT­IR spectrometer was used for additional characterization of the cream
formulation after removing the film from the PET support.

Raman Characterization of Pure Compounds

study is based on the definition provided by the DermaLab® Single manual, which de­
scribes the LEB as the low­echogenic portion of the upper dermis visible in the Super
A­scan [27]. The scientific literature presents different interpretations of LEB behaviour:
many high­frequency ultrasound studies associate high levels of LEB with photo­damaged
or aged skin. However, no clear studies exist that specifically describe how the LEB re­
sponds to short­term hydration changes.
Based on the information provided in the DermaLab documentation, the LEB should not
be interpreted as a standalone marker of chronic skin damage but it can be seen as the
section of the upper dermis that appears less echogenic during a given measurement.
In the context of the conducted experiments, where the time scale is on the order of
hours, changes in LEB are more plausibly attributed to acute hydration effects. Increased
water uptake, swelling of dermal structures and a temporary reduction in tissue density
can all bring to an increase of the LEB values. This interpretation is consistent with the
results obtained with the Corneometer and with the structural parameters measured by
DermaLab, suggesting that the observed rise in LEB after cream application reflects short­
term moisturization rather than long­term photoaging or structural damage.
Overall, the combined Corneometer and DermaLab measurements indicate that the S4410_G60_15VC
cream produces a more balanced, deeper, and longer­lasting hydration effect compared
to the commercial cream. This enhanced performance likely derives from the humectant­
rich glycerol phase and the semi­occlusive silicone matrix, which together modulate water
transport and slow the evaporation process, while facilitating the penetration of Vitamin C
into the epidermis.

4
Conclusion
4.1
Summary of Findings
The primary objective of this thesis was to determine whether a miniaturized Raman spec­
trometer can serve as a reliable and non­invasive analytical tool for monitoring the pen­
etration of L­ascorbic acid (L­AA) through the epidermis with in vivo testing. Thanks to
a structured experimental plan, starting from the characterization of the pure reference
compounds to the in vivo penetration studies with the use of glycerol–silicone cream and
patch formulations, this work demonstrates that these goals were successfully achieved.
The first measurements performed on pure L­AA and on the liquid glycerol–silicone liquid
mixtures allowed for a clear monitoring and identification of the characteristic VC peak.
The identification of the reference spectra was essential to confirm that the mini Raman
device was capable of selectively detecting L­AA without any overlapping of the spectra
with other formulation components. The mini Raman performance was then tested for in
vivo studies of liquid mixtures, cream and patch formulations. When applied to these in
vivo penetration studies of VC, the device consistently captured the characteristic Raman
features of the active compound at probe depths corresponding to the viable epidermis
(approximately 80–120 µm).
This allowed to confirm the effective penetration and detectability of the active ingredient.
The penetration of VC, in all the different formulations and systems developed by Glysious
(liquid glycerol–silicone mixtures, cream and patch formulations), through the epidermis
was successfully monitored. This stepwise approach so verified that the Mini Raman
spectrometer possesses the sensitivity and depth resolution required for monitoring the
penetration of active compounds through the skin.
4.2
Evaluation of Mini Raman Performance
A key aspect of the project was to study how factors such as formulation complexity, skin
physiology and inter­individual variability affected the intensity and quality of the Raman
signal.
Pure compounds and glycerol–silicone liquid mixtures had more evident VC peaks, while
for more complex formulations (patch and cream) the peaks were less evident but still the
obtained spectra did not compromise detection of the L­AA fingerprint.
Another very important part of the testing were the measurements conducted on the dif­
ferent individuals for the patches. The measurements conducted across the different
subjects showed that mini Raman is sensitive to several factors such as age, sex as well
as environmental and skin physiology factors. Despite the variability, the penetration of
L­AA was still detected and monitored for the different subjects, demonstrating that the
system performs reliably under practical cosmetic­testing conditions. In fact, mini Raman
revealed to be sensitive to factors including subject movement, skin heterogeneity, and
varying levels of hydration but still allowed for the detection of the VC penetration through
the epidermis.
Overall, the results confirms that the Mini Raman platform is suitable for rapid, non­
destructive, and real­time penetration monitoring of active compounds used in cosmetic
science.

4.3
Biophysical Characterisation of Formulations
In addition to spectroscopic analysis, complementary biophysical measurements were
taken in order to further test the performance of the different formulations.
Tests were taken with the Courage + Khazaka Corneometer that measures skin hydra­
tion, and with the DermaLab SkinLab ultrasound probe that measures paramteres such
as LEB, skin thickness and dermal echogenicity which have been interpretaed to reflect
overall skin hydration.
The obtained results show how the both the cream and patch Glysious formulations have
important and evident long­lasting hydration effects. These results are in line with the
known humectant properties of glycerol.
Differences between the cream, patch, and commercial product were also observed; al­
though also the commercial serum provided a certain level of hydration, performance of
the S4410_G60_15VC cream was superior as it provided a more evident long­lasting hy­
dration of the skin even after 24 h.
These findings highlight the value of combining Raman spectroscopy with classical bio­
physical tools to form a more complete picture of topical product performance.
4.4
Overall Significance
Overall, the thesis establishes that mini Raman spectroscopy is a powerful technique for
monitoring the penetration of unstable active ingredients such as Vitamin C into the epi­
dermis.
The demonstrated ability to detect intact L­AA within human skin, across multiple formu­
lations and subjects, confirms the suitability of this approach for formulation development,
stability evaluation, and cosmetic R&D applications. The further integration of the spec­
troscopic studies with the biophysical measurements provides a deeper insight into the
different delivery mechanisms and formulations and highlights even more the potential of
mini Raman spectroscopy as next­generation tools for non­invasive skin analysis.

5
Future Works
This project has demonstrated that mini Raman spectroscopy can be used to detect and
monitor the penetration of L­ascorbic acid (L­AA) into the epidermis through in vivo test­
ing.
At the same time, several practical and methodological limitations emerged during the
experiments. Addressing these limitations is essential to increase the robustness, repro­
ducibility and interpretability of future studies and experiments. The main directions for
improvement are discussed below.
5.1
Methodological Improvements in Raman Measurements
5.1.1
Optimization and Comparison of Different Probes
In this thesis, Raman measurements were performed using a single Mini Raman probe
configuration designed to target a depth of 80–120 µm. This depth range corresponds
to the viable epidermis, which was considered the most relevant region for detecting in­
tact Vitamin C as the epidermis is the skin layer in which the active compound is more
abundant. Before the in­vivo testing, preliminary attempts were made to test alternative
probes, but the resulting spectra were not satisfactory since both signal intensity and
spectra resolution were noticeably worse compared to the 80–120 µm probe.
The Mini Raman system offers other probe configurations, targeting depths of 0–20 µm
and 50–70 µm. These ranges could be use to monitor VC penetration in more superficial
layer such as the stratus corneum and the upper layer of the epidermis.
Although these other probes were not explored in this work and although Vitamin C is
less stable on the skin surface, these tests could provide valuable information for future
studies. A controlled comparison between all three depths (0–20, 50–70, and 80–120 µm)
would allow a more complete picture of VC penetration pathway. Investigating multiple
depths could also show if the formulation composition can affect diffusion in the differ­
ent layers of the skin. Such studies would be particularly beneficial for refining both the
analytical methodology and the design of future delivery systems.
5.1.2
Baseline Handling and Skin Structure Compensation
An important source of variability in the different tests that have been performed in the
study was the skin itself, due to it’s complex structure, thickness, different hydration lev­
els and the fact that it can be easily influenced by environmental factors. These factors
all affected the recorded Raman signal.
In future work, the protocol should include the acquisition of an individual baseline spec­
trum for each subject and site, before the application of the tested product.
This baseline spectrum can then be subtracted from subsequent spectra at each time
point, or used as a reference to normalize L­AA peak intensities.
This approach is useful as it would reduce the influence of inter­indiviual differences in the
skin and give more accurate results of the Raman signal intensities from in­vivo testing of
the different formulations. Implementing this would make the quantification of penetration
more reliable and more comparable between the tested subjects.

5.1.3
Standardisation of Measurement Conditions
In order to make the tests more accurate and to obtain more consistent and reliable results,
future studies should implement more strict experimental conditions across the different
volunteers, including:
• controlling room temperature and relative humidity during all measurements,
• standardising pre­conditioning of the skin (e.g. washing procedure, resting period
before measurements, avoidance of skincare products before testing takes place),
• take the tests in the same day and environment to reduce variability due to environ­
mental and external factors,
Implementation of this procedure will help reduce variability of the tests between the dif­
ferent subjects and so making the trends in the Raman intensities more interpretable.
5.2
Formulation Optimisation and Use of Vitamin C
Penetration Enhancers
5.2.1
Incorporation of Penetration Enhancers
Another promising direction for future work is the optimization of VC delivery by incorpo­
rating chemical penetration enhancers (CPEs) into the formulations. Although the current
tested formulations already allow for a measurable diffusion of L­AA into the skin, several
studies have demonstrated how selected CPEs can significantly increase the penetration
and stability of the active compound and so this could lead to an increase in the intensity
of the Raman signal.
CPEs alter the skin barrier by partioning into stratum corneum (SC) and interacting with
it’s components to temporarily reduce its occlusive properties and facilitate topical drug
transport [28].
A recent study identified eighteen different CPEs capable of enhancing the skin perme­
ation of VC across biological and reconstructed membranes [28]. These include non­ionic
surfactants, phospholipids, amino acids, terpenes, polyols, esters, fatty acids, colloids
and polymers. CPEs should promote a reversible decrease in skin barrier properties to
maintain the skin’s protective properties but high­permeation enhancing potency is often
associated with high toxicity and irritation potential [28]. For these reasons, the proper
selection of the CPEs should take into account both safety and efficacy of the compound
when incorporated to the formulation [28].
Overall, the incorporation of selected CPEs to the formulations represents a powerful
strategy to further enhance Vitamin C penetration and accelerate detection by Raman
spectroscopy.
5.2.2
Managing Peak Overlap and Spectral Complexity
The introduction of additional ingredients to the formulation however brings to some lim­
itations: the risk of the Raman peak overlap between L­AA and the new components of
the formulations.
This issue is particularly important for molecules that contain groups with intense Raman
signal in the same regions as VC reference peak.
The incorporation of these new ingredients should be done by taking into consideration
the following aspects:
• testing candidate penetration enhancers before adding them to the formulation to
check potential overlap of the peaks,
38

• favouring adding excipients with weak Raman profiles in the spectral region used to
monitor L­AA,
By taking into account these aspects it is so possible to enhance the penetration of the
active compound without losing the ability to selectively detect L­AA.
5.3
Use of Alternative Skin Membranes to Reduce Variability
One limitation of the performed tests when it come to in vivo human experiments is the
large standard deviation in the Raman intensities due to natural subject­to­subject vari­
ability. To complement the results obtained with the in vivo tests and also to increase the
robustness and reliability of the conclusions, future work should consider using alternative
skin membranes and model systems.
5.3.1
Reconstructed Human Epidermis and Artificial Membranes
Synthetic membranes that are utilized in diffusion studies for topical and transdermal for­
mulations are usually porous thin polymeric sheets for example cellulose acetate (CA)
and polysulfones[29]. In a particular study [29] the permeability of the skin was compared
to that of two synthetic membranes: cellulose acetate and Strat­M membranes.
Cellulose acetate membranes are porous synthetic membranes for assessing topical for­
mulation performance that acts as a barrier with no rate­limiting property [29]. Limitations
for using these membranes though are that its properties are not completely relatable to
those of the human skin. Strat­M membranes, on the other hand, are synthetic polymeric
membranes for transdermal diffusion testing that is more predictive of dif­ fusion in human
skin without lot­to­lot variability, safety and storage limitations [29].
For the purpose of this project, the use of Strat­M membranes has been considered, the
big limitation was their small surface area and elevated cost.
Considering alternative membranes to perform in vivo tests could bring to:
• a reduction in standard deviations by eliminating inter­individual biological differ­
ences,
• systematic comparison of different formulations under identical conditions,
These models should not replace in vivo testing but could be used as an intermediate
step to screen the different formulations and understanding basic penetration mechanisms
before moving to volunteer studies.
5.4
Broadening the Volunteer Panel and Demographic
Diversity
In this project, the number and demographic diversity of the tested subjects was limited.
To have more realistic results and more representative of the population, expanding the
subject pool in the future investigations should be considered. This could also be useful
to help clarify how skin type and physiology influence the intensity of the Raman signal.
5.4.1
Age, Sex and Ethnicity Effects
Future studies should deliberately recruit volunteers with:
• a wider range of ages (young, middle­aged and elderly skin),
• both sexes in balanced numbers,
• a broader range of skin phototypes and ethnic backgrounds.

This would allow to investigate how the penetration of L­AA, intensity of the Raman signal,
skin responses change when taking into consideration different demographic groups.
Another very important aspect should be to make sure that all tested subjects follow ex­
actly the same protocol (pre­conditioning, product application, rest time, measurement
time points). In fact, combining standardized conditions and individual baseline spectra
will make possible to see how the differences in the skin types and background of the
volunteers influences both penetration and Raman signal behavior.
Overall, future work should focus on improving the Mini Raman methodology (probe de­
sign, baseline handling, signal processing), optimising formulations (using penetration
enhancers), incorporating tests on alternative skin membranes for more reliable results,
and expanding the diversity and number of test subjects. Together, these factors will help
improve the conducted research and obtain a more comprehensive and statistically ro­
bust framework for in vivo monitoring of Vitamin C and other cosmetic actives in human
skin.
