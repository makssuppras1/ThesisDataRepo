Abstract
As more than half of the global population is having a root canal treatment the interest
to secure a higher success rate for this treatment is extensive. One of the most important
steps in a root canal treatment is to clean the root canal eﬃciently using the least possible
use of irrigation.
The goal of this Master’s thesis is to investigate how to clean the cavity most eﬃciently
using the lowest volume of irrigant. The study focuses on two of the main parameters
that aﬀect the cleaning and exchange of ﬂuid in the cavity, which is the type of needle
and ﬂow rate. It is investigated what combination of needle type and ﬂow rate results in
the most eﬃcient cleaning of a cavity.
The thesis covers a theoretical background of the state-of-the-art cleaning, and how
diﬀerent ﬂuid mechanical aspects aﬀect the cleaning and exchange of ﬂuid in cavities. It
also touches upon what is done in practice through interviews with dentists. It introduces
the experimental set up and discusses the obtained results. Three of the tested needles
are already at the market, while three other needles are prototypes developed at DTU,
due to test a broader range of needles.
It is found that the most eﬃcient needle to clean the entire cavity is the open-ended six-
lumen needle. Using the open-ended six-lumen needle at a ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s proved
to be the most eﬃcient combination to clean the entire cavity resulting in a volume of
0.129 mL. In contrast the open-ended ﬂat needle, which is used as the reference needle,
used a volume of 0.183 mL and the open-ended ﬂat one-blocked needle, which was the
worse needle, used a volume of 0.26 mL. Furthermore, looking at the bottom region of
the cavity the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle proved to be the most eﬃcient needle.
Using the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle in combination with a ﬂow rate of 0.15 mL/s
proved to be the most eﬃcient combination, resulting in a used volume of 0.068 mL.
With these two needles being the most eﬃcient ones, it can be concluded that multiple
lumen needles with exits in diﬀerent heights are more eﬀective than single lumen needles
regarding eﬃcient cleaning and exchanging ﬂuid of a cavity.
Overall, this thesis demonstrates that some needle types are preferable over others, which
is also the case for the ﬂow rate. Thus, a more eﬃcient cleaning and exchanging of ﬂuid
in a root canal can be obtained using the right combination of diﬀerent parameters.

Introduction
In everyday life, we are cleaning surfaces and cavities all the time. A challenge dentists are faced with
when making a root canal treatment (RCT) is to rinse eﬀectively to ensure that tissue residue, bacteria
and bioﬁlm in the drilled tooth root are removed. More than half of the global population is having a
root canal treatment [1] and research has demonstrated that after 5 years the success rate for RCT’s
are at 85%, and after 20 years it has decreased to only being 60% [2]. How a root canal treatment
is performed diﬀers from clinic to clinic and from dentist to dentist. Due to the high number of the
population who experience a failure in their RCT, and diﬀerences in the treatment, there is a need to
investigate and determine the most eﬃcient way of cleaning a root canal during a routine root canal
treatment.
1.1
Endodontic Treatment
Endodontic treatment treats the inside of a tooth. One type of endodontic treatment is a root canal
treatment. Inside the tooth, there is a soft tissue called the pulp, which is located under the white enamel
and a hard layer called the dentin. The pulp contains blood vessels, nerves, and connective tissue and
creates the surrounding hard tissues of the tooth during development. The pulp extends from the crown
of the tooth to the tip of the root where it connects to the tissues surrounding the root. This little
opening is called the apical foramen, which is normally situated 0.2-3.8 mm from the very tip of the
tooth. The tip of the tooth is referred to as the apex. At Figure 1.1, one can see the anatomy of a tooth.
During a tooth’s growth and development, the pulp is critical. However, once a tooth is fully mature, it
can survive without the pulp [3]. When the pulp becomes inﬂamed or infected, it is necessary to perform
an endodontic treatment. The inﬂammation or infection can have a variety of causes, including but not
limited to deep decay, repeated dental procedures on the tooth, or a crack or chip in the tooth. If pulp
inﬂammation or infection is left untreated, it can cause pain or lead to an abscess. [4]
Endodontic treatments are quite an invasive and diﬃcult procedure, especially in complex anatomies.
During or after a root canal procedure, complications can arise [5]. Some of the common factors that
are attributable to endodontic failure can include: inadequate ﬁlling of the canal (canals that are poorly
cleaned and obturated), untreated canals (both major and accessory) [6], instrumental complications,
and persistent bacterial infections or reinfections.
Persistent infection is the major cause of short-
term endodontic failures. This includes inadequate shaping, cleaning, and obturation procedures, poor
restorations, and exposure through a fracture. The remedies are dependent on the primary cause of the
reinfection and the aﬀected parts. [5]
RCT is the most frequent treatment carried out by endodontists. By determining the worldwide preva-
lence of root-ﬁlled teeth, one can give an indication of how many people are receiving an RCT. RCT’s

1.2
Root Canal Treatment
A root canal procedure consists of many diﬀerent steps. It is performed by a dentist or an endodontist.
In the majority of cases, the procedure will be performed while the patient is under local anesthesia.
Below each step of a root canal treatment is described: [8][9]
1. Anesthetic: A small amount of numbing medication will be placed on the gum near the aﬀected
tooth. Once it has taken eﬀect, a local anesthetic will be injected into the gums.
2. Removing the pulp: When the tooth is numb, the endodontist will make a small opening in
the top of the tooth. Once the infected or damaged pulp is exposed, it will be carefully removed
using special tools called ﬁles. All canals/pathways in the tooth will be cleaned out.
3. Mechanical shaping and cleaning: Tiny instruments such as ﬁne ﬁles will be used to shape
and extend the canals to ensure eﬃcient irrigation. During the mechanical shaping, the canal will
be irrigated with an antibacterial and antiseptic irrigant between the use of ﬁles.

4. Irrigation and cleaning: When the canal has reached a suﬃcient size in which eﬀective ir-
rigation can take place, the root canal will be irrigated with other antibacterial and antiseptic
irrigants, each having a speciﬁc purpose. This eliminates the bacteria and treats the infection
located in the canals.
5. Filling the canals and access hole: The endodontist will use gutta-percha to ﬁll the canals.
This rubber-like material is placed inside the canals and then heated. This will be compressed so
it ﬁts snuggly against the walls. The endodontist adds adhesive cement to further seal the canals.
Properly sealed canals keep bacteria out. Additionally, the hole used to access the canals must
be sealed.
6. Healing and antibiotics: The patient is sent home to heal and, in some cases, provided with a
prescription for antibiotics. The medication kills the rest of the infection. The patient will also
be given post-care instructions.
7. Adding the crown: If the root canal was on a back tooth or the tooth has signiﬁcant damage,
the patient will need to return for a crown. The crown is placed around the treated tooth to
add strength and stability. The permanent crown is fabricated to match the existing teeth. Once
placed, the tooth can handle the forces of chewing once again.
A sequence analysis of how a root canal treatment is performed can be seen in Figure 1.3. This sequence
analysis has been developed in collaboration using a sequence analysis from an earlier report [10] and
steps have been added from dentists who were interviewed for this project. The mechanical shaping and
cleaning are performed by small ﬁles shaping and extending the canal. Between each ﬁle shift the canal
is irrigated with NaOCl and this process is completed over several rounds using diﬀerent sizes of ﬁles.

only used in procedures where the entire treatment is performed in the same day. If the treatments are
performed over multiple days, these two steps are omitted. This thesis is focusing on the irrigation steps
which are showed as step 6, 7, 9, and 11 at Figure 1.3.

1.3
Project Scope
The goal of this Master’s thesis is to investigate how diﬀerent parameters such as needle type and
ﬂow rate inﬂuence the cleanliness of narrow cavities, and which combination results in a more eﬀective
cleaning. In this project, eﬃcient cleaning is deﬁned as the ability to exchange the ﬂuids in the root
canal with the least possible use of irrigation. Regarding the needle type, the design of the tip varies
considerably. The design of a needle can be divided into two categories: open-ended and closed-ended
needles. In Figure 2.1, one can see a small selection of diﬀerent needle tip designs. The needles used
in practice are mainly single lumen needles. This thesis will not only investigate single lumen needles
but will also test needles with multiple lumens. This will be assessed from a series of experiments which
will be performed. The hypothesis to be investigated is whether multiple lumen needles with exits in
diﬀerent heights are more eﬀective than single lumen needles regarding eﬃcient cleaning of a cavity. This
project aims to answer the two following research questions:
RQ1 How do diﬀerent parameters such as, type and size of needle, viscosity, ﬂow rate, volume and
shear stress aﬀect the cleanliness in root canals?
RQ2 Which combined parameters result in a more eﬃcient cleaning of a root canal?
RQ3 How much volume of the irrigant is necessary to use before the root canal is considered eﬀectively
cleaned?
To answer the research questions, a thorough literature review has been conducted in order to present
earlier ﬁndings and discuss what has already been investigated.
Interviews with dentists have been
conducted. The ﬁndings from the interviews have been used to discover if there are any similarities
between the existing literature and what is done in practice. The ﬁndings from the interviews have
also been informative for developing the experimental setup and determining in which intervals each
parameter should be investigated.
The novelty of this thesis lies in the investigation of which combination of needle and ﬂow rate is the most
eﬃcient for cleaning a root canal. It is important to mention that some assumptions and simpliﬁcations
have been made in this project. First of all, for simplicity, the experiments will be performed using
a conical cavity in an acrylic block instead of real root canals.
Second, it is assumed that this is
representative of an excavated root canal in a tooth and therefore the results can be used to determine
what is most eﬃcient in cleaning root canals. Third, two regions in the cavity will be investigated: top
and bottom. If no blue irrigant remains at the very bottom of the cavity, it is assumed that when the
top region is clean this represents that the entire cavity has undergone an eﬃcient cleaning. This will
be described more thoroughly in Section 3.3.
Using the video processing software OpenCV, which is elaborated in Section 3.3, it was decided that the
cavity is considered clean when the mean red color intensity reaches a value of 195.

State-of-the-Art
To date, there have been many diﬀerent studies regarding the ﬂow parameters within a root canal treat-
ment. Parameters, such as irrigation methods, types of irrigants and their viscosity, types of irrigation
needles, ﬂow rate, volume of the irrigant, and shear stress, have been investigated in order to assess
their inﬂuence on eﬃcient cleaning in root canals. This chapter will discuss what earlier studies have
shown for each parameter. To gain further insight regarding what is actually done and used in practice,

2.1
Irrigation Methods
During a root canal procedure, the irrigant fulﬁll multiple roles. First, it is used to lubricate the root
canal before the insertion of instruments. It is important that the root canal is lubricated while working
in it. Second, the irrigation is used to ﬂush out loosened debris that is generated during root canal ﬁling
or reaming from the root canal system. Lastly, the irrigant is important to dissolve and disrupt bacterial
bioﬁlm and pulpa tissue and to kill bacteria and denature toxins to the outer-most reaches of the root
canal system. [11]
The irrigant is commonly delivered continuously from a syringe through a needle. The factors aﬀecting
the eﬃcacy of needle irrigation include the diameter of the needle, needle design, depth of the irrigating
needle engaged in the root canal, and the ﬁnal size of the root canal preparation. There are both manual
methods for delivering the irrigant, such as using a syringe, and automated methods for delivering the
irrigant using one of several commercially available devices. In some cases, syringe irrigation will be
enough, though in other cases a method involving agitation of the irrigant is needed. [11]
2.1.1
Syringe Irrigation
Syringe irrigation remains the most popular technique for delivering irrigants inside root canals [12].
For manual injection, the volume of the used irrigant may vary, whereas for the automated injection
one can control the amount of irrigant used. This will be discussed later in Section 2.3.3. Examples of
automated delivery systems can be continuous delivery through one needle (Quantec-E) and continuous
delivery and evacuation achieved through two needles (EndoVac) [11]. Several ex vivo studies and one
clinical trial have shown that there is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between syringe irrigation and a variety of
other methods, including negative-pressure irrigation and sonic and ultrasonic activation, when it comes

2.1.2
Agitation Technique
Agitation happens by "activating" the irrigant as it is introduced to the root canal. This can be done
by moving the instrument within the canal with a reciprocating, oscillating, or rotating action. This
movement can be achieved by several methods such as manual reciprocation of the instrument within
the canal, ultrasonic or sonic activation to achieve oscillation of the instrument, and introduction into
the canal of a mechanically driven rotary instrument. [11]
The simplest method for agitating the irrigant is to introduce the needle into the canal and to manually
redistribute it along the canal with a reciprocating action. However, ultrasonic activation is the most
popular irrigant activation method and the second-most popular irrigation method. This method includes
physical contact with the wall, due to ultrasonic ﬁles oscillating in the root canal. [13]
Activation of irrigant with ultrasonic devices involves the agitation of either a syringe-delivered bolus
of ﬂuid in the canal or by simultaneous delivery (via custom reservoir) and agitation [11].
Despite
the frequent wall contact, ultrasonic ﬁles act primarily by agitating the surrounding irrigant.
Their
oscillatory motion generates acoustic streaming, which stirs up the irrigant in the main canal, transports
the irrigant farther into remote areas of the root canal system, and improves the cleaning by increasing
the wall shear stress to detach targets from the root canal wall. [12]
According to Teplitsky, 1987, ultrasonic agitation allows for better penetration of the irrigant than man-
ual syringe irrigation when a viscous irrigant (viscosity approaching 5.8 times that of sodium hypochlo-
rite) was used.
When the irrigant viscosity was close to that of sodium hypochlorite, there was a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence between syringe irrigation and ultrasonic agitation in favor of ultrasonic agitation.
The experiment also showed that in narrow canals (smaller than ISO size 30 (Ø 0.3 mm)), it was more
beneﬁcial to use ultrasonic agitation. [14]
2.2
Equipment
When a root canal treatment is performed, several pieces of equipment will be used. Firstly, the type of
irrigants used for disinfection and bacterial removal from the root canal is an important factor. Secondly,
the type and size of needle needs to be decided. Lastly, the root canal’s anatomy varies from tooth to
tooth and needs to be considered and analyzed before starting the treatment and irrigation.

the organic part and then chelators should be used as the ﬁnal irrigant. Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid
(EDTA) is the most common choice for this role and is considered necessary as a supplementary action
of a demineralizing agent [12]. EDTA is employed as a ﬁnal rinse and is recommended in a concentration
of 17% for a period of one minute. [15]
Chlorhexidine (CHX) is eﬀective against gram-positive and -negative bacteria, spores of bacteria,
lipophilic viruses, yeast, and fungi. Since CHX is pH-dependent, these eﬀects are greatly reduced when
organic matter is present. CHX is incapable of removing bioﬁlms and other organic debris, which is
the important reason for the use of NaOCl [13]. A CHX solution of 2% after the chemo- mechanical
preparation provides the appropriate antibacterial eﬀect and CHX is therefore mostly advocated as a
ﬁnal irrigant. [12]
The most recommended irrigation regimen employs a combination of 5.25% NaOCl followed by a ﬁnal
rinse with 17% EDTA. A combination of NaOCl and EDTA has shown to be eﬀective in removal of the
smear layer from the coronal and middle thirds of a root canal. However, their higher viscosity would
be detrimental to irrigation dynamics in the essential apical third in terms of tissue dissolution, smear
layer removal, and antimicrobial ability. [15]
2.2.2
Needles
Within the endodontist area, Birmingham wire gauges (BWG) are used. BWG is a system of measure-
ments describing the thickness or diameter of the needles. Regarding wire and ﬁne tubing, the gauge
number is used to specify the outer diameter of the product, whereas for larger mechanical tubing it
speciﬁes the wall thickness. The gauge numbering ranges from 5 to 36, where size 36G is the smallest
size in diameter. [17]
Nowadays, the 30G needle is considered as the clinical standard, but given the trends in root canal
instrumentation, 31G needles may become the standard in the near future [12]. In the past large needles
(21-25G) was commonly used, but they only allowed the irrigant to reach up to the middle third of the
root canal. This may have had implications for the eﬀectiveness of c leaning the cavity. Narrower gauge
needles, such as 30G are more eﬀective than larger gauge needles in removing debris from curved canals.
[18]
Currently, there are two types of needles used for irrigation: needles that allow the irrigant to ﬂow
straight through their tip irrespective of its particular shape (open-ended) and needles with a closed tip
preventing direct outﬂow, so the irrigant ﬂows through one or more side vents (closed-ended). [12]

Isthmus and Accessory Canal
An isthmus is a narrow, ribbon-shaped connection between two root canals that may contain vital tissue,
necrotic pulp, bioﬁlms, or residual ﬁlling material. An isthmus may present diﬀerent conﬁgurations, and
its prevalence is dependent on the type of teeth, the root level, and the patient’s age [22]. An isthmus
can be seen at Figure 2.4 indicated as letter D.
Any branch of the root canal that communicates with the periodontal ligament is called an accessory
canal. A lateral canal is deﬁned as an accessory canal located at the coronal or middle third of the root.
Accessory canals represent potential pathways where bacteria and/or their products from the necrotic
root canal can reach the periodontal ligament and cause disease. One study investigated the frequency,
location, and direction of the accessory canals and showed that 27.4% of the samples had accessory
canals [22]. An accessory canal can be seen at Figure 2.4 indicated as letter B, C E.

Apical Canal
The main root canal ends at the apical foramen (major foramen), which frequently opens laterally on the
root surface, at a mean distance between 0.2 and 3.8 mm from the anatomic apex. The anatomic apex is
the tip or the end of the root. The diameter of the apical foramen has been described between 0.21 and
0.39 mm. Another relevant variation of the root canal at or near the apex is the apical ramiﬁcation [22].
The main canal may divide into multiple accessory canals, which can be seen at Figure 2.4 indicated as
letter F.
Root Canal Curvature
In order to choose the appropriate chemomechanical protocol when preparing for the root canal system
it is important to know about the root canal curvature. Nearly all root canals are curved in the apical
third. The curvature may vary from gradual curvature of the entire canal, sharp curvature of the canal
near the apex, or a gradual curvature of the canal with a straight apical ending. The Schneider’s method
has been the most widely used method to determine root canal curvature. The angle of curvature and
its degree is classiﬁed as straight (5◦), moderate (10–20◦), or severe (25–70◦). [22]

In ﬂuid dynamics, two main types of ﬂow exist: laminar ﬂow, which is characterized by a smooth and
constant ﬂuid motion, and turbulent ﬂow, which has chaotic eddies, vortices and other ﬂow instabilities.
The type of ﬂow is related to the dimensionless Reynolds number (Re). In the case of a root canal,
similar to a tube, Re is given by:

The Reynolds number expresses the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces. At a low Reynolds number

laminar ﬂow may be associated with a higher penetration depth of the irrigant, whilst turbulent ﬂow
may allow better removal of debris on the canal wall. [23]

2.3.1
Viscosity
One of the most essential parameters related to ﬂuid ﬂow characteristics is its dynamic viscosity (µ),
which is the internal resistance to root canal irrigant ﬂow deformed by tensile or shear stresses. Viscosity
is a property arising from collisions between neighboring particles in the ﬂuid which are moving at
diﬀerent velocities. When the ﬂuid is forced through a tube/needle, the particles which comprise the
ﬂuid generally move faster near the tube’s axis and more slowly near its walls. As the viscosity get
lesser, the easier is the ﬂuid movement. The reduction in viscosity facilitates the spread of the liquid in
long and tight canals. Therefore, it has been demonstrated that the reduction in viscosity improve the
ﬂow of the endodontic irrigant into root canal intricacies. One of the primary inﬂuencing variables that
aﬀects the ﬂuid viscosity is the temperature. [24]
An in vitro study, performed by Gopikrishna [15], has assessed the inﬂuence of temperature (25◦C,
45◦C, and 60◦C) and concentration (1.25%, 2.6%, and 5.25%) on the dynamic viscosity of NaOCl in

One study compared the use of the EndoVac system to syringe irrigation [30]. An EndoVac uses suction
to pull down the irrigant to the apex of the root canal, and then up through the needle. The study
showed that the use of the EndoVac system resulted in statistically signiﬁcant more debris removal at 1
mm from WL than syringe irrigation. For the EndoVac system 1.6% debris was left, while for the needle
irrigation 5.7% debris was left. One of the advantages of the EndoVac system is the ability to safely
deliver the irrigant to the working length. However, the study also showed that, the EndoVac system
delivered a signiﬁcant higher volume of irrigant, than the volume delivered with syringe irrigation over
the same amount of time. The EndoVac system used an irrigant volume of 42.2 mL, while the syringe
irrigation only used a volume of 15.7 mL.
The volume used for each irrigation is one of the parameters that varies the most. Studies has shown that
using more volume at the same ﬂow rate is more eﬃcient in bacterial removal. However, other studies
show that using less volume with the right ﬂow rate can have the same eﬃciency as higher volumes
delivered by continuous ﬂow. With this said the volume is an interesting parameter to assess and will
be used to determine what is more eﬃcient. A measurement having the same value of cleanliness as
another measurement but using a smaller volume of irrigant will be considered more eﬃcient.

2.5
Partial Conclusion
The ﬁndings from this chapter is used to design the experimental setup and what will be investigated
during the experiments in Chapter 3.
This chapter showed that a combination of the two irrigants
NaOCl and EDTA is widely used, whereas the third and ﬁnal irrigant can diﬀer from clinic to clinic.
A reduction in viscosity of the irrigant will improve the ﬂow into root canals. Therefore, an irrigant
with a lower viscosity is more eﬃcient in the apical part of the root canal. However, the removal of
bacteria and smear layer is more important, which causes NaOCl and EDTA to be used in some speciﬁc
concentrations for root canal treatments.
The needles used for irrigation is usually of a size 29G to 31G, but the type of needle diﬀers. The used

ﬂow rate depends on the size of needle and the dentist who performs the treatment. It is shown that
the ﬂow rate for a needle of size 30G often lies in the interval of 0.09 - 0.26 mL
s . Using these parameters,
it was revealed that the ﬂow is in the laminar ﬁeld as well the mass transport of the ﬂuid is dominated
by advection. The ﬂow rate might be the most important parameter to look at due to its inﬂuence
regarding the replacement of the irrigant. Using the right ﬂow rate can decrease the used volume and
outweigh disadvantages related to closed-ended needles.
Two parameters that diﬀers from treatment to treatment is the tooth anatomy and the volume. The
anatomy diﬀers from tooth to tooth. The coronal and middle third of the main canal is often simple to
clean out, whereas accessory canals and the apical part of the main root can be diﬃcult to clean out.
Here an activation of the irrigant is needed such as agitation, which makes the ﬂow turbulent.
The volume diﬀers which is in good accordance with the variability in ﬂow rate. For the ﬁnal irrigation
the dentists used between 1-3 mL for each irrigation. Lastly, the inﬂuence of the shear stress was looked
at. This is a parameter that is diﬃcult to measure in physical experiments and it is therefore often
investigated using CFD. Also, there are no quantitative data telling what the minimal shear stress value
should be in order for it to have a bigger inﬂuence in cleaning narrow cavities.
The used irrigant is a very important parameter however, the best combinations and concentrations are
already well developed and therefore reasonably determined. The used volume depends on which ﬂow
rate is used. The value and area that is exposed for shear stress depends on the needle type. Lastly, a
larger needle can deliver a higher ﬂow rate. From this it can be concluded that the needle size and type
as well as the ﬂow rate are the most important parameters when it comes to clean and exchange the
ﬂuid in the cavity, since the other parameters depend on those two.

• Use one speciﬁc cavity size for simplicity.

• Use a ﬂow rate in the interval of 0.03 - 0.27 mL

• Use the volume as a parameter to determine the eﬃciency.

dynamic viscosity is µ = 1 mPa · s. Since the experiments are performed at a temperature between
20-25◦C, the viscosity of the irrigant will take a value between the two mentioned viscosities during the
performing of the experiments.
One thing that is needed is to distinguish between the ﬂuid already being in the root canal and the
newly injected irrigant. Therefore, a mixture of demineralized water and blue fruit coloring has been

canal. As the blue irrigant is a mixture of demineralized water and blue fruit color this blue irrigant will
have a diﬀerent dynamic viscosity and density than pure demineralized water. However, the dynamic
viscosity and density do not have a big inﬂuence and the diﬀerence in dynamic viscosity and density has
therefore been neglected.

A free open-source software called OpenCV has been used for the data analysis. This software is a

programming languages such as C++, Python, Java, etc. OpenCV-Python is a Python wrapper for the

[42]
The cleanliness in the cavity is analyzed with regards to two regions in the cavity. One region at the top
of the cavity and one right below the needle opening. The two regions are showed in Figure 3.6d. Only
one region is analyzed at a time. So, the cleanliness is either analyzed with respect to the top region or
the bottom region. The cleanliness is measured as the mean red color intensity for the chosen region.
It is important to mention that when the top region is considered clean it is assumed that it represents

The data has been analyzed using color codes. The videos have been analyzed in the color space RGB
(red, green and blue), which deﬁnes a color as the percentages of red, green, and blue hues mixed together

colors), whereas if the values are [0, 0, 0] there is minimum intensity of all colors and it is black [44].

when the blue irrigant is in the cavity the color values for the investigated region will take [0, 0, 255],
whereas when the cavity becomes clean (has the color white) the color values for the investigated region
will take [255, 255, 255]. It will therefore not be possible to see any change in the blue color code since it
will have a high value through the entire video, whereas the values for red and green will increase during
the video. The red color intensity, at the area right under the needle opening of the cavity, at Figure
3.6b was used as the determination for when a cavity is considered to be clean. With this said a mean
red color intensity at a value of 195 is decided to be the value for an eﬃcient cleaning.

3.4
Design of Experiments
Design of Experiments (DOE) is a specialized ﬁeld within applied statistics that oﬀers a systematic and
eﬃcient approach to planning, executing, analyzing, and interpreting controlled tests [45]. These tests
aim to assess the factors inﬂuencing the value of a parameter or a set of parameters. By facilitating
the exploration of relationships between multiple input variables (factors) and key output variables
(responses), DOE empowers scientists and engineers in diverse experimental scenarios. [46]
Unlike the traditional "one factor at a time" (OFAT) method, where experiments involve altering one
variable while keeping others constant, DOE allows for the simultaneous manipulation of multiple in-
put factors. This concurrent variation enables the identiﬁcation of crucial interactions that might go
unnoticed when factors are modiﬁed individually.
A well-strategized and executed experiment under the DOE framework can yield comprehensive insights
into the impact of one or more factors on a response variable. This approach proves to be more eﬃcient
compared to the OFAT method, where factors are altered sequentially.
Overall, DOE emerges as a
potent tool for data collection and analysis, oﬀering valuable applications across various experimental
contexts.
Despite this project’s progression to include two variables in the experimental setup — needle type and
ﬂow rate — one might assume that employing Design of Experiments (DOE) could reduce the number
of required experiments. However, this is not feasible because needle type is a categorical variable. Since
a comprehensive comparison is necessary across all needle types at various ﬂow rates, the experimental
setup has utilized the "one factor at a time" (OFAT) method. This approach entails investigating each
needle type individually at all potential ﬂow rates.

4.1.1
Two-way ANOVA
One important thing to analyze is the eﬀect of using diﬀerent needles and ﬂow rates. One can analyze
if the results depend on the use of diﬀerent needles and ﬂow rates or, if the results are as close to each
other, that using diﬀerent needles and ﬂow rates do not have an inﬂuence on the results.
An ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) is a statistical test used to analyze the diﬀerence between the means of
more than two groups. A two-way ANOVA can be used, when you have collected data on a quantitative
dependent variable at multiple levels of two categorical independent variables [48].
In this case the
quantitative variable is the frame number where the mean red color intensity reaches the value of 195,
and the two categorical variables are the needle type and ﬂow rate. Using a two-way ANOVA with
interaction one can test three null hypotheses at the same time:
• There is no diﬀerence in frame number for any needle type.
• There is no diﬀerence in frame number for any ﬂow rate.
• The eﬀect of needle type on the frame number does not depend on the eﬀect of the ﬂow rate
(a.k.a. no interaction eﬀect).
A two-way ANOVA has been performed and the results of the test can be seen in Table 4.2. The entire
analysis is given in Appendix D.2. The ANOVA has been performed using the frame numbers achieved
for when the bottom region of the cavity is considered clean.
The largest of the three p-values are
1.35 · 10−23, which is much smaller than the traditional cutoﬀvalue for statistical signiﬁcance of 0.05.
Therefore, I reject the null hypothesis. [49]

The experimental ﬁndings align well with the theoretical principles outlined in Section 2.2.2. The theory
stated that open-ended needles would outperform closed-ended needles of the same size in terms of
irrigant penetration and exchange [12]. The experiments conﬁrm this by establishing the open-ended
ﬂat needle as the most eﬀective in terms of irrigant penetration and exchange at the bottom region.
Additionally, the experiments proved the closed-ended single side-vented needle to be the least eﬀective
needle in terms of irrigant penetration and exchange with respect to both the bottom and top region in
the cavity.
The literature also stated that increasing the ﬂow rate for a closed-ended needle would enhance irrigant
penetration and exchange [20]. The experiments validate this by demonstrating that using the closed-
ended single side-vented needle at a low ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s fails to clean the bottom region but
increasing the ﬂow rate to 0.15 mL/s results in eﬀective cleaning.
The closed-ended single side-vented and closed-ended conical needles showed that using these two needles
resulted in the very bottom of the cavity did not become clean. Some blue irrigant was still remaining
at the bottom of the cavity regardless of what ﬂow rate was used. This aligns with the theory stated in
Section 2.2.2. The literature states that the optimum position for an open-ended needle is at 2-3 mm
short of the working length, whereas closed-ended needles needs to be placed within 1 mm from the
working length, due to their poorer ability to penetrate and exchange the ﬂuid. [12]
At the bottom of the cavity there is the same amount of ﬂuid between the needle tip and the working
length but not at the top region. This is caused by each needle displace a diﬀerent amount of ﬂuid in the
cavity. The closed-ended conical needle showed to be the most eﬃcient needle with respect to cleaning
the entire cavity. However, this needle does also displace around 50% of the cavity, whereas the open-

4.3.4
Discussion and Conclusion of the Prototype Needles Developed
at DTU
Analyzing the three prototype needles developed at DTU reveals a trend: as the ﬂow rate increases the
faster the investigated regions become clean. However, the most eﬃcient cleaning is achieved at lower
ﬂow rates.
For the bottom region, the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle proves to be the most eﬃcient across all
ﬂow rates. The optimal combination involves using this needle at a ﬂow rate of 0.15 mL/s, resulting in a
minimal used volume of 0.068 mL. Conversely, the open-ended six-lumen needle is identiﬁed as the least
eﬃcient needle for all ﬂow rates, except at 0.21 mL/s, where the least eﬃcient needle is the open-ended
ﬂat one-blocked. Speciﬁcally, the least eﬃcient cleaning occurs using the open-ended six-lumen needle
at a ﬂow rate of 0.27 mL/s, resulting in a used volume of 0.115 mL.
It is interesting to look at how many times the ﬂuid at bottom region of the cavity have been replaced.
From the tip of the needle to the bottom of the cavity, which has a length of 3 mm the volume of the
cavity is 0.24 mm3. For the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen, open-ended ﬂat one-blocked and open-ended
six-lumen needle the volume of used irrigant to clean the bottom region of the cavity at a ﬂow rate of
0.21 mL/s is 0.072, 0.096 and 0.091 mL respectively. This means the ﬂuid at the bottom of the cavity
is replaced 300, 400 and 379 times using the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen, open-ended ﬂat one-blocked
and open-ended six-lumen needle respectively. This is another conﬁrmation that the open-ended ﬂat
two-lumen needle turns out to be the most eﬃcient needle to clean the bottom region of the cavity.
The conﬁrmation of this is due to the ﬂuid at the bottom of the cavity is replaced fewer times using
the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle before the bottom region is considered clean compared to the two
other needles.
In the top region of the cavity, the open-ended six-lumen needle proves to be the most eﬃcient across all
ﬂow rates. The most eﬀective combination is using a ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s with this needle, resulting
in a used volume of 0.129 mL. However, the open-ended ﬂat one-blocked needle exhibits signiﬁcantly
lower eﬃciency at all ﬂow rates due to blue irrigant inﬁltration between the needles, leading to prolonged
retention in the top region. The least eﬃcient combination involves using this needle at a ﬂow rate of
0.27 mL/s, resulting in a used volume of 0.59 mL.
An important observation is that while the open-ended six-lumen needle is the least eﬀective for the
bottom region, it proves to be the most eﬃcient for the top region.

Conclusion
The main objective of this Master’s thesis was to investigate what would be the most eﬃcient combination
to clean a root canal during a root canal treatment.
A theoretical study was performed to form a basic knowledge of how diﬀerent parameters such as type
and size of needle, viscosity, ﬂow rate, volume and shear stress aﬀect the cleaning in root canals. A
qualitative method in form of interviews was carried out. Two dentists and one who studies to be a
dentist was interviewed in order to gain an insight into what is being done and used in practice, as well
what is done in practice is consistent with what the literature states.
An experimental setup was built on which experiments could be carried out. It was investigated what
eﬀect diﬀerent needle types and sizes and what eﬀect diﬀerent ﬂow rates had on the cleanliness in a
cavity.
From the results it can be concluded that, using the open-ended six-lumen needle at a ﬂow rate of 0.09
mL/s is the most eﬃcient combination to clean the cavity, resulting in a used volume of 0.129 mL. For
all four diﬀerent investigated ﬂow rates the open-ended six-lumen showed to be the most eﬃcient needle
with respect to clean the entire cavity. The open-ended ﬂat two-lumen proved to be the second most
eﬃcient needle for cleaning the cavity. With these two needles being the most and second most eﬃcient
needles for cleaning the cavity it can be concluded that multiple lumen needles with exits in diﬀerent
heights are more eﬀective than single lumen needles regarding eﬃcient cleaning of a cavity.
Looking at the bottom region right under the needle tip the open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle in com-
bination with a ﬂow rate of 0.15 mL/s proved to be the most eﬃcient combination, resulting in a used
volume of 0.068 mL. The open-ended ﬂat two-lumen needle also proves to outperform the other needles
at all other ﬂow rates. With this said it can be concluded that using a needle with two lumens in diﬀerent
heights are more eﬀective to clean the bottom of the cavity than needles with only one lumen.
An initially study of the state of the art showed that some parameters had a larger inﬂuence on the
cleaning eﬃciency and exchange of ﬂuid than others. The best combinations and concentrations for
irrigants are already well developed and therefore reasonably determined. The used volume depends on
which ﬂow rate is used. The value of shear stress and the area that is exposed for shear stress depends
on the needle type. Lastly, a larger needle can deliver a higher ﬂow rate. From this it can be concluded
that the needle size and type as well the ﬂow rate are the most important parameters when it comes to
clean and exchange the ﬂuid in the cavity, since the other parameters depend on those two.
It was observed that increasing the ﬂow rate decreased the time used before the cavity became clean.
However, the results showed that higher ﬂow rates also meant an increasing in volume of irrigant used
to clean the cavity. Regarding the top region of the cavity a ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s proved to be the
most eﬃcient ﬂow rate. Considering the bottom region either a ﬂow rate of 0.09 or 0.15 mL/s proved to
be the most eﬃcient ﬂow rate. With this said it can be concluded that to clean the cavity it is desirable

Finally, to answer the research questions of this Master’s thesis:
RQ1 How do diﬀerent parameters such as, type and size of needle, viscosity, ﬂow rate, volume and
shear stress, aﬀect the cleanliness in root canals?
RQ1 answer: Type and size of needle as well the ﬂow rate has the biggest inﬂuence on the cleanliness
and exchange of ﬂuid in root canals. The volume depends on the used ﬂow rate, whereas the shear
stress depends on the type of needle. Since the best combinations and concentrations for irrigants
are already well developed and therefore reasonably determined, the viscosity of the irrigant will
also already be determined.
RQ2 Which combined parameters results in a more eﬃcient cleaning of a root canal?
RQ2 answer: Using the open-ended six-lumen needle at a ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s proved to be the
most eﬃcient combination with respect to clean the entire cavity. Considering cleaning the entire
cavity a ﬂow rate of 0.09 mL/s proved to be the most eﬃcient ﬂow rate for all needles.
RQ3 How much volume of the irrigant is necessary to use before the root canal is considered eﬀectively
cleaned?
RQ3 answer: Regarding the bottom region of the cavity a volume between 0.068 to 0.5 mL was used
depending on the needle type and ﬂow rate. For the entire cavity a volume between 0.129 to 0.59
mL was used depending on the needle type and ﬂow rate. To clean the root canal eﬀectively less
than 0.6 mL is necessary to use.
Hypothesis Multiple lumen needles with exits in diﬀerent heights are more eﬀective than single lumen
needles regarding eﬃcient cleaning of a cavity.
Hypothesis answer: This can be conﬁrmed, as the open-ended six-lumen and open-ended ﬂat two-
lumen needles proved to be the most and second most eﬃcient needles for cleaning the cavity.
5.1
Future Aspects
Upon completion of this thesis, areas of potential improvement and further analysis have been recognized.
Listed below are some noteworthy suggestions along with brief explanations.
• Test needles that all displace the same volume, so a more correct comparison between their design
can be conducted. This includes that so-called obstacles/blockage must be avoided in the design
which is the case for the open-ended ﬂat one-blocked needle.
• A complete analysis of the movement of ﬂuid in the cavity. This would require a better microscope
and camera that could deliver the necessary quality of the recordings.
• Conduct experiments using diﬀerent types of cavities. This would give an insight into whether a
particular type of needle is more eﬀective for a speciﬁc type of cavity. This could include diﬀerent
sizes and curvatures.
