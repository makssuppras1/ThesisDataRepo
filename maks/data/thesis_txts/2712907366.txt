Abstract
This master’s thesis analyses the dynamics of a wind turbine with multiple rotors that are
operating at different rotor speeds. A methodology to obtain the modal parameters of a multi
rotor wind turbine with two isotropic rotors supported on a Y-structure is presented.
As
a baseline for the project, modal analyses are performed on a simplified two-rotor system.
The system equations of motion are derived using Euler-Lagrange equation.
The periodic
terms are removed by applying the Coleman transformation on each rotor separately yielding a
linear time-invariant system. A model of the two-rotor system is constructed in the aeroelastic
simulation tool HAWC2 and the response is analyzed and compared to the linear model. A
modal analysis was performed for synchronous and asynchronous rotors and revealed that the
rotor modes of a dual rotor turbine behave similarly to a single rotor turbine. Aeroelasticity
was included by combining HAWC2 and HAWCStab2, and the aeroelastic response has been
evaluated in the entire range of operation. The analysis showed that the dual rotor does not
experience any aeroelastic instabilities, but some of the modes are in potential risk of resonance.
Finally, this thesis provides the first insight into how the angle of the arms affects the
standstill modes of a dual rotor. The standstill frequencies were calculated using HAWC2 for
various angles. The results are visualized in a Campbell diagram and show how the different
modes are affected. The most affected modes were 1) the tower side-side mode which slightly
increased as the arms became more horizontal, and 2) the asymmetric arm motion, which
decreased significantly as it experienced a large contribution from the tower motion.
The study of a multi rotor wind turbine is still relatively new and this project only presented
preliminary research, further studies are needed in the field of multi rotor wind turbines.

Introduction
Upscaling wind turbines in numbers and capacity is necessary to meet the rising energy con-
sumption and demand for renewable energy sources. Traditionally upscaling wind turbines have
been accomplished by increasing the rotor size of the turbines, as the area swept by the rotor is
proportional to the power produced. The weight of the rotor scales with the cubic of the radius,
whereas the generated power only scales with the radius squared. This makes it increasingly
difficult to upscale wind turbines cost-efficiently [1]. Another set of issues arises during the
manufacturing, transportation and installation of larger components. Designing wind turbines
with multiple rotors on a single support structure is a possible solution to these challenges [1].
The concept of the multirotor is, to overcome the challenges related to the single rotor turbine
by reducing the length of the rotor blades, while still maintaining the power output. This is
accomplished by dividing the rotor area into n rotors with a reduced radius of
R
√n.
Figure 1 shows a single rotor turbine, with a power output equal to a dual rotor supported
on a Y-structure and a reduced rotor size of almost 30 [%]. The decrease in blade length could
reduce the risk of vortex induced vibrations [2]. A study conducted by Vestas Wind Systems
A/S on a quad-rotor turbine found, that the rotor interaction could potentially increase the
annual power production by 1.5 [%], adding to the potential benefits of interaction between the
rotors. Additionally, the wake recovers faster compared to a single rotor turbine, which could
reduce the space required for wind farms [3].
Figure 1: Reduction in rotor radius for equivalent power output
A disadvantage of the multirotor is the increased structural complexity.
An essential step
when designing a wind turbine is to perform a stability analysis, to ensure that resonance does
not occur, as it could significantly reduce the fatigue life of the turbine. Understanding the
natural frequencies, damping ratios and the mode shapes of the turbine is imperative to avoid
instabilities.
The modes provide valuable information on system behavior when excited at
natural frequencies. The damping ratio of a given mode describes how the oscillations increase
1
==PAGE:8==
if it is unstable or decrease if it is stable. Stability analysis of a single rotor turbine is a highly
researched topic [4] [5], however, only a limited number of papers regarding eigenvalue analysis
of a multirotor were found in the literature. A study [6] concluded, that a multirotor system
with isotropic rotors can be turned time-invariant by applying the Coleman transformation
on each rotor separately. The first investigation of the modal dynamics of a multirotor was
performed in [7], where a stability analysis was performed on a simplified dual rotor model
and compared with a high-fidelity model in HAWC2 [8]. This dissertation aims to expand the
existing research to include a dual rotor supported on a Y-structure with flexible arms.
Section 2 of this dissertation will establish, an analytic model of a dual rotor with 9 degrees
of freedom. The time-dependent equations of motion will be derived using the Euler-Lagrange
equations, and turned time-invariant by applying Coleman transformation on each rotor. With
the linear time-invariant system matrices, an eigenvalue analysis can be performed to obtain
the frequencies and mode shapes of the system. In section 3, a model of the dual rotor is built in
the aeroelastic code HAWC2. The model is used to calculate the system standstill frequencies
and as a base case for the analytic model. Section 4 contains a comparison of the standstill
natural frequencies of the two models and an investigation of changes to the mode shapes when
the rotors operate with synchronous and asynchronous rotor speeds. Section 6 presents changes
in the dynamics of the structure when the arms are positioned at different angles. Section 7
summarizes important findings and concludes the thesis.

The equations of motion for the system are obtained using the Euler-Lagrange equations.
The motion of the dual rotor is simplified to 9 degrees of freedom. One degree of freedom to

Figure 2: Multirotor wind turbine [9]
The flowchart in Figure 3, depicts the method for performing an eigenvalue analysis and evalu-
ating the stability of a dual rotor system. The second-order equations of motions are obtained
by evaluating the kinetic and potential energy of the system using Euler-Lagrange equations.
After applying linearisation and the small-angle assumption, the linear time-variant system
matrices are obtained. The system matrices are time-variant due to the periodic terms describ-
ing the azimuthal position of the blades, ψj,i(t), hence it is not possible to directly perform
3
==PAGE:10==
the eigenvalue analysis, as this would yield time-dependent eigenvalues. The time dependency
of the system matrices is removed by applying the Coleman transformation [10], which is a
method, that transforms the rotating coordinates from the rotating frame to the non-rotating
frame. The transformation yields a linear time-invariant system, from which the generalized
eigenvalue problem can be solved to obtain the natural frequencies and damping ratios, which
are used to identify the critical modes.
Figure 3: Flowchart for performing stability analysis of the rotating system
To investigate the dynamics of the dual rotor, an analytic model is established. The two three-
bladed isotropic rotors are supported on a Y-structure as shown in Figure 2. The motion of
each rotor is simplified into three degrees of freedom, which captures the motion of the blade
edgewise deflection denoted θj,i. The edgewise stiffness of the blades is modeled with a torsional
spring with stiffness kb = Mbl2
bω2
0, where ω0 is the natural edgewise frequency of the blade. The
arms are positioned with an angle −β and β, with respect to vertical, for the left and right
arm respectively. The motion of a single arm is simplified into one degree of freedom αj, which
corresponds to the in-plane deflection. The stiffness of the arms is modeled with a torsional
spring with the stiffness ka. The tower motion is represented with a single degree of freedom
to account for the side-side deflection denoted xt. The stiffness of the tower is modeled with a
lateral spring. The analytic model is shown in Figure 4.

2.4
Linear time-invariant matrix system
After obtaining the last governing equation, the equations are put into a matrix formation in
the following form.
[M]¨⃗q + [C] ˙⃗q + [K]⃗q = ⃗0
(2.59)
Where ⃗q is a vector containing the system degrees of freedom, Mq,q is the mass matrix, Cq,q is
the damping matrix, and Kq,q is the stiffness matrix respectively. The subscript (•)q,q indicates
the size of the system matrices. It is noted, that the equations of motion are time-varying
matrices as they depend on the azimuthal position of the blades ψj,i. Due to this time depen-
dency, it is not possible to directly perform an eigenvalue analysis to extract the eigenvalues
and eigenvectors. Various methods exist to remove the time dependency and express the equa-
tions of motion in a non-rotating frame. In the case of an isotropic rotor with three or more
equally spaced blades, one of the methods which can be used to remove the periodic terms
is the Coleman transformation also known as multi-blade coordinate transformation [14]. The
Coleman transformation is a linear transformation, that transforms the rotating coordinates
from the rotating frame to a new non-rotating coordinate system. A study [6] has shown, that
a linear time-variant multirotor system can be turned time-invariant by applying the Cole-
man transformation on each rotor, regardless of their rotational speed and thereby expanding
the applicability of the Coleman transformation. The new rotor coordinates, that relate the
rotating degrees of freedom θj,i to the non-rotating frame are given by.
θj,i = a0(t) + a1(t) cos(ψj,i) + b1(t) sin(ψj,i)
(2.60)

the modal components are assumed to be constant.

HAWC2 Model
A numerical model of the dual rotor is built in the time-domain aeroelastic tool HAWC2 [8].
The model of the 20 [MW] dual rotor wind turbine is inspired by the DTU 30-MW Tri-Rotor
Wind Turbine [16]. The tri-rotor is a fictitious horizontal axis turbine, where each rotor nacelle
assembly is based on the DTU 10 MW reference turbine [17]. The dual rotor wind turbine
consists of two identical 10 [MW] rotor nacelle assemblies on one support structure. For further
specifications of the rotors see [17]. The numerical model in Figure 6 is based on a multi-body
system [18], that enables the possibility to model nonlinear geometric effects. The non-linear
effects are accounted for by discretizing each main body of the turbine structure into a number
of sub-bodies, where the non-linear effects are allowed between, but not within sub-bodies. This
enables a simplification of the model and eliminates the need for a complex nonlinear model.
Each body is an assembly of linear Timoshenko beam elements, where the orientation of each
main body is defined relative to the previous body via Euler angle rotations.
(a) HAWC2 model - Front view
(b) HAWC2 model - Side view
Figure 6: As indicated by the lines the substructures are modeled based on a multi-body system
3.0.1
Tower
The conical tower is 130 [m] high and has a base diameter of 10.2 [m] varying linearly to the
top diameter of 6.74 [m]. The tower is divided into 5 sub-bodies and 27 sections with constant
properties in each section. The discretization of the tower is modeled using linear Timoshenko
beam elements, which have 6 DOFs in each node, 3 translations and 3 Euler-angle rotations.
The local coordinate system follows the global coordinate system.The tower is tuned to have
a 2 [%] logarithmic damping. The bottom of the tower is fixed to the ground in translation
and rotation. As the only tower motion considered in the analytic model is the tower side-side

Aeroelasticity
Aeroelasticity is a combination of inertia-, aerodynamic- and elastic forces with significant
interaction as shown in Figure 11.
Figure 11: Collar’s triangle defining aeroelasticity [21]
Evaluating the aeroelastic properties is essential to avoid instabilities such as stall induced vi-
brations for stall turbines and classical flutter for pitch regulated turbines [22]. Stall induced
vibrations happen when a mode is negatively damped [23], and flutter happens due to the
aeroelastic coupling between the flapwise modes and the first torsion mode [24]. Resonance is
another potential problem, which occurs when an external forcing frequency is close or equal
to the natural frequency of one of the structural modes.
The aeroelastic effects have been included in the model by combining the tools HAWC2 and
HAWCStab2. HAWCStab2 is a frequency domain aeroelastic tool, suited for stability anal-
ysis of three-bladed horizontal axis wind turbines [25]. The tool has been used to calculate
the edgewise aeroelastic frequency and damping for a single isolated blade. The results from
the aeroelastic simulation are combined with the structural matrices to perform an aeroelastic
stability analysis. The aeroelastic stiffness of the blade is calculated based on the simulation
performed in HAWCStab2.

Conclusion
Two models for analyzing the modal properties of a multirotor were established in this project.
The second-order equations of motion representing the analytic model were derived using Euler-
Lagrange equations. The periodic terms were removed by the Coleman transformation enabling
eigenvalue analysis. When comparing the standstill frequencies and mode shapes of the sim-
plified model to a model built in the aeroelastic simulation tool HAWC2, it was found, that
the mode shapes for the two models coincide fairly well. The exception to this was the asym-
metric vertical arm motion, which in the HAWC2 model was significantly affected by the tower
motion. Two structural Campbell diagrams were created for a range of rotor speeds between
0-10 [rpm]. In the first case, the two rotors operated at equal rotational speed Ω1 = Ω2 and in
the second case, they operated at asynchronous rotor speed where Ω2 = 2Ω1. Analyzing the
eigenvalues revealed, that the rotor modes of the dual rotor in both cases behaved similarly to
a single rotor turbine. The aeroelastic properties of the dual rotor were evaluated by utilizing
both HAWC2 and HAWCStab2. The stability properties of the system were assessed through
the real part of the eigenvalues. Evaluating the stability in the entire range of operation, from
cut-in wind speed of 4 [m/s] to cut-out wind speed of 24 [m/s], indicated the system is stable.
The dual rotor was evaluated for potential resonance issues. The visualization of eigenvalue
analysis in the aeroelastic Campbell diagram showed that the tower side-side mode was at
risk for resonance as the natural frequency is close to the 1P excitation frequency and the
mode is lowly damped.
Further, the frequency of the edgewise rotor modes were close to
the 6P excitation frequency, to determine whether this is problematic, a load simulation with
turbulent wind would have to be performed.
The focus of this dissertation was to provide a suitable method for performing a stability
analysis. The results indicated a redesign of the support structure is prudent to minimize the
risk of resonance with the 1P excitation frequency.
The analyses for the position of the arms at different angles showed, that two of the sub-
structure modes were affected by the changes to the angle. The tower side-side mode increased
slightly when the arms dropped from 45 degrees to 90 degrees. The asymmetric arm mode de-
creased significantly as it experienced a larger contribution from the tower motion. The rotor
modes and the symmetric arm motion were almost not affected by the change in angle.
These findings can be used in the design phase to shift the frequencies at risk of resonance.

Future studies
The research related to multirotor wind turbines is very limited compared to studies made in
the field of single rotor wind turbines. Further investigations are needed in the field of multi
rotor wind turbines to optimize the design. The following section contains thoughts and inspi-
ration for future work with the multirotor concept.
The analytic model presented in Section 2 yielded some insight into the dynamic system, how-
ever, it does not match with the model build in HAWC2. This indicates that some dynamic
interaction have not been accounted for. By including additional degrees of freedom in the
analytic model, it should be possible to capture the mode shapes from HAWC2. The mass and
stiffness parameters needed for the eigenvalue analysis should be extracted from the HAWC2
model.
The results presented in Section 5 indicated that the current design is at critical risk of reso-
nance due to the 1P excitation force and the tower side-side mode. The current design of the
dual rotor is based on the DTU 30MW tri-rotor turbine. Hence it should be possible to change
and optimize the construction for the dual rotor and in that process eliminate the risk of res-
onance. Likewise the current rotors used are designed for a single rotor constructions, making
further studies with the purpose of redesigning and thereby optimizing them for a multirotor
system relevant.
Section 6 presents the first insight into how the angle of the arm affects the standstill fre-
quencies of the system. Observations revealed that the angle had a significant influence on
the natural frequency of the tower mode and the modes which had a contribution from the
tower motion. Further studies are needed to understand how the modal behavior is affected.
Additionally the studies could be expanded to include research whether the damping values
are affected, and how the frequencies and potentially damping values change when the turbine
rotates. It could be an important factor to consider in the design phase of a multirotor turbine.
The analysis could be performed by running a forced time series in HAWC2. Visualizing the
results in a PSD plot, the frequency changes can be identified by the peaks.
