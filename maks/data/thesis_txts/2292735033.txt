Abstract 
In this project Cu exchanged zeolites will be examined using EPR and activity 
measurements. The purpose is to investigate the effects of K+ poisoning on Cu and V. 
Literature from numerous authors relevant to the subject of zeolites and SCR will be 
presented. The Cu zeolite was ion exchanged and afterwards, some of the Cu and V 
catalyst was incipient wetness impregnated with K+ to poison the catalysts. All the 
samples were measured using EPR under conditions relevant to the selective catalytic 
reduction (SCR) reaction. The results were examined and the distributions of EPR 
active species under different conditions were presented together with the species 
contributing to the spectra. These results were complimented with activity 
measurements of the catalysts, under both dry and wet SCR conditions. The 
difference and effects of the different conditions and poisoning will be presented and 
discussed throughout the report and in the conclusion and discussion.

1 Introduction 
The aim of this project is to examine some of the limits and possibilities for Cu 
exchanged zeolites as catalyst in selective catalytic reduction (SCR) used for 
abatement of nitrogen oxides (NOx) from flue gas. In the second chapter the 
background for the project will be presented, the numerous implications of NOx on 
the environment and how SCR is used as NOx abatement. The third chapter presents 
the observations and results of other authors on the subject of SCR and the behaviour 
of Cu in zeolites. In the fourth chapter the results of the investigation of Cu-ZSM5, to 
determine the behaviour under different conditions relevant for the SCR process, will 
be presented. Experiments have been carried out with both a fresh and a K+ poisoned 
catalyst, since K+ has been recognised as a significant deactivation agent. The fifth 
chapter will present how a mapping of the distribution of Cu in the zeolite framework 
and also a determination of the amount of Cu available to participate in the SCR 
reaction, were conducted. Afterwards, it was determined how much remained active 
after poisoning with K+. The experimental work has mainly been carried out with 
EPR and complimented with Matlab based simulations.  
Deactivation of the traditional V2O5 due to poisoning by K+ has inspired this 
investigation zeolites and their ability to retain K+ for the purpose of having the 
zeolite act as an SCR active guard bed. The two catalyst formulation were combined 
in a single bed reactor and investigated by EPR while monitoring the catalytic 
activity. Thus, the possibility for use of this combination of catalysts was investigated. 
The consequences of the production and poisoning methods were both investigated 
with EPR and activity measurements. The EPR measurements constitute the basis for 
the conclusions reached during this project. Due to prior experience with EPR and 
activity measurements1–4 the theory behind EPR will not be presented in this report.

2.1.1 NOx formation 
NOx originate from combustion of fossil fuels, for the purpose of this work. Three 
kinds of NOx can be distinguished in the flue gas: 
 
-­‐ 
Thermal NOx, formed by oxidation of atmospheric nitrogen. 
-­‐ 
Fuel NOx, formed from combustion of nitrogen containing fuel. 
-­‐ 
Prompt NOx, is formed when hydrocarbon remains are oxidized through an 
HCN intermediate. 
 
Thermal NOx is explained as the reaction of atmospheric nitrogen with atmospheric 
oxygen at elevated temperatures.8

2.1.3 NOx Removal techniques  
NOx emission can generally be approached from two angles, preventing the formation 
of NOx and, removing the NOx after it has been formed. 
Controlling the reaction conditions (e.g. engine tuning) can help prevent or reduce 
thermal NOx formation. As described earlier, there are generally three kinds of NOx 
formed in combustion reactions. All of these can be decreased through control of the 
reaction conditions. Having a lower combustion temperature decreases the formation 
of thermal NOx
8, having nitrogen free fuel prevents the formation of fuel NOx. This 
however increases the amount of VOC, which is also a pollutant10. In order to reduce 
CO2 emissions, bio-fuel has become a commonly used fuel in power plants. Bio fuels 
contain higher amounts of nitrogen, which leads to an increase in NOx emission. So, 
in some cases environmental considerations force the use of nitrogen containing fuels, 
making it impossible to avoid the formation NOx.  
When the formation of NOx cannot be prevented, the alternative solution is to remove 
it from the flue gas. Different technique exists and the most relevant of these will be 
discussed in the following section.

al14. The most commonly used catalyst formulation is V2O5 and WO3 on anatase. 
However, there are several other alternative formulations using other transition metals 
including Mn and Fe. Furthermore, metal exchanged zeolites such as, Fe exchanged 
ZSM-5 and Cu exchanged SSZ-13 can also be used as catalysts in different relevant 
applications.  
The Standard SCR reaction is given as: 
 
4NH! + 4NO + O! →4N! + 6H!O 
( 7 ) 
In the presence of NO2, the total reaction becomes: 
 
2NH! + NO + NO! →2N! + 3H!O 
( 8 ) 
 
This reaction is significantly faster - up to one order of magnitude - compared to the 
standard SCR reaction. When NO and NO2 are present in equal amounts, all NOx is 
reduced by this reaction, which is referred to as “fast SCR”15.  
There are of course a number of unwanted side reactions, such as the formation of 
N2O. N2O is a particularly unwanted molecule as it is a stronger green house gas16 
than both NO, NO2 and, which in turn is stronger than CO2. Therefore the selectivity 
towards N2 is immensely important. Future legislation is expected to have strict 
emission limits for N2O.

2.2.2 Tungsten(VI)oxide as a promoter 
Promoters are generally used to enhance specific properties of a catalyst. According 
to Lietti et al.21 there is a correlation between the amount of WO3 and the conversion 
of NO, without decreasing the selectivity towards N2, tested in samples ranging from

structure of the oxides of vanadium, titanium and wolfram on the surface. These 
interactions increase the reducibility of vanadium, compared to the catalyst consisting 
of only vanadium and titanium oxides. The correlation between the increased 
reducibility and an increase in activity supports the hypothesis presented by Topsoe et

2.2.3 Ionic Liquid in NOx abatement 
A lot of research is going into Ionic liquids (ILs) as co-catalyst and solvent for 
different reactions; NOx abatement is one of them. Ionic liquids are liquid salts often 
with similar physical characteristics, including; little to no vapour pressure, non-
polarity and high viscosity. Depending on the ion composition, ILs can have very 
different properties; miscibility with water and other solvents, Brønsted 
acidity/basicity. When ILs are used in NOx abatement, the NO is oxidized to HNO3 
by reaction with excess oxygen and water in the flue gas. The HNO3 is subsequently 
absorbed in the ionic liquid, but can be desorbed by heat treatment.

3 Zeolites 
There are a vast number of different zeolite groups, all with different silicium to 
aluminium ratios, coupled with different cations. Zeolite groups are defined by 
composition and structure. This project focuses on the most relevant zeolite in the 
SCR process, Cu-ZSM-5, and will in this section be compared to Cu-SSZ-13.  
 
3.1.1 History of zeolites 
The Swedish scientist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt discovered zeolites in 1756. The name 
is derived from the two greek words zeo, which means “to boil” and lithos, which 
means “stone”. The name refers to the large amounts of water that zeolites can 
contain at ambient conditions, which produces steam when heated.  
A zeolite is an aluminasilicate with a three-dimensional structure. Zeolites are built 
from SiO2 and AlO(OH), where Si+4 and Al+3 are tetrahedrally coordinated by O 
atoms. The structure is built up of tetrahedrals linked by oxygen bridges, forming 
interconnected cages and tunnels. These tunnels can harbour mobile water molecules 
and several different types of cations, including Cu and K+. The cations can be 
exchanged up to a degree of 50%34 of the aluminium in the framework.  Zeolites 
occur naturally but are also produced artificially on an industrial scale. They are used 
in a wide variety of different catalytic reactions other than NOx abatement. These 
include size selection catalysis reactions, stereo selective reactions35 and electrophilic 
substitution of aromatic ring-systems10.

3.1.2 Fundamental chemistry of zeolites 
Zeolites are often used in catalysis as supports for transition metals due to the high 
surface area and in some cases to provide size selectivity. When used as support for a 
transition metal catalyst, the zeolite can provide many different coordination 
environments for the transition metal and the framework36. 
 
3.1.3 Zeolite composition 
Zeolites consist primarily of silicium tetrahedrally coordinated to four oxygen as 
SiO4. SiO4 in the framework is neutral because they share the excessive negative 
charges on O-2 with an adjacent SiO4, thus, the total ratio of oxygen to silicium 
becomes two, corresponding to SiO2. Since the tetrahedrally coordinated Si atoms are 
neutral, the entire structure will naturally be neutral as well. This neutral charge state 
changes, however, when Al+3 is added to the framework. Due to the +3 oxidation state 
of Al, the sharing of oxygen atoms is inadequate to remain neutral. The ratio of 
aluminium to silicium and can be determined by several methods, such as ICP-
OES/MS. For each aluminium T-atom, the framework will have a negative charge. 
The negative charge allows for addition of cations. These cations are typically 
alkaline, but other cations such as transition metals and rare earth metals can also be 
added. Thus, the basis for ion exchanged zeolite supported catalysis is formed.36

3.2.1 Periodic Building Units

framework, including the unit cell. 
A PerBU consists of a number of T-atoms arranged in a framework specific pattern. A 
T-atom is a tetrahedral molecule based around a silicate or aluminate in the form of 
SiO4 or AlO4. The framework is based on T-atoms, connected to each other through

3.4 Metal Exchanged zeolites 
Natural zeolites are known for their significant water, alkaline- and alkaline earth- 
metal content. These atoms and molecules are located in various sites throughout the 
zeolite. Alberti et al40 has examined natural chabazites  (CHA framework) as well as 
chabazites exchanged with Na+, K+, Ca2+ and Sr2+. The work describes some of the 
different sites and whether or not they are likely to be occupied by water or a cation. 
The chabazites were examined by using crystallographic methods such as x-ray 
diffraction. A discrepancy between the diffraction accessed number of cations and the 
chemically accessed number of cations was found. The Na exchanged chabazite had 
four sites for Na, and the rest were occupied by water. For the K-exchanged 
chabazites, it was not possible to determine whether water or K+ occupied one of the 
sites, thus K+ might be capable of occupying different sites than sodium. The Ca+2 and 
Sr+2 exchanged zeolites had some similarities, however, the occupancy by cations in 
the sites was different. Furthermore, one of the occupied sites was not observed in the 
Sr-exchanged zeolite.  
 
3.5 Redox properties of ions exchanged into zeolites 
The ability to change between two oxidation states is an important characteristic for 
ions exchanged into zeolites. The SCR reaction and the catalytic cycle depend on 
optimal conditions for the redox of the Cu ions exchanged into the zeolites 
framework. Bulanek et al.41 has examined the redox capabilities of Cu in zeolites with 
the frameworks; MFI, FER and ERI, MFI is the only one without a 6-membered ring: 
6mr. The redox properties were examined with regards to Propane, CO, H2 and 
temperature dependency. They were examined using IR at different ion exchange 
loadings. The author concluded that Cu’s coordination in the zeolite have a significant 
influence on the redox properties of the Cu ion. Some of the major influences on this 
is of course the coordination environment  and, more importantly, the total 
delocalized negative charge of the framework. The charge of the framework is 
determined by the distribution of Al atoms in the lattice and the effect of these on the 
lattice oxygens. The author arguments that the zeolites examined all promoted Cu+.

3.2 Hypotheses 
The hypotheses that underlay this work is based on the results of the literature study 
presented above. The suggestions presented have also been based on discussion 
within the CSC group at the Technical University of Denmark.  
 
3.3 Cu in the d6r in FAU framework 
• The correct additives can manipulate the Cu site preferences. 
Additives in the form of alkaline or transition metals will compete for the sites in the 
zeolite framework. There will to some extend be preferences between sites and 
different cations, and using these preferences it will be possible to make the Cu2+ 
coordinate in the d6r. This is achieved by having all other possible Cu2+ sites 
occupied by additive. Some of the additives considered are; Na+, K+, Cs+ and Zn2+. 
These elements have been evaluated to be more attractive because of the nature of 
their electron configuration, oxidation state and size. It is the sites II and II*, see 
Figure 13. Because site II and II* were preferred by Cu+, thus, it is likely that other 
metals with oxidation state +1 or a full outer shell would be attracted to this site.  This 
will hopefully force the Cu2+ ions into the site I*, where it is possible to identify it 
using EPR. The I* site is located inside the zeolite structure, so the Cu2+ is isolated 
from the environment. By doing this it should be possible to connect a specific 
coordination with a specific EPR spectrum, and, following up with SCR activity 
measurements, confirm or disprove this coordination site as being catalytically active.  
 
• Cu-Y Zeolite activity in SCR reaction is diffusion limited due to the placement 
of the d6r. 
In case of successfully locating the Cu2+ in the I* site. The activity studies of the Cu-
zeolite should prove to be diffusion limited because of the framework location of the 
d6r. This could prove to be difficult; because of the high mobility NH3 gives the Cu 
ions and the heat at which the SCR reaction takes place, the Cu ion might leave the 
d6r in favour of more exposed sites. Hopefully this will be prevented because of the 
additives that isolated the Cu ions in the first place.    
• Additives will not interfere with the d6r in Y-zeolite, unless they have a 
competing preference.  
==PAGE:37==
	  
 
 
25 
Because of the different electron configurations and sizes of the additives, they will 
hopefully not compete for the position in the d6r. They have all been chosen for their 
similarities with Cu+1, either for the oxidation state or the full outer shell, based on the 
assumption that this will make them prefer coordination too site II and II* over site 
I*. 
 
3.4 K+ migration from Cu-Zeolite to V2O5 supported by Anatase 
• The zeolite framework is capable of binding K+ and preventing them from 
migrating to the V2O5 catalyst under operando conditions.  
The basis for this hypothesis is the strong sites for cation coordination located in the 
zeolite framework. K+ is a known inhibitor for the SCR reaction when it coordinates 
on the surface of the V2O5 supported anatase. If the zeolite is capable of binding K+ 
strong enough so that it does not migrate through the zeolite onto the anatase, then 
there is merit for having a guard bed of zeolites to adsorb the K+. To examine this, a 
K+ poisoned sample of Cu-zeolite will be placed upstream from a V supported anatase 
and the SCR activity and EPR spectrum, will be measured as a function of time and 
temperature. It has been suggested by Putluru et al.61 that Cu exchanged zeolites lose 
less activity when exposed to K+ poisoning. It has however been suggested that other 
alkaline elements increases the reactivity of Cu-ZSM559.

4.1 Catalyst preparation methods 
Zeolites show little to no SCR activity. However, metal ions can be exchanged into 
the structure, thus enhancing the catalytic activity significantly. The processes, 
method and purpose of exchanging the natural ions in the framework with metal ions 
of a specific preference will be described in this chapter.  
The procedure used to synthesize the Cu exchanged ZSM-5 will be described here 
together with the reactants and the methods.  
 
Zeolite material: NH3-ZSM-5 
Ion exchange compound: Cu(NO3)2 
Solvent: Water 
 
5g of ZSM-5 was mixed with a 0.1M Cu(NO3)2 in vast excess of water. It was then 
left over night, approximately 24 hours, under stirring. After the ion exchange the 
zeolite was filtered and then washed in up to 1 liter of water, or until the filter water 
had no trace of blue. The zeolite was then dried on the filter paper, collected and 
fractioned in the range 150µμm to 300µμm. The procedure was inspired by the work of 
Kustova et al40,41, according to whom the procedure should result in 1%wt and 
1.58wt% Cu.  
 
After the initial ion exchange, before the fractioning, half the sample was saved, and 
the rest was put through the procedure again to increase the Cu loading. 
After the ion exchange 1g from each batch, including the 5wt% V2O5 reference 
catalyst, was impregnated with potassium, in the form of KNO3, using incipient 
wetness impregnation (IWI). The amount of potassium used for the IWI process was 
determined based on the reference catalysts. The aim was to have a 1:1 K+:V ratio.

4.6 Oxidation State Distribution 
When attempting to determine the effects experienced by a catalyst under different 
conditions, being able to determine the amount of the catalytically working metal 
atoms is of great interest. For the purpose of quantifying the amount of EPR active 
species in a defined sample, two reference curves have been produced. One reference 
curve was produced on the basis of VOSO4 where known amounts of EPR active V+4 
form the basis for the curve. The other curve was created on the basis of Cu(NO3)2. In 
both cases the salts were diluted with K2SO4 or K2NO3.  
 
From these curves the mass percentage of Cu+2 and V+4 has been determined in the 
samples. This number has been used to define the total amount of available metal 
atoms.

5 Discussion and conclusion 
One aim of this project was to investigate the Cu-ZSM5 zeolite and map the 
distribution and possible Cu coordination environments inside the zeolite. 
Furthermore Cu-ZSM5 was investigated as a SCR active guard bed to prevent 
poisoning by K+ for V2O5, which is reported to be much more sensitive to K+. This 
was done as part of the general research into NOx abatement through the SCR 
reaction using catalysts of different types. In this project the focus was placed on Cu 
exchanged ZSM5 and WO3 promoted V2O5 supported on anatase. The ion exchanged 
Cu-ZSM5 zeolite prepared for this project and investigated using in-situ and operando 
EPR and independent measurements of the SCR activity. The EPR investigations 
made it possible to map the distribution between Cu+ and Cu+2 as well as between V4+ 
and V5+ under SCR conditions, reducing and oxidising SCR conditions. This provided 
us with information about the amount of redox active metals available to the SCR 
reaction, and an indication as too where the rate determining step may lie. Based on 
the results presented in the chapter “Distribution of Cu+1 and Cu+2 under SCR 
conditions”, i suggest that the majority of Cu is in oxidation state +1 under SCR 
conditions. This definitely corroborates the suggestion that the rate-determining step 
might start from Cu+1. This conclusion is reasonable because of the SCR activity, 
which was measured simultaneously, the results can be seen in Table 7.. The 
distribution between ... after treatment with NO was examined to provide more 
information about step 2 as seen in Figure 4. The distribution after treatment with NO 
of Cu+1 and Cu+2 gives us information about the balance between species B and 
species C, to species A. Cu+1 is most likely species A, while Cu+2 is either coordinated 
as species B or C. The results of the distribution indicates a strong likelyhood Cu+2 to 
stay as species C or B, but around 20% of the Cu get reduced back to species A.  
 
When observing the effects of K+ poisoning on these distributions both appear to have 
higher degrees of Cu+2 present. This could indicate that the poisoning stabilises a Cu+2 
state. This can be deduced because of the way the distribution is determined, any 
species that is not reducible after treatment with NH3 + NO, will be determined as 
inaccessible. The activity measurements show that a high amount of SCR activity is 
retained in the Cu-ZSM5, compared to the vanadium catalyst, where all activity is

completely lost. The ratio was based on a mmol/g calculation whereas for the 
vadadium catalyst it was based on a molar ratio (1:1 K+:V). This means that if the Cu 
content is larger than the V content, the poisoning ratio will be smaller and this may 
be the reason for some of the retained activity. Another possibility being that the 
amount of Cu catalytic sites per gram of catalyst might be higher than that of

remain unaffected. Another possibility is that Cu ZSM-5 are more resistant to 
poisoning than vanadium, this claim is supported by Fehrmann et al.61, and might be 
an explanation as to why they do not suffer the same decrease in conversion as the 
poisoned vanadium does. 
 
The Cu species found are largely confirmed by the literature; Cu-A, Cu-C and Cu-D 
have all been reported before. Cu-B was not found in the literature and Cu-E is not 
well enough defined to be reported as a stand-alone species. There is a general

catalyst, because if this was possible further testing should be adjusted. The test was 
based around the measurements of the differences between the fresh and poisoned Cu

6 Future Experiments 
To further investigate the Cu-ZSM5 zeolites some changes can be made on the setup 
used in this project to gain further information about the relevant processes.  
For instance if the catalyst was examined in-situ the rate of oxidation might be 
observed as the intensity of the spectrum increases. This trend was observed by 
Isaksen71 in his experiments, conducted parallel to this project, with low Cu wt% in 
the CHA zeolite framework. If a similar study, different Cu wt%, was conducted on a 
wider range of zeolite framework types, MFI or FAU, and under both oxidizing and 
reducing conditions; this information might lead to a more thorough understanding of 
how Cu interacts in zeolites.  
 
It might also be interesting to investigate this and other zeolites under similar 
conditions with IR or another Cu-NO sensitive method, to investigate if it is possible 
to determine the amount of Cu available to the gas stream and if K+ poisoning 
changes this distribution. IR should be capable of measuring Cu-NO interactions, 
either the Cu-N bond or the change in the N-O stretch should be detectable. With this 
information a more detailed mapping of the Cu distribution in the zeolite would be 
possible.  
 
A combination of the above mentioned methods tested on a broader range of zeolite 
types might be able to provide more details as to where Cu prefers to locate itself. The 
Y-zeolite also has a d6r for the Cu to coordinate in, which are not available to the gas 
stream. Similar sites are present in the CHA framework, but here these sites are 
available to the gas stream. A combined study of these two frameworks, if the 
methods here proposed are capable of providing the information wanted, might allow 
for a specific description for the conditions under which Cu coordinates to different 
sites in the framework.
