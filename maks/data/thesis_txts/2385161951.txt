1. ABSTRACT
Optimal Design of Nesting forces - 2017
1
Abstract
This report give details on how to make an optimization script that can be used on Exact Constrained
Designs (ECD). The focus is on designs made with uni-directional constraints. These constraints need a
nesting force/preload in order to constrain an object.
The optimization routine, assumes rigid bodies and no friction, and optimizes with regards to the place-
ment of constraints and nesting forces. It is shown that constraint placement are of primary concern,
and the placement of the nesting forces is secondary.
High accuracy and repeatability are of interest when assemblies are made with uni-directional constraints.
The accuracy of an assembly is often found by tolerance vector loops. It is found that an assembly’s sen-
sitivity is related to the sensitivity of the reaction force in the assembly. The sensitivity can be quantiﬁed
with a single scalar value: The determinant of a system matrix used when calculating force equilibrium.
This scalar does not make up a full analysis, but is easily calculated and can be used to compare concepts
in early stage product development.
Friction and high stresses in contact points are the biggest issues when using uni-directional constraints,
guidelines and suggestions on how to avoid the inﬂuence of these are given.

2. INTRODUCTION

2
Introduction
2.1
The problem
Exact constraint design is a robust design method for assemblies. Meaning that it removes sensitivty in
a design without removing the causes [6]. In this method it is done by using only the exact number of
constraints needed to lock the intend degrees of freedom. By doing this there are no ambiguities about
the load paths in the design. There are also no internal stress in the parts caused by constraints trying
to lock the same degree of freedom.
A speciﬁc area within exact constraint design is when only unidirectional constraints are used, this will
in the ideal case be point contacts. Assuming no friction a contact point constraints 1 degree of freedom.
Because the constraints are unidirectional there is a need for force pressing the body up against the
constraints. This force is called nesting force or preload.
Assemblies made this way have high accuracy and repeatability but are load sensitive.
The correct
placement of nesting force and constraints, will make them less sensitive to loads.
2.2
The current state
Regarding unidirectional exact constraint design D. Blanding [1] has made a graphical method which
gives a nesting window. Placing a force in this window insures that no tension occurs in any of the
contact points.
The method however tells nothing about how well placed the nesting force is. It also has the limitation
of only working in 2D. Gravity is also not taken into account. This means that the method only makes
sense to use if the weight of the object is low compared to the other forces acting on the body, or simply
that the 2D plane in which the method is used is perpendicular to gravity.
The placement of the nesting forces is only a little part, of holding a body with unidirectional constraints.
Blanding provides a lot of information on how to place the constraints. In Table 1 Blanding’s rules for
how to avoid over constraints using unidirectional constraints is shown. Those rules are build on what
Blanding is calling Constraint pattern analysis.
Whitney [27] has used screw theory to determined if an assembly is exactly constraint (statically de-
termined) or not. The method also tells in which dimension a design is over constrained. If the design
has some degrees of freedom it can also tell the direction of this freedom. The method can be seen as a
more precise and also more complicated version of the Kutzbach equation which gives the mobility of a
mechanism under certain assumptions.
What Whitney’s method is lacking is way of determining how close to being over/under constraint an
assembly is. The method works like an on/oﬀswhich meaning it can not be used for ﬁnding the optimal
placement of the constraints. This is illustrated in Figure 1.
A plate is ﬁxed to a base by two joints. 1 pinhole joint that takes up two translational degrees of freedom
and one Pin slot joint in combination with the pin hole joint constains the rotational degree of freedom. In
situation A) the slot has the angle 0◦and the system is correctly constraint in B) the slot has the angle 90◦
and the pin slot joint does not constraint the rotational degree of freedom. The system is in other words
statically indeterminate. In Figure 1b The result of an analysis with Whitney’s method is shown. The
slot angle is varied with from 0◦to 180◦. The analysis is numerical and 1001 calculations was done. It is
seen that only at exactly 90◦the result is defriend from the other calculations. The reason for using 1001
calculations was that the statically indeterminate point was not calculated if only 1000 points was used.
The example was adopted from [27] where it can also be read how to make the script used for the analysis.
K.W. Chase has made a Tollerencing method called Direct Linearisation Method or for short DLM
[17]. The method involves making a number of vector loops though an assembly. These vector loops
include depend- as well as independent variables , the method is a little complicated, but the result is
sensitivity matrix that gives information about how much variation in the assemblies joints inﬂuences
placement of the objects in the assembly. The DLM method will be explained more in detail in section 8.
The method is not a exact constraint design method, but A.M. Hammond [8] part of it as a quantiﬁable

3
Deﬁnition of Nesting force
In this section the basics of Exact constraint design will be gone through, including the deﬁnition of a
nesting force and how the nesting force window is done in 2D.
This section is heavily inspired by D.L Blanding [1] and J.G Skakoon [2] books.
3.1
Exactly constraint design
A body in free space has 6 Degrees of Freedom (DOF’s), 3 translational x,y,z and 3 rotational θx,θy,θz
In order to ﬁx the body 6 constraint is needed.
1 constraint for each degree of freedom.
If a 3D
object DOF’s is ﬁxed with 6 constraint it is said to be exact constraint meaning that object is statically
determined and the reaction force can be calculated because there is no doubt about where the force is
being absorbed
For 2D it’s a little simpler and only 3 Degrees of freedom needs to be constraint x,y,θz Exactly constraint
design has the following advantages [3]:
• No binding
• No Play
• Repeatable positioning, despite loose-toleranced parts
• No internal stress (from Assembly)
• Easy assembly
• Robustness to wear and Environment
3.2
Assumption
When making exactly constraint design with unidirectional constraints the following assumptions are
made.
• Point contact exist
– a point has by deﬁnition no size, so adding a force to it leads to an inﬁnite high stress. It is
however a ﬁne assumption as long as contact area remains small relative to the object size. In
subsection 9.1 contact points are investigated.
• Objects and constraint are stiﬀ
– How valid the assumption is depends upon size,material and load.
• No friction in the contact points.
– This depends on the materials and surface roughness in the contact points size of the friction
coeﬃcient in section 9 this is investigated further.

because the constrains can be placed such that they lock the same DOF. In Table 1 some rules are given
describing when constraints lock the same DOF. The rules are made by D. Blanding [1]

3.4
Nesting force
In Figure 2 3 situations are shown:
A) A single point contact is constraining a square object in the y- direction. The point contact however
only constrain in the negative y-direction. Because of this a force is needed to press the object
against the point contact. This force is called the Nesting force. Parts of the nesting force acts
along the constraint line. The constraint line is normal to the object surface and goes though the
contact point.
Because the nesting force acts along the constraint line, it can not contribute to any moment around
point’s on this line, and therefore can not constrain rotation around any point on the constraint
line. In other words. The object is free to rotate around any point along the constraint line, but
non other.
B) A second point contact is used to constraint the object in the x-direction. The point where the two
constraint lines intersect is called the Virtual pivot or Instant center of rotation [1]. This is the only
point from which the object can rotate. This is not a physical point on the object, but deﬁned by
the constraint lines only. Meaning that if the object is rotated the virtual pivot placement on the
object will change.
In the ﬁgure the square is rotated, illustrated with light grey colors. The red path shows the point
on the object which was ﬁrst in the VP before the square got rotated and all it’s points though the
rotation.
C) By adding another contact point two Virtual pivots are made. If the two VP’s are not acting in
the same point the rotation around the Z axis is also constraint, and the body can not move in 2D.
For the square to be properly nested the object shall be in compression at all the contact points. D.
Blanding has constructed a 2D methood where a nesting window is made visual. Placing a nesting force
in this window insure that no tension occurs at the contact points. The method will be gone though in
the next section.

4. OPTIMAL PLACEMENT OF 2D NESTING FORCE

A force is deﬁned by it size, direction and attack point [4]. In order to evaluate how good the nesting
force is the size is irrelevant. The size of the reaction force will be given as a percentage of the nesting
force. Diﬀerent sizes of the nesting force will not change this percentage therefore the size is irrelevant.
The placement and direction will be changed while the size of the force vector will be unity.
Because the nesting force is used in exactly constraint designs, the reaction forces in the contact points
can always be calculated.
One way of ﬁnding the optimal placement of the nesting force in 2D will then be to place a lot of points

that the minimum reaction forces is highest.

Relation between assembly placement and reaction forces.
In order to ﬁnd how sensitivity the placement of the triangle is to the placement of the constraints,
the DLM method [17] is used to ﬁnd the worst case placement of the 3 corners of the triangle , when
constraint number 3 is moved along the triangle side.
DLM stands for Direct linearisation method, it is developed by K.W Chase. As the name indicates, the
method is only an approximation because an linearisation is done to a set of non linear vector loops. The
method will be explained, while showing on the triangle example.
The ﬁrst step is to make a number of vector loops though the assembly. These vector loops are used to ﬁnd
the depend variables, also called the assembly variables. The depend variables needs to be found in order
to ﬁnd out how the object behaves do to change in the independ variable, also called the manufactured
variables. These variables are for instanse the placement of the contact points. The needed number of
closed vector loops can be calculated from the following equation [17]
Lo = J −Pt + 1
(27)
where Lo is the number of closed vector loops, J is the number of joints (in this example the point
contact) and Pt is the number of parts. In this example there is a need for two closed loops, because
there is 3 joints and two bodies in the assembly. The second body is not shown.
Some rules needs to be followed when making the loops [7].
• When going from one part to another, this must be done though a joint
• Follow the independent or dependent dimensions to a Coordinate System CS
• Follow the independent or dependent dimensions leading to another joint
• Exit to the next adjacent part in the assembly
• No loop can pass through the same part or same joint twice.
• Loops must pass through each part and each joint in the assembly, but a single loop does not
necessarily pass through every part or every joint.

10. DISCUSSION

10
Discussion
In this project two scripts have been made:
1) one very simple, ﬁnding only the optimal placement of a single nesting force, when constraints are set.
This script uses loops to calculated the reaction forces, when moving the nesting force along a speciﬁc
path, and varying its angle. A very simple objective was made, specifying that the optimal placement of
the nesting force is where the lowest reaction force is the highest. This objective should only be used,
when constraints placement are set and reasonably chosen. This is because poor placement of the con-
straints gives high reaction forces, which is not a sign of robustness.
The script makes a contour plot, and prints the reaction forces in the optimum placement. The user can
use this information to evaluate the constraint placement. The distance and angles in the contour plot
gives the sensitivity of the assembly, if it is high it is a sign of bad constraint placement. The same is
true if large diﬀerence are seen in the reaction forces.
The script works for 2D and 3D , but computation time increase from a few seconds to a few minutes,
when going from 2D to 3D. The amount of time used on the analyis is mostly caused by the mathematical
description of the assemblies surfaces, in 2D it can be done relatively quick for simple geometries, In 3D
it’s time consuming.
In 2D the contour plot gives a good visualization, witch can be seen as an extension of Blanding’s method.
In 3D it is hard to interpretate.
In order not to rely the sensitivity analyses on visual feedback and to optimize on the constraint place-
ment a second script was made.
2). The second script is an extension to the ﬁrst script, but where one of Matlab’s optimization function
was used in order to reduce the computation time.
Making it possible to optimize on both the the
placement of the constraints and several nesting forces.
The objective functions for this script therefore became both the reaction forces and a sensitivity function
capable of taking into account manufacturing tolerances.
The Matlab function can have problems with ﬁnding ﬁnding global optimum, if local exists, this can be
handled, with randomising the staring points for the optimization and looking at the results. This is not
the best solution to the problem. It would have been better to ﬁnd a better optimisation function, this
was however out of the scope for this project. A user of the script would still have to describe the surfaces
of a the body, to the script. Now in more detail than in the ﬁrst script. The script also has some bugs,
meaning that the user has to have some experience, in using it.
As it is now the time spend on using the script compared to what is gained from designing with intuition
and guidelines is very little. Meaning that it only makes sense to use if optimal design is really need.
The used time in the analyse, could however be signiﬁcantly reduced if the script was combined with a
CAD software, where the body surfaces are already designed. The script does also not take into account
friction, which has a big inﬂuence on the optimal design. The design of an assembly would therefore not
be complete if this are not taking into account.
One way of incorporating friction into the script could be to combine it with a FEM program. This would
however make the use even more complicated. Not thinking this was the right way to go, it was looked
more into, how to design the assembly so that it is insensitive to friction.

1) It was found that there was a big relation between an assemblies accuracy and the reaction forces
in it’s the contact points. It turned out that determinant of the system matrix [A] used in the force
equilibrium equation has the same absolute values as the determinant of the Jacobian matrix of depend
variables, used in the DLM method.
This is interesting because the system matrix is easily found, more so than the Jacobian matrix. Getting
a low value of det([A]−1) tells that the constraint arrangement in an assembly is good. Very simpliﬁed
det([A]−1) is an assemblies sensitivity analysis summarized into a single scalar, meaning of cause that it
does not tell the full picture. As state earlier the constraint arrangement is of primary concern.
Thinking on force analysis the second step to assign a good placement for the nesting force.
Thinking on accuracy analysis the second step is to assign tolerances to the components.
Meaning that a low value of det([A]−1) is not an end result, but a value that can be used to evaluate
assemblies in early stage product development. One can not specify when a low value of det([A]−1) is low
enough. This is because the constraint placement are in [A] meaning that only assemblies of same size
can be compared to each other. det([A]−1) is therefore a relative measure between concept for the same
purpose. In late stages product development, where designers need to know in speciﬁcs the accuracy of
an assembly the DLM or other tolerances method still needs to be used.
2) Idea with/in was used to look into how a designer can make a design insensitive to friction and
high contact stress, by use of cascading and ﬂexible elements. The author of this report, does not con-
tribute to anything in this area, but collected what other has done, and looked at what the strength and
weaknesses of these design ideas. This gives an overview that is helpful in the beginning of a design phase.
3) One of the biggest limitations when making exact constraint designs with uni-directional constraints,
is high stress in the contact points. Having little experience with point contact designs, one asks: how
big force can a point contact carry ?.
Hertz contact theory is the basis of contact mechanics, one of it’s biggest strengths is that an equivalent-
radius’s R∗and young modulus E∗can be calculated.
Making it possible to compare diﬀerent con-
ﬁgurations to each other with regards to stress. A maximum pressure can be found via hertz theory,
this pressure can however not be directly compared to the yield strength of a material, because it is in
compression.
Slocum has made a experiment where he investigates the repeatability of an kinematic clamp in a situation
with high contact stresses. Enough information about the kinematic clamp, was given such that the
maximum contact pressure could be found. Deciding that the experiment was a good representative for
loading in contact points used in ECD, it was decided to use this pressure as the maximum allowed. This
made it possible to make a graph giving the maximum reaction force in a contact point as a function of
a it’s radius.
Some of the assumption made in hertz theory was violated, meaning that the graph should only be seen
as "in the order of" graph. This is however ﬁne for getting a quick estimate, of weather a design can be
made with single contact points.
