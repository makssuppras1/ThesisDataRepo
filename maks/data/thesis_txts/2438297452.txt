Abstract
A promising alternative to commonly used HFC based refrigerants is CO2, because
of its low global warming potential. The coefficient of performance of CO2 cycles can
be improved by the implementation of an ejector in order to reduce the throttling
losses occurring in conventional expansion valves. The design of this component poses
a challenge because of the supersonic, phase transitioning flow. In the conventional
numerical modelling approach to analyze the flow with computational fluid dynamics,
the temperature based energy equation is replaced by a formulation with enthalpy
as the independent variable. In addition to this, different phase transition models
take metastable effects into account instead of assuming thermodynamic equilibrium.
Several simulations show that the accuracy of this method is limited compared to
experimental measurements.
In the present thesis a new modelling approach is introduced where the fluid properties
are implemented with a temperature and pressure glide in the two phase region. The
advantage of this implementation is that the temperature based energy equation can
be used without any modification.
Different simulations of single phase and two
phase flows with the new approach are successfully carried out. Nevertheless, the
new modelling technique requires further development to be competitive with the
conventional methodology in terms of stability and computational time.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
CO2 (R744) is a promising working fluid in refrigerant cycles for future applications
because of its low global warming potential (GWP) compared to commonly used HFC
based refrigerants. As an additional advantage, CO2 is nontoxic and non-flammable.
Nevertheless, CO2 based systems usually have a low coefficient of performance (COP)
because of high throttling losses. A possible solution to reduce these losses is the
implementation of a two phase ejector into the cycle for expansion work recovery.
An ejector uses the energy of a high pressure motive fluid to raise the pressure of
a secondary low pressure fluid. This reduces the required compression work from
the primary compressor. However, a properly designed ejector is essential for COP
improvements. Multiple attempts made by the scientific and refrigeration community
have enhanced the understanding of CO2 ejectors significantly. Although different
studies containing 0D, 1D, 2D and 3-D modeling and experimental investigations to
analyze the flow inside the CO2 ejector, predicting the performance and designing a
proper geometry of the ejector is still subject to uncertainties. The main challenge in
modeling the flow is the two phase flow with phase transition. Different computational
models of the two phase flow, the phase transition and the turbulence have been
applied to the simulations in order to achieve the best accuracy with the experimental
results.
Some of these models have also been implemented into commercial and
open source computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software. Nevertheless, numerical
simulations still show insufficient agreement with experimental results. Furthermore,
the models have a limited area of application considering operation parameter. The
objective of this master thesis is to compare different state of the art two-dimensional
numerical modeling methods and determine their accuracy compared to experimental
results. Improving the models in order to achieve a higher accuracy and a wider
application range is the long term goal of the research in this field.
1.2
Previous studies
Different studies to increase the understanding of the CO2 ejector were carried out in
the past. Numerous studies focus on ejectors in general, but only few are devoted to
CO2 as a working fluid. (Elbel and Lawrence 2016) and (Banasiak et al. 2016) give
a good overview over state of the art developments in CO2 ejector technology. Both

1.2.1
Experimental studies
Several studies focus on the experimental investigation of CO2 ejector systems (Ba-
nasiak et al. 2015, Elbel and Hrnjak 2008, Nakagawa et al. 2009, Lucas et al. 2013
and Kærn et al. 2018).
(Lucas et al. 2013) established a correlation for the ejec-
tor efficiency based on experimental data. However, this correlation depends on the
ejector geometry and can not be used as a general design tool. Experimental investi-
gations are often limited because of the high pressures (up to 150 bar) and the small
dimensions (Nozzle diameter in the order of 1mm) in the system. Therefore, flow
visualization and flow velocity determination by laser Doppler anemometry has not
been conducted for CO2 ejectors so far. However, (Marynowski, Desevaux, and Mer-
cadier 2009) provides an example where the condensation is visualized for a moist air
ejector.
1.2.2
Zero-dimensional models
The simplest approach to predict the performance of two phase ejectors with a theoret-
ical approach is made with zero-dimensional models, where the ejector is divided into
sections (motive nozzle, suction nozzle, mixing section and diffuser). For each section
the thermodynamic state at the inlet and the outlet is determined. Zero-dimensional
models usually assume thermodynamic and mechanical equilibrium at all points. The
==PAGE:19==
1.2 Previous studies

first attempt by (Kornhauser 1990) was extended by (Li and Groll 2005). A further
extension is presented by (Zheng et al. 2015) and (Zheng et al. 2016), where a dy-
namic model is implemented to account for changes between operating points. This
model is validated with experimental data in (Zheng and Deng 2017). The major
problem in this type of description is to assign a proper efficiency to all the compo-
nents. A model to predict the components efficiencies was introduced by (Liu and
Groll 2013). Furthermore, zero-dimensional models fail to predict the influence of
geometric features such as angles and lengths. In addition to this the experimental
validation of those models is rather difficult.
As mentioned above, zero-dimensional models are limited in predicting the perfor-
mance dependening on geometric features. An enhancement of the zero-dimensional
models are one-dimensional models and CFD models in two or three spacial dimen-
sions. One-dimensional models can take geometric features into account. They are
discretized in the flow direction and assume uniform flow over a cross section. This
means they still fail to predict local flow characteristics throughout the ejector, es-
pecially in the mixing section, where the flow is clearly not uniform over the cross
section. CFD-model can resolve local flow characteristics to model the turbulent mix-
ing process and the non-equilibrium flow in the nozzle. CFD-models are the most
complex way to model the flow and can be extended by different models to increase
the accuracy of the results. However, this does not necessarily mean that they predict
the performance the best.
1.2.3
One-dimensional models
(Banasiak 2011) developed a one-dimensional approach that assumes uniform flow
over the cross section. It takes metastability effects, mixing of the two flow and shock
waves in the mixing section and the diffuser into account. An often used model in
one-dimensional models is the delayed equilibrium model (DEM) introduced by the
continuation of the previous study in (Banasiak 2013). This allows one-dimensional
models to account for the non-equilibrium phase transition. The delayed equilibrium
model has been improved by (Shaik 2004) and validated with experimental data of a
capillary tube with R22. (Bartosiewicz et al. 2011) and (Lorenzo et al. 2017) provide
a comparison between different approaches to model the phase transition and prove,
that the DEM outperforms the other one-dimensional models for most operation
points. (Vergé 2015) developed an one-dimensional model of the ejector tested and
used for research purposes at DTU.
1.2.4
Two- and three-dimensional CFD models
An overview over recent research activities in CFD modelling of CO2 ejectors is pro-
vided by (Nowak et al. 2015). CFD simulations in all three spacial dimension were
rarely carried out. Usually CFD models are simplified to two dimensional axisymmet-
ric models. Although, with this approach, it is not possible to account for 3-D effects,
==PAGE:20==
4
1 Introduction
it decreases computational time significantly. An overall assessment of turbulence
models was conducted by (Besagni and Inzoli 2017) for steam ejectors. It concludes
that the k-ω shear-stress-transport (SST) model delivers the most accurate global
and local parameter prediction for this application. However, it is unclear if the k-ω
SST model also performs best with CO2 as a working fluid and how the turbulence
models are influenced by the phase transition models.
(Smolka et al. 2013) developed a CO2 ejector model with a reasonable compromise
between computational time and accuracy for industrial development. It uses a 3-D
approach with the homogeneous equilibrium model (HEM). Heterogeneous models
increase the computational time, especially in a three dimensional approach. It also
states that a simplified two dimensional formulation of this problem misses the three
dimensional effects that occur in the suction nozzle because of the radial or tangential
inlet.
However, (Palacz et al. 2015) simplifies the model of (Smolka et al. 2013) to an
axisymmetric two dimensional domain to analyze the accuracy of the HEM-model
for a wide operation range. It concludes that the model predicts the massflows of
the suction and motive nozzle well, except for low pressures and temperatures. As
a continuation (Palacz et al. 2017) implemented the HRM method into the same
model and compared the accuracy of the HRM and the HEM model. It concludes
that the accuracy depends on the operation point. For operating regimes above the
critical point the HRM had a lower accuracy than the HEM. On the other side, for
operating regimes below the critical point, the HRM improved the accuracy of the
model. Furthermore, the relaxation time in the HRM formulation should be improved
to achieve a better agreement with the experimental data.
On the basis of the CFD model by (Smolka et al. 2013), (Palacz et al. 2016)
introduces a routine to optimize the geometric parameter of an ejector to increase
the ejector efficiency. It shows that ejector geometries can be improved using CFD
models. However, this optimization procedure uses the HEM which is proven to be
inaccurate for certain operation regimes (Palacz et al. 2015).
(Haida et al. 2018) uses the results from CFD-simulations in (Haida et al. 2017)
and combines it with experimental measurements to create a reduced-order model.
This allows to predict the performance of an ejector withing reasonable CPU time.
The discrepancy of the motive nozzle massflow in the reduced-order model stays below
5% compared to the experimental data. The discrepancy compared to experimental
data of the suction nozzle massflow stays below 15%, for some ejectors even lower.
However, this model relies heavily on measurement data. This shows that there is
still room for improvement from the simulation side.
Future work on CO2 ejector may lead to an experimental determination of the
flow patterns and the velocity field inside the ejector. With such data results from
a CFD-model could be validated even better. A first attempt to visualize the flow
was made in (Elbel and Hrnjak 2007). Unfortunately the resolution of the result is
not sufficient to fully identify the flow patterns or determine bubble sizes. Similar
studies like (Desevaux et al. 2013) using air to air ejectors could be conducted on
CO2 ejectors to enhance the qualitative understanding.

1.3
Thesis progress report
The first tasks of the thesis, including a literature research and modelling of a R141b
single phase ejector, were all completed successfully.
But some tasks within this
thesis turned out to be much more complicated than expected.
First of all, the
implementation of the enthalpy based energy equation with a user-defined scalar and
the implementation of the material data with user-defined functions in ANSYS Fluent
turned out to be more complicated than expected (detailed description in section
2.7.3.1). Therefore, the decision was made to conduct the analysis with STAR-CCM+.
The material data in STAR-CCM+ has to be implemented with user-defined tables.
Furthermore, the fluid properties have to be manipulated to introduce a temperature
and pressure glide in the two phase region. This implementation and the validation
was also quite time consuming. After this, it turned out that the solver in STAR-
CCM+ was very unstable for this type of simulations. Numerous attempts were made
in order to achieve a stable model and a converged solution without success. In the
end, no real operation point could be simulated. The only converged solution was
achieved with an operation point where both inlets are in the vapor phase. This means
that there is no direct comparison to measurements or calculations in the literature.
Nevertheless, simulations on the converging-diverging nozzle and the vapor-vapor
ejector were conducted to prove, that in principle the developed simulation approach
works.
The whole workflow of the simulations conducted in STAR-CCM+ is shown in
figure 1.2. It can be seen that the simulations were conducted step by step. After
the successful simulation of the single phase ejector using R141b as a working fluid, a
single phase ejector simulation using CO2 was carried out. The results of the R141b
single phase ejector are shown in appendix C. From the single phase CO2 simulation,
the temperature in the motive nozzle was decreased to reach condensation in the
motive nozzle. The results of this simulation are shown in chapter 4.1. Because this
ejector simulation showed unexpected results in the motive nozzle, the nozzle was
analyzed additionally in a separate model. A simulation of the converging-diverging
nozzle with liquid instead of vapor was also carried out. The results of both nozzle
simulations are shown in chapter 4.3.
From this point, a simulation of the real
liquid-vapor ejector was set up.
With this model no converged solution could be
achieved. Therefore, the subsequent simulations shown in figure 1.2 were not carried
out anymore. These models were planned to compare and improve the phase change
models and assess the turbulence models. Nevertheless, the theory for all the models
is still provided in chapter 2.
A preliminary grid convergence study was carried out on the singe phase R141b
ejector. The final grid convergence study was planed on the liquid-vapor CO2 ejector.
Because the simulations on this ejector never converged, this was not possible. Instead,
the grid convergence study was carried out on the vapor-vapor ejector with phase
transition. This determines at least the range of cells needed for these simulations.

1.4
Thesis structure
The introduction in this chapter gives a motivation and an overview over the state of
technology of CO2 ejectors. It represents also a collection of papers on the subject,
divided into their modelling approach.
The theoretical background for this thesis is shown in chapter 2. This includes the
explanation of transcritical CO2 refrigeration cycles and the qualitative explanation
of the work principle of an ejector. Furthermore, the basics of the CFD modelling
techniques used in this thesis are summarized. This is the framework for the software
assessment that is also included in this chapter. Special attention is also paid on the
phase transition models, even though non of them are used in the end in this thesis.
This helps understanding one of the main topics for future investigations.
The whole CFD model starting from the geometry and continuing over the mesh-
ing to the implementation of the material data is explained in chapter 3. The most
efficient ways of how to set up the model found from experience are shared in this
chapter as well.
All the results of the CFD analysis are then shown in the results section. The
results are also compared to results from published research papers wherever there
are comparable results available.
After the conclusion of this thesis, an outlook into the future gives an overview of
how the work that can be proceeded on the basis of this thesis and the general state
of the art. This includes future tasks and analysis that can be carried out at DTU or
other research facilities.
In the appendix, additional information is provided about the implementation of
the material data into STAR-CCM+ and the software capabilities of STAR-CCM+
and ANSYS Fluent. The appendix also contains the results from the single phase
R141b ejector. The appendix is created to keep the main report short and focused.
The whole thesis can be understood without reading the appendix, but it provides
additional information.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
CO2 as a refrigerant
CO2 has had a long tradition in the refrigeration technology reaching far into the
19th century. It has no ozone depleting potential, a negligible direct global warming
potential (GWP=1), is chemically inactive, non-flammable and not toxic in the classi-
cal sense (Bitzer 2016). Recently, CO2 has experienced an increased importance as a
refrigerant because of the regulations on CFC´s, HCFC´s and HFC´s. Furthermore,
CO2 is a byproduct of many industrial processes and therefore the price of CO2 is
low.
The ozone depletion potential (ODP) is a measurement of the ozone depletion of
a refrigerant. It indicates the ozone depletion caused by the refrigerant compared
to the depletion of an equivalent mass of R11. In most countries of the world ozone
depleting refrigerants are banned nowadays.
The contribution to global warming of a refrigerant is indicated by the global
warming potential (GWP). The GWP is a relative measure to the contribution to
global warming of an equivalent mass of CO2. It is related to a reference time horizon.
Usually the compared time horizons are 20, 100 or 500 years. The GWP for 100 years
of selected refrigerants is shown in table 2.1. Although only a selection of refrigerants
is shown in this table, it can be concluded that in general CFC´s, HCFC´s and HFC´s
have a much higher global warming potential compared to natural refrigerants.

consumed by the refrigeration system is considered. This value depends of course on
the energy mix. The total equivalent warming impact (TEWI) can be calculated to
evaluate the environmental performance of a refrigeration system (Bitzer 2016).
TEWI = GWP · L · n + GWP · mrefr · (1 −αrecovery) + n · Eannual · β
(2.1)
The calculation of the total global warming potential shows why the energy effi-
ciency of the refrigeration system is very important from an environmental perspec-
tive. While CO2 refrigeration systems have a low contribution from the direct global
warming impact, their low COP has a negative drawback on the overall global warm-
ing impact. An increase in COP would therefore reduce their global warming impact
and, in addition to this, reduce the operational costs of the system. By implementing
a well designed ejector into the system this can be achieved. That is why current
research focuses on CO2 ejectors.

2.2
CO2 cycles
Because CO2 has a low critical temperature (Tcrit = 31.03 °C) it is very often operated
in a transcritical cycle (figure 2.2). Subcritical cycles would be more efficient, but
they are only possible in refrigerant applications in cold climates where the condensa-
tion temperature is well below 30 °C. For heat pump applications or for refrigeration
applications in hot climates this is not the case. One solution are cascade systems
where the CO2 cycle is combined with another cycle with a different working fluid to
reach the high temperature. This solution is rather complicated and therefore more
expensive compared to a transcritical CO2 cycle. Furthermore, the environmental
advantages of the CO2 cycle are affected by the secondary cycle. Therefore, a trans-
critical CO2 cycle is most desirable for many applications, even though it has a lower
COP. Increasing the COP of the transcritical CO2 cycle by implementing an ejector
is therefore very beneficial for this technology.

2.4
Multiphase flow
Multiphase flow is defined as the simultaneous flow of fluids with different chemi-
cal properties or different states (Brennen 2005). In the context of this thesis only
multiphase flow with different states (either gas or liquid) are considered. Usually
flows are divided into the primary (continous) and the secondary (dispersed) phase.
Modelling the flow with fluids of different properties and their interaction is the main
challenge to address in multiphase models in CFD. In addition to this, each phase
may be laminar or turbulent.
To select the appropriate multiphase model in CFD, the characteristics of the
flow must be known beforehand. An overview over the different types of multiphase
models that are implemented in most commercial CFD software is shown in figure
2.7. The multiphase models are divided into Lagrangian and Eulerian models. The
Lagrangian models track individual particle paths and the Eulerian models resolve
the dispersed phases into the continuous phase.
Figure 2.7: Multiphase models overview.
In the Lagrangian models the level of complexity increases when taking particle
interaction into account. The simplest approach is to consider a one-way coupling
where the continuous phase interacts with the particles with a drag model. When
taking interactions between the particles or from the particles to the continuous phase
into account, the complexity of the model increases.
Similarly the Eulerian models are classified by their level of complexity.
The
simplest approach is to treat the fluid as a mixture where the volume and mass
fraction of the different phases are tracked. In this method only one momentum and
one energy equation has to be solved for the fluid mixture. The algebraic slip model
==PAGE:33==
2.4 Multiphase flow

allows then to drop the assumption of mechanical equilibrium (same velocity) between
the phases by adding an empirically derived slip velocity model. The most complex
approach is to consider each phase separately and solve the continuity, momentum
and energy equation for each phase separately.
This method is numerically very
expensive.
The flow problem in this thesis desires either a volume of fluid model (VOF) or an
algebraic slip model (ASM) to solve the problem in an efficient way. The multiphase
model is extended by adding different phase change models to cover the condensation

2.6

In CFD turbulent flows can either be solved by direct numerical simulation (DNS) or
by turbulence models. DNS resolves all time and all spacial scales of the turbulence.
These simulations are numerically very expensive and usually not used for practical
problems. Furthermore, also in research only certain problems can be solved by DNS,
mostly flows with low Reynolds numbers. Therefore, turbulent flows are usually mod-
elled with turbulence models. A hybrid solution is provided in large eddy simulations
(LES) where large eddies (large-scale motion) are computed while the smallest eddies
are modelled.

was introduced by (Wilcox 1988) and improved to the shear-stress transport (SST)
k-ω-model by (Menter 1993). It includes one transport equation for the turbulence
kinetic energy (k) and one transport equation for the specific dissipation rate (ω). The
k-ω-model is also often used in CFD applications. Since it is not used for simulations
in this thesis, it will not be described in detail in this section. However, it should be
mentioned since it is used in some of the simulations in the literature.

The mesh of the ejector is created using 2-D polygonal cells. There are two refinement
zones in the mesh. The first refinement zone covers the motive nozzle. The grid size
in this zone has to be fine enough to resolve all physical effects appearing in this zone.
Especially the condensation and the evaporation in the motive nozzle, as well as the
transition to supersonic flow, have to be properly resolved. The second refinement

section has to be refined. If both have a discrepancy in the same range, the basic

3.4.1
Unsteady approach
Although the expected solution is steady, the simulation is set up transient. This is
because unsteady phenomena can occur before reaching the steady solution. Several
single phase test simulation prove that the steady solution is often not found by
applying a steady approach. However, every steady solution can in principle also be
achieved from a transient approach. But this costs more computational time.

3.4.2
Initial conditions and ramp
Choosing the correct initial conditions can be a challenge in setting up the simulations.
There are three different pressures (motive nozzle, suction nozzle and outlet) and two
temperatures (motive nozzle and suction nozzle) in the model.
Ramping up the
temperatures is not recommended.
Once the desired temperature is reached, the
fluid has to flow from the inlet, where the temperature is ramped, to the outlet to
include the influence of the new temperature. Since the physical timestep is very low
once the flow is developed (because of high velocity and constant Courant number as
shown in equation 3.1), this takes a very long time. Therefore, the inlet temperatures
in the model are in general not ramped. Nevertheless, an initial temperature has to
be applied to the fluid region. Best practice shows, that the lower inlet temperature
is preferable since the temperature in the motive nozzle increases when the motive
nozzle inlet pressure is ramped up. This is explained in the next section.
Exposing the initial condition directly to the final pressures can lead to instabilities.
Therefore the inlet and outlet pressures in the model are ramped up or ramped down
respectively. Different strategies in ramping the pressures can be applied:
• Starting from the lowest pressure (suction pressure) in the model and ramp up
the motive inlet pressure and the outlet pressure
• Starting from the highest pressure in the system (motive inlet pressure) and
ramp down the suction pressure and the outlet pressure
• Starting from an intermediate pressure and ramp up the motive inlet pressure
and ramp down the suction and outlet pressure
All strategies have their advantages and disadvantages. The initial phase also
needs to be considered when ramping up the pressures. When starting from a low
pressure, the fluid is usually in the vapor phase, which means the liquid phase occurs
at a later stage in the motive nozzle. This phase has then to flow through the whole
ejector before reaching a steady solution. On the other hand, when starting from a
high pressure, the fluid is usually in the liquid phase or the supercritical state, which
means the vapor phase occurs after decreasing the pressure in the suction nozzle.
Therefore, the vapor phase from the suction nozzle has to flow through the whole
ejector before a steady solution can be achieved.
Starting from the lowest pressure in the system is the most stable if the motive
nozzle pressure is ramped up first and the outlet pressure is ramped up at a later time.
This lets the flow develop faster. If the backpressure is ramped up too early, reversed
flow to the suction nozzle occurs. An example of the pressure ramps is shown in
figure 3.12. Ramping up the pressure from the lowest pressure has the disadvantage
that the fluid in the motive nozzle gets compressed. This leads to an increase in
temperature. The simulation time increases because the high temperature fluid has
to flow through the nozzle before reaching the right temperature. Nevertheless, this
strategy is often the fastest way to reach a steady solution.

3.4.4
Mesh sizes
The basic simulations are started with a coarse mesh until the flow is developed. This
saves a lot of computational time. Especially in transient simulations the grid size
has a significant influence on the computational time. Since the timestep depends
on the grid size (with a constant Courant number), the flow develops much faster in
a coarse grid. Even though the real flow physics may not be resolved correctly in a
coarse grid, it gives a good initial condition. Once the flow is developed, the grid is
changed to a finer grid to resolve the flow correctly. To avoid numerical issues, this
should be done stepwise until the desired grid size as shown in the grid convergence
study in chapter 4.2 is reached.
3.4.5
Solver
There are three solver options in STAR-CCM+:
• Segregated flow solver
• Coupled flow solver
• PISO solver
The difference between the coupled and the segregated flow solver can be seen
when comparing figure 2.14 and figure 2.15. The coupled flow solver solves the momen-
tum and continuity equations simultaneously wile the segregated flow solver solves
the momentum equations first and solves the continuity equation afterwards. The
PISO solver is also a segregated flow solver. It uses instead of the SIMPLE algorithm
the so called PISO algorithm by (Issa 1986). It generally gives more stable results
and takes less CPU time but not suitable for all processes.
All three solvers are tested and are capable to solve the flow as long as it stays
withing one phase. As soon as a phase change occurs, only the segregated flow solver
is stable. The relaxation factor of the energy equation needs to decrease significantly
when entering the two phase region. Otherwise the simulation gets unstable. The
relaxation factor can not be adjusted for the energy equation separately in the coupled
solver. This is one of the reasons why it gets unstable.
With a low relaxation factor, the simulations converge very slowly. For this rea-
son the most stringent convergence criteria is the maximum number of iteration per
timestep. This means, that the simulation does not converge in every timestep. This
would take too long time. But because the changes per timestep decrease at long
simulation times, the solution converges in the end to a steady solution. However,
the ”solutions” on the way to the converged solution are not always physical.

4.2
Grid refinement study
A grid refinement study is performed on the vapor ejector flow (chapter 4.1). The
investigation shows the influence of the grid size and the number of cells on the result
of the entrainment ratio. The grid size is linked to the accuracy of the result. The
finer the grid, the closer the solution converges to the exact solution with of a model
with infinite small grid size. However, a smaller grid size increases the computational
time. Furthermore, in transient simulation, the timestep decreases with smaller grid
size when keeping the Courant number constant. Therefore, the grid is chosen to be
accurate enough, if the error stays below a defined tolerance. The tolerance depends
on the specific case and the accuracy requirements. Previous project show that the
maximum error using the HEM method is usually between 10-15%. Therefore, the
acceptable tolerance is chosen to be 2% compared to the finest grid. This means
that the error from the finite volumes is small compared to the error from modelling
the physics of the flow. The entrainment ratio is chosen as the main result of the
simulation, because it is one of the parameters to describe the ejector performance.
The goal of this study is to find the minimum number of cells needed to simulate
this kind of flow. Table 4.1 describes the settings of the grid for each considered case.
The different refinement areas are shown in figure 3.3 and 3.4.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
This thesis gives a broad overview of the state of the art numerical modelling ap-
proaches of phase transitioning flows in CO2 ejectors and their theoretical background.
The multiphase and phase transitioning models used in current research papers are
described and compared. Furthermore, the possibilities of analyzing flows with the
most commonly used commercial and open source software packages is discussed.
A new modelling approach is introduced where the fluid properties are imple-
mented with a pressure and temperature glide in the two phase region.
Multiple
simulations in this thesis prove that analyzing of two phase CO2 flows is possible
with the new modelling approach. The simulations on a converging-diverging nozzle
agree qualitatively well with published results of current research. However, some sim-
ulations show results that disagree qualitatively with the results from current research
papers. This applies especially to the conducted ejector simulations. In addition to
this, the present routine is very unstable and requires more computational time than
conventional models. For this reason, simulating a real operation point of the CO2
ejector is not possible yet and needs further development. An exact comparison to ex-
perimental measurement is therefore not possible. Nevertheless, further development
could improve this technique to become a promising alternative to the conventional
methodology.
This thesis provides a good starting point for future analysis of the CO2 ejector.
It summarizes all the theory and all the challenges that appear in phase transition-
ing CO2 ejector simulations. Furthermore, it gives a good overview over solution
strategies for this problem.

CHAPTER 6
Future work
There is no converged solution from the simulations on the liquid-vapor ejector in
this thesis, even tough different attempts are made, for example changing boundary
conditions, the material data, the slope of the temperature and pressure glide in the
phase transition, the solver settings, the solver itself, the number of grid points and
the ramp up time. This means the model should be developed step by step. The
simulation on the liquid converging-diverging nozzle can be used as a starting point
for future simulations. First of all it would be interesting to see the result for different
outlet pressures. Until this point only one simulation with a constant outlet pressure
is performed. Understanding the two phase CO2 nozzle is one of the key points to
understand the ejector.
From the nozzle simulation, the model can be extended. In a first step it could
be beneficial to include only the suction nozzle and part of the mixing section. From
that point the full ejector model can be developed. This way the reason why the
simulations are not converging with the modeling approach used in this thesis may
be found.
If the simulations on the real ejector operation points can be established, they can
be compared to the modelling approach in ANSYS Fluent (used in Smolka et al. 2013,
Palacz et al. 2015, Palacz et al. 2016, Palacz et al. 2017, Haida et al. 2017 and Besagni
and Inzoli 2017) and Open FOAM (used in Lucas et al. 2014 and Colarossi et al. 2012).
The performance in terms of simulation time and accuracy could be compared.
The model can then be used for further simulations as shown in figure 1.2. This
could include studies on phase transition models and turbulence models.
All phase transition models explained in chapter 2.5, especially the HRM model,
are mainly developed from experimental data from steam. Therefore it is not a sur-
prise that they do not improve the accurancy compared to the HEM model. Therefore,
the modelling constants for the relaxation time used in those model need to be ad-
justed for CO2. This can either be done by conducting the same super moby dick
experiment, that the HRM is based on, for CO2. Another approach could be to adjust
the result of all the constants to the result of CO2 ejector measurements. This means
that every constant has to be modified in a defined range. For this a large number of
simulations need to be conducted in order to adjust and improve the HRM to CO2
ejectors. The same applies to the cavitation models. For example the bubble size and
the nucleation density in the Hertz-Knudsen equation can be investigated.
However, the most promising way to simulate the flow in a two phase ejector seems
==PAGE:88==
72
6 Future work
to be the approach in ANSYS Fluent, where the temperature based energy equation
is replaced by the enthalpy based energy equation through the implementation by a
user-defined scalar. For practical use this is the fastest and most accurate approach
at this time.
Because most simulations are conducted in a 2D axis symmetric domain, the
influence of 3D effects can be subject to further investigation. 3D models require a
much higher number of cells which then increases the simulation time significantly.
Another issue that is not paid attention to are the slip or drift flux models. Only
(Colarossi et al. 2012) deals with a slip model between the phases. All the other
approaches assume mechanical equilibrium. It is expected that slip models can have
a significant influence on the result in the mixing section and therefore on the entrain-
ment ratio.
Grid convergence studies show that the grid size in the mixing section influences
the result significantly. Therefore, future models can be set up using an automatic
remesh where the gradient of the quality and the gradient of the velocity is highest
(not in wall region). This could lead to more accurate results and the benefit that only
in the region where the mixing occurs, a high number of cells is needed. Therefore,
the simulations could run with fewer cells and would be faster.

Conclusion
The results are not very accurate compared to the experimental measurements. This
is probably because the geometry is not completely correct.
It is noticeable that
the entrainment ratio is always over-predicted compared to the measurement data.
Nevertheless, this simulations show that the basic simulation methods are working,
at least for a single phase ejector. Therefore, it can be concluded that the goal of
this simulations is achieved. Furthermore, the grid convergence study shows that the
mesh should contain approximately 80 000 cells in order to obtain an accurate result.
This number can be used as a basic estimation for the first CO2 simulations.
