Abstract
This project focuses on bridging the gap of full system models in Modelica for solar district
heating systems by developing a holistic system-level model. The lack of comprehensive
models in Modelica hampers the evaluation and optimization using this modeling language.
The main objectives of this study are to develop or modify component models to create a
closed-loop solar district heating model and validate the model’s performance.
The validation process involves comparing the model’s predictions with data from the Dron-
ninglund solar thermal plant. Positive results were obtained from the validation and numer-
ical analysis of the individual component models.
However, the full system model falls short of generating the required energy when compared
to TRNSYS. The solar field collectors in the model produce only 83% of the observed energy,
indicating a need for improvements. It is suggested that the high inlet temperatures to the
solar field contribute to this issue.
To enhance the accuracy and reliability of the Modelica full system model, improvements
to the PTES and solar collector field models are necessary. With these advancements, the
Modelica model has the potential to become a viable alternative to proprietary software like
TRNSYS for numerical modeling of solar district heating systems. The improved model can
provide a comprehensive and reliable representation of the system’s performance, enabling
informed decision-making for future developments and optimizations in this field.

Introduction
Lowering greenhouse gas emissions is essential for minimizing the effects of climate change, an
urgent worldwide concern. When fossil fuels such as oil, coal, and gas are burned, greenhouse
gases such as carbon dioxide are released into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat and
contribute to global warming, which in turn leads to climate change. [1]
About fifty percent of the world’s total final energy consumption is attributable to heat, the
bulk of which is derived from the residential and industrial sectors. In EU residential sectors,
space heating and domestic hot water can account for up to 80% of total energy use. In the
European Union, just 16 percent of the energy required for heating and cooling comes from
renewable sources, whereas 84 percent comes from fossil fuels. [2]
By reducing the demand for fossil fuel-based energy sources, solar district heating systems
and heat storage can play a significant role in mitigating the use of fossil fuels in the space
heating sector. By integrating solar heating and energy storage technologies into conventional
heating systems, it is possible to dramatically reduce the use of fossil fuels.
In some countries, the integration of solar thermal systems with district heating systems
is becoming increasingly widespread. The purpose of incorporating solar collector fields in
district heating networks is to reduce or even meet the network’s low heat demand during
the summer months. [3]
Recent research has demonstrated that a high solar factor in a solar district heating is only
possible with a large-scale seasonal storage system. Incorporation of solar where storage
is utilized could increase the solar fraction from 5 - 15% to 25 - 50%. Therefore, a large
scale seasonal storage is an effective way to improve the efficiency of large-scale solar district
heating systems. [4]
Solar district heating systems are typically more complicated than traditional district heating
systems.
For this reason, having a solid understanding of the thermodynamic behavior
of such networks is adviced in order to maximize the effectiveness of their design, their
operation, and the formulation of their control strategy.
These kinds of insights can be
gained through the use of numerical simulation.
Modeling real systems numerically offers numerous benefits, including prediction, compre-
hension, optimization, experimentation, and communication. By mathematically represent-
ing a system, we can predict its behavior under varying conditions and make informed
decisions regarding how to control or optimize it.
In addition, numerical models can shed light on the underlying mechanisms that govern the
behavior of a system, allowing us to gain a deeper understanding of how it operates and
which factors are most crucial to its performance. Numerical models can also be used to
optimize the performance of a system by determining the optimal settings for its inputs and
parameters, resulting in enhanced efficiency, productivity, and cost-effectiveness. In addition,
these models can simulate experiments that would be difficult or impossible to conduct in
the real world, allowing us to test hypotheses and investigate various scenarios without the

need for costly or time-consuming physical experiments.
For the majority of the solar district heating systems operational in Denmark. The numerical
models have been developed in the TRNSYS software.
TRNSYS is a proprietary software dedicated to the modeling and simulation of the transient
behavior of thermal systems. Many component library options compatible with the TRNSYS
environment are currently accessible. These libraries provide models for a variety of SDH
components, including heat exchangers, heat storage, solar collectors, etc. Experimental
findings have validated several of them. The commercial TESS library is mostly utilized for
solar district heating simulation needs. [5]
However, interest in developing models in open-source and free software is increasing. Open-
source software is often free to use, making it more accessible to researchers, students, and
small businesses that may not have the financial resources to purchase expensive software. In
addition, open-source software, provides greater flexibility than licensed software, as users are
able to modify the software to suit their needs. This is particularly important for researchers
who may need to tailor the software to their specific applications. The user community that
open-source software attracts is an additional advantage. These communities frequently offer
assistance, share information and resources, and collaborate on projects. This is particu-
larly important for novices who may require assistance getting started with the software.
Transparency is another advantage of open-source software; the source code is available
for inspection and modification, which can increase confidence in the software’s accuracy
and dependability.
Finally, open-source software can frequently be combined with other
open-source software to create a modeling and simulation environment that is more compre-
hensive and customizable. While proprietary software such as TRNSYS may have its own
advantages, open-source software offers a compelling alternative that should be considered.
In this paper, the open-source alternative to TRNSYS is the Open Modelica editor run with
the Modelica language.
Modelica is an object-oriented programming language designed for multi-physics simulation
and natural modeling. One of Modelica’s key features is its ability to model systems in
a highly modular and hierarchical manner. This facilitates the reuse and combination of
existing models, decreasing the time and effort necessary to generate new models. Modelica
models are also fully customizable, allowing for the insertion of user-defined components as
well as the ability to readily adjust model parameters for analysis.
Using Modelica in large-scale industrial hydrodynamic systems is beneficial since it enables
accurate and realistic simulations, capturing complex interactions and dynamics through it’s
ability to integrate multiple disciplines and capture complex phenomena in a unified frame-
work. The outcomes of these simulations aid in system design, optimization, troubleshooting,
and predictive maintenance.
Modelica also provides several other benefits. For example, there are high-quality and well-
documented open-source libraries available for various domains, such as thermodynamics,
which make it easier for users to build models. Additionally, Modelica offers several non-

1.1
Literature review
This literature review examines the current state of numerical analysis for thermal solar
plants for district heating. This includes a review of the existing literature and research
findings on of numerical analysis on a variety of different components, such as solar collectors,
heat storage, system integration, and performance assessment.
A study by Olsthort et al. reviews the modeling and optimization of integration of storage
and renewable energy into district heating systems. The paper provides a comprehensive
review of recent advancements in the energy production, modeling, and optimization of
District Heating Systems. It presents a classification of energy sources based on their sus-
tainability and ease of integration into a district heating system. The paper also compares
different modeling methods and optimization objectives for DHS. Additionally, the paper
discusses the integration of heat storage techniques and renewable sources into DHS. From
the study it is evident that the majority of system modeling case studies of integrating solar
into district heating systems is conducted using the TRNSYS software. [6]
A paper by Fabian Ochs et al. compares different models for large-scale thermal energy
storage systems in various simulation platforms, including COMSOL Multiphysics, TRNSYS,
Modelica/Dymola, and MATLAB/Simulink. The TES models include buried tanks, pits, and
hybrids, and can be built either completely buried or partially buried. The study validates
the models against measured data from a pit TES and compares the results with respect
to thermal losses and temperature stratification. The paper shows that good to acceptable
agreement between the tools was achieved after a review phase and after eliminating bugs
and user influence, and overall, the study provides insights into the complexity of thermal
energy storage systems and the importance of simulation tools for their optimization. [7]
A further investigation pitting TRNSYS and Modelica against each other have been con-
ducted by Giraud et al. The paper compares TRNSYS and Modelica, for modeling Solar
District Heating components and systems. The simulations show that both tools yield sim-
ilar results, but Modelica has native multi-physics simulation capabilities, making it more
flexible and cost-effective for modeling solar district heating systems. The paper concludes
that the research group has selected Modelica/Dymola as the preferred tool for simulation
activities related to solar district heating. [8]
In recent years, an increasing number of papers on numerical modeling and optimization
of integration of storage and renewable energy into district heating systems have been pub-

lished, but full scale systems in Modelica remain underrepresented in the field of solar district
heating modeling. Recent published studies have demonstrated a greater emphasis on the

Two papers describe the development of models for Pit Thermal Energy Storages (PTES)
in the Modelica environment. In the first paper by Dahash et al. a validated model is used
to study key parameters such as the aspect ratio of the storage tank and the insulation
thickness on the Levelized Cost of Storage. The model is based on the partial stratified tank
from the Modelica Buildings library, with modifications to avoid water mixing and include
multiple inlets/outlets. It is combined with a soil model based on the thermal conductivity
equation in radial and vertical directions, with logarithmic meshing in those directions away
from the fluid domain. The boundary conditions are set based on ambient temperature and
convection at the surface of the water and soil regions, with adiabatic conditions at the
radial and vertical bottom limits. The model is then compared to a COMSOL model that
was previously validated against Dronninglund PTES data by the same authors, using a
one-year simplified district heating profile with constant heat flows over three-month spans
and a daily time-step. The results show good agreement with less than 0.1% difference for
system losses and temperature profiles. [7]
In the second paper by Reisenbichler et al. a modified stratified storage tank model from
the Modelica Buildings library is coupled with a soil model derived from the radial heat
transfer model from the same library. The tank model is further modified to decouple the
insulation selection at the top, middle, and bottom walls, and to allow for different initial
temperatures in the layers of the tank.
The soil meshing is increased with a factor of
1.5. The validation of this model is done against experimental data from the Dronninglund
plant, and the simulation results are compared with existing models in TRNSYS types 342,
1300/1301, and 1322, as well as COMSOL models in truncated cone and pyramid shapes.
The comparison shows accurate results with an overall deviation in efficiency and charging
and discharging energies of less than 1%.
The computational time of the simulations is
discussed, indicating that there is room for improvement, particularly in the meshing of the
fluid and soil to reduce simulation times, which currently last on the order of a few hours.
[9]
To further build on the papers by Dahash et al and to Reisenbichler et al. Nathan Fourner
developed a new PTES model as his master’s thesis project. The main objective of this work
was to develop a Modelica-based model for system-level preliminary studies with a signifi-
cantly lower computing time, specifically for parametric analysis to optimize the volume of
a Pit Thermal Energy Storage (PTES) in interaction with heat producers and consumers.
The model was developed based on previous work that utilized the stratified tank model
from the IPBSA library, combined with a soil model from the MoBTES library. To achieve
the goals of the work, which included a maximum absolute deviation of 5% for charged and
discharged energy and computational time below 10 minutes for a bi-annual simulation, the
default meshing for the water and soil regions was set based on sensitivity analyses. This
default meshing, consisting of 20 layers in the water region and 10 nodes in each direction for
the soil meshing, was designed to obtain asymptotic behaviors of the simulation, rather than

For other vital components, such as solar collectors and heat pumps, numerical investigations
of have been conducted, but in many cases less from an energy standpoint as much as from
a mechanical one. In software such as TRNSYS and Modelica, the used components are
frequently predefined models of heat exchangers and solar collectors.
However, investigation of impacts on integrating heat pumps with large scale water based
thermal energy storage have been conducted by Alice Tosatto et al.
The paper uses a
1D equation-based model for the TES and a 2D or 3D finite element-based model for the
surrounding ground in COMSOL Multiphysics to investigate the impact of integrating a
heat pump with a large-scale water-based thermal energy storage system in District Heating
systems. The study evaluates the TES-HP performance based on environmental aspects
(ground temperature and CO2 emissions) and technical performance (energy and exergy
efficiency). A simplified equation-based model for the HP using the TES as an evaporator
heat source is also implemented to investigate its impact on the TES performance and on
the surrounding ground temperatures.
The results show that integrating the HP with the TES increases the TES efficiency by 6%

1.2
Problem formulation
There has been a notable increase in the development of Thermal Energy Storage (PTES)
models in Modelica, indicating the growing interest in utilizing Modelica as a tool for nu-
merical investigation in energy systems. However, there remains a significant gap in the
availability of full system models in Modelica that integrate various components of solar
thermal systems, such as solar collectors, heat exchangers and auxiliary systems.
This gap in full system models in Modelica hampers the comprehensive evaluation and
optimization of solar thermal systems using this language. Therefore, there is a need to bridge
this gap by developing holistic system-level models in Modelica that can provide accurate
and reliable simulations of solar thermal systems, enabling researchers and practitioners to
better understand the performance, dynamics, and potential improvements of these systems.
By addressing this research gap, this project aims to contribute to the advancement of solar
thermal system modeling and simulation in Modelica, and provide a valuable tool for system
design, analysis, and optimization, ultimately promoting the efficient utilization of solar
thermal energy in various applications.
This paper expands on the previously conducted master’s thesis by Nathan Fournier that
mostly focused on modeling the performance of PTES (Pit thermal energy storage). The
thesis demonstrated good performance of the Modelica model developed, compared to real
data and a TRNSYS model, and paved the way for this paper to expand the scope of the
analysis to encompass the whole system.
In particular, this paper seeks to construct a
comprehensive Modelica-based model of the entire system around the PTES model, which
includes the other system components, such as solar collectors, heatpumps, transmission
pipes and heat exchangers.

2.1
Solar collector field
The solar collector field has 2,982 solar panels, or 37,572 m2, manufactured by Arcon Solar.
The solar collector field is divided into two sections located next to each other, see figure 3.
Each field of solar panels is connected to a heat exchanger in a technology building. The
maximum design power output from the collector fields is 26 MW, which can be compared
to the maximum heat load during the coldest winter months, which is around 12 MW. Each
collector field row contains 21 solar collectors connected in series and the water is gradually
heated by the 21 solar collectors.
In the solar collector field, a glycol water mixture is used as the working fluid. The glycol
water mixture serves as the medium that collects and transfers heat from the solar collectors
to the technology building where the heat is utilized. Water is the main component of a
glycol water mixture, with a small fraction of glycol added to offer anti-freezing qualities.
The working fluid is transported from the solar collector field to the technology building
through transmission pipes. The working fluid is heated as it goes through the solar col-
lectors. The heated glycol water mixture is then pumped through the transmission pipes,
which are carefully insulated to minimize heat loss, to the technology building.
The working fluid is passed through a heat exchanger in the technology building. The heat
exchanger is intended to transfer heat from the solar collectors to a secondary water loop.
The heat exchanger transfers heat from the glycol water combination to the secondary water
loop without mixing the two fluids. This keeps the glycol water combination isolated from the
secondary water loop and prevents contamination. The hot water is subsequently distributed
to the rest of the system.
The solar collectors installed are of the type flat plate collectors. It consists of a rectangular,
flat box with glass, insulation, and a metal absorber plate made of copper or aluminum.
When sunlight penetrates the cover and strikes the absorber plate, it absorbs the heat. The
heat is then transferred to the working fluid that runs via tubes or channels in the collector.

Absorption heat pump
The energy required to pump the fluid from a low pressure in the absorber to a higher
pressure in the generator is comparatively small in an absorption refrigerant cycle, and the
remainder of the work (vaporizing the refrigerant) can be accomplished with heat instead
of electricity. This fact makes absorption heat pumps especially valuable in cogeneration
systems where waste heat from steam and other processes is abundant.
The heat pump is an absorption heat pump that is powered by a bio oil boiler located in

absorption heat pump. The heat pump uses storage water as its heat source and generates
district heating on the load side.

Media
The media package in Modelica is and interface between models of great importance as it
enables the modeling and simulation of physical systems involving different substances, such
as fluids, gases, or mixtures.
It provides a standardized and reusable way to define the

3.3
Absorption heat pump component
An absorption heat pump is a type of heat pump that transfers heat from a low-temperature
source to a higher-temperature sink using the absorption refrigeration concept. Absorption
heat pumps work differently than traditional vapor compression heat pumps, which employ
a compressor to boost the temperature of the refrigerant.
The working fluid of an absorption heat pump is made up of a refrigerant and an absorbent.
The absorption of the refrigerant by the absorbent, often in a generator or absorber, starts
the process. The mixture is then heated, causing the refrigerant to evaporate and separate
from the absorbent. A condenser subsequently condenses the refrigerant vapor, delivering
heat to the higher-temperature sink. The absorbent is returned back to the generator or
absorber, now diluted with the refrigerant, to restart the cycle. [23]

The PTES model consists of a stratified tank model with multiple water layers, each at a
uniform temperature. The thermal behavior of each layer follows the convection-diffusion
equation.
The stratified tank model in the PTES model represents the underground pit, and is divided
into 20 layers of water. Each layer is connected to neighboring layers through thermal- and
fluid interfaces, which represent, respectively, the conduction between layers and the fluid
transfer from one layer to another.
Each layer in the stratified tank is connected to a soil meshing model representing the sides
of the pit. The soil models are represented as ground elements connected in a matrix in
a finite element approach, simulating the heat exchange between the stratified tank and
the surrounding soil. The outer boundaries of the soil model have prescribed temperatures,
representing the undisturbed soil temperature far from the storage region.
Additionally, the top volume of the stratified tank is subjected to heat interaction with the
ambient temperature through an air convection model and the lid insulation, simulating the
effects of ambient temperature changes on the stratified tank. The bottom volume of the
tank is not only connected to the side meshing but also to the bottom meshing, representing
the heat transfer to the underlying soil.
In order to account for the geometry simplification from a truncated pyramid shape to a
cylinder, a factor is applied to each heat transfer from the tank to the ambient or the soil.
It is equal to the factor between the relevant area of the truncated pyramid, to that of the
cylinder.
The model also includes three ports for charging and discharging the thermal energy. One
port is located at the top of the stratified tank, one in the volumetric middle layer, and one
at the bottom layer. These ports represent the points where thermal energy can be charged
or discharged from the PTES.
For a more detailed description of the PTES model, see the paper "Numerical Modeling of
a Pit Thermal Energy Storage in Modelica" by Nathan Fournier.

Full system
In figure 25 the full system as it is viewed in OpenModelica is presented. The full system

behavior of the solar thermal plant. The package encompasses all the necessary components,

required mass flow rates in the system.
The solar collector loop, responsible for capturing solar energy, is connected to the PTES

facilitate heat transfer between the two loops without mixing the fluids. This arrangement
allows the transfer of thermal energy from the solar collector loop to the PTES loop, ensuring
efficient utilization of solar energy for storage.

heat pump model or another heat exchanger. Here the flow is also subjected to transmission
losses through distribution pipes, which are crucial for a realistic representation of the plant’s
performance. The necessity to include these losses can be observed in Figure 3. The choice
between the heat pump and heat exchanger connection is determined by the district heat
control model. This connection also employs a heat exchanger with a constant effectiveness
to facilitate heat transfer between the loops.
Within the load loop, a heater is incorporated to ensure that the forward temperature to the
consumers is maintained at 75 °C, and to provide flexibility in the flow distribution, a flow
separator model is implemented. The flow separator model takes a flow fraction input from
an external file, which determines the distribution of flow. The flow separator distributes
the flow either directly to the boiler from the heat exchanger or through the heat pump.

Validation method
Validating a model is an important step in ensuring its correctness and dependability. The
individual models are validated in the context of numerical analysis to measure their per-
formance. The model’s predictions are compared to observed data from the Dronninglund
plant.
Once the individual models have been validated, the next step is comparing the results of the
full system model with those of the individual models. This comparison helps evaluate the
overall performance of the integrated system and assess the compatibility and consistency
of the individual models within the larger framework. By examining the agreement between
the individual models and the full system model, it is possible to identify any discrepancies
or inconsistencies that may need to be addressed.
To gain an understanding of how the developed models perform compared to more well es-
tablished models the results will also be compared to a TRNSYS model of the Dronninglund
plant.
From these comparisons, it is possible to aquire a thorough picture of the model’s per-
formance by comparing the outcomes of the entire system model, individual models, and
TRYNSYS model to the observed data. This procedure allows for isolating areas for devel-
opment, evaluate the model’s accuracy, and gain confidence in its capacity to make credible
predictions.
4.1
Numerical analysis
When evaluating the performance of a model, it is essential to employ multiple validation
methods to thoroughly assess its accuracy and reliability.
In this report the validation
methods are R-squared (R2), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Root Mean Squared Error
(RMSE).
Each method offers a unique perspective on the model’s accuracy and reliability, taking
into account various aspects of prediction errors. Through these measures it is possible to
acquire a more comprehensive understanding of a model’s strengths and shortcomings by
employing several validation methods, allowing them to make educated decisions regarding
its applicability for their unique needs.
4.1.1

R-squared (R2) is a statistical measure that quantifies the proportion of the variation in

model. It is a number between 0 and 1, with 0 indicating that the independent variable(s)
explain no variation in the dependent variable and 1 indicating that the independent vari-
able(s) explain all variance in the dependent variable.

To ensure the accuracy of models that simulate the behavior of a system or process, it is
necessary to validate them using real-world data. In the case of the Dronninglund plant,
data was gathered from temperature, flow, and energy sensors.

4.3
Simulation setup
The sensors installed on the plant collect data in 10-minute intervals. This data is analyzed
to create a comprehensive picture of the performance of the solar plant over a predetermined
time period. However, the validation of the Modelica model was based solely on the data
collected in the year 2015.
The data collected from the sensors is crucial to validating the Modelica model as it pro-
vided real-world data that could be used to compare the model’s predictions to the actual
performance of the solar plant.
However, sensor measurements are not always accurate due to various sources of errors such
as measurement noise, drift, and bias. These errors can lead to discrepancies between the
actual system behavior and the measurements provided by the sensor.
When validating simulated values against real measured data, sensor inaccuracy can have
significant implications.
If the simulation model accurately represents the system being
modeled but the sensor measurements are inaccurate, the simulated results may not match
the measured data. In this case, the discrepancies between the simulated and measured
data may not be due to errors in the simulation model, but rather due to errors in the
sensor measurements. Therefore, it is important to consider sensor accuracy when validating
simulated values against real measured data.
The inaccuracy of the measuring sensors in the plant is taken into account when comparing
the simulated results to the measured data.
Any discrepancies below the uncertainty margin of the sensors may not be significant enough
to justify adjusting the simulation model. In other words, if the differences between the
simulated values and the measured data are smaller than the sensor uncertainty margin, it
may be reasonable to assume that the simulation model is accurate and the discrepancies
are due to sensor inaccuracy.

comparison between the two is not subjected to a bias.
The model will be run with a

over time.

4.3
Simulation setup
4.3.1
Individual component simulation
To test the accuracy and reliability of a solar energy system, it is essential to test and
validate each component of the system separately. In this case, the solar irradiation model,
solar collector field model, and absorption heat pump model were all evaluated and simulated
independently to ensure their accuracy and reliability, leading to key insights in the individual
components before incorporating them into a full system model.
The solar irradiation model takes input data of raw beam radiation, diffused radiation,
and ground radiation. The model then calculates the total solar irradiance, which is to be
compared with measurements of total radiation to validate the model’s accuracy.
The validated solar irradiation model is then used as an input to the solar collector field
model, along with the flow rate and real input temperature. The output temperature is the
desired variable for this simulation.
The absorption heat pump model takes real data as inputs for the inlet flow and inlet
temperature on both the source and load side as inputs. The hot side power, the cold side
power extraction, and the power into the heat pump are the desired variables for this model.
4.3.2
Full system simulation
The inputs in the form of hourly values given to the full system are the real measured flow
rates of the media in the system. The flow rates are taken as inputs op the four pumps in the
system responsible from moving the fluid in each loop of the system(see figure 25). Other
inputs are the beam, diffuse and ground reflected irradiation, a source side temperature
cooling set point for the absorption heat pump and the heat load imposed on the system by
the consumers.
An important aspect of the simulation is that it will be run over two years, but the data
comparison will only be made for the last year. This is because the first year will be used
to preheat the soil models primarily in the pit storage, which will help to ensure that the
system is operating under realistic conditions before the data comparison is made.
The decision to only analyze data from the last year of the simulation is based on the
assumption that the system will have reached a stable state by this point. This is known
as the steady-state assumption and is commonly used in numerical analysis to simplify
complex systems. By only analyzing the data from the second year, it’s possible to focus on
the system’s behavior under stable conditions, which can provide valuable insights into its
long-term performance.

Discussion
The validation process and numerical analysis conducted for each of the individual models
developed within the project yielded positive results. In particular, the boundary models,
which include the irradiation and flow models, demonstrated a remarkable ability to accu-
rately predict real values. This close alignment between predicted and actual values instills
a strong sense of confidence in evaluating the performance of the larger system-dependent
components.
The validation model for the solar collector field demonstrates a high level of accuracy, par-
ticularly when there is a flow present through the collector loop. However, one of the primary
challenges associated with this model lies in accurately representing the indoor temperature
of the technology building. This difficulty arises due to the absence of temperature mea-
surements within that specific building. As a result, when the flow rate approaches zero, the
model’s temperature values tend to converge towards the indoor temperature. This aspect
can distort the plots and create an impression of a less reliable model.
Nonetheless, it is important to note that this limitation does not affect the power genera-
tion aspect of the system. The power produced by the solar collector field remains a highly
dependable data set, and its reliability serves as a solid basis for making comparisons. There-
fore, despite the challenges encountered in modeling the indoor temperature accurately, the
power data remains a reliable and robust parameter for assessing and evaluating the perfor-
mance of the solar collector field.
The heat pump model exhibits satisfactory performance, with particularly accurate power
outputs observed on both the cold side (i.e., PTES side) and the hot side (load side). How-
ever, there is a noticeable discrepancy between the measured values and the power input in
the form of hot water into the heat pump from the boiler.
This discrepancy presents an opportunity for improvement through the execution of addi-
tional simulations and subsequent adjustments to the lookup tables. By refining the model
parameters based on these simulations and adjustments, a more precise representation of
the power input from the boiler can be achieved.
Furthermore, in a later phase of development, it is advisable to incorporate lookup table
data from the heat pump manufacturer, as per the intended approach in TRYNSYS. This
integration of manufacturer-provided data will enhance the accuracy and reliability of the
heat pump model, further improving its performance and aligning it closely with the real-
world behavior of the system.
In the full system model, the solar collector field model falls short of generating the required
energy to be considered a viable option to TRNSYS. Producing only 83% of the observed
energy, this outcome cannot be deemed satisfactory.

solar collector field model, which will subsequently enhance the energy flow to the PTES
model. By increasing the energy content of the storage through improved energy generation,
the PTES model will be able to discharge a greater amount of energy, aligning the results
more closely with the other models. This adjustment will contribute to a more consistent
and accurate representation of the overall system performance in the Modelica full system
model.

6.1
Further development
6.1
Further development
In order to further advance this project, attention should be directed towards ensuring
accurate enthalpy flows in the PTES flow models. While the project heavily focused on,
and managed to achieve precise mass flows to reflect the measured values, to understand the
reasons behind the lower energy production of the solar collector field, it is crucial to also
ensure that the flows possess the correct temperatures.
Once the flows are accurately represented, the development of a control model becomes fea-
sible. While some PID controllers are already employed in the existing model, incorporating
flow control should not be excessively challenging, particularly in the load side loop and the
collector loop. Implementing control for the charging and discharging processes of the PTES
may pose a greater challenge, but the flow separator models developed within this project
can simplify the integration of such a control model.
Regarding the heat pump, further work is required to address the discrepancies observed
during validation of the individual model. In subsequent stages, it would be advantageous
to replace the mathematical "black box" model developed in this project with a grey or
even white box model. This transition would involve utilizing physical models in Modelica
to mimic the operations of the absorption heat pump, enhancing the transparency and
accuracy of the heat pump model.

Summary & conclusion
This research addressed the existing research gap by developing full system models in Mod-
elica for solar thermal systems. With a notable increase in the utilization of Modelica for
Thermal Energy Storage models, there was a pressing need to integrate various components
of solar thermal systems into comprehensive system-level models. Building upon a previ-
ously conducted master’s thesis that focused on PTES modeling, this paper expanded the
scope to include the entire system surrounding the PTES model, including solar collectors,
heat pumps, transmission pipes, and heat exchangers.
The primary goals of this research were to develop new component models or modify existing
models to create a closed-loop solar district heating plant model.
The validation process and numerical analysis conducted for the individual models developed
within the project have yielded highly positive results. The boundary models, specifically the
irradiation and flow models, have demonstrated impressive accuracy in predicting real values,
providing a solid foundation for evaluating the performance of the larger system-dependent
components. The power data generated by the solar field remains reliable, despite challenges
in accurately modeling the indoor temperature of the technology building. While improve-
ments are needed in representing the temperature dynamics, the power output remains a
dependable metric for assessing solar field performance.
The heat pump model showcases satisfactory performance, particularly in power outputs on
both the cold and hot sides. However, there is a notable discrepancy in the power input
from the boiler, primarily related to the hot driving flow. This presents an opportunity for
refinement through additional simulations and adjustments to lookup tables. Incorporating
manufacturer-provided data can further enhance the accuracy and reliability of the heat
pump model, aligning it more closely with real-world behavior.
In the full system model, the solar field falls short of generating the necessary energy to
be deemed a viable option to the TRNSYS software. The discrepancy is attributed to in-
let temperatures in the solar field, potentially influenced by high temperature fluctuations
on account of the flow models. Addressing this issue requires a focus on improving energy
production from the solar field and mitigating temperature disparities. Additionally, dis-
crepancies between the Modelica full system model and the other models regarding PTES
behavior, highlight the complexity and potential limitations of flow models, urging further
exploration and optimization.
The heat pump in the full system model performs well, with strong results in accumulated
energy and delivered energy. Looking at both energy and and outlet temperature the Mod-
elica models outperform the TRNSYS model, and given the similarities in power as well as
temperature output the model results can be considered reliable.
When comparing PTES models, both based on energy and on temperature, differences can
be observed. This is likely due to low energy production from the solar field. To rectify this,
emphasis should be placed on improving energy generation to the PTES, thereby enhancing
67
==PAGE:75==
energy content as well as energy charged and and discharged. By addressing these areas of
improvement, the full system model can offer a more consistent and accurate representation
of overall system performance.
With improvements to the PTES and solar field models, the Modelica full system model has
the potential to become a viable alternative to the TRNSYS model. By refining the repre-
sentation of temperature dynamics in the technology building and addressing the challenges
related to the inlet temperatures in the solar field in the full system model, the accuracy and
reliability of the model can be significantly enhanced. Additionally, optimizing the heat pump
model by refining the power input from the boiler and incorporating manufacturer-provided
data will further align the model with real-world behavior. With these advancements, the
Modelica model has the capacity to provide a comprehensive and reliable representation of
the system’s performance, facilitating informed decision-making for future developments and
optimizations, and subsequently providing an alternative to proprietary software’s such as
TRNSYS in the field of numerical modeling of solar district heating systems.
