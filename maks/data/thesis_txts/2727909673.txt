ABSTRACT 
 
This study compares the performance and cost-effectiveness of Photovoltaic-Thermal 
(PVT) systems with conventional Photovoltaic plus Thermal (PV+PT) systems for 
residential applications. The investigation focuses on three key aspects: electricity 
generation, domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating (SH), and overall cost 
analysis. The comparison is also made between a single-family house and two multi-
family buildings. 
In terms of electricity generation, the research examines the percentage of self-
production in each system, highlighting the efficiency and self-sufficiency of the 
installations. Results show that while PVT systems exhibit a slightly higher self-
production percentage in single-family homes, the difference is only marginal (2%) 
compared to PV+PT systems. However, for larger multi-family buildings, PVT systems 
outperform PV+PT systems significantly, indicating their superior efficiency for higher 
energy demands. 
For domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating (SH), the investigation evaluates the 
systems' ability to meet the total energy demand. Although both PVT and PV+PT 
systems fall short of consistently meeting the demand, PVT proves to be more efficient 
in multi-family buildings, where its consumption remains higher than the demand, 
unlike PV+PT systems. This suggests that PVT is better suited for bigger residential 
buildings in terms of thermal energy provision. 
The cost analysis primarily focuses on the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) over the 
systems' lifetime. In single-family houses, PVT systems do not exhibit a significant 
advantage over PV+PT systems, as the LCOE is nearly identical for both. However, for 
multi-family buildings, PVT systems showcase considerable cost advantages, offering 
approximately 20% cheaper energy per kWh compared to conventional PV+PT 
systems. Despite the higher initial cost of PVT systems, their long-term affordability 
makes them a more profitable choice for bigger residential buildings. 
In conclusion, PVT systems have the potential to make a strong impact in the 
residential market. They prove to be more economical and energy-efficient for multi-
family buildings than PV+PT systems, while the choice for single-family houses is less 
clear-cut due to marginal differences in energy production and LCOE. Further research 
is recommended to explore the relationship between building dimensions and system 
efficiency and affordability. Overall, PVT systems are gaining traction and represent a 
promising option for meeting residential energy needs effectively.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
A transition towards renewable energy sources is needed to mitigate the effects of 
climate change. Renewable energy is key because it can supply two-thirds of the total 
global energy demand and contribute to the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, 
necessary to limit the average surface temperature to 1.5ºC [1]. For this reason, the 
United Nations created the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) in 2015, aiming to 
address this problem among other issues in the social, economic, and environmental 
sectors. This research falls within Goal 7, Affordable and clean energy. 
In the last SDG report from 2022, they stated that the current pace of progress is 
insufficient to achieve Goal 7 by 2030. They mentioned that between 1990 and 2010, 
the annual improvement in energy intensity was only half of the target set. This means 
that now, to be able to reach the goal by 2030, that target has to increase. “The target 
remains within reach, but only with significant investment in cost-effective energy 
efficiency improvements on a systematic scale” [2]. However, at this moment, public 
financing for renewable energy has gone down since 2017 (Figure 1). 
 
Apart from the SDG, in 2015, 196 parties at the UN Climate Change Conference 
(COP21) adopted a legally binding international treaty on climate change. This is the 
Paris Agreement, which entered into force on 4 November 2016 [3]. Countries have to 
submit their national climate action plans.  
Denmark takes part in this agreement, and their latest long-term strategy is from 2019. 
There, they mention how they plan on achieving the goals and the statistics until 2019, 
with a forecast for the next 20 years.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
2.1. Description of PV+PT and PVT systems 
PV systems convert solar radiation into electrical power using the principle of 
photovoltaic effect. When light falls on PV cells, the energy from photons is transferred 
to the charged holes and negatively charged electrons due to the electric field across 
the junction. This results in the flow of current if a closed path is provided to the circuit 
by connecting a load [6,7]. 
The solar PV system is composed of a PV module, a converter, and an inverter. As PV 
systems only collect electricity, they can be accompanied by a photothermal collector 
(PT) to collect heat that can be used for DHW and space heating [6,7]. 
 Solar panels absorb the solar irradiance and convert it into electrical power. Then, a 
converter is used as an interface between the PV module and the PV system. An 
inverter is used to convert DC to AC [6]. 
The solar cells that compose the solar panels are made of different materials, which 
are [6]: 
- 
Single/mono-crystalline silicon solar cell. These panels have been used for a 
long time due to their longevity. The sunlight to electrical power conversion 
efficiency of monocrystalline PV panels is 14-17,5% which is the highest among 
all other materials. 
- 
Multi-crystalline silicon. These panels use multifaceted crystalline material and 
have a slightly lower efficiency of 12-14% compared to mono-crystalline cells. 
- 
Copper, Indium, Gallium, and Selenium (CIGS). CIGS is a quaternary 
semiconductor comprising the four compounds. Its efficiency is between 10-
12%, and it is cost-effective. 
- 
Cadmium telluride (CdTe). CdTe is a semiconductor and the cheapest solar 
cell. It is chemically stable when compared to other thin-film photovoltaic 
technologies (like CIGS). Its light absorption capacity is higher, thereby 
improving the efficiency, which lies between 9%-11%. 
- 
Organic cells. These cells use organic electronics, which deals with conductive 
organic polymers or organic molecules capable of absorbing light and 
generating electrical power. They are mainly made of polymer material and are 
economical, but their efficiency is 9%. 
- 
Dye-sensitized solar cells. This type of cell uses dye material between the 
electrodes. This technique is economical but has poor optical absorption 
characteristics in the near-infrared region, resulting in low conversion efficiency 
and stability problems. Its efficiency is only 3-4%. 
However, the absorbed solar radiation that it is not converted into electricity increases 
the temperature of the PV cells, leading to a decrease in efficiency. To avoid this, PV 
cooling is necessary. The usual solution for PV cooling is to use water or air circulation 
through a heat exchanger in thermal contact with the PV panel rear surface. If the heat 
removal fluid is used not only for PV cooling but also for heating DHW, for example, 
hybrid PVT systems are obtained [7]. 
Solar hybrid photovoltaic-thermal (PVT) collectors represent a new technology on the 
market that produces electricity and heat simultaneously. The dual function of the PVTs 
enables a more effective use of solar energy in a lower area than a system that has a 
==PAGE:13==

PV module and a thermal collector together. Such a feature could be effectively used in 
building applications with high and constant use of thermal energy throughout the year 
but with limited roof area, e.g., DHW in multifamily buildings, blocks of flats, etc., or in 
solar active houses with a high solar fraction, e.g., self-sufficient houses with seasonal 
storage. [8,9] 
Some of the advantages of PVT are its dual purpose, as the same system can be used 
to produce electricity and heat output; its efficiency, as the combined efficiency can be 
higher than using two independent systems and is especially useful when roof-panel 
spacing is limited; and its wide application, as the heat output can be used for both 
heating and cooling applications depending on the season, making it suitable for 
domestic applications [8]. This is why it is interesting to investigate how high its 
efficiency is compared to conventional PV+PT systems in different residential models. 
 
2.2. District heating in Denmark 
Countries like Denmark have a district heating infrastructure, which combines space 
heating and domestic hot water. The aim is to have an energy-efficient and 
environmentally friendly energy consumption system. [10] 
The collective heat supply mostly consists of district heating technology, whereby hot 
water is distributed from a central district heating plant to houses and other buildings. 
Two out of three Danish houses are heated in this way. Another form of collective heat 
supply is natural gas, which supplies gas to each household via a collective grid of gas 
pipes. Individual natural gas boilers produce the heat and hot water in each house. 
Fifteen percent of all houses are heated this way. Collective forms of supply are mainly 
advantageous in urban areas or other areas with a high heat density. 
 
The collective forms of heat supply are regulated by the Heat Supply Act. 
 
The municipalities and utility companies are the key players in the collective heat 
supply. The municipalities carry out the heat planning and are responsible for 
expanding grids and for the demarcation of the different forms of collective heating. 
The municipalities also ensure that this is done in accordance with the Heat Supply 
Act. 
 
District heating, cogeneration, and natural gas companies hold operational 
responsibility for delivering heat to consumers. 
 
The Danish Energy Agency has put forward the general conditions for establishing and 
operating the collective forms of supply. The purpose of this regulation is to ensure a 
positive bottom line for utility companies and favourable prices for consumers [11]. 
 
2.3. Previous studies in efficiency and economy of PVT systems 
As early as 2015, studies were conducted to determine whether PVT could be more 
efficient than PV+PT systems and if its application in residential settings is more 
expensive and by how much. 
T. Matuska, B. Sourek, V. Jirka, and N. Pokorny presented a performance and 
economic analysis of PVT collectors in 2015 [12]. In their research, they aimed to find 
the competitive specific price range for solar hybrid PVT collectors. 
==PAGE:14==
7 
Investigation of PVT systems 
Their analysis was performed for a combined solar heat and power system for a 
multifamily residential building. The performance and economic analyses were based 
on the solar energy system with the combined heat and electricity production for a 
residential building with 45 flats and 100 occupants. Solar heat was used for DHW 
preparation, and solar electricity was used for the building appliances load. They 
investigated three different alternatives for PVT and four for PV and PT. 
To define the competitive price in the market for the PVT collectors they were 
investigating, the following economic conditions were set: heat and electricity prices, 
prices for solar PT and PV collectors, and system cost. 
Their results showed that unglazed PVT collectors were not competitive with 
conventional combinations of photothermal and photovoltaic collectors, even in 
Southern Europe. The situation seemed slightly better for glazed collectors, although 
only in Southern Europe. 
While the conclusion in their research in 2015 was that PVT was still not competitive 
with conventional photothermal and photovoltaic collectors, it is proposed now that the 
situation might have changed. Current electricity prices are much higher, and since 
2015, material costs might have been reduced. Therefore, in this investigation, it is 
planned to provide results on whether the situation has improved for PVT systems in 
residential areas. The investigation is set in Denmark.

3.2. Simulation 
As mentioned in the introduction, simulations in Polysun must be run to determine the 
energy consumption in each of the three cases studied in this research. The 
parameters that vary during the simulations are the same for all three cases, and each 
case is studied individually in terms of energy consumption and cost calculation. The 
comparison is made within each case, considering the results obtained from changing 
certain parameters.  
Initially, a wider range of parameters was considered for change, but it was later 
simplified to ensure timely simulation. The selected parameters for change are: 
electrical consumption (with three different consumption levels per family), type of 
convector (radiator or floor heating), DHW demand, and the number of family members 
(ranging from a couple to a family of 5 members).  
To simplify these parameters and reduce the number of simulations, it was assumed 
that each person consumes an average of 50l/day of hot water. This assumption 
determined the DHW demand per family based on the number of family members. 
Electrical consumption was also simplified and linked to the number of family members 
using the electricity calculator from the Danish Energy Agency [14]

The total price on the first year is important because it includes the initial investment 
cost, and the energy cost during the first year, which is the most expensive one. But 
after that, the only relevant cost is the maintenance cost and the energy cost, which 
includes only the difference between the electricity bought from the grid and the 
electricity sold to the grid. This is because the system is already installed and 
producing energy, therefore the only expense relevant during the next years after the 
installation is the extra electricity bought from the grid, and the electricity that can be 
sell to the grid.

4. RESULTS 
 
The results have been divided in five categories: electricity, domestic hot water (DHW), 
space heating, heat pump and cost. 
 While DHW and space heating are both part of the energy consumption and are both 
fed from a common tank (as mentioned before in this report, that is the usual system in 
Denmark), they have been presented separately with the total energy consumption in 
common. This is because presenting their results separately provides a better 
understanding of their impact in the total energy consumption of the systems, and how 
they change between cases and systems. 
Heat pump is also included in its own section to provide a better understanding on its 
function and impact on the system. It also provides a better understanding of the 
relationship between electrical consumption and heat production, and their role as 
auxiliary heaters in a solar system. 
 
4.1. Electricity 
In this category is interesting to see two types of results. On a first table, the electricity 
results are shown. The four interesting results for this table are the following: 
- 
Total electricity consumption. This gives an overview of the total electricity 
consumption of the system which includes the thermal components (heat pump, 
heat exchanger…) and the electricity consumption of the profiles. The profiles, 
as described before, are the families defined for each case. They go from 2 
members per consumption profile, to 5 members per consumption profile.  
- 
Electricity production to system. Own electricity production, which is used to 
cover the demand of the profiles.  
- 
Electricity production to grid. Electricity sold to the grid when the production is 
superior to the demand. This usually happens during the summer months. 
- 
Electricity from the grid. Electricity bought from the grid when the production 
could not meet the demand.

to see how much is bought and sold, but also to compare both systems in terms of

the panels, it will not be included in the discussion. This is due to its link to the energy

4.4. Heat pump 
While the heat pump doesn’t bring relevant information to compare both systems for 
their use in residential buildings, it is interesting to see its use in each case as it brings 
information on how much auxiliary heating was required in the system. The most 
relevant results to check are two: 
- 
Electricity consumption of heat pump. Having the result on how much electricity 
it consumes, a better overview on the electrical consumption is achieved. The 
aim is to use it as less as possible. 
- 
Seasonal performance factor. It is defined as an indicator used for heat pumps 
to evaluate their efficiency expressed as a ratio of the total heat supplied to a 
==PAGE:41==
34 
Investigation of PVT systems 
building (by the heating system) to the electricity used by the heat pump (to 
drive the compressor of a heat pump) and other devices of the heating system 
over the year [27].  
The SPF, in the case of a solar space heating system providing heat for space heating 
and DHW partly by solar energy and partly by the auxiliary electric heater, will take the 
following form [27]: 
𝑆𝑃𝐹=
𝑄𝑠𝑜𝑙+ 𝑄𝑎𝑢𝑥,𝑒𝑙
𝐸𝑒𝑙,𝑡𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙+ 𝐸𝑎𝑢𝑥,𝑒𝑙
 
Where Qsol is the heat by solar energy, Qaux,el is the heat from auxiliary electric heaters, 
Eel,total is the electricity used by all electrically driven devices and subsystems, and 
Eaux,el is the electricity used by  the electrical auxiliary heater [27] 
Therefore a low Seasonal Performance Factor (SPF) means that the heat provided is 
low in relation to the electricity used. 
The SPF only provides an overview on the relation between the electricity consumption 
of the heat pumps and the energy provided to the system. Also, the electricity 
consumption is part of the total electrical consumption of the system, which is already 
being compared and therefore doesn’t require to be mentioned again for heat pump. 
For these reasons, this section is informative but it will not be included in the 
discussion. 
4.4.1. Case A 
As results show in Table 20, PVT systems consume more electricity than PV+PT 
systems. This is due to two reasons. First, the heat pump required for PVT systems are 
more powerful than the minimum ones required for the PV+PT systems, therefore 
every time they are used, PVT ones will consume more electricity. Second reason is 
the controllers of the system. When designing a system in Polysun, controllers are 
needed in order for the system to know which component to use when and under which 
circumstances. The controllers for PVT systems are different from the PV+PT ones, 
therefore the systems work differently and make a different use of the heat pump. PVT 
systems use heat pumps more, giving high results on energy production. PV+PT 
systems make less use of heat pump, which translates into lower energy values and 
lower electricity values as well.

5. DISCUSSION 
 
To compare the results previously presented, three sections have been created: 
electricity, domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating (SH), and cost. DHW and SH 
will me compared together as they will be compared by the energy 
consumption/demand, which is the total of both. 
 
5.1. Electricity 
In terms of electricity, it is interesting to see which percentage of the total electricity 
consumption is self-production. This gives an overview on how efficient the systems 
are, as the goal is to be as self-sufficient as possible.  
 
Figure 45. Comparison between cases A, B and C on their percentages of self-production on the total 
electricity production. 
Overall, the self-production percentage is not high in any of the cases. This is because 
the system designed is quite basic and simple. Therefore, it works good enough, but 
with better implementations and components, like batteries, the percentage of self-
production would increase. As the goal of this study is not to create a self-sufficient 
system but just to compare PVT with PV+PT, it was not necessary to make a more 
complex and efficient system. 
As shown in Figure 45, in a single-family house like the one designed for case A, both 
systems work good in terms of providing electricity. PVT provides slightly more, but the 
difference is a 2%. Considering this, PVT is not a better choice than conventional 
PV+PT systems when it comes to single-family houses. 
In case B, PVT is already working better than PV+PT with a noticeable difference. And 
the gap keeps getting bigger in case C. This proves that, for multi-family buildings with 
bigger consumption, a PVT system works better than a PV+PT, as it is able to still 
provide a decent amount of electricity for the system, while PV+PT’s own production 
drops for big buildings.

5.2. Domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating (SH) 
As mentioned before in this report, the total energy demand and total energy 
consumption includes both the domestic hot water and the space heating 
demand/consumption. For that reason, they will be looked at together by the total 
energy demand/consumption for this comparison.  
Ideally, the system would provide the thermal energy needed to cover the demand. 
That is not usually the case due to weather conditions, daily use and needs (some 
days there is a bigger use of thermal energy, other less than estimated), outside 
temperatures, etc. Most of the time, the consumption will be either higher or lower than 
the energy demand.  
According to Polysun, the energy consumption is the energy effectively consumed by 
the consumers, including the energy consumption for domestic hot water, heating and 
cooling of the building, and for pool heating, and the energy demand is the energy 
demanded by consumers in order to meet the comfort requirements, including the 
energy demand for domestic hot water, and for heating and cooling of the building. In 
this investigation, there is no cooling system, neither pool heating, therefore only DHW 
and SH is included.  
 It is also explained that energy consumption Quse can differ from the energy demand 
Qdem. Quse > Qdem leads to higher temperatures at the hot water tap than required. If on 
the contrary Qdem > Quse, there is an energy deficit. Regarding the building it means that 
the desired temperature has not been reached. 
Considering this information, we can analyse the obtained results throughout this 
investigation. For this, the following formula has been used to create the matrix in

6. CONCLUSION 
 
To conclude, it can be confirmed that nowadays, PVT can definitely find a place in the 
residential market. While this investigation shows how different the results are between 
a single-family house and a multi-family building, it is still competitive enough in both. 
Although it does work better for multi-family buildings, whether PVT is better than 
PV+PT in single family houses is not as clear yet. In this research, it has been seen 
that, while it can still provide a bit more energy than PV+PT, its initial cost is much 
higher and the LCOE is almost the same. Considering this and that the amount of 
energy produced for the system is not much more than the one provided by a PV+PT 
system, PVT might not be the clearest choice for a single-family house. Although it is 
worth mentioning that it can be much more competitive at the moment, as opposite of 
what T. Matuska concluded in 2015 [9].  
This means that PVT is definitely gaining a position in the market and, while not 
winning in small demands as the ones in a single-family house, it has definitely been 
proven that its implementation in multi-family buildings can be more economical on the 
long term, as well as energy efficient on a yearly basis. It would be really interesting to 
do some further research on the relation between the dimensions of the building and 
the efficiency and affordability of the system. This is because in this research it has 
been proven that PVT is more efficient and economical than PV+PT systems in multi-
family buildings. But the difference between both systems has been bigger in the 
residential building with 10 floors than in the one with 6 floors, where the gap in results 
between PVT and PV+PT was already high.
