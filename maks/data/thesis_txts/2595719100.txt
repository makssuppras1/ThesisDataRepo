Abstract
In this Master Thesis report, the reader will ﬁnd a full description of experiments on
a two-cylinder ﬂexible ﬂoating structure carried out in DHI [1] wave basin as part
of the the FloatStep project [2]. The experiments aim is to investigate the behavior
of non-rigid wind turbine ﬂoating foundations and their hydro-elastic response. A
primary analysis of the experiments results is done. In parallel to those experiments,
a numerical model of the ﬂexible structure was developed and is also presented in
this report.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Recently it has become clear that climate-related general awareness and global mind-
set are shifting. Citizens and their respective governments are demanding and striv-
ing for more renewable energy sources in their energy mix. Wind energy is one of
those renewable energy sources. Wind turbines were ﬁrst developed in Denmark
and the Netherlands. Initially, countries started exploiting the wind resources avail-
able onshore. Eventually, multiple countries started to consider the possibilities of
exploiting the wind resources available at sea. The countries with access to shallow
waters, such as the countries of around the North Sea, were logically the ﬁrst to
build oﬀshore wind farms. The ﬁeld of oﬀshore wind energy got started with bot-
tom ﬁxed foundations and the number of bottom ﬁxed wind farms is still expending.
The installed capacity oﬀshore went from 2 GW in 2009 to 22 GW in 2019 in Europe
[5, Fig. 5, p. 14].
However, not all countries that have access to the sea have shallow waters. Coun-
tries with deep waters are just as much interested in the possibilities oﬀered by a
clean source of energy like the wind energy. Furthermore, the farther one goes from
shore, the lesser the impact on many levels (visual, acoustic, etc) and the higher the
wind resource. Only one issue remains: the farther one goes into the sea, the deeper
the waters. Bottom ﬁxed design costs are increasing with water depth until a limit
where they are no longer proﬁtable.
This is when oﬀshore ﬂoating foundations come into the picture. Many countries
such as Japan, France or Norway for example are investing in ﬂoating projects. In
2019, Europe as a whole invested €6bn in oﬀshore wind energy out of which €0.5bn
are dedicated to ﬂoating [5, Sec. 2, p. 23]. Many demonstration projects of ﬂoating
wind turbines and wind farms are being built. One example is the Hywind wind
farm project built oﬀthe shores of Norway [6]. Many research projects are aiming
at understanding further and speeding up the development and industrialisation of
ﬂoating wind foundations.
As the objective of oﬀshore ﬂoating wind farms is to harvest the strong winds at sea,
12
==PAGE:15==

the logic step is to go higher in wind turbine dimensions. This trend can be seen in
the increase in rotor sizes that oﬀshore wind turbine manufacturer are competing
with, latest being GE 12 MW wind turbine [7] or Siemens Gamesa 14 MW design
[8]. As rotors increase in size, so do ﬂoating foundations. Moreover, as the ﬂoating
foundations aim to be competitive with bottom ﬁxed design, a lot of eﬀort is put
into more eﬀective designs. Reducing the costs often implies reduction in material
and structural optimization. As the ﬂoating foundations become bigger or thinner,
they also become more ﬂexible. With this fact comes another: the assumption that
ﬂoating foundations can be considered rigid in hydrodynamic modeling might no
longer hold.
1.2
Objective
The objective of this project is to investigate the hydro-elastic response of ﬂoating
structures in the light of the context presented above. In most ﬂoating related works,
the hydrodynamic response of the structure would not be impacted by the elastic
behavior of the structure. The assumption of a rigid structure was the trend, as
only smaller ﬂoating foundations where considered. The objective of the project
was to investigate the ﬂexible behavior and response of a ﬂexible structure excited
by non-linear wave through both a numerical and an experimental approach.
1.3
FloatStep
This master thesis and the experiments carried out are part of a larger Danish re-
search project funded by the Danish Innovation Fund (IFD): the FloatStep project
[2].
The aim of the FloatStep project is to provide scientiﬁc and technological solutions
to the existing gaps in the value chain of the oﬀshore ﬂoating wind ﬁeld. "The devel-
opment undertaken in FloatStep to support the TRL (Technology Readiness Level)
transition consists of tools for optimized ﬂoater and tower design that include the
dynamic eﬀects of mooring and control dynamics; new Engineering methods for the
excitation of low-frequency platform modes and ﬂexible motion of the large-scale
ﬂoaters; and CFD methods for computation of the installation process. The instal-
lation process is further de-risked by dedicated physical model tests at DHI" [2].
The project spans from 2018 to 2022 and gathers the following partners: DTU Vin-
denergi (Wind Energy), DHI, Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy, Stiesdal Oﬀshore
Technologies A/S, Stromning IVS and the University of Western Australia.

The waves and corresponding structure responses will comprise linear component
and higher order components. We consider a Stokes type expansion of the surface
elevation for a regular incident wave, which to the fourth order gives
η(θ) = A(η11 cos (θ)) + A2(η20 + η22 cos (2θ)) + A3(η31 cos θ + η33 cos (3θ))

notation of Stokes expansions, these coeﬃcients correspond to sum and diﬀerence
frequency components. It is demonstrated in [17] that a generalization of the regular-

γ = 1) are considered and are stated in [17] as valid.

The objective of this thesis is to investigate the behavior of a ﬂexible structure. Very

3.6
Generalized Modes Formulation in WAMIT
WAMIT is a radiation/diﬀraction panel program developed for the linear (and sec-
ond order) analysis of the interaction of surface waves with various types of ﬂoating
and submerged structures. WAMIT is based on a potential ﬂow formulation and
woks in ﬁnite or inﬁnite depth. Full details on the theory and governing equations
are given in the WAMIT manuals of Version 6 [13] and Version 7 [14].
Version 6 of the WAMIT software was used for the single cylinder structure. How-
ever for the 2 cylinders structure, to include the ﬂexible mode, Version 7 was used.
The WAMIT software includes the possibility of analysing generalized body modes
beyond the usual 6 rigid-body motion (3 translations and 3 rotations). The gener-
alized modes are deﬁned by the user to describe the structural deformation of the
body, hinged-body motion or other variety of motions [14]. The generalized modes
speciﬁc to this thesis structure will be brieﬂy detailed hereafter; for more in depth
understanding the reader is welcome to Chapter 9 of WAMIT Version 7 user manual
[14].
The generalized modes formulation in WAMIT uses a DLL (Dynamic Link Library)
ﬁle NEWMODES.F containing the NEWMODES routine as well as the FLEX_GM
subroutine in which the generalized modes are deﬁned.
Two input parameters,
NEWMDS and IGENMDS, control the implementation of the generalized mode
option. NEWMDS speciﬁes the number of generalized modes. IGENMDS is an
integer used to specify the deﬁnition of the generalized modes (must be non-zero
in order to call the NEWMODES routine, the parameter value is arbitrary and
of no consequence). In our case NEWMDS=1 or 2 (depending on the ﬂexibility
formulation) and IGENMDS=100.
The main inputs to NEWMODES routine are the Cartesian coordinates of a point
on the body surface, speciﬁed in the vector form XI(1),XI(2),XI(3), and the corre-
sponding components of the unit normal vector XN at the same point [14]. The
outputs, speciﬁed by the user for each generalized mode in the FLEX_GM sub-
routine, are the symmetry index of the modes in an array (IMODE), the normal
component of the displacement (VELH), see Figure 3.8, and the vertical component
of the displacement (ZDISP). The symmetry indices (IMODE) identify the sym-
metry of each mode (assuming the body has one or two planes of symmetry) by
assigning the values (1,2,3,...,6) to indicate the same symmetries as the correspond-
ing rigid-body modes. The normal component of the displacement is computed from
the product of the displacement vector and the normal vector.

The structure is rotating about the hinge point (x, y, z) = (0, 0, FB), with FB
the freeboard. With unit rotation, the displacement at a point with radial distance
r′ from the hinge is a vector with magnitude r′ tangential to the circular arc. The
Cartesian components of this vector are d = (FB−z, 0, x). Thus XDISP=FB-XI(3)
and ZDISP=XI(1). The displacement vector for the second cylinder is determined
symmetrically. In the case of the ﬂoating conﬁguration this symmetric hinge mode
is the only one needed since the heave and pitch motions of the coupled structure
plus the symmetric hinge mode are suﬃcient to represent all possible motions of the
coupled structure.

4.2.2.1
General Problem: The ﬂexible Structure
The whole content of this thesis is focusing on experimenting on and modeling a
two cylinders ﬂexible structure. The objective of this section is to guide the reader
on the path taken from a general problem idea to a feasible layout and to the ﬁnal
design.
4.2.2.2
Approaches
The base design idea for the ﬂexible structure was to connect the two cylinders
with a beam and place a device in the middle of the beam that would enable some
ﬂexibility. The said device should be adjustable in a way that would allow diﬀerent
natural frequencies in this new ﬂexible mode.
Flexible Plates vs. Hinge
The two options considered was a hinge of adjustable
stiﬀness and a ﬂexible plate at the center. The plate at the center could be removed
and replaced to reach the various target frequencies of the ﬂexible mode. No such
hinge solution being known to us, the decision was made to go for a set of ﬂexible
plate.
Multiple Layer vs. Single Layer
Once the decision to use a ﬂexible plate in
the center of the beam settled, came the question of the sizing. Appendix C derives
the equation, for a simpliﬁed approach, of the behavior of the structure. From the
ﬁnal relation Eq. 2 in Appendix C, liking the natural frequency of the ﬂexible mode
to the plate dimensions, one can notice that four parameters can be modiﬁed: the
length, the width, the thickness of the plate and the inter-column distance (distance
52
==PAGE:55==
CHAPTER 4. EXPERIMENTS
between the two cylinders). Modifying the plate length l or the inter-column dis-
tance L heavily modify the structure which would both be impractical to re-model
and lacking consistency between the tests. Thus the single layer approach was aban-
doned. As the width of the plate b has a square root relation to the frequency, large
variation would be needed to yield signiﬁcant variations in frequencies; therefore
this approach is also abandoned. Finally, the multiple layer approach consisting of
varying the thickness of the plate by adding multiple layers of plate was preferred.
This solution is both practical and gives room for adjustment.
Materials Investigation
Three diﬀerent materials were investigated for the ﬂex-
ible plates: steel, aluminium and polycarbonate. Appendix C gathers the investi-
gation of those three materials. Based on their respective yield strength, Young’s
modulus and weight aluminium and polycarbonate were the only two options. Sets
of plates for those both materials were ordered only the aluminium was ﬁnally used.

Final Plates Design
Figure 4.10 presents the ﬁnal design of the plates with its dimension. The useful
length of the plate is l = 0.10 m.As the set-up is symmetrical this value is valid for
both sides. The other dimensions were ﬁxed by practical matters, the only relevant
one the the frequency is the width b, which was chosen to match the support rigid

From Figure 4.11 and 4.12, one can draw multiple conclusions. Firstly, the three
softer conﬁgurations (h = 12, 16 and 22 mm) one can see that the peak in the fre-
quency response around 0.5 Hz is driven by hydrodynamics. This natural frequency
is expected to be very close to the heave/pitch natural frequency of the structure.
The initial assumption which was considering the two useful length of the ﬂexible
plate to be completely independent because rigidly clamped in the middle is wrong.
From visual observation ﬁrst we can see that by exciting one cylinder and one side
of the plate we excite also the other. Furthermore in the frequency response for
h = 2 × 12 mm where it is more obvious, we can see two peak frequencies. They
correspond to two diﬀerent vibration mode. The lowest frequency around 2 Hz cor-
respond to both cylinders going in opposite directions, this mode will be referred
to as the "S mode". The second mode of higher frequency corresponds to the two
cylinders moving symmetrically up and down, this mode will be referred to as the
"bird ﬂy mode". This deduction are based on the fact that as the two cylinders move
up at the same time the global stiﬀness of the plates will increase thus yielding a
higher frequency than in the case where the cylinders move opposite.

5.2.1.1
Fixed Conﬁguration
Two types of decay tests are carried out for the ﬁxed set-up. Since the structure
is rigidly clamped to the bridge all the rigid body motions are prevented, this only
leaves the structure with a signal degree of freedom the ﬂexible mode. The ﬁrst
decay test consists in pulling up Cylinder 2 (Stern) and releasing. This action is
repeated 5 times. The second decay test is similar to the ﬁrst one but both cylinders
are pulled at an equal high (as equal as can be visually done by a human at least)
and released. Once again this action is repeated 5 times. Figures 5.3 and 5.4 present
the time series and frequency responses of those two type of decay tests for the two
plate layup (h = 12 mm and h = 2 × 12 mm). As previously explained in Section

one accelerometer, a positive pitch for the other will imply the cylinder moving

stern/right/2 is cylinder moving up). For h = 2×12 mm, the two ﬂexible modes are

In Figure 5.20 one can notice the dips present in the wave range of the frequency

Chapter 6
Conclusions
6.1
Overview
First of all, the second order wave forcing is investigated and applied to a simple
monopile structure. Then, the experiments on the 2-cylinder ﬂexible structure are
simultaneously prepared and planned as well as numerically model.
This initial
numerical modeling of the experiments was a good way of getting some insight in the
behavior of the structure. Two conﬁgurations are investigated, a ﬁxed and a ﬂoating
conﬁguration. Two diﬀerent ﬂexible stiﬀnesses (or frequencies) are investigated for
each conﬁguration. The experiments are then carried out at DHI for a month and
a half. Finally, the results of the experiments are investigated and analysed. The
ﬁxed and ﬂoating conﬁgurations showed diﬀerent behaviors. Two distinctive ﬂexible
modes ("S mode" and "Bird Fly mode") could be observed for each ﬂexible stiﬀness in
the ﬁxed conﬁguration. On the other hand only one ﬂexible mode could be observed
for each stiﬀness set-up in the ﬂoating conﬁguration. The diﬀerent contributions of
the ﬁrst, second, third and fourth harmonic in the excitation of the ﬂexible modes
as well as the rigid body degrees of freedom are also investigated.
6.2
Future Work
Possible future works and improvements are listed hereafter. The order does not
denote priority of any kind.
• Apply ﬁndings from Stefan Master Thesis work [11] : drag and damping into
the modeling of the ﬂexible structure.
• Compare results of this thesis to real life ﬂoaters.
• Compare the modeling of the ﬂexible structure to experiments results in a
statistical approach.
• Improvement of experimental setup, in particular the tuning of structure ﬂex-
ibility. Or, expand structural theory to take multiple plates into account such
that the natural frequencies can be easily estimated.
