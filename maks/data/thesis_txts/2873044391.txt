Abstract 
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are a group of persistent, bioaccumulative 
contaminants frequently detected in groundwater and drinking water sources. This study 
investigates the adsorption behaviour of seven PFAS compounds onto granular activated 
carbon (GAC), specifically AquaSorb 6300 (AS6300), using batch experiments conducted 
with Solhøj groundwater. The experimental setup included various GAC concentrations 
(ranging from 5 to 50 mg/L) and monitored PFAS concentrations at multiple timepoints over 
a seven-day period. 
Despite a carefully structured methodology, the reliability of the results was significantly 
impacted by procedural errors. These included the use of incorrectly prepared stock 
solutions and non-PFAS-certified 0.2 µm nylon syringe filters, which caused compound-
specific losses—particularly of long-chain PFAS—during the sampling process. These 
issues limited the calculation of Kd values for most PFAS compounds. 
However, one compound, 6:2 fluorotelomer sulfonate (6:2 FTS), yielded a valid Kd value of 
approximately 531,000 L/kg. This is nearly five times higher than literature values reported 
for similar GAC materials and may be influenced by either increased affinity for AS6300 or 
overestimation due to experimental artifacts. Notably, 6:2 FTS, along with PFHpA and 
PFHxA, displayed adsorption behaviour that aligned more closely with theoretical 
expectations at GAC concentrations above 20 mg/L, suggesting that longer-chain PFAS 
may overcome filter-related distortions at higher adsorbent dosages. 
Short-chain PFAS such as PFBA and PFPeA showed low removal efficiencies across all 
GAC concentrations, consistent with their lower hydrophobicity and reduced affinity for 
activated carbon. Kinetic trends across most compounds were inconsistent or non-
monotonic, likely due to experimental delays, uneven sampling intervals, and filter 
interference. A separate comparative test also indicated that competitive adsorption may 
reduce removal efficiency in mixed PFAS systems. 
Although conclusive quantitative analysis was limited, the study provides valuable insight 
into the compound-specific adsorption behaviour of PFAS on AS6300 and highlights the 
critical role of experimental standardisation. Chain length and functional group were 
reaffirmed as key factors influencing adsorption, and 6:2 FTS emerged as a compound of 
particular interest. The findings underscore the need for PFAS-certified materials, accurate 
stock preparation, and controlled sampling procedures in future research. This work serves 
as a foundation for improving PFAS removal strategies in drinking water treatment and 
guiding the design of more robust adsorption experiments.

1 Introduction 
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) are long-lasting chemicals that break down 
extremely slowly over time. Due to their widespread use and persistence in the 
environment, these chemicals are present in water, air, fish, and soil across the globe. They 
are also found at low levels in food products as well as in the blood of humans and animals 
worldwide. (EPA, 2024). 
Various research has shown that PFAS can have harmful effects on the human liver and 
contribute to higher serum cholesterol levels in adults (Zhang et al., 2023; Andersen et al., 
2021). Known as "forever chemicals," PFAS are particularly concerning because they build 
up in the liver and kidneys and remain in the bloodstream for long periods. However, the 
way these substances are processed and eliminated from the body is still not well 
understood (Vujic et al., 2024). 
In Denmark almost a 100% of the drinking water is extracted from the groundwater 
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2025). Recent studies have analysed groundwater samples from 1.835 
intake points between 2022-2023, where at least one PFAS compound was detected in 299 
of the 1.835 intakes, which corresponds to 16% of the intake points. 165 of the intake 
points with PFAS detections contained multiple PFAS compounds. In 81 intake points 
corresponding to 4.4%, the combined concentration of 4 PFAS compounds (PFOS, PFOA, 
PFHxS, and PFNA) exceeded the regulatory limit of 0.002 µg/L set by the Danish 
environmental ministry (Thorling et al., 2024). 
PFAS pose significant challenges for conventional remediation methods, limiting the 
development of fast and effective treatment solutions. As a result, commercial adsorbents 
like granular activated carbon (GAC) and ion-exchange resins (IEXs) have become the 
standard rapid response approach. However, the long-term costs of treating PFAS-
contaminated groundwater are expected to be high. Additionally, research indicates that 
the effectiveness of most remediation technologies is influenced by the length of the 
perfluoroalkyl chain, the type of GAC and resin used (Ross et al., 2018). 
Another critical factor influencing the effectiveness of activated carbon filtration is the 
presence of naturally occurring organic matter (NVOC) in the water. NVOC can compete 
with PFAS for adsorption sites on the carbon surface, reducing the filter’s capacity 
(Clausen et al., 2024). Organic matter in groundwater is typically older and has a different 
molecular composition than that found in surface water, which could have a significant 
diminishing effect on the efficiency of PFAS removal in Danish water supplies. 
The limited experimental data on the interaction between NVOC and PFAS in Danish 
groundwater highlights the need for further investigation. Although previous studies have 
explored the interaction between NVOC and PFAS in Danish groundwater, further research 
is needed to better understand the specific impact of NVOC concentrations on the

how varying NVOC levels influence the adsorption capacity of GAC for PFAS. 
This knowledge is crucial for optimizing treatment technologies and ensuring the future

2 Literature Review 
2.1 PFAS Structure and classification 
"Organoflourine compounds" refer to a broad category of organic and inorganic 
compounds containing at least one fluorine (F) atom, exhibiting diverse physical, chemical, 
and biological properties. Synonyms include "fluorochemicals" and "fluorinated 
chemicals"(Buck et al., 2011). A specific subset comprises per- and polyfluoroalkyl 
substances (PFAS), defined as fluorinated compounds containing at least one fully 
fluorinated methyl (–CF₃) or methylene (–CF₂–) carbon atom, without any hydrogen (H), 
chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), or iodine (I) atoms attached (Wang et al., 2011). A better 
overview of this subset can be seen on Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1. A schematic overview of how PFAS is categorised in the ocean of organoflorine compounds. Modified from (OECD, 2021). 
There is no globally accepted categorisation form for PFAS compounds due to their high 
complexity and diversity, and there is no single grouping system that can meet all needs 
(OECD, 2021). The OECD has provided three structural parts for categorising PFAS, the 
functional groups: types and structure and polymeric or non-polymeric, the florinated 
carbon chain: Alkyl or alkylether, perfluoro or polyfluoro, linear, branched or cyclic, 
saturated or non-saturated, polymeric or non-polymeric and the chain-length of the 
florinated carbon chain, and the stoichiometry connection between the two previous 
structures: 1:0, 1:1, 1:3 2:1 etc.  (OECD, 2021). 
PFAS classification is mainly done through the identification of the fluorinated carbon chain 
length in a range between 4C-17C, which is seen as a good predictor of the 
physicochemical properties and the environmental impact (Panieri et al., 2022).

The chain length of the PFAS compounds is counted according to the amount of 
fluorinated carbon atoms (FC-atoms), therefore PFOA has 7 FC-atoms and PFOS 8 FC-
atoms since the 8th carbon in PFOA is not fluorinated but rather part of the carboxylic 
functional group.  
PFAS chain length are categorised into 2 main groups namely: long-chained and short-
chained. There is not a unified distinction regarding this categorisation, but in this project 
long-chained perfluoroalkyl carboxylic acids (PFCA) are defined as FC  7 and long 
chained perfluoroalkane sulfonates (PFSA) are defined as FC  6 as according to the 
OECD (Buck et al., 2011). The definition of “long-chain” for the two groups is different, 
because of the higher tendency for PFSA’s to bioaccumulate in the environment in 
comparison to PFCA’s with equal numbers of FC-atoms (Buck et al., 2011). An overview of

2.1.1 Classification scope of this project 
The 12 PFAS compounds are all non-polymeric per-fluoroalkyl substances, except 6:2 
FTSA which is a non-polymeric1 poly-fluoroalkyl substance. 6:2 FTSA and PFOSA (also 
known as: FOSA) are the only two compounds that do not belong in the perfluoroalkyl 
acids (PFAA’s) since they either lack a fully fluorinated carbon chain or do not possess a 
carboxylic or sulfonic acid functional group. However, they belong to fluorotelomer 
sulfonates (FTSA) and perfluoroalkyl sulphonamides (FASA’s) respectively (see Figure 2), 
which are well known “PFAA precursors” (Pickard H. M., 2022). 
PFAA’s are among the simplest and most commonly monitored types of PFAS in 
environmental studies. These compounds can form through the biotic or abiotic breakdown 
of various PFAS substances. Due to their environmental persistence and resistance to 
further degradation, PFAAs are often termed “terminal PFAS” or “final degradation 
products.” In contrast, polyfluoroalkyl compounds that transform into PFAAs through 
degradation processes are classified as PFAA precursors (Buck et al., 2011).

2.2 PFAS Physiochemical properties 
Due to the fluorine atom’s high ionization potential, electronegativity and small atomic size, 
the carbon-fluorine (C-F) bonds on the PFAS tail provide an outstandingly strong thermal 
and chemical stability and resistance to degradation. These substances are amphiphilic, as 
they consist of a strongly hydrophobic perfluorinated alkyl tail and a hydrophilic terminal 
functional head —typically sulfonic or carboxylic acids. Because of this PFAS functions as 
a highly effective surfactant, capable of substantially reducing the surface tension of water 
in comparison to their hydrocarbon-based counterparts. This variation in physicochemical 
properties, in conjunction with differences in molecular structure and functionalization, has 
contributed to a diverse range of industrial applications. Consequently, PFAS have been 
widely manufactured and utilized in commercial products since the 1940s. (Wang et al., 
2017; Alam et al., 2024; Leung et al., 2023; Panieri et al., 2022). 
PFAS compounds exhibit very low intra- and intermolecular interactions due to the low 
polarizability of fluorine atoms. This characteristic contributes to their significantly higher 
volatility and lower boiling points relative to hydrocarbon analogues of comparable 
molecular mass. Additionally, the weak intermolecular forces result in exceptionally low 
surface tension, thereby enhancing surface wettability. The absence of van der Waals 
interactions adds oleophobic1 properties to these compounds. Although the carbon–
fluorine bond is highly polarized, the symmetrical distribution of these bonds leads to 
cancellation of local dipole moments, rendering the overall molecule nonpolar and 
hydrophobic in nature (Leung et al., 2023). 
In the environment pH, perfluoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) predominantly exist in their anionic 
form, due to their generally low pk_a values, which makes them strongly water soluble. 
PFAAs with longer alkyl chains tend to partition more readily into the organic phase due to 
their increased hydrophobicity, while shorter-chain PFAAs are less likely to do so (Wu et al., 
2020). This difference in behaviour is a primary factor contributing to the challenges of 
capturing shorter-chain PFAAs in GAC filters. 
The majority of PFAS compounds are characterized by low volatility, primarily due to their 
low vapor pressure, low Henry’s law constants, and high boiling points. However, several 
neutral PFAS species—such as perfluorooctane sulfonamides (FOSAs), fluorotelomer 
alcohols (FTOHs), sulfamidoethanols (FOSEs), and fluorotelomer acrylates (FTCAs)—
exhibit volatile or semi-volatile behavior, allowing them to transition into the atmospheric 
phase (Bossi et al., 2016; Panieri et al., 2022). 
In Appendix 7.1, Table 8 presents estimated physicochemical standard properties of 
selected PFAS compounds. Experimental data on the physicochemical properties of PFAS 
remain limited due to their unique chemical characteristics and dynamic behaviour under 
ambient conditions (PubChem, 2025; VMR, 2022; ITRC, 2023; Jesper et al., 2015). 
Furthermore, the few available studies often report values that vary by several orders of

2.3 PFAS Toxicity 
In plants, PFAS can be absorbed through roots and translocated to aerial parts, leading to 
physiological and developmental disturbances. Exposure has been associated with 
inhibited root elongation, reduced biomass, chlorosis, and oxidative stress, likely due to 
interference with water and nutrient uptake mechanisms. The uptake of PFAS by crops also 
presents a potential route of human exposure through the food chain, highlighting the 
relevance of phytotoxicity not only for plant health but also for food safety and ecosystem 
function (Jesper et al., 2015). 
In animals, especially aquatic organisms, PFAS compounds such as PFOA and PFOS have 
been shown to cause a range of sublethal and lethal effects. In fish, PFAS exposure results 
in altered behaviour, suppressed reproductive function, liver damage, and disruption of 
endocrine pathways. Accumulation occurs predominantly in protein rich tissues such as 
the blood, the liver, and the kidneys, as a result of PFAS’s tendency to bind to proteins. 
PFAS can trigger oxidative stress, immune suppression, and developmental delays in both 
aquatic and terrestrial species. The bioaccumulative nature of PFAS poses risks not only to 
individual organisms but to entire food webs (Fenton et al., 2020). 
In humans, PFAS exposure has been epidemiologically linked to multiple adverse health 
outcomes. Notable effects include elevated serum cholesterol, altered liver enzyme levels, 
immune suppression—particularly reduced antibody responses to vaccines—and 
reproductive toxicity, such as decreased fertility and low birth weights. Long-chain PFAS 
such as PFOS and PFOA have long biological half-lives (several years), leading to 
accumulation in blood and tissues. Additional concerns include endocrine disruption, 
thyroid dysfunction, and potential carcinogenicity. Both prenatal and early-life exposures 
are of particular concern due to transplacental transfer and PFAS presence in breast milk, 
which may affect infant development (EFSA, 2018).  
Collectively, the available evidence highlights the wide-ranging toxicity of PFAS across 
biological systems. Their persistence, mobility, and bioaccumulative potential make them a 
critical target for environmental regulation and remediation efforts.

2.4 GAC History 
Activated carbon (AC) has been used for its adsorptive properties since ancient Egypt 
around 1500 BC, where it was applied in water purification and medicine (Tadda et al., 
2016). In Denmark, activated carbon filtration has been part of drinking water treatment 
since the late 1960s, particularly at waterworks treating contaminated groundwater that 
requires post-treatment. Initially, AC filters were introduced to remove substances such as 
BAM and chlorinated solvents. Today, due to the increasing detection of pesticide 
metabolites and emerging contaminants like PFAS in groundwater, AC filtration is widely 
employed as a cost-effective treatment technology, as many of these substances exhibit 
strong adsorption to activated carbon (Arvin et al., 1998). 
2.5 GAC Manufacturing 
Activated carbon (AC), also known as active carbon, activated coal or activated char, is a 
porous adsorption media with extremely high internal porosity, total pore volume, and can 
have an internal surface area in the range 30 – 3000 m2/g (Spencer et al., 2024). ACs are 
commonly manufactured by carbonization, which is treating the raw materials with high 
temperatures and no oxygen, followed by the activation process (Abdulah et al., 2011). 
The activation process can be carried out either physically or chemically. In physical 
activation, the carbonized material is treated with oxidizing gases such as steam or carbon 
dioxide at high temperatures and inert environment. This step removes remaining volatile 
substances and partially burns off the carbon, which helps to form and expand the pore 
structure and creates mesopores. In chemical activation, a chemical substance is mixed 
with the raw material before it is heated in an inert atmosphere. During heating, chemical 
reactions such as dehydration and oxidation occur, which generate the porous structure. 
This method usually requires lower temperatures and often results in a material with a 
more developed porosity and larger surface area compared to physical activation (Abdulah 
et al., 2011). 
AC are either produced from non-renewable unsustainable raw materials: bituminous coal, 
dub-bituminous coal, lignite, anthracite, peat or renewable and sustainable raw materials: 
wood, coconut shells or other carbon rich materials. The raw materials mostly used for AC 
production is Bituminous and sub bituminous coal (Spencer et al., 2024).

2.6 GAC Types and applications 
Activated carbon (AC) contains a network of 
pores classified by IUPAC into micropores 
(<2 nm), mesopores (2–50 nm), and 
macropores (>50 nm). Micropores dominate 
surface area, while larger pores aid in 
molecular transport. These pore sizes are far 
smaller than typical bacteria (1–2 μm), 
preventing microbial intrusion (Lu et al., 2020). 
There are hundreds of AC products available, 
each tailored to specific uses. AC types are 
commonly categorized by production method, 
particle size, and industrial application. This 
classification helps determine suitability for 
water treatment, air purification, or chemical 
recovery (Spencer et al., 2024). The following section outlines key AC types and their 
properties. 
 
Granular activated carbon (GAC), describes AC’s that have been 
milled to size averaging between 0.2 and 5 mm (Ganjoo et al., 2023). 
They are commonly used in column fillers for gas or liquid filtration 
and in the final step of water treatments, they also have the 
possibility of being regenerated for further use, though they do suffer 
from a slow intraparticle diffusion in water (Abdulah et al., 2011, 
Spencer et al., 2024).  
Micro-grain activated carbons (μGACs) are adsorbents with particle sizes ranging from 
0.1 to 0.8 mm, made from materials like bituminous coal, vegetal matter, or 
regenerated sources. They exhibit high surface areas (500–1250 m²/g) 
with micro- and mesoporous volumes. Their properties, including ash 
content, influence adsorption efficiency. μGACs are used in 
wastewater treatment to remove organic micropollutants and 
dissolved organic matter, offering advantages like fluidizability similar 
to powdered AC (PAC) and regenerability similar to GAC, making 
them suitable for advanced industrial water purification processes 
(Guillossou et al., 2020).

Powdered-or pulverised activated carbon (PAC) is a finer type of 
GAC (Spencer et al., 2024) and describes AC’s that have been milled 
to a very fine size averaging <0.075 mm and a mean particle 
diameter (MPD) at <0.045 mm. They are commonly used directly in 
process such as, gravity filters, clarifiers and water intakes (Ganjoo 
et al., 2023). PAC has a large outer surface and a low diffusion 
distance which results in a faster adsorption velocity and a better 
adsorption in liquid phases (Abdulah et al., 2011). 
Activated Carbon Fibers (ACFs) are highly porous fibrous adsorbents derived from 
carbon-rich precursors such as polyacrylonitrile, pitch, phenolic resins, and lignocellulosic 
biomass. They typically possess a fibre diameter between 5–10 µm. 
ACFs are characterized by their exceptional specific surface area—
often exceeding 2000 m²/g—, high microporosity, rapid 
adsorption/desorption kinetics, and ease of regeneration. These 
physicochemical properties make them suitable for advanced 
industrial applications including CO₂ capture, volatile organic 
compound (VOC) removal, water purification, and electrochemical 
devices like supercapacitors (Hassan et al., 2020). 
extruded (pelletised) activated carbon (ECP/EAC), are made from 
anthracite or charcoal together with a compatible binder and then 
extruded at high pressure into cylindric shapes with diameters 
averaging between 0.8 to 130 mm to form “extruded AC” or 
“pressed pellets”. Due to their enhanced strength, lower dust 
content and low- pressure loss, they are applied in gas phase 
treatments (Ganjoo et al., 2023).  
Bead activated carbon (BAC), derived from petroleum pitch, typically 
ranges in diameter from 0.35 to 0.80 mm. It is characterized by high 
mechanical strength, low contaminant concentration, and minimal 
pressure drop during operation. While sharing similar 
physicochemical properties with extruded activated carbon (EAC), 
its spherical shape and smaller particle size offer advantages in water 
filtration applications (Ganjoo et al., 2023).  
Impregnated activated carbon (IAC) is a modified adsorbent material 
in which specific chemical agents—such as metal salts or oxides—
are introduced into the pore structure to enhance selectivity and 
reactivity. It is typically produced from carbon-rich precursors like 
coal or petroleum pitch and formed into granules or pellets with 
diameters ranging from 1 to 4 mm (Ganjoo et al., 2023). The 
impregnation process chemically alters the surface, improving the

capture of contaminants through chemisorption mechanisms, rather than solely relying on 
physical adsorption (Henning & Schäfer, 1993).  
Common impregnating agents include silver, copper, iodine, and iron compounds, 
depending on the targeted pollutant. These materials are particularly effective in removing 
toxic gases such as hydrogen sulfide, ammonia, arsine, and radioactive iodine, and are 
widely applied in air purification, industrial gas treatment, water filtration, and protective 
equipment (Ganjoo et al., 2023; Henning & Schäfer, 1993). 
2.7 Adsorption 
Adsorption is a process in which molecules from a gas or liquid phase attach to the surface 
of a solid material. This process releases heat, making it exothermic. The solid material that 
provides the surface for adsorption is known as the “adsorbent”, while the substances that 
accumulate on its surface are referred to as “adsorbates”. Adsorbents are typically 
characterized by having an extensive network of internal micropores, often on the 
nanometre scale, which is why they are highly effective at capturing and retaining 
adsorbate molecules (Abdulah et al., 2011). 
2.7.1 Adsorption classification 
Adsorption mechanisms are broadly categorized into physical adsorption (physisorption) 
and chemical adsorption (chemisorption). Physisorption involves weak intermolecular 
forces such as Van der Waals, hydrogen bonds, or dipole–dipole interactions, and is 
typically reversible with negligible activation energy. It usually occurs in gas-phase systems 
via molecular condensation in the pores of solids, followed by the diffusion of solute 
molecules into the carbon’s capillary pores. This diffusion rate increases with solute 
concentration and temperature, decreases with molecular weight, and is inversely 
proportional to the square of the particle diameter. In contrast, chemisorption involves the 
formation of strong covalent or ionic bonds via electron exchange, making it irreversible 
and more exothermic due to the strength of the binding forces (Abdulah et al., 2011). 
The adsorption efficiency of AC varies depending on the type of PFAS and the specific AC 
used. Adsorption is primarily driven by hydrophobic interaction. Hydrophobic interactions1 
are more significant for long-chain and sulfonated PFAS due to their higher hydrophobicity, 
making them more readily attracted to the non-polar surfaces of AC (Murray et al., 2021). A 
recent study has concluded that the adsorption behaviour of PFAS is jointly determined by 
two independent factors: the functional groups electrostatic interactions, and the 
fluorinated tails hydrophobic interactions, both contributing additively to the overall 
adsorption affinity (McCall & Thomas, 2025). 
 
 
1 Hydrophobic interactions are Van der Waals forces, but rather a behaviour related to enthalpy and entropy (Sun et al., 2022).

2.7.2 GAC Adsorption dependency 
Granular Activated Carbon (GAC) adsorption for PFAS removal is influenced by several key 
factors related to the GAC structure, the waters physiochemical and the presence of other 
contaminates and particles. One of the most critical parameters is the pore size distribution 
and the surface area of the GAC, with larger surface areas providing more active sites for 
adsorption (Yadav et al., 2022). 
The physicochemical characteristics of the water such as pH, temperature, the presence of 
dissolved organic matter, and co-existing solutes also play a major role. For instance, high 
levels of organic matter can compete with PFAS for adsorption sites, reducing adsorption 
efficiency. GAC’s surface charge, which changes with pH relative to its point of zero charge 
(pHpzc), affects the degree of electrostatic attraction or repulsion to anionic PFAS 
compounds. Most GAC is negatively charged at alkaline pH, which can repel PFAS, 
reducing adsorption. Additionally, Contact Time affects how long water remains in contact 
with the GAC, influencing removal rates. In general, longer contact times can improve 
PFAS removal but may also lead to more organic matter blockage, limiting available 
adsorption sites (Yadav et al., 2022). 
Particles in the water and substances that can precipitate in the carbon, can significantly 
reduce the adsorption capacity of activated carbon by clogging its fine pore system. 
Several factors can contribute to this reduction in performance, such as the accumulation 
of fine organic and inorganic particles within the pores, and the precipitation of substances 
like iron, manganese, and calcium carbonate (lime). In addition, the adsorption of non-
volatile organic compounds (NVOC) can also diminish the effectiveness of the carbon 
(Arvin et al., 1998). 
GAC performance is a balance of material properties, water chemistry, and operational 
conditions.

2.8 NVOC 
Non-volatile organic compounds (NVOCs) are a group of organic substances characterized 
by their low volatility, high molecular weight, and often high polarity or thermal instability. 
These properties render them unsuitable for analysis by conventional gas chromatography 
(GC), distinguishing them from volatile organic compounds (VOCs). NVOCs are ubiquitous 
in aquatic environments, originating from both natural and anthropogenic sources. Natural 
sources include the degradation of plant and microbial matter, which contributes humic 
and fulvic acids to water systems. Anthropogenic sources encompass industrial 
discharges, agricultural runoff containing pesticides and herbicides, pharmaceutical 
residues, and byproducts formed during water treatment processes such as chlorination or 
ozonation. The presence of NVOCs in water is of significant concern due to their potential 
health effects, particularly when they transform into halogenated compounds during 
disinfection, which may exhibit mutagenic or carcinogenic properties. (Crathorne et al., 
1982) 
2.8.1 NVOC in Danish groundwater 
The primary source of NVOCs is fossil organic material preserved in marine and glacial 
deposits, such as Miocene and Quaternary sediments. These sediments, rich in ancient 
organic matter like humic and fulvic acids, are particularly prevalent in regions with 
historical marine influence, such as Western Jutland, Southern Jutland, and parts of 
Zealand. Over time, groundwater interacts with these sediments, dissolving the organic 
compounds under specific hydro chemical conditions (Ernstsen et al., 2005). 
Key factors facilitating the release of NVOCs into groundwater include ion exchange 
reactions and redox processes. In marine sediments, sodium ions (Na⁺) often replace 
calcium (Ca²⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺) in clay minerals, leading to higher pH levels and 
reduced calcium concentrations. This ion exchange enhances the solubility of organic 
matter, allowing NVOCs to leach into the groundwater. Additionally, anoxic (oxygen-
depleted) conditions in deep, confined aquifers slow down microbial degradation,

The geographical distribution of NVOC-rich groundwater aligns with areas dominated by

artesian aquifers with limited water flow, such as those in Ribe, Ringkøbing, and Als. These 
aquifers often have long groundwater residence times, further promoting the dissolution 
and accumulation of organic matter in the groundwater (Ernstsen et al., 2005). 
Once NVOCs enter the groundwater, they are extracted through wells and boreholes, 
becoming part of the drinking water supply. The presence of these compounds can lead to 
aesthetic issues, such as brown discoloration and unpleasant taste, as well as challenges in 
water treatment (Ernstsen et al., 2005). 
2.8.2 NVOC and GAC adsorption 
The complexity and diversity of NVOCs in water pose considerable challenges for their 
isolation and analysis. Common techniques for isolating NVOCs from water include freeze-
drying, reverse-osmosis, activated carbon adsorption, and XAD resin adsorption. Among 
these, adsorption onto granular activated carbon (GAC) is widely employed due to its 
efficiency in concentrating a broad range of organic compounds, particularly non-polar to 
moderately polar species (Crathorne & Watts, 1982). 
A study conducted in 2024 found that NVOC competes with PFAS to the GAC active sites, 
and results in a lower PFAS adsorption to the GAC (Clausen et al., 2024). Denmark has set 
a drinking water limit of 4 mg/L NVOC to maintain aesthetic quality (colour, taste, odour), 
ensure microbial stability, and reduce the risk of disinfection byproduct formation, such as 
trihalomethanes. The limit is not health-based but helps preserve overall water quality 
(Ernstsen et al., 2005).

The 60% removal threshold was selected as a practical target that balances two competing 
risks: firstly, using too much GAC, which could lead to complete adsorption and hinder the 
ability to observe equilibrium behaviour, and secondly, using too little GAC, which may 
result in insufficient adsorption, especially for shorter-chained PFAS, making it difficult to 
detect clear trends in the equilibrium curve. Thus, 60% removal provides a representative 
middle ground, ensuring that adsorption dynamics are observable and quantifiable while 
avoiding saturation or negligible adsorption. 
To account for uncertainties such as compound competition, the possibility of equilibrium 
not being reached, or discrepancies between theoretical and actual adsorption behaviour, 
a range of GAC concentrations was selected both above and below the calculated values. 
The final experimental scheme can be seen in Appendix 7.2, Table 10.

PFAS compounds of different chain lengths. Longer-chain PFAS are expected to 
outcompete shorter chains due to stronger adsorption affinity. For this test, PFHpA (C7)

selected for individual bottle experiments. Each was tested at a concentration of 2 µg/L, 
matching their respective concentrations in the main mixture experiment. The GAC 
concentration was set at 5 mg/L for both compounds, based on the M_AS6300 calculation. 
While this side test is not intended to provide definitive evidence, it serves as an initial

3.5 Initial Batch experiment 
3.5.1 preparations 
Initially, three stock solutions were prepared for the PFAS compounds. The first solution 
contained a mixture of 11 PFAS compounds, the second contained only PFHpA, and the 
third contained only PFPeA. These solutions were prepared by the lab supervisors using 
individual raw stock solutions with concentrations of 1 g/L1 for each of the 11 PFAS. The 
laboratory supervisor prepared stock solutions by adding 10 µL of each of the individual 
raw stock solutions for the 11 PFAS compounds to 890 µL of methanol. This resulted in a 
combined, diluted stock solution containing all 11 PFAS, each at a final concentration of 
10 mg/L2. Throughout this project, this prepared diluted mixture will be referred to as “the 
diluted stock solution”. Additionally, separate stock solutions of PFHpA and PFPeA were 
prepared by adding 10 µL of each compound to 990 µL of methanol, also yielding final 
concentrations of 10 mg/L for each compound. All prepared stock solutions were stored in 
labelled LC-MS-grade glass vials.  
Using a mass balance calculation (an example can be seen in Appendix. 7.2 in 
subsection7.2.1), the required contaminant volume to be added to the solution mixture was 
determined to be 0.04mL. The corresponding volumes of diluted activated carbon were 
also calculated and are shown in Appendix.7.3, Table 13. 
3.5.2 Initiation 
A total of 10 numbered 200mL volumetric flasks were prepared, each filled to 
approximately two-thirds with Solhøj groundwater. The 11-PFAS stock solution was added 
to 83 of the flasks, while PFHpA and PFPeA stock solutions were added to the remaining 
two flasks separately with a P100 (10-100 µL) micropipette. A 10-and 100-times dilution of 
the GAC stock mixture was prepared. The calculated volumes of diluted GAC (shown in 
Appendix.7.3, Table 13) were added to each flask using a P5000 (500–5000 µL) or a 
P10000 (1000–10000 µL) micropipette and the time of addition was recorded. To simplify 
later sample extraction, a 3–5minute interval was maintained between each addition of the 
GAC dilutions. After adding the GAC dilution, each flask was filled with water up to the 
calibration line, capped, and inverted three times to ensure proper mixing. 
The content of the 11 volumetric flasks was transferred to 11 labelled bluecap bottles and 
transported to the orbital shaker in the 10-degree room at 90 rpm. 
In order to find the equilibrium point, an interval of different GAC concentration with a 
constant contaminant- and NVOC-concentration is experimented with over a period of 7 
days and samples will be taken after 2hours, 2days, 3d, 4d, 5d, 6d, and 7days to ensure 
that the equilibrium state is identified if it has been reached.

Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) represents the integration of two 
highly selective analytical techniques, enabling the isolation and quantification of target 
analytes within complex mixtures. The liquid chromatography (LC) component separates 
compounds based on their physicochemical properties—such as polarity, size, and affinity 
for the stationary phase—while the mass spectrometry (MS) component further 
distinguishes compounds by their mass-to-charge (m/z) ratios. This dual-mode selectivity 
significantly enhances the analytical resolution and sensitivity of the method, rendering LC-
MS a powerful and widely adopted tool in both qualitative and quantitative analysis. As 
illustrated in Figure 10, the mass spectrometer not only functions as a detector for the LC 
system but also provides structural information about the analytes, thereby allowing for the 
identification of compounds corresponding to each chromatographic peak based on their 
unique mass spectra (Sargent, 2013).

on their physicochemical properties and mass-to-charge (m/z) ratios. Retrieved from (Sargent, 2013).

process, aside from transferring the samples and the diluted stock solution vials in to the

concentrations observed prompted a thorough review of both the experimental initiation

Upon presenting all materials used during the experiment to the laboratory supervisors, it 
was identified that the Minisart® NML syringe filters were the only component not certified 
for use in PFAS analysis under the laboratory's standard procedures. This highlighted the 
filters as a potential source of PFAS loss due to sorption to the filter. The use of syringe 
filters in this study was based on methodologies employed in previous experiments 
conducted at DTU, where identical filters were used in similar experiments (Vujic, 2024; 
Weinreich, 2023). 
Upon further investigation, it was observed that the syringe filters used in the studies by 
Vujic (2024) and Weinreich (2023) were 0.45 µm Minisart® NML filters composed of 
cellulose acetate, whereas the filter used in the present experiment was a 0.2 µm Minisart® 
NML filter made of nylon. Several studies have demonstrated that PFAS compounds can 
adsorb to various plastics (Shi et al., 2023; Bhagwat et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2024), with 
specific evidence highlighting significant adsorption to polyamides (PA), such as nylon 
(Mejías et al., 2023). Additionally, a study (He et al.,2024) directly examined PFAS mass 
retention in syringe filters, including both 0.45 µm glass-fiber cellulose acetate and 0.45 µm 
nylon filters, further emphasizing the potential for material-dependent PFAS losses during 
filtration. 
 
Figure 12. “Percent of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) mass retained as a function of filtrate volume for different filter 
chemistries. The data were collected by passing solutions with 100 µg L−1 PFAS at pH 7.6 through (b) 0.45-µm glass-fiber cellulose 
acetate, (c) 0.45-µm nylon, Data are the average of three replicates” (He et al., 2024). 
The study by He et al. (2024) demonstrates that nylon filters exhibit significantly higher 
PFAS retention compared to cellulose acetate filters, particularly at low filtrate volumes (≤1 
mL). In the present experiment, where only 1 mL of sample was filtered, the retention of 
PFOS and PFOSA exceeded 95%, while PFHxS retention surpassed 50%. These findings 
align with the observed discrepancies in PFAS concentrations, as the near-complete 
retention of certain compounds (e.g., PFOS, PFOSA) and substantial losses of others (e.g., 
PFHxS) would lead to artificially low measured values in the filtrate. 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
Nylon (PA) membranes possess amide functional groups that facilitate strong interactions 
with PFAS, particularly long-chain and hydrophobic species (Mejías et al., 2023). The 
extreme retention at low volumes (<5 mL) is attributed to initial adsorption onto unoccupied 
binding sites on the filter matrix, as demonstrated by He et al. (2024), who reported that 
PFAS retention stabilizes only after conditioning with ~30 mL of sample as seen on Figure 
12c. This explains why the present experiment, which used minimal filtrate volume (1 mL), 
resulted in near-total loss of certain PFAS. 
While all filters retain PFAS to some degree, reported retention efficiencies vary due to: 
1. Filter Material: Nylon consistently shows higher retention than cellulose acetate 
2. Filtrate Volume: Using larger volumes (e.g., 30 mL), lower retention due to filter 
saturation. 
3. PFAS Properties: Hydrophobicity and chain length dictate retention, with long-chain 
PFAS (e.g., PFOS, PFOA) retained more strongly (He et al., 2024). 
4. Water Matrix: Dissolved organic matter (DOM) can compete for sorption sites, 
reducing PFAS retention (Qi et al., 2022). 
In contrast to nylon, cellulose acetate filters exhibit lower PFAS retention, as evidenced by 
Vujic (2024) and Weinreich (2023), who reported better permeation using 0.45 µm 
cellulose acetate filters. However, even these studies likely underestimated true aqueous 
PFAS concentrations, as cellulose acetate filters still retain 17–38% of PFOS at equilibrium 
(He et al., 2024). The smaller pore size (0.2 µm) of the nylon filter used in the present study 
may have contributed to increased retention of PFAS, potentially exacerbating compound 
losses during filtration (He et al., 2024). This may help explain the substantial reduction in 
PFAS concentrations observed in Table 7, where losses of approximately 49% or greater 
were recorded. 
Overall, two primary factors likely account for the observed inconsistencies in PFAS 
concentration results. First, the original stock solutions used for preparing the mixtures 
were likely mislabelled or inaccurately concentrated, as evidenced by LC-MS/MS analysis 
and supervisor input. Second, the use of 0.2 µm nylon syringe filters introduced substantial 
compound-specific PFAS losses, particularly for long-chain species, due to strong 
adsorption at low filtrate volumes. These combined issues significantly undermined the 
accuracy of the measured concentrations.

4.2.1 Comparison to literature. 
This project succeeded in calculating the Kd value for only one of the seven investigated 
PFAS compounds. The compound for which the Kd value was estimated—6:2 FTS —is not 
examined in any of the literature referenced in this project, except for the study by Xiao et 
al. (2017), who reported a Kd value of 109,648 L/kg on GAC F300. The Kd value obtained 
in this study is approximately five times higher.  
Assuming AS6300 has similar Kd values to F400 due to their more comparable 
physicochemical properties, the results appear unexpected. Typically, F300 demonstrates 
better adsorption of PFOS and PFHxS than F400 (as shown in Table 2), indicating a higher 
affinity for PFAS compounds with sulfonic functional groups. Since 6:2 FTS is a known 
PFOS precursor and also contains a sulfonic group, it would be reasonable to expect a 
lower Kd value for F400. 
Structurally, the main difference between 6:2 FTS and PFOS lies in the first two carbon 
centers after the sulfonic group: in 6:2 FTS, these are bonded to hydrogen atoms rather 
than fluorine atoms. While C–F bonds are often associated with greater electronegativity, 
the enhanced hydrophobicity of perfluorinated compounds is primarily attributed to their 
increased molecular volume and surface area. As shown by Dalvi & Rossky (2010), this 
“fatness” leads to a greater free-energy cost for cavity formation in water, thereby 
strengthening hydrophobic interactions. The replacement of fluorine atoms with smaller 
hydrogen atoms reduces this effect, likely making 6:2 FTS less hydrophobic and less 
strongly adsorbed than PFOS. Therefore, it is unlikely that F400 would show stronger 
adsorption for 6:2 FTS than for PFOS. 
Although AS6300 differs from F400 and could potentially exhibit higher overall adsorption 
capacity, the reliability of the result presented here is questionable due to several factors: 
major experimental errors, the absence of replicates, and a lack of supporting literature for 
validation. Therefore, the Kd value estimated in this study should be interpreted cautiously 
and regarded only as an indicative or comparative value for future research—not as a 
definitive or conclusive result.

4.3 Comparing separate and mixed PFAS adsorption 
 
Figure 17. Separate PFHpA & PFPeA adsorption on 4.2 mg/L GAC (PFHpA sep, PFPeA sep) compared to mixed PFHpA & PFPeA 
adsorption (PFHpA, PFPeA) from the 11 PFAS mix on 4.2 mg/L GAC.   
The goal of this experiment was to investigate whether there is a significant difference in 
the adsorption of PFAS to GAC when the compounds are tested individually versus in a 
mixed PFAS solution. 
Although it was not possible to calculate the Kd values for most compounds due to 
experimental errors and lack of quantifiable data, the previous section demonstrated that 
data points collected within the same sampling period showed strong internal consistency 
and a clear trend. Based on this, it is reasonable to compare data collected at the same 
time point across different experimental setups. 
Figure 17 shows some differences between the two scenarios for PFBA, with a lesser 
noticeable variation observed for PFHpA. The results suggest that individually tested PFAS 
compounds exhibit higher adsorption to GAC than when present in a mixture. This implies 
potential competition or interaction between PFAS compounds that may inhibit their 
adsorption in mixed solutions. 
Due to the differing sensitivities of the compounds to experimental error sources, it is not 
possible to directly compare short- and long-chain PFAS in this study. PFBA, being a 
shorter-chain compound, is less affected by adsorption to the nylon filters, whereas longer-
chain compounds like PFHpA are more strongly influenced by this effect. As a result, any 
observed differences in adsorption behaviour between short- and long-chain PFAS cannot 
be reliably interpreted within the scope of this experiment. 
This study suggests that mixing PFAS compounds influences their overall adsorption 
behavior. Although the experiment was not replicated, the primary objective—to investigate 
whether there is an indication of a difference between individual and mixed compound 
adsorption—has been achieved.

5 Conclusion 
This project investigated the adsorption behaviour of seven per- and polyfluoroalkyl 
substances (PFAS) onto the granular activated carbon (GAC) type AquaSorb 6300 
(AS6300) using batch experiments in Solhøj groundwater. Although the experimental 
framework was carefully designed, significant procedural errors compromised the reliability 
and interpretability of the results. Chief among these were discrepancies in the stock 
solution concentrations, identified as likely stemming from mislabelled or inaccurately 
diluted PFAS standards, and the unintended use of 0.2 µm nylon syringe filters not certified 
for PFAS analysis, which led to substantial compound-specific losses during sampling. 
Despite the challenges encountered, some meaningful insights could still be drawn from 
the study. Of the seven PFAS compounds analyzed, 6:2 fluorotelomer sulfonate (6:2 FTS) 
was the only compound for which a valid and quantifiable distribution coefficient (Kd) could 
be determined, estimated at approximately 531,000 L/kg. This value is notably higher than 
the Kd reported by Xiao et al. (2017) for the same compound on GAC F300 (109,648 L/kg), 
suggesting a potential overestimation in the current study. Given the comparable 
physicochemical properties of AS6300 and F400, and the generally lower adsorption 
affinity of 6:2 FTS compared to more perfluorinated compounds like PFOS, this 
unexpectedly high Kd value warrants caution. 
Nevertheless, the fact that 6:2 FTS maintained a coherent adsorption pattern—particularly 
at GAC concentrations above 20 mg/L—despite the known filter-induced losses, suggests 
that its affinity for AS6300 may be strong enough to partially overcome the distorting 
effects of the filtration process. This resilience may also be observed in PFHpA and PFHxA, 
the next longest-chain PFAS in the mixture, which begin to follow expected adsorption 
trends as GAC concentration increases. 
Other PFAS compounds, especially long-chain species such as PFOS, PFOA, and PFNA, 
were mostly undetectable after filtration, likely due to extreme adsorption to the nylon 
filters. These losses were consistent with published data showing that nylon membranes 
exhibit near-complete retention of long-chain PFAS at low sample volumes. While shorter-
chain PFAS like PFBA and PFPeA were more consistently measurable, their adsorption to 
GAC was low, as expected based on their weaker hydrophobic interactions. 
Kinetic analysis revealed inconsistent trends. Instead of approaching equilibrium, many 
compounds showed fluctuating or increasing concentrations over time, contradicting 
typical sorption behaviour. This anomaly may stem from filter sorption dominating early 
sampling points, combined with procedural delays and inconsistencies in timing (as noted 
in Appendix 7.3.2). The presence of such time-related inconsistencies further complicates 
any attempt to model meaningful kinetic behaviour from the dataset. 
A comparison between PFHpA and PFPeA in individual versus mixed solutions was also 
attempted to explore potential competitive adsorption effects. Although the experiment was 
too limited to draw firm conclusions, results suggested slightly higher removal in the 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
separate setups, possibly due to reduced inter-compound competition. However, this trend 
was not supported by replicate data and must be considered only indicative. 
In summary, while the project encountered several methodological setbacks, it did produce 
some indicative results that support existing literature. Chain length and functional group 
clearly remain critical factors in PFAS adsorption to GAC, with longer chains showing 
greater affinity. The experiment underscores the importance of high-quality, PFAS-certified 
materials, accurate stock solution preparation, and precise timing in sampling. Moreover, 
the unusual robustness of 6:2 FTS in the face of data noise may point toward interesting 
compound-specific properties that warrant further study. 
Future investigations should include replicate experiments, a broader range of GAC 
materials, and a systematic evaluation of filter materials to reduce experimental variability. 
The use of PFAS-certified filters and standardized sampling times is especially crucial to 
obtain accurate concentration profiles. Despite its limitations, this study contributes 
valuable insights to the field of PFAS adsorption, particularly with respect to the 
performance of AS6300 and the behaviour of mid-length PFAS like 6:2 FTS under practical 
water treatment conditions.

First experiment (Equilibrium investigation) 
First experiment Purpose: To identify the point at which PFAS removal by GAC-AS6300 
stabilizes at a consistent level below complete removal, indicating an adsorption 
equilibrium. 
1. 8 (250mL) bluecap bottles are filled with measured water from Solhøj groundwater.  
(The amount of groundwater added is different for every bottle since the volume of 
stock liquids added are different. The proportional amount can be seen on Table 13 
for every bottle) 
2. 11PFAS stock solution is added to all 8 bottles. 
(The total 11PFAS concentration in each of the 8 bottles should be 22µg/L, where 
each PFAS compound is 2 µg/L) 
3. The bottles are slowly shaken to mix the liquids. 
4. The GAC stock suspension is rotated 10 times to ensure evenly distribution of GAC 
particles. 
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
5. The necessary volume of GAC suspension is added to 1 of the bluecap bottles 
(250mL), containing Solhøj groundwater, through a pipette to reach the specific 
GAC concentrations needed. The start time for the first bottle is immediately noted 
after adding the active carbon suspension. 
(The necessary amount of GAC suspension can be seen on Table 13 for all 8 
bottles) 
(Make a diluted Gac suspension for the smaller volumes)  
6. The bluecap bottle containing all the ingrediencies is now covered with aluminium 
paper, capped with the bluecap, rotated 10 times and placed on the orbital shaker in 
the 10oC room at 90rpm. 
7. The same procedure in step 5 and 6 is repeated on 6 of the remaining 7 bluecap 
bottles, while varying the GAC concentration for every bottle. 
8. The last bluecap bottle is left as a control for the experiment. 
After 2 hours. 
9. One of the bluecap bottles is taken from the orbital shaker for sampling. The bottle 
is rotated 10 times before sampling to ensure a homogenic mixture. A sample of 1 
mL is taken from the mixture and filtrated with 3 mL Terumo ® syringe and a 
Minisart® NML syringe filter (0.45 µm) made from cellulose acetate and the bottle is 
returned to the orbital shaker in its original state.  
10. The same procedure of step 9 is done on the remaining 7 bottles. 
11. Step 9 and 10 will be repeated after respectively 1day, 2days, 3days, 4days, 5days, 
6 days, and 7days. 
Second experiment (Competition hypothesis) 
Second experiment Purpose: To see whether the presence of longer chain PFAS’s affect 
the adsorption of other long- and short chained PFAS’s. 
1. 2 (250mL) bluecap bottles are filled with measured water from Solhøj groundwater. 
2. PFHpA stock solution is added to 1 bottle and PFPeA stock solution is added to 
another bottle. 
(The total 11PFAS concentration in each of the 2 bottles should be 2µg/L) 
3. The bottles are slowly shaken to mix the liquids. 
4. The GAC stock suspension is rotated 10 times to ensure evenly distribution of GAC 
grains. 
5. The necessary amount of GAC suspension is added to 1 of the bluecap bottles 
(250mL), containing Solhøj groundwater, through a pipette to reach the specific 
GAC concentrations needed. The start time for the first bottle is immediately noted

the 10oC room at 90rpm. 
7. The same procedure in step 5 and 6 is repeated on the second bottle.
